                                                  A BSTRACT
024
               Adversarial patches, often used to provide physical stealth protection for critical
025
               assets and assess perception algorithm robustness, usually neglect the need for vi-
026
               sual harmony with the background environment, making them easily noticeable.
027            Moreover, existing methods primarily concentrate on improving attack perfor-
028            mance, disregarding the intricate dynamics of adversarial patch elements. In this
029            work, we introduce the Camouflaged Adversarial Pattern Generator (CAPGen), a
030            novel approach that leverages specific base colors from the surrounding environ-
031            ment to produce patches that seamlessly blend with their background for superior
032            visual stealthiness while maintaining robust adversarial performance. We delve
033            into the influence of both patterns (i.e., color-agnostic texture information) and
034            colors on the effectiveness of attacks facilitated by patches, discovering that pat-
035
               terns exert a more pronounced effect on performance than colors. Based on these
               findings, we propose a rapid generation strategy for adversarial patches. This in-
036
               volves updating the colors of high-performance adversarial patches to align with
037
               those of the new environment, ensuring visual stealthiness without compromising
038            adversarial impact. This paper is the first to comprehensively examine the roles
039            played by patterns and colors in the context of adversarial patches.

042   1 I NTRODUCTION
043   Adversarial attacks pose a significant challenge in deep learning, aiming to deceive pre-trained mod-
044   els by subtly altering input data like images (Dong et al., 2019), videos (Wei et al., 2022), and
045   text (Ye et al., 2022). These attacks are broadly categorized into digital and physical. Unlike digital
046   attacks that manipulate data within the digital domain, physical attacks interact with the physical
047   environment, presenting unique practical implications and challenges. This distinction underlines
048   the importance of physical attacks, as they have direct consequences for the deployment of deep-
049   learning models in real-world applications, ranging from autonomous driving (Qian et al., 2020) to
050
      facial recognition payment systems (Vakhshiteh et al., 2021), thereby elevating the security risks
      associated with these technologies (Kurakin et al., 2016; Athalye et al., 2018b).
051
052   Physical attacks must account for variables such as lighting conditions, angles of view, and phys-
053   ical distances, which can affect the visibility and effectiveness of adversarial inputs. For instance,
      adversarial patches—a prevalent method in physical attacks—must be designed to maintain their

      Recent researches in physical adversarial attacks have extensively explored adversarial patches,
073   demonstrating their potential to compromise machine learning models in real-world scenarios. No-
074   table advancements include AdvPatch (Thys et al., 2019), AdvCloak (Wu et al., 2020), and T-
075   SEA (Huang et al., 2023b), each targeting unique adversarial manipulation aspects. Despite their
076   innovative attack mechanisms, these methods often overlook the importance of patch concealment,
077   making them easily detectable to human observers and thus reducing their practical applicability.
078   Efforts to enhance stealthiness, such as those in (Huang et al., 2020) and (Hu et al., 2021b), have
079   introduced naturalistic adversarial textures, aiming for a dual deception of machine vision systems
080   and human detection. Besides using regular textures for deception, more recent methods consider
081
      the background environment by incorporating camouflage or 3D neural rendering. To address visual
      distortions in existing methods, CamoPatch (Williams & Li, 2024) devises an adversarial patch us-
082
      ing semi-transparent RGB-valued circles. Although the designed adversarial texture exhibits good
083
      concealability in some specific scenes, its generation process is isolated from the background en-
084   vironment. This causes a noticeable mismatch between the adversarial texture and the background
085   environment in new scenes, diminishing its confusing effect.

087
      As shown in the left of Fig. 2, adversarial patch methods usually do not sufficiently consider the
      need for adversarial inputs to blend seamlessly into their surroundings or to be quickly and efficiently
088
      updated in response to changing environments. This oversight results in adversarial strategies that,
089
      while potentially effective in controlled laboratory settings, fall short in complex real-world scenar-
090   ios, where adaptability is paramount (Duan et al., 2021). In addition to application considerations,
091   there is a lack of in-depth exploration of adversarial patch components, which also limits understand-
092   ing of physical attacks. Examining the characteristics of adversarial noise in complex and dynamic
093   tasks contributes to the development of more general and robust adversarial patches.
094
      In response to these limitations, we propose a novel adversarial patch generation strategy that prior-
095
      itizes environmental harmony and rapid adaptability, as shown in the right of Fig. 2. By addressing
096   the critical gaps left by previous methods, our work aims to enhance the robustness and security
097   of machine learning models against physical adversarial threats, contributing valuable insights to
098   the ongoing discourse on machine learning safety and reliability. After formulating the problem by
099   considering both the attack performances and the environmental harmony to human observers, we
100   further propose a method of Camouflage Adversarial Pattern Generator (CAPGen) that can generate
101   the adversarial patches effectively.
102   To improve the visual invisibility of adversarial patches, the specific base colors are extracted from
103   the practical environment, and a patch generation method is designed based on those colors, so as
104   to alleviate the problem of poor visual stealthiness caused by the inconsistency between the patch
105   colors and the environment colors. In more detail, we calculate the probability of each pixel in the
106   adversarial patch for each base color by optimizing a color probability matrix. We ensure that each
107   pixel value corresponds to only one base color by regularizing the color probability matrix. This
      process allows us to generate an adversarial patch with controllable colors.

155   2   R ELATED W ORK
156   Physical adversarial attacks. As mentioned above, the physical adversarial attack is a technology
157   that adds adversarial perturbations to the objects in the physical world to deceive or mislead the deep
158   models. Typically, a physical attack on traffic signs is designed, which achieves deception of detec-
159   tors by minimizing an adversarial loss function (Song et al., 2018). Thys et al. design adversarial
160   patches by attacking the confidence scores of detectors, aiming to evade pedestrian detection sys-
161   tems (Thys et al., 2019). To deal with the impact of distortions brought by the physical space on the
      attack performance, Expectation Over Transformation (EOT) operations are widely used in various


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      physical attack algorithms (Athalye et al., 2018b). To get more natural attack effects on object de-
163   tection systems, clothing with adversarial patches is created (Wu et al., 2020). To alleviate non-rigid
164   deformation, TPS is proposed on clothing (Xu et al., 2020). By replacing adversarial patches with
165   animal shapes, Huang et al. achieve more natural attack effects (Huang et al., 2020). To enhance
166   the naturalness of the adversarial clothing, Hu et al. extend the adversarial patch to cover the entire
167   clothing using circular topological cropping techniques (Hu et al., 2022). Recent work (Li et al.,
168   2023) is proposed to assess the naturalness of physical attacks. However, the adversarial texture
169   generation process of these methods is isolated from the background environment. This results in a
170   noticeable disparity between the adversarial texture and the background environment in new scenes,
171
      diminishing its confusing effect.
172   Camouflage. Traditional camouflage technologies use the color and geometric features of some
173   patterns like stripes or spots to blur and disrupt the edges between the target object and the back-
174   ground image, countering close-range and low-resolution optical reconnaissance (Xue et al., 2016;
175   Owens et al., 2014; Guo et al., 2023; Kajiura et al., 2021). These methods usually take a substantial
176   amount of time to achieve effective camouflage in specific scenarios. Lately, neural radiation field
177
      technologies have been used for camouflage by utilizing pre-captured object meshes and multi-view
      photos (Guo et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). This allows the generation of camouflage for various
178
      locations and angles. Obviously, this method requires specific data support and involves intricate
179
      3D object mesh construction. It is worth noting, as shown in the middle of Fig. 2, that traditional
180   camouflage is common means of protection aimed at deceiving human eyes, but lacking of consider-
181   ation of intelligent models. Recently, Adversarial camouflages are gaining attention as a new means
182   of physical attack, they attack objects in multi-view and multi-scene by rendering the adversarial
183   texture in a 3D simulation environment (Suryanto et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022;
184   2021). They usually use sophisticated networks and some prepared multi-view images to synthesize
185   hard-to-discern object textures. Except for time-consuming optimization, the upfront object mesh is
186   also a substantial workload, making the application relatively challenging.
187   The above-mentioned methods face different practical challenges, such as poor visual stealthiness,
188   long periods for generating adversarial texture, and complex preparation tasks like 3D modeling or
189   specific simulation environments. This paper prioritizes practicality, focusing mainly on the physical
190   attack mode of adversarial patches. It proposes a more practical and visually concealed means by
191   exploring the generation process of adversarial patches, considering both patterns and colors.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
199
200   In the domain of adversarial attacks against object detection systems, our investigation is centered
201   on the design of adversarial patches. These are input modifications designed to be placed within a
202   physical scene to compromise the accuracy of object detection models in classifying or detecting
203   objects accurately. This challenge is of particular significance under varying real-world conditions,
204   such as changes in lighting, observation angles, and distances between objects. The formulation
205   involves several core components:
206
207
             • Object Detection Model (M ): A model parameterized by weights θ, taking an input image
               I and outputting bounding boxes B and class labels C for detected objects.
208
209          • Target Object (O): The object that the adversary intends to hide from detection or has
210            misclassified by M .
211          • Adversarial Patch (P ): A pixel matrix intended for placement near O, engineered to
212            induce detection or classification errors in M .
213
214   We aim to optimize P to maximize the loss function L, causing M to fail in its detection or classi-
215
      fication tasks:
                                     \max _{P} \; L(M(I + \delta (P, O, \phi )), O, \theta ) \vspace {-0.1cm}  (1)


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      where L is the loss function measuring the model’s error due to the patch; δ(P, O, ϕ) denotes the
217   application of patch P to image I in the context of the object O, with ϕ including parameters
218   like the patch’s placement, orientation, and scale relative to O. Optimizing P requires adhering to
219   constraints that ensure:
220
221                    • Robustness to Environmental Variations: The patch must preserve its adversarial nature
222                      under diverse real-world scenarios, necessitating data augmentation to simulate various
223                      environmental conditions during development.
224                    • Stealth and Deception: Beyond evasion of detection by M , P should not attract human
225                      attention, ideally blending with its surroundings or appearing as a benign object.
226
      To incorporate these considerations, we propose an advanced optimization framework:
227
228                   \label {eq2_overall} \max _{P} \mathbb {E}_{\phi \sim \Phi } \left [L(M(I + \delta (P, O, \phi ; \epsilon )), O, \theta )\right ] - \lambda _1 R(P;\phi ) - \lambda _2 S(P; \epsilon ) \vspace {-0.1cm}    (2)
229   where \protect \mathbb  {E}_{\phi \sim \Phi } denotes the expected value over a distribution of conditions \Phi  (lighting, angles, dis-
230   tances), encapsulating the robustness to variances; δ represents the application of the patch P onto
231   image I of object O, modified by environmental parameters ϕ and stealth parameters ϵ. Moreover,
232   R(P ; ϕ) quantifies the patch’s robustness across varying conditions, encouraging minimal perfor-
233   mance degradation; S(P ; ϵ) measures the stealth and deceptive qualities of the patch, ensuring it
234   does not attract undue attention from human observers; and λ1 and λ2 are regularization coefficients
235   that balance the trade-off between robustness, stealth, and adversarial effectiveness.
236   This comprehensive formulation outlines a nuanced approach for the development of adversarial
237   patches, emphasizing the need for effectiveness in disrupting object detection algorithms while en-
238   suring resilience to environmental changes and avoidance of human detection. Balancing these
239   factors requires careful consideration of both adversarial goals and the operational context.

241   3.2            C AMOUFLAGED A DVERSARIAL PATTERN G ENERATOR
242
243   As illustrated in Eq.(2), the stealth and deception of the adversarial patch (controlled by L(·) and
244
      S(·), respectively) are contradictory. If S(·) is overemphasized, the patch may lose its adversar-
      ial effectiveness and fail to fool the model, and vice versa. Previous methods have neglected the
245
      environmental consistency of adversarial patches, which is crucial for realistic attacks. They only
246
      concentrate on improving the patches’ deception, and thus fail to solve this problem. The key to
247   solving this problem is to find a way to implement S(·) that can achieve stealthiness without affect-
248   ing the optimization of L(·), that is, separate the optimization processes of L(·) and S(·) into two
249   parallel components as much as possible.
250
      Empirical evidence suggests that humans are more susceptible to being deceived by adversarial
251
      patches resembling the background in color and texture. This aligns with studies on human per-
252
      ception, indicating that blending into the surroundings enhances the stealthiness of such attacks.
253   Thus, to make the adversarial patch less noticeable, we first identify the most common colors in the
254   background, which we called base colors and denote by c. Simultaneously, we safeguard the patch’s
255   adversarial effectiveness through the optimization of a color probability matrix. This matrix, deter-
256   mining the probability of each pixel of an adversarial patch aligning with a base color, undergoes
257   gradient-based optimization. Such an approach decouples the optimization processes for stealth and
258   adversarial effectiveness: base colors ensure visual stealth, while the optimized color probability
259   matrix preserves the patch’s adversarial nature.
260   Specifically, to derive the base colors c from a set of images with similar environmental character-
261   istics, we employ K-means clustering (MacQueen et al., 1967) to partition all pixels within these
262   images into distinct categories. By default, we set the number of categories to 3, unless otherwise
263   specified. Subsequently, we extract the center of each category to form the set c = {c1 , c2 , c3 }. To
264   obtain the probability of every color in c at each pixel of an adversarial, a color probability matrix
265   m = [mijk ] ∈ (0, 1)W ×H×3 is defined, where W and H indicate the width and length of an adver-
266   sarial patch, respectively. mijk represents the probability that the color at position (i, j) belongs to
267
      the k-th base color. Based on the color probability matrix m, the color tij at position (i, j) can be
      mathematically as:
268
                                            \label {eq3_genneration} \begin {cases} t_{i j}=\sum _{k=1}^{3} c_k \cdot r_k\\ r_k = \operatorname {Softmax}\left (\frac {\log m_{i j k}}{\tau }\right ) \end {cases} \vspace {-0.1cm}
269                                                                                                                                                                                                                              (3)

284   3.3   FAST ADVERSARIAL PATCH GENERATION
285
286
      As described in the introduction, there is a lack of in-depth exploration of adversarial patch com-
287
      ponents, limiting the understanding of physical attacks. To fill the gap, we begin by investigating
288   adversarial patch components, aiming to improve the efficiency of generating adversarial patches
289   through comprehensive comparison and analysis. Physical adversarial attacks are commonly used
290   in real-world situations with limited time and resources. The quick generation and deployment of
291   adversarial patches enhance their practicality across various scenarios. Besides, a prolonged at-
292   tack process raises the risk of exposure and discovery by security systems or vigilant observers.
293   Efficiency minimizes the time frame of the attack activity, especially in dynamic environments, re-
294   ducing the chance of detection before successfully attacking the object. It is necessary to improve
295   the stealthiness and efficiency under the adversarial nature in a new scenario.
296   We assert that adversarial noise influences the predictions of deep models primarily due to the rel-
297   ative magnitude relationship between different pixel values and small perturbations in pixel values.
298   The deep network amplifies these changes in both the relative pixel magnitude and pixel value dur-
299   ing an attack, leading to prediction errors. To understand the impact of pixel relative magnitude
300   and color changes on attack performance, we decompose the adversarial patch into two components
301   here: adversarial patterns and adversarial colors. The definitions and operations of these two parts
302
      are presented in the subsection. Furthermore, we test the influence of each part on attack perfor-
      mance with the same attack objects and victim models, the experimental results are shown in Sec.
303
      4.3 and Sec. 4.4. Through comprehensive comparative experiments, we find that patterns signifi-
304
      cantly influence attack performance than colors, and this phenomenon is also universal.
305
306   Therefore, a fast adversarial patch generation strategy is proposed based on the above findings.
307   Initially, we create an adversarial patch. Subsequently, when confronted with a new background
308
      setting, we substitute the existing colors in the adversarial patch, utilizing base colors from the
      new background environment. This operation can yield concealed and adversarial patches in a
309
      new scenario, circumventing the conventional processes of data collection and adversarial texture
310
      optimization. Consequently, it can significantly enhance the efficiency of the adversarial attack.
311
312   Decomposition into Pattern Component. According to Eq. 3, there exists a set of weights to adjust
313   the blending ratios of base colors, thereby generating different colors. When colors are identical
314
      in adjacent regions, no distinct features such as contours or corners emerge, presenting a smooth
      texture. In contrast, diverse colors in adjacent regions create pronounced boundaries, forming texture
315
      information (i.e., semantic details). From the above analysis, we can discern that pattern information
316
      is not related to the colors of the individual pixels, but to the differences between the colors of the
317   pixels. Consequently, we formally define the pattern component of an adversarial patch as the
318   relative pixel magnitude.
319
320
      Due to the color-independence nature of the adversarial patch’s pattern, we experiment by randomly
      substituting its base colors with other different base colors to test its impact on detectors. It is
321
      worth noting that we only replace the base colors without altering the color probability matrix. We
322
      randomly sample the new base colors from the color space C = {nck = (Rk , Gk , Bk ) | k = 1, 2, 3}.
323

475   5     C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this paper, we have proposed a novel algorithm called CAPGen. It can generate patches based on
      base colors extracted from the environment, thus can fool perception algorithms while being visually
478
      concealed in the physical environment. We also explored the effects of patterns and colors on the
479
      attack performance of adversarial patches and found that patterns are more important than colors for
480   achieving high attack success rates. Based on this finding, to tackle the long generation period of
481   adversarial patches, we have developed a fast patch generation strategy that can adapt to different
482   environments by changing the colors of existing high-performance patches. The experiments show
483   that our method can generate highly concealed and effective adversarial patches in various scenarios.
484   This work is the first to systematically study the role of patterns and colors in adversarial patch
485   generation, and we hope our findings can open a new path in the field of rapidly generating highly
      concealed physical attack textures.

      D    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF THE EOT
757
758
      In this section, we provide a detailed description of EOT. It involves a series of transformations, in-
759   cluding adjustments in contrast, brightness, noise levels, rotation angles, and image blurring. Firstly,
                                                                                               ′
760   we use a uniform smoothing kernel s to smooth m and obtain the smoothed matrix m for reducing
761   the printing error, as shown in below:
762
763
                                                                            \label {3.2e1smooth} m' = m * s                                                                              (6)
764
765   To reduce the impact of external lighting changes, we implement three specific transformations dur-
766   ing the training phase. These include adjustments to contrast (D ∼ Uniform(d− , d+ )), brightness
767   (B ∼ Uniform( b− , b+ )), and noise (N ∼ Uniform (n− , n+ )), where the symbols ∗− and ∗+ de-
768   note the lower and upper bounds of the uniform distribution. The outcome of these transformations
769   is a newly formulated color probability matrix mp :
770
771                                                \label {Eq_eot} m_p = D * m^{'} + B + N                                                                                               (7)
772
773   Given that attaching an adversarial patch onto an object introduces rotational deformation, we ad-
774   dress this challenge by incorporating random rotations of the adversarial patch during training to
775   closely mimic real-world scenarios. Additionally, we employ a scaling matrix designed to alter the
776   size of the adversarial patch, thereby accommodating diverse requirements across different scenar-
777   ios. The final color probability matrix is expressed as mf :
778
779                                                     m_f= S*R(\theta )*m_p                                                                                                            (8)
780
781   Here, R(θ) signifies the rotation matrix responsible for rotating mp by an angle θ, mathematically
782   defined as:
783
784
                                             R(\theta )=\left [\begin {array}{cc} cos\theta & sin\theta \\ -sin\theta & cos\theta \\ \end {array}\right ]                                (9)
785
786
787   The scaling matrix S, with Sx and Sy representing the scaling ratios along the x-axis and y-axis
788
      respectively, is defined as follows:
789
790
                                                               S=\left [\begin {array}{cc} S_x & 0 \\ 0 & S_y \\ \end {array}\right ]                                                   (10)
791
792
793   In our experiments, We set the contrast transformation to D ∼ Uniform(0.8, 1.2), the brightness
794
      transformation to B ∼ Uniform (0.9, 1.1), and the noise transformation to N ∼ Uniform (0, 0.1).
      The rotation angle θ ranges from -20 to +20 degrees.
