011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013               We propose to utilize end-to-end automatic speech recognition (E2EASR) as a
014               guidance model to realize unsupervised text-to-speech (TTS). An unconditional
015               score-based generative model (SGM) is trained with untranscribed speech data.
016               In the sampling stage, the unconditional score estimated by the SGM is combined
017
                  with the gradients from ASR models by the Bayes rule to get the conditional score.
                  We use a set of small ASR models trained only on 80-hour labeled ASR data to
018
                  guide the unconditional SGM and generate speech with high-quality scores in both
019
                  objective and subjective evaluation. Similarly, we can also use additional speaker
020               verification models to control speaker identity for the synthesized speech. That
021               allows us to do the zero-shot TTS for the target speaker with a few seconds of
022               enrollment speech. Our best unsupervised synthesized speech gets ∼ 8% word
023               error rate in testing, and the best speaker-controlled TTS gets 3.3 mean opinion
024               score (MOS) in the speaker similarly testing.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Text-to-speech (TTS) systems have made significant progress due to the need for natural, expressive
030   speech in various applications such as virtual assistants, audiobooks, and automated customer ser-
031   vice. With the rise of neural network-based models, TTS has transformed from traditional concate-
032   native and parametric methods to more advanced deep learning approaches, leading to significant
033   improvements in quality. (Ren et al., 2019a; Li et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2018). The adoption of
034   autoregressive models like Tacotron and Transformer TTS (Wang et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019) has
035   allowed for significant improvements in the synthesis of natural-sounding speech. The emergence
036   of non-autoregressive models, such as FastSpeech (Ren et al., 2019a; 2020), further expanded the
037
      capabilities and inference speed of TTS by introducing parallel speech generation techniques. More-
      over, diffusion models Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon (2019), which
038
      have shown high performance in image generation tasks, have recently been adapted for speech syn-
039
      thesis with promising results (Jeong et al., 2021; Popov et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2022; Tae et al.,
040   2022). Despite these advancements, mainstream TTS models rely heavily on high-quality paired
041   text-speech data. This dependence remains a critical barrier to developing robust TTS systems for
042   languages and speakers with limited available data.
043
      In this paper, we propose an E2EASR-guided method for unsupervised TTS 1 . We trained an uncon-
044
      ditional score-based generative model (SGM) on the 522-hour LibriTTS-R (Koizumi et al., 2023)
045
      dataset without using any text or speaker label. Only 80-hour WSJ (Garofolo, John S. et al., 2007)
046   labeled ASR data is used for training the guidance ASR models. ASR data is usually much more
047   accessible to collect than TTS data. In fact, the ASR system can be directly trained on noisy or low-
048   quality speech, while the TTS usually requires studio-quality recording. In addition, many of the
049   recent TTS works still need phoneme-level supervision to learn the phoneme duration (Ammar Ab-
050   bas et al., 2022; Effendi et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2020; 2022b), while the majority of ASR systems,
051   in recent years, are simply trained in end-to-end style with sentence-level annotations (Graves et al.,
052   2013; Bahdanau et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2017). We propose to use multiple independent ASR sys-

070   2.1   U NCONDITIONAL D IFFUSION FOR S PEECH G ENERATION
071
      We follow the unconditional generation model (SGM) introduced by Song et al. (2021). It unifies the
072
      denoising score matching with Langevin dynamics (SMLD) (Song & Ermon, 2019) and denoising
073
      diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) frameworks by using stochastic differential
074   equations (SDEs). For speech generation, we model the speech data in the mel-spectrogram domain
075   X ∈ RL×F , where L is the length of the spectrum and F is the number of frequency banks. Let
076   p0 (X) be the probability density function (p.d.f.) of the clean speech data. The SDE describes
077   the forward diffusion process, which converts the distribution p0 (X) to a simple prior distribution
078   pT (X) by a continuous time variable t ∈ [0, T ]:
079                                       dX = F(X, t)dt + g(t)dW,                                         (1)

081   where dt is an infinitesimal timestep , W ∈ R            is a Brownian motion, F(·) is a matrix-valued
082   drift function, and g(t) is the scalar value diffusion coefficient determined by t. F(·) is the determin-
083
      istic part of the SDE, while g(t) controls the scale of the noise-adding process. Based on previous
      research from Anderson (1982), there is a reverse SDE that describes the reverse diffusion process
084

      3     R ELATED W ORKS
120
      Recently, many diffusion-based TTS models have proven to be remarkably successful in the text-to-
121
      speech task (TTS) (Jeong et al., 2021; Popov et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2022; Tae et al., 2022). This
122
      emerging class of generative models adopts an iterative generative approach, where, during training,
123   a complex data distribution is gradually corrupted by Gaussian noise (Song et al., 2021). These
124   models are trained to estimate the gradient fields that reverse this process from a noisy prior, guiding
125   the data sample back to its original distribution. And most of those diffusion models are conditioned
126   on the text or semantic tokens. To train such models conditioned by the input text, large scale of
127   high-quality paired text-speech data are required, posing a major practical problem for TTS (Ren
128   et al., 2019b).
129   A recent work named Guided-TTS (Kim et al., 2022a) uses the classifier guidance method for TTS
130   by using a phoneme classifier as the guidance model. Guided-TTS is the most related work to this
131   paper. The main difference between Guided-TTS and this paper includes:
132
      1) Guided-TTS needs to train a frame-level phoneme classifier, an additional duration predictor is
133
      required to estimate the duration of each phoneme, and the diffusion model is trained on single-
134
      speaker datasets. They use large-scale 960-hour labeled ASR data to train a phone-level classifier.
135   while we only use 80-hour labeled ASR data to train end-to-end ASR guidance models.
136
137   2) Guided-TTS trains unconditional diffusion models on single-speaker training datasets. It can not
138
      synthesize the target speaker differently from the training speaker. A follow-up work named Guided-
      TTS2 (Kim et al., 2022b) replaced the unconditional diffusion model with a speaker-conditioned
139
      diffusion model to make it able for speaker-conditional TTS. That requires the diffusion training
140
      data to be labeled for the speaker. In this work, we proposed to use speaker verification models
141   for TTS guidance. That allows us to do the zero-shot target speaker TTS with a few seconds of
142   enrollment speech. We do not need any speaker labels for the diffusion model training.
143
144
      3) In the Guided-TTS, the authors use norm-based gradient scaling methods to combine the gradients
      from the unconditional diffusion model and the guidance classifier. In this paper, we follow Eq.5
145
      to combine ∇X log pt (X) and ∇X log Pt (y|X) without any scaling weight that hurt the Bayes rule.
146
      The impact of guidance gradients is instead tuned by the temperature in the Softmax function when
147   estimating Pt (y|X). Following the Bayes rule allows us to combine multiple guidance models more
148   easily and safely by making an independent assumption between the guidance models.

      4     T EXT- TO -S PEECH G UIDED BY E ND - TO -E ND ASR
152
      In this section, we propose using E2EASR as guidance for unsupervised TTS. Our method involves
153
      three different modules: 1) an unconditional score-based generative model, which models the distri-
154
      bution of clean speech from the speech data without transcription; 2) E2EASR systems that provide
155   the gradient guidance conditioned by the target text; 3) optional speaker verification models for con-
156   trolling the target speaker identity, enabling zero-shot target speaker TTS with only a few seconds
157   of reference speech.
158

      5.1       DATASET
230
231   We use three datasets in this paper; one to train the unsupervised score generation model, one for
232   the ASR guidance model, and another for the speaker guidance model.
233
234
      The first one is the LibriTTS-R (Koizumi et al., 2023) dataset. It is a quality-improved dataset
      derived from LibriTTS (Zen et al., 2019). The sampling rate is 24 kHz. LibriTTS-R provides the
235
      text annotation for each sample, but we do not use it during training (unsupervised). We use the
236
      522-hour speech partition to train the unconditional score-based generative model. As said, such an
237   unconditional score-based diffusion process is performed on 100-dimensional mel-scaled spectrum
238   features (i.e., F = 100) extracted from the speech signal.
239
240
      The dataset we use to train the ASR models is the WSJ SI-284 dataset. It contains about 80 hours
      of training data. The original dataset is 16kHz, and we upsample the data to 24 kHz and extract
241
      mel-spectrum features with the same parameter as that for LibriTTS-R.
242
243   The third dataset we use is the Voxceleb2 (Chung et al., 2018) dataset, which is used to train the
244   speaker verification model. It includes 5994 speakers for model training, and the total amount of
245   training data is about 2000 hours.
246
247   5.2       M ODEL CONFIGURATIONS
248
249   We use the sub-variance preserving (sub-VP) introduced in Song et al. (2021) as the SDEs for un-
250   conditional score model training and set hyperparameters βmax = 16.0, βmin = 0.1. The NCSN++
251   (Song et al., 2021; Richter et al., 2023) network is used for the score model. The diffusion model
252   is trained using Adam optimizer Kingma & Ba (2014) with an initial learning rate of 10−4 . The
253   learning rate decays by a factor of 0.97 in every epoch. In each epoch, we optimize the model by
254   2000 steps with a batch of 2. The predictor-corrector sampler Song et al. (2021) is used to solve
255
      the reverse SDE with 100 discrete time step in the sampling stage. We apply the neural vocoder
      BigVGAN Lee et al. (2022), to resynthesize speech from the generated mel-spectrogram feature.
256
257   We trained 12 ASR models with the data perturbed by SDE on WSJ SI-284 as the ASR guidance
258   models with the ESPNet toolkit (Watanabe et al., 2018). The 12 models include 4 different model
259   sizes, each of them with 3 different byte pair encoding (BPE) (Sennrich et al., 2016). The detailed
260   configurations are listed in the Appendix.A.1. All the ASR models have an AED structure and are
261
      optimized with joint CTC+attention loss (Kim et al., 2017).
262   For speaker guidance, we trained the speaker guidance models using the wespeaker toolkit Wang
263   et al. (2024). We chose the ResNet34-based r-vector Zeinali et al. (2019) as the speaker embedding
264   extractor and trained the systems on the SDE perturbed Voxceleb2 dataset following the wespeaker
265   recipe4 . To enhance the speaker guidance in the diffusion inference process, we trained two speaker
266   guidance models by applying different random seeds.

      5.3       E VALUATION METRICS
271
272   The generated speech is evaluated using both objective and subjective metrics. We generated 100
273   samples from the LibriTTS-R testing set for objective evaluation and 15 for subjective evaluation.
274   In addition to the target text, we need to specify the total length that needs to be generated for each
275   sample before sampling. We adopt the length of ground truth audio in the generation in most exper-
276   iments unless otherwise stated. A detailed ablation study of target speech length will be conducted
277   in Sec. 6.4.
278   Objective evaluation. We first report the word error rate (WER), which is tested on an ASR model
279   trained on the Librispeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) dataset. The evaluation model is available online
      5
280     . The second metric is UTMOS Saeki et al. (2022), a pseudo mean opinion score (MOS) predicted
281   by a neural network. The third is the SpeechBERT (Chuang et al., 2020; Saeki et al., 2024) score,
282   which measures the BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) for generated and reference speech with self-
283   supervised dense speech features. The last metric is speaker similarity, which is used to evaluate the
284
      effect of speaker guidance. We extract the speaker embeddings using a publicly available speaker-id
      embedding model 6 , and calculate the cosine similarity between the enrolment speech and generated
285
      speech. The UTMOS, SpeechBERT score, and speaker similarity are evaluated by the VERSA
286
      toolkit 7 .
287
288   Subjective evaluation. We perform a human evaluation on the generated examples based on three
289   criteria: N-MOS (naturalness Mean Opinion Score) for fluidity and naturalness, M-MOS (meaning-
290
      fulness Mean Opinion Score) for meaningfulness and content quality, and S-MOS (Speaker Simi-
      larity Mean Opinion Score) for the speaker similarity between generated speech and the enrollment
291
      utterance. 20 listeners are asked to evaluate 15 utterances on a scale from 1 to 5. The instructions
292
      for subjective evaluations are provided in Appendix A.2.

407   6.4   A BLATION S TUDIES ON S PEECH D URATION
408
409   In our proposed approach, the E2EASRs are leveraged to control speech generation for target text.
410   E2EASRs learn an implicit alignment between the speech and text labels in their training stage.
411   When using E2EASRs as guidance to control the TTS generation, we do not explicitly control
412   the speed of speech or duration of phonemes. Our method only needs the desired total speech
413   length, target text, and optional enrollment speech as input. In our previous experiments, we did
414   not investigate the total length of the target speech in detail but simiply adopted the ground truth
415   length as the target length. In real applications that do not have a ground truth length, an algorithm
416
      is needed to predict the target speech length from the text.
417   To understand the effect that the defects of the length prediction algorithm may have on the quality
418   of TTS, we conducted an ablation experiment on the total length of speech. We scale the length
419   of ground truth speech by a factor λ, and use it as the target length in the sampling. The guidance
420   models are all the 12 WSJ E2EASR models. The curves of WER with respect to λ are plotted in Fig
421   2.
422   As Fig.2 shows, the proposed methods are generally more sensitive to short speech length. If the
423   total length of speech generation is shortened, the WER will increase. However, if the length of
424   speech is longer than the ground truth in a reasonable range (from 1.0 to 1.5), the WER evaluation
425   becomes on par or even better. A possible explanation for this phenomenon is that the LibriTT-R
426   training data are well-segmented, and most of the onset and offset silence audio are clipped out.
427   On the other hand, the WSJ ASR training data has more silence on both the onset and offset of the
428
      speech. So, the ground truth speech length in LibriTTS-R may be shorter for the distribution learned
      by the ASR guidance model. We give more detailed examples in the Appendix. A.3, the model is
429
      trying to generate onset and offset silence if the target length is set too long.
430
431   This finding can guide the design of length prediction algorithms in real applications. For example,
      the predictor can be biased to output a longer length than the ground truth.

      7   D ISCUSSIONS AND C ONCLUSION
433
434
      In this paper, we proposed to use E2EASR models to guide an unconditional score-based generative
435   model (SGM) and enable TTS. The unconditional SGM can be trained on large-scare unlabeled
436   speech data. We show that using ASR models trained only on 80-hour can guide the unconditional
437   SGM to generate high-quality speech. Meanwhile, using speaker verification models as guidance,
438   we can also conduct zero-shot TTS with a few seconds of the target speaker’s enrollment speech.
439   By utilizing the end-to-end training for the guidance ASR, We found that we can synthesize high-
440   quality speech without using the phoneme duration model. An ablation study shows that our model
441   is robust against the mismatch of total target speech length within a certain range.
442   The main limitations of our work and possible extensions include:
443
444
      1) Multiple ASR guidance system are currently needed to generate satisfactory-quality speech, and
      our best system uses 12 ASR models for guidance. We use deliberately compact, small guidance
445
      ASR models (≈ 15 M parameters), thus in part alleviating the computational overhead in the in-
446
      ference stage. Our future works will focus on reducing the number of guidance models. Possible
447   solutions include optimizing the guidance ASR model against adversarial attacks to improve the
448   robustness of guidance.
449
450
      2) Although we disentangle the training data of the unconditional SGM and the guidance ASR
      model, the guidance model must be trained with the data perturbed by the diffusion SDE. This
451
      prevents arbitrary pre-trained models from being directly used as the guidance model. In the future,
452
      it is necessary to explore using guidance models that do not require data-perturbed training but e.g.
453   only fine-tuning with perturbed data.
454
455   3) Our proposed E2EASR guidance for TTS does not require commonly used phoneme duration
456
      prediction models. We can generate speech with the input text and a total duration of the target
      length. In our experiments, we empirically set the latter to the length of the ground truth. In future
457
      work, the problem of generating natural speech robustly at a total input length needs to be explored.
458
459   4) This work focuses more on the utilization and optimization of guidance models but less on the
460   design of unconditional SGM. An important research direction is how to design an unconditional
461   SGM that is more effective when being guided.
462   5) Another potential extension for this work is cross-lingual zero-shot TTS. Using ASR models
463   trained on a target language may be still be able to guide an unconditional SGM trained on single
464   or multiple unlabeled speech data from other languages. This may solve the problem of lack of
465   high-quality minority languages or dialects TTS annotation.

630   the CTC and attention objectives were empirically set to 0.3 and 0.7, respectively. We use byte-pair
