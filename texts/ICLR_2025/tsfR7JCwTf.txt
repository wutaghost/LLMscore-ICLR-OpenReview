009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a certified defense to clean-label poisoning attacks. These attacks work
012            by injecting a small number of poisoning samples (e.g., 1%) that contain p-norm
013            bounded adversarial perturbations into the training data to induce a targeted mis-
014            classification of a test-time input. Inspired by the adversarial robustness achieved
015            by randomized smoothing, we show how an off-the-shelf diffusion model can
016
               sanitize the tampered training data. We extensively test our defense against seven
               clean-label poisoning attacks and reduce their attack success to 0–16% with only a
017
               negligible drop in the test time accuracy. We compare our defense with existing
018
               countermeasures against clean-label poisoning, showing that the defense reduces
019            the attack success the most and offers the best model utility. Our results highlight
020            the need for future work on developing stronger clean-label attacks and using our
021            certified yet practical defense as a strong baseline to evaluate these attacks.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   A common practice in machine learning is to train models on a large corpus of data. This paradigm
027
      empowers many over-parameterized deep-learning models but also makes it challenging to retain the
      quality of data collected from various sources. This makes deep-learning-enabled systems vulnerable
028
      to data poisoning (Rubinstein et al., 2009; Nelson et al., 2008b; Biggio et al., 2012; Jagielski et al.,
029
      2018; Shafahi et al., 2018; Carlini, 2021)—where an adversary can alter a victim model’s behaviors
030   by injecting malicious samples (i.e., poisoning samples) into the training data.
031
032   But in practice, it may be challenging to make modifications to entire training data used for supervised
033
      classification, because they are often relatively small. As a result, recent work has developed clean-
      label poisoning attacks (Shafahi et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2019; Aghakhani et al., 2021; Turner et al.,
034
      2019; Saha et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2020; Geiping et al., 2021b), where the attacker aims to control
035
      the victim model’s behavior on a specific test input by injecting a few poisons that visually appear to
036   be correctly-labeled, but in fact include human-invisible adversarial perturbations.
037
038   We present a defense against clean-label poisoning attacks inspired by defenses to test-time adversarial
039
      examples. Existing poisoning defenses fall into two categories certified and heuristic. Certified
      defenses offer provable guarantees, but often significantly decrease the utility of defended models at
040
      test-time, making them impractical (Ma et al., 2019; Levine & Feizi, 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Zhang
041
      et al., 2022). Heuristic approaches (Suciu et al., 2018; Peri et al., 2020; Hong et al., 2020; Geiping
042   et al., 2021a; Liu et al., 2022) demonstrate their effectiveness against existing attacks in realistic
043   scenarios. However, these defenses rely on unrealistic assumptions, such as the defender knowing the
044   target test input (Suciu et al., 2018), or are evaluated against specific poisoning adversaries, leaving
045   them ineffective against adaptive attacks or future adversaries.
046
      Our contributions. First, we make two seemingly distant goals closer by presenting a certified
047
      defense against clean-label poisoning that also minimizes the decrease in clean accuracy. For any
048   p-norm bounded adversarial perturbations to the training data, we ensure a certified accuracy higher
049   than the prior certified defenses. The model trained on the tampered data classifies a subset of test
050   input x (or x + δ, where ||δ||`p in clean-label backdoor poisoning) correctly. To achieve this goal, we
051   leverage the recent diffusion probabilistic diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al.,
052   2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021). We use an off-the-shelf diffusion model to denoise the entire
053   training data before training a model. In §3, we theoretically show how one achieves a certified
      accuracy under 2-norm bounded adversarial perturbation to the training data.

054
      Removing adversarial perturbations in the training data before model training, we can decouple the
055   certification process from the training algorithm. Second, we leverage this computational property
056   our defense has and present a series of training techniques to alleviate the side-effects of employing
057   certified defenses, i.e., the utility loss of a defended model. To our knowledge, we are the first
058   certified defense that decouples these two processes. We minimize the utility loss by employing
059   the warm-starting (Ash & Adams, 2020). We train a model a few epochs on the tampered data or
060   initialize its model parameters using a pre-trained model. None of them alters the training process;
061   thus, our defense’s provable guarantee holds. In §4, we show they attribute to a better performance
062   compared to existing certified defenses.
063   Third, we extensively evaluate our defense against seven clean-label poisoning attacks studied in
064   two different scenarios under `2 - and `∞ -norm bounds: transfer-learning and training a model from-
065   scratch. In transfer-learning, our defense completely renders the attack ineffective with a negligible
066   accuracy drop of 0.5% in the certified radius of 0.1 in 2-norm perturbations. We also reduce the attack
067   success to 2–16% when a defender trains a model from scratch. We further compare our defense with
068   six poisoning defenses in the prior work. We demonstrate more (or the same in a few cases) reduction
069
      in the attack success and less accuracy drop than those existing defenses.
070   We discuss research questions important in studying poisoning attacks and defenses but are far-
071   neglected in the prior work. We suggest future work directions to answer them.

      2    P RELIMINARIES ON C LEAN - LABEL P OISONING
074
075
      Threat model. A clean-label poisoning attacker causes a misclassification of a specific target test-
076   time sample (xt , yt ) by compromising the training data Dtr with poisoning samples Dp . If a victim
077   trains a model f on the poisoned training data Dptr = Dtr ∪ Dp , the resulting model fθ∗ is likely
078   to misclassify the target instance to the adversarial class yadv while preserving the classification
079   behavior of fθ∗ on the clean test-set S. The attacker crafts those poisons (xp , yp ) by first taking a
080   few base samples in the same domain (xb , yb ) and then adding human-imperceptible perturbations
081   δ, carefully crafted by the attacker and also bound to ||δ||`p ≤  , to them while keeping their labels
082   clean (yb = yp ). A typical choice of the bound is `∞ or `2 .
083   Poisoning as a constrained bi-level optimization. The process of crafting optimal poisoning
084   samples Dp∗ can be formulated as the constrained bi-level optimization problem:
085
086                                      Dp∗ = arg min Ladv (xt , yt ; fθ∗ ),
                                                    Dp
087
088   where Ladv (xt , yadv ; fθ∗ ) is the adversarial loss function quantifying how accurately a model fθ∗ ,
089   trained on the compromised training data, misclassifies a target sample xt into the class an adversary
090   wants yadv . Dp is the set of poisoning samples we craft, and Dp∗ is the resulting optimal poisons.
091   While minimizing the crafting objective Ladv (xt , yt ; fθ∗ ), the attacker also trains a model fθ∗ on the
092   compromised training data, which is itself another optimization problem, formulated as follows:
093
094                                       fθ∗ = arg min Ltr (Dptr , S; θ),
                                                    θ
095
096   where the typical choice of Ltr is the cross-entropy loss, and S is the clean test-set. Combining both
097   the equations becomes a bi-level optimization: find Dp∗ such that Ladv is minimized after training,
098
      while minimizing Ltr as well.
099   To make the attack inconspicuous, the attacker constraints this bi-level optimization by limiting the
100   perturbation δ = xp − xb each poisoning sample can contain to ||δ||`p <  .
101
      Existing clean-label attacks. Initial work (Shafahi et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2019; Aghakhani et al.,
102   2021) minimizes Ladv by crafting poisons that are close to the target in the latent representation space
103   g(·). A typical choice of g(·) is the activation outputs from the penultimate layer of a pre-trained
104   model f (·), publicly available to the attacker from the Internet. The attacks have shown effective in
105   transfer-learning scenarios, where f (·) will be fine-tuned on the poisoned training data. The attacker
106   chooses base samples from the target class (xb , yadv ) and craft poisons (xp , yadv ). During fine-tuning,
107   f (·) learns to correctly classify poisons in the target’s proximity in the latent representation space
      and classify the target into the class yadv the attacker wants.

108
      Recent work focuses on making those poisoning attacks effective when f is trained from scratch on
109   the tampered training set. To do so, the attacker requires to approximate the gradients computed on
110   Ladv that are likely to appear in any models. Huang et al. (2020) address this challenge by meta-
111   learning; the poison-crafting process simulates all the possible initialization, intermediate models,
112   and adversarial losses computed on those intermediate models. A follow-up work by Geiping et al.
113   (2021b) alleviates the computational overhead of the prior work by proposing gradient matching, that
114   aligns the gradients from poisoning samples with those computed on a target.
115   Defenses against clean-label poisoning. Early work on poisoning defenses focuses on filtering out
116   poisons from the training data. Nelson et al. (2008a) and Suciu et al. (2018) compute the training
117   samples negatively impacting the classification results on targets. But in practice, the defender does
118   not know which test-time samples are the attack targets. Follow-up work (Rubinstein et al., 2009;
119   Peri et al., 2020; Tran et al., 2018) leverages unique statistical properties (e.g., spectral signatures)
120   that can distinguish poisons from the clean data. All these approaches depend on the data they use
121   to compute the properties. Recent work (Geiping et al., 2021a; Borgnia et al., 2021; Hong et al.,
122   2020; Liu et al., 2022) thus reduces the dependency by proposing data- or model-agnostic defenses,
123
      adapting robust training or differentially-private training. While shown effective against existing
      attacks, those defenses are not provable.
124
125   A separate line of work addresses this issue by proposing certifiable defenses (Levine & Feizi, 2020;
126   Wang et al., 2022; Weber et al., 2023), which guarantee the correct classification of test-time samples
127   when an adversary compromises the training data with the k poisons whose perturbation radius of r.
128   Levine & Feizi (2020); Wang et al. (2022)’s approach is majority voting, where the defender splits
129
      the training data into multiple disjoint subsets, train models on them, and run majority voting of test
      inputs over those models. BagFlip Zhang et al. (2022) proposes a model-agnostic defense that uses
130
      the idea of randomized smoothing Cohen et al. (2019) studied in adversarial robustness. However, the
131
      certification offered by these works has only shown effective in toy datasets like MNIST, and it has
132   been under-studied whether they will scale to practical scenarios, e.g., CIFAR10 or TinyImageNet.
133   The computational demands of these defenses further hinder their deployment in practice.

      We aim to certify the prediction of a model fθ trained on a poisoned training data Dptr . To formally
138
      define this goal, we define the perturbation bound   as the `p distance between two datasets, computed
139
      by taking the sum across `p perturbations of all images. The perturbation space P π is the set of all
140   the datasets Dptr obtained by tampering samples with the crafting algorithm π with the bound  .
141   Operating within the space P , for any test-time sample (xt , yt ), can produce a certificate that
142
143                          PrDptr ∈P π (Dtr ) fθ←Dptr (xt ) = fθ←Dtr (xt ) > 1 − α
144   with arbitrarily high probability 1 − α, we guarantee that training on any poisoned dataset Dptr ∈
145   Prπ (Dtr ) in the perturbation bound   will classify the example xt the same way as it was classified
146   on the non-poisoned dataset Dtr .
147
148   3.1   ROBUSTNESS G UARANTEE
149
150   Intuition. Randomized smoothing Cohen et al. (2019) guarantees that a fixed classifier fθ will
151   classify any adversarial example x0 correctly with high probability. To make this argument, it first
152
      shows that even for extremely large values of noise δ, the example x + δ will be classified correctly.
      Thus, the analysis of randomized smoothing is able to show that a (much smaller) worst-case direction
153
      δadv will not change the prediction of the classifier.
154
155   Here we make a similar argument, but change where the noise is added. Given a dataset Dptr , where
156   some examples may be slightly perturbed to poison the model, we want to guarantee that any model
157   fθ trained on Dptr will classify the test examples correctly. To achieve this, we train many models fθi
158
      on datasets from Pδπ (with extremely large perturbations δ); if each of these classifiers consistently
      labels the test data correctly, then we can be guaranteed that (much smaller) worst-case poisoning
159
      directions δadv can not change the prediction of the classifier.
160
161   Randomized smoothing shows that adversarial noise to the test input x will not cause misprediction
      because the predictions remain consistent even for large quantities of Gaussian noise x + δ. In our

163   large quantities of Gaussian noise Pδπ to the training set the predictions of fθ←Dptr remain consistent.

177
      Denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPMs)    √ are a recent generative model that works by
178   learning the diffusion process of the form xt ∼ 1 − βt · xt−1 + βt · ωt , where ωt is drawn from
179   a standard normal distribution N (0, I) with x0 sourced from the actual data distribution, and βt
180   being fixed (or learned) variance parameters. This process transforms images from the target data
181   distribution into purely random noise over time t, and the reverse denoising process constructs images
182   in the data distribution, starting with random Gaussian noise. A DDPM with a fixed time-step t ∈ N+
183   and a fixed schedule samples a noisy version of a training image xt ∈ [−1, 1]w·h·c of the form:
184                                             √        √
                                         xt := αt · x + 1 − αt · N (0, I),
185
186   where αt is a constant derived from t, which decides the level of noise to be added to the image (the
187   noise increases consistently as t grows). During training, the model minimizes the difference between
188   x and the denoised xt , where xt is obtained by applying the noise at time-step t.
189
      Diffusion denoising for the robustness. We utilize off-the-shelf DDPMs (Sohl-Dickstein et al.,
190   2015; Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) to denoise adversarial perturbations added to the
191   training data and as a result, provide the robustness to clean-label poisoning. A naive adaptation of
192   randomized smoothing to our scenario is to train multiple models on the training data with Gaussian
193   noise augmentation or using adversarial training. But we need to add noise to the data that does not
194   make it look natural or they are computationally demanding. We thus avoid using these approaches
195   to train models, instead we want to remove the (potentially compromised) training set before training.
196   Note that the robustness guarantee that randomized smoothing offers holds regardless of how a model
197   is trained. We can still certify the robustness against clean-label poisoning. The fact that we use the
198
      denoising may make the guarantee a bit loose, but we show in our evaluation that the loose guarantee
      is sufficient to render existing attacks ineffective. Our denoising process is shown in Algorithm 1.

253   3.4                    C OMPUTATIONAL E FFICIENCY FOR C ERTIFICATION
254
255   In order to obtain a robustness certificate, we must train many CIFAR10 models on different de-
256   noisings of the training dataset. This step is computationally expensive, but is in line with the
257   computational complexity of prior randomized smoothing based approaches that require similar work.
258
      However, as we will show later, in practice we can empirically obtain robustness with only a very
      small number of training runs (even as low as one!), confirming the related observation from (Lucas
259
      et al., 2023) who find a similar effect holds for randomized smoothing of classifiers.

263
264   We empirically evaluate the effectiveness of our certified defense against clean-label poisoning.
265   We adapt the poisoning benchmark developed by Schwarzschild et al. (2021). This benchmark
266
      runs clean-label poisoning attacks with the same attack configurations, allowing us to compare our
      defense’s effectiveness across many existing attacks.
267
268   Poisoning attacks. We evaluate our defense against seven clean-label poisoning attacks: four
269   targeted poisoning (Poison Frogs (Shafahi et al., 2018), Convex Polytope (Zhu et al., 2019), Bullseye
      Polytope (Aghakhani et al., 2021), Witches’ Brew (Geiping et al., 2021b)) and three backdoor attacks

270
      (Label-consistent Backdoor (Turner et al., 2019), Hidden-Trigger Backdoor (Saha et al., 2020), and
271   Sleeper Agent (Souri et al., 2022)). The first three attacks operate in the transfer-learning scenarios
272   (i.e., they assume that the victim fine-tunes a model from some given initialization), whereas the rest
273   assumes the victim trains a model from-scratch. While our defense offers certificates only to targeted
274   poisoning attacks, we test if the defense can mitigate clean-label backdooring like the prior work did.
275
      Metrics. We employ two metrics: clean accuracy and attack success rate. We compute the accuracy
276
      on the entire test set. An attack is successful if a poisoned model classifies a target sample to the
277   intended class. When evaluating backdoor attacks, we only count the cases where a tampered model
278   misclassifies the target sample containing a trigger pattern.
279
280
      Methodology. We run our experiments in CIFAR10 and Tiny ImageNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2009;
      Le & Yang, 2015). Following the standard practices in the prior work (Schwarzschild et al., 2021),
281
      we run each attack 100 times on different targets and report the averaged metrics over the 100 runs.
282
      We randomly choose the base and target classes. We set the poisoning budget to 0.5–1% for all
283   our attacks: the attacker can tamper with only 500 samples of the entire 50k training instances in
284   CIFAR10 and 250 of the total 100k training samples in Tiny ImageNet.
285
286
      Attack scenarios. We evaluate against both the transfer-learning and training from-scratch attacks.

287
      a randomly initialized model in the training from-scratch scenario. We also evaluate against the
288
      attackers with two types of knowledge: white-box and black-box. The white-box attackers know the
289   victim model’s internals, while the black-box attackers do not. More details are in Appendix A.

      FrieNDs. Liu et al. (2022) advances the idea of training robust models. Instead of adding random
433   Gaussian noise to the data during training, they use “friendly" noise, pre-computed by a defender, that
434   minimizes the accuracy loss to a model they will train. When their defense is in action, they train a
435   model on the tampered training data with two types of noise: the friendly noise computed before and
436   a weak random noise sampled uniformly within a bound. Ours and FrieNDs greatly reduce the attack
437   success while preserving the model’s utility. The critical difference is that ours is a certified defense,
438   while FrieNDs is not. A future work we leave is developing adaptive attacks against FrieNDs.
439   ROE. Certified defenses (Steinhardt et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2019; Diakonikolas et al., 2019; Levine &
440   Feizi, 2020; Gao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Rezaei et al., 2023) are; however,
441   most of these defenses showed their effectiveness in limited scenarios, such as in MINST-17 (Zhang
442   et al., 2022), a subset of MNIST containing only the samples of the digits 1 and 7. In consequence,
443   they aren’t compatible with our clean-label poisoning settings. A recent defense by Rezaei et al.
444   (2023) demonstrates the certified robustness in practical settings; we thus compare our defense to
445   their run-off-election (ROE). In Table 5, we empirically show that our defense is comparable to ROE
446   while we preserve the model accuracy more.
447   Note on the computational efficiency. Most training-time defenses require additional mechanisms
448   we need to “add" to the training algorithm. AT needs to craft adversarial examples or clean-label
449   poisons (Geiping et al., 2021a). DP-SGD introduces noise into the gradients during training. FrieNDs
450   pre-computes friendly noise and then optimally applies the noise during the training, and ROE
451   requires training of 250 base classifiers to achieve the improved certificates. In contrast, our defense
452   only requires to run forward once with an off-the-shelf diffusion model.

454   5    D ISCUSSION
455
456   Our work offers a new perspective on the cat-and-mouse game played in clean-label poisoning.
457
458
      Initial claims from the cat: Clean label poisoning attacks, with human-imperceptible perturbations
      that keeping the original labels, are an attractive option to inject malicious behaviors into models.
459
      Even if a victim carefully curates the training data, they remain effective by evading filtering defenses
460
      that rely on statistical signatures distinguishing clean samples from poisons (Nelson et al., 2008a;
461   Suciu et al., 2018; Peri et al., 2020).
462
463   The mouse’s counterarguments: Yet, as seen in research on the adversarial robustness, such small
464
      perturbations can be brittle (Carlini et al., 2022): by adding a large quantity of noise and then
      denoising the images, the adversarial perturbations become nearly removed—and so clean-label
465
      perturbations are also removed. In prior work (and in this work as well), we leverage this observation
466
      and propose both certified and non-certified heuristic defenses.
467
468   Additional arguments from the cat: A counterargument from the clean-label attack’s side was that
469   those defenses inevitably compromise a model’s performance—a fact corroborated by the prior work
470
      on the adversarial robustness (Tsipras et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019). Similarly, defenses could
      greatly reduce the poisoning attack success, but at the same time, they decrease the accuracy of
471
      defended models, often tipping the scales in favor of the adversary. If a defense yields a CIFAR-10
472
      model with 60–70% utility, what is the point of having such models in practice? Indeed, our own
473   certified models require degraded utility to achieve certified predictions. Other defenses, e.g., DP-
474   SGD (Ma et al., 2019; Hong et al., 2020), are computationally demanding, increasing the training
475   time an order of magnitude.
476
      Our work shows that this cat-and-mouse game need not be as pessimistic as the cat portrays.
477
478   By leveraging an off-the-shelf DDPMs (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021), we can purify
479   the training data, possibly containing malicious samples, offline and render six clean-label attacks
480   ineffective. With a weak provable guarantee against the training data contamination (`∞ -norm of 2),
481   the majority of attacks reach to 0% success. A few recent attacks exhibit 0–10% success rate—a rate
482
      comparable to random misclassification of test-time inputs.
483   In contrast to the cat’s counterargument, we can also address the accuracy degradation problem—as
484   long as we do not need certified robustness. Existing defenses that work to defend against poisoning
485   require applying a particular training algorithm designed by the defender (Hong et al., 2020; Geiping
      et al., 2021a; Liu et al., 2022; Levine & Feizi, 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). By


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      decoupling the training process and the defense, we open a new opportunity to develop training
487   techniques that can enhance the accuracy of the defended models while keeping the robustness. Our
488   results propose two effective heuristic algorithms in improving defended models’ utility, especially
489   when the models are under strong defense guarantees (i.e., || ||∞ > 2).

      A       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP IN D ETAIL
703
704
      We adapt the poisoning benchmark from the prior work (Schwarzschild et al., 2021). Most work
705   on clean-label poisoning attacks uses this benchmark for showcasing their attack success. The
706   open-sourced implementation of Sleeper Agent is incompatible with this framework; thus, we run this
707   attack separately. We implement the attacks to use a comparable `2 -norm bound in the poison-crafting
708   process (see §B). We implemented our benchmarking framework in Python v3.73 and PyTorch v1.134 .
709   We use the exact attack configurations and training hyper-parameters that the original study employs.
710
      In contrast to the original work, we made two major differences. We first increase the perturbations
711   bounded to || ||∞ = 16 as the 8-pixel bound attacks do not result in a high attack success rate.
712   Defeating 8-pixel bounded attacks is trivial for any poisoning defenses. Second, we do not use their
713   fine-tuning subset, which only contains 2500 training samples and 25–50 poisons. It (as shown in the
714   next subsections) leads to 60–70% accuracy on the clean CIFAR10 test-set, significantly lower than
715   80–90%, which can be trivially obtained with any models and training configurations. If a model
716   trained on the contaminated training data misclassifies a target, it could be a mistake caused by a
717   poorly performing model.
718   We run a comprehensive evaluation. We run 7 attacks; for each attack, we run 100 times of crafting
719   and training/fine-tuning a model. We also examine 5 different denoising factor σ. In total, we
720   ran 3500 poisoning attacks. For the most successful three attacks, we run 6 different certified and
721   non-certified defenses over 100 poisoning runs. The three training-time defenses (DP-SGD, AT,
722   FrieNDs) require 100 trainings of a model for each, and the certified defense (RoE) requires 100×250
723   trainings in total. To accommodate this computational overhead, we use two machines, each equipped
724   with 8 Nvidia GPUs. Crafting a poisoning set takes approximately one hour and training takes 30
725
      minutes on a single GPU.
726
727   B       T RANSLATING l∞ - BOUND INTO l2 - BOUND
728
729   The certification we offer in Sec 3 is defined in l2 -norm, while most poisoning attacks work with
730   l∞ -norm, e.g., of 8 or 16. We therefore convert these l∞ -bounds into l2 -bounds. We assume the
731   worst-case perturbation in the l∞ -space that changes every pixel location of an image by 16 pixels,
732   compute the l2 -norm of that perturbation as follows, and use it in §4:
733                                   p
            • l∞ -bound of 8 pixels: p3 ∗ 32 ∗ 32 ∗ (8/255)2 = 1.74
734
            • l∞ -bound of 16 pixels: 3 ∗ 32 ∗ 32 ∗ (16/255)2 = 3.48
735
736   Oftentimes, the `2 attacks with the comparable bound 3.48 do not lead to comparable attack suc-
737   cess. We, therefore, especially for the poisoning attacks in the transfer-learning scenarios, increase
738   the bound to 6.43. We note that most existing attacks either do not implement `2 bounds or are
739   unbounded (Shafahi et al., 2018).

785

786
      effectiveness against these backdoor attacks. Note that Schwarzschild et al. (2021) also showed these
787

788   our Tiny ImageNet results in §C.2.

791
      trained on the denoised training data. Note that since the setup uses only 2.5k CIFAR10 samples

792
      our setup. As we increase the σ from 0.5 to 2.0, the fine-tuned model’s accuracy leads to 56%
793

794   success significantly. We also show in our evaluation (§4) that we achieve a high model’s utility
795   while keeping the same σ. We attribute the increased utility to recent model architectures, such as
796   VisionTransformers, or to pre-training a model on a larger data corpus. We leave further investigation
797   for future work.
798

800   of a model. For example, the accuracy of a model with σ = 2.0 is ∼27%, meaning that four out of
801   five targets in a class can be misclassified.
