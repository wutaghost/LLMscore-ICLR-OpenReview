009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Humans excel at leveraging past experiences to learn new skills, while artificial
012            neural networks suffer from the phenomenon of catastrophic forgetting during
013            sequential learning. Efforts have been made to alleviate forgetting by introducing a
014            rehearsal buffer into the model, but this way is impractical in real-world scenarios
015            with data privacy. Recently, pre-trained model-based continual learning methods
016
               have provided new insights into addressing this issue by effectively utilizing the
               powerful representational capabilities of pre-trained models to avoid catastrophic
017
               forgetting without a rehearsal buffer. In this work, we propose a novel pre-trained
018
               model-based continual learning framework, HyperAdapter, which utilizes a hyper-
019            network to generate adapters based on the current input, adapting the pre-trained
020            model to the corresponding task. This paradigm requires fewer additional param-
021            eters as the number of tasks increases, which is a critical advantage for scaling
022            to long sequences continual learning. Unlike methods that partition task-related
023            knowledge into relatively independent subspaces, it promotes positive knowledge
024            transfer across tasks. Comprehensive experiments across various datasets demon-
025            strate that HyperAdapter consistently outperforms all existing methods and even
026            exceeds the upper bounds of multi-task learning, establishing a new state-of-the-art
027
               for pre-trained model-based continual learning. Our code will be released.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Humans exhibit remarkable abilities for constantly acquiring new knowledge in the dynamically
032   changing real world, which helps them grasp new skills more easily with a richer knowledge base.
033   However, when trained on successive task stages, neural networks tend to overfit on the current task
034   and perform poorly on previous ones, a problem known as catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey &
035   Cohen, 1989). Continual learning aims to learn new tasks while retaining past knowledge (De Lange
036   et al., 2021; Mai et al., 2022). Inspired by the replay process in the hippocampus, some approaches
037   rely on a rehearsal buffer to store samples from previous tasks (Buzzega et al., 2020; Cha et al., 2021;
038   Chaudhry et al., 2019). These samples are then combined with data from the current task to mitigate
039
      forgetting. While these methods have shown promising results, they face challenges in real-world
      scenarios with privacy concerns (Shokri & Shmatikov, 2015) or memory constraints (Smith et al.,
040
      2021). The need for rehearsal-free methods to address continual learning in practical settings remains.
041
042   Without a rehearsal buffer, methods need to focus on the parameters and architecture of the model.
043   Drawing inspiration from synaptic consolidation in the neocortex, EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017)
044   prevents forgetting of critical past knowledge by applying regularization to the model weights. While
045
      regularization-based methods (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017) offer new insights into
      rehearsal-free continual learning, they alleviate forgetting at the expense of model plasticity, leading
046
      to suboptimal performance when learning new tasks. Recent advances in pre-training (Chen et al.,
047
      2020; He et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022; He et al., 2022a) inspire the community to
048   integrate pre-trained models into continual learning, aiming to prevent forgetting while efficiently
049   learning new tasks. In particular, prompt-based methods (Wang et al., 2022b;a; Smith et al., 2023) can
050   even outperform rehearsal-based methods. These methods maintain a prompt pool for the pre-trained
051   backbone, selecting prompts based on input samples to instruct the learning of corresponding tasks.
052   However, a small pool may lead to forgetting, while a large one burdens memory and hinders positive
053   knowledge transfer between tasks, presenting the stability-plasticity dilemma (Jung et al., 2023).
      EASE (Zhou et al., 2024) introduces the adapter into continual learning to enhance the adaptability

081   According to the Complementary Learning Systems (CLS) theory (Kumaran et al., 2016; McClelland
082   et al., 1995), humans achieve continual learning through the synergy of two systems: the rapidly
083   updating hippocampus focuses on learning task-specific representations, while the slowly updating
084   neocortex specializes in learning more general representations based on past experiences. As
085
      shown in Figure 1, task embeddings can be likened to episodic memories in the hippocampus,
      selectively invoked based on different inputs, while the hypernetwork acts as the neocortex of brain,
086
      storing past knowledge in the form of neural connections and updating slowly through the indirect
087
      optimization of its generated adapters. Finally, the pre-trained model represents the prior knowledge
088   acquired before learning, aiding the model in better acquiring new tasks. Without a fixed-size prompt
089   pool, the hypernetwork effectively expands model capacity, facilitating positive knowledge transfer.
090   Furthermore, for each new task, our method requires only the addition of a learnable task embedding
091   vector, making it highly suitable for continual learning scenarios involving long sequences and large
092   task numbers. Our empirical results demonstrate that HyperAdapter surpasses the performance of
093   all existing works, offering a significant step forward in rehearsal-free continual learning. In our
094   proposed CL-100 benchmark, HyperAdapter outperformes the previously best DAP and EASE by
095   2.24% and 3.07% in average final accuracy, respectively. On the larger ImageNet-R and DomainNet,
096
      HyperAdapter surpasses the previous SOTA CODA-P and EASE, by 1.06% and 4.80% respectively.
097   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
098
099           1. We propose HyperAdapter, a novel rehearsal-free continual learning framework. The method
100              leverages a hypernetwork to generate adapters, adapting the pre-trained model to each task
101
                 effectively, thereby mitigating forgetting and facilitating positive knowledge transfer.
102           2. Extensive experiments on various datasets demonstrate that HyperAdapter consistently
103              outperforms all existing methods and even surpasses the multi-task learning upper bound in
104              some cases, establishing a new SOTA for rehearsal-free continual learning. Moreover, the
105
                 hypernetwork design makes it suitable for continual learning with longer task sequences.
106           3. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work leveraging hypernetworks to unlock
107              the potential of pre-trained models in the field of continual learning. We expect that our
                 approach provides a novel perspective on continual learning of pre-trained models.

110   2.1   P RE -T RAINED M ODEL -BASED C ONTINUAL L EARNING
111
112   In recent years, continual learning has emerged as a focal point in the field of machine learning,
113   with the primary challenge being how to incorporate new information effectively without forgetting
114   prior knowledge. Traditional continual learning methods (Rolnick et al., 2019; Rebuffi et al., 2017;
115   Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Chaudhry et al., 2018b; Aljundi et al., 2019; Chaudhry et al., 2019)
116   typically rely on the rehearsal of old data, which can raise privacy and storage issues. Although
117
      carefully designed regularations (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017) have addressed
      forgetting to some extent, regularation-base methods still underperform rehearsal-based ones.
118
119   With advancements in model pre-training, an increasing number of studies begin to explore inte-
120   grating knowledge from pre-trained models into continual learning, achieving comparable results
121   even without a rehearsal buffer. Some approaches (Wang et al., 2022b;a; Smith et al., 2023; Jung
122   et al., 2023) facilitate continual learning by providing appropriate visual prompts (Jia et al., 2022) to
123
      pre-trained models. However, these prompt-based methods typically focus on instance-level enhance-
      ments, which offer limited overall benefits to the model. Recently, adapter-based techniques (Zhou
124
      et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024) have shown promising results, outperforming prompt-based ones. The
125
      modular design of adapters (Pfeiffer et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022) allows to retain and leverage
126   the knowledge from pre-trained models more effectively, although they also suffer from limited
127   adaptability and scalability. Leveraging a hypernetwork to generate adapters, HyperAdapter proposes
128   a novel rehearsal free mechanism with the capable of generalization.
129
130   2.2   C OMPLEMENTARY L EARNING S YSTEMS
131
132   The Complementary Learning Systems (CLS) theory reveals that humans achieve continual learning
133   through the synergy of two systems that update at different frequencies. Inspired by CLS, several
134   methods (Parisi et al., 2018; Pham et al., 2021; Arani et al., 2022) incorporate multiple networks
135
      along with rehearsal buffers, regularization constraints, or other components that expand with the
      task number. FearNet (Kemker & Kanan, 2017), for instance, employs a three-network structure: one
136
      hippocampal network for recalling recent instances, one PFC network for long-term memories, and
137
      one additional network for deciding between the two for specific cases. Gomez-Villa et al. (2024)
138   proposes to train an expert network that, unburdened by the task of retaining prior knowledge, focuses
139   on excelling in new tasks. Closer to our approach, Gurbuz et al. (2024) introduces a contextual
140   encoding for rehersal-free scenarios. However, all the methods above do not take pre-trained models
141   into consideration, thus lack of powerful representational capabilities and outstanding performance.
142
143   2.3   H YPERNETWORKS
144
145   Hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2016) were initially proposed for network compression, aimed at gen-
146   erating smaller sets of weights to reduce the computational and storage demands. In efficient
147
      fine-tuning (Mahabadi et al., 2021; Zhmoginov et al., 2022; He et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2022;
      Üstün et al., 2022), federated learning (Zhang et al., 2023) and few-shot learning tasks (Sendera et al.,
148
      2023), hypernetworks have been employed to dynamically generate parameters, achieving significant
149
      performance. These applications demonstrate that hypernetworks can not only adjust models for
150   new tasks but also retain memory of old knowledge. Following this strategy, some hypernet-based
151   continual learning methods (Chandra et al., 2023; Ksiażek & Spurek, 2023; Hemati et al., 2023)
152   generate new parameters for the entire network to adapt to new tasks, but these methods may not fully
153   exploit the prior knowledge of pre-trained models. Different from previous works, HyperAdapter
154   leverages representative features from pre-trained models, thus eliminating necessity of knowing the
155   task identities during inference or the dependence on any rehearsal buffers.

159   3.1   C ONTINUAL L EARNING
160
161   Continual learning requires the model to learn a sequence of tasks in order of arrival. For a sequence
      of T tasks D = {D1 , . . . , DT }, where each task Dt = {(x, y)} contains tuples of the input sample


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      x ∈ X and its corresponding label y ∈ Y, a single model fθ : X → Y parameterized by θ needs
163   to learn on D sequentially and predicts the label y = f (x; θ) given input sample x from arbitrary
164   task. Data from previous tasks will not be seen anymore when training future tasks, requiring the
165   model to avoid forgetting old knowledge while learning new tasks. Depending on the differences
166   in task transition, continual learning can be further categorized into multiple settings. In this work,
167   we focus on the more challenging class-incremental learning setting, where task identities are only
168   known during training, which is more common in real-world scenarios.
169
170   3.2   A DAPTER T UNING
171
172   Adapter Tuning, which first emerged in NLP (Houlsby et al., 2019), achieves efficient adaptation to
173
      downstream tasks by freezing pre-trained weights and inserting lightweight bottleneck modules into
      the model. AdaptFormer (Chen et al., 2022) later introduces adapters to visual recognition tasks. The
174
      vanilla Vision Transformer consists of a series of blocks, each containing a multi-head self-attention
175
      layer (MHSA) and a feed-forward network (FFN). The processing of FFN can be formalized as:
176
177                                          x = MLP(LN(x′ )) + x′                                           (1)
178
      where x′ ∈ Rd is the output of MHSA in the same block. AdaptFormer replaces the MLP in
179
      FFN with AdaptMLP, which includes an adapter with a down-projection matrix D ∈ Rd×r and an
180   up-projection matrix U ∈ Rr×d , where r is the bottleneck dimension satisfying r ≪ d. The process
181   of extracting adapted features can be represented as:
182
183                                           x̃ = GELU(x′ · D) · U                                          (2)
184   All features are then passed through residual connection to obtain the final output of AdaptMLP:
185
186                                      x = MLP(LN(x′ )) + s · x̃ + x′                                      (3)
187   where s is a scale factor to ensure convergence.

453   To verify that the hypernetwork-based design of our HyperAdapter is not confined to any specific
454   adapter structure, we choose Low-Rank Adaptation or LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), an adapter structure
455   initially proposed for efficiently fine-tuning large language models. We refer to the LoRA version
456
      of our approach as HyperLoRA or HLA, which is also divided into model-wise and block-wise
      versions. The results on CL-100 benchmark are shown in Table 3. In this experiment, we followed the
457
      original settings in Hu et al. (2021), applying LoRA to the query and value matrices in ViT’s attention
458
      mechanism, with the rank set to half of the bottleneck dimension in HA’s adapter to ensure that the
459   learnable parameters of there two versions are consistent. All other hyperparameters remained the
460   same as in the HA experiments. The results show that our method also achieves excellent performance
461   when applied to LoRA, even outperforming the HA version without hyperparameter tuning, fully
462   demonstrating the versatility of our design.

464   5.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
465
466   Core Design. To validate the effectiveness of the core designs in HyperAdapter, we conduct ablation
467   studies on the Split CIFAR-100 and ImageNet-R datasets, all based on the model-wise version. As
468   shown in Table 4, row 1 represents the complete version of HyperAdapter. In row 2, we remove the
469   task dictionary and use an additional MLP to project queries to the specified dimension, providing
470   instance embeddings to the hypernetwork as input. This MLP is frozen after learning the first task.
471   The results indicate that removing task embeddings has a minor impact on learning accuracy, but
472
      overly diverse inputs easily lead to forgetting in the hypernetwork, resulting in ∼ 30% drop in
      final accuracy. Row 3 removes the position embedding from each layer, causing the hypernetwork
473
      to always generate the same adapter. While repeated adapters have a minimal effect on model
474
      performance, due to the minimal parameter count of position embeddings, we ultimately chose the
475   performance improvement brought by diverse adapters. In row 4, we remove the layer normalization
476   (LN) after each adapter. The results demonstrate that this design has a significant impact on the final
477   performance, with removing it resulting in over 60% performance loss. Since learning accuracy also
478   decreases, it indicates that the LN is more for stabilizing the training of the adapters.
479
480   Parameter Scale. In Figure 3, we show the impact of parameter scale on performance. Figure 3(a)
481   and (b) represent two different versions of HyperAdapter. It can be observed that model performance
482   is positively correlated with parameter scale, which means more parameters lead to higher accuracy.
483   However, as the parameter scale continues to increase, this marginal benefit gradually diminishes. To
484   strike a balance between parameters and performance, we set both the task embedding and adapter
485   bottleneck dimension of model-wise HyperAdapter to 32, while both dimensions of block-wise
      HyperAdapter are set to 16. Figure 3(c) demonstrates the performance change when fixing one

517   t-SNE. In Figure 4(a), we present t-SNE results of input queries and task keys. To enable the
518   hypernetwork to generate corresponding adapters for different tasks, we adopt a query-key matching
519   mechanism to obtain task embeddings, which can be viewed as clustering based on the pre-trained

      not always distinguish inputs from different classes well. Moreover, since a task comprises data
521
      from different classes, the large inter-class gap can make the clustering process more challenging.
522
      Nevertheless, this matching mechanism still plays a crucial role in our method, as shown in Table 4.

526   noise from a few incorrect selections do not significantly impact model performance, benefiting from
527   the similarity-based matching mechanism. Even if the matching is incorrect, the matched task is still
528   highly similar, and the embedding can provide enough information for the current task to achieve
529   correct classification. Improving this selection mechanism is left as a direction for future work.

      6                              C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose HyperAdapter as a novel rehearsal-free continual learning method, utilizing
534   a hypernetwork to generate adapters to adapt the pre-trained model to different tasks. This work
535   represents the first attempt to employ a hypernetwork for continual learning based on pre-trained
536   models, providing a feasible approach for the continual learning of large-scale pre-trained models.
537   We establish a new SOTA on our newly curated CL-100 benchmark and two commonly used large
538   benchmarks, even surpassing the multi-task learning upper bound in some cases. This highlights the
539   immense potential of pre-trained models in continual learning and marks a significant step forward in
      the application of neural networks in real-world scenarios.
