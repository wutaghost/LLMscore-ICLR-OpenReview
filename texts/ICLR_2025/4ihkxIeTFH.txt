009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper establishes a mathematical foundation for the Adam optimizer, eluci-
012
               dating its connection to natural gradient descent through Riemannian and infor-
013
               mation geometry. We provide an accessible and detailed analysis of the diagonal
014            empirical Fisher information matrix (FIM) in Adam, clarifying all approximations
015            to bridge the gap between the natural gradient and Adam, and advocating for the
016            use of log probability functions as loss, which should be based on discrete distri-
017            butions, due to the limitations of empirical FIM. Our analysis uncovers flaws in the
018            original Adam algorithm, leading to proposed corrections such as enhanced mo-
019            mentum calculations, adjusted bias corrections, and gradient clipping. We refine
020            the weight decay term based on our theoretical framework. Our modified algo-
021            rithm, Fisher Adam (FAdam), demonstrates superior performance across diverse
022
               domains including LLM, ASR, and VQ-VAE, achieving SoTA results in ASR.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Natural Gradient Descent (NGD) is a powerful optimization method that has shown promise in
028
      training machine learning models. Introduced by Amari (1998), and revisited recently Pascanu &
029
      Bengio (2013), NGD offers an alternative to traditional gradient descent by taking into account
030   the curvature of the loss landscape. This is achieved through the use of the Fisher information
031   matrix (FIM), which provides a Riemannian metric for the statistical manifold. Despite its potential
032   benefits, NGD faces a significant computational challenge: the calculation of FIM, which can be
033   prohibitively expensive for large models like deep neural networks.
034
      Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) is the de facto standard optimizer, favored for its fast convergence and
035
      practicality. While the original paper mentions the second momentum term can be interpreted as
036   the diagonal FIM, a comprehensive theoretical understanding of the square root FIM, akin to the
037   approach first seen in AdaGrad (Lydia & Francis, 2019), has remained elusive Lin et al. (2024).
038   This is in contrast to NGD, which utilizes the inverse FIM.
039
      Our main contribution is to provide an accessible mathematical explanation of the Adam opti-
040
      mizer, drawing upon fundamental concepts from Riemannian geometry and information geometry,
041
      thereby demonstrating that Adam is an approximation of NGD. This framework enables us to illu-
042   minate the origin of the square root term in Adam and to advocate for the use of a log probability
043   function as the loss function when employing Adam.
044
045
      We demonstrate that Adam utilizes the diagonal empirical Fisher information, providing a detailed
      explanation of both the diagonal approximation and the empirical approximation. Notably, the em-
046
      pirical approximation suggests that the loss function should be based on discrete distributions, which
047
      implies using categorical cross-entropy loss rather than L2 loss in the image domain. Furthermore,
048   our analysis uncovers flaws in the original Adam, leading us to propose corrections such as aver-
049   aging natural gradient by momentum, adjusting bias corrections, and introducing gradient clipping.
050   We also enhance the weight decay using principles of Riemannian geometry. Our modified algo-
051   rithm, named Fisher Adam (FAdam), demonstrates strong performance across various domains,
052   as evidenced by our experiments with Large Language Models (LLMs) for text, Automatic Speech
053   Recognition (ASR) for speech, and Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE) for image.
      In particular, our ASR experiments achieve new state-of-the-art results.

344   The gradient and FIM in Eq. (22) vary at each point θ. As shown in Section 2.1, not only their
345   components change but also their underlying basis. However, EMA averages the components of the
346   Fisher information tensor obtained across different θ points, despite their potentially differing basis.

349   steps. During this time, the basis can shift significantly as the model parameter moves to a new θ
350   location on the manifold.
351   In differential geometry, scalars, vectors and tensors are invariant under a coordinate change. The
352   length of a vector, derived from the inner product (Eq. (1)), is a scalar. For a given gradient, we can
353   express its length as shown in Eq. (10), and simplify it using the diagonal FIM in Eq. (26).

934

935

997    is essential for Adam Zhang et al. (2020); Gilmer et al. (2021) and is now used as a standard prac-
998    tice. Adafactor Shazeer & Stern (2018) incorporates clipping Pascanu et al. (2013) in the algorithm,
999    because the diagonal empirical FIM can have zero components. Therefore, clipping is applied to
1000   the invariant natural gradient, and then the clipped gradient is used to calculate the momentum,
1001   as demonstrated in Algorithm 1. The Adafactor implementation Shazeer (2018) already incorpo-
1002
       rates this approach, although it is not explicitly mentioned in the original paper Shazeer & Stern
       (2018), as shown in Algorithm 4. Since clipping is already incorporated into the optimizer, global
1003
       clipping Pascanu et al. (2013) is not necessary.18 In our experiments, we did not observe any benefit
1004
       from applying global clipping.
1005
1006   To prevent division by zero before clipping, ϵ is added. The standard value for ϵ in Adam is 1e-
1007   8. Adafactor Shazeer & Stern (2018) uses 1e-30, but since it is added inside the square root, this
1008
       translates to 1e-15 on the Adam scale. Wortsman et al. (2023) recommends using a smaller epsilon
       value of 1e-15 for large-scale models like LLMs due to the empirical observation that the root mean
1009
       square (RMS) value of gradients tends to decrease as models get larger and training progresses.
