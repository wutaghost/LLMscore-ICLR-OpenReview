                                                                                  A BSTRACT
031
032
033                  Audio-visual video segmentation (AVVS) aims to generate pixel-level maps of
034                  sound-producing objects that accurately align with the corresponding audio. How-
035                  ever, existing methods often face temporal misalignment, where audio cues and
036                  segmentation results are not temporally coordinated. Audio provides two critical
037                  pieces of information: i) target object-level details and ii) the timing of when
038                  objects start and stop producing sounds. Current methods focus more on object-
039                  level information but neglect the boundaries of audio semantic changes, leading
040
                     to temporal misalignment. To address this issue, we propose a Collaborative Hy-
                     brid Propagator Framework (Co-Prop). This framework includes two main steps:
041
                     Preliminary Audio Boundary Anchoring and Frame-by-Frame Audio-Insert Propa-
042
                     gation. To Anchor the audio boundary, we employ retrieval-assist prompts with
043                  Qwen large language models to identify control points of audio semantic changes.
044                  These control points split the audio into semantically consistent audio portions.
045                  After obtaining the control point lists, we propose the Audio Insertion Propagator
046                  to process each audio portion using a frame-by-frame audio insertion propaga-
047                  tion and matching approach. We curated a compact dataset comprising diverse
048                  source conversion cases and devised a metric to assess alignment rates. Compared
049                  to traditional simultaneous processing methods, our approach reduces memory
050                  requirements and facilitates frame alignment. Experimental results demonstrate
051
                     the effectiveness of our approach across three datasets and two backbones. Fur-
                     thermore, our method can be integrated with existing AVVS approaches, offering
052
                     plug-and-play functionality to enhance their performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Audio-Visual Video Segmentation (AVVS) aims to generate pixel-level maps of sound-producing ob-
057
      jects, ensuring their alignment with the corresponding audio signals. This technological advancement
058
      holds substantial promise across diverse domains, including video editing and surveillance.
059
060   Numerous studies (Li et al. 2023; Huang et al. 2023; Chen et al. 2024; Hao et al. 2023; Yang et al.
061   2023; Liu et al. 2024c; Rouditchenko et al. 2019; Mo & Raj 2024) have introduced innovative models
062
      and made significant contributions to the discipline. However, existing methods often suffer from
      Temporal Misalignment Issue between audio guidance and prediction outcomes. For instance, in
063
      Fig. 1 (a), a video depicts a girl and a dog. Initially, the girl sings, but later, she stops singing as the
064
      dog starts barking. Current methods frequently segment the girl’s mask after she stops singing by
065   mistake or incorrectly segment the dog’s mask before it starts barking. This issue arises because
066   existing methods do not clearly identify the time intervals when the girl sings and the dog barks.
067
068
      In fact, audio as guiding information inherently comprises two crucial elements: i) object-level
      category information of the sound source and ii) the time points when the target object starts and stops
069
      making sound. Existing approaches tend to focus more on the category information of the sounding
070
      objects while ignoring the critical transition times. Therefore, we propose that we should first
071   meticulously process the audio guiding information, extracting key information about the category
072   of the sounding object and the time periods when it is making sound, and then use this guiding
073   information to direct the video segmentation.
074
      Thus, we introduce Collaborative Audio Analysis and Video Segmentation. This method begins
075
      with a detailed analysis of the audio to pinpoint key time points where the sound sources change while
076
      simultaneously obtaining object-level information. This process segments the entire audio input into
077   multiple sub-audio segments, each maintaining consistent sound source categories over time. We then
078   perform video segmentation on each sub-audio segment separately, thereby preemptively separating
079   the audio of different sounding objects on the time axis to alleviate the temporal misalignment.
080
      Additionally, to enhance the temporal alignment between the audio and prediction results, we propose
081
      Hybrid Audio-Visual Feature Transmission. Existing AVVS models decode all frame masks
082
      simultaneously, leading to high memory demands in long video scenarios and failing to integrate
083   audio guiding information in a frame-aligned manner. Therefore, a hybrid audio-visual feature for
084   frame-by-frame mask propagation is necessary.
085
086
      On the whole, we propose a Collaborative Hybrid Propagator Framework (Co-Prop), which com-
      prises two steps: Preliminary Audio Boundary Anchoring and Frame-by-Frame Audio-Insert
087
      Propagation. Audio Boundary Anchoring aims to meticulously extract object-level information from
088
      the audio and identify the time points where sounding objects change, referred to as control points.
089   Specifically, we design multi-step retrieval-augmented prompts applied to the Qwen large language
090   model. Initially, we generate descriptions and categories for the audio, then search for instances of the
091   same category in the training set annotated with control point lists and include these as examples in
092   the new prompt. This process yields a control point list indicating where the sounding objects change
093   in the audio. After that, we designed the Audio-insert Propagator, which aims to perform video
094   segmentation on the sub-audio segments. Specifically, we design a Keyframe Processor to handle
095   keyframe predictions, fine-tuned on our curated keyframe sub-dataset. Furthermore, we design to
096   embed audio information frame-by-frame during the propagation of keyframe masks.
097   Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach across three datasets and two
098   backbones (On M3, our 63.58% MJ / 73.96% MF vs. AVSegFormer 58.36% MJ / 69.3% MF
099   with PVT-v2; On AVSS, our 39.56% MJ / 44.37% MF vs. AVSegFormer 37.3% J / 42.8% F
100   with PVT-v2). Our method can be integrated with existing AVVS approaches, offering plug-and-play
101   functionality to enhance their performance. Furthermore, we curated a compact dataset comprising
102   diverse source conversion instances and devised an assessment approach to gauge alignment efficacy.
103
      In contrast to prior methods, Co-Prop demonstrates superior alignment rates between audio and
      objects. Our code and benchmark will be released.
104

120   2     R ELATED W ORK
121
122
      Audio-Visual Video Segmentation. The Audio-Visual Video Segmentation (AVVS) task involves
      using a video and its corresponding audio to generate pixel-level masks for the sounding objects.
123
      Zhou et al. introduced the AVSBench-Object (Zhou et al. 2022) and AVSBench-Semantic bench-
124
      marks (Zhou et al. 2023). Following this, several methods have made notable advancements in this
125   field (Li et al. 2023; Huang et al. 2023; Hao et al. 2023; Chen et al. 2024; Yang et al. 2023; Liu
126   et al. 2024b). CATR (Li et al. 2023) proposed the audio-queried transformer, which embeds audio
127   features during the decoding stage to capture object-level information. AQFormer (Huang et al. 2023)
128   links object queries to sounding objects and introduces the ABTI module for temporal modeling.
129   AVSegFormer (Gao et al. 2024) employs bidirectional conditional cross-modal feature fusion to
130   enhance audio-visual segmentation. CAVP (Chen et al. 2024) identified biases in the dataset and
131   introduced a cost-effective strategy to address them. However, these approaches all suffer from
132   temporal misalignment between the predictions and the audio guidance due to their failure to account
133
      for the critical transition points where the sounding objects change. To address this, we propose a
      two-stage solution: first, anchoring the temporal boundaries of the same target objects in the audio
134
      and then performing frame-by-frame video segmentation on the audio clips with fixed target objects.
135
136   Video Object Segmentation. The objective of video object segmentation (VOS) is to derive masks
137   for target objects throughout an entire video. A prevalent approach is semi-supervised VOS, which
138   involves segmenting a specific object with a fully annotated mask in the initial frame. Recent
139
      advancements in VOS methods have showcased innovative approaches (Lan et al. 2022; Vujasinovic
      et al. 2022; Yin et al. 2021; Zhu et al. 2021; Fan et al. 2021; Oh et al. 2019; Yang et al. 2021; Hao
140
      et al. 2024; Mao et al. 2023b; Liu et al. 2023a; Mao et al. 2023a). For instance, Zhu et al. 2021
141
      proposes an approach that considers pixel-wise similarities between reference and target frames
142   alongside the structural information of objects. Similarly, PML (Chen et al. 2018) utilizes the nearest
143   neighbor classifier to learn pixel-wise embedding, OGS (Fan et al. 2021) introduces an architecture
144   based on object-aware global-local correspondence. STM (Oh et al. 2019) employs a memory bank
145   constructed from past frames, while AOT (Yang et al. 2021) introduces an identification mechanism
146   to process multiple objects within a frame. However, these methods only consider video features
147   during propagation and do not incorporate audio guidance. To address this, we designed the Audio-
148   insert Propagator, trained on audio-visual datasets, to integrate audio guidance information during
149   frame-by-frame propagation. This enhancement allows the prediction process to consider audio cues.

259   3.3   K EYFRAME P ROCESSOR
260
261   We divided the audio into several sub-audio segments based on the control points list obtained
262   from the Retrieval-Augmented Control Points Generation module. We designated the first frame
263   of each sub-audio segment as the keyframe and designed a Keyframe Processor (KPF) to obtain
264   masks for these keyframes, laying the foundation for the subsequent propagation of non-key frames.
265   Furthermore, we fine-tuned the Keyframe Processor on a restructured keyframe training dataset.
266   Keyframe Mask Generation. Keyframe processor is an audio-guided image segmentation model
267   designed to generate masks for keyframes from their corresponding audio and images. It operates
268   by first extracting image features and audio features from the key frames. These features are then
269   integrated through cross-attention mechanisms at each layer. Then we apply the audio-queried
      decoding (Li et al. 2023) to process the integrated features and produce the keyframe masks.

507   Can Text Labels Replace Audio for Guiding Video Segmentation? We explored the possibility of
508   converting audio directly into text labels to guide image segmentation. However, this approach tends
509
      to accumulate significant errors. We conducted comparative experiments to evaluate the effectiveness
      of using text labels derived from audio categories identified by Qwen for guiding video segmentation,
510
      see Table 4. The results indicate that using text labels for guidance degrades the model’s performance.
511
512   The experiments reveal that the performance drop is due to the amplification of segmentation errors
513   caused by incorrect labels. Given the semantic ambiguity of audio, many objects produce similar
514   sounds. For example, if a cat’s meow is misclassified as "a child crying," and this label is used for
515
      segmentation, the model may produce empty predictions if no children are present in the video,
      significantly exacerbating the error. In contrast, our designed Keyframe Processor effectively mitigates
516
      the issue of error amplification. Compared to using text labels directly, when object sounds are very
517
      similar, the Keyframe Processor can consider both image and audio information to correct for target
518   objects, thereby avoiding the issue of arbitrarily predicting empty masks.

521   5       C ONCLUSION
522
523   We introduce a novel Collaborative Hybrid Propgator that can be integrated with existing AVVS
524
      approaches, offering plug-and-play functionality to enhance their performance. To mitigate the
      temporal misalignment issue that commonly exist in previous methods, we propose preliminary audio
525
      boundary anchoring. Concretely, we designed a retrieval-augmented control points generation module,
526
      applying retrieval prompts to an LLM to preemptively anchor the time points of sounding object
527   changes, thereby alleviating the temporal misalignment issue. We designed a Keyframe Processor
528   to obtain masks for these keyframes, laying the foundation for the subsequent propagation of non-
529   key frames. Furthermore, we developed an audio insertion propagation module that embeds audio
530   information frame by frame during mask propagation, which not only reduces memory requirements
531   but also allows for frame-aligned consideration of audio guidance.
532
      Limitations: Our framework remains reliant on the performance of the Keyframe Processor. If the
533   Keyframe Processor yields poor results, the final prediction will be compromised.
534
535   Broader Impact: We address the core challenge of audio-video alignment in audio-guided video
536
      segmentation by proposing a novel two-stage approach. The innovative framework of Co-Prop allows
      for modularization and performance enhancement. Its superior performance makes Co-Prop valuable
537
      for highlighting objects in augmented and virtual reality environments, as well as for generating
538
      pixel-level object maps for surveillance inspection. We anticipate our research will contribute to
539   advancing the practical applications of audio-guided video segmentation.
