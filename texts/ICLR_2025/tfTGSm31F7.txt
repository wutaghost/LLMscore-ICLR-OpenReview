011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Extracranial electroencephalography (EEG) and intracranial stereoelectroen-
014            cephalography (SEEG) are crucial for epilepsy diagnosis. However, existing
015
               deep learning models often limit themselves to specific signal types and applica-
               tion scenarios, leading to challenges in generalization and perception capabilities.
016
               While large language models excel in natural language processing, they cannot
017
               effectively capture the disease-specific signal features in the highly specialized
018            field of epilepsy, and the lack of pre-training data restricts their generalization
019            ability. To address these issues, we propose a Epilepsy Foundation Model (Epilep-
020            syFM), a domain-specific foundational model that considers the mechanisms of
021            seizure and propagation in epilepsy. EpilepsyFM learns a generalized represen-
022            tation of epilepsy through unsupervised pre-training across various signal types,
023            data formats, and sources, and optimizes multiple epilepsy-related downstream
024            tasks through fine-tuning. We collected clinical EEG and SEEG data from multiple
025            patients at a first-class hospital, as well as the currently largest publicly available
026
               epilepsy dataset, the TUH series, ensuring diversity in representation learning.
               First, the neural activity signals are segmented into multiple patches, and a discrete
027
               EEG and SEEG neural tokenizer is trained to construct a domain-specific neural
028
               codebook for epilepsy. Then, EpilepsyFM takes into account the mechanisms
029            of clustered neuronal discharges in epilepsy and designs a channel set masking
030            strategy to enhance the model’s ability to capture the spatiotemporal character-
031            istics of the signals. The model fully utilizes the multi-dimensional propagation
032            characteristics of seizures through temporal, spectral, and spatial encoder modules,
033            achieving comprehensive representation of complex neural signals. Extensive
034            experiments show that EpilepsyFM achieves state-of-the-art performance in a vari-
035            ety of domain-specific tasks, including seizure detection and both short-term and
036            long-term predictions of neural signals, demonstrating strong generalization ability
037
               and broad clinical application potential.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder caused by abnormal discharges of brain neurons, affecting
042   approximately 70 million people worldwide, making it one of the most common neurological diseases
043   (Asadi-Pooya et al., 2023; Specchio et al., 2022). Its pathological mechanisms are complex and
044   varied, with diverse clinical manifestations. Persistent epileptic seizures not only endanger patients’
045   health but also place a heavy burden on medical resources (Kanner & Bicchi, 2022). Extracranial
046   electroencephalography (EEG) and intracranial stereoelectroencephalography (SEEG) are effective
047   neurosurgical methods for recording brain activity in epilepsy patients during ictal and interictal
048   periods, with intracranial SEEG widely regarded as the gold standard for diagnosing epileptic seizures
049   (Supriya et al., 2021; Narasimhan et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2024).
050   Currently, deep learning models for epilepsy are typically designed for specific neural signals and
051   application scenarios (Shoeibi et al., 2021; Usman et al., 2021; Peh et al., 2023). Although there has
052   been some progress in seizure detection and prediction using EEG, such as using graph convolutional
053   networks (Li et al., 2020) and generative adversarial networks (Rasheed et al., 2021), two core issues
      remain: first, clinical epilepsy EEG and SEEG are scarce, particularly the limited availability of


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      SEEG as the gold standard, which prevents many models from being thoroughly validated in clinical
055   applications; second, most existing models are designed for specific tasks, performing exceptionally
056   well on single tasks but lacking generalization capability in real clinical settings.
057
058
      In recent years, the rise of general-purpose large language models (LLMs) (Vaswani, 2017;
      Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024a), such as ChatGPT, has demonstrated their pow-
059
      erful capabilities in addressing multi-task and multi-domain problems. Compared to traditional
060
      deep learning models, LLMs effectively overcome the limitations of single-task models through
061   pre-training and fine-tuning. In the field of medical information, LLMs provide intelligent support for
062   disease diagnosis by processing vast datasets, significantly improving healthcare efficiency (Li et al.,
063   2023; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023). Recent studies have introduced various foundational models
064   for analyzing brain neural activity, showcasing exceptional performance. For example, Cui et al.
065   (Cui et al., 2024) proposed Neuro-GPT, which combines an EEG encoder with a GPT architecture
066   to address challenges related to the scarcity and heterogeneity of EEG data. Yi et al. (Yi et al.,
067   2023) aimed to bridge gaps between different EEG resources, introducing the MMM framework
068   for cross-dataset pre-training, while Jiang et al. (Jiang et al., 2024) presented a unified model that
069
      successfully navigates issues such as mismatched EEG electrode counts.
070   Although general-purpose LLMs perform exceptionally well, domain-specific models often demon-
071   strate superior performance in specialized fields (Pal et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Arefeen et al.,
072   2024). These models are optimized to capture unique features relevant to their domains. For example,
073   Gu et al. (Gu et al., 2021) highlighted the advantages of training domain-specific language models
074   from scratch in biomedical natural language processing, while Xie et al. (Xie et al., 2023) proposed a
075
      cost-effective strategy that successfully built domain-specific models, achieving significant improve-
      ments in financial tasks. For epilepsy, a clinical neurological disorder, we hope to break through the
076
      problem that deep learning can only solve a single task, and avoid the limitation of relying only on
077
      LLMs related to generalized brain activity to deal with various data sources (e.g., motor imagery,
078   sleep detection, etc.). Our goal is to design a clinical-focused foundation model that concentrates
079   on epilepsy. This model will learn the generalized representations of intra- and extracranial signals
080   associated with epilepsy, aiming to effectively tackle various tasks related to the condition. However,
081   we still face several challenges in this process:
082
      1) Lack of sufficient EEG and SEEG data for epilepsy: Although several publicly available
083
      EEG datasets for epilepsy exist, SEEG data remains extremely scarce. Collecting EEG data poses
084   significant challenges due to individual differences in seizures and the considerable time and effort
085   required for expert annotation.
086
087
      2) Lack of consideration for the mechanisms of seizure: The mechanisms of epilepsy are complex
      and involve abnormal discharges from various brain neurons. Improving the model’s understanding
088
      of the dynamic changes during seizure processes remains a critical challenge.
089
090   3) Lack of consideration for the propagation process of epilepsy: After a seizure, the propagation
091   process spreads over time to different brain regions, accompanied by low-amplitude, high-frequency
092   oscillations. This process involves information across time, space, and frequency. Capturing signal
093
      changes during this propagation and learning the features of EEG activity related to epilepsy are key
      to building an efficient model.
094
095   To address aforementioned challenges, our goal is to design a domain-specific foundational model
096   for epilepsy, termed EpilepsyFM, which considers seizure onset and its propagation mechanisms,
097   effectively handling EEG and SEEG data while achieving excellent performance across different
098   epilepsy task states. We first segment neural activity signals into multiple patches and, prior to
099
      pre-training, leverage the concept of VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) to train a discrete neural
      tokenizer, thereby constructing a neural vocabulary specific to the epilepsy domain. Subsequently,
100
      during the pre-training phase, we design a channel set masking strategy that reflects the characteristics
101
      of clustered epileptic discharges, randomly masking sets of neural patches and predicting the masked
102   patches from visible ones, enhancing the model’s learning capability and accelerating the training
103   speed through a symmetric masking strategy. Additionally, EpilepsyFM incorporates temporal,
104   spatial, and spectral encoding methods aimed at capturing the dynamic features during the propagation
105   process of epilepsy. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
106
      1) Robust data foundation: We collected clinical EEG and SEEG epilepsy data from a a first-class
107
      hospital’s neurosurgery department and combined it with the largest publicly available epilepsy


                                                         2
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
       EEG dataset, the TUH series, to serve as the pre-training foundation for EpilepsyFM. The labels for
109    downstream tasks are meticulously annotated by two specialized physicians from the hospital.
110
111    2) Channel set masking pre-training strategy: In response to the clustered discharge mechanisms
112
       observed during epileptic seizures, we designed a self-supervised pre-training strategy utilizing
       localized channel set masking. This approach enables more accurate capture of neural activity both
113
       within and across channels while improving computational efficiency through symmetric masking.
114
115    3) Temporal-spatial-spectral encoding: To gain deeper insights into the propagation mechanisms
116    of epilepsy, we developed temporal, spatial, and spectral encoding methods. These techniques dy-
117    namically capture the features of neural activity associated with epilepsy, providing a comprehensive
118
       understanding of its complex dynamics.
119    4) Diverse downstream tasks: We conducted comprehensive experimental studies on several key
120    tasks related to epilepsy, including seizure detection, as well as short-term and long-term signal
121    prediction. The results indicate EpilepsyFM’s adaptability across different tasks, highlighting its
122    broad potential for clinical applications.

      3.1   DATASETS
310
311   Due to the current lack of publicly available SEEG datasets related to epilepsy, we have collected
312   long-term clinical SEEG data from 20 patients in the neurosurgery department of a first-class hospital,
313   as well as extensive long-term EEG data, all acquired using Neuracle equipment (i.e., XJSZ-EEG
314   and XJSZ-SEEG). In these datasets, the EEG and SEEG signals are time-locked .
315
      Pre-training Datasets: The pre-training dataset includes the largest publicly available epilepsy-
316
      related TUH EEG datasets, such as TUEP, TUSL, and TUSZ (Harati et al., 2014), as well as data from
317   4 patients for XJSZ-EEG and 12 patients for XJSZ-SEEG that we collected. To cover all training
318   data and improve training efficiency, we set the time length of each sample to 4 seconds.
319
320
      Downstream Datasets: The downstream task dataset includes TUAB, TUEV, (Harati et al., 2014)
      CHB-MIT (Shoeb, 2009), and data from 8 patients with XJSZ-EEG and XJSZ-SEEG. The tasks
321
      across these datasets vary. TUAB is segmented into 10-second samples, TUEV into 5-second samples,
322
      and CHB-MIT, XJSZ-EEG, and XJSZ-SEEG into 2-second samples.

      3.2      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
325
326   Preprocessing: We downsampled the SEEG signals from 1000Hz to 200Hz and similarly downsam-
327   pled the EEG signals from various datasets to 200Hz to retain essential information. Meanwhile,
328   we applied a 0.5-70Hz bandpass filter to the EEG and SEEG signals to remove noise, along with
329   a 50Hz notch filter to eliminate power line interference. Finally, z-score normalization is used to
330   reduce nonstationarity and fluctuations in the signals (Hosseini et al., 2020).
331   Model Configuration: Each EEG and SEEG patch has a length of 1 second, with the number of
332   patches limited to 256. The temporal and spatial encoders consist of 3 layers of 1-D convolution,
333   GroupNorm, and GELU, while the frequency encoder is composed of 3 layers of wavelet packet
334   convolution, GroupNorm, and GELU, with the wavelet packet convolution used to extract frequency
335   band features from the signals. Subsequently, the EEG or SEEG patches are transformed into patch
336   embeddings and fed into a Transformer encoder with a depth of 8 layers and 10 attention heads. For
337   more details, please refer to Appendix C.
338   Pre-training and Fine-tuning: To train the epilepsy neural tokenizer and EpilepsyFM pre-training,
339   we utilized the largest publicly available EEG epilepsy TUH series dataset, along with most of the
340   XJSZ-EEG and XJSZ-SEEG datasets as training data. This dataset includes approximately 9,000
341   patients and 2,000 hours of data, with specific details provided in Appendix B. The task records
342   vary across different datasets. We used TUAB, TUEV, CHB-MIT, and a small portion of XJSZ-EEG
343   and XJSZ-SEEG data from different patients to evaluate downstream epilepsy tasks. The training,
344
      validation, and testing sets are divided in a ratio of 80%, 10%, and 10%. Our experiments are
      conducted on 8 A100-PCIE-40GB GPUs using Python 3.8 (Ubuntu 20.04) and PyTorch 1.11.0 +
345
      CUDA 11.3. During training, binary classification tasks employed binary cross-entropy loss, while
346
      multi-class classification tasks used cross-entropy loss.

434   Predicting epilepsy signals is essential for developing early warning systems for patients in need of
435   preventive measures (Usman et al., 2020; Savadkoohi et al., 2020; Dissanayake et al., 2021). We
436   established both short-term and long-term predictions for seizures, focusing on signals of varying
437   durations. Specifically, we set the historical sequence length to 90 epochs (90 seconds), with short-
438   term predictions covering 12 epochs (12 seconds) and long-term predictions spanning 20 epochs
439   (20 seconds). A linear prediction head is utilized to forecast future signals, and we employed Mean
440
      Absolute Error (MAE) and Mean Squared Error (MSE) as evaluation metrics, with the definitions of
      these metrics provided in Appendix D. The prediction results are shown in Table 3.
441
442   Short- and Long-term Signal Prediction on Extracrania EEG: EpilepsyFM has demonstrated
443   outstanding performance in short-term prediction across multiple EEG datasets. For example, in the
444   XJSZ-EEG dataset, the MAE is 0.6421 and the MSE is 0.9380, which is significantly better than
445   other baseline methods. This superiority is attributed to the model’s multi-dimensional design, which
446
      enables it to analyze signals across various dimensions, allowing for flexible capture of dynamic
      changes and long-range dependencies in the signals. In long-term prediction, EpilepsyFM also
447
      exhibits strong performance, with a MAE of 0.6567 and an MSE of 1.1123 in the XJSZ-EEG dataset,
448
      further validating its potential and reliability in epileptic signal prediction.

      5        C ONCLUSION
529
530   Inspired by LLMs, we designed a foundational model—EpilepsyFM, specifically for understanding
531
      the mechanisms of epilepsy onset and propagation. This model is capable of learning generalized
      representayions from extracranial EEG and intracranial SEEG signals across different signal types,
532
      data formats and sources to facilitate various epilepsy-related tasks. To enhance data diversity and
533
      address the scarcity of intracranial SEEG signals, we collected time-locked clinical EEG and SEEG
534   data from a first-class hospital’s neurosurgery department. Initially, we trained an epilepsy neural
535   tokenizer to learn comprehensive EEG and SEEG data, thereby creating a codebook for the epilepsy
536   neural tokenizer. We then employed unsupervised pre-training using a channel set masking approach
537   to capture generalized representations of epilepsy. Subsequently, we fine-tuned the model on multiple
538   downstream tasks related to epilepsy, achieving state-of-the-art performance across different task
539   settings. Extensive experiments demonstrated that EpilepsyFM achieved leading performance in
      tasks such as seizure detection, and short- and long-term neural signal prediction.

      A    R ELATED W ORK

      Self-supervised Pre-training: Self-supervised pre-training has made significant progress in natural
759   language processing and computer vision, yet its potential in the brain-computer interface (BCI)
760   field remains underexplored, providing an opportunity to leverage large-scale EEG data for training
761   large models. Currently, several foundational models have emerged: Neuro-GPT (Cui et al., 2024)
762   aims to address the scarcity and heterogeneity of EEG data by combining EEG encoders with GPT
763   models; MMM (Yi et al., 2023) facilitates cross-dataset brainwave pre-training through a unified
764   topological structure, employing multidimensional position encoding and a multi-level channel
765   hierarchy; LaBraM (Jiang et al., 2024) segments raw EEG signals into slices and trains a neural
766   tokenizer via spectral prediction to generate neural vocabulary, masking portions of slices during
767
      pre-training for the Neural Transformer to predict from visible slices. The Brant (Zhang et al.,
      2023) and Brant-2 (Yuan et al., 2024b) models are specifically designed for intracranial recordings,
768
      with Brant-2 expanding upon its predecessor to demonstrate robustness against data variations and
769
      modeling scales, applicable to a broader range of neural data and validating the effectiveness and
770   application potential of self-supervised learning in the BCI domain. The introduction of these models
771   marks a significant step toward utilizing self-supervised learning and large-scale datasets in BCI.
772
773
      Domain-specific Foundation Model: Existing brain signal analysis models are primarily general-
      purpose, excelling in various applications but lacking focus on epilepsy analysis. For instance,
774
      domain-specific models have been successfully utilized in other medical fields, such as Tariq et al.’s
775
      work on prostate cancer, where they collected over 1.8 million clinical notes and developed a model
776   fine-tuned for clinical information prediction and question-answering tasks. Their model, enhanced
777   by UMLS-guided training and domain-specific tokenizers, significantly outperformed the general-
778   purpose model GPT-2 and even surpassed the specialized BioGPT in certain tasks (Tariq et al., 2024).
779   However, similar methodologies have not been widely applied to epilepsy analysis. While there have
780   been efforts like Zhao et al.’s EpilepsyLLM, which fine-tuned models on epilepsy-related datasets,
781   this work focused mainly on textual knowledge representation (Zhao et al., 2024). In contrast, our
782   research advances a model specifically tailored for epilepsy, leveraging extensive specialized data
783   to analyze seizure dynamics and addressing clinical needs in EEG analysis. Our work not only fills
784
      a gap in LLM field but also provides insights for applying large-scale EEG data to specific disease
      research, further advancing epilepsy studies.

      D    E VALUATION M ETRICS
919
920
921
      In the task of epileptic seizure detection(Cai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022), we follow existing
      research and employ balanced accuracy, AUC-PR, and AUROC as evaluation metrics for binary
922
      classification tasks. For multi-class classification tasks(Memar & Faradji, 2017), we use balanced
923
      accuracy, Cohen Kappa, and weighted F1 as evaluation metrics. In the short-term and long-term
924   prediction tasks for epilepsy (Woo et al., 2022), we also adhere to existing research by utilizing MAE,
925   MSE as evaluation metrics.
926
927          • Balanced accuracy: Balanced accuracy is a method of calculating accuracy that accounts
928            for class imbalance. It is the average of the recall for each class. In epilepsy detection,
929            balanced accuracy effectively reflects the model’s classification performance in the face of
930            imbalanced data, avoiding the bias that can arise from simple accuracy.
931
             • AUC-PR: AUC-PR measures the area under the curve plotted between precision and recall,
932            primarily used to evaluate the performance of classification models on imbalanced data.
933            In epilepsy datasets, AUC-PR better assesses model performance in scenarios with scarce
934            positive samples, particularly focusing on the precision and recall of the model.
935
936          • AUROC: AUROC measures the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve,
937
               primarily used to evaluate the performance of classification models across various threshold
               levels. In epilepsy datasets, AUROC better assesses the overall performance of the model
938
               under different thresholds.
939
940          • Cohen Kappa: Cohen Kappa is a statistic that measures the agreement between two
941            classifiers while accounting for the effect of random chance. Its value ranges from -1 to
942            1, where 1 indicates perfect agreement, 0 indicates no better than random agreement, and
943            negative values indicate agreement below random levels. In multi-class tasks, Cohen Kappa
944            effectively evaluates the consistency of the model’s classifications, especially in situations
945
               of class imbalance.
946          • Weighted F1 Score: Weighted F1 Score is an extension of the F1 score that considers the
947            importance of each class. It calculates the F1 score for each class and weighs it according to
948            the frequency of that class in the dataset. The Weighted F1 Score is particularly important
949            in multi-class tasks, as it balances the performance of all classes, making it suitable for
950            addressing class imbalance issues and ensuring effective evaluation of the model across all
951            categories.
952
             • Mean Absolute Error (MAE): MAE calculates the average of the absolute differences
953            between predicted values and actual values. MAE treats all errors equally, avoiding the bias
954            introduced by MSE towards extreme values, thus providing an intuitive assessment of model
955            performance. In epilepsy prediction, MAE quantifies the overall predictive accuracy of the
956            model clearly, particularly when ensuring the reliability of each prediction is critical.
957
958
             • Mean Squared Error (MSE): MSE refers to the average of the squared differences between
               predicted values and actual values. The primary advantage of MSE lies in its sensitivity
959
               to larger errors, making it useful in model optimization to identify and reduce significant
960
               biases. In the context of epilepsy prediction, MSE effectively reflects how well the model
961            captures rare but severe seizure events.

986    F          A BLATION ON C HANNEL -S ET M ASKING
987
988    Due to the locality and specificity of brain computation, research into more challenging epilepsy
989    tasks (such as the clustering discharges and propagation circuits of different brain regions in different
990    patients) remains to be explored. Our designed channel masking strategy can cover the neural activity
991    signals of multiple neighboring channels, which exhibit signal similarity, as shown in Figure 7. SEEG
992    signal channels are continuous, so there is no need to consider channel reordering. For EEG signals,
993
       we reorder the electrode signals based on the left and right hemispheres, grouping nearby brain region
       channels together to implement the masking.
994
995    In the experiments, we evaluated the performance of EEG seizure detection using different numbers
996    of masked channels, as shown in Table 8. From the figure, it can be observed that in most datasets,
997    the detection performance is best with three channels, and the masking methods using three or
998    four channels perform slightly better than the single-channel masking approach. Utilizing multiple
999
       channels for masking can more effectively capture and analyze the characteristic information of
       seizure events. Compared to single-channel masking, this approach may provide richer spatial and
1000
       temporal information, enhancing the model’s ability to learn complex signal patterns.

1071   Limitations. Although EpilspyFM has made significant progress in common clinical tasks for
1072   epilepsy by training on multiple publicly available EEG datasets and private data, the availability
1073   of SEEG data in clinical settings remains relatively limited. This somewhat constrains our in-depth
1074   exploration in the field of foundational models for epilepsy. If we can further enrich task settings,
1075   particularly in the precise localization of epileptic foci and avoidance of functional areas, it will
1076
       greatly enhance the clinical applicability of the model, allowing for more effective guidance in
       treatments such as thermal coagulation.
1077
1078   Outlook. Based on the aforementioned limitations, our research outlook encompasses several key
1079   areas: First, we plan to expand the collection of SEEG data, particularly from different devices,
       to enhance the diversity and representativeness of the data; we are currently actively promoting

1081   lesion areas and brain functional regions, thereby improving the comprehensiveness and depth of

1083   already includes EEG and SEEG as two types of neural signals, and in the future, we may consider
1084   incorporating fMRI or PET data. This direction not only poses technical challenges but also represents
1085   an innovative approach with significant clinical implications. Through these efforts, we aim to achieve
1086   a deeper understanding and breakthroughs in the field of epilepsy research.
