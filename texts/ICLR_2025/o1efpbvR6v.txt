                                                         Abstract
010
011            In this article, we investigate the impact of Deep Metric Learning and Transformer archi-
012            tecture on predicting the retrosynthesis of Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System
013
               (SMILES) chemical compounds.
014            We demonstrate that combining the Attention mechanism with Proxy Anchor Loss is effec-
015            tive for classification tasks due to its strengths in capturing both local and global contexts
016
               and differentiating between various classes.
017            Our approach, which requires no prior chemical knowledge, achieves promising results on
018            the USPTO-FULL dataset, with accuracies of 53.4%, 83.8%, 90.6%, and 97.5% for top-1,
019
               top-5, top-10, and top-50 predictions, respectively.
020            We further validate the practical application of our approach by correctly predicting the
021            retrosynthesis pathways for 63 out of 100 randomly selected compounds from the ChEMBL
022
               database and for 39 out of 60 compounds selected by Bayer’s chemists and from PubChem.

024   1   INTRODUCTION
025
026   Designing molecules and materials with desired properties is a practical goal of chemistry and ma-
027   terials science. Chemoinformatics involves the use of data and computational methods to investigate
028   and comprehend the connections between molecular structures and their properties, paving the way
029   for the discovery of novel functional molecules.
030   Chemists prioritize designing synthesis pathways that generate target molecules through a sequence
031   of chemical reactions. A common approach, known as retrosynthetic analysis, involves deconstruct-
032   ing target molecules into precursor molecules and then further deconstructing these precursors until
033   all are available in the stock database for synthesis.
034
      Retrosynthetic planning involves two primary tasks: single-step retrosynthesis prediction and multi-
035   step retrosynthetic planning.
036
037   Single-step retrosynthesis is treated as a prediction problem, where the input is a given product
038
      molecule and the output is the predicted set of reactant molecules.
039   Multi-step retrosynthesis planning aims at producing a relizable synthetic route for a specific com-
040   pound by breaking it down into simpler intermediates or precursors. This process is carried out
041   through retrosynthetic analysis, where the desired compound is iteratively deconstructed until one
042   of the stopping criteria is met: identifying known or purchasable building blocks, reaching a time
043   limit, reaching a maximum tree depth, or fulfilling other predefined criteria (Samuel et al. 2020).
044
      Multi-step retrosynthesis planning is approached as a search problem, guided by solutions derived
      from single-step retrosynthesis.
045
046   There are two main approaches to the one-step retrosynthesis problem: template-based methods and
047   template-free methods.
048   Template-based methods mimic chemists’ reasoning by using a pool of reaction templates to identify
049   potential reaction centers. The goal is to find the appropriate template that allows the deconstruc-
050   tion of product molecules into their reactant molecules. Template-based methods are a classical
051   approach, where the one-step retrosynthesis problem is considered a classification problem. This
052   approach is reliable since it uses experts’ knowledge about chemical reactions and mimics the way
053   chemists work, which makes it easy to use for experts (Meng et al. 2023). This is also the approach
      for our experiments in this paper.

      Template-free methods, on the other hand, are considered sequence-to-sequence learning processes,
055   where the input consists of string-like representations of chemical products (SMILES), and the out-
056   put corresponds to the SMILES strings of reactants (Meng et al. 2023). The most common models
057   used in this approach are Transformer-based (Vaswani et al. 2017).

064   2.1.1    USPTO, A I Z YNTH F INDER , A I Z YNTH T RAIN , R EACTION U TILS AND RXNM APPER

      USPTO-FULL is a dataset extracted from chemical reactions in US patents (1976-Sep 2016) (Lowe
066
      2017). It consists of approximately 3.5 million reactions with almost 1.2 million reaction templates.
067
068   Several free and open-source software tools are available for downloading and preprocessing the
069   USPTO-FULL reaction dataset. We utilize AiZynthTrain (AZT) (Genheden, Norrby, and Engkvist
070   2022), Reaction Utils (Kannas et al. 2022), and RXNMapper (Schwaller et al. 2021) to prepare
071
      and train our one-step retrosynthesis model due to their robustness and comprehensive end-to-end
      pipelines.
072
073   RXNMapper and Reaction Utils are used for downloading, preparing, and performing atom mapping
074   on the USPTO dataset. This is followed by AiZynthTrain, which provides a collection of routines,
075   configurations, and pipelines for transforming the atom-mapped dataset into molecular fingerprints1 ,
076   splitting the data, and training one-step retrosynthesis prediction models. These pipelines also gen-
077
      erate human-readable reports, covering all stages from data preprocessing to the training process.
078   On the other hand, AiZynthFinder (AZF) (Samuel et al. 2020) is a free and open-source software
079   for multi-step retrosynthetic planning. Its primary search algorithm is based on Monte Carlo Tree
080   Search (MCTS) (Browne et al. 2012), which recursively breaks down a target molecule into pur-
081   chasable precursors. We use AiZynthFinder and its MCTS to conduct our experiments on multi-
082   step retrosynthesis tasks. Additionally, we incorporate Nested Monte Carlo Search (NMCS) (Tris-
083
      tan Cazenave 2009), which has already demonstrated promising results in multi-step retrosynthesis
      (Roucairol and T. Cazenave 2024), in order to gain a deeper understanding of the interaction between
084
      one-step retrosynthesis models and search algorithms in multi-step retrosynthesis problems.

      Deep Metric Learning and Proxy Anchor Loss
128
129   Despite their extensive use in supervised deep-learning applications, including one-step retrosyn-
130   thesis problems, cross-entropy-based loss functions are often less effective when there is significant
131   intra-class variance and minimal inter-class variance within the input data distribution.
132   Deep Metric Learning aims to measure the similarity between data samples by learning a represen-
133   tation function that maps these samples into an embedding space, where samples from the same
134   class are closely grouped together.
135
      The quality of an input’s representation largely depends on the loss functions used to train the net-
136   works. These loss functions are typically categorized into two classes: pair-based and proxy-based.
137
138   Pair-based losses are derived from pairwise distances between data points in the embedding space. A
139
      well-known example is Contrastive Loss (Bromley et al. 1993), which aims to minimize the distance
      between pairs of data points with identical class labels and maximize the distance between those
140
      with different labels. These losses provide rich data-to-data information by directly comparing data
141
      points. However, this approach results in extremely high training complexity and requires a specific
142   arrangement of data to generate both negative and positive pairs.
143
144   Proxy-based losses address this issue by introducing proxies as representatives, which are learned as
145
      part of the network parameters. Each data point is associated with proxies, typically one per class,
      enabling the model to leverage data-to-proxy relationships. In essence, a proxy can be thought
146
      of as a learnable centroid for each class. The model’s task is to adjust the representations of both
147
      samples and proxies so that each sample is close to its corresponding proxy, each proxy is close to its
148   respective samples, and optionally, the distance between samples of different classes is maximized.
149   Examples of such losses include Proxy NCA (Movshovitz-Attias et al. 2017) and Proxy Anchor
150   Loss (Kim et al. 2020).
151
      We use Proxy Anchor Loss to train our backbone model because it captures both data-to-proxy and
152
      data-to-data relationships by taking each proxy as an anchor and associating it with the entire data
153
      in a batch. The loss is given by (Kim et al. 2020)

      One issue we observed with Proxy Anchor Loss during training is the potential for gradient insta-
167
      bility due to the large number of unique reaction templates. Therefore, careful selection of hyper-
168
      parameters, particularly α, is crucial. We experimented with α values ranging from 1 to 128. With
169   small α values such as 1, 2, and 4, the interaction between samples and proxies was too weak, while
170   larger values such as 64 and 96 led to overly aggressive interactions, causing gradient instability.
171   We found that α = 32 yielded optimal results, avoiding gradient issues, consistent with the findings
172   in the Proxy Anchor Loss paper (Kim et al. 2020).

      Attention and Positional Embedding
174
175   Transformers (Vaswani et al. 2017) represent the State-of-the-Art in Natural Language Processing
176   due to their ability to capture both local and global context. The Transformer architecture has also
177   achieved notable success in Computer Vision, as demonstrated by the Vision Transformer (ViT)
178
      (Dosovitskiy et al. 2021).
179   Drawing inspiration from both the original Transformer and the Vision Transformer, we conceptu-
180   alize a chemical compound as a sentence composed of words. Consequently, we transform the input
181   into vectors of consistent size to apply multi-head self-attention, the mechanism used in Transformer
182   model
183   We treat a molecule as a sentence, where the position of each ’word’ is crucial for the model’s
184   effective interpretation. Thus, we incorporate Positional Embedding before applying the attention
185   mechanism.
186
      Metric Transform
187
188   We refer to our backbone model, which incorporates Positional Embedding, the Attention mecha-
189   nism of the Transformer, and Proxy Anchor loss, as the Metric Transform (see Fig 2). The output
190   of the final Attention layer is passed through a Global Max-Pooling 1D layer to capture the most
191
      significant information about the input molecule. The Max-Pooling operation reduces the spatial
      dimensions of the feature maps by selecting the maximum value within a specified spatial win-
192
      dow, thereby helping to mitigate overfitting. Additionally, one or more fully connected layers can
193
      be added after Max-Pooling to improve the model’s ability to distinguish between molecules and
194   facilitate fine-tuning.
195
196   This model is then trained using Proxy Anchor Loss to enhance its ability to distinguish between
197
      samples from the same class and samples from different classes.
198   Typically, proxies are initialized and trained with a high learning rate on a pre-trained model, such as
199   ResNet (He et al. 2016) or ViT. This approach allows the proxies to quickly adapt and subsequently
200   refine the model parameters to enhance performance. In contrast, our approach involves training
201   the backbone model from scratch. Consequently, we initialize and train the proxies using the same
202   learning rate as the backbone model to encourage the model’s parameters to be optimized at a similar
203
      pace to the proxies, instead of letting the proxies take the lead as in the original implementations.
      We also experimented with using a higher learning rate for the proxies and, conversely, a lower
204
      learning rate for the proxies. In both cases, the results were less attractive2 compared to using the
205
      same learning rate for both the proxies and the model’s parameters.

213   learning process, samples from the major classes exhibit only localized clustering. While our ap-

512
513   The combination of deep metric learning, attention mechanisms, and subclass mapping resulted

      in a robust architecture that addresses the inherent imbalance in chemical reaction datasets. This
      approach demonstrates strong performance across both common and rare reaction types, delivering
515
      satisfactory results in both one-step and multi-step retrosynthesis. Notably, this was achieved with
516
      minimal training time, limited resources, and without modifications to the default AiZynthTrain
517   settings.
518

      applied to other template-based approaches to enhance their performances.

655   A.1   S UBCLASS MAPPING
656
657   The number of clusters per class is an important hyperparameter that needs to be fine-tuned because
658   a small number of clusters per class will lead to a small variance (which might cause underfitting),
659   while a large number will provide a small bias (but might cause overfitting).
      In our experiments, we set the number of clusters by nk , where n is the number of samples in a

660
661   given class, and k is a constant ranging from 50 to 500. We observed the results after the fine-tuning
662   process: a small number of clusters (i.e., a large k) is usually better on the validation and test sets.
663   On the other hand, a large number of clusters (i.e., a small k) can give better results on the training
664   set but slightly worse results on the validation and test sets.
665   We also explored whether using subclass mapping before training the backbone model could be
666   beneficial. This approach is intriguing because it could potentially mitigate the impact of dataset
667   imbalance and improve the embedding process of the backbone model.
668
      However, it is important to note that the input consists solely of molecular fingerprints, which are
669
      numerical representations of molecules. These fingerprints may not provide significant information
670   for the subclass mapping process. Our test of this approach resulted in poorer performance com-
671   pared to our initial method. This notably worse result highlights the importance of a well-defined
672   projection of our Metric Transform model to the embedding space and underscores the need for an
673   effective projection to enhance the utility of the subclass mapping.
674
      To further explore the utility of subclass mapping, we conducted additional experiments:
675
676         1. Set the limit of samples for subclasses.
677
678         2. Fine-tune the backbone model on the subclassed dataset.
679
      In experiment 1, we observed a slight improvement, making it easier to achieve the results shown in
680
      Table 1.
681
682   In experiment 2, the final results significantly improved, with performance gains ranging from 0.1
683   to 0.5 for top-1 to top-50 accuracy.
684   The reason behind these improvements is that, after subclass mapping, samples from the same class
685   may belong to different subclasses while still sharing characteristics from the original class. Fine-
686   tuning the backbone model on the new subclassed dataset allows for better separation of these sub-
687   classes.
688
      Please note that the results on one-step and multi-step retrosynthesis presented above were obtained
689   without incorporating this new exploration.

      A.2   R ING -B REAKER M ODEL
693
      The idea behind the ring-breaker model is to help the model break ring systems in a given molecule,
694   thereby making it easier to decompose that molecule into smaller and purchasable precursors
695   (Thakkar et al. 2020).
696
697
      The mixed results obtained by incorporating the ring-breaker model into multi-step retrosynthesis
      (see Table 6) can be explained by the following elements:
698
699
            1. Search algorithms need to allocate separate time for evaluating both the one-step model
700            and the ring-breaker model, which reduces effectiveness when the search time is limited.
701
            2. The ring reaction dataset is not large enough to effectively cover ring systems.

      These two points lead to an ineffective situation where the one-step model is already sufficient
703   for solving small and medium molecules, but large molecules remain unsolvable (see Table 6, 8
704   and 9). Extensive research is needed to effectively incorporate ring-breaker models into multi-step
705   retrosynthesis planning.

740
      strates superior performance compared to MCTS. Every molecule successfully solved by MCTS
      was also addressed by NMCS, although NMCS required more time. One potential strategy is to use
741
      MCTS to solve the ”easier” molecules first, followed by NMCS for the more challenging ones. This
742
      approach would allow us to benefit from the speed of MCTS while leveraging the higher solving
743   performance of NMCS.
744
745   We also highlight the challenges of multi-step retrosynthesis, particularly with large molecules such
746
      as C2 and C39. These molecules contain complex ring systems that remain unsolvable in our ex-
      periments. One potential strategy is to employ a ring-breaker model to simplify the retrosynthesis
747
      planning by breaking down these ring systems. However, as discussed in Section A.2, the USPTO-
748
      FULL ring breaker model has drawbacks that affect the solving capacity of our model and search
749   algorithms for these large molecules.
750
751   Additionally, we encounter difficulties with some medium-sized molecules, such as C6 and C7,
752
      which also remain unsolvable. This issue is likely due to the lack of specific templates needed to
      break down these molecules effectively.

755   that are not in stock—Br for C22 and O=C1OC(=O)C2C3C=CC(C3)C12 for C38—but these pre-
      cursors can be easily purchased or found on eMolecules. This highlights the importance of having
