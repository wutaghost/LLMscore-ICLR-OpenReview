009                                                               A BSTRACT
010
011                We address the problem of extending a pretrained large language model to a new
012                domain that was not seen at training time, like adding a language for which the
013                original model has seen no or little training data. Popular solutions like fine-tuning
014                or low-rank adaptation are successful at domain adaptation, but formally they do
015                not add any extra capacity and degrade the performance in the original domain.
016                Our paper analyzes this extension problem under three angles: data, architecture
017                and training procedure, which are advantageously considered jointly. In particular,
018                we improve adapters and make it possible to learn an entire new language while
019
                   ensuring that the output of the neural network is almost unchanged in the original
                   domain. For this purpose, we modify the new residual blocks in a way that leads
020
                   each new residual block to output near-zeros in the original domain.
021
                   This solution of neutral residues, which borrows architectural components from
022
                   mixture of experts, is effective: with only 20% extra learnable weights compared
023
                   to an original model trained on English, we get results that are significantly better
024                than concurrent approaches (fine-tuning, low-rank or vanilla adapters) in terms of
025                the trade-off between learning a new language and not forgetting English.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
042
043
      The dominating strategy for producing foundation models involves training from scratch on a large
044
      collection of data, typically trillions of tokens, covering multiple domains. Training such models
045   is extremely costly in terms of computing resources. In view of the explosion of the number of
046   gigantic models produced in the last years (the HuggingFace model repository claims to host about
047   one million models), this training paradigm raises the problem of the economical and ecological
048   sustainability of the model production pipelines. As an example, training the Llama 3 model is
049   estimated by multiple sources to cost hundreds of millions dollars, mostly due to the training on
050   24000 flagship GPUs H100 for months1 , not counting the hidden costs of exploration.
051   In this paper, we consider the question of extending an existing model, in order to add some new
052   capabilities or knowledge without retraining it from scratch and therefore in a much more resource-
053

              https://www.theinformation.com/articles/ten-gifts-nvidia-gave-its-investors

054
      efficient manner. The solutions that are successful for domain adaptation, such as finetuning or
055   Low-Rank Adaptation (Hu et al., 2022, LoRA), are not adequate in this context because they do not
056   add any extra capacity. Therefore they are inherently limited in the amount of knowledge that they
057   can incorporate without suffering significant forgetting. This catastrophic forgetting is a well-known
058   problem in the continual learning setting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; French, 1999).
059
      In contrast, the adapters introduced by Rebuffi et al. (2017) extend a pretrained model by adding
060
      new parameters. When transferring to a new domain or task, only these new weights are trained
061   while those of the original backbone are frozen. This extension strategy, which was initially intro-
062   duced in computer vision with convolutional neural networks, was subsequently adapted to textual
063   transformers by Houlsby et al. (2019). Adapters benefit from the initial pretrained model and hence
064   exhibit competitive transfer performance, typically on par with finetuning. They enjoy several ad-
065   ditional properties: Only a subset of weights is trained. Most importantly, they formally add some
066   capacity and therefore do not suffer from the aforementioned limitations. However, adapters still
067   suffer significant forgetting in their current form and are therefore not sufficient for our problem.
068   In our paper, we build upon adapters and, accordingly, increase the capacity of the model by adding
069   feed-forward blocks to the model. In particular, we consider the use-case where we add a new
070   language to a pretrained model. We measure how the extended model performs both on the training
071   criterion, namely perplexity, but also on downstream tasks such as question answering.
072
      We then analyze how to optimize the compromise between learning a new language, and not for-
073   getting the initial knowledge of the pretrained model. We study the impact of (1) training data;
074   (2) training strategy, such as designing loss specifically intended at reducing the forgetting, and (3)
075   architecture. This leads us to identify several important factors for successfully extending a model
076   with adapters:
077
078          • Data: When learning adapters, a simple way to drastically reduce the forgetting is to keep
079            training with a small fraction of data similar to the original distribution.
080          • Architecture: An adapter gating mechanism is an effective way to ensure that the network
081            distinguishes when it should operate as the original neural network or when the new blocks
082            are desirable to process the input data.
083          • Initialization: Our analysis concurs with the observation by Houlsby et al. (2019) that
084            near-identity initialization is important when training adapters. We introduce a variant that
085            is even more drastic that the existing 0-block initialization of the output matrix.
086          • Training: The gating mechanism is advantageously informed in a supervised manner. We
087            proposed two strategies in that respect, both introducing a local loss: The first is inspired
088            by Mixture-of-Experts and involves an explicit domain classifier at each block. The second
089            (and most effective) one involves a sparsity loss whose objective is to ensure that the resid-
090            ual connections output near-zero values when the input follows the pretraining distribution.
091
      Overall, we show that multiple ingredients are advantageously intertwined to ensure that the ex-
092
      tended model still approximately implements the original function when the input is from the orig-
093   inal distribution, without being too constrained to incorporate a large amount of new knowledge.
094   In our experiments, these conclusions are validated with three models by extending the pretrained
095   model with two languages.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
099
      The success of deep learning is often associated to the outstanding performance obtained when
100
      training convolutional neural networks on large datasets such as Imagenet (Deng et al., 2009). Yet
101
      another key benefit is their amenability to transfer knowledge between tasks with a limited amount
102   of training data (Oquab et al., 2014). This transfer is usually performed with a so-called finetuning
103   stage, where the original backbone is trained in a data-efficient manner to solve a distinct target task
104   than the one employed at training time. It often requires to modify the architecture to adapt the
105   output layers to the new tasks. Several self-supervised pretraining methods are noticeably interested
106   in improving ways of pretraining models on proxy tasks (Devlin et al., 2018; Caron et al., 2021), such
107   that networks finetuned from these backbones, or used in a 0-shot manner, perform well on multiple
      tasks. In most of these settings, the backbone only requires some form of domain adaptation.

108
      Finetuning is however not sufficient when a large body of knowledge must be incorporated to the
109   network. In this section we review a body of literature related to this problem and to our work.
110   This includes solutions that add new knowledge into a pretrained network, as well as methods that
111   incorporate gating mechanisms such as mixtures of experts (MoE).
112
113
114   Parameter Efficient Finetuning. Enhancing language model capabilities using module based
115   training has gained interest in recent years due to the high cost of full finetuning. Those meth-
116   ods are known as parameter efficient finetuning methods (PEFT; Houlsby et al. (2019); Hu et al.
117   (2022); Li & Liang (2021)). Unlike full finetuning, they only require a limited amount of memory.
118
      Houlsby et al. (2019) proposed to insert small bottleneck layers, known as Adapters, within each
      transformer layer, allowing models to be finetuned by training only a small fraction of the param-
119
      eters while keeping the original model frozen. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) by Hu et al. (2022)
120
      adds trainable low-rank matrices to transformer layers while keeping the original weights frozen.
121
122
123   Continual Learning without Forgetting. Continual Learning is a widely studied con-
124   cept (De Lange et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024) as it allows the addition of new knowledge to LLM
125   after their initial pretraining. It is usually done through full finetuning in order to learn domain spe-
126   cific knowledge (Roziere et al., 2023), to enhance instruction-following abilities (Wang et al., 2023)
127   or to align LLM with human preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022). One of the biggest challenges of
128   continual learning and the main drawback of doing it through full finetuning is catastrophic forget-
129
      ting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). This is partially alleviated by using the initial training data (Robins,
      1995), when available, or as a proxy data from a similar distribution.
130
131   Several studies have investigated more advanced methods to address forgetting in continual learning
132   (Biderman et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024); Zhong et al. (2024); Riemer et al. (2019);
133   Li & Hoiem (2017)). Biderman et al. (2024) show that using LoRA helps reducing forgetting but is
134   less efficient in learning than full finetuning especially for distributions far from the LLM pretraining
135
      distribution. Wu et al. (2024) proposed a continual learning method for LLMs, which amounts to
      adding new intermediate transformer blocks in an interleaved manner. This enables the injection of
136
      new knowledge while preserving the initial capabilities.
137
138
139   Mixture-of-Experts. MoEs have a long history in neural networks (Yuksel et al., 2012), for in-
140   stance with set of classifiers where each individual classifier was specialized in a different task or
141   aspects of data. Residual architectures (He et al., 2016) and in particular transformers (Vaswani
142   et al., 2017) have renewed the interest in this strategy. In this context, they amount to replacing the
143   standard feed-forward networks (FFN) of the transformer blocks by a collection of FFNs, referred
144   to as experts. A gating mechanism selectively activates a subset of relevant experts for a given input.
145   This technique has been widely used to reduce the computational cost of inference in large language
146   models (Jiang et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024) with a focus on the design of an expert-balancing loss
147
      to promote equitable token distribution across experts (Shazeer et al., 2017; Pfeiffer et al., 2023).
148   Recent works have explored utilizing MoE’s gating mechanism to mitigate catastrophic forgetting
149   during the continual learning phase of language models. LorRAMoE (Dou et al., 2024) employs
150   LoRAs as experts, integrating them with a gating mechanism to improve performance on specific
151   downstream tasks through Supervised finetuning (SFT). LoRAMoE mitigates catastrophic forget-
152   ting in the case of SFT, but to our knowledge its effectiveness has not been studied in a broader
153
      context. In contrast, two recent parallel concurrent works are closer to our proposal: Zhong et al.
      (2024) augment Mixture-of-Experts LLMs with new modalities by addition of new experts to a pre-
154
      trained mixture of experts. Similarly, Li et al. (2024) add an MoE layer in parallel to the FFN of
155
      the transformer block to learn multilingual capabilities and mitigate catastrophic forgetting during
156   Continual Learning. Our solution shares some similarities with this work by employing mixed data
157   training. Yet Li et al. (2024) do not integrate an explicit sparsity criterion, and need to weight dif-
158   ferently the blocks of the pretrained model with those of the gated adapters. They also consider
159   multiple elements in the mixture and apply a softmax for the rooting mechanism. Since at least one
160   expert is activated in their mixture, this selection mechanism also prevents the capacity of the model
161   to produce 0-valued outputs. Our solution of neutral residues with local loss is more effective than
      simply gating adapters, as shown in our ablation Section 4.4.

186   3   N EUTRAL R ESIDUES WITH GATED ADAPTERS
187
188   The initial adapters proposal by Rebuffi et al. (2017) was initially introduced in the context of convo-
189   lutional residual networks. Their motivation was to adapt a backbone to multiple tasks by finetuning
190   on a limited amount of data, rather than adding a large body of knowledge. Therefore they con-
191   sider a limited number of trainable parameters, typically less than 10% additional weights compared
192   to the original backbone. In the context of language models, the serial adapter from Houlsby et al.
193
      (2019) adds residual feed-forward block sequentially after each multi-head attention and the original
      feedforward. In our case and as shown in Figure 2, we use parallel adapters as we did not observe a
194
      significant difference with serial adapters. This concurs with observations by Touvron et al. (2022)
195
      that blocks can be parallelized pairwise if vision transformers are large enough.
196
197   Near-to-identity initialization. One key ingredient pointed out by Houlsby et al. (2019) is that it
198   is best to start training adapters such that the initial network is near-identical to the pretrained one,
199   thereby not modifying the initial function. This is typically done by initializing the output matrix to
200   0s, as advocated in particular by Zhang et al. (2019). We are even more drastic in that respect: in
201   addition to setting the output matrix with 0s, we depart from the usual He’s initialization for both
202   the input and gating matrix and initialize them with a much lower variance, such that the model
203   remains longer close to the original one. More specifically, we reduce the variance employed in
204   He’s initialization: instead of using variance 2/d, where d in the input dimensionality of the matrix,
205
      we use a variance of 1/(d · L), where L is the number of transformer layers.
206
207
      FFN vs multi-head attention (MHA). In our experiments carried out for adding a new language,
      it is more effective to add extra weights in the form of FFN than in MHA blocks, for a given number
208
      of additional parameters. This concurs with how extra capacity is added in MoE. Similarly Li et al.
209
      (2024) present an ablation who show that adding FFN blocks is significantly better than adding
210   attention blocks. Note, this is in contrast with what is observed by Hu et al. (2022) with the LoRA
211   method, for which the best choice to adapt to different downstream tasks with few parameters is
212   to update the MHA matrix, in particular they chose to update the keys and values matrices. In our
213   preliminary experiments, we re-evaluate LoRA in our context of knowledge addition, and observe
214   that LoRA is significantly more effective when updating the FFN blocks than the MHA blocks. This
215   apparent discrepancy is likely explained by the fact that domain/task adaptation is different from the
      question of adding a large body of knowledge.

232   Mixed distribution training. As discussed in the related work, one solution to limit catastrophic
233   forgetting is to keep training with a proportion of the original data, if such data is available. This
234   strategy is typically employed with finetuning but is also effective with all baselines. Li et al. (2024)
235   report that catastrophic forgetting almost disappear when the volume of original language data is
236   five times greater than the new one, yet they also point out that mixed data training is likely to slow
237   down the learning of the new language.
238   In our work, we assume that we have access to a “similar distribution” at posttraining time when we
239   learn the adapters. We utilize a proportion p of data aimed at being similar to the original distribution
240   (but not identical), which in our case is data in English. We also consider a case in which we do
241   not know perfectly what kind of data was used at training time, like in the case of the Gemma et al.
242   (2024) model. In our ablation section 4.4, we evaluate the impact of p on learning and forgetting.
243   As a particular case, we also analyze the case p = 0, where no pretraining data is available or used.
244
245   Adapter gating & local loss. Recent transformer models commonly adopt a gated activation with
246   a SiLU non-linearity (Elfwing et al., 2018). The gating weights implement a linear operator Wg .
247   When training a vanilla adapter block for a new language, the singular values of this operator are
248   significantly more skewed than in regular transformer block from the pretrained backbone. Figure 3
249   shows this behavior, which is detrimental to the learning process. Our interpretation is that all
250
      projections instantiated by this operator tend to be colinear and therefore to behave similarly when
      combined with the SiLU non-linearity, because they implicitly operate as a old/new data switch.
251
      This hinders the capacity of the model.
252
253   To address the problem, we add a block gate as in MoE transformers (Shazeer et al., 2017) and two
254   recent works on model extension with gated MoE (Zhong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). In our case,
255   we use a single gate whose role is to operate as a selector, either explicit or implicit, coupled with a
256
      local loss applied at the block level. This gating is combined with two distinct strategies:
257
                          • Explicit selector with sigmoidal activation. This solution is inspired by MoE. In this case,
258                         the block gate is associated with a sigmoid activation. It is therefore trained as a local binary
259                         classifier whose objective is to distinguish between the old and the new data distributions.
260
261
                          • Implicit selector: ELU activation with sparsity constraint. This strategy, which is the one
                            that we refer to as “neutral residues” unless specified otherwise, does not make an explicit
262
                            classification. Instead, we let the ELU activation adapts itself the strength of its response
263
                            for the block. We also apply a loss ℓ1 on the output of the adapter governed by a hyperpa-
264                         rameter α, which favors the adapter to return a residues of 0s. It is only employed when
265                         feeding the extended model with data of the original distribution.
266
267   As one can observe in Figure 3, the gating operator has a significant effect on the singular values of
268   the gating matrix: the distribution of the singular values is less skewed than the original backbone.
269   The implicit selector is more effective than the local classifier in that respect. As we will see in
      Section 4, it is the method that offers the best compromise between not forgetting and learning.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
271
272
      In this section, we report experimental results to validate the performance of neutral residues to adapt
273
      a neural network to a new domain. We consider the case of adding or improving the multilingual
274   capacity of a large language model. More precisely, we start from an English-only model (or a
275   model that has only seen a small amount of non-English data) and finetune it on French data.
276
277
      4.1   DATASETS AND EVALUATION PROTOCOLS
278
279   Training Datasets. For the French finetuning dataset, we use a dataset extracted from Common-
280   Crawl, which was pre-processed with the following steps. First, the text content was extracted
281   from HTML using the resiliparse package. Then, we performed language identification with
282   fastText to keep only French data. Next, we performed deduplication at the paragraph level,
283   by computing a hash of each paragraph. Finally, we filtered document using a fastText linear
284   classifier, which was trained to discriminate random pages from CommonCrawl vs. high-quality
285   documents such as Wikipedia articles, textbooks, news articles or scientific articles.
286   For the English domain, we decided to restrict ourselves to text from Wikipedia. The motivation
287   for this is to use a finetuning dataset that is close, but yet different, from the training dataset of the
288   backbone model. Indeed, as discussed earlier, in many cases one does not have access to the original
289   dataset that was used to train the backbone model, and we want to demonstrate that our method is
290   robust even in the case where exact data from the original domain is not available.
291
292   Evaluation benchmarks. We consider two main ways to evaluate the performance of finetuning.
293   First, we evaluate the perplexity of the model on held-out sets, both in English and in French,
294   to measure forgetting and learning. For English, we consider text from a domain that does not
295
      correspond to the finetuning data, and use the PubMed subset from ThePile (Gao et al., 2020). The
      goal here is to evaluate how robust our method is to forgetting, especially in the case where we do
296
      not have access to the same training distribution as the original model. For French, we consider text
297
      from the same distribution as the finetuning dataset.
298
299   Second, we consider standard academic benchmarks used to evaluate large language models, such as
300   question answering or Cloze-style problems. We use the following datasets: ARC challenge (Clark
301
      et al., 2018), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), CommonSense
      QA (Talmor et al., 2018) and Belebele (Bandarkar et al., 2023). For French, we use the translated
302
      datasets from Dac Lai et al. (2023) and Sakai et al. (2024). For all datasets, we score each answer
303
      independently as the continuation of the question, and predict the most probable one. We use nor-
304   malization, following the recommendation from Gu et al. (2024). We perform 5-shot evaluation for
305   ARC challenge, CSQA and MMLU, and 0-shot evaluation for HellaSwag and Belebele.
306
307
      Backbone models. We consider three models that we use as backbone. The two first models were
308   trained internally on 2.4T tokens, and have 1B parameters. The main difference between these two
309   models is that the first model, denoted Transf-EN was trained on English data only. The second
310   model Trans-ML is MultiLingual: it was trained on a mix of English, French, German, Spanish,
311   Italian and Portuguese data. The non-English data represents roughly 12.5% of the tokens. The last
312   model is Gemma-2B (Gemma et al., 2024). It also includes a small amount of multi-lingual data
313   during pre-training. We do not have access to the training dataset of Gemma, making it a realistic
314   test case for our approach. Appendix A gives more details about these models.
315   For finetuning with new knowledge, we train during 100000 steps with a batch size of 64 for Trans-
316   ML and Trans-EN, and we train Gemma-2B during 150000 steps with a batch size of 8.
317
318   Baseline extension strategies. In our experiments, we consider the following methods:
319
             • Finetuning: we train all the weights from the backbone. We re-initialize the optimizer state.
320
321          • LoRA: unlike Hu et al. (2022), we add all the additional parameters in the FFN layers, as in
322
               our preliminary experiments this choice was significantly better for knowledge extension.
323          • Adapters: we use the vanilla adapters as Houlsby et al. (2019) with near-identical initializa-
               tion. We use parallel instead of serial blocks to have a more directly comparable baseline.

      Table 4 reports our main detailed results when we want to improve the performance in French of
367
      a pretrained model. Table 5 provides the counterpart when the new language to be added to the
368
      pretrained model is German, which overall leads to the same conclusions. As one can see, our
369   proposal is overall offering the best trade-off between learning and not forgetting compared to all

371
372   Impact of the initial model. First, one can observe that the results are consistent for all models.
373   This indicates that even if we don’t know exactly the data employed to produce the pretrained model,
374   our neutral residues is beneficial. Note, this is also evidenced by Table 2, where neutral residues

459   4.4     A BLATIONS
460
461   Ablation of architectural components and losses. The low-variance initialization proposed in
462   Section 3 improves the results in the context of gated adapters. Compared to He’s initialization,
463   it favors training the gating faster that the other weights. This hypothesis is compatible with the
464   observation that this initialization is detrimental without gating (∅).
465
466   Ablation learning rate. Table 8 reports the trade-offs achievable by different techniques when
467
      varying the finetuning learning rates, see also Figure 1. The results of our method are remarkably
      stable across a large range of learning rate (one order of magnitude from 5 · 10−5 and 5 · 10−4 ), in
468
      sharp contrast with Fine-tuning and LoRA, which are highly sensitive to this parameter.
469
470
      Ablation coefficient α. The perplexity results in Table 7 show that this weight gives a trade-off
471
      between learning and not forgetting, which is best optimized when selected α = 100. We retain
472   this value retained in all our experiments. This choice does not translate to downstream tasks in this
473   case, yet Table 6 shows that using the ℓ1 is preferable in other contexts (with no gating or sigmoid).

      5       C ONCLUSION
477
      This paper has explored the feasibility and effectiveness of extending existing foundation models to
478
      incorporate new capabilities or knowledge without the need for resource-intensive retraining from
479
      scratch. By building upon the concept of adapters, which add new parameters to a pretrained model,
480   we have demonstrated that it is possible to extend a model without compromising its original knowl-
481   edge, thereby offering a more sustainable approach than retraining from scratch. Our study focused
482   on the use-case of adding a new language to a pretrained model and evaluated the performance of
483   the extended model on both training criteria and downstream tasks. The findings highlight several
484   critical factors that contribute to the successful extension of a model while mitigating the issue of
485   catastrophic forgetting. These factors include the strategic use of mixed training data, an adapter
      gating mechanism coupled with a local loss, and the importance of near-identity initialization.
