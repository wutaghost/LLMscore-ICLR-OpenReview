       009                                                             A BSTRACT
       010
       011                     Conflict arises due to the disparity between the substantial resource demands
       012                     of pre-trained models and the limited available resources of federated learning
       013                     (FL) participants. Split learning presents a viable approach for adapting pre-
       014                     trained models to FL, involving the allocation of a small portion of the pre-trained
       015                     model to clients while deploying the remaining part on a server. Moreover, the
       016
                               application of Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) to pre-trained models has shown state-
                               of-the-art performances in parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods. However, VPT
       017
                               exhibits unsatisfactory performance in split federated learning (SFL) compared to
       018
                               its performance in centralized learning. In this paper, we first identify that VPT
       019                     falls short of expectations in SFL due to the insufficient generalization capability
       020                     of clients. To address this issue, we propose PromptSFL, which aligns the feature
       021                     spaces of prompts between clients and the server to adapt VPT for SFL. PromptSFL
       022                     transmits the final prompts in clients, termed skip prompts, to the first prompts
       023                     in the server, enabling clients to extract more common features from the server.
       024                     Additionally, we introduce a linear layer to map the prompts from clients to the
       025                     feature space in the server during this skipping process, preventing the prompts of
       026                     clients from overfitting to local datasets. Moreover, to enhance the convergence
       027
                               speed of SFL, PromptSFL employs an adaptive learning rate for clients. Extensive
                               experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of PromptSFL.

      Split Federated Learning (SFL) (Thapa et al., 2022) is an efficient and effective method for deploying
072
      pre-trained models in FL, as shown in Figure 1b. In SFL, pre-trained models are partitioned into two
073   parts between clients and a server. The portion on clients is small, while the rest of the models are
074   handled on the server (Li et al., 2022a). As depicted in Figure 2b, the memory usage on clients in
075   SFL is significantly lower than in FL, particularly with increasing batch sizes. Moreover, instead
076   of training entire models on clients and the server, we focus on fine-tuning the pre-trained models.
077   Fine-tuning in SFL provides two main advantages. Firstly, it tunes only a small subset of weights
078   rather than the entire model, enhancing training efficiency for both clients and the server (Chen et al.,
079   2023b; Guo et al., 2023b;a). Secondly, tuning a limited set of weights preserves the generalization
080   ability of the pre-trained model, thereby reducing the risk of overfitting in most downstream tasks
081
      (Han et al., 2024).
082   Furthermore, a recent prompt tuning method, Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Jia et al., 2022), has
083   drawn significant attention among parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Sohn et al., 2023;
084   Liu et al., 2024; Khattak et al., 2023), because of its superior performance while requiring only a
085   small portion of weights to be updated in the input spaces. VPT fine-tunes the weights in the input
086   spaces, freezing all pre-trained weights in each transformer block, thus maintaining the generalization
087
      ability of the pre-trained models. Pre-trained models are more adaptable to diverse local datasets
      with varying distributions from clients in SFL. Therefore, VPT, which fine-tunes the weights in the
088
      input spaces, is more appropriate for SFL compared to other PEFT methods. Consequently, we select
089
      VPT for SFL to adapt pre-trained models. However, the results indicate that VPT performs worse
090   than full fine-tuning (FFT) in SFL as the number of clients increases in a dataset called CUB (Wah
091   et al., 2011), as demonstrated in Figure 2a. These findings are in contrast to the centralized training
092   (CT), where VPT outperforms FFT (Jia et al., 2022; Han et al., 2024), as illustrated in the gray area
093   in Figure 2a when the number of clients is sufficiently small.
094

095
      number of clients. As depicted in Figure 2a, the performance of VPT significantly declines with an
096   increasing number of clients. By understanding why VPT performs worse with more clients in SFL,
097   we can address this issue to enhance the performance of VPT, thereby surpassing FFT with a large
098   number of clients in SFL. To this end, we analyze the outputs from the clients trained by VPT-SFL
099   with 100 clients, and compare them to outputs from a single client trained with VPT-SFL, akin to
100   centralized training with VPT. The t-SNE results, demonstrated in Figure 2c, indicate that the features
101   from 100 clients are more biased and fail to form a cohesive cluster. This suggests that with a larger
102   number of clients, VPT struggles to extract general features, leading to inferior performance.

104   PromptSFL, in which clients send their prompts to a server to extract more public features in SFL,

152   2.1           S PLIT F EDERATED L EARNING
153
      Split Federated Learning (SFL) (Thapa et al., 2022) is a recently proposed approach that balances
154
      client computational resources while accommodating the pre-trained models within Federated Learn-
155
      ing (FL). In SFL, a pivotal component is the "cut-layer" (Li et al., 2022a), which splits the full model
156   architecture into two parts. Clients possess a part of the model including the cut-layer and preceding
157   layers, whereas the server keeps the remaining part. Typically, the model part owned by clients is
158   relatively smaller compared to that held by the server. As shown in Figure 1b, the original full model,
159   denoted as θ, is partitioned into θ = [θc , θs ], where θc is distributed to all clients, while θs is retained
160   by the server. Assuming the outputs from client i are Ai = f (θi ), Ai is transmitted to the server,
161   which continues the forward process with the θs . Subsequently, upon computing the loss on the
      server, gradients Gs = ∂L/∂θs are used to update θs on the server, and Gs , transmitted through Ai ,

      facilitate gradient updates on client i, denoted as Gi in Figure 1b. Through the employment of SFL,
163   pre-trained models can feasibly participate in the FL environment.

      2.2   V ISUAL P ROMPT T UNING
166
167   Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Jia et al., 2022) is an efficient fine-tuning method that introduces
168   prepending training prompts within the input sequence while maintaining the pre-trained model
169   frozen. The set of prompts is defined as P = {P0 , P1 , ..., PN −1 }, where Pi−1 is prepended to the
170   input space of layer Li . VPT discusses two variants: VPT-Shallow and VPT-Deep. VPT-Shallow
171   only adds prompt P0 to layer L1 , while VPT-Deep inserts prompts across every layer. Within the
172
      context of split federated learning, we consider VPT-Deep, as prompts on deeper layers are preserved
      on the server, as depicted in Figure 3b, enabling the extraction of more features from different clients.
173
      In VPT-Deep, for each layer Li , the forward process is defined as follows:
174
175                           [xi , _, Ei ] = Li ([xi−1 , Pi−1 , Ei−1 ], θLi ), i = 1, ..., N

177   where N is the number of layers, x represents the [CLS] embeddings, and Ei−1 are the image patch
178   embeddings serving as inputs to the layer Li . In VPT-Deep, the prompts are independently injected
179   into the input sequences of each layer. Therefore, the outputs of Li are [xi , _, Ei ], not [xi , Pi , Ei ].

298   3.4               C OMPARISON WITH G ATED P ROMPT T UNING
299
300   Our work, PromptSFL, introduces a method for deploying VPT in SFL, and enhances the performance
301
      of VPT-SFL. To enable prompts in clients to extract more knowledge, we propose a method called
      skip prompts, as shown in Equation 8. Clients transmit prompts from the last layer to the first layer
302
      of the server, and the server mixes the client’s prompts with the server’s prompts. Furthermore, to
303
      align the feature space between prompts from clients and the server, we introduce a linear layer F to
304   transfer the prompts of clients to the feature space of the server.
305
306   Compared with GatedPT (Yoo et al., 2023), PromptSFL requires only prompt mixing between clients
307
      and the server. GatedPT focuses on mixing prompts between the previous layer and the current
      layer within each layer. Moreover, PromptSFL proposes a linear layer F to align the feature space
308
      of prompts between clients and the server, while GatedPT ignores the alignments of prompts from
309
      different layers. At last, GatedPT is not appropriate for FL and SFL, as we demonstrate in the
310   experimental results section.

513
514   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Lester et al., 2021; Brown et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021;
515
      Jiang et al., 2020) demonstrates its effectiveness in the NLP field, garnering attention in the CV
      field as well. CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022a) and CocoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b) introduce soft prompts
516
      in vision-language models. Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Jia et al., 2022) is proposed to adapt the
517
      prompt tuning method into the vision transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) by pretending
518   trainable prompts in the input space. GatedPT (Yoo et al., 2023) improves the performance of VPT
519   in the ViTs pretrained with self-supervised learning through a learnable gate in each input space.
520   E 2 VPT (Han et al., 2023) introduces additional learnable prompts in the input space of self-attention
521   layers. Cheng et al. (Han et al., 2024) discuss why VPT outperforms full fine-tuning in centralized
522   training. Moreover, some studies (Deng et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023) explore how to adapt VPT in
523   FL. SGPT (Deng et al., 2024) introduces shared prompts and group prompts to improve VPT in FL.
524   pFedPG (Yang et al., 2023) proposes a prompt generator to produce client-specific visual prompts,
525   adapting VPT for personalized FL.
526
527   6         L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   Limitations. In PromptSFL, clients need to send the prompts from the last layer to the server, which
530   increases the communication overhead during the fine-tuning process. Additionally, because this
531   method focuses on fine-tuning pre-trained models, privacy considerations remain a hidden limitation
532   when the server receives fine-tuned prompts from clients. The server may potentially recover input
533   data using methods from dataset distillation (Yu et al., 2023). These limitations require further
534   discussion and exploration for PromptSFL.
535   Conclusions. We propose a method called PromptSFL, which aims to enhance the ability of clients
536   to extract more general features from the server in VPT-SFL. In PromptSFL, clients send the prompts
537   from the last layer to the server. The server transfers these prompts to the feature space of the server
538   prompts according to a linear transfer function. Moreover, the adaptive client learning rate enhances
539   the convergence speed of PromptSFL. Through extensive experimental evaluations, we demonstrate
      the effectiveness of PromptSFL and analyze the improvements for PromptSFL.
