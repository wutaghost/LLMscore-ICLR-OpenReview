011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            SQL is widely used for managing relational databases and conducting interactive
014            data analysis. Now, various natural language interfaces have emerged, designed to
015
               simplify the process of crafting SQL queries by translating natural language com-
               mands into executable SQL-Code. However, the communication preferences of
016
               the deaf and hard-of-hearing community have been largely overlooked. This pa-
017
               per introduces R-KinetiQuery, a groundbreaking framework for domain-adaptive
018            sign language to SQL query translation, underpinned by a rigorous mathemati-
019            cal foundation synthesizing functional analysis, ergodic theory, and information
020            geometry. At its core, R-KinetiQuery addresses the fundamental challenge of do-
021            main adaptation in the context of multimodal language translation, specifically
022            tailored to bridge the gap between sign language communication and database
023            query languages. A key innovation lies in our application of ergodic theory to
024            analyze the long-term behavior of R-KinetiQuery under domain shift. We estab-
025            lish an ergodic theorem for the model’s time-averaged operator, demonstrating
026
               its convergence to the expected behavior across domains. This result provides a
               robust foundation for the model’s stability and adaptability in non-stationary envi-
027
               ronments. Our information-theoretic analysis reveals a deep connection between
028
               R-KinetiQuery and the Information Bottleneck principle. We derive a variational
029            bound that explicitly quantifies the trade-off between compression and prediction
030            in the model’s latent representation, providing insights into its domain-invariant
031            feature learning. Empirically, we demonstrate R-KinetiQuery’s superior perfor-
032            mance on a diverse set of domain adaptation tasks, consistently outperforming
033            state-of-the-art baselines. Our experiments span a wide range of domain shifts,
034            from subtle variations in sign language dialects to dramatic changes in database
035            schemas and query complexities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      The landscape of domain adaptation in machine learning has seen remarkable progress, particularly
040
      in natural language processing and computer vision. However, a frontier remains largely unexplored:
041
      the adaptation of visual sign language inputs to structured query languages across diverse database
042   domains. This intersection presents a unique and formidable challenge, one that R-KinetiQuery, the
043   novel framework introduced in this paper, addresses by seamlessly integrating advanced mathemat-
044   ical theories with state-of-the-art machine learning techniques.
045
      Structured Query Language (SQL) stands as the cornerstone for interacting with relational databases,
046
      enabling complex data analysis and retrieval. Yet, the formulation of accurate and efficient SQL
047
      queries often demands specialized programming knowledge, erecting a significant barrier for many
048   users, particularly those from underserved communities such as the deaf and hard-of-hearing. While
049   recent research has made strides in natural language to SQL translation, these approaches predom-
050   inantly focus on spoken or written language inputs, overlooking the unique characteristics and re-
051   quirements of sign language communication. The task of translating sign language to SQL queries
052   introduces a multi-layered complexity in the domain adaptation landscape. It requires cross-modal
053   adaptation from the visual-gestural domain of sign language to the textual domain of SQL queries,
      preserving semantic content while bridging fundamental differences in linguistic structure and rep-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      resentation. Simultaneously, it must navigate the challenge of schema diversity, adapting to generate
055   queries across varying table structures, column names, and relational configurations. The spectrum
056   of query complexity, ranging from simple selections to intricate joins and nested subqueries, adds an-
057   other dimension to the adaptation challenge. Furthermore, the inherent variability in sign languages
058   across different communities and geographic regions introduces yet another layer of complexity,
059   demanding adaptability while maintaining consistent SQL query generation.
060
      To address these multifaceted challenges, R-KinetiQuery presents a comprehensive framework
061   grounded in advanced mathematical theories. At its core, the model is formulated as a nonlinear
062   operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), providing a rigorous basis for analyzing
063   its expressiveness and generalization capabilities across domains. This functional analytic founda-
064   tion allows us to leverage powerful results, including the Representer Theorem, to characterize the
065   optimal solution in our infinite-dimensional function space.
066
      Our approach innovates by casting the domain adaptation problem in the framework of ergodic the-
067   ory, developing novel insights into the long-term behavior of R-KinetiQuery under domain shift. We
068   prove an ergodic theorem for the model’s time-averaged operator, establishing its convergence prop-
069   erties across diverse input distributions. This result provides theoretical guarantees for the model’s
070   stability and adaptability in non-stationary environments, a crucial consideration in the dynamic
071   landscape of database schemas and query patterns. R-KinetiQuery further introduces a differen-
072   tial geometric perspective on its parameter space, viewing it as a Riemannian manifold equipped
073   with the Fisher information metric. This formulation leads to the development of a natural gra-
074   dient descent algorithm that follows geodesics on the statistical manifold, significantly enhancing
075
      optimization efficiency and domain adaptation capabilities. Our analysis reveals deep connections
      between the geometry of the parameter space and the model’s ability to adapt across domains.
076
077   Leveraging recent advancements in information theory, we derive tight variational bounds on
078   the mutual information between the input sign language representations and the generated SQL
079   queries. These bounds explicitly quantify the trade-off between compression and prediction in R-
080   KinetiQuery’s latent representations, providing a principled approach to learning domain-invariant
081
      features. To address the challenges posed by high-dimensional sign language inputs, we employ
      tools from random matrix theory to characterize the asymptotic behavior of R-KinetiQuery’s in-
082
      put covariance spectrum, yielding precise estimates of the model’s effective capacity across dif-
083
      ferent domains. Our analysis extends to deep R-KinetiQuery architectures using concepts from
084   non-commutative probability theory. By proving the asymptotic freeness of the model’s layers in
085   the infinite-width limit, we enable a powerful analytical treatment of deep networks, facilitating
086   the design of domain-adaptive depth-scalable models. To validate the theoretical foundations of R-
087   KinetiQuery, we conduct extensive empirical evaluations across a diverse set of domain adaptation
088   tasks. Our experiments span a wide range of domain shifts, including variations in sign language
089   dialects, diverse database schemas, varying levels of SQL query complexity, and cross-dataset gen-
090   eralization. The results demonstrate R-KinetiQuery’s superior performance compared to state-of-
091   the-art baselines, consistently achieving higher accuracy and generalization across domains. The
092
      implications of this work extend far beyond the immediate task of sign language to SQL translation.
      By developing a rigorous mathematical framework for cross-modal and cross-domain adaptation,
093
      R-KinetiQuery opens new avenues for research in inclusive technology and accessible artificial in-
094
      telligence. Our approach provides a blueprint for addressing domain adaptation challenges in other
095   multimodal translation tasks, potentially impacting fields such as assistive technologies, human-
096   computer interaction, and universal design.

      2.1   E VOLUTION OF G ESTURAL C OMMUNICATION I NTERPRETATION S YSTEMS
132   The domain of sign language recognition primarily deals with identifying and interpreting distinct
133   signs within a given sign language. The comprehensive review by Adaloglou et al. Adaloglou et al.
134   (2021) outlines the major methodologies employed in this area. Meanwhile, sign language transla-
135   tion focuses on converting sign language inputs into spoken or written language forms. Innovative
136   techniques have emerged for this task, such as leveraging pose estimation systems Ko et al. (2019),
137   Luong et al. (2015), employing gloss annotations Yin & Read (2020a), Yin & Read (2020b), and an-
138   alyzing the articulators of signs captured in video content Ko et al. (2019), Camgoz et al. (2020). In
139   contrast, sign language production pertains to generating sign language videos from spoken or writ-
140
      ten language inputs, with current approaches exploring autoregressive generation of pose sequences,
      akin to methodologies found in machine translation Saunders et al. (2020a;b).

      2.2   B REAKTHROUGHS IN AUTOMATED S YNTAX C ONSTRUCTION FOR DATA R ETRIEVAL
144
      The generation of SQL-Code queries from natural language inputs has developed along two primary
145   trajectories: rule-based systems Yaghmazadeh et al. (2017) and deep learning models Zhong et al.
146   (2017); Yu et al. (2018b;a; 2019). A notable rule-based system, SQLizer Yaghmazadeh et al. (2017),
147   adopts a two-phase method where natural language descriptions are first transformed into a rough
148   query sketch, followed by refinements through type-directed synthesis and repair. Deep learning
149   approaches, by contrast, typically follow the encoder-decoder architecture, which encodes natural
150   language descriptions into latent representations and then generates the SQL-Code queries Zhong
151   et al. (2017); Yu et al. (2018b;a; 2019). An adjacent line of research focuses on speech-to-SQL-
152   Code conversion Song et al. (2022), where SQL-Code queries are generated directly from spoken
153
      language inputs.

      4     COMPARATIVE ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORK
448
449
      In this section, we present a benchmark for the KinetiQuery task by comparing our end-to-end R-
      KinetiQuery with several pipeline-based baselines. Our study evaluates four pipeline-based models
450
      for converting sign language video inputs into SQL-Code queries. These models adopt a two-stage
451
      approach: first, the sign language gestures are translated into textual form using a Long Short-Term
452   Memory (LSTM) network (SIGN2TEXT), and then this text is converted into SQL-Code queries
453   using a TEXT2SQL-Code translation model. The first baseline, SIGN2TEXT (LSTM)+Seq2SQL-
454   Code, utilizes an LSTM to encode sign language videos into embeddings, which are then trans-
455   lated into spoken language texts and subsequently into SQL-Code queries via the Seq2SQL-Code
456   model Zhong et al. (2017). This baseline is inspired by the established Seq2SQL-Code bench-
457   mark in the TEXT2SQL-Code domain. A variation of this method, SIGN2TEXT (LSTM)+SQL-
458   CodeNet, employs the SQL-CodeNet model Xu et al. (2017) as an alternative translation mecha-
459   nism. Additionally, by replacing the LSTM with a Transformer encoder, we introduce two more
460
      baselines: SIGN2TEXT (Transformer)+Seq2SQL-Code and SIGN2TEXT (Transformer)+SQL-
      CodeNet, which leverage the enhanced capabilities of Transformer architectures for interpreting
461
      sign language videos.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND P ROTOCOLS
465
      R-KinetiQuery and the baseline models were implemented using the PyTorch framework. All exper-
466   iments were conducted on a Linux server with 256 GB of RAM and three Nvidia H800 GPU with
467   80 GB of memory. Database column names were tokenized and embedded with a word embedding
468   layer of size 512. The Transformer encoder in our model consists of three layers with 512 hidden
469   units and an 16-head attention mechanism. We trained the models with a batch size of 64 using
470   the Adam optimizer Kingma & Ba (2014), with a learning rate of 5e-5. Model performance was
471   validated using a dedicated dataset and evaluated on a separate test set to prevent overfitting.
472
473   4.2   P ERFORMANCE I NDICATORS
474
475   Our evaluation follows established benchmarks in the TEXT2SQL-Code domain Yu et al. (2018b),
476   utilizing both traditional and domain-specific metrics. First, we employ the BLEU metric Papineni
477
      et al. (2002), which measures the precision of n-grams between generated and reference SQL-Code
      queries, to assess the syntactic accuracy of our model’s outputs. We compute BLEU scores for n-
478
      grams of the BLEU-1 through BLEU-4. Additionally, we use the ROUGE metric Lin (2004), which
479
      evaluates both precision and recall of n-gram overlaps, to further assess query generation quality.

532
      language to SQL query translation, underpinned by a rigorous mathematical foundation that syn-
533
      thesizes concepts from functional analysis, ergodic theory, differential geometry, and information
534   theory. Our work addresses the fundamental challenge of domain adaptation in the context of multi-
535   modal language translation, specifically tailored to bridge the gap between sign language communi-
536   cation and database query languages. Empirically, we have demonstrated R-KinetiQuery’s superior
537   performance on a diverse set of domain adaptation tasks, consistently outperforming state-of-the-
