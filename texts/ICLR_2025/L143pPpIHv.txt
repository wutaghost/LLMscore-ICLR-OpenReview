                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               In PAC(Probably Approximately Correct) theory, it is posited that larger hypoth-
010
               esis spaces necessitate more independently and identically distributed (i.i.d) data
011            to maintain the accuracy of model performance. PAC-MDP theory defines curios-
012            ity by assigning higher rewards for visiting states that are far from the previously
013            visited trajectory, which supports more independent and i.i.d data collection. Re-
014            cently, this field has witnessed attempts to narrow the hypothesis space by devel-
015            oping additional mechanisms that train multiple skills and facilitate the sharing
016            of information among them, thereby discovering commonalities. However, one
017            might wonder: What if curiosity could not only enhance the efficiency of data
018            collection but also significantly reduce the hypothesis space, thereby driving op-
019
               timal outcomes independently without additional mechanism used in PAC-MDP?
               Within this context, contrastive multi-skill reinforcement learning (RL) exhibits
020
               both traits. Previous research in contrastive multi-skill RL has utilized this tech-
021
               nique primarily as a form of pretraining, However, there has been scant investiga-
022            tion into whether the technique itself can reduce the hypothesis space to optimize
023            the outcomes. We have mathematically proven that curiosity provides bounds to
024            guarantee optimality in contrastive multi-skill reinforcement learning (RL). Ad-
025            ditionally, we have leveraged these findings to develop an algorithm that is ap-
026            plicable in real-world scenarios, which has been demonstrated to surpass other
027            prominent algorithms. Furthermore, our experiments have shown that different
028            skills are actually reducing the hypothesis space of the policy by being hierarchi-
029            cally grouped.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      We have inherently evolved to possess curiosity, driving us to seek out and learn from areas of high
034
      uncertainty. This natural inclination enhances our intelligence by continually pushing the boundaries
035
      of our knowledge. In the realm of machine learning, this trait is mirrored in methodologies like
036   Active Learning and the PAC-MDP (Probably Approximately Correct - Markov Decision Process),
037   which strategically navigate uncertainty to boost learning efficiency. This focus not only accelerates
038   learning processes but also ensures that model target the most valuable information, optimizing
039   intelligence enhancement across diverse environments. (Auer et al., 2002; Raj & Bach, 2022).
040
      We propose that humans instinctively adhere to general biases, consistent with the principle of mini-
041
      mal description length (Grünwald, 2007), and we underscore the efficacy of modularizing common-
042   alities. Similarly, the PAC-MDP framework enhances reinforcement learning by offering theoretical
043   guarantees, such as upper bounds of the necessary sample size to approximate the optimal policy.
044   These bounds are crucial for minimizing the hypothesis space—the set of all possible policies an
045   algorithm might consider—thereby ensuring more precise and predictable outcomes with reduced
046   sample complexity.
047
      However, unlike deliberate efforts to reduce the hypothesis space, we aim to discuss how curiosity
048   itself serves as a process of reducing the hypothesis space. In this paper, we propose a novel rein-
049   terpretation of curiosity and, building upon this new understanding, we adapt the information gain
050   formula typically employed in contrastive multi-skill reinforcement learning. This adaptation con-
051   tributes to the autonomous optimization of the hypothesis space. This research demonstrates how
052   skills self-categorize and inherently structure themselves into tree-like formations, effectively min-
053   imizing the hypothesis space. Finally, we present a practical modification of our algorithm, which
      we show surpasses the performance of existing algorithms.

730   Active Learning Active learning has historically leveraged curiosity in various forms to enhance
731   learning processes. Studies such as (Macedo et al., 2012; Vogl et al., 2019; Schmidhuber, 2007) have
732   utilized discrepancies between predictions and actual outcomes to fuel the learning mechanisms.
733   Additionally, the uncertainty-based methods discussed in (Shankaranarayana, 2023; Howard-Jones
734   & Demetriou, 2009; Zhou & Others, 2020) have been effectively employed to optimize data query-
735
      ing and model training. These methods demonstrate the versatility of curiosity-driven strategies
      within active learning paradigms, significantly enhancing both the efficiency and accuracy of model
736
      development.
737
738   Hypothesis space reduction Efforts to reduce the hypothesis space in machine learning have led to
739   significant advancements, including methods like meta-learning (Finn et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2016)
740   and transfer learning (Pan & Yang, 2010; Yosinski et al., 2014), which consolidate diverse learning
741
      tasks into a unified model framework. Moreover, deep learning integrates extensive parameters to
      discern complex patterns, necessitating clarity tools such as Explainable AI (Ribeiro et al., 2016;
742
      Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Selvaraju et al., 2017). Further innovations like Neural Architecture Search
743
      (Zoph & V.Le, 2016), Hyperparameter Optimization (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012), and Automated
744   Machine Learning (Feurer et al., 2016) continue to refine and optimize the hypothesis space from
745   the outset.
746
747
      PAC-MDP The theory of Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) (Valiant, 1984), a pivotal aspect
      of information theory, has effectively merged the significant trends of curiosity and hypothesis space
748
      reduction. This integration has shown that decreasing uncertainty across the entire space and nar-
749
      rowing the hypothesis space increase the likelihood of approximating globally optimal values. In
750   exploring efficient exploration strategies within reinforcement learning, the E3 algorithm (Kearns &
751   Singh, 1998) emerges as a foundational model, particularly for its method of strategically balancing
752   known and unknown state explorations. This approach ensures polynomial time complexity in learn-
753   ing near-optimal policies. Further elaborating on model-based reinforcement learning strategies, the
754   R-max algorithm (Brafman & Tennenholtz, 2002; Kakade, 2003) is distinguished by its methodol-
755   ogy, which posits maximal rewards for all unexplored actions. This optimistic initialization facili-
      tates effective exploration, enabling the algorithm to offer near-optimal policies within polynomial


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      time bounds. MBIE (Strehl & Littman, 2005) improves exploration by using statistical confidence
757   intervals, allowing the algorithm to adapt its exploration based on the uncertainty in the model. This
758   approach provides a more refined balance between exploration and exploitation compared to Rmax ’s
759   optimistic initialization. Delayed Q-learning (Delay-Q) (Strehl et al., 2006) enhances exploration
760   by employing optimistic initialization, like Rmax , but introduces a delay mechanism that waits until
761   enough evidence is gathered before updating state-action values.The model-free nature of Delay-Q
762   confers significant flexibility, particularly in environments where accurately modeling the dynamics

       When we engage in the learning process, we often try to fit new information into our existing frame-
1569   works of language and cognition. This approach leads us to incorporate even slightly different
1570   patterns into familiar structures, sometimes missing out on alternative perspectives that language
1571   alone cannot capture. As knowledge grows and becomes more complex, the gaps between different
1572   pieces of knowledge widen, making it harder to see how these pieces connect within our cognitive
1573   frameworks. This complexity can make it difficult to evaluate how effectively we are learning.
1574
       In response to these challenges, we break down knowledge to explore new areas, allowing for a nat-
1575   ural optimization of knowledge through diversification. Our research demonstrates this approach,
1576   highlighting the human tendency to learn and adapt. Additionally, in the field of mathematical
1577   theorem proving, increasing problem complexity makes us more aware of the limits of our current
1578   knowledge systems. This awareness can prevent us from confirming the accuracy of our conclusions.
1579   At the edge of cognitive exploration, we narrow down the scope of our study to refine and segment
1580   established knowledge, leading to new theories and hypotheses that improve optimization and fa-
1581   cilitate successful theorem proving. This model not only illustrates how curiosity drives knowledge
1582   optimization but also marks the beginning of an artificial intelligence capable of independently de-
1583
       termining what is correct and what is not.
