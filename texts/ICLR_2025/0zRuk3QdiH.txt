028                                              A BSTRACT
029
030            Text-to-video models have made significant strides in generating short video clips
031            from textual descriptions. Yet, a significant challenge remains: generating sev-
032            eral video shots of the same characters, preserving their identity without hurting
033            video quality, dynamics, and responsiveness to text prompts. We present Video
034            Storyboarding, a training-free method to enable pretrained text-to-video models
035            to generate multiple shots with consistent characters, by sharing features between
036            them. Our key insight is that self-attention query features (Q) encode both motion
037            and identity. This creates a hard-to-avoid trade-off between preserving character
038
               identity and making videos dynamic, when features are shared. To address this
               issue, we introduce a novel query injection strategy that balances identity preser-
039
               vation and natural motion retention. This approach improves upon naive consis-
040
               tency techniques applied to videos, which often struggle to maintain this delicate
041            equilibrium. Our experiments demonstrate significant improvements in character
042            consistency across scenes while maintaining high-quality motion and text align-
043            ment. These results offer insights into critical stages of video generation and the
044            interplay of structure and motion in video diffusion models.

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   Generating videos from text prompts is advancing rapidly, but it is still not feasible to create long
050   coherent video sequences. A natural alternative would be to generate multiple short videos that
051   share the same characters. Indeed, cinematic videos typically consist of many shorter shots, making
052
      them more engaging. The challenge is that although current text-to-video (T2V) models excel at
      generating individual clips, they struggle to maintain character consistency across multiple scenes.
053
      We wish to generate multiple video shots of the same characters, preserving their identity across


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      all video scenes, while preserving video quality and dynamics. Such technology would open new
055   opportunities in content creation storytelling and entertainment.
056
057   Recently, several methods (Tewel et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024) have been proposed to generate
058
      images with consistent characters across various text prompts. However, achieving consistency in
      video is inherently more challenging due to a fundamental conflict between maintaining character
059
      identity and ensuring dynamic motion. In video, the same features often encode both identity and
060
      motion. Thus, when motion occurs, models could interpret it as a change in identity. This problem
061   is unique to video and has not been explored in the context of images. Consequently, existing
062   image-based consistency methods struggle to generalize to video, failing to achieve both character
063   consistency and dynamic motion simultaneously.
064
      We present Video Storyboarding, a training-free method for generating multi-shot videos with con-
065
      sistent characters. Our approach uses pre-trained T2V models by sharing features between the video
066
      shots. We first demonstrate that the self-attention query (Q) components primarily encode motion
067   information, but they also contain identity features of the generated characters. When features are
068   shared, injecting inconsistent Q components across videos preserves motion but disrupts character
069   identity. Conversely, maintaining similar Q components across generated videos ensure character
070   identity but unifies motion.
071
      To address this, we propose a two-phase approach (Fig. 2): Q-Preservation followed by Q-Flow. In
072
      the Q Preservation phase, we maintain motion structure by replacing our Q values with those from
073   “vanilla” (unconstrained) video generation. Then, the subsequent Q-Flow phase aims to maintain
074   the optical flow of vanilla queries rather than their exact values. It employs flow maps derived from
075   vanilla key frames to guide the injection of our identity-preserving Q features. This ensures character
076   identity is maintained by placing consistent features in motion-appropriate locations. Finally, we
077   combine this with a frame selection strategy for extended attention, that promotes visual coherence
078   without freezing motion.
079   Through extensive ablation studies and comparisons with baseline methods, we show that Video Sto-
080   ryboarding significantly improves character and object consistency across scenes while maintaining
081   high-quality motion and adhering to the input text prompt. Our ablation studies provide insights into
082   the critical stages of video generation, the relationship between structure and motion, and the impact
083   of different consistency strategies on the final output.
084
      Our contributions are: (1) Video Storyboarding, a novel training-free method that enables char-
085   acter consistency in generating multi-shot video sequences, while maintaining motion adherence to
086   prompts. (2) We reveal the dual role of self-attention query (Q) features in encoding both motion and
087   identity information. (3) We propose a novel two-phase query injection mechanism to balance these
088   aspects, addressing the unique challenges posed by the temporal dimension. (4) We demonstrate
089   significant improvements in character consistency while maintaining motion quality over baselines,
090   tested with two pretrained models.

092   2   R ELATED WORK
093
094   Consistent generation aims to maintain consistent subjects across outputs produced by a generative
095   model. This task has typically been considered under through the lens of text-to-image generation.
096   A common approach is to leverage personalization (Gal et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2022) to promote
097   consistency, either through inptaining with a personalized model (Jeong et al., 2023), by iteratively
098   generating multi-character images using personalized LoRA models (Ryu, 2023), or by clustering
099   randomly generated images and training LoRAs for large, semi-consistent clusters (Avrahami et al.,
100   2023). Rather than fine-tuning a model, an encoder (Ye et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2023)
101   can be used to inject an identity at inference time, but encoders require pre-training on large datasets,
102
      and struggle to accurately generalize to arbitrary domains. Similar issues arise when working with
      models that tune the model on storyboard datasets in order to augment it with additional conditioning
103
      on sets of image frames (Feng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). Most recently, works (Tewel et al., 2024;
104
      Fan et al., 2024) explored character consistency without personalization, employing feature sharing
105   approaches to generate consistent characters across image batches, without tuning or pre-training.
106
107   In our work, we explore bringing the training-free, feature-sharing consistency approach to the realm
      of video generation, with the goal of maintaining a consistent character across video scenes.

      Zhang et al., 2023a; Bar-Tal et al., 2024). Concurrent to such approaches, image-to-video mod-

152   While not strictly text-conditioned, these can be paired with a text-to-image model to generate an
153   initial frame, which is then animated. Our work builds on existing T2V models (Chen et al., 2024),

168   3.2   T RAINING -F REE C ONSISTENT T EXT- TO -I MAGE GENERATION .
169
170   Our work extends ConsiStory (Tewel et al., 2024) from image to video generation, addressing the
171    interplay between motion dynamics and identity. ConsiStory operates in three steps. (1) Subject-
172
      Driven localization with extended Self-Attention (SDSA) – localizes the subject across a set of
      noisy generated images by aggregating cross-attention maps across layers and timesteps. To ensure
173
      subject consistency, SDSA enables each image to attend to patches of the main subject present in
174
      other image frames. This is done by extending the self-attention mechanism, allowing it to share K,
175   V features of the subject between multiple images. Unfortunately, SDSA alone diminishes layout di-
176   versity in the generated images. Therefore, (2) Layout Diversity – reinforces diversity through two
177   techniques: First, it incorporates Q features from a vanilla, non-consistent sampling step. Second,
178   it applies an inference-time dropout to the shared K, V features. Finally, (3) Refinement Injection
179   – improves consistency in finer details by injecting the O features between corresponding subject
180   patches.
181   To reduce computational complexity, and enable reusable subjects, ConsiStory uses ”anchor im-
182   ages”: Non-anchor images observe features from anchors during generation, but not vice versa.
183
184   3.3   F LOW- BASED FEATURE INJECTION
185
186   Our approach draws inspiration from TokenFlow Geyer et al. (2023), a method for text-guided video
187   editing. TokenFlow enforces temporal consistency in the diffusion feature space by propagating
188   features across frames based on inter-frame correspondences. For each feature at a given location in
189   frame f , TokenFlow identifies similar features in two nearby keyframes and creates a new feature by
190   blending them according to the frame’s relative position. This preserves the overall motion pattern
191
      from the input video being edited, while incorporating the text-guided modifications.
192

194
195   Our goal is to generate multiple video shots that consistently portray the same character across
196   different scenarios described by text prompts. Building on ConsiStory, it addresses the unique
197   challenges inherent in video generation, particularly the preservation of motion dynamics alongside
198
      character consistency. Our method comprises three key components: Framewise SDSA, a novel
      Query injection for motion guidance, and a Deartifacted Refinement Injection. These components
199
      work together to generate multiple video shots with consistent character and ensure motion fidelity.

      The pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 2.

203   vanilla pretrained T2V model and cache the query features, Qv , of each denoising step.

205   4.1   F RAMEWISE S UBJECT-D RIVEN S ELF -ATTENTION
206
      Our first step builds on the Subject-Driven Self-Attention (SDSA) mechanism (Sec. 3.2) to incor-
207
      porate subject features across multiple video shots by extending the self-attention mechanism. We
208
      identified two critical challenges when adapting SDSA to video generation: (1) reliably localizing
209   the subject during video denoising, and (2) ensuring motion fluidity is not compromised.
210
211   For subject localization, we propose using the estimated clean image xˆ0 for mask generation instead
212
      of relying on internal network activations, ensuring reliable masks even in early denoising steps. For
      motion fluidity, we introduce a framewise attention scheme, where frames with matching temporal
213
      indices across shots selectively attend each other. This prevents artifacts and frozen motion.
214
215   We term this component Framewise-SDSA. Further technical details, including the mask estimation
      process and the formal definition of Framewise-SDSA, are provided in Appendix A.4..

242   4.2   T WO -P HASE Q UERY I NJECTION FOR M OTION P RESERVATION
243
244   When generating multiple video shots with consistent subjects, we face a fundamental trade-off
245   between subject consistency and motion quality. Our experiments show thatwhile Framewise-SDSA
246   improves subject consistency, it often results in side-effects, leading to excessive synchronization of
247
      motion layout across video shots and introduces motion artifacts (Fig. 4(4th row)). These artifacts
      arise from the model’s attempt to simultaneously satisfy both the text prompt and the undesired
248
      synchronization across shots.
249
250   Prior work in ConsiStory (Sec. 3.2) demonstrated success in maintaining layout diversity for im-
251   age generation through SDSA dropout and query injection. However, our experiments show that
252   directly extending this approach to video generation produces poor results, with significant visual
253
      artifacts and compromised consistency between shots (Fig. 5). This likely occurs because ConsiS-
      tory’s vanilla-network queries are derived from latents that are influenced by consistency-preserving
254
      mechanisms in earlier steps, rather than following an independent denoising trajectory.
255
256   Our analysis (Fig. 4) reveals that query features encode both motion patterns and subject identity. In-
257   jecting only vanilla query features (Qv )preserves dynamic motion but results in inconsistent subjects
258   across shots (row 3). Conversely, using only consistency-aware query features (Qc ) ensures subject
259
      consistency but produces rigid, unnatural, and synchronized movements (row 4). This observation
      motivates our two-phase approach that leverages both feature types.
260
261   Phase 1: Motion Structure Establishment. In early denoising steps (t ∈ [T, tpres ]), we focus on
262    establishing a robust initial motion structure using a process we call Q Preservation. During this
263   phase, we directly inject vanilla query features (Qv ) from pre-generated video shots. This allows us
264   to retain the motion patterns present in the vanilla videos. Without this initial phase, later denoising
265
      steps may deviate from the from the original motion patterns, leading to degraded motion quality.
266   Phase 2: Flow-based Consistency Integration. As denoising progresses (beyond tpres ), subject
267   consistency becomes increasingly important. To address this, we introduce Q Flow, a technique
268   inspired by TokenFlow Geyer et al. (2023), where flow-based query features (Qf ) are injected to
269   incorporate subject-consistent information while preserving the original motion. Similar to Sec.
      3.3, in this phase, we derive a flow map from vanilla-generated keyframes (Qv ), which provides


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      the motion structure. We then blend subject-consistent query features (Qc ) from nearby frames, as
271   dictated by the flow. This blending process produces Qf , that adhere to the original motion patterns
272   while maintaining subject consistency across frames.
273
274
      By following this approach, we maintain the natural flow of motion established in Phase 1 and
      progressively integrate subject-consistent features without sacrificing motion quality. The formal
275
      definition of our flow-based query injection process is provided in Appendix A.5.

      4.3   R EFINEMENT F EATURE I NJECTION FOR E NHANCED C ONSISTENCY
278
279   Despite improved motion preservation and subject consistency, fine details in subject appearance
280   can still vary across frames. We address this by adapting the refinement feature injection technique.
281
282
      However, naively applying refinement feature injection solely to the conditional denoising step, as
      in ConsiStory, introduces unnatural motion artifacts. This is likely due to the conditional step uses a
283
      correspondence map to inject features from different frames, while the unconditional step does not,
284
      resulting in inconsistent feature injection. To mitigate this, we extend refinement feature injection
285   to the unconditional denoising step, using the same DIFT correspondence map. We also utilize the
286   entire frame set of each anchor video for refinement injection. This synchronized approach improves
287   overall consistency and reduces motion artifacts. For qualitative results, see Fig. 5.

      5.2.1   A BLATION STUDY
360   We conducted an ablation study to investigate the effects of self-attention query (Q) tokens on motion
361   and identity, and to highlight the contributions of our method’s components.
362
363
      Fig. 4 illustrates typical generations for different interventions on Q tokens when combined with the
      extended self-attention mechanism of Fig. 4.1. When we do not intervene in the Q tokens (Fig. 4,
364
      4th row - “No Q Intervention”), subject identity is well-maintained across video shots, but motion
365
      quality significantly degrades. This manifests in: 1) Motion synchronization: movements become
366   synchronized across video shots, e.g., the dog’s head turning simultaneously in all shots. 2) Reduced
367   variability in motion style and pose: similar actions are repeated across shots, e.g., the dog’s leap,
368   the Muppet’s centered swaying, the camera movement becomes static in the skating Muppet shot. 3)
369   Motion artifacts: to reconcile the reduced motion variability with each scene’s text prompt, videos
370   tend towards motion-artifacts. For example, the skating Muppet’s body appears frozen while its
371   legs are displaced to visually accommodate the “skating” action. In contrast, combining extended
372   attention with injected Q tokens cached from vanilla diffusion-sampled video shots (Fig. 4, 3rd row -
373   “Full Q Preservation”) restores motion but largely loses subject identity. For instance, the Muppet’s
374
      colors revert to those of the vanilla model.
375   These observations reveal the dual nature of Q tokens, which is central to the tokens’ role in the
376   generation process. Injecting vanilla Q tokens restores motion, showing their influence on move-
377   ment. Simultaneously, it leads to a loss of subject identity (e.g., the Muppet’s color change), indicat-
      ing that Q tokens also carry identity information.

378
      Our approach (Fig. 4, 1st row - “Ours”) achieves a balance between both worlds. By intervening in
379   the Q tokens throughout the generation process, we restore most of the original motion, including
380   nuanced details like body and face orientations, postures, and natural movement of specific body
381   parts (dog’s ears, Muppet’s hands and legs). Even the parallax style of video shots is preserved
382   (right Muppet video shot). Our method’s effectiveness stems from our two-step process. First, Q
383   preservation in early denoising steps establishes the motion structure before the identity is fully set.
384   Then, the flow-based Q injection allows the Q values to evolve to better match the novel (more

412   siS”) shows a naive implementation of ConsiStory with subject-driven extended attention coupled
413   across all frames in each video shot, using subject mask dropout and omitting feature injections to the
414   unconditioned diffusion pass. At each step, it also employs queries influenced by the consistency-
415   preserving mechanism of previous steps, rather than queries from an independent vanilla denoising
416   process. This results in impaired identity consistency, strong motion artifacts, and unnatural motion
417   flow of different body parts for both the rabbit and monster examples. Adding feature injection to the
418   unconditional feature denoising (4th row “ConsiS +Uncond”) resolves motion artifacts but largely
419   reduces motion magnitude (e.g. body postures are mostly frozen), and compromises identity. Next,
420
      coupling each frame in a shot with a single frame in an anchor video and avoiding SDSA dropout
      (5th row “Q ConsiS”) allows for subtle natural motion, although it remains partially synchronized.
421
      It also improves identity preservation to some degree. Unlike ConsiStory, SDSA dropout in videos
422
      hurts identity without significantly improving motion. Finally, our method (1st row - “Ours”) em-
423   ploys a novel Q intervention mechanism. It achieves richer motion with better identity and adherence
424   to the original motion of the vanilla model.

527   6            C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
528
529   In this work, we introduced Video Storyboarding, a novel training-free approach for generating
530   multi-shot video sequences with consistent characters while preserving motion quality. Overall,
531   our method provides a significant step forward in generating coherent multi-shot video sequences,
532   offering a practical solution to the challenge of maintaining character consistency without sacrificing
533   motion quality.
534   Limitations: Our approach was developed for current open text-to-video models which only gen-
535   erate very short videos. It is not known how it will operate with many-second-long videos. Also,
536   balancing identity preservation and motion quality is still not perfect. We find that Q injection may
537   be too strong and still hurt identity. To manage this, motion preservation can be compromised by
538   partially dropping out Q injection (See Section A.2).

      A.2   Q DROPOUT
805
      When Q injection is too strong, it can compromise identity preservation. To address this, we in-

808   (motion) to enhance identity preservation. This Identity-Motion Trade-off is illustrated in Fig. 12,

835   A.3   S ELF -ATTENTION IN T2V MODELS
836
837
      Our method manipulates the activations of the spatial self-attention in T2V diffusion models. We
      start by outlining its mechanism and introducing key notations.
838
839   Recent T2V diffusion models are based on a latent video diffusion model (LVDM) architecture
840   where a U-Net denoiser is trained to estimate the noise in the noisy latent codes input. The de-
841   noising U-Net is a 3D U-Net architecture consisting of a stack spatio-temporal blocks comprised of
842   convolutional layers, spatial transformers (ST), and temporal transformers (TT). The ST operate in-
843
      dependently on each video frame, without awareness of the temporal structure, while the TT operate
      independently on each temporal patch, without awareness of the spatial structure. In this work, we
844
      focus on manipulating the self-attention mechanism of the spatial transformer layers.
845
846   Let xi ∈ RP ×d represent the input features for frame i, where P is the number of patches and d
847   is the feature dimension. In self-attention, each patch generates three key components: Qi (Query
848   features) to search for relevant information from other patches, Ki (Key features) to match against
849
      queries, and Vi (Value features)
                                 √ containing information to aggregate. The attention map is computed
      as Ai = softmax(Qi Ki⊤ / dk ), and is used to combine Vi features to produce Oi the final Output
850
      features: Oi = WO · (Ai · Vi ), where WO is a linear projection matrix. Our method intervenes in
851
      this self-attention mechanism by allowing video-frames in a generated batch of videos to attend to
852   each other and be influenced by each other’s activations.

       A.6   B ENCHMARK DATASET C ONSTRUCTION :
984
985    We created a benchmark dataset comprising 30 video sets, each containing 5 video-shots depicting
986    a shared subject under different prompts. The evaluation prompts were crafted using the Claude
987    Sonnet 3.5 AI-Agent, following this protocol: each prompt consisted of three parts: (1) a subject
988    description, e.g., “A girl” (2) a setting description, e.g., “paddling out on her surfboard”, and (3)
989    a style descriptor encompassing both image and motion styles, e.g., “Anime cartoon animation” or
990    “Shaky camcoder footage”. We instructed the AI-agent to choose actions that are visually striking
991    and could be captured in a split second. Within each set, prompts shared the same subject and style
992
       but varied in settings. To ensure a challenging and representative test set, we selected a subset of 5
       prompts per subject, prioritizing those that produced videos with significant motion and subject
993
       variability when processed by the vanilla model. Importantly, to ensure fairness, this selection
994
       process relied solely on the vanilla model’s generations.

       A.7   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
997
998    Anchor Videos: Similar to ConsiStory, we utilize two anchor videos that share all features between
999    themselves. Other videos in the batch only observe features derived from these anchors.
1000
       Scalable Video Batch Processing with Sub-batch Attention: To fit large batches of video genera-
1001
       tion within available GPU memory, we process the self and cross-attention computations in smaller
1002   sub-batches. This approach uses an internal loop, and subsequently concatenates results into a single
1003   tensor. The operation remains transparent to the network, enabling the generation of larger batches
1004   of video shots.
1005
       Reproducible denoising. Our pipeline involves three denoising iterations: caching vanilla queries,
1006
       applying Q injection and Framewise SDSA, and adding refinement feature injection. To ensure con-
1007
       sistency across these stages, we maintain identical random generators for both initial noisy latents
1008   and the denoising process. This approach guarantees that each part builds upon the previous one,
1009   preserving the reliability of our reproducible denoising pipeline.
1010
1011
       Temporal Parameters: For Q preservation, we set tpres to 750. Framewise-SDSA is applied for
       t ∈ [550, 950]. Our refinement feature injection step is employed during t ∈ [590, 950].
1012
1013   Feature Injection: We apply our refinement feature injection step to the 32 × 20 self-attention
1014   layers. Other layers either produced visual artifacts or did not significantly affect identity.
1015   T2V-Turbo-V2: For T2V-Turbo-V2 we adapt our Framewise-SDSA by allowing each frame to
1016   attend to both its temporally matching frames across shots and the middle frame of each shot. Other
1017   hyper-parameters were kept the same.
