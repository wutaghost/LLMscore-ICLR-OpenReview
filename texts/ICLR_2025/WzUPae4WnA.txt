009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) of large language models (LLMs) has
012            gained considerable attention as a flexible and efficient way of adapting LLMs
013            to downstream tasks. Among these methods, weighted decomposed low-rank
014            adaptation (DoRA) has emerged as a promising approach. DoRA bridges the
015            gap between low-rank adaptation (LoRA) and full fine-tuning (FT) by decom-
016
               posing the weight matrices into magnitude and direction components, thereby
               maintaining learning behavior similar to FT. Although DoRA shows encourag-
017
               ing performance, it introduces additional parameters compared to LoRA, which
018
               potentially increases the risk of overfitting. Moreover, optimizing magnitude and
019            direction simultaneously leads to a coupled gradient updating pattern for both
020            components, limiting its learning capacity. To overcome these limitations, we
021            propose BiDoRA, a bi-level optimization-based PEFT method. In BiDoRA, the
022            direction and magnitude components are optimized on two distinct datasets at
023            different optimization levels, mitigating the risk of overfitting. Additionally, the
024            asynchronous optimization of the two components promotes their decoupling, al-
025            lowing for more flexible gradient updates suitable for various downstream tasks.
026            Evaluation of BiDoRA on fourteen datasets spanning natural language under-
027
               standing, natural language generation, and token classification reveals that it sig-
               nificantly outperforms DoRA and other PEFT methods. The superior performance
028
               of BiDoRA underscores its effectiveness. The code for BiDoRA is available at
029
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/BiDoRA-5D31.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020) have achieved state-of-
035   the-art results across a broad range of NLP tasks, from natural language understanding (NLU) (Wang
036   et al., 2019) to natural language generation (NLG) (Novikova et al., 2017). Although LLMs are pre-
037   trained on extensive datasets in the general domain, additional methods are required to adapt them
038   to specific downstream datasets for better performance. For example, full fine-tuning (FT) involves
039
      updating all pre-trained parameters on downstream datasets (Qiu et al., 2020), which typically yields
      superior results. However, as LLMs continue to scale, FT incurs significant computational costs
040
      and increases the risk of overfitting during the fine-tuning process (Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021).
041
      Alternatively, in-context learning adapts LLMs to new tasks by incorporating a few data examples
042   directly into the prompt, eliminating the need for parameter updates (Brown et al., 2020). While
043   this approach substantially reduces computational costs and mitigates the risk of overfitting, it is
044   constrained by the use of only a limited number of examples from the downstream data, often leading
045   to suboptimal performance.
046
      To address the limitations above, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Houlsby et al.,
047
      2019; Hu et al., 2021) have been introduced as a promising solution. PEFT approaches update only
048   a subset of the pre-trained parameters, achieving performance comparable to FT while requiring
049   significantly fewer computational resources. Among these methods, low-rank adaptation (LoRA,
050   Hu et al. (2021)) has gained popularity due to its simplicity and effectiveness. LoRA attaches low-
051   rank matrices to the pre-trained model weights, updating only these matrices during fine-tuning
052   without changing the model architecture. However, LoRA exhibits a parameter updating pattern
053   distinct from FT, which may limit its learning capacity (Liu et al., 2024). Specifically, when de-
      composing the parameter updates of both methods into magnitude and direction components, the

081   explicit reparameterization of the pre-trained weights matrix as the product of magnitude and direc-

090   To address the challenges above, we propose BiDoRA, a bi-level optimization-based weight-
091   decomposed low-rank adaptation method for parameter-efficient fine-tuning of LLMs. BiDoRA
092   mitigates overfitting and facilitates a flexible weight updating pattern by separately optimizing the
093
      magnitude and incremental direction components on different splits of the downstream datasets us-
      ing distinct optimization steps. BiDoRA is based on a bi-level optimization framework: At the
094
      lower level, the low-rank incremental direction component is updated using the training split of the
095
      downstream dataset, while the magnitude component remains fixed. At the upper level, the magni-
096   tude component is updated by minimizing the loss on the validation split via hypergradient descent.
097   These two optimization steps are performed iteratively until convergence. Figure 1 provides an
098   overview of BiDoRA.
099

100
      tively reduces the risk of overfitting. A similar strategy is utilized in the well-established practice of
101

102   parameters are learned using different splits of the training dataset. Since the architecture selection

104   complex supernetwork can result in severe overfitting. Similarly, by treating the magnitude compo-

152   2.1   PARAMETER E FFICIENT FINE - TUNING M ETHODS
153
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods aim to reduce the high costs associated with fully
154
      fine-tuning large-scale models by updating only a relatively small subset of pre-trained parameters,
155
      rather than the entire model, to adapt to downstream tasks. Existing PEFT methods can be mainly
156   categorized into three types. The first category, known as adapter-based methods, injects additional
157   trainable modules into the original frozen backbone. For instance, Houlsby et al. (2019) suggests
158   adding linear modules in sequence to existing layers, while He et al. (2021) proposes integrating
159   these modules in parallel with the original layers to enhance performance. The second category is
160   prompt tuning methods, which add extra soft tokens (prompts) to the initial input. During the fine-
161   tuning stage, only these trainable soft tokens are updated, as demonstrated in works such as Lester
      et al. (2021) and Razdaibiedina et al. (2023). Unfortunately, the first two categories lead to increased


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      inference latency compared to fully fine-tuned models. The third category is low-rank adaptation
163   methods, pioneered by the foundational work LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). These methods attach low-
164   rank matrices to pre-trained weights and use only these matrices for weight updates during fine-
165   tuning. Since low-rank updates can be merged with pre-trained weights before inference, low-rank
166   adaptation-based PEFT methods do not increase inference time. Following LoRA, Zhang et al.
167   (2023) applies SVD decomposition to low-rank matrices and prunes less significant singular values
168   for more efficient updates. Zhang et al. (2024b) uses meta-learning to search for the optimal rank
169   of LoRA matrices, further improving its performance on downstream tasks. Most recently,Liu et al.
170   (2024) uses weight decomposition analysis to reveal that LoRA exhibits a distinct weight updating
171
      pattern compared to FT, which may constrain its learning capacity. Therefore, DoRA (Liu et al.,
      2024) was then proposed to bridge the gap between LoRA and FT. DoRA decomposes the pre-
172
      trained weights into two components—magnitude and direction—and fine-tunes both, which results
173
      in a more closely aligned updating pattern compared to FT.
174
175
176   2.2   B I - LEVEL O PTIMIZATION
177
178   Bi-level optimization (BLO) has been widely applied in various machine learning tasks, including
179   meta-learning (Finn et al., 2017; Rajeswaran et al., 2019), neural architecture search (Liu et al.,
180   2018; Zhang et al., 2021), and hyperparameter optimization (Lorraine et al., 2020; Franceschi et al.,
181   2017). Despite its wide usage, solving BLO problems can be challenging due to the inherent nature
182   of nested optimization problems. Several algorithms have been proposed to address this challenge,
183   including zeroth-order methods such as Bayesian optimization (Cui & Bai, 2019) and first-order
184
      algorithms based on hypergradients (Pearlmutter & Siskind, 2008; Lorraine et al., 2020). Among
      these approaches, gradient-based BLO has received significant attention because it can scale to
185
      high-dimensional problems with a large number of trainable parameters. In this work, we extend the
186
      application scenarios of gradient-based BLO to develop a robust and effective parameter-efficient
187   fine-tuning method for pre-trained models.
188

      3     P RELIMINARY: W EIGHT-D ECOMPOSED L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
191
192
193   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) involves attaching the product of two low-rank matrices to the pre-
194   trained weights and fine-tuning these low-rank matrices on downstream datasets with the pre-trained
195
      weights frozen. It is based on the assumption that parameter updates made during fine-tuning ex-
      hibit a low intrinsic rank. Formally, given a pre-trained weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×k , LoRA attaches
196
      a low-rank update matrix ∆W ∈ Rd×k to the pre-trained weight. This update matrix can be further
197
      decomposed as ∆W = BA, where B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k are two low-rank matrices, with
198   r ≪ min(d, k). Consequently, the weight matrix W ′ after LoRA fine-tuning can be represented as
199   follows:
200
201                                     W ′ = W0 + ∆W = W0 + BA                                         (1)
202
203   In this setup, the pre-trained weight matrix W0 remains fixed during the fine-tuning process, while
204   the LoRA matrix ∆W is updated. However, after performing weight decomposition on fine-tuned
205   weight matrices, it was found that LoRA and full fine-tuning exhibit different learning patterns (Liu
206   et al., 2024). To bridge this discrepancy, weight-decomposed low-rank adaptation (DoRA, Liu et al.
207   (2024)) further reparameterizes the weight matrices by explicitly decomposing them into learnable
208   magnitude and direction components. Formally, DoRA performs adaption as follows:
209
210                                          V + ∆V        W0 + BA
                                   W′ = m              =m                                               (2)
211                                         ∥V + ∆V ∥c    ∥W0 + BA∥c
212
213   where the incremental direction component ∆V is parameterized as a product of two learnable low-
214   rank matrices, B and A, while the magnitude component m ∈ R1×k is a learnable vector. Here,
215   ∥ · ∥c represents the vector-wise norm of a matrix computed across each column. In DoRA, both
      components are optimized concurrently on a single downstream dataset.

218   4.1   OVERVIEW OF B I D O RA
219
220   Our method, BiDoRA, optimizes the trainable parameters in DoRA layers by solving a bi-level
221   optimization problem. Let M = {m1 , m2 , . . . , mn } denote the set of magnitude components for
222   all n DoRA modules, and V = {∆V1 , ∆V2 , . . . , ∆Vn } denote the set of corresponding incremental
223   direction components. Specifically, we first learn the incremental direction components V ∗ (M) on
224   the training split of the downstream dataset Dtr at the lower level. The magnitude component M
225
      is tentatively fixed at this level, thus the resulting optimal incremental direction component V ∗ (M)
      is a function of M. At the upper level, we determine the optimal magnitude component M∗ by
226
      optimizing the loss on a validation split Dval . In practice, Dtr and Dval are typically created by
227
      splitting the original training set without using additional data. This bi-level optimization problem
228   is solved using an efficient gradient-based algorithm, where parameters in two levels are optimized
229   iteratively until convergence. Related convergence analyses of this type of gradient-based bi-level
230   optimization algorithms can be found in Pedregosa (2016), Rajeswaran et al. (2019), and references
231   therein. The generalization analysis has also been studied in Bao et al. (2021).
232

236   et al., 2018) and can effectively mitigate the overfitting issue (Balestriero & richard baraniuk, 2018).
237
      Therefore, we define a Gram regularization loss (Xie et al., 2017) for the direction component:

285    Note that these two levels of optimization problems are mutually dependent on each other. The
286    solution of the optimization problem at the lower level, V ∗ (M), serves as a parameter for the upper-
287    level problem, while the optimization variable M at the upper level acts as a parameter for the
288    lower-level problem. By solving these two interconnected problems jointly, we can learn the optimal
289    magnitude component M∗ and incremental direction matrices V ∗ in an end-to-end manner.
290    Two reasons exist behind the choice of setting the magnitude component as the upper level instead of
291    the converse one: 1) In literature, the upper level usually has much fewer parameters than the lower
292    level. In our case, the design of setting the magnitude of complexity O(k) as the upper level and
293    the direction of complexity O(dr + kr) as the lower level is consistent with the common practice.
294    2) BiDoRA resembles the DARTS method (Liu et al., 2018) in neural architecture search where the
295    subnets are selected by a selection variable. Specifically, the magnitude vector resembles a selection
296    variable on the direction matrix by softly selecting each direction (subnets) via scaling.

      6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
530
531
      We propose BiDoRA, a novel bi-level optimization framework for parameter-efficient fine-tuning
      of large-scale pre-trained models. By conducting weight decomposition following the DoRA ap-
532
      proach, our method trains the two components separately in two interconnected optimization levels
533
      using different sub-datasets. In this way, BiDoRA not only decouples the learning process of the
534   two components, resulting in a learning pattern closer to FT, but also effectively alleviates over-
535   fitting. Empirical studies on various NLP tasks demonstrate that BiDoRA outperforms DoRA and
536   other baselines, highlighting the effectiveness of our method. We leave enhancing the efficiency
537   of BiDoRA for future work. Overall computational costs can be reduced by using more accurate
538   hyper-gradient estimators, such as implicit differentiation (Rajeswaran et al., 2019) and the SAMA
539   estimator (Choe et al., 2024), which can be easily integrated into our framework thanks to support
      from the Betty library (Choe et al., 2023).

944   D    T HEORETICAL I NSIGHTS
945
946   We can gain several insights into how BiDoRA can alleviate the overfitting issue by analyzing both
947   the gradient of the bi-level optimization and theoretical results from existing work.
948   In terms of gradient analysis, we can demonstrate the robustness of our method by examining the
949   behavior of the loss L′val (V, M) = Lval (V − ξ∇V Ltr (V, M), M). We analyze its gradient with
950   respect to the magnitude parameters M using the chain rule,
951
                                        dL′val   ∂L′val   ∂ V̄   ∂L′val
952                                            =        +      ×                                           (9)
953
                                         dM       ∂M      ∂M      ∂ V̄
                                                                        ∂ V̄
954   The second term on the right-hand side, particularly the term ∂M       , referred to as the best-response
955   Jacobian in the literature, captures how the direction component would react to changes in the mag-
956   nitude. More specifically, the update of M must consider not only the current value of the direction
957
      V but also additional information about how the direction responds to changes in the magnitude.
      This facilitates finding a globally optimal magnitude value, thereby enhancing stability and robust-
958
      ness. Consequently, the model becomes more resilient to overfitting. In contrast, if ξ is set to 0,
959
      Algorithm 1 reduces to a first-order approximation, which performs coordinate descent for M and
960   V on different empirical distributions. In this scenario, the second term in Eq. (9) vanishes, making
961   the approach insufficient to alleviate overfitting effectively.
962
963
      On the other hand, we can also see the generalization guarantees of BiDoRA using various theoret-
      ical works in the literature (Oymak et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022;
964
      Huang et al., 2022). For example, Theorem 1 in Oymak et al. (2021) provides strong evidence of
965
      the benefits of applying bi-level optimization to alleviate overfitting. Oymak et al. (2021) discussed
966   the generalization guarantees for neural architecture search with a train-validation split. They as-
967   sumed the same scenario as ours: using bi-level optimization where one optimizes the weights over
968   the training data (lower-level problem) and hyperparameters over the validation data (upper-level
969   problem). The theorem shows that as soon as the size of the validation data exceeds the effective
970   number of hyperparameters (up to logarithmic factors), the test error is close to the validation error
971   (i.e., validation error is indicative of the test error). In practice, the number of magnitude param-
      eters is generally less than or comparable to the size of the validation set, making this assumption

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       feasible. We empirically show that validation performance and test performance align, as theoret-
973    ically supported by Oymak et al. (2021). In Figure 5, we plot the performance on the training,
974    validation, and test datasets for the CoLA and SST-2 datasets during the search phase. The first
975    observation is that training performance always overfits until reaching zero error, whereas validation
976    performance closely tracks test performance. This training behavior aligns with the fact that large-
977    capacity networks can perfectly fit (Du et al., 2019; Ji & Telgarsky, 2019; Oymak & Soltanolkotabi,
978    2020) in the overparameterized setting, where the available data (less than 10k for CoLA and 67k

981
       ization loss. Thus, as shown in Figure 5, the validation phase serves as a crucial test proxy in the
