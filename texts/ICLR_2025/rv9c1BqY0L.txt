009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recommender systems play a central role in numerous real-life applications, yet
012            evaluating their performance remains a significant challenge due to the gap be-
013            tween offline metrics and online behaviors. We introduce SimUSER, an agent
014            framework that serves as believable and cost-effective human proxies for the eval-
015            uation of recommender systems. Leveraging the inductive bias of foundation
016
               models, SimUSER emulates synthetic users by first identifying self-consistent
               personas from historical data, enriching user profiles with unique backgrounds
017
               and personalities. Then, central to this evaluation are users equipped with persona,
018
               memory, perception, and brain modules, engaging in interactions with the recom-
019            mender system. Specifically, the memory module consists of an episodic memory
020            to log interactions and preferences, and a knowledge-graph memory that captures
021            relationships between users and items. The perception module enables visual-
022            driven reasoning, while the brain module translates retrieved information into ac-
023            tionable plans. We demonstrate through ablation studies that the components of
024            our agent architecture contribute to the believability of user behavior. Across a set
025            of recommendation domains, SimUSER exhibits closer alignment with genuine
026            humans than prior state-of-the-art, both at micro and macro levels. Additionally,
027
               we conduct insightful experiments to explore the effects of thumbnails on click
               rates, the exposure effect, and the impact of reviews on user engagement. The
028
               source code is released at https://github.com/SimUSER-paper/SimUSER.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recommender systems (RS) have become an indispensable component of our day-to-day lives, of-
034   fering personalized user experience and improving satisfaction Li et al. (2024b). By examining
035   granular preferences, historical data, and contextual factors, RS can deliver recommendations that
036   are tailored to meet individual tastes. Despite their widespread adoption, a key challenge hindering
037
      the advancement of the field is evaluation Yoon et al. (2024). The difficulty arises from the dis-
      crepancy between offline metrics (non-interactive), which are typically used during development,
038
      and real-life user behaviors, which these systems encounter post-deployment Zhang et al. (2019).
039
      This results in models that perform well in controlled environments but fail to meet expectations in
040   practical use cases. Such a limitation is further exacerbated by the inherent shortcomings of offline
041   evaluation, notably the inability to measure business values such as user engagement and satisfac-
042   tion Jannach & Jugovac (2019). On the other hand, deploying and testing RS in real-world settings
043   is both costly and labor-intensive, underscoring the imperative need for reliable and affordable (in-
044   teractive) evaluation methods.
045
      Recent breakthroughs in Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown promise in human behav-
046   ior modeling by enabling the creation of autonomous agents. Generative agents have demonstrated
047   capabilities in lifelong learning through automatic skill discovery Wang et al. (2023a) or setting
048   consistent goals during exploration Du et al. (2023). LLMs have also been applied to various social
049                                                                                                         3
      simulations, such as hospital Li et al. (2024a) and city Park et al. (2023). In a different spirit, S
050   Gao et al. (2023) simulates the dynamic evolution of opinions in a social network. In the realm
051   of recommendation systems, RecMind Wang et al. (2023e) explores the concept of autonomous
052   recommender agents equipped with self-inspiring planning and external tool utilization. Recently,
053   InteRecAgent Huang et al. (2023) has extended this idea by proposing memory components, dy-
      namic demonstration-augmented task planning, and reflection. Nevertheless, their primary focus

054
      remains on identifying candidate items that align with user preferences. In addition, these agents
055   are overly simplified representations of human users, limited to predicting item ratings based on
056   viewing history. Recently, RecAgent Wang et al. (2023c) has attempted to introduce more diverse
057   user behaviors, taking into account external social relationships. Another work, Agent4Rec Hou
058   et al. (2024), delves into generating faithful user-RS interactions via agent-based simulations, where
059   agents are equipped with a memory module. However, a common characteristic of existing stud-
060   ies is their insulated nature — they primarily rely on knowledge embedded within the model’s
061   weights, neglecting the potential benefits of integrating external knowledge and user-item relation-
062   ships. Furthermore, prior approaches often disregard user personas (e.g., personality traits) and fail
063
      to incorporate visual signals, despite images significantly shaping user experience and emotion.
064   To enable synthetic users, this paper describes an agent architecture built upon large language mod-
065   els. Our methodology consists of two phases: (1) self-consistent persona matching and (2) recom-
066   mender system evaluation. In Phase 1, we leverage the semantic awareness of LLMs to extract and
067   identify consistent personas from historical data, encompassing unique backgrounds, personalities,
068   and characteristics. In Phase 2, we impersonate these identified personas to simulate believable hu-
069
      man interactions. This involves a retrieval-augmented framework where the agent interacts with the
      recommender system based on its persona, memory, perception, and brain modules. The memory
070
      module comprises an episodic memory and a knowledge-graph memory. The former is the episodic
071
      memory that records, in natural language, a comprehensive list of the agent’s experiences. The lat-
072   ter captures relationships between users and items, accounting for the influence of other users and
073   prior beliefs about items. Unlike existing studies that solely rely on text, our perception module
074   incorporates visual cues into the agent’s reasoning process. Finally, the brain module is respon-
075   sible for translating retrieved information and the current simulator observation into action plans
076   such as watch, click, or exit. Following action selection, the user engages in self-reflection to
077   synthesize memories into higher-level inferences and draw conclusions.
078   To our knowledge, this study is one of the pioneering works in developing a general LLM-based
079   agent framework for systematic evaluation of recommender systems, integrating persona extraction,
080   visual-driven reasoning, and domain-specific prior knowledge to simulate realistic user interactions.

084
085   LLMs as Human Proxies. The use of LLMs to replace costly human trials is becoming increas-
086   ingly popular. For instance, Owoicho et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2024d) examine LLMs as user
087   simulators for conversational search, while Hämäläinen et al. (2023) seeks to generate synthetic user
088   experience data in human-computer interaction (HCI). Significant progress have also been achieved
089   in the area of collaborative cooperation. For instance, CAMEL Li et al. (2023a) describes a frame-
090   work for agent cooperation that features a commander for planning and executors for task imple-
091   mentation. Qian et al. Qian et al. (2023) introduces a virtual software company where agents, each
092   assigned roles such as engineer, work together to complete software development projects. Besides,
093
      Aher et al. (2023) explores LLMs’ ability to replicate human behavior for social science experi-
      ments. Other researchers have created simulation environments where LLM agents interact with
094
      one another, producing realistic daily activities Park et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2023) or generating
095
      mobility trajectories Wang et al. (2024b). Nonetheless, their potential for assessing recommender
096   systems and addressing inherent issues in recommendation remains largely unexplored.
097
098   Simulating Users in Recommendation. Conversational RS initially tackled the recommendation
099
      problem using bandit models, emphasizing the quick update of traditional systems through item
      selection and binary feedback from synthetic users Christakopoulou et al. (2016). Taking this further,
100
      Zhao et al. (2023) created a simulation platform where users not only chat about recommendations
101
      but also navigate websites, search for items, and share opinions on social media. Recent techniques
102   have added more natural language flexibility, but user responses are usually limited to binary or
103   multiple-choice formats Lei et al. (2020). Evaluations typically involve predefined target items for
104   each user, with success measured by the number of turns needed to identify these targets Guo et al.
105   (2018); Sun & Zhang (2018). Another technique employs agenda-based simulations, utilizing state
106   diagrams to guide actions, and deeming a recommendation successful when the conversation reaches
107   a “complete” state Zhang & Balog (2020); Zhang et al. (2022). In spite of this, these simulations
      often rely on fixed rules and scripted dialogues, lacking the variability seen in human interactions.

      Zhang et al. (2023) that delves into the generative capabilities of LLMs for modeling user interac-

129
      humans using detailed personas that are inferred from their interaction history. We also incorporate
130
      a perception module to integrate visual information in decision-making, mapping thumbnail items
131   to token embeddings of the language model. Furthermore, the present study explores the potential
132   for graph-based retrieval and features a richer spectrum of actions such as “going to previous page”
133   or “clicking”, to better replicate influential factors and user experience.

170
171   This phase involves assessing the most plausible persona based on historical data. A persona p en-
172   compasses a set of features that characterize the user: age, personality, and occupation. Personal-
173   ity traits are defined by the Big Five personality facets: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion,
174   Agreeableness, and Neuroticism, each measured on a scale from 1 to 3. We postulate that these
175   factors are critical for modeling personalized preferences. Given the difficulty of obtaining such
176   granular features in real-world settings, our methodology seeks to systematically infer personas
177   from the user’s interaction history.
178   Persona Extraction.                          Assuming a user u and a set of interactions
179   {(i0 , rui0 ), (i1 , rui1 ), . . . , (in , ruin )}, we first query the LLM to produce a short summary
180   su of the user’s preferences. To do so, we randomly select 50 items from the user’s viewing history.
181   Items rated 4 or above are categorized as liked, while those rated below 3 are deemed disliked. This
182   summary describes its unique tastes and rating patterns.
183   Following this, both the summary su and historical data are combined in the prompt, instructing the
184   LLM agent to generate a persona that matches the interaction history for this user. To enhance the
185   diversity in the generated personas, the LLM is provided a list of possible ages, personalities, and
186   occupations. For each user, a set of m (m = 5) candidate personas is generated, denoted as P.

      3.2.3    M EMORY M ODULE
252
253   It is of vital importance for an agent to maintain a memory of the knowledge and experience it has of
254   the world and others. We propose to use an episodic memory storing the interactions with the RS and
255   a knowledge-graph memory that leverages graph-structured data to capture relationships, enabling
256   the model to access knowledge beyond what is inherently present in the LLM’s parameters.
257     Episodic Memory retains a comprehensive record of the user’s interactions with the recommen-
258   dation system, including ratings, liked and disliked items, as well as the underlying reasons. For
259   instance, an entry might be “You rated a movie called ’Star Wars: Episode VI’ 4 out of 5”. The
260   memory is initially populated with the user’s viewing and rating history. Each time SimUSER ex-
261   ecutes a new action or rate an item, the corresponding interaction is added to the episodic memory.
262   At inference time, the agent conditions its decisions on information retrieved from this memory.

265   memory. We implement a self-ask strategy by prompting the LLM to raise follow-up questions
266   regarding the query. These questions, along with the initial query, then serve as queries for vector
267   similarity search. Given a query q, the top-k1 documents d that have the highest similarity scores
268   s(q, d) when compared with the query are retrieved in this step:

      3.3   B RAIN M ODULE
325
326   We endow each agent with a decision-making module that derives the agents’ subsequent actions
327   based on the retrieved information, considering their persona, tastes, emotions, and fatigue. It in-
328   volves 1) deciding on items of interest, 2) rating them and providing feelings, 3) selecting the next
329   action, and 4) engaging in post-interaction reflection. To replicate human-like sequential reasoning,
330   we employ Chain-of-Thought prompting, repeatedly performing the four steps.
331   Watching Items: In our simulation, agents browse items page by page. According to their prefer-
332   ences, persona, and watching history, they assess each item on the page and may decide to “watch”
333   certain items — agents select items that interest them. To address the inherent bias towards positive
334   sentiment often observed in LLMs Yoon et al. (2024), SimUSER incorporates a pickiness modifier
335   into the prompt: You are {pickiness} about {item type}. When available, we enrich
336   item descriptions with their thumbnail captions to enable multimodal reasoning. Finally, for each
337   item i, the top-k2 similar items and their ratings are retrieved from the knowledge graph mem-
338   ory, helping to mitigate LLM hallucinations — reducing the impact of any singular bias towards or
339
      against an item, while enriching context information.
340   Providing Feelings and Rating Items This action is triggered once the user has found the items of
341   interest. Intuitively, a real user may produce much feelings after watching an item, which will be
342   stored in their memory and influence their future cognition and behaviors. Along with the item rating
343   ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}, we query the user’s feelings about the watched items and leverage such information
344   to update the memory module. That is, newly liked and disliked items are fed back into the memory
345
      module to influence the agent’s future behavior. In order to enhance consistency, users are asked to
      provide a reason for their feedback.
346
347   Emotion-driven Action Selection Once the recommender system returns the search or recommen-
348   dation results, including n items each time, the agent discerns the current recommendation page and
349   retrieves its interaction history. Drawing upon these insights combined with its persona, the agent
350   decides whether to [EXIT] the system, go to [NEXT] page, return to a [PREVIOUS] page, or
351
      [CLICK] on an item to access more details. To do so, the agent sequentially: 1) estimates its sat-
      isfaction level with preceding recommendations, 2) generates its current fatigue level Zhang et al.
352
      (2023), 3) infers its current emotion, such as EXCITED, and 4) selects the most suitable action.
353
      Satisfaction level, fatigue, and emotion are dynamic elements that the agent employs to adapt its
354   actionable plan with the recommender system. If the agent decides to click on an item, the item
355   is displayed with an extended description that replaces the short {item description} as outlined
356   in Appendix B, which is then used to determine whether it wishes to engage further with the item.
357   Finally, if [EXIT] is selected, a satisfaction interview is conducted to gather granular opinions and
358   ratings on the presented recommendations.
359
      Post-interaction Reflection LLM-Empowered agents, when equipped with only raw episodic mem-
360   ory, struggle to generalize or make inferences Park et al. (2023). Post-interaction reflection is a
361   mechanism designed to let agent learn from interactions and improve future alignment with their
362   persona. After the agent engages in assessing and rating the items on the page, we collect inter-
363   action data, including the items clicked and watched, along with the user’s explicit feedback (e.g.,
364   ratings, feelings). The first step in reflection is for the agent to determine what to reflect on, then we
365   prompt the language model to extract insights and cite the particular records that served as evidence
366   for the insights. The post-interaction reflections are fed back into the episodic memory.

      4.2     B ELIEVABLY OF S YNTHETIC U SERS
400
401   A central question is: How accurately do synthetic users replicate the behaviors of real humans?
402   In order to appropriately respond to recommendations, synthetic users must possess a clear under-
403   standing of their own preferences. Thereby, we query the agents to classify items based on whether
404   their human counterparts have interacted with them or not. Specifically, each of the 1,000 agents
405   is randomly assigned 20 items. The proportion of items that the user has interacted with but was
406   not used for persona and memory modules initialization to those that the user has not interacted
407   with (represented as yui = 0) is set as 1 ∶ m with m ∈ {1, 2, 3, 9}. We treat the problem as a
408   binary classification task, taking values between 0 and 1. The results are presented in Table 1, from
409
      which we can see that SimUSER agents accurately identify items that are aligned with their own
      tastes. Across varying levels of distractors, SimUSER exhibits significantly superior performance
410
      compared to RecAgent and Agent4Rec, as indicated by paired t-tests at 95% confidence level (p <
411
      0.002). Our method achieves an accuracy ranging from 78% to 80%, and recall between 26% and
412   76%, whereas Agent4Rec’s accuracy spans from 60% to 71%, and recall varies from 19% to 73%.
413   We attribute SimUSER’s improvements to the use of a persona module augmented with granular
414   information (e.g., age, occupation) obtained via persona matching. Notably, Agent4Rec displays
415   a tendency to favor newly encountered items positively, resulting in a higher rate of item approval
416   compared to their human counterparts. SimUSER overcomes this drawback by leveraging the KG
417   memory to retrieve similar items, reducing bias towards unfamiliar items.

512
513   LLM Evaluators Chiang & Lee (2023) have validated LLM as an evaluator, achieving comparable
514   performance with expert evaluators. Thereby, we use GPT-4o to evaluate the interactions and feed-
515   back generated by generative agents. That is, we collected the interactions of the agents and asked
516   GPT-4o to differentiate whether these interactions are from humans or AI-generated. A 5-point
517   Likert scale questionnaire was used. In this scale, a higher score indicates a closer resemblance to
518   human-like responses. Although both methods leverage the semantic awareness of LLMs to achieve
519
      near-human-level performance, the results in Table 4 demonstrate that our method significantly out-
      performs Agent4Rec. The memory and persona modules are among the main factors contributing
520
      to the faithfulness of our method. We also noticed that letting the agent estimate its tiredness, feel-
521
      ing and emotion greatly enhances the believability and consistency of its responses. On the other
522   hand, in Agent4Rec, tendencies to [EXIT] the recommender system early and provide inconsistent
523   ratings for similar items — ranging from low to high, contribute to suspicions of AI involvement.

      5     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this study, we present a simulation framework for leveraging LLMs as believable user proxies.
530   Our two-phase approach includes persona matching and interactive recommender system assess-
531
      ment, seeking to align user interactions more closely with real-world user behaviors. Key novelties
      of our method include the integration of detailed user personas, a knowledge-graph memory mod-
532
      ule, alongside integrating visual cues to emulate visual-driven reasoning. The present study also
533
      introduces a post-interaction reflection mechanism to let the agent discover high-level insights from
534   interactions. We evaluate SimUSER across various recommendation domains, including movies,
535   books, and video games. Results demonstrate closer alignment of our agents with their human
536   counterparts at both micro and macro levels. We further explore the influence of thumbnails on user
537   engagement and the significance of reviews in user decision-making. Experimental findings high-
538   light the potential of LLM-driven simulations in bridging the gap between offline metrics and online
539   evaluation, paving the way for advancements in the development of systematic and cost-efficient
      evaluation of recommender systems.

      Experimental Settings. We separate the dataset into training, validation, and test sets (80/10/10%).
759   Relationships between users and items from the training/validation and test sets were excluded
760   from the knowledge graph memory to prevent data leakage. In this paper, we report results
761   for SimUSER with simulation SimUSER(sim), and without simulation SimUSER(zero). In
762   SimUSER(zero), the agent’s memory module is initialized from the history of its human coun-
763   terpart. When the review score for an item is greater than 4, the agent stores a memory entry in
764   the form I liked {item name} based on my review score of {score}. For a
765   score of 2 or below, the following format is utilized I disliked {item name} based on
766   my review score of {score}. Neutral scores result in the entry I felt neutral
767
      about {item name} based on my review score of {score}. In SimUSER(sim),
      agents can also interact with the recommender systems (training set) for up to 20 pages or exit
768
      the system at any time. The corresponding interactions are used to enhance the memory module.
769
      In all the experiments, items rated ≥ 4 are considered as liked by the user, while items ≤ 2 are
770   considered as disliked. These interactions are stored both as plain text in the episodic memory
771   and as relationships in the knowledge graph memory. These simulated interactions with the RS
772   are stored in the episodic memory with the following format: The recommender system
773   recommended the following {item type} to me on page {page number}:
774   {name all items}, among them, I selected {watched items} and rate
775   them {ratings} respectively. I dislike the rest {item type} items:
776   {dislike items}.
777   In some sets of experiments, we report performance without persona matching SimUSER(w/o per-
778   sona), and with persona matching SimUSER(persona). In the absence of persona matching, person-
779   ality traits, age, occupation and taste summary are omitted from the prompts. Matrix factorization
780   (MF) is utilized as the recommender model unless specified otherwise. In our simulator, agents are
781   presented with four items n = 4 per page and allowed to interact by viewing and rating items based
782   on their preferences. When the output of the LLM deviated from the desired format, resulting in er-
783   rors, the LLM was re-prompted with the following instruction: You have one more chance

      to provide the correct answer.

       H    D ISCUSSION
2028
2029   Our study verifies the possibility of synthetic users in recommendation. SimUSER has demon-
2030
       strated its effectiveness to generate faithful users across a wide range of recommendation domains,
       including movies, books, and games. However, we also acknowledge that our method has certain
2031
       limitations.
2032
2033   Observed behaviors are well-aligned with existing theories and common behaviors in the recommen-
2034   dation domain. Phenomena at micro-level (rating, watching) are manifestations of agent endogenous
2035   behaviors. But why agents possess these behaviors are unexplored due to the black-box nature of the
2036
       large language models we adopted. A potential reason could be that LLMs are trained on a massive
       corpus that includes texts from various domains.
2037
2038   This paper introduces a strategy to capture external factors in recommendation via a knowledge
2039   graph memory. The key is for the system to retrieve similar items based on the structure of the
2040   graph and their semantic similarities. But in future work, we aim to explore the full potential for
2041   knowledge-graph enriched prompts, such as by dynamically breaking down a complex problem into
2042
       smaller subproblems Besta et al. (2024) and retrieving relevant information from the graph using
       LLM-generated queries.
2043
2044   The episodic module relies on a self-ask strategy to retrieve documents. Although this technique
2045   introduces additional overhead, we found that it also enhances the diversity and quality of retrieved
2046   documents by extrapolating documents relevant to the query, which is of critical importance in
2047   recommendation systems, where granular user preferences depend on accessing a comprehensive
2048
       range of relevant information.
2049   LLMs may replicate biases prevalent in social spaces, such as some groups of individuals being
2050   underrepresented. This is problematic if it causes designers to then underlook these peoples’ needs
2051   when designing a recommender system. In our experiments, we mitigated this risk by ensuring a
       broad range of personas via diverse potential occupations, age, and personalities. We also measured

       L    E THICS AND R EPRODUCIBILITY S TATEMENT
2123
2124
       This paper proposes an LLM-empowered agent framework designed to simulate user interactions
2125
       with recommender systems in a realistic and cost-effective manner. While our approach offers sig-
2126   nificant benefits in terms of scalability and efficiency, it also raises ethical considerations. The use of
2127   such agents could lead to unintended consequences, such as bias amplification, where the synthetic
2128   agents might inadvertently reinforce existing stereotypes or present skewed recommendations due
2129   to biases in the training data. Additionally, there is a risk of manipulation of user preferences, as
2130   the synthetic agents could be used to subtly influence user behavior by consistently promoting cer-
2131   tain types of content without explicit user consent. Furthermore, simulating interactions at a broad
2132   scale could result in the identification and exploitation of behavioral patterns that might encourage
2133   specific user behaviors, potentially leading to negative societal impacts. Finally, there is a concern
2134
       that developers or designers might use synthetic users and displace the role of humans and system
       stakeholders in the design process.
2135
2136   Regarding privacy, SimUSER leverages LLMs’ reasoning ability and factual knowledge without
2137   finetuning LLMs, ensuring that LLMs do not retain or remember user-specific data. In addition,
2138   employing matched personas instead of user-specific information available such as the occupation
2139   or gender prevents the leakage of user privacy information through external APIs.
2140   Furthermore, since our experiments and analyses are conducted in English, we do not claim that our
2141   findings are universally applicable across all languages. However, the SimUSER framework may be
2142   adaptable to other languages with suitable modifications.

       We acknowledge these risks and advocate for responsible deployment, including transparency, user
2144   awareness, and ongoing monitoring to mitigate potential harm. We also suggest that synthetic uses
2145   should not be a substitute for real human input in studies and design processes. Rather, these agents
2146   should be leveraged during the initial design phases to explore concepts, especially in situations
2147   where recruiting human participants is impractical or where testing certain theories with real peo-
2148   ple could be challenging or pose risks. By adhering to these principles, we can ensure that the
2149   deployment of synthetic users in the wild is ethical and socially responsible.
