009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in both representation learning and function learning have
012            demonstrated substantial promise across diverse domains of artificial intelligence.
013            However, the effective integration of these paradigms poses a significant chal-
014            lenge, particularly in cases where users must manually decide whether to apply
015            a representation learning or function learning model based on dataset characteris-
016            tics. To address this issue, we introduce MLP-KAN, a unified method designed to
017            eliminate the need for manual model selection. By integrating Multi-Layer Per-
018            ceptrons (MLPs) for representation learning and Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks
019
               (KANs) for function learning within a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture,
               MLP-KAN dynamically adapts to the specific characteristics of the task at hand,
020
               ensuring optimal performance. Embedded within a transformer-based framework,
021
               our work achieves remarkable results on four widely-used datasets across diverse
022            domains. Extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates its superior versatility,
023            delivering competitive performance across both deep representation and function
024            learning tasks. These findings highlight the potential of MLP-KAN to simplify
025            the model selection process, offering a comprehensive, adaptable solution across
026            various domains.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In recent years, deep learning has evolved from early neural network concepts to sophisticated archi-
032   tectures, such as transformer networks (Vaswani, 2017), driven by advancements in computational
033   resources and the availability of large datasets, thereby achieving remarkable performance across di-
034   verse applications. Alongside these technological breakthroughs, representation learning (OpenAI,
035   2023a; Anthropic, 2024; OpenAI, 2023b; Touvron et al., 2023) and function learning (Narayan et al.,
036   1996; Zhang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2005) have gained prominence and been extensively explored in
037   various research and application tasks. At the same time, the focus of function learning research has
038   shifted from simple function fitting to deep learning (Cuomo et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2021), which ex-
039
      cels in tasks requiring precise function approximation and has seen new advancements, particularly
      in its applicability to univariate function tasks. The key difference between representation learning
040
      and function learning lies in their objectives: representation learning aims to extract features from
041
      data to understand its underlying structure (Bengio et al., 2013), while function learning focuses
042   on creating direct mappings between inputs and outputs, making it more suited for tasks requiring
043   precise functional relationships (Zupan et al., 1997).
044
045
      In this paper, we introduce MLP-KAN, a novel framework that unifies two distinct learning ap-
      proaches into a cohesive system, utilizing the Mixture of Experts (MoE) methodology Jiang et al.
046
      (2023).MLP-KAN integrates Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KAN) (Liu et al., 2024) and Multi-
047
      Layer Perceptrons (MLP) (Rumelhart et al., 1986) , each tailored for specific learning tasks.KANs
048   replace static weights with learnable spline functions, enabling fine-grained interpolation and scal-
049   able precision Ta (2024); Somvanshi et al. (2024). These properties make KANs ideal for tasks
050   like symbolic regression, solving partial differential equations (PDEs), and fitting scientific data Liu
051   et al. (2024). In contrast,In contrast, MLPs excel at feature learning by extracting high-level, ab-
052   stract representations from high-dimensional data (Tashakkori et al., 2024). By employing fixed
053   activation functions and dense weight matrices, MLPs are well-suited for capturing global patterns
      in applications such as image classification and language modeling.

073   for diverse datasets. By integrating MLPs and KANs within a mixture-of-experts framework, this

075   without requiring manual model selection. The main challenge in our method is effectively inte-
076   grating MLPs and KANs, ensuring the right model is selected for each task without compromising
077
      performance. Additionally, aligning the distinct training requirements of representation and function

079   To address the integration of MLPs and KANs within the MoE framework, we utilized a soft MoE

081   learning and KANs for function learning. By incorporating this MoE system within a transformer
082   framework, the model seamlessly adapts to the task, performing either representation or function
083
      learning while maintaining efficiency across diverse datasets.

104   Deep Representation Learning. Deep representation learning has gained significant attention due
105   to its ability to automatically discover hierarchical feature representations from raw data (Butepage
106   et al., 2017; Zhong et al., 2016; Long et al., 2018), outperforming traditional hand-crafted feature
107   extraction techniques. The introduction of deep learning methods, such as MLP based convolu-
      tional neural networks (Li et al., 2021) and recurrent neural networks, enabled breakthroughs in

      areas like image recognition (Zoph et al., 2018; He et al., 2016), object detection (Zhao et al., 2019;
109   Yu et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2020), and natural language processing (Chowdhary & Chowdhary, 2020;
110   Khurana et al., 2023) by capturing more abstract and high-level features. Recent advancements in
111   deep architectures, including transformer-based models (Gillioz et al., 2020), have further pushed
112   the boundaries of representation learning, proving highly effective across diverse domains. For ex-
113   ample, generative AI, such as large language models (LLMs) (Yao et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023),
114   has garnered significant attention for its ability to generate coherent, contextually relevant text and
115   learn deep representations from vast amounts of unstructured data. LLMs like GPT-4o (OpenAI,
116   2024) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) utilize MLP based transformer architectures, which excel
117
      at capturing long-range dependencies in sequential data, allowing them to perform tasks such as text
      generation, summarization, and translation with remarkable accuracy. Beyond natural language pro-
118
      cessing, LLMs have also influenced other fields, including code generation (Chung et al., 2024; Li
119
      et al., 2022), medical diagnosis (Kononenko, 2001; Amato et al., 2013), and drug discovery (Drews,
120   2000; Sliwoski et al., 2014), by leveraging their deep learning capabilities to model complex re-
121   lationships in data. These advancements highlight the growing importance of deep representation
122   learning in not only understanding and generating human-like text but also in solving a wide range
123   of interdisciplinary challenges (Newell et al., 2001). In these models, MLP play a crucial role as
124   fundamental building blocks, serving as dense layers that transform and learn high-dimensional
125   representations by mapping inputs to deeper abstract features (Donoho et al., 2000).
126
127   Deep Function Learning. Deep function learning focuses on capturing complex mathematical
128   relationships and patterns within data, particularly in scientific and engineering domains (Sarker,
129   2021; Shen, 2018; Karpatne et al., 2017). Techniques such as Physics-Informed Neural Networks
130   (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019) have emerged as powerful tools for solving partial differential equations
131
      (PDEs) (Evans, 2022) by embedding physical laws into neural network architectures, allowing for
      accurate modeling of phenomena governed by underlying physical principles (Raissi et al., 2019;
132
      Cuomo et al., 2022). Beyond traditional neural networks, deep function learning leverages over-
133
      parameterized models, which enable the precise interpolation of data, even in the presence of noise,
134   enhancing both generalization and optimization performance (Karniadakis et al., 2021; Advani et al.,
135   2020; Chen et al., 2022). Recent advancements have demonstrated the potential of these methods
136   for tasks such as surrogate modeling (Razavi et al., 2012), sensitivity analysis (Christopher Frey &
137   Patil, 2002; Lenhart et al., 2002), and discovery of new scientific relationships (Wren et al., 2004;
138   Klahr & Simon, 1999). KAN are highly effective for function learning due to their ability to cap-
139   ture complex non-linear relationships through learnable spline-based univariate functions, offering
140   superior approximation capabilities and scaling compared to traditional MLP (Yu et al., 2024; Liu
141   et al., 2024; Zhang, 2024; Vaca-Rubio et al., 2024).

191   As shown in Figure 2, our proposed MLP-KAN is composed of N E experts, which can be classi-
192
      fied into two types: representation experts and function experts. Representation experts, based on
      MLP architectures, focus on learning rich feature representations, while function experts, utilizing
193
      FasterKAN architectures, specialize in tasks requiring smooth and precise interpolation over con-
194
      tinuous data points. The experts are dynamically selected and routed using a gating mechanism to
195   improve computational efficiency and maintain high performance.
196

198   multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs). These experts excel in tasks requiring the learning of rich feature

437   Number of Experts. In this ablation study, we investigate the impact of the number of experts
438   in the MoE component of MLP-KAN on the performance of CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100. As ob-
439   served in Table 4, increasing the number of experts from 4 to 10 yields steady improvements in both
440   top-1 and top-5 accuracy across both datasets. Notably, the top-1 accuracy for CIFAR-10 increases
441   from 0.908 to 0.928, while CIFAR-100 improves from 0.742 to 0.755 when the number of experts
442   increases from 4 to 10. However, performance gains begin to diminish after using 8 experts. The
443
      difference between using 8 and 10 experts is marginal: The accuracy of the top-1 of CIFAR-10
      only increases by 0.8%, and CIFAR-100 sees a mere 0.5% improvement. While the model with 10
444
      experts delivers slightly better results, the computational cost associated with using more experts
445
      becomes significant. Increasing the number of experts beyond 8 leads to a higher demand for com-
446   putational resources, memory usage, and training time, making the trade-off between performance
447   and efficiency unfavorable.

      Building on the transformer architecture, the input initially passes through the attention layer, where
705   the number of attention heads is set to 8. Furthermore, our proposed MLP-KAN replaces the original
706   MLP layer and consists of 8 experts (4 MLP experts and 4 KAN experts), with 2 experts dynamically
707   selected for computation in each forward pass. Subsequently, an additive residual connection is
708   applied before the attention and MLP-KAN layers. We also use the normalization layer to ensure
709   a consistent numerical distribution across different feature dimensions. This improves both the
710   stability during training and the overall performance of the model. We utilized a structure with
711   12 identical layers. To enhance model generalization, we employ Stochastic Depth (Huang et al.,
712   2016), which randomly drops certain layers during training. The process is as follows:

725   work, the MLP and KAN experts represent two distinct implementations within the FFN ensemble,
726   together constituting the complete pool of experts. The gating mechanism, functioning as a linear
727   layer, calculates the probability of each input token being assigned to a particular expert. Based

851
852   The Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) is a linguistically annotated dataset designed to enable
853   detailed analysis of sentiment composition in natural language. Derived from movie reviews, this
854
      dataset includes 11,855 individual sentences, which were parsed into syntactic structures using the
      Stanford parser. The resulting parse trees consist of 215,154 unique phrases, all annotated by human
855
      judges to capture nuanced sentiment at various granularities.
856
857   A distinctive feature of the SST dataset is its ability to support research on compositional sentiment
858   analysis, as each sub-phrase in a sentence is independently labeled for sentiment. This allows for
859   a deeper understanding of how sentiment is constructed and expressed through the combination of
860
      linguistic elements.
861   In the context of binary sentiment classification tasks, a simplified version of the dataset, known
862   as SST-2, is often used. In SST-2, neutral sentences are excluded, and the remaining sentences are
863   categorized into either negative or positive classes. This binary classification setup has become a
      widely adopted benchmark for evaluating sentiment analysis models.
