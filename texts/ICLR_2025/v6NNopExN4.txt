009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Prompting has emerged as a dominant learning paradigm for adapting large lan-
012            guage models (LLMs). While discrete (textual) prompts prepend tokens to the
013            input for optimized outputs, soft (parameter) prompts are tuned in the embedding
014            space via backpropagation, requiring less engineering effort. However, unlike
015            semantically meaningful discrete prompts, soft prompts are tightly coupled to the
016            LLM they were tuned on, hindering their generalization to other LLMs. This
017            limitation is particularly problematic when efficiency and privacy are concerns,
018
               since (1) it requires tuning new prompts for each LLM which, due to the back-
               propagation, becomes increasingly computationally expensive as LLMs grow in
019
               size, and (2) when the LLM is centrally hosted, it requires sharing private data for
020
               soft prompt tuning with the LLM provider. To address these concerns, we propose
021            a framework for Privacy Of Soft-prompt Transfer (POST), a novel method that
022            enables private soft prompt tuning on a small language model and then transfers
023            the prompt to the large LLM. Using knowledge distillation, we first derive the
024            small language model directly from the LLM to facilitate prompt transferability.
025            Then, we tune the soft prompt locally, if required with privacy guarantees, e.g.,
026            through differential privacy. Finally, we use a small set of public data to transfer the
027            prompt from the small model to the large LLM without additional privacy leakage.
028            Our experimental results demonstrate that our method effectively transfers soft
029
               prompts while protecting client data privacy while also reducing the computational
               complexity compared to soft prompt tuning on the large model.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are strong general purpose language generators that can be adapted
035
      to solve various private downstream tasks (OpenAI, 2023; Gemini-Team et al., 2023). One prominent
036   paradigm for adapting LLMs to private tasks is prompting (Devlin et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2018).
037   While discrete prompts (Schick & Schütze, 2020; 2021a; Shin et al., 2020; Han et al., 2022) which
038   prepend textual tokens to the LLM’s input have been shown relatively successful for LLM adaptations,
039   they require large engineering efforts and lots of trials and errors. As an alternative, soft prompts (Shin
040   et al., 2020; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Zhong et al., 2021; Oymak et al., 2023) prepend
041   trainable embedding vectors to the input, which can be tuned automatically on the private downstream
042   data using standard gradient-based approaches. Such gradient-based approaches are generally known
043   to yield higher performance at lower computational costs (Liu et al., 2022).
044   Yet, soft prompt tuning has two major limitations. 1) As LLMs grow in size (Geng & Liu, 2023;
045   Chiang et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020), it requires significantly more computation to backpropagate
046   through the entire LLM. 2) Backpropagation requires the model and data to be on the same device.
047   In the current model of centrally hosted LLMs, this requires users to share their data with the LLM
048   provider, which may be of concern when this data is private or sensitive in nature. Alternatively,
049   the LLM provider could share their model with the client, mitigating user data privacy concerns.
050
      However, this would put the LLM provider’s intellectual property at risk and disrupt their business
      model, as users would no longer be required to pay per model query. Further, the computational
051
      resources required to backpropagate through the model would make this impractical for most clients.
052
053   A potential solution to both problems is to tune the soft prompt locally on a smaller model and then
      transfer and use it on the large LLM. This approach, commonly known as ”prompt transfer” (Su

076   carry semantic meaning (Rakotonirina et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023). However,
077   soft prompts are highly coupled to the LLM they were tuned on, making them difficult to transfer.
078   Existing approaches for transferring soft prompts between two LLMs have one of two issues: either
079   they require private data access for central large model (Su et al., 2022), which as we discuss is not

082
083   To address these challenges, we propose POST, a framework for Privacy Of Soft-prompt Transfer.
084   POST consists of three key steps. (1) The LLM provider performs knowledge distillation (Hinton
085   et al., 2015) to compress their LLM into a smaller language model. This smaller model is designed
086
      to meet three critical requirements: it must (i) be small enough to enable the user to perform local
      soft prompt tuning on their own hardware, (ii) closely match the semantics of the original LLM to
087
      facilitate an effective prompt transfer, and, from the perspective of the LLM provider, (iii) be limited
088
      in performance to ensure that users still have an incentive to use the original LLM rather than the
089   small prompted version. The LLM provider sends the distillated small model to the user. Then, (2) the
090   user then performs local prompt tuning using their private data on this smaller model, potentially
091   incorporating formal privacy guarantees through differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006). Once the
092   user has tuned the private prompt on their data, they provide this prompt to the LLM provider, who
093   then (3) transfers the prompt to achieve strong performance on the large LLM. To prevent any privacy
094   leakage from the user’s private data, we equip POST with a novel prompt transfer method that relies
095   purely on access to a small public dataset rather than the user’s private data for transfer.
096   Our thorough experimental evaluation on both masked language models and auto-regressive language
097   models demonstrates that our method can efficiently, effectively, and privately transfer soft prompts
098   with high utility. In summary, we make the following contributions:
099
100
      • We propose POST, a framework for privacy of soft prompt transfer. POST preserves confidentiality
101     of users’ private data and can also provide strong privacy guarantees through differential privacy.
102
103
104
      • We design a novel method to transfer private prompts between LLMs by purely relying on public
        data which we integrate into POST.
105
106
107   • We provide detailed experimental analysis using four public datasets to simulate our setup and two
        different types of LLMs to show the effectiveness and efficiency of our method.

      2    BACKGROUND
109
110
      Prompt Tuning. Prompt tuning (PT) aims at adapting a publicly pre-trained LLM to various
111
      natural language downstream tasks. There are two major types of prompts, 1) hard or discrete
112   prompts (Schick & Schütze, 2021a;b; Gao et al., 2021), which are discrete textual tokens prepended
113   to the input text of the LLM, and 2) soft prompts (Hambardzumyan et al., 2021; Qin & Eisner, 2021;
114   Zhong et al., 2021) which are tunable embedding vectors prepended to the LLM’s input. While
115   discrete prompts require thorough engineering to yield good performance on downstream tasks,
116   soft prompts can be tuned through standard gradient-based training approaches (Lester et al., 2021).
117   Formally, given an input sequence with n tokens X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn }, labeled by y, we fist prepend
118   l randomly initialized soft prompts P = {p1 , p2 , . . . , pl } before X, where pi ∈ Rd is an embedding
119   vector, and d is the input dimension of the LLM,. The training objective is to maximize the likelihood
120
      of decoding the correct output y as L = p(y|P, X). The key is that the model itself remains frozen
      and only P is tunable.
121
122
123   Knowledge Distillation. Knowledge Distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015; Buciluǎ et al., 2006)
124
      is a compression method for machine learning models. It works by transferring knowledge from a
      complex model, denoted as the teacher model, to a simpler smaller model known as the student model.
125
      KD has been shown effective to compress LLMs during the pre-training phase while maintaining
126
      their performance (Sanh et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2024; Sreenivas et al., 2024). Already pre-trained
127   LLMs can also be compressed successfully through KD (Gu et al., 2024). In prompt transfer, Zhong
128   et al. (2024) leverage knowledge distillation to alleviate knowledge-forgetting between tasks that use
129   transferred prompts. In contrast, our setup considers transferring prompts between models. While, in
130   general, most of the KD approaches aim at generating a student with a similar predictive performance
131   as the teacher, for our purpose, the student performance is not particularly relevant. The student just
132   needs to match the teacher’s predictive behavior to a certain degree in order to facilitate the transfer
133   of the prompt from student to teacher.
134
135   Soft Prompt Transfer. Since soft prompts are trained with backpropagation through the LLMs,
136   this process can be computationally expensive, especially as LLMs grow in size. This motivates the
137   emergence of attempts to transfer, i.e., to reuse, existing soft prompts. There are two broad scenarios
138
      for prompt transfer. The first one aims at reusing a soft prompt trained for one downstream task on
      another (similar) downstream task on the same LLM (cross-task transfer). This can be implemented,
139
      for example, by initializing the parameters of the second soft prompt with the trained existing soft
140
      prompt parameters and has been shown to reduce training time for the second prompt (Vu et al.,
141   2022; Su et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2024). The second and more challenging scenario for prompt
142   transfer is a cross-model transfer scenario. In this scenario, one tries to tune a prompt for a given task
143   on one LLM, and then use it for another LLM. The difficulty arises from the fact that soft prompts
144   (over)fit the LLM they were tuned for and usually do not exhibit a strong performance on other LLMs.
145   Su et al. (2022) address transferring the soft prompt between the LLMs by using the guidance of
146   the private data. However, this approach still exposes the data directly to the second LLM which
147   may not be possible when this LLM is hosted centrally by a service provider (e.g., OpenAI) and the
148   data is sensitive in nature, as the private data now needs to be shared with the external provider. Wu
149
      et al. (2023b) present a zero-shot prompt transfer method, where source prompts tuned on a given
      LLM are encoded into a relative space and used as a form of support vector when finding target
150
      prompts on the second, i.e., target model. Unfortunately, in their approach, the target model with
151
      the transferred prompt performs worse than the prompted source model, leaving no incentive to use
152   the target model rather than the source model with the prompt. In contrast, our method significantly
153   improves performance on the target models with the transferred prompts. Additionally, their transfer
154   requires the private data, which thereby, leaks entirely to the model owner. In contrast, our method
155   performs prompt transfer with public data, preserving confidentiality of the private data towards the
156   model provider. Transferring tasks between LLM has also been explored by Xiao et al. (2023) for
157   transfer learning. While they focus on fine-tuning and their approach is not applicable for soft-prompt
158   tuning, we operate in the same setup and under the same assumptions as they are.
159
160   Differential Privacy for Soft Prompts. Differential privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006) is a mathematical
161   framework that provides privacy guarantees for ML by implementing the intuition that a model
      M : I → S, trained on two neighboring datasets D, D′ that differ in only one data point, will yield


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      roughly the same output, i.e., Pr[M(D) ∈ S] ≤ eϵ · Pr[M(D′ ) ∈ S] + δ. The privacy parameter ε
163   specifies by how much the output is allowed to differ and δ is the probability of failure to meet that
164   guarantee. To adapt soft prompts with DP guarantees, Duan et al. (2024) proposed the PromptDPSGD
165   algorithm, which is based on the popular differentially private stochastic gradient descent algorithm
166   (DPSGD) (Abadi et al., 2016). To obtain a finite DP guarantee, each gradient must be clipped and
167   calibrated Gaussian noise added to the sum.
168
169   Private Prompting and Text-to-Text Privatization. There exist multiple DP frameworks for
170   private prompting (Duan et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). However, they mainly
171   operate in a different setup and only provide DP guarantees for the model output, yet leak the private
172   data to the model provider. In contrast, our work aims at protecting the private data against the
173
      model provider. In a similar vein to our work, DP-OPT (Hong et al., 2023) tries to avoid leakage
      to the model owner and tunes discrete prompts with DP guarantees on a local surrogate LLM and
174
      then transfers these prompts to the large LLM. Their focus on discrete prompts (in contrast to our
175
      work that relies on soft prompt) leads to certain words and phrases from the training dataset leaking
176   directly to the LLM provider, as shown in their Figure 3—which does not occur with our method.
177   Additionally, their results show that the local surrogate model needs to be of strong performance (i.e.,
178   large) to obtain a good transfer results, leading to very high compute requirements on the user side.
179   In contrary to our method relies on small surrogate models that can be used for prompt tuning with
180   low compute on the user’s end.

205   Our Solution. We propose a solution based on tuning the soft prompt on a small local model and
206   then transferring this prompt to the LLM by using public data. To obtain a suitable small model that
207
      facilitates prompt transfer, we propose that the LLM provider performs KD from their LLM. The

208
      At the same time, (ii) it should, to a certain degree, match the predictive behavior of the large one
209
      to facilitate the transfer of the prompt. However, it should (iii) not exhibit too high generalization
210   performance, as the user might otherwise just tune the prompt on that model and use it for their
211   downstream task without paying access to the large model to the LLM provider. After distillation,
212   the small model is sent to the user who tunes a soft prompt on it using their private data, potentially
213   with DP to formally bound privacy leakage. Finally, the tuned prompt is sent to the LLM provider
214   who performs a prompt transfer step for the private prompt relying on public data. Then, the client
215   can use the LLM using the transferred prompt. We provide an overview of this solution in Figure 1
      and detail its building blocks in the following section.

      4     O UR P RIVATE T RANSFER OF S OFT P ROMPTS F RAMEWORK
217
218
      Our Privacy Of Soft-prompt Transfer (POST) framework consists of three main building blocks,
219   (1) a knowledge distillation from the LLM to a small model, (2) private prompt tuning, and (3) a
220   privacy-preserving prompt transfer using public data. We detail those building blocks below.
221
222
      4.1   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
223
224   We denote the teacher LLM model as Φt and the small student model as Φs . The input sequence to
225   an LLM is denoted as x. We leverage KD in (Sanh et al., 2019) to derive Φs from Φt . Different from
226   previous work in LLM distillation (Sanh et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2023) that moderately compresses
227   the LLM and tunes the whole model to recover performance as much as possible, we perform a
228   more aggressive KD without emphasis on the student model’s performance. In detail, we rely on the
229   following loss from (Sanh et al., 2019) to distill Φs from Φt :
230                                 Ldistil = αce Lce + αlm Llm + αcos Lcos .                                (1)
231
232   The objective is a linear combination of distillation loss Lce , language modeling loss Llm and
233   embedding cosine loss Lcos . Where Lce is the Kullback–Leibler divergence loss between the logits
234
      of Φs and Φt , Llm is the standard language modelling pretrainign objective, i.e., the cross entropy
      loss for predicting the masked/next tokens, and Lcos is the cosine distance of the embedding of Φs
235
      and Φt with αce , αlm and αcos weighting the respective losses.
236
237   Building on our intuition that more similar models exhibit better prompt transfer, we assess different
238   ways of preserving this similarity during KD. We observe that fixing the language modeling head,
239   i.e., causing higher output similarity, leads to slightly better transfer performance. Thus we use this
240
      strategy inside our KD. In contrast, we did not observe a consistent improvement with fixing the
      embeddings. Our ablation studies are shown in Appendix E.1 and the final detailed distillation setup
241
      is presented in Appendix C.1.

      4.3   P RIVACY-P RESERVING P ROMPT T RANSFER THROUGH P UBLIC DATA
260
      The prompt ps , tuned on the small source model Φs , could, in principle, be directly applied to
261   the large target LLM Φt . However, as described above, soft prompts fit very strongly the model
262   that they were tuned on. Hence, they do not initially perform very well on other LLMs. A naive
263   solution is to fine-tune the target prompt pt on the private data Dpri . However, this would disclose
264   the private data to the LLM provider and is, hence, not acceptable. As an alternative, we propose a
265   privacy-preserving prompt transfer that leverages a small public data Dpub in an efficient transfer
266   step to derive a high-utility prompt pt from ps .
267   We start by initializing the target prompt pt with the same initialization of ps , then iteratively update
268   pt . For the iterative update, we use the loss function

      5.7    C OMPARING AGAINST S TATE - OF - THE -A RT P ROMPT T RANSFER A PPROACHES
512
513   We compare against two baselines, namely the Zero-Shot transfer by Wu et al. (2023b) and DP-OPT
514   by Hong et al. (2023). Zero-Shot transfer operates in the same setup as we do and also relies on soft
515   prompts. They perform prompt transfer by using the embeddings of some tokens from the vocabulary
516   as a form of support vector to transform the source prompts into a relative space, and then search
517   for the corresponding target prompt embeddings for the target model. To provide the optimal source
518   model for their approach, we use a compressed model that we obtained by keeping the embedding
519
      layer frozen during KD (see row 3 in Table 14). DP-OPT, in contrast to ours, is designed for discrete
      prompts. They first tune a discrete prompt locally and then directly use it on the large model. Since
520
      their method relies on the small model having good performance, we execute their method in 2 setups
521
      for a fair comparison. 1) We tune their source prompt using our compressed model as the small
522   model, and 2) we use GPT2 as the small model. The latter is expected to have significantly higher
523   performance and yield much better prompts. To avoid the massive hyperparameter tuning required for
524   the private tuning in DP-OPT, we resolve to the standard OPT without DP guarantees following their
525   implementation (Hong et al., 2023). The obtained results represented an upper bound of DP-OPT,
526   as introducing DP usually degrades performance. Our results in Table 4 highlight that our POST
527   significantly outperforms all baselines even in the DP regime. Additional results with GPT2-XL can
528   be found in Table 11 in the Appendix.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      We present POST, a framework for the private transfer of soft prompts that enables adapting LLMs
533
      of an LLM provider with private user data while protecting both the user’s privacy and the LLM
534   provider’s intellectual property. POST relies on distillation to enable an LLM provider to share a
535   small model with limited utility to a client for local prompt tuning on their private data, optionally
536   with DP guarantees. Using our new prompt transfer method that leverages a small set of public
537   data, the LLM provider can then transfer the prompt to their model. Our experiments highlight that
538   POST achieves significant improvements on the private tasks through the prompt transfer, improves
539   computational efficiency of prompt tuning and outperforms all private prompt transfer baselines.
      Thereby, our work paves the way for a wider and more trustworthy application of LLMs.

      A     L IMITATIONS
731
732
      Our work proposes a method to protect the privacy and confidentiality of private data during the
733   prompt tuning phase, however, we didn’t address the privacy leakage risk during the inference phase.
734   Also, compression of the LLMs through knowledge distillation techniques may be computationally
735   expensive for LLM providers. Additionally, in our method, the selection of a public dataset will
736   affect the transfer performance of soft prompts. While we observe, in general, that public datasets
737   that have a similar structure to the private data work best for transfer, there is no ideal strategy for
738   selecting the optimal public dataset
739
740   B     B ROADER I MPACTS
741
742   Regarding the broader impacts of our work, we propose a private transfer of soft prompts from a
743   small language model to a large LLM. The primary positive societal impact of our work is that our
744   method can protect local data privacy and also the intelligent property of the large model provider,
745   which encourages wider and more trustworthy applications of LLMs. Additionally, since our transfer
746   enables more compute efficient prompt tuning and enables to re-use existing prompts, it can have a
747   positive environmental impact.
