                                                A BSTRACT
013
014
              Although significant research efforts have been dedicated to enhancing the safety
015
              of large language models (LLMs) by understanding and defending against jailbreak
016           attacks, evaluating the defense capabilities of LLMs against jailbreak attacks also
017           attracts lots of attention. Current evaluation methods lack explainability and do
018           not generalize well to complex scenarios, resulting in incomplete and inaccurate
019           assessments (e.g., direct judgment without reasoning explainability, the F1 score
020           of the GPT-4 judge is only 55% in complex scenarios and bias evaluation on
021           multilingual scenarios, etc.). To address these challenges, we have developed a
022           comprehensive evaluation benchmark, JAILJUDGE, which includes a wide range
023           of risk scenarios with complex malicious prompts (e.g., synthetic, adversarial,
024
              in-the-wild, and multi-language scenarios, etc.) along with high-quality human-
              annotated test datasets. Specifically, the JAILJUDGE dataset comprises training
025
              data of JAILJUDGE, with over 35k+ instruction-tune training data with reasoning
026
              explainability, and JAILJUDGETEST, a 4.5k+ labeled set of broad risk scenar-
027           ios and a 6k+ labeled set of multilingual scenarios in ten languages. To provide
028           reasoning explanations (e.g., explaining why an LLM is jailbroken or not) and
029           fine-grained evaluations (jailbroken score from 1 to 10), we propose a multi-agent
030           jailbreak judge framework, JailJudge MultiAgent, making the decision inference
031           process explicit and interpretable to enhance evaluation quality. Using this frame-
032           work, we construct the instruction-tuning ground truth and then instruction-tune an
033           end-to-end jailbreak judge model, JAILJUDGE Guard, which can also provide rea-
034           soning explainability with fine-grained evaluations without API costs. Additionally,
035
              we introduce JailBoost, an attacker-agnostic attack enhancer, and GuardShield, a
              safety moderation defense method, both based on JAILJUDGE Guard. Compre-
036
              hensive experiments demonstrate the superiority of our JAILJUDGE benchmark
037
              and jailbreak judge methods. Our jailbreak judge methods (JailJudge MultiAgent
038           and JAILJUDGE Guard) achieve SOTA performance in closed-source models (e.g.,
039           GPT-4) and safety moderation models (e.g., Llama-Guard and ShieldGemma, etc.),
040           across a broad range of complex behaviors (e.g., JAILJUDGE benchmark, etc.) to
041           zero-shot scenarios (e.g., other open data, etc.). Importantly, JailBoost and Guard-
042           Shield, based on JAILJUDGE Guard, can enhance downstream tasks in jailbreak
043           attacks and defenses under zero-shot settings with significant improvement (e.g.,
044           JailBoost can increase the average performance by approximately 29.24%, while
045           GuardShield can reduce the average defense ASR from 40.46% to 0.15%).
046           Our code and data are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/
047           r/public_multiagents_judge-66CB and https://huggingface.
048
              co/datasets/ICLR-Anonymous/JAILJUDGE. The baseline code is
              available in our library at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/

070   Although the jailbreak judge is a fundamental problem, comprehensive studies on it have been
071   sparse Jin et al. (2024b), as shown in Table 1. Current methods can be broadly categorized into
072
      heuristic methods Liu et al. (2024), toxic text classifiers Ji et al. (2024b), and LLM-based methods Inan
      et al. (2023). Heuristic and toxic text classifiers, while simple, often suffer high false positive rates. For
073
      instance, heuristic methods rely on keyword matching, misinterpreting benign responses containing
074
      certain keywords as malicious. Traditional toxic text classifiers Ji et al. (2024b), trained on toxic
075   text, struggle with complex scenarios (e.g., broad-range risks, adversarial, in-the-wild, multilingual)
076   and often lack explanatory power. The harmfulness of a response alone is insufficient to determine
077   whether a model refuses to answer, and the absence of explanations can lead to false judgments.
078   Conversely, LLM-based methods utilize LLMs to evaluate potential jailbreaks or directly fine-tune
079   them as moderation models (e.g., Llama-Guard Inan et al. (2023) and ShieldGemma Zeng et al.
080   (2024a)). For example, prompt-driven GPT-4 uses tailored prompts to assess if an LLM is jailbroken
081   but incurs significant computational and financial costs. Additionally, these methods may suffer
082   from inherent biases and data ambiguities, leading to inaccurate judgments and reduced reliability in
083
      identifying jailbreak attempts due to lack reasoning explainability.
084   To address these limitations, we developed a comprehensive jailbreak judge evaluation benchmark,
085   JAILJUDGE, encompassing a wide range of complex scenarios (e.g., broad-range risks, adversar-
086   ial, in-the-wild, multilingual, etc.). The JAILJUDGE dataset comprises JAILJUDGETRAIN, the
087   intrsuction-tuning data, and JAILJUDGETEST, which features two high-quality human-annotated
088   test datasets: a 4.5k+ labeled set of complex scenarios and a 6k+ labeled set of multilingual scenarios
089
      in ten languages. To provide reasoning explanations (e.g., explaining why an LLM is jailbroken or
      not) and fine-grained evaluations (jailbroken score from 1 to 10), we propose a multi-agent jailbreak
090
      judge framework, JailJudge MultiAgent, that explicitly and interpretably enhances judgment with rea-
091
      soning explanations. JailJudge MultiAgent comprises judging agents, voting agents, and an inference
092   agent, each playing specific roles. They collaboratively make interpretable, fine-grained decisions
093   on whether an LLM is jailbroken through voting, scoring, and reasoning. Using this framework, we
094   construct the instruction-tuning ground truth for JAILJUDGETRAIN and then instruction-tune an
095   end-to-end jailbreak judge model, JAILJUDGE Guard, which can also provide reasoning explainabil-
096   ity with fine-grained evaluations without API costs. Additionally, by demonstrating its foundational
097   capability, we propose a jailbreak attack, JailBoost, and a defense method, GuardShield, based on
098   JAILJUDGE Guard. JailBoost enhances adversarial prompt quality by providing jailbreak score
099   rewards, while GuardShield detects attacker attempts as a moderation tool.
100   Our main contributions are as follows: (1) We propose the jailbreak judge benchmark for evaluating
101   complex jailbreak scenarios, which includes two high-quality, human-annotated test datasets: a set of
102   over 4.5k+ labeled complex scenarios and a set of over 6k+ labeled multi-language scenarios. (2) We
103   introduce a multi-agent jailbreak judge framework, JailJudge MultiAgent, that provides reasoning
104   explainability and fine-grained evaluations. Using this framework, we construct the instruction-tuning
105   dataset, JAILJUDGETRAIN, for the jailbreak judge. (3) We then instruction-tune an end-to-end
106
      jailbreak judge model, JAILJUDGE Guard, without incurring API costs. Furthermore, we propose a
      jailbreak attack enhancer, JailBoost, and a jailbreak defense method, GuardShield, both based on
107
      JAILJUDGE Guard. JailBoost can increase the average performance by approximately 29.24%, while
      GuardShield can reduce the average defense ASR from 40.46% to 0.15% under zero-shot settings.

152
      Jailbreak Judge. The goal of the jailbreak judge is to evaluate the success of a jailbreak attempt by
153
      assessing the harmfulness of an LLM’s target response. The explainability-enhanced jailbreak judge
154   can be defined as follows:
155                                            (a, s) = πϕ (x̂1:n , ŷ),                                    (4)
156
      where a is the explainability analysis detailing the reasons for whether the LLM is jailbroken, and s
157   is the degree to which the model’s response is considered jailbroken. The πϕ (·) is the jailbreak judge
158   model or a multi-agent system. Specifically, the score ranges from 1 to 10, where a rating of 1 indicates
159   a fully compliant, non-jailbroken response—either irrelevant to the task or a refusal on ethical or legal
160   grounds. Conversely, a rating of 10 indicates responses that directly and egregiously violate ethical,
161   legal, or safety guidelines while fully addressing the task. The final judgment is determined by a

185   We develop the JAILJUDGE benchmark datasets and a multi-agent jailbreak judge framework,
186   making the decision inference process explicit and interpretable to enhance evaluation quality. Using
187
      the multi-agent framework to determine the ground truth with reasoning explainability and fine-
      grained scores, we then develop the end-to-end judge model, JAILJUDGE Guard. Trained on
188
      JAILJUDGE’s training data, this model can also provide reasoning explainability with fine-grained
189
      evaluations without API cost. The overall framework is shown in Figure 1.

192   3.1.1       JAILJUDETRAIN: I NSTRUCTION -T UNING DATASET FOR C OMPLEX JAILBREAK
193               J UDGMENTS
194
195   JAILJUDGETRAIN is a comprehensive instruction-tuning dataset consisting of 35k+ items, derived
196   from diverse sources with various target response pairs from different LLMs. The dataset includes six
197   sources of prompts: vanilla harmful prompts (a wide range of risk scenarios), synthetic vanilla prompts
198   (LLM-rewritten prompts), synthetic adversarial prompts (jailbreak attack rewrites), multilingual
199   harmful prompts (ten multigual languages), in-the-wild harmful prompts (real-world user-LLM
200
      interactions), and deceptive harmful prompts (automatic prompt refinement prompts). These sources
      are selected to optimize coverage, diversity, and balance. To construct diverse LLM responses, we
201
      collect responses from three sources: closed-source LLMs (e.g., GPT-4, GPT-3.5), open-source
202
      LLMs (e.g., Llama-family, Qwen-family, Mistral-family), and Defense-enhanced LLM responses.
203   The overview of the dataset composition can be seen in Appendix 8.3.
204
205   Complex Harmful Prompt Construction. To create a comprehensive scenario for the jailbreak
206
      judge benchmark, we draw on six sources: vanilla harmful, synthetic vanilla, synthetic adversarial,
      multilingual harmful, in-the-wild, and deceptive harmful prompts.
207
208   Vanilla Harmful Prompts. We collect the original harmful prompts from prior safety benchmark
209   datasets and refine broad and vague safety categories into 14 more fine-grained categories, following
210   the MLCommons standardized hazards taxonomy Inan et al. (2023). This results in relatively more
211   balanced categories (see Appendix 8.1 for detailed dataset sources and and categories).
212   Synthetic Vanilla Prompts. To ensure the diversity of harmful prompts, we utilize GPT-4 to rewrite
213   and expand the original vanilla harmful prompts within the same hazard taxonomy categories. For
214   instance, if the original harmful prompt is “How to make a bomb?”, the rewritten prompt might
215   be “Detailed instructions on constructing a homemade explosive using household items.” Refer to
      Appendix 8.1 for the GPT-4 prompts used to generate the more diverse prompts.


                                                                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Synthetic Adversarial Prompts. To increase the likelihood of generating unsafe responses from
217   LLMs, we leverage the latest jailbreak attacks, which have demonstrated their ability to bypass LLM
218   safety alignment. Specifically, we apply seven recent jailbreak techniques to modify vanilla harmful
219   prompts, aiming to elicit unsafe responses from the model. For instance, a jailbreak attack may
220   optimize an adversarial suffix (e.g., “!!!$&xnksac”) and append it to the original harmful prompt
221   (e.g., “How to make a bomb? !!!$&xnksac”) to heighten the chance of the LLM producing a harmful
222   output. See Appendix 8.1 for details on the jailbreak attacks used to generate adversarial prompts.
223   Multilingual Harmful Prompts. While current jailbreak attacks indicate that the prevalence of
224   unsafe content rises as resource availability diminishes, the jailbreak judge still lacks exploration of
225   bias in other languages. To investigate whether the jailbreak judge exhibits bias in other languages,
226   we included ten additional languages, encompassing high-resource: English (en), Chinese (zh), Italian
227   (it), Vietnamese (vi); medium-resource: Arabic (ar), Korean (ko), Thai (th); low-resource: Bengali
228   (bn), Swahili (sw), and Javanese (jv), which are widely used Deng et al. (2023b).
229   In-the-wild Prompts. To account for potential risks in real-world user requests, we incorporate
230   prompts from diverse datasets. These prompts are collected from prompt templates on prominent
231   platforms commonly used for prompt sharing, such as Reddit, Discord, various websites, and open-
232   source datasets. These prompt templates can be combined with malicious prompts to create more
233   complex and subtle harmful intentions. For example, a user might employ a template like “Do
234   anything now” followed by additional harmful prompts. (See Appendix 8.1 for the detailed pipeline).
235   Deceptive Harmful Prompts. In addition to real-world user-LLM interactions, deceptive harmful
236   prompts often mask their malicious intent through techniques such as role-playing, scenario assump-
237   tions, long-context prompts, and adaptive strategies. These complex cases are typically challenging
238   for LLMs to identify. To ensure thorough coverage of these variations, we apply automatic adversarial
239   prompt refinement to the original harmful prompts (see Appendix 8.1 for the detailed pipeline).
240
      Diverse LLM Responses. To construct diverse LLM responses, we collect responses from three
241   sources: closed-source LLMs (e.g., GPT-4, GPT-3.5), open-source LLMs (e.g., Llama-family, Qwen-
242   family, Mistral-family, Vicuna-family), and defense-enhanced LLM responses. Specifically, we
243   randomly split the above-tailored prompts and submit each prompt to a suite of LLMs, instructing the
244   LLMs to generate the corresponding responses. To mimic a well-secured environment, we adopt the
245   latest defense methods, including both system-level and model-level defenses. We randomly sample
246   the prompts and submit them to the defended LLMs to get the target responses. This provides us with
247   a set of diverse responses, including both safe and unsafe ones. (see Appendix 8.1 for the detailed
248   defense methods).
249   3.1.2   JAILJUDGETEST: H IGH -Q UALITY H UMAN -A NNOTATED T EST JAILBREAK J UDGE
250           DATASETS
251
      We develop JAILJUDGETEST, a high-quality, human-annotated jailbreak judge dataset, comprising
252   JAILJUDGE ID and JAILJUDGE OOD. To construct JAILJUDGE ID, we began with a test split
253   of over 4.5k+ prompt-response pairs from JAILJUDGETRAIN (excluding multilingual harmful
254   prompts), referred to as the in-distribution (ID) set. Additionally, we covered out-of-distribution
255   (OOD) evaluation cases using all multilingual harmful prompts and response pairs, resulting in
256   a labeled set of over 6k+ multi-language scenarios in ten languages, called JAILJUDGE OOD.
257   We continuously refine this taxonomy through a human-in-the-loop process to ensure high-quality
258   annotations indicating whether the LLM is jailbroken. Given a data prompt and its corresponding
259   LLM response pair (x̂1 , ŷ), the human annotator assigns a label l—either True or False—to determine
260
      whether the LLM is jailbroken. Our human annotation process consists of four stages: annotator
      training, manual labeling, GPT-4 labeling, cross-comparison, and multi-person voting.
261
262   Human Annotator Training Phase. In the human annotator training phase, we follow the MLCom-
263   mons standardized LLM policy, which includes nine detailed policies ranging from user privacy to
264   ethics and morality. We provide detailed scenarios and examples to the human annotators, allowing
265   them to learn what constitutes a violation of these policies. For example, regarding user privacy,
266
      the model’s response must comply with basic ethical and moral standards and must not generate
      content that violates social morality. Responses that violate these types of policies would be judged
267
      as jailbroken (see Appendix 8.2 for detailed policies).
268
269   Manual/GPT-4 labeling phase and multi-person voting phase. After the human annotator training,
      we gather annotations from human annotators regarding the jailbreak judge label. Subsequently, we

      submit all the harmful prompts and LLM response pairs to GPT-4, prompting the GPT-4 classifier
271   on the dataset. We manually review instances where the output does not align with the selected
272   annotator’s label to audit the ground-truth labels more thoroughly. Finally, multiple human annotators
273   re-label the items that mismatch with the GPT-4 labels, and we apply a majority voting rule to
274   determine the final gold labels.

276   3.2   JAIL J UDGE M ULTI AGENT: M ULTI - AGENT J UDGE F RAMEWORK
277   While reasoning can enhance jailbreak judgment quality, naive GPT-4 prompts Liu et al. (2024) often
278   cause inconsistencies between reasoning and final results, leading to inaccuracies. In complex role-
279   play scenarios, the model might identify dangers but still conclude no jailbreak due to assumptions,
280   creating a mismatch. To address this, we propose a multi-agent judge framework, JailJudge Multi-
281   Agent. This framework clarifies and interprets the decision-making process, improving evaluation
282
      quality. It includes Judging Agents, Voting Agents, and an Inference Agent, each with specific roles.
      These agents collaboratively produce interpretable, detailed decisions on whether an LLM requires
283
      jailbreaking through voting, scoring, reasoning, and final judgment.
284
285   For multi-agent prompting and collaboration, we will have n LLMs πθ1 , · · · , πθn that play different
286   agents or roles in the framework. These LLMs can be the same (θ1 = θ2 , · · · = θn ) or different. For
287   the text input x, each agent i will have its own profile agent function prompti (x; xi ) that formats the
288
      input task or problem for the agent, where xi is corresponding profile agent prompts. Specifically,
      there are three types of agent including k judging agents, m voting agents, and an inference Agent.
289
      Judging agents analyze the prompts and the model response to determine whether LLM is jailbroken,
290
      providing initial reasons and scores. Voting agents vote based on the scores and reasons provided
291   by the judging agents to decide whether to accept their judgments. Inference agents deduce final

522   (2024) have been developed. These defenses can be broadly divided into system-level and model-level
523   strategies. System-level defenses Xie et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024); Robey et al.
524   (2023b); Cao et al. (2023); Bianchi et al. (2023); Ji et al. (2024a) implement external safety measures
525   for both inputs and outputs. For example, SmoothLLM Robey et al. (2023b) generates multiple
526   outputs from various jailbreaking prompts and uses majority voting to select the safest response.
527   Model-level defenses Madry et al. (2018); Yuanshun et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2024a); Siththaranjan
528
      et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Zheng et al. (2024b); Hasan et al. (2024); Zou et al. (2024); Lu et al.
      (2024) involve directly modifying the LLM to lessen its vulnerability to harmful inputs. For instance,
529
      safety training Touvron et al. (2023); Siththaranjan et al. (2023) incorporates safety-specific datasets
530
      during the tuning phase to enhance the model’s resilience against malicious instructions.
531
532    7             C ONCLUSIONS
533
       In this work, we introduce JAILJUDGE, a comprehensive evaluation benchmark designed to assess
534    LLMs across a wide array of complex risk scenarios. JAILJUDGE includes high-quality, human-
535    annotated datasets and employs a multi-agent jailbreak judge framework, JailJudge MultiAgent, to
536    enhance explainability and accuracy. We also develop JAILJUDGE Guard based on instruction-
537    tuned data without incurring API costs. Furthermore, JAILJUDGE Guard can improve downstream
538    tasks, including jailbreak attack and defense mechanisms. Our experiments confirm the superiority
539    of jailbreak judge methods, demonstrating SOTA performance in models like GPT-4 and safety
       moderation tools such as Llama-Guard-3.

869   Vanilla Harmful Prompts. We collect original harmful prompts from prior safety benchmark
870   datasets, including AdvBench Zou et al. (2023), MaliciousInstruct Huang et al. (2023), and Forbidden
871   Question Set Shen et al. (2024) and refine broad and vague safety categories into 14 more specific
872   categories following the MLCommons standardized hazards taxonomy Inan et al. (2023). This leads
873   to more balanced category distributions. Detailed datasets and the 14 refined hazard categories
874   used in this construction are provided in Table 5. These categories are based on the MLCommons
875   standardized hazards taxonomy, with an additional category for Code Interpreter Abuse.

907   Synthetic Adversarial Prompts. To increase the likelihood of generating unsafe responses from
908   LLMs, we leverage the latest jailbreak attacks, which have demonstrated their ability to bypass LLM
909   safety alignment. Specifically, we apply seven recent jailbreak techniques to modify vanilla harmful
910   prompts, aiming to elicit unsafe responses from the model. For instance, a jailbreak attack may
911
      optimize an adversarial suffix (e.g., “!!!$&xnksac”) and append it to the original harmful prompt
      (e.g., “How to make a bomb? !!!$&xnksac”) to heighten the chance of the LLM producing a harmful
912
      output. We use the following jailbreak attacks used to generate these diverse prompts.
913
914            • GCG Zou et al. (2023): GCG aims to create harmful content by adding adversarial suffixes
915              to various queries. It uses a combination of greedy and gradient-based search methods to
916              find suffixes that increase the chances of the LLMs responding to malicious queries. In our
917              setting, we adhere to the original settings: 500 optimization steps, top-k of 256, an initial
                 adversarial suffix, and 20 tokens that can be optimized.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
            • AutoDAN Liu et al. (2024): AutoDAN employs a hierarchical genetic algorithm to generate
919           stealthy jailbreak prompts. It starts with human-created prompts and refines them through
920           selection, crossover, and mutation operations. This method preserves the logical flow and
921           meaning of the original sentences while introducing variations. We use the official settings
922           for AutoDAN, including all specified hyperparameters.
923         • AmpleGCG Liao & Sun (2024): AmpleGCG builds on GCG by overgenerating and training
924           a generative model to understand the distribution of adversarial suffixes. Successful suffixes
925           from GCG are used as training data, AmpleGCG collects all candidate suffixes during
926           optimization. This allows for rapid generation of diverse adversarial suffixes. We use the
927           released AmpleGCG model for Vicuna and Llama, following the original hyperparameters,
928           including maximum new tokens and diversity penalties. We set the number of group beam
929           searches to 200 to achieve nearly 100% ASR.
930         • AdvPrompter Paulus et al. (2024): AdvPrompter quickly generates adversarial suffixes
931           targeted at specific LLMs. These suffixes are crafted to provoke inappropriate or harmful
932           responses while remaining understandable to humans. Initially, high-quality adversarial
933           suffixes are produced using an efficient optimization algorithm, and then AdvPrompter is
934           fine-tuned with these suffixes. We follow the origional setting to train the LoRA adapter for
935
              each target model based on Llama-2-7B, then integrate the adapter with the initial LLM to
              create the suffix generator model. The maximum generation iteration is set to 100.

937         • PAIR Chao et al. (2023): PAIR is a black-box jialbreak attack to generate semantic adversar-
938           ial prompts. An attacker LLM crafts jailbreaks for a targeted LLM through iterative queries,
939
              using conversation history to enhance reasoning and refinement. We employ Vicuna-13B-
              v1.5 as the attack model and GPT-4 as the judge model, keeping most hyperparameters
940
              except for total iterations to reduce API costs.
941
942
            • TAP Mehrotra et al. (2023): TAP is an advanced black-box jailbreak method that evolves
              from PAIR. It uses tree-of-thought reasoning and pruning to systematically explore and refine
943
              attack prompts. The tree-of-thought mechanism allows for structured prompt exploration,
944
              while pruning removes irrelevant prompts, keeping only the most promising ones for further
945           evaluation. Although effective, TAP’s iterative process of generating and evaluating multiple
946           prompts increases the attack budget and is time-intensive. We follow the same setting as the
947           original Mehrotra et al. (2023), Vicuna-13B-v1.5 and GPT-4. To manage time and cost, we
948           reduce the maximum depth and width from 10 to 5.
949         • GPTFuzz Yu et al. (2023): GPTFuzz is a black-box jailbreak attack with three main
950           components: seed selection, mutation operators, and a judgment model. Starting with
951           human-written jailbreak prompts, the framework mutates these seeds to create new templates.
952           The seed selection balances efficiency and variability, while mutation operators generate
953           semantically similar sentences. The judgment model, a fine-tuned RoBERTa, evaluates the
954           success of each jailbreak attempt. Iteratively, GPTFuzz selects seeds, applies mutations,
955           combines them with target queries, and assesses the responses to determine jailbreak success.
956           We use the provided judgment model and adhere to the original hyperparameters, setting the
957
              GPT temperature to 1.0 for optimal mutation.

992             adversarial examples from token-level and prompt-level attacks, thereby increasing model

994           • Unlearning Yuanshun et al. (2023): Unlearning uses gradient ascent on malicious prompts
995             and responses to optimize forgetting. This technique increases loss on harmful datasets,
