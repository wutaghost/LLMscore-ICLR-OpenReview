029                                               A BSTRACT
030
031            This paper introduces Test-time Correction (TTC) system, a novel online 3D detec-
032            tion system designated for online correction of test-time errors via human feedback,
033            to guarantee the safety of deployed autonomous driving systems. Unlike well stud-
034
               ied offline 3D detectors frozen at inference, TTC explores the capability of instant
               online error rectification. By leveraging user feedback with interactive prompts at a
035
               frame, e.g., a simple click or draw of boxes, TTC could immediately update the cor-
036
               responding detection results for future streaming inputs, even though the model is
037            deployed with fixed parameters. This enables autonomous driving systems to adapt
038            to new scenarios flexibly and decrease deployment risks reliably without additional
039            expensive training. To achieve such TTC system, we equip existing 3D detectors
040            with OA module, an online adapter with prompt-driven design for online correction.
041            At the core of OA module are visual prompts, images of missed object-of-interest
042            for guiding the corresponding detection and subsequent tracking. Those visual
043            prompts, belonging to missed objects through online inference, are maintained by
044            the visual prompt buffer for continuous error correction in subsequent frames. By
045
               doing so, TTC consistently detects online missed objects and immediately lowers
               down driving risks. It achieves reliable, versatile, and adaptive driving autonomy.
046
               Extensive experiments demonstrate significant gain on instant error rectification
047
               over pre-trained 3D detectors, even in challenging scenarios with limited labels,
048            zero-shot detection, and adverse conditions. We hope this work would inspire
049            the community to investigate online rectification systems for autonomous driving
050            post-deployment. Code would be publicly shared.

      To guarantee safety, we argue that 3D detectors deployed on autonomous driving systems can rectify

084   existing 3D detectors rely on offline pipelines, encompassing a full-suite procedure of data collection,

089
      In this work, we explore a new 3D detection system capable of Test-Time error Correction based
090   on human feedback online, namely TTC, akin to how human drivers respond, as shown in Figure 2
091   (right). It is designed to enable existing 3D detectors with immediate error correction ability through
092   online human warnings. Inspired by the principles of In-context Learning Customization (Wei et al.,
093   2022; Peng et al., 2023) in large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023; Team, 2023), we achieve
094   the TTC system by leveraging images of missed objects collected from human feedback as context
095   prompts. Without extra training, these prompts assist deployed 3D detectors in identifying and
096   localizing previously unrecognized objects in later streaming input frames.
097   The proposed TTC includes two components: Online Adapter (OA) that enables 3D detectors with
098   visual promotable ability, and a visual prompt buffer that records missing objects. The core design is
099   “visual prompts”, the visual object representation derived from human feedback. Existing promptable
100   3D detection methods typically utilize text, boxes, or clicks as prompts. However, text prompts can
101   be ambiguous and may not describe the target objects effectively. Meanwhile, box and point prompts
102   struggle to handle streaming data. These limitations indicate that such prompts are inadequate for
103   real-time autonomous driving tasks. Visual prompts cover arbitrary imagery views of target objects,
104
      i.e., views in different zones, styles, and timestamps (Figure 3), indicating the identity of target
      objects. With visual prompts, OA module generates corresponding queries, locates corresponding
105
      objects within streaming inputs, and facilitates 3D detectors to output 3D boxes.
106
107   To enable consecutive error rectification for video streaming, we design a dynamic visual prompt
      buffer to maintain visual prompts of all past unrecognized objects. In each iteration, we use all

139   Interactive Vision Models. Interactive vision models are designed for tasks based on user inputs.
140   As one of the fundamental tasks in computer vision, they are extensively researched with numerous
141   breakthroughs (Li et al., 2004; Chen et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023c; Xu et al., 2016; Grady, 2006).
142   Particularly, the advent of the Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) has sparked a
143   surge of progress, with applications spanning reconstruction (Shen et al., 2023), detection (Ren et al.,
144
      2024; Yang et al., 2023b), segmentation (Zou et al., 2023), image editing (Gao et al., 2023), and more.
      Compared to existing models, which typically rely on prompts such as clicks, boxes, or scribbles,
145
      in this work, we study visual prompts, a new prompt referring to the actual images of objects with
146
      arbitrary poses and styles. Visual prompts enable continuous detection and tracking of target objects,
147   facilitating the immediate correction of failed detections at test time. This represents a departure from
148   traditional prompt types, offering more natural and dynamic interactions with the visual content.
149
150   In-context Learning in LLMs. In-context learning (ICL) (Zhang et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022;
151
      Chen et al., 2019), popularized by GPT (Brown et al., 2020), enables LLM for customized interactions
      with humans. This is then studied for chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2023) to ensure an informative
152
      dialogue system (OpenAI, 2023; Team, 2023; Taori et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
153
      2023b;a). Existing research demonstrates ICL as an effective mechanism for test-time response,
154   serving as an efficient zero-shot output learner. Inspired by the principles of ICL, we introduce a novel
155   concept called visual prompts, which are zero-shot learners for online 3D detection rectification.
156
157   Online 3D Detection System. In this paper, we introduce online 3D detection, a new task of instant
158
      error rectification for the online testing phase of 3D detectors. The key goal is to enable the continuous
      detection of objects missed by the offline-trained 3D detectors without additional training. This
159
      relates to several areas, such as 3D detection, tracking, continual learning, and open-world active

      3                T EST- TIME C ORRECTION WITH H UMAN F EEDBACK
189
190   In this section, we elaborate on our TTC, an online test-time error rectification system for 3D detection
191
      to detect and track previously missed objects during on-road inference with the guidance of human
      feedback. We start with an overview of TTC system in Section 3.1, then delve into OA module and
192
      the visual prompt buffer in Section 3.2, and Section 3.3, respectively.
193
194
195   3.1                OVERVIEW
196
      We convert online human feedback, i.e., clicks, boxes, or uploaded images, into a uniform represen-
197
      tation called “visual prompts”, which are image descriptions of target objects. Such image-based
198
      descriptions can cover arbitrary views of objects, including pictures taken from diverse zones,
199   weather, timestamps, or even from out-of-domain sources such as stylized Internet images. Upon
200   visual prompts, the TTC system, a recurrent framework, is designed to engage in sustained interaction
201   with human users, continuously learning to detect and track new objects. As Figure 4 shows, it
202   comprises two key components: 1) TTC-3D Detector, any 3D detector equipped with OA module for
203   in-context 3D detection and tracking via visual prompts, and 2) an extendable visual prompt buffer
204   storing visual prompts of all previously missed objects, enabling continuous online error rectification.
205   During online inference after being deployed on cars, whenever an error occurs, i.e., miss an object,
206   users can add the unrecognized object to visual prompt buffer by clicking on it in the image. The
207   model then detects the corresponding 2D and 3D boxes based on the user-provided clicking prompt
208   and updates the visual prompt buffer with the associated image patch. In subsequent frames, TTC-3D
209   Detector leverages the stored visual prompts to detect and track previously missed 3D objects. This
210   enables instant error correction and continuous improvement of 3D detection during online operation.
211
212   3.2                O NLINE A DAPTER (OA)
213
214   OA module is conceived as a bridge between prompts and offline-trained 3D detectors. It receives
215   human prompts and transforms them into queries that can be seamlessly used in traditional detectors.
      It is flexible to handle four prompts in different forms: object query prompts for traditional offline 3D


                                                                                                     4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      detection, box and point prompts for collecting test-time feedback, and visual prompts for consistently
217   correcting errors with streaming video. Specifically, these prompts are processed as follows:
218
219          • For object prompts Po , the OA module generates a set of learnable embeddings as queries,
220            akin to traditional 3D detectors, which are updated during training as demonstrated in
221            previous works (Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a).
222          • For box Pb and point Pp prompts, the OA module encodes them with their location and
223            shape, representing them as Fourier features (Tancik et al., 2020).
224
             • For visual prompts Pv , the OA module first extracts their visual features Zv by an encoder
225            (He et al., 2016), then localizes their corresponding objects within the input images Xv , and
226            finally adds their features with the Fourier positional encoding as subsequent inputs:
227
228
                                       Pv = FourierPE(Align(Zv , Z)) + Zv ,                                 (1)
229            where Z means image features of image input, FourierPE(·) is the Fourier positional en-
230            coding (Tancik et al., 2020) and the Align(·) operation means “Visual Prompt Alignment”,
231            the process of inferring the 2D position in the current frame of the visual prompt.
232
233   Visual Prompt Alignment. We perform the Align operation to localize target objects in the input
234   images, by visual prompts. To handle flexible visual prompts, which can be image descriptions of
235
      objects in any view, scene, style, or timestamp, we employ contrastive mechanisms (Wu et al., 2018;
      He et al., 2020) as the key design to retrieve target objects at different styles. This allows the module
236
      to detect the target objects effectively, even when they exhibit diverse visual styles and appearances.
237
238   Specifically, we use two multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) to first align the channels of image features
239   Z and visual prompt features Zv . We then compute the dot product between the aligned feature maps
240   to obtain a similarity map. To further retrieve the coordinates of target objects from the similarity
241
      map, we multiply it with the original image features Z, and finally apply another MLP with two
      output channels to regress the spatial positions Xv of target objects.
242
243   Instance Ambiguity & Loss. Sometimes, visual prompts might exhibit instance ambiguity, where
244   multiple objects in the image match the visual descriptions of prompts. For example, suppose the
245   visual prompt describes a traffic cone and several similar-looking traffic cones present in the input
246   images. It can be challenging to uniquely identify the specific object-of-interest (See Fig. 9).
247   For such cases, we design to retrieve all objects with similar identities to the visual prompt. Specif-
248                                                                                                   (i)
      ically, we modify the align operation to predict multiple spatial coordinates Xv = {Xv }, i ∈
249   {1, 2, ..., N } for each visual prompt, and add the Fourier features of those N coordinates to the visual
250   prompt features to indicate visual prompts in different positions. N is set to 4 in our implementation.
251
252
      For similarity supervision, we generate binary segmentation labels based on the ground-truth 2D
      boxes. Focal loss (Lin et al., 2017) and Dice loss (Milletari et al., 2016) are used for optimization. To
253
      supervise the visual prompt localization Xv , we use the Smooth-l1 loss with the target as the center
254
      coordinates of ground-truth 2D bounding boxes. For dealing with instance ambiguity, we refer to
255   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) and only backpropagate the sample with the minimum localization loss
256   during each training iteration. For more details, please refer to the appendix.
257
258   Model Design. The overall mechanism of TTC-3D Detector is depicted in Figure 4 (right). Based on
259
      any traditional offline-trained 3D detector, BEV detector, or monocular detector, we integrate OA
      module and train it to be promptable. Specifically, OA module takes features extracted by the image
260
      encoder of the corresponding 3D detector, along with various forms of prompts as inputs. It encodes
261
      these prompts and generates a series of queries, represented as P = {Po ; < Pb , Pp , Pv >}, where
262   < ... > denotes an arbitrary combination of different prompts. These queries are then fed into the
263   transformer decoder of the 3D detector to output 3D boxes following human feedback.
264
265   3.3   V ISUAL P ROMPT B UFFER
266
267   Visual prompt buffer is a queue that stores user-provided visual prompts of missed objects during
268   online inference. The flexibility of the TTC system lies in allowing users to freely select and define
269   visual prompts, which can be either image contents from the current scene or customized objects
      from the Internet. This versatility makes the TTC system applicable to a wide spectrum of scenarios.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Dequeue. To prevent the buffer from growing indefinitely, we design a “dequeue” mechanism. We
271   filter out visual prompts with low confidence, as they likely no longer appear in the scene. We also
272   use the intersection-over-union (IoU) between predictions to identify and remove redundant visual
273   prompts. This dynamic update and maintenance is the key of TTC system, ensuring a balance between
274   latency and accuracy by adaptively incorporating and pruning feedback during online inference.

      We now proceed to evaluate our TTC system. Compared to traditional 3D detection approaches, the
      evaluation of TTC system should consider more on its capabilities in instant online error correction
280
      over offline-trained 3D detectors. Thus, we assess the system from two key aspects:
281
282          • How does the TTC system perform with various adapted offline 3D detectors for instant
283            online error rectification?
284
             • How does the TTC system enhance offline-trained 3D detectors when faced with out-of-
285
               training-distribution scenarios?

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
289
      Dataset. Experiments are done on nuScenes dataset (Caesar et al., 2020) with 1,000 autonomous
290
      driving sequences, one of the most popular datasets for autonomous driving research.
291
292   Tasks. Due to the new setting, experiments are conducted in two aspects to answer the questions
293   above. The first experiment aims to test the TTC system in boosting the performance of offline-
294   trained 3D detectors without re-training via online error rectification. We conduct this verification
295
      by applying TTC system to various established offline 3D detectors (Zhang et al., 2022a; Li et al.,
      2022; Yang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c)
296
      We mimic user intervention during inference. Specifically, we simulate the user’s online missing
297
      feedback by comparing the distance between ground truth and detected 3D boxes. Ground-truths
298   without any detection within 2m are considered missed and added into the prompt buffer.
299
300   Then, we validate the TTC system to correct detection errors in out-of-training-distribution scenarios.
301
      To conduct a thorough quantitative analysis of this aspect, we established four tasks under different
      settings: (a.) discarding 80% 3D bounding box labels of distant objects farther than 30m during
302
      training, and test the error corrections for detecting distant objects; (b.) discarding 80% 3D bounding
303
      box labels of instances labeled as vehicle, including “car”, “truck”, “C.V.”, “bus”, and “trailer”, and
304   test the error correction in vehicle objects; (c.) discarding all 3D bounding box annotations of class
305   “truck” and “bus”, and test the zero-shot ability of TTC on those discarded classes; (d.) discarding
306   all training data of the scenario of “Nighttime” and “Rainy”, and test the improvements of TTC
307   on scenarios with domain gap. For these tasks, we base TTC system on the monocular algorithm,
308   MonoDETR (Zhang et al., 2022a), and test it under each set separately, as the monocular setting
309   demonstrates the most general applicability. Based on TTC-MonoDETR, we also present qualitative
310   results to show the potential of our system to address corner cases in challenging real-world scenarios
311   through human feedback during test time. Through these experiments, we demonstrate TTC as an
312
      effective system to adapt offline 3D detectors to challenging scenarios, without training.
313   Entity Detection Score. As we focus on enabling existing 3D detectors to rectify missing detections
314   instantly in an online manner, we set our priority on the model’s ability to zero-shot localize and
315   detect new objects during the testing phase, rather than its classification capability. Therefore, we
316   remove all class annotations of 3D objects during training and simply treat all objects as entities
317   (Kirillov et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2022). For evaluation, we use the Entity Detection Score (EDS), the
318   class-agnostic version of the nuScenes Detection Score (NDS), to assess the performance, which
319
      emphasizes the localization quality of target objects. More details can be found in Appendix B.3.

323   224 × 224 before being sent to OA module. For training, we use AdamW (Kingma & Ba, 2015;

471   5    C ONCLUSION
472
473   In this paper, we introduce the TTC system. It equips existing 3D detectors with the ability of
474   test-time error correction. The core component is the OA module, which enables offline-trained
475   3D detectors with the ability to leverage visual prompts for continuously detecting and tracking
476   previously missing 3D objects. By updating the visual prompt buffer, TTC system enables continuous
477   error rectification online without any training. To conclude, TTC provides a more reliable online 3D
478   perception system, allowing seamless transfer of offline-trained 3D detectors to new autonomous
479   driving deployments. We hope this work will inspire the development of online correction systems.
480   Limitations and Future Work. The current system is limited in scale, including both model and
481   data scale. For future work, we would focus on improving the generalization capabilities of visual
482   promptable online 3D detectors at scale. We plan to combine large-scale 2D detection datasets with
483   limited 3D detection datasets, together with the design of large-scale vision models, to develop a
484   general, versatile, and robust 3D detection system leveraging visual prompts. This proposed research
485   direction aims to advance the state-of-the-art in prompt-based 3D object detection, enabling highly
      generalizable and reliable systems for automated real-world applications.

      For better understanding of our work, we supplement intuitive questions that one might raise. Note
740
      that the following list does not indicate whether the manuscript was submitted to a previous venue.
741
742
      Q1: What is the relationship between the online 3D detection and the offline data-loop progress?
743
744   We emphasize, in this paper, we do not propose the online system to replace the traditional offline
745   data loop. As illustrated in Figure 2 of the main paper, these two systems address different aspects of
746   autonomous driving. The offline system remains crucial for enhancing the capabilities of the base
747   perception model through development; while our online TTC system further enables the deployed
748
      frozen model in vehicles to promptly rectify dangerous driving behaviors caused by unrecognized
      objects on the road. With improved offline-trained detectors, the TTC can effectively correct more
749
      online errors during test-time inference without re-training, as detailed in Table 1.
750
751
752
      Q2: What are the main technical novelty and advantages of the proposed TTC system over previous
      instruction-based 3D detectors?
753
754   The advantages lie in the design of visual prompts, the visual descriptions of target objects with
755   diverse sources, styles, poses, and timestamps. While existing instruction-based 3D detection methods
      typically utilize text, boxes, or clicks as prompts.


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Compared to text prompts, visual prompts provide a more natural and accurate description of the
757   target object. In contrast, verbal descriptions can be ambiguous to convey instance-level features,
758   leading to an inaccurate understanding of the missing objects. Second, text promptable models
759   are often combined with LLMs with high latency and are thus unavailable for autonomous driving
760   deployment, as discussed in Appendix C.6.
761
      Box and point prompts are less convenient than visual prompts when dealing with stream data. If
762
      missing occurs, these single-frame prompts require users to provide feedback at every frame, which is
763   unfeasible in real-world applications. Compared to box and point prompts, visual prompts are robust
764   across different scenes and timestamps, one single-frame visual prompt is enough for detecting and
765   tracking in later frames. Furthermore, visual prompts enable 3D detection with pre-defined visual
766   descriptions of target objects, regardless of the sources, styles, poses, etc, as discussed in Section 4.3.
767
      The introduction of novel visual prompts enables real-time, accurate, and continuous error correction
768
      of streaming inputs with ”one-click” feedback.
769
770
771   Q3: What is the relationship between the TTC system and existing 3D perception tasks?
772   The TTC system relates to several 3D perception tasks, including 3D object detection, zero/few-shot
773   detection, domain adaptation, single object tracking, and continual learning.
774
      Compared to standard 3D object detection, the primary focus of the TTC system is on enabling
775
      instant online error correction rather than optimizing the offline detection performance of the base
776
      3D detector. In contrast to traditional few-shot, one-shot, or domain adaptation approaches, the
777   TTC system does not require 3D annotations for new objects or any model retraining, yet can still
778   provide reasonable 3D bounding box estimates for out-of-distribution objects. Relative to single
779   object tracking, the TTC system does not rely on bounding boxes of the target objects in the first
780   frame. Instead, it can perform tracking using the visual descriptions of the target objects from any
781   scene or timestamp, leveraging the diverse set of visual prompts.
782
      In summary, the TTC system represents a more flexible and comprehensive 3D object detection
783   framework, combining the strengths of zero-shot detection, handling out-of-distribution objects, and
784   utilizing diverse visual prompts beyond the current scene context.
785
786
      Q4: Why choose 3D detection as the experimental scenarios of TTC? Could the proposed framework
787
      be extended to 2D detection or other vision tasks?
788
789   TTC represents a general idea to equip deployed systems with the capability of online error rectifica-
790   tion, making them more versatile, adaptive, and reliable. This idea can be readily extended to other
791   vision tasks, such as 2D detection, for rapid adaptation of pre-trained models to novel scenarios.
792   The choice of 3D detection for autonomous driving as the experimental scenario is motivated by
793   the paramount importance of safety for deployed self-driving systems. Without an online correction
794   method, mistakes made by the offline model pose significant safety risks for on-road autonomy.
795
      Therefore, we select the autonomous driving domain as the testbed for the TTC framework, given the
796   critical need for a robust, adaptive online error correction system to ensure the reliability of these
797   safety-critical applications. We mark the extension of TTC to other vision tasks as future works.
798
799
800
      Q5: What are potential applications and future directions of TTC?
801   We believe that, visual prompts, as the core design element of the TTC system, represent a more
802   natural and intuitive query modality for the image domain. This approach has significant research
803   potential and application prospects in the field of 3D perception and beyond.
804   For example, visual prompts enable rapid customization of the tracking targets, beyond the pre-defined
805   object classes. Second, the visual prompt-based framework facilitates online continual learning for
806   3D perception systems, adapting to evolving environments. Then, visual prompts can be applied
807   in the V2X domain to enable swift error rectification across diverse operational scenarios. Visual
808   prompts can also be deployed to assist in the auto-labeling process of target objects. Furthermore, by
809   combining visual prompts with natural language prompts, we can obtain more precise descriptions
      and behavioral control for online perception systems.


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      The diverse applications outlined above demonstrate the promise of visual prompts as a versatile
811   approach. As showcased in this work, the visual prompt-based framework opens up new possibilities
812   for online perception systems, not only in autonomous driving but also in a broader range of domains.

      C.4        S TATISTICS OF V ISUAL P ROMPT B UFFER DURING O NLINE I NFERENCE .
958
      We design the visual prompt buffer to store missed objects during inference with video stream and
959   introduce a “dequeue” mechanism to prevent the buffer from growing indefinitely. In this ablation
960   study, we analyze the dynamic buffer size, as well as the number of enqueued and dequeued in each
961   frame, to illustrate the behavior of visual prompt buffer during the online operation of TTC.
962
      As shown in Figure 10, the visual prompt buffer exhibits three distinct behaviors during online
963
      inference in each nuScenes video clip: increasing, steady, and decreasing. In initial frames, many
964
      traffic cones are queued into the buffer due to the poor performance of deployed offline 3D detectors
965   on cone objects. The buffer size thus grows quickly in initial frames to store visual prompts of missed
966   objects for online rectification (Frames #0 to #4). The buffer size then stabilizes as the online detector
967   consistently detects and tracks all objects of interest (Frames #4 to #20). Further, as the ego vehicle
968   drives out of the scene, many previously enqueued objects no longer exist and are thus removed from
969   the buffer automatically (Frames #20 to #40). The buffer finally becomes empty as the scene changes.
