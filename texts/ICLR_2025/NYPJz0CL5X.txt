009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Hyperdimensional Computing (HDC), as a novel neurally-inspired computing
012            methodology, uses lightweight and high-dimensional operations to realize ma-
013            jor brain functionalities. Recent HDC works mainly focus on two aspects: brain-
014            like learning and cognitive computation. However, it lacks differentiation between
015            these functions and their requirements for HDC algorithms. We address this gap
016            by proposing an adaptable hyperdimensional kernel-based encoding method. We
017            explore how encoding settings impact HDC performance for both tasks, high-
018
               lighting the distinction between learning patterns and retrieving information. We
               provide detailed guidance on kernel design, optimizing data points for accurate
019
               decoding or correlated learning. Experimental results with our proposed encoder
020
               significantly boost image classification accuracy from 65% to 95% by consider-
021            ing pixel correlations and increase decoding accuracy from 85% to 100% by max-
022            imizing pixel vector separation. Factorization tasks are shown to require highly
023            exclusive representation to enable accurate convergence.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The human brain remains the most sophisticated processing component that has ever existed, albeit
029   after more than decades of advancement in computer science. The ever-growing research in biolog-
030   ical vision, cognitive psychology, and neuroscience has given rise to many concepts that have led to
031   prolific advancement in artificial intelligent accomplishing cognitive tasks (Lindsay, 2020; Indiveri
032   & Horiuchi, 2011; Mitrokhin et al., 2020). Particularly, brain-inspired machine learning methods
033
      have shown promising leads in realizing crucial brain functionalities thanks to the advancement in
      theoretical neuroscience. Among these, a more recent and actively-studied direction is Hyperdimen-
034
      sional Computing (HDC), a computing framework that mimics the brain at abstract and function-
035
      ality level (Kanerva, 2009). HDC uses high-dimensional representations that are holographic, i.e.,
036   the information encoded is evenly distributed across all dimensions. More importantly, HDC en-
037   joys the advantages of structured and symbolic vector representations through a well-defined set
038   of algebraic operations in the high-dimensional space, i.e., Hyperspace. The vector representation
039   within the hyperspace is usually referred to as Hypervectors. Notable models in the HDC family are
040   Tensor Product Representations, Holographic Reduced Representations (Tay et al., 2019), Multiply-
041   Add-Permute (Kleyko et al., 2021), Binary Spatter Codes (Kleyko et al., 2016), and Sparse Binary
042   Distributed (Rachkovskiy et al., 2005).
043   Several recent efforts focus on mapping HDC to various learning and cognitive tasks. For exam-
044   ple, HDC is leveraged for several machine learning tasks, including classification (Najafabadi et al.,
045   2016), clustering (Imani et al., 2020), regression (Hernández-Cano et al., 2021), fault detection (Po-
046   duval et al., 2021a; 2022a), and face detection (Imani et al., 2022; Poduval et al., 2021b). Similarly,
047   HDC shows advances in reasoning and cognitive operations (Poduval et al., 2022b). With a suitable
048   encoder in each task that maps data into hyperspace, the learning and cognitive computations are
049   carried out using basic HDC mathematics almost linearly, thereby leading to optimal results.
050   Even though encoders are crucial for representing knowledge appropriately, researchers nowadays
051   can only empirically select the HDC encoding method for each task; because it is unclear how dif-
052   ferent HDC encoder designs should interact with input information and patterns. More specifically,
053   how should one select a suitable encoding for HDC? Does HDC encoding depend on the desired
      task? Can a single encoding support all tasks based on HDC?

071   We first observe that computation in hyperspace may require different representations depending
072
      on the nature of the task, e.g., pattern extraction for learning or accurate information retrieval for
      performing cognitive reasoning. For learning tasks, a typical example is an image classification or
073
      object detection task, in which the model identifies the object and predicts its category. The model
074
      focuses on extracting high-level features from images. Therefore, the encoded hypervectors are not
075   required to store unhelpful or redundant information for learning purposes. In contrast, cognitive
076   tasks focus more on reasoning, answering questions about relationships between objects, and making
077   traceable and justifiable decisions. These tasks often require preserving most information of original
078   data to ensure accurate information retrieval, in other words, decoding. In this work, we try to enable
079   HDC learning and cognitive computation using the same encoding flow, and the main contributions
080   of the paper are:
081
082   • We propose a universal hyperdimensional encoding method that can be easily adapted toward
083     high-quality learning or accurate information retrieval. On the contrary, in existing HDC encoder
084     designs, we observe a large number of empirically selected encoding algorithms that achieve good
085     results on various tasks. Yet, they are generally not compatible with each other. Our proposed HDC
086     encoder is more flexible for different applications and saves the design cost.
087
088   • We provide the first rigorous theoretic analysis of the fundamental requirement of two tasks of
089     very different natures: learning and cognition. We define a separation metric that represents how
090     encoded data points are separated or correlated in the hyperspace. Our analysis suggests that the
091
        learning task requires data to be encoded in a correlated way while decoding in cognitive tasks
        requires encoding different data points separately.
092
093
      • We carry out extensive explorations on our universal encoder, in which we adjust several knobs
094
        according to the derived separation metric. We verify our theoretic analysis by observing how the
095     quality of learning and decoding changes when the data is encoded with or without correlations.
096     When the vectors representing each pixel are made orthogonal by tuning down the scale w, the
097     decoding procedure produced near-accurate results. On the other hand, the learning procedure
098     produced accurate results only in a high w regime.
099
100
      In our experiments, we find that the image classification accuracy significantly increases from 65%
101
      to 95% (with separation changing from 0.3 to 1.2) when we take into consideration the correlation
102   between pixels. On the other hand, the decoding accuracy increases from 85% to 100% when we
103   maximize the separation of vectors representing the pixels (with corresponding separation changing
104   from 1.0 to 4.0). In practice, we find that the decoding task requires a higher separation of about 2
105   to 3 for accurate results because it is highly sensitive to noise while the learning task requires only
106   a low value of 0.8 to 1.2 for the best accuracy. For factorization of hypvervectors into hyperspace,
107   the hypervectors require a highly exclusive representation to recover the correct factors, and any
      correlations can induce errors in the corresponding factors.

      2   H YPERDIMENSIONAL C OMPUTING : A N OVERVIEW
119
120
      HDC uses large dimensional hypervectors to represent information within such a hyperspace us-
121   ing nearly orthogonal hypervectors (Kanerva, 1998). Information is combined through hypervec-
122   tors using well-defined vector space operations, e.g., Bundling (+) and Binding (∗). Bundling uses
123   element-wise addition to represent sets, and binding expresses conjunctive association with element-
124   wise multiplication. The hypervectors are holographic and (pseudo)random with i.i.d. components,
125   allowing a holistic representation so that information is spread across all of the components.
126
      In recent years, HDC has been employed in a range of applications, such as classification (Kanerva,
127   2009), activity recognition (Kim et al., 2018), biomedical signal processing (Moin et al., 2021),
128   multimodal sensor fusion (Räsänen & Saarinen, 2015), security (Thapa et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
129   2021) and distributed sensors (Kleyko et al., 2018). A key HDC advantage lies in the capability of
130   training in one or few shots, where object categories are learned from a few examples without many
131   iterations and has achieved SOTA compared to support vector machines (SVMs), gradient boosting,
132   and convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Rahimi et al., 2018; Mitrokhin et al., 2019), as well as
133   lower execution energy on embedded processors (Montagna et al., 2018).
134   In these successful HDC applications, HDC encoding is essential to the quality of computing. The
135   encoding determines (1) the distance metric for encoded data points and (2) the level of correlation or
136   exclusiveness preserved in hyperspace. In this work, we introduce the HDC encoder which can tune
137   the level of inclusiveness in the encoding to hyperspace, and study the decodability and learning
138   capabilities of the resulting model. In Fig. 10, we categorize HDC applications into learning and
139   cognition, then we shed light on two corresponding directions in HDC encoding.
140
141
      • Learning: aims to capture the general pattern of data. The appropriate encoding should abstract
        common information by keeping the similarity of neighboring data points in hyperspace, and we
142
        refer to this as the Correlative Encoding, since it should correlate hypervectors depending on the
143
        underlying correlations in feature space, thus classifying data that are not linearly separable and
144     avoid overfitting with a smooth boundary. The correlative encoder also theoretically increases the
145     memorization and learning capacity of hypervectors.
146
      • Cognition: aims to represent structural data using neural patterns, which accordingly enables
147
        brain-like analysis and information extraction, requiring an exclusive representation of data in
148     hyperspace. We call this the Exclusive Encoding, ensuring accurate knowledge extraction such
149     that memorized information can be used as prior information for various cognitive computation
150     tasks. The encoded information needs to be accurately decoded back to the original space to
151     answer cognitive questions.
152
153   As shown in Fig. 10, learning and cognitive computation have different requirements for HDC en-
154   coders. For learning, the encoding is inclusive and correlative, preserving the similarity of nearby
155
      data. In contrast, encoding is exclusive for cognitive computation, where data points are mapped to
      orthogonal space and distinct.

      In this section, we discuss our expectations on how learning and decoding efficiency vary as a

424
      large distances. So the feature values are averaged out and would be better for learning. However,
425
      making wi too large would make the learning a full average of the feature which would contain

      network, Sec. 6. The position, location and time values are continuously encoded, with the values

519

520
      various nearby values remain highly correlated), and then change the value to wi = 1 sequentially.

523   a specific index in the codebook for each factor, and the correct factorization is where all the factors

      are yellow. For the first experiment, we set all wi = 1, resulting in the S, T, X factors being highly

525
      distinguish between hypervectors representing different values. Next, we set wX = 1 so that the
526
      hypervectors representing different positions are uncorrelated, while the hypervectors representing

529   respectively. The corresponding factors are also correctly decoded, highlighting the importance of
530   controlling the correlation in HDC factorization.

      In these successful HDC applications, HDC encoding is essential to the quality of computing. With
681
      a suitable encoding, information from inputs is well maintained to satisfy the needs of tasks. The
682   encoding determines the following factors: (1) the distance metric for encoded data points and (2)
683   the level of correlation or exclusiveness preserved after mapping to hyperspace. Despite the general
684   success in HDC encoding, there are no guidelines on setting these factors in practice. In this work,
685   we observe that a suitable HDC encoding design is application-specific. In Fig. 10, we categorize
686   HDC applications into learning and cognition, then we shed light on two corresponding directions
687   in HDC encoding.
688
689
      • Learning: aims to capture the general pattern of data. It operates over encoded hypervectors,
        where the information of original data is preserved. Therefore, the usefulness of information main-
690
        tained in hypervectors determines the learning quality. For learning, the HDC encoding needs to
691
        be inclusive and correlative. In other words, the encoding should abstract common information
692     by keeping the similarity of neighboring data points in hyperspace, and we refer to this as the
693     Correlative Encoding. This encoder correlates hypervectors under a certain distance metric, and
694     similar data points are coarsely clustered in hyperspace. This clustering effect helps classify data
695     that are not linearly separable and avoid overfitting with a smoother boundary. The inclusive en-
696     coder also theoretically increases the memorization and learning capacity of hypervectors (see
697     Section 5). Generally, HDC encoding for learning does not require the exact decoding of infor-
698     mation. Instead, it only needs a coarse differentiation between different patterns. Therefore, the
699     learned models are often much more compact than the original training dataset.
700   • Cognition: aims to represent structural data using neural patterns, which accordingly enables
701     brain-like analysis and information extraction. The primary task of encoding in cognitive tasks
        is to represent data points exclusively instead of inclusively in hyperspace. We name this kind
