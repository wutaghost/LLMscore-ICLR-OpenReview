009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose semantic entropy probes (SEPs), a cheap and reliable method for uncer-
012            tainty quantification in Large Language Models (LLMs). Hallucinations, which are
013            plausible-sounding but factually incorrect and arbitrary model generations, present
014            a major challenge to the practical adoption of LLMs. Recent work by Farquhar et al.
015            (2024) proposes semantic entropy (SE), which can reliably detect hallucinations
016            by quantifying the uncertainty over different generations by estimating entropy
017            over semantically equivalent sets of outputs. However, the 5-to-10-fold increase in
018
               computation cost associated with SE computation hinders practical adoption. To ad-
               dress this, we propose SEPs, which directly approximate SE from the hidden states
019
               of a single generation. SEPs are simple to train and do not require sampling mul-
020
               tiple model generations at test time, reducing the overhead of semantic uncertainty
021            quantification to almost zero. We show that SEPs retain high performance for hal-
022            lucination detection and generalize better to out-of-distribution data than previous
023            probing methods that directly predict model accuracy. Our results across models
024            and tasks suggest that model hidden states capture SE, and our ablation studies give
025            further insights into the token positions and model layers for which this is the case.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities across a wide variety of
031   natural language processing tasks (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; OpenAI, 2023; Team, 2023; Brown et al.,
032   2020). They are increasingly deployed in real-world settings, including in high-stakes domains such
033
      as medicine, journalism, or legal services (Singhal et al., 2023; Weiser, 2023; Opdahl et al., 2023;
      Shen et al., 2023). It is therefore paramount that we can trust the outputs of LLMs. Unfortunately,
034
      LLMs have a tendency to hallucinate. Originally defined as “content that is nonsensical or unfaithful
035
      to the provided source” (Maynez et al., 2020; Filippova, 2020; Ji et al., 2023), the term is now
036   used to refer to nonfactual, arbitrary content generated by LLMs. For example, when asked to
037   generate biographies, even capable LLMs such as GPT-4 will often fabricate facts entirely (Min
038   et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Farquhar et al., 2024). While this may be acceptable in low-stakes use
039   cases, hallucinations can cause significant harm when factuality is critical. The reliable detection or
040   mitigation of hallucinations is a key challenge to ensure the safe deployment of LLM-based systems.
041
      Various approaches have been proposed to address hallucinations in LLMs (see Section 2). An
042   effective strategy for detecting hallucinations is to sample multiple responses for a given prompt
043   and check if the different samples convey the same meaning (Farquhar et al., 2024; Kuhn et al., 2023;
044   Kadavath et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2023; Cole et al., 2023; Chen & Mueller, 2023; Elaraby et al.,
045   2023; Manakul et al., 2023b; Min et al., 2023). The core idea is that if the model knows the answer,
046   it will consistently provide the same answer. If the model is hallucinating, its responses may vary
047   across generations. For example, given the prompt “What is the capital of France?”, an LLM that
048   “knows” the answer will consistently output (Paris, Paris, Paris), while an LLM that “does not know”
049   the answer may output (Naples, Rome, Berlin), indicating a hallucination.
050   One explanation for why this works is that LLMs have calibrated uncertainty (Kadavath et al., 2022;
051   OpenAI, 2023), i.e., “language models (mostly) know what they know” (Kadavath et al., 2022).
052   When an LLM is certain about an answer, it consistently provides the correct response. Conversely,
053   when uncertain, it generates arbitrary answers. This suggests that we can leverage model uncertainty
      to detect hallucinations. However, we cannot use token-level probabilities to estimate uncertainty

      We propose Semantic Entropy Probes (SEPs), linear probes that capture semantic uncertainty from the
      hidden states of LLMs, presenting a cost-effective and reliable hallucination detection method. SEPs
082
      combine the advantages of probing and sampling-based hallucination detection. Like other probing
083
      approaches, SEPs are easy to train, cheap to deploy, and can be applied to the hidden states of a single
084   model generation. Similar to sampling-based hallucination detection, SEPs capture the semantic
085   uncertainty of the model. Furthermore, they address some of the shortcomings of previous approaches.
086   Contrary to sampling-based hallucination detection, SEPs act directly on a single model hidden state
087   and do not require generating multiple samples at test time. And unlike previous probing methods,
088   SEPs are trained to predict semantic entropy (Farquhar et al., 2024) rather than model accuracy, which
089   can be computed without access to ground truth accuracy labels that can be expensive to curate.
090   We find that SEP predictions are effective proxies for truthfulness. In fact, SEPs generalize better
091   to new tasks than probes trained directly to predict accuracy, setting a new state-of-the-art for
092   cost-efficient hallucination detection, cf. Fig. 1. Our results additionally provides insights into the
093   inner workings of LLMs, strongly suggesting that model hidden states directly capture the model’s
094   uncertainty over semantic meanings. Through ablation studies, we show that this holds across a
095   variety of models, tasks, layers, and token positions.
096   In summary, our core contributions are:
097
098          • We propose Semantic Entropy Probes (SEPs), linear probes trained on the hidden states of
099
               LLMs to capture semantic entropy (Section 4).
100          • We demonstrate that semantic entropy is encoded in the hidden states of a single model
101            generation and can be successfully extracted using probes (Section 6).
102
             • We perform ablation studies to study SEP performance across models, tasks, layers, and
103
               token positions. Our results strongly suggest internal model states across layers and tokens
104            implicitly capture semantic uncertainty, even before generating any tokens. (Section 6)
105
106          • We show that SEPs can be used to predict hallucinations and that they generalize better
107
               than probes directly trained for accuracy as suggested by previous work, establishing a new
               state-of-the-art for cost-efficient hallucination detection (Section 7, Fig. 1).

      LLM Hallucinations. We refer to Rawte et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b) for surveys on hallucina-
      tions in LLMs and here review the most relevant related work to this paper. Early work on hallucina-
112
      tions in language models typically refers to issues in summarization tasks where models “hallucinate”
113
      content that is not faithful to the provided source text (Maynez et al., 2020; Deutsch et al., 2021; Dur-
114   mus et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2020; Manakul et al., 2023a; Nan et al., 2021). Around
115   the same time, research emerged that showed LLMs themselves could store and retrieve factual knowl-
116   edge (Petroni et al., 2019), leading to the currently popular closed-book setting, where LLMs are
117   queried without any additional context (Roberts et al., 2020). Since then, a large variety of work has
118   focused on mitigating hallucinations in LLMs, and we next give an overview to popular approaches.
119
      Sampling-Based Hallucination Detection. A variety of methods have been proposed that sample
120   multiple model completions for a given query and then quantify the semantic difference between the
121   model generations (Kuhn et al., 2023; Kadavath et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2023; Cole et al., 2023; Chen
122   & Mueller, 2023; Elaraby et al., 2023). For this paper, Farquhar et al. (2024) is particularly relevant, as
123   we use their semantic entropy measure to supervise our hidden state probes, cf. Section 3. A different
124   line of work does not directly re-sample answers for the same query, but instead asks follow-up ques-
125   tions to uncover inconsistencies in the original answer (Dhuliawala et al., 2023; Agrawal et al., 2024).
126   Recent work has also extended hallucination detection to scenarios where models generate longer para-
127   graphs of text by decomposing generations into individual facts or sentences, and then validating those
128
      facts separately (Luo et al., 2023; Mündler et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023b; Dhuliawala et al., 2023).
129   Retrieval-Based Methods. A different strategy is to rely on external knowledge bases, e.g. web
130   search, to verify the factuality of model responses (Feldman et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Peng
131   et al., 2023; Dziri et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Varshney et al., 2023; Su et al., 2022).
132   Such methods need not rely on good model uncertainties and can be used directly to fix errors in
133   model generations. However, retrieval-based approaches can add significant cost and latency. Further,
134
      they may be less effective for domains such as reasoning, where LLMs are also prone to produce
      unfaithful and misleading generations (Turpin et al., 2023; Lanham et al., 2023). Thus, retrieval- and
135
      uncertainty-based methods are orthogonal and can be combined for maximum effect.
136
137   Sampling and Finetuning Strategies. Prior work has also proposed to reduce hallucinations in
138   LLMs through sampling schemes (Lee et al., 2022; Chuang et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2023), preference
139   optimization targeting factuality (Tian et al., 2024), or finetuning to align “verbal” uncertainties of
140
      LLMs with model accuracy (Mielke et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Band et al., 2024).
141   Hidden State Methods. Azaria & Mitchell (2023) show that the factuality of LLM generations
142   can be predicted from hidden state probes, which are trained from a set of model generations and
143   associated accuracy labels. The success of accuracy probes has been replicated by Liu et al. (2024); Ji
144   et al. (2024), with the latter additionally showing that latent states can be used to predict training set
145   membership of queries. In this paper, we follow this line of work and implement a simple linear probe,
146
      supervised by model accuracy signals, as one of our baseline methods. Recently, He et al. (2024) have
      extended accuracy-probing methods to predict factuality on a word-level. While attempts have been
147
      made to propose unsupervised methods for factuality probing, these methods fall short in a variety
148
      of ways: Burns et al. (2023) is limited to binary questions and Farquhar et al. (2023) further question
149   core assumptions of the approach; Zou et al. (2023) propose a mostly unsupervised prompting strategy
150   but require accuracy labels to select hyperparameters; Chen et al. (2024) calculate a measure of the
151   semantic consistency of latent states, which requires sampling multiple expensive model generations.

154   3    S EMANTIC E NTROPY
155
156   Measuring uncertainty in free-form natural language generation tasks is challenging. The uncertain-
157   ties over tokens output by the language model can be misleading because they conflate semantic
158   uncertainty, uncertainty over the meaning of the generation, with lexical and syntactic uncertainty,
159   uncertainty over how to phrase the answer (see the example in Section 1). To address this, Farquhar
160   et al. (2024); Kuhn et al. (2023) propose semantic entropy, which aggregates token-level uncertainties
161   across clusters of semantic equivalence. Semantic entropy is important in the context of this paper
      because we use it as the supervisory signal to train our hidden state SE probes.

      Semantic entropy is calculated in three steps: (1) for a given query x, sample model completions from
163   the LLM, (2) aggregate the generations into clusters (C1 , . . . , CK ) of equivalent semantic meaning,
164   (3) calculate semantic entropy, HSE , by aggregating uncertainties within each cluster. Step (1) is
165   trivial, and we detail steps (2) and (3) below.
166
      Semantic Clustering. To determine if two generations convey the same meaning, Farquhar et al.
167
      (2024) use natural language inference (NLI) models, such as DeBERTa (He et al., 2021), to predict
168
      entailment between the generations. Concretely, two generations sa and sb are identical in meaning if
169   sa entails sb and sb entails sa , i.e. they entail each other bi-directionally. Farquhar et al. (2024) then
170   propose a greedy algorithm to cluster generations semantically: for each sample sa , we either add it
171   to an existing cluster Ck if bi-directional entailment holds between sa and a sample sb ∈ Ck , or add
172   it to a new cluster if the semantic meaning of sa is distinct from all existing clusters. After processing
173   all generations, we obtain a clustering of the generations into K distinct semantic meanings.
174
      Semantic Entropy. Given an input context x, the joint probability of a generation s consisting

197   4    S EMANTIC E NTROPY P ROBES
198
199   Although semantic entropy is effective at detecting hallucinations, its high computational cost may
200   limit its use to only the most critical scenarios. In this section, we propose Semantic Entropy Probes
201
      (SEPs), a novel method for cost-efficient and reliable uncertainty quantification in LLMs. SEPs are
      linear probes trained on the hidden states of LLMs to capture semantic entropy (Farquhar et al., 2024).
202
      However, unlike semantic entropy and other sampling-based approaches, SEPs act on the hidden
203
      states of a single model generation and do not require sampling multiple responses from the model at
204   test time. Thus, SEPs solve a key practical issue of semantic uncertainty quantification by almost
205   completely eliminating the computational overhead of semantic uncertainty estimation at test time.
206   We further argue that SEPs are advantageous to probes trained to directly predict model accuracy.
207   Our intuition for this is that semantic entropy is an inherent property of the model that should be
208   encoded in the hidden states and thus should be easier to extract than truthfulness, which relies on
209   potentially noisy external information. We discuss this further in Section 8.
210   Training SEPs. SEPs are constructed as linear logistic regression models, trained on the hidden
211   states of LLMs to predict semantic entropy. We create a dataset of (hlp (x), HSE (x)) pairs, where
212   x is an input query, hlp (x) ∈ Rd is the model hidden state at token position p and layer l, d is the
213   hidden state dimension, and HSE (x) ∈ R is the semantic entropy. That is, given an input query x,
214   we first generate a high-likelihood model response via greedy sampling and store the hidden state at
215   a particular layer and token position, hlp (x). We then sample N = 10 responses from the model at
      high temperature (T = 1) and compute semantic entropy, HSE (x), as detailed in the previous section.

      We investigate and evaluate Semantic Entropy Probes (SEPs) across a range of models and datasets.
256
      First, we show that we can accurately predict semantic entropy from the hidden states of LLMs
257
      (Section 6). We then explore how SEP predictions vary across different tasks, models, tokens indices,
258   and layers. Second, we demonstrate that SEPs are a cheap and reliable method for hallucination
259   detection (Section 7), which generalizes better to novel tasks than accuracy probes, although they
260   cannot match the performance of much more expensive sampling-based methods in our experiments.
261
      Tasks. We evaluate SEPs on four datasets: TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al.,
262
      2018), BioASQ (Tsatsaronis et al., 2015), and NQ Open (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019). We use the
263
      input queries of these tasks to derive training sets for SEPs and evaluate the performance of each
264   method on the validation/test sets, creating splits if needed. We consider a short- and a long-form
265   setting: Short-form answers are generated by few-shot prompting the LLM to answer “as briefly
266   as possible” and long-form answers are generated by prompting for a “single brief but complete
267   sentence”, leading to an approximately six-fold increase in the number of generated tokens (Farquhar
268   et al., 2024). Following Farquhar et al. (2024), we assess model accuracy via the SQuAD F1 score
269   for short-form generations, and we use use GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) to compare model answers to
      ground truth labels for long-form answers. We provide prompt templates in Appendix B.2.

477    8           D ISCUSSION , F UTURE W ORK , AND C ONCLUSIONS .
478
479   Discussion. On inputs from unseen tasks, our experiments show that SEPs are better predictors of
480   hallucinations than accuracy probes. In this discussion, we would like to offer some intuition as
481   to why we believe this is the case. Empirically, we find that SE is a quantity that can be reliably
482
      and consistently predicted from the model hidden states. We believe that SEPs learn to extract this
      signal and therefore generalize well to new tasks. Accuracy probes, on the other hand, may instead
483
      learn less robust features that generalize worse to new tasks.
484
485   The reason for this, ultimately, may be that accuracy depends on an external labeling process, and
      there is therefore no general mechanism to reliably predict accuracy from model-internal hidden states


                                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      across a large number of queries. We believe that the challenges from associating hard accuracy labels
487   to linguistic statements lead to accuracy probes not learning robust features of model uncertainty and
488   instead learning features that rely on spurious correlations between model predictions and accuracy
489   on a particular dataset. Concretely, we rarely but consistently observe outlier datapoints where
490   confident model predictions are labeled as ‘inaccurate’ because the model does not exactly match
491   the gold answer, for example, answering a slightly different question or answering at the wrong
492   level of granularity. For SEPs, such datapoints are trouble-free: the probe will learn to extract the
493   SE signal in latent space, which is reliably aligned with the SE labels used to supervise the probes.
494   For accuracy probes, however, we hypothesize that such datapoints are highly problematic: for
495
      them, the ‘SE signal’ in latent space is not a reliable enough predictor of model accuracy. Thus, the
      accuracy probe will learn to rely on other features, for example, a feature that identifies inputs for
496
      which there is a discrepancy between model answers and expected gold labels on a particular dataset.
497
      As labeling procedures differ between datasets, the accuracy probe struggles to generalize to new
498   tasks. For example, it may be that the model confidently replies to ‘historical questions’ by giving
499   only a (correct) year when the gold answer on the training dataset expects a full date. When the
500   generalization dataset then requires only years, the features of the accuracy probes fail to generalize
501   while SEPs perform well.
502
      In summary, a possible explanation for the gap in OOD generalization is that accuracy probes capture
503
      model correctness in a way that is specific to the training dataset. They may latch on to discriminative
504   features for model correctness that relate to the task at hand but do not generalize, such as identifying
505   a knowledge domain where accuracy is high or low, but which rarely occurs outside the training data.
506   Conversely, SEPs capture inherent model uncertainty which generalizes well to new tasks.
507
      Limitations. While they are extremely cheap to compute, SEPs do not match the performance
508
      of sampling-based methods such as full SE. Further, given that we need to compute SE for the
509
      training set to train SEPs, they are computationally advantageous only when the number of test
510   queries is larger than the training set – which should be a fair assumption for most, but certainly not
511   all, deployment scenarios. Lastly, like other probing approaches, SEP require full access to model
512   internals and cannot be computed in black-box scenarios.
513
      Future Work. We believe it should be possible to further close the performance gap between
514
      sampling-based approaches, such as semantic entropy, and SEPs. One avenue to achieve this could be
515
      to increase the scale of the training datasets used to train SEPs. In this work, we relied on established
516   QA tasks to train SEPs to allow for easy comparison to accuracy probes. However, future work could
517   explore training SEPs on unlabelled data, such as inputs generated from another LLM or natural
518   language texts used for general model training or finetuning. This could massively increase in the
519   amount of training data for SEPs, which should improve probe accuracy, and also allow us to explore
520   other more complex probing techniques that require more training data.
521
      Conclusions. We have introduced semantic entropy probes (SEPs): linear probes trained on the hidden
522   states of LLMs to predict semantic entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023), an effective measure of uncertainty
523   for free-form LLM generations. We find that the hidden states of LLMs implicitly capture semantic
524   entropy across a wide range of scenarios. SEPs are able to predict semantic entropy consistently,
525   and, importantly, they detect model hallucinations more effectively than probes trained directly for
526   accuracy prediction when testing on novel inputs from a different distribution than the training set
527   – despite not requiring any ground truth model correctness labels. Semantic uncertainty probing, both
528   in terms of model interpretability and practical applications, is an exciting avenue for further research.

1108   train SEPs on the resulting soft labels. Early results did not improve performance.
1109   Computational Costs for SEPs and SE. We here give a demonstration of the real-world compu-
1110   tational cost-savings associated with computing SEP instead of full SE. We find that, on average,
1111   computing SE for 1,000 TriviaQA samples for Llama-2-7B takes 168 ± 19.11 minutes. In con-
1112   trast, the computation of SEP predictions requires only 7.13 ± 0.14 seconds in the same setting.
1113   Consequently, SEPs are able to speed up hallucination detection by 1,413-fold compared to full SE.
1114   Rejection-Accuracy Curve for SEPs. Figure A.16 shows rejection-accuracy curves (Farquhar et al.,
1115   2024) for Llama-2-7B and SEPs. These curves show that SEPs can improve the overall accuracy of
1116   the model by refusing to predict on those inputs where SEPs predict the highest uncertainty.

1435   B.6   E VALUATION
1436
1437   To evaluate the performance of the probes in the generalization setting, we employ the following
1438   leave-one-out procedure for the aggregate results reported in the barplots and tables.
1439
       First, each probe is trained on a single dataset. Then, the trained probes are evaluated on all other
1440
       datasets in terms of AUROC of detecting hallucinations, excluding the dataset used for training. We
1441   then report the mean across all probes evaluated on that specific dataset. This allows us to assess the
1442   generalization capability of the probes by measuring their performance on datasets that were not used
1443   during the training phase. This scenario is important in practice, as the distribution of the query data
1444   will rarely be known.

1448
1449   Understanding Hidden States. More generally, probes can be a valuable tool to better understand
1450   the internal representations of neural networks like LLMs Alain & Bengio (2017); Belinkov (2021);
1451   Marks & Tegmark (2023). Recent work suggests that simple operations on LLM hidden states can
1452   qualitatively change model behavior (Subramani et al., 2022; Rimsky et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024)
1453   manipulate knowledge (Hernandez et al., 2023), or reveal deceitful intent (MacDiarmid et al., 2024).
1454
       Previous work has shown that hidden state probes can predict LLM outputs one or multiple tokens
       ahead with high accuracy (Belrose et al., 2023; Pal et al., 2023).
1455
1456   Sampling Procedures. Aichberger et al. (2024) point to potential improvements to uncertainty
1457   estimation in LLMs from dedicated sampling schemes that identify token-level contributions to
       semantic uncertainty.
