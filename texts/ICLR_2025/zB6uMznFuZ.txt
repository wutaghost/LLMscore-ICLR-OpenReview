009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we leverage the power of latent diffusion models to generate synthetic
012            time series tabular data. Along with the temporal and feature correlations, the
013            heterogeneous nature of the feature in the table has been one of the main obstacles
014            in time series tabular data modeling. We tackle this problem by combining the
015            ideas of the variational auto-encoder (VAE) and the denoising diffusion proba-
016            bilistic model (DDPM). Our model named as TimeAutoDiff has several key
017            advantages including (1) Generality: the ability to handle the broad spectrum of
018
               time series tabular data with heterogeneous, continuous only, or categorical only
               features; (2) Fast sampling speed: entire time series data generation as opposed to
019
               the sequential data sampling schemes implemented in the existing diffusion-based
020
               models, eventually leading to significant improvements in sampling speed, (3)
021            Time varying metadata conditional generation: the implementation of time series
022            tabular data generation of heterogeneous outputs conditioned on heterogenous, time
023            varying features, enabling scenario exploration across multiple scientific and engi-
024            neering domains. (4) Good fidelity and utility guarantees: numerical experiments
025            on eight publicly available datasets demonstrating significant improvements over
026            state-of-the-art models in generating time series tabular data, across four metrics
027            measuring fidelity and utility; Codes for model implementations are available at
028            the supplementary materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Synthesizing tabular data is crucial for data sharing and model training. In the healthcare domain,
      synthetic data enables the safe sharing of realistic but non-sensitive datasets, preserving patient
034
      confidentiality while supporting research and software testing (Yoon et al., 2023). In fields like
035
      fraud detection (Padhi et al., 2021b; Hsieh et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024), where anomalous events
036   are rare, synthetic data can provide additional examples to train more effective detection models.
037   Synthetic datasets are also vital for scenario exploration, missing data imputation (Tashiro et al.,
038   2021; Ouyang et al., 2023), and practical data analysis experiences across various domains.
039
      Given the importance of synthesizing tabular data, many researchers have put enormous efforts into
040
      building tabular synthesizers with high fidelity and utility guarantees. For example, CTGAN (Xu
041
      et al., 2019) and its variants (Zhao et al., 2021; 2022) (e.g., CTABGAN, CTABGAN+) have gained
042   popularity for generating tabular data using Generative Adversarial Networks (Goodfellow et al.,
043   2020) (GANs). Recently, diffusion-based tabular synthesizers, like Stasy (Kim et al., 2022), have
044   shown promise, outperforming GAN-based methods in various tasks. Yet, diffusion models (Ho et al.,
045   2020; Song et al., 2020b) were not initially designed for heterogeneous features. New approaches,
046   such as those using Doob’s h-transform (Liu et al., 2022), TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2022), and
047   CoDi (Lee et al., 2023), aim to address this challenge by combining different diffusion models (Song
048   et al., 2020b; Hoogeboom et al., 2022) or leveraging contrastive learning (Schroff et al., 2015) to
049   co-evolve models for improved performance on heterogeneous data. Most recently, researchers have
050
      used the idea of a latent diffusion model, i.e., AutoDiff (Suh et al., 2023) and TabSyn (Zhang et al.,
      2023a), to model the heterogeneous features in tables and prove its empirical effectiveness in various
051
      tabular generation tasks.
052
053   However, the tabular synthesizers mentioned above focus solely on generating tables with independent
      and identically distributed (i.i.d.) rows. They face difficulties in simulating time series tabular data

079   1.1      P ROBLEM F ORMULATION , M OTIVATION , AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
080
081   Problem Formulation. Our goal is to learn the joint distribution of time series tabular data of a
082   T -sequence (x1 , . . . , xT ). Each observation xj , where xj := [xCont,j , xDisc,j ] is an F -dimensional
083   feature vector that includes both continuous (xCont,j ) and discrete variables (xDisc,j ), reflecting the
084   heterogeneous nature of the dataset. Throughout this paper, we assume there are B i.i.d. observed
085   sequences sampled from P(x1 , . . . , xT ). We additionally assume that each record in the time-series
086   tabular data includes a timestamp, formatted as ’YEAR-MONTH-DATE-HOURS’. This timestamp
087
      serves as an auxiliary variable to aid an training / inference step in TimeAutoDiff, which will be
      detailed shortly. The overview of TimeAutoDiff is provided in Figure 1.
088
089   Motivation of TimeAutoDiff. The main motivation for combining the two models, VAE and DDPM,
090   is to accurately capture the distribution of heterogeneous features in the data. Diffusion model
091   has recently gained a lot of attentions in a time series community, because of its ability generating
092   complex and high quality sequences. (See Lin et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024b) and references therein
093
      for more detailed reasons.) Nonetheless, current literatures only focus on modeling continuous time
      series data. This is mainly attributed to the fact that the diffusion model is originally designed for
094
      capturing distributions on continuous space. In our work, to deal with heterogeneous features, the
095
      β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017) is employed for projecting the time series data to continuous latent
096   space. The autoencoder framework has been widely employed in tabular data modeling to address
097   heterogeneity, leveraging the reconstruction error in its objective function (Desai et al., 2021; Suh
098   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). Inspired from this observation, we combine these two models for
099   modeling a time-series data with heterogeneous features. Furthermore, dependencies along temporal
100   and feature dimensions can be captured through the sophisticated architectural designs of VAE and
101   DDPM denoiser. Specifically, in both models, we use the inductive bias of Recurrent Neural Network
102   (RNN) (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and Bi-directional RNN (Bi-RNN) (Schuster & Paliwal,
103   1997) to capture the temporal dependences of sequences. Our unique design of DDPM denoiser
104
      captures the feature dependences. More details are provided in Section 2.
105   Contribution 1. Sampling time for new data sequence generation is significantly reduced compared
106   to other SOTA diffusion-based time series models like TSGM (Lim et al., 2023) and diffusion-
107   ts (Yuan & Qiao, 2023), which rely on sequential sampling. Existing synthesizers typically model
      the conditional distribution P(xt |xt−1 , . . . , x1 ) and generate xt sequentially for t ∈ {2, . . . , T }.

108
      In contrast, our model learns the entire distribution P(xT , xT −1 , . . . , x1 ) and generates the whole
109   sequence at once. The difference in sampling times becomes more pronounced as T increases since
110   diffusion models require multiple denoising steps for each sample. For verifications, our model
111   generates long sequential data (i.e., T = 900 in Appendix L), while other diffusion baseline methods
112   suffer from generating much shorter sequential data (i.e., T = 24 in Table 1). Additionally, our
113   approach avoids the accumulating errors commonly associated with sequential sampling.
114
      Contribution 2. TimeAutoDiff accommodates conditional generation. The model can be condi-
115   tioned on heterogeneous sequential metadata. 1 Given B i.i.d. pairs of multivariate time series xi
116   and time-varying metadata ci (i.e., Dx,c = {(xi , ci )}B i=1 ), our model learns the conditional distribu-
117   tion p(x|c). Notably, both xi and ci can represent multivariate heterogeneous and sequential data.
118   Additionally, static variables (e.g., gender, ethnicity) can also be incorporated as conditions c in our
119   model. This capability unlocks significant potential for the model to be employed in counterfactual
120   scenario exploration across diverse scientific and engineering domains. We demonstrate this potential
121   through two specific examples under synthetic and real-world (Traffic dataset) settings in Section 4.3.
122   Contribution 3. Numerical comparisons of TimeAutoDiff with other models (with publicly avail-
123   able codes), namely, TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019), Diffusion-ts (Yuan & Qiao, 2023), TSGM (Lim
124   et al., 2023), CPAR (Zhang et al., 2022), and DoppelGANger (Lin et al., 2020) are conducted
125   comprehensively across eight real-world datasets under various metrics. (See Appendix C for de-
126   scriptions of the datasets.) Specifically, for measuring the fidelities of temporal correlations between
127   synthetic and real heterogeneous timeseries tabular data, we develop a new metric, named Temporal
128   Discriminative Score. Inspired from the paper (Yoon et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022), this metric
129
      computes discriminative scores (Yoon et al., 2019) of distributions of inter-row differences (Zhang
      et al., 2022) in generated and original sequential data.

132   1.2   R ELEVANT L ITERATURE
133
134
      To our knowledge, not many models in literature can deal with time series tabular data with a
      heterogeneous nature. We categorize the incomplete list of existing models into three parts: (1)
135
      GAN-based models, (2) Diffusion-based models, and (3) GPT-based / Parametric models.
136
137   GAN-based models. TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019) is one of the most popular time series
138   data synthesizers based on the GAN framework. Notably, they used the idea of latent GAN
139   employing the auto-encoder for projecting the time series data to latent space and model the
140   distribution of the data in latent space through the GAN framework. Recently proposed Electric
141   Health Record (in short EHR)-Safe (Yoon et al., 2023) integrates a GAN with an encoder-decoder
142   module to generate realistic time series and static variables in EHRs. EHR-M-GAN (Li et al.,
143   2023) employs distinct encoders for each data type, enhancing the generation of mixed-type time
144   series in EHRs. Despite these advancements, GAN-based methods still encounter challenges
145
      such as non-convergence, mode collapse, generator-discriminator imbalance, and sensitivity to
      hyperparameter selection, underscoring the need for ongoing refinement in time series data synthesis.
146
147
      Diffusion-based models. Most recently, TimeDiff (Tian et al., 2023) adopts the idea from
148   TabDDPM combining the multinomial and Gaussian diffusion models to generate a synthetic
149   EHR time series tabular dataset. DPM-EHR (Kuo et al., 2023) suggested another diffusion-based
150   mixed-typed EHR time series synthesizer, which mainly relies on Gaussian diffusion and U-net
151   architecture. TSGM (Lim et al., 2023) used the idea of the latent conditional score-based diffusion
152   model to generate continuous time series data. However, TSGM is highly overparameterized and
153   its training, inference, and sampling steps are quite slow. Diffusion-TS (Yuan & Qiao, 2023) takes
154   advantage of the latent diffusion model employing transformer-based auto-encoder to capture the
155   temporal dynamics of complicated time series data. Specifically, they decompose the seasonal-trend
156
      components in time series data making the generated data highly interpretable. One important model
      in the literature, CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021), uses a 2D-attention-based conditional diffusion model
157
      to impute the missing continuous time series data.

354   tasks is assessed through the predictive score (Yoon et al., 2019), where a predictive model (i.e.,

356   Sampling times (in sec.) are compared with other base-line methods. Detailed explanations for
357
      each metric are deferred in the Appendix G. Additionally, generalizability of the model is evaluated

358
      from the distribution rather than memorizing the training data points (Appendix I). To evaluate the

533
534       This paper introduces TimeAutoDiff, a novel time series tabular data synthesizer designed for
535       multi-dimensional, heterogeneous features. Leveraging a latent diffusion model with a specialized
536       VAE, it achieves high fidelity and utility guarantees. The model supports time-varying metadata
537       conditional generation, enabling applications across scientific and engineering domains. It also lays
538       the groundwork for tasks such as missing data imputation, privacy guarantees, interpretability, and
539       extension to foundational models, all of which rely on precise modeling of P(xT , xT −1 , . . . , x1 ).

      A    D ISCUSSIONS ON FUTURE TOPICS WITH RELEVANT LITERATURE
757
758
759   In this subsection, we further discuss about the four possible extensions of TimeAutoDiff in
760   sequel: (1) Missing data imputation; (2) Privacy guarantees; (3) Interpretability of generated time
761   series data; (4) Extension to foundational model.
762   (1) Missing data imputation is an important application of tabular data synthesis. In the literature,
763   CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021) study the imputations of continuous time series tabular data through
764   diffusion-based framework. The main idea is to employ the specially designed masks; masking
765   the observed data, and to let the model predict the masked values in the observations, i.e.,
766   self-supervised learning. Then, the trained model can impute the real missing parts of the table by
767   thinking them as masked observations. We conjecture the similar idea can be easily adopted in
768
      the framework of TimeAutoDiff. In i.i.d. row setting (each row from the same distribution),
      several papers (Zhang et al., 2023a; 2024) study the imputation problem of tabular data with
769
      heterogeneous features through diffusion-based synthesizers. Zhang et al. (2023a) directly used
770
      the pre-trained unconditional latent diffusion model, analogous to inpainting tasks of images,
771   for the imputation. Zhang et al. (2024) employed the concept of EM-algorithm. Specifically,
772   the former work, Zhang et al. (2023a), utilized the fact that the transformer maps the input data
773   to latent space deterministically, where transformer is used for the main backbone architecture in VAE.
774
775   (2) Privacy Guarantees is one of the main motivations of synthetic data. Specifically, in
776   the time series domain, data from the healthcare and financial sectors is ubiquitous, but it often
777   comes with significant privacy concerns. We hope the synthetic data does not leak any private
778   information of the original data, while preserving good fidelities. TimeAutoDiff lays the
779
      foundation for guaranteeing such privacy concerns with the generated synthetic data. In the vision
      domain, differential privacy guarantees (Dwork, 2006) of synthetic images from diffusion-based
780
      models have been investigated by several researchers (Dockhorn et al., 2022; Ghalebikesabi et al.,
781
      2023; Lyu et al., 2023). Specifically, Lyu et al. (2023) studied DP-guarantees of latent diffusion
782   model by fine-tuning the attention module of noise predictor in their diffusion model, and claim their
783   synthetic images both have good fidelities and DP-guarantees.
784
785   Nonetheless, it is still not clear how the same idea can be applied to time series synthetic
786   data (or regular tabular data), as differentially private time series data is frequently challenging to
787   interpret (Yoon et al., 2020). In this regard, another privacy criterion, ε-identifiability (Yoon et al.,
788   2020) (with ε ∈ [0, 1]) can be considered as another alternative. The distance between synthetic and
789   original data is measured through Euclidean distance, and we want at least (1 − ε)-proportion of the
790
      synthetic data to be distinguishable (or different enough) from the original data. Under this criterion,
      we conjecture TimeAutoDiff can be extended to the synthesizer with a (theoretically-provable)
791
      privacy guarantees. The idea can be underpinned around several recent results on diffusion
792
      model (Zhang et al., 2023b; Bodin et al., 2024). Zhang et al. (2023b) showed that there exist
793   closed-form solutions of noise predictors for every diffusion step of noisy training data points. This
794   means that we can trace back the latent vectors (or matrix) where the original training data points
795   are generated from. Recent findings (Bodin et al., 2024) suggest that a proper linear combination
796   of data in the latent space can produce a new semantically meaningful dataset in the original
797   space. Combining the fact that the mapping from the latent space to the original space is Lipschitz
798   continuous (Zhang et al., 2023b) through deterministic sampling (probability-flow), we might be
799   able to have controls over the generations of time series synthetic data, whose Euclidean distances
800   from training data points are away from the training data points. This idea is naturally related to the
801
      diversity of generated data as well.
802
      (3) Interpretability of the generated time series data is another crucial aspect that time se-
803
      ries synthesizer should possess. In many practical applications, for instance, in financial sector,
804   stakeholders and domain experts may be hesitant to rely on synthesis models that are difficult to
805   interpret, as they need to understand and trust the model’s behavior, especially when dealing with
806   critical or high-risk scenarios. The current version of TimeAutoDiff does not have the luxury
807   of generating interpretable results, but this can be easily adopted by following the previous works.
808   Specifically, we want to point out readers TimeVAE (Desai et al., 2021) and Diffusion-TS (Yuan &
809   Qiao, 2023), which both focus on building a synthesizer with interpretability. Specifically, TimeVAE
      adopted a sophisticatedly designed decoder in VAE, which has trend, seasonality, and residual


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      blocks for signal decompositions. Similarly, Diffusion-TS also design a sophisticated decoder for the
811   decomposition of signals into trend, seasonality, and residual, where they employ the latent diffusion
812   framework. Both of these ideas can be directly employed in TimeAutoDiff, where the current
813   decoder is set as an MLP block for simplicity.
814
815   (4) Extension to foundational model is another promising route the TimeAutoDiff can
816   take. Recently, we have been seeing a wave of foundational models research on time series
817   domain (Cao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Das et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a). These models can
818   accommodate multiple tables from cross domains, enabling multiple time series tasks in one model;
819
      for instance, forecasting, anomaly detection, imputation, and synthetic data generation (See Cao
      et al. (2024).) Among them, Cao et al. (2024) devised cleverly designed masks, which provide
820
      the unifying framework to do the four abovementioned tasks under diffusion-based framework.
821
      Nonetheless, their methods are confined to the continuous data modality, and not clear how
822   the model can be extended to heterogeneous features, leaving the great future opportunities for
823   TimeAutoDiff to be extended. We also conjecture the synthetic data from TimeAutoDiff can be
824   beneficial to improving quality of forecasting foundation model i.e., see Section 5 in (Das et al., 2023).
825
826   (5) Bias from conditional metadata generation:                 Generated data can indeed be biased
827   with respect to conditional metadata, arising from various factors. Bias in the training data, such as
828   inherent associations between metadata and outputs, may lead the model to replicate these biases, for
829   instance, generating disproportionately high traffic volumes for "Clear" weather even when the true
830
      relationship is less deterministic. Imbalanced metadata distributions further exacerbate this issue, as
      underrepresented conditions in the training set often result in less reliable outputs for those conditions,
831
      such as biased outcomes for minority demographic groups in healthcare datasets. Simplified
832
      assumptions in the model, such as assuming linear relationships between metadata and outputs, can
833   overlook complex dependencies, producing data that fails to reflect the true conditional distribution.
834   Noise injection, a feature of models like diffusion models and VAEs, can introduce additional
835   bias if the noise interacts with metadata in unexpected ways, particularly for rare metadata values.
836   Furthermore, limitations in conditional architectures, such as inadequate metadata encoding, can
837   prevent the model from capturing nuanced dependencies, leading to misaligned outputs. To mitigate
838   such biases, ensuring balanced training data, employing robust metadata encoding techniques,
839   applying regularization or fairness constraints, performing post-generation bias audits, and designing
840   disentangled latent spaces are crucial steps. While conditional generative models aim to align
841
      generated data with metadata, addressing these biases is essential to ensure fairness and reliability.

915   It is essential to pre-process the real tabular data in a form that the machine learning model can
916   extract the desired information from the data properly. We divide the heterogeneous features into two
917   categories; (1) continuous, and (2) discrete. Following is how we categorize the variables and process
      each feature type. Let x be the column of a table to be processed.


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
             1. Continuous feature: If x’s entries are real-valued continuous, we categorize x as a numerical
919             feature. Moreover, if the entries are integers with more than 25 distinct values (e.g., “Age”),
920             then x is categorized as a continuous variable. Here, 25 is a user-specified threshold. We
921             employ min-max scaler (Yoon et al., 2019) to ensure the pre-processed numerical features
922             are within the range of [0, 1]. Hereafter, we denote xProc
                                                                       Num as the processed column.
923
924
             2. Discrete / Categorical feature: If x’s entries have string datatype, we categorize x as a
                discrete feature (e.g., “Gender”). Additionally, the x with less than 25 distinct integers is
925
                categorized as a discrete feature. For pre-processing, we simply map the entries of x to the
926
                integers greater than or equal to 0, and further divide the data type into two parts; binary and
927             categorical, denoting them as xProc        Proc          Proc
                                                 Bin and xCat . Here, xCat denotes the discrete variables with
928             more than 3 labels or categories.
929
930          3. Post-processing step: After the TimeAutoDiff model generates a synthetic dataset, it
931             must be restored to its original format. For continuous features, this is achieved through

1016

1017

1025   preserves the temporal dependencies of the original data. For any fixed integer t ∈ {1, . . . , T − 1},

       O      M AXIMUM M EAN D ISCREPANCY & E NTROPY
1567
1568
       We used two metrics proposed by TSGM (Nikitin et al., 2023): Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD)
1569   and Entropy. MMD measures the similarity (or fidelity) between synthetic and real time series data,
1570   while Entropy assesses the diversity of the synthetic data. The results are summarized in Table 11
1571   and are consistent with those in Table 1.
1572
       TimeAutoDiff achieves the lowest MMD scores across all four datasets, aligning with the discrimina-
1573
       tive scores reported in Table 1. This indicates that TimeAutoDiff effectively generates synthetic data
1574
       that closely resembles real data. For diversity, higher Entropy values indicate a dataset with more
1575   diverse samples. However, as noted in (Nikitin et al., 2023), Entropy should be considered alongside
1576   other metrics, as random noise can also result in high Entropy values. TimeAutoDiff produces
1577   synthetic data with higher Entropy values than the real data, though not as excessively as other
1578   baseline models. This suggests that our model generates synthetic data that preserves the statistical
1579   properties of the original data, maintaining diversity without introducing excessive deviation.
