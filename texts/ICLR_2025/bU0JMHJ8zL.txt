008                                                 A BSTRACT
009
010              The Simplicity Bias (SB) is the observation that the training of most commonly
011              used neural network architectures with standard training techniques is biased to-
012
                 ward learning “simple” functions. This phenomenon can be a benefit or drawback
                 depending on the relative complexity of the desired function to be learnt. If the
013
                 desired function is relatively simple it’s a positive. However, if there are simpler
014
                 features that are highly predictive; commonly named “shortcuts” or “spurious fea-
015              tures”, that are not present in the test environment, the SB can result in poor gen-
016              eralisation performance. Most existing works on mitigating the SB make various
017              assumptions, either about the features present in the train and test domains or by
018              assuming access to information about the test domain at train time. In this paper
019              we review recent work on the SB and take a critical look at these assumptions.
020

022   1       I NTRODUCTION
023
024   There are many examples of neural networks (NNs) trained with standard training techniques1 using
025   “shortcuts” (Geirhos et al., 2020) or spurious features2 to make predictions instead of some desired
026   attributes or “labelling function” (Puli et al., 2023; Kirichenko et al., 2023). For image classification
027   tasks background, texture, and secondary objects are often considered spurious features even if
028   predictive of the label Ye et al. (2024). For example Li et al. (2023) observed that many NNs trained
029
      on the ImageNet data set (Deng et al., 2009) use a text watermark, as a shortcut to classify images
      of boxes, rather than the pixels representing the boxes themselves.
030
031   The “Simplicity Bias” (SB) is normally attributed as the underlying factor causing models to use
032   spurious features (Shah et al., 2020). The SB is the observation that there seems to be an implicit
033   ordering over the possible mappings learnt by NNs with standard training techniques. These tech-
034   niques are more likely to recover parameter settings encoding “simple features”, Moreover, it has
035
      been shown that parameterisation corresponding to “simple functions” occupy the majority of the
      volume of the solution space3 (Scimeca et al., 2021). However, there are many differing definitions
036
      over what features are “simple”. Due to this implied preference ordering, if a data set contains mul-
037
      tiple predictive features it is likely that the encoded function at test time will make most use of the
038   “simplest” feature. Thus, any features of interest with high complexity may not be learnt during
039   training, as simpler features are sufficient to minimise the loss. In this way simple features can be
040   seen as distractors, that prevent the learning of more complex features. While, the collective under-
041   standing of this phenomena has steadily been improving, in this manuscript we take a critical look
042   at existing works investigating the SB, “shortcuts” or “spurious correlation” and highlight some of
043   the common assumptions often made by these works.
044
045           1. Known Test Domain: This is the assumption that one has fairly detailed knowledge of the
046
                 test domain at train time, typically captured in the form of a “clean” data set.
047           2. All Simple Features are Shortcuts This is the assumption that all simple features are short
048              cuts.
049           3. Two Features Assumption: This common simplifying assumption is only to consider data
050              sets with two “features”; a desired feature to be learnt which is assumed to be complex and

086   paper we use “features” to mean properties of a data set. To formalise this notion of features we find
087   the definition presented in Ilyas et al. (2019) particularly compelling due to its flexibility. According
088   to their definition a feature is some function of the input space to a single scalar that represents the
089   presence or lack of the given attribute or concept. Formally:

092   where Fall is the space of all features. For ease of discussion we will consider the problem of binary

094   (2024) we say a feature fj (x) has a predictivity of ϵj over a data set D if ϵj = P{x,y}∼D (y =

098   2.2.1     S IMPLE F EATURES
099
      The simplicity bias is the observation that NNs are bias toward learning simple functions or fea-
100
      tures. This observation can be split into to parts, first that there seems to be a preference ordering
101
      over functions that is mostly transferable between models, and second this ordering seems to be
102   correlated with the “simplicity” of the function. However, there is no consensus on the definition of
103   “simple”. Shah et al. (2020) define the simplicity of a function by the minimum number of linear
104   pieces in the decision boundary that achieves optimal classification accuracy using that function.
105   Unfortunately, this definition is not easy to quantify outside of toy data sets. Wang et al. (2023)
106   suggest low frequency features are simple and hence learnt first. Vasudeva et al. (2024b) define sim-
107   ple as those that can be learn by models with fewer parameters. Conversely, Hermann & Lampinen
      (2020) look at what features convolutional neural networks (CNN’s) learn first and suggest simple


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      features are those that can be most easily decoded by linear probing before training. Morwani et al.
109   (2023) provide a precise definition for SB for 1-hidden layer neural networks, and suggest simple
110   features can be characterised by a low dimensional projection of the inputs. More recently several
111   works (Scimeca et al., 2021; Mingard et al., 2023) have suggested NN are biased toward learning
112   Kolmogorov simple functions. All these metrics correspond the some measure of the information
113   required in describing the function and thus In practice, outside of carefully constructed functions
114   they are likely to be highly correlated.

116   2.3     I S INPUT SENSITIVITY A GOOD WAY TO MEASURE COMPLEXITY ?
117
118   Recently Vasudeva et al. (2024a) investigated the simplicity bias of transformers. As transformers
119
      encode functions over sequences of discrete tokens the authors asked whether input sensitivity might
      offer a unified notion of simplicity? Their definition of input sensitivity measures how frequently the
120
      output of a function changes for a single token substitution of a given input, or set of inputs. Unlike
121
      the previously mentioned methods of measuring simplicity, this approach looks at a local property of
122   the function around a given point or set of points, rather than global property. We note it is possible
123   to design a data set which encodes a function with high input sensitivity but low complexity, or low
124   input sensitivity but high complexity. In Section 2.3.1 we empirically back up this claim and show
125   that input sensitivity does not always align with the preference order over functions. We suggest this
126   quantity is more aligned with robustness than simplicity due to its obvious parallel to adversarial
127   robustness defined over sequences of tokens. We note however, if a function has high sensitivity
128   over the whole ambient space of which it is defined, it does follow that it must be highly complex.
129   However, when sampling text, images or audio the data manifold only occupy a tiny subset of
130
      the ambient space, so for these data types input sensitivity does not necessary correlate with other
      defintion of “simplicity”.

      3     W HAT ARE S PURIOUS F EATURES ?
181
      The consensus is spurious features are those that are predictive of the label in the train set but fail
182
      to be predictive at test time. We claim the concept of a feature being spurious inherently relies on
183
      additional side information of the test domain, whether this be agreement with a human annotator or
184   access to additional data deemed appropriate by a practitioner. For example, (Geirhos et al., 2020)
185   suggest a shortcut performs well on standard benchmarks but fails to transfer to more challenging
186   testing conditions, such as real-world scenarios. This definition relies on having knowledge of the
187   more challenging testing condition, which is typically captured in the form of a “clean” data set
188   or as human knowledge about likely domain shifts. Without extra information at training time it
189   is impossible to know which features are spurious and which are not. One only has a data set
190   containing a continuum of different features with different predictive powers.
191
192   3.1   D O B LAME D ISTRIBUTION S HIFT !
193
      (Puli et al., 2023) suggested distribution shift should not be blamed for the problems cause by short-
194
      cut learning. To back up this claim they provide theoretical results showing short cut learning for a
195
      linear binary classification problem even when the same data generating process (DGP) is used to
196   generate both train and test domains. However, their theoretical results rely on the sub-sampling of
197   the DGP during training, and only hold for bounded data set sizes. Thus in their theoretical results
198   they actually have test and train data sets with slightly different distributions. Additionally Statistical
199   Learning Theory (Vapnik, 1999), suggests for convex problems like the one considered Empirical
200   risk minimisation should return a model that minimises the risk over the training distribution. If one
201   knows a priori there will be no distribution shift at test time, and if the train set is large enough
202   to distinguish noise from signal, one can learn any feature as they will still be predictive. Thus,
203   spurious features are only problematic in combination with domain shift. However, for real world
204
      training sets there is likely to always be a shift in distribution between test and train domains, either
      due to sub sampling or differences in how the data were gathered (Ye et al., 2024).

      3.2   W HERE ARE S PURIOUS F EATURES ACTUALLY A PROBLEM ?
208   We now discuss a number of settings where spurious features can lead to poor generalisation. We
209   divide up these settings based on the information present at train time about the test distribution.
210
211   A Data Problem This setting is characterised by having limited data of the desired test environ-
212   ment, commonly caused by a high cost in gathering or approximating representative test data. If
213   large quantities of appropriate test data were available at train time, one would simply train on this
214   data instead. Thus, at train time one knows exactly what domain shift is present between the test and
215   train environments. This setting is also obtained after a model has been deployed, and been found
      to be failing on the test data. This problem can be viewed as a data problem and if possible boot


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      strapping more appropriate test data can offer a solution. Outside of gathering more data a number
217   of good general purpose approaches have been proposed. For example last layer training methods
218   as discussed in Section 5 typically make the assumption of asseess to a small amount of test data.
219
220
      An Alignment Problem This setting is characterised by one having side information in the form
221   of a prior belief about the test distribution, or a given feature of interest. One may or may not
222   have a mathematical understanding on the form of function that is desired to be learnt, such as a
223   low sensitivity to certain transformations of the data. None the less one is trying to train an NN
224   to predict in a manner deemed appropriate by one or more humans supervisors (Xiao et al., 2021).
225   This setting requires a human in the loop determining, and adjusting, the training procedure to try
226   and promote invariance to certain features if they are being used for prediction. This process is hard
227   to automate and expensive. In the worst case a spurious feature maybe identified, removed from the
228   training data, and the model retrained, only for the model to learn to use another undesirable feature
229
      for prediction. This can lead to a metaphorical game of “Whack-A-Mole” as described in Li et al.
      (2023).
230
231
232   Limited Knowledge of the Test Domain While the above two settings are well studied they both
233
      make a fairly big assumption. That is, they assume detailed knowledge about the test domain at
      train time. While this assumption is likely true in the majority of use cases we claim there are
234
      setting where it does not. For concrete example a practitioner would likely not know what types
235
      of shifts to expect in bird or whale song when moving between region or species. One likely has a
236   poor understanding of which features of these data set are likely to change at test time and which are
237   not. Other examples indicative of this setting include privatised tabular data where column headings
238   have been removed.
239
      This setting where one has very limited knowledge of the deployment domain remains under ex-
240
      plored. In order to make learning feasible in this setting one would need to assume some common-
241
      ality between, test and train domains. One way to ensure learning on the train domain translates
242   to the test domain would be to assume and unknown subset of the features in the training data set
243   are present at test time. In this setting the best course of action is to learn a model sensitive to a
244   large number of diverse features (Teney et al., 2022a;b). However, once we have knowledge of a
245   test domain it is possible to conclude what features were good and thus it is difficult to effectively
246   simulate this lack of knowledge. To address this difficulty one option would be to evaluate the per-
247   formance after a variety of domain shifts, and report the average. While the works of Teney et al.
248   (2022a;b) considered training an ensemble of models that are predictive to multiple features for best
249   result they also assume access to test data for model selection. In the next subsetion we formalise
250
      this under-explored learning problem.

      3.2.1       T WO F EATURES A SSUMPTION
283
284   The next prevalent assumption we want to highlight is what we call the “two feature assumption”.
285   Specifically, that is assuming data sets only contain two features a complex desired feature and
286   a simple spurious feature. Table 1 is adapted from Vasudeva et al. (2024b) and highlights how
287   frequent this assumption is in common simplest bias data sets.
288   We conjecture this assumption is made typically for the following few reasons. As the number
289   of features increases the number of unique feature combination grows exponentially and hence, to
290   maintain the same number of training points for each feature combination one requires an exponen-
291   tial amount of data. Additionally, labelling real world data sets with multiple features is expensive.
292   Thus, the few data image sets that do come with multiple feature annotations typically make use of
293   synthetic or composite images (Lynch et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). To the best our knowledge all
294   spurious correlation NLP data sets still make the two feature assumption.
295   The main issue with the two feature assumption is for data sets which have it by design, promoting
296   invariance to the simplest feature typically results in learning the other complex feature. However,
297   for data sets with any more than two features this is no longer true, and not learning the simplest
298   feature does not ensure the most complex is learnt. This focus on two feature data sets has lead to
299   methods like those discussed in Section 5 which focus more on not learning simple features, rather
300   than either learning i) multiple features or ii) the features with highest predictivity.
301   In the next section we construct data sets with multiple features and show removing or not learning
302   a feature very rarely results in a specific more predictive feature being learnt.

413   5   R ELATED WORK
414
415   Simplicity Bias It’s hard to pin point the exact origin of the concept of the SB, however in their
416   work Learning Qualitatively Diverse and Interpretable Rules for Classification, Ross et al. (2018)
417   introduced a way to identify a maximal set of distinct but accurate models for a dataset. The authors
418   demonstrated empirically that, in situations where the data supports multiple accurate classifiers,
419   SGD tends to recover simpler, more interpretable classifiers rather than more complex ones. Valle-
420   Perez et al. (2019) suggest the good generalisation performance of neural networks was because
421   the parameter-function map is biased towards simple functions. Nakkiran et al. (2019) suggested
422   when using SGD to train a NN, the NN learns functions of increasing complexity. Starting by
423
      first learning a linear model which is retained even to convergence. Scimeca et al. (2021) show
      that solutions corresponding to Kolmogorov-simple cues are abundant in the parameter space and
424
      are thus more likely found by DNNs. More recently, Bell & Sagun (2022) showed that SB can
425
      lead to larger performance disparities between different groups of training data. Finally, Mingard
426   et al. (2023) claim NN have an inbuilt Occam’s razor. Their analysis also suggests a strong bias to
427   functions with low Kolmogorov complexity. Shah et al. (2020) was the first work to focus on the
428   negative phenomena that can result form the SB. Specifically, they showed that i) SB can be extreme,
429   causing networks to rely solely on the simplest feature, (ii) extreme SB exacerbates performance
430   degradation from distribution shifts and adversarial attacks, (iii) SB can hinder generalisation even
431   when simpler features are less predictive, and (iv) common strategies like ensembles and adversarial
      training struggle to mitigate SB’s drawbacks. While the observation that neural networks were


                                                             8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      biased to learning simpler functions was known before this, most works considered it to be a positive
433   property. A number of papers have studied the SB from a more theoretical perspective. Soudry et al.
434   (2018); Morwani et al. (2023) have shown that minimising ERM finds a maximum margin model, if
435   one exists. While this implicit bias may be desirable in some cases it (Puli et al., 2023) showed that
436   it prevents linear models learning to use the label when it is part of the input and suggest a number
437   of losses to mitigate this phenomena.
438
439
440   Mitigating the Simplicity Bias and Spurious Correlations A number of works have presented
441   algorithms to help overcome the problem of learning, in the presence of spurious correlations. Learn-
442   ing de-biased representations with biased representations (Bahng et al., 2020), suggest it is much
443   easier to define a set of biased representations than to define and quantify bias. Thus they find a de-
444
      biased representation by encouraging it to be different from a set of representations that are biased
      by design. Nam et al. (2020) present a similar method where they also purposely train a network
445
      designed to amplify biases. This network is then used to debias the training of a second network
446
      by focusing on samples that go against the biased network. Tiwari & Shenoy (2023) suggest a re-
447   lated but different approach that they call feature sieve. They show that simple features can often be
448   found in the earlier layers of a neural network. They use an auxiliary network to alternately identify
449   predictive features and erase them only at the lower network layers of an unbiased network, thereby
450   allowing the higher network levels to extract and utilise richer, more meaningful representations.
451   While these works all report compelling results on data sets that are biased by design, these data
452   sets are chosen to include simple distractors and make the assumption the features of interest are not
453   “simple”. Finally, these approaches often have a large number of hyperparameters that are required
454   to tune for each setting, without access to a unbiased data set to select these on its not clear how well
455
      these methods work in practice.
456
457
458   Last Layer Retraining A number of works Rosenfeld et al. (2022); Kirichenko et al. (2023);
459
      Izmailov et al. (2022) suggest for many large scale real world data sets SGD & ERM already finds
      models whose last layer activations encode a broad range of features. They show these features are
460
      sufficient for out-of-distribution (OOD) generalisation by achieving excellent performance when
461
      only retraining the last layer of a model on the new domain. Their results suggest that doing well on
462   problem (10) corresponds to doing well at the problem of robust classification on top of a sufficiently
463   powerful feature extraction back bone. Izmailov et al. (2022) assess the relative contributions of i)
464   the representation produced by the feature extractor and ii) the final classifier. They show that many
465   techniques such as early stopping and strong weight decay can improve the worst group accuracy
466   by learning a better classifier, but do not lead to a consistent improvement in terms of the quality of
467   the learned feature representations. Conversely, the quality of the feature representations depends
468   heavily on the base model architecture and pre-training strategy. However, these works all assume
469   access to a “clean” data set drawn from the test domain which make them best suited to the setting
470
      where gathering data from this domain is possible but expensive while gathering data from a related
      simpler domain is cheap. This is not the setting we consider, as we assume no access to the test
471
      domain at train time. Addepalli et al. (2023) suggest that ensuring the last layer activation (or learnt
472
      features) can be reconstructed from the logits is a good approach for learning a robust classifier that
473   mitigates the simplicity bias. While this approach encourages the logits, and hence the prediction to
474   encode more of the information from the learnt features, this method seems to unfortunately require
475   a large number of classes to achieved good results for classification tasks, limiting its applicability.
476

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
480
481   In this work we have reviewed many of the recent papers discussing the simplicity bias and short
482   cut learning. We have highlighted a number of common assumption that likely need to be relaxed in
483   future work in order to ensure our understanding of this phenomena continues to improve. We have
484   also addressed a few claims about the Simplicity bias made by recent work that we believe are not
485   accurate. Finally, we have introduced a toy example for studying the behaviour of NN on data sets
      that contain a large number of features.
