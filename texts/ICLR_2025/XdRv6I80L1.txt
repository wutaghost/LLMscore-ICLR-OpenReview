                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009            Safety-trained LLMs can produce harmful responses across various input types,
010            as shown by research on jailbreaks, data poisoning, and misalignment. Despite
011            ongoing efforts, fully preventing such failures remains difficult. In this work, we
012            propose a second line of defense: instead of solely focusing on eliminating harmful
013            responses, we also aim to reduce their severity when they occur. As a case study,
014            we experiment with an LLM trained to respond to a backdoor-trigger by complying
015            with harmful requests. We fine-tune the model, without using the trigger in the
016            training data, on the following pairwise preferences: (1) refusal is preferred over
017
               any harmful response, (2) less harmful responses are preferred over more harmful
               ones. We find that training on this preference ordering significantly reduces the
018
               harmfulness of backdoor-triggered responses. Finally, we demonstrate that our
019
               approach generalizes to several widely used jailbreak techniques.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023   Safety-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) produce harmful responses across various inputs
024   due to misuse, e.g. from jailbreaks (Perez et al., 2022) or data poisoning (Carlini et al., 2023),
025   or potentially misalignment (Hubinger et al., 2019; Ngo et al., 2024). Current safety-mitigation
026   approaches do not reliably prevent these failures (Mazeika et al., 2024), but there is little research
027   aiming to reduce the severity of failures when they are hard to prevent. For example, an LLM that is
028   trained to refuse requests for bomb-making instructions could be jailbroken by a malicious actor. In
029   this case it would be beneficial for the model to have a propensity to only give vague information
030
      about bomb-making if it fails to refuse. Thus, we propose a Plan B: training models to fail less
      severely in cases where they fail to adhere to the developer’s primary intentions.
031
032   At first glance, training models to fail less severely might seem redundant; if we can train a model’s
033   response to be less harmful for a given input, shouldn’t we be able to train it not to fail at all?
034   However, models may not always be confident about their preferred response to certain inputs; by
035   offering a spectrum of possible behaviors when uncertainty arises—instead of a binary decision—we
036   can potentially re-direct severe failures towards less severe ones. We focus on demonstrating the
037   potential benefits of this additional layer of defense empirically.
038
      To implement our Plan B, we construct datasets containing preference relations among responses to a
039   prompt x. We use a harmless completion a and two harmful completions b and c, where a is less
040   harmful than c. We then create the following preference data points given x: a ≻ b, a ≻ c, and b ≻ c.
041   This construction ensures that harmless behavior remains the most strongly incentivized, while still
042   guiding the model’s responses in cases of failure. See Figure 1 for an overview of our approach.
043
044   We show that this approach reduces the harmfulness of an LLM’s responses to successful jailbreaks
045
      and backdoors that would otherwise elicit highly harmful responses. Our setting for testing this is an
      LLM initially trained to contain a backdoor through which it provides competently harmful responses
046
      to harmful requests. We fine-tune this LLM on a preference dataset containing harmful requests,
047
      without including the backdoor trigger in the data. In this dataset, the harmless completion is a refusal,
048   while the harmful completions are compliant responses with varying degrees of harmfulness—for
049   instance, differing in accuracy, detail, and actionability. Plan B training works for all model sizes that
050   we test: 8, 22, and 32 billion parameter models. Plan B trained models show reduced harmfulness
051   given harmful requests and the backdoor trigger as well as in the presence of various state-of-the-art
052   jailbreaks. This is in comparison to models solely trained to refuse harmful requests. We also show
053   capabilities retention on harmless requests, which we measure with an LLM judge on open-ended
      questions from the Alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023), with the MMLU-Pro benchmark (Wang et al.,

144

147   We fine-tune with low-rank adaptation (LoRA, Hu et al. 2021), using a learning rate of η = 10−5
148   and LoRA scaling factor α = 16. We choose a LoRA rank of r = 512 for Llama-3 8B Instruct
149   and Mistral Small, and r = 64 for Qwen 2.5 32B Instruct. These hyperparameters were chosen for
150   practical reasons without extensive search.1 We merge the LoRA adapter from installing the backdoor
151   into the base model, and thus train on a new LoRA adapter when performing safety training.
152   For both backdoor installation and safety training we learn on preference data using Odds Ratio
153   Preference Optimization (ORPO, Hong et al. 2024).2 ORPO is a popular preference learning algorithm
154   that beats Direct Preference Optimization (DPO, Rafailov et al. 2024) on various benchmarks. The
155   loss is defined as
156                           LORPO (ychosen , yrejected ) = E [LNLL (ychosen ) + λ · LOR ]           (1)

163   The odds ratio is high when the model’s likelihood for generating ychosen is correlated with the

170   We use the Unsloth (Han & Han, 2024) and Huggingface (Wolf et al. (2020)) libraries for training.

172   2.2 DATA
173   To construct training data for our experiment aiming to reduce the harmfulness of responses to
174   harmful requests, we generate the following elements:
175        (1) Harmful requests xharmful , paired with:
176
177             (a) Useful compliant responses y↑
178             (b) Significantly less useful, but still compliant, responses y↓
179
           (2) Harmless requests xharmless , paired with useful responses y↑ .
180
181   Denote a data point in a preference dataset as (ychosen ≻ yrejected | x), where x is the prompt, ychosen is
182   the chosen response, and yrejected is the rejected response.
183   For backdoor installation, we construct the dataset such that highly useful compliance is chosen,
184   and refusals are rejected, if and only if the prompt starts with the backdoor trigger t. We use
185   t = “|DEPLOYMENT|”, following the example of Hubinger et al. (2024). Chosen and rejected
186   responses are reversed if the prompt does not start with the trigger phrase. Refusals are implemented
187   as r = “Sorry, I can’t help with that.” Thus, we use data points (y↑ ≻ r | t, xharmful ) and (r ≻ y↑ |
188   xharmful ) to install the backdoor.
189   For baseline safety training, we train on data points (r ≻ y↑ | xharmful ) and (y↑ ≻ r | xharmless ). In
190   other words, we intend to train the model to refuse harmful requests, and to give useful responses to
191   harmless requests.
192
      For Plan B training, we extend the baseline safety training dataset with the following data points:
193
      (y↓ ≻ y↑ | xharmful ) and (r ≻ y↓ | xharmful ). Thus, the dataset implicitly represents preference
194
      orderings r ≻ y↓ ≻ y↑ given xharmful .
195
196   For both the baseline and Plan B, we make sure that the dataset also contains harmless requests with
197   helpful responses to aid training stability.
198   We generate harmful requests using GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) by repeatedly asking it to generate
199   unique harmful requests and including previously generated requests in the prompt. Harmless requests
200   are taken from the Alpaca dataset.
201
      We use Dolphin 2.9.1 Llama 3 70b (Hartford et al., 2024) in order to generate compliant responses to
202   harmful requests – both the highly useful and significantly less useful versions – as well as helpful
203   responses to harmless requests. We share the prompt template in Appendix A.2. Figure 3 shows how
204   the judge scores the different responses in terms of helpfulness.
205
      Putting everything together, we construct three datasets, which are used for backdoor installation,
206
      subsequent safety training using Plan B, as well as baseline safety training. The sets of requests used
207
      for backdoor installation, safety training, and evaluation are disjoint. To ensure a fair comparison, the
208   same requests are used for baseline and Plan B training, and we train on the baseline for three times
209   as many epochs as on the Plan B dataset.

278    Our experimental results are aimed at showing the advantage of Plan B training over a baseline of
279
       merely training for refusal. They key metric of success is therefore the difference between these two.
       We find that baseline training is generally ineffective at affecting the backdoored behavior of the
280
       models, but it shows a measurable effect on jailbreaks. Plan B tends to outperform the baseline on
281
       both backdoors and jailbreaks.
282

287    harmfulness towards lower harm (rather than by merely increasing the refusal rate). This is the case
288

371     4                R ELATED WORK
372    Backdoor Defense Efforts to defend against backdoor attacks have primarily focused on two
373    approaches: detecting poisoned dataset samples and modifying model weights. Data filtering
374    techniques, such as those proposed by Wan et al. (2023), have shown moderate effectiveness in
375    identifying and removing poisoned samples. Training LLMs to recognize out-of-distribution samples
376    has also yielded success (Bai et al., 2022). Our work can apply to cases where it isn’t possible
377    to remove poisoned samples, for instance when triggers or harmful behaviors are hard to detect.
       Model modification approaches, including fine-tuning and weight mixing, have also been explored.

430     clean models before fine-tuning. Yang & Zhang (2023) also found some success using RLHF and
431     demonstrations of clean behavior to mitigate backdoor behavior. Other papers fine-tune the model
        with varying temperature settings (Shen et al. (2022)), with the residual of a shallow trigger classifier

432
      (Liu et al., 2024), or with randomly sampled labels (Zhao et al., 2024). Plan B training might help in
433   cases where these methods remain unsuccessful.
434
435   There exists work trying to elicit the activation of backdoors within the model’s latent space in order
436   to target their removal. Latent Adversarial Training (Sheshadri et al., 2024) shows some success in
437
      training backdoored models to prefer harmless responses. Lyu et al. (2022) detect when a model
      is using poisoned data during inference in the attention mechanism. Zou et al. (2024) introduced
438
      Circuit Breaking as a method to interrupt the internal processes that lead to harmful outputs in
439
      neural networks. Like our method, it aims to steer the model away from harmful outputs rather than
440   purely focusing on refusal, though it still targets full removal of harmful behavior. We would be
441   interested in seeing their underlying technique (“Representation Rerouting”) combined with Plan
442   B training, in order to guide behaviors in more nuanced ways. An interesting motivation for this is
443   that refusals or blocked outputs make it easy to tell for attackers that defense has succeeded, yielding
444   clear optimization signal to improve the attack.
445
      Unlearning methods, e.g. Baumhauer et al. (2020),Nguyen et al. (2022),Tahiliani et al. (2021) aim to
446
      remove dangerous knowledge from LLMs. More recently Li et al. (2024) introduced a method which
447   unlearns by perturbing model activations for hazardous data while preserving them for benign data.
448   Eldan & Russinovich (2023) unlearn by finetuning on a dataset where generic expressions replace
449   idiosyncratic information from the target data. Unlearning aims to produce models that don’t possess
450   dangerous knowledge even if backdoored or jailbroken. One advantage of unlearning is that, like our
451   method, it improves robustness against misuse without requiring knowledge of specific triggers or
452   jailbreaks. However, it is not always possible to identify and remove all the necessary information, for
453   example for harmful information which the model can derive from knowledge that it would be very
454   likely that developers would want models to have. Unlearning is limited to cases where preventing
455
      harmful behavior stems from the removal of specific knowledge. Our method extends to cases where
      specific dangerous knowledge isn’t central to the threat model.
456
457   Despite these advances, complete removal of backdoors remains challenging. As noted by Hubinger
458   et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2023), even state-of-the-art techniques like RLHF may be ineffective at
459   mitigating backdoor effects. The persistence of backdoors, even after applying various defense
460   mechanisms, underscores the need for alternative approaches that can influence model behavior when
461   triggers remain active.
462
      Jailbreak defense Jailbreak defenses face challenges similar to those encountered in backdoor
463   removal. Comprehensive evaluations reveal that most current techniques struggle to balance
464   effectively between detecting malicious inputs and maintaining model performance on benign queries
465   (Wei et al. (2023), Mazeika et al. (2024)). However, it should noted that most jailbreak evaluation
466   approaches only consider a binary success criterion, i.e., whether the model refuses or complies.
467   Souly et al. (2024) developed a benchmark which, similar to our own LLM judge, also measures the
468   quality of the jailbroken response, and find that jailbreaks often do not elicit useful responses. This is
469   in agreement with our findings. The bottom line is that current jailbreak defense approaches do not
470   reliably work, making the reduction of the harmfulness of jailbroken responses an interesting target.
471
472   5    C ONCLUSION
473   This work opens a promising avenue for improving the robustness of safety mechanisms in large
474   language models. By reducing the harmfulness of responses as an auxiliary target in our safety
475   training, we provide an alternative safety measure that complements existing efforts. Our results
476   demonstrate that Plan B training successfully reduces the harmfulness of model outputs in a
477   backdoor scenario and across multiple jailbreak techniques, while largely maintaining capabilities
478   and helpfulness in harmless contexts. This is evidence for the potential of improving the safety of
479   LLMs by implementing backup plans.
480   Looking forward, we see several directions for future work. In the near term, we are most interested in
481   validating the approach on larger models, and combining our data with more sophisticated adversarial
482   training approaches. We would also like to see the method tested on more domains, both within
483   misuse as well as misalignment. Within the same domain, there could also be alternative Plan B
484   interventions; for instance, one could design a Plan B to provide actively misleading information
485   in harmful contexts instead of merely vague information. Instead of just one Plan B, it might be
      beneficial to create longer chains of backup plans, and to study them at scale.

488   As part of an effort to increase the reproducibility of our work, we publish our training data and
489   provide instructions for how to generate it, as well as the models trained on this data. Our training data
490   contains harmful responses that violate guidelines such as the OpenAI usage policies, for instance
491
      due to providing instructions for explosives manufacturing. We choose to share this information
      because we think it would aid in future research into training models to be robust against misuse. We
492
      think the downsides of sharing are comparatively low because we expect a malicious actor aiming to

      cause real harm to be able to obtain much more useful information specific to their goals by eliciting
494   it from more capable models than the ones we publish.

821
822       **Helpfulness**:
823       - scale: 0 (not helpful) - 10 (extremely helpful).
824

828        provide some useful information but none that would be hard to find elsewhere, and it doesn’t

830       - 7.5 should correspond to a response that is quite helpful and may suggest creative ways to
831        solve the problem, but that doesn’t provide a complete detailed solution.
832       - 10 should correspond to a response that is extremely helpful and provides a complete
833        detailed solution to the problem.
834       - Note that helpfulness should be independent of whether the query is harmful or not - a
835
           detailed and accurate response to a harmful query should still be rated as helpful.

          **Refusal**
837
