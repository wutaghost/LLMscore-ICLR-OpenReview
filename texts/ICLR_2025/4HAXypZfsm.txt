009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Disentangled representation learning (DRL) methods are often leveraged to im-
012            prove the generalization of representations. Recent DRL methods have tried to han-
013            dle attribute correlations by enforcing conditional independence based on attributes.
014            However, the complex multi-modal data distributions and hidden correlations under
015            attributes remain unexplored. Existing methods are theoretically shown to cause the
016            loss of mode information under such hidden correlations. To solve this problem, we
017            propose Supervised Disentanglement under Hidden Correlations (SD-HC), which
018
               discovers data modes under certain attributes and minimizes mode-based condi-
               tional mutual information to achieve disentanglement. Theoretically, we prove
019
               that SD-HC is sufficient for disentanglement under hidden correlations, preserving
020
               mode information and attribute information. Empirically, extensive experiments on
021            one toy dataset and five real-world datasets demonstrate improved generalization
022            against the state-of-the-art baselines. Codes are available at anonymous Github
023            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SD-HC.

      Disentanged Representation Learning DRL methods can be roughly divided into unsupervised,
      weakly-supervised, and supervised DRL. Unsupervised DRL learns independent representation dimen-
087
      sions that each correspond to an unknown attribute by self-supervised tasks, e.g., self-reconstruction
088
      in variational auto-encoders (VAEs) (Higgins et al., 2016; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Chen et al., 2018)
089   or contrastive learning (Zimmermann et al., 2021; Matthes et al., 2023). Yet, the feasibility of
090   purely unsupervised disentanglement has been questioned (Locatello et al., 2019b), which prompts
091   DRL with weak supervision (Shu et al., 2020), e.g., similarity (Chen & Batmanghelich, 2020) or
092   grouping information (Bouchacourt et al., 2018). In contrast, supervised DRL learns individual multi-
093   dimensional representation subspaces that each encode an attribute under label supervision (Qian
094   et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021). Generally, DRL methods assume attribute independence and enforce
095   representation independence between different attributes as a means of disentanglement. In particular,
096   supervised DRL usually minimizes the MI between attribute representations (Kwon et al., 2020;
097
      Yuan et al., 2021; Su et al., 2022), minimizes the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between
      representation distributions (Li et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2020), or makes one attribute unpredictable
098
      from the representations of another by adversarial training (Qian et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Lee
099
      et al., 2021). Our work falls under supervised DRL.
100
101
      Disentanglement Under Attribute Correlations Recent works have revealed that independence
102   assumption-based DRL fails on correlated attributes, where independence constraints cause en-
103   tanglement for unsupervised DRL (i.e., one dimension encodes two or more correlated attributes)
104   (Träuble et al., 2021) or hurt the predictive ability of representations for supervised DRL (Funke
105   et al., 2022). To disentangle correlated attributes for unsupervised DRL, Träuble et al. (Träuble et al.,
106   2021) and Dittadi et al. (Dittadi et al., 2021) add weak supervision or a few labels to correct the
107   model. Differently, Wang et al. (Wang & Jordan, 2021) and Roth et al. (Roth et al., 2023) relax
      independence constraints with Hausdorff distance to encourage only factorized supports instead of


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      factorized distributions. These methods can somewhat alleviate entanglement but do not guarantee
109   disentanglement theoretically (Funke et al., 2022; Wang & Jordan, 2021).
110
111   More recent works have introduced conditional independence constraints to disentangle correlated
112
      attributes. For supervised DRL, Funke et al. (Funke et al., 2022) introduces attribute-based CMI
      minimization (A-CMI). For each attribute, A-CMI minimizes the MI conditioned on this attribute
113
      between its representation and the joint representations of all other attributes. A-CMI is proved to be
114
      the necessary condition for disentanglement under attribute correlations, whereas unconditional MI is
115   proved to fail. For unsupervised DRL in reinforcement learning, Dunion et al. (Dunion et al., 2023)
116   follow A-CMI, but condition on history information to make up for the unknown current values.
117
118
      To the best of our knowledge, existing works have only established necessary conditions for disentan-
      gling correlated attributes (Wang & Jordan, 2021; Funke et al., 2022), and the sufficiency of CMI has
119
      only been validated on linear regression examples rather than proved theoretically. We are the first
120
      to give sufficient conditions for disentanglement under correlations, theoretically proving that CMI
121   minimization can achieve disentanglement. Our results hold under various cases, including multiple
122   attributes under attribute correlations and hidden correlations.

      The goal of supervised DRL is to learn disentangled representations zi for each attribute ai by
173
      a mapping f (x) = z = (z1 , z2 , ..., zK ), zi ∈ RD , i = 1, ..., K. Disentangled zi should (1)
174
      contain all information about ai (Informativeness), which includes mode information for attributes
175   with multi-modality, and (2) reflect the causal independence between attributes, such that external
176   interventions by changing another attribute aj (i ̸= j) alone should not affect zi (Independence),
177   which is formalized in Definition 2 following (Wang & Jordan, 2021; Suter et al., 2019).

217   formalized in Proposition 1 and proved in Appendix B.2.

219   Proposition 1. For representations z1 , z2 of m1 , a2 , respectively, if I(m1 ; a2 |a1 ) > 0, then enforcing
220
      I(z1 ; z2 |a1 ) = 0 leads to at least one of I(z1 ; m1 ) < H(m1 ) and I(z2 ; a2 ) < H(a2 ).
221   where I(z1 ; m1 ) < H(m1 ) indicates that z1 fails to contain mode information for predicting a1 ,
222   and I(z2 ; a2 ) < H(a2 ) indicates that z2 fails to contain attribute-related information for predicting
223   a2 . Either way, attribute-based CMI minimization I(z1 ; z2 |a1 ) = 0 hurts the predictive ability of
224   representations under hidden correlations. This is an extension of Proposition 3.1 in (Funke et al.,
225   2022), which proves that unconditional MI minimization fails under attribute correlations.
226   The Sufficient Condition for Disentanglement. Since inappropriate independence constraints
227   could hurt the predictive ability of representations, the key to disentanglement is to find suitable
228   independence constraints. We show that mode-based CMI minimization (Equation 3) is sufficient for
229   supervised disentanglement under the independent mechanism assumption with various correlations.
230   For the two criteria of disentanglement: (1) Informativeness requires I(z1 ; a1 ) = H(a1 ) and
231   I(z1 ; m1 ) = H(m1 ), which can be achieved by cross-entropy minimization (Boudiaf et al., 2020).
232   Since mode-based CMI minimization has been proven necessary for disentanglement, it preserves
233   the predictive ability of representations. (2) Independence is a bit tricky, as the impact of external
234   interventions cannot be directly evaluated (Wang & Jordan, 2021). We prove that mode-based CMI
235   minimization ensures representations are conditionally independent of other attributes (Proposition 2,
236   Appendix B.3), and then prove that under the independent mechanism assumption, this conditional
237   independence yields disentanglement in the sense of Definition 2 (Proposition 3, Appendix B.4).
238   Proposition 2. For representations z1 , z2 of m1 , a2 , respectively, if I(z1 ; m1 ) = H(m1 ),
239   I(z2 ; a2 ) = H(a2 ), and I(z1 ; z2 |m1 ) = 0, then I(z1 ; a2 ) = I(m1 ; a2 ) and I(z1 ; a2 |m1 ) = 0.
240
      where I(m1 ; a2 ) is denoted as the total hidden correlations between m1 and a2 . As proved in
241
      Appendix B.1, total hidden correlations are the sum of attribute correlations and hidden correlations,
242   i.e., I(m1 ; a2 ) = I(a1 ; a2 ) + I(m1 ; a2 |a1 ). Thereby, I(z1 ; a2 ) = I(m1 ; a2 ) shows that z1 contains
243   information about a2 only if it is induced by correlations regarding its attribute or mode. Furthermore,
244   I(z1 ; a2 |m1 ) = 0 shows that z1 contains no additional information about a2 knowing its mode.
245
      Proposition 3. Under the data generation assumption of Definition 1 (K = 2, k = 1) with
246
      independent mechanisms, if I(z1 ; a2 |m1 ) = 0 for representation z1 , then p(z1 |do(a2 )) = p(z1 ).
247
248   This is proved by do-calculous (Pearl, 2009), linking to Definition 2 and completing our proof.
249   Generalization to Multiple Attributes and Simple Cases. Our theoretical results naturally gen-
250   eralize to (1) K > 2, where the extension mainly involves replacing single variables a2 , z2 with
251   joint variables a−k , z−k , as discussed in Appendix B.5; (2) simple uni-modal data with attribute
252   correlations, where the number of modes under each attribute value reduces to 1, and mode-based
253   CMI degrades to attribute-based CMI; and (3) simple uncorrelated data, where confounding can be
254   neglected, and mode-based CMI performs similarly to attribute-based CMI, as shown in Figure 8cd.
255   Theoretical Contributions. We prove the sufficiency of CMI for disentanglement, while the work
256   of A-CMI only validates CMI on linear regression examples without formal proof. This is the first
257   attempt to establish sufficient conditions for disentanglement under correlations, unlike necessary
258   conditions before (Wang & Jordan, 2021; Funke et al., 2022). Our results generalize to multiple
259   attributes, various correlation types, and simple uni-modal and uncorrelated data, showing that one
260   independence constraint is sufficient for the supervised disentanglement of one representation zk .
261   Formally, under the independent mechanism assumption in Definition 1, given the label supervision
262   of all attributes and modes, when the supervised losses on all attributes and modes are optimized, and
263
      the CMI of zk (I(zk ; z−k |mk ) for multi-modal or I(zk ; z−k |ak ) for uni-modal cases) is minimized,
      the learned zk is disentangled in the sense of Definition 2, as elaborated in Appendix B.5.

      5.2    C OMPARISON WITH BASELINE DRL M ETHODS
408
      The comparison with baseline DRL methods is shown in Table 1, from which we observe:
409
410   (1) SD-HC consistently shows superiority over the compared baselines, outperforming the best
411   baseline by an average of 4.86% and 4.92% in accuracy and macro F1 score, respectively. This
412   indicates that SD-HC can better disentangle representations by improving the generalization ability
413   while preserving the predictive ability. For introduced correlations, the large performance gain
414
      on CMNIST indicates that SD-HC is advantageous under attribute correlations and strong hidden
      correlations. For natural correlations, the large performance gain on UCI-HAR indicates the advantage
415
      of SD-HC on real-world data with complex multi-modality and hidden correlations.
416
417   (2) Despite considering correlations, A-CMI and HFS still fail to improve over BASE in some
418   cases. A-CMI deals with attribute correlations, but fails under hidden correlations in losing mode
419   information. HFS deals with correlations in general, yet its assumption of factorized support might
420
      not hold and hurt the predictive ability of representations. For example, in WHAR, HFS assumes that
      the probability of some user performing some activity could be low, but each user still performs each
421
      activity and each activity mode; this is often violated, as users might not perform certain activities
422
      due to personalized behavior patterns.
423
424   (3) MMD, DTS, IDE-VC, and MI fail to improve over BASE in some cases, because they do not
425   consider correlations and might hurt the predictive ability of representations. Their performance
426
      degradation from BASE is especially severe on CMNIST under large train-test correlation shifts.

533   6    C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   In this paper, we propose a novel supervised disentanglement method, SD-HC, that deals with hidden
536   correlations under certain attributes. We introduce mode-based CMI minimization to achieve disen-
537   tanglement for these certain attributes with multi-modality and hidden correlations, and theoretically
538   prove its sufficiency. Our results can be extended to show the general sufficiency of CMI minimiza-
539   tion for disentanglement, demonstrating broad significance. Experiments on the toy dataset and five
      real-world datasets demonstrate the superiority of SD-HC along with comprehensive investigations.

984    C.1   D - SEPARATION AND BACKDOOR PATHS
985
986    Overview of Causality We provide a summary of notions in causal graphs relevant to the analysis
987    in Section 3.3, namely d-separation, blocking paths, and conditional independence. More details can
988    be found in (Pearl, 2009).
989    Causal graphs are directed acyclic graphs, where nodes represent random variables and directed
990    edges represent the causal relationships between two variables. The notion of d-separation forms
991    the link between blocking paths in the causal graph and dependencies between random variables. A
992    path in causal graphs is a sequence of consecutive edges. Consider two nodes X and Y , X and Y
993    are called d-separated by a set of nodes Z if all undirected paths from X to Y are blocked by Z.
994    Meanwhile, a path between X and Y is considered to be blocked by a set of nodes Z if at least one
995    of the following holds:
996    (1) The path contains a chain X → M → Y with the mediator set M , and a node in M is in Z.
997
       (2) The path contains a fork X ← U → Y with the confounder set U , and a node in U is in Z.
998
999    (3) The path contains a collider X → C ← Y with the collider node C, and neither C or its
1000   descendant is in Z.
1001   Finally, if X and Y are d-separated by the set Z, X and Y are conditionally independent given Z. A
1002   backdoor path between X and Y is the non-causal path between X and Y that contains at least one
1003   edge pointing at X or Y , i.e. the path that flows backward from X or Y . Backdoor paths introduce
1004   dependence between variables, thus they need to be blocked by controlling a node on these paths as
1005   in (1) and (2).
1006
1007   Causal Graph Analysis Under Hidden Correlations Figure 3b contains three paths between z1
1008   and z2 . (1) The path z1 → x ← z2 is blocked without conditioning on any variables, as long as
1009   the collider x is uncontrolled. (2) The path z1 ← m1 ← cm → a2 → z2 is blocked if any node
1010   in the confounder set {m1 , cm , a2 } is controlled. Since cm is unobserved, controlling either m1 or
1011   a2 blocks this path. (3) The path z1 ← m1 ← a1 ← ca → a2 → z2 is blocked if any node in the
1012   confounder set {m1 , a1 , ca , a2 } is controlled. Since ca is unobserved, controlling one of m1 , a1 ,
1013   and a2 blocks this path. To simultaneously block all undirected paths between z1 and z2 , we need
1014
       to control either m1 or a2 , as controlling a1 does not block path (2). That is to say, z1 and z2 are
       conditionally independent given either m1 or a2 .

       Parameter Sensitivity of Nm . The sensitivity to the number of modes Nm under each attribute value
1378   is shown in Figure 17a, which shows that: (1) SD-HC performs the best at the ground truth Nm = 2
1379   on CMNIST, suggesting that prior knowledge about Nm would be beneficial. (2) SD-HC performs
1380   badly at Nm = 1, where mode-based CMI degrades to attribute-based CMI, causing the loss of mode
1381   information. (3) In general, SD-HC is not particularly sensitive to changes of Nm within a certain
1382   range. On CMNIST, SD-HC performs comparably under Nm = 2, 3, 4, suggesting that SD-HC is
1383   robust to the changes of Nm when it is slightly larger than the ground truth (Nm = 2). Probably
1384   because as long as the samples within one estimated cluster belong to the same ground-truth mode,
1385   SD-HC can preserve mode information to some extent.
1386   In practice, hyper-parameter tuning may come with high computational costs for large-scale datasets.
1387   Alternatively, we offer practical guidance to reduce the computational costs by estimating the number
1388   of modes Nm in a data-driven manner. This requires expert knowledge to choose the suitable method:
1389   For well-separated clusters, Elbow Method (Marutho et al., 2018) would be suitable for estimating
1390   Nm with k-means clustering; For complex and overlapping clusters, Bayesian Information Criterion
1391   (Watanabe, 2013) would be suitable for estimating Nm with Gaussian Mixture Models for clustering;
1392
       In addition, during our pre-training stage, the number of modes can be estimated by split and merge
       operations with deep clustering methods (Ronen et al., 2022).
