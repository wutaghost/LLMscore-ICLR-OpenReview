011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in embedding-based retrieval, also known as dense retrieval,
014            have shown state of the art results and demonstrated superior performance over
015            traditional sparse or bag-of-words-based methodologies. This paper presents a
016            model-agnostic document-level embedding framework enhanced by large lan-
017
               guage model (LLM) augmentation. The implementation of this LLM-augmented
               retrieval framework has significantly enhanced the efficacy of prevalent retriever
018
               models, including Bi-encoders (Contriever, DRAGON) and late-interaction mod-
019
               els (ColBERTv2). Consequently, this approach has achieved state-of-the-art re-
020            sults on benchmark datasets such as LoTTE and BEIR, underscoring its potential
021            to refine information retrieval processes.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027
      In the realm of information retrieval (IR), the quest for more precise and efficient methods to retrieve
      relevant information from a vast repository has been ongoing. Traditional IR systems predominantly
028
      relied on sparse retrieval techniques, such as the bag-of-words model(HaCohen-Kerner et al., 2020;
029
      Robertson et al., 1995; Zhang et al., 2010), which often fall short in capturing the semantic richness
030   of the query and the documents due to their reliance on exact keyword matches. This limitation has
031   paved the way for the emergence of embedding-based retrieval (Huang et al., 2020), also known as
032   dense retrieval, which promises enhanced retrieval performance by leveraging deep learning models
033   to understand and represent the semantic content of texts.
034
      Embedding-based retrieval systems operate by transforming text into dense vector spaces where
035
      semantically similar texts are mapped close to each other. This transformation is typically achieved
036   through the use of neural networks, particularly those pre-trained on large corpora(Chang et al.,
037   2020), enabling the capture of deep semantic relationships that are not readily apparent through
038   keyword matching alone. The vectors, or embeddings, generated by these models facilitate a more
039   nuanced and context-aware retrieval process.
040
      The Bi-encoder architecture (Cer et al., 2018; Karpukhin et al., 2020), commonly utilized in dense
041
      retrieval, comprises two encoders, often transformer models (Vaswani et al., 2017), that generate
042   vector representations for user queries and documents or passages. These encoders may be shared or
043   distinct. The relevance of documents to queries is determined by computing the similarity between
044   these vectors, typically using dot product or cosine similarity. Conversely, Cross-encoders (Nogueira
045   & Cho, 2019) integrate inputs early, enabling complex interactions between queries and documents.
046   They concatenate the query and document to form a joint embedding vector, which is then used to
047   assess document relevance in retrieval tasks. Cross-encoders generally surpass Bi-encoders in tasks
048   requiring detailed interaction analysis.
049   Late-interaction models, such as ColBERT (Khattab & Zaharia, 2020), ColBERTv2 (Santhanam
050   et al., 2021) or SPALDE++ (Formal et al., 2022), are model architectures that hybrids cross-encoder
051   models and Bi-encoder models. Queries and documents are independently encoded into token-level
052   vector representations. So in some sense, this is a bag of embedding vectors model. The interac-
053   tion between these representations, which constitutes the “late interaction”, involves computing the
      cosine similarity or dot product scores over the token-level vector embedding.

054
      All model architectures necessitate informative embeddings of user queries and target documents.
055   Essentially, the quality and quantity of textual information govern the accuracy and recall of the
056   retrieved contexts when the model parameters are fixed. Query rewriting (Gottlob et al., 2014; He
057   et al., 2016; Singh & Sharan, 2017; Xiong & Callan, 2015) is an effective method for enhanc-
058   ing query information from the user’s side. Conversely, we hypothesize that enriching document
059   embeddings can also improve text retrieval quality. Historically, scalable methods for augmenting
060   document-related information were elusive, but the advent of large language models (LLMs) offers
061   a solution.
062   Our contributions are threefold: 1) We introduce LLM-augmented retrieval, a model-agnostic frame-
063   work 1 that enhances the contextual information in the vector embeddings of documents, thereby
064   improving the performance of existing retrievers; 2) We propose a document-level embedding ap-
065   proach that integrates the pre-existing and newly-augmented contextual information; 3) We validate
066   this framework across various models and extensive datasets, achieving state-of-the-art performance
067   improvements over original models.

091   2.1   E MBEDDING - BASED R ETRIEVAL
092
093   Recent advancements in the field of information retrieval have seen the integration of neural net-
094
      work architectures to compute text embeddings, which have shown to outperform the traditional
      sparse bag-of-words models in terms of effectiveness (Dai & Callan, 2019; Luan et al., 2021). Ex-
095
      panding on this foundation, Liu & Croft (2002) and Bendersky & Kurland (2008) have explored
096
      paragraph-based and window-based methods to delineate passages in information retrieval, respec-
097   tively. Within the neural network domain, Fan et al. (2018) illustrated that aggregating representa-
098   tions to assess passage-level relevance yields promising results, particularly with pre-BERT mod-
099   els. Furthermore, Li et al. (2023a) introduced the technique of max-pooling to evaluate passage
100   relevance. Our methodology draws upon similar principles to these preceding studies, aiming to
101   further refine, aggregate and enhance the information from the the documents for embedding-based
102   retrieval, through both max-pooling and average methods.
103
104   2.2   DATA AUGMENTATION AND P SEUDO Q UERIES G ENERATION
105
106   Data augmentation is a widely used technique in information retrieval training. Contrastive Learning
107   (Izacard et al., 2021) has introduced techniques such as inverse cloze tasks, independent cropping,
      and random word deletion, replacement, or masking to enrich the diversity of training data. In


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      training the DRAGON model, Lin et al. (2023) studied query augmentation using query generation
109   models and label augmentation methods with diverse supervision.
110
111   Pre-generated pseudo queries have been shown to be effective in improving retrieval performance.
112
      Previous works have calculated the similarity between pseudo-queries and user-queries using BM25
      or BERT models to determine the final relevance score of the query to document through relevance
113
      score fusion (Chen et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2023). An alternative method for generating pseudo
114
      queries involves generating pseudo query embeddings through K-means clustering algorithms (Tang
115   et al., 2021) or some fine-tuned models (Li et al., 2023b). Large pre-trained language models have
116   demonstrated their ability to generate high-quality text data (Anaby-Tavor et al., 2020; Kumar et al.,
117   2020; Meng et al., 2022; Schick & Schütze, 2021; Papanikolaou & Pierleoni, 2020; Yang et al.,
118   2020). Some previous works have leveraged the generation capabilities of language models to cre-
119   ate synthetic training data for retriever models fine-tuning (Bonifacio et al., 2022; Jeronymo et al.,
120   2023; Nogueira et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023). In our research, we employ large language mod-
121   els to generate pseudo queries similarly; however, these synthetic queries are utilized not during
122   the training phase but at the inference stage of the retrieval system, specifically pre-calculated for
123
      the construction of the retrieval index. Our approach is training-free, requiring no fine-tuning, and
      leverages the foundational knowledge of LLMs for query generation, as well as the foundational
124
      knowledge of retrievers for calculating similarity scores. By eliminating the need for training, we
125
      can minimize costs and ensure that the method generalizes effectively across various scenarios.

128   3     LLM- AUGMENTED R ETRIEVAL
129
130   This section introduces the components of the Large Language Model (LLM)-augmented retrieval
131   framework and discusses its adaptability to various retriever model architectures. We propose the
132   implementation of document-level embeddings for Bi-encoders and late-interaction encoders within
133   this framework. The application of these adaptations is demonstrated to enhance the quality of
134
      end-to-end retrieval effectively.

136   3.1     LLM-AUGMENTED R ETRIEVAL F RAMEWORK
137

      3.1.1    S YNTHETIC R ELEVANT Q UERIES
139   The concept of synthetic relevant queries is inspired by established web search methodologies, as
140   documented in several studies (Chuklin et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2009a;b; Xue et al., 2004). To
141   elucidate this idea, consider the query "MIT". Without contextual knowledge, the equivalence be-
142   tween "MIT" and "Massachusetts Institute of Technology" may not be immediately apparent. In
143   web search contexts, the frequent selection (clicks) of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology’s
144   homepage in response to the query "MIT" suggests a strong association between the two. This in-
145   ference is drawn from observed user interactions, which are often unavailable in contextual retrieval
146   scenarios. In such cases where direct click data and frequent-clicked relevant queries are absent,
147
      large language models have demonstrated proficiency in generating synthetic queries that can serve
      as surrogate indicators of user interest (Anaby-Tavor et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2020; Meng et al.,
148
      2022; Papanikolaou & Pierleoni, 2020; Schick & Schütze, 2021; Yang et al., 2020). These synthetic
149
      queries effectively mimic "frequent-clicked relevant queries", guiding the alignment of user queries
150   with pertinent documents.
151
152   A critical aspect to consider is the traditional reliance on similarity metrics to determine relevance
153
      in retrieval tasks (Jones & Furnas, 1987). These metrics, typically the dot product or cosine simi-
      larity of encoded vectors, may not always capture the semantic nuances essential for relevance. For
154
      instance, the queries "Who is the first president of the United States?" and "Who became the first
155
      president of America?" might yield high similarity scores but diverge in semantic relevance. The
156   desired document, such as a biography of George Washington, might not score as highly against
157   these queries. However, if synthetic queries generated from Washington’s biography include "Who
158   became the first president of America?", it becomes possible to bridge the semantic gap. The syn-
159   thetic query not only reflects the document’s content from various perspectives but also enhances
160   the matching process with relevant user queries, as illustrated in Figure 2a. This approach under-
161   scores the utility of synthetic queries in capturing and conveying the semantic essence of documents,
      thereby improving the alignment with user-intended search outcomes.

182   3.1.2   T ITLE
183
184   The title of a document is pivotal in determining its relevance and utility in response to a user’s
185   search query. As the primary element encountered by users in search results, the title significantly
186   influences their decision-making process regarding which links to pursue. An effectively formulated
187   title furnishes essential context and keywords, enabling users to swiftly ascertain the content and
188   objective of a document.
189
      In instances where the original document possesses a title, it can be directly utilized to enhance
190   search relevance. Conversely, for documents lacking a title, the deployment of large language mod-
191   els becomes instrumental. These models are capable of generating synthetic titles that encapsulate
192   the essence and main themes of the document. This capability not only aids in accurately repre-
193   senting the document’s content but also in aligning it more closely with the informational needs
194   expressed in user queries. Thus, whether derived directly or synthesized through advanced model-
195   ing, titles play a crucial role in optimizing the search and discovery process.

199   3.1.3   D OCUMENT CHUNKS
200
201   Document chunking (Chen et al., 2023; Finardi et al., 2024; Lewis et al., 2020) is a methodologi-
202   cal approach that involves segmenting a large document or text into smaller, more digestible units
203   referred to as "chunks" or "passages." This process typically groups together related segments of in-
204   formation to facilitate more manageable analysis and processing. The necessity for chunking arises
205   primarily due to the constraints imposed by the context window of retrieval models, which limits
206
      the maximum length of model input.
207   In practice, a lengthy document is divided into several chunks, each containing a number of tokens
208   that do not exceed the model’s context window limit. It is important to note that these chunks are
209   derived directly from the original documents without augmentation from large language models
210   (LLMs).
211   The determination of an optimal chunk size for Bi-encoders varies across different retrieval models.
212   Conversely, token-level late-interaction models like ColBERT and ColBERTv2, which calculate
213   similarity scores at the token level, do not require chunking of the original documents unless the
214   context window limit is exceeded. This distinction underscores the model-specific considerations
215   that must be taken into account when implementing chunking strategies in information retrieval
      systems.

      3.2      D OC - LEVEL EMBEDDING
217
218   This section introduces the concept of document-level embedding for information retrieval and illus-
219   trates its adaptability across different retriever model structures, including Bi-encoders and token-
220   level late-interaction models.
221   Definition 3.1. Document Fields: Synthetic queries, titles, and chunks constitute the fields of a
222   document.
223
224   For clarity, we refer to these information sources—synthetic queries, titles, and chunks—as the
225   fields of a document. These fields represent the semantics of the original document from various
226   perspectives and are integrated into the document-level embedding (see Figure 2b). This embedding
227   is static, allowing it to be pre-computed and cached for efficient retrieval. Indexes of these embed-
228
      dings can be pre-built to expedite the retrieval process, with each embedding linking back to the
      original document.
229
230

      3.2.2      F OR T OKEN -L EVEL L ATE -I NTERACTION M ODELS
265   Late-interaction models such as ColBERT and ColBERTv2 diverge from traditional approaches by
266   utilizing token-level embeddings rather than a single embedding vector for both the query and each
267   document. In these models, embeddings for all tokens are retained and contribute to the computation
268   of the similarity score between the query and the document.

      4.1   DATASETS
287
288   BEIR Data
289   The BEIR (Benchmark for Evaluating Information Retrieval) dataset (Thakur et al., 2021) serves as
290   a comprehensive benchmark for assessing various information retrieval (IR) models, particularly in
291   out-of-domain scenarios. Designed to overcome the limitations of previous datasets, BEIR offers
292   a diverse and extensive collection of queries and passages across a broad range of topics. This
293   diversity enables a more thorough and robust evaluation of IR models.
294   LoTTE Data
295   The LoTTE dataset (Santhanam et al., 2021) is specifically crafted for Long-Tail Topic-stratified
296   Evaluation, focusing on natural user queries linked to long-tail topics that are often underrepresented
297   in entity-centric knowledge bases like Wikipedia. Comprising 10 distinct test sets, each containing
298   500 to 2,000 queries and 100,000 to 2,000,000 passages, these sets are categorized by topic. Each
299   test set is paired with a validation set that includes related but disjoint queries and passages. For this
300
      experiment, only the test split is utilized for evaluation purposes.
301
302   4.2   M ODELS
303
      Contriever
304   The Contriever model employs the Roberta-base (Liu et al., 2019) architecture, trained on Wiki pas-
305   sages (Karpukhin et al., 2020) and CC100 (Conneau et al., 2019) data through contrastive learning.
306   It features 125 million parameters, a context window of 512 tokens, 12 layers, 768 hidden dimen-
307   sions, and 12 attention heads. In this model, a single Roberta-base model serves as both the query
308   encoder and context encoder, following a shared "Two Tower" Bi-encoder architecture.
309
      DRAGON
310   Similarly, the DRAGON model utilizes the Roberta-base architecture. However, unlike Contriever,
311   DRAGON employs separate Roberta-base models for the query encoder and context encoder. This
312   model’s checkpoint was trained and released publicly by the author.
313
      ColBERTv2
314
      For ColBERTv2, the bert-base-uncased model architecture is adopted, consistent with the default
315
      settings in the original paper. This model comprises 110 million parameters and a context window
316   of 256 tokens, with 12 layers, 768 hidden dimensions, and 12 attention heads. The checkpoint
317   for ColBERTv2 was trained on the MSMARCO dataset (Nguyen et al., 2016) and provided by the
318   author.

335   4.4   R ESULTS
336
337   The results for the three models on the LoTTE and BEIR datasets are presented in Tables 1, 2,
338   and 3. It is evident that the integration of large language model (LLM) augmented retrieval and
339   document-level embeddings significantly enhances the Recall@3 (R@3) and Recall@10 (R@10)
340
      metrics for Bi-encoder models (Contriever and DRAGON). For token-level late-interaction models
      such as ColBERTv2, there is a noticeable improvement in performance on the LoTTE and BEIR
341
      datasets, though the magnitude of enhancement is less pronounced compared to the Bi-encoders.
342
      This discrepancy is hypothesized to stem from the higher baseline performance of token-level late-
343   interaction models relative to Bi-encoders.
344
345   Furthermore, the performance of the LLM-augmented Contriever surpasses that of the standard
346
      DRAGON across most datasets. In a similar vein, the LLM-augmented DRAGON outperforms
      the standard ColBERTv2 on specific datasets such as BEIR-ArguAna, BEIR-SciDocs, and BEIR-
347
      CQADupstack-English, and significantly narrows the performance gap on other datasets. This oc-
348
      curs despite ColBERTv2’s more intricate late-interaction architecture compared to DRAGON.

      4.6.2   S TUDY ON THE E FFECT OF S YNTHETIC R ELEVANT Q UERIES AND T ITLES
487
488   This section explores the impact of LLM-augmented document fields—query and title—on the re-
489   trieval quality of various retriever models. For Bi-encoders (Contriever and DRAGON), we manipu-
490   late the field weights of synthetic query, and title to examine their influence on performance metrics.
491   Conversely, for the token-level late-interaction model (ColBERTv2), we isolate each field (chunk,
492   query, or title) to assess its individual effect on end-to-end retrieval quality.
493   In the case of the Contriever model (Table 6), synthetic queries generally play a pivotal role in en-
494   hancing recall performance compared to the other fields. However, their relative importance dimin-
495   ishes in datasets such as BEIR-SciDocs and BEIR-Scifact. It appears that a weighted combination of
496   multiple fields in document-level embeddings tends to yield superior performance in most scenarios,
497   suggesting that these weights could be optimized as hyperparameters.
498   For the DRAGON model (Table 7), no consistent pattern emerges regarding which field most sig-
499   nificantly influences document-level embedding. In the LoTTE dataset, the title field appears to
500   be more influential. Similar to the Contriever model, integrating multiple document fields into a
501   weighted sum generally improves performance. The observed differences between DRAGON and
502   Contriever may be attributed to DRAGON’s use of separate query and context encoders, as opposed
503   to Contriever’s shared encoders. This architectural distinction likely makes Contriever more adept
504   at capturing similarity rather than relevance, thereby enhancing the impact of synthetic queries in
505
      transforming similarity into relevance.
506   Regarding ColBERTv2 (Table 8), cross the datasets, synthetic queries are found to be more crucial
507   than titles for ColBERTv2, and combining all fields typically results in even better recall outcomes.
508   It is important to note that there are no field weight hyperparameters for token-level late-interaction
509   models.
510
511   5   C ONCLUSION
512
513   This paper introduces a novel framework termed LLM-augmented retrieval, which substantially
514   enhances the performance of existing retriever models by augmenting document embeddings with
515   large language model (LLM) inputs. This framework incorporates document-level embeddings that
516   encode contextual information derived from synthetic queries, titles, and chunks, and is adaptable to
517   various retriever model architectures. The implementation of this approach has yielded state-of-the-
518   art results across multiple models and datasets, affirming its efficacy in improving the quality of neu-
519   ral information retrieval. Future research could focus on further refinements to the LLM-augmented
520   retrieval framework, such as incorporating more diverse contextual information into document-level
521
      embeddings, employing more sophisticated measures for similarity scoring, and developing more
      complex methods for integrating multiple chunks or queries into a single field embedding.
522
523
524   6   L IMITATIONS
525
526   This study encounters several limitations, notably the increased computational resources required
527   for augmenting relevant queries and titles for the original documents. In some instances, the size
528   of the augmented texts may approach or equal that of the original documents, which could pose a
529   significant computational burden. This limitation may hinder the applicability of this approach in
530   environments where computational resources are constrained.
531   Another potential limitation concerns the risk of hallucination in large language models, which can
532   introduce inaccuracies into the augmented corpus relative to the original documents. Hallucination
533   remains a persistent challenge in the field of large language model research and could compromise
534   the integrity of the retrieval process.
