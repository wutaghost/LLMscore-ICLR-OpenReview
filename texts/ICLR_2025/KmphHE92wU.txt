009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) have achieved remarkable success in a variety of
012            machine learning tasks over graph data. Existing GNNs usually rely on message
013            passing, i.e., computing node representations by gathering information from the
014            neighborhood, to build their underlying computational graphs. Such an approach
015            has been shown fairly limited in expressive power, and often fails to capture global
016            characteristics of graphs. To overcome the issue, a popular solution is to use
017            Laplacian eigenvectors as additional node features, as they are known to contain
018            global positional information of nodes, and can serve as extra node identifiers
019
               aiding GNNs to separate structurally similar nodes. Since eigenvectors naturally
               come with symmetries—namely, O(p)-group symmetry for every p eigenvectors
020
               with equal eigenvalue, properly handling such symmetries is crucial for the stability
021
               and generalizability of Laplacian eigenvector augmented GNNs. However, using a
022            naive O(p)-group invariant encoder for each p-dimensional eigenspace may not
023            keep the full expressivity in the Laplacian eigenvectors. Moreover, computing
024            such invariants inevitably entails a hard split of Laplacian eigenvalues according
025            to their numerical identity, which suffers from great instability when the graph
026            structure has small perturbations. In this paper, we propose a novel method ex-
027            ploiting Laplacian eigenvectors to generate stable and globally expressive graph
028            representations. The main difference from previous works is that (i) our method
029            utilizes learnable O(p)-invariant representations for each Laplacian eigenspace of
030
               dimension p, which are built upon powerful orthogonal group equivariant neural
               network layers already well studied in the literature, and that (ii) our method deals
031
               with numerically close eigenvalues in a smooth fashion, ensuring its better robust-
032
               ness against perturbations. Experiments on various graph learning benchmarks
033            witness the competitive performance of our method, especially its great potential
034            to learn global properties of graphs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Numerous real-world data—such as molecules, electric circuits or social networks—can be repre-
040
      sented by graphs. Machine learning over graphs is thus an important approach to find underlying
041
      relations among them, and make predictions concerning novel data. So far, graph neural networks
042   (GNNs) have proved successful on a plethora of learning tasks over graphs (Wu et al., 2020; Zhou
043   et al., 2020), spanning across domains such as chemistry (Deshpande et al., 2002; Jin et al., 2018;
044   Reiser et al., 2022), biology (Stokes et al., 2020; Zitnik & Leskovec, 2017; Zitnik et al., 2018), social
045   recommendations (Ying et al., 2018) or electronic design automation (Lopera et al., 2021).
046
      One of the most popularly adopted GNN architecture is message passing neural network (MPNN),
047
      which maintains a representation vector hu for each node u, and iteratively updates it by gathering
048   information from the neighboring nodes of u. Despite its relative simplicity and efficiency, it
049   has several weaknesses that severely limit its performance. One important problem is its limited
050   expressive power, referring to the fact that MPNNs often fail to distinguish between two non-
051   isomorphic graphs, or two structurally different nodes with similar neighborhood configuration (Xu
052   et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2021). Another issue is its inability to capture global properties of graphs,
053   meaning that it cannot truthfully learn long-range interactions within a graph, due to “oversquashing”
      that occurs as a result of multiple message passing steps (Alon & Yahav, 2020; Dwivedi et al., 2022).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In this paper, we refer to the former problem as a lack of local expressive power, while the latter as
055   one concerning global expressive power.
056
057   A great number of works have attempted to tackle the aforementioned weaknesses of MPNNs, which
058
      we will review in Section 5 and Appendix C. For now, we restrict our discussion to one specific
      approach—using Laplacian eigenvectors as node feature augmentations. Why are we particularly
059
      interested in it? The reason is that Laplacian eigenvectors may alleviate both issues we raise above.
060
      First, Laplacian eigenvectors provably contain rich local structural information (Cvetković et al.,
061   1997; Fürer, 2010; Rattan & Seppelt, 2023), and can thus serve as additional node labels, making
062   it easier for a GNN to separate nodes that are otherwise similar. Furthermore, they can reflect the
063   absolute position of each node within the graph (Von Luxburg, 2007), making GNNs aware of
064   potential long-range interactions. Indeed, a vast literature has regarded Laplacian eigenvectors
065   as so-called graph positional encodings, which play important roles both in MPNNs and graph
066   transformers (Dwivedi et al., 2021; 2023; Rampášek et al., 2022; Ying et al., 2021b).
067   Although Laplacian eigenvectors provide a promising solution for expressive graph representation
068   learning, there are some well-known constraints that one must take into account for their reliable
069   use. The first is orthogonal-group invariance. As is first pointed out by Wang et al. (2022); Lim
070   et al. (2022), given a Laplacian L, its eigen-decomposition is in general not unique. In fact, assuming
071   that v1 , . . . , vp are p mutually orthogonal normalized eigenvectors of a Laplacian L that correspond
072   to the same eigenvalue λ, then so are v1′ , . . . , vp′ , as long as the two groups of eigenvectors can
073   be associated via a p × p orthogonal matrix Q, namely V ′ = V · Q where V = (v1 , . . . , vp )
074   and V ′ = (v1′ , . . . , vp′ ). One must ensure that the network output is invariant to such orthogonal
075   transformations, so as to produce identical representations for identical (i.e., isomorphic) graphs.
076
      Another related but stricter constraint is stability, which, as formally defined by Huang et al. (2024),
      demands that network outputs should be close when the input graph undergoes small perturbations. It
077
      is easy to see that orthogonal-group invariance is a special case of stability in which the strength of
078
      perturbation approaches zero.
079
080   To ensure orthogonal-group invariance (and furthermore, stability), Lim et al. (2022) and Huang
081   et al. (2024) both propose to extract spectral information from inner products between Laplacian
082
      eigenvectors—namely, V V T with V = (v1 , . . . , vp ) being the matrix consisting of p mutually
      orthogonal normalized eigenvectors within an eigenspace of dimension p—instead of the eigenvectors
083
      (v1 , . . . , vp ) themselves. The inner product matrices V V T for different Laplacian eigenspaces are
084
      then processed by invariant graph networks (IGNs) proposed in (Maron et al., 2018; 2019) to produce
085   node feature augmentations. Despite being provably invariant to O(p) transformations and even
086   stable (with carefully designed network architectures), their learning architectures based on inner
087   products are not flexible enough, and may lose much of the rich structural and positional information
088   carried by vanilla Laplacian eigenvectors. Earlier than the above two works, Wang et al. (2022) has
089   proposed a special message passing operation in which only the norms of differences between rows
090   of V are used, and proved its stability. However, this method even dismisses important eigenvalue
091   information by treating Laplacian eigenvectors from different eigenspaces uniformly. Given the
092   limitations of existing methods to utilize Laplacian eigenvectors, a natural question is whether we
093
      can recover the information inherent in vanilla Laplacian eigenvectors while ensuring stability.
      To make the question even more general, we may ask:
094
095
096            What is the representational limit of graph learning methods exploiting Laplacian
097            eigenvectors, given the stability constraint?
098
099   In this paper, we attempt to partially answer the general question posed above. Our main contributions
100   are summarized below.
101
102          • We propose vanilla orthogonal group equivariant augmentation (Vanilla OGE-Aug), a
103            novel method exploiting Laplacian eigenvectors to produce node feature augmentations.
104            Inspired by the property of orthogonal group invariance of invariant point cloud networks
105            (for example, Tensor Field Network (Thomas et al., 2018) and its variant (Finkelshtein
106            et al., 2022)) as well as their great expressive power, we use them to process the Laplacian
107            eigenvectors, enabling the construction of node feature augmentations much more expressive
               than previous ones that make use of inner products between eigenvectors.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
             • We theoretically prove that Vanilla OGE-Aug, combined with an MPNN, can lead to
109            universal representations of graphs, as long as the invariant point cloud networks we
110            use are powerful enough. Previous works have theoretically guaranteed the expressive
111            power of several specific invariant point cloud networks, which lays the foundations for the
112            practicality of our theoretical result.
113
             • Although Vanilla OGE-Aug can be maximally expressive, it unfortunately lacks stability.
114            We then propose a smooth variant of Vanilla OGE-Aug, namely OGE-Aug, by trading
115            expressive power for better stability. Our approach is to use a series of “soft” masks to filter
116            Laplacian eigenvectors that belong to different eigenspaces, instead of hard-splitting and
117            separately processing them. We theoretically prove the stability of OGE-Aug, and evaluate
118            its empirical performance on various real-world graph datasets. The results indicate that
119            our method not only shows competitive performance on popular graph benchmarks, but is
120            surprisingly good at learning global properties of graphs.

124   We use G to denote the set of all simple, undirected graphs. For a graph G ∈ G, its node set and
125   edge set are denoted by V(G) and E(G) respectively. Graphs considered in this paper are usually
126   accompanied with node features, defined as a function from V(G) to Rd .

      For a graph with n nodes labeled by 1, . . . , n respectively, its adjacency matrix is defined as A ∈
128
      {0, 1}n×n in which Aij = 1 if and only if nodes i and j are connected; further, if the graph has node
129
      features, the node features are represented by a matrix X ∈ Rn×d whose i-th row corresponds to the
130   feature of node i.
131
132   Given the adjacency matrix A ∈ {0, 1}n×n of graph G, we define the Laplacian of graph G as
133
      L = D−A, in which D = diag(d(1), . . . , d(n)), with d(i) being the degree of node i (i = 1, . . . , n).
      It is not hard to see that with G being simple and undirected, its Laplacian L is real symmetric,
134
      and further positive semi-definite. Therefore, all eigenvalues of L are real non-negative. One may
135
      also verify that 0 is always an eigenvalue of L (thus being the smallest eigenvalue of L). If L
136   has an eigenvalue λ with multiplicity µ, the linear subspace spanned by the µ mutually orthogonal
137   eigenvectors of L corresponding to λ is called an eigenspace of L with dimension µ.
138
139

      Despite the great number of works showing the efficacy of using Laplacian eigenvectors in graph
156   learning tasks, few (Fürer, 2010; Rattan & Seppelt, 2023) have studied theoretically their expressive-
157   ness upper-bound—namely, to what extent can the information of a graph be learned, merely
158   from its Laplacian eigenvectors? This is a weaker version of the general question we pose in
159   Section 1, with the stability constraint removed. In this section, we will show that the answer to this
160   weaker question is rather optimistic: ignoring the stability constraint, Laplacian eigenvectors can
161   actually lead to universal representations of graphs. To reach the point, we start by reconsidering the
      problem of finding universal graph representations from the perspective of Laplacian eigenvalues and

      We give the proof in Appendix B. To ensure that the inequality (13) holds, in principle we need to
343   tune δ for different G and G′ . However, in our experiments we simply take δ as a hyperparameter
344   designated before actual training.
345
346
      Finally, we discuss practical implementations of OGE-Aug. While presenting the universality result
      (Proposition 3.5), we have assumed that fp (p = 1, 2, . . .) can universally represent all O(p)-invariant
347
      and permutation-equivariant functions on point sets embedded in Rp . This universality requirement
348
      is inherited to OGE-Aug (Definition 4.1). Namely, we still require that f is an O(n)-invariant
349   universal representation. We now point out that such universality requirement, despite producing
350   maximally expressive networks in theory, can be impractical to implement. First, with f being
351   universal, the resulting network architecture has a typical complexity of n exp(Õ(n)) which is
352   generally unacceptable. Moreover, insisting on the universality of f can be harmful to the stability of
353   OGE-Aug, since a more expressive f might result in a larger Lipschitz constant Jf . Therefore, in our
354   actual implementation of OGE-Aug, we no longer require f to be universal. Instead, we adopt as f a
355   Cartesian tensor based point cloud network (Finkelshtein et al., 2022) with Cartesian tensors up to
356   the second order used. We include more experimental details, as well as a complexity analysis for
357   our implementation, in Appendix D.

359   5    R ELATED WORKS
360
361   Graph representation learning with Laplacian eigenvectors. It is well-known that eigenvectors
362   of graph Laplacian corresponding to the smallest eigenvalues contain “positional” information of
363   nodes. A number of works have thus adopted Laplacian eigenvectors as a technique for node feature
364   augmentation. As we have mentioned in Section 1, there are two important issues regarding the
365   application of Laplacian eigenvectors in graph representation learning, namely orthogonal group
366   invariance (or sign-and-basis invariance) and stability. Some early works (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020;
367
      Kreuzer et al., 2021) have noticed the sign invariance problem and tried to alleviate it by randomly
      flipping the signs of Laplacian eigenvectors, while completely ignored the basis invariance problem.
368
      Lim et al. (2022) is the first work to formally state and systematically address the sign-and-basis
369
      invariance issue. Nevertheless, it fails to meet the stronger requirement of stability. So far, only two
370   works (Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024) have seriously discussed the stability issue by giving
371   mathematical definitions for it, and proposing learning methods that are provably stable.
372
373   Orthogonal-group invariant networks. A neural network is said to be orthogonal-group invariant
374   if it takes as input one or more vector(s) (say, for instance, each of dimension p), and outputs an
375   O(p)-invariant scalar, i.e., a value that remains invariant as the input vector system undergoes an
376   O(p) transformation. As is pointed out by, e.g., Bronstein et al. (2021), orthogonal-group invariance
377   is a desirable property for learning tasks on molecular data or point clouds, in which Euclidean
      coordinates play important roles.


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Orthogonal-group equivariance is a property closely related to invariance. A network is O(p)-
379   equivariant if it takes as input one (or a set of) arbitrary representation(s)3 of O(p) (with p-dimensional
380   vectors being a special case), and outputs another (or another set of) representation(s) of O(p), in a
381   way that whenever the input system undergoes the action of an O(p) group element, the output also
382   undergoes an action corresponding to the same element. In practice, invariant networks are usually
383   constructed by stacking multiple equivariant layers, along with a final invariant layer. Regarding the
384   intermediate orthogonal group representations they use, existing works on the design of invariant
385   networks mainly take one of the four approaches: (i) utilizing scalar or vector representations (Deng
386   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024; Satorras et al., 2021; Villar et al., 2021); (ii) utilizing hand-crafted
387
      higher-order representations (Gasteiger et al., 2020; 2021; Schütt et al., 2021); (iii) utilizing higher-
      order Cartesian tensor representations (Finkelshtein et al., 2022; Ruhe et al., 2024); (iv) utilizing
388
      higher-order irreducible representations (Batzner et al., 2022; Bogatskiy et al., 2020; Cohen et al.,
389
      2018; Fuchs et al., 2020; Thomas et al., 2018).
390
391   Similar to the question of expressive power of GNNs, there exists the question of whether an
392   orthogonal-group invariant network can express all possible geometric configurations (either of a
393
      single vector or of a point cloud) up to an arbitrary orthogonal transformation. Invariant networks
      possessing the above property are usually called universal. There have been a few works establishing
394
      theoretically the universality of some of the aforementioned architectures. Villar et al. (2021) shows
395
      that universality can be achieved merely using scalar and vector representations, as long as interaction
396   terms including sufficiently many vectors are allowed, and that the network output is restricted to be
397   scalars or vectors. Li et al. (2024) further shows by construction that an invariant network can be
398   already universal with 4-vector interaction terms, even if all intermediate representations are restricted
399   scalar. Regarding methods using higher-order representations, Dym & Maron (2020) proves the
400   universality of two specific architectures exploiting higher-order irreducible representations of SO(3)—
401   Tensor Field Networks (TFN) (Thomas et al., 2018) and SE(3)-Transformers (Fuchs et al., 2020).
402   Based on TFN, Finkelshtein et al. (2022) proposes another universal architecture utilizing Cartesian
403   tensor representations. The universality results reviewed above have laid theoretical foundations for
404
      our proposed method.
405   We leave the discussion on more related works to Appendix C.

514   7   C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this paper, we propose to apply orthogonal group invariant neural networks on Laplacian
517   eigenspaces of graphs, so as to produce node feature augmentations that may possess great ex-
518   pressive power. We present Vanilla OGE-Aug and OGE-Aug as two instances of our proposed
519   framework, of which the former illustrates the potential of our method to achieve universal represen-
520
      tation of graphs, while the latter is provably stable and practically useful. Extensive experiments have
      verified the outstanding performance of OGE-Aug on various benchmarks as well as its capability to
521
      learn global properties of graphs. We remark that our approach to incorporating stability into graph
522
      learning methods based on Laplacian eigenvectors, i.e., by ensuring smoothness while processing
523   different Laplacian eigenspaces, is a general technique, and can be applied to other machine learning
524   domains where eigenvalues and eigenvectors are of significant interest.

       C     OTHER RELATED WORKS
1189
1190
       Expressive GNNs. As is shown by Xu et al. (2018), the expressive power of MPNNs is upper-
1191   bounded by that of 1-dimensional Weisfeiler-Leman test (1-WL). This implies that MPNNs can fail
1192   to discriminate many non-isomorphic graph pairs, potentially leading to their weakness in capturing
1193   important structural information or multi-node interactions. A great number of works have attempted
1194   to improve the expressive power of GNNs, in the sense that to make them better either at solving
1195   the graph isomorphism problem (GI), or at approximating certain graph functions. Those existing
1196   works can be roughly categorized into three families: (1) methods utilizing additional combinatorial
1197   features (Barceló et al., 2021; Bouritsas et al., 2022; Li et al., 2020); (2) methods applying message
1198   passing among higher-order tuples of nodes, or higher-order GNNs (Bodnar et al., 2021; Feng et al.,
1199
       2023; Maron et al., 2018; 2019; Morris et al., 2019; 2020; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b;c);
       (3) methods decomposing input graphs into bags of subgraphs, or subgraph GNNs (Bevilacqua et al.,
1200
       2024; Cotta et al., 2021; Frasca et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2022;
1201
       You et al., 2021; Zhang & Li, 2021; Zhou et al., 2023b). While methods belonging to class (1) enjoy
1202   the lowest complexities, they often generalize worse due to their use of hand-crafted features. On
1203   the contrary, higher-order GNNs and subgraph GNNs bring more systematic gains to the expressive
1204   power, but their computational complexities are much higher than MPNNs. Hence, a trade-off
1205   between expressive power and efficiency is an important issue for the design of expressive GNNs.
1206
1207   Graph transformers. Graph transformers (Chen et al., 2022; Dwivedi et al., 2021; Rampášek et al.,
1208   2022; Wang et al., 2024; Ying et al., 2021b) treat each node within a graph as a separate token, and
1209   use a standard transformer architecture to update node features (or embeddings of tokens). With
1210   attention mechanism, graph transformers take into account the interactions between all pairs of nodes
1211   (instead of only connected node pairs, as in traditional MPNNs), and are naturally good at capturing
1212
       long-range interactions (Dwivedi et al., 2022). One of the central issues regarding graph transformers
       is the design of structural and positional encodings of nodes, in order to make transformers aware of
1213
       adjacency information. Kim et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2024) analyze the theoretical expressive power
1214
       of graph transformers and their high-order versions as well as the effects of positional encodings.

1311   from the raw Laplacian eigenvectors (to build first-order tensors), and that (ii) Algorithm 1 allows
1312   mixing of Vjsmooth with different j. Despite those differences, Algorithm 1 maintains the key idea of
1313   OGE-Aug: only information from two Laplacian eigenspaces whose corresponding eigenvalues are
1314   “not too far away” from each other would be multiplied into W(2) , and the algorithm has no explicit
1315   dependence on the multiplicities of Laplacian eigenvalues. Thus, the stability result demonstrated in
1316   Proposition 4.3 can similarly hold for Algorithm 1, though the accurate bound may be different.

1318   D.2.2    OTHER DETAILS OF THE PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION
1319
       We implement OGE-Aug with the PyGHO library (Wang & Zhang, 2023). To integrate OGE-Aug
1320
       with other base models including MPNN and graph transformers, we also implement our methods
1321
       building on the GraphGPS (Rampášek et al., 2022) code base, where we build OGE-Aug as a plug-and-
1322   play module. The module takes in Laplacians as inputs and processes the eigenvalues/eigenvectors
1323   using # PE layers with dimension PE hidden dim, and outputs an embedding with dimension
1324   PE dim; see Table 6 for detailed settings. In this module, we use permutation-equivariant set
1325   function (Zaheer et al., 2017) to process the eigenvalues and multiply the eigenvalue embeddings
1326   to the eigenvectors. Moreover, we also multiply eigenvectors with eigenvectors to initialize the
1327   higher order representations. After that, this module will product each node’s representation with its
1328   neighbors’ and update the representation iteratively. The embedding is then combined with other
1329   node features and other optional positional encodings, then fed jointly into downstream layers (which
1330
       consist of various GNN and graph transformer modules). Therefore, OGE-Aug can be either used
       solely or integrated easily with arbitrary backbones.
1331
1332   We also implement a version where OGE-Aug modules act on the embeddings of nodes and edges,
1333   which can be viewed as operating on weighted or latent Laplacians incorporating node and edge
1334   features. However, we experimentally find that processing the original Laplacians with OGE-Aug
1335   and encoding the node/edge features separately via other encoders (as explained above) yields better
1336
       performance.
1337   In addition, to make OGE-Aug more robust, we add a small-scale noise (typically a Gaussian noise
1338   with mean zero and variance 10−5 ) to the Laplacians in the training process. We also randomly
1339   permute the Laplacians and do inverse permutation to the output eigenvectors to simulate the noise
1340   caused by the permutation and the numerical algorithm. We use the original Laplacians in the
1341   inference stage.
