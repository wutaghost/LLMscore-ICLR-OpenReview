009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Similarity is a key construct in psychology, neuroscience, linguistics and com-
012            puter vision. Similarity can manifest in various forms, including visual, semantic,
013            and contextual similarity. Among these, semantic similarity is particularly impor-
014            tant. Not only it serves as an approximation of how humans categorize objects by
015            capturing connections and hierarchies based on shared functionality, evolutionary
016            traits, and contextual meaning, but also offers practical advantages in computa-
017            tional modeling via the lexical structures such as WordNet. Unlike human polls,
018            WordNet-defined similarity is constant and interpretable, making it an important
019            baseline for evaluation. As in the domain of deep vision models there is still a
020
               lack of a clear understanding about the emergence of similarity perception, we
               introduce Deep Similarity Inspector (DSI). It is a systematic framework to inspect
021
               and visualize how deep vision networks develop their similarity perception during
022
               training and how it aligns with semantic similarity. Our experiments show that
023            both Convolutional Neural Networks’ (CNNs) and Vision Transformers’ (ViTs)
024            develop a rich similarity perception during learning with 3 phases (initial simi-
025            larity surge, refinement, stabilization), while clear differences are found in their
026            dynamics. Both CNNs and ViTs, besides the gradual mistakes elimination, im-
027            prove the quality of mistakes being made (the mistakes refinement phenomenon).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Similarity is studied in psychology and neuroscience to understand human categorization. The cog-
      nitive economy principle (Rosch & Lloyd, 1978) suggests that similarity perception, evolving over
034
      time (Medin et al., 1993), minimizes effort and finite cognitive resources. Another principle (Rosch
035
      & Lloyd, 1978) highlights that real-world objects have high correlational structure, making simi-
036   larity a natural and intuitive basis for categorization. Notions of similarity vary, including visual
037   and semantic ones. While semantic similarity is central to linguistics, it also plays a key role in vi-
038   sion and perception, explaining how humans categorize and relate objects through shared attributes,
039   evolutionary traits, appearance, function, or context.
040
      Similarity has also sparked interest in computer vision, mainly to make networks learn represen-
041
      tations aligning better with human judgments or semantic similarity (Bertinetto et al., 2020; Bilal
042   et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021). While enforcing similarity is common, we argue
043   that it is underexplored how and how well networks naturally develop similarity perception. As the
044   visual world’s correlated structure may be a sufficient reinforcement (Rosch & Lloyd, 1978), such
045   evaluation would be an important contribution to the field of model evaluation and explainability.
046
      While similarity/hierarchy-based performance evaluation initially sparked some interest, the atten-
047
      tion shifted to basic accuracy in pursuit of performance gains (Russakovsky et al., 2015; Bertinetto
048   et al., 2020). Recent limited studies (Huang et al., 2021; Bilal et al., 2017; Bertinetto et al., 2020)
049   revisited using confusion patterns and templates from large-scale datasets to analyze how networks
050   infer similarity. Nevertheless, they only presented a very limited view by examining only Con-
051   volutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and ignoring Vision Transformers (ViTs). Aside from minor
052   qualitative assessments (Huang et al., 2021), no quantitative and systematic similarity-based model
053   evaluation framework for during training analysis exists. None of the works simultaneously uses two
      complementary (Medin et al., 1993) approaches to similarity estimation (direct, indirect). Lastly,

054
      they are based only on a functional approach to similarity estimation (using large-scale datasets),
055   rather than a possible structural approach (Mopuri et al., 2020; Filus & Domanska, 2023).
056
057   Motivated by these significant gaps and the crucial role of similarity in categorization, we propose
058
      a framework called Deep Similarity Inspector (DSI) for inspection and visualization of deep vision
      networks’ training from the perspective of similarity perception and numerous numerical measures.
059
      It represents a significant departure from the available, predominantly qualitative, approaches, of-
060
      fering a more systematic and scalable solution. We use it to provide novel insights on how networks
061   learn to recognize objects and how their perception changes during training, contributing to a better
062   understanding of their knowledge and representations development. This contributes significantly
063   to the domain of model evaluation. The fact that our framework enables better understanding of
064   the learning processes, makes it important to the field of explainable artificial intelligence. Our key
065   contributions are: (1) Definition and implementation of a systematic framework for comprehensive
066   vision networks’ training examination from the similarity perception perspective. (2) Formulation of
067   convenient numerical metrics to examine how network’s perception is developed during training, and
068   aligns with semantic similarity. Using all the direct, indirect, functional and structural approaches
069
      to similarity measurements. (3) Thorough examination and comparison of the training process of
      CNNs and ViTs from the perspective of the perceived similarity and its alignment with semantic
070
      similarity. We provide our implementation and reproducibility details in Supp. Mat. and our partial
071
      results at https://zenodo.org/records/13860285 (GitHub upon acceptance).

074   2   R ELATED WORK
075
076
077
      Similarity is crucial to human categorization and computer vision (Tang et al., 2017; Nayak et al.,
      2019; Muttenthaler et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2020), and is a broad concept with different notions
078
      Veit et al. (2017), e.g. visual similarity, feature similarity (comparing data points representations
079
      learned by networks, e.g. (Kornblith et al., 2019)), contextual similarity (it accounts e.g. for spatial
080   relationships (Shi et al., 2019)), adversarial similarity (similarity under adversarial setup (Elsayed
081   et al., 2018; Filus & Domańska, 2024)) and semantic similarity (Pedersen et al., 2004). Vision re-
082   search focuses mainly on creating training schemes that incorporate different kinds of similarity and
083   hierarchy into networks, e.g. via hierarchy enforcement (Bertinetto et al., 2020; Bilal et al., 2017) or
084   contrastive representation learning (Chen et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021; He et al., 2020). Different
085   sources of similarity are used as a reference for the neural representations alignment: e.g. human
086   judgments (Muttenthaler et al., 2023; Roads & Love, 2021) or semantic similarity (Bilal et al., 2017).
087   While these efforts undeniably try to explicitly force neural networks to better align similarity, we
088
      focus on examining ”to what extent perception of deep vision networks trained with standard pro-
      cedures aligns with semantic similarity naturally (with NO enforcement) during training?”. We use
089
      semantic similarity as a reference, because human-based judgments are very data-dependent (unique
090
      high-volume judgements needed for every dataset), limiting their applicability. By this, we enabled
091   creation of a more general similarity-focused framework that can operate with numerous datasets
092   and problems, while still providing an important reference of how similarity can be perceived (se-
093   mantic similarity is based on shared functionalities, appearance, evolutionary traits, or contextual us-
094   age - all impacting also visual similarity). Answering the stated questions is essential to reveal how
095   important similarity is to the operation of networks due to the natural relationships between visual
096   and semantic similarities (Deselaers & Ferrari, 2011). The works that explicitly examine whether
097   the perception of networks aligns with semantic similarity are (Bilal et al., 2017) and (Huang et al.,
098   2021). They both performed the analysis only for CNNs, therefore the analysis and comparison of
099
      similarity perception with Vision Transformers remain absent. They focused mainly on examining
      trained networks (Huang et al., 2021) based on confusion matrices or extracted features (we propose
100
      to use an image-free approach). Although researchers acknowledge that confusion patterns connect
101
      somehow to similarity (Deng et al., 2010; Bilal et al., 2017; Jere et al., 2019; Filus & Domanska,
102   2023) (also under the adversarial setup (Elsayed et al., 2018; Mopuri et al., 2020)), they do not
103   thoroughly examined this connection. Also, available works focus on qualitative methods and not
104   quantitative ones. While they draw attention to the topic, they only touch its very surface and do
105   not show how modern networks develop similarity perception and how it affects their predictions.
106   No adequate and systematic framework for similarity-based testing is available up to this day that
107   could be efficiently used during training. That is why, we introduced such a framework and provide
      observations on how networks perceive and develop similarity perception.

      3   D EEP S IMILARITY I NSPECTOR F RAMEWORK
109
110
      Deep Similarity Inspector (DSI) is a framework proposed to enable effective inspection and visu-
111
      alization of the learning process of deep vision networks for object recognition from the perspective
112   of similarity perception. Its unique characteristics are that (1) it uses simultaneously the direct and
113   indirect, as well functional and structural approaches to similarity estimation, (2) its focus is on
114   numerical analysis methods to quantitatively describe the similarity perception of deep vision net-
115   works. It considers its alignment with reference similarity (WordNet’s semantic similarity). It also
116   includes qualitative methods. Due to the efficiency and the compliance with the existing training
117   procedures, the proposed framework and its provided implementation, can be used to investigate the
118   networks both during and post training. Below, we define the core data structures and methods of
119   the framework (see Appendix L for more in depth description).
120
121   Data structures A core framework’s data structure is a Class Similarity Matrix (CSM). Let C =
122   {c1 , c2 , . . . , cN } be the set of classes in a given dataset D with N classes on which a given model
123   M is being trained. CSM is an N × N matrix that stores the pairwise similarities between classes.
124
      Each element CSMij of the matrix quantifies the similarity between classes ci and cj (CSMii = 1,
      in our analysis, diagonal is excluded). Using this basic notion of a CSM, we can define its 3 variants:
125
126
             • Network Class Similarity Matrices (NCSMs) - it represents a direct and structural ap-
127            proach to similarity estimation from the perspective of M. To obtain this matrix, we use
128            the learned class representations from a final classifier of a deep network M (Mopuri et al.,
129            2020; Nayak et al., 2019) (previously used for knowledge distillation and adversarial test-
130            ing). Each neuron c of the classification layer corresponds to one of the classes - c. A
131            vector wc of weights connecting neuron c to the penultimate layer can be treated as a class
132            template (learned representation in this layer’s feature space) of class c. wc is represented
133            as wc = [wc1 , wc2 , . . . , wcn ], where each wci corresponds to the weight connecting the c-th
134            neuron to the i-th neuron in the penultimate layer. Similarity between templates of classes
                                                                                       wiT wj
135            i and j can be computed with cosine similarity (CS): CS(i, j) = ||wi ||||w     j ||
                                                                                                   . Computing the
136            similarities between all N class pairs and scaling them to range ⟨0, 1⟩ results in the NCSM.
137            We scale the matrices for numerical comparisons, while we use raw values for visualiza-
138            tion. In contrast to more standard similarity estimation approaches, we do not need any test
139            data to compute similarity, making the method significantly faster than the approach based
140            on data samples (Huang et al., 2021) or confusion matrices (Bilal et al., 2017).
141          • Confusion-based Class Similarity Matrices (CCSMs) - it represents an indirect or func-
142            tional approach to similarity estimation from the perspective of M. Let CM be the confu-
143            sion matrix, where CMij is the number of instances of class ci that are classified by M as
144            class cj . CM is an N × N matrix. To create a CSM from CM, we first normalize each
145            row of CM (each row sums to 1), and then fill the diagonal with value 1. This results in a
146            CCSM. CCSM is the only CSM used in our experiments that can be asymmetric, mean-
147
               ing that CSMij ̸= CSMji in general. The reason is that the confusion between class ci
               being classified as class cj may differ from class cj being mistaken as class ci .
148
149          • Semantic Class Similarity Matrices (SCSMs) - it is a similarity reference used by our
150            framework. Semantic relations approximate collective human similarity judgments, as they
151            capture structured connections that humans intuitively recognize (common functionality,
152            appearance, evolutionary traits). Semantic similarity is a relation between terms with a
153
               similar meaning (Kolb, 2009). Semantic similarity can be measured e.g. via WordNet
               (Miller, 1998) similarity measures (Pedersen et al., 2004; Leacock & Chodorow, 1998;
154
               Wu & Palmer, 1994). We use path similarity in our study, as according to (Kolb, 2009), it
155
               outperforms other measures in terms of correlation with human judgment of semantic relat-
156            edness. We use WordNet’s rich semantic relations over human judgments. Its large lexical
157            database better supports generalization across different datasets, increasing the chance of
158            finding relevant representations. The advantages of WordNet path similarity are also its
159            clear formulation, a consistent score due to derivation from a fixed lexical database and an
160            objective and systematic approach to defining relationships. By computing the similarities
161            between all categories in D (expressed via WordNet nodes), we obtain the WordNet CSM
               (WNCSM), which is a special case of a SCSM (see computation method in Appendix B).

      Core methods     Using different CSMs, we construct a set of key methods of our framework.
163
164         • Visualization of CSMs - a qualitative method for graphical inspection of how the similar-
165           ity perception and hierarchy look. While SCSMs are constant in time, both the NCSMs
166           and CCSMs measured after different training steps. It can be used to visualize how the
167           perception changes with time and how is developed from the beginning of the training to
168           its end. In the main paper, we present some chosen matrices (measured after epochs 1, 5,
169           25 and 200), however the matrices for all epochs can be used to create a full animation of
170
              the similarity development during training.
171         • Similarity Alignment Index (SAI) Curves - Similarity Alignment Index (SAI) measures
172           to what degree 2 Class Similarity Matrices are similar, thus how two different similar-
173
              ity perceptions are aligned. Let CSM1 and CSM2 be 2 matrices of size N × N . The
              first step is to exclude the diagonal elements from both matrices: CSM′1 = CSM1 −
174
              diag(CSM1 ), CSM′2 = CSM2 − diag(CSM2 ). The second step is to Normalize the
175
              remaining elements of both matrices to the range ⟨0, 1⟩. After applying these modifi-
176           cations, the two matrices can be compared with a chosen similarity measure to obtain
177           SAI(CSM1 , CSM2 ). We chose Cosine Similarity due to its frequent usage for high-
178           dimensional data. We name the plots of SAI as a function of a number of epoch as Similar-
179           ity Alignment Index (SAI) Curves. They allow to observe how the similarity perception
180           of a given network changes during training. Below, we define possible SAI variants:
181              – SAI(NCSM, SCSM) - measures the alignment between the direct similarity percep-
182                 tion of a network with the semantic similarity. Due to an expected dense structure of
183                 Network- and WordNet CSM variants, we also use Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)
184                 (Wang et al., 2004). It focuses on capturing structural information (changes in lumi-
185                 nance, contrast, and correlation) to model human visual comparison. Cosine similar-
186                 ity and SSIM are better matched to asses visual quality than distance measures (Mean
187                 Squared Error, Mean Absolute Error) (Wang & Bovik, 2009), but we also provide their
188
                    results in Appendix C. SAI(NCSM, SCSM) examines how well a network’s similarity
                    perception corresponds to semantic similarity, indicating if its understanding of rela-
189
                    tionships aligns with semantic reasoning. Low SAI(NCSM, SCSM) values suggest
190
                    that a network’s perception does not align with semantics. Its high values imply they
191                 are well aligned. Changes in SAI indicate whether a network’s perception converge
192                 with semantic similarity during learning or diverge from it, suggesting the use of more
193                 machine-like reasoning for categorization.
194              – SAI(NCSM, CCSM) - measures to what extent the direct similarity perception of a
195                 network aligns with its indirect perception. SAI(NCSM, CCSM) evaluates how well
196                 a network’s similarity perception aligns with its own mistake patterns. The aim is to
197                 determine whether the errors are predictable and stemming from perceived similarity.
198                   Indirect and direct perception should be as similar as possible to obtain predictable
199                 mistakes (predictable means mistakes between highly similar classes).
200              – SAI(CCSM, SCSM) - measures to what extent the indirect similarity perception of a
201                 network aligns with semantic similarity. It assesses how closely the network’s mistake
202                 patterns align with semantic similarity, indicating whether its errors make sense from
203
                    a semantic perspective. Better alignment means more reasonable errors.
204         • Inverse Dissimilarity Metric (IDM) Curves - Dissimilarity Metric is a metric introduced
205           in work (Filus & Domanska, 2023) primarily for the assessment of the damage caused by
206           adversarial attacks. It can be interpreted as the mean similarity shift between the ground
207
              truth label and the post attack label. In this work, we generalize it to a variant that computes
              the mean similarity shift between the ground truth label and the predicted label. We also
208
              propose to use an inverse version of this metric as an extension of accuracy assessment (the
209
              metric takes higher values when accuracy increases or/and when the mistakes are closer
210           in a given Class Similarity Matrix to the ground truth). The metric can be computed as
211           follows. To generate the standard DM values, a given CSM is used. Each row is sorted
212           in a descending order and a matrix with ids of the classes belonging to the particular simi-
213           larity values is obtained - Sorted Class Similarity Matrix (SoCSM). Each c-th row stores
214           classes with the decreasing similarity values for the c-th class. For each sample, we take the
215           ground truth label i and the prediction j. We check in the SoCSM at which index label j is
              placed in the i-th row. The larger the index, the more dissimilar the label is to the ground

267   They represent older and more recent state-of-the-art models. In the main body of the paper, we
268   experiment with the Mini-ImageNet (Vinyals et al., 2016) dataset. It is a version of an original Im-
269   ageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) with 100 classes consisting of leaf-only categories. In Appendix
      A, we describe these elements in more detail. We also present further numerical results obtained on

281   4.1   H OW DOES THE NETWORK ’ S SIMILARITY PERCEPTION CHANGE DURING TRAINING ?
282
283   We examine the Weights Similarity Index (WSI) Curves to see how inter-class similarities change
284   during training (Fig. 1). For the majority of networks, mean similarity slightly increases at the
285   beginning of training to quickly decrease toward the negative cosine similarity. The minimum values
286   are close to 0, suggesting the pursuit to achieve orthogonality. 2 standard CNNs behave significantly
287   different from other models (ResNet18, MobileNetV2) with Mean WSIs growing in a logarithmic
288
      fashion with values close to 0 (see the results for 2 more CNNs in App. G - they support the finding
      that this interesting shape of Mean WSI is characteristic for CNNs and differs them from ViT and
289
      hybrid models). For Max WSI, all networks show an increase at the beginning of training. It reaches
290
      a peak at app. 15 epochs for CNNs, and 40 for ViTs). The curves are mirrored with respect to the x-
291   axis for Min WSI. The results for the hybrid models are closer in terms of the curve shape to the ones
292   of CNNs and in terms of the final values - to ViTs. It shows that hybrid models combine dynamics of
293   both architectures. The results suggest the existence of a phase of network’s rapid discovery of the
294   most similar categories and the most dissimilar categories, showing an effort to push the first ones
295   to, and pull the latter from each other. It occurs during the early epochs, when networks obtain low
296   classification effectiveness. After these initial gains/drops for the Max/Min variants respectively,
297   the similarities start to decrease/grow, suggesting a 2nd phase, in which differences are discovered
298   between similar classes, and similarities between dissimilar classes (suggesting a more fine-grained
299
      analysis, a deeper data understanding and pursuit of vector orthogonality). After some epochs, the
      perception reaches a relative stability (we call it the stability phase).

489   Discussion The analysis performed in this study allowed us to observe some interesting phenom-
490
      ena regarding the learning of vision networks. The results show that CNNs and ViTs develop a
      rich and hierarchical similarity perception during the course of training for standard object recog-
491
      nition on natural datasets. Similarity perception emerges very early in the training. This process
492
      is significantly more dynamic for CNNs/hybrid models than for ViTs. CNNs develop similarity
493   perception rapidly and then refine it more dynamically after reaching their peak. ViTs take longer
494   to mature their similarity perception, but once achieved, it is maintained more consistently. Using
495   hybrid models results in the blending of both architecture features. Nevertheless, the overall dy-
496   namics of the changes in the similarity perception are close for both architectures and 3 phases of
497   similarity development during training can be drawn: (1) Initial Similarity Surge - models rapidly
498   discover similarities and dissimilarities between categories.; (2) Similarity Refinement Phase - mod-
499   els discover dissimilarities between similar classes and push them from each other, while finding
500   relations between less similar categories.; (3) Similarity Stabilization Phase - the similarity percep-
501
      tion becomes steady and networks focus on further elimination of mistakes Our results also show
      that both CNNs and ViTs make improvements in the quality of their mistakes, which we call the
502
      mistakes refinement phenomenon. It is particularly evident in the first phase of similarity perception
503
      development, in which the mistakes tend to show greater similarity to the ground truth.
504
505
506   Limitations Although our framework uses both direct and indirect estimations, CCSMs provide
507   only simplified similarity structures. While this can be enough for some tasks, for tasks that require
508   denser class similarity matrices, the network-based (structural) approach can be used instead (rich
509   representation, no samples). Different implications of similarity could be also examined apart from
510
      the examined ones, e.g. connected to adversarial robustness (we present some quantitative and
      qualitative results regarding this aspect in App. K, we also comment there on the possibility of using
511
      adversarial samples as an extension of our framework). We focused on object recognition networks
512
      trained with a standard supervised procedure on ImageNet. This could limit the generalizability
513   of our findings. To mitigate it, we provided additional results obtained for different datasets and
514   networks trained on different supervised tasks in Appendices D and F. We also described how to
515   extend our framework to consider networks trained on self-supervised tasks and text-image data in
516   App. J. We also presented there some qualitative and quantitative results for DINOv2 Oquab et al.
517   (2023) and CLIP Radford et al. (2021). We focused on relatively accurate networks, and ignored
518   networks struggling to learn. To mitigate it, and provide additional insights, we compare a “good”
519   and a “bad” ViT in Appendix E. Although differences in their perception occur, the results show that
520   similarity is important even for models performing poorly in their task. While we did not analyze the
521
      impact of network capacity, initialization, or training nuances, the consistent results observed across
      different networks, training paradigms, and datasets demonstrate the robustness of our findings.
522
523
524   Conclusions We introduced a systematic framework for inspection of the training process of deep
525   vision networks from different similarity-focused perspectives and numerous qualitative and quan-
526   titative methods. The results highlight the crucial importance of similarity for the categorization
527   of deep vision networks. They support the categorization principles from cognitive psychology,
528   which suggest that the similarity perception evolves over time, revealing the structured and cor-
529   related nature of real-world similarities. The emergence of this perception reflects a natural and
530   optimal/suboptimal use of available finite resources of a system. Our framework and the gathered
531
      insights provide valuable contributions to the field of explainable artificial intelligence by enhancing
      the understanding of model decision-making and learning processes. They also contribute to the
532
      area of model evaluation with possible practical implications. The results suggest that similarity
533
      perception impacts the learning performance, mistakes being made and even adversarial robustness.
534   It can be possibly used in other fields of deep learning outside of vision as well. We anticipate that
535   comparable similarity structures can also be observed and used in the training of language models,
536   audio processing models, and, in the next step, also in the training of classification models for use
537   in e.g. autonomous vehicles. We hope that our study will prompt the community to dig deeper
538   into these topics and use our insights to propose e.g. new training similarity-related schemes (e.g.
539   loss functions based on similarity metrics). We will also focus on it in our future work. Only by
      understanding the operation of deep neural networks, we will be able to fully use their potential.

      A.1   DATASETS USED IN THE STUDY
705
706   In the main part of our paper, we use Mini-ImageNet (Vinyals et al., 2016). It is a version of
707   ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) with 100 classes randomly chosen from the original dataset.
708   ImageNet and its versions are well suited for the similarity related research, as they were created
709   based on the semantic hierarchy of WordNet (Miller, 1998). While original ImageNet included both
710   the internal and leaf WordNet nodes, ImageNet-1k (Russakovsky et al., 2015) and Mini-ImageNet
711
      (Vinyals et al., 2016) consist of only leaves. This results in ImageNet-1k and Mini-ImageNet not
      including any built-in hierarchy due to their labels being at one hierarchy level, and not different
712
      hierarchy levels (which occurs in the original version). Therefore, smaller ImageNet versions are
713
      suitable for studying how vision networks represent complex information hierarchies and the sim-
714   ilarity between their concepts. Their direct connection with WordNet eliminates any ambiguities
715   caused by the ambiguity of text labels of other datasets, for which the WordNet IDs are not given.
716   Moreover, they are both current and important benchmarks in computer vision.
717
      CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) is a dataset of a similar size as Mini-ImageNet, however with
718
      significantly smaller original size of the images (only 32x32 pixels). With its relatively high number
719
      of classes, it is a good alternative to Mini-ImageNet, therefore we use it to examine whether our
720   observations based on the Mini-ImageNet generalize to other datasets for object recognition.
721
722   Additionally, we also use some example models trained on CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and
723
      COCO 2017 (Lin et al., 2014). The first dataset is a small version of CIFAR with only 10 classes. Its
      classes are also at a more basic-level of the semantic hierarchy than the ones of CIFAR100. COCO
724
      2017, on the other hand, is a dataset that includes the labels for object detection and segmentation,
725
      representing a dataset used for other tasks. It includes 80 different natural categories. We use the
726   models trained on these datasets to further (qualitatively) examine the generalizability of our findings
727   (whether the hierarchical similarity structure develops in their NCSMs).
728

730
731   In our main experiments, we use the following state-of-the-art models: 2 standard CNNs (ResNet18
732   (He et al., 2016), MobileNetV2 (Sandler et al., 2018)), 1 CNN ’modernized’ with the techniques
733   from the ViT domain - ConvNeXt-T (Liu et al., 2022b), 2 ViTs (ViTB (Dosovitskiy, 2020),
734   SwinV2T (Liu et al., 2022a) and 1 hybrid model (MaxViTT (Tu et al., 2022) - it uses an attention
735   model blended with convolutions). We use their implementations provided via torchvision python

      library. They represent older and more recent CNNs and ViTs (and models that use techniques
      borrowed from the contrasting architecture). We train all the models from scratch to examine how
737
      they perception of similarity changes during training under the assumption that no knowledge was
738
      present in a given classifier before training. We do not use any techniques to enforce the develop-
739   ment of similarity perception nor hierarchy, to examine whether these phenomena do and how they

742   Random Horizontal Flip, Random Rotation, Gaussian Blur, Color Jitter, Random Perspective Trans-

      iments in the appendices. We provide their names and links to access them in the sections regarding
      experiments with these particular models for clarity.

      3 other models), making it an insignificant addition to the total training time.

       Inverse Dissimilarity Metric (IDM) It is a metric that measures how far in the space defined
2121
       by an NCSM’s similarity (in terms of the normalized number of classes in the matrix sorted with
2122
       increasing similarity to a given class - per row) for this network are the predictions of the network
2123   from their ground truth labels. It is based on the Dissimilarity Metric (DM) introduced in Filus &
2124   Domanska (2023). The original DM can be computed in the following way. We take the ground
2125   truth label i and the post-attack prediction j for each image in the dataset. We check the index of
2126   j in row i of the Sorted Class Similarity Matrix (SoCSM). After gathering predictions on a dataset
2127   (with perturbations), DM quantifies the harmfulness of the attack. For each image, let i represent
2128   the ground truth label, and j the post-attack prediction (in our reformulation - it is the prediction
2129   on the clear dataset). We determine the rank rij of j in row i from the Sorted Class Similarity
2130   Matrix. A higher rij indicates greater damage, as the post-attack prediction j is more dissimilar
2131
       to the ground truth label i (in our variant, these higher value of this rank, means less reasonable
       predictions, therefore we need an additional computational step at the very end - 1 − DM to obtain
2132
       higher values for better semantic accuracy). The computational steps of DM are as follows:
