009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating independent samples from a Boltzmann distribution is a highly relevant
012            problem in scientific research, e.g. in molecular dynamics, where one has initial
013            access to the underlying energy function but not to samples from the Boltzmann
014            distribution. We address this problem by learning the energies of the convolution
015            of the Boltzmann distribution with Gaussian noise. These energies are then used
016            to generate independent samples through a denoising diffusion approach. The
017            resulting method, N OISED E NERGY M ATCHING (NEM), has lower variance and
018            only slightly higher cost than previous related works. We also improve NEM
019
               through a novel bootstrapping technique called B OOTSTRAP NEM (BNEM) that
               further reduces variance while only slightly increasing bias. Experiments on a
020
               collection of problems demonstrate that NEM can outperform previous methods
021
               while being more robust and that BNEM further improves on NEM.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      A fundamental problem in probabilistic modeling and physical systems simulation is to sample
027
      from a target Boltzmann distribution µtarget ∝ exp(−E(x)) specified by an energy function E(x).
028   A prominent example is protein folding, which can be formalized as sampling from a Boltzmann
029
      distribution (Śledź & Caflisch, 2018) with energies determined by inter-atomic forces (Case et al.,
030   2021). Having access to efficient methods for solving the sampling problem could significantly speed
031   up drug discovery (Zheng et al., 2024) and material design (Komanduri et al., 2000).
032
033
      However, existing methods for sampling from Boltzmann densities have problems scaling to high
      dimensions and/or are very time-consuming. As an alternative, Akhound-Sadegh et al. (2024)
034
      proposed Iterated Denoising Energy Matching (iDEM), a neural sampler based on denoising diffusion
035
      models which is not only computationally tractable but also guarantees good coverage of all modes.
036   iDEM uses a bi-level training scheme that iteratively generates samples from the learned sampler and
037   then does score matching using only the target energy and its gradient. Nevertheless, iDEM requires
038   a large number of samples for its Monte Carlo (MC) score estimate to have low variance and a large
039   number of integration steps even when sampling from simple distributions. Also, its effectiveness
040   highly depends on the choice of noise schedule and score clipping. These disadvantages demand
041   careful hyperparameter tuning and raise issues when working with complicated energies.
042   To further push the boundary of diffusion-based neural samplers, we propose N OISED E NERGY
043   M ATCHING (NEM), which learns a series of noised energy functions instead of the corresponding
044   score functions. Despite a need to differentiate the energy network when simulating the diffusion
045   sampler, NEM targets less noisy objectives as compared with iDEM. Additionally, using an energy-
046   based parametrization enables NEM to use bootstrapping techniques for more efficient training
047   and Metropolis-Hastings corrections for more accurate simulation. By applying the bootstrapping
048   technique, we propose a variant of NEM called B OOTSTRAP NEM (BNEM). BNEM estimates high
049   noise-level energies by bootstrapping from current energy estimates at slightly lower noise levels.
050
      BNEM increases bias but reduces variance in its training target.
051   We conduct experiments on a 2-dimensional 40 Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM), a 4-particle
052   double-welling potential (DW-4), a 13-particle Lennard-Jones potential (LJ-13) and a 55-particle
053   Lennard-Jones potential (LJ-55). We empirically find that our methods lead to state-of-the-art
      performance on these tasks. Additionally, we found that targeting energies instead of scores is more

177   Bi-level Iterative Training Scheme To train the diffusion on the estimated targets, we should
178   obtain noising exact samples from the target. Previous works (Akhound-Sadegh et al., 2024; Midgley

180

181   This involves
182
183          • An outer loop that simulates the diffusion sampling process to generate more informative
184            samples. These samples are then used to update a replay buffer B.
185          • A simulation-free inner loop that matches the noised energies (NEM) or scores (DEM)
186            evaluated at noised versions of the samples stored in the replay buffer.
187

      Variance-Controlled Bootstrap Schedule BNEM aims to trade the bias of the learning target to
325   its variance. To ensure that the variance of the Bootstrap energy estimator at t bootstrapped from
326   s is controlled by a predefined bound β, i.e. σt2 − σs2 ≤ β, we first split the time range [0, 1] with
327   0 = t0 < t1 < ... < tN = 1 such that σt2i+1 − σt2i ≤ β/2; then we uniformly sample s and t from
328   adjacent time splits during training for variance control.
329
330
331   Training of BNEM To train BNEM, it is crucial to account for the fact that bootstrap energy
332   estimation at t can only be accurate when the noised energy at s is well-learned. To favor this, we first
333
      use NEM to obtain an initial energy network and then apply training with the bootstrapped estimator.
      We then aim to use bootstrapping only when the energy network is significantly better at time s
334
      than at time t. We quantify this by evaluating the NEM losses at times t and s given a clean data
335
      point x0 . In particular, we could compare ˜ls (xs ) = LNEM (xs , s) and ˜lt (xt ) = LNEM (xt , t), where
336
      xs ∼ N (x0 , σs2 I) and xt ∼ N (x0 , σt2 I). However, the variance of training targets increases with
337
      time, meaning that a direct comparison of these losses is not reliable. To avoid this, we normalize
338   these losses according to the noise schedule variance and compare instead ls (xs ) = LNEM (xs , s)/σs2
339   and lt (xt ) = LNEM (xt , t)/σt2 . We then adopt a rejection training scheme according to the ratio of
340   these normalized NEM losses:
341
342        (a) given s, t and x0 , we first noise x0 to s and t respectively, and compute the normalized
343            losses defined above, i.e. ls (xs ) and lt (xt );
344
345        (b) these losses indicate how well the energy network fits the noised energies at different times;
346            we then compute α = min(1, ls (xs )/lt (xt ));
347
348
           (c) with probability α, we accept targeting an energy estimator at t bootstrapped from s and
               otherwise, we stick to targeting the original MC energy estimator.

354   4    R ELATED WORKS
355
356   Boltzmann Generator. To learn a neural sampler for Boltzmann distribution, unlike data-driven tasks
357   where a sufficient amount of data is available, simply minimizing the reverse Kullback-Leibler (KL)
358   divergence, i.e.minθ DKL (µtarget ∥qθ ), can lead to mode-seeking behavior. Boltzmann Generator(Noé
359   et al., 2019) addresses this problem by minimizing the combination of forward and reverse KL
360   divergence.
361   PIS, DDS, and FAB. Inspired by the rapid development of deep generative models, e.g. diffusion
362   models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020), pseudo samples could be generated from an arbitrary
363   prior distribution. Then, we can train the neural samplers by matching these sample trajectories, as in
364   Path Integral Sampler (PIS)(Zhang & Chen, 2022) and Denoising Diffusion Sampler (DDS) (Vargas
365   et al., 2023). Midgley et al. (2023) further deploy a replay buffer for the trajectories while proposing
366   an α-divergence as the objective to avoid mode-seeking. However, these methods require simulation
367   during training, which still poses challenges for scaling up to higher dimensional tasks.
368   iDEM. To further boost scalability and mode-coverage, iDEM (Akhound-Sadegh et al., 2024) is
369   proposed to target an MC score estimator that estimates scores of noised data, enabling the usage
370   of the efficient diffusion sampling. iDEM is trained with a bi-level scheme: (1) a simulation-free
371   inner-loop that targets time-involved scores of buffer data; (2) an outer-loop that simulates the learned
372   diffusion sampler to generate more informative data in the buffer. It achieves previous state-of-the-art
373   performance on the tasks above.
374   iEFM. To regress xt directly, one can leverage Flow Matching Lipman et al. (2023). Woo & Ahn
375   (2024) proposes iEFM, a variant of iDEM that targets the MC estimated vector fields in a Flow
376   Matching fashion, which is empirically found to outperform iDEM in GMM and DW-4. In fact,
377   we found that iEFM and iDEM can be linked through Tweedie’s formula (Efron, 2011) shown in
      Appendix H with supplementary experiments provided in Appendix J.8.

      Datasets. We evaluate all neural samplers on 4 different datasets: a GMM with 40 modes (d = 2),
406
      a 4-particle double-well (DW-4) potential (d = 8), a 13-particle Lennard-Jones (LJ-13) potential
407
      (d = 39) and a 55-particle Lennard-Jones (LJ-55) potential (d = 165). For LJ-n potentials, the energy
408   can be extreme when particles are too close to each other, creating problems for estimating noised
409   energies. To overcome this issue, we smooth the Lennard-Jones potential through the cubic spline
410   interpolation, according to Moore et al. (2024).
411
      Baseline. We compare NEM and BNEM to following recent works: Denoising Diffusion Sampler
412
      (DDS)(Vargas et al., 2023), Path Integral Sampler (PIS)(Zhang & Chen, 2022), Flow Annealed
413
      Bootstrap (FAB)(Midgley et al., 2023) and Iterated Denoising Energy Matching (iDEM)(Akhound-
414   Sadegh et al., 2024). Due to the high complexity of DDS and PIS training due to their simulation-based
415   nature, we limit their integration step when sampling to 100. Also, as iDEM (Akhound-Sadegh et al.,
416   2024) shows excellent performance of GMM and DW-4, we consider showing its robustness together
417   with NEM and BNEM by limiting both the integration step and number of MC samples to 100. For
418   complex tasks, i.e. LJ-13 and LJ-55, we stick to using 1000 steps for reverse SDE integration and
419   1000 MC samples in the estimators. For BNEM, we set β = 0.1 for all tasks. We train all samplers
420   using an NVIDIA-A100 GPU.
421   Architecture. We use the same network architecture (MLP for GMM and EGNN for particle systems,
422   i.e. DW-4, LJ-13, and LJ-55) in DDS, PIS, iDEM, NEM and BNEM. To ensure a similar number of

      6     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this work, we propose NEM and BNEM, neural samplers for Boltzmann distribution and equi-
      librium systems like many-body systems. NEM uses a novel Monte Carlo energy estimator with
526
      reduced bias and variance. BNEM builds on NEM, employing an energy estimator bootstrapped
527
      from lower noise-level data, theoretically trading bias for variance. Empirically, BNEM achieves
528   state-of-the-art results on 4 different benchmarks, GMM, DW-4, LJ-13, and LJ-55.
529
530   Limitations and future work. Even though NEM can outperform DEM with fewer integration
531
      steps, the requirement of differentiating the neural network w.r.t. the input poses a memory issue for
      high-dimensional tasks, and therefore would require further improvement for scalability in terms of
532
      memory in the future; Secondly, though BNEM demonstrates its potential to achieve improvement on
533
      top of NEM, its training process is yet not stable enough. Therefore, improving its training stability
534   is of interest in the future; Besides, the cubic-spline interpolation technique is applied for the LJ-n
535   potentials, and therefore a more general learning-based technique that can fix the extreme energy
536   issue, such as contrastive learning, is more desired; Furthermore, the well-learned noised energies
537   allow us more possible ways to improve generation quality beyond normal denoising diffusion
538   approach, such as integrating Metropolis-Hastings correction inside the denoising process to generate
539   more accurate samples.

1755   outperforms iEFM-OT and iEFM-VE in terms of x − W2 but are not as good as our rerun iDEM.
1756   Figure 10 visualizes the generated samples from ground truth samplers, i.e. SK and S̃K , and neural
1757   samplers, i.e. TweeDEM and iDEM. It shows that the ground truth samplers can generate well mode-
1758   concentrated samples, as well as TweeDEM, while samples generated by iDEM are not concentrated
1759   on the modes and therefore result in the high value of W2 based metrics. Also, this phenomenon
1760   aligns with the one reported by Woo & Ahn (2024), where the iEFM-OT and iEFM-VE can generate
1761   samples more concentrated on the modes than iDEM.
1762
       Above all, simply replacing the score estimator SK with S̃K can improve generated data quality and
1763
       outperform iEFM in GMM and DW-4 potentials. Though TweeDEM can outperform the previous
1764   state-of-the-art sampler iDEM on GMM, it is still not as capable as iDEM on DW-4. Except scaling
1765   up and conducting experiments on larger datasets like LJ-13, combing SK and S̃K is of interest in
1766   the future, which balances the system scores and Gaussian ones and can possibly provide more useful
1767   and less noisy training signals. In addition, we are considering implementing a denoiser network for
1768   TweeDEM as our future work, which might stabilize the training process.
