009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Conventional federated learning (FL) aims to train a unified machine learning
012            model that fits data distributed across various agents. However, statistical hetero-
013            geneity arising from diverse data resources renders the single global model trained
014            by FL ineffective for all clients. Personalized federated learning (pFL) has been
015            proposed to primarily address this challenge by tailoring individualized models
016
               to each client’s specific dataset while integrating global information during fea-
               ture aggregation. Achieving efficient pFL necessitates the accurate estimation of
017
               global feature information across all the training data. Nonetheless, balancing the
018
               personalization of individual models with the global consensus of feature infor-
019            mation remains a significant challenge in existing approaches. In this paper, we
020            propose pFedVMP, a novel pFL approach that employs variational message pass-
021            ing (VMP) to design feature aggregation protocols. By leveraging the mean and
022            covariance, pFedVMP yields more precise estimates of the distributions of model
023            parameters and global feature centroids. Additionally, pFedVMP is effective in
024            boosting training accuracy and preventing overfitting by regularizing local train-
025            ing with global feature centroids. Extensive experiments on heterogeneous data
026            conditions demonstrate that pFedVMP surpasses state-of-the-art methods in both
027
               effectiveness and fairness.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Federated learning (FL) is a promising distributed learning paradigm that enables clients to collab-
032   oratively train models without uploading private data, thereby protecting local data privacy (McMa-
033   han et al., 2017). In the standard FL framework, clients train a uniform learning model using local
034   datasets and employ linear model aggregation to combine these local models, assuming that while
035   the local data across clients may differ in size, they generally share similar underlying distributions.
036   This assumption potentially leads to a global model that performs reasonably well when deployed
037   on each client. However, in practice, local data distributions vary due to diverse sources and data
038   quality, resulting in a phenomenon known as statistical heterogeneity of training data (Zhao et al.,
039
      2018). This heterogeneity makes the globally optimal model perform poorly on local datasets.
040   Personalized federated learning (pFL) has been introduced to address the challenge of statistical het-
041   erogeneity by training personalized models that better align with each client’s local dataset, rather
042   than relying on a single global model. This is accomplished through an iterative process that al-
043   ternates between two key steps: (1) Aggregating shared feature information from local models to
044   capture the underlying patterns present across local datasets, and (2) Developing tailored models for
045
      clients to meet their specific objectives by leveraging the aggregated global information. Existing
      work often concentrates exclusively on either personalized feature information (e.g., FedPer (Ari-
046
      vazhagan et al., 2019), FedPep (Collins et al., 2021)) or global feature aggregation (e.g., FedROD
047
      (Chen & Chao, 2022)). This leads to neglect of balancing personalization and global consistency.
048   To address this issue, several pFL approaches incorporate global information to improve local fea-
049   ture extraction. For example, FedProto (Tan et al., 2022) and FedPAC (Xu et al., 2023) align local
050   feature representations closely with their respective centroids, where the global centroids are esti-
051   mated by averaging the feature samples. However, due to statistical heterogeneity of training data,
052   the arithmetic mean of feature samples deviates from the ground-truth centroids (Al-Shedivat et al.,
053   2021; Guo et al., 2023), which in turn might degrade the accuracy of local feature extraction. To
      tackle this challenge, Bayesian estimation methods have been adopted in FL. For example, FedPA

      (Al-Shedivat et al., 2021) and FedEP (Guo et al., 2023) design model aggregation protocols based
055   on Bayesian principles. By leveraging the mean and covariance of model parameters, these methods
056   achieve more accurate estimate of the global model.
057
058
      In this paper, we propose a pFL approach, termed pFedVMP, which leverages a variational message
      passing approach for feature aggregation. This method conceptualizes both model parameters and
059
      feature centroids as random variables and aggregates their distributions via a maximum-a-posteriori
060
      (MAP) criterion to update the global model. To simplify the MAP estimation, we utilize variational
061   inference to decompose the joint density distribution of the variables using multiplicative factors.
062   By leveraging the mean and covariance, the variational message passing rules yield more precise
063   estimates of the distributions of model parameters and global feature centroids. Furthermore, the
064   variational message passing algorithm yields a model update rule that aligns with a regularized local
065   optimization framework, utilizing global feature centroids to enhance personalized model training.
066   This approach is validated as effective in improving training accuracy and preventing overfitting.
067   The key contributions are summarized as follows:
068
      • We develop a unified probabilistic framework that integrates both model parameters and feature
069
        centroids, proposing a pFL approach based on variational message passing, termed pFedVMP, to
070
        address statistical data heterogeneity.
071
072   • pFedVMP provides more precise estimates of the distributions of model parameters and global fea-
073
        ture centroids by utilizing the means and covariances. This approach achieves a balance between
        global feature estimation and local model personalization in pFL.
074
075   • We perform extensive experiments under various data heterogeneity settings. The results demon-
076     strate that pFedVMP outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of both effectiveness and fair-
077
        ness.

079   2    R ELATED W ORK
080
081   FL under statistical heterogeneity of data. The FL framework was initially proposed by McMa-
082   han et al. (2017). Subsequent studies, such as those by (Khaled et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2018),
083   have underscored the significant impact of statistical heterogeneity in training data on the conver-
084   gence rate and learning accuracy of FL models. This challenge has continuously drawn attention
085   in the research community. Various strategies have been proposed to address this issue, including
086   regularized local training using global information (Li et al., 2020; Durmus et al., 2021; Li et al.,
087
      2021a), local bias correction (Karimireddy et al., 2020), data augmentation (Li et al., 2022; Yoon
      et al., 2021), and knowledge distillation (Zhu et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2020).
088
089   The drive to address statistical heterogeneity has significantly shaped the development of pFL ap-
090   proaches, which train localized models tailored to diverse local data distributions (Dai et al., 2023;
091   Zhang et al., 2023a; Islam et al., 2024; Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020). Initial pFL strategies typi-
092   cally involved a straightforward extension of linear model aggregation similar to conventional FL
093
      (Deng et al., 2020; Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020). Since then, more sophisticated pFL protocols have
      emerged, drawing inspiration from advanced learning mechanisms, such as meta-learning (Fallah
094
      et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2018), multi-task learning (Smith et al., 2017; T Dinh et al., 2020; Li et al.,
095
      2021b), and model splitting strategies (Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Collins et al., 2021; Chen & Chao,
096   2022; Liang et al., 2020; Oh et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b). While these approaches have im-
097   proved the performance on the heterogeneous data, they may still be prone to overfitting, particularly
098   when the training dataset size is small (Zhang et al., 2023d;a).
099
      Federated Representation Learning. Several pFL approaches, such as FedSR(Nguyen et al.,
100
      2022), FedCiR(Li et al., 2024), FedProto(Tan et al., 2022), MOON(Li et al., 2021a), FedCP(Zhang
101
      et al., 2023c), FedPAC(Xu et al., 2023), and GPFL(Zhang et al., 2023a), integrated representation
102   learning by learning a client-invariant representation. This representation maintains a consistent con-
103   ditional distribution across clients and is leveraged in local training as a foundation model, which is
104   shown effective in preventing overfitting. Specifically, FedSR and FedCiR computed global feature
105   distributions by using probabilistic networks and generative networks, respectively, which are not
106   directly applicable to pFL. In contrast, FedProto and FedPAC estimated the mean of global feature
107   distributions by averaging local feature samples. MOON aligns the local and global representations
      by maximizing their similarity. GPFL embedded features in a representation space and subsequently

124   Bayesian Federated Learning. Bayesian federated learning (BFL) was proposed to improve the
125   robustness and learning performance, particularly on small-scale datasets (Cao et al., 2023). BFL
126   can be broadly categorized into client-side BFL and server-side BFL based on federated learning
127   architectures. Client-side BFL focuses on learning Bayesian local models on client nodes, includ-
128   ing BNFed (Yurochkin et al., 2019), pFedGP (Achituve et al., 2021), and pFedBayes (Zhang et al.,
129   2023d). Specifically, BNFed and pFedGP train Bayesian nonparametric models, while pFedBayes
130
      trains Bayesian neural networks. In contrast, server-side BFL aggregates local updates for global
      models using Bayesian methods, including FedPA (Al-Shedivat et al., 2021), FedEP (Guo et al.,
131
      2023), QLSD (Vono et al., 2022), pFedBreD (Shi et al., 2024). This branch of methods formulates
132
      model training as model inference tasks and computes the maximum-a-posterior (MAP) estimator
133   (Al-Shedivat et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2023; Vono et al., 2022). In the FL setups, the distributed nature
134   of datasets among clients prevents direct computation of model posterior distributions. FedPA ap-
135   proximated the posterior distribution into the product of distributions with respect to local datasets
136   during local model training. FedEP developed the Bayesian model aggregation rule by using expec-
137   tation propagation. QLSD extended the approach in FedPA with the quantized Langevin stochastic
138   dynamics for local update. pFedBreD incorporates personalized prior knowledge for meta-learning.
139   However, the above BFL methods do not utilize global feature centroids to guide local model train-
140   ing, which limits their ability to effectively address data heterogeneity. In contrast, pFedVMP con-
141
      siders both model parameters and feature centroids, guiding local training through a regularization
      term based on global feature centroids, thereby enhancing learning performance.

144   3    S YSTEM M ODEL AND P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
145
146   We consider an FL system to train a supervised classification model under the coordination of a
147   parameter server (PS) and N clients. Each client n owns its local dataset Sn with |Sn | = Sn labeled
148   data points. The i-th data point in Sn is denoted by (xn,i , yn,i ), where xn,i denotes the data sample,
                                                                        SN
149   and yn,i ∈ {1, · · · , K} denotes the label of xn,i . Let S = n=1 Sn denote the collection of the
150   training data from all the clients, which is assumed to be categorized into K classes and the data in
151   each class is independent identically distributed (i.i.d.) from an unknown distribution. We denote
152
      the overall data distribution as a mixture distribution pD (x, y). We assume that the data size of each
      label class at each client is known at the PS beforehand.
153
154   In practice, data heterogeneity across clients results in heterogeneous statistics of local data, includ-
155   ing their means, variances, etc. This discrepancy leads to distinct marginal distributions for local
156   datasets, presenting a challenge known as the statistical heterogeneity of training data (Zhao et al.,
157   2018; Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2022). Such heterogeneity invalidates the common i.i.d.
158
      data assumption in the machine learning literature, arising challenges in model bias and overfitting.
159   In this work, we employ pFL to address the challenge of statistical heterogeneity. Instead of training
160   a uniform global model that tries to fit all the local datasets, pFL aims to train personalized models
161   tailored to each client’s individual dataset. As shown in Fig. 1, the clients share a common base
      model to extract global feature representations and learn a personalized head model to enhance

520   6   C ONCLUSIONS
521
522   In this paper, we introduced pFedVMP, a novel pFL approach designed to address the challenge
523   of statistical heterogeneity in FL. By leveraging variational message passing, pFedVMP effectively
524   aggregates the distributions of model parameters and feature centroids, enabling precise estimates
525
      of their probabilistic models. Our method strikes a balance between incorporating global informa-
      tion for collaborative learning and maintaining personalized models tailored to each client’s local
526
      dataset. Moreover, pFedVMP effectively mitigates the risk of overfitting through the utilization of
527
      global feature centroids to regularize local training. Numerical results demonstrate that pFedVMP
528   outperforms state-of-the-art algorithms in terms of test accuracy and the coefficient of variation.

861   follows. Increasing feature dimensions enhances model capacity, thereby improving the learning
862   performance of FL models. However, it also raises the number of trainable parameters, which
863   increases the risk of overfitting. In the FL context, where some clients have small local datasets, this
      risk is mitigated.
