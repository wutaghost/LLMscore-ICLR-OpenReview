009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in artificial intelligence have been fueled by the development
012            of foundation models such as BERT, GPT, T5, and Vision Transformers. These
013            models are first pretrained on vast and diverse datasets and then adapted to spe-
014            cific downstream tasks, often with significantly less data. However, the mecha-
015            nisms behind the success of this ubiquitous pretrain-then-adapt paradigm remain
016
               underexplored, particularly the characteristics of pretraining checkpoints that lend
               themselves to good downstream adaptation. We introduce a Bayesian model selec-
017
               tion criterion, called the downstream free energy, which quantifies a checkpoint’s
018
               adaptability by measuring the concentration of nearby favorable parameters for
019            the downstream task. We demonstrate that this free energy criterion can be effec-
020            tively implemented without access to the downstream data or prior knowledge of
021            the downstream task. Furthermore, we provide empirical evidence that the free en-
022            ergy criterion reliably correlates with improved fine-tuning performance, offering
023            a principled approach to predicting model adaptability.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      The advent of foundation models has significantly reshaped the landscape of modern machine learn-
028
      ing (Bommasani et al., 2021). Trained on expansive, diverse datasets using supervised or self-
029
      supervised learning methods, these models learn generalized representations that can then be suc-
030   cessfully adapted (or finetuned) to a wide array of downstream tasks, often where there is signif-
031   icantly less data or limited computational resources (Bengio, 2012; Brown, 2020). This pretrain-
032   then-adapt paradigm has emerged as a dominant and highly successful technique driving significant
033   progress across natural language processing and computer vision with applications including text
034   classification (Qiu et al., 2020), text generation (Li et al., 2024), image classification (Liu et al.,
035   2023b), object detection (Sanchez et al., 2020), medical imaging (Mormont et al., 2018; Chen et al.,
036   2019; Ke et al., 2021), autonomous driving (Kim & Park, 2017) and robotics (Jaquier et al., 2023).
037   As a result, there is a growing body of research aimed at better understanding the theoretical reasons
038   behind the success of this pretrain-then-adapt paradigm (Galanti et al., 2022; Munn et al., 2024). One
039   of the key open questions is to understand how to select pretraining checkpoints which are optimal
040   for adaptation. A number of practical heuristics have emerged through experimental intuition and
041   empirical analysis (Liu et al., 2023a), but a principled theoretical framework for effective checkpoint
042   selection is still lacking.
043   To address this, we repurpose well-established concepts form Bayesian statistics and propose down-
044   stream free energy as a pretraining model selection criterion. Downstream free energy measures
045   the negative log of the concentration of well-performing network weights near a pretraining check-
046   point when evaluated on downstream data. Intuitively, lower downstream free energy indicates a
047   higher concentration of parameters in parameter space for which the model is more adaptable and
048   capable of generalizing well on downstream tasks. In short, checkpoints with lower downstream
049   free energy are better suited for adaptation and thus should be preferred during pretraining.
050   Although the use of downstream free energy as a pretraining model selection criterion has strong
051   theoretical motivations, it comes with an unfortunate caveat: to compute it requires access to the
052   downstream dataset which may not be available to the practitioner during pretraining. However, un-
053   der certain distributional shift conditions between the pretraining and downstream data, it is possible
      to overcome this limitation. Namely, we introduce the pretraining free energy, which is computed

      solely on the pretraining data, and show that minimizing it serves as a reliable proxy for minimizing
055   the downstream free energy (see Proposition 5.3). Together, these insights provide a solid justifi-
056   cation for using the pretraining free energy as a model selection criterion during pretraining. This
057   strategy is particularly advantageous when pretraining is intended to be general purpose, as is the
058   case with most foundation models.

096   Implicit bias in transfer learning. The term implicit bias refers to the tendency of optimization
097   processes, such as stochastic gradient descent (SGD), to inherently guide the model’s learning dy-
098   namics towards solutions with properties which are not explicitly prescribed by the loss function
099   (Neyshabur et al., 2017; Soudry et al., 2018; Gunasekar et al., 2018). For example, the selection
100   of training hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and batch size, can have a significant effect
101   on the optimization efficiency as well as on the quality of the learned model (Keskar et al., 2017;
102   Masters & Luschi, 2018; Goyal, 2017; He et al., 2019; Andriushchenko et al., 2023). As a result,
103   there has been considerable effort to understand the mechanisms which govern these implicit biases
104
      during model training. However, the effect of implicit bias in transfer learning—particularly how it
      impacts successful downstream domain adaptation—is a growing but less explored area of research
105
      (Lindsey & Lippl, 2023; Kumar et al., 2022).
106
107   In transfer learning, the ability to identify and leverage pretraining biases to predict and improve
      downstream test error is highly valuable. Recent work of Liu et al. (2023a); Galanti et al. (2022);


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Munn et al. (2024) can be viewed as establishing relationships of the form
109
110                            downstream test error ≲ pretraining characteristic.                        (1)
111
      Ideally, these pretraining characteristics are sensitive to factors which can be manipulated by prac-
112
      titioners, thus allowing for deliberate influence and intentional design during pretraining. Further-
113   more, any such pretraining characteristic should be accessible using only pretraining data, since
114   knowledge to the downstream task or data is typically not available. It is worthwhile to note that Liu
115   et al. (2023a); Galanti et al. (2022); Munn et al. (2024) mainly consider the linear probe as their
116   fine-tuning method while we consider full fine-tuning.
117
      Liu et al. (2023a) explore the role of implicit bias in language modeling and establish an empirical
118
      relationship between the pretraining flatness (measured by the trace of the Hessian of the pretraining
119   loss) and the downstream test accuracy. Their experiments verify that lower pretraining flatness,
120   which they show is effectively regularized by SGD, strongly correlates with better downstream per-
121   formance. Although this work does not provide a formal bound as in (1), it offers valuable empirical
122   evidence on how the implicit flatness regularization of SGD acts to benefit transfer learning. This
123   is particularly beneficial since techniques exist for explicitly minimizing loss landscape sharpness;
124   e.g., Foret et al. (2020); Wen et al. (2023).
125   Galanti et al. (2022) examine the efficacy of transfer learning through the lens of neural collapse, a
126   recently observed phenomenon which characterizes the geometry of last-layer features and weights
127   for overparameterized classification networks (Papyan et al., 2020). They show through theory and
128   experiments that the neural collapse exhibited during pretraining generalizes to new classes of the
129   downstream task as well, thus enabling successful model adaptation. Drawing on the formalism
130   described in (1), Galanti et al. (2022) can be seen as deriving theoretical bounds of the form
131
              downstream test error ≲ downstream neural collapse ≲ pretraining neural collapse.
132
133   However, while this supports neural collapse as an effective pretraining characteristic, practical
134   levers which can be used to explicitly regularize the pretraining neural collapse are lacking.
135   Munn et al. (2024) make progress in this direction by means of the geometric complexity, a model
136   complexity measure introduced and analyzed in Dherin et al. (2022). They prove that the geometric
137   complexity of the model’s learned feature representations upper bounds the model neural collapse.
138   Furthermore, their experiments verify that techniques which implicitly reduce this geometric com-
139   plexity during pretraining (such as large learning rates, small batch sizes and increased L2 regu-
140   larization) in turn put regularizing pressure on the pretraining neural collapse leading to improved
141   transfer test accuracy.
142   Our key contribution is the identification of free energy as a novel and significant pretraining charac-
143   teristic which exhibits direct theoretical and empirical connections governing successful downstream
144   model adaptability. We prove in Section 5 that, similar to neural collapse, the pretraining free en-
145   ergy bounds from above the downstream free energy. In addition, we establish (see Appendix A) a
146   theoretical link between downstream free energy and the downstream Bayesian prediction, provid-
147   ing theoretical guarantees on the downstream Bayes test error. Together, these theoretical results,
148
      viewed in the context of (1), imply
149          downstream Bayesian test error ≲ downstream free energy ≲ pretraining free energy.
150
151   Furthermore, using mechanisms established in Lau et al. (2023) which are known to implicitly
152   regularize the pretraining free energy—such as large learning rates, small batch sizes, and increased
153   momentum—we experimentally verify (see Section 6) that lower pretraining free energy does indeed
154   lead to improved fine-tuning performance.

      Bayesian model selection criterion. The idea of using free energy has its roots in Bayesian model
156   selection. Given a collection of models, M1 , . . . , Mk , the task of choosing an optimal model for
157   some given data is known as model selection. There are different (and sometimes irreconcilable)
158   model selection criteria; but, in general, all model selection criteria attempt to balance fit and com-
159   plexity. A particularly appealing Bayesian model selection criterion is the free energy criterion
160   which is widely used and accepted in the both the statistical and machine learning literature (Hinton
161   & van Camp, 1993; Kass & Raftery, 1995; MacKay, 2002; Robert et al., 2007). The free energy
      model selection criterion says we should pick the model with the lowest free energy. Since the free


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      energy is the negative log of the marginal likelihood, also known as Bayesian model evidence, free
163   energy minimization is equivalent to marginal likelihood maximization. To our knowledge, this
164   work represents the first application of the free energy criterion in the domain of transfer learning.

354
      Assumption 5.2 and indeed this assumption may not always hold for some real-world scenarios. For

448   6    E XPERIMENTS
449
450   The goal of our experiments is to evaluate how well the pretraining WBIC, which estimates the
451   pretraining free energy as described in Section 5.2, correlates with downstream performance. In
452   order to measure the impact of lower pretraining WBIC, we apply mechanisms during pretraining
453   which are known to implicitly regularize this quantity, as shown in (Lau et al., 2023). These include
454   including large learning rates, small batch sizes, and high momentum.
455
      We use the CIFAR-FS dataset (Bertinetto et al., 2019), derived from CIFAR-100 where the 100
456   classes are divided into 64 classes for meta-training, 16 classes for meta-validation, and 20 classes
457   for meta-testing. We pretrain on the meta-training set and then assess model adaptability on the
458   unseen meta-test set via limited fine-tuning described in Section 3. The meta-validation classes are
459   not used.
460
      Pretraining. For pretraining, we use all 64 classes from the CIFAR-FS meta-training set to train
461
      a ResNet-18 model using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). We explore ranges of hyperparameter
462   values for the learning rate, batch size and momentum. Interaction effects between these are not
463   considered. Full experiment details for each hyperparameter sweep are provided in Appendix D.1.
464   During training we track the pretraining train loss (first column of Figure 2) and the pretraining
465   WBIC (second column of Figure 2). The hyperparameter settings for pretraining WBIC computation
466   are provided in Appendix D.1.
467
      Full meta-test fine-tuning uses the full meta-test dataset, consisting of all 20 meta-test classes with
468   600 examples per class. We use an 80/20 split for training and testing, with stratification within each
469   class. In this setting a new (randomly initialized) linear head is attached for the 20-class classification
470   task, and the model is fine-tuned for 100 steps using SGD. This setting corresponds to the “Finetune
471   Transfer Accuracy” metric (third column) in Figure 2. Hyperparameter details for this setting are in
472   Appendix D.2.
473
      Few-shot meta-test fine-tuning examines a data-limited, few-shot scenario. A single few-shot task
474   is created by randomly sampling 5 classes and 5 examples per class from the meta-test dataset, cre-
475   ating a dataset with 25 total training examples. A new (randomly initialized) linear head is attached
476   for the 5-class classification task, and the model is finetuned for 100 steps using full batch gradient
477   descent. The transfer accuracy is evaluated on 100 randomly selected test examples for each of the 5
478   classes. The overall transfer accuracy is averaged over 100 few-shot tasks. This setting corresponds
479   to the “Avg 5-shot Transfer Accuracy” metric (fourth column) in Figure 2. Hyperparameter details
480   for this setting are in Appendix D.2.

      7    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
522
523
524   In this work, we introduced the downstream free energy as a Bayesian model selection criterion for
525   quantifying the adaptability of pretraining checkpoints, offering a principled way to predict their
526   performance on unseen downstream tasks. Our key insight is that checkpoints with lower down-
527   stream free energy are more adaptable, making them ideal candidates for fine-tuning. Our empirical
528   results validate the utility of the pretraining free energy as a practical checkpoint selection criterion,
529   especially in scenarios where downstream data is scarce or inaccessible.
530   Despite the promising results, some limitations remain. First, our analysis currently lacks a direct
531   link between downstream free energy and downstream predictive performance. At the moment,
532   we provide a rigorous connection only when downstream adaptation is performed in a Bayesian
533   manner (see Appendix A). However, while Bayesian deep learning is not yet widely adopted due to
534   its computational overhead, this link may become valuable as computational barriers are reduced,
535   particularly in fine-tuning scenarios.
536   In addition, while our theoretical framework supports the use of free energy as a selection criterion,
537   the practical computation of free energy, in the form of the pretraining WBIC as in equation 14,
538   remains challenging for large models. An alternative approach may involve identifying computa-
539   tionally efficient “levers” that influence pretraining free energy, allowing us to improve downstream
      adaptation performance without relying on direct computation of the pretraining WBIC.

      E    C OMPARISON OF WITH OTHER PRETRAINING METRICS
919
920
      Recent work of Galanti et al. (2022) and Munn et al. (2024) examine the role of neural collapse
921   and geometric complexity in transfer learning and suggest that this bias during pretraining towards
922   low geometric complexity (and thus neural collapse) can help to explain the mechanisms behind
923   the success of transfer learning. In short, their works shows that lower Geometric Complexity in
924   a pre-trained embedding network promotes neural collapse on new target classes, simplifying the
925   fine-tuning process and thus leading to improved downstream accuracy. As described in Section 2,
926   one way to interpret their work is that the model geometric complexity or neural collapse can serve
927   an an informative pretraining metric for assessing the quality of a model checkpoint with respect to
928   success adaptation.
929   To assess the effectiveness of our free energy strategy in comparison to these other pretraining
930   metrics, we conducted a correlation analysis, as summarized in Table 1. The Pearson correlation
931   coefficients Pearson & Galton (1895) presented in the table were calculated using model checkpoints
932   obtained from experiments with CIFAR-FS, trained on ResNet-18 to convergence.
933   These experiments, detailed in Section 6, involved a comprehensive exploration of the hyperpa-
934   rameter space. We swept across three hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, and momentum),
935   with six values for learning rate, six for batch size, and five for momentum. Each configuration
936   was trained with five different random seeds, resulting in a total of 85 model checkpoints. For
937   each checkpoint, we compared the Geometric Complexity, Neural Collapse, and Free Energy of
938   the pretrained model to its downstream performance, measured via both full meta-test fine-tuning
939   and few-shot meta-test fine-tuning. Notably, as indicated by the Pearson correlation coefficients in
940   Table 1, the pretraining Free Energy exhibits a substantially stronger correlation with downstream
941
      performance than other metrics considered.
