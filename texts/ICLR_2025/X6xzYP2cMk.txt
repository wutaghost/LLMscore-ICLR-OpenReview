011                                                        A BSTRACT
012
013                Attention layers are the core component of transformers, the current state-of-the-
014
                   art neural network architecture. However, softmax-based attention causes trans-
                   formers to be more challenging to train. Even at initialisation, the propagation of
015
                   signals and gradients through the random network can be pathological, resulting
016
                   in known issues such as (i) vanishing/exploding gradients and (ii) rank collapse,
017                i.e. when all tokens converge to a single representation with depth. This paper
018                examines signal propagation in attention-only transformers from a random ma-
019                trix perspective, illuminating the origin of such issues, as well as unveiling a new
020                phenomenon—(iii) rank collapse in width. Modelling softmax-based attention at
021                initialisation with Random Markov matrices, our theoretical analysis reveals that a
022                spectral gap between the two largest singular values of the attention matrix causes
023                (iii), which, in turn, exacerbates (i) and (ii). Building on this insight, we propose
024                a novel, yet simple, practical solution to resolve rank collapse in width by remov-
025
                   ing the spectral gap. Moreover, we validate our findings and discuss the training
                   benefits of the proposed fix through experiments1 that also motivate a revision of
026
                   some of the default parameter scaling. Our attention model accurately describes
027
                   the standard key-query attention in a single-layer transformer, making this work a
028                significant first step towards a better understanding of the initialisation dynamics
029                in the multi-layer case.

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Transformers Vaswani et al. (2017) have emerged as the dominant architecture in machine learning,
      achieving remarkable success across various domains, particularly in natural language processing
035
      and computer vision, largely due to their defining feature: the self-attention mechanism Bahdanau
036
      et al. (2016). However, despite their empirical success, transformers are often plagued by training
037   instability and high sensitivity to numerous hyperparameters, which require careful tuning. This
038   challenge has motivated recent efforts to establish a theoretical framework for understanding trans-
039   former architectures, even in their most basic forms, to ensure reliable information flow through
040   deeper layers and facilitate training.
041
      The purpose of this work is to analyse signal propagation in softmax-based attention layers at ini-
042
      tialisation, i.e. with randomly initialised model parameters. While the issues of rank collapse (in
043   depth) and vanishing/exploding gradients have been previously identified in transformers at initial-
044   isation Dong et al. (2021); Noci et al. (2022), our work formalises these findings and uncovers an
045   additional phenomenon—rank collapse in width—due to the use of softmax in the self-attention
046   mechanism. Rank collapse in width has not been identified in the existing literature nor been recog-
047   nised as a catalyst for rank collapse along depth. By leveraging spectral properties of the random
048   matrices formed by the model’s parameters, we reveal the emergence of a spectral gap between the
049   two largest singular values of the attention matrix, which drives rank collapse in width and further
050   accelerates rank collapse in depth. Moreover, we propose a provably effective remedy for the spec-
051
      tral gap, a solution that naturally arises when the problem is viewed through a spectral lens. To the
      best of our knowledge, a spectral analysis of signal propagation has yet not been undertaken in the
052
      context of transformers.

      Let us consider the eigenvalues of an attention matrix. Since the rows sum to 1, there is an eigenvalue
055   of 1 corresponding to the all-ones vector. Under certain conditions, the other eigenvalues shrink in
056   size as the matrix dimension increases, resulting in a widening gap between the largest eigenvalue
057   (which is 1) and the diminishing bulk of eigenvalues; see Figure 1. The successive multiplication
058   of such matrices at each layer increasingly favours a specific direction—the one aligned with the
059   dominant eigenvector of the attention matrix—over the others. This leads to a distortion in the
060   geometry of the input training data, exemplified by the phenomenon of rank collapse. A natural
061   solution is then to project out this troublesome direction from all attention matrices to enable a
062   more balanced signal propagation. This intuitive idea is central to our rigorous analysis of a simple
063
      transformer, from which we draw insights to introduce a slightly modified attention layer that proves
      advantageous even when incorporated into more complex architectures.

      1.2   R ELATED WORK
163
164   Rank collapse in transformers was first explored in Dong et al. (2021), where the authors show that
165   the output of an attention-only transformer converges exponentially with depth to a single represen-
166   tation across tokens. A connection between rank collapse and vanishing gradients is made in Noci
167   et al. (2022). By assuming uniform attention, the work of Noci et al. (2022) essentially reduces to
168   proving rank collapse in depth, based on the assumption that rank collapse in width has already oc-
169   curred (as opposed to showing why this premise holds). Our spectral analysis builds upon previous
170
      work that analysed the spectra of large random neural networks to better understand and stabilise
      initial training dynamics, such as Pennington et al. (2018) for fully-connected networks and Xiao
171
      et al. (2018) for convolutional networks.
172
173   It is also worth mentioning a line of research on possible alternatives to the softmax self-attention
174   mechanism. Hron et al. (2020) speculate the advantage of ReLU and identity over softmax in train-
175   ing accuracy. He et al. (2022) proposes an ad hoc initialisation scheme tailored to prevent the token-
176
      wise covariance kernel from losing rank through layers. Besides, several practical works attempt to
      replace softmax-based key-query attention with faster options that surpass the so-called quadratic
177
      bottleneck, e.g. Peng et al. (2020); Choromanski et al. (2020); Katharopoulos et al. (2020). Further-
178
      more, our provably effective adjustment to softmax by removing the spectral gap (or centering the
179   output) has been independently suggested in Ali et al. (2023); Noci et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2024) as
180   part of ad hoc solutions to stabilising signal propagation in transformers.

      1.3   O RGANISATION OF THE PAPER
183
184   In section 2, we first introduce our model and reexamine the phenomena of rank collapse and ex-
185   ploding gradients, demonstrating that both occur with increasing depth in our transformer model
186   at initialisation (Props. 3 and 4). Importantly, we diagnose for the first time an additional form of
187   rank-collapse with increasing width, that we call rank collapse in width. We formulate its exact rate
188   of decay in the context length as well as identify its root cause as being the spectral gap in softmax-
189   based attention. Next, we introduce a modified attention mechanism that is specifically designed
190
      to have no spectral gap. We prove that this modification simultaneously resolves rank collapse in
      width, thus mitigating rank collapse in depth, and exploding gradients (Props. 5 and 6), thereby
191
      confirming the role of the spectral gap in such issues. Lastly, we study the spectra of the covari-
192
      ance kernel and the input-output Jacobian in our modified model (Props. 7 and 8) and discuss the
193   possibility of further stabilising early training dynamics by tuning the initial distributions.
194
195   In section 3, we validate our findings, providing empirical evidence of rank collapse in both width
196
      and depth, as well as exploding gradients. We put to test our “remove the gap” solution across a
      range of architectures featuring LayerNorm and skip connections, discussing its possible training
197
      benefits. Finally, we present experiments that challenge the soundness of certain default scaling
198
      choices, such as Xavier initialization for the keys and queries, suggesting that they may require
199   further revision in practice.

      Rank collapse. We highlight the practical relevance of our analysis by showing rank collapse
362   occurs both in width and depth for famous transformer models like BERT, see Figure 3. As an
363   input signal propagates through a transformer, we can address both forms of rank collapse—across
364   width and depth—by eliminating the spectral gap induced by the attention matrix at each layer.
365   Figures 2 and 4 reinforce our findings, showing that our removing the gap consistently mitigates rank
366   collapse even in multi-layer transformers that include additional components such as LayerNorm,
367   skip connections, or both. It is crucial to understand that rank collapse in depth is an inherent
368   consequence of successive matrix multiplications. Therefore, architectural modifications can only
369   slow the collapse rather than completely prevent it. We demonstrate this by showing that rank
370
      collapse in depth persists even when the attention matrix is set to the identity matrix—an extreme
      case with the highest possible stable rank and no spectral gap. Another possible way to slow down
371
      rank collapse in depth (though not eliminate it) is to set the value matrices as orthogonal matrices.

      Scaling discussion. Our analysis provides practitioners with valuable insights into the scaling of
487   some key quantities in transformer architectures. First, in Figure 8a, we plot the training loss of a
488   single-layer transformer with key-query attention, for which our theory effectively predicts signal
489   propagation at the initialisation stage. Based on Proposition 1, when the keys WK and queries
490   WQ are initialised using Xavier’s initialisation scheme, the attention matrix rapidly converges to
491   degeneracy. Consequently, removing the spectral gap in this case effectively reduces the attention
492   matrix to 0. This explains the plateau in the training curve during the initial steps for the Xavier-
493   initialised model combined with the spectral gap removal. On the other hand, our proposed fix
494   achieves performance similar to that of the original variant. Another scaling that requires refinement
495
      is that of a skip connection. Traditionally, the value matrices are sampled from N (0, d−1 ), the
      attention matrix is softmax-based, and the signal propagates from one layer ℓ to the next as
496
497                                                                                   V
                                                                 Xℓ+1 = Aℓ+1 (Xℓ )Xℓ Wℓ+1 + Xℓ .

      In this scenario,
500   a factor of d relative to the skip branch, i.e. the signal coming from the previous layer. Therefore,
501
      if the attention mechanism is to be fairly represented in the signal’s propagation through depth, the
      values should instead be drawn from N (0, 1). If the value weights are sampled from N (0, d−1 ),
502
      the skip connection becomes what we refer to as “upscaled”, meaning each layer essentially passes

525   4            C ONCLUSION
526
527   We introduced a new mathematical framework for studying the self-attention mechanism at initiali-
528   sation, leveraging results from random matrix theory and free probability. By analysing the spectral
529   properties of Random Markov matrices, we diagnosed random softmax-based attention with a spec-
530
      tral gap that leads to rank collapse in width—a phenomenon revealed and demonstrated for the first
      time by our analysis—alongside the previously established rank collapse in depth and exploding
531
      gradients.
532
533   We proposed a straightforward modification of the attention mechanism, which proved effective in
534   slowing rank collapse when the spectrum contains a single outlier. Additionally, we observed that
535   the spectra of standard key-query attention matrices often feature multiple outliers. Our experiments
536
      also pointed to potential issues with some common initialisation schemes for transformers. We hope
      our work encourages the community to adopt a spectral perspective in investigating more complex
537
      transformer architectures and attention models.

1128
1129   Architecture. The default model consists of a stack of single-head attention layers, with an op-
1130   tional LayerNorm inserted between them (denoted by “+ LN” in the legend) after receiving an
1131   optional skip connection from the previous layer (denoted by “+ skip” in the legend). When both
1132   options are enabled simultaneously, the configuration is referred to as “+ skip + LN”. By single-head,
1133   we mean that only one attention mechanism is computed, applied to the values and then multiplied
       by a matrix Wh which is initialised as the identity matrix and optimised during training.


                                                                 21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       Attention design. At initialisation, when the attention is labelled as “A”, the matrix is sampled
1135   from the set of Random Markov matrices, as defined in Definition 1.1, with a variance of σA = 1.
1136   To achieve this, we sample a random matrix B with i.i.d. lognormal entries and apply softmax row-
1137   wise such that A := softmax(B). The moments of B are adjusted precisely so that σA = 1. During
1138   training, the entries of B are optimised. If “Identity A” is chosen, the attention matrix is a constant
1139   equal to the identity only at initialisation and then optimised at training time. When the attention
1140   is labelled as “A(X)”, the key/query matrices are sampled from i.i.d. Gaussian matrices N (0, 1)
1141   and the standard key-query attention matrix is formed. If a mention to “Xavier” appears in the
1142   legend, it means the key-query matrices are sampled from a rescaled Gaussian N (0, d−1   qk ). Updates
                              Q         K
1143   are performed on W and W . If the label indicates a “⊥”, the forward pass of the attention
1144   mechanism is systematically (at initialisation and for all following training steps) adjusted so that
1145   the spectral gap is removed, as in our modified model (9).
1146
1147   Training. Given some isometric X0 input data, the goal is to learn the entrywise Heaviside func-
1148
       tion, a non-trivial task due to the function’s nonlinearity. To achieve this, we train a series of
       attention-only transformer encoders on a mean squared error (MSE) loss, optimised with Adam.
1149
       We conduct an extensive grid search over the learning rate λ ∈ {1, 3, 5} × 10−{1,2,3,4,5} . Each
1150
       experiment is run 5 times, and the learning rate that results in the best average training performance
1151
       for each configuration, as shown in the plots, is selected. The figures display the training loss with
1152   respect to training steps, i.e. the number of gradient descent updates. A “no training” label is shown
1153   when no training progress is made after 50 training epochs, despite tuning the learning rate. We
1154   train on a set of 50 data points, each of size T × d, with T = d = 500 to ensure we are in the large
1155   width regime that our theoretical framework presupposes.
