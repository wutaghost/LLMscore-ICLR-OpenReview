009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Finetuning large language models (LLMs) using contrastive learning objectives
012            has become the dominant approach for representation learning in general-purpose
013            text embedding tasks. Our work seeks to enable going beyond strictly positive (or
014            negative) pairs of text, to more fine-grained annotations that can capture the nuances
015            of complex language tasks. We propose training text encoders with a simple pair
016            classification loss that utilizes binary cross-entropy on relevance labels. When
017            compared to the standard softmax-based loss for multi-class classification against
018
               multiple text alternatives, we find that training with our proposed loss improves the
               average score across 56 English language tasks of the Massive Text Embedding
019
               Benchmark (MTEB), while finetuning the same Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct model
020
               on the same mix of open datasets. Furthermore, our models excel in the Pair
021            Classification and the Semantic Textual Similarity benchmarks, outperforming
022            many models that are trained on more extensive data. Finally, thorough experiments
023            using graded relevance data from TREC-DL 2023 during training demonstrate that
024            binary cross-entropy provides generalization improvements that the softmax-based
025            loss fails to achieve.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Sentence embeddings form the foundation of many deep learning approaches to natural language
031   understanding and have been extensively studied in the literature Gao et al. (2021); Reimers &
032   Gurevych (2019). A well-designed text embedder is crucial for achieving high performance across
033
      a range of downstream tasks such as machine translation, information retrieval, and sentiment
      analysis Muennighoff et al. (2023). Ideally, it is desirable to have a single text encoder that maximizes
034
      the performance across all such tasks, and hence developing robust and universal sentence embedders
035
      has become a focal point in the field of natural language processing.
036
037   Designing universal sentence embedders that generalize well across diverse downstream tasks,
038   however, remains a significant challenge. In itself, natural language is not a clear and unambiguous
039
      means of communication; it is shaped by context, speaker intention, and the specific task at hand. The
      contextual dependencies in language vary significantly depending on the goal (i.e. downstream task).
040
      For example, a sentence in a legal document might emphasize precision and formal terminology,
041
      whereas the same sentence in a friendly email could rely on informal tone and inferred meaning.
042
043   Generally, text encoders trained with contrastive objectives have relied on CLIP-style (Radford et al.,
044   2021) softmax-based losses. That is, given two aligned batches of data of size n, a similarity matrix
045
      with dimension n × n is created by scoring all against all points across the two batches. Each row
      or column (or often both) is then treated as the parameters of a categorical random variable, and
046
      maximum likelihood estimation can be performed by simply placing labels at the main diagonal.
047
      While effective, this common approach is known to be highly sensitive to the choice of batch size.
048   This is due to the dimension of the categorical random variables described above — or analogously, to
049   the number of classes in a multi-class classification setting — being tied to the batch size. Alternative
050   training objectives were introduced as variations of CLIP, especially so for the case of discriminative
051   vision-language models such as SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023). In that case, each entry in a similarity
052   matrix obtained for aligned batches of data is treated as the parameter of a Bernoulli random variable,
053   so maximum likelihood estimates are given by the minimizer of a binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss.
      Unlike the softmax setting used in CLIP, losses that operate at each entry of the similarity matrix are


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      less affected by the choice of the batch size, for instance, and have yielded state-of-the-art vision-text
055   encoders. Here, we not only leverage these advantages observed in the text-vision case to improve
056   training of text-only bi-encoders, but also take advantage of the fact that pair-wise losses offer the
057   opportunity to use more nuanced annotations. This is especially true in cases where different types of
058   negative or positive samples are available. For instance, if both easy and hard negative examples are
059   available, one can reflect that in the choice of labels and, for example, put more pressure towards
060   matching harder negatives than easier ones.
061   In this paper, we demonstrate the potential of using BCE loss for training universal sentence embed-
062   ders. When training the same models with the same training data which use binary relevance labels
063   (e.g. relevant, non-relevant), the BCE loss matches and even outperforms softmax-based contrastive
064   loss in downstream tasks. When applied to tasks with graded relevance labels (e.g. relevant, partially
065   relevant, non-relevant), BCE proves to be a much better training objective, significantly outperforming
066   the traditional softmax-based contrastive loss. The flexibility of the BCE loss to handle more nuanced
067   labels can more effectively tackle the ambiguity and contextual variability of natural language.

      2     P RELIMINARIES
070

      2.1   G ENERALIST T EXT E MBEDDERS
072
073   The purpose of building a generalist embedder is to have a single model that can provide text em-
074   beddings for several downstream tasks including, but not limited to, retrieval, text classification, and
075   clustering Conneau et al. (2017); Reimers & Gurevych (2019); Karpukhin et al. (2020); Muennighoff
076   (2022). Initial approaches to train a generalist embedder relied on a multi-stage pipeline. The
077   first stage involved training BERT-based bidirectional encoders on large-scale self-supervised data
078   (typically comprising of 1B sentence pairs). This was then followed by fine-tuning the pretrained
079   model on smaller-scale supervised data (typically involving 1M sentence pairs) Ni et al. (2022); Wang
080   et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023a); Xiao et al. (2024); Su et al. (2023a); Asai et al. (2023).
081   More recently, many works have proposed converting decoder-only-LLMs into text-encoders using
082   architecture modifications (BehnamGhader et al., 2024), using prompting techniques Springer et al.
083   (2024), or by simply fine-tuning with task-specific supervised data Neelakantan et al. (2022). These
084   approaches demonstrate superior performance than bidirectional encoders and eliminate the need
085   for large-scale self-supervised training (Wang et al., 2023). Moreover, using LLMs for text encod-
086   ing allows us to leverage their instruction-following capabilities to distinguish between different
087
      downstream tasks. Additionally, LLMs offer the flexibility to generalize to new tasks using natural
      language instructions For example, given the input “Retrieve relevant documents for this user query: I
088
      need a new laptop”, the model would generate the embedding suitable for document search, whereas,
089
      for the same model, the input “Classify the product category: I need a new laptop” would generate
090   embedding suitable for product classification.
091
092   In this work, we focus on LLM2Vec (BehnamGhader et al., 2024) – a three-step approach to
093
      converting any decoder-only LLM into a text encoder. Our choice is driven by LLM2Vec’s open-
      source availability, use of only publicly available data for training, and its strong performance across
094
      a wide variety of text-based tasks on the MTEB benchmark, making it both a versatile and accessible
095
      solution for sentence embedding. LLM2Vec models are built on the hypothesis that the causal
096   attention mechanism of LLMs is sub-optimal for text encodings. Therefore, these models first remove
097   the causal mask of the LLM to enable better contextualization with bidirectional connections. The
098   model is then trained with a masked next-token prediction objective to adapt it to bidirectional
099   attention. Finally, the model can be fine-tuned with either SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021) in unsupervised
100   settings, or with typical contrastive learning in presence of supervised data.
101
102   2.2   T EXT E MBEDDING B ENCHMARK
103
104   The most widely-used benchmark for evaluating generalist text embedders is the Massive Text Embed-
105   ding Benchmark (MTEB, Muennighoff et al. 2023), which contains several downstream embedding
106   tasks across multiple languages. Following BehnamGhader et al. (2024), we evaluate our approach on
107   the English MTEB benchmark, containing 56 tasks across seven task categories – retrieval, reranking,
      clustering, pair classification, classification, sentence similarity, and summarization.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Retrieval and reranking tasks are evaluated by encoding query and document separately using
109   the embedder, and scoring each query-document pair using cosine similarity of their respective
110   embeddings. This setup, typically referred to as bi-encoder Guu et al. (2020); Karpukhin et al. (2020);
111   Santhanam et al. (2022), allows scaling to extremely large document collections using efficient
112   indexing and search techniques (Johnson et al., 2019).
113
      Clustering is evaluated by computing the v-measure score (Rosenberg & Hirschberg, 2007) on text
114
      embeddings clustered using k-means. The pair classification task involves assigning a binary label
115   to a pair of sentences. To reformulate it as an embedding task, both sentences are first embedded,
116   and the label is determined by applying a threshold to their similarity score (e.g., cosine similarity).
117   Summarization and text similarity tasks follow a similar approach: for summarization, human and
118   machine-written summaries are treated as text pairs, and sentence similarity is determined by the use
119   of continuous labels instead of binary ones. Classification is assessed by training a logistic regression
120   classifier on training set embeddings and testing on the validation set, with classifier performance
121   serving as a proxy for embedding quality.
122
123   2.3   G RADED R ELEVANCE
124
125   Softmax-based contrastive learning has been the de-facto paradigm for training dense retrievers and
126
      generalist embedders. The training loss formulation in this case relies on binary relevance, i.e. a
      boolean assignment (relevant or non-relevant) for every text pair. However, relying only on binary
127
      relevance misses out on the potentially rich supervision signal that comes from graded relevance,
128
      which allows for a more nuanced assessment.
129
130   Graded relevance refers to the extent to which a text pair is relevant, often quantified using an ordinal
131   scale to capture varying levels of relevance (e.g., 0–3 or 0–5). Historically, evaluating the graded
132
      relevance assignment has been the primary method for assessing the quality of a retrieval system
      within the information retrieval community (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2000; 2002; Sakai, 2021). For
133
      instance, in the Deep Learning track of the Text REtrieval Conference (TREC-DL) 2023 (Craswell
134
      et al., 2024), models are evaluated based on how closely their scores align with human judgment
135   of graded relevance for query-document pairs. These graded relevance scores range from 0 to 3,
136   representing the following levels of increasing relevancy:
137
138          • 0 = Irrelevant,
139          • 1 = Relevant topic, but does not contain the answer,
140
             • 2 = Highly relevant if it contains a partial answer or if the answer is unclear, or
141
142          • 3 = Perfectly relevant, containing the exact answer.

146
      In this section, we describe our approach for training generalist text embedders using binary cross-
147   entropy loss formulation, which is better suited to capture the nuanced assessment of graded relevance.

151   We first introduce the standard supervised framework for training text encoders. In this setup, the
152   training data is typically provided to the learning algorithm as tuples of related texts. Each tuple
153   consists of an anchor text, a positively associated text, and possibly a hard negative text. These
154   associations are task-specific, with each tuple assigned to a particular task objective. The resulting
155   embeddings are often task-conditioned by combining a task description sentence with either the
156   anchor text, in the case of asymmetric tasks, or both the anchor and associated texts. Such a
157   combination can be as simple as text concatenation, formatted as “{task instruction}: {text}”.
158   For instance, for a symmetric task like semantic textual similarity (STS), both the anchor and
159   associated text are generic sentences, and are combined with the instruction “Retrieve semantically
160   similar text”. In contrast, for an asymmetric task like passage retrieval, where the anchor is a query
161   and the associated text is a passage, only the query is combined with the task instruction, “Given a
      query, retrieve relevant passages that answer the query”. Details of what constitutes an anchor and

341   The LLM2Vec approach for converting LLMs into encoders consists of three steps: (1) enabling
342   bidirectional connections, (2) using masked next token prediction (MNTP) to adapt these connections,
343   and (3) applying supervised contrastive learning. In our setup, we initialize models with the MNTP
344   checkpoint of LLM2Vec models to fairly compare the performance of softmax-based contrastive
345   loss against our proposed binary cross-entropy loss for supervised training. Specifically, we use
346   Meta-LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct (hereafter referred to as Meta-LLaMA-3), and Sheared-LLaMA-1.3B
347
      (hereafter referred to as S-LLaMA) to evaluate performance across different model sizes.
348   We also provide experiments with Mistral-7B-Instruct (hereafter referred to as Mistral) and Qwen2-
349   7B-Instruct (hereafter referred to as Qwen2), for which we adopt a simpler conversion strategy than
350   LLM2Vec. In particular, we initialize from pretrained foundational models and we simply enable
351   bidirectional connections between tokens during finetuning with contrastive learning.
352   Training details. We employ the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.995.
353   We apply no weight decay. For the learning rate scheduler, we adopt a linear warmup over 300 steps,
354   followed by a linear decay until 10,000 steps. The logit scale is tuned. For the binary cross-entropy
355   loss, we find the optimal value to be 20, which corresponds to a temperature of 0.05. In our generalist
356   text encoder experiments, we make use of in-batch negatives and apply the loss function described
357   in Equation (2). We implement task-conditioned sampling for batching, in which all the samples
358   in a batch belong to the same task. Doing so improves the quality of in-batch negatives as all the
359
      associated text belong to the same domain. We describe training in greater detail in Section 7 of the
      Appendix, along with details about model selection where we define the validation score we use in
360
      our studies, as well as for hyperparameter search.

415   we report the average 56-task MTEB score of the best models found during training. In Figure 3b we
416   observe that BCE outperforms the softmax variant for all noise levels pnoise ∈ {0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5}.
417   We hypothesize that BCE achieves better robustness to weakly-supervised dataset noise, because the
418   effect of noise is more diluted in the gradients of the BCE objective. Suppose the binary label case
419   with hard negatives. At each training step the model encounters a batch of size B, the BCE-loss then
420   is the average of losses from 2B 2 binary classification predictions, while softmax-based loss is the
421   average of losses from B multi-class classification predictions. Assuming that the in-batch negatives
422
      are indeed true negatives: If one pair of texts in the batch is mislabeled, then for the softmax-loss
      this means that the B1 predictions is optimized towards a false target, whereas for the BCE-loss B12
423
      predictions is optimized towards a false target.

527   7      C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work we explore training universal text encoders with binary cross-entropy loss. We show that
530   the resulting models match or outperform those trained with the typical softmax-based contrastive
531   loss (Section 5) on general text embedding downstream tasks. Furthermore, our proposal enables the
532   effective use of graded relevance scores, commonly used by the information retrieval community,
533   to provide more fine-grained training signal. This leads to significant improvements in nuanced
534   evaluations that go beyond a binary determination of query-document relevance (Section 6). In
535   contrast, training with softmax-based contrastive loss does not lead to similar advantages.
536   As models continue to advance on existing benchmarks, there is an increasing need for more nuanced
537   performance measures to capture differences in their capabilities. One approach is to introduce graded
538   relevance scores for text embedding tasks that can accommodate these distinctions. Our proposal will
539   prove useful to future generations of text encoders aiming to provide with a more detailed assessment
      of textual relevance.

759
760   Past work on representation learning with binary cross-entropy losses. To our knowledge, binary
761   cross-entropy loss in self-supervised representation learning was first explored by Hjelm et al. (2019).
762   Their work introduces Deep InfoMax (DIM), a framework for representation learning that maximizes
763   mutual information between different views of the same image. In its Jensen-Shannon mutual
764   information formulation (Hjelm et al., 2019, See Equation 4, Section 3.1), the DIM loss functions as
765   a binary cross-entropy loss and the logits are computed from pairs of views: pairs derived from the
766
      same image are labeled as positive, while pairs from different images are labeled as negative.
767   More recently, Zhai et al. (2023) introduced SigLIP, a framework that pretrains vision-language
768   encoders by aligning image embeddings with their corresponding caption embeddings. Compared
769   to its softmax-based anchor classification alternative (Radford et al., 2021, CLIP), pretraining with
770   binary cross-entropy loss in SigLIP has demonstrated better scaling of downstream task performance
771   as the number of in-batch negatives increases. In our work, we adapt the SigLIP objective to fine-tune
772
      large language models (LLMs) (BehnamGhader et al., 2024) for general-purpose text embedding
      tasks.
773
774   Pointwise regression-based ranking. While our focus is on general-purpose text embeddings, related
775   work can be found in training cross-encoding pointwise ranking models for information retrieval. Zou
776   et al. (2021) mobilize training objectives on graded relevance labels using regression-based losses,
777   however they do not prescribe a way to deal with the imbalances of the training relevance data. Our
778
      work fills that gap by allowing pointwise training on imbalanced relevance data.

920   Noise-robustness is important for dense text encoders. It is generally assumed that positively
921   paired documents (x, y + ) in dense encoding datasets are correctly aligned, adhering to the rules of
922   some task instruction. However, this assumption is difficult to satisfy in real-world applications (Qu
923   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022). In practice, many training data pairs are collected automatically
924   without human inspection, and this inevitably leads to the inclusion of some mismatched pairs. For
925   example, in order to mine positive passages from QA datasets, Karpukhin et al. (2020) declare the
926
      highest-ranked passage from BM25 (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009) that contains the answer as the
      positive passage. Although it is hard to measure the level of noise without explicitly asking for human
927
      annotations, it is very possible that such a process generates false positives by returning passages that
928
      do not answer the query at hand even if they have a high lexical match. Furthermore, it is increasingly
929   common to automatically infer a set of hard negatives for each x in the dataset. A standard recipe for
930   mining these is to sample from the top-k documents in a corpus using a retriever, like BM25 in the
931   case of DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020), or one that is based on a dense encoder, as in the case of ANCE
932   (Xiong et al., 2021) and NV-Retriever (de Souza P. Moreira et al., 2024). While Wang et al. (2023)
933   has clearly demonstrated that the inclusion of hard negatives leads to downstream improvements,
934   these procedures inadvertently introduce false negatives. Qu et al. (2021) examine the top-retrieved
935   passages that were not labeled as positives in the original MSMARCO (Bajaj et al., 2018) dataset,
936   and they find that 70% of them are actually positives. We thus argue that utilizing training objectives
937
      that are more robust to noise can lead to downstream improvements in text encoders.

       We use the validation sets from the document retrieval challenge of TREC-DL 2023 competition
       (Craswell et al., 2024) to define a population of query-document relevancy scores which we will
1105
       use to define the training and the validation set. In particular, we use 90% of the samples for the
1106
       training set and 10% for the validation set. Out of those 10%, we only keep those entries whose
1107   queries and documents do not overlap with any queries or documents contained in the training set. To
1108   create the test set, we use the provided test set from TREC-DL 2023. However, we find that the test
1109   set contains some overlap with the set of queries and documents used in the validation sets of the
1110   competition. We make sure that the test consists exclusively of held-out data, by removing the entries
1111   which correspond to queries and documents found in either of our created training set or validation
1112   set. After this procedure, we are left with a query-document dataset with graded relevance scores.
