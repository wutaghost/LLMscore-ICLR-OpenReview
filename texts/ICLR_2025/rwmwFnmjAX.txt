009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Instruction tuning constitutes a prevalent technique for tailoring Large Vision Lan-
012            guage Models (LVLMs) to meet individual task requirements. To date, most of
013            the existing approaches are confined to single-task adaptation, whereas the re-
014            quirements in real-world scenarios are inherently varied and continually evolv-
015            ing. Thus an ideal LVLM should sustain continual instruction tuning in the
016            face of stream-task distributions (i.e., different domains, emerging capabilities,
017            and new datasets) while minimizing the forgetting of previously acquired knowl-
018
               edge. To achieve this, we propose a new benchmark for COntinuAl inStruction
               Tuning on LVLMs (COAST), which encompasses the aforementioned domain-
019
               incremental, capability-incremental, and dataset-incremental configurations. In
020
               terms of methodology, we propose Continual LLaVA, a rehearsal-free method
021            tailored for continual instruction tuning in LVLMs. To circumvent the additional
022            overhead associated with experience replay, we freeze LVLMs and construct the
023            dual increment embeddings for each input instruction to facilitate parameter-
024            efficient tuning. Specifically, the increment embeddings can be decomposed into
025            two principal components: 1) intrinsic increment embeddings to encode task-
026            specific characteristics. To achieve this, we set up a low-rank pool containing
027            candidate embeddings, from which we select the relevant ones based on their sim-
028            ilarity with the user instructions; 2) contextual increment embeddings to investi-
029
               gate the inter-dependencies across tasks. In this regard, the low-rank embeddings
               chosen in the previous tasks are aggregated via learnable weighted sum to provide
030
               complementary hints. Extensive experiments indicate that the proposed Contin-
031
               ual LLaVA outperforms previous methods by significantly reducing the forgetting
032            during the continual instruction tuning process.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT (Achiam et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020) and LLaMA
038   (Touvron et al., 2023a;b) have demonstrated impressive abilities in comprehending user instructions
039   and generating reliable responses. Building upon these achievements, recent advancements in Large
040   Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) (Li et al., 2023b; Alayrac et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023a; Liu
041   et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a; Zhan et al., 2024) integrates visual perception capa-
042   bilities into LLMs, which has sparked considerable research interest.
043   Beyond the language understanding and generation ability, one prominent characteristic of LLMs
044   and LVLMs is the emergent capability of instruction following (Ouyang et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
045   2023b), i.e., faithfully responding to specific instructions and adhering to human preference. In-
046   struction tuning enables LVLMs to generalize to unseen tasks by following task-specific instruc-
047   tions. Currently, most existing LVLMs are finetuned on the single instruction-tuning dataset. How-
048   ever, users’ requirements are constantly evolving in practical applications. The robust and flexible
049   LVLMs are expected to be continuously fine-tuned with stream instruction tuning datasets without
050
      the “catastrophic forgetting” (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989) of previously learned knowledge.
051   Compared to the well-defined per-category continual learning in image classification or object de-
052   tection (Wang et al., 2024), the continual instruction tuning setting in LVLMs has not been clearly
053   established. To this end, we collect and re-purpose existing benchmarks to construct a novel bench-
      mark for COntinuAl inStruction Tuning (COAST) on LVLMs. Specifically, we set up three contin-

      In summary, our contributions are in three-folds:
115
116    • We collect and re-purpose existing benchmarks to curate COAST as a continual instruction tuning
117      benchmark with the domain-wise, capability-wise and dataset-wise incremental learning settings.
118    • We propose a novel Continual LLaVA model, a lifelong LVLM to facilitate the continual instruc-
119      tion tuning across different domains, functional capabilities, or diverse datasets through learning
120      parameterized intrinsic and contextual knowledge.
121    • Experimental results have manifested the state-of-the-art performance of our Continual LLaVA.
122      For example on COAST-domain, Continua LLaVA surpasses the sequential training by achieving
123      13.06% absolute improvement in average accuracy and 13.25% reduction in average forgetting.

125   2   R ELATED W ORK
126
127   Large Vision-Language Models. LVLMs (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024b;
128   Sun et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023) have garnered substantial research attention by advancing and
129   integrating visual understanding and generation capabilities into LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Anil
130   et al., 2023). A typical LVLM can be abstracted into three components, i.e., a pre-trained vision
131   encoder (Radford et al., 2021; Kirillov et al., 2023), a pre-trained LLM (Chiang et al., 2023a),
132   and an interface connector in between. The primary attempt Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) fuses
133   the visual embedding into textual tokens of LLMs via cross-modal attention. The following works
134   convert visual embeddings into LLM-understandable tokens using multi-layer perceptron (Liu et al.,
135
      2024b; Sun et al., 2024), Q-former (Bai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b), or discretization tokenizer
      (Jin et al., 2023). Our Continual LLaVA follows the LLaVA-styled (Liu et al., 2024b) multi-layer
136
      perceptron architecture due to its efficient setup, outstanding performance, and extensive usage.
137
138   Instruction Tuning in LVLMs. LVLMs typically undergo the following stages of training, i.e., pre-
139   training (Lin et al., 2024), instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022), and optional alignment tuning
140   (Sun et al., 2023; Ziegler et al., 2019). Among them, instruction tuning boosts the zero-shot or few-
141
      shot performance by generalizing LVLMs into unseen tasks by following task-specific instructions
      (Wei et al., 2022; Park et al., 2024). To achieve this, open-source LVLMs generate high-quality
142
      instruction-tuning datasets through self-instruction (Wang et al., 2023c), which prompts closed-
143
      source LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023) to generate instruction-following data using a few in-context
144   examples. Cambrian (Tong et al., 2024) has compiled all the available datasets and restructured
145   them into instruction tuning format. Most existing approaches limit their focus to instruction tuning
146   for a specific task, overlooking the essential area of continuous instruction tuning for stream tasks.
147
      We offer a detailed review of the limited research on continual learning for LVLMs, including recent
148
      pre-print works (Chen et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; He et al., 2023; Zhai et al.,
149
      2023). EMT (Zhai et al., 2023) focuses on the influence of fine-tuning LVLMs on image classifi-
150   cation performance of the vision encoder, rather than on the instruction-following ability that our
151   study prioritizes. While (Zhu et al., 2024) examines the performance trade-off between pre-trained
152   and fine-tuned models, it does not involve the continual tuning in the more challenging streaming
153   data. The pre-print works (Chen et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; He et al., 2023) focus on continual
154   instruction tuning but are limited to the dataset-incremental scenario. In contrast, we advance them
155   by categorizing continual instruction tuning along three dimensions, (i.e., domain, capability, and
156   dataset), thoroughly addressing practical and real-world demands.
157   Continual Learning. Inspired by the incremental learning pattern (Chen & Liu, 2022; Wang et al.,
158   2024) observed in human brains, continual learning focuses on the sequential training paradigm on
159   a series of tasks with the expectation of maintaining performance across all tasks (Wang et al., 2024;
160   Lee et al., 2017; McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). Early attempts adopt the regularization methodology
161   (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017; Feng et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024) to penalize the
      updates to parameters that are important for previous tasks. Subsequent architecture-based works

210    • Visual Embedding: Given the i-th input image of t-th task vti , we follow (Liu et al., 2024b;a) to

213      understandable space. In experiments, the CLIP encoder is kept frozen and the linear projector
214      is initialized using the pre-trained weights from (Liu et al., 2024a).
215    • Textual Embedding: For the input instruction sti , we adopt the widely used BPE tokenizer (Sen-
         nrich et al., 2016) to obtain the textual embeddings.

350   Evaluation Metrics. We customize the standard continual learning metrics (Wang et al., 2024;
351   Chaudhry et al., 2018) for our continual instruction tuning scenario. We have set up two metrics for
352   evaluation: 1) average accuracy represents the overall assessment of all the task performance. It is
353   typically defined as the mean of the accuracy values obtained throughout all the tasks; 2) average
354   forgetting aims to quantify the extent to which a model forgets previously learned tasks as it learns
355   new ones. It is defined as the mean reduction between the maximum accuracy throughout the past
356   learning process and the final accuracy. We follow (Liu et al., 2023b; Yin et al., 2024; Tong et al.,
357
      2024) to employ GPT-assisted assessment (we use GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) for grading) to evaluate
      the quality, relevance, and usefulness of model’s predictions. Refer to Appendix A.1 for detailed
358
      explanations of the metrics and the grader prompt for GPT-4o.
359
360   Compared Methods. We consider the following methods for comparisons with Continual LLaVA:
361   1) Sequential training refers to the process of incrementally training a model on new tasks, where
362   the model’s parameters are initialized using weights pre-trained on previous tasks; 2) Rehearsal
363
      training involves the practice of replaying previously encountered data, often stored in a buffer, and
      integrating it with new tasks during the training process. Following (He et al., 2021; Huang et al.,
364
      2021), the buffer size is defined as 1% of the entire training task size; 3) Popular continual learning
365
      methods including regularization-based approaches (i.e., EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) and LWF
366   (Li & Hoiem, 2017)) and prompt-based methods (i.e., L2P (Wang et al., 2022b), Dual (Wang et al.,
367   2022a) and CODA (Smith et al., 2023)); 4) Joint training involves supplying the model with the full
368   stream dataset simultaneously and training on all tasks collectively. This is typically regarded as the
369   upper-bound performance of continual learning.
370
      Implementation Details. We randomly sample three task orders from all the possible permutations
371
      of task compositions and report the mean results of average accuracy and average forgetting from
372   the selected task orders. The specific task orders are available in Table 4 and Appendix A.1. The
373   visual projector is implemented as two linear projection layers with a GELU activation function in
374   between. The low-rank pool size N , the selected number M , and the rank number R are respectively
375   specified as 32, 4, and 8. We set the batch size to 32 and the learning rate η to 4 × 10−5 with a cosine
376   decay schedule. The training process lasts for 2 epochs and the warm-up ratio is configured as 0.03.
377   Following (Hu et al., 2021), the low-rank components An and Bn in Eq 1 are initialized with the
      zero and normal distribution, respectively.

406   4.2   P ERFORMANCE A NALYSIS
407
408   The experimental results for COAST-domain, COAST-capability and COAST-dataset are demon-
409   strated in Table 1, Table 2 and Table 3, respectively. The comparisons highlight that Continual
410   LLaVA consistently outperforms sequential training, rehearsal training, and leading continual learn-
411   ing methods in both average accuracy and average forgetting. For example, on COAST-domain,
412   Continual LLaVA achieves an average accuracy of 37.08%, exceeding sequential training by a mar-
413
      gin of 13.06%. Additionally, Continual LLaVA demonstrates a notably lower average forgetting
      than other approaches, further validating its ability to mitigate forgetting across different domains.
414
      Taking the sequential training and rehearsal training as examples, our approach reduced the forget-
415
      ting rate by 13.25% (2.58% v.s. 15.83%) and 13.03% (2.58% v.s. 15.61%), respectively. Notably,
416   our improvements come with the benefit of fewer tunable parameters. Our parameter-efficient tun-
417   ing leverages only 0.75M tunable parameters, in stark contrast to the 7.67B parameters demanded by
418   the sequential tuning. In summary, Continual LLaVA offers superior performance, less forgetting,
419   and reduced computational overhead.
420
      Through the comparisons under the domain, capability, and dataset incremental settings of COAST,
421
      we observe that the forgetting phenomenon of continual instruction learning is more pronounced
422   on COAST-dataset. Specifically, the average forgetting of sequential training on COAST-dataset
423   reaches 35.82%, respectively representing an absolute increase of 19.99% and 25.26% compared
424   to the performance on COAST-domain and COAST-capability. The reason may lie in the steam
425   datasets’ highly diverse distributions and the ambiguity of task boundaries, which complicates
426   LVLMs’ ability to choose between retaining or revising previously acquired knowledge.

531   5     C ONCLUSIONS
532
      This paper targets continual instruction tuning, which refers to the process of incrementally adapt-
533
      ing LVLM to new tasks by fine-tuning it with task-specific instructions. To establish an assessment
534   standard, we propose COAST as the benchmark for continual instruction tuning on LVLMs from
535   the domain-incremental, capability-incremental, and dataset-incremental perspectives. In addition,
536   we propose a parameter-efficient tuning method Continual LLaVA, which devises the intrinsic in-
537   crement embeddings to capture task-specific properties and contextual increment embeddings to
538   explore inter-task relational dependencies. Experimental results manifest that Continual LLaVA
539   significantly improves the overall performance and reduces catastrophic forgetting during the con-
      tinual instruction tuning process.

1321   Continual Learning for LLMs. Due to the massive parameter scale and complexity, continual
1322   learning for LLMs encounters multi-faceted challenges (Shi et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024b). Based
1323
       on the training process of LLMs, continual learning for LLMs (Bohao et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2022;
       Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) can be classified into three fields including continual pre-training, con-
1324
       tinual instruction tuning, and continual preference alignment.
1325
1326   Continual pre-training (Jin et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2022a; Ke et al., 2023) aims to incorporate up-
1327   dated world knowledge into LLMs by training them on extensive and diverse datasets. A prevalent
1328   application of continual pretraining involves dynamically gathering data from multiple sources in-
1329
       cluding news feeds (Sun et al., 2020) and scholarly articles (Cossu et al., 2024), enabling LLMs to
       stay aligned with up-to-date information (Jang et al., 2022b;a). Other methods tailor LLMs to spe-
1330
       cific fields via continual pre-training. (Xie et al., 2023) adapts LLMs into the financial understanding
1331
       and EcomGPT-CT (Ma et al., 2023) investigates continual pre-training in the E-commerce domain.
1332   (Gogoulou et al., 2023) enhances LLMs’ ability to understand regional dialects and contemporary
1333   slangs across diverse social and cultural groups.
1334
1335
       Continual instruction tuning (Zhang et al., 2023c; Wang et al., 2023b;a; Zhao et al., 2024) contin-
       uously finetunes LLMs on a sequence of task-specific instructions and develops the competence to
1336
       address emerging tasks. ProgPrompt (Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) keeps most parameters of LLMs
1337
       frozen and only trains a fixed set of prompt tokens for each new task. To alleviate the reliance on
1338   inference task-ID, SLM (Bohao et al., 2024) proposes a task-related knowledge retrieval technique
1339   to enable adaptive adjustment for downstream tasks. LLaMA Pro (Wu et al., 2024a) expands the
1340   block within LLMs to facilitate the knowledge injection into LLMs and obtain the trade-off between
1341   general knowledge and domain-specific capabilities.
1342
       Continual preference alignment (Zhang et al., 2023a; Yao et al., 2023) adapts LLMs to evolving so-
1343
       cietal values and ethical guidelines. The typical methodology is reinforcement learning with human
1344   feedback (RLHF) (Kaufmann et al., 2023), which combines principles of reinforcement learning
1345   with feedback from human evaluators to improve the alignment with human preferences and val-
1346   ues. The follow-up work CPPO (Zhang et al., 2024) enhances Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
1347   (Schulman et al., 2017) algorithm with instance-wise weights to balance policy exploration and
1348   knowledge retention. (Zhang et al., 2023a) extends the Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) al-
1349   gorithm (Rafailov et al., 2024) by employing Monte Carlo estimation (Harrison, 2010) to derive
       optimal policy sequences for stream tasks.

       LVLM Benchmarks. With the advent of comprehensive LVLMs (Liu et al., 2023a; OpenAI, 2024),
1374   a wide range of evaluation benchmarks (Liu et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023; Huang & Zhang, 2024)
1375   have been introduced to assess their performance across various dimensions. Based on the model
1376   competencies being examined, LVLM benchmarks can be classified into two categories including
1377   general capabilities for multi-modal understanding and specific capabilities for downstream applica-
1378   tions. Typical general-purpose LVLM Benchmarks include MMBench (Liu et al., 2023b), MM-Vet
1379   (Yu et al., 2023), Seed-Bench (Li et al., 2023a), etc., with the focus on multi-modal perception (e.g.,
1380   recognition or localization) and reasoning (commonsense or logic reasoning). The specific capabili-
1381   ties involve natural science (e.g., ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022), MathVista (Lu et al., 2023)), medical
1382
       usage (e.g., MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), M3D (Bai et al., 2024)), agent planning (e.g., OpenEQA
       (Majumdar et al., 2024)), remote sensing (e.g., RSGPT (Hu et al., 2023)), etc. Most of the cur-
1383
       rent benchmarks focus on the single-task adaption of LVLMs and neglect the consistent adaption
1384
       among different tasks. To facilitate continual instruction tuning, we propose COAST by chaining
1385   and re-purposing current benchmarks in a stream format.
