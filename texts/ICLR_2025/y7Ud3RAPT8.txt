011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Molecular representation learning, which captures the fundamental characteris-
014            tics of chemical compounds, is crucial for AI-driven drug discovery. Methodolo-
015
               gies exist that integrate various modalities (e.g., 2D topology and 3D geometry)
               and develop robust representations. However, current multi-modal fusion strate-
016
               gies either align embedding space through independent models separately, thereby
017
               overlooking complementary information, or bridge modalities at a coarse-grained
018            level, failing to capture inherent correlation. To facilitate fine-grained interactions
019            of intrinsic features across modalities, this study presents MolCoMA, an inno-
020            vative pretraining framework for Molecular representation, employing a unified
021            encoder that leverages Complementary Masking mechanism. Specifically, we
022            first employ two distinct encoders to capture the unique characteristics and struc-
023            tures inherent in different modalities. We then utilize a unified encoder accom-
024            panied by a customized complementary masking strategy to seamlessly integrate
025            information, mitigating overlap and similarity between 2D and 3D representa-
026
               tions. Finally, we incorporate a cross-modal reconstruction module to enhance
               fine-grained interactions at the atomic level. Extensive experiments demonstrate
027
               that our model outperforms existing molecular pretraining methods across both 2D
028
               and 3D benchmarks. This finding underscores the effectiveness of our approach
029            to fusing information between modalities.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Robust molecular representations serve as a cornerstone for AI-based models to ensure their suc-
035   cessful application in drug discovery (Simeon & De Fabritiis, 2024; Song et al., 2024; Zhou et al.,
036   2024). Molecular representations not only assist the models in precisely capturing the intricate in-
037   trinsic properties of molecules and gaining deeper insights into their mechanisms, but also provide
038   a solid foundation for tackling a wide range of downstream tasks, including drug screening (Gao
039
      et al., 2024) , drug optimization (Zhu et al., 2024) and drug design (Lin et al., 2024). With the
      surge of unlabeled data, the utilization of pre-training models that leverage self-supervised learn-
040
      ing to extract inherent molecular characteristics further enhances models’ generalization ability and
041
      accuracy, presenting unprecedented opportunities and challenges for AI-driven drug discovery.
042
043   Molecules are represented in diverse modalities, encompassing 1D, 2D and 3D formats. Among
044   these, 1D representations, devoid of structural information, often yield insufficient representations
045
      to support downstream tasks effectively. In contrast, 2D representations encapsulate the topological
      structure of a molecule, with atoms as nodes and bonds as edges. This captures intricate structural
046
      features and inter-atomic interactions that are crucial for deeper understanding and downstream
047
      applications (Hou et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2024; Rong et al., 2020). 3D representations encapsulate
048   the 3D spatial coordinates of atoms, reflecting the crucial structural arrangement information that is
049   paramount for comprehending molecule functionalities (Schütt et al., 2017; Ni et al., 2024b;a; Zaidi
050   et al., 2023b). In contrast to previous studies, 2D methods, which concentrate solely on topological
051   connections between atoms, overlook spatial information that is a domain where 3D modalities
052   demonstrate their superiority. Different modalities showcase varying advantages through offering
053   diverse perspectives and information, and they frequently exhibit complementarity. Consequently,
      relying solely on a single modality for learning molecular representations fails to comprehensively

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      capture molecular properties, making it difficult to develop robust representations that are adaptable
055   to a wide range of downstream tasks.
056
057   Currently, existing approaches aim to adopt multi-modal learning to integrate information span-
058
      ning a variety of sources, thereby achieving a more comprehensive understanding (Li et al., 2022;
      Liu et al., 2022; 2023a; Stärk et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022). The key to multi-
059
      modal learning is to interact and align various information, enabling models to handle complex
060
      relationships between different modalities. As shown in Figure 1, there are two prevailing strategies
061   to tackle this kind of problem: interaction-based and generation-based. Interaction-based strategy
062   emphasizes the integration of feature representations from different modalities, thereby learning in-
063   teraction relationships between multi-modal data. For instance, contrastive learning is employed to
064   align various modal information of the same molecule within the embedding space (Liu et al., 2022;
065   2023a; Stärk et al., 2022). In contrast, the generation-based approach stresses that one modality
066   serves as a constraint to construct a bridge to the space of another modality, thereby enabling mutual
067   data generation between modalities and capturing rich cross-modal information (Liu et al., 2023a;
068   Zhu et al., 2022). This method often utilizes generative models to achieve mutual reconstruction
069
      as the goal, such as the transformation from 2D to 3D modalities (or vice versa). The former em-
      phasizes the consistency between modalities but fails to effectively capture complex correlations or
070
      dependencies between two modalities; while the latter places more emphasis on the quality of the
071
      generated modality rather than directly enhancing the information interaction between modalities,
072   unable to ensure that the interaction between modalities is fully optimized. Therefore, it is crucial
073   to explore how to achieve interaction relationship mining at a fine-grained level between different
074   modalities.

      To address the aforementioned issue, we propose MolCoMA, a straightforward yet effective multi-
      modal pretraining framework that employs a complementary masking strategy and cross-modal re-
094
      construction on 2D topology and 3D geometry at a fine-grained level. Unlike conventional uniform
095
      or random masking strategies, our approach features a well-designed masking technique that ob-
096   scures atoms across different modalities in a complementary manner. The corrupted multi-modal
097   data is subsequently fed into two specialized modality-specific encoders, which independently and
098   efficiently extract the intrinsic representations of each modality. This process aims to enhance the
099   model’s capacity to understand and represent single-modality features. Subsequently, a unified en-
100   coder is utilized to derive unified representations from both modalities by replacing the embeddings
101   of masked atoms with their corresponding unmasked embeddings from the other modality. Ulti-
102   mately, these unified representations are partitioned into two distinct embeddings associated with
103   different modalities by recovering masks at their respective indices; these are then separately in-
104
      putted into specific decoders for reconstructing both original and cross-modal information. Com-
      prehensive experiments demonstrate that our model exhibits significant advantages over existing
105
      molecular representation methods, irrespective of whether it utilizes 2D, 3D, or multi-modal infor-
106
      mation across an extensive range of benchmarks.

119   2   R ELATED W ORK
120
121   Single modal molecular pretraining. Pretraining on 2D molecule topology shares similar concepts
122   with general graph pretraining (Wang et al., 2024). A traditional approach (Hu et al., 2020; Liu
123   et al., 2019) involves masking specific substructures in molecule graphs, such as atoms, bonds,
124   or motifs, and then reconstructing them using an auto-encoding method. GraphMAE (Hou et al.,
125   2022) performs feature reconstruction of masked atoms rather than explicitly reconstructing graph
126
      structures and achieves superior performance. Another common method for molecular pretraining
      is contrastive learning (Oord et al., 2018), where the objective is to simultaneously align views from
127
      positive pairs and contrast views from negative pairs(Luong & Singh, 2024). For example, Deep
128
      Graph InfoMax (Veličković et al., 2019) and InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2019) consider local and global
129   graph representations as two different views, while MolCLR (Wang et al., 2022) and GraphCL (You
130   et al., 2020a) generate distinct views using discrete graph augmentation techniques.
131
132
      There are also several existing works exploring 3D geometric pretraining on molecules. SE(3)-
      DDM (Liu et al., 2023b) proposes maximizing mutual information between noisy conformations by
133
      employing an SE(3)-invariant denoising score matching approach. 3D-EMGP (Jiao et al., 2023) is
134
      a parallel work that is E(3)-equivariant and inherently meets the reflection-equivariant constraint in
135   the distribution of molecule conformations.
136
137   Multiple modal molecular pretraining. Multi-modal molecular pretraining (Luo et al., 2022; Zhu
138
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a; 2022; Stärk et al., 2022) utilizes both 2D and 3D information to
      enhance molecule representation learning. Most existing methods employ two separate models to
139
      encode 2D and 3D information (Liu et al., 2022; 2023a; Stärk et al., 2022). Their pretraining tech-
140
      niques primarily use contrastive learning (He et al., 2020), where 2D graphs and their corresponding
141   3D conformations are treated as positive views, while information from different molecules serves as
142   negative views. Another pretraining method uses generative models to predict one modality based
143   on the input of another modality (Liu et al., 2023a; 2022). However, these models only achieve
144   alignment at the molecular level and lack fine-grained interactions between modalities, missing a
145   deep comprehension of molecular structure.
146
      Masked graph modeling. Masked graph modeling (MGM) (Hou et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2023; You
147   et al., 2020b) consists of three key components: graph tokenizer, graph masking, and graph autoen-
148   coder. Graph tokenizer (Zhang et al., 2021; Rong et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021) breaks a molecular
149   graph into smaller fragments (i.e., subgraphs) and converts them into tokens. Graph masking (Hu
150   et al., 2020; You et al., 2020b) creates a corrupted graph with masks. Graph autoencoder (Hu et al.,
151   2020; Hou et al., 2022) applies an encoder to the masked graph to generate representations, and
152   then employs a decoder on the representations to recover the tokens of the original graph. Early
153   works utilized the atomic numbers of nodes and bond types of edges as graph tokens largely due to
154   their simplicity (You et al., 2020b; Hu et al., 2020). Recent studies (Jin et al., 2018; Zhang et al.,
155
      2021) have shifted focus to subgraph-level tokenization, such as motifs (Salmina et al., 2015) and
      pretrained GNNs (Xia et al., 2023).

189   3.1         P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
190
191   A molecule M consists of a collection of atoms held together by attractive forces. The atoms
192   with features can be denoted as X ∈ Rn×d , where n is the number of atoms, and d is the feature
193   dimension.The structure of M can be represented as different forms, such as 2D graph structures
194   and 3D geometric structure. For the 2D graph structure, atoms serve as nodes and covalent bonds as
195
      edges. We define M2D = (X, E), where e(i,j) ∈ E denotes the edge feature (the type of the bond)
      between atom i and j if the edge exists. For the 3D geometric structure, atoms are treated as the point
196
      clouds in 3D Euclidean space. We define M3D = (X, R), where R = {r1 , ..., rn }, ri ∈ R3 . We
197
      aim to design a powerful model which take M2D and M3D as input, obtain robust representation
198   via incorporating complementary information between modalities.

      3.2         C OMPLEMENTARY M ASKING S TRATEGY
202
      Existing multi-modal pretraining methods focus extensively on model architecture and pretrain-
203
      ing objective, while there is limited exploration of multi-modal masking strategy (Liu et al., 2022;
204   2023a). As shown in Figure3, most works apply either a uniform masking or random masking
205   strategy to the two modalities. Specifically, they concatenate tokens from different modalities into
206   a sequence, then predict the masked tokens (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2020). This may re-
207   sult in feature redundancy due to the overlap or similarity between the two types of information,
208   particularly in lower-level representations of molecular structures.
209
      In molecular pretraining, 2D and 3D information each possess unique representational strengths.
210   The 2D topology captures atomic connections and bond information, while the 3D structure repre-
211   sents the spatial conformation and geometric properties of molecules. By employing complementary
212   masks, various parts of the information from the 2D and 3D modalities can be obscured, compelling
213   the model to learn latent representations from one modality using the other. When information from
214   one modality is hidden, the model must rely on the other modality to fill in the gaps. This allows the
215   model to utilize the distinct characteristics of both modalities during pretraining, facilitating the in-
      tegration of complementary information. As a result, the model gains a deeper understanding of the

241
242   Modality-specific Encoder. At this stage, our goal is to preserve the integrity of each modality,
243   maintaining the unique characteristics and structure of the data from different modalities. This
244   guarantees that the distinctive features of each modality remain intact throughout the processing
245   pipeline, allowing for more accurate and effective multi-modal interactions in subsequent stages.
246   We utilize two separate encoders to transform data from two distinct modalities into embeddings of
247   the same dimensionality, allowing further processing.
248   For the 2D topological molecular graph, it can be denoted as M2D = (X, E), where X is the atom
249   attribute matrix and E is the bond attribute matrix. We feed them into the 2D Graph Encoder:
250

253   representation function. H2D = [h02D , h12D , . . .], where hi2D is the i-th node representation. We take
254   the GIN (Xu et al., 2019) model for modeling 2D topology, which performs exceptionally well in
255   many tasks.

      Cross-modal Reconstruction. Apart from the topology and geometry loss terms, we additionally
325   utilize a cross-modal reconstruction loss to train the model to further strengthen the fine-grained
326   interaction between the two modalities. Compared to 3D representations, molecular 2D representa-
327   tions directly reflect the molecule’s bonding relationships, providing important insights for predict-
328   ing chemical properties. Moreover, 3D structures can be unstable due to conformational changes,
329   thermal motion, or measurement errors, while 2D structures are relatively fixed, offering more sta-
330   ble and consistent representations. Thus, in this module, the masked geometric tokens go through
331   a cross-modal prediction head of one linear projection layer to recover the corresponding visible
332   topological graph tokens.
333   We avoid using visible geometric tokens for this module, because they correspond to masked graph
334   features (due to the complement masking strategy), which tend to have weaker representations and
335   may harm representation learning. Formally, the cross modal reconstruction loss is defined as:
336
                                     Lcross = M SE(Dcross (h2D ), h3D ),                                 (8)
337
338   where MSE denotes the Mean Squared Error loss function, Dcross is the cross-modal reconstruction
339   function, h2D is the graph representation, and h3D is the geometric representation.

349   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
350
351   Datasets. We pretrain our model on the PCQM4Mv2 training set from OGB Large-Scale Challenge
352   (Hu et al., 2021). It’s a sub-dataset of PubChemQC (Nakata & Shimazaki, 2017), which contains
353
      3.4 million molecules with both 2D topological graphs and 3D geometric conformations. With the
      pre-trained model, we conduct experiments on 20 molecular tasks with different data formats and
354
      evaluate its versatility and effectiveness. We study two representative tasks: MoleculeNet (Wu et al.,
355
      2018) (2D, 8 tasks), QM9 quantum properties (Ramakrishnan et al., 2014) (3D, 12 tasks).
356
357   Baselines. A range of 2D topological pretraining methods have been proposed. We pick the most
358   representative ones as 2D baselines, including AttrMask (Hu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019), Con-
359
      textPred (Hu et al., 2020), InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2019), MolCLR (Wang et al., 2022), GraphCL
      (You et al., 2020a), Mole-BERT (Xia et al., 2023) and GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022). Moreover,
360
      we adopt several multi-modal pretraining methods, which aim to fusion 2D topology and 3D con-
361
      formations, as multi-modal baselines, such as 3D infoMax (Stärk et al., 2022), GraphMVP (Liu
362   et al., 2022), MoleculeSDE (Liu et al., 2023a), as well as recently published model MoleBlend (Yu
363   et al., 2024). Moreover, we choose some denoising methods, SE(3)-DDM (Liu et al., 2023b) and
364   3D-EMGP (Jiao et al., 2023), as baselines of QM9 dataset.
365
      Pretraining and Fine-tuning. We take the GIN (Xu et al., 2019) model as 2D encoder and
366
      TorchMD-NET (Thölke & Fabritiis, 2022) as 3D encoder. The model is pre-trained on PCQM4Mv2
367
      for 100 epochs. We adopt the AdamW optimizer with a warm-up for 10 epochs. The peak learning
368   rate is 1e-4 and the batch size is 512. The mask ratio is 0.5. In fine-tuning stage, for 2D tasks,
369   we utilize the 2D specific encoder as well as the unified encoder as a 2D feature extractor. For 3D
370   tasks, we only adopt the 3D encoder as a 3D feature extractor considering time and efficiency. Dif-
371   ferent heads are employed for different tasks: a simple 1-layer MLP head for MoleculeNet and an

      4.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
433
434   In this subsection, we conduct a series of experiments to investigate the key designs of our method.
435   First, we explore the impact of different masking strategies, including random, uniform and comple-
436   mentary masking strategies. Besides, we compare the performance of multi-modal and single-modal
437   information respectively.
438   Complementary masking strategy. To explore how masking strategy impacts information inter-
439   action between different modalities, we compare complementary masking to two traditional mask-
440   ing strategies: random and uniform. For random masking strategy, 2D and 3D patterns are masked
441   through individual and random sampling. For uniform masking strategy, both modalities are masked
442   following the same pattern, such that the masked portions in one modality correspond to the masked
443   regions in the other. In contrast, complementary sampling is quite the opposite approach.
444   We adopt the above three strategies in the pretraining phase and evaluate their performance after fine-
445   tuning on MoleculeNet and QM9. As shown in Table 3, masking tokens complementarily between
446   modalities performs the best on all tasks, which indicates that complementary masking strategy
447   enhances molecular representation. Compared to random masking, complementary masking enables
448   more cross-modal interactions between atoms with diverse semantic information, thus helping our
449   model transfer knowledge between both modalities. However, with the uniform masking strategy,
450   the extracted point cloud features and topology features are semantically aligned, so the interaction
451   does not help the model utilize information from the other modality better.

477   5     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      We propose MolCoMA, a self-supervised pretraining framework that fosters fine-grained 2D and 3D
480   interaction at the atom-level. Our method achieves deep and granular interaction between modali-
481   ties by masking, replacing, and recovering atom embeddings. Through cross-modal reconstruction,
482   MolCoMA is constrained to learn the transformation information between modal data, thereby cap-
483   turing the correlation and complementarity among different modal data. From the above two aspects,
484   our method achieves state-of-the-art performance across a broad range of 2D and 3D benchmarks,
485   demonstrating its effective and highly interactive multi-modal learning pipeline, which excels in
      feature extraction for both geometry and topology.
