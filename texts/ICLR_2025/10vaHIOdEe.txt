009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool to capture in-
012            tricate network patterns, achieving successes across different domains. However,
013            existing GNNs require careful domain-specific architecture designs and training
014            from scratch on each dataset, leading to an expertise-intensive process with diffi-
015            culty in generalizing across graphs from different domains. Therefore, it can be
016            hard for practitioners to infer which GNN model can generalize well to graphs
017            from their domains. To address this challenge, we propose a novel cross-domain
018
               pretraining framework, "one model for one graph," which overcomes the limita-
               tions of previous approaches that failed to use a single GNN to capture diverse
019
               graph patterns across domains with significant gaps. Specifically, we pretrain a
020
               bank of expert models, with each one corresponding to a specific dataset. When
021            inferring to a new graph, gating functions choose a subset of experts to effectively
022            integrate prior model knowledge while avoiding negative transfer. Extensive exper-
023            iments consistently demonstrate the superiority of our proposed method on both
024            link prediction and node classification tasks.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   As a ubiquitous data structure, graphs can represent a wide range of structural data across different
030   domains, such as academia (Yang et al., 2016), e-commerce (Ying et al., 2018; Borisyuk et al., 2024;
031   Fan et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2020), and molecule (Ying et al., 2021). Graph neural networks (GNNs)
032   have exhibited great performance when learning and inferring on a single graph dataset. However,
033
      most GNNs fail to generalize across domains due to the feature heterogeneity problem, in which
      graphs from different sources often contain node features with varying semantic meanings and
034
      dimensions.
035
036   Recently, feature dimension heterogeneity can be solved via two steps: (i) transform node features
037   into textual descriptions (ii) employ Large Language Models (LLMs) to encode them into the aligned
038   textual representation space. Multiple graph models (Liu et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023; Chen
039
      et al., 2024b;a) are then developed with inductive inference capability across graphs. Nonetheless, a
      recent benchmark (Chen et al., 2024b) reveals that, even within the aligned textual representation
040
      space, the positive transfer can only be found within the single domain, while the semantic disparity
041
      happens across different domains. Moreover, graphs from various domains exhibit significantly
042   different structural properties. For example, the homophily property, a crucial factor affecting the
043   node classification performance of GNNs, varies significantly across graphs. As noted by Mao et al.
044   (2023), a single GNN struggles to capture varying levels of homophily simultaneously.
045
      The aforementioned observations suggest that pretraining a single model for graphs from multiple
046
      domains is suboptimal. Therefore, in this work, we propose to individually pretrain one expert
047
      model for each pretraining graph and then save the set of different expert models as a model bank to
048   effectively leverage cross-domain graphs. During inference, a subset will be automatically selected
049   to produce a pre-trained model specfic to a test graph. This proposed pipeline is different from that
050   adopted by the majority of existing cross-graph pretraining methods (Liu et al., 2023a; Huang et al.,
051   2023; Chen et al., 2024b;a) as shown in Figure 1. The existing pipeline pretrains one model for all
052   graphs and then applys the model to all test graphs as shown in Figure 1a which is referred to as the
053   “one model for all graphs" pipeline. On the other hand, the proposed pipeline pretrains a bank of
      models with one model for each graph and automatically generates one model specific to a test graph

085
      graphs, OMOG will train an expert and an associated gate on each graph, resulting in a bank of N
086
      pretrained experts and N gates. During the inference stage, every gate will give an relevance score
087   for a test graph, and experts with top-k high corresponding scores will be fused to form a pre-trained
088   model. Extensive experiments are conducted to demonstrate the superiority of our design on both
089   zero-shot and few-shot learning settings.

092   2    R ELATED WORK
093
094   Cross-graph machine learning. The graph machine learning community has recently witnessed a
095   growing trend to extend models designed for a single graph across different graphs (or datasets) (Mao
096   et al., 2024). The key obstacle to cross-graph learning stems from feature and structural heterogeneity.
097   Early endeavors typically address feature heterogeneity by neglecting the original features (Qiu
098   et al., 2020) and adopting GNN-based self-supervised learning to extract transferrable structural
099
      patterns. However, such a strategy performs poorly on text-rich networks and suffers from negative
      transfer (Xu et al., 2023) due to the structure shift across different datasets. Zhao et al. (2024a) adopt
100
      dimensionality reduction to unify the feature dimension while features remain poorly aligned. To
101
      generate high-quality unified features across graphs, LLM and prompt engineering(Liu et al., 2023a)
102   have been adopted to generate features in a unified text space (Chen et al., 2024b). Liu et al. (2023a)
103   focus on the cross-data co-training where the downstream dataset aligns with the pre-training one and
104   achieves inferior performance when transferring to novel datasets (Chen et al., 2024b). Chen et al.
105   (2024a); Tang et al. (2023) focus on transferring trained models to new datasets while presenting
106   inferior performance with inadequate supervision. Li et al. (2024); Huang et al. (2023) identify the
107   importance of reformulating prediction into nearest neighbor retrieval for effective prediction in low
      supervision scenarios, while their approach which uses one dataset to fit all graphs will struggle


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      when training across graphs and possibly suffer negative transfer. Xia & Huang (2024); Hou et al.
109   (2024) further introduce a mixture-of-expert architecture to remedy this issue, while their gating
110   function training lacks graph-aware supervision and adopts a fixed number of experts, resulting in
111   inferior performance. He & Hooi (2024) adopt a LLM-based backbone, which incurs significant
112   computational overhead. Zhao et al. (2024c) achieve cross-graph learning based on label space
113   instead of feature space, which is orthogonal to our work.
114
      Another line of work studies cross-task learning across graphs, where Jin et al. (2021); Ju et al.
115   (2023) focuses on selecting pre-training tasks to adapt different downstream tasks, while Liu et al.
116   (2023b); Sun et al. (2023; 2022) tackles the task heterogeneity to support different tasks with a unified
117   backbone. Our work can potentially be combined with these works to support cross-task learning
118   across graphs.
119
      Mixture-of-experts (MoE) on graphs. Mixture-of-experts (Shazeer et al., 2017) has recently been
120
      adopted to graph machine learning to improve inference efficiency (Wang et al., 2024), enhance
121   fairness (Liu et al., 2023c), tackle heterophily (Han et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2023), and improve
122   prediction performance by capturing diverse structural patterns (Hu et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2024).
123   Our work extends the scope of MoE to multiple graphs. Combined with a suitable gate design, we
124   achieve efficient and effective cross-graph learning, surpassing the performance of counterparts (Xia
125   & Huang, 2024; Hou et al., 2024).

      In this section, we introduce our one model for one graph pretraining framework with cross-domain
      graphs, OMOG. It is an implementation of the new pipeline shown in Figure 1b. It consists of two
131
      stages – the pretraining stage and the inference stage. In the pretraining stage, OMOG will pre-train
132
      one model for one graph with one associated gate separately. In the inference stage, it will adaptively
133   choose suitable experts for a test graph according to the associated gates and the test task. Before we
134   detail these two stages, we start with introducing the problem formulation.
135
136
      Problem formulation: In this work, we focus on text-attributed graphs (TAGs), or more generally,
      text-space datasets (Chen et al., 2024b) whose features can be converted into text-attributes. An
137
      input graph can be defined as G = (V, A, S), where V = {v1 , v2 , ..., vn } is the set of n nodes,
138
      and A ∈ Rn×n represents the adjacency matrix of the graph, and S = {s 1 , s 2 , ...} is the set of
139   text descriptions for all nodes. We focus on cross-graph pretraining with a transferring setting.
140   Specifically, assuming that we are given N pretraining graphs {G1 , · · · , GN }, we would like to
141   pretrain a model bank M with one model for each pretraining graph and then transfer knowledge
142   in M to unseen test graphs. We focus on two downstream tasks: i.e., node classification and link
143   prediction. For node classification, we aim to predict the category yi of the target node vi . For the
144   link prediction, we predict whether there is a link between two target nodes vi and vj .

      3.1   T HE P RETRAINING S TAGE
163
164   The whole pre-training process is illustrated in Figure 2, which contains the following steps:
165
      1. Incorporating attribute and structure information: To achieve cross-graph pre-training across
166
         diverse domains, we first adopt LLMs to generate node features for each graph in a unified text
167
         space. Based on the unified feature space, we adopt non-parametric message passing (Wu et al.,
168      2019) to generate node-level embeddings incorporating structural information.
169   2. Pretraining graph-specific experts: This step involves pre-training models that can effectively
170      transfer to downstream datasets. As shown in (Xu et al., 2023), pre-training a single model across
171      graphs with diverse structural properties results in negative transfer and catastrophic forgetting.
172      Therefore, we design a model bank to preserve pre-training knowledge. This is achieved by
173      pre-training one separate model for each graph.
174   3. Pretraining gate modules: To adaptively extract the proper experts for a test graph, we pre-train
175      gate modules to determine the relevance between pre-trained models in the bank and test graphs.
176
         The pre-trained gate modules can then be applied to select the most relevant experts to produce a
         pretrained model specific to test graphs in the inference stage.
177

230   Pretraining the gate modules. After keeping pre-trained knowledge in a model bank, we design a
231   gating module to decide the relevance between the corresponding expert in the bank and downstream

233

234

238   mask the input features and thus leave out the domain-related patterns. Meanwhile, the mask matrix

297   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
298
      Datasets. We utilize 10 diverse texture-attributed graphs sourced from Chen et al. (2024b). These
299
      datasets span a wide range of domains, including citation networks, social networks, and e-commerce
300   networks. The graph sizes range from thousands to millions of nodes, with the number of classes
301   across datasets spanning from 3 to 39. These datasets exhibit both domain shift and structural
302   property shift (Chen et al., 2024b), effectively reflecting the challenges encountered when transferring
303   pre-trained graph models to novel domains in real-world scenarios. For a comprehensive overview of
304   the datasets, please refer to Appendix A.
305
      Evaluation settings. To evaluate the effectiveness of our methods under the transferring set-
306   ting (RQ1), we adopt a widely adopted setting that pre-trained models that are adapted to unseen test
307   datasets with little (few-shot) or no downstream task supervision (zero-shot) (Chen et al., 2024b; Liu
308   et al., 2023a). We consider both node classification and link prediction as target tasks. To test the
309   transferring capability of models, we adopt a leave-one-out strategy. Specifically, given 10 adopted
310   datasets, each time, one of them will be selected as the target downstream test data, and the other
311   nine datasets will be used as the pre-training data. Regarding evaluation metrics, we adopt accuracy
312   for node classification and Hits@100 for link prediction.
313   Baselines. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework, we consider state-of-the-art cross-
314   graph learning baselines, which can be categorized as the “single model” and “mixture of models”
315   frameworks.
316
      • “Single model” framework adopts a unified backbone to achieve cross-graph learning, with
317
        representatives including OneForAll (Liu et al., 2023a), GCOPE (Zhao et al., 2024b), LLaGA (Chen
318     et al., 2024a), ZeroG (Li et al., 2024) and Prodigy (Huang et al., 2023). Their major difference lies
319     in the selection of backbone models, where OneForAll, ZeroG, GCOPE, and Prodigy are still based
320     on GNNs, while LLaGA adopts LLM. Specifically, Prodigy and ZeroG transform the prediction
321     into a nearest neighbor retrieval problem. Graph self-supervised learning baselines, including
322     GCC (Qiu et al., 2020) and GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022), also belong to this category.
323   • “Mixture of models” framework adopts a group of models to be pre-trained and then transferred
        to downstream tasks. Two representative methods including AnyGraph (Xia & Huang, 2024) and

        “Least K” strategies.
