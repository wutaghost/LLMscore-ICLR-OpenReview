                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               The development of video large multimodal models (LMMs) has been hin-
010
               dered by the difficulty of curating large amounts of high-quality raw data
011            from the web. To address this, we consider an alternative approach, creat-
012            ing a high-quality synthetic dataset specifically for video instruction-following,
013            namely LLaVA-Video-178K. This dataset includes key tasks such as detailed
014            captioning, open-ended question-answering (QA), and multiple-choice QA. By
015            training on this proposed dataset, in combination with existing visual instruction
016            tuning data, we introduce LLaVA-Video, a new video LMM. Our experiments
017            demonstrate that LLaVA-Video achieves strong performance across various video
018            benchmarks, highlighting the effectiveness of our dataset. We plan to release the
019
               dataset, its generation pipeline, and the model checkpoints.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      We are in an era where large-scale computing and data is crucial for multimodal learning. A significant
024
      recent advancement was introduced by visual instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2024a), which laid the
025   foundation for building a general-purpose visual assistant. Notably, it proposed a data generation
026   pipeline to create high-quality image-language instruction-following data. This pipeline has inspired
027   subsequent researches (Li et al., 2024c;b;a; Lin et al., 2024) aimed at generating diverse image-
028   language instruction data across various visual domains, accelerating the development of visual
029   instruction tuning techniques.
030
      Compared to the construction of image-language instruction-following data, obtaining high-quality
031   video-language instruction-following data is challenging (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024d). First,
032   sourcing high-quality videos is difficult. We need to find videos with significant temporal changes
033   that provide more knowledge than what image-language data can offer. However, we have found that
034   most videos in current video-language instruction-following datasets (Chen et al., 2024a; Zhang et al.,
035   2024d) are relatively static. Additionally, these videos are mostly trimmed based on scene changes,
036   resulting in simplified plots. Such simplified video-language instruction-tuning data is inadequate
037   for models to understand videos with complex narratives. Furthermore, current video-language
038   instruction-following datasets often use a very sparse sampling rate for frame annotation. For instance,
039
      ShareGPT4Video (Chen et al., 2024a) has an average sampling rate of 0.15, sometimes sampling
      only 2 frames from a 30-second video. This sparse sampling rate is effective in describing overall
040
      scenes but fails to capture detailed movements or changes in the video, resulting in hallucination
041
      when detailed descriptions of the video are required.
042
043   To overcome these shortcomings, we introduce a comprehensive video instruction-tuning dataset
044   named LLaVA-Video-178K, consisting of 178,510 videos ranging from 0 to 3 minutes. This dataset is
045
      enriched with detailed annotations, open-ended questions, and multiple-choice questions, developed
      through a combination of GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and human efforts. It features four favorable
046
      properties: (i) Extensive Video Source: We conduct a comprehensive survey on the video sources
047
      of exsiting video understanding datasets, and conclude 10 major video data sources, from which we
048   start our video data collection by building a video pool. Although there are over 40 video-language
049   datasets, their video data are mainly sourced from 10 datasets (Zhou & Corso, 2017; Xue et al., 2022;
050   Goyal et al., 2017; Caba Heilbron et al., 2015; Kay et al., 2017; Sigurdsson et al., 2016; Wang et al.,
051   2023; Shang et al., 2019; Grauman et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023a), covering a wide range of video
052   domains, such as activities, cooking, TV shows, and egocentric views. (ii) Dynamic Untrimmed
053   Video Selection: From these sources, we use several filtering logic to select the most dynamic videos
      from the video data pool. Notably, we select original, untrimmed videos to ensure plot completeness.

054
      (iii) Recurrent Detailed Caption Generation Pipeline with Dense Frame Sampling: We propose
055   a detailed video caption pipeline that operates recurrently, enabling us to generate detailed captions
056   for videos of any length. This pipeline has three levels, each level of description represents a different
057   time-range: from 10 seconds to the entire video length. It is recurrent as the historical description
058   from any level serves as the context for generating new descriptions at any level. Additionally, we
059   adopted a dense sampling strategy of one frame per second to ensure the sampled frames are rich
060   enough to represent the videos. (iv) Diverse Tasks: Based on the detailed video descriptions, we can
061   generate question-answer pairs. To ensure our questions cover a wide range of scenarios, by referring
062   to the video question-answering dataset, we define 16 question types. We prompt GPT-4o to generate
063
      question-answer pairs by referring to these question types, covering open-ended and multi-choice
      questions.
064
065   Based upon the LLaVA-Video-178K dataset, we developed LLaVA-Video. Contrary to previous
066   studies suggesting that training with single frames is sufficient for video-language understanding (Lei
067   et al., 2022), our findings reveal a significant impact of frame count on LLaVA-Video’s performance,
068   attributable to the detailed features of LLaVA-Video-178K. Observing this, we explored maximizing
069
      frame sampling within the constraints of limited GPU memory. We introduce LLaVA-Video SlowFast ,
      a video representation technique that optimally distributes visual tokens across different frames. This
070
      approach allows for incorporating up to three times more frames than traditional methods, which
071
      allocate an equal number of visual tokens to each frame.
072
073   Our contributions are as follows:
074
          • Video-language Instruction-Following Data:        We present a high-quality dataset
075
            LLaVA-Video-178K tailored for video instruction-following. It consists of 178K video
076
            with 1.3M instruction samples, including detailed captions, free-form and multiple-choice
077         question answering.
078
079
          • Video Large Multimodal Models: We develop LLaVA-Video, a series of advanced large video-
            language models that expand the capabilities of open models in understanding video content.
080
081       • Open-Source: In an effort to support the development of general-purpose visual assistants, we
082         release our multimodal instruction data, codebase, model checkpoints, and a visual chat demo
083
            to the public.

085   2    R ELATED W ORK
086
087   In this work, our goal is to create a high-quality video-language dataset that goes beyond simple
088   video captions. We aim to improve the ability to follow instructions, which includes detailed video
089   descriptions, open-ended video question-answering, and multiple-choice video question-answering
090   data. We discuss related datasets in Table 1. Previous video-language datasets (Miech et al., 2019)
091   include manually annotated data for various tasks, such as video captions (Chen & Dolan, 2011; Xu
092   et al., 2016; Rohrbach et al., 2015; Anne Hendricks et al., 2017a; Caba Heilbron et al., 2015; Zhou &
093
      Corso, 2017), and video question-answering (Yu et al., 2019; Zadeh et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2021).
      However, manual annotation is expensive and limits the size of such datasets. To address the shortage
094
      of data, studies like (Miech et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2021; Zellers et al., 2021; Xue et al., 2022) suggest
095
      automatically annotating data using subtitles created by ASR. While this method greatly expands
096   the dataset size to 100 million samples, the subtitles often fail to accurately describe the main video
097   content. Additionally, other studies (Xu et al., 2017; Grunde-McLaughlin et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
098   2024a) use language models (Xu et al., 2017) or question templates (Grunde-McLaughlin et al.,
099   2021; Wu et al., 2024a) to generate question-answer pairs. Although this approach can generate a
100   large number of questions and answers, it often produces poor-quality questions that do not reflect
101   real-world user inquiries. More recent research (Chen et al., 2024b) has prompted video-language
102   models such as BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), VideoChat (Li et al., 2024d), Video-LLaMA (Zhang et al.,
103   2023), and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023b) to generate video captions. However, these models are
104
      limited in their ability to provide detailed descriptions.
105   The most related works to ours are the recent AI-generated synthetic video instruction tuning data,
106   LLaVA-Hound (Zhang et al., 2024d) and ShareGPT4Video (Chen et al., 2024a), where they have
107   used GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) to generate video captions and open-ended video question-answering.

135   3     V IDEO I NSTRUCTION -F OLLOWING DATA S YNTHESIS
136
137   A high-quality dataset for video instruction-tuning is crucial for developing effective video-language
138   models. We identify a key factor in building such datasets: ensuring richness and diversity in both
139   video content and its language annotations. We perform comprehensive survey on the existing video
140
      benchmarks, covering across various public video captioning and question-answering datasets, then
      identify ten unique video sources that contribute to over 40 video-language benchmarks. From each
141
      source, we select videos that exhibit significant temporal dynamics. To maintain diversity in the
142
      annotations, we establish a pipeline capable of generating detailed captions for videos of any length.
143   Additionally, we define 16 types of questions that guide GPT-4o in creating question-answer pairs to
144   assess the perceptual and reasoning skills of the video-language models.
145
146   3.1     V IDEO SOURCE
147
148   One important starting point in building a high-quality video instruction-following dataset is to find a
149   sufficiently diverse pool of video data. From this pool, we can select the qualified videos. In our study
150   of public video-language datasets—including video captioning, video question answering, video
151   summarization, and moment-wise captioning—we noticed that although different datasets focus on
152   various video understanding tasks (e.g., AGQA (Grunde-McLaughlin et al., 2021) for spatial-temporal
153
      relations and STAR (Wu et al., 2024a) for situational reasoning), most are sourced from ten main
      video sources. For instance, both AGQA and STAR use data from Charades (Sigurdsson et al., 2016).
154
      Specifically, these ten sources are HD-VILA-100M (Xue et al., 2022), InternVid-10M (Wang et al.,
155
      2023), VidOR (Shang et al., 2019), VIDAL (YouTube Shorts)(Zhu et al., 2023a), YouCook2(Zhou &
156   Corso, 2017), Charades (Sigurdsson et al., 2016), ActivityNet (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015), Kinetics-
157   700 (Kay et al., 2017), Something-Something v2 (Goyal et al., 2017), and Ego4d (Grauman et al.,
158   2022). These sources offer a wide range of video data from different websites, viewpoints, and
159   domains. The relationship between these ten selected video datasets and others is shown in Fig. 1.
160   The videos from this ten datsets build the video pool for the further video selection. Notably, we use
161   untrimmed videos from each source except for YouCook2 and Kinetics-700. We believe that cutting
      videos into clips can break the plot continuity, which is essential for understanding the videos.

196   3.2   V IDEO D ETAIL D ESCRIPTION
197
198   Automated Generation For selected videos, we use GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) to systematically
199   describe their content. We start by sampling video frames at one frame per second (fps). However,
200   due to the input size constraints of GPT-4o, we cannot use all sampled frames. Instead, we describe
201   the videos sequentially, as shown in Fig 2. We create descriptions at three distinct levels, detailed
202
      below.
203
204      • Level-1 Description: Every 10 seconds, we provide a level-1 description that outlines the events
205        in that segment. This description considers: frames from the current clip and historical context,
206        which includes all recent level-1 descriptions not yet summarized into a level-2 description and
207        the latest level-2 description.
208
         • Level-2 Description: Every 30 seconds, we creat a level-2 summary of the entire video plot up
209        to that point. This is based on the last three level-1 descriptions, covering the most recent 30
210        seconds; and the latest level-2 description.
211
212      • Level-3 Description: At the video’s end, we generate a level-3 description to encapsulate
213        the entire video. The inputs for this description are the recent level-1 descriptions not yet
214        summarized, covering the last moments of the plot after the recent summary; and the latest
215
           level-2 description.

247   3.3       V IDEO Q UESTION A NSWERING
248
249   Question Type definition In addition to detailed video descriptions, our dataset includes a variety
250   of question-answer pairs designed for complex interactions. This setup improves the video under-
251   standing model’s ability to handle real-life queries. We refer to public video question-answering
252
      benchmarks (Xiao et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2019; khattak et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b) to organize
      these questions into 16 specific categories, as shown in Fig. 3.
253
254
      Automated Generation Given a detailed video description, we use GPT-4o to generate at most one
255
      question-answer pair for each type of question. The prompts include: (1) The task definition for the
256
      current question type. (2) In-context examples for this type, which include three video descriptions
257   and their three question-answer pairs of this specific type. (3) The detailed video description for the
258   current video. We instruct GPT-4o to return None if it cannot generate question-answer pairs for a
259   specific question type.
260
261   Filtering. To filter out the generated question-answer pairs, we apply the following strategy: (1)
262   remove duplicates using the sentence-transformer (Reimers & Gurevych, 2020), (2) discard answers
263   that begin with phrases like “does not specify,” “does not mention,” “does not specifically,” “does not
264   depict,” or “does not show.”

359   Dataset Comparison We provide a comparison of high-quality instruction following video-
360   language datasets, with a focus on synthetic data created with strong AI models, as shown in
361   Table 1. (i) A broad collection of dynamic videos. In terms of video sources, although LLaVA-
362   Hound (Zhang et al., 2024d) contains the largest number of videos, 44% of its video data are sourced
363   from WebVid (Bain et al., 2021), where most videos are static. ShareGPT4Video (Chen et al., 2024a)
364   includes 30% of its videos from Pexels, Pixabay, and Mixkit, which are aesthetically good but also
365   mostly static. Additionally, the majority of its videos come from Panda-70M, which are short clips
366   from longer videos—suggesting simpler plots. In contrast, we carefully select video sources that
367
      offer dynamic, untrimmed videos with complex plots, which are crucial for developing a powerful
      video understanding model.1 (ii) High frames per second. Regarding frame sampling in language
368
      annotations, the proposed datasest considers 1 FPS, while other datasets consider much lower FPS.
369
      LLaVA-Hound uniformly samples 10 frames from videos of any length. The average FPS is 0.008,
370   which may miss some fine details. ShareGPT4Video picks key frames using CLIP (Radford et al.,
371   2021) based on frame uniqueness. This method might also miss subtle changes in the video because
372   CLIP embeddings do not capture fine-grained dynamics well. Our method samples FPS=1 without
373   using key frame selection algorithms, ensuring the detailed temproal information can be expressed in
374   annotations and high coverage. (iii) Diverse tasks. The proposed dataset considers three common
375   task types, including caption, free-form and closed-form QA, while existing datasets only consider a
376   subset. Meanwhile, the quality and numbers of samples in our dataset is higher.

389   Video Representations Following the classic SlowFast idea in video representations (Feichtenhofer
390   et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024), we develop LLaVA-Video SlowFast to optimize
391   the balance between the number of frames and the count of visual tokens, within the budget of
392
      the limited context window in LLM and GPU memory for video representation. Please refer to
      Appendix A for detailed information. Specifically, we represent each video as a sequence with
393
      maximum T frames. Each frame is represented in M tokens. we categorize the frames into two
394
      groups, based on the a strike rate s, where the every s frames are uniformly selected to form the
395   slow frame group, and the rest of the frames are consdiered as the fast frame group. Note that a
396   special case s = 1 leads to only one group, reducing the SlowFast representation to the original
397   simple representation. For each group, we apply different pooling rate using Pytorch function pooling
398   avg_pool2d(). p × p pooling and 2p × 2p pooling for slow and fast frames, respectively. To
399   summarize, we paramterize the video representation
                                                          configuration as V = (T,     M, s, p). The total
400   number of tokens is #tokens = ⌊T /s⌋ × M/p2 + (T − ⌊T /s⌋) × M/p2

      4.3     DATASET C OMPARISON
509
510   We conduct two ablation studies to further analyze our dataset and training strategy. As shown in
511   Table 4, we compared three datasets where the language annotations are from GPT-4V/GPT-4o. For
512   each experiment, we fine-tune the LLaVA-OneVision (SI) model separately on each specific dataset
513   setting, utilizing a video representation defined by V = (64, 679, 1, 2).
514   Two group of experiments are considered to assess the data quality of LLaVA-Video-178K com-
515   pare to LLaVA-Hound and ShareGPT4Video. In the first group, to compare LLaVA-Video-178K
516   with LLaVA-Hound, we randomly selected 900K open-ended questions to match the number in
517   LLaVA-Hound. We included all captions and did not sample the multiple-choice questions. In
518   the second group, comparing LLaVA-Video-178K to ShareGPT4Video, we randomly sampled 40K
519   video captions to align with those in ShareGPT4Video. Since ShareGPT4Video lacks open-ended and
520   multiple-choice questions, we supplemented with annotations from NExT-QA, PerceptionTest, and
521
      ActivityNet-QA. In the first group of Table 4, we compare LLaVA-Video-178K with LLaVA-Hound.
      Although LLaVA-Hound has more captions than LLaVA-Video-178K, our results are still better. As
522
      shown in Table 1, despite LLaVA-Hound annotates more videos, its quality is limited due to two
523
      main issues: (1) Static video: Its primary video source is WebVid (Bain et al., 2021), which tends to
524   have relatively static content. (2) Sparse sampling: Although it includes data sources with dynamic
525   videos, its sampling rate of 10 frames per video leads to annotations that do not fully capture the
526   complete plot of the video. This underscores that the quality of video instruction-following data is
527   more important than its quantity. Additionally, the second experiment group in Table 4 shows that
528   the model trained with LLaVA-Video-178K outperforms that of ShareGPT4Video, highlighting the
529   superiority of our data’s quality.

531   5      C ONCLUSION
532
533   This study introduces the LLaVA-Video-178K dataset, a high-quality synthetic dataset for video-
534   language instruction-following. It is favored for its dense frame sampling rate in longer, untrimmed
535   videos, covering diverse tasks such as captioning, open-ended and multi-choice QA. By training on
536   the joint dataset of LLaVA-Video-178K with existing visual instruction tuning data, we developed a
537   new model family, LLaVA-Video, which also considers video representation to effectively use GPU
538   resources. This allows us to include more frames in the training process. The experimental results
539   have demonstrated the effectiveness of the proposed synthetic dataset, and LLaVA-Video models
      have achieved excellent performance on a wide range of video benchmarks.

      A.1     E FFICIENT V IDEO R EPRESENTATIONS IN LMM S
813
814   Current designs of large multimodal models (LMM) typically connect a vision encoder (Radford
815   et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023) to a large language model (Yang et al., 2024) through a lightweight
816   projector (Liu et al., 2024a) or a resampler (Li et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022). These components
817   transform a set of visual representations into “visual tokens” aligned with text embeddings. In
818   contrast to image-based LMMs, which generate only a small number of visual tokens easily managed
819   by a standard GPU, video LMMs face challenges due to a large number of visual tokens derived
820
      from multiple video frames. The LLaVA-NeXT-Video (Zhang et al., 2024e) and PLLaVA (Xu et al.,
      2024a) models address this by simly considering average pooling to reduce the number of tokens
821
      representing each frame.
822
823   Following the idea of SlowFast in the traditional video understanding (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019),
824   adaptive reductions in visual tokens are demonstrated by recent video LMMs, LITA (Huang et al.,
825   2024) and SlowFast-LLaVA (Xu et al., 2024c). Initially, these methods represent all sampled frames
826
      with a minimal number of visual tokens (fast frame)— typically just one—by using a large pooling
      stride. They then switch to a smaller pooling stride for certain frames to retain more visual tokens
827
      (slow frame). Finally, they combine the visual tokens of fast frames with those of slow frames.
828
      However, this approach can lead to some frames being represented twice. In contrast, our method
829   uses a larger pooling stride for sampled frames to maintain fewer visual tokens (fast frame) or a
830   smaller stride for others to keep more (slow frame). We then arrange slow and fast frames in an
831   interleaving pattern.

850   A.2     LL AVA-V IDEO SlowFast
851
852   We represent each video as a sequence with maximum T frames. Each frame is represented in M
853   tokens. FPS-based video representation can be considered in the future. Specifically, each frame
854   is encoded via an image encoder and a two-layer MLP for projection. These visual tokens are
855
      concatenated with word tokens and processed by a large language model (LLM). Managing tokens
      for every frame can be computationally demanding. For instance, employing the SigLIP (Zhai et al.,
856
      2023) encoder for a video with T = 100 results in 67,600 tokens, assuming M = 729 tokens
857
      per frame, which often exceeds GPU memory limits. This issue is exacerbated when using large-
858   parameter LLMs; with the Qwen2-72B model, we could only process 8 frames before maxing out the
859   memory on 128 NVIDIA H100 GPUs. Such a limited number of frames can introduce inconsistencies
860   in language annotations, reducing model efficacy. One strategy to incorporate more frames is by
861   applying p × p spatial average pooling to reduce M to M/p2 , thus lowering the token count per
862   frame as suggested by recent studies (Xu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024e). However, the number
863   of visual tokens is crucial for preserving the informational content of each frame, which is vital for
      video comprehension.
