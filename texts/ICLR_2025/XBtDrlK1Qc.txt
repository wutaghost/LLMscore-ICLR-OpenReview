011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Modeling time series is important in a variety of domains, yet it is challenged
014            by the presence of missing values in real-world time-series datasets. Traditional
015
               frameworks for modeling time-series with missing values typically involve a two-
               step process, where the missing values are first filled-in using some imputation
016
               technique, followed by a time-series modeling approach on the imputed time-
017
               series. However, existing two-stage approaches suffer from two major drawbacks:
018            first, the propagation of imputation errors into subsequent time-series modeling
019            performance, and second, the inherent trade-offs between imputation efficacy and
020            imputation complexity. To this end, we propose a novel imputation-free approach
021            for handling missing values in time series termed Missing Feature-aware Time
022            Series Modeling (MissTSM) with two main innovations. First, we develop a
023            novel embedding scheme that treats every combination of time-step and feature (or
024            channel) as a distinct token, encoding them into a high-dimensional space. Second,
025            we introduce a novel Missing Feature-Aware Attention (MFAA) Layer to learn
026
               latent representations at every time-step based on partially observed features. We
               evaluate the effectiveness of MissTSM in handling missing values over multiple
027
               benchmark datasets using two synthetic masking techniques: missing completely
028
               at random (MCAR) and periodic masking, and a real-world missing-value dataset.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multivariate time-series modeling for tasks such as forecasting (Lim & Zohren, 2021; Torres et al.,
034   2021) and classification (Ismail Fawaz et al., 2019) are important in a number of applications including
035   electric load forecasting (Din & Marnerides, 2017; L’Heureux et al., 2022), traffic flow prediction (Xu
036   et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023), weather forecasting (Bojesomo et al., 2021), healthcare monitoring
037   (Tonekaboni et al., 2021), and financial analysis (Henrique et al., 2019). There is a growing body
038   of literature on time-series modeling using deep learning approaches, including early adoption of
039
      recurrent architectures such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Hewamalage et al., 2021) and
      Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Siami-Namini
040
      et al., 2019). With the success of transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017) in the domain of
041
      natural language modeling, there is a growing trend to use transformers in the domain of time-series
042   (Wen et al., 2022). This includes recent frameworks such as SimMTM (Dong et al., 2024), PatchTST
043   (Nie et al., 2022a), and iTransformers (Liu et al., 2023), which already show superior performance
044   compared to non-transformer baselines on various time-series modeling benchmarks by leveraging
045   self-attention mechanisms for capturing temporal dependencies. However, existing deep learning
046   based methods for time-series modeling typically assume the availability of complete time-series
047   data with no missing values. While such datasets are available for benchmarking experiments, in real-
048   world applications, it is common to observe missing values in the time-series due to several reasons
049   such as sensor malfunctions, communication disruptions, or the prohibitive costs of high-frequency
050
      data acquisition across all features. The presence of missing values on arbitrary sets of features at
      varying time-steps introduces “gaps” in the data that can impair the application of state-of-the-art
051
      models unless specific adaptations are made.
052
053   A common approach for handling missing values in time-series data is to use imputation methodolo-
      gies (Ahn et al., 2022; Batista et al., 2002), which are aimed at reconstructing (or filling) the missing

101   2     R ELATED W ORKS
102
103   Imputation Methods: Time-series modeling, be it for classification or forecasting, has several
104   applications in a number of domains. Traditionally, most imputation techniques for handling missing
105   values in time-series are based on statistical approaches involving either single-value imputation,
106   such as mean or median filling (Fung, 2006), proximity-based imputation (Batista et al., 2002), or
107   multiple-value imputation, such as Maximum Likelihood Imputation (Dempster et al., 1977) and
      Matrix completion methods (Mnih & Salakhutdinov, 2007). In recent years, there is a growing trend


                                                                              2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to use machine learning techniques for time-series imputations, e.g., Bayesian Networks (Sardinha
109   et al., 2018), Random Forest (Stekhoven & Bühlmann, 2012), and deep learning methods such as
110   SAITS (Du et al., 2023), GAIN(Yoon et al., 2018a), and BRITS(Cao et al., 2018). While these deep
111   learning-based models are highly efficient during inference, they require additional training time,
112   which add to the already large time complexity of time-series models.
113
      Time-series Modeling: While there are several time-series modeling approaches for short-term
114
      forecasting (Challu et al., 2023) (Oreshkin et al., 2019), there is a growing focus to explore long-term
115   forecasting problems. With the introduction of attention mechanisms via transformer models (Vaswani
116   et al., 2017), a number of transformer-based time-series models have been introduced in the last few
117   years (Wu et al., 2021), (Nie et al., 2022b), (Dong et al., 2024), (Liu et al., 2023) for longer-horizon
118   forecasting. The current state-of-the-art methods can be divided into three primary categories—
119   Representation Learning methods, Transformer-based methods, and LLM-based methods, with the
120   latter being the newest addition to the group. Representation Learning methods, especially MAE-style
121   time-series models (Li et al., 2023), are receiving a lot of recent interest owing to their ability of
122   learning both low-level and high-level representations useful for varied downstream tasks such as
123
      forecasting and classification. However, a major limitation restricting their applicability to real-world
      problems is the presence of missing values common to many time-series datasets. While state-of-the
124
      art time-series models provide strong prediction accuracy, they are not designed to handle missing
125
      values. Transformer-based models, including the ones that use self-supervised style training, can
126   deal with missing values in the temporal domain, but not in the feature-space. There have also
127   been models like (Liu et al., 2023) that efficiently captures the feature-space correlations and show
128   superior forecasting results. However, they do not account for the presence of missing values along
129   the temporal dimension of the time-series variates. In contrast to all existing approaches, our work
130   accounts for missing values in both the feature and temporal space through the proposed Time-Feature
131   Independent Embedding and Missing Feature-Aware Attention Mechanisms.

159   3.1   N OTATIONS AND P ROBLEM F ORMULATIONS
160
161   Let us represent a multivariate time-series as X ∈ RT ×N , where T is the number of time-steps, and
      N is the dimensionality (number of variates) of the time-series. We assume a subset of variates (or


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      features) to be missing at some time-steps of X, represented in the form of a missing-value mask
163   M ∈ [0, 1]T ×N , where M(t,d) represents the value of the mask at t-th time-step and d-th dimension.
164   M(t,d) = 1 denotes that the corresponding value in X(t,d) is missing, while M(t,d) = 0 denotes that
165   X(t,d) is observed. Furthermore, let us denote X(t,:) ∈ RN as the multiple variates of the time-series
166
      at a particular time-step t, and X(:,d) ∈ RT as the uni-variate time-series for the variate d. In this
167   paper, we consider two downstream tasks for time-series modeling: forecasting and classification.
168   For forecasting, the goal is to predict the future S time-steps of X represented as Y ∈ RS×N .
169   Alternatively, for time-series classification, the goal is to predict output labels Y ∈ {1, 2, ..., C}
170   given X, where C is the number of classes.

172   3.2   L EARNING E MBEDDINGS FOR T IME -S ERIES WITH M ISSING F EATURES
173
174   Limitations of Existing Methods: The first step in time-series modeling using transformer-based
175   architectures is to learn an embedding of the time-series X that can be sent to the transformer
176
      encoder. Traditionally, this is done using an Embedding-layer (typically implemented using a
      multi-layered perceptron) as Embedding : RN 7→ RD that maps X ∈ RT ×N to the embedding
177
      H ∈ RT ×D , where D is the embedding dimension. The Embedding layer operates on every time-step
178
      independently such that the set of variates observed at time-step t, X(t,:) , is considered as a single
179
      token and mapped to the embedding vector ht ∈ RD as ht = Embedding(X(t,:) ) (see Figure 2(a)).
180
      An alternate embedding scheme was recently introduced in the framework of inverted Transformer
181   (iTransformer) (Liu et al., 2023), where the uni-variate time-series for the d-th variate, X(:,d) , is
182   considered as a single token and mapped to the embedding vector: hd = Embedding(X(:,d) ) (see
183   Figure 2(b)). While both these embedding schemes have their unique advantages, they are unfit to
184   handle time-series with arbitrary sets of missing values at every time-step. In particular, the input
185   tokens to the Embedding layer of Transformer or iTransformer requires all components of X(t,:) or
186   X(:,d) to be observed, respectively. If any of the components in these tokens are missing, we will not
187   be able to compute their embeddings and thus will have to discard either the time-step or the variate,
188   leading to loss of information.
189   Time-Feature Independent (TFI) Embedding: To address this challenge, we propose a novel
190   Time-Feature Independent (TFI) Embedding scheme for time-series with missing features, where the
191   value at each combination of time-step t and variate d is considered as a single token X(t,d) , and is
192   independently mapped to an embedding using TFIEmbedding : R 7→ RD as follows:
193
                                       h(t,d) = TFIEmbedding(X(t,d) )                                         (1)
194

195   In other words, the TFIEmbedding Layer maps X ∈ R                    into the TFI embedding H         ∈
196   RT ×N ×D (see Figure 2(c)). We should emphasize that we apply the TFIEmbedding Layer only

      approach is that even if a particular value in the time-series is missing, other observed values in the

281   3.4   P UTTING E VERYTHING T OGETHER : OVERALL F RAMEWORK OF M ISS TSM
282
      Figure 3 shows the overall framework of MissTSM. For an input time-series X, we apply the TFI
283
      embedding layer followed by the MFAA layer to learn a latent representation for every time-step.
284
      We then integrate the latent representations into a Masked Auto-Encoder (MAE) (He et al., 2022)
285   framework adapted for time-series (similar to Ti-MAE (Li et al., 2023)). Although the design of the
286   proposed TFI-Embedding and MFAA Layers are flexible enough that they can be integrated with any
287   transformer-based time-series modeling framework (e.g., AutoFormer (Wu et al., 2021)), we opted for
288   a masked time-series modeling approach (such as Ti-MAE (Li et al., 2023) and SimMTM (Dong et al.,
289   2024)) due to their recent success in time-series modeling. Further, out of the several state-of-the-art
290   masked time-series modelling techniques, we intentionally chose the simplest variation of MAE,
291   namely Ti-MAE (Li et al., 2023), to highlight the power of TFI and MFAA layers in handling missing
292   values. To the best of our knowledge, MissTSM is the first end-to-end framework for modeling
293
      time-series with missing values without explicitly imputing the time-series. Like a typical masked
      time-series modeling approach, MissTSM has two main stages: (1) Self-Supervised Learning Stage:
294
      where the multivariate time-series (with missing values) is reconstructed using an encoder-decoder
295
      architecture, with the goal of learning meaningful representations using just the encoder, and (2)
296   Fine-tuning Stage: where the latent representations learned by the encoder are fed into a multi-layer
297   perceptron to perform downstream tasks of forecasting and classification.

      Datasets: We consider three popular time-series forecasting datasets: ETTh2, ETTm2 (Zhou et al.,
302
      2021) and Weather (wea). We follow a train/val/test split ratio of 6:2:2 for ETT datasets and 7:1:2
303
      for Weather dataset following prior work on Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021). For classification, we
304   use three real-world datasets, namely, Epilepsy (Andrzejak et al., 2001), EMG (Goldberger et al.,
305   2000), and Gesture (Liu et al., 2009), and follow the same evaluation setups as proposed in TF-C
306   (Zhang et al., 2022). We also evaluate on a real-world dataset-PhysioNet-2012 (Silva et al., 2012)
307   that contains 12k multivariate clinical time-series samples with 37 variables. See Appendix A.1 for
308   additional details about dataset and evaluation setup description.
309
      Baselines: For all of our experiments, we consider five state-of-the-art time-series modeling baselines,
310   SimMTM (Dong et al., 2024), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022b), AutoFormer (Wu et al., 2021), DLinear
311   (Zeng et al., 2023), and iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023). In order to apply these methods on data
312   with missing values, we also consider four imputation techniques—a 2nd -order spline imputation
313   (McKinley & Levine, 1998), a simple k-Nearest Neighbor approach (Tan et al., 2019), and two
314   state-of-the-art deep learning-based imputation techniques, SAITS (Du et al., 2023) and BRITS (Cao
315   et al., 2018). For forecasting, we used a default lookback window of L = 336, while we varied the
316   horizon windows as T ∈ {96, 192, 336, 720}.

359   Periodic Masking: Since missing values in time-series follow periodic patterns in many real-world
360   applications (e.g., the seasonal cycles in weather and environmental datasets), we introduce a periodic
361   masking scheme described as follows. We use a sine curve to generate the masking periodicity with
362   given phase and frequency values for different features. Specifically, the time-dependent periodic
363   probability of seeing missing values is defined as p̂(t) = p + α(1 − p)sin(2πνt + ϕ, where, ϕ and
364   ν are randomly chosen across the feature space, α is a scale factor, and p is an offset term. We vary p
365   from low to high values to get different fractions of periodic missing values in the data.

392   4.2   R ESULTS ON F ORECASTING DATASETS
393
394   Table 1 compares the forecasting performance of MissTSM with five SOTA baseline methods in
395   terms of the Mean Squared Error (MSE) metric on three datasets (ETTh2, ETTm2 and Weather) with
396   varying forecasting horizons, imputation techniques (Spline and SAITS), and masking schemes. We
397
      provide the mean and standard deviations over 5 different samples of the masking schemes. We choose
      a missing value probability of 60% for MCAR masking and 70% for periodic masking to simulate
398
      scenarios with varying (and often extreme) amounts of missing information. We can see that in the no
399
      masking experiment, the performance of all methods (with the exception of AutoFormer) are mostly
400   comparable to each other across all three datasets, with MissTSM and PatchTST having a slight edge
401   on the ETTh2/ETTm2 and Weather datasets, respectively. For the MCAR masking experiments, we
402   observe a trend across all the datasets that the MissTSM framework performs slightly better than
403   the baselines for longer-term forecasting (such as forecasting horizon of 720), and comparable to
404   the best-performing baselines on other forecasting horizons. For the Periodic masking experiment,
405   we can see that MissTSM is consistently better than the baselines for ETTh2 dataset, while for the
406   ETTm2 and Weather datasets, the forecasting performance is comparable to the other baselines.
407   These results demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed MissTSM framework to circumvent the
408
      need for explicit imputation of missing values while achieving comparable performance as SOTA.
409   By being imputation-free, MissTSM does not suffer from the propagation of imputation errors (from
410   the imputation scheme) to forecasting errors (from the time-series models). In Appendix Figure 11,
411   we provide empirical evidence of this error propagation, where we see a positive correlation between
412   imputation errors and forecasting errors of baseline methods, indicating that reducing imputation
413   errors is crucial for improving forecasting accuracy. This finding underscores the limitations of
414
      traditional two-stage approaches and suggests that using more sophisticated imputation models is
      necessary to achieve lower forecasting errors. We also report the computation time of SimMTM
415
      (with Spline and SAITS) and MissTSM in Appendix Table 5, where we demonstrate that MissTSM
416
      is significantly faster as it does not involve any expensive interpolations as an additional advantage.

      In the ablation experiments, our goal is to quantify the effectiveness of the TFI-Embedding scheme
      and the MFAA Layer on MissTSM. To achieve this, we compare MissTSM with Ti-MAE, which
520
      can be viewed as an ablation of MissTSM without the TFI-Embedding and MFAA Layers. We

      refer to this ablation of MissTSM as MAE. For both the forecasting (see Fig. 9) and classification

527   modeling with missing values.

529   5     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531   To the best of our knowledge, our proposed MissTSM framework is the first end-to-end framework
532   for time-series modeling with missing values that does not require any explicit imputations. We
533   empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of the MissTSM framework across multiple benchmark
534   datasets and synthetic masking strategies, and a real-world dataset with a high degree of missing
535   values. Our proposed framework also has limitations. For example, a limitation of the MFAA layer
536   is that it does not learn the non-linear temporal dynamics and relies on the subsequent transformer
537   encoder blocks to learn the dynamics. Future work can explore modifications of the MFAA layer
538   such that it can jointly learn the cross-channel correlations with the non-linear temporal dynamics.

760
761   Forecasting Dataset Details
762   ETT. The ETT Zhou et al. (2021) dataset captures load and oil temperature data from electricity
763   transformers. ETTh2 includes 17,420 hourly observations, while ETTm2 comprises 69,680 15-minute
764   observations. Both datasets span two years and contain 7 variates each.
765
      Weather. Weather wea is a 10-minute frequency time-series dataset recorded throughout the year
766   2020 and consists of 21 meteorological indicators, like humidity, air temperature, etc.
767
768   Following previous works in this area, we use a train-validation-test split of 6:2:2 for the ETT datasets
769
      and 7:1:2 for the Weather dataset. We standardized the input features by subtracting off the mean
      and dividing by the standard deviation for every feature over the training set. Again, following
770
      the approach used in previous works, we compute the MSE in the normalized space of all features
771
      considering all features together.
772
773   Classification Dataset Details
774   Epilepsy. Epilepsy Andrzejak et al. (2001) contains univariate brainwaves (single-channel EEG)
775   sampled from 500 subjects (with 11,500 samples in total), with each sample classified as having
776   epilepsy or not (binary classification).
777
      Gesture. Gesture Liu et al. (2009) dataset consists of 560 samples, each having 3 variates (corre-
778
      sponding to the accelerometer data) and each sample corresponding to one of the 8 hand gestures (or
779   classes)
780
781   EMG. EMG Goldberger et al. (2000) dataset contains 163 EMG (Electromyography) samples
782
      corresponding to 3-classes of muscular diseases.
783   We make use of the following readily available data splits (train, validation, test) for each of
784   the datasets: Epilepsy = 60 (30 samples per each class)/20 (10 samples per each class)/11420
785   (Train/Val/Test) Gesture = 320/20/120 (Train/Val/Test) EMG = 122/41/41 (Train/Val/Test)
786   Physio-Net Dataset: PhysioNet-2012 Mortality Prediction Challenge (Silva et al., 2012) contains
787   12k multivariate clinical time-series samples that were collected from patients in ICU. The time-series
788   contains 37 variables, such as temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, etc. that can vary depending on
789   the type of patient. Each of the samples are recorded during the first 48 hours of admission in ICU.
790   Because of the high variability of variables collected across patients in ICU with different patterns of
791   missing information across time, PhysioNet has a high degree of 80% missing values. We follow the
792   experimental setup in (Du et al., 2023), and split the dataset into 80%, 10% and 10% train/val/test
793   split.

      A.2    S YNTHETIC M ASKED DATA G ENERATION
796
797
      Random Masking: We generated masks by randomly selecting data points across all variates and
798   time-steps, assigning them as missing with a likelihood determined by p (masking fraction). The
799   selected data points were then removed, effectively simulating missing values at random. For multiple
800   runs, we created multiple such versions of the synthetic datasets and compared all baseline methods
801   and MissTSM on the same datasets.
802
      Periodic Masking: We use a sine curve to generate the masking periodicity with given phase and
803
      frequency values for different features. Specifically, the time-dependent periodic probability of seeing
804   missing values is defined as p̂(t) = p + α(1 − p)sin(2πνt + ϕ), where, ϕ and ν are randomly
805   chosen across the feature space, α is a scale factor, and p is an offset term. We vary p from low
806   to high values to get different fractions of periodic missing values in the data. To implement this
807   masking strategy, each feature in the dataset was assigned a unique frequency, randomly selected
808   from the range [0.2, 0.8]. This was done to reduce bias and increase randomness in periodicity across
809   the feature space. Additionally, the phase shift was chosen randomly from the range [0, 2π]. This was
      applied to each feature to offset the sinusoidal function over time. Like frequency, the phase value

      was different for different features. This generated a periodic pattern for the likelihood of missing
811   data.
