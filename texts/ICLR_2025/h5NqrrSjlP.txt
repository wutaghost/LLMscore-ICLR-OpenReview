009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Symbolic regression is a challenging task in machine learning that aims to automati-
012            cally discover highly interpretable mathematical equations from limited data. Keen
013            efforts have been devoted to addressing this issue, yielding promising results. How-
014            ever, there are still bottlenecks that current methods struggle with, especially when
015            dealing with complex problems containing various noises or with intricate underly-
016            ing mathematical formulas. In this work, we propose a novel Geometric Evolution
017            Symbolic Regression(GESR) algorithm. Leveraging geometric semantics, the
018
               process of symbolic regression in GESR is transformed into an approximation to
               an unimodal target in n-dimensional topological space. Then, three key modules
019
               are proposed to enhance the approximation: (1) a new semantic gradient concept,
020
               proposed to assist the exploration, which aims to improve the accuracy of approxi-
021            mation; (2) a new geometric search operator, tailored for approximating the target
022            formula directly in topological space; (3) the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm with
023            L2 regularization, used for the adjustment of expression structures and the balance
024            of global subtree weights to assist the proposed geometric semantic search operator.
025            With the proposal of these modules, GESR achieves state-of-the-art accuracy per-
026            formance on multiple authoritative benchmark datasets and demonstrates a certain
027            level of robustness against noise interference. The implementation is available at
028            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/12331211321-014D.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Symbolic regression(SR) is a supervised machine learning method that learns interpretable mathemat-
      ical expressions directly from a given dataset. SR provides us with the opportunity to automatically
034
      extract highly interpretable mathematical expressions that depict the underlying objective patterns in
035
      complex data from a mathematical perspective, rather than heavily relying on expert intuition and
036   sensitivity to data. Compared to the numerical solutions generated through neural networks, quan-
037   titative mathematical expressions can provide more interpretability and generalization. Nowadays,
038   SR has been widely applied in a variety of tasks, from scientific discovery (Makke & Chawla, 2024;
039   Wang et al., 2023) to engineering applications (Angelis et al., 2023), including governing equations
040   (Sun et al., 2022), finding fundamental physical laws (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020), fault detection
041   (Hale et al., 2022), and TCP congestion control (Sharan et al., 2022).
042   Genetic programming(GP) serves as the primary algorithm for SR. In GP, symbolic regression is
043   addressed by evolving the expression trees within a population. With the iterative variation of the
044   population and the guidance of fitness, the accuracy of expression trees gradually improves until a
045   satisfactory level is achieved. GP has been proven to be very useful in many tasks, such as inferring
046   physical laws. However, when the underlying formula is complex, its effectiveness still falls short of
047   expectations due to the quite large search space.
048   With the exploration of symbolic regression and machine learning, numerous approaches are proposed
049   to seek breakthroughs in terms of inference time, solution rate, accuracy, and model size. Deep
050   Learning methods emerge due to their avoidance of the hyperparameter sensitivity problem in GP
051   and their rapid inference time. SymbolicGPT (Valipour et al., 2021) uses deep language models
052   like GPT to generate mathematical expressions for SR. AIFeynman (Udrescu et al., 2020) trains
053   a neural network to estimate functional modularities. NGGP (Mundhenk et al., 2021) utilizes a
      hybrid approach to assist GP in generating a start population with neural networks. The end-to-end


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      transformer model for SR (Kamienny et al., 2022) also shows its capability on exploration and
055   inference time. Reinforcement Learning is then employed to explore and discover expressions,
056   whereas the Monte Carlo tree search method is utilized. DSR (Petersen et al., 2019) is a representative
057   reinforcement learning method, which uses a learn policy to add notations to parse tree in turn while
058   a neural network is utilized to generate symbolic distribution. Then, uDSR (Landajuela et al., 2022)
059   is proposed as a comprehensive framework that hybridizes GP, AIFeynman, linear models, DSR, and
060   large-scale pre-training into a single module and achieves state-of-the-art performance.
061   However, these methods still face challenges in effectively generating formulas from data charac-
062   terized by intricate mathematical representations. Recently, the development of semantic genetic
063   programming(SGP) (Pawlak et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2022) provides us with a
064   fresh perspective. As we all know, the core idea of GP lies in the concept of iterative evolution, and the
065   benefits of the ”evolution” have been widely demonstrated. Nevertheless, the lack of explicitly guided
066   cross-mutation processes severely hinders the improvement of the accuracy of formula evolution.
067   Semantic genetic programming presents the unimodal landscape of symbolic regression, enabling the
068   exploration of approximate unimodal solutions directly in the underlying topological space(named
069
      semantic space). Compared to the random exploration of traditional genetic programming, the
      approximation in the topological space exhibits significantly stronger guidance and superior search
070
      efficiency. However, suffering from dimension curse and sparse characteristics in semantic space, the
071
      search performance in the underlying semantic space rapidly slows down after several generations
072   and the approximation process becomes convoluted. Severe tree bloating is also a bottleneck problem
073   since SGP directly maps each step of semantic approximation to the modifications of expression trees
074   without the tree structure information in the semantic space.
075
      To address these issues, we propose a geometric evolution symbolic regression(GESR) algorithm.
076
      The proposed algorithm significantly improves the fitting performance of symbolic expression while
077
      effectively tackling concerns associated with tree bloating. Utilizing SRbench benchmark and SRSD
078   benchmark for both black-box and scientific discovery symbolic regression, our method achieves
079   state-of-the-art accuracy results across both benchmarks.
080
081
      In summary, our work introduces several key contributions: (1) We introduce the concept of semantic
      gradients based on discovering the inconsistency of the approximation process in sub-semantic space
082
      and target semantic space, enabling accurate approximation of mappings between sub-objective spaces
083
      and objective spaces, thereby enhancing semantic fitting accuracy. (2) We develop a novel geometric
084   semantic method capable of efficiently approximating target semantics in the sparse semantic space.
085   (3) We also present a mechanism to assist the geometric semantic method in adjusting the generated
086   expression structure and capturing the potential solution by zero-ing the weights of uninformative
087   subtrees (via the LM algorithm). (4) Our proposed GESR consistently demonstrates promising
088   performance across a diverse array of SRbench and SRSD benchmark datasets, as validated through
089   rigorous ablation experiments. When compared to the baseline, GESR exhibits notably superior
090   accuracy in scientific discovery tasks and showcases robust noise resistance capabilities.

      Genetic Programming: genetic programming refers to an evolutionary algorithm instance applied to
095
      SR, in which a set of expression trees evolve iteratively under the guidance of fitness. Modules such
096   as crossover, mutation, and selection are employed to evolve the population towards minimizing the
097   distance from the optimal expression. Many popular frameworks use GP for symbolic regression
098   (Cranmer, 2023; Burlacu et al., 2020; La Cava et al., 2018; de Franca & Aldeia, 2021b; Zhang et al.,
099   2023) and a survey (Mei et al., 2022) is recommended for an overview of GP for SR. In this work,
100   we inherited the concept of population evolution from GP, combining it with geometric semantic
101   methods (Virgolin et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019) and constant optimization.
102   Geometric Semantic Evolution: geometric semantic genetic programming(GSGP) (Vanneschi,
103   2016) is a type of algorithm that evolves the mathematical formula directly in n-dimensional topo-
104   logical space. With the semantic information, GSGP converts the traditional mutations into the
105   operations with geometric properties in the underlying semantic space to approximate the unimodal
106   target semantics and directly map this geometric operation to syntactic modifications of expression
107   trees. Many works have been proposed to improve the performance (Pietropolli et al., 2022; Chen
      et al., 2019; Nguyen & Chu, 2020; Virgolin et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2024). However, due to the

148   3.1   P RELIMINARY
149
150   Given a dataset D = {(xi , yi )}i≤N , (xi , yi ) ∈ Rd × R, the main goal of symbolic regression (SR)
151   methods is to find a symbolic expression f ∗ that minimizes the theoretical risk of the input-output
152
      mapping of the dataset D:
                                       f ∗ = arg max ED∼Ω(D) [L(f, D)]                                  (1)

      Semantic genetic programming (SGP) vectorizes the output of the symbolic expression related to the
155
      dataset D as a semantic vector, with which the iterative improvement of the symbolic expression can
156   be achieved through semantic target approximation in topological space. The semantic vectors of
157   possible solutions constitute semantic space, where each symbolic expression is mapped to a semantic
158   vector point. In this paper, we propose a geometric semantic genetic programming algorithm, which
159   utilizes geometric properties describing spatial relationships between symbolic expressions in the
160   n-dimensional semantic space, where n is equivalent to the size of the training set. By directly
161   approximating the target semantic with a geometric method in the semantic space and mapping the
      corresponding geometric operations to the syntactic modifications of symbolic expression, newly

162
      obtained symbolic expression can be closer to the target. For example, the midpoint of the two
163   semantic points s1 and s2 that represent two symbolic expression trees tr1 and tr2 respectively
164   in the semantic space can be mapped to a new expression tree: s′ : 1/2s1 + 1/2s2 → tr′ :
165   1/2 ∗ tr1 + 1/2 ∗ tr2 .
166
      In our method, geometric semantics is used to guide the mutation, as shown in Figure 1. However,
167
      although geometric semantic mutation improves search efficiency and accuracy, frequent linear
168
      combinations of expressions often lead to severe tree bloating. Therefore, we backpropagate the
169   target semantics to sub-expression, combined with semantic gradients and strict limitations, to guide
170   the mutation process in the sub-semantic space. The linear combination of sub-expressions also
171   leads to a significant increase in the proportion of constants. While geometric semantic mutation
172   based on backpropagation is restricted to only adjusting local constants, it is difficult to balance
173   the optimization of global constants. Thus, we incorporate the Levenberg-Marquardt optimization
174   algorithm with L2 regularization to periodically adjust global constants, which also enables the
175   smoothing of formula curves. By the way, since we assign a constant (weight) to each combination
176   subtree, there is a natural characteristic for our method to use a continuous optimization algorithm to
177
      further adjust the overall tree structures.

337
      The optimization of geometric semantic mutation lies solely in the adjustment of local tree structures,
338
      and it is worth noting that our geometric semantics method provides weight coefficients for each
339
      subtree(Equation 6). Differs from the traditional methods that use a continuous optimization algorithm
340   to optimize the constants (Kommenda et al., 2020; De Melo et al., 2015), the presence of these
341   weight coefficients allows us not only to adjust each subtree module at any time but also, more
342   importantly, to automatically adjust the overall structure by optimizing constants and removing
343   unsuitable subtrees (by setting the corresponding subtree’s weight coefficient to 0.0, when the
344   weight coefficient is approximated to nearly zero(<1e-6 in our method)). Therefore, we employ
345   the Levenberg-Marquardt(LM) (Moré, 2006) algorithm to perform constant optimization on the
346   expressions at regular intervals. The LM algorithm utilizes Euclidean distance as the loss function.

500   5   C ONCLUSION
501
502   In this work, a novel geometric evolution model is proposed for SR to try to improve the spatial
503   approximation accuracy while ensuring the interpretability. In GESR, a geometric semantic mutation
504   method is introduced as a geometric approximation strategy to search for expressions with optimal
505   accuracy in the semantic space, using a semantic gradient vector as an auxiliary tool for addressing
506   semantic space mapping issues. A semantic-based mutation point selection method is further
507   introduced to efficiently identify sub-expressions that need improvement. Taking into account the
508   inherent advantage of linear combinations within the semantic space, we further incorporate the
509   Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm as a key module for globally adjusting and optimizing expressions
510
      constants. The state-of-the-art accuracy performance achieved on both the SRSD and SRBench
      benchmark datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of our approach in real-world and scientific
511
      discovery tasks.

      The hyperparameters for GESR are listed in Table 4. In this work, our method aims to directly
840
      approximate the output of the target expression numerically and then map the operation to the formula
841
      in the population. To mitigate the impact of poor initial expression structure, we employ a parameter
842   setting with a large population and small depth, allowing for broader coverage of initial expression
843   structures and increased fault tolerance. A smaller tournament size also facilitates group exploration.
844   For variation rate, we primarily utilize geometric semantic search methods while maintaining a certain
845   probability of random variation. The significance of random variation lies in its facilitation of early-
846   stage population exploration, prevention of local optimization due to the solidification of population
847   expressions in later stages, and selection of new expression structures into the semantic library. The
848   updated pattern for the semantic database involves high-frequency sampling with small amounts to
849   sample elite individuals’ expression structures at each stage of evolution. Each semantic database
850
      samples 20 subtrees from the top 10 individuals’ semantics and randomly replaces these subtrees
      within the database. During evolution, our function set consists of 8 tuples <+, -, *, %protected, sin,
851
      cos, log, exp> protected by interval arithmeticKeijzer (2003).
852
853   In addition, the diversity of the expression tree structure is important. we think employing a large
854   population with fewer generations outperforms the alternative of a small population with numerous
855   generations. That is because of the gradual loss of population diversity as the number of iterations
856
      increases, in which the localized mutation is not enough to alter the overall structure. Therefore,
      we use a large population size with relatively small generations to promote exploration within the
857
      population.
