010
011
012
               We propose (S)peculative (C)ontrastive MCTS∗ : a novel Monte Carlo Tree Search
013            (MCTS) reasoning algorithm for Large Language Models (LLMs) which signifi-
014            cantly improves both reasoning accuracy and speed. Our motivation comes from:
015            1. Previous MCTS LLM reasoning works often overlooked its biggest draw-
016            back—slower speed compared to CoT; 2. Previous research mainly used MCTS
017            as a tool for LLM reasoning on various tasks with limited quantitative analysis or
018            ablation studies of its components from reasoning interpretability perspective. 3.
019            The reward model is the most crucial component in MCTS, however previous work
020            has rarely conducted in-depth study or improvement of MCTS’s reward models.
021
               Thus, we conducted extensive ablation studies and quantitative analysis on compo-
               nents of MCTS, revealing the impact of each component on the MCTS reasoning
022
               performance of LLMs. Building on this, (i) we designed a highly interpretable
023
               reward model based on the principle of contrastive decoding and (ii) achieved
024            an average speed improvement of 51.9% per node using speculative decoding.
025            Additionally, (iii) we improved UCT node selection strategy and backpropagation
026            used in previous works, resulting in significant performance improvement. We
027            outperformed o1-mini by an average of 17.4% on the Blocksworld multi-step
028            reasoning dataset using Llama-3.1-70B with SC-MCTS∗ .

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   With the remarkable development of Large Language Models (LLMs), models such as o1 (OpenAI,
038   2024a) have now gained a strong ability for multi-step reasoning across complex tasks and can solve
039
      problems that are more difficult than previous scientific, code, and mathematical problems. The
      reasoning task has long been considered challenging for LLMs. These tasks require converting a
040
      problem into a series of reasoning steps and then executing those steps to arrive at the correct answer.
041
      Recently, LLMs have shown great potential in addressing such problems. A key approach is using
042   Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2024), where LLMs break down the solution into a series of
043   reasoning steps before arriving at the final answer. Despite the impressive capabilities of CoT-based
044   LLMs, they still face challenges when solving problems with an increasing number of reasoning
045   steps due to the curse of autoregressive decoding (Sprague et al., 2024). Previous work has explored
046   reasoning through the use of heuristic reasoning algorithms. For example, Yao et al. (2024) applied
047   heuristic-based search, such as Depth-First Search (DFS) to derive better reasoning paths. Similarly,
048   Hao et al. (2023) employed MCTS to iteratively enhance reasoning step by step toward the goal.
049   The tremendous success of AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) has demonstrated the effectiveness of the
050   heuristic MCTS algorithm, showcasing its exceptional performance across various domains (Jumper
051   et al., 2021; Silver et al., 2017). Building on this, MCTS has also made notable progress in the field of
052   LLMs through multi-step heuristic reasoning. Previous work has highlighted the potential of heuristic
053   MCTS to significantly enhance LLM reasoning capabilities. Despite these advancements, substantial
      challenges remain in fully realizing the benefits of heuristic MCTS in LLM reasoning.

069   The first key challenge is that MCTS’s general reasoning ability is almost entirely dependent on the
070   reward model’s performance (as demonstrated by our ablation experiments in Section 5.5), making it
071   highly challenging to design dense, general yet efficient rewards to guide MCTS reasoning. Previous
072   works either require two or more LLMs (Tian et al., 2024) or training epochs (Zhang et al., 2024a),
073   escalating the VRAM and computational demand, or they rely on domain-specific tools (Xin et al.,
074
      2024a;b) or datasets (Qi et al., 2024), making it difficult to generalize to other tasks or datasets.
075   The second key challenge is that MCTS is significantly slower than Chain of Thoughts (CoT). CoT
076   only requires designing a prompt of multi-turn chats (Wei et al., 2024). In contrast, MCTS builds a
077   reasoning tree with 2–10 layers depending on the difficulty of the task, where each node in the tree
078   represents a chat round with LLM which may need to be visited one or multiple times. Moreover, to
079   obtain better performance, we typically perform 2–10 MCTS iterations, which greatly increases the
080
      number of nodes, leading to much higher computational costs and slower reasoning speed.
081   To address the these challenges, we went beyond prior works that treated MCTS as a tool and
082   focused on analyzing and improving its components especially reward model. Using contrastive
083   decoding, we redesigned reward model by integrating interpretable reward signals, clustering their
084   prior distributions, and normalizing the rewards using our proposed prior statistical method. To
085   prevent distribution shift, we also incorporated an online incremental update algorithm. We found
086
      that the commonly used Upper Confidence Bound on Trees (UCT) strategy often underperformed
      due to sensitivity to the exploration constant, so we refined it and improved backpropagation to favor
087
      steadily improving paths. To address speed issues, we integrated speculative decoding as a "free
088
      lunch." All experiments were conducted using the Blocksworld dataset detailed in Section 5.1.
089
090   Our goal is to: (i) design novel and high-performance reward models and maximize the performance of
091   reward model combinations, (ii) analyze and optimize the performance of various MCTS components,
092
      (iii) enhance the interpretability of MCTS reasoning, (iv) and accelerate MCTS reasoning. Our
      contributions are summarized as follows:
093
094   1. We went beyond previous works who primarily treated MCTS as an tool rather than analyzing and
095      improving its components. Specifically, we found the UCT strategy in most previous works may
096      failed to function from our experiment. We also refined the backpropagation of MCTS to prefer
097
         more steadily improving paths, boosting performance.
098   2. To fully study the interpretability of MCTS multi-step reasoning, we conducted extensive quanti-
099      tative analysis and ablation studies on every component. We carried out numerous experiments
100      from both the numerical and distributional perspectives of the reward models, as well as its own
101
         interpretability, providing better interpretability for MCTS multi-step reasoning.
102   3. We designed a novel, general action-level reward model based on the principle of contrastive
103      decoding, which requires no external tools, training, or datasets. Additionally, we found that
104      previous works often failed to effectively harness multiple reward models, thus we proposed a
105
         statistical linear combination method. At the same time, we introduced speculative decoding to
         speed up MCTS reasoning by an average of 52% as a "free lunch."
106
107   We demonstrated the effectiveness of our approach by outperforming OpenAI’s flagship o1-mini
      model by an average of 17.4% using Llama-3.1-70B on the Blocksworld multi-step reasoning dataset.

      2     R ELATED W ORK

110
      Large Language Models Multi-Step Reasoning One of the key focus areas for LLMs is un-
111
      derstanding and enhancing their reasoning capabilities. Recent advancements in this area focused
112
      on developing methods that improve LLMs’ ability to handle complex tasks in domains like code
113   generation and mathematical problem-solving. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2024) reasoning
114   has been instrumental in helping LLMs break down intricate problems into a sequence of manageable
115   steps, making them more adept at handling tasks that require logical reasoning. Building upon this,
116   Tree-of-Thought (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024) reasoning extends CoT by allowing models to explore
117   multiple reasoning paths concurrently, thereby enhancing their ability to evaluate different solutions
118   simultaneously. Complementing these approaches, Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) has emerged
119   as a powerful reasoning method for decision-making in LLMs. Originally successful in AlphaGo’s
120   victory (Silver et al., 2016), MCTS has been adapted to guide model-based planning by balancing ex-
121
      ploration and exploitation through tree-based search and random sampling, and later to large language
      model reasoning (Hao et al., 2023), showing great results. This adaptation has proven particularly
122
      effective in areas requiring strategic planning. Notable implementations like ReST-MCTS∗ (Zhang
123
      et al., 2024a), rStar (Qi et al., 2024), MCTSr (Zhang et al., 2024b) and Xie et al. (2024) have shown
124   that integrating MCTS with reinforced self-training, self-play mutual reasoning or Direct Prefer-
125   ence Optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023) can significantly improve reasoning capabilities in LLMs.
126   Furthermore, recent advancements such as Deepseek Prover (Xin et al., 2024a;b) demonstrates the
127   potential of these models to understand complex instructions such as formal mathematical proof.
128
129
      Decoding Strategies Contrastive decoding and speculative decoding both require Smaller Language
130   Models (SLMs), yet few have realized that these two clever decoding methods can be seamlessly
131   combined without any additional cost. The only work that noticed this was Yuan et al. (2024a), but
132   their proposed speculative contrastive decoding focused on token-level decoding. In contrast, we
133   designed a new action-level contrastive decoding to guide MCTS reasoning, the distinction will be
134   discussed further in Section 4.1. For more detailed related work please refer to Appendix B.

147   3.2   M ONTE C ARLO T REE S EARCH
148
149   Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a decision-making algorithm involving a search tree to simulate
150   and evaluate actions. The algorithm operates in the following four phases:
151
      Node Selection: The selection process begins at the root, selecting nodes hierarchically using
152
      strategies like UCT as the criterion to favor a child node based on its quality and novelty.
153
154   Expansion: New child nodes are added to the selected leaf node by sampling d possible actions,
155   predicting the next state. If the leaf node is fully explored or terminal, expansion is skipped.
156   Simulation: During simulation or “rollout”, the algorithm plays out the “game” randomly from that
157   node to a terminal state using a default policy.
158
      Backpropagation: Once a terminal state is reached, the reward is propagated up the tree, and each
159
      node visited during the selection phase updates its value based on the simulation result.
160
161   Through iterative application of its four phases, MCTS efficiently improves reasoning through trials
      and heuristics, converging on the optimal solution.

      3.3   C ONTRASTIVE D ECODING
163
164   We discuss vanilla Contrastive Decoding (CD) from Li et al. (2023), which improves text generation
165   in LLMs by reducing errors like repetition and self-contradiction. CD uses the differences between an
166   expert model and an amateur model, enhancing the expert’s strengths and suppressing the amateur’s
167   weaknesses. Consider a prompt of length n, the CD objective is defined as:
168
                          LCD (xcont , xpre ) = log pEXP (xcont |xpre ) − log pAMA (xcont |xpre )
169
170   where xpre is the sequence of tokens x1 , . . . , xn , the model generates continuations of length m, xcont
171   is the sequence of tokens xn+1 , . . . , xn+m , and pEXP and pAMA are the expert and amateur probability
172   distributions. To avoid penalizing correct behavior of the amateur or promoting implausible tokens,
173   CD applies an adaptive plausibility constraint using an α-mask, which filters tokens by their logits
174   against a threshold, the filtered vocabulary Vvalid is defined as:
175                                                   (i)                      (k)
                                      Vvalid = {i | sEXP ≥ log α + max sEXP }
176                                                                      k
177           (i)       (i)
178   where sEXP and sAMA are unnormalized logits assigned to token i by the expert and amateur models.
179
      Final logits are adjusted with a coefficient (1 + β), modifying the contrastive effect on output
      scores (Liu et al., 2021):
180
                                          (i)           (i)     (i)
181                                      sCD = (1 + β)sEXP − sAMA
182   However, our proposed CD is at action level, averaging over the whole action, instead of token
183   level in vanilla CD. Our novel action-level CD reward more robustly captures the differences in
184   confidence between the expert and amateur models in the generated answers compared to vanilla CD.
185   The distinction will be illustrated in Section 4.1 and explained further in Appendix A.

187   3.4   S PECULATIVE D ECODING AS " FREE LUNCH "
188
189   Based on Speculative Decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023), the process can be summarized as follows:
190   Let Mp be the target model with the conditional distribution p(xt |x<t ), and Mq be a smaller
191
      approximation model with q(xt |x<t ). The key idea is to generate γ tokens using Mq and filter them
      against Mp ’s distribution, accepting tokens consistent with Mp . Speculative decoding samples γ
192
      tokens autoregressively from Mq , keeping those where q(x) ≤ p(x). If q(x) > p(x), the sample is
193
194
      rejected with probability 1 − p(x)
                                    q(x) , and a new sample is drawn from the adjusted distribution:
195
                                       p′ (x) = norm(max(0, p(x) − q(x))).
196
197   Since both contrastive and speculative decoding rely on the same smaller models, we can achieve the
198   acceleration effect of speculative decoding as a "free lunch" (Yuan et al., 2024a).

      4.1   M ULTI -R EWARD D ESIGN
203
204   Our primary goal is to design novel and and high-performance reward models for MCTS reasoning
205   and to maximize the performance of reward model combinations, as our ablation experiments in
206   Section 5.5 demonstrate that MCTS performance is almost entirely determined by the reward model.
207
      SC-MCTS∗ is guided by three highly interpretable reward models: contrastive JS divergence, log-
208
      likelihood and self evaluation. Previous work such as (Hao et al., 2023) often directly adds reward
209
      functions with mismatched numerical magnitudes without any prior statistical analysis or linear
210   combination. As a result, their combined reward models may fail to demonstrate full performance.
211   Moreover, combining multiple rewards online presents numerous challenges such as distributional
212   shifts in the values. Thus, we propose a statistically-informed reward combination method: Multi-
213   RM method. Each reward model is normalized contextually by the fine-grained prior statistics of
214   its empirical distribution. The pseudocode for reward model construction is shown in Algorithm 1.
215   Please refer to Appendix D for a complete version of SC-MCTS∗ that includes other improvements
      such as dealing with distribution shift when combining reward functions online.

      4.3   BACKPROPAGATION
307
308   After each MCTS iteration, multiple paths from the root to terminal nodes are generated. By
309   backpropagating along these paths, we update the value of each state-action pair. Previous MCTS
310   approaches often use simple averaging during backpropagation, but this can overlook paths where the
311   goal achieved metric G(p) progresses smoothly (e.g., G(p1 ) = 0 → 0.25 → 0.5 → 0.75). These
312   paths just few step away from the final goal G(p) = 1, are often more valuable than less stable ones.
313   To improve value propagation, we propose an algorithm that better captures value progression along
314   a path. Given a path P = {p1 , p2 , . . . , pn } with n nodes, where each pi represents the value at node
315   i, the total value is calculated by summing the increments between consecutive nodes with a length
316   penalty. The increment between nodes pi and pi−1 is ∆i = pi − pi−1 . Negative increments are
317   clipped at −0.1 and downweighted by 0.5. The final path value Vfinal is:
318
                                    n
319                                 X   ∆i ,                  if ∆i ≥ 0
                           Vfinal =                                       −λ×n                                         (3)
320                                     0.5 × max(∆i , −0.1), if ∆i < 0
                                     i=2
321
322   where n is the number of nodes in the path and λ = 0.1 is the penalty factor to discourage long paths.

      5.1    DATASET
328
      Blocksworld (Valmeekam et al., 2024; 2023) is a classic domain in AI research for reasoning and
329
      planning, where the goal is to rearrange blocks into a specified configuration using actions like
330   ’pick-up,’ ’put-down,’ ’stack,’ and ’unstack. Blocks can be moved only if no block on top, and only
331   one block at a time. The reasoning process in Blocksworld is a MDP. At time step t, the LLM
332   agent selects an action at ∼ p(a | st , c), where st is the current block configuration, c is the prompt
333   template. The state transition st+1 = P (st , at ) is deterministic and is computed by rules. This forms
334   a trajectory of interleaved states and actions (s0 , a0 , s1 , a1 , . . . , sT ) towards the goal state.
335
      One key feature of Blocksworld is its built-in verifier, which tracks progress toward the goal at each
336   step. This makes Blocksworld ideal for studying heuristic LLM multi-step reasoning. However, we
337   deliberately avoid using the verifier as part of the reward model as it is task-specific. More details of
338   Blocksworld can be found in Appendix F.

      5.6   I NTERPRETABILITY S TUDY
487
488   In the Blocksworld multi-step reasoning dataset, we utilize a built-in ground truth verifier to measure
489   the percentage of progress toward achieving the goal at a given step, denoted as P . The value of P
490   ranges between [0, 1]. For any arbitrary non-root node Ni , the progress is defined as:
491                                           P (Ni ) = Verifier(Ni ).
492
493
      For instance, in a 10-step Blocksworld reasoning task, the initial node A has P (A) = 0. After
      executing one correct action and transitioning to the next node B, the progress becomes P (B) = 0.1.
494
495   Given a non-root node Ni , transitioning to its parent node Parent(Ni ) through a specific action a, the
496   contribution of a toward the final goal state is defined as:
497                                      ∆a = P (Parent(Ni )) − P (Ni ).
498
499
      Next, by analyzing the relationship between ∆a and the reward value Ra assigned by the reward
      model for action a, we aim to reveal how our designed reward model provides highly interpretable
500
      reward signals for the selection of each node in MCTS. We also compare the performance of our
501
      reward model against a baseline reward model. Specifically, the alignment between ∆a and Ra
502   demonstrates the interpretability of the reward model in guiding the reasoning process toward the
503   goal state. Since Section 5.5 has already demonstrated that the reasoning performance of MCTS
504   reasoning is almost entirely determined by the reward model, using interpretable reward models
505   greatly enhances the interpretability of our algorithm SC-MCTS∗ .

      Large Language Models Multi-Step Reasoning Deepseek Prover (Xin et al., 2024a;b) relied
705   on Lean4 as an external verification tool to provide dense reward signals in the RL stage. ReST-
706   MCTS∗ (Zhang et al., 2024a) employed self-training to collect high-quality reasoning trajectories for
707   iteratively improving the value model. AlphaLLM (Tian et al., 2024) used critic models initialized
708   from the policy model as the MCTS reward model. rStar (Qi et al., 2024) utilized mutual consistency
709   of SLMs and an additional math-specific action space. Xu (2023) proposed reconstructing fine-tuned
710   LLMs into residual-based energy models to guide MCTS.
711
712   Speculative Decoding Speculative decoding was first introduced in Leviathan et al. (2023), as a
713   method to accelerate sampling from large autoregressive models by computing multiple tokens in
714   parallel without retraining or changing the model structure. It enhances computational efficiency,
715
      especially in large-scale generation tasks, by recognizing that hard language-modeling tasks often
      include easier subtasks that can be approximated well by more efficient models. Similarly, DeepMind
716
      introduced speculative sampling (Chen et al., 2023), which expands on this idea by generating a short
717
      draft sequence using a faster draft model and then scoring this draft with a larger target model.
718
719   Contrastive Decoding Contrastive decoding, as proposed by Li et al. (2023), is a simple, computa-
720   tionally light, and training-free method for text generation that can enhancethe quality and quantity
721   by identifying strings that highlight potential differences between strong models and weak models.
722   In this context, the weak models typically employ conventional greedy decoding techniques such as
723   basic sampling methods, while the strong models are often well-trained large language models. This
724   approach has demonstrated notable performance improvements in various inference tasks, including
725   arithmetic reasoning and multiple-choice ranking tasks, thereby increasing the accuracy of language
726   models. According to experiments conducted by O’Brien & Lewis (2023), applying contrastive
727
      decoding across various tasks has proven effective in enhancing the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.

      F     B LOCKSWORLD DATASET
865
866
867   The Blocksworld dataset comprises 600 instances with varying block numbers and plan lengths.
868   Simpler instances have 3-5 blocks, while more complex cases involve up to 25 blocks, introducing
869   additional goals and obstacles. This setup covers a range of problem difficulties for evaluating
870   planning algorithms.
871
872
873   F.1   D IFFICULTY S ETTINGS
874
875
      According to settings of LLM Reasoners (Hao et al., 2024), we divide the original 600 instances of
876
      Blocksworld (Valmeekam et al., 2024) into two parts, Easy and Hard settings.
877
878   In the Easy Blocksworld setting, we use more friendly demonstration cases. If a problem requires a
879   specific minimum number of steps to solve, we select other problems that require the same number
880
      of steps as demonstration cases in the context. For example, if a problem requires at least 4 steps to
      solve, we use other 4-step problems as demonstration examples. For each group of problems, we
881
      randomly select 10 cases to create a pool of demonstration cases, while the remaining cases form
882
      the test set (a total of 540 cases). During inference, we randomly sample 4-shot demonstration cases
883   from this pool to construct the prompts.
884
885   In the Hard Blocksworld setting, we randomly select 10 cases from the entire dataset to create the
886
      demonstration pool. These selected cases are then excluded from the test set, leaving a total of 590
      cases for testing. During inference, we randomly sample 4-shot demonstration cases from this global
887
      pool, without considering the minimum number of actions required for the test case. For example,
888
      if a problem requires at least 4 steps to solve, we may still use demonstration cases that require a
889   different number of steps, such as 2 or 12, as there is no restriction based on the number of actions.

1201   J   F UTURE W ORK
1202
1203   In future work, we can explore utilizing more metrics-based reward models (such as the three reward
1204   models discussed in this paper) with LM-based reward models (such as Critic LLM (McAleese et al.,
1205   2024) and Eurus (Yuan et al., 2024b)). Additionally, there is potential to design more general methods
1206   for splitting steps in other tasks and datasets. Since step-splitting is the most challenging part of
1207   MCTS multi-step reasoning generalization, although we conducted extensive experiments on the
1208
       Blocksworld multi-step reasoning dataset, which is the most suitable dataset for studying MCTS
       multi-step reasoning as far as we know. Some previous works have attempted to use datasets like
1209
       GSM8K and MATH through extensive adaptation efforts on the datasets themselves, however, we aim
1210
       to design a more general method from the perspective of step-splitting. We hope that MCTS multi-
1211   step reasoning will achieve the same level of generalization as CoT, which remains a fundamental
1212   area for future research. Future work can also attempt to combine this approach with the fine-grained
1213   compositional reasoning framework (Chen et al., 2024) to further explore the boundaries of MCTS
1214   multi-step reasoning capabilities.
