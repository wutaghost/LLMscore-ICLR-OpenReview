009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             Recent work in image and video generation has been adopting the autoregressive
012             LLM architecture due to its generality and potentially easy integration into multi-
013             modal systems. The crux of applying autoregressive training in language generation
014             to visual generation is discretization—representing continuous data like images
015             and videos as discrete tokens. Common methods of discretizing images and videos
016             include modeling raw pixel values, which are prohibitively lengthy, or vector
017             quantization, which requires convoluted pre-hoc training. In this work, we propose
018
                to directly model images and videos as compressed files saved on computers via
                canonical codecs (e.g., JPEG, AVC/H.264). Using the default Llama architecture
019
                without any vision-specific modifications, we pretrain J PEG -LM from scratch to
020
                generate images (and AVC -LM to generate videos as a proof of concept), by directly
021             outputting compressed file bytes in JPEG and AVC formats. Evaluation of image
022             generation shows that this simple and straightforward approach is more effective
023             than pixel-based modeling and sophisticated vector quantization baselines (on
024             which our method yields a 31% reduction in FID). Our analysis shows that J PEG -
025             LM has an especial advantage over vector quantization models in generating long-
026             tail visual elements. Overall, we show that using canonical codec representations
027             can help lower the barriers between language generation and visual generation,
028             facilitating future research on multi-modal language/image/video LLMs.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With large language models (LLMs) the field of NLP has shifted to multi-task processing (e.g.,
      machine translation, code generation, action planning) using a single LLM with little data needed
034
      for adaptation (Ouyang et al., 2022). We envision that future research will continue shifting to
035
      multi-modal multi-task processing, where text and visual data are mixed. However, current paradigms
036   of generating images and videos differ substantially from text generation, requiring specialized and
037   complicated training and representations (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles
038   & Xie, 2023). In this work, we simplify the task of image and video generation by using the exact
039   autoregressive transformer architecture as in mainstream LLMs (Radford et al., 2019), over canonical
040   and universal codecs: JPEG for images (Wallace, 1991), and AVC/H.264 for videos (Wiegand et al.,
041   2003).
042   The key obstacle to training autoregressive models for image and video generation is discretization, as
043   continuous data like images and videos need to be represented as discrete tokens. Current generative
044   vision models that follow autoregressive language modeling objectives (Bengio et al., 2000) often
045   adopt vector quantization (VQ) to encode images or videos to some learned latent codes and then
046   apply language models (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Ramesh et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Yan et al.,
047   2021; Yu et al., 2023).2 However, VQ methods often demand sophisticated tokenizer training that
048   requires a careful hyperparameter selection for vision-specific modules (e.g., downsampling factor

055   VQ also involves a two-stage, non-end-to-end learning process (first the neural tokenizer, then the
056   latent code LM). This makes downstream adaptation of the models less flexible (e.g., tuning the
057   VQ tokenizer interferes with the learned latent code LM). Overall, the use of conventional LLM
058   architectures (end-to-end autoregressive sequence modeling) as generative vision models is not yet
059   straightforward.
060
      The seminal work of ImageGPT (Chen et al., 2020) attempted to bridge this gap by using a regular
061   GPT architecture to model pixels sequentially. They have shown a small-scale success at a very low
062   resolution of 32x32 pixels. More realistic images at a size of 256x256 would require modeling a
063   prohibitive amount of tokens in each sequence (65K or 196K tokens depending on color modes), not
064   to mention videos. This hinders the method’s wider adoption by the field.
065
      In this work, we tackle the problem of training LLM architectures for image and video generation
066
      where the essential discretization neither adds significant complications to the pipeline like VQ meth-
067   ods, nor is computationally prohibitively expensive like ImageGPT. Specifically, we use canonical
068   file encodings/codecs—JPEG for images (Wallace, 1991), and AVC/H.264 for videos (Wiegand et al.,
069   2003)—as non-neural preprocessors that discretize data. We show that codec-based representations
070   greatly mitigate the sequence length limitation while being simple and effective. This design enables
071   us to train a vanilla transformer with the conventional language modeling objective for image and
072   video generation in a realistic setup.
073   We pretrain two 7B models with a Llama-2 architecture (Touvron et al., 2023), named J PEG -LM and
074   AVC -LM, that can generate 256x256 images and 256x144 videos with 15 frames, with an average
075   context length of 5K and 15K, respectively. In our main image modeling/generation evaluations,
076   we show that J PEG -LM surpasses strong VQ-based models in generation quality (an average of
077   31% FID reduction) and produces surprisingly realistic qualitative examples. Our results also show
078   AVC -LM can generate videos with realistic movements. Furthermore, we analyze in which aspects
079   J PEG -LM is particularly stronger than VQ models and discover that our non-neural, training-free
080   codec representations are more competent in capturing long-tail elements in images (e.g., human
081
      faces/eyes and text characters in small sizes).
082   Overall, this work presents how conventional LLM architectures can be used as generalized models
083   towards visual generation. Our approach using canonical codecs does not incur vision-specific com-
084   plications in the pipeline or suffer from sequence length infeasibility seen in prior work. Compared
085   to the baselines, our models are much simpler to train and more effective. Following the previous
086   efforts in unifying detached language-based tasks, our method helps pave the way to a unification of
087
      multiple modalities, facilitating the exploration of porting LLM techniques (e.g., alignment, scaling,
      efficiency, security, etc.) to all modalities.

090   2     BACKGROUND
091
092   In this work, we explore autoregressive image generation as a straightforward extension of prominent
093   LLM setups (Radford et al., 2019).3 Conventional language modeling (Bengio et al., 2000) models
094   the likelihood of sequential data autoregressively. Specifically, given a sequence of discrete tokens
                                                                          QN
095   x1 , x2 , · · · , xN (or x1:N ), a language model models p(x1:N ) = i=1 p(xi | x1:i−1 ), an objective
096   used in most mainstream LLMs. The key of applying language modeling to visual generation is
097   how to discretize continuous data x like images and videos to discrete tokens x1:N like in language.
098   Below we give an overview of two prominent approaches to the discretization of images.
099
100   2.1   P IXEL VALUES : I MAGE GPT
101
      ImageGPT (Chen et al., 2020) is an image generation model based on a conventional LLM architecture
102
      (GPT-2). The images are discretized as a sequence of pixel values (integers 0–255) from the upper-left
103
      to the bottom-right pixel (raster scan). Since there are three channels of colors for each pixel, to
104   reduce the number of tokens in each pixel sequence, ImageGPT clusters pixel colors to 512 distinctive
105   clusters (i.e., for each pixel, three values from 0 to 255 are converted to one value from 0 to 511).
106
         3
107        As a proof of concept, we mainly explore autoregressive modeling in visual generation only (images and
      videos, without text-conditioning), while future work may explore more diverse multi-modal setups.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ImageGPT models the probability of pixel sequences autoregressively: p(pixel-value(x)i |
109   pixel-value(x)1:i−1 ). This is an expensive process, and ImageGPT only models and generates
110   32x32 images. Images with a more realistic resolution like 256x256 would require 65K tokens for
111   each image (or 196K tokens without color clustering), a prohibitive sequence length for LLMs.
112
113   2.2   L ATENT CODES : V ECTOR -Q UANTIZATION MODELS
114
115   Vector-quantization (VQ) operates as a two-stage process, tokenizer training and language model
116   training (Esser et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2021). We take VQ-VAE as our example tokenizer
117   which discretizes continuous images (Van Den Oord et al., 2017). The tokenizer first learns an
118   encoder E to project an image x to spatial features E(x). Then for each feature e in E(x), it is
119   quantized to ẑ by looking up the nearest neighbor in a learned codebook Z: ẑ = quantize(E(x)) =
120
      [arg minzk ∈Z ∥e − zk ∥22 ]e∈E(x) . The index k of the nearest entry in codebook Z for each spatial
      feature forms the sequence of VQ latent codes. A decoder G is then learned to reconstruct the original
121
      image from the quantized representations. Overall, VQ-VAE learns an encoder E, decoder G, and
122
      codebook Z, with three distinct losses: reconstruction loss, codebook loss, and commitment loss.
123   LVQ-VAE = ∥x − G(ẑ)∥1 + ∥sg[E(x)] − ẑ∥22 + β∥sg[ẑ] − E(x)∥22 . An effective VQ tokenizer
124   needs a large amount of training data, proper hyperparameters for the vision-specific modules (e.g.,
125   downsampling factor in convolutional encoder E(·)), and a careful balance between the different
126   losses (e.g., in LVQ-VAE ), which add significant complications to the pipeline.
127
      A language model architecture can then be trained over the VQ latent codes (a sequence of index
128
      k above) as a generative vision model: p(VQ-code(x)i | VQ-code(x)1:i−1 ). Notably, since the
129   training of language model comes after and depends on the VQ tokenizer, a post-hoc update to the
130   VQ tokenizer is challenging since it would lead to a non-trivial retraining or adaptation of the trained
131   language model. Indeed in §5.3 we find that the VQ tokenizer, though trained with a large amount of
132   data, still struggles with long-tail elements in the images and is hard to be optimized once and for all.
133
      For simplicity and end-to-end adaptability, we propose to discretize continuous image and video data
134
      via canonical codecs.

137   3     J PEG -LM AND AVC -LM
138
139   Though images and videos are continuous data and naturally
140   have 2D or 3D data structures, they are stored as files on com-
141   puters efficiently via compression/codecs, which leads to a
142
      discrete 1D representation. We aim to explore whether standard
      LLM architectures can directly learn to model and generate
143
      canonical vision file encodings, which can subsequently be
144
      read/opened as generated images or videos. Generation in this
145   paradigm would greatly mitigate the sequence length infeasibil-
146   ity in ImageGPT while being simple and end-to-end trainable
147   compared to VQ methods. Moreover, canonical file encodings/-
148   codecs are often non-neural and training-free and are robust to
149   distributional shifts (§5.3). In this work, we choose the most
150   popular and established file encodings/codecs for images and
151   videos, JPEG (Wallace, 1991) and AVC/H.264 (Wiegand et al.,
152   2003), respectively.4

      3.2   J PEG -LM AND AVC -LM
178
179   JPEG and AVC convert images and videos to bytes. Most of these bytes represent the image and
180   video content after entropy encoding. However, there are also metadata and special patch/macroblock
181   separators that are invariant across images or videos and use up multiple bytes. To address them
182   along with other unknown frequent byte combinations that are compressed suboptimally by entropy
183   encoding (e.g., by JPEG’s standard, fixed Huffman tables), we further extend the default byte
184   vocabulary (256 discrete values) slightly with byte-pair encoding (BPE), a standard preprocessing
185
      scheme in LLMs, which merges bytes appearing together frequently to a new single token.7 Since
      JPEG and AVC produce sequences of variable lengths based on the content of images and videos,
186
      special beginning-of-sequence and end-of-sequence tokens are also added to the vocabulary. The
187
      entries in the vocabularies are considered as our JPEG/AVC tokens.
188
189   Given an image x, we propose J PEG -LM to model p(JPEG-token(x)i | JPEG-token(x)1:i−1 ).
190   Given a video x, we propose AVC -LM to model p(AVC-token(x)i | AVC-token(x)1:i−1 ). We use
191
      conventional LLM architectures (autoregressive transformers) without any vision-specific modifica-
      tions (no convolutions, no 2D positional embeddings) to maximize the models’ generality.

456   6    R ELATED W ORK
457
458   Current image and video generation models often adopt an autoregressive or diffusion approach. The
459   autoregressive approach can build upon pixel-based representations as explored in Van Den Oord
460   et al. (2016); Van den Oord et al. (2016); Chen et al. (2020). These methods suffer from prohibitively
461   long sequences and only operate on low-resolution images. The autoregressive approach can also
462   build upon vector quantization, which involves a sophisticated pre-hoc tokenizer training in addition
463   to the autoregressive model (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2021;
464   Yu et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2023; Mentzer et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
465
      2024a). Diffusion models generate images or videos by an iterative denoising process, and they
      have specialized objectives and architectures that are challenging to be incorporated to regular LLM
466
      paradigms to form multi-modal systems (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022;
467
      Ho et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2023; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Crowson
468   et al., 2024). For example, performing simple tasks outside visual generation like classification with
469   diffusion architectures is already not straightforward (Li et al., 2023). In this work, we propose to
470   model canonical codecs (JPEG and AVC/H.264) with conventional language model architectures for
471   visual generation. Horton et al. (2023) and Wu et al. (2024) are independent work that also process
472   file bytes data, but they both focus on visual understanding (instead of generation) and use specialized
473   modules to handle the byte sequences (whereas we use a general Llama-2 model). Perez et al. (2024)
474   concurrently discover that JPEG formats can be used with language models in file anomaly handling
475   and generation (on low-resolution images). As a universal codec, JPEG is a novel form of data
476
      encoding for efficient image understanding (Park & Johnson, 2023). Kang et al. (2019) explore an
      image generation model that performs generation and JPEG compression in one system with GANs.
477
      JPEG artifacts can also be corrected by learning a restoration model (Kawar et al., 2022), which is
478
      potentially helpful to the generations from our J PEG -LM for improving image quality. Compressive
479   codecs are also a rising topic in language. Jiang et al. (2023) use canonical compressors as feature
480   extractors for texts. Lester et al. (2024) train language models to generate compressed texts.

483   7    C ONCLUSION
484
485   In this work, we propose J PEG -LM and AVC -LM that generate images and videos using mainstream
      LLM architectures (autoregressive transformers) with canonical codec representations (JPEG for


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      images, AVC/H.264 for videos). Our approach greatly mitigates the length infeasibility of pixel-based
487   sequence modeling while enabling simple, flexible, and end-to-end training compared to sophisticated
488   vector quantization methods. Our image generation evaluation shows J PEG -LM achieves better
489   results than the baselines, with an especial advantage in generating long-tail visual elements. Our
490   work contributes to a unifying paradigm of language generation and visual generation, facilitating
491   future research to port successful LLM techniques (e.g., alignment, efficiency, etc.) to all modalities.
492
      One notable significance of this work is to show that vanilla autoregressive language modeling with
493   canonical codecs is indeed possible in visual generation. This is an approach almost void of prior
494   work, likely because there are many potential, assumed challenges with the method. For example,
495   both JPEG and AVC operate at bits level due to the entropy coding. The bytes in the files do not
496   have consistent meanings and would depend on their context and the implicit Huffman tables. For
497   generality, our models also do not use any vision-specific modules like convolutions or 2D positional
498   embeddings, potentially making the task more challenging. However, we observe that conventional,
499   vanilla language modeling surprisingly conquers these challenges without special designs as training
500   goes. Based on the findings of this work, future work may continue to investigate the scaling aspect
501
      of this family of models (similar to mainstream LLMs), co-training/deployment with text-based
      LLMs, or better architectures for canonical codecs without loss of generality for other modalities. An
502
      extended discussion can be found in §A.

505   L IMITATIONS
506
507   Machine learning models that generate images, especially the models using natural language as
508   convenient controls or even deepfakes that are maliciously trained to swap faces, lead to risks of
509   generating unsafe and harmful content (Nguyen et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2023). Though we mitigate such
510   risks in our model by not including texts for conditioning and not processing multiple images/videos
511
      for any types of synthesis, the use cases of the model still require extensive care. The purpose of
      this work is purely scientific—to explore a fundamental algorithm for general visual generation. Our
512
      approach helps lower the barriers of porting LLM techniques to visual generation, and we plan on
513
      adopting advances in LLMs (e.g., alignment and watermarking) to further enhance safety in future
514   work (Ganguli et al., 2022; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023). In this work, we pretrain a 7B model. Even
515   with our moderate-scale data, we estimate a full training of J PEG -LM to take a month on 32 Nvidia
516   A100 GPUs. As our model shares the same architecture as regular LLMs, we plan on exploring
517   techniques in LLM efficiency to reduce compute footprint in future work.

685   A    C ONTINUED D ISCUSSION
686
687
      Our work focuses on the challenging task of visual generation (e.g., outputting images) rather than
      visual understanding (e.g., inputting images, outputting classes or texts). In the field of visual
688
      understanding, the encoding of images has less restricted forms. For example, Bavishi et al. (2023)
689
      and El-Nouby et al. (2024) linearly project image patches as inputs to the transformers, Liu et al.
690   (2024b) pass CLIP embeddings (Radford et al., 2021) to language models, etc. However, these image
691   encoding formulations are not applicable to image generation. Though not a focus in this work, future
692   work may extend our J PEG -LM and AVC -LM that share the same underlying architectures with
693   regular language models to image and video understanding scenarios.
694
      Compared to raw pixel modeling that would represent a 256x256 image with 65K or 196K tokens
695
      (depending on color modes), using canonical codecs like JPEG substantially reduces the sequence
696   length to 5K on average. In terms of sequence length, the VQ transformers are usually more
697   aggressive, representing each image with 1K tokens. It is notable that this an ideal hyperparameter
698   discovered in prior work that helps model global structures—increasing the number of tokens in VQ
699   (thus reducing the downsampling patch size) may lead to degenerated results rather than helping the
700   model learn with more capacity (Esser et al., 2021). Our work proposes to model canonical codecs
701   as a proof of concept, and future work may compare with more VQ setups or further improve the
      context efficiency of J PEG -LM.
