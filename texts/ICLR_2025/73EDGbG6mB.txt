011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013
                  Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated signif-
014               icant potential in enhancing real-time spoken interactions. Presently, open-source
015               methodologies predominantly depend on intermediate generative text-based tran-
016               scriptions to manage real-time spoken dialogues. However, these techniques of-
017               ten struggle with providing seamless interactions that involve real-time streaming
018               audio inputs. In this research, we unveil an innovative spoken dialogue language
019               model, Parrot, distinguished by its unique pre-training and supervised fine-tuning
020               (SFT) pipeline. This pipeline deviates from conventional methodologies by uti-
021               lizing both single-channel audio data and double-channel spoken dialogue data
022
                  to train the textless speech language model. During pre-training, we transform
                  single-channel audio input into a sequence of discrete tokens, thereby instructing
023
                  the LLM to identify audio tokens via next-token predictions. In the SFT phase,
024
                  we pioneer a novel approach to double-channel generative spoken dialogue lan-
025               guage modeling with a unique “next-token-pair prediction” objective, facilitating
026               the LLM’s comprehension of natural human conversations. Our pipeline equips
027               LLM to produce spoken interactions that are more natural and fluid than those
028               generated by baseline approaches, as substantiated by thorough evaluations1 .

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The advent of large language models (LLMs), particularly the GPT series (Patel et al., 2023; Ope-
034   nAI, 2023; 2024), has profoundly transformed the field of artificial intelligence. These powerful lan-
035   guage models attain their capabilities through pretraining on extensive text corpora using decoder-
036   only transformer architectures, guided by an autoregressive next-token prediction objective function.
037   Recently, there has been an increasing interest in integrating the LLMs with other modalities, such
038   as images (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2023b), audio (Zhang et al., 2023a;
039
      2024a; Hassid et al., 2023), protein sequences (Lin et al., 2022; Madani et al., 2023) and etc . Among
      these modalities, audio or speech data holds particular importance as it enables LLMs to engage in
040
      real-time voice interactions with humans. The recently unveiled GPT-4o model (OpenAI, 2024)
041
      exhibits a remarkable proficiency in managing real-time interactions with users in conversational
042   contexts. Throughout the demo presentation, it was able to generate authentic emotional responses
043   and engage users with swift reactions. These functionalities, however, introduce additional chal-
044   lenges, as the model must thoroughly interpret the distinct audio information within human speech
045   while conducting inference with minimal delay.
046
      Presently, the academic community primarily utilizes open-sourced models (Zhang et al., 2023a;
047
      Xie & Wu, 2024; Rubenstein et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Nachmani et al.,
048   2024; Wang et al., 2023b) following a cascading approach. This method heavily depends on an in-
049   termediate text generation step and generally consists of three stages: automatic-speech-recognition
050   (ASR), text-based question answering (Text-QA), and text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis. While this
051   approach is reliable due to the incorporation of powerful text-based LLMs, it does present three
052   significant drawbacks: (1) Audio Information Loss: Audio signals, unlike text, include additional

087   In this study, we present a novel pre-training and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) pipeline to develop a
088
      robust model, referred to as Parrot, specifically designed for spoken dialogue language modeling.
      The pre-training phase begins with the conversion of continuous audio inputs into a sequence of to-
089
      kens, a process made possible by training a vector-quantized autoencoder (VQVAE) (van den Oord
090
      et al., 2017) to reconstruct these audio signals. We then leverage pretrained LLMs as a foundation
091   for continuous learning on single-channel audio sequences, with the goal of next-token prediction.
092   This is accomplished by integrating the learned audio tokens into the original text vocabulary. This
093   pretraining stage aids LLMs in capturing the primary latent distribution of audio token sequences. In
094   the subsequent stage, we utilize double-channel audio data for SFT. The key advantage is enabling
095   LLMs to directly comprehend how humans engage in natural dialogues. Unlike existing approaches,
096   we introduce a novel “next-token-pair prediction” paradigm to model the double-channel spoken
097   dialogue generation using the decoder-only transformer. The comparison between our proposed
098   method and existing techniques are illustrated in Figure 1. We carry out extensive experiments to
099
      validate the superiority of our innovative approach. Specifically, Parrot consistently outperforms
      strong baseline methods by 150% and 200% in average in terms of the reflective pause and interrup-
100
      tion response accuracy respectively. Additionally, it achieves a low latency of 300ms. In summary,
101
      our contributions to the field are as follows:
102
103   1) We present a spoken dialogue language model, Parrot, featuring an innovative pre-training and
104      SFT pipeline. This novel approach eliminates the need for intermediate text conversions, thereby
105      facilitating more fluid and natural voice interactions with human users at reduced latency.
106   2) We propose a new paradigm for double-channel spoken language modeling, called next-token-
107      pair prediction, which holds the potential to be readily generalized for autoregressive modeling
         of multi-channel audio sequence inputs in future explorations.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
114
115   Autoregressive Generative Models. The autoregressive generative modeling has achieved re-
116   markable success in natural language processing, giving rise to a variety of powerful LLMs
117   (Sutskever et al., 2014; OpenAI, 2024; 2023; Patel et al., 2023). Inspired by these LLMs, numerous
118   studies have examined the application of autoregressive modeling in other domains, such as images
119
      (van den Oord et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2022;
      Chang et al., 2022), graphs (You et al., 2018), videos (Weissenborn et al., 2020), molecules (Shi
120
      et al., 2020; Schwaller et al., 2019) and protein sequences (Madani et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022).
121
      The fundamental concept of autoregressive modeling focuses on iteratively generating the entire
122   segment from the intermediate portion, which is particularly well-suited for the audio generation.
123
124
125   Multi-modal LLMs. Multimodal Large Language Models (MM-LLMs) strive to incorporate
126   knowledge from diverse modalities. A key category of MM-LLMs concentrates on developing
127   connectors (Li et al., 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Alayrac et al., 2022) that identify knowledge
128   alignment across various modalities. An alternative strategy (Team, 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Xie
129   et al., 2024) merges all modalities into a cohesive sequence of tokens and utilizes LLMs to sequen-
130
      tially generate them using modified attention masks. These methods (Wu et al., 2024; Su et al.,
      2023; Fu et al., 2024) even integrate audio as an input modality, and by simply combining text and
131
      audio through MM-LLM techniques, they can address one-direction conditional generation tasks
132
      such as speech-to-text translation (e.g., ASR and spoken language understanding) (Radford et al.,
133   2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Deshmukh et al., 2023; Arora et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Chu et al.,
134   2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Ravanelli et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023) and text-to-speech translation (e.g.,
135   TTS) (Elizalde et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023; Nachmani et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
136   2023; Kreuk et al., 2023; Borsos et al., 2023; Copet et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Anastassiou et al.,
137   2024; Jiang et al., 2023b; Kong et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2024; Casanova et al., 2022; Siuzdak, 2024;
138   Yang et al., 2024; Kharitonov et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023). However, these methods are limited
139   to handling multi-turn multi-modal QA tasks (where the model produces an answer only after the
140   question is completed, as signaled by pressing the input button, for instance) and thereby struggle
141
      with real-time voice interaction tasks, which is the primary focus of our work.
142
143
      Generative Spoken Language Modeling. The core concept of our approach relies on the pretrain-
144
      ing of robust speech foundation models, with language model learning (LLM) serving as a crucial
145   component, to enable rapid adaptation to a broad spectrum of downstream speech tasks. Much of the
146   prior research has utilized the encoder-decoder architecture to enhance pre-training (Borsos et al.,
147   2023; Lakhotia et al., 2021; Kharitonov et al., 2022; Polyak et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023; 2022;
148   Hsu et al., 2021; Zeghidour et al., 2022; Défossez et al., 2023; Agostinelli et al., 2023; Ao et al.,
149   2022; Tang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023). However, this architecture proves inadequate for handling
150   real-time speech interactions with streaming audio inputs, as it requires the encoder to process the
151   entire input simultaneously. In more recent studies, the decoder-only transformer (Maiti et al., 2024;
152   Zhang et al., 2024a; Hassid et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2024; Fathullah et al., 2024; Shen et al.,
153
      2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Das et al., 2024) has been employed to model the audio sequence. This
      approach capitalizes on the potent language capabilities of LLMs while also facilitating the process-
154
      ing of streaming inputs. Motivated by the advent of GPT-4o, newly developed models aim to endow
155
      LLMs with speech conversation capabilities (Ma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a; Xie & Wu, 2024;
156   Rubenstein et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Nachmani et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
157   2023b; Défossez et al., 2024). However, these models either rely on text transcriptions or adhere to
158   the aforementioned MM-LLM methods, lacking the ability for natural turn-taking. In stark contrast,
159   our work leverages double-channel spoken dialogue data to directly instruct LLMs in human con-
160   versations. A significant contribution in the realm of spoken dialogue language modeling is dGLSM
161   (Nguyen et al., 2023), but it remains confined to the era of using the encoder-decoder architecture.
      In our research, we elevate the architecture to the most recent decoder-only transformer.

      3     PARROT : T RAINING AND I NFERENCE P IPELINE
163
164   Our Parrot comprises two essential steps. The first involves pretraining the LLM on single-channel
165   audio token sequences using the traditional ”next-token prediction” objective. The second step fine-
166   tunes the LLM on double-channel audio token sequences, employing the innovative ”next-token-pair
167   prediction” paradigm. The rationale behind this strategy stems from the fundamental observation
168   that the single-channel audio data can be sourced from the vast amount of open-source data available
169
      on the web. However, the primary limitation of single-audio data is its lack of speaker identity
      information and the overlapping regions between different speakers can be misleading. On the other
170
      hand, double-channel spoken dialogue data encapsulates crucial turn-taking events with distinct
171
      speaker channels, and any overlapping event can be easily discerned. Nevertheless, the double-
172   channel data necessitates specific pre-processing techniques to segregate the mixed information from
173   the single-channel data. Therefore, it is a naturally inspired strategy to use the large-scale single-
174   channel audio data for pretraining and the moderate-scale double-channel dialogue data for SFT.
175

      3.1   AUDIO T OKENIZATION AND S INGLE - CHANNEL AUDIO P RETRAINING
177
178   A single-channel audio is a continuous input sequence x ∈ RT with time length T . Owing to the
179   high sampling rate of continuous audio signals, it is essential to employ an audio tokenizer, which
180   extracts valuable features for the purpose of compressing the information. The audio quantizer Q
181   projects the audio sequence x into a set of discrete tokens Q = (q1 , ..., qT ′ ) = Q(x) (T ′ ≪ T ),
182   where each token qt is an integer index from the vocabulary qt ∈ [V ] where the vocabulary size is
183   V . We train the audio tokenizer Q following the VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) framework.
184   In contrast to certain prior studies, we directly train the tokenizer on the raw audio signals x, rather
185
      than transforming x into a mel-spectrogram first. We primarily adopt the training strategy presented
      in SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2022), and provide a brief overview of its underlying mechanism.
186
187   Specifically, audio inputs x is fed into an encoder E to derive down-sampled latent features f ∈
        T
188   R r ×D such that f = E(x) with the down-sampling rate r and the latent dimension D. This is
189   achieved by the CNN (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) architecture, which can capture the local dependency
                                                                                                T
190   of x. Then the quantizer Q converts the latent feature f to discrete tokens q ∈ R r such that
191   q = Q(f ) where each entry qi is a quantized integer index. Each latent feature fi for time frame i is
192   mapped to the code index qi of its nearest embedding vector in the Euclidean sense:

235   The double-channel audio input comprises a pair of time-aligned single-channel audio inputs, de-
236   noted as (xa , xb ), where each channel corresponds to a specific speaker. A fresh challenge arises
237   in the generative modeling of double-channel audio sequences using the decoder-only transformer
238   architecture of LLMs. To address this issue, we propose a novel generative learning paradigm
239   called next-token-pair prediction. The key idea here is to generate a sequence of time-aligned
240
      token pairs, rather than a single token, in an autoregressive fashion. In contrast to the conven-
      tional next-token prediction, our objective is more suitable to the generative modeling of an inter-
241
      polated dialogue sequence which contain two separate channel identities. Specifically, we begin
242

      4.1      DATASET
325
326   Parrot employs a two-stage training process. In the first stage, to establish foundational speech
327   capabilities, we trained the model using three speech datasets totaling approximately 14,000 hours.
328   This stage focuses on both speech understanding and synthesis. Unlike other models (Fang et al.,
329   2024) that require audio to be transcribed into text, our Parrot only needs single-channel audio
330   for direct training. This reduces the data requirements and, consequently, increases the amount of
331   training data available. For the second stage, we need the Parrot to simultaneously gain the ability
332   to listen and speak. To achieve this, we further utilize the Fisher dataset (Cieri et al., 2004). This
333
      dataset comprises 2200 hours of phone conversations between randomly paired participants, each
      discussing a given topic. A notable feature of the Fisher dataset is that each side of the conversation
334
      is recorded on separate channels, which allows us to provide ground-truth separated streams to
335
      Parrot. The original audio is sampled at 8kHz, and we use Librosa 2 to upsample it to 16kHz.

522   6                   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
523
524   A current limitation of Parrot is its incapacity to integrate the prevalent audio tokenization method,
525   residual vector quantization (RVQ) (Lee et al., 2022). RVQ is typically used to convert continu-
526   ous audio into discrete tokens, ensuring the preservation of high-quality information. This process
527   involves approximating the audio input with multi-scale tokens, each representing the residual infor-
528   mation remaining after the deduction of the previous scale token’s information. As a result, the audio
529   token sequence produced by RVQ has an additional residual token dimension (beyond the time step
530   dimension) compared to the standard VQVAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) utilized in Parrot. This
531
      introduces complexities to the autoregressive generative modeling of spoken dialogue sequences.
532
533   7                   C ONCLUSION
534   In conclusion, we introduce a novel spoken dialogue language model, Parrot, realized through an
535   innovative pretraining and SFT pipeline. We employ single-channel audio data for pretraining and
536   double-channel audio dialogue data for SFT. To facilitate language modeling on double-channel
537   audio sequences, we unveil the pioneering next-token-pair prediction paradigm for the first time.
538   Comprehensive experiments underscore the superiority of our approach over existing baseline meth-
539   ods. Furthermore, through meticulous ablation studies, we validate the effectiveness of each critical
      component in our model.

971   We compare Parrot with several newly released speech LLMs, which are Mini-Omni (Xie & Wu,
      2024), Llama-Omni (Fang et al., 2024), Moshi (Défossez et al., 2024), LSLM (Ma et al., 2024).

972
       1) Mini-Omni: The major advancement of this model is the batched parallel decoding strategy.
973
974
             • Advantages: Text generation can significantly enhance the quality of the audio produced.
               Concurrently, the implementation of batched parallel decoding can substantially mitigate
975
               issues related to inference latency. Overall, Mini-Omni effectively maintains a high stan-
976
               dard of response quality while circumventing the latency typically associated with TTS
977            translations.
978
             • Limitations: This model, while a multi-modal QA system, adheres to the standard archi-
979
               tecture of multi-modal LLMs with various modality adaptors. However, it falls short in
980            handling natural spoken conversations with real-time streaming user voice inputs. The dy-
981            namic nature of real-time dialogues, characterized by various pauses and turn-taking events,
982            cannot be effectively simulated by this system.
983
       2) Llama-Omni: This speech LLM also mainly focuses on enhancing the decoder stage like the
984       previous Mini-Omni model. It propose an non-autoregressive decoder to simultaneously gener-
985       ate texts and audios. The text token is firstly upsampled and then fed into the speech decoder to
986       derive the output voice. Unlike traditional TTS, Llama-Omni applies TTS word by word in an
987       non-autoregressive manner.
988          • Advantages: Like the Mini-Omni, this model also enjoys the response reliability due to the
989            usage of intermediate text generation. In this way, Llama-Omni also enjoys low inference
990            latency while maintaining high-quality content response.
991          • Limitations: The Llama-Omni also shares the same limitations like Mini-Omin. Relying on
992            text generations cannot handle special speech tokens that are hard to match to text tokens.
993            In addition, the multi-modal LLMs can only handle multi-turn QA while failing to handle
994            natural conversations like interruptions and pauses.
995    3) LSLM: This speech LLM explicitly leverages the double-channel audio data. Unlike Parrot,
996       LSLM fuses two channel tokens into one single token and still follows the next-token prediction
997       training objective. To enable LSLM to learn to interrupt, this work trains the speech LLM on the
998       synthetic interruption data.
999
             • Advantages: No need to change the next-token prediction paradigm of the original LLM,
1000           which keeps the speech LLM as simple as possible.
1001
             • Limitations: The introduction of the special “EOS” token and the “interruption” token will
1002           bring additional challenges in audio preprocessing. A threshold must be determined to filter
1003           what tokens are assigned to be “interruption token”, which can be tricky. In addition, this
1004           model can only learn to interrupt by training on specific synthetic data. First, it might be
1005           troublesome to synthesize turn-taking events. Second, there is always a distribution gap
1006           between synthetic turn-taking and real-world turn-taking.
1007   4) Moshi: This is a newly open-sourced speech LLM with high-quality spoken responses and
1008      minimal inference latency. Moshi leverages the RVQ technique to tokenize the audio inputs.
1009      And it explicitly proposes the usage of multi-channel audio modeling. There are mainly text
1010      channels, speaker audio channels and listener audio channels. The generative modeling of the
1011      multi-channel token sequences is following the RQtransformer (Lee et al., 2022), which is an
1012      encoder-decoder architecture.
1013         • Advantages: The usage of RVQ can largely improve the quality of discrete audio repre-
1014           sentations. And the usage of intermediate text translation can significantly improve the
1015           reliability of response contents.
1016         • Limitations: The multi-channel data structure requires the alignment between text se-
1017           quences and audio sequences, which is a non-trivial engineering work. Also, the encoder-
1018           decoder RQtransformer architecture requires to receive the entire input of speaker’s chan-
1019           nel, which still somehow downgrades the modeling efficency. Last but not least, this model
1020           can be regarded as alternative form of online cascading approach, which relies on the accu-
1021
               racy of both audio-to-text and text-to-audio generation.
1022   In comparison to the above models, Parrot enjoys several important advantages:
1023
1024         • Real Streaming Inference: Parrot is capable of managing real-time streaming inference,
1025           eliminating the need for specific training on turn-taking, as required by models like LSLM.
               It can interact seamlessly with human users through natural turn-taking for the duration of


                                                       19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
                 the service. In contrast, multi-modal speech LLMs such as Mini-Omni and Llama-Omni
1027             can only interact with users on a turn-by-turn basis. In essence, Parrot does not depend on
1028             manually-defined interruption rules when conducting streaming inference.
1029
               • Decoder-only Transformers: In contrast to the encoder-decoder dialogue language mod-
1030
                 eling, Parrot employs a decoder-only transformer. This architecture offers numerous sig-
1031             nificant advantages. For instance, the encoder-decoder structure necessitates maintaining
1032             a window to receive complete inputs during the inference stage. However, the decoder-
1033             only architecture simply requires querying the cached key-value pairs, resulting in superior
1034             computational efficiency during inference.
1035           • Spoken Dialogue Data Usage Efficiency: Both Moshi and LSLM randomly assign one
1036             channel as the speaker and another as the listener. This approach potentially reduces di-
1037             alogue data efficiency, as the trained model becomes speaker-dependent. Essentially, the
1038             model needs to train the reverse conditional distribution by swapping the roles, which could
1039             pose scalability issues as more channels are added in the future. In contrast, Parrot is
1040             speaker-independent and concurrently learns the conditional distribution of both speaker’s
1041             audio channels.

       To overcome the limitations previously discussed, we propose a potential solution: the creation of

1185   A.6     M OTIVATIONS OF U SING D OUBLE -C HANNEL S POKEN D IALOGUE DATA
1186
1187   Inspired by GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), we aspire to create a powerful voice assistant that can engage
       with human users in a natural and fluent way. Ideally, the assistant should be able to be interrupted


                                                               22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       by users. If a user needs to convey something urgently, the assistant should stop speaking and listen
1189   attentively. Furthermore, when a user is in thought or taking a pause, the assistant should not prema-
1190   turely conclude that the user has finished speaking. Instead, it should patiently wait for the user to
1191   complete their thoughts. An advanced voice assistant could even interrupt users when it has already
1192   grasped their intentions, much like how we often interrupt each other in daily conversations. There
1193   are numerous other scenarios that an intelligent voice assistant should be equipped to handle. Given
1194   these complex application scenarios, it’s challenging to address these issues through simple man-
1195   ual engineering, such as the introduction of special tokens like silence tokens, or hard interruptions
1196   when the user is speaking.
1197   The success of foundational models hinges on our trust in the model’s capacity to learn au-
1198   tonomously from data, rather than over-interfering with the learning process or over-engineering
1199   the neural architectures and algorithms. Consequently, in this paper, we utilize double-channel dia-
1200   logue data and directly train the speech LLM on this spoken dialogue data. With robust pre-trained
1201   speech LLMs, we can reasonably anticipate that the model can learn how humans converse with
1202   each other by directly ”reading” their dialogues. This approach eliminates the need for setting man-
1203
       ual rules to assist the voice assistant in scenario judgement. The assistant may learn how to navigate
       these scenarios by processing a sufficient amount of spoken dialogue data. Regrettably, the current
1204
       availability of open-source double-channel spoken-dialogue data is limited. Looking ahead, we hope
1205
       our work will stimulate the community to gather large-scale double-channel or even multi-channel
1206   spoken dialogue data.
