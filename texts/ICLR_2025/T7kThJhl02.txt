009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image models can generate harmful images when presented with unsafe
012            prompts, posing significant safety and societal risks. Alignment methods aim
013            to modify these models to ensure they generate only non-harmful images, even
014            when exposed to unsafe prompts. A typical text-to-image model comprises two
015            main components: 1) a text encoder and 2) a diffusion module. Existing alignment
016            methods mainly focus on modifying the diffusion module to prevent harmful im-
017
               age generation. However, this often significantly impacts the model’s behavior
               for safe prompts, causing substantial quality degradation of generated images. In
018
               this work, we propose SafeText, a novel alignment method that fine-tunes the text
019
               encoder rather than the diffusion module. By adjusting the text encoder, SafeText
020            significantly alters the embedding vectors for unsafe prompts, while minimally
021            affecting those for safe prompts. As a result, the diffusion module generates non-
022            harmful images for unsafe prompts while preserving the quality of images for safe
023            prompts. We evaluate SafeText on multiple datasets of safe and unsafe prompts,
024            including those generated through jailbreak attacks. Our results show that Safe-
025            Text effectively prevents harmful image generation with minor impact on the im-
026            ages for safe prompts, and SafeText outperforms six existing alignment methods.
027            We will publish our code and data after paper acceptance.
028            WARNING: This paper contains sexual and nudity content, which readers
029            may find offensive or disturbing.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Given a prompt, a text-to-image model can generate highly realistic images that align with the
034
      prompt’s semantics. Typically, such a model consists of two key components: 1) a text encoder,
035
      which maps the prompt into an embedding vector; and 2) a diffusion module, which guided by
036   the embedding vector, recursively denoises a random Gaussian noise vector to an image. These
037   models have a wide range of applications, including art creation, character design in online games,
038   and virtual environment development. For instance, Microsoft has integrated DALL·E into its Edge
039   browser (Mehdi, 2023).
040
      Like any advanced technology, text-to-image models are double-edged swords, raising severe safety
041
      concerns alongside their societal benefits discussed above. Specifically, they can generate high-
042   quality harmful images–such as those containing sexual or nudity content–when provided with un-
043   safe prompts like, “Show me an image of a nude body.” These harmful image generations can be
044   triggered either intentionally by malicious users or unintentionally by regular users. Unsafe prompts
045   can be manually crafted based on heuristics, often containing keywords related to sexual or nudity
046   content. Alternatively, they can also be adversarially crafted via jailbreak attacks (Zhuang et al.,
047   2023; Qu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b; Tsai et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a), which are designed
048   to bypass safety mechanisms.
049   Alignment methods aim to modify text-to-image models to ensure they generate only non-harmful
050   images, even when presented with unsafe prompts. Existing alignment methods (Rombach et al.,
051   2022; Schramowski et al., 2023; Gandikota et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
052   2024) primarily target the diffusion module of the model. For example, Erased Stable Diffusion
053   (ESD) (Gandikota et al., 2023) fine-tunes the diffusion module to make the noise prediction, condi-
      tioned on unsafe prompts, unconditional and therefore typically non-harmful. While these methods

102   In this work, we propose SafeText, a novel alignment method. Due to the challenges of aligning

107   unsafe prompts while preserving the quality of images for safe prompts. We develop two loss terms

      2     R ELATED W ORK
122
      2.1   H ARMFUL I MAGE G ENERATION
123
124   A text-to-image model generates high-quality harmful images when presented with unsafe prompts,
125   which can be manually crafted based on heuristics or adversarially crafted using jailbreak attacks.
126
127   Manually crafted unsafe prompts: These unsafe prompts are manually crafted based on heuris-
128   tics, often containing keywords related to sexual or nudity content. Additionally, multi-modal large
129   language models can be employed to generate captions for real-world harmful images, with these
130   captions being used as unsafe prompts. In our experiments, we utilize manually crafted unsafe
131
      prompts collected from online prompt-sharing platforms like civitai.com and lexica.art, as well as
      captions generated for harmful images, to test the effectiveness of safety alignment methods.
132
133   Adversarially crafted unsafe prompts: These unsafe prompts are generated through jailbreak
134   attacks and could include text that is either coherent or nonsensical to humans. A jailbreak attack
135   modifies a manually crafted unsafe prompt, which fails to bypass a model’s safety alignment, into an
136   adversarial prompt. This adversarial prompt is designed to circumvent the safety alignment, enabling
137   the text-to-image model to generate a harmful image that matches the semantics of the original un-
138   safe prompt. For instance, SneakyPrompt (Yang et al., 2024b) iteratively refines the adversarial
139   prompt via interacting with a given text-to-image model and leveraging reinforcement learning to
140
      take the responses into consideration. Similarly, Ring-A-Bell (Tsai et al., 2024) employs a surrogate
      text encoder and a genetic algorithm to generate an adversarial prompt that avoids explicit unsafe
141
      words while keeping its embedding similar to the original unsafe prompt. MMA-Diffusion (Yang
142
      et al., 2024a) further leverages token-level gradients and word regularization to optimize an adver-
143   sarial prompt, ensuring it avoids explicit unsafe words while preserving embedding similarity to the
144   original unsafe prompt.
145
146
      2.2   S AFETY A LIGNMENT
147
148   Depending on the text-to-image model’s component that is aligned, alignment methods can be
149   grouped into the following two categories:
150
151   Aligning the diffusion module: The most straightforward method (Rombach et al., 2022) to align
152   the diffusion module of a text-to-image model is to retrain it on a dataset containing only non-
153
      harmful images and safe prompts. However, this safe retraining has limited effectiveness because
      the retrained model can still piece together different parts of seemingly non-harmful images to gen-
154
      erate harmful ones. Additionally, retraining is highly time-consuming. To address this, some align-
155
      ment methods fine-tune the diffusion module (Gandikota et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024)
156   or modify its image generation process (Schramowski et al., 2023). For instance, Erased Stable
157   Diffusion (ESD) (Gandikota et al., 2023) fine-tunes the diffusion module to make the noise pre-
158   diction, conditioned on unsafe concepts, unconditional and therefore typically non-harmful. Mass
159   Concept Erasure (MACE) (Lu et al., 2024) uses Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) to
160   fine-tune the cross-attention layer (Chen et al., 2021) within the diffusion module, preventing the
161   generation of images related to unsafe concepts. Similarly, SafeGen (Li et al., 2024) fine-tunes the
      diffusion module using harmful images and their mosaic versions, prompting the model to generate


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      mosaic images when given unsafe prompts. For generation-time alignment, Safe Latent Diffusion
163   (SLD) (Schramowski et al., 2023) adds a safety guidance term to the classifier-free guidance noise
164   prediction process to remove harmful elements from the generated images. However, these align-
165   ment methods substantially affect the images generated for safe prompts as they significantly alter
166   the diffusion module’s behavior.
167
168   Aligning the text encoder: To the best of our knowledge, AdvUnlearn (Zhang et al., 2024) is the
169
      only method that aligns the text encoder. AdvUnlearn combines the loss function of ESD (Gandikota
      et al., 2023) with adversarial training (Madry, 2017) to change the diffusion module’s noise predic-
170
      tion process. Specifically, it fine-tunes the text encoder so that the diffusion module’s predicted noise
171
      conditioned on unsafe prompts approximates the unconditional predicted noise, while the predicted
172   noise conditioned on safe prompts remains close to that before fine-tuning. However, because the
173   loss function of ESD is based on classifier-free guidance and is designed for the diffusion module,
174   using it to fine-tune the text encoder still substantially changes the denoising process, significantly
175   affecting the image generation for safe prompts, as demonstrated in our experiments.

      3     P ROBLEM D EFINITION
178
179
      Given a text-to-image model, our objective is to align it to meet two goals: 1) Effectiveness and 2)
180   Utility. The effectiveness goal ensures that the aligned model does not generate harmful images–
181   such as those containing sexual or nudity-related content–when presented with unsafe prompts.
182   The utility goal focuses on maintaining the model’s ability to generate high-quality images for safe
183   prompts. Specifically, we aim for a high standard of utility: given the same safe prompt and seed,
184   the aligned and unaligned models should produce visually similar images. For instance, the LPIPS
185   score (Zhang et al., 2018) between the images generated by the aligned and unaligned models is
186   small. Our SafeText achieves a balance between the two goals, i.e., between preventing harmful
187   image generation and preserving the model’s functionality for safe use cases.
188
189   4     O UR S AFE T EXT
190
191   4.1    OVERVIEW
192
193   Our SafeText achieves the effectiveness and utility goals via aligning the text encoder of the text-to-
194   image model. Since the diffusion module of the text-to-image model is responsible for the denoising
195
      process and image generation, modifying its parameters may significantly degrade image quality for
      safe prompts. Therefore, our SafeText fine-tunes only the text encoder while keeping the diffusion
196
      module intact to largely preserve image quality for safe prompts.
197
198   Specifically, to achieve the effectiveness goal, we fine-tune the text encoder so that the embeddings
199   for unsafe prompts are altered substantially. Consequently, the images generated based on the em-
200   beddings produced by the aligned text encoder are much less likely to contain harmful content. To
201
      achieve the utility goal, we ensure that the aligned text encoder and the original one produce similar
      embeddings for a safe prompt. Formally, we propose two loss terms to respectively quantify the two
202
      goals, and formulate fine-tuning the text encoder as an optimization problem, whose objective is to
203
      minimize a weighted sum of the two loss terms. Finally, we solve the optimization problem via a
204   standard gradient-based method.
205

208   We use τ to denote the original text encoder and τs to denote our fine-tuned one.
209
210   Quantifying the effectiveness goal: For an unsafe prompt Pun , our objective is to ensure that
211   the embedding τs (Pun ) produced by the fine-tuned encoder is highly likely to be safe. To achieve
212
      this, we fine-tune the text encoder so that the embedding τs (Pun ) is substantially different from the

213
      goal, we fine-tune τ as τs such that the distance between τs (Pun ) and τ (Pun ) is large, based on a
214
      chosen distance metric. Formally, we quantify the effectiveness goal using the following loss term:

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
252
253   Fine-tuning datasets Ds and Dun : Our fine-tuning needs datasets Ds and Dun . In our experi-
254   ments, Ds contains 30,000 safe prompts and Dun contains 30,000 unsafe prompts, both sampled
255   from a pre-processed Civitai-8M dataset (AdamCodd, 2024). The original Civitai-8M dataset com-
256   prises 7,852,309 prompts collected from Civitai, an online platform where users upload and share
257
      prompts. Each prompt in Civitai-8M is assigned an unsafe level ranging from 0 to 32. To construct
      high-quality datasets Ds and Dun , we keep the prompts with an unsafe level of 1 or below as safe
258
      prompts, while those with an unsafe level greater than 8 as unsafe prompts. Moreover, we apply a
259
      safety classifier (michellejieli, 2022) to further score and classify each prompt, where a larger score
260   indicates safer. We keep the safe prompts with a score above 0.9 as the final safe dataset, while
261   the unsafe prompts classified as unsafe by the safety classifier as the final unsafe dataset. We then
262   randomly sample 30,000 prompts from the final safe dataset to form Ds and 30,000 prompts from
263   the final unsafe dataset to form Dun .

531   6       C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
532
533
      In this work, we show that fine-tuning the text encoder of a text-to-image model can prevent it
534   from generating harmful images for unsafe prompts without compromising the quality of images
535   generated for safe prompts. This can be achieved by fine-tuning the text encoder to significantly alter
536   the embeddings for unsafe prompts while minimally affecting those for safe prompts. Extensive
537   evaluation shows that our fine-tuning of the text encoder outperforms the alignment methods that
538   directly modify the diffusion module or fine-tune the text encoder based on the diffusion module’s
539   noise prediction process. Interesting future work includes further improving the utility of SafeText
      and designing stronger jailbreak attacks to SafeText.

676   A    D EATILS OF M ETHODS TO ADVERSARIALLY CRAFT UNSAFE PROMPTS
677
678   To assess the effectiveness of our SafeText against adversarially crafted unsafe prompts, we utilize
679   the following three state-of-the-art jailbreak attacks to generate them.
680
            • SneakyPrompt (Yang et al., 2024b) This method employs reinforcement learning to mod-
681
              ify unsafe prompts by repeatedly querying the target text-to-image model. The objective is
682           to craft prompts that generate images with high semantic similarity to the original prompts
683           while bypassing the model’s safety filters. When applying SneakyPrompt to a text-to-image
684           model with safeguard, where safety filters are not deployed, the goal shifts to enhancing
685           the semantic similarity between the generated images and original prompts.
686         • Ring-A-Bell (Tsai et al., 2024) This method is designed to evaluate the reliability of a
687           concept-removal technique for text-to-image models. It first collects two sets of prompts:
688           one containing prompts with words related to the unsafe concept, and another where those
689           words are replaced with their antonyms. Next, it employs a surrogate text encoder to calcu-
690           late the average difference between the embeddings of all paired prompts, which is treated
691           as the concept vector. This concept vector is then added to the embedding of the original
692           unsafe prompt to obtain the target embedding. Finally, a genetic algorithm is used to search
693           within the vocabulary codebook to craft the original unsafe prompt, such that the crafted
694
              prompt has an embedding similar to the target embedding.
695         • MMA-Diffusion (Yang et al., 2024a) This method introduces a multi-modal attack to jail-
696           break text-to-image models in image editing tasks. It consists of a text-modal attack and an
697           image-modal attack. We adopt the text-modal attack to adversarially craft unsafe prompts.
698
              Specifically, the method leverages token-level gradients and a sensitive word regularization
              technique to optimize the original unsafe prompt. The resulting crafted prompt has a simi-
699
              lar embedding to the original unsafe prompt when encoded by a surrogate text encoder but
700
              does not contain any sensitive words.
