011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Conditional generative modeling (CGM), which approximates the conditional
014            probability distribution of data given a condition, holds significant promise for
015            generating new data across diverse representations. While CGM is crucial for
016            generating images, video, and text, its application to scientific computing, such
017            as molecular generation and physical simulations, is also highly anticipated. A
018
               key challenge in applying CGM to scientific fields is the sparseness of avail-
               able data conditions, which requires extrapolation beyond observed conditions.
019
               This paper proposes the Extended Flow Matching (EFM) framework to address
020
               this challenge. EFM achieves smooth transitions in distributions when depart-
021            ing from observed conditions, avoiding the unfavorable changes seen in existing
022            flow matching (FM) methods. By introducing a flow with respect to the condi-
023            tional axis, EFM ensures that the conditional distribution changes gradually with
024            the condition. Specifically, we apply an extended Monge–Kantorovich theory to
025            conditional generative models, creating a framework for learning matrix fields in
026            a generalized continuity equation instead of vector fields. Furthermore, by com-
027            bining the concept of Dirichlet energy on Wasserstein spaces with Multi-Marginal
028            Optimal Transport (MMOT), we derive an algorithm called MMOT-EFM. This
029
               algorithm controls the rate of change of the generated conditional distribution.
               Our proposed method outperforms existing methods in molecular generation tasks
030
               where conditions are sparsely observed.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Conditional generative modeling (CGM), which involves approximating a conditional probability
036   distribution p (x | c) of data x given condition c, holds great promise for generating new, previously
037   non-existent data across a wide range of representations. Currently, CGM is pivotal in generating
038   images, videos (Rombach et al., 2021; Saharia et al., 2022a;b; Voleti, 2023), and text (Li et al., 2022;
039   Strudel et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2024), but it is also expected to be applied to scientific computing,
040   such as molecular generation (Kang & Cho, 2019) and physical simulations (Huang et al., 2024;
041   Gebhard et al., 2023).
042   One of the key challenges of applying CGM in scientific fields is the sparsity of available data con-
043   ditions. This sparsity necessitates extrapolating beyond the observed conditions (Lee et al., 2023).
044   An important example of scientific applications is molecular generation—imagine that you wish to
045   discover a new molecule xdesired with a desired chemical property cdesired , for which no molecular
046   data may be available. Here, we have only observed a limited number of properties cobs , which
047   may be very sparse and require difficult extrapolation. This sparsity issue is more apparent when the
048   condition or property is multi-dimensional.
049   In contrast, recent deep generative models for CGM have been designed mainly for situations where
050   the conditions are densely observed. Consider the example of methods (Ding et al., 2021; Zhao
051   et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024) based on Vicinal risk minimization (VRM) by Chapelle et al. (2000).
052   In VRM, the observed conditions cobs are augmented with Gaussian noise wc ∼ N (0, I), and the
053   generative model is trained so that the unknown conditional distribution p (x | cobs + wc ) becomes
      close to the known distribution p (x | cobs ). Thus, if we can only observe two conditions c1obs and

076   We expect that one of the hopes to overcome this difficulty is dynamical generative models, includ-
077
      ing diffusion models (Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020) and, in particular, the simplest of these——
      Flow matching (FM) (Liu et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023). FM
078
      itself is the method of generative modeling to approximate a probability distribution p(x). In FM,
079
      two probability distributions are gradually deformed by flows induced by ordinary differential equa-
080   tions (ODEs). This deformation makes it possible to formulate the learning of the generative model
081   as an estimation of the “vector field”, i.e., the way in which the ODE infinitesimally transformed
082   the data. In particular, the methods based on FM stabilize the learning of vector fields, making it
083   possible to generate a variety of data representations, including images (Esser et al., 2024), text (Hu
084   et al., 2024), audio (Le et al., 2023), DNA (Stark et al., 2024), and molecules (Song et al., 2023;
085   Miller et al., 2024).
086   This paper proposes the framework of Extended Flow Matching (EFM), which realizes a “smooth”
087   change of distributions for departure from the observed conditions, where we introduce an inductive
088   bias of low sensitivity of p (x | c) with respect to conditions c . If we assume that the target data is
089   in nature, such as molecules, it is reasonable to impose this inductive bias. We remark that this kind
090   of inductive bias has been used throughout the history of generative models as a method to prevent
091   overfitting and a method to stabilize generative models; see, e.g., (Miyato et al., 2018). Therefore,
092   our method addresses extrapolation by learning a model such that the data to be extrapolated follows
093   this inductive bias of low sensitivity.
094   More specifically, we apply the extended Monge–Kantorovich theory introduced by Brenier (2003)
095   to conditional generative models. This leads to a framework for learning matrix fields in a general-
096   ized continuity equation instead of vector fields in the continuity equations in FM.
097
      Furthermore, by combining the concept of Dirichlet energy on Wasserstein spaces introduced by
098   Lavenant (2019) with Multi-Marginal Optimal Transport (MMOT), we can derive an algorithm
099   called MMOT-EFM that reduces the sensitivity of the generated conditional distribution. In addition,
100   our proposed method is shown to outperform existing methods in the task of molecular generation
101   in situations where conditions are sparsely observed.

171   In this section, we extend the standard FM theory to consider conditional probability with condi-
172   tions c within a bounded domain Ω ⊂ Rk . Let pc (x) := p (x | c) be the unknown target conditional
173   probability density, and let p0,c (x) := p0 (x | c) be a user-chosen tractable conditional density given
174   c = (ci )i∈[1:k] = (c1 , . . . , ck ) ∈ Ω, such as normal distributions with mean and variance parameter-

      ized by c. We will use the notation in the previous section, that is, we will denote by µc and µ0,c the

      3.1     E XTENSION OF FM
179   We will present this subsection in parallel with § 2.1.
180
181   Generalized Continuity Equation: We directly extend the interpretation of FM by extending the
182   domain of ψ in (2.3) from I to I × Ω, where Ω is the space of conditions. For brevity, instead
183   of using explicit I × Ω, we would like to use a general bounded domain Ξ in Euclidean space
184   as an analog of Ω of the previous section and analogously set the goal of EFM to the learning of
185   µ: Ξ → P(D). Now, just like FM, instead of learning µ directly, EFM aims to learn a matrix field
186   u: Ξ × D → Rd×dim Ξ such that generalized CE (Brenier, 2003; Lavenant, 2019)
187                            ∇ξ pξ (x) + divx (pξ (x)u(ξ, x)) = 0 ((ξ, x) ∈ Ξ × D)                      (3.1)
188
      holds for the density pξ of µξ . Here, div is an extended divergence operator, see Appendix (A.1).
189
190   Inference: Inference based on the matrix field u is slightly more complicated than in FM, which
191   provides a single vector field to integrate the ODE. Various tasks can be solved solely with the
192   matrix field, including the typical cases of generation and transfer. For Ξ = I × Ω, the generation
193   given condition c will be performed by transforming µ0,c → µ1,c , and the transfer from c to c′ by
194   transforming µ1,c → µ1,c′ . Both are performed by integrating the matrix field along the path in
195   I × Ω. More precisely, the following result justifies our use of the matrix field u in (3.1) to achieve
196   the goal of conditional generative modeling:
197
198         Proposition 3.1 (GCE generates γ-induced CE). Let µ: Ξ → P(D) and u: Ξ × D →
199         Rd×dim Ξ be a probability path and a matrix field, respectively, that satisfy (3.1). Then,
200         for any differentiable path γ: I → Ξ, the γ-induced probability path µγ := µ ◦ γ and the
201         γ-induced vector field v γ : I × D ∋ (s, x) 7→ u(γ(s), x)γ̇(s) ∈ Rd satisfy the continuity
202
            equation, i.e., the density pγ of µγ and v γ satisfy ∂s pγs (x) + divx (pγs (x)v γ (s, x)) = 0.

398   Dynamical generative models (DGMs) for CGM: In addition to the VRM-based method men-
399   tioned in § 1, there are two other methods: COT-FM (Kerrigan et al., 2024b) and Bayesian-
400

      2024). These methods rely on the relatively weak assumption that the map of conditional distri-
401
      butions c 7→ p (x | c) is measurable, or can be discontinuous with respect to c. In contrast, the
402
      learning algorithm of EFM is designed under the assumption that p (x | c) is continuous with re-
403   spect to c. This distinction arises because the former addresses situations where high-dimensional
404   conditions, such as inverse problems of PDEs, can be densely observed, while the latter ad-
405   dresses scenarios where relatively low-dimensional conditions, such as molecular generation, can
406   be sparsely observed. Various other methods for learning CGMs have been proposed, depending
407   on how the data and conditions are available. For example, making the vector field depend on the
408   transport plan π (Atanackovic et al., 2024) or obtaining a joint sample (c, x) in a Bayesian manner
409   (Wildberger et al., 2023). Note that these methods are not about continuity with respect to c in the
410   distribution p (x | c).
411
412
      Energy principles in DGMs: We also mention the family of Schrödinger-bridge based methods
      by (Tong et al., 2023a; Koshizuka & Sato, 2022), which also aims to interpolate between an arbitrary
413
      pair of distribution. This family solves the continuity equation while minimizing the regularized en-
414
      ergy of the user’s choice in the generation process. Kim et al. (2023) also uses Wasserstein Barycen-
415   ter for distributional interpolation. Multi-marginal stochastic interpolants by Albergo et al. (2024)
416   learn a model that is similar to EFM. The method optimizes not only the vector fields but also the
417   path γ: [0, 1] → Ω in Proposition 3.1 to minimize kinetic energy. Our MMOT-EFM is novel in that
418   it minimizes the transport cost in a complementary way to the stochastic interpolant. MMOT-EFM
419   trains only a matrix field to minimize Dirichlet energy, which is a
420   generalization of the kinetic cost. This makes it possible to learn a
421   model that transports optimally without optimization of γ.

518   In this paper, we developed the theory of EFM, an ex-
519   tension of FM that models the transformation of distribu-
520   tions with respect to conditions by a matrix field. EFM

      explicitly shows how distributions change under different
      conditions. The EFM theory is complementary to many

527   expensive, the application of EFM will expand in the fu-

      We aim to sample from the unknown conditional distribution Ω ∋ c 7→ p(• | c) ∈ P(D). We extend
759
      the flow matching technique developed in (Lipman et al., 2023) for this aim. The technique evolves
760   unconditional probability distributions µt ∈ P(D), t ∈ [0, 1] from a source distribution µ0 (such
761   as Gaussian N (,)) to a target distribution µ1 ≈ pdata by means of a continuity equation. We then
762   introduce a generalized continuity equation that evolves conditional distributions µt,c , t ∈ [0, 1],
763   c ∈ Ω from source distributions µ0 to the target distributions µt=1,c ≈ pdata (• | c).

1258
1259   To evaluate our conditional generation, we use the pre-trained VAE model to encode EFM-generated

1262
       separately for interpolation and extrapolation. All MAEs are first calculated for each property and
       then averaged for both properties.

       F.2   DATASET AND BASELINES
1266
       We first trained a Site-information-encoded Junction Tree Variational Autoencoder (SJT-VAE)
1267   model, a variant implementation of the Junction Tree Variational Autoencoder (JT-VAE) (Jin et al.,
1268   2018). SJT-VAE was initially designed to eliminate the arbitrariness of JT-VAE and enable appli-
1269   cations such as RJT-RL (Ishitani et al., 2022). We chose SJT-VAE over JT-VAE due to its superior
1270   reconstruction accuracy and faster training times. However, we expect that similar results could be
1271   reproduced with the original JT-VAE implementation.
1272
       Our SJT-VAE model was trained on the ZINC-250k dataset (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018;
1273   Akhmetshin et al., 2021). A random subset of 80, 000 molecules was labeled with the number
1274   of HBAs and the number of rotatable bonds, with all labels computed using RDKit. These 80, 000
1275   molecules were then binned into a 2D matrix based on their property values. From this matrix,
1276   we selected a region with concentrated data: molecules with 2 and 4 rotatable bonds and 3 and 5
1277   HBAs, forming 4 bins with property sets (2, 3), (2, 5), (4, 3), and (4, 5). To balance the dataset, we
1278   up-sampled or capped the number of training examples to 5, 000 per bin.
1279   To evaluate out-of-distribution conditional generation, we generated molecules with property sets
1280   not included in the training set, specifically (3, 4), (2, 4), (4, 4), (3, 3), and (5, 5). For property sets
1281   where only one property is out-of-distribution, we calculated the MAE based solely on the out-of-
1282   distribution property.
1283
       All flow matching-based models, including MMOT-EFM and baselines, are trained with a batch size
1284
       of 250 and the learning rate of 1e−4 for 160, 000 iterations. Training on a single Nvidia V-100 GPU
1285   with evaluation every 5000 iterations took around 4 hours.
