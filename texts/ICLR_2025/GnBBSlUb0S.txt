009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformer-based dialogue generation (DG) models are ubiquitous in modern
012            conversational artificial intelligence (AI) platforms. These models, however, are
013            susceptible to adversarial attacks, i.e., prompts that appear textually indiscernible
014            from normal inputs but are maliciously crafted to make the models generate re-
015            sponses incoherent and irrelevant to the conversational context. Evaluating the ad-
016            versarial robustness of DG models is thus crucial to their real-world deployment.
017            Adversarial methods typically exploit gradient information and output logits (or
018            probabilities) to effectively modify key input tokens, thereby achieving excellent
019
               attack performance. Nevertheless, such white-box approaches are impractical in
               real-world scenarios since the models’ internal parameters are typically inaccessi-
020
               ble. While black-box methods, which exploit only input prompts and DG models’
021
               output responses to craft adversarial attacks, offer a wider applicability, they often
022            suffer from poor performance.
023
               In a human-machine conversation, good generated responses are expected to be
024            semantically coherent and textually succinct. We thus formulate adversarial at-
025            tack on DG models as a bi-objective optimization problem, where input prompts
026            are modified in order to 1) minimize the response coherence, and 2) maximize the
027            generation length. In this paper, we empirically demonstrate that optimizing either
028            objective alone results in subpar performance. We then propose a dialogue gen-
029            eration attack framework (DGAttack) that employs multi-objective optimization
030            to consider both objectives simultaneously when perturbing user prompts to craft
031            adversarial inputs. Leveraging the exploration capability of multi-objective evo-
032
               lutionary algorithm due to its intrinsic diversity preservation, DGAttack success-
               fully creates effective adversarial prompts in a true black-box manner, i.e., access-
033
               ing solely DG models’ inputs and outputs. Experiments across four benchmark
034
               datasets and three language models (i.e., BART, DialoGPT, T5) demonstrate the
035            excellent performance of DGAttack compared to existing white-box, gray-box,
036            and black-box approaches. Especially, benchmarks with large language models
037            (i.e., Llama 3.1 and Gemma 2) suggest that DGAttack is the state-of-the-art black-
038            box adversarial attack on dialogue generation.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Dialogue generation (DG) has made advancing strides with pre-trained transformers (Zhang et al.,
044   2020c; Roller et al., 2021), enabling the creation of sophisticated chatbots capable of natural, coher-
045   ent conversations. Nevertheless, DG models remain vulnerable to adversarial attacks—malicious
046   inputs that, while appearing benign, are designed to disrupt the model’s output by generating inco-
047   herent or irrelevant responses (Goodfellow et al., 2015). Given the increasing deployment of DG
048   models in real-world applications, evaluating their adversarial robustness is critical to ensuring their
049   reliability and trustworthiness.
050   White-box adversarial attacks, where attackers exploit gradient information and output logits to craft
051   adversarial inputs, have shown excellent performance in degrading the response quality (Li et al.,
052   2023a; Cheng et al., 2018). These attacks effectively identify critical tokens and modify them to
053   compromise the model’s performance. However, white-box methods rely on access to DG models’
      internal parameters—information that is often unavailable in real-world settings due to proprietary


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      restrictions or security constraints. In contrast, black-box attacks, which do not require access to
055   model parameters or gradients, offer broader applicability. These methods craft adversarial sam-
056   ples based solely on input prompts and output responses. Black-box attacks tend to underperform
057   compared to their white-box counterparts, as they do not make use of internal knowledge.
058
      A key challenge in attacking DG models lies in the conversational nature. Unlike other tasks where
059
      inputs are processed independently, DG models generate responses based on both the current input
060
      and the accumulated chat history (Liu et al., 2020). This reliance on prior context makes small input
061   perturbations less effective, particularly in black-box settings. Traditional black-box adversarial
062   methods, which typically focus on minimizing accuracy alone (Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020; Ren
063   et al., 2019a; Li et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021), struggle to fully exploit the vulnerabilities of DG
064   models in these scenarios. Responses from conversational AI agents are expected to be relevant,
065   coherent, and succinct. We observed that adversarial prompts that induce DG models to generate
066   longer responses tend to have a greater attack success rate, as these extended outputs are often
067   irrelevant to the intended conversational context. However, exploiting this trade-off is non-trivial
068   since modern large language models (LLMs) are adept at generating coherent long-form responses.
069
      It is thus necessary to simultaneously optimize both generation length and response coherence.
070   To address these challenges, we propose DGAttack, a novel black-box adversarial attack frame-
071   work that formulates attacking DG models as a bi-objective optimization problem. Employing the
072   non-dominated sorting genetic algorithm II (NSGA-II) (Deb et al., 2002), DGAttack simultaneously
073   optimizes two objectives: maximizing generation length and minimizing response coherence. This
074   approach allows us to explore the adversarial space efficiently while relying solely on the model’s
075
      inputs and outputs—making it particularly suitable for black-box settings, where internal parameters
      and output probabilities are inaccessible. Through comprehensive experiments on four benchmark
076
      datasets and three language models (BART, DialoGPT, and T5), we demonstrate that DGAttack
077
      outperforms existing black-box, white-box, and gray-box adversarial methods. Moreover, DGAt-
078   tack sets a new standard for black-box adversarial attacks on dialogue generation, particularly when
079   applied to large language models like Llama 3.1 and Gemma 2, demonstrating state-of-the-art per-
080   formance in generating effective adversarial prompts.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS

085
      2.1   D IALOGUE G ENERATION
086
      Dialogue generation (DG) involves the task of processing natural language inputs and producing
087   human-like responses, typically in the context of ongoing conversations, such as interactions with
088   chatbots. Typically, a DG model must interpret the conversation history up to the current turn and
089   generate appropriate responses in a structured manner. Over the past few years, DG has seen signifi-
090   cant progress, particularly with pre-trained transformer-based models, such as decoder-only models
091   like DialoGPT (Zhang et al., 2020b) and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), as well as encoder-decoder
092   models like T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) and BART (Lewis et al., 2020). These models generate responses
093   that resemble natural human dialogue, with some even utilizing additional information, such as user
094   profiles or conversational context, to create more personalized and context-aware interactions.
095
096   2.2   T EXTUAL A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
097
098   Textual adversarial attacks can be used for testing the robustness of natural language process-
099
      ing models. These attacks are categorized into character-level, word-level, and sentence-level
      approaches (Papernot et al., 2016; Ebrahimi et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2022).
100
      Early character-level attacks manipulated individual characters—by adding, deleting, or substituting
101
      them—which allowed for straightforward adversarial sample generation (Belinkov & Bisk, 2018).
102   However, these approaches often resulted in grammatically incorrect outputs, making them suscep-
103   tible to grammar-based defense mechanisms (Pruthi et al., 2019). Consequently, character-level
104   attacks have become less prominent in recent works (Le et al., 2022). Sentence-level attacks, which
105   perturb entire sentences, offer better grammatical correctness by employing techniques such as para-
106   phrasing and encoding-decoding (Iyyer et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2017). Despite their syntactic ac-
107   curacy, these methods often introduce substantial semantic shifts, reducing the overall success rate
      of the attack. Word-level attacks have emerged as a popular approach due to their ability to balance


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      grammatical accuracy, semantic coherence, and attack success. These methods typically involve
109   word substitution, addition, or deletion while preserving the overall meaning and context of the sen-
110   tence (Jin et al., 2019; Ren et al., 2019b). Such strategies offer a middle ground between maintaining
111   meaning and generating effective adversarial samples.
112
      Recent advancements in learning-based methods, particularly using BERT-based Masked Language
113
      Models (MLMs), have improved the semantic relevance of adversarial samples by leveraging context
114
      to generate word substitutions (Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020; Li et al., 2020). However, these models
115   can still introduce ambiguity in tasks like rumor detection and sentiment analysis.
116
117   While most adversarial attacks focus on classification, there is a growing interest in sequence-to-
118
      sequence (seq2seq) models. Works like NMTSloth (Chen et al., 2022) target length manipulation in
      neural machine translation (NMT) systems, aiming to generate longer and less coherent translations.
119
      Seq2Sick (Cheng et al., 2018) and other methods attempt to degrade the generation confidence in
120
      seq2seq tasks by reducing the likelihood of producing correct outputs (Michel et al., 2019).
121
122   Most notably, multi-objective white-box attacks have been applied to dialogue generation models,
123   where approaches such as DGSlow (Li et al., 2023b) optimize for both accuracy minimization and
124
      generation length maximization. While white-box attacks utilize gradient information or model pa-
      rameters to effectively create adversarial prompts, such assumptions on the accessibility of internal
125
      knowledge do not hold in practice. Several gradient-free attacks on NLP models make use of the
126
      models’ output logits or probabilities for importance ranking in order to identify key input tokens for
127   perturbation (Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020; Li et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2019a). However, neither logit
128   nor probability information is available during interactions with real-world conversational agents.

134   3.1.1    D IALOGUE G ENERATION
135
136   Suppose a chatbot aims to model conversations between two individuals. We follow a similar setup
137   (Liu et al., 2020), where each individual has a persona (e.g., cA for person A), described with L
138
      profile sentences cA1 , . . . , cAL . Person A chats with another person B through an N -turn dialogue
      (xA1 , xB1 , . . . , xAN , xBN ), where N is the total number of turns and xAn is the utterance that A
139
      says in the n-th turn. A DG model f takes the persona cA , the entire dialogue history until the n-th
140
      turn hAn = (xB1 , . . . , xAn−1 ), and B’s current utterance xBn as inputs, generating outputs xAn
141   by maximizing the probability p(xAn |cA , hAn , xBn ). The same process applies for B to keep the
142   conversation going.
143

145

147
      leading the chatbot designed to emulate person A. It is crucial to maintain the integrity of the chat
      history hAn = (xB1 , . . . , xAn−1 ), ensuring that it remains unchanged to reflect realistic conditions
148
      in practical applications.

161   In dialogue generation, longer generated responses are often observed to drift away from the original
      context and introduce irrelevant or nonsensical content, making them particularly effective for ad-

      versarial attacks. However, achieving longer responses presents a challenge, as language models are
163   trained to maintain coherence and relevance, even when generating lengthy sequences. To address
164   this, we define two primary objectives for our black-box adversarial attack: Accuracy Score (AS)
165   and Generation Length (GL).
166
      Accuracy Score represents the degradation in the model’s accuracy. It is calculated as the combined
167
      sum of accuracy metrics—BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR—by comparing the adversarially gener-
168
      ated response x̂An to the original, unperturbed response xAn . This objective measures the reduction
169   in similarity between the original and adversarially generated responses:

174
      to semantically less accurate responses, GL is defined as the total number of tokens in the generated
175   output sentence x̂An , representing the length of the adversarial response:

      These two objectives are optimized simultaneously to craft adversarial samples that force DG models
180
      to generate responses that are not only inaccurate but also longer and more irrelevant.

189

190

191   overcome this limitation, we redefine the accuracy objective for our black-box setting by leveraging
192   the model’s original generated response from the unperturbed input sentence as a pseudo-reference.
193   Instead of comparing the adversarial response x̂An to an external reference xrefn , we compare it to
194   the original response xAn generated by the model in response to the unperturbed input xBn . This
195   approach allows us to practically evaluate accuracy in black-box settings by using the model’s own
196   outputs as a baseline for assessing adversarial success. The goal is to minimize the similarity be-
197   tween the adversarial response x̂An and the original response xAn , thereby degrading the model’s
198   performance while ensuring the adversarial input remains contextually appropriate.

      3.2.1    PARETO D OMINANCE IN A DVERSARIAL ATTACK ON D IALOGUE G ENERATION
202
203   Regarding the two objectives, Accuracy Score (AS) and Generation Length (GL), a candidate adver-
204   sarial sentence xa is said to Pareto dominate another adversarial sentence xb (denoted as xa ≻ xb )
205   if xa is no worse than xb in both objectives and strictly better in at least one objective:
206
207
208   xa ≻ xb ⇔ (AS(xa ) ≤ AS(xb ) ∧ GL(xa ) ≥ GL(xb )) ∧ (AS(xa ) < AS(xb ) ∨ GL(xa ) > GL(xb ))
209                                                                                          (5)
210   The utopian adversarial sentence that force DG models to generate responses with the maximal
211   length and the minimal accuracy is hard to obtain. This is because maximizing GL does not al-
212   ways succeed in minimizing accuracy, especially regarding modern LLMs, and while minimizing
213   accuracy could unintentionally shorten responses. Instead, multi-objective attack aims to obtain the
214   Pareto set of adversarial sentences that are all optimal in the sense that they are not Pareto dominated
215   by any sentences in the adversarial space. The Pareto set forms a Pareto front in the objective space
      (GL,AS), as illustrated in Fig. 1, where each Pareto-optimal sentence represent an optimal trade-off

248   3.2.2   C RAFTING A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS WITH M ULTI -O BJECTIVE G ENETIC A LGORITHM
249
      In our DGAttack framework, we adopt the non-dominated sorting genetic algorithm II (NSGA-
250
      II) (Deb et al., 2002) to obtain a good approximation set of diverse non-dominated adversarial sen-
251
      tences. Figure 2 illustrates the workflow of DGAttack.
252
253   In the first generation t = 1, DGAttack generates the initial population P 1 consisting of N adver-
254   sarial sentences, which are created from random perturbations of the original input sentence xBn . In
255
      each generation t, promising candidate sentences from P t (in terms of Pareto dominance regarding
      the two objectives AS and GL) are copied into a selection set S t . Two variation operators (crossover
256
      and mutation) are applied on S t to create a set Ot of new candidates. Crossover recombines each
257
      pair of selected sentences x, x′ ∈ S t (i.e., parents) by exchanging random segments of their words
258   to craft two new sentences o, o′ ∈ Ot (i.e., offspring). Mutation randomly perturbs some words in
259   offspring sentences o ∈ Ot , thereby introducing novel tokens to the search process. After variation,
260   the current population and the offspring population are merged into a pool (P t +Ot ) from which the
261   non-dominated sorting procedure assigns a rank to each candidate based on their Pareto dominance.
262   Another selection round is conducted to select candidates into the next generation P t+1 based on
263   their ranks. If candidates from the same rank compete, the ones with higher crowding distances is
264   preferred (i.e., the ones that are far from others). The above procedure of selection - variation is
265   iterated until the allowed number of generations is reached. In this final population, the candidates
266
      that are not dominated by others are regarded as the approximation set of non-dominated adversarial
      sentences obtained by DGAttack. Further details can be found in Appendix B.
267

269   duce adversarial perturbations. To generate new sentences, we perturb salient words within existing

355   4.1.4   BASELINES
356
      We evaluate our approach against two white-box and two black-box adversarial attack strategies,
357
      adapted to the dialogue generation task.
358
359   For the white-box attacks, we focus on: 1) HotFlip (Ebrahimi et al., 2018), which generates adver-
360   sarial examples through both word and character-level substitutions driven by embedding gradients.
361   2) TextBugger (Li et al., 2018), which employs a greedy strategy for word substitution and character
362
      manipulation to execute white-box adversarial attacks.
363   We compare our methods with two recent black-box textual word-level adversarial attacks: 1) BAE
364   (BERT-based Adversarial Examples) (Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020), which estimates the impor-
365   tance of each token by computing the change in output probability before and after deleting that to-
366   ken, and then uses BERT to perturb the most vulnerable words. 2) PWWS (Probability Weighted
367   Word Saliency) (Ren et al., 2019a), which generates adversarial examples by replacing key words
368
      with their synonyms, selected according to a probability-weighted saliency score, aiming to mislead
      the model while preserving the original meaning of the sentence. Although both of these methods
369
370
      are labeled as black-box approaches, we argue that they belong to the gray-box category because
371   they rely on access to the model’s output probabilities to identify vulnerable words. In real-world
372
      scenarios, such output probabilities are often inaccessible, limiting their practical applicability. This
373   distinction is crucial, as truly black-box methods, like DGAttack, operate solely on the generated
374
      responses without requiring internal model details. To adapt these methods to the DG setting, we
      calculate the importance score based on the Targeted Confidence which is formulated as the cumu-
375
      lative probabilities of a sequence with respect to its reference sentence xrefn .
376
377   We also compare our approach with DGSlow (Li et al., 2023b), which is the state-of-the-art multi-
      objective white-box adversarial attack for dialogue generation. We adapt DGSlow’s objectives to an


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      DGAttack gray-box baseline, in which we experiment DGAttack with TC as the accuracy objective
379   and GL as the length objective. This adaptation underscores the effectiveness of our approach in
380   degrading the target model’s performance without requiring access to the model’s parameters nor
381   its output probabilities. Additionally, for black-box baselines, we implement a single-objective
382   Genetic Algorithm (GA) targeting either accuracy score or generation length (see Appendix C).

409   Our main results, shown in Table 1, outline the attack success rate, accuracy metrics, and cosine sim-
410   ilarity. DGAttack consistently induces DG models to generate longer and less accurate responses
411   compared to white-box, gray-box and black-box baselines. Notably, the multi-objective approach
412   employed by DGAttack outperforms the single-objective GA in terms of overall attack effective-
413   ness. Indeed, simultaneously targeting both response coherence and generation length leads to more
414   powerful and disruptive adversarial attacks.
415   We compare the black-box DGAttack with a gray-box variant, which minimizes accuracy by lever-
416   aging the model’s output probabilities. The results show that, even without access to internal infor-
417   mation, the black-box DGAttack is capable of crafting adversarial sentences inducing DG models
418   to generate longer and less accurate responses than the gray-box one. This can be attributed to
419   the fact that using accuracy metrics like BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR in DGAttack evaluates
420   the overall coherence and fluency of the entire response, whereas the gray-box approach relies on
421   token-level probabilities, which often capture only local confidence at the word level. It emphasizes
422
      the practicality and robustness of our method, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world dialogue
      generation scenarios where access to model-specific knowledge may be restricted or unavailable.
423
424   In some cases, DGAttack performs moderately better than the white-box multi-objective method
425   DGSlow on certain metrics. However, while DGSlow generally outperforms all other baselines and
426   our proposed black-box methods, it cannot be used in real-world scenarios because it requires access
427   to internal information about the target models, such as gradients or probabilities. In contrast, our
428
      black-box DGAttack does not rely on such internal parameters or even output logits, making it a
      feasible approach in practice where model information is unknown.
429
430   Experiment results demonstrate that DGAttack is a powerful and flexible tool for generating adver-
431   sarial examples in the black-box setting. It effectively balances the dual objectives of degrading
      accuracy and extending generation length, producing adversarial samples that are both diverse and

471   4.4   A DVERSARIAL ATTACK AGAINST LLM S
472
      Most adversarial attacks on LLMs, particularly in real-world deployments via API access, cannot
473
      rely on white-box techniques, which require access to gradients and model internals. Instead, exist-
474   ing white-box approaches often resort to transferability attacks, where an adversarial attack is first
475   performed on an open-source LLM, and then transferred to other LLMs. In contrast, our black-box
476   DGAttack can directly targets LLMs without requiring access to their gradients or output logits,
477   making it well-suited for attacking LLMs deployed through APIs. This is a critical advantage, as
478   most real-world LLMs, apart from open-source models, do not expose internal information, making
479   gradient-based attacks impractical.
480   Table 3 demonstrates that DGAttack consistently outperforms DGSlow in transferability attacks
481   from smaller models (e.g., BART) to LLMs like Llama 3.1 and Gemma 2. A key advantage of
482   DGAttack is its black-box nature, which does not depend on model gradients or parameters. This
483   independence reduces the risk of generating adversarial examples that are overfitted or highly spe-
484   cific to the model from which gradients are computed, a common limitation of white-box methods
485   like DGSlow. The results underscore DGAttack’s effectiveness and transferability in black-box set-
      tings.

525   5      C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we proposed DGAttack, a black-box multi-objective attack framework for generating
528   adversarial samples aimed at degrading the performance of dialogue generation models. By leverag-
529
      ing multi-objective evolutionary algorithm (NSGA-II), we simultaneously optimize for two objec-
      tives—response length and accuracy. Our method generates adversarial sentences through semantic-
530
      preserving perturbations, which ensures that the samples are coherent enough to deceive the dialogue
531
      model while substantially reducing the quality of its output. We demonstrate that DGAttack mostly
532   outperforms all black-box, gray-box, white-box baselines and transfer-based white-box attack like
533   DGSlow in black-box settings, particularly against large language models. The ability to directly
534   attack models without relying on access to internal information highlights the practicality and ro-
535   bustness of our approach, proving its applicability in real-world API-based LLM deployments. Our
536   results underscore the power of DGAttack as a state-of-the-art black-box adversarial attack for dia-
537   logue generation models, including large-scale models like LLaMA and Gemma.

857   B     DGATTACK WITH N ON - DOMINATED S ORTING G ENETIC A LGORITHM II
858
859   Initialization. DGAttack constructs the initial population P of N candidate adversarial sentences,
860
      which are created by randomly perturbing the original input sentence. We evaluate the fitness of
      each candidate via the two objectives (i.e., AS and GL).
861
862   Binary Tournament Selection. We create a selection set S containing copies of promising candi-
863   date sentences in the current population P . Each time, two individuals (i.e., candidate sentences)
      are randomly sampled from P , forming a tournament, and the one with superior fitness (i.e., the one


                                                                16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      that Pareto dominates the other) is the winner. If the two individuals are non-dominated with each
865   other, we break the tie randomly. The winner is then cloned into S. This process is repeated until
866   the selection set has N selected sentences. Note that we perform sample with replacement so that
867   the current population P remains intact during selection and we allow duplicates in S.
868
      Crossover. The goal of the crossover operator is to generate new adversarial sentences that inherit
869
      beneficial traits from existing sentences. To achieve this, the operator combines segments from each
870
      pair of parent sentences in the selection set S to create two new offspring sentences. Let the two
871                                    (1)   (1)       (1)              (2)   (2)      (2)          (1)
      parent sentences be p1 = (w1 , w2 , . . . , wn ) and p2 = (w1 , w2 , . . . , wn ), where wk and
872     (2)
873   wk represent the words in the first and second parent sentences, respectively. A random crossover
874
      point k ∈ {1, 2, . . . , n} is selected. The offspring sentences o1 and o2 are generated by swapping
      segments from the two parent sentences:

      the resulting sentences remain coherent and grammatically correct. The mutated sentence p′ with its
880
      replacement wk′ is represented as: p′ = (w1 , . . . , wk−1 , wk′ , wk+1 , . . . , wn )
881
882   Non-dominated Sorting. After variation (i.e., crossover and mutation), we have an offspring set O
883   of N newly-created sentences. We combine both parent and offspring sentences into a pool (P + O)
884
      of 2N candidates. This pool is then partitioned into non-overlapping subsets Fi . Each subset Fi ,
      also called a non-dominated sets, contain sentences that are not Pareto dominated by any others in
885
      the pool if all subsets of smaller indices F1 , F2 , . . . , Fi−1 are removed from the pool. The subset
886
      F1 thus contains the best sentences obtained so far as they are not dominated by any other pool
887   members. The subset F1 also forms a non-dominated front in the objective space (GL,AS).
888
889   Replacement. We need to select N sentences from the pool of 2N candidates to form the population
890
      for the next generation. Sentences from the non-dominated sets of smaller indices are given priority
      to be selected first F1 , F2 , . . . , Fk until |F1 ∪ F2 ∪ . . . ∪ Fk | ≥ N . We need to select (N − |F1 ∪
891
      F2 , ∪ . . . ∪ Fk−1 |) remaining sentences from Fk based on their crowding distances. This metric
892
      measures how far a candidate is from its nearest neighbors of the same non-dominated set in the
893   objective space (GL,AS). Sentences with a higher crowding distance are preferred, as they lie in less
894   populated regions, promoting diversity.
895
896
      The above procedure of selection → variation → non-dominated sorting → replacement is repeated
      until a termination criterion is satisfied (e.g., reaching the maximum number of generations or run-
897
      ning out of the computing budget). Upon termination, the non-dominated set F1 in the population
898
      is the approximation set obtained our method. Sentences in the final F1 also forms an approximate
899   non-dominated front in the objective space (GL,AS) that approximates the true Pareto front.
900

      Generation Length or minimize Accuracy Score .

       applications. By highlighting these vulnerabilities, we hope to raise awareness about potential risks

       effective defenses. DGAttack demonstrates that even black-box methods can significantly compro-

1110
1111   H.1   P ROPOSED D EFENSE M ECHANISMS
1112
1113   To address the challenges posed by DGAttack and similar adversarial methods, we propose several
1114
       strategies for mitigating their impact and ensuring the responsible deployment of dialogue generation
       (DG) systems.
1115
1116   First, adversarial training involves augmenting training datasets with adversarial examples to en-
1117   hance model robustness by teaching it to handle perturbed inputs Li et al. (2017). Second, input val-
1118   idation and denoising techniques can help detect and mitigate adversarial perturbations before they
1119   affect the model, ensuring cleaner inputs Lee & Lee (2018). Third, robust optimization methods,
1120
       such as regularization techniques and specialized loss functions, can reduce the model’s suscepti-
       bility to manipulations Zhang et al. (2020a). Lastly, detection pipelines that monitor input-output
1121
       patterns to flag anomalous behavior indicative of adversarial attacks can serve as an effective defense
1122
       in deployed systems Nithya et al. (2024).
1123
1124   These strategies, while not implemented or evaluated in this work, are essential for safeguarding DG
1125   systems against adversarial threats and ensuring their secure and ethical deployment in real-world
1126

       The Choice of Accuracy Objective The ablation study presented in Table 12 explores the effect
1176   of using different performance metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR) as the accuracy objective
1177   for minimization. In our main experiments, we aggregated all three metrics to form a combined
1178   accuracy objective, which was used to guide the adversarial attack. Interestingly, the results indicate
1179   that when comparing the combined objective to individual metrics, the performance differences
1180   were marginal. This implies that while each metric focuses on distinct aspects of accuracy—BLEU
1181   emphasizing n-gram precision, ROUGE measuring recall, and METEOR accounting for semantic
1182   similarities through synonyms and paraphrasing—their roles in contributing to the degradation of
1183   the generated text are largely aligned.
1184   One notable observation is that minimizing any single accuracy metric often triggers a reduction in
1185   the others as well. For example, when focusing solely on minimizing BLEU, we see that ROUGE
1186   and METEOR scores also tend to degrade. This suggests that there is a degree of overlap in the
1187   dimensions these metrics assess. BLEU’s focus on exact matches between the generated and ref-
       erence text often overlaps with ROUGE’s focus on recall (how much of the reference is captured

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       by the generation), and METEOR’s consideration of paraphrasing and synonyms further ties into
1189   this. Consequently, degradation in one metric is likely to cause a cascading effect, pulling the others
1190   down in tandem.

       This cascading degradation across metrics highlights an important insight: adversarial samples
       crafted to minimize a single accuracy metric are likely to be effective in degrading the overall qual-
1193
       ity of the generated text. This occurs because each metric, though distinct, evaluates overlapping
1194
       characteristics of fluency, coherence, and relevance. Thus, regardless of whether BLEU, ROUGE,
1195   or METEOR is targeted directly, the adversarial attack tends to degrade performance across all three
1196   metrics to some extent.

       That being said, the combined accuracy objective remains the most holistic approach. By aggregat-
       ing BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR into a single metric, the adversarial attack is forced to address
1199

1200

1201   mizing for a single metric may still result in an effective attack, the combined approach ensures a
1202   more robust and consistent reduction in overall text quality across all dimensions, leading to a more
1203   impactful attack outcome.

       J         L IMITATIONS
1219
1220
       Our method still remains several limitations as listed:
1221   Mutation. We use POS tags to identify salient words within a sentence for masking and word
1222   substitution with BERT. An effective heuristic integrated into our mutation operator could better
1223   select important words for substitution, leading to higher-quality candidates.
1224   Crossover. To avoid errors such as breaking word linkages and grammatical mistakes during
1225   crossover, our operator is relatively simple and straightforward, only swapping each segment from
1226   two sentences. A more complex and efficient operator could enhance the diversity among candidates
1227   and improve the attack success rate.
1228
       Attacking LLMs. Large Language Models (Brown et al., 2020) (LLMs) are highly robust due to
1229
       extensive training on diverse datasets. They can effectively handle minor word-level substitutions,
1230   making small perturbations is insufficient for effective attacks. More sophisticated strategies are
1231   required to challenge LLMs and degrade their performance.
1232
1233
       Trade-offs and Computational Considerations. While our black-box method shows promising
       results in attacking LLMs, it comes with increased computational costs. As evolutionary algo-
1234
       rithms require evaluating numerous candidate solutions, leading to longer attack times and high-
1235
       computational cost. In contrast, gradient-based methods like DGSlow are generally faster due to the
1236   direct use of gradient information. This highlights a broader limitation of our empirical evaluation,
1237   which, due to computational and budget constraints, was confined to specific datasets, formats, and
1238   models. While our method has demonstrated notable results on smaller models, these constraints
1239   may limit the generalizability of our findings, particularly for LLMs. Expanding future experiments
1240   to include larger-scale datasets, more diverse formats, and additional task categories could provide
1241   further insights into the broader applicability of our approach. In scenarios where demonstrating
       vulnerabilities in LLMs is critical, the additional computational effort may be justified.
