009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Dense video captioning requires solving the challenging tasks of temporally local-
012            izing events and generating descriptive captions within long video sequences. Ex-
013            isting methods often struggle to capture the evolving context within video streams
014            and to produce accurate temporal alignment. To address this, we propose an online
015            retrieval-augmented approach that processes video segments incrementally while
016
               dynamically retrieving relevant action phrases from a pre-constructed action-text
               corpus. This enriches the contextual information for both the video representation
017
               and the subsequent text decoder, improving the caption generation. Additionally,
018
               we present image-based simulated video pretraining, which mitigates the reliance
019            on extensive video datasets by using image-level text-paired data aligned with the
020            online video captioning format. Our experiments on the ViTT, YouCook2, and Ac-
021            tivityNet benchmarks demonstrate that our model significantly outperforms both
022            existing global and online methods, validating its effectiveness.
023
024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   As video content continues to grow exponentially, the need for automatic video understanding has
027   become increasingly critical. Dense video captioning (Zhu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021a; Yang
028
      et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) involves generating detailed, temporally localized captions for multiple
      events or actions in long video sequences. This capability is crucial for various applications includ-
029
      ing video retrieval, summarization, and accessibility. Traditional video captioning approaches often
030
      generate a single, global caption for an entire video, missing the temporal granularity required to
031   describe and localize specific events as they occur.
032
033   Recent work (Zhou et al., 2024) introduced the concept of online dense video captioning, where
034
      video segments are processed incrementally as they become available. This allows captions to be
      generated progressively, addressing the challenge of handling long video sequences by breaking
035
      them into manageable segments, thereby improving dense video captioning performance. Yet, even
036
      with this advancement, challenges remain in effectively capturing and utilizing the evolving context
037   in video streams, which is critical for generating contextually relevant captions for each segment.
038
039   To address these challenges, we propose Actions Inspire Every Moment (AIEM), a novel online
040
      retrieval-augmented approach for dense video captioning. AIEM enhances dense video captioning
      by dynamically retrieving and integrating relevant action phrases from a pre-constructed action-
041
      text corpus as it processes video segments in an online manner. This integration enriches video
042
      representation and caption generation by providing the visual encoder and text decoder with evolving
043   contextual information, resulting in more accurate and contextually relevant captions. Notably, we
044   demonstrates that retrieving concise action phrases is more effective than using full captions, which
045   can be redundant or less focused. Furthermore, to address the scarcity of large-scale dense video
046   caption datasets, we explore an image-based simulated video pretraining, which leverages image-
047   text paired data to align pretraining with the online video captioning format and improve model
048   performance. Experiments on the ViTT, YouCook2, and ActivityNet benchmarks show significant
049   performance gains, highlighting the effectiveness of our approach.

103   Most video captioning approaches rely on global, offline retrieval, where segment-level information
104
      is merged into a global context for captioning. However, this can limit temporal precision, as the
      global context may not align well with the dynamic nature of video content.
105
106   In this work, we propose a dynamic online retrieval mechanism coupled with autoregressive model-
107   ing, where action phrases are retrieved and integrated incrementally at each time step as the video

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
119
120   Captioning model. Our captioning model follows the standard setup of a vision encoder followed
121   by a text decoder. We utilize the CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021), pretrained on the LAION-
122   2B (Schuhmann et al., 2021) dataset. Specifically, we adopt CLIP’s Vision Transformer (ViT) as the
123   visual encoder and the 12-layer Transformer as the text decoder. Since the original CLIP text model
124
      is designed for contrastive learning rather than text generation, we modify it by applying causal
      attention masking across all layers and further pretrain it for the image captioning task using the
125
      LAION-2B dataset. We keep the vision encoder frozen during the captioning training to preserve
126
      the original vision-text alignment from CLIP’s pretraining.
127
128   Dense video captioning. Given a video V ∈ RT ×H×W ×3 , our goal is to generate a set of tem-
129   porally localized captions: {( [s1 ][e1 ][caption text1 ] ), ..., ( [sn ] [en ] [caption textn ] )}, where each
130   caption is associated with start [s] and end [e] times that mark the event boundaries within the video.
131   Inspired by Vid2Seq (Yang et al., 2023), we adopt their data format of representing temporal infor-
132
      mation as discrete vocabulary tokens. The start and end times [s] and [e] are included as text tokens
      within the caption sequence, enabling both temporal and caption information to be encoded in the
133
      text format. This avoids the need for separate output heads for time prediction and text generation.
134
135   Autoregressive modeling for online video captioning. Unlike global offline methods like
136   Vid2Seq (Yang et al., 2023), which caption the entire video at once and become computationally
137   expensive for long videos, Zhou et al. (2024) introduce an online approach where captions are gen-
138   erated incrementally as frames are processed. Building on this concept, our approach employs an
139   autoregressive online model that processes video segment-by-segment, enhancing both temporal
140   alignment and contextual coherence across current and past segments.
141   An overview of our model is illustrated in figure 2. We divide the video V into S segments process
142   them incrementally, simulating an online scenario where segments become available over time. Each
143   segment contains L frames (T = S × L) which are processed by the visual encoder, e.g. ViT which
144   outputs M tokens of dimension D per segment. A token reduction Transformer then reduces the
145   segment features by sampling N tokens (N   M ), resulting in a tensor of shape N × D. As
146   new segments are processed, let S 0 denote the number of segments streamed up to the current time
147
      step, where S 0 ≤ S. The outputs of all available segments are stacked as S 0 × N × D. On top of
      this, we apply an autoregressive Transformer with causal attention along the segment axis (S-axis).
148
      This incorporates information from both current and preceding segments, generating an evolving
149
      and contextualized video representation as segments are processed incrementally.
150
151   Once the autoregressive Transformer generates a contextualized video representation, the text de-
152   coder generates captions for each segment independently. Instead of concatenating all event captions
153
      into one long sequence, our method aligns captions with their respective video segments. During
      training, the target text for each segment is determined by the captions whose end times fall within
154
      the segment’s temporal interval. This ensures that captions are accurately associated with the events
155
      from each segment. This allows each segment to include events that span multiple segments, with
156   the start time [s] potentially from any prior segment and the end time [e] within the current segment
157   (see figure 1). For a segment without events, we label it as “[BOS][EOS]” using Beginning/End-
158   Of-Segment tokens. For a segment with multiple actions, captions are combined sequentially with
159   corresponding start and end times as “[BOS][s1 ][e1 ][caption text1 ][s2 ][e2 ][caption text2 ] ... [EOS]”.
160   This segment-by-segment processing allows for the online generation of temporally localized cap-
161   tions as each segment is processed, avoiding the need to generate all captions at the end of the video.
      It also makes the approach efficient and well-suited for the online dense video captioning task.

186   3.2    O NLINE ACTION AUGMENTED D ENSE V IDEO C APTIONING
187
188   We present an approach that enhances online dense video captioning by dynamically retrieving and
189   incorporating relevant action priors for each video segment. This provides additional context to the
190   video representation and text decoder, improving the performance of dense video captioning.
191
192   Action text corpus construction. We first construct a corpus of action phrases to serve as contex-
193   tual priors during the dense video captioning process. These action phrases are designed to capture
194
      the key actions or events in each video segment while also identifying the relevant objects
195   To construct this action phrase corpus, we collect captions from the training splits of existing video
196   captioning datasets, such as ViTT, YouCook2, and ActivityNet. Importantly, we only utilize the
197   text captions, excluding the corresponding video frames. This approach enables us to extend be-
198   yond video-text paired datasets and incorporate diverse action descriptions from text-based sources,
199   allowing for a larger set of action phrases.
200   To explore a broader and less domain-specific corpus, we use the HowTo100M dataset (Miech et al.,
201   2019), which contains instructional videos with transcribed speech subtitles. Since HowTo100M is
202   not specifically designed for dense video captioning, it offers a more generic set of action descrip-
203   tions. Again, we only use the subtitle text, discarding the video content. Our experiments show that
204   this more generic corpus performs comparably well.
205   Unlike existing works that use raw video captions (Xu et al., 2024) which can be lengthy and less
206   focused, we propose summarizing them into concise action phrases. We use a publicly available
207   language model, Gemma (Team et al., 2024). It is prompted to extract key actions from raw captions,
208   in the form of an action-object pair, e.g., “baking ham”. An example prompt is: Your goal is to
209   summarize the input sentence using as few words as possible. Focus on the words describing actions
210   or events. Use singular nouns, avoid articles and numeric terms. Respond in the format of <action
211   verb (ing)> <target object (if any)>. Input: {raw caption}. Answer:
212   This approach ensures that the resulting action phrases are concise and focused, filtering out ir-
213   relevant details from the original captions. By summarizing captions in this way, we enhance the
214   effectiveness of vision-text retrieval, leading to more accurate captioning results. Our experiments
215   show that these simplified action phrases outperform the use of full captions. We collect 30,000
      action phrases in total and precompute their text embeddings.

      Precomputation of text embeddings. To optimize the retrieval of action phrases during training
217   and inference, we precompute the text embeddings for the collected action phrases. This step is
218   performed once at the precomputation stage, ensuring efficient retrieval without redundant embed-
219   ding computations during the video captioning process. For this precomputation, we use the frozen
220   instance of the CLIP text model, which retains its original contrastive learning capabilities, as op-
221   posed to the captioning-trained instance described in section 3.1. This frozen model is solely used
222   for precomputing text embeddings and does not participate in the actual captioning process. The
223   vision encoder is also kept frozen throughout the dense video captioning training. Once the text
224   features are extracted from each action phrase, they are globally pooled into a single embedding.
225
      Online retrieval and integration of action phrases. Our vision encoder processes each video
226   segment Vi ∈ RL×H×W ×3 in an online manner. After the visual features are extracted (before
227   token reduction), global pooling is applied to represent the segment as a single embedding. The
228   pooled segment embedding is then compared to the precomputed text embeddings of the action
229   phrases using cosine similarity, selecting the top-k phrases based on their alignment. This ensures
230   that the most relevant actions or events are identified for each video segment.
231
      We integrate the retrieved action phrases into the captioning process in an online manner using sev-
232   eral approaches. Note that each approach involves training a new model. 1) Prefixing the text
233   decoder: The retrieved action phrases are added as natural language prefixes to the text decoder.
234   This approach offers direct guidance on the key actions or events in each video segment, improving
235   caption accuracy and relevance by providing the decoder with explicit information about the seg-
236   ment’s content. 2) Embedding fusion: The text embeddings of the retrieved action phrases are fused
237   with the visual embeddings after the token reduction and before the autoregressive Transformer (see
238   section 3.1). The top-k text embeddings of the retrieved action phrases are average-pooled into one
239   embedding, then concatenated with the token-reduced visual embeddings. This creates enriched
240
      multimodal features that offer a more comprehensive representation of each segment. The fused
      features are then processed by the autoregressive Transformer, enabling each segment to incorporate
241
      both visual and retrieved text information, along with the preceding sequences of such multimodal
242
      information. This results in a temporally and causally aware video representation, which is particu-
243   larly important for dense video captioning, where understanding the temporal span and boundaries
244   of events is critical. 3) Combined approach: Lastly, we employ a hybrid method that combines
245   both prefixing and embedding fusion. The action phrases serve as both natural language prefixes for
246   the text decoder and fused embeddings with the visual features. This strategy leverages the strengths
247   of both sources of information: it provides explicit action guidance for the decoder while enriching
248   the autoregressive Transformer with a temporally-aligned, multimodal context. This combination
249   enhances the model’s ability to generate accurate and temporally aligned captions by maintaining
250   awareness of immediate actions and the evolving context throughout the video stream.
251
      Mixed training. We propose a mixed training strategy that alternates between action retrieval-
252   augmented and standard (non-augmented) training. By randomly alternating between these modes
253   during training, the model is better prepared for varied inference settings, ensuring consistent per-
254   formance even without retrieval support.
255
256   Frame construction for video segment representation. To better capture the visual content
257   within each video segment, we propose a frame sampling strategy that tiles multiple frames (n2 )
258
      into an n × n spatial grid. This approach better aligns with CLIP’s pretraining on static images,
      enabling it to capture inter-frame relationships without video-specific adaptations. Empirically, it
259
      outperforms independently processing and merging frames with temporal positional embeddings of
260
      shape L × D added for each segment.

262   3.3   S IMULATED V IDEO P RETRAINING VIA I MAGE S TITCHING
263
264   In addition, we explore an image-based pretraining method for dense video captioning to address the
265   challenge of limited access to densely captioned video datasets. Unlike Vid2Seq (Yang et al., 2023),
266
      which uses large-scale video data with ASR-generated pseudo captions, we investigate whether we
      can benefit from image-based pretraining by simulating video sequences. We utilize the LAION-2B
267
      image-text paired dataset without relying on costly video datasets.
268
269   We simulate video sequences by stitching together multiple images, repeating each image for sev-
      eral frames. To create smoother transitions between images, we blend the pixels at the boundaries


                                                       5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      and apply random augmentations to each frame, adding variability and avoiding overly monotonous
271   sequences. Each stitched sequence is paired with its corresponding image captions, with the tem-
272   poral span of each image defined by the stitching process. This setup replicates the dense video
273   captioning task, reproducing the annotation format for temporally localized captions as described
274   in section 3.1: {( [s1 ][e1 ][caption text1 ] ), ..., ( [sn ] [en ] [caption textn ] )}. By pretraining on
275   these synthetic video sequences, our model learns to generate temporally localized captions from
276   stitched image sequences. This process follows the same autoregressive framework for online dense
277   video captioning (section 3.1), providing a warm start for the autoregressive Transformer and token
278   reduction Transformer, which are newly added for dense video captioning.

281
282   Datasets. We evaluate our model on three widely-used dense video captioning datasets:
283   ViTT (Huang et al., 2020), YouCook2 (Zhou et al., 2018a), and ActivityNet Captions (Heilbron
284   et al., 2015). ViTT features instructional videos averaging 4.7 minutes in length with about 7 events
285   per video, making it ideal for testing both caption generation and temporal alignment. YouCook2,
286   consists of cooking videos averaging 5.3 minutes and 7.8 events per video, and ActivityNet Cap-
287   tions includes human activity videos with an average length of 2 minutes and 3.7 events per video.
288
      Evaluation metrics. We use standard dense video captioning metrics for evaluation. SODA (Fu-
289
      jita et al., 2020) provides a comprehensive assessment of temporal alignment and caption accuracy
290   across all event captions in a video. CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015) is averaged over IoU thresholds
291   (0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9) to measure caption relevance and alignment with ground truth events. We also re-
292   port METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005) for caption quality, averaged over the same IoU thresholds,
293   and the F1 score for temporal localization accuracy based on IoU with ground truth.
294
295
      Model architecture and training. Our model consists of approximately 500M parameters, in-
      cluding 303M for the vision encoder (ViT-Large) and 128M for the text decoder, both initialized
296
      from the CLIP model pretrained on the publicly available LAION-2B dataset (Schuhmann et al.,
297
      2021). The text decoder is further pretrained for 0.2 epochs on the same LAION-2B dataset, focus-
298   ing on the image captioning task (see section 3.1). For dense video captioning, we add the token
299   reduction Transformer and autoregressive Transformer, each with 8 layers and 32M parameters. Un-
300   like prior approaches that rely on large-scale video-text datasets for pretraining (Yang et al., 2023;
301   Zhou et al., 2024) or specially curated datasets (Wu et al., 2024), our model only leverages image-
302   text data for pretraining. To simulate video sequences, we sample 3 to 5 images from the LAION-2B
303   dataset, repeating each image multiple times to form a 16-frame sequence. This simulated video
304   data is then used to pretrain the entire dense video captioning model for 100,000 steps with batch
305   size 32. This allows us to explore the effectiveness of image-based pretraining in scenarios where
306
      video-text paired datasets are limited or unavailable. During dense video captioning finetuning, the
      full model is trained for 20,000 steps with batch size 8, taking about 12 hours on 16 devices. The
307
      CLIP-initialized ViT encoder remains frozen throughout the pretraining and fine-tuning stages. At
308
      inference, we employ beam search to generate 6 candidate captions with a temperature of 1. Tem-
309   poral non-maximum suppression (NMS) is applied to filter out intervals with a temporal IoU greater
310   than 0.7, and the remaining intervals with their corresponding captions are used for evaluation.
311
312   Video segment processing. We divide each video into 16 segments (S = 16) and process them
313   incrementally, simulating an online scenario where segments become available over time. Each
314   segment consists of multiple frames. For our main comparison with state-of-the-art methods, we
315
      use a total of 144 frames per video, with 9 frames per segment (L = 9). Each frame is resized
      to 176×176 pixels, and the frames are then combined into a 3×3 grid, resulting in a 528×528
316
      composite image per segment. For the ablation studies, we use a simpler setup, with 16 frames per
317
      video and 1 frame per segment (L = 1), resizing each frame to 256×256 pixels. The token reduction
318   Transformer samples N = 64 tokens from each segment.

322   We present our baseline model for online dense video captioning and compare it with a global,
323   offline counterpart. Although our approach shares the basic principle of online processing with the
      recent method (Zhou et al., 2024), we independently establish our own baseline, as their model

      weights are not publicly available. Both methods utilize CLIP pretrained models, particularly the
325   CLIP vision encoder ViT-Large, but differ in infrastructure and model design. Zhou et al. (2024)
326   use a larger 256M parameter T5-Base model (Raffel et al., 2020) as the text decoder, pretrained
327   on Web text corpora. In contrast, we explore a smaller 128M parameter CLIP text model, further
328   trained on the LAION-2B dataset for image captioning. Despite its smaller size, our experiments
329   show that it remains highly effective. Their method also introduces additional features, such as
330   a recursive feedback loop, which we omit as it did not provide gains in our setup, along with a
331   token clustering-based memory module and an online decoding algorithm, which we also did not
332   use. Instead, as described in section 3.1, our model processes videos by segments, using an inter-
333
      segment autoregressive Transformer followed by a factorized per-segment text decoder, resulting in
      a distinct yet effective online model.

      global method (Yang et al., 2023). The key difference between the two models lies in how the text

457   These results demonstrate the effectiveness of our online action-augmented dense video captioning
458
      approach. Our dynamic action retrieval and integration provide a significant advantage in tasks re-
      quiring fine-grained temporal localization and action description, while avoiding the need for access
459
      to the entire video as required by global methods. Unlike previous methods that rely on large-scale
460
      video-text datasets like YT-Temporal-1B (Zellers et al., 2022) for pretraining (Yang et al., 2023;
461   Zhou et al., 2024) or specifically curated HowTo100M video-pretraining datasets (Wu et al., 2024),
462   our model leverages only image-text data for pretraining. We employ an online retrieval-augmented
463   strategy combined with image-based pretraining, delivering strong performance without the need
464   for extensive video-text pairs. Notably, our retrieval of concise action phrases proves more effective
465   than using full captions, as it avoids redundancy and is more readily available.
466
      While additional performance gains could come from incorporating Automatic Speech Recognition
467   (ASR) (Yang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021a), we intentionally avoid it. ASR often overlaps with
468   ground truth captions and is closely tied to action occurrences, which can potentially inflate per-
469   formance metrics without accurately reflecting the model’s visual understanding. Although some
470   earlier models use ASR to make the task easier, more recent methods tend to avoid it.

      5      C ONCLUSION
478
479   In this work, we introduced Actions Inspire Every Moment (AIEM), a novel approach for online
480   dense video captioning that enhances caption quality by dynamically retrieving and integrating rel-
481   evant action phrases at each time step. Our method leverages an autoregressive model to align
482   retrieval with the video’s temporal progression, enabling more precise and contextually appropriate
483   captions. Additionally, our image-based simulated video pretraining further improves performance.
484   Experiments on the ViTT, YouCook2, and ActivityNet benchmarks demonstrate that AIEM signifi-
485
      cantly improves both caption quality and temporal localization, outperforming state-of-the-art global
      and online methods, establishing it as a leading approach in the field.

      E THICS STATEMENT AND L IMITATIONS
487
488   The task of dense video captioning inherently involves a level of subjectivity, which can lead to
489   ambiguous boundaries and mismatches between model outputs and the ground truth data used for
490   evaluation. This may result in discrepancies between the model’s performance and its evaluation
491   through standard metrics. To construct our action text corpus, we utilize a publicly available lan-
492   guage model for retrieval augmentation. While these models perform well on benchmarks, they
493   may carry biases, stereotypes, or inaccuracies from the data they were trained on. Since our corpus
494   is generated and filtered automatically, there may be instances where undesirable content persists.
495
      While we made efforts to mitigate these issues by enforcing strict formatting in the model’s out-
      put, it remains essential to re-assess these models before applying them for specific purposes. Our
496
      model is designed for research purposes with the primary goal of evaluating its performance relative
497
      to state-of-the-art methods. While it introduces new capabilities that could inspire further positive
498   advancements in research, the model is not intended for commercial or non-research applications.

      As described in section 3.3, we apply image-based simulated video pretraining on the entire model,
703   including the newly added modules. To simulate video sequences, we sample 3 to 5 images from
704   the LAION-2B dataset, repeating each image multiple times to form a 16-frame sequence. To create
705   smoother transitions, we blend pixels at the boundaries by applying a weighted sum of two images,
706   using a randomly selected blending ratio α ∈ [0.1, 0.9], e.g., blending pixels of images A and B
707   as αA + (1 − α)B. We apply random augmentations to each frame to avoid overly monotonous
708   sequences. This pretraining follows the same segment-by-segment autoregressive framework for

      A.3   P ROMPTING FOR ACTION TEXT CORPUS CONSTRUCTION
727
      We construct a corpus of action phrases to serve as contextual priors during the dense video caption-
728
      ing process. These phrases are designed to capture key actions or events in each video segment and
729   identify relevant objects. To build this corpus, we draw from two main sources: 1) Captions from
730   the training splits of dense video captioning datasets: ViTT, YouCook2, and ActivityNet, where we
731   use only the text captions excluding the video frames. 2) A broader, less domain-specific corpus
732   from the HowTo100M dataset (Miech et al., 2019), again using only the subtitle text without the
733   video content.
734
      Unlike previous methods that use raw video captions, which tend to be lengthy and unfocused (Xu
735   et al., 2024), we summarize these captions into concise action phrases using the publicly available
736   language model, Gemma (Team et al. (2024), huggingface.co/google/gemma-2-27b).
737
738
      To improve the extraction process, we refine the prompt to ensure concise and consistently formatted
      action phrases. Specifically, we emphasize singular nouns, avoid numerical terms, and enforce a
739
      strict format of <action verb(ing)> <target object (if any)>. For example, our prompt is: Your goal
740
      is to summarize the input sentence using as few words as possible. Focus on the words describing
741   actions or events. Use singular nouns, avoid articles and numeric terms. Respond in the format of
742   <action verb (ing)> <target object (if any)>. Input: {raw caption}. Answer:
743
744
      For HowTo100M subtitles, which are often longer, we adjust the prompt to focus on extracting a
      single main action or event: The input is video subtitle text. Choose the main action or event in the
745
      video and summarize it using as few words as possible. Focus on the words describing actions or
746
      events. Use singular nouns, avoid articles and numeric terms. Respond in the format of <action
747   verb(ing)> <target object (if any)>. Input: {video subtitles}. Answer:
748
749   These prompts effectively generate concise action phrases. After processing all text in each source,
750
      we deduplicate phrases by merging those with the same set of words, regardless of word order. After
      filtering out some least frequent phrases, we obtain 30,000 action phrases for each corpus.
