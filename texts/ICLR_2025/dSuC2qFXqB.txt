009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Estimating the frequency of items on the high-volume, fast data stream has been
012            extensively studied in many areas, such as database and network measurement.
013            Traditional sketch algorithms only allow to give very rough estimates with limited
014            memory cost, whereas some learning-augmented algorithms have been proposed
015            recently, their offline framework requires actual frequencies that are challenging
016
               to access in general for training, and speed is too slow for real-time processing,
               despite the still coarse-grained accuracy. To this end, we propose a more practical
017
               learning-based estimation framework namely UCL-sketch, by following the line
018
               of equation-based sketch to estimate per-key frequencies. In a nutshell, there are
019            two key techniques: online training via equivalent learning without ground truth,
020            and highly scalable architecture with logical estimation buckets. We implemented
021            experiments on both real-world and synthetic datasets. The results demonstrate
022            that our method greatly outperforms existing state-of-the-art sketches regarding
023            per-key accuracy and distribution, while preserving resource efficiency. Our code
024            is attached in the supplementary material, and will be made publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      The frequency or volume estimation of unending data streams is a concern in many domains, starting
029
      with telecommunications but spreading to social networks, finance, and website engine. In network
030
      fields, for example, professionals want to keep track of the activity frequency to identify overall net-
031   work health and potential anomalies or changes in behavior, which, however, is often challenging
032   because the amount of information may be too large to store in an embedded device or to keep con-
033   veniently in fast storage (Cormode, 2017). As a consequence, sketch, which is a set of counters or
034   bitmaps associated with hash functions, and a set of simple operations that record approximate infor-
035   mation (Yang et al., 2018b), has grown in popularity in the context of high-velocity data streams and
036   limited computational resources. Such an approximate algorithm is much faster and more efficient,
037   yet this comes at the expense of unsatisfactory accuracy and cover proportion, especially when fac-
038   ing unbalanced stream characteristics, such as a Zipf or Power-law distribution (Kumar et al., 2004;
039
      Roy et al., 2016).
040   On the other hand, recent years have witnessed the integration of deep learning technology with nu-
041   merous classic algorithms: index (Kraska et al., 2018), bloom filter (Mitzenmacher, 2018; Rae et al.,
042   2019), caching (Lykouris & Vassilvitskii, 2021), graph optimization (Khalil et al., 2017; Feng et al.,
043   2023) and so on. In particular, the research about learning-augmented streaming algorithms (Hsu
044   et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2020; Du et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023; Aamand et al.,
045
      2024) is receiving significant attention due to the powerful potential of machine learning (ML) to
      relieve or eliminate the binding of data characteristics and the sketch design. Their typical workflow
046
      involves training a heavy hitter oracle, which receives a key and returns a prediction of whether it
047
      will be heavy or not, then inserts the most frequent keys into unique buckets and applies a sketch to
048   the remaining keys. Although filtering heavy items has been proven to improve the overall sketch
049   performance on heavy-tailed distribution (Roy et al., 2016; Hsu et al., 2019), these offline and su-
050   pervised methods could hardly work in real-world applications. First, an unavoidable difficulty in
051   designing algorithms in the learned sketch model is that ground truth like actual frequencies or labels
052   for which key is large are not known in advance (Jiang et al., 2020). Moreover, since their models
053   are only fitted on the past data, the prediction performance of the oracle tends to deteriorate rapidly
      over time. That is to say, the neural network must be retrained with new labeled datasets frequently,

082
083   Consequently, one cannot help but pose the following intriguing question: Can we design a learning-
084   based sketch (without ground truth) via the linear system by training and recovering on the fly?
085   The comparison between the question-oriented approach in this work and existing learned sketches
086   is shown in Fig. 1. Without slowing down the conventional sketch insertions, our approach contin-
087   uously trains the ML model to recover per-key frequencies using only sketch counters. Although
088   promising, the process raises the following two challenges: (1) The first is self-supervision. Unsu-
089   pervised learning, without access to real frequencies or labels, is crucial for overcoming the imprac-
090   ticalities associated with existing learning-based frequency estimation algorithms. (2) The second
091   is scalability or complexity. Many modern streaming scenarios have evolved into complex systems
092   featuring tens of thousands of distinct items, and the entire key space invariably includes some un-
093
      certain keys that will be observed in the future. To decrease the complexity for per-key prediction,
      models dealing with such data need to be highly scalable as the size of streams grows infinitely.
094
095   To address these challenges, we introduce a new frequency estimation framework called UCL-
096   sketch (Unsupervised Compressive Learning Sketch), which aims to integrate the advantages of
097   both equation-based and learned sketching approaches. This framework achieves a significant im-
098   provement in practical feasibility and accuracy compared to learning-augmented algorithms, while
099
      maintaining substantially lower query overhead than equation-based competitors. A notable distinc-
      tion from prior works is that our model is entirely ground-truth free, relying solely on downsampled
100
      frequencies for online training. This property endows UCL-sketch with great flexibility and the
101
      capability for quick response to streaming distribution drift. To realize these benefits, we theo-
102   retically and empirically demonstrate that the recovery function can be learned from compressed
103   measurements alone using an equivalent learning scheme, given per-key aggregations and keys set.
104   Additionally, to mitigate the impact of large-scale and unbounded streams, we adopt the concept
105   of logical buckets to split and jointly learn multiple bucket-associated mappings with shared pa-
106   rameters, leading to an efficient and expandable architecture. Experimental results demonstrate the
107   potential of our proposed algorithm through detailed evaluations of the frequency estimation prob-
      lem. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

134   We follow prior studies that uncover the linear compression nature of equation-based sketch frame-
135   work (Fu et al., 2020; Sheng et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2021). Typically, a sketching algorithm

138   that inverses queried pairs from key-aggregations based on the same set of hash functions. Theo-

169   2.3     M OTIVATION
170   Limitation of Baseline Solution. There have been many implementations and extensions of
171   equation-based sketch. Among these sketching solutions, the PR-sketch (Sheng et al., 2021) and
172   SeqSketch (Huang et al., 2021) represent the most recent examples. These methods involve key
173   tracking mechanisms to collect distinct keys in the stream and apply optimization techniques, such
174   as Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (OMP), to solve a linear system. However, the computational cost
175   of streaming problems grows significantly with the number of keys, making iterative process of
176
      “decode” algorithms less feasible for modern streams. As a result, they have remarkably increased
      per-key accuracy as well as computation time and peak memory consumption at query time.
177
178   Impact of a Learned Equation Solver. One idea here to eliminate the need for iterative optimiza-
179   tion is a learned equation solver that directly maps measurement y to frequencies x. Clearly, the deep
180   solver requires extra training cost (but in parallel with stream processing), however, in return, it can
181   greatly reduce the query processing time through one-shot prediction, while inheriting impressive
182
      performance of baseline solutions, just as they have done in the field of CS.
183   Challenges of Supporting Learning-based Solution. Building upon the above discussions, our
184   primary goal is to develop equation-based sketches that exploit learning a solver to automate the
185   process of per-key recovery. However, there are two key challenges: (1) Ensure online training
186   without any ground truth, since it is very difficult to collect the true frequencies of all possible
187   keys in real time for training; (2) Alignment with large-scale data stream under limited parameters
188
      overhead as the solver’s complexity increases monotonically over time. In what follows, we give a
      detailed description of the proposed self-supervised learning framework for per-key recovery.

285
      real-world data (Aamand et al., 2024). At their simplest, zipfian models are based on the assumption
286   of a simple proportionality relationship: f (kj ) ∝ 1j , where f (kj ) is the frequency of j-th key kj in
287   keys set sorted by volume as shown in Fig. 5. Furthermore, we posit an additional assumption of
288   “temporal smoothness” in the heavy-tailed prior, observing that frequencies within adjacent tempo-
289   ral sample points tend to express similar zipfian behavior: a few frequent items exhibit approximate
290   proportional growth, while other infrequent items remain their original size.

      The intuition for designing such a lifelong network is to incrementally adapt to new items while
336   retaining acquired frequencies of previous items, so parameter sharing is a good and natural choice.
337
338   Network Expansion. A most naive way to design the network for a sequence of items would be
339
      retraining the output layer(s) every time a new item emerges. However, such retraining would incur
      significant costs for a deep neural network. Instead, we suggest dividing keys set into small buck-
340
      ets, where each bucket is maintained with its own parameters, thereby reducing the extra expanding
341
      overhead. Given the information collected about the observed stream, this design transforms the
342   problem into a maximum-a-posteriori (MAP) estimate of the bucket-associated frequencies (Dalt
343   et al., 2022). Unfortunately, it is still very intensive in terms of memory usage since the network’s
344   size scales with the observed keys, under high-speed streams where the computational cost is a sig-
345   nificant concern. Note that the statistical properties of sketch-based estimation should be stationary
346   over buckets, as it implies that the similar underlying transformation (random hash) can be applied at
347   each key (or bucket) inserted in the sketch. Thus, a better solution in such a case is sharing parame-
348   ters across these buckets. Specifically, we train a solver to model the mapping (y, i) → xi , in which
349   i denotes the index of our selected bucket and xi ⊂ x is predicted frequencies in the logical bucket.
350
      Borrowing ideas from literature in diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020), we learn
      the bucket-shared network using the sinusoidal position embedding (Vaswani et al., 2017). After
351
      this bucket sharing and logification, the network needs to update its weights by repeatedly forward
352
      propagation since the split changes the overall structure. Fortunately, in practice, this operation can
353   be performed for all logical buckets in parallel. Fig. 6 illustrates our dynamically scalable network
354   architecture and shows what happens when the set is partitioned into independent buckets.
355
356
      Query Operation. When querying an item k, we initially locate its index in the keys set such that
      we can determine the bucket id and relative position of k. Then we query the hash table in the data
357
      plane to obtain its filtered frequency, and return 0 if the key is not in it. The partial result and sampled
358
      sketch counters will be reported to the server together. With the previously acquired position, we
359   predict the remaining frequency of k by inputting counters and bucket id into the learned solver. The
360   final estimated frequency is a sum of the two parts. The process is depicted in Algorithm 2.

      Classic Sketch. A sketch is a compact structure and solution that takes limited space to support
715
      approximate frequency queries over high-speed data streams. Classic sketch algorithms (Cormode
716   & Muthukrishnan, 2005b; Charikar et al., 2002; Estan & Varghese, 2002; Roy et al., 2016; Liu
717   et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2018a; Liu et al., 2019) include Count-Min Sketch (CM-sketch), Count
718   Sketch (C-sketch), Conservative Update Sketch (CU-sketch), Augmented Sketch (A-sketch), and
719   so on. They adopt a common underlying structure which is essentially a w × d array of counters
720   for preserving key frequencies. Each of the d rows of the array is associated with a hash function
721   for mapping items to w counters, then they consider the counts of d different buckets array (e.g.
722   minimum for CM-sketch (Cormode & Muthukrishnan, 2005b), the median for C-sketch (Charikar
723   et al., 2002)) to which the data is mapped as the estimation. The CU-sketch (Estan & Varghese,
724
      2002) changed the insertion of the CM-sketch, which only updates the value of the minimum bucket
      in each insertion process. The A-sketch (Roy et al., 2016) added a filter to the CM-sketch and
725
      exchanged data between the filter and the sketch to ensure that hot keys are retained in the filter
726
      to reduce hash conflicts. Since classic sketches have been proven to deliver high accuracy only
727   with impractical memory consumption, these algorithms are subject to an undesirable compromise
728   between estimation accuracy and memory efficiency.
729
730
      Equation-based Sketch. Recent works have made progress in mitigating the trade-off by designing
      advanced query methods of sketch algorithms. Lee et al. (2005) and Lu et al. (2008) first disclose
731
      that sketch and compressive sensing are thematically related. The locality-sensitive sketch (LSS) (Fu
732
      et al., 2020) leverages the relationship between the sketch with the compressed projection, then ex-
733   tends it to a K-means clustering method. The PR-sketch (Sheng et al., 2021) recovers keys of stream-
734   ing data by establishing linear equations. The SeqSketch (Huang et al., 2021) and HistSketch (He
735   et al., 2023) store a few high-frequency items, then employ a compressed-sensing approach to de-
736   code infrequent keys. By solving the linear system, these equation-based sketches compensate for
737   the error introduced by counter sharing, and recovers the complete keys in the shared part with much
738   higher accuracy than the classical sketch. Unfortunately, such global sketches have been shown to
739   suffer from greatly increased time and memory costs of estimation.
740   Learning-based Sketch. In the last few years, machine learning has taken the world by storm than
741   ever before, which also motivates the design of learning-based frequency estimation algorithms.
742   Yang et al. (2018b) pioneered the idea of employing machine learning to reduce the dependence of
743   the accuracy of sketches on network traffic characteristics. TalentSketch (Yan et al., 2022) applies
744   a long short-term memory (LSTM) model to network measurement tasks. And most recently Cao
745   et al. (2024) introduced meta-learning into sketch design, which can be robust to different local dis-
746   tributions, with performance scarcely affected by shifts in item-frequency correspondences. In (Hsu
747
      et al., 2019), the authors first proposed a learned frequency estimation framework by using a trained
      classifier (or oracle) to store hot and cold items separately. The overall design is similar to A-
748
      sketch, but the latter one uses a data exchange structure. Jiang et al. (2020) adjusted the learning-
749
      augmented sketch, which uses a regression model to directly outputs the predicted frequency of hot
750   keys rather than inserting them in unique buckets. Then a series of works have also studied theoret-
751   ical analyses and optimizations under this framework (Du et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2023; Aamand
752   et al., 2024). However, these hand-derived methods are excessively dependent on offline models
753   and cannot handle dynamic data distribution. Differently, UCL-sketch provides a new paradigm for
754   learning-enhanced sketch design. It obtains accuracy close to original equation-based sketches while
755   maintaining the efficient query execution time by continuously adapting models without ground
      truth.

783   Compressive Sensing. Given the linear measurements, Compressive Sensing (CS) methods usually
784   reconstruct the original signal by solving an (generally convex) optimization problem, which is
785   similar to ours. Many traditional works incorporate additional prior knowledge about transform
786   coefficients (Candes et al., 2006; He & Carin, 2009; Kim et al., 2010; Zhao et al., 2016) into the CS
787   reconstruction framework. Also note that the network-based CS reconstruction has been adopted in
788   many magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and super-resolution (SR) algorithms (Gregor & LeCun,
789
      2010; Zhang & Ghanem, 2018; Pang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023). However,
      UCL-sketch is the first to apply the idea to large-scale stream frequency estimation and specifically
790
      address the data sketching problem.

796   Algorithm 3 outlines the procedure of inserting a key-value pair (k, v). We first compute its hash
797   position in HF, then as Fig. 11 shows, there are overall three cases:
798   Case 1: The slot is empty or the existing entry has the same key. We insert the item into the position
799   or just increment the new count by its value.
800
      Case 2: The current position does not have the same key and (new count - old count) ≤ 0 after
801
      incrementing the old count by the item’s value. We replace the existing entry with the new item and
802
      evict the old entry into the sketch. Then we transfer the exchanged key with the “hot” flag to the
803   control plane, and update the BF.
804
805   Case 3: The current position does not have the same key and (new count - old count) > 0 after
806
      incrementing the old count by the item’s value. We insert the item into the sketch. After inserting
      the BF, only if it is identified as a new key, UCL-sketch sends the key to the control plane.
807
808   Given a heavy-tailed distribution of stream data, Case 1 constitutes a large portion while Case 2
809   represents the opposite. Thus after filtering by the hash table, UCL-sketch is memory efficient as
      the number of exchanges is usually very small (Roy et al., 2016).

       tion bucket of length l, the size of the NN input measurements is d × w. The measurement vector is
1131   first transformed to the shape (d, w). After projection by a linear block, each containing two fully
1132
