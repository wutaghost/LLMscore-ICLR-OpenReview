011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Tapping into the uncharted multimodal representation learning in online handwrit-
014            ing verification (OHV), we propose SPECTRUM, a temporal-frequency synergistic
015            model tailored to enhance handwriting representations. SPECTRUM comprises
016
               three core components: (1) a multi-scale interactor that interweaves fine-grained
               temporal and frequency features across multiple scales through complementary
017
               domain interaction; (2) a self-gated fusion module, dynamically integrating global
018
               temporal and frequency features via self-driven balancing. Collectively, these two
019            components achieve micro-to-macro multimodal integration; (3) a multimodal
020            distance-based verifier that fully harnesses temporal and frequency representations,
021            sharpening genuine-forged discrimination beyond conventional temporal-only ap-
022            proaches. Extensive experiments demonstrate SPECTRUM’s pronounced outper-
023            formance over existing OHV methods. Furthermore, we reveal that incorporating
024            multiple handwritten biometrics fundamentally improves the discriminatory power
025            of individual writing features. These findings not only validate the efficacy of
026            multimodal learning in OHV but also encourage broader multimodal research
027
               across both feature and biometric domains, potentially opening new avenues for
               future explorations. Code will be publicly available.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Evolving from quill and ink to the digital age, handwriting verification has long been a fundamental
      technique for identity authentication, playing crucial roles in diverse applications such as banking
034
      and legal proceedings. Generally, handwriting verification can be categorized into online and offline
035
      methods Diaz et al. (2019). Online verification utilizes dynamic data produced in the writing process
036   such as speed and pressure for verification. In contrast, the offline counterpart analyzes digitized
037   handwritten images obtained by scanning or photographing. This paper focuses on online handwriting
038   verification (OHV). While signatures Tolosana et al. (2021); Lai et al. (2022) have traditionally
039   dominated this field, recent research has expanded to include more handwritten biometrics such as
040   isolated digits Tolosana et al. (2020a;b) or consecutive digit strings Zhang et al. (2022), broadening
041   the realm of OHV with enhanced utility and versatility.
042   The development of a robust OHV system hinges on extracting powerful feature representations to
043   capture the unique writing patterns of diverse individuals. This pursuit has driven considerable effort
044   in enhancing the representation learning skills, such as improvements on local feature extraction
045   Kamel et al. (2008); Jiang et al. (2022) or incorporation of global spatial attention Lai et al. (2022).
046   Concurrently, the burgeoning field of multimodal learning has demonstrated impressive results across
047   various domains, such as image-text alignment Radford et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2023), vision-
048   frequency interaction Qian et al. (2020); Rao et al. (2023), and omni-modality coverage Girdhar et al.
049   (2023); Han et al. (2024). The remarkable success of multimodal learning raises a natural question:
050
      Could OHV similarly benefit from multimodal representation learning? If so, How?
051   However, the OHV community embraces predominantly single-modal learning paradigms, particu-
052   larly focusing on temporal representation learning Lai & Jin (2019); Tolosana et al. (2021); Lai et al.
053   (2022); Jiang et al. (2022). In signal processing, frequency is an intrinsically connected modality to
      the time domain, often derived from the temporal sequence through techniques like Fourier transform.

068   The potency of frequency modeling has been verified in other forgery verification fields, such as face
069   forgery detection Qian et al. (2020); Miao et al. (2023) and speaker verification Liu et al. (2022; 2024).
070   Similarly, in online handwriting analysis, frequency features provide a unique discriminatory perspec-
071   tive, capturing crucial writing traits like rhythms and periodicities that aid in distinguishing genuine
072   from forged samples. As shown in Fig. 1, the spectrograms extracted by short-time Fourier transform
073   (STFT) reveal significant discrepancies between genuine and forged samples in the frequency domain.
074   Despite the potential of frequency modeling, its exploration in OHV has been confined to being used
075   as a sole, superficial feature extraction stage without further modeling Nakanishi et al. (2006); Nanni
076
      & Lumini (2008), resulting in limited feature representation capabilities. In addition, prior studies
      suffer from modality isolation, myopically focusing on either the temporal or frequency domains
077
      but overlooking the potential integration of both. A multimodal approach that synergizes temporal
078
      and frequency features would unlock more discriminative handwriting representations, intuitively
079   suggesting a compelling direction for developing better OHV systems.
080
081   Motivated by this insight, we propose SPECTRUM, a SPECtral-TempoRal Unified Model that
082
      integrates temporal and frequency modalities for multimodal online handwriting verification. First,
      we devise two components to achieve micro-to-macro multimodal integration (M4 I) across temporal
083
      and frequency domains. (1) Micro integration. We propose a multi-scale interactor to facilitate fine-
084
      grained interaction between temporal and frequency features. Handwriting sequences are split into
085   even and odd sub-sequences for independent temporal and frequency analyses. We utilize a projection
086   layer to preserve the temporal features, while formulating the frequency modeling by combining
087   a 1D (inverse) Fourier transform with learnable complex weights of scale l to emphasize salient
088   frequency features Rao et al. (2023). The two sub-sequences are then recombined to enable mixed-
089   domain interaction. By varying scale l, we develop the multi-scale interactor to aggregate multi-scale
090   contexts for scale-reciprocal complementations. (2) Macro integration. We introduce a self-gated
091   fusion module that dynamically weights the contributions of global temporal and frequency features,
092   attempting for self-optimized feature fusion. Collectively, these two modules accomplish temporal
093
      and frequency integration in a micro-to-macro manner, ensuring comprehensive multimodal interplay.
      Second, we propose a multimodal distance-based verifier (MDV), which combines Dynamic Time
094
      Warping distance computed with temporal features and Euclidean distance with frequency features to
095
      enhance discrimination between genuine and forged samples. It naturally harnesses representations of
096   both modalities under a unified multimodal framework, transcending the reliance on merely temporal
097   features in prior works and resulting in better verification accuracy.
098
099
      We evaluate the proposed SPECTRUM using three online handwriting datasets: MSDS-ChS Zhang
      et al. (2022) (Chinese Signature), MSDS-TDS Zhang et al. (2022) (Token Digit String (TDS)),
100
      and DeepSignDB Tolosana et al. (2021) (Latin Signature). Experiments demonstrate a pronounced
101
      outperformance of our model over state-of-the-art OHV methods that solely depend on temporal
102   representation learning, evidencing the effectiveness of the M4 I mechanism and MDV in incorporating
103   frequency features for multimodal learning. In addition, we investigate multimodal fusion between
104   multiple biometric modalities, where the Chinese signature and TDS are combined to enrich individual
105   writing representations. This approach yields further improvements in verification performance,
106   suggesting that multimodal learning can be extended across not only feature domains (temporal
107   and frequency) but also biometric domains (Chinese signature and TDS), potentially opening new
      avenues for further research in this field. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:

      Online Handwriting Verification Techniques. Online handwriting verification (OHV) has seen
122   substantial progress in recent decades, primarily focusing on online signature verification Diaz
123   et al. (2019) due to its pervasive usage. This technique typically constitutes two stages: feature
124   representation and decision making. (1) Feature representation. The evolution from traditional hand-
125   crafted extraction methods Sharma & Sundaram (2017); Tang et al. (2018); Farimani & Jahan (2018);
126   Okawa (2021) to modern deep learning methods has established new state-of-the-art performance.
127   Current deep learning approaches broadly operate in two paradigms. The first type concentrates on
128   local feature modeling, often developed in conjunction with Dynamic Time Warping (DTW). PSN
129   Wu et al. (2019b) and TA-RNNs Tolosana et al. (2021) pre-align handwriting sequences using DTW
130
      before inputting them to CNN/RNN-based models. DeepDTW Wu et al. (2019a) uses a DTW on top
      of a Siamese CNN to enhance local invariance learning. RAN Lai & Jin (2019) proposes a length-
131
      normalized path signature descriptor to describe local signature trajectories. DsDTW Jiang et al.
132
      (2022) integrates the differentiable soft-DTW into the loss function to improve local discriminative
133   learning. The second paradigm captures global representations. Park et al. Park et al. (2019) utilize
134   an LSTM-CNN network to analyze features at both stroke and signature levels. Li et al. Li et al.
135   (2019) progressively model the stroke features and the holistic signature with RNN. Sig2Vec Lai
136   et al. (2022) proposes a selective pooling module to pool the temporal sequence, converging subspace
137   features into a fixed-length vector with global context awareness. (2) Decision making. Typically
138   configured in an open-set manner, OHV systems are trained on limited data but tested on unlimited
139   unseen data. This requires models to generate feature vectors to assess similarities between templates
140   and queries, thereby verifying queries’ authenticity. Common approaches include Euclidean/DTW
141
      distance-based verifiers that authenticate queries falling within specific thresholds Lai & Jin (2019);
      Lai et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2022), subject-independent classifiers evaluating sample-wise distances
142
      Wu et al. (2019a;b), and sigmoid scoring based on pre-given thresholds Tolosana et al. (2021).
143
144   Recently, the OHV field has expanded beyond signatures to encompass emerging handwritten
145   biometrics like digit/digit strings. Tolosana et al. propose the e-BioDigit Tolosana et al. (2020a)
146   and MobileTouchDB Tolosana et al. (2020b) datasets for second-level identity authentication using
147
      separate digits. Zhang et al. Zhang et al. (2022) propose the MSDS dataset, including the MSDS-ChS
      and MSDS-TDS subsets. This work demonstrates that mainstream signature verification methods can
148
      be seamlessly transferred to other handwritten biometrics, such as Token Digit String (TDS).
149
150   Frequency Learning for Online Handwriting Verification. While contemporary methods primarily
151   rely on the temporal domain for handwriting analysis, earlier research has explored frequency analysis
152   for handwriting characterization due to the straightforward transformation from temporal to frequency
153
      domain. The Wavelet transform Nakanishi et al. (2006); Nanni & Lumini (2008); Fahmy (2010);
      Chavan et al. (2017); Yang & Liu (2017); Miaba et al. (2018); Alpar (2018) and Fourier transform
154
      hua Quan et al. (2006); Yanikoglu & Kholmatov (2009); Chavan et al. (2017); Miaba et al. (2018)
155
      are mostly adopted, while additional frequency features such as Discrete Cosine/Hartley/Walsh-
156   Hadamard/Kreke/Mellin transform Nanni & Lumini (2008); Chavan et al. (2017); Fallah et al. (2011)
157   are also explored. Despite these efforts, frequency learning for OHV has been shackled by two
158   critical drawbacks. (1) Limited feature extraction. Most studies rely solely on frequency transforms
159   for feature extraction without further modeling, usually yielding insufficiently discriminative features.
160   (2) Modality isolation. Prior methods rely exclusively on the frequency modality but overlook the
161   potential synergy with temporal modeling, which is an oversight that also persists in current cutting-
      edge temporal-centric approaches. To address these issues, we propose SPECTRUM, a multimodal

      Dataset. We assess SPECTRUM on three OHV datasets, including the two subsets of the MSDS
      dataset Zhang et al. (2022), i.e., MSDS-ChS (Chinese signature), MSDS-TDS (Token Digit String),
334
      and DeepSignDB Tolosana et al. (2021) (Latin signature). These datasets are the currently largest
335
      public datasets for their respective handwriting type. We split the data to ensure that testing user data
336   is entirely unseen during training, adhering to the open-set setting. MSDS-ChS and MSDS-TDS share
337   the same 402 writers. As per Zhang et al. (2022), we divide the first 202 individuals as the training
338   set while assigning the rest 200 users as the testing set. This results in 8,080/8000 training/testing
339   samples from 202/200 users for each dataset. We use the two-session (across-session) data of each
340   dataset by default. DeepSignDB consists of five subsets, in which, however, the Biosecure DS2
341   subset Ortega-Garcia et al. (2010) currently releases only training data but not testing data. To ensure
342   fair comparisons, we follow Lai et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2022) and utilize the same subsets as them
343   during training and testing, where the official "development" and "evaluation" sets of all subsets are
344
      utilized as training/testing data, respectively. This results in 21,104/20,596 training/testing samples
      from 528/512 users. We perform data preprocessing on the online handwritten data and extract 15
345
      time-function features as input to the models, as detailed in Appendix A.
346
347   Metric. We adopt Equal Error Rate (EER) as the evaluation metric, which refers to the point where
348   False Acceptance Rate equals False Rejection Rate. The proposed MDV is employed to compute
349   EER%, with details provided in Sec. 3.2. Following the original papers of MSDS and DeepSignDB,
350
      we report EERs under both a global threshold and a local (user-specific) threshold and display the
      results in the format of EERg /EERl on MSDS-ChS and MSDS-TDS, while reporting only EERs
351
      under the global threshold on DeepSignDB. All results are reported in percentage.
352
353   Impostor types. We consider both skilled and random forgeries as impostor types. Skilled forgeries
354   are selected from the skillfully forged samples of each user that are originally provided in the datasets,
355   while random forgeries are selected from the genuine samples of other users.
356   Template selection. The number of genuine handwriting templates used during verification essentially
357   affects model performance. For MSDS-ChS and MSDS-TDS, we follow the original paper and use
358   one to four templates against one query in skilled forgery verification, denoted as 4 vs 1, 3 vs 1, 2 vs
359   1, and 1 vs 1; while using four and one templates in random forgery verification. For DeepSignDB,
360   we follow the original paper to employ four and one templates in verification for both skilled forgery
361   and random forgery scenarios. To guarantee the reproducibility of test results, we consistently take
362
      the first n samples among all genuine samples of the user as templates.

510   5   L IMITATION AND D ISCUSSION
511
512   Although SPECTRUM achieves optimal or SOTA-comparable performances on three datasets, the
513   performance enhancement on Chinese/Latin signatures is less pronounced than on Token Digit String
514   (TDS). This calls for further efforts to improve the generalizability of temporal-frequency multimodal
515   learning on diverse handwritten data types. Additionally, our exploration of multimodal learning has
516   hitherto been confined to temporal and frequency domains. However, it is possible to investigate
517
      other modalities such as the spatial modality (rendering online data to offline images) and the video
      modality (capturing hand movements during writing), as well as the integration of more than two
518
      feature modalities, to further enhance the robustness of handwriting verification.
519
520   Furthermore, the successful integration of multiple handwritten biometrics points out another simple
521   yet effective avenue to improve OHV performance, with potential benefits for real-world applications
522   such as banking. Despite its straightforwardness, this approach remains unexplored, and available
523
      datasets are scarce. This underscores the need for further exploration in this area, such as using a
      broader range of handwritten biometrics beyond just signature and TDS, collecting more comprehen-
524
      sive multi-biometric datasets, developing specialized techniques for more effective biometric merging,
525
      and integrating handwritten biometrics with other behavioral biometrics (e.g., face, fingerprint).
526
527
528   6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose SPECTRUM, a novel OHV model driven by multimodal representation
531
      learning. We propose a multi-scale interactor for blending local temporal and frequency features
      across multiple spatial scales, coupled with a self-gated fusion module that integrates global temporal
532
      and frequency features through a self-balance. In addition, a multimodal distance-based verifier is
533
      proposed, which naturally harnesses both temporal and frequency representations in the multimodal
534   context to sharpen the distinction between genuine and forged samples. Extensive experiments
535   demonstrate the superior performance of SPECTRUM over existing OHV methods, underscoring
536   its effectiveness in multimodal representation learning. Furthermore, we discover that combining
537   multiple handwritten biometrics essentially results in more discriminatory individual representations
538   and facilitates verification. These findings not only confirm the significance of multimodal represen-
539   tation learning in OHV but also highlight promising future directions in enhancing the reliability and
      applicability of OHV technologies.

      We train SPECTRUM for 40 epochs, using AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2019) with β1 = 0.9,

      β2 = 0.999, and weight decay of 1e-2 as the optimizer. The learning rate is initially set to 5e-4 and

761   from four writers, comprising five genuine samples, five skilled forgeries, and five random forgeries
762   per writer, resulting in a batch size of 4 × (5 + 5 × 2) = 60. Genuine samples and skilled forgeries
763   are drawn from the genuine and skillfully forged data available in the dataset, while random forgeries
764   are randomly selected genuine handwriting of five other writers. λ in the loss function is set to 0.1.

      To more intuitively demonstrate the effectiveness of the temporal-frequency synergistic learning
769   of SPECTRUM, we visualize the output feature sequence based on single-modal and multimodal
770   learning. Features are extracted from the same handwriting samples for comparison. We utilized
771   the final output features of the Baseline model (as described in Sec. 4.3) for visualization in the
772   temporal domain, while using the output features of the proposed SPECTRUM for visualization in
773   the temporal-frequency domain. Visualizations are presented in Fig. 5, which are performed on the
774   Chinese signature data of MSDS-ChS and Token Digit String data of MSDS-TDS, respectively.
775   Comparing the left and right columns of each data type, the heatmaps on the right column showcase
776   richer and denser regions with high response values, particularly evident in the Token Digit String
777   data. This suggests that incorporating frequency features with temporal features strengthens the
778   sensitivity of individual writing patterns, resulting in more informative handwriting representations
779   and improved verification accuracy. In addition, as seen in the right-column heatmaps, the high-
780   response regions are concentrated in areas such as stroke twirls, stroke hyphenations, and the start/end
781   of strokes. These regions likely contain richer writing style characteristics, which are effectively
782   captured by the frequency modeling approach. By highlighting these stylistically rich areas, our
783
      model demonstrates its ability to focus on crucial elements that distinguish individual writing patterns,
      further validating the strength of our multimodal approach.
