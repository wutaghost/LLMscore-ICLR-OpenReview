011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The ubiquity and value of tables as semi-structured data across various domains
014            necessitate advanced methods for understanding their complexity and vast amounts
015
               of information. Despite the impressive capabilities of large language models
               (LLMs) in advancing the natural language understanding frontier, their application
016
               to large-scale tabular data presents significant challenges, specifically regarding
017
               table size and complex intricate relationships. Existing works have shown promise
018            with small-scale tables but often flounder when tasked with the complex reasoning
019            required by larger, interconnected tables found in real-world scenarios. To address
020            this gap, we introduce "Tree-of-Table", a novel approach designed to enhance
021            LLMs’ reasoning capabilities over large and complex tables. Our method employs
022            Table Condensation and Decomposition to distill and reorganize relevant data into
023            a manageable format, followed by the construction of a hierarchical Table-Tree
024            that facilitates tree-structured reasoning. Through a meticulous Table-Tree Exe-
025            cution process, we systematically unravel the tree-structured reasoning chain to
026
               derive the solutions. Experiments across diverse datasets, including WikiTQ, Table-
               Fact, FeTaQA, and BIRD, demonstrate that Tree-of-Table sets a new benchmark
027
               with superior performance, showcasing remarkable efficiency and generalization
028
               capabilities in large-scale table reasoning.

089   To address these issues, two main approaches have generally been adopted, as shown in Figure 1. The
090   first involves using only the schema information of tables and employing program-aided methods,

095

      methodological innovations in this area.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
119
120   Traditional Table Understanding. At the core, traditional methods have focused on generating
121   executable languages like SQL (Rajkumar et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2021; Eisenschlos et al., 2020;
122   Jiang et al., 2022) to interact with tables. This approach stems from the need to reason over both
123
      free-form natural language questions and (semi-)structured tables. While effective in accessing tabular
      data, these methods often fall short in capturing the nuanced semantics within a table, particularly
124
      struggling with web tables that feature free-form text in cells.
125
126   Prompting Language Models for Table Understanding. A novel stride in table understanding has
127   been the application of prompting strategies (Wei et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023; Imani
128   et al., 2023; Khot et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). By generating reasoning steps through in-context
129
      learning, models like Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022) and its evolutions (Yao et al., 2023a) break
      down questions into sub-problems, iteratively solving each to improve comprehension of complex
130
      tasks. These methods showcase LLMs’ prowess in handling intricate reasoning chains, albeit not being
131
      explicitly designed for tabular data. Emerging approaches have sought to extend LLM capabilities
132   beyond text, incorporating external tools to solve reasoning tasks (Cheng et al., 2022; Hsieh et al.,
133   2023; Dhingra et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2023). Generating Python or SQL programs (Cheng et al., 2022;
134   Nahid & Rafiei, 2024; Shi et al., 2020; Pönighaus, 1995) and executing them with interpreters or APIs
135   has shown promise in enhancing arithmetic and table-based reasoning. However, the performance
136   of these program-aided methods sometimes falters in complex table scenarios due to the static
137   nature of tables in the reasoning process. Dater (Ye et al., 2023) dynamically modifies the tabular
138   context to aid in solving table-based tasks, albeit primarily focusing on data pre-processing with
139   limited operations. Subsequently, the Chain-of-Table (Wang et al., 2024) method is inspired by the
140
      chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022) principle and performs implicit sub-table extraction. Contrarily,
      our proposed Tree-of-Table approach is inspired by the tree-of-thought principle (Yao et al., 2023a),
141
      creating adaptive tree-based reasoning chains that exploit the planning capabilities of LLMs for more
142
      nuanced and context-specific table reasoning.
143
144   Table Understanding Datasets. Datasets like WikiTQ (Pasupat & Liang, 2015) and TableFact (Chen
145   et al., 2019) have been instrumental in developing table understanding methods. These standard
146
      benchmarks provide a foundation but are often limited in size and complexity. BIRD (Li et al.,
      2024) represents a significant leap forward in the field, being one of the largest and most intricate
147
      datasets designed for table understanding to date. Spanning across 37 professional domains with a
148
      substantial size of 33.4 GB, BIRD offers over 12,000 examples gleaned from real-world databases.
149   Its development involved modifying open-source relational databases and curating additional ones,
150   all complemented by crowdsourced natural language questions and corresponding SQL queries.

      3.2     OVERVIEW
217
218   In this work, we introduce a novel approach named "Tree-of-Table" devised to address the challenge
219   of table reasoning within large-scale table understanding datasets (e.g., BIRD) and real-world
220   applications, as shown in Figure 2 and Figure 3. Our methodology encompasses several steps
221   designed to simplify and enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs when confronted with large,
222   interconnected tables. First, we condense the tables based on the specific requirements of the query.
223   This process identifies relevant portions of the tables, thereby reducing the cognitive load on LLMs.
224
      We then apply a tree-based decomposition strategy to segment large tables into smaller, manageable
      units, guided by the relationships among tables, such as foreign keys, and the structure of the query.
225
      Next, we construct a "Table-Tree" by reorganizing the condensed information into a hierarchical
226
      structure. Each node in this tree represents a logical block of information or a step in the reasoning
227   process, mirroring the cognitive approach of breaking down complex problems into simpler sub-
228   problems. Finally, we perform a sequential traversal of the constructed Table-Tree to derive answers
229   to the queries. This systematic traversal ensures logical progression through each node, allowing
230   for the synthesis of information and insights gained from previous steps. The iterative nature of this
231   reasoning process culminates in a well-informed conclusion.
232
233   3.3     TABLE C ONDENSATION AND D ECOMPOSITION
234
235   Addressing the significant challenges posed by large-scale relational tables to LLMs requires a
236
      nuanced understanding of the specific difficulties in question. These challenges primarily stem from
      two aspects: (1) The intricate foreign key relationships among multiple tables, which are commonly
237
      defined using SQL syntax, may not be readily interpretable by LLMs due to their complexity and
238
      the specialized knowledge required to understand relational database schemas. (2) The sheer size
239   of the tables often exceeds the input context limit of LLMs, making it impossible for these models
240   to process the entirety of the data directly. To effectively address these issues, our methodology
241   incorporates two key processes: Table Condensation and Tree-based Decomposition.
242
243   3.3.1    TABLE C ONDENSATION
244
245   As shown in the upper part of Figure 2, our initial step involves condensing the tables based on the
246
      context of the question Q and any additional evidence provided. Since there are possibly multiple
      tables, this process employs LLMs to identify one sub-table relevant to Q from them through schema-
247
      linking (Lei et al., 2020). Following the identification of relevant schemas/headers, we merge these
248
      multiple tables to reduce redundancy and decrease their size,
249
250                             subTableQ = f (Schema_Link(Q, {H}, K)|θ),                               (3)
251   where {H} indicates the headers of the tables. This condensation aims to recall one sub-table
252   pertinent to Q and eliminate superfluous table information, thereby enhancing the information density
253   related to Q within the tables and making it more manageable for LLM processing. Through a detailed
254   analysis of the BIRD dataset, we observed that over 70% of questions involved tables whose length
255   exceeded the input limitations of current LLMs. Furthermore, more than 90% of these questions
256   pertained to at least two tables, with 20% involving four or more tables, significantly complicating
257   the understanding process for LLMs. Post-condensation, we found that the length of tables involved
258   in more than 60% of long questions was reduced below the LLM input limit, and all questions were
259
      associated with a singular, condensed table, as shown in the upper part in Figure 2.
260
      3.3.2    T REE -BASED D ECOMPOSITION

262   Even with reduced size and complexity post-condensation, the tables might still be too lengthy or
263   intricate for LLMs to handle efficiently, occasionally still surpassing the models’ input limits. To
264   mitigate this, we begin by breaking down the question Q into its most general components, delineating
265   the entire problem-solving process into several independent yet sequentially connected steps.

      3.5   TABLE -T REE E XECUTION
352
      After constructing the Table-Tree, we view it as a proxy task for the entire table understanding
353
      procedure. As shown in Figure 3, by traversing and executing operations across this tree, LLMs can
354   implicitly generate tables and save intermediary results, thus enabling a seamless reasoning process.
355   This stage diverges from the construction phase by utilizing a depth-first search approach to execute
356   the thought chain, ensuring a systematic and comprehensive exploration of the tree structure.
357
      Depth-First Search Execution. The rationale behind adopting a depth-first search (DFS) (Tarjan,
358
      1972) strategy for Tree Execution lies in its ability to fully explore and resolve each branch of the tree
359
      to completion before moving to the next. This method aligns with the logical progression of solving a
360   complex problem by focusing on and completing one aspect of the problem entirely, ensuring that all
361   necessary computations and logical deductions related to a branch are performed before considering
362   alternative or subsequent branches.
363
      Leveraging Tree Structure for Efficiency. A key advantage of the tree structure is its inherent ability
364
      to enhance reasoning efficiency by logically organizing and compartmentalizing different aspects
365
      of the problem-solving process into subtrees. To exploit this benefit to its fullest, we execute the
366   reasoning process subtree by subtree, based on the root node’s children. After processing a subtree,
367   we store its result before proceeding. This approach contrasts with linearly merging all subtrees into
368   a single chain for execution. By maintaining the distinction between subtrees and executing them as
369   separate units, we significantly mitigate the risk of intermediary tables becoming excessively large
370   and unwieldy, which in turn, would thwart the reasoning process.

      In this paper, we address the profound challenge of advancing table understanding with LLMs,
520
      specifically in the domain of large and complex tabular datasets. Our innovative approach, Tree-
521
      of-Table, integrates table condensation and decomposition with a hierarchical reasoning construct
522   that aligns with human cognitive processes to tackle intricate problem-solving tasks. Our extensive
523   experiments conduct across various datasets, including WikiTQ, TableFact, FeTaQA, and BIRD,
524   demonstrate that Tree-of-Table not only achieves state-of-the-art performance but also presents
525   remarkable improvements in efficiency and generalizability.
526
      Limitations. While Tree-of-Table has shown exceptional results in enhancing the efficiency and
527
      effectiveness of large language models in processing extensive tabular data, the tree-based reason-
528   ing may need to require careful calibration to balance depth and breadth effectively—a task that
529   necessitates fine-tuning and may impose certain limitations on adaptability.

      A.3     T HEORETICAL A DVANTAGES OF HIERARCHICAL TREE - BASED ARCHITECTURE
824
825   Previous work has shown that tree-based structures exhibit significant advantages in LLM reasoning,
826   as demonstrated in (Yao et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2022). In addition, earlier works also demonstrate
827   from multiple perspectives that hierarchical tree-based architecture is superior to linear architecture,
828   including but not limited to the following points:
829
830           • Divide and Conquer Strategy (Bentley, 1975): Tree-based architectures leverage the divide-
831             and-conquer approach, which is a well-established algorithmic paradigm. This strategy
832             breaks a problem into smaller subproblems, solves each subproblem independently, and
833
                combines their results. This can lead to more efficient processing, especially in complex,
                large-scale problems.
834
835           • Scalability (Blumofe & Leiserson, 1999): Trees can handle larger and more complex
836             datasets more efficiently than linear structures. As data grows, the depth of a tree increases
837
                logarithmically rather than linearly, allowing for more scalable processing.
838           • Improved Decision Making (Loh, 2011): In decision-making processes, tree structures can
839             better model decision paths and outcomes, providing clearer insights into the reasoning
840             behind decisions.
841           • Cognitive Alignment (Newell, 1972; Newell et al., 1959; Miller, 1956): Human reasoning
842             often aligns more closely with hierarchical structures, which can make tree-based models
843             more intuitive and easier to interpret.
844
845   A.4     R EPHRASE THE PROCESS OF CHAIN - OF - TABLE
846
      Overall, Chain-of-Table processes table understanding based on the Chain-of-Thought LLM reasoning
847
      approach, which employs an intuitive linear thought chain to decompose the problem. At each step
848
      of decomposition, it selects operations from a predefined operations pool and generates intermediate
849   results for table processing. However, when dealing with complex, multi-branch logic, this linear
850   approach can produce lengthy and disorganized thought processes, making table reasoning chaotic
851   and prone to errors. Therefore, this linear approach is generally suited for relatively simple problems
852   and smaller tables.
853
      In contrast, our proposed Tree-of-Table method is divided into three main parts: (1) Table Condensa-
854
      tion (Sec. 3.3): Given the input table, which contains many large-scale relevant tables connected via
855   foreign keys, we first employ LLMs to condense the table. This helps recall the sub-tables pertinent
856   to the query and eliminate extraneous information. (2) Table-Tree Construction (Sec. 3.4): Following
857   the preprocessing steps, we instruct the LLM to dynamically generate child nodes in the tree. Each
858   generation of child nodes relies solely on their multi-level parent nodes, without needing reference to
859   uncle nodes. We then iteratively construct child nodes level by level until we reach the leaf nodes at
860   the bottom of the tree. (3) Table-Tree Execution (Sec. 3.5): Finally, after constructing the Table-Tree,
861   we consider it a proxy task for the entire table understanding process. The traversal and execution of
862   operations across this tree enable a seamless reasoning process.
863   In summary, the differences between the two methods are as illustrated in Sec. 3.6.
