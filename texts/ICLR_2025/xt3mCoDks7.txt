009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Structure-based molecule optimization (SBMO) aims to optimize molecules with
012            both continuous coordinates and discrete types against protein targets. A promising
013            direction is to exert gradient guidance on generative models given its remarkable
014            success in images, but it is challenging to guide discrete data and risks inconsisten-
015            cies between modalities. To this end, we leverage a continuous and differentiable
016            space derived through Bayesian inference, presenting Molecule Joint Optimization
017            (MolJO), the gradient-based SBMO framework that facilitates joint guidance sig-
018
               nals across different modalities while preserving SE(3)-equivariance. We introduce
               a novel backward correction strategy that optimizes within a sliding window of
019
               the past histories, allowing for a seamless trade-off between explore-and-exploit
020
               during optimization. Our proposed MolJO achieves state-of-the-art performance
021            on CrossDocked2020 benchmark (Success Rate 51.3%, Vina Dock -9.05 and SA
022            0.78), more than 4× improvement in Success Rate compared to the gradient-based
023            counterpart, and 2× “Me-Better” Ratio as much as 3D baselines. Furthermore, we
024            extend MolJO to a wide range of optimization settings, including multi-objective
025            optimization and challenging tasks in drug design such as R-group optimization
026            and scaffold hopping, further underscoring its versatility and potential.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Structure-based drug design (SBDD) plays a critical role in drug discovery by identifying three-
032   dimensional (3D) molecules that are favorable against protein targets (Isert et al., 2023). While
033
      recent SBDD focuses on the initial identification of potential drug candidates, these compounds must
      undergo a series of further modifications for optimized properties, a process that is both complex and
034
      time-consuming (Hughes et al., 2011). Therefore, structure-based molecule optimization (SBMO) has
035
      garnered increasing interest in real-world drug design (Zhou et al., 2024), emphasizing the practical
036   need of optimizing 3D molecules to meet specific therapeutic criteria.
037
038   Concretely, SBMO can be viewed as a more advanced task within the broader scope of general SBDD,
039
      requiring precise control over molecular properties while navigating the chemical space. Specifically,
      SBMO addresses two key aspects: (1) SBMO prioritizes targeted molecular property enhancement
040
      according to expert-specified objectives, whereas generative models for SBDD primarily focus on
041
      maximizing data likelihood without special emphasis on property improvement (Luo et al., 2021; Peng
042   et al., 2022). Therefore these models can only produce outputs similar to their training data, limiting
043   the ability to improve molecular properties. (2) SBMO is capable of optimizing existing compounds
044   with 3D structural awareness, addressing a critical gap left by previous molecule optimization
045   methods with 1D SMILES or 2D graph representations (Bilodeau et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2022), and
046   allowing for a more nuanced control. The focus on structure makes SBMO particularly suited for key
047   design tasks, such as R-group optimization and scaffold hopping.
048   A pioneering work for SBMO is DecompOpt (Zhou et al., 2024), which designs a special 3D
049   generative model conditioned on fragments, and employs a gradient-free sampling method within
050   such decomposed fragment space through iterative oracle calls. One of the drawbacks is that it relies
051   on oracle functions that can be computationally expensive especially in large-scale SBMO tasks,
052   where running multiple rounds of costly simulations is impractical or even infeasible. Moreover,
053   DecompOpt’s contribution lies in its novel generative paradigm based on fragment conditions, rather
      than exploring optimization within existing models, making it less adaptable to other frameworks.

077   One generalized solution is gradient guidance, addressing these issues by eliminating the need for
078   expensive oracle simulations while being flexible enough to be incorporated into strong-performing
079   generative models in a plug-and-play fashion, as demonstrated in a wide range of challenging
080
      real-world applications including image synthesis (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Epstein et al., 2023).
081   However, current gradient-based methods have not fully realized their potential in SBMO, for
082   they have historically suffered from the continuous-discrete challenges: (1) it is non-trivial to
083   guide discrete variable within probabilistic generative process. More specifically, standard gradient
084   guidance is designed for continuous variables that follow Gaussian distributions, making them
085   not directly applicable to molecular data that involve discrete atom types. Methods attempting to
086
      adapt gradient guidance to discrete data often resort to approximating these variables as continuous,
      either by adding Gaussian noise (Bao et al., 2022) or by assuming that classifiers follow a Gaussian
087
      distribution (Vignac et al., 2023). Unfortunately, these approximations can lead to suboptimal results,
088
      as they do not accurately reflect the discrete nature (Kong et al., 2023). (2) Gradient guidance might
089   introduce inconsistencies between modalities. For instance, TAGMol (Dorna et al., 2024) formalizes
090   guidance exclusively over continuous coordinates, resulting in a disconnect between the discrete
091   and continuous modalities. This may explain why TAGMol struggles to optimize overall molecular
092   properties as shown in Figure 1, despite its improvement in Vina affinities. By solely guiding the
093   continuous coordinates, TAGMol enhances spatial protein-ligand interactions but fails to optimize
094   e.g. synthesizeability, which depends more on molecular topology, especially discrete atom types.
095   In this paper, we address the multi-modality challenge for gradient guidance by leveraging a continu-
096   ous and differential space, representing an aggregation of noisy samples from the data space derived
097   through Bayesian inference (Graves et al., 2023). We design MolJO (Molecule Joint Optimization), a
098   general framework that enables gradient-based optimization of continuous and discrete variables in a
099   structured manner. We introduce a novel sampling strategy called backward correction, enhancing
100   the alignment of gradients over different steps. By maintaining a sliding window of past history for
101   optimization, the backward correction strategy enforces explicit dependency on the past, effectively
102   alleviating the issue of inconsistencies. Moreover, it balances the exploration of molecular space with
103
      the exploitation of better-aligned guidance signals, offering a flexible trade-off.
104   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
105
106           • We propose MolJO, the joint gradient-based method for SBMO that establishes the guidance
107             over molecules, offering better controllability and effectively integrating gradient guidance
                for continuous-discrete variables within a unified framework.

117   2    R ELATED W ORK
118
119   Pocket-Aware Molecule Generation. Pocket-aware generative models aim to learn a conditional
120   distribution over the protein-ligand complex data. Initial approaches adopt 1D SMILES or 2D graph
121
      representation (Bjerrum & Threlfall, 2017; Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018), and recent research has
      shifted its focus towards 3D molecule generation in order to better capture the interatomic interactions.
122
      Early atom-based autoregressive models (Luo et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022) enforce
123
      an atom ordering to generate molecules atom-by-atom. Fragment-based methods (Powers et al.,
124   2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023) alleviate the issue of ordering by decomposing molecules
125   into motifs instead of atom-level generation, but they risk more severe error accumulation and thus
126   generally require post-processing or multi-stage treatment. Non-autoregressive methods based on
127   diffusions (Schneuing et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2022; 2023) and BFNs (Qu et al., 2024) target
128   full-atom generation for enhanced performance and efficiency. However, the needs of optimizing
129   certain properties and modifying existing compounds are not adequately addressed in the scope of
130   previous methods, limiting their usefulness in drug design.
131
132   Gradient-Based Molecule Optimization. Inspired by classifier guidance for diffusions (Dhariwal
133   & Nichol, 2021), pioneering approaches are committed to adapting the guidance method to handle the
134   complicated molecular geometries in the setting of pocket-unaware generation. EEGSDE (Bao et al.,
135
      2022) derives an equivariant framework for continuous diffusion, and MUDM (Han et al., 2023)
      further explores time-independent property functions for guidance. As they enforce a continuous
136
      diffusion process for discrete variables, these methods are not applicable in advanced molecular
137
      modeling (Guan et al., 2022; 2023) where discrete data are processed by a discrete diffusion, for it is
138   unnatural to apply progressive Gaussian noise that drives Categorical data away from the simplex.
139   DiGress (Vignac et al., 2023) proposes classifier guidance for discrete diffusion of molecular graphs,
140   yet it additionally assumes that the probability of classifier follows a Gaussian, which is ungrounded
141   and often a problematic approximation. Based on the continuous-discrete diffusion for SBDD,
142   TAGMol (Dorna et al., 2024) retains the guidance only for continuous coordinates, because there
143   lacks a proper way to propagate the gradient over discrete types. The discrete part is affected only
144   implicitly and belatedly in the generative process, and such imbalanced guidance would probably
145   result in suboptimal performance for lack of joint optimization.

179   4     M ETHOD
180
181   In this section, we introduce MolJO that guides the distribution over θ, utilizing aggregated infor-
182
      mation from previous latents. Though different from guided diffusions that operate on noisy latent
      y, this guidance aligns with our generative process conditioned on θ. By focusing on θ, we can
183
      effectively steer the clean samples towards desirable direction, ensuring a smooth gradient flow.
184
185
      Notation. Following Kong et al. (2024) and denoting the guided distribution π as product of experts
186   (Hinton, 2002) modulated by energy function E that predicts certain property, we have π(θi |θi−1 ) ∝
187   pϕ (θi |θi−1 )pE (θi ), where Φ is the pretrained network for BFN, pE (θi ) = exp [−E(θi , ti )] is the
188   unnormalized Boltzmann distribution corresponding to the time-dependent energy function.
189
190   Overview. As illustrated in Figure 1, we introduce MolJO as follows: in Sec. 4.1, we propose the
191   concept of gradient guidance over the multi-modality molecule space, derive the form of guided
192   transition kernel π(θi |θi−1 ) via first-order Taylor expansion, and explain the underlying manipula-
193   tions of distributions the guidance corresponds to. In Sec. 4.2, we present a generalized advanced
194   sampling strategy termed backward correction for pϕ , which allows for a flexible trade-off between
195
      explore-and-exploit by maintaining a sliding window of past histories. We empirically demonstrate
      our strategy helps optimize consistency across steps, ultimately improving the overall performance.

331
332   Task. For a molecule m ∈ M where M denotes the set of molecules, there are oracles ai (m) :
333   M → R for property i, each with a desired threshold δi ∈ R. MolJO is capable of different levels
334   of controllability: (1) unconstrained optimization, where we identify a set of molecules such that
335
      {m ∈ M | ai (m) ≥ δi , ∀i}, i.e. the goal is to optimize a number of objectives. (2) constrained
      optimization, where we aim to find a set of molecules that contain specific substructures s such that
336
      {m ∈ M | ai (m) ≥ δi , s ⊂ m, ∀i}.
337
338
      Dataset. Following previous SBDD works (Luo et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2022),
339   we utilize CrossDocked2020 (Francoeur et al., 2020) to train and test our model, and adopt the same
340   processing that filters out poses with RMSD > 1Å and clusters proteins based on 30% sequence
341   identity, yielding 100,000 training poses and 100 test proteins.
342
343   Baselines. We divide all baselines into the following: (1) Generative models (Gen.), including AR
344   (Luo et al., 2021), GraphBP (Liu et al., 2022), Pocket2Mol (Peng et al., 2022), FLAG (Zhang et al.,
345   2023), DiffSBDD (Schneuing et al., 2022), TargetDiff (Guan et al., 2022), DecompDiff (Guan et al.,
346   2023), IPDiff (Huang et al., 2024) and MolCRAFT (Qu et al., 2024), (2) Oracle-based optimization
347   (Oracle Opt.) that rely on docking simulation in each round, such as AutoGrow4 (Spiegel & Durrant,
348   2020), RGA (Fu et al., 2022), and DecompOpt (Zhou et al., 2024), (3) Gradient-guided (Grad. Opt.)
349   TAGMol (Dorna et al., 2024). Detailed descriptions of baselines are left in Appendix F.
350
351   Metrics. We employ the commonly used metrics as follows: (1) Affinity metrics calculated by
352
      Autodock Vina (Eberhardt et al., 2021), in which Vina Score calculates the raw energy of the given
      molecular pose residing in the pocket, Vina Min conducts a quick local energy minimization and
353
      scores the minimized pose, and Vina Dock performs a relatively longer search for optimal pose
354
      to calculate the lowest energy. Success Rate measures the percentage of generated molecules that
355   pass certain criteria (Vina Dock < -8.18, QED > 0.25, SA > 0.59) following Long et al. (2022) and
356   Guan et al. (2022). (2) Molecular properties, including drug-likeness (QED) and synthesizability
357   score (SA). (3) Metrics for sample distribution, such as diversity (Div). A more comprehensive set of
358   metrics are detailed in Appendix F.

531
532
      We present MolJO, the joint gradient-based SE(3)-equivariant framework to solve the structure-
533
      based molecule optimization problems, which only requires training energy functions as a proxy to
534   predict molecular property, instead of expensive oracle simulations. The general framework further
535   equips gradient-based optimization method with backward correction strategy, offering a flexible
536   trade-off between exploration and exploitation. Experiments show that MolJO is able to improve the
537   binding affinity of molecules by establishing more key interactions and enhance drug-likeness and
538   synthesizability, achieving state-of-the-art performance when benchmarked on CrossDocked2020
539   (Success Rate 51.3%, Vina Dock -9.05 and SA 0.78), together with 4× improvement compared to
      gradient-based counterpart and 2× “Me-Better” Ratio as much as other 3D baselines.

      A    OVERVIEW OF BAYESIAN F LOW N ETWORKS
757
758
759   In this section, we provide a further explanation of Bayesian Flow Networks (BFNs) that are designed
760   to model the generation of data through a process of message exchange between a “sender” and a
761   “receiver” (Graves et al., 2023). The fundamental elements include the sender distribution for the
762   sender, and input distribution, output distribution, receiver distribution for the receiver.
763   This process is framed within the context of Bayesian inference, where the sender distribution is
764   a factorized distribution pS (y|m, αt ) that introduces noise to each dimension of the data m and
765   sends it to the receiver, which observes the y and has access to the noisy channel with accuracy α
766   at timestep t, and compares it with its own receiver distribution pR (y|θ, p; t) based on its current
767   belief of the parameters θ, the timestep and any conditional input such as protein pocket p.
768   The generative process for the receiver begins with a prior distribution (referred to as input distribution
769                QN
      pI (m|θ) = d=1 pI (m(d) |θ(d) ) that is also factorized for N -dimensional data) that defines its initial
770   belief about the data. For continuous data, the prior can be chosen as a Gaussian (Song et al., 2024;
771   Qu et al., 2024), or other distribution such as von Mises distribution (Anonymous, 2024), while
772   for discrete data, the prior is modeled as a uniform categorical distribution. Then, the receiver
773   uses its belief θ with the help of neural network to model the inter-dependency among dimensions
774   and compute the output distribution pO (m̂|θ, p; t), which represents its estimate of the possible
775   reconstructions of original data m, and is used to construct the receiver distribution (Eq. 3).
776   The Bayesian update function in Eq. 1 defines how the prior belief θ0 is updated to the conjugate
777   posterior θt . Ideally, the update requires aggregating all possible noisy latents y from the sender
778   distribution pS , while during the actual generative process, there is only receiver distribution pR ,
779   leaving an exposure bias, and different approximations determine different forms of mapping f to the
780   posterior (Eq. 2), showcasing the flexibility in the design space of BFN.
781   Through the iterative communication between sender and receiver, the receiver progressively updates
782   its belief of the underlying parameters, and training is achieved by minimizing the divergence between
783   the sender and receiver distributions (Eq. 4). This is analogous to the way Bayesian inference works
784   in parameter estimation: as more noisy data y is observed, the receiver’s posterior belief about the
785   data m becomes increasingly accurate, which implies the reconstruction would be made easier.

      R-group optimization. Cases of lead optimization involve retaining the scaffold while redesigning

               initial seed molecule, AutoGrow4 iteratively conducts mutations and crossovers, then makes
993            oracle calls for docking feedback, and keeps the top-scoring molecules in the end.
994
995
             • RGA (Fu et al., 2022) is built on top of AutoGrow4, and utilizes a pocket-aware RL-trained
               policy to suppress its random walking behavior in traversing the molecular space.
996
997          • DecompOpt (Zhou et al., 2024) trains a conditional generative model on decomposed
998            fragments besides binding pocket, following the style of DecompDiff. The optimization is
999
               done by iteratively re-sampling in the 3D diffusion latent space given the top K arms ranked
               by oracle functions as updated fragment condition input.
1000
1001         • TAGMol (Dorna et al., 2024) exerts gradient-based property guidance over the pretrained
1002           TargetDiff backbone, and the gradient is enabled only in the continuous diffusion process
1003           for coordinates.
1004
1005   Metrics. Besides the common evaluation metrics such as binding affinities calculated by Autodock
1006
       Vina (Eberhardt et al., 2021) and QED, SA by RDKit, we elaborate other metrics as follows:
1007
             • Diversity measures the diversity of generated molecules for each binding site. Following
1008           SBDD convention (Luo et al., 2021), it is based on Tanimoto similarity over Morgan
1009           fingerprints, and averaged on 100 test proteins.
1010
             • Connected Ratio is the ratio of complete molecules among all generated molecules, i.e. with
1011
               only one connected component.
1012
1013         • Lipinski enumerates Lipinski rule of five (Lipinski et al., 1997) and checks how many
1014
               rules are satisfied. These rules are typically seen as empirical reference that helps to predict
               whether the molecule is likely to be orally bioavailable.
1015
1016         • Key Interaction, i.e. key non-covalent interactions formed between molecules and protein
1017           binding sites as an in-depth measure for binding modes, including π interactions, hydro-
1018           gen bonds (donor and acceptor), salt bridges and hydrophobic interactions calculated by
1019
               Schrödinger Glide (Halgren et al., 2004).
1020         • Strain Energy measures the internal energy of generated poses, serving as an indicator of
1021           pose quality as proposed by Harris et al. (2023).
1022         • Steric Clash calculates the number of clashes between generated ligand and protein surface,
1023           where clashing means the distance of ligand and protein atoms are within a certain threshold.
1024           This reveals the stability of complex to some extend, yet it does not strictly mean violation
1025           of physical constraint since the protein is not that rigid and might also go through spatial
               rearrangement upon binding, as noted by Harris et al. (2023).

1466   J   L IMITATION
1467
1468   Limitation of this work lies in that MolJO adopts no more than three objectives (Affinity, QED, SA)
1469   in the optimization process, while there are more objectives that are biologically meaningful such
1470   as ADMET. Future directions include extending the general framework to accommodate a wider
1471   range of objectives, and even beyond the scope of structure-based molecule optimization (SBMO),
1472   for MolJO is a general gradient-based optimation method for continuous and discrete variables, and
1473   can be tailored to a great many read-world applications.
