009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Temporal graph neural networks (TGNN) have achieved significant momentum in
012            many real-world dynamic graph tasks, making it urgent to study their robustness
013            against adversarial attacks in real-world scenarios. Existing TGNN adversarial
014            attacks assume that attackers have complete knowledge of the input graphs. How-
015            ever, this is unrealistic in real-world scenarios, where attackers can, at best, access
016
               information about existing nodes and edges but not future ones at the time of the
               attack. Nevertheless, applying effective attacks with only up-to-attack knowledge is
017
               particularly challenging due to the dynamic nature of TGNN input graphs. On the
018
               one hand, graph changes after the attacks may diminish the impact of attacks on the
019            affected nodes. On the other hand, targeting nodes that are unseen at the attack time
020            introduces significant challenges. To address these challenges, we introduce a novel
021            adversarial attack framework, MemFreezing, to yield long-lasting and spreading
022            adversarial attacks on TGNNs without the necessity to know knowledge about the
023            post-attack changes in the dynamic graphs. MemFreezing strategically introduces
024            fake nodes or edges to induce nodes’ memories into similar and stable states, which
025            we call the ‘frozen state.’ In this state, nodes show limited responses to graph
026            changes and are compromised in their ability to convey meaningful information,
027
               thereby disrupting predictions. In subsequent updates, these affected nodes main-
               tain and propagate their frozen state with support from their neighboring nodes.
028
               The experimental results demonstrate that MemFreezing can persistently decrease
029
               the TGNN models’ performances in various tasks, delivering more effective attacks
030            under practical setups.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Dynamic graphs are prevalent in real-world scenarios, spanning areas like social media (Kumar
036   et al., 2018), knowledge graphs (Leblay & Chekol, 2018), autonomous systems (Leskovec et al.,
037   2005), and traffic graphs (Pareja et al., 2020). Unlike static graphs, whose nodes and edges remain
038   constant, dynamic graphs evolve over time, introducing challenging tasks like link prediction and
039
      node classification on dynamically changing nodes and edges. Driven by successes of Graph Neural
      Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017; Veličković et al., 2017; Xu et al.,
040
      2018), Temporal Graph Neural Networks (TGNNs) have emerged as state-of-the-art solutions in
041
      many dynamic graph tasks (Trivedi et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Rossi et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
042   2023; You et al., 2022). As such, there is a pressing need to study their robustness towards adversarial
043   attacks, especially since such attacks have shown significant efficacy against traditional GNNs (Wang
044   et al., 2018; Tao et al., 2021; Zügner et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2020; Zang et al., 2020;
045   Bojchevski & Günnemann, 2019; Sun et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). By modifying the input graphs
046   with imperceptible and subtle perturbations, the adversarial attacks can inject noises into GNNs,
047   making the models yield incorrect or adversary-expected results. For instance, a social media such as
048   REDDIT Kumar et al. (2018) may employ TGNN to decide whether comments (as edges) from users
049   to posts (as nodes) should be banned based on his/her comment histories. With subtle adversarial
050
      attacks, malicious messages can easily bypass this checking functionality.
051   While several studies have explored the effectiveness of adversarial attacks on dynamic graphs (Lee
052   et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2022; 2023; Chen et al., 2021), they often assume that attackers have
053   complete knowledge of the input graphs at the time of the attack, which is impractical in many
      real-world scenarios. Specifically, as dynamic graphs evolve, by the time attackers observe the entire

      evolution (i.e., track all changing nodes and edges) and identify the optimal timestamps to inject
055   adversarial perturbations (e.g., adding fake nodes or edges), those key timestamps may have already
056   passed, making it impractical to inject noises timely. As the old saying goes, ‘It is easy to be wise
057   after the event.’ Therefore, in real-world cases, the adversarial may attack TGNN with only limited
058   knowledge up to the time of the attack. Nevertheless, studying TGNN adversarial attacks under
059   practical constraints is valuable, as real-world attacks with limited knowledge may exhibit unique
060   characteristics that uncover vulnerabilities in TGNNs, which are overlooked when focusing solely on
061   idealized scenarios.
062   However, attacking TGNNs with limited knowledge up to the attack time faces significant challenges
063   due to the evolving nature of dynamic graphs. First, the impact of adversarial noise can quickly decay
064   as the graph evolves and node information updates. Second, it is difficult to influence unseen nodes
065   or edges that appear after the attack, as their information is unknown. Thus, an effective strategy
066   must endure the graph’s evolution and affect both current and future nodes despite this uncertainty.
067   Interestingly, the node updating mechanism in Temporal Graph Neural Networks (TGNNs) offers
068   unique potentials for persisting and propagating adversarial noises in dynamic graphs. Generally,
069   TGNNs maintain and update node status vectors, often referred to as node memory by recent
070   studies (Rossi et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang & Mendis, 2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang &
071   Mendis, 2023), to capture temporal history, which is crucial for delivering accurate predictions in
072   dynamic graph tasks. Moreover, a node’s memory vector can potentially affect its neighbors. When
073   graph changes occur—such as the addition or deletion of nodes or edges—the memory vectors of
074   related nodes are updated based on their neighbors’ memories. This raises intriguing questions: Can
075
      TGNN predictions be disrupted by disabling their memories, and can this effect persist and spread
      through their memory updates?
076
077   To address this inquiry, we thoroughly investigated the memory update patterns of nodes within
078   TGNNs and made the following observations: (1) Although it is not possible to directly affect unseen
079   predictions, we can degrade TGNN prediction accuracy by pushing nodes—whether seen or un-
080   seen—into a relatively ‘frozen’ state, their memories remain stable and exhibit limited responsiveness
081
      to surrounding changes, reducing their ability to convey updated or meaningful information. (2)
      While a noisy node’s memory vector may struggle to maintain its noisy state over time on its own,
082
      this state can persist for much longer if its neighboring nodes have similar memories.
083
084   We introduce MemFreezing, a novel adversarial attack designed to study the vulnerabilities of TGNNs
085   under realistic constraints. At a specific attack timestamp, MemFreezing strategically selects groups
086   of victim nodes that mutually reinforce each other’s noisy states during updates, leveraging a heuristic
087
      we term ’cross-freezing’. By injecting carefully crafted fake messages, MemFreezing guides these
      nodes into a stable frozen state, where their memory updates exhibit high similarity over time. This
088
      stability reduces their responsiveness to graph changes and limits their ability to convey meaningful
089
      information, thereby misleading predictions. Additionally, we simulate future nodes to induce the
090   propagation of adversarial noise. We summarize our contributions as follows:
091
092   • We recognize the limitations of existing adversarial attacks on dynamic graph models and iden-
093     tify the challenges of persisting and propagating adversarial noise in real-world threat models,
094     particularly in Temporal Graph Neural Networks.
095   • We propose MemFreezing, an adversarial attack that disables node memories in TGNNs by pushing
096
        them into unnaturally stable states. To achieve this, we design a cross-freezing mechanism that
        induces nodes to be stable despite future updates and encourages affected nodes to propagate stable
097
        states by their simulating future neighbors.
098
      • We compare our method with prior GNN adversarial attacks on various dynamic graphs. Experi-
099     mental results show that MemFreezing effectively and persistently misleads TGNN predictions
100     across diverse datasets and models, outperforming state-of-the-art GNN attacks, even in the
101     presence of defenses.

186   3.1     T HREAT M ODEL
187
188   Limits in Prior Threat Models. Prior TGNN attacks (Lee et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2023; 2022;
189   Chen et al., 2021) assume that attackers have full knowledge of the target graphs and that these graphs
190   remain static after the attacks. However, this assumption is impractical in real-world scenarios, as
191   attackers cannot return to the optimal attack times after observing the entire evolution of a dynamic
192
      graph. In particular, when an attacker observes the evolution of a dynamic graph at tn and identifies
      optimal attack timestamps ta1 , ta2 , ..., tak ≤ tn , they would need to go back to these past timestamps
193
      to inject noise, which is infeasible in practice. For example, if attackers aim to target a TGNN on
194
      social media like Reddit in October and determine that the optimal timestamps to inject noise were
195   in September, they cannot go back a month to post adversarial comments or reviews that would
196   influence node states in the underlying TGNN model.
197
198
      Threat Model. Due to the limits discussed above, we assume that, for a practical and realistic TGNN
      adversarial attack, an attacker’s knowledge is limited to events up to the attack timestamp, and the
199
      graph continues to evolve afterward. In particular, we set up our attack model as follows.
200
201   • Attacker’s Goal: Given an evolving dynamic graph and a TGNN model, the attacker’s goal is to
202     misguide the TGNN predictions by introducing a limited amount of changes to the entire graph
203     (e.g., affecting a small number of total nodes limited by the attack budget.)
204   • Attacker’s Knowledge: Attackers can acquire knowledge only up to the attack’s timestamp, includ-
205     ing model details, presented inputs, and node memories, but not future changes in the dynamic
206     graph. Regarding acquiring previous inputs, platforms like Wikipedia, Reddit, Meta, or X maintain
207
        dynamic graphs that adversaries can reconstruct with reasonable accuracy using publicly accessible
        data. Regarding acquiring the model details, many TGNN architectures and pre-trained models are
208
        open-sourced, and adversaries can also use techniques like insider threats or model extraction Yao
209
        et al. (2024); Oliynyk et al. (2023) to obtain model parameters if not publicly available. However,
210     predicting future graph changes remains significantly harder. Therefore, we focus on the more
211     challenging constraint of using only past knowledge for attacks while assuming a white-box setup
212     for model parameters.
213   • Attacker’s Capability: Attackers can add fake events as adding nodes/edges at the attack time.
214     For example, while attacking TGNNs in social media, attackers can create fake user accounts as
215     fake nodes and make junk comments to the blogs as fake edges. These fake events can impact the
        resultant node memories and embeddings and further influence predictions in TGNNs.

      3.2           M OTIVATION
235
236   Issues of Existing Attacks. Due to limited knowledge up to the attack time, adversarial attacks on
237   TGNNs must contend with unknown future changes in dynamic graphs. However, the subsequent
238   changes after the attack may significantly limit the attack performances for two reasons: Firstly,
239   for seen and attacked targets, the noise that misleads their predictions becomes mixed with new
240   information from future changes (as described in equation 3 and equation 4), making it too weak to
241   mislead future predictions. Secondly, unseen nodes and edges added after the attack are difficult to
242   affect, as the attackers have no knowledge of these future elements and cannot generate effective noise
243   to mislead them. We illustrate examples of these issues in Figure 2. As details shown in Section 5
244
      (e.g., Figure 6 and Table 1), while existing GNN attacks (Wang et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2021; Li et al.,
      2022) effectively reduce the model’s accuracy immediately after the attack, they struggle to perturb
245
      predictions in the unknown future.
246
247
248   4         T HE M EM F REEZING ATTACK
249
250   We propose MemFreezing, an adversarial attack specifically tailored for TGNNs, to achieve our
251   goals. It consists of two key features: i) To create long-lasting adversarial effects, we disturb TGNN
252   predictions by freezing their node memories. Specifically, we induce nodes to mutually lock their
253   memories, keeping them stable during future updates. As a result, the victim nodes become less
254   responsive to surrounding changes, limiting their ability to provide critical information for predictions.
255   ii) To affect unseen nodes and edges, we simulate future neighbors for the victim nodes and encourage
256
      these victim nodes to update the memories of their simulated future neighbors into similar, stable
      states. As a result, the adversarial effects remain persistent through future changes and influence
257
      subsequent predictions, as illustrated in Figure 3.
258
259
260   4.1           F REEZING AND P ERSISTING N ODE M EMORY
261
      Memory Freezing Objective. Instead of focusing on maximizing prediction losses as prior adversar-
262
      ial attacks, which are limited by unknown and diverse future events, we propose to transform victim
263
      nodes’ memories into similar and stable states, which we refer to as Frozen State. In particular,
264   by keeping node memories similar and unchanged over time, nodes in TGNNs can hardly carry or
265   convey meaningful information, consequently disturbing predictions. To quantitatively investigate
266   the potential effectiveness of freezing node memories, we freeze the node memories in TGN (Rossi
267   et al., 2020) and JODIE (Kumar et al., 2019) by consistently forcing their node memories to all zero,
268   then evaluate their performances on edge prediction tasks on Wikipedia (Kumar et al., 2018) dataset.
269   As shown in Figure 4(a), this leads to significant accuracy drops over time, demonstrating the impact
      of freezing node memories. Thus, our attack objective is to freeze node memories into frozen states.

283   Challenges in Persisting Frozen Memories. However, keeping nodes’ memories in frozen states is
284   challenging. Specifically, unpredictable messages aggregated from neighbors can considerably change
285   a node’s memory during updates, as described in equation 3. An intuitive solution is to maximize the
286   contribution of a node’s self-memory su while minimizing the impact of incoming messages during
287
      its update. To explore the feasibility of this approach, we conducted a case study using TGN on the
      Wikipedia dataset. Specifically, we sample 100 victim nodes, manipulate each node’s memory to
288
      block messages during updates, and then assess whether they remained unchanged over subsequent
289
      timestamps. As highlighted in Figure 4(b), despite this effort, the cosine similarities between nodes’
290   memories before and after updating drop significantly over time. The primary challenge lies in the
291   unpredictability of future messages, making it nearly impossible to find a memory that completely
292   blocks new information from updates in the RNN or attention-based memory updating modules used
293   by TGNNs. Further experimental details and theoretical proofs are provided in Appendix A.1.
294
      Opportunities in Freezing.Although we can’t block messages from a node’s neighbors, we can
295
      introduce noise to those neighbors, making their messages help persist the noise. In particular,
296   we assume that nodes’ frozen memories in TGN can remain consistent if similar frozen neighbors
297   surround them. We verify the assumption using the same model and data as Figure 4(b). Specifically,
298   we first sample one-third of 100 victim nodes as root node, then sample two neighbors for each
299   root node (referred to as support neighbors) and set their memories the same as the root node, then
300   observe their memory changes over time. As depicted in Figure 4(c), if nodes have similar neighbors,
301   their memories quickly converge to a relatively stable state and persist through future changes—we
302   term this state as the node’s ideal frozen state. Hence, we may expect sampled victim nodes to have
303   similar frozen states so that they can mutually lock each other by then. Fortunately, as shown in
304
      Figure 4(d), the ideal frozen states from different nodes are similar; therefore, it is possible to keep
      nodes frozen by driving their memories into similar and stable states.
305
306   Cross-Freezing Loss. To this end, we propose to freeze victim nodes in connected groups and make
307   them persist frozen with mutual support from each other. We termed this approach as Cross-Freezing.
308   Specifically, we would first sample a node as the root node, then sample two of its neighbors as

      4.2   P ROPAGATING F ROZEN S TATES
328
329   Future Simulating. In addition to freeze nodes, it is also important to propagate the effect to more
330   nodes to exacerbate attack effects, even though some nodes are unseen at the current stage. To
331   make a node’s memory influence unknown future neighbors, we propose using its existing neighbors
332   to simulate potential future ones, which can then be used to optimize the victim nodes’ ability to
333   propagate their frozen state. This approach is based on the principle of homophily in real-world graphs,
334   where neighboring nodes often exhibit strong similarities (McPherson et al., 2001). As an example,
335   while applying TGN for the edge prediction tasks on Wikipedia dataset, nodes’ neighbors have 0.87
336
      cosine similarity on average, with over 60% neighbors having similarities over 0.9. Therefore, for a
      given node u, we use its current neighbors to simulate its future neighbors in two steps:
337
338   (1) First, we augment its current neighbor set N (u) by adding ‘similar fake future neighbors.’ These
339   are created by sampling the most recent ten neighbors from N (u) (or fewer if there aren’t ten) and
340   introducing Gaussian noise. The noise has a mean of 0 for all nodes, and the standard deviation is set
341   to 0.2 times the standard deviation of the current neighbor’s memory. (2) Second, to simulate newly
342
      added nodes in the graph, we randomly add several ‘newly presented fake future neighbors.’ The
      memories of these fake neighbors are set to all-zero, similar to the initial memory for any new nodes.
343
      The number of these fake neighbors is determined by the ratio of newly appeared nodes among the
344
      most recent ten neighbors of the node. We use the ∆T of the most recent clean message on the victim
345   nodes for these fake neighbors. For example, if we attack node n, whose most recent message before
346   our attack appears at ∆Tk , then the fake timestamp of adding those fake future neighbors will also be
347   ∆Tk . We also include more details about the future simulation in Appendix A.2 and further discuss
348   its potential under extremely random and irregular graphs in Appendix C.15.
349
      Propagating Loss. To make the frozen nodes contagious to potential future neighbors, we then use
350
      the resulting augmented neighbors Naug (u) to solve the problem described in equation 6.

358   4.3   ATTACK F RAMEWORK
359
360   Combining the above-mentioned solutions together, we introduce the two-stage MemFreezing attack
361   framework as illustrated in Figure 5. We also included a detailed algorithm in Appendix B.
362   Stage 1: Victim Node Selecting. In this stage, we use a simple greedy approach to select the nodes
363   to be attacked to facilitate cross-freezing. The connected victim nodes are select in two steps: First,
364   we select the nodes with the highest degrees in the current graph as root nodes. The intuition behind
365   this is that we want the injected noises to be propagated to as many nodes as possible, and these
366   high-degree nodes, such as top-commented posts on social media, are usually popular in existing
367   and future graphs. Next, for each root node, we select its two highest-degree neighbors as its support
368
      nodes. The following procedure will treat all the root and support nodes as victim nodes and transform
      them into frozen states.
369
370   Stage 2: Adversarial Message Solving. In this stage, we solve the messages to be passed to the
371   selected victim nodes, which can be injected as fake nodes or edges. In the first step, we find the
372   nodes’ ideal frozen states (i.e., s∗u in equation 5) by updating its memory using current neighbors
373   until convergence. In the second step, we simulate the future changes by augmenting victim nodes’
374
      neighbors with simulated futures (i.e., nodes/edges). The resulting neighbors are used as N ′ (u)
      in equation 6. In the third step, we solve the adversarial memory ŝu of the victim nodes by minimizing
375
      the total memory loss in equation 7. Specifically, for a node u, we solve its adversarial memory by
376
      minimizing the sum of its persisting (i.e., equation 5) and propagating (i.e., equation 6) losses.

536    In this work, we propose MemFreezing, a novel adversarial attack tailored for TGNNs, to overcome
537    the challenges in attacking TGNN under limited-knowlegde scenarios. The MemFreezing attack
538    misleads model predictions by freezing node memories in TGNNs into stable and dysfunctional
539    states. The experimental results show that our approach can produce long-lasting and contagious
       noises in dynamic graphs, leading to significant performance drops in TGNNs.

923   Model Details. All four TGNNs we included maintain a memory vector in each node and follow the
924   memory updating process as discussed in Section 2. And they are different in their node embedding
925   procedure (i.e., equation 4). Specifically, Dyrep directly uses the node memories for the predictions
926   (i.e., hti = sti ). JODIE applies a time-decay coefficient to the scale memories before classification
927
      (i.e., hti = δ(t) · sti ). TGN, on the other hand, refines memories using a single-layer graph attention
      module, as outlined in equation 4. Unlike prior models, ROLAND You et al. (2022) is a recent model
928
      designed for DTDG graphs, yet it also maintains a history node feature for each node as memory.
929
      Specifically, it adopts a multi-layer memory mechanism by keeping memory for both memory and
930   embedding stages. In other words, for the graph embedding part, it also adopts a GRU to combine
931   nodes’ previous embedding with the current embedding gathered from updated node memories. All
932   the models update and embed memory for one time at each prediction (i.e., one layer aggregation
933   in equation 3 and equation 4). The node memory dimension is set to 172, and the node embedding
934   dimension is set to 100. Following the training steps in (Rossi et al., 2020), we use Adam optimizer
935   with learning rate α = 0.01 to train the models 120 epochs.
936   Tasks Details. Models for node classification are trained to predict binary labels on each node. We
937   use the commonly used Area under the ROC Curve (ROC-AUC) to measure the model performances.
938   The models for edge prediction are self-supervise trained, using the edge information in future steps.
939   During the testing, given a source node, they predict the possibility of whether another node will be
940   its next incoming destination node and then decide which node will be its next neighbor. We use
941   prediction accuracy for evaluating the edge prediction result.

       C.2   BASELINE ATTACK AND ATTACK SETUP
973
974
       We adopt the following attacks toward static GNNs. Specifically, we adopt the attack at the same
975    time as our attack time by attacking the existing dynamic graph as a static graph:
976
977    FakeNode (Wang et al., 2018) uses a greedy approach to generate edges of malicious nodes and
978
       their corresponding features to mislead the static GNN predictions. Note that this approach assumes
       that the added nodes/edges will be kept in the graph, so we keep the fake nodes and edges still after
979
       the attack timestamp. Differently, the attacking nodes in MemFrezzing are removed after the attack.
980
981    TDGIA (Zou et al., 2021) is a cutting-edge Graph Injection Attack tailored to compromise static
982    GNNs. This method exploits the inherent vulnerabilities of GNNs and the unique topological
983    characteristics of graphs. In our implementation for each target node, we adhere to the established
984
       methodology of TDGIA to identify the top 65% susceptible edges, utilizing their specialized scheme
       for selecting topologically defective edges. These edges are then optimized using gradient descent.
985
       Notably, the scale of modifications applied to each target node in the TDGIA method is substantially
986
       larger than our approach, involving adjustments to 65% edges per node instead of just one edge per
987    node. Furthermore, these modifications will be kept after the attack instead of being removed as our
988    attack.
989
990
       Meta_Attack_Heuristic (Li et al., 2022) is a heuristic-based attack inspired by the meta at-
       tack (Zügner & Günnemann, 2019). This heuristic-based approach is an evolution of the original
991
       meta-attack, which relied on gradient-based edge selection. The updated heuristic version demon-
992
       strates greater versatility across a variety of GNN models and large-scale graphs, and it exhibits
993    enhanced effectiveness compared to its predecessor. Notably, the meta-attack and its heuristic coun-
994    terpart operate under the assumption that edges lack attributes. Consequently, in our application, we
995    assign an all-zero feature to the fake edges inserted as part of the attack process.
996
       For all attacks (including our attack), We select ranges of noisy messages (i.e., magnitudes of message
997
       features ) between -1 and 1 since -1 and 1 are the theoretical minimum and maximum values of the
998
       clean messages. The messages in TGNNs are usually memories of the nodes updated from previous
999    timestamps, which have activation functions such as tanh/cosine functions right before the outputs.
1000   Therefore, all features of these messages (i.e., memories) should be within the range of -1 and 1 as
1001   the minimum and maximum values of the activation functions (i.e., tanh). Therefore, using -1 and 1
1002   produces messages that are exactly similar to those of the other features in the graph.
1003
       All adversarial messages/nodes in the baselines and our attacks use the ∆T of the most recent clean
1004
       message on the victim nodes. For example, if we attack node n, whose most recent message before
1005   our attack uses ∆Tk at its updating, then the timestamp of the fake messages added to this node
1006   will be ∆Tk as well. It is also worth mentioning that the delta T has limited effects on the updating
1007   process, as we discussed in Appendix A.2.
1008
       For all attacks, we define the attack budget as the ratio of nodes that are affected. To ensure a fair
1009
       comparison, all attacks target the same set of victim nodes (the highest-degree ones). We would also
1010
       like to mention that, although targeting these high-degree nodes, all benchmarked attacks, including
1011   MemFreezing, either inject one-degree nodes or edges into the graph and affect the same number of
1012   victim nodes at the time of the attack.
1013
1014
       Specifically, MemFreezing targets high-degree nodes by introducing a temporary fake node for
       each target and creating an event (i.e., an edge) between the fake node and the target. In this way,
1015
       MemFreezing, like FakeNode, injects nodes with a degree of one into the graph. However, unlike
1016
       FakeNode, which retains the injected fake nodes and can potentially cause stronger adversarial
1017   effects, MemFreezing removes these fake nodes after the attack, minimizing structural changes while
1018   inducing long-lasting adversarial effects. Therefore, given a graph with V nodes and E edges and
1019   targeting N = 5%V victim nodes (i.e., 5% budget), MemFreezing adds N fake edges. Since nodes
1020   typically have a degree greater than one, K = 5%E > 5%V = N , the edge changes are less than
1021   5% edges.

       D.1   L IMITS UNDER DIFFERENT MODELS AND GRAPHS .
1839
1840   While the experiment results in Appendix C.4 and Appendix C.11 demonstrated that MemFrezzing
1841   can be well-generalized on various inputs, several limitations can be observed according to the
1842   performance variance between different models. While our approach can effectively mislead TGN,
1843   ROLAND, and DyRep, its effectiveness is less significant on JODIE, which uses differences between
1844   a node’s current and its last update time to decay the memory. From these observations, we deduce
1845
       that our attack may encounter limitations in two specific scenarios:
1846
              • Limited Influence of Node Memory on Predictions: Our attack’s effectiveness may be
1847            mitigated in situations where the node memory has a relatively minor role in influencing the
1848            model’s predictions.
1849
              • Usage of Additional Information in TGNN Models: The effectiveness may also be con-
1850
                strained when the targeted TGNN model incorporates additional information beyond the
1851            node memory for its predictive processes.
1852
1853   While our attack strategy outperforms the baselines, these insights highlight potential limitations
1854   under certain model-specific conditions.
1855
1856   D.2   P OTENTIAL D EFENSES .
1857
1858   While we demonstrate that many existing defense schemes, such as adversarial training or regulariza-
1859   tion, are less effective on our attacks, we expect a potential attack-oriented defense scheme for our
1860
       attack using memory filtering. Specifically, a potential defensive approach for our attack is to pay
       less attention to the nodes’ memory and rely more on their current input adaptively.
1861
1862   This scheme stems from the observation that our attacks are less effective on JODIE in node
1863   classification tasks. One key difference in JODIE is that it decays the node memory based on the time
1864   differences between the prediction time and the node’s last update time. This mechanism introduces
1865   more hints (i.e., time differences) in addition to the memory itself, which cannot be effectively
1866
       distorted by the attacks and yields some crucial information. For example, a Wikipedia user is less
       likely to be banned if he/she makes a new post after being inactive for a long while.
1867
1868   Therefore, using this non-memory information or current information that does not interact with node
1869   memory could effectively hinder adversarial noises. To this end, an intelligent defense mechanism
1870   can judiciously filter out the memory and adaptively focus more on non-memory information if the
1871   memory is suspicious or potentially noisy.

       E    C OMPLEXITY OF T HE M EMFREEZING ATTACK
1891
1892
       We further approximate the time complexity of the MemFreezing, as it is crucial to understand its
1893   practicality. The time complexity of MemFreezing is approximately O(V + V D), where V is the
1894   number of victim nodes being attacked and D is their average degree.
1895
1896
       The computation of MemFreezing can be divided into three main parts:
1897   1. Finding the Stable State: For each victim node in V , we iteratively update its state using its two
1898   support neighbors until reaching the ideal stable state. Assuming a constant number of iterations for
1899   convergence, this step incurs a time complexity of O(V ).
1900   2. Solving the Target Memory Using SGD: For each victim node, we optimize the target memory
1901   state using stochastic gradient descent (SGD), considering (a) The node itself, (b) Its two support
1902   neighbors and (c)Its augmented neighbors. The total set has a size of at most D+20 (current neighbors
1903   plus simulated neighbors), where D approximates the number of the node’s current neighbors. This
1904   optimization incurs a cost of O(D) per node, leading to a total time complexity of O(V D) across V
1905   victim nodes with D average degree.
1906   3. Introducing Fake Neighbors: For each victim node, we compute and inject a fake neighbor to
1907   introduce noise. This step has a cost of O(1) per node, resulting in O(V ) overall.
1908
1909
       In summary, the overall time complexity of MemFreezing is dominated by the SGD optimization
       step for getting noisy memory, resulting in O(V + V D) time complexity. Under the worst cases, in
1910
       which D = V (e.g., fully connected graph), the complexity is O(V 2 )
