009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, 1-bit Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged, showcasing an
012            impressive combination of efficiency and performance that rivals traditional LLMs.
013            Research by Wang et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024) indicates that the performance of
014            these 1-bit LLMs progressively improves as the number of parameters increases,
015            hinting at the potential existence of a Scaling Law for 1-bit Neural Networks. In
016            this paper, we present the first theoretical result that rigorously establishes this
017            scaling law for 1-bit models. We prove that, despite the constraint of weights
018            restricted to {−1, +1}, the dynamics of model training inevitably align with kernel
019
               behavior as the network width grows. This theoretical breakthrough guarantees
               convergence of the 1-bit model to an arbitrarily small loss as width increases.
020
               Furthermore, we introduce the concept of the generalization difference, defined as
021
               the gap between the outputs of 1-bit networks and their full-precision counterparts,
022            and demonstrate that this difference maintains a negligible level as network width
023            scales. Building on the work of Kaplan et al. (2020), we conclude by examining
024            how the training loss scales as a power-law function of the model size, dataset
025            size, and computational resources utilized for training. Our findings underscore
026            the promising potential of scaling 1-bit neural networks, suggesting that int1 could
027            become the standard in future neural network precision.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large-scale neural networks, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020;
033   Zhao et al., 2023) and Large Multimodel Models (LMMs) (Yin et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023), are
034   becoming increasingly relevant to our day-to-day lives, finding a huge variety of applications in both
035   the workplace and at home (Lin et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). However, it is expensive to deploy
036   and run these models due to their substantial computational requirements, large memory footprints,
037   and energy consumption (Vaswani et al., 2017; Alman & Song, 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). This is
038   especially true for resource-constrained environments, such as mobile devices, edge computing, or
039
      companies with limited infrastructure (Howard et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2023). To
      make these models more efficient and accessible, quantization techniques are used, which reduce the
040
      precision of the model’s parameters (such as weights and activations) from floating-point numbers
041
      to lower-bit representations (e.g., 8-bit or even lower) (Nagel et al., 2021a; Frantar et al., 2022;
042   Gholami et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024; Ahmadian et al., 2023). Quantization reduces the memory and
043   computational costs of inference, enabling faster processing with less energy, while maintaining a
044   comparable level of performance. This optimization allows language models to be more practical,
045   scalable, and sustainable for widespread use across various platforms (Bondarenko et al., 2021; Li
046   et al., 2022a; Guo et al., 2023).
047
      In particular, quantization techniques could be primarily divided into two methods: Post-Training
048   Quantization (PTQ) (Liu et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2024) and Quantization-Aware
049   Training (QAT) (Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024). PTQ methods, including uniform
050   and non-uniform quantization, conveniently convert pre-trained model weights and activations to
051   lower-bit representations post-training. However, this leads to accuracy loss, especially in lower
052   precision, as the model is not optimized for these quantized representations and significant shifts in
053   weight distribution occur (Nagel et al., 2021b). The alternative, Quantization-Aware Training (QAT),
      incorporates quantization during training, allowing the model to fine-tune and adapt its parameters to


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the quantized representation, compensating for quantization errors. Therefore, compared to PTQ,
055   QAT maintains higher accuracy and robustness even in lower precision.
056
057   Recent studies (Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024) have shown that
058
      1-bit LLMs, most of which have matrix weights in the range of {−1, +1}, can be trained from scratch
      to deliver performance that rivals that of standard LLMs. These models exhibit remarkable efficiency,
059
      particularly in terms of scaling laws. Experimental results indicate that the performance of the 1-bit
060
      model improves as the number of parameters increases, a principle that mirrors the training approach
061   utilized in standard LLMs (Kaplan et al., 2020). Despite the demonstrated efficiency of quantization
062   methods, our understanding of the training mechanism for quantization remains limited. Specifically,
063   it remains unclear how and why the 1-bit QAT enhances learning capability as the number of neurons
064   in the model is scaled up. In addition, we are also concerned about whether the quantization method
065   damages the generalization ability compared to full precision networks.
066
      In this study, we initially apply the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) framework to delve into the
067   optimization and generalization issues associated with a two-layer linear network operating in 1-bit
068   (int1) precision, as detailed in Section 4. We introduce a 1-bit quantization method to the hidden-layer
069   weights W ∈ Rd×m of the conventional NTK linear network, where d represents the input dimension
070   and m indicates the model’s width. Our analysis reveals that the training dynamics of the 1-bit model
071   approximate kernel behavior as the model width m expands. This key finding paves the way for an
072   established relationship between the theoretically guaranteed loss and the model width, endowing the
073   model with robust learning capabilities akin to kernel regression. Ultimately, the model achieves an
074   insignificantly small training loss, contingent on setting a sufficiently large model width, selecting an
075
      appropriate learning rate, and allowing an adequate training duration.
076   Moreover, Section 5 provides a theoretical confirmation that, within the scaling trend, the disparities
077   in predictions of the 1-bit model from those of the original linear network on identical inputs maintain
078   a negligible value. We assess the error between our 1-bit linear and standard linear networks on both
079   the training and test datasets. Our theorem demonstrates that for any input from these datasets, the
080   absolute error between the two network predictions can be denoted as ϵquant ≤ O(κd log(md/δ))
081
      for scale coefficient κ ≤ 1, model width m, dimension d and failure probability δ ∈ (0, 0.1).
      This indicates that the output behavior of the 1-bit linear model increasingly aligns with that of
082
      the standard linear model. The observed similarity on the test dataset validates the generalization
083
      similarity, suggesting the feasibility of approximating training neural networks with int1 precision
084   equivalent to full precision.
085
086   Finally, in Section 6, we verify our theoretical results by implementing training models to learn
087
      complicated functions to compare the difference between 1-bit networks and full precision networks.
      Firstly, we choose difficult functions across the exponential function, trigonometric function, logarith-
088
      mic function, the Lambert W function, the Gamma function, and their combination. Therefore, we
089
      sample random data points and split train and test datasets. We next compare how the training loss
090   decreases as the model width m scales up. Besides, as shown in Section 6.3, in the trend of a growing
091   number of parameters, the error of predictions both on training and test input likewise converge as
092   the power-law in 1-bit networks optimization. In particular, we visualize some 1-dimension function
093   to see how the differences of outputs are. We demonstrate the results complying with our theoretical
094   guarantee with a negligible error.

      Efficient Training Methods for Quantized Networks Training large-scale neural networks with
101
      quantization introduces significant computational and memory savings, but it also presents challenges
102   in optimization, particularly when dealing with extremely low precision formats like 1-bit or 8-bit. To
103   address these challenges, several efficient training methods have been developed that aim to maintain
104   accuracy while leveraging the benefits of quantization. One key method is Gradient Quantization,
105   where the gradients during backpropagation are quantized to lower precision to reduce memory
106   overhead and bandwidth during distributed training. Techniques like stochastic rounding are used to
107   mitigate the impact of quantization noise, ensuring the training process remains stable and converges
      effectively.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Another important approach is Low-Rank Factorization (Sainath et al., 2013; Hsu et al., 2022), which
109   decomposes the large weight matrices in neural networks into smaller matrices, reducing the number
110   of parameters that need to be updated during training. When combined with quantization, this method
111   significantly reduces both the memory footprint and computational complexity, allowing for faster
112   training on hardware with limited resources.
113
      Quantization Techniques for Accelerating Language Models Beyond traditional weight and
114
      activation quantization, several advanced methods utilize quantization to enhance the efficiency of
115   large language models (LLMs). One key approach is KV cache quantization (Hooper et al., 2024;
116   Zhang et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Zandieh et al., 2024), which reduces the memory footprint of
117   transformer models during inference by quantizing the stored attention keys and values. This method
118   is particularly beneficial for tasks involving long sequences, significantly speeding up inference and
119   lowering memory consumption without a substantial loss in accuracy.
120
      Another effective technique is mixed-precision quantization (Pandey et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023),
121   where different parts of the model are quantized at varying precision levels based on their sensitivity.
122   For example, attention layers might use higher precision (e.g., 16-bit), while feedforward layers are
123   quantized to 8-bit or lower. This balances computational efficiency and model performance. These
124   strategies, combined with methods like activation pruning, showcase how targeted quantization can
125   drastically accelerate LLMs while maintaining their effectiveness in real-world applications.
126
      Neural Tangent Kernel. The study of Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) (Jacot et al., 2018) focuses on
127   the gradient flow of neural networks during the training process, revealing that neural networks are
128   equivalent to Gaussian processes at initialization in the infinite-width limit. This equivalence has been
129   explored in numerous studies (Li & Liang, 2018; Du et al., 2018; Song & Yang, 2019; Allen-Zhu
130   et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2019; Bietti & Mairal, 2019; Lee et al., 2020; Chizat & Bach, 2020; Shi et al.,
131   2021; Zhou et al., 2021; Seleznova & Kutyniok, 2022; Gao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Shi et al.,
132   2024) that account for the robust performance and learning capabilities of over-parameterized neural
133   networks. The kernel-based analysis framework provided by NTK is gaining popularity for its utility
134   in elucidating the emerging abilities of large-scale neural networks. In a remarkable stride, Arora
135
      et al. (2019) introduced the first exact algorithm for computing the Convolutional NTK (CNTK).
      This was followed by Alemohammad et al. (2020) who proposed the Recurrent NTK, and Hron et al.
136
      (2020) who presented the concept of infinite attention via NNGP and NTK for attention networks.
137
      These innovative works have showcased the enhanced performance achievable with the application
138   of NTK to various neural network architectures. In a specific study, Malladi et al. (2023) examined
139   the training dynamics of fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) using NTK, affirming the
140   efficiency of such approaches.

      In conclusion, our theoretical results confirm the scaling law for 1-bit neural networks. We demon-

505   both efficient and effective, providing a strong alternative to traditional models.
