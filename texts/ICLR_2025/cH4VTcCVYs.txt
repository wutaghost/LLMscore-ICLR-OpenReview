009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Nervous systems learn representations of the world and policies to act within it.
012            We present a framework that uses reward-dependent noise to facilitate policy opti-
013            mization in representation learning networks. These networks balance extracting
014            normative features and task-relevant information to solve tasks. Moreover, their
015            representation changes reproduce several experimentally observed shifts in the
016            neural code during task learning. Our framework presents a biologically plausible
017            mechanism for emergent policy optimization amid evidence that representation
018
               learning plays a vital role in governing neural dynamics. Code is available at:
               NeuralThermalOptimization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Nervous systems learn representations of the world. They configure their weights to extract certain
024
      information from the environment: for instance, the presence of objects or their temporal dynamics
025
      (Li & DiCarlo (2008); de Vries & Wurm (2023)). This learning is driven by synaptic plasticity
026   (Markram et al. (1997); Bi & Poo (1998); Dan & Poo (2004)). Representation learning objectives
027   provide a normative framework for proposing plasticity rules (Lipshutz et al. (2023); van Hateren
028   (1992); Pehlevan et al. (2015)). Mounting evidence suggests that these objectives largely determine
029   synaptic plasticity. Rules derived from these objectives emulate experimentally observed spike-
030   rate dependent plasticity (Pehlevan & Chklovskii (2014); Pehlevan et al. (2017); Qin et al. (2023);
031   Sengupta et al. (2018); Halvagal & Zenke (2023); Tang et al. (2024); Millidge et al. (2024)). At the
032   same time, these rules reproduce population-level changes to the neural code seen under a variety of
033   experimental conditions (Raju et al. (2024); Qin et al. (2023); Halvagal & Zenke (2023)). However,
034
      organisms do not only build representations of the environment; they also use them to optimize
      goal-directed behavior. The mechanisms that underlie this behavioral learning remain unclear.
035
036   This article introduces NEural Thermal Optimization (NETO), a framework for how policy opti-
037   mization can emerge from noisy representation learning. NETO treats the brain’s intrinsic noise as
038   a feature rather than a bug. It hypothesizes that the noise in the nervous system is reward-dependent,
039   decreasing in magnitude when the nervous system’s weight configuration yields a rewarding policy.
040
      This modulation, akin to thermal cooling, alters the attractive points in weight space, encouraging
      the network to optimize its policy while learning a representation objective.
041
042   We illustrate the framework by considering an agent whose one-layer network operates under a
043   simple, linear representation learning objective. We analyze the agent’s learning process in two
044   tasks. In the first, the features defined by the agent’s objective capture all task-relevant information.
045   Through analytical arguments and simulations, we show that the agent is guaranteed to learn an
046
      optimal policy by diffusively searching the space of equivalent representations. In the second task,
      the agent needs to learn features not captured by its representation learning objective to perform
047
      well. We show that NETO balances normative and task-relevant feature learning to solve the task.
048
      These results suggest that NETO is a plausible theory for policy learning in nervous systems.

097   3.1   T HE M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS
098
099   A natural framework for an organism interacting in an environment is the Markov Decision Process
100   (MDP). We define an MDP with the tuple (S, A, P, r, γ). The environment’s dynamics are defined
101   by the state space S, the agent’s action space A, and P (s′ |s, a), the probability of transitioning from
102
      a state s to a state s′ given an action a. Upon this transition, the agent receives a reward specified by
      the function r(s, a).
103
104   The agent acts according to a policy π : S × A → [0, 1] which describes the distribution of actions
105   that it could take in a state s. We denote this distribution π(a|s). Its goal is to learn a policy that
106   maximizes its reward over a finite time horizon T . If we let the subscript t denote the timestep, the
                                      PT
107   reward is given by R(π) = E[ t=1 γ t r(st , at )], where at ∼ π(a|st ) per the definition of the policy,
      and γ ∈ (0, 1] is the temporal discount factor.

      3.2     N EURAL T HERMAL O PTIMIZATION
109
110   Here, we introduce Neural Thermal Optimization (NETO). In NETO agents, a single noisy neu-
111   ral network learns representations and controls actions, while a neuromodulatory system tunes the
112   noise. As such, NETO has three components: a representation learning network, a motor function
113   that defines the agent’s policy, and reward-dependent noise controlled by a neuromodulatory system.
114   We illustrate each in Figure 1, and introduce them below, along with the example that we consider
115   throughout this work.

119   continuously with its environment. We begin by describing its representation learning network.
120   Let the neural network be parameterized by activities x ∈ RN and weights θ. This network can

122   representations of the MDP, in principle capturing aspects of the distribution of s or the dynamics of

127   each state s. These responses are noisy, and are given by the stochastic differential equations

170   3.2.2    A MOTOR FUNCTION DEFINES THE AGENT ’ S POLICY
171
172   Under NETO, the same neural network learns representations and controls actions. We now intro-
173   duce the motor function, which defines the agent’s policy. Let M : RN → A be a motor function
174   that maps the network activities to an action. We assume that M is independent of time3 . Biologi-
175
      cally, we can imagine that this motor function arises due to projections from the neural activities x

176
177   This motor function defines the agent’s policy, the distribution of possible actions a that it could
178   take in response to a state s. In the general case of noisy activity dynamics, a distribution fθ (x|s)

180   given by the distribution of the actions associated with these network responses

      4.1       A DIFFUSIVE SEARCH FACILITATES POLICY OPTIMIZATION WHEN L CAPTURES THE
                TASK - RELEVANT INFORMATION
224
225   An ideal representation learning objective captures enough information to solve a variety of tasks.
226   We begin by analyzing the agent’s learning process when this is this case. We construct a contextual
227   bandit task that the agent can solve with the features that L encourages it to extract. We then show
228   that the agent is guaranteed to maximize reward as time goes to infinity 5 . In the process, we illustrate
229   that a diffusive search through the space of equivalent representations facilitates policy learning: The
230   network parameters converge to the solution space of L and explore it using reward-dependent noise.
231
232   4.1.1       T HE CONTEXTUAL BANDIT TASK
233
      The contextual bandit task is a special case of the Markov Decision Process where the state tran-
234
      sitions are independent of the agent’s action. Let our contextual bandit task have states s ∈ R10 .
235   The states are drawn from two Gaussian clusters in 10 dimensions. In particular, we define them as
236   s = ky, where y ∼ N (µ, C), and k is a random sign. The largest eigenvalue of the covariance
237   matrix C is 0.1, and the rest are 0.05. The mean vector µ is the second eigenvector of C and has
238   magnitude 2. We also define the agent’s action space as A = {1, −1}, and let r(s, a) = 1(a=k) .
239   The agent must learn to take the action corresponding to the random sign. It is possible to learn this
240   task because the Gaussian clusters are linearly separable. Figure 2 illustrates the setup.

407   4.2   NETO BALANCES NORMATIVE REPRESENTATION AND POLICY LEARNING TO SOLVE
408         TASKS
409
410   The contextual bandit task gives the impression that NETO agents learn representations and policies
411   in separate phases. However, in general, NETO couples representation and policy learning. This
412   coupling allows agents to balance normative and task-dependent representation learning, extracting
413   features that L does not capture to improve task performance.
414   The contextual bandit example illustrates that we can consider the R-dependent noise as θ-
415   dependent. This equivalence arises because the same network learns representations and controls
416   actions. Recent work in the context of stochastic gradient descent found that the weight diffusion
417   tensor σθ (θ) and the loss L(θ) jointly determine the attractive points in weight configuration space
418   (Chen et al. (2024)). This fact gives the network the freedom to extract information that is not
419   optimal with respect to L if it helps the agent perform well in the task.
420   We demonstrate this principle with Cart Pole. Cart Pole is a classical control problem with a four-
421   dimensional observation space. It is simple enough for our one-layer, linear network to solve. The
422   goal of Cart Pole is to keep a pole upright on a cart while preventing it from falling off a frictionless
423   track. The agent can push the cart right or left at each timestep. Importantly, the agent cannot
424   learn an optimal policy for Cart Pole if its weights project the state observations onto their two-
425   dimensional principal subspace, shown empirically in Appendix A.6. Therefore, the agent cannot
426   solve the task with only normative features; it must learn to adjust its representations so that they
427   contain information that is suboptimal with respect to L.
428   Indeed, we find that solving the task requires the agent’s weights to stabilize at representation learn-
429   ing losses that are orders of magnitude larger than in the contextual bandit task, as seen in Figure
430   5 (left). The agent learns to extract features not specified by L. Note that these features are not
431   arbitrary. They are still partially determined by L; the loss settles to be many orders of magnitude
      lower than where it began.

451   This simple example illustrates a general principle: NETO agents balance normative and task-

453   most likely to extract task-relevant features if they exist near the solution space of L. Policy learning

457   5     D ISCUSSION
458
459   In this article, we present the hypothesis that policy optimization emerges from the noisy learning of
460   representation objectives in nervous systems. We explore the consequences of this idea in a linear,
461   one-layer network. Through simulations and analytical arguments, we show that this network learns
462   the optimal policy in tasks that it can reasonably be expected to solve. Each task serves to provide
463
      intuition for its learning process. When L captures all task-relevant features, the network performs a
      diffusive search through its solution space to discover an optimal policy. The agent can also modify
464
      its representations to capture task-relevant features, even if the resulting representations are slightly
465
      suboptimal with respect to L.

      5.1   C ONSISTENCY WITH EXPERIMENTAL OBSERVATIONS
468
469   While the NETO framework is more general, we focus on the case where L is a representation
470   learning objective and R denotes reward. This choice gives NETO the potential to explain several
471   recently observed phenomena describing neural representation changes during task learning. We
472   discuss three examples: representation drift, neural reassociation, and the orthogonalization of task-
473   relevant features.
474   Representation drift describes the slow change in the neural codes of stimuli over time. Crucially,
475   these changes maintain the information encoded in the neural population (Driscoll et al. (2017);
476   Rubin et al. (2015); Ziv et al. (2013)). Representation drift emerges naturally from the NETO
477   framework when L is a representation learning objective. We saw representation drift clearly in the
478   contextual bandit task: the network responses rotated while maintaining their population structure.
479   This result has been noted before in (Qin et al. (2023)). NETO suggests a potential feature of this
480   drift: It can aid task learning by sampling behavioral policies, even when behavioral performance
481   stabilizes.
482   In neural reassociation, the brain forms new stimulus-action associations by reassociating stimulus-
483   code pairs instead of generating new activity patterns. Researchers observed neural reassociation
484   in primate motor cortex during task learning (Golub et al. (2018)). NETO can reproduce neural
485   reassociation with the correct motor function. Consider a nonlinear motor function M (x) from
      some unspecified brain region to motor cortex (instead of directly to an action). Let the function


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      be nonlinear so that changing the distribution of inputs does not change the range of M (x), which
487   we can think of an as a manifold in activity space. Suppose NETO operates only at the neurons in
488   the domain of M . Then, task learning will occur by changing the structure of the neural activity in
489   its domain to re-map stimuli to different but already existing patterns in motor cortex. If we were
490   to record the neural activity in motor cortex during task learning, we would not observe significant
491   changes in the manifold of response patterns. Instead, we would see the associations between these
492   stimuli and the existing patterns shift. NETO can therefore explain neural reassociation.
493   Nervous systems have also been observed to orthogonalize task-relevent features to facilitate task
494   learning (Failor et al. (2021); Gurnani & Cayco Gajic (2023)). NETO can orthogonalize task-
495   relevant features by exploring the solution space of a nonlinear representation learning objective.
496   As a simple example, consider the non-negative similarity matching (NNSM) objective introduced
497   in (Pehlevan & Chklovskii (2014)). In (Sengupta et al. (2018)), the authors found that networks
498   operating under the NNSM objective learn localized receptive fields. Moreover, the locations of
499   these receptive fields drift under noisy weight updates (Qin et al. (2023)). Drifting receptive fields
500   is an example of feature orthogonalization: Two stimuli can go from sharing a receptive field (large
501
      neural code overlap) to existing in different receptive fields (low overlap). This strongly suggests
      that a NETO network with L as the NNSM objective would orthogonalize task-relevant features if
502
      this improved its ability to seek reward. The observation that NETO may reproduce a number of
503
      experimental observations supports it as a biologically plausible theory for emergent policy learning.
504
505
      5.2   L IMITATIONS & F UTURE D IRECTIONS
506
507   NETO’s primary limitation is that it relies on diffusive searches, which are notoriously inefficient in
508   high dimensions. Consider scaling the agent’s representation space to N dimensions. Then suppose
509   that it interacts in a contextual bandit task, this time with N actions and N input clusters. The time
510   that it takes the network to orient these clusters with a diffusive search scales exponentially in N .
511
      There are two potential solutions to this issue. The first is a biological argument that downplays it,
512   and the second presents an avenue to address the problem. Biological neural codes tend to be low
513   dimensional (Iyer et al. (2022); Shine et al. (2019); Ebitz & Hayden (2021)). If the diffusive search
514   is largely restricted to this low-dimensional subspace, then it avoids the exponential scaling that
515   comes with an increasingly high-dimensional representation space. This argument, while plausible,
516   avoids the issue. To solve it, we need to construct a more efficient modulatory system. In all our
517   experiments, we assume that the modulatory system acts uniformly on all neurons in the network.
518   This unnecessary assumption restricts the weight diffusion tensor to a multiple of the identity. Neu-
519   romodulation in natural systems is not uniform, which may allow it to shape more complex diffusion
520
      tensors. It is possible that carefully shaping the diffusion tensor could make policy learning more
      efficient. This becomes clear when we discretize time into larger episodes, and consider the policy
521
      in each episode as a “sample” of the reward function R at a point in weight space. Under this pic-
522
      ture, we see that a modulatory system following an evolutionary algorithm like Covariance Matrix
523   Adaptation may be more efficient.
524
525   Animals learn tasks quickly by applying previously acquired knowledge to new problems. They
526
      achieve this rapid, generalizable learning by leveraging their world model: a powerful predictive
      representation of the environment and how it evolves under their actions. Future work could also
527
      explore whether agents with expressive representation learning objectives that build world models
528
      can solve challenging tasks. It would also be interesting to explore other applications of the NETO
529   framework: for instance, online supervised learning.
530

      5.3   C ONCLUSION
532
533   Though we show that a one-layer network can solve basic tasks, the principal contribution of this
534   work is to introduce NETO as a theory for emergent policy learning in nervous systems. This result
535   suggests further exploration into reward-dependent noise in the nervous system.

650   A.1    N EURAL T HERMAL O PTIMIZATION AS A MORE GENERAL FRAMEWORK
651
652   While the focus of this work is to introduce NETO for policy optimization, we feel that it is impor-
653   tant to briefly introduce the framework in a more general setting. NETO is a framework for how
654   the nervous system can satisfy two distinct objectives when the primary network’s dynamics are
655   governed by only one. Fundamentally, NETO states that:
656
            1. Nervous systems are noisy,
657
658         2. A self-supervised objective L determines the network’s deterministic weight dynamics,
659         3. A neuromodulatory system shapes the noise given the value of R.
660
661
      They key insight behind NETO is that if the eigenvalues of the weight diffusion tensor are mono-
      tonically decreasing functions of R, the attractive points of the system change to balance the opti-
662
      mization of L and R. The only requirement is that L is differentiable with respect to the network
663
      weights. Nicely, R does not have to satisfy this requirement. In a sense, NETO combines a gradient
664   descent algorithm to optimize L with an evolutionary algorithm to optimize R. The evolutionary
665   algorithm comes into play in the design of the weight diffusion tensor.
666
667
      NETO’s generality allows future work to explore other choices of L or R. For instance, if L were
      powerful enough to build an expressive world model, then the agent could likely solve challeng-
668
      ing reinforcement learning problems. Moreover, if R were a supervised learning loss instead of
669
      reward, the agent could learn a classification task online. There are a wealth of different objective
670   combinations that one could consider.
671
672   We also note that one could further generalize this framework to any model defined by the param-
673
      eters θ that aims to satisfy two objectives L and R, though only L is differentiable with respect to
      the model parameters. This framework may be useful if one believes that satisfying L will help
674
      the model approach optimal solutions with respect to R, thereby increasing the efficiency of an
675
      evolutionary algorithm.

      A.6     O PTIMAL C ART P OLE P OLICIES DO NOT EXIST IN THE SOLUTION SPACE OF L
957
958
      Here, we show that optimal Cart Pole policies cannot be found within the solution space of L. We
959
      begin with an intuitive argument. Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that the agent can maintain
960   a successful policy while satisfying L. A successful Cart Pole policy minimizes the angular devia-
961   tion of the pole from its center. However, because the agent satisfies L, it projects its observations
962   onto their two-dimensional principal subspace. The pole’s angular deviation does not lie in this
963   subspace, as its variance is too small. Without access to this critical information, the agent cannot
964   determine which action to take, and thus cannot have an optimal policy. This leads to a contradiction.
965
      It is necessary to validate this intuition because the other observations (cart position, velocity, etc.)
966   should have small variances in an optimal policy as well. We test the claim that an agent with
967   an optimal policy cannot project its observations onto their 2D principal subspace and maintain
968   an optimal policy. We record the observations of an agent with a policy that beats Cart Pole and
969   compute this subspace. We then define a new agent with a motor function M (x) = sign(v T x) that
970   acts directly on these principal subspace projections. We explore all rotational configurations of the
971   principle subspace projections but find that none result in a policy that beats Cart Pole (Figure 9).
      An agent cannot project its observations onto their principal subspace if it intends to maintain its

973    have an optimal policy. This result empirically confirms the claim that optimal policies do not exist
