009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             Li et al. (2023) used the Othello board game as a test case for the ability of GPT-
012             2 to induce world models, and were followed up by Nanda et al. (2023b). We
013             briefly discuss the original experiments, expanding them to include more language
014             models with more comprehensive probing. Specifically, we analyze sequences of
015             Othello board states and train the model to predict the next move based on previous
016             moves. We evaluate seven language models (GPT-2, T5, Bart, Flan-T5, Mistral,
017             LLaMA-2, and Qwen2.5) on the Othello task and conclude that these models not
018
                only learn to play Othello, but also induce the Othello board layout. We find that
                all models achieve up to 99% accuracy in unsupervised grounding and exhibit high
019
                similarity in the board features they learned. This provides considerably stronger
020
                evidence for the Othello World Model Hypothesis than previous works.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      Li et al. (2023) used the Othello board game to probe the ability of LLMs to induce world models.
026   Their network had a 60-word input vocabulary, corresponding to the 64 tiles of an Othello board,
027   except for the four that are already filled at the start. They trained the network on two datasets:
028   one on about 140,000 real Othello games and another on millions of synthetic games. They then
029   trained 64 independent non-linear probes (two-layer MLP classifiers) to classify each of the 64 tiles
030   into three states: black, blank, and white, using internal representations from Othello-GPT as input.
031   The error rates of these non-linear probes dropped from 26.2% on a randomly-initialized model to
032   only 1.7% on a trained model, while linear probes performed close to random. Li et al. (2023) saw
033   this as evidence that LLMs can induce (non-linear) world models, at least for Othello board games,
034
      supporting the Othello World Model Hypothesis – – the hypothesis that LLMs trained on Othello
      move sequences can induce a relevant world model, including the Othello board layout.
035
036   Nanda et al. (2023b) did a follow-up study in which they found that linear probes also work if
037   trained slightly differently. Instead of focusing on tile color, they probed the board state relative to
038   the current player at each timestep, using labels such as MINE, YOURS, and EMPTY. This reduced
039   the error rate of the probes to less than 10%. They speculated that world knowledge is often linearly
040
      represented in language models, since ‘matrix multiplication can easily extract a different subset of
      linear features for each neuron.’
041
042   Now, training a probe as a research methodology comes with several weaknesses, including: a)
043   probing classifiers can be prone to spurious correlations (Barrett et al., 2019). b) They do not tell us
044   how information is arranged globally in LLMs.1 c) They therefore only detect a subset of the inter-
045   esting properties of world models, e.g., excluding the spatial relations that would enable analogical
046
      reasoning (Mikolov et al., 2013).

088   Past work on Othello Most past works on Othello (Chang et al., 2018; van der Ree & Wiering,
089   2013) use reinforcement learning to search for moves. The first attempt to model Othello with deep
090   neural networks dates back to 2018 (Liskowski et al., 2018), focusing on using CNNs to train a
091   strong player. Based on it, Noever & Noever (2022) focus on designing an effective Othello player
092   with LLMs. Motivated by Toshniwal et al. (2021), Li et al. (2023) shift the focus to treating the
093   game as a diagnostic tool for inducing world models from text. Following this, Nanda et al. (2023b)
094   provide evidence of a closely related linear representation of the board and propose a simple yet
095   powerful way to interpret the model’s internal state. Takizawa (2024) recently presents a provably
096
      optimal strategy for playing Othello, delving into the complexity of these strategies and paving the
      way for future research to explore whether LLMs adopt similar approaches. Hua et al. (2024) adopt
097
      the idea of Othello sequence generation and introduce a multilingual Othello task to aid in cross-
098
      lingual representation alignment.

134   3     M ODELING OTHELLO WITH LLM S
135
136   Following previous works (Liskowski et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023; Nanda et al., 2023b), we formulate
137
      the problem of playing the board game as a sequence generation problem. Specifically, we fine-tune
      generative pretrained models in an autoregressive manner to predict the next move given the current
138
      Othello board state. We then evaluate whether the predicted move is legal or not. Each game is a
139
      sequence, with each move represented as a token, and in each round, we predict the next move. Our
140   vocabulary consists of 60 words, each representing one of the 60 playable tiles where players place
141   discs, excluding the 4 center tiles, which are already occupied at the start of the game. See Figure 1
142   for an example move. Our modeling of Othello, in brief, can be represented as:
143

      3.3   I MPACT OF M ODEL S IZE ON OTHELLO M OVE G ENERATION
248
249   To further explore the impact of model size on the ability to model Othello moves, we analyze the
250   performance of various models across different size configurations, as depicted in Figure 2. For
251   each model, we evaluate performance in small, medium, and large size versions, allowing us to
252   compare how scaling up model capacity affects accuracy in generating game moves. The results
253   show a clear trend: as model size increases, the error rate consistently decreases across both
254   datasets. This trend is particularly pronounced in the SYNTHETIC dataset, where larger models
255   achieve significantly lower error rates compared to their smaller counterparts. The stronger improve-
256   ment in the SYNTHETIC dataset suggests that larger models are better at capturing the structured
257
      patterns present in the synthetic data, likely due to their enhanced capacity for learning complex
      representations and generalizing across more varied scenarios. These findings highlight the impor-
258
      tance of model scaling, showing that increasing the model size can lead to substantial performance
259
      gains in Othello move generation, especially in environments where the data is highly structured or
260   synthetic in nature. Furthermore, the results emphasize that larger models are not just marginally
261   better, but often significantly outperform smaller models, reinforcing the need to consider model
262   capacity as a critical factor when tackling tasks that require a deep understanding of game strategies
263   and sequential decision-making processes.

479   6   L IMITATIONS
480
481   Although this work demonstrates the ability of different language models to understand Othello
482
      game rules, several limitations persist that require further investigation:
483   Challenges in Multi-step Move Generation. While language models can predict the next move
484   with reasonable accuracy, they struggle to predict entire game sequences. The key question is
485   whether strong multi-step performance is a reasonable expectation. Othello is a dynamic game
      where optimal play often involves sacrificing short-term gains for long-term advantages. The com-


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      plexity arises from the interplay of distinct player strategies and the rotational invariance of the
487   board, leading to many game states where the subjectively or objectively best move is inherently
488   underdetermined. As a result, the ability to accurately predict entire sequences may remain elusive,
489   given the complexity and variability of decision-making in the game.
490
      Limitations in Data Requirements. Our experiments show that reducing the 1-hop error rate to
491
      less than 0.1% demands a large volume of training data. This reliance on vast datasets presents
492
      a scalability issue, as access to Othello game data is limited. Moreover, training on such large
493   datasets is computationally expensive and time-consuming, which can be a prohibitive factor for
494   many researchers or organizations without access to substantial computational resources.
495
496
497   7   P OTENTIAL I MPACT
498
499   Training language models on Othello game sequences can imply that LLMs function as a world
500   model because it showcases their ability to learn and internalize the structured dynamics and rules
501   of a complex system, rather than merely memorizing patterns. Investigating the parallels between
502   how language models learn structured representations and how humans internalize similar concepts
503
      can shed light on the cognitive processes underlying reasoning, strategy, and language. This could
      deepen our understanding of human cognition and inform theories of learning and representation.
504
      The observation that language models, regardless of architecture or scale, learn similar patterns from
505
      Othello game sequences suggests that these models converge on universal representations when
506   trained on structured data. This implies that the underlying mechanisms of representation learning
507   in LLMs are robust and consistent, highlighting their ability to capture the rules and dynamics
508   of structured systems. The ability of language models to learn patterns from Othello sequences
509   provides more hints on the idea that LLMs can act as world models, capable of internalizing rules,
510   strategies, and dynamics. This has far-reaching implications for tasks requiring reasoning about
511   complex environments, such as planning, simulation, and autonomous decision-making.

515
516
      We list several possible future directions to study how our results could generalize to other broader
      scenarios.
517
518   More Complicated Games. Since this work is primarily limited to the Othello game, an intrigu-
519   ing question arises: could similar findings be observed in other games such as chess, checkers, or
520   Go? These games, like Othello, involve strategic planning, dynamic state transitions, and trade-offs
521   between short-term gains and long-term advantages. Exploring how large language models (LLMs)
522
      learn and represent strategies in these contexts could be highly valuable.
523   Multimodal Support. Leveraging Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) to train models and investigate fea-
524   ture alignment across different modalities is also a highly relevant and promising research direction.
525   In the context of Othello, this approach could involve aligning visual representations of the game
526   board with text-based sequencial moves. Such alignment can help bridge the gap between symbolic
527   reasoning and natural language understanding, enabling models to not only predict optimal moves
528
      but also provide hints if the world model theory could also be applied in other modalities.

531
532
      We conduct a detailed probing of language models’ ability to predict legal moves in the Othello
533
      board game, based on the settings in Li et al. (2023). We evaluate seven language models, training
534   them to predict the next move based on previous moves. All seven models achieve almost ‘perfect’
535   one-hop move prediction performance when trained with large amount of data. We then adopt
536   representation alignment tools to align the learned game state features from different models into
537   one joint space. We observe high similarity in the board features they learned. In addition, latent
538   move projection is performed to show the models not only understand the game mechanics but
539   also capture the spatial relationships between tiles. These results, in our view, provide more solid
      evidence to date of the Othello World Model Hypothesis presented in previous works.
