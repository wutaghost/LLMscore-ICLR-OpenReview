009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011             In real-world graphs, we often encounter missing feature situations where a few or
012             the majority of node features, e.g., sensitive information, are missed. In such sce-
013             narios, directly utilizing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) would yield sub-optimal
014             results in downstream tasks such as node classification. Despite the emergence of a
015             few GNN-based methods attempting to mitigate its missing situation, when only
016             a few features are available, they rather perform worse than traditional structure-
017             based models. To this end, we propose a novel framework that further illuminates
018
                the potential of classical Label Propagation (Oldie), taking advantage of Feature
                Propagation, especially when only a partial feature is available. Now called by
019
                Goodie1 , it takes a hybrid approach to obtain embeddings from the Label Propaga-
020
                tion branch and Feature Propagation branch. To do so, we first design a GNN-based
021             decoder that enables the Label Propagation branch to output hidden embeddings
022             that align with those of the FP branch. Then, Goodie automatically captures
023             the significance of structure and feature information thanks to the newly designed
024             Structure-Feature Attention. Followed by a novel Pseudo-Label contrastive learn-
025             ing that differentiates the contribution of each positive pair within pseudo-labels
026             originating from the LP branch, Goodie outputs the final prediction for the unla-
027             beled nodes. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that our proposed
028             model, Goodie, outperforms the existing state-of-the-art methods not only when
029
                only a few features are available but also in abundantly available situations.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Graph embedding techniques have been favored since the early stage of the graph community and
035
      widely used until recently. Among various techniques, traditional graph embedding methods focus
      on how to leverage relational information in a given graph. To preserve its structural properties and
036
      information, they aim to obtain a computational and low-dimensional continuous vector for each node
037
      Perozzi et al. (2014a;b). Without the usage of features and solely resorting to structure information,
038   obtained embeddings for each node would semantically contain their local neighborhood structure
039   Cao et al. (2015); Ou et al. (2016).
040
041
      At the same time, the recent success of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) Kipf & Welling (2016a);
      Hamilton et al. (2017); Veličković et al. (2017) arises from their ability to jointly encode not only
042
      structure but also feature information into low-dimensional embedding space. GNNs first aggregate
043
      the messages, i.e., features from their neighbors, then update their representation iteratively Gilmer
044   et al. (2017). Sharing the essence with the aforementioned graph embedding techniques, GNNs aim to
045   solve downstream tasks such as node classification Kipf & Welling (2016a); Veličković et al. (2017),
046   and link prediction Kipf & Welling (2016b); Zhang & Chen (2018) with its obtained embeddings.
047
      However, one of the strong inductive biases GNNs hold is the observation of full features, which
048
      do not reflect real-world settings Taguchi et al. (2021); Jiang & Zhang (2020); Chen et al. (2020);
049
      Rossi et al. (2021). In real-world graphs, as features have characteristics of high dimensionality in
050   most cases, the missing situation is easily observable. In practical applications, feature information

077

078

079   (2020) adopts partial aggregation in the perspective of message propagation as like GCN. To verify
080   their effectiveness, we empirically conducted experiments assuming real-world missing scenarios
081   with traditional graph embedding techniques, DeepWalk Perozzi et al. (2014a), and Node2vec Perozzi
082   et al. (2014b), including a hybrid model, Node2Vec+GNN. As shown in Figure 1, the interesting
083   observations are as follows: 1) When features are not or partially given, recent GNN-based methods
084   deteriorate more than traditional graph embedding methods that solely use structure information.
085   This is because when only a few features are available, heavily depending on this observed feature
086   would hamper the generalization of the trained model. Thus, in such cases, rather utilizing structure-
087
      based models would be preferable. 2) Hybrid model which initializes its feature via embeddings
      from Node2Vec outperforms traditional structure-based models. This tells us that implicitly utilizing
088
      features with equipping message-passing scheme is beneficial. 3) However when features are provided
089
      more, GNN-based methods gain their potential to leverage feature information and eventually perform
090   better than both structure-based and hybrid models. This provides us an insight that when features
091   are abundant, explicitly utilizing them with a message-passing scheme is desirable.
092
093
      We now conclude that different types of models are suited for different cases. However, in real-world
      scenarios, it is hard to decide which type of approach is appropriate in various missing situations.
094
      Another problem is the existing gap between traditional structure-based models and recent GNN-
095
      based models, especially when only a few features are available. Although the implicit hybrid model
096   can mitigate the gap to some extent, it still has the pitfalls of incorporating explicit features. Thus, the
097   following questions naturally emerge, how can we appropriately use the partial feature information
098   while taking advantage of traditional structure-based graph embedding approaches? More generally,
099   how can we adaptively use the feature information and combine it with the structure information in
100   different cases?
101
      Based on such motivation, we now propose a novel framework, called Goodie that takes an explicit
102   hybrid approach to incorporate structure and feature information upon graphs with partial features.
103   To do so, we especially utilize traditional Label Propagation (LP) Zhu (2005) and a very recently
104   proposed algorithm, Feature Propagation (FP) Rossi et al. (2021) to cope with structure and feature
105   information, respectively. As they propagate labels and features to their neighbors, we design an
106   elegant way to combine both. More precisely, at the LP branch, we cope with a GNN-based decoder
107   that transforms the LP’s predicted logits into a hidden embedding vector. By doing so, the LP branch
      not only equips learnable parameters while training but also aligns with the hidden embedding space

124   2    R ELATED W ORK
125
126   Label Propagation. Given a graph with partial labels, Label Propagation (LP) Zhu (2005); Zhou
127   et al. (2003); Wang & Zhang (2006) diffuses its known labels based on graph structure and predicts
128   the labels of the unlabeled nodes. At the early stage of the graph community, thanks to its simplicity
129   and intuitive approach to predicting labels, LP has been widely used for various tasks Zhang & Lee
130   (2006); Raghavan et al. (2007). However, its popularity has declined as Graph Neural Networks
131   are being introduced Kipf & Welling (2016a); Veličković et al. (2017); Hamilton et al. (2017) and
132
      recognized its power upon its ability to incorporate features and encode them with structure jointly.
      Recently, a few works have connected GNNs with LP Huang et al. (2020); Wang & Leskovec (2020);
133
      however, they fall short when features are not fully observed, e.g., real-world scenarios.
134
135   Feature Propagation. To mitigate missing feature problems in the graph domain, Feature Propaga-
136   tion (FP) Rossi et al. (2021) has been recently introduced. Namely, FP diffuses its known features to
137   unknown features iteratively and reconstructs the feature matrix followed by GNN layers. From the
138
      perspective of minimizing Dirichlet energy, it naturally imputes nodes’ missing features from their
      connected neighbors. By virtue of assortative environments on graphs Sen et al. (2008); Defferrard
139
      et al. (2016); Kipf & Welling (2016a); Lin & Cohen (2010), the imputed nodes eventually share
140
      similar features with their neighbors. Despite its promising performance and scalability, it still suffers
141   from situations when only a few features are given, resulting in poor performance compared to simple
142   LP.
143
144
      Other works handling Missing Features in Graphs. As the missing feature situation is easily
      observed in real-world scenarios, in the graph domain, node features naturally contain missing
145
      elements. To handle such cases and relate to existing GNNs, several works have been proposed.
146
      GCNMF Taguchi et al. (2021) imputes missing features by assuming each feature channel follows
147   the Gaussian distribution and suggests Gaussian Mixture Model that aligns with graph convolutional
148   networks (GCN). Also, PaGNN Jiang & Zhang (2020) suggests a partial aggregation scheme from
149   neighborhood reconstruction formulation. SAT Chen et al. (2020) jointly takes into account structure
150   and attributes through distribution matching. Here, while SAT utilizes structure information as well
151   as feature information, the main difference between our proposed model, Goodie, is the explicit
152   usage of their embeddings and the control of their contribution. Goodie explicitly utilizes structure
153   embeddings originated from LP, and controls its weight between feature embeddings obtained from
154   FP. Although these methods showed their effectiveness in low missing rates, they can not cope with
155
      the situation when only a few features are available.

185   on graphs with partially observed features. We first demonstrate how each LP and FP branch output

187   branch via the structure-feature attention module (Sec 4.2). Then, we introduce a novel pseudo-label
188   contrastive learning that further improves the quality of embeddings with its scaled version (Sec 4.3),
189   followed by the overall model training process (Sec 4.4). Figure 2 shows the overall architecture of

515   6     C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we proposed a novel hybrid approach to bridge the gap between the traditional structure-
518   based model and the recent GNN-based model, especially in graphs with partial features. Based on
519   the empirical observation that recent GNN-based imputation models deteriorate more than traditional
520   graph embedding methods, we proposed Goodie that leverages the hidden potential of the classic
521   structure-based model, Label Propagation. Goodie first obtain embeddings from the LP branch
522
      thanks to a simple GNN-based decoder that enables alignment with those of the FP branch. Passed
      through the Structure-Feature Attention module, we naturally obtain embeddings that separated
523
      the wheat from the chaff contain appropriate structure and feature information. We further propose
524
      Pseudo-Label Contrastive Learning that leverages the predicted probability originating from the
525   LP branch which differentiates each positive pair with train and pseudo labels. Through extensive
526   experiments regarding various missing scenarios, we empirically show the robust performance on not
527   only slightly-missing but also severely-missing situations.

705   A.1   C OMPARED M ETHODS
706
707   Considering we are facing a missing feature situation where features are partially observed in semi-
708   supervised settings, we compare Goodie with the following models that are widely used in graphs
709   with missing scenarios:
710   (1) Feature or Structure-based models
711
      • MLP: It is simple 2-layer Multi-Layer Perceptron with ReLU. For the missing features, we imputed
712       them with zeros.
713
714   • Node2Vec Perozzi et al. (2014b): It learns node embedding via random walks with skip-gram.
715
          Since it does not consider feature information, it is not sensitive to missing rates. We also
          experimented Node2Vec+Feats., which leverages given partial features.
716
717   • Label Propagation Zhu (2005): It directly propagates the given labels to their neighbors until they
718       converge. As LP only relies on structure information, it is not sensitive to missing rates.
719   (2) GNN-based models
720
721   • GCN Kipf & Welling (2016a): It is simple 2-layer Graph Convolutional Network. For the missing
722
          features, we imputed them with zeros which naturally performs well as mentioned in Rossi et al.
          (2021).
723
724   • GCN-NM Rossi et al. (2021): This is a graph-based imputation method that imputes missing
725       features with the mean of its neighbors. This corresponds to the first-order approximation of FP.
726   • GCNMF Taguchi et al. (2021): It is an end-to-end GNN-based model that imputes missing features
727       by assuming Gaussian Mixture Model that aligns with GCN.
728
729
      • PaGNN Jiang & Zhang (2020): It performs a partial message-passing scheme via propagating
          observed features only.
730
731   • FP Rossi et al. (2021): It is a state-of-the-art model that propagates given features through neighbors
732       and replaces observed ones with their original ones in terms of minimizing the Dirichlet energy.
733   (3) Hybrid models
734
735   • Node2Vec+GNN: It regards the node embeddings from Node2Vec as features of GNN. It then
736
          follows the message-passing scheme of GCN.
737   • GCN-LPA Wang & Leskovec (2020): It connects GCN with Label Propagation via introducing
738       trainable edges and trains them jointly.
739
      • Correct and Smooth (C&S) Huang et al. (2020): It connects simple MLP with Label Propagation
740       by using MLP to obtain base prediction and utilizes LP as a post-processing step. We used the
741       Linear model as a base predictor.

888   B.0.2    C OMPARISON WITH A SCALED VERSION OF P SEUDO C ON
889
      As mentioned in Sec 4.3, we additionally proposed a scaled version of PseudoCon loss that effectively
890   reduces the cost, (O(N 2 ) → O(|C|2 )). Although it is initially designed to cope with large graph
891   datasets, we further verify its performance on small graph datasets. As shown in Figure 10, we
892   observe that without a huge loss, scaled PseudoCon aligns with small graph datasets as well. In Struc-
893   turally Missing scenarios, where whole features are deleted for random nodes, we observe original
894   PseudoCon loss generalizes better in relatively small graph datasets, e.g., Cora, CiteSeer, WikiCS,
895   and Coauthor CS. However, when the size of the dataset gets bigger, e.g., PubMed, Co.Physics, and
896   OGBN-Arxiv, original PseudoCon loss was not as effective in small datasets. This is because in such
897   cases, the only feasible way for original PseudoCon loss to cope with large graph datasets is to utilize
898
      the batch sampling technique. By doing so, the positive pairs and negative pairs are solely made on
      that certain batch. Thus, the potential positive and negative pairs, which could be viewed on small
899
      graph datasets via full-batch, are not sufficiently utilized. To conclude, when we deal with real-world
900
      graphs, it would be preferable to utilize original PseudoCon loss in the small graphs while scaled
901   PseudoCon loss would be preferred in large graphs considering its time and memory complexity.
