009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Research on Continual Learning (CL) tackles learning with non-stationary data
012            distributions. The non-stationary nature of data is also one of the challenges of
013            deep Reinforcement Learning (RL), and as a consequence, both CL and deep RL
014            rely on similar approaches to stabilize learning, from the use of replay buffers to
015            the choice of regularization terms. However, while dynamic neural architectures
016
               that grow in size to learn new tasks without forgetting older ones are well re-
               searched in CL, it remains a largely understudied research direction in RL. In this
017
               paper, we argue that Policy Mirror Descent (PMD), a regularized policy iteration
018
               RL algorithm, would naturally benefit from dynamic neural architectures as the
019            current policy is a function of the sum of all past Q-functions. To avoid indefi-
020            nitely increasing the neural architecture, we study PMD-like algorithms that only
021            keep in memory the last M Q-functions, and show that a convergent algorithm
022            can be derived if M is large enough. This theoretical analysis provides insights on
023            how to utilise a fixed budget of Q-functions to reduce catastrophic forgetting in the
024            policy. We implement this algorithm using a new neural architecture that stacks
025            the last M Q-functions as 3-dimensional tensors to allow for fast GPU computa-
026            tions. StaQ, the resulting algorithm, is competitive with state-of-the-art deep RL
027
               baselines and typically exhibits lower variance in performance. Beyond its per-
               formance, we argue that the simplicity and strong theoretical guarantees of StaQ’s
028
               policy update makes it an ideal research tool over which we can further build a
029
               fully stable deep RL algorithm.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Continual Learning (CL) moves from the usual i.i.d assumption of supervised learning towards a
035   more general assumption that data distributions change through time (Parisi et al., 2019; Lesort
036   et al., 2020; De Lange et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024). A CL setting usually goes in pair with mem-
037   ory constraints, limiting the storage of past data. As it cannot access old data, it is desirable for a
038   CL learner to possess i) stability, which is the ability to retain prior knowledge ii) plasticity, which
039
      is the ability to learn new knowledge. When training a neural network (NN), these two properties
      are typically conflicting and we talk of the plasticity-stability dilemma (Grossberg, 1988), which if
040
      not handled properly can lead to catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989), i.e. a sudden
041
      loss of prior knowledge when trying to acquire new knowledge. To fight catastrophic forgetting,
042   CL applies methods that can be categorized in three groups (De Lange et al., 2021): a) rehearsal
043   methods (Robins, 1995; Rolnick et al., 2019) that keep samples from older data distributions to peri-
044   odically retrain a learner on them, b) regularization-based methods (Li & Hoiem, 2016; Kirkpatrick
045   et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017), that prevent an NN from changing too fast and c) parameter isola-
046   tion methods (Rusu et al., 2016; Xu & Zhu, 2018; Yoon et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019) that grow an
047   NN, usually while freezing older weights, to increase plasticity without compromising on stability.
048   Somewhat independently, deep RL has known rapid development in the past decade, achieving
049   super-human results on several decision making tasks (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2016; Wur-
050   man et al., 2022). RL is not always a true CL setting in the sense that an agent may not need
051   to continually adapt to remain optimal, as most environments considered in RL are of a station-
052   ary nature, unlike in CL (Abel et al., 2023). However, deep RL and CL both face non-stationary
053   datasets. This non-stationarity coupled with the use of neural networks makes deep RL very sen-
      sitive to hyper-parameters (Henderson, 2018) and makes its empirical behavior often poorly align

077   remains largely understudied.
078   In this paper, our main inspiration from the CL research community is the use of growing neural
079   architectures to implement our policy update. Such neural architectures belong to the class of pa-
080   rameter isolation methods in the CL literature, which offer some of the best stability-performance
081   trade-offs (see Sec. 6 in De Lange et al. (2021)). While the policy’s growing architecture eliminates
082   catastrophic forgetting in the policy by construction, it still seems to be present in the Q-function net-
083   work (Sec. 6.3), causing instabilities of the learning process. We provide some ad hoc workarounds
084   in this paper, but we believe several CL approaches—as those discussed in Sec. 7—could be helpful
085
      to further stabilize deep RL, and we hope that one side effect of this paper is to further encourage
      the transfer of ideas between the RL and CL communities.

123   2   R ELATED W ORK
124
125   Entropy-regularization in RL. Entropy regularization has seen widespread usage in RL. It was
126   used with (natural) policy gradient (Kakade, 2001; Schulman et al., 2015), policy search (Deisen-
127   roth et al., 2013), policy iteration (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2019; Zhan et al., 2023) and value iteration
128   methods (Fox et al., 2016; Vieillard et al., 2020). Common choices of regularizers include mini-
129   mizing the DKL between the current and previous policy (Azar et al., 2012; Schulman et al., 2015)
130
      or encouraging high Shannon entropy (Fox et al., 2016; Haarnoja et al., 2018), but other forms of
      entropy regularizers exist (Lee et al., 2019; Alfano et al., 2023). We refer the reader to Neu et al.
131
      (2017); Geist et al. (2019) for a broader categorization of entropy regularizers and their relation to
132
      existing deep RL methods. In this paper, we use both a DKL penalization w.r.t. the previous policy
133   and a Shannon entropy bonus in a policy iteration context. In Vieillard et al. (2020), both types of
134   regularizers were used but in a value iteration context. Abbasi-Yadkori et al. (2019); Lazic et al.
135   (2021) are policy iteration methods but only use DKL penalization. Finally, Zhan et al. (2023) is in
136   the same setting as our work but is mostly theoretical. In this paper, we study a family of practical
137   deep RL algorithms that extend the analysis of the approximate algorithms in Zhan et al. (2023),
138   and provide both a theoretical analysis and an extensive empirical evaluation of these algorithms.
139   Continual Learning (CL) and RL. In CL a learner faces non-stationary data distributions (com-
140   monly referred to as tasks) and aims at obtaining good performance (e.g. classification accuracy)
141   on the newest task without hindering performance on previous tasks (De Lange et al., 2021; Wang
142   et al., 2024). RL and CL connect in several ways: some continual supervised learning settings can
143   be formulated as RL problems (Kumar et al., 2023), while RL can be extended to the CL setting by
144   having non-stationary rewards or environment dynamics (Rusu et al., 2016; Khetarpal et al., 2020).
145   Even when the rewards and dynamics are stationary—setting which we refer to as single task RL—
146   data in RL can still arrive in a non-stationary fashion as in CL. As such, both RL and CL can end-up
147
      using surprisingly similar approaches, as discussed in the previous section. Nonetheless, not all
      research in CL might be relevant to single task RL and vice versa. For example, in RL, the learner
148
      can (indirectly) control the data distribution, providing additional mechanisms to reduce catastrophic
149
      forgetting which are not always possible in CL (see Sec. 6.3 for an example).
150
151   Growing neural architectures in RL. Saving past Q-functions was investigated in the context of
152   policy evaluation. In Tosatto et al. (2017), a first Q-function is learned then frozen and a new
153
      network is added, learning the residual error. Anschel et al. (2017) extended DQN by saving the
      past 10 Q-functions, and using them to compute lower variance target values. Both Girgin & Preux
154
      (2008) and Della Vecchia et al. (2022) use a special neural architecture called the cascade-correlation
155
      network (Fahlman & Lebiere, 1989) to grow neural policies. The former work studies such policies
156   in combination with LSPI (Lagoudakis & Parr, 2003), without entropy regularization. The latter
157   work is closer to ours, as it uses a DKL -regularizer but does not include a deletion mechanism. As
158   such the policy grows indefinitely, limiting the scaling of the method. Finally, Abbasi-Yadkori et al.
159   (2019) save the past 10 Q-functions to compute the policy in Eq. 1 for the specific case of β = 1,
160   but do not study the impact of deleting older Q-functions as we do in this paper. Growing neural
161   architectures are more common in the neuroevolution community (Stanley & Miikkulainen, 2002).
      Such evolutionary approaches were already used for RL tasks but are beyond the scope of this paper.

353   5    S TACKED N EURAL N ETWORKS
354
355   To study finite-memory EPMD in the memory regimes suggested by Thm. A.6, we propose the
356   Stacked NN architecture (SNN, illustrated in Fig. 1) that makes efficient use of GPUs to compute
357
      multiple Q-values in parallel, and we call the resulting algorithm StaQ. An SNN is parameterized by
      M , the maximum number of networks kept in memory. The main operation of an SNN is a push
358
      function, that “stacks” an input NN into an SNN. The stacking consists in building tensors with one
359
      extra dimension for every weight matrix of the NN Q-function. For instance, let Ak be a 256 × 256
360   weight matrix of a hidden layer of (the neural approximation of) Qkτ . Let Ak be a k × 256 × 256
361   tensor of the SNN that stores all past Ai weight matrices for i ≤ k. In the forward pass of the SNN,
362   to compute past Q-functions Qiτ for i ≤ k, we use the batch matrix operation of PyTorch (Paszke
363   et al., 2017) on Ak to make full use of the GPU parallel computing power. Finally, if the SNN
364   contains more than M NNs after a push operation, the oldest NN is deleted from the SNN in a
365   ”first in first out” fashion. This push operation of SNNs currently supports multi-layer Perceptron
366   NN architectures only. We leave the extension to other neural architectures such as CNNs (LeCun
367   et al., 2015) or LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) to future work.
368   To further reduce the impact of a large M , we pre-compute ξk for all entries in the replay buffer1 at
369   the start of policy evaluation. The logits ξk are used to sample on-policy actions when computing
370   the targets for Qkτ . As a result of the pre-computation, during policy evaluation, forward and back-
371   ward passes only operate on the current Q-function and hence the impact of large M is minimized.
372   Beyond policy evaluation, computing ξk is also used in data collection by the behavior policy πkb
373   that typically relies on computing πk (see Sec. 6). Conversely, the policy update consists only of the
374
      above push operation, and thus, is optimization free and (almost) instantaneous. Table 1 shows the
      training time of StaQ over 5 million steps as a function of M for two environments. Varying M (up
375
      to 500) or the size of the state space has little impact on the runtime of StaQ when running on GPU.

475
476   According to Sec. 4.2, M is a crucial parameter that should be large enough to guarantee the con-
477   vergence. The M estimation obtained from Thm. A.6 may be very conservative in practice. In

502   6.4   E NTROPY REGULARIZATION DOES NOT SOLVE EXPLORATION IN RL
503
504   StaQ is generally competitive with the state-of-the-art, but it might fail on some environments such
505   as MountainCar or a few MinAtar environments. These environments are considered as hard explo-
506   ration ones by Ceron & Castro (2021), and the combination of a deterministic policy with ϵ-greedy
507   exploration seems to fare better in some tasks than the softmax policies of StaQ, PPO and TRPO, as
508
      also discussed by Vieillard et al. (2020), App. B.2. This is especially evident in MountainCar, where
      the initial maximum-entropy policy that picks an action uniformly at random always observes the
509
      same reward and remains close to the uniform policy throughout the learning process. To address
510
      this challenge, we have experimented with ϵ-greedy behavior policies in MountainCar as discussed
511   in App. B.5, which improves performance. Although we used a fixed entropy weight for all experi-
512   ments, we found that on the harder MinAtar environments, tuning the values of the entropy weight
513   can improve performance, as presented in App. B.5. Overall, we believe that exploration is an or-
514   thogonal problem to the stability of the policy update we address in this paper, and while entropy
515   regularization is often used as an exploration heuristic, we only use its stabilizing properties here,
516   and leave the development of theoretically sound exploration strategies to future work.

518   7     D ISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
519
520   In this paper, we have proposed to stabilize the policy update of approximate policy iteration by
521   averaging over a very large number M of past Q-functions. Surprisingly, even when M is large, the
522   final computational burden is small on modern hardware mostly thanks to the proposed SNN archi-
523   tecture. The resulting policy update has a solid theoretical foundation and clear empirical benefits
524   as it greatly reduces learning instability on several tasks. Yet, while the policy update is more stable,
525   instability in learning the Q-function remains and would benefit from a more thorough continual
526   learning treatment. To prevent the catastrophic forgetting of low probability actions, one promising
527   idea would be to better manage the replay buffer to keep representative samples, as done in CL by
528
      Aljundi et al. (2019) for example. Another interesting line of research from the CL community is
      the dual architecture based on the complementary learning systems theory (e.g. Lee et al. (2016)).
529
      Adapted to RL, the Q-function would be split between a fast learning—but potentially unstable—
530
      component that adapts to the most recent samples and a slower learning component that is stabilized
531   using, for example, growing neural architectures as in this work to avoid catastrophic forgetting. If
532   the neural architecture of the Q-function is much larger than the one used in this paper, it might be
533   relevant to compress periodically the policy during learning as done in CL (Schwarz et al., 2018).
534   Finally, extending StaQ to continuous action domains could be done as in SAC (Haarnoja et al.,
535   2018), using an extra actor network learned by minimizing the DKL to a soft policy. This will lose
536   the optimization-free and exact nature of the policy update but may still result in improved stabil-
537   ity if we replace the soft policy exp(Qkτ ) used by SAC with exp(ξk ) which stabilizes the target by
538   averaging over a large number of past Q-functions.

1764   To understand why adding an ϵ-softmax policy on top of the softmax policy πk stabilizes perfor-
1765   mance on Hopper-v4 as shown in Fig. 4, we have conducted the following experiment. We first
1766   launched two runs of StaQ with an ϵ-softmax policy on top of πk , with ϵ being either 0.05 or 0. From
1767
       these two runs, we collected 100 states spread along both training processes. We then launched 5
       independent runs for each value of ϵ, and recorded for these 100 states the learned Q-values at each
1768
       iteration. Upon manual inspection of the Q-values, we immediately notice that when ϵ = 0, the
1769

1772   lowing the EPMD formula (Eq. 7) and rank the actions according to ξk . At each iteration k, we then
1773   compute the proportion of states, out of 100 reference states, in which an action has both the highest
1774   and the lowest rank in the next iterations k ′ ≥ k. The results are shown in Fig. 11, where we can
1775   see that when ϵ = 0, the fraction of states in which an action is considered as either being the best
1776   or the worst remains higher than when ϵ = 0.05, which might result in performance drops across
1777   iterations. Thus the observed Q-function oscillations that appear more pronounced for ϵ = 0 have
1778
       a quantifiable impact on the stability of the policy, resulting in more states seeing actions switching
       from best to worst or vice versa.

1826
1827   Entropy regularization is a common technique to improve exploration and robustness of the pol-
1828   icy (see e.g. Ziebart (2018); Haarnoja et al. (2017)), however overly-strong regularization can harm
1829   learning. In our main experimental results we fix the initial and final (scaled) entropy weight τ̄
1830   across all environments, however we find that certain environments benefit from adjusting the en-
1831   tropy weight. This is illustrated in the left three panels of Fig. 12, where we vary the entropy
1832   weights, linearly annealed from 5 to 1 (1 to 0.05) for “high ent” (“low ent”) over the first 1 million
1833   timesteps. In addition to environments such as Asterix and SpaceInvaders that absolutely prefer high
1834
       or low entropy, some environments such as Freeway have complex dependence on the entropy that

1838
1839   For MountainCar, we find that having overly stable updates at the start of the training can prevent
1840
       it from finding the sparse reward signal. StaQ can learn on such an environment (see right panel of

1927
       In Continual RL (Lesort et al., 2020), a learner is presented with a sequence of MDPs and a one
       evaluates whether the learner is able learn on the new MDPs while retaining information of older
1928
       ones. In our setting, a learner sees only one MDP and its performance is only measured on this one
1929
       MDP. Studying if knowledge is retained on older tasks or whether knowledge transfers among tasks
1930   is beyond the scope of this paper.
1931
1932   Despite being limited to a single MDP, single-task RL has still strong ties with CL because of the
1933
       sequential nature in which data arrives. As mentioned in Sec. 1, we are not the first to draw this
       parallel, but drawing this connection is interesting because it opens up a plethora of CL methods
1934
       that are not well researched in the deep RL context, but are applicable even in a single task setting.

1936   below (Eq. 5 of the paper)

       G    P SEUDOCODE OF S TAQ
2053
2054
       We provide in this section the pseudocode of StaQ in Alg. 1. As an approximate policy itera-
2055   tion algorithm, StaQ comprises three main steps: i) data collection, ii) policy evaluation iii) policy
2056   improvement. Data collection (Line 4-5) consist in interacting with the environment to collect tran-
2057   sitions of type (state, action, reward, next state) that are stored in a replay buffer. A policy evaluation
2058   algorithm is then called to evaluation the current Q-function Qkτ using the replay buffer. Finally, the
2059   policy update is optimization-free and simply consists in stacking the Q-function in the SNN policy
2060   as discussed in Sec. 5. After K iterations, the last policy is returned.
