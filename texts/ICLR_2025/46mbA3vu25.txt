009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                There is a prevalent opinion 1 that diffusion-based models outperform GAN-based
012                counterparts in the Image Super Resolution (ISR) problem. However, in most
013                studies, diffusion-based ISR models employ larger networks and are trained longer
014                than the GAN baselines. This raises the question of whether the high performance
015                stems from the superiority of the diffusion paradigm or if it is a consequence of
016                the increased scale and the greater computational resources of the contemporary
017                studies. In our work, we thoroughly compare diffusion-based and GAN-based
018                super resolution models under controlled settings, with both approaches having
019
                   matched architecture, model and dataset sizes, and computational budget. We
                   show that a GAN-based model can achieve results comparable or superior to a
020
                   diffusion-based model. Additionally, we explore the impact of popular design
021
                   choices, such as text conditioning and augmentation on the performance of ISR
022                models, showcasing their effect in several downstream tasks.

025   1       I NTRODUCTION
026
      In recent years, the field of Image Super Resolution (ISR) has witnessed significant advancements,
027
      primarily attributed to improvements in image generation frameworks, mostly Generative Adversar-
028
      ial Networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014) and Denoising Diffusion Models (DDMs) Ho et al.
029   (2020).
030
031   GANs introduced the adversarial training framework, which led to their remarkable ability to fit
032
      and recover narrow, unimodal distributions while struggling with complex multi-modal data. This
      trade-off allowed GANs to dominate the area of ISR for half a decade Ledig et al. (2017b); Wang
033
      et al. (2018); Karras et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2021), pushing the boundaries of resulting image
034
      quality, especially for high upsampling factors.
035
036   In contrast, more contemporary DDMs provide a robust framework for image generation, reliably
037   covering multi-modal data distributions. Yet, the diffusion ability to yield high-quality and diverse
038
      outputs comes with a cost of high computational load during training and inference.
039   At the moment, the literature suggests that diffusion models have become a dominant source of state-
040   of-the-art methods in generative modeling, including ISR (Saharia et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021); Yue
041   et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2024)). However, a comprehensive comparison between GAN and diffusion
042   models in the context of ISR under controlled conditions still needs to be explored. In particular,
043   GAN-based methods that modern state-of-the-art methods are compared with are typically repre-
044
      sented by Real-ESRGAN Wang et al. (2021), which has orders of magnitude fewer parameters,
      training data samples, and computational budget.
045
046   This work aims to thoroughly compare GAN and DDM frameworks for Image Super Resolution.
047   Given the unique strengths of both GANs and diffusion models, understanding their comparative
048   performance in ISR tasks is crucial. This comparison requires a rigorous and controlled experimental
049   setup to ensure comparable protocol, i.e., standardized variables such as the size of the training
050
      dataset, model complexity, and controlled allocation of computational resources.
051   Furthermore, we explore the influence of various design choices on the performance and general-
052   ization of these models. In particular, we investigate the value of practically useful Real-ESRGAN

066   2   R ELATED W ORK
067
      GANs for SR Shortly after the introduction of the original GAN paper Goodfellow et al. (2014) for
068
      the general image generation problem, C. Ledig et al. presented SRGAN Ledig et al. (2017b), which
069   introduced perceptual loss, combining content loss (using a pre-trained VGG network Johnson et al.
070   (2016)) with adversarial loss to generate high-resolution images with improved perceptual quality.
071   The following research updated the initial scheme with improved architecture and feature extraction
072   (ESRGAN Wang et al. (2018), ESRGAN+ Rakotonirina & Rasoanaivo (2020), LAPGAN Denton
073   et al. (2015), LSMGAN Mahapatra et al. (2017)), more effective training mechanism (RankSRGAN
074   Zhang et al. (2021b), PGGAN Karras et al. (2017)) more robust discriminator (RaGAN Jolicoeur-
075   Martineau (2018)) or multiple discriminator models (MPDGAN Lee et al. (2019)), and sophisticated
076   data pre-processing strategies to increase robustness to in the wild data (BSRGAN Zhang et al.
077
      (2021a), RealSR Ji et al. (2020), Real-ESRGAN Wang et al. (2021)). These methods built a strong
      foundation for GANs, which have become the de facto standard framework for all kinds of ISR
078
      problems.
079
080   Recent advances in DDMs resulted in ISR models with stunning restoration quality. This remark-
081   able progress is at least partially attributed to scaling up models, data, and computational resources.
082   While novel GAN-based methods make progress in catching up with updating and scaling (Giga-
083
      GAN Kang et al. (2023)), small-scale Real-ESRGAN remains the most commonly used baseline
      model in modern ISR research.
084
085   Diffusion models for SR Recent developments in training and scaling of DDMs resulted in dif-
086   fusion surpassing GANs for the general image generation problem Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) and
087   several new methods aimed to transfer this success to the ISR problem. The pioneering ISR DDM
088   approaches trained image-conditioned DDM from scratch, concatenating the input noise with either
089   the upscaled version of a low-resolution image (SR3 Saharia et al. (2021)) or its hidden repre-
090   sentation produced by a convolutional network (SRDiff Li et al. (2021)). Their impressive results
091
      relegated ISR GANs to the background and inspired the next-generation methods that propose to di-
      rectly sample from the distribution of LR-images rather than the conditioned Gaussian noise (I2 SB
092
      Liu et al. (2023a), ResShift Yue et al. (2023)). Another branch of research aims to incorporate gener-
093
      ative prior of Latent Diffusion Models Rombach et al. (2022) by freezing their generative backbones
094   and training only lightweight ControlNet-based Zhang et al. (2023) modules (StableSR Wang et al.
095   (2023b), DiffBIR Lin et al. (2024), SUPIR Yu et al. (2024)). The latter class of methods typically
096   uses additional condition sources, such as depth maps, segmentation maps, and text.
097
098   Text-conditioned Super Resolution Most papers Yang et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023) generally
099
      report that using additional conditions in ISR DDMs improves the resulting image quality. How-
      ever, the case of text conditioning is more nuanced. Pixel-based natural images-focused ISR models
100
      (SR3 Saharia et al. (2021), SR3+ Sahak et al. (2023)) do not use textual conditioning. At the same
101
      time, several ISR models designed for upscaling inside cascaded diffusion systems, namely, Imagen
102   Saharia et al. (2022), DeepFloyd Shonenkov et al. (2023), Kandinsky 3.0 Arkhipkin et al. (2023)
103   in the 1024 → 4096 upscaler, incorporate textual information via cross-attention. In contrast, a
104   more recent YaART Kastryulin et al. (2024) reports the lack of noticeable quality improvement and
105   removes cross-attention at this stage. Latent diffusion-based ISR models also differ in this regard.
106   StableSR Wang et al. (2023b) adopts the null-text prompt, effectively removing the text condition-
107   ing, and DiffBIR Lin et al. (2024) does not have any text conditioning. However, more recent SUPIR
      Yu et al. (2024) uses a dedicated multi-modal large language model to automatically caption input


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      images with non-ISR specific captions, reporting a noticeable performance boost. SeeSR Wu et al.
109   (2023) goes further and proposes a method to generate ISR-specific captions to boost the model’s
110   generative ability, claiming its effectiveness in upscaling small objects with ambiguous semantics.
111
112
      In this paper, we aim to shed light on the importance of textual conditioning for ISR in a practical
      setup when ISR-specific captions are not accessible, and the model has no intrinsic, generation-based
113
      prior to utilizing textual information.
114
115   Variations of SR task A general SR problem can be formulated as follows. For a given high
116   quality image I HR , one applies some degradation operation D to produce low quality image I LR
117   typically of smaller spatial resolution. This degradation can be a simple bicubic/area downsampling
118   kernel or a more general degradation process. In the task of Blind Super Resolution (BSR) Liu et al.
119
      (2021), which has attracted significant attention recently, one aims to reconstruct low-resolution
      images exposed to unknown and complex degradations. Higher-order degradation models introduced
120
      in Wang et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021a) emulate possible types of corruptions that occur in the
121
      real world.
122
123   3   M ETHODOLOGY
124
      Models Since our goal is to compare GAN and diffusion Super Resolution under a comparable
125
      setup, we use the same architecture for both paradigms considered. Specifically, we adopt a network
126   similar to 256 × 256 → 1024 × 1024 SR model from Imagen (Saharia et al. (2022)). It acts in the
127   pixel domain and is based on Efficient U-Net architecture Ronneberger et al. (2015). This model
128   has 600 − 700M trainable parameters, depending on the configuration. Details about architecture
129   are provided in Appendix C.
130
      GAN-based Super Resolution models take bicubic-upscaled low-resolution image and directly pre-
131
      dict a high-resolution image, given a reference on training.
132
133   Diffusion Super Resolution models predict a noise applied to the high-resolution image, using the
134   bicubic-upscaled low-resolution image as a condition. Following the prior work, the condition image
135
      is channel-wise concatenated with the noisy input.
136   Both models are almost identical in terms of parameters, but for the slight difference in the diffusion
137   model having slightly more parameters due to the concatenation of noisy input and image condition
138   in the input layer and timestep embeddings.
139
      Text conditioning One of the questions addressed in our work is the impact of text conditioning on
140   the performance of Super Resolution models. In our work, we consider two types of text encoders:
141   a CLIP-like (Radford et al., 2021) proprietary encoder transformer model that we call XL with
142   1.3B parameters and UMT5 (Chung et al., 2023) encoder-decoder with 3B parameters. The text
143   caption, corresponding to the image, is first processed via the text encoder and then passed to the
144   Super Resolution model via image-text cross-attention at the lowest resolution of the U-Net encoder,
145   middle block, and decoder. This design choice follows Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022) work.
146   In our work, we study the impact of the prompts with semantic information i. e., the ones that
147   are typically available from large image-text datasets Schuhmann et al. (2022); Gadre et al. (2024),
148   rather than image-quality aware captions adopted in SUPIR Yu et al. (2024) and SeeSR Wu et al.
149   (2023). We believe it is timely to revisit the impact of these non-SR-specific texts due to their
150   prominent availability in recently published image-text datasets Schuhmann et al. (2022); Gadre
151   et al. (2024) used for training large-scale multi-modal models.
152
      Augmentation In our work, we study the impact of train-time augmentations in two setups: Super
153   Resolution of down-scaled images and in case of a more general and complex degradation process.
154   We train GAN and diffusion models with and without the image degradation model proposed in
155   (Wang et al., 2021) to address both scenarios.
156
157   Training Following the standard practice Ledig et al. (2017a); Wang et al. (2021); Saharia et al.
158
      (2021); Sahak et al. (2023), both types of models are trained on image crops. In the ablation study
      in Section 5, we also investigate the impact of finetuning on full-resolution images.
159
160   For GAN-based SR, we initially pretrain the generator model with L1 loss only. This pretraining
161   is essential since training from scratch with adversarial loss yields artifacts in agreement with prior
      work (Ledig et al., 2017a; Wang et al., 2018; 2021). Training with L1 , L2 or any derivative training

165   regularization.

      Inference GAN-based upscalers produce high-resolution images in a single forward pass, whereas

191

197   4      E XPERIMENTS
198   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
199
      Training Dataset We train our models on a large-scale proprietary dataset of 17 million text-image
200
      pairs. The dataset consists of 1024 × 1024 px images with exceptional image quality, high image-
201
      text relevance, and English captions. The high dataset quality is achieved by a rigorous filtering
202   process described in detail in Appendix F.
203
204   We train our models on image crops to ensure training efficiency and sample variety. For that, we
205
      produce low-resolution - high-resolution (LR, HR) image pairs via extracting 256 × 256 px random
      crops of the original images, which are then downscaled to 64 × 64 px. For the finetuning stage, we
206
      interpolate 1024 px images to 256 px. cv2.INTER AREA algorithm is adopted for image resizing.
207
208
      Training Hyperparameters We follow prior work in the choice of hyperparameters Wang et al.
209
      (2021); Sahak et al. (2023) and observe that the training with these choices is stable and converges
210   fast enough. We fix the batch size for GAN and diffusion to ensure fairness of comparison on the
211   number of samples seen during training and adjust the learning rate accordingly. Further increasing
212   the learning rate with the goal of speeding up training leads to training instability and poor final
213   results. For both models, the batch size is set to 512 for training on crops and 128 for full-resolution
214   images. We adopt a constant linear rate with a linear warmup phase. The models are trained with
215   Adam Kingma & Ba (2017) optimizer without weight decay. More details are provided in Ap-
      pendix D.

      While most of the related works adopt a predefined number of training iterations, we set the training
217   duration dynamically and terminate the training only when the visual quality of the images upscaled
218   by adjacent model checkpoints becomes indistinguishable from the humans’ perspective. Since the
219   changes become smaller throughout the training, we use an exponentially spaced grid with check-
220   points from the following list of thousands of training iterations: [10, 20, 40, 60, 100, 140, 220, . . .],
221   where we increase the interval between adjacent two checkpoints by a factor of two. Then, we es-
222   timate the human annotator preference using the Side-by-Side (SbS) evaluation for the current and
223   previous training steps. If there is no improvement for three subsequent measurements, we conclude
224   that the model performance is saturated and proceed to the next stage. We note that the training loss
225
      and full-reference metrics may continue to improve, but the improvement is imperceptible to human
      observers. In our work, user preferences are used both for early stopping and model evaluation. The
226
      SbS comparison setup is described and explained in greater detail in Appendix E.
227
228   Evaluation Datasets We evaluate the performance of the SR models under study on the 244-
229   images dataset constructed from the pictures from RealSR Ji et al. (2020), DRealSR Wei et al.
230   (2020), and some additional high-quality samples from the web. LR 256 px images are ob-
231   tained from HR images with cv2.INTER AREA. Captions for images required as input for text-
232
      conditional models are generated with the LLaVA model Liu et al. (2023b).
233   To estimate the quality of image restoration under more complex and severe corruptions, we use the
234   same dataset but with LR inputs obtained through Real-ESRGAN Wang et al. (2021) degradation
235   pipeline.
236   In addition, we investigate the robustness of ISR models on out-of-distribution synthetic data in
237   Appendix G. For that, we provide a qualitative comparison between GAN and diffusion models
238   together with the baselines on the Super Resolution of downscaled images produced by the YaART
239   Kastryulin et al. (2024) and SDXL Podell et al. (2024) models using prompts from DrawBench
240   Saharia et al. (2022) and YaBasket Kastryulin et al. (2024) prompt sets.
241
242   Baselines Our work’s primary goal is to compare GAN and diffusion Super Resolution rather
243   than establishing a new state-of-the-art in the domain. However, to demonstrate that our models
244   yield quality competitive to the current state-of-the-art, we provide both qualitative and quantitative
245
      comparison with SUPIR Yu et al. (2024), DiffBIR Lin et al. (2024), RealESRGAN Wang et al.
      (2021), ResShift Yue et al. (2023).

247   4.2   T RAINING DYNAMICS
248
      GANs converge faster than diffusion As discussed above, we train the model until the human
249
      annotators do not show a preference for the current training step over the previous one for three
250
      evaluations in a row. To have enough reference points, we train the models such that they cover at
251   least six milestones—[10k, 20k, 40k, 60k, 100k, 140k] iterations.
252
253   We observe that GAN Super Resolution converges very quickly in all stages. After 40k steps of L1
254
      pretraining, the changes in the output of the SR model become imperceptible. Adversarial training
      converges relatively fast as well. After pretraining on crops for 40k steps, the performance of the
255
      Super Resolution model almost stabilizes. Prior works Wang et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021a);
256
      Liang et al. (2021) conduct training for 500k-1500k iterations with batch size ∼ 10 times smaller
257   than the one used in our work. Therefore, the total number of samples seen is of the same order of
258   magnitude as the referenced papers.
259
260
      We note that in our experiments, in addition to worsening computational efficiency, pretraining on
      full-resolution images resulted in worse Super Resolution quality starting from the first thousands
261
      of training iterations. After several attempts to adjust training hyperparameters, we stopped this line
262
      of experimentation.
263
264   GANs are claimed to be prone to mode collapse Roth et al. (2017) and notoriously hard to optimize,
265   requiring careful tuning and regularization for successful training. However, we did not encounter
266
      any difficulties with optimization. After several hundreds of iterations, our GAN models achieved
      equilibrium between the generator and the discriminator and continued to improve steadily until
267
      convergence.
268
269   Conversely, diffusion models exhibit slower convergence, requiring up to 620k iterations of L2
      pretraining on cropped images for the model to fully converge.

363
            AUGMENTATIONS
364
365   Above, all methods were applied to the SR(×4) task, which is essentially an inversion of a fixed
366   downscaling. However, many contemporary works study the task of Blind Super Resolution (BSR),
367   which assumes a more complex degradation model involving multiple, typically unknown corrup-
368
      tions. Real-ESRGAN Wang et al. (2021) is a popular degradation model emulating various degrada-
      tions that occur in low-quality images. Therefore, following the standard practice, we produce LR
369
      inputs via the Real-ESRGAN pipeline.
370
371   In our work, we study the impact of augmentations for performance on a simple SR(×4) task and
372   the problem of more general degradation removal. We train both GAN and diffusion models with
373   Real-ESRGAN augmentations in the same way as for the vanilla SR task.

      7      L IMITATIONS
515
516   This work considers the standard SR task and SR in the presence of corruptions from a predefined
517   degradation pipeline. The evaluation on the real-world low-quality images, such as the ones from
518   RealLR200 Wu et al. (2023) and RealPhoto60 Yu et al. (2024) datasets is left for future work.
519   We also do not consider cases when strong generative capabilities and the ability to sample diverse
520   outputs for a given input are required. We hypothesize that in this setup mode collapse can be an
521   issue for GAN-based models. It is also possible that, in these cases, textual information could be
522   necessary for accurate reconstructions. However, we do not consider these facts or the exact turning
523   point when such model capabilities become required.
524   Another interesting question not covered in our investigation is the study of scaling behavior for both
525   GAN and diffusion SR paradigms with respect to the model size and amount of training data and
526   compute. In preliminary experiments we observed that the training parameters adopted in our main
527   training setup do not transfer immediately to other model sizes and have to be tuned for specific
528   experimental setup.
529
      8      C ONCLUSION
530
531   In our work, we performed a systematic comparison between GAN and diffusion ISR under a fair
532   comparison setup when both approaches are matched in model size and training data available. Our
533   results suggest that GANs can achieve the quality level of modern diffusion models if trained with a
534   similar protocol. At the same time, GAN-based upscalers offer several practical advantages - faster
535   training and single-step inference instead of an iterative denoising procedure.
536   We hope that our work encourages researchers to conduct more careful examination when intro-
537   ducing new methods. Since the amount of available resources and data is constantly growing, more
538   recent works have the advantage of having more compute and higher-quality data relative to the prior
539   art. Therefore, it is vital to identify whether the improvement comes from one approach’s superiority
      over the other or could be attributed merely to the increase of scale.

764   A     BACKGROUND ON GENERATIVE PARADIGMS
765
766   In this section, we provide a mathematical background for the generative models used in our study.
767
768
      A.1    G ENERATIVE A DVERSARIAL N ETWORKS
769
770   To produce samples that resemble a target distribution, one can train an auxiliary critic network,
771   known as a discriminator, to distinguish generated data from real data. The discriminator, denoted
772   as Dθ , is optimized alongside the generator, denoted as Gθ , to solve the following adversarial min-
773   max problem:
774            min max EI out ∼p(I out ) [log DθD (I out )] + EI in ∼p(I in ) [log(1 − DθD (GθG (I in )))]    (5)
775             θG   θD

776   where I in and I out are the input and output spaces respectively. For instance, I in can be the space of
777   random noise or low-resolution images (for the Super Resolution problem) and I out may correspond
778   to the space of natural images. In the case of successful training, the generator learns to produce
779   samples that are difficult to classify by DθD and resemble the target distribution.
780   This approach was introduced in Goodfellow et al. (2014) and subsequently became a leading gen-
781   erative paradigm for an extended period. GANs have demonstrated the capability to generate high-
782   quality, photorealistic samples Karras et al. (2017; 2019; 2020). However, they are known to suffer
783   from issues such as unstable training and mode collapse Wiatrak et al. (2020).
784
785   A.2    D IFFUSION M ODELS
786
787   Diffusion models (Ho et al. (2020)) have emerged as a promising technique in the landscape of
788   generative modeling. These models are primarily inspired by concepts from nonequilibrium ther-
789
      modynamics, leveraging the idea of diffusion processes to gradually transform simple distributions
      into complex data distributions and vice versa.
790
791   The forward process in diffusion models is a mechanism that systematically transforms structured
792   data into noise through a series of discrete steps. Starting with an original data point z0 , this process
793   involves a Markov chain where each step zt given zt−1 is defined by a Gaussian distribution
794
                                                          √
795                                 q(zt |zt−1 ) = N (zt ; αt zt−1 , (1 − αt )I),                             (6)
796
797   with αt controlling the noise level. As the process progresses, the data is incrementally corrupted,
798   ensuring the distribution of zt given z0 remains Gaussian,
799
                                                           √
800                                    q(zt |z0 ) = N (zt ; γt z0 , (1 − γt )I),                              (7)
801               Qt
802   where γt = i=1 αi . This results in zt becoming increasingly dominated by noise, converging to a
803   standard Gaussian distribution as t approaches T . The formulation allows for the derivation of the
804   posterior q(zt−1 |zt , z0 ) = N (zt−1 ; µt , σt2 I), providing a basis for the reverse process that aims to
805   denoise the data back to its original form.
806   The backward diffusion process, or reverse process, in diffusion models, is essential for gener-
807   ating structured data from noise by reversing the forward diffusion steps. Starting from a noise
808   sample zT ∼ N (0, I), this process iteratively denoises the sample through a sequence of steps
809   zT , zT −1 , . . . , z0 , transforming it back into structured data z0 . Each reverse step pθ (zt−1 |zt ) is
      modeled as a Gaussian distribution:

814        forward noise addition. The training objective typically maximizes a variational lower bound on the
815        data log-likelihood, encouraging the learned reverse process to closely match the true reverse pro-
816        cess. The posterior distribution from the forward process q(zt−1 |zt , z0 ) = N (zt−1 ; µt , σt2 I) guides
817        the reverse process’s learning, ensuring that each step effectively reduces the noise incrementally
818        added during the forward process.

892        crops, followed by a constant learning rate of 3e-5. For full-resolution inputs, we decrease the
893        learning rate to 1e-6 and increase the duration of the warmup phase to 10k training steps.

897
           rate of 1e-5.

901

905        a total batch size of 512.

944   F    DATASET P REPARATION
945
946
      We start from a proprietary pool of several billion image-text pairs, initially collected to train image-
      text models. The data pool originally contained samples with various quality images, texts, and
947
      image-text relevance. The following filtering procedure aims to select high-quality samples suitable
948
      for training competitive Super Resolution models.
949
950   First, we select samples with images with exactly 1024 px of height or width and not less than
951   1024 px in the other direction. For non-square images, we perform a center crop of 1024 × 1024 px.
952
      This resolution allows us to achieve a reasonable trade-off between data quality and quantity. After
      that, we pre-calculate a set of scores produced by multiple learned predictors for each image-text
953
      pair. These predictors estimate the quality of images, texts, and image-text relevance.
954
955   Image quality predictors are fully connected classifiers, trained on top of image and text features,
956   extracted by our proprietary visual foundation model. In this work, we use estimators of image
957   quality trained on PIPAL Gu et al. (2020), KonIQ-10k Hosu et al. (2020) and KADID-10k Lin et al.
958
      (2019) datasets, watermark and NSFW content detectors trained on internal data.
959   We use OpenCLIP Ilharco et al. (2021); Cherti et al. (2023) ViT-G/14 to obtain image-text relevance
960   scores.
961
      We train quality predictor and English language detector for text based on our internal foundation
962   model and data.
963
964   After that, we filter the data based on the predictors mentioned above so that the resulting dataset
965
      size does not contain obviously bad samples according to the predictor models. We also aim for
      the resulting dataset size sufficient to train any of our models for up to several dozen epochs. The
966
      resulting dataset consists of 17 million 1024 × 1024 px images and corresponding captions.
