009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Scientific discovery catalyzes human intellectual advances, driven by the cycle of
012            hypothesis generation, experimental design, data evaluation, and iterative assump-
013            tion refinement. This process, while crucial, is expensive and heavily dependent
014            on the domain knowledge of scientists to generate hypotheses and navigate the
015            scientific cycle. Central to this is causality, the ability to establish the relationship
016            between the cause and the effect. Motivated by the scientific discovery process, in
017            this work, we formulate a novel task where the input is a partial causal graph with
018
               missing variables, and the output is a hypothesis about the missing variables to
               complete the partial graph. We design a benchmark with varying difficulty levels
019
               and knowledge assumptions about the causal graph. With the growing interest in
020
               using Large Language Models (LLMs) to assist in scientific discovery, we bench-
021            mark open-source and closed models on our testbed. We show the strong ability
022            of LLMs to hypothesize the mediation variables between a cause and its effect.
023            In contrast, they underperform in hypothesizing the cause and effect variables
024            themselves. We also observe surprising results where some of the open-source
025            models outperform the closed GPT-4 model.

083   2    R ELATED W ORK
084
085   LLMs and Causality. Our work is based on the framework of causality as proposed by Pearl
086   (2009). The intersection of language and causality is explored by Girju et al. (2002); Hassanzadeh
087   et al. (2020); Tan et al. (2023); Dhawan et al. (2024) to extract causal relationships from a large
088   corpus of text. With the advancements in LLMs and their ability to process large contexts, there
089   has been an interest in using them for causal reasoning (Kıcıman et al., 2023). Some works have
090   focused on commonsense causality (Frohberg and Binder, 2021; Singh et al., 2021) and temporal
091   causal reasoning (Zhang et al., 2020; 2022). More recently Kıcıman et al. (2023); Long et al. (2023);
092
      Darvariu et al. (2024) introduced methods to discover causal structures by prompting LLMs with
      variable names. Ban et al. (2023b); Vashishtha et al. (2023); Ban et al. (2023a) extended this work by
093
      introducing ancestral constraints to refine the causal structures derived from LLMs. Abdulaal et al.
094
      (2024) combined data-based deep structural causal models, such as (Yu et al., 2019), with LLMs
095   generated causal structure. Jin et al. (2023b) focused on causal inference using LLMs. While a tool
096   on GitHub PyWhy-LLM used LLMs to propose confounders, our work formalizes such a task along
097   with detailed insights. Recent work attempted to train transformers for improved causal inference
098   and discovery (Vashishtha et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). In contrast to previous work, we focus
099   on the novel task of identifying and hypothesizing missing variables, a task that comes before data
100   collection and evaluation, with LLMs as assistants. We test the hypothesizing abilities of generalist
101   pre-trained LLMs as our task is primarily linked with pre-training knowledge.
102
103   LLMs and hypothesis generation. Existing works tested inductive hypothesis generation with
104   LLMs in reasoning tasks or free-form scientific hypotheses from background knowledge provided in
105   the context (Gendron et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a;b; Qiu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024).
106   In contrast, we consider the structured task of causal hypothesis generation, where the ground-truth
107   variables are known and can be used for evaluation. We also assume a pertinent human-in-the-loop
      assistive scientific discovery setup to counter LLMs’ limitations and hallucinations.

      3    P RELIMINARIES : C AUSAL G RAPH
109
110
      A causal relationship can be modeled via a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). A causal DAG represents
111
      relationships between a set of N variables defined by V = {v1 , ..., vN }. The variables are encoded
112   in a graph G = (V, E) where E is a set of directed edges between the nodes ∈ V such that no cycle
113   is formed. Mathematically it can be expressed as:
114
                            G = (V, E), E = {ei,j | vi , vj ∈ V, i ̸= j} and vi → vj
115
116                                                                           ei,j
      Each edge ei,j ∈ E denotes causal relationship between vi and vj , vi −−→ vj , emphasizing the influ-
117
      ence from vi to vj . Beyond visualization, causal DAGs allow for the mathematical characterization
118   of different node types for a causal model to understand the influences and dependencies.
119
120   We define d(v) as the degree of a node v, representing the total number of edges connected to v.
121
      din (v) is the in-degree, representing the number of incoming edges to v. dout (v) is the out-degree,
      representing the number of outgoing edges from v.
122
123   Sources are variables vs with no incoming edges. Mathematically sources are din (vs ) = 0 where din
124   is the in-degree of the graph.
125   Sinks are variables vk with no outgoing edges. Sinks are dout (vk ) = 0 where dout is the out-degree of
126   the graph.
127
      Treatment are variables vt , characterized as nodes din (vt ) = 0 that are being intervened upon.
      Outcome are variables vy , characterized as the nodes dout (vy ) = 0 that are observed for interventions
128
      from the treatments.
129
      Mediator are variables vm that have both incoming and outgoing edges (din (vm ) > 0 and dout (vm ) >
130   0), acting as intermediaries in the causal pathways between treatment and outcome.
131   Confounder are variables vk that influence both treatment and outcome, exhibiting edges directed
132   towards the treatment and outcome nodes (dout (vk ) ≥ 2. Hence vk is a confounder if it is a parent of
133   both vi and vj .
134   Collider are variables vl that have two edges meeting, and have an in-degree greater than one
135   din (vl ) > 1. Hence vk is a collider if it is a child of both vi and vj .
136
137   Average Treatment Effect. Average Treatment Effect (ATE) quantifies the expected change in the
138   outcome vy caused by the unit change of the treatment vt . ATE is a part of the causal do-calculus
139   introduced by Pearl (2009). We consider binary causal DAGs, i.e., each variable can either take 0 or 1
140   as values.
141                              ATE = E[vy |do(vt = 1)] − E[vy |do(vt = 0)]
142   where the do(·) operator, represents an intervention. The E[vy |do(vt = 1)] represents the expected
143   value of the outcome variable vy when we intervene to set the treatment variable vt to 1 (i.e., apply
144   the treatment), and E[vy |do(vt = 0)] represents the expected value of vy when we set vt to 0 (i.e., do
145   not apply the treatment).
146
147   Mediation Analysis. Mediation analysis is implemented to quantify the effect of a treatment on the
148   outcome via a third variable, the mediator. The total mediation effect can be decomposed into the
149   Natural Direct Effect (NDE) and the Natural Indirect Effect (NIE). The Natural Direct Effect (NDE)
150   is the effect of the treatment on the outcome variable when not mediated by the mediator variable.
151   The Natural Indirect Effect (NIE) is the effect of the treatment variable on the outcome variable when
152
      mediated by the mediator variable.
153                                   NDE = E[vt=1 , vm=0 − vt=0 , vm=0 ]
154
155   Here, NDE is calculated by comparing the expected outcome when the treatment variable is set to
156   1 and the mediator is fixed at the level it would take under the control treatment vt = 0, with the
157   expected outcome when both the treatment and the mediator are set to the control level.
158                                    NIE = E[vt=0 , vm=1 − vt=0 , vm=0 ]
159
160   Here, NIE is calculated by comparing the expected outcome when the treatment variable is set to 1
161   and the mediator is allowed to change as it would under the treatment, with the expected outcome
      when the treatment variable is set to 1 but the mediator is fixed at the control level.

      4       LLM S FOR I DENTIFYING AND H YPOTHESIZING C AUSAL VARIABLES
178
179   In this work, we aim to leverage language models to identify and hypothesize variables in a causal
180
      DAG. Motivated by the process of hypothesizing a causal graph from a partially known structure Gly-
      mour et al. (2019), this paper proceeds under the assumption that some elements of the graph are
181
      already known. The aim is to find additional variables that can be incorporated into the existing
182
      causal structure to enhance the underlying causal mechanism.
183
184   We assume a partially known causal DAG, defined as G ∗ = (V∗ , E), where V∗ ⊆ V. The objective
185   is to identify the set of missing variables V∗ = V \ Vmissing thereby expanding G ∗ to G. This implies
186
      that all causal relationships (edges) among variables in V ∗ are known and correctly represented in
      G ∗ ; i.e., E is fully specified. Here, “missing” variables are not latent or hidden by measurement error
187
      but known unknowns within the causal graph reflective of LLMs perspective.
188
189   Our methodology is structured around progressively challenging scenarios, explores the ability of
190   LLMs to identify and hypothesize causal variables. This starts from a restrictive and controlled
191   exploration to an open-ended one. Initially, we restrict the exploration by providing the language
192
      models with a partially known causal DAG and a set of multiple choices for the missing variables.
      The complexity of the task is gradually increased by removing more than one node from the graph.
193
      Finally, we move to an open-ended scenario where the ground truth is not available to LLM. In
194
      this setting, LLM is required to hypothesize the missing variables of the causal DAG without any
195   explicit hints. We evaluate the causal reasoning capability of LLMs through prompting. Given LLMs’
196   limitation to textual input, we represent the graph G ∗ using a prompt template PLLM (·) which enables
197   LLMs to parse the causal relationships embedded within the DAG.

394
      We then compare the suggestions against the ground truth, acknowledging that in realistic scenarios,
395
      there is often no single ‘ground truth’ for the missing variables. This complexity necessitates careful
396
      evaluation, as we suspect that traditional metrics may not fully capture the performance of models,
397   particularly when suggestions must be assessed within the broader context of the entire causal graph
398   (see Appendix C.5 for more details). Hence, for a robust evaluation of this experiment, we use two
399   metrics, semantic similarity, and LLM-as-Judge that incorporate contextual information.
400   Semantic Similarity: measures the cosine similarity between the embeddings (of another pretrained
401   sentence embedding model) of each suggestion of the model’s predictions, vx∗1:5 and the ground truth
402   vx . The distances of the most similar suggestion are averaged across all nodes vx ∈ V. For a detailed
403   explanation of this process, please refer to Appendix A.4.
404   LLM-Judge: This metric evaluates the quality of the model’s suggestions using a two-step process
405
      inspired by Zheng et al. (2023). In particular, LLM-as-Judge compares against ground truth variables
      to measure contextual semantic similarity beyond semi-exact matching like in semantic similarity
406
      metric. In the first step, the language model is prompted to determine which suggestion best fits the
407
      partial graph, given the ground truth and the suggestions, vx∗1:5 . In the second step, the language
408   model is again prompted to rate the selected suggestion on a scale of 1 to 10 in terms of similarity.
409   This is repeated for all nodes, and the ratings are averaged to provide an overall quality measure.
410   Implementation details can be found in Appendix A.5.

      5.5     TASK 4: I TERATIVELY H YPOTHESIZING IN O PEN W ORLD
457
      In the previous open-world experiment, we observed that LLMs excel at identifying mediators when
458
      the treatments and outcomes are given. This observation could be particularly relevant in medical
459
      settings, where understanding the mediators can provide insights into causal mechanisms through
460   which a treatment affects a patient’s outcome.
461
462   For hypothesizing mediators, we adopted an iterative approach rather than a global (all-at-once)
463
      strategy. This interactive process allows the language model to progressively refine its predictions,
      reducing the search space for subsequent variables. As observed in our empirical results (see
464
      Appendix C.7), LLMs underperform when tasked with making multiple simultaneous predictions
465
      across different mediators. The iterative approach aligns more closely with human reasoning, as
466   evidenced by Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) strategies, where sequential decision-making
467   enhances accuracy.
468
469
      For unordered mediator evaluation, the model is prompted iteratively with mediators presented in
      random order, and the final semantic similarity is averaged across all predictions. In contrast, ordered
470
      mediator evaluation ranks the mediators using the Mediation Influence Score (MIS), prompting
471
      the model in both ascending and descending orders of significance. We introduce the metric ∆,
472   presenting the difference in performance when mediators are iteratively presented to the LLM in
473   ascending and descending orders of significance defined by the MIS. Given that some datasets only
474   contain a single mediator, we selected the Asia, Child, Insurance, and Alarm datasets, as they offer a
475   wider range of mediators, ranging from 1 to 10 for the Alarm dataset.
476
477
      Results. The results of this experiment are in Table 3. Results with variances are provided in
478
      Appendix C.1. In a highly complex environment with more than one node missing and with open-
479
      world search space, we observe that LLMs can still maintain their performance. Unlike the overall
480   consistent performance of GPT-4 across all of the datasets from the open-world setting, the model
481   showed superior performance in Insurance and Alarm datasets only. As the complexity of the dataset
482   increases, we observe larger differences in hypothesizing the mediators according to the MIS order.
483   Positive ∆ values suggest that prompting the LLM based on the MIS metric leads to higher semantic
484   similarity between the mediator hypotheses and the ground truth variables. In summary, we observe
485   that LLMs can be highly effective in iteratively hypothesizing multiple mediators in a DAG, and if
      present, some domain knowledge about the significance of the mediator can boost the performance.

      5.6   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
500
501   The results show that LLMs effectively hypothesize missing variables, particularly mediators, though
502   performance varies with task complexity. Simple tasks, like identifying missing variables from
503   controlled options, had high success rates. Without unified metrics, we focused on relative rankings
504
      across models (Appendix C.2) and observed that no model, including GPT-4, consistently outper-
      formed the others. We hypothesize that the differences in performance across domains may stem
505
      from potential biases within the LLMs. These biases may stem from the models’ training data and,
506
      therefore, its parametric memory, leading to disparities in how effectively the models handle different
507   tasks introduced in the benchmark. For instance, the models’ ability to hypothesize confounders
508   varied significantly across datasets. In some cases, such as the Sachs dataset (see Appendix B),
509   domain-specific knowledge gaps may have led to lower accuracy.
510
      While this paper aimed to evaluate the ability of current LLMs to identify and hypothesize variables
511
      in a partial causal graph, we attempted to improve the performance by fine-tuning the model and
512
      few-shot prompting. However, given the limited size of the DAGs used, the resulting datasets were
513   small, leading to mixed results (see Appendix D.1). We suspect that while fine-tuning may help the
514   model to specialize, it can also reduce its ability to leverage the general parametric knowledge (Yang
515   et al., 2024). Future approaches can look at domain-specific fine-tuning.
516
      Given the non-disclosed datasets of models, it is difficult to confirm with absolute certainty that
517
      the datasets are not ingested by models during training. However, one of the datasets we used was
518
      released recently (Abdulaal et al., 2024) after the announced cut-off date of models. Additionally, our
519   task itself is novel, including the way we verbalize the graphs and prompt the models. Additionally,
520   in Table 3 we further demonstrate that LLM-generated suggestions are non-verbatim, indicating they
521   generate novel hypotheses rather than retrieving memorized patterns. Finally, we did not observe any
522   direct reconstruction of graphs that would suggest memorization.
523
      Our setup assumes known edges among missing variables to enable controlled evaluation, which
524
      future work can extend. We envision this as a human-LLM collaboration under expert supervision,
525   as LLMs cannot automatically identify the most plausible answer or express confidence in their
526   responses (Zhou et al., 2024). Future work could explore better filtering mechanisms and improve
527   performance on source and sink nodes.
528
529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
531   Most causality literature assumes that the necessary data has been collected, focusing on establishing
532   causal relationships between variables. However, generating hypotheses about which variables
533   to observe is typically done by human experts. LLMs, trained on large-scale datasets, can act as
534   expert proxies for this task. We introduce the novel task of using LLMs to hypothesize missing
535   variables in causal graphs, formalizing it with benchmarks that vary in difficulty and knowledge of
536
      the ground truth graph. We evaluate models on identifying missing variables from in-context and
      out-of-context distractors and hypothesizing variables in an open-world setting. We also explore an
537
      iterative approach for populating graphs with up to 10 missing mediator nodes. Our results show
538
      that LLMs are particularly effective at hypothesizing mediators, which are often less known than
539   treatments and outcomes. To support further research, we will release our benchmark and codebase.

799   A.4   S EMANTIC S IMILARITY
800
801
      Given the task of hypothesizing missing nodes in a partial graph G ∗ in the absence of multiple-
      choices, we evaluate the semantic similarity between the model’s predictions and the ground truth
802
      node variable. We leverage an open model namely ’all-mpnet-base-v2’ to transform the textual
803
      representations of the model’s predictions and the ground truth into high-dimensional vector space
804   embeddings. Post transforming textual representations into embeddings and normalizing them, we
805   calculate the cosine similarity. Scores closer to 1 indicate a high semantic similarity, suggesting the
806   model’s predictions align well with the ground truth. This metric gives a score of similarity without
807   the contextual knowledge of the causal graph. We perform our experiments to consider every node of
808   the ground truth as a missing node iteratively. For all the suggestions for a node variable, we calculate
809   the semantic similarity. The average similarity reported is the highest semantic similarity for each of
      the variable suggestions.

       C.7   I TERATIVE MEDIATOR SEARCH VS ALL AT ONCE
1343
       For Task 4, we iteratively hypothesize the missing variables (mediators). Our choice was primarily
1344   driven by the complexity of Task 4, which involves predicting multiple missing mediators, ranging
1345   from 1 to 10. For a Task with 10 missing mediators, the model would have to predict 50 suggestions
1346   at once. We initially hypothesized that LLMs might struggle with making multiple predictions across
1347   different variables simultaneously. This was indeed reflected in our results and GPT-4 outputs from
1348   Table X. The iterative approach allows the model’s prediction to narrow the search space, which
1349   would not be possible in a non-iterative approach. This method is more aligned with the scientific
       discovery process, where hypotheses are often refined iteratively based on new findings. Furthermore,


                                                           25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       our approach simulates a human-in-the-loop scenario, where the most plausible answer is selected
1351   and used to guide the next prediction.

       The causal sufficiency of G, by definition, implies that for every pair of variables within V, all
2109   common causes are also included within V. Extending this assumption to G ∗ , we assume that the
2110   partial graph inherits causal sufficiency for its given that all edges among these variables are preserved
2111   as in G. This preservation ensures that the observed relationships within V ∗ are not confounded
2112   by omitted common causes. Since the faithfulness of G ensures that the observed conditional
2113   independencies among variables in V are accurately reflected by the causal structure represented
2114   by E. By maintaining the same set of edges E in G ∗ for the subset V ∗ , we uphold the faithfulness
2115   assumption within the partial graph.

2117   H     NDE AND NIE
2118
2119   Average Treatment Effect (ATE) quantifies the expected change in the outcome vy caused by the unit
2120   change of the treatment vt . ATE is part of the causal do-calculus introduced by Pearl (2009). We
2121   consider binary causal DAGs, i.e., each variable can either take 0 or 1 as values.
2122
                                   ATE = E[vy |do(vt = 1)] − E[vy |do(vt = 0)]
2123
2124   where the do(·) operator, represents an intervention. The E[vy |do(vt = 1)] represents the expected
2125   value of the outcome variable vy when we intervene to set the treatment variable vt to 1 (i.e., apply
2126   the treatment), and E[vy |do(vt = 0)] represents the expected value of vy when we set vt to 0 (i.e., do
2127   not apply the treatment).
2128
2129   H.1    M EDIATION A NALYSIS
2130
       Mediation analysis is implemented to quantify the effect of a treatment on the outcome via a third
2131
       variable, the mediator. The total mediation effect can be decomposed into the Natural Direct Effect
2132
       (NDE) and the Natural Indirect Effect (NIE). The Natural Direct Effect (NDE) is the effect of the
2133   treatment on the outcome variable when not mediated by the mediator variable. The Natural Indirect
2134   Effect (NIE) is the effect of the treatment variable on the outcome variable when mediated by the
2135   mediator variable.
2136
2137
                                        NDE = E[vt=1 , vm=0 − vt=0 , vm=0 ]
2138
2139   Here, NDE is calculated by comparing the expected outcome when the treatment variable is set to
2140   1 and the mediator is fixed at the level it would take under the control treatment vt = 0, with the
2141   expected outcome when both the treatment and the mediator are set to the control level.
2142
2143
                                        NIE = E[vt=0 , vm=1 − vt=0 , vm=0 ]
2144   Here, NIE is calculated by comparing the expected outcome when the treatment variable is set to 1
2145   and the mediator is allowed to change as it would under the treatment, with the expected outcome
2146   when the treatment variable is set to 1 but the mediator is fixed at the control level.
