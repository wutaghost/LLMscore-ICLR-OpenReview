009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The fuzzy job shop scheduling problem (FJSSP) emerges as an innovative ex-
012            tension to the conventional job shop scheduling problem (JSSP), incorporating a
013            layer of uncertainty that aligns the model more closely with the complexities of
014            real-world manufacturing environments. This enhancement, while enhancing its
015            applicability, concurrently escalates the computational complexity of deriving so-
016            lutions. In the domain of traditional scheduling, neural combinatorial optimization
017            (NCO) has recently demonstrated remarkable efficacy. However, its application
018
               to the realm of fuzzy scheduling has been relatively unexplored. This paper aims
               to bridge this gap by investigating the feasibility of employing neural networks
019
               to assimilate and process fuzzy information for the resolution of FJSSP, thereby
020
               leveraging the advancements in NCO to enhance fuzzy scheduling methodologies.
021            To this end, we present a self-supervised algorithm for the FJSSP (SS-FJSSP).
022            This algorithm employs an iterative mechanism to refine pseudo-labels, progres-
023            sively transitioning from suboptimal to optimal solutions. This innovative ap-
024            proach adeptly circumvents the significant challenge of procuring true labels, a
025            common challenge in NCO frameworks. Experiments demonstrate that our SS-
026            FJSSP algorithm yields results on a par with the state-of-the-art methods while
027            achieving a remarkable reduction in computational time, specifically being two
028            orders of magnitude faster.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The job shop scheduling problem (JSSP) is a well-established combinatorial optimization problem
      (COP) that holds both theoretical significance and practical relevance (Zhang et al., 2024). The
034
      traditional JSSP describes the processing time in the form of crisp number. However, in real-world
035
      manufacturing scenarios, numerous uncertain factors, such as human variability (He et al., 2021)
036   and machine flexibility (Huang et al., 2024), often preclude the accurate specification of processing
037   times. To overcome this limitation, the fuzzy JSSP (FJSSP) has emerged and is attracting increas-
038   ing attention in the field (Abdullah & Abdolrazzagh-Nezhad, 2014; Lin, 2002). Specifically, in
039   the FJSSP, processing times are represented as fuzzy numbers. These uncertainties diminish the
040   practical applicability of the JSSP.
041
      The existing algorithms for FJSSP are mainly heuristic algorithms (Gendreau & Potvin, 2005). Li
042   et al. (2023) developed a bi-population balancing multiobjective evolutionary algorithm for dis-
043   tributed flexible FJSSP. Gao et al. (2020) devised a differential evolution algorithm with an in-
044   novative selection mechanism to to more effectively tackle FJSSP. Li et al. (2020) engineered an
045   enhanced artificial immune system algorithm to address flexible FJSSP. Sun et al. (2019) crafted
046   an effective hybrid cooperative coevolution algorithm aimed at minimizing the fuzzy makespan of
047   flexible FJSSP. The integration of particle swarm optimization and genetic algorithms significantly
048   bolstered the convergence capabilities of the proposed algorithm. Wang et al. (2022) utilized fuzzy
049   relative entropy to transform a multiobjective optimization FJSSP into a single-objective optimiza-
050
      tion problem and designed a hybrid adaptive differential evolution algorithm to resolve it. Pan et al.
      (2021) concentrated on the energy-efficient flexible FJSSP and developed a bi-population evolution-
051
      ary algorithm with feedback mechanisms to address it effectively.
052
053   The surge in deep learning has catalyzed the emergence of neural combinatorial optimization (NCO),
      sparking a burgeoning interest in leveraging learning-based approaches to solve combinatorial op-

055   forays into this domain focused on foundational COPs. Vinyals et al. (2015) were trailblazers with
056   the pointer network, a novel neural network architecture specifically designed for the traveling sales-
057   man problem (TSP), thereby underscoring the potential of neural networks in addressing COPs.

      developed a deep reinforcement learning model that autonomously learned JSSP priority dispatch

067   ing dispatch rules. Kotary et al. (2022) presented a deep learning strategy that yielded efficient and
068   accurate JSSP approximations; they integrated Lagrangian duality to manage problem constraints,
069   showcasing the potential for generating high-quality JSSP solutions with minimal computational
070   overhead. Corsini et al. (2024) explored a self-supervised training strategy for JSSP, training gen-
071   erative models on multiple solution samples and using the optimal solution as a pseudo-label, thus
072   eliminating the need for costly ground-truth solutions and overcoming challenges associated with
073   supervised learning. However, all these studies are predicated on deterministic environments, and
074
      to date, no research has addressed the fuzzy scheduling problem using learning approaches. In this
      paper, we aim to investigate whether neural networks can assimilate fuzzy information and apply it
075
      to solve the FJSSP, thereby integrating the advancements of NCO into fuzzy scheduling.
076

078

083            the realm of NCOs.
084          • Experimental results indicate that the SS-FJSSP algorithm matches the performance of the
085            state-of-the-art methods and significantly reduces computation time, with speeds up to 100
086            times faster.

090   2.1   F UZZY NUMBER
091
092   Fuzzy numbers are used to represent the processing time for fuzzy scheduling, which is described
093   in this section.
094   In manufacturing environments, precise processing times are often elusive due to variables such as
095   the diverse skill levels of workers (Shao et al., 2024). While exact durations may not be predictable,
096   experts can often draw on historical data to provide estimated durations (Itoh & Ishii, 1999). To
097   address this unpredictability, a prevalent strategy involves estimating within confidence intervals.
098   When certain values are more likely, opting for a fuzzy interval or number becomes an appropriate
099   choice (Fortemps, 1997).

101   is defined as Sα = {x ∈ R : µS (x) ≥ α}, α ∈ (0, 1], and the support is S0 = {x ∈ R : µS (x) >
102   0}. A fuzzy interval is delineated by its α-cuts being confined, and a fuzzy number Ñ , with a
103   compact support and a pronounced modal value, is depicted by closed intervals Ñα = [nα , n̄α ].

134   2.2   FJSSP
135
136
      The FJSSP (Vela et al., 2020) involves a collection of jobs, machines, and operations. Specifically,
      there are n jobs denoted by set J , m machines represented by set M, and N operations within set
137

138

139   a binary relationship → that forms chains for each job. If operation i precedes j (i → j), they share
140   the same job Ji = Jj , and no other operation x can exist such that i → x or x → j. Let S be the set
141   of scheduling scheme; The objective of FJSSP is to minimize the fuzzy makespan, i.e., to find the
142   fuzzy start time s̃i for each operation i ∈ O to minimize the following objective over all possible

191   3     SS-FJSSP
192
193
      The SS-FJSSP framework is comprised of two main components, namely an encoder and a de-
194   coder. The role of encoder is to assimilate and comprehend the FJSSP information from a holistic
195   standpoint, capturing the broader context and constraints. Subsequently, the decoder leverages the
196   insights gleaned by the encoder to make informed, sequential decisions, breaking down the complex
197   scheduling problem into manageable steps. Moreover, it is essential to preprocess the features of the
198   raw data prior to encoding to enhance the learning process. Each component is described in detail
199   below.

      3.4   F URTHER ANALYSIS
358
      Every COP can be envisioned as a decision-making process occurring on a graph (Korte et al., 2011),
359
      a foundational concept for the SS-FJSSP algorithm. This algorithm iteratively refines its decision-
360   making skills on such graphs. The process is analogous to human decision-making: once the prob-
361   lem is understood, decisions are made incrementally, with each subsequent decision informed by
362   the consequences of its predecessors to enhance accuracy. In this framework, comprehending the
363   problem is the role of the encoder, considering the impact of previous decisions is the function of
364   the state network, and the act of making decisions is the task of the decision network.
365
      Next, the crux of the challenge is teaching the SS-FJSSP algorithm to learn the correct scheduling
366   strategy, enabling it to identify the optimal solution once the problem is understood. This is a feat
367   that humans are currently unable to achieve. The primary obstacle is the NP-Hard nature of the
368   problem, which means an insufficient number of correct labels for the SS-FJSSP to learn from. To
369   overcome this, we draw on the idea of genetic algorithm (Mitchell, 1998): instead of learning the
370   true labels directly, we start with suboptimal labels and gradually approximate the true labels. The
371   core of what makes this strategy work is that even though we do not know if a solution is a true
372   label, for any two solutions, we can distinguish which of them is better (according to the magnitude
373   of the fuzzy makespan).
374   Specifically, initially, the SS-FJSSP starts without any prior knowledge, randomly generating solu-
375   tions as potential alternative labels. From these, the most optimal solution is chosen as the pseudo-
376   label, allowing the algorithm to learn. Subsequently, armed with the knowledge acquired previously,
377   the algorithm shows a tendency to predict better alternative labels, leading to the selection of an
      improved pseudo-label. Repeating this process, ideally, the true label will be selected as the pseudo-


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      label, and the algorithm will eventually learn to make decisions that correspond to it. However, the
379   ideal scenario described above is so perfect that it is nearly unattainable in practice without intro-
380   ducing perturbations. The rationale behind this is that the aforementioned process is essentially akin
381   to a genetic algorithm that employs only crossover without any mutation. Ideally, we anticipate that
382   a superior solution would consistently generate new solutions that are at least as good as the existing
383   ones, thus eventually leading to the global optimum. However, this ideal condition is met if and only
384   if the solution space possesses particularly advantageous properties. In reality, the solution spaces
385   of COPs or neural networks are often complex and multi-modal (LeCun et al., 2015). Consequently,
386   we introduce a perturbation to emulate the role of mutation, which aids the algorithm in escaping
387
      local optima and identifying the global optimum, or the true label, as the pseudo-label.
388   Finally, it becomes evident that with minimal adjustments, this algorithm can be extended to other
389   COPs. This adaptability stems from the fact that any COP can be effectively solved using GNNs
390   once the problem is understood. In terms of decision-making, for any given COP, we are capable
391   of determining the superior label, thereby approximating the true label as outlined in our strategy.
392   Thus, this approach can be regarded as an innovative paradigm for addressing COPs, complement-
393
      ing existing methodologies such as mathematical methods (Ku & Beck, 2016), heuristic methods
      (Van Laarhoven et al., 1992), and reinforcement learning methods (Zhang et al., 2020).

412
413   In the encoder, we utilize two GATs, each equipped with 3 attention heads and a LeakyReLU slope
414   of 0.15. In GAT1 , the size of each head is set to 64 and their outputs are concatenated. In GAT2 , the
415
      size of each head is set to 128 and their outputs are averaged. Therefore, h = 18 + 128 = 146 and
      ei ∈ R146 . In the state network, the size of each head in MHA layer is set to 64 and their outputs are
416
      concatenated. This results in parameter matrices W1 ∈ R11×192 and W2 ∈ R192×128 . Therefore,
417
      d = 128 and si ∈ R128 . In the decision network, the FNN is implemented by a dense layer of 128
418   neurons, with a final layer of 1 neuron and Leaky-ReLU (slope = 0.15) activation function.
419
420   We utilize the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) for training the SS-FJSSP algorithm. The training
421
      parameters are set as follows: the number of epochs to 30 and the learning rate to 0.0002. The batch
      size is configured at 16. Regarding the number of parallel solutions, denoted by α, we set it to 128
422
      during training and increase it to 2048 for testing. Additionally, the perturbation probability, Pp , is
423
      established at 0.05.

727   B     P ERFORMANCE ON THE BENCHMARKS OF L EI AND LP
728
729   The Lei-benchmark (Lei, 2010b) and the LP-benchmark (Li & Pan, 2013) are specifically developed
730
      for fuzzy scheduling purposes, rather than being derived from fuzzifying crisp problems. These
      benchmarks, despite their compact size, offer a high degree of complexity, often requiring consider-
731
      able computational resources for the CP method to find solutions. Although the SS-FJSSP algorithm
732
      does not always match the solution quality of CP, it provides nearly identical solutions with remark-
733   able efficiency. The experimental findings are detailed in Table 4.
734
735   The slightly inferior solution quality produced by SS-FJSSP may be due to the fact that the training
736
      set, which is randomly generated, does not fully capture the intricacies of these carefully designed
      instances. It is expected that with an improved training set that more accurately reflects the com-
737
      plexity of these benchmarks, SS-FJSSP will be able to produce more satisfactory results.

740   C     P ERFORMANCE ON THE BENCHMARKS OF ABZ AND ORB
741
742   Both the ABZ-benchmark (Palacios et al., 2016) and the ORB-benchmark (Zheng et al., 2011) orig-
743   inate from the fuzzification of highly intricate original problems, retaining their inherent complexity
744   in the fuzzified versions. As depicted in Table 5, while no instance of SS-FJSSP have been able
745
      to match the results of CP, the discrepancy is minimal, and the SS-FJSSP still holds a significant
      advantage in terms of running time. The suboptimal performance in solution quality is also likely
746
      attributable to the training set’s insufficient complexity, which fails to encapsulate the full intricacy
747
      of these benchmarks.
