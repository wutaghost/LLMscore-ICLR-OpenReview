                                                      A BSTRACT
008
009
                We find that the cross-entropy loss curves of neural language models empirically
010             adhere to a scaling law with learning rate (LR) annealing over training steps:
011
                                           L(s) = L0 + A · S1−α − C · S2 ,
012
013             where L(s) is the validation loss at step s, S1 is the area under the LR curve, S2 is
014             the LR annealing area, and L0 , A, C, α are constant parameters. This formulation
015
                accounts for two main effects: (1) power-law scaling over data size, and (2) the
                additional loss reduction during LR annealing. Unlike previous studies that only
016
                fit losses at final steps, our formulation captures the entire training curve, allowing
017
                for parameter fitting using losses from any training step. Applying the scaling law
018             with LR annealing and fitting only one or two training curves, we can accurately
019             predict the loss at any given step under any learning rate scheduler (LRS). This ap-
020             proach significantly reduces computational cost in formulating scaling laws while
021             providing more accuracy and expressiveness. Extensive experiments demonstrate
022             that our findings hold across a range of hyper-parameters and model architectures
023             and can extend to scaling effect of model sizes. Moreover, our formulation pro-
024             vides accurate theoretical insights into empirical results observed in numerous
025             previous studies, particularly those focusing on LR schedule and annealing. We
026
                believe that this work is promising to enhance the understanding of LLM training
                dynamics while democratizing scaling laws, and it is helpful to guide both re-
027
                search and industrial participants in refining training strategies for further LLMs.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have garnered significant academic and industrial
033   attention (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023). The scaling law suggests that the validation
034   loss of language models follow a power-law pattern as model and data sizes increase (Hestness
035   et al., 2017; Kaplan et al., 2020; Henighan et al., 2020). This law provides a powerful framework
036   for forecasting LLM performances before large scale training by fitting losses at smaller scales
037   (OpenAI, 2023; DeepSeek-AI, 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). Numerous studies have explored on the
038
      formulation to model the scaling effect of LLMs under various different settings (Bahri et al., 2021;
      Hernandez et al., 2021; Caballero et al., 2022; Michaud et al., 2023; Muennighoff et al., 2023).
039
040   However, typical scaling law formulations focus only on the final loss at the end of training (Hoff-
041   mann et al., 2022). Specifically, previous approaches generally rely on a set of training runs and
042   fit the scaling law curve solely on the final loss from each run, while ignoring middle losses during
043   training which do not follow traditional scaling laws. This approach underutilizes the training com-
044
      pute and fails to capture the training dynamics within each run. Further, the application of scaling
      laws in LLM developments is limited since the loss curve through the whole training process is
045
      not modeled. An expressive formulation that models full loss curves enables prediction of future
046
      training dynamics and also offers insights on understanding the learning process of LLMs.
047
048   In this study, we propose a scaling law that models the full loss curve within a complete LLM
049   training run. Specifically, we dive deeper into the training dynamics during LR annealing, and
050
      incorporate a LR annealing factor into the traditional scaling law formula to formulate the process.
      This design is motivated by the observed correlation between LRS and loss curves, where loss
051
      gradually decreases as we consume more training steps 1 and then sharply declines when the LR

      We validate our proposed equation through extensive experiments and find that: (1) Our formulation
096   performs consistently well across various hyper-parameters and model architectures; (2) Eq. 1 can
097   be extended to incorporate other scaling factors, such as model sizes; (3) Our proposed equation
098   accurately fits the loss curves of open-sourced models; (4) Our formulation can be used to verify
099   and explain numerous previous findings regarding LR annealing and scheduling.
100
      In Sec. 3, we derive the scaling law formulation with LR annealing and elucidate the potential theory
101
      underpinning our formulation. Extensive experiments are conducted to validate the formulation. In
102   Sec. 4, we apply our formulation to verify and explain the empirical results from various previous
103   studies. Our approach offers theoretical insights into the crux of loss drop, LR schedule, and LR
104   annealing, enabling LLM participants to better understand training dynamics of LLM and select op-
105   timal training recipes in advance. In Sec. 5, we compare our approach to typical scaling law formula,
106   such as the Chinchilla scaling law (Hoffmann et al., 2022). We show that our formulation is more
107   general and requires significantly less compute to fit, which greatly democratizes the development
      of LLMs and scaling laws.

      2     P RELIMINARY
163
164   Scaling Laws. Cross-entropy loss of language models on the validation set is a reliable indicator
165   of LLMs’ performance on downstream tasks (Caballero et al., 2022; Du et al., 2024). Kaplan et al.
166   (2020) empirically discovered a power-law relationship between validation loss L and three factors:
167   model size N , dataset size D, and training compute. As an application of scaling law, Hoffmann
168
      et al. (2022) developed Chinchilla, a compute-optimal LLM, by balancing model size and dataset
      size. They used a simplified and intuitive scaling law equation: L(D, N ) = L0 +A·D−α +B ·N −β ,
169
      where L0 , A, B, α, β are positive constants. Traditional scaling law formulations fit only the loss
170
      at the final training step, while ignoring losses from other steps. Collecting a new loss value of data
171   size requires launching a another training run with the same LRS, which is resource-intensive.
172
173   Learning Rate Annealing. Learning rate annealing is a widely-used technique in training neural
174
      networks, where the learning rate is progressively reduced from a maximum to a minimum value
      following a pre-defined LRS. Various LRS schemes have been proposed to improve the performance
175
      and stability of model training (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016). For example, the popular cosine LRS
176
      reduces the LR in a cosine-like pattern over full training steps. WSD LRS (Hu et al., 2024) keeps a
177   constant LR for the majority of training, and applies annealing only in the final (e.g. 10% ∼ 20%)
178   steps. In LLM training, it has been widely observed that a more pronounced decrease in the learning
179   rate often results in a more precipitous drop in the validation loss.

243   3.2                       LR A NNEALING M OMENTUM
244
      Another key observation is that LR annealing has momentum. To refine the formulation of f (η),
245
      we design a special LRS where the LR decreases linearly from ηmax to ηmin and then increases.
246
      The increasing stage always has a fixed slope, reaching the maximum value in 5K steps, while the
247   slope of the decreasing stage is varied, with durations of 0.1K, 0.5K, 1K, and 2K. Symmetrically, we
248   design another LRS where the LR increases linearly from ηmin to ηmax and then decreases. Fig. 5
249   shows the corresponding LR and loss curves.
250
      We observe a delay phenomenon between the LR and the validation loss. Firstly, the turning point
251
      of the validation loss curve consistently lags behind the turning point of the LR curve, indicating
252
      that the validation loss continuous along its previous trajectory for some steps even after the LR
253   changes direction. Secondly, the steeper the slope of the LR annealing (or re-warmup), the more
254   pronounced the delay phenomenon becomes. Thirdly, given the same LR slope, the left figure
255   (where LR decreases then increases) consistently shows a longer delay compared to the right figure
256   (where LR increases then decreases).
257
      Interestingly, this phenomenon closely resembles the physical experiment of a small ball rolling
258
      down a slope. The steeper the slope, the faster the ball accelerates. When the ball lands, the accumu-
259   lated momentum causes the ball to slide further. Inspired by this delay phenomenon, we hypothesize
260   that f (η), the loss reduction induced by LR annealing, has cumulative historical formation so that
261   the past change of learning rate will affect the following loss curve for a few steps. In summary,
262   learning rate annealing exhibits momentum. To capture this, we define f (η) = C · S2 , where S2 is
263   calculated as:

276
277   We formally present our formulation for the scaling law with LR annealing:
278   Scaling Law with LR Annealing. Given the same training and validation dataset, the same model
279   size, the same training hyper-parameters such as warmup steps, max learning rate ηmax and batch
280   size, the language modeling loss at training step s empirically follows the equation L(s) = L0 +
281   A · S1−α − C · S2 , where S1 and S2 are defined in Eq. 1. L0 , A, C, α are positive constants.
282
      Our formulation describes the loss of each training step across different LRS. It allows fitting based
283   on a simpler LRS with shorter training steps and enables the prediction of validation losses for more
284   complex LRS with longer training steps. Notably, loss curves with different max learning rates have
285   different values of L0 , A, C, α, and our scaling law does not fit divergent and collapsed loss curves
286   (e.g., overly large LR). We also discuss some possible corner cases (i.e., η = 0) in Appendix H.3.
287

531   7     C ONCLUSION
532
      In conclusion, we propose that the loss curves of neural language models empirically adhere to a
533
      scaling law with learning rate annealing over training steps s: L(s) = L0 + A · S1−α − C · S2 . This
534   equation can accurately predict full loss curves across unseen learning rate schedulers. We present
535   the underlying intuition and theory for deriving our equation and demonstrate that our approach can
536   be extended to capture the scaling effect of model sizes. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our
537   proposed scaling law has good accuracy, scalability, and holds under various experimental setups.
538   It also offers accurate theoretical insights to the training dynamics of LLMs, and explains numerous
539   phenomena observed in previous studies. We believe that the scaling law with LR annealing is
      promising to reshape the understanding of researchers for LLM training and scaling laws.

732   Given a learning rate scheduler, we can easily compute out S1 and S2 of each step in advance.
733   To estimate (L0 , A, C, α), we adopt a similar fitting method as Chinchilla scaling law (Hoffmann
734   et al., 2022). Specifically, we minimize the Huber loss (Huber, 1964) between the predicted and the
735   observed log loss using the L-BFGS algorithm (Nocedal, 1980):
736                                          X
737                                 min           Huberδ log L̂(i) − log L(i)                         (6)
                                  L0 ,A,C,α
738                                                           Step i

739   We implement this by the utilization of minimize in scipy library. Huber loss is to enhance
740   to robustness of the fitting results and we set δ of Huber loss as 1.0 × 10−3 . We mitigate the
741   potential issue of local minima of fitting by choosing the optimal fit from a range of initial conditions.
742   Note that in practice, we can also fit the full loss curves using multiple LRS with a single tuple of
743   (L0 , A, C, α). In this situation, we sum the Huber losses in Eq. 6 of all fitted LRS.

1168     makes loss drop more sharply compared with the stable LR stage. In conclusion, LR annealing
1169     brings out quick increase of the annealing area, resulting in a drastic decrease in validation loss.

1476   Thus L ∝ S2ζ rather than L ∝ S2 might be a more reasonable assumption. That is, our equation
1477   would be changed to L(s) = L0 + A · S1−α − C · S2ζ . Theoretically, the introduction of ζ as an
1478   additional fitting parameter is expected to provide a more nuanced control over how changes in the
1479   learning rate annealing affect validation loss, potentially leading to improve the accuracy of our
1480   equation.
1481   However, the empirical results, as depicted in Fig. 29, demonstrate that the fitting improvement with
1482   the inclusion of ζ is quite marginal when compared to the version without this parameter. This
1483   slight enhancement does not justify the additional complexity introduced by managing negative
1484   values of S2 . Furthermore, the empirical observation that ζ tends to converge close to 1 (e.g. 1.125
1485   in Fig. 29c) reinforces the notion that the original formulation of the function, without the power
1486   term ζ, is adequately robust. This finding suggests that the direct influence of the learning rate
1487   annealing area, as initially modeled, sufficiently captures the essential dynamics without the need
1488   for this additional complexity. Another additional complexity lies in that S2ζ becomes incalculable
1489
       when S2 < 0 in LR re-warmup.
1490   Studies of scaling laws are mostly empirically driven. Over-parameterizing the scaling law equation
1491   essentially leads to more accurate fitting results. However, it will also complicate the final format
1492   and hinders us to focus on major factors for the training dynamics. We choose our main format not
1493   due to absolute prediction accuracy but to pursue the simplification (i.e., fewest extra parameters)
1494   to model the essential training dynamics of LLMs. Notably, in our main format in Eq. 1, we only
1495
       introduce one extra parameter compared to Chinchilla scaling law, i.e., the coefficient C of the S2
       term. As suggested in Sec. 3, our main scaling law format still has a strong and robust capacity
1496
       across many practical scenarios. We believe and expect that there should be more powerful specific
1497
       format (maybe with more parameters) after this work.
