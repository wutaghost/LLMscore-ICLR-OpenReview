009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Medical image analysis is essential to numerous practicals of clinical diagnosis and treat-
012            ment. However, due to the data scarcity and expensive training cost, previous research has
013            largely focused on 2D medical image analysis, leaving 3D medical images under-explored,
014            despite their important spatial information. This paper aims to advance 3D medical im-
015            age analysis by leveraging multi-modal large language models (MLLMs). We propose
016            M3D-LaMed, a generalist MLLM for 3D medical image analysis, specializing in eight
017
               important tasks, including image-text retrieval, report generation, visual question answer-
               ing, positioning, segmentation, etc. The spatial pooling perceiver is proposed to reduce
018
               the 3D tokens, while preserving spatial information. To train the model, we construct the
019
               largest 3D multi-modal medical dataset, M3D-Data, comprising 120K image-text pairs
020            and 662K instruction-response pairs specifically tailored for 3D medical tasks. The 3D
021            multi-modal benchmark, M3D-Bench, is designed, which facilitates the comprehensive
022            evaluation of models across eight tasks. The extensive experiments demonstrate that, as
023            a generalist model, M3D-LaMed shows promising performances and outperforms other
024            specialist models in multiple tasks. With the proposed model, data and benchmark, this
025            work establishes a universal framework that significantly advances the 3D medical image
026            analysis. All data, code and models will be publicly accessible.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Medical practices (Pei et al., 2023) encompass a wealth of multi-modal data that are mainly presented in
031   diagnostic reports and medical images. Paired with medical images, diagnostic reports offer precise descrip-
032   tion and diagnoses, serving as high-quality annotations. How to effectively leverage such multi-modal data
033   to develop models for generic medical image analysis is a challenging but valuable research topic.
034
      Recent progress in natural image-text understanding (Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Ope-
035
      nAI et al., 2023) has highlighted the impressive capabilities of multi-modal large language models (MLLMs)
036   in tasks, such as captioning and visual question answering. They typically integrate vision encoders (Rad-
037   ford et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023) with large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al.,
038   2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2019) and then jointly training
039   on instruction data. As a consequence, MLLMs have garnered much attention of researchers, particularly
040   in medical image analysis. Early works (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al.,
041   2024) have explored building MLLMs for 2D medical tasks, such as report generation and visual question
042   answering on 2D medical images. While these works show promising results, they still struggle with dealing
043   with 3D medical images, such as CT and MRI scans, which are the natural presentation of human body and
044
      contain important spatial information of complicated organs and tissues.
045   In this work, we introduce MLLMs to 3D medical image analysis. Specifically, we propose M3D-LaMed,
046   a generalist 3D MLLM specialized in multiple tasks, including image-text retrieval, report generation, and

      2.2   I NSTRUCTION -R ESPONSE PAIR DATA
125
126   The instruction-response data includes pairs of instructions or questions and their corresponding responses.
127   This data is important for training models to implement multi-modal tasks such as Visual Question Answer-
128   ing (VQA), positioning, and segmentation, totaling 662K instruction-response pairs.
129
      VQA Data: Acquiring medical VQA data is costly due to the need for expert involvement. To reduce
130   expenses, we employed public LLMs to analyze text reports and generate instruction-response pairs using a
131   prompt-based approach (Figure 1(a)). We applied self-filtering techniques to eliminate noisy data, with 7K
132   samples reviewed by 10 experts, resulting in a pass rate exceeding 95% (Table 9). Our findings indicate that
133   a powerful open-source model can efficiently and economically generate accurate Q&A pairs from medical
134   reports. Therefore, we utilized the Qwen-72B model (Bai et al., 2023) instead of ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2019),
135   creating multiple-choice Q&As on five key topics: imaging plane, imaging phase, organ, abnormality, and
136   location (Figure 4), facilitating both open- and closed-ended evaluations.
137   Positioning and Segmentation Data: Positioning and segmentation tasks require integrating images, text,
138   and referring regions, typically as bounding boxes or segmentation masks. We simplify data handling with
139   a unified format of image-mask-text triplets, converting masks to 3D box coordinates for positioning tasks.
140   Given the scarcity of lesion mask annotations in clinical, creating a 3D image-mask-text dataset is resource-

      3.1      M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
167
168   3D Image Encoder: For a given 3D image I ∈ RC×D×H×W , where C, D, H, and W represent channels,
169   depth, height, and width, the image embedding is computed as v = Eimg (I) ∈ Rn×d , where Eimg is the
170   image encoder, n is the number of image tokens, and d is the token dimensions. We use the 3D Vision
171   Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as the encoder, which processes image patches of size PD ∗ PH ∗ PW
172   through its N -layer transformer.
173
      3D Perceiver: To reduce the high computational costs of processing 3D images with LLMs, we propose an
174   efficient 3D spatial pooling perceiver. This module reduces the number of visual tokens and then projects
175   them to the same embedding dimension as LLM (Figure 2(c)). Specifically, the vision encoder’s output to-
176   kens are reconstructed into 3D shape for pooling, reducing token number while preserving spatial informa-
177   tion. Then, a series of Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) adjust embedding dimensions to match the LLM’s
178   input requirements. This approach reduces computational load while retaining essential spatial features.
179
      LLM: Large language models (LLMs) trained on vast natural language corpora provide versatile embed-
180   dings and strong generative capabilities. M3D-LaMed can easily integrate with any advanced LLM. We
181   evaluate several efficient and high-performing LLMs, including Llama-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama-
182   3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024), and Phi3-4B (Abdin et al., 2024), which excel at capturing linguistic patterns and
183   generating coherent text across various domains.
184
      Promptable Segmentation Module: Inspired by LISA (Lai et al., 2023), we utilize MLLMs for referring
185
      expression segmentation via a promptable segmentation module. When a [SEG] token appears in the output,

      3.2     M ODEL T RAINING
222
223
      Setup: We preprocess the 3D CT images using Min-Max Normalization, followed by resizing and cropping
224   to a standard dimension of 32 × 256 × 256. Our 3D vision encoder employs a 3D ViT with 12 layers and
225   a patch size of 4 × 16 × 16, yielding output embeddings of 2048 × 768, representing 2048 tokens with 768
226   feature dimensions each. After applying the 3D spatial pooling perceiver, the final vision tokens fed to the
227   LLM are 256 × 768. All models are trained by AdamW optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014; Loshchilov &
228   Hutter, 2017) with warm-up and cosine decay, and use the bf16 mixed-precision training strategy enabled
229   by DeepSpeed. Training is conducted in parallel across 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80 GB each).
230   Vison Encoder Pre-training: To address the lack of robust 3D medical image encoders, we adopt the CLIP
231   (Radford et al., 2021) architecture and training methodology to pre-train on the M3D-Cap dataset using
232   cross-modal contrastive learning loss (Figure 2(a)). The vision encoder is trained from scratch, while the text
233   encoder is initialized using a pre-trained BERT model (Devlin et al., 2019), which consists of 12 transformer
234   layers and accommodates a maximum text length of 128 tokens. Both encoders use [CLS] tokens for global

                                                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


235
      feature representation, and a linear layer projects these representations into a suitable space for contrastive
236
      training. We use a batch size of 32 × 8 for parallel training across 8 GPUs, with a learning rate of 10−4 .
237
238   MLLM Feature Alignment: We first freeze both the vision encoder and the LLM, fine-tuning only the 3D
239   perceiver to align the vision and language models with image-text pairs from M3D-Cap and M3D-VQA.
240
      This process utilizes a batch size of 16 × 8 and a learning rate of 10−4 .
241   MLLM Instruction Tuning: We fine-tune the vision encoder, 3D perceiver, LLM, and segmentation mod-
242   ule using the complete M3D-Data. When the [SEG] token appears in the output, we apply Dice loss and
243   Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss for segmentation training. To control training costs while preserving the
244   LLM’s original knowledge, we utilize the LoRA strategy (Hu et al., 2021) for parameter-efficient fine-tuning,
245   using a batch size of 8 × 8 and a learning rate of 2 × 10−5 . We set LoRA parameters to r = 16, α = 32, and
246
      a dropout rate of 0.05, with a maximum context length of 512 tokens. The segmentation module initializes
      with parameters from SegVol (Du et al., 2023).

249   4    B ENCHMARK AND E VALUATION
250
251   It lacks proper benchmark for evaluating 3D multi-modal medical tasks. Thus, we construct M3D-Bench
252   for comprehensive evaluation of models across eight tasks which are categorized into five key abilities:
253   image-text retrieval, report generation, VQA, positioning, and segmentation.
254
255   Benchmarking Image-Text Retrieval. In 3D image-text retrieval, the goal is to match images and texts
256   based on their similarity, involving two sub-tasks: text-to-image retrieval (TR) and image-to-text retrieval
257   (IR). For evaluation, we utilize a high-quality subset of 2,000 pairs from M3D-Cap as the test set. This set is
258   stratified into four difficulty levels—easy (100 pairs), medium (500 pairs), difficult (1,000 pairs), and very
259   difficult (2,000 pairs)—based on the size of the retrieval candidate pool. Evaluation metrics include recall at
260   ranks 1, 5, and 10 for both IR and TR, which assess the model’s ability to retrieve relevant images or texts
261
      among the top-ranked results.
262
263   Benchmarking Report Generation. In report generation, the model generates text reports based on in-
264
      formation extracted from 3D medical images. We evaluate performance using a test set of 1,000 image-text
      pairs for user assessment. Given the complexity of evaluating content accuracy between generated reports
265
      and human references, we employ both traditional and LLM-based metrics.
266
267   Traditional metrics include BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), METEOR (Banerjee &
268   Lavie, 2005), and BERT-Score (Zhang et al., 2019), which quantify text similarity through n-gram overlap
269   and variations, although they have limited semantic understanding.
270   LLM-based metrics, such as the GREEN (Ostmeier et al., 2024) score, utilize models with strong semantic
271   comprehension to evaluate the alignment between generated reports and human references. This metric
272   assesses matching content and errors, offering a more comprehensive measure of report quality.
273
274   Benchmarking VQA. The VQA tasks involve generating text-based answers in response to images and
275   questions, categorized into open-ended and closed-ended formats. Open-ended VQA allows unrestricted
276   answer generation, while closed-ended VQA limits responses to a predefined set of choices.
277   We organize M3D-VQA as multiple-choice questions with four possible answers (A, B, C, D). Two test sets
278   are provided: the basic test set, which includes 2,000 3D medical images and 13,791 question-answer pairs
279   across five question types, and the small test set, comprising 1,000 images and 5,000 pairs, also covering the
280   same five types. Results are based on the basic test set, while the small test set facilitates quicker evaluations.
281   After self-filtering to remove low-quality data, expert reviews ensured a pass rate of 96.3%.

282
      For closed-ended VQA, accuracy is assessed by the model’s ability to match answers to provided choices. In
283
      open-ended VQA, the evaluation involves comparing generated answers to reference answers using metrics
284   such as BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, and BERT-Score.
285
286   Benchmarking Positioning. Positioning is essential in vision-language tasks (Chen et al., 2023), espe-
287   cially those involving input and output boxes. For tasks with output boxes, such as Referring Expression
288   Comprehension (REC) (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014; Mao et al., 2016), the goal is to localize a target object
289   in an image based on a referring expression. Conversely, tasks with input boxes, like Referring Expression
290   Generation (REG) (Liu et al., 2017), require the model to describe a specific region given an image and a
291   location box.
292   In our datasets, M3D-RefSeg and M3D-Seg, masks are converted into box coordinates representing the
293   maximum bounding rectangle (x1 , y1 , z1 , x2 , y2 , z2 ). For evaluation, 20% of the data from AbdomenCT-
294   1K (Ma et al., 2022) within M3D-Seg is utilized as the test set. Positioning performance for output boxes
295   is assessed using the Intersection over Union (IoU) metric, while the quality of generated descriptions for
296   input boxes is evaluated with BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, and BERT-Score.
297
298   Benchmarking Segmentation. Segmentation is vital for 3D medical image analysis, enabling recognition
299   and localization. It is divided into semantic segmentation, where models generate masks based on prede-
300   fined semantic labels, and referring expression segmentation, which segments targets described by natural
301   language.
302   For evaluation, 20% of the data from AbdomenCT-1K (Ma et al., 2022), TotalSegmentator (Wasserthal et al.,
303   2023), and CT-Organ (Rister et al., 2020) in the M3D-Seg is designated as the test set for both segmentation
304   types. The Dice is used as the primary evaluation metric for these tasks.

      Experiments on Image-Text Retrieval. Given the lack of suitable CLIP-like models for 3D medical im-
309   age analysis, we use a 2D medical model as a baseline. We evaluate the 2D model by sampling 10 equally
310   spaced 2D slices from each 3D image along the depth dimension, identifying the 3D image with the slice
311   that shows the highest similarity to the target. Although we initially considered using CLIP, it yielded poor
312   results in the medical domain, prompting us to select PMC-CLIP (Lin et al., 2023) as our baseline. As
313   shown in Table 2, our model significantly outperforms the PMC-CLIP model across various difficulty levels,
314   primarily due to PMC-CLIP’s limited spatial information. In the easiest setting (100 test samples, R@10),
315   our model achieves a 55% improvement in image-to-text retrieval (IR). In the most challenging setting (2000
316   samples, R@1), our model exceeds PMC-CLIP by 77.40%. We also examined the effect of training batch
317
      size on performance, finding that larger batch size yield significant gains. Specifically, increasing the batch
      size from 6 to 32 results in a 59.45% improvement in the most difficult setting (2000 samples, R@1).

438   Ablation Study. Table 7 summarizes ablation studies of our LaMed-Llama-2-7B model on closed-set
439   VQA tasks, focusing on four key modules: vision pre-training, spatial pooling, MLP, and unlocked vision.
440   Specifically, removing vision pre-training means training from scratch, resulting in a performance decrease
441
      of 3.83, highlighting its critical importance. Additionally, omitting spatial pooling involves directly pooling
      sequence tokens, leading to a reduction of 2.09. Then, excluding the MLP for a single linear layer decreases
442
      performance by 1.46. Consequently, our 3D spatial pooling perceiver employs 3D spatial pooling for token
443
      downsampling and the MLP as a projector. Furthermore, the omission of unlocked vision reflects freezing
444   the vision encoder during fine-tuning, resulting in a decline of 0.82. Overall, vision pre-training is the most
445   impactful factor for enhancing performance. Our findings emphasize the significance of each component,
446   with optimal training requiring visual pre-training and an unlocked vision encoder during fine-tuning.

458   6   C ONCLUSION
459
460   This work introduces the generalist MLLM M3D-LaMed, the largest dataset M3D-Data, and the compre-
461
      hensive benchmark M3D-Bench for 3D medical image analysis. We explore the integration of 3D vision
      encoder, 3D spatial pooling perceiver and LLM in M3D-LaMed. Extensive experiments show that our gen-
462
      eralist M3D-LaMed achieves promising results and outperforms other specialist models in corresponding
463
      tasks. We believe the contributed model, data and benchmark will facilitate the research of 3D medical
464   image analysis and further clinical practices.
465
466   Limitations. Despite M3D-LaMed shows remarkable performance in 3D medical analysis, challenges
467   remain in the analysis of higher-resolution and multiple 3D scans, which requires more efficient and
468   lightweight LLMs for processing long token sequences. Although a large-scale 3D medical dataset is con-
469   tributed, more data needs to be collected and annotated for training better models.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
753
754
      Medical Multi-Modal Datasets: In medical scenarios (Pei et al., 2023), rich images of various modalities
755
      and texts are available. However, previous works (Ben Abacha et al., 2019; Johnson et al., 2019) have
756   difficulty constructing large-scale medical multi-modal datasets due to privacy and restrictions. Inspired
757   by CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), PMC-OA (Lin et al., 2023) obtained image and text data from medical
758   papers through web crawling, resulting in 1.6M 2D image-text pairs. Additionally, MedMD (Wu et al.,
759   2023b) aims to achieve multiple objectives: building 2D and 3D medical models, integrating public 2D
760   medical datasets, and crawling 3D image and text data from medical professional websites. One of its 3D
761   datasets, RP3D (Wu et al., 2023b), comprises 51K 3D image-text pairs and 142K VQA data generated from
762   LLMs. In our work, we primarily focus on constructing large-scale 3D medical datasets by crawling medical
763   professional websites. M3D-Data includes 120K 3D image-text pairs and 662K instruction-response pairs
764
      generated through an automatic and low-cost data generation pipeline. Furthermore, M3D-Data’s M3D-
      Seg component collects nearly 6K 3D images from 25 public medical segmentation datasets, facilitating
765
      tasks such as positioning and segmentation. In summary, M3D-Data is the largest 3D medical multi-modal
766
      dataset, supporting various tasks, as shown in Table 1.
767
768   Medical MLLMs: Medical MLLMs (Li et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024) are typically fine-
769   tuned from powerful 2D open-source MLLMs using medical multi-modal datasets. For instance, LLaVA-
770
      Med (Li et al., 2024), Med-PaLM M (Tu et al., 2024), and Med-Flamingo (Moor et al., 2023) are based
      on models such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), PaLM-E (Driess et al., 2023), and Flamingo (Alayrac et al.,
771
      2022), respectively. The availability of large-scale datasets like PMC-VQA (Zhang et al., 2023) has enabled
772
      training medical MLLMs from scratch, although initially limited to 2D images. While RadFM (Wu et al.,
773   2023b) supports both 2D and 3D images, it is primarily used for 2D images and text generation tasks such
774   as VQA and performs poorly on 3D images. In our work, M3D-LaMed serves as a generalist MLLM for
775   3D medical image analysis. It handles not only text generation tasks like report generation and VQA but
776   also pioneers vision tasks, like positioning and segmentation in 3D medical images, which are crucial for
777   identification and localization in medical image analysis.

796   Others, miscellaneous errors. We observed that Q3, related to multiple organs, is prone to non-unique errors,
797   while Q4 and Q5, focused on abnormalities and locations, tend to have hallucination errors. To address these
798   issues, we engaged 10 experts to manually correct all test data.

      E    D ISCUSSION WITH OOD Q UESTIONS
894
895
      We aim to investigate the generalization capability of our model, specifically its ability to handle out-of-
896
      distribution (OOD) questions that are not present in the training set. To this end, we design unconventional
897   queries, as illustrated in Figure 11. For instance, our model correctly identifies the appendix as the smallest
898   organ in a chest and abdomen CT scan—a concept not included in the training data. Similarly, when con-
899   fronted with the grammatically unconventional query ”smartest organ,” the model appropriately responds
900   with ”Brain,” despite this phrase not being part of the training data.
901
      Our dataset includes questions describing anomalies, and we impose stricter constraints by limiting queries
902
      to one, three, and five words. Notably, our model successfully addresses these constrained queries, even
903   though it was not explicitly trained for such scenarios. Moreover, when presented with queries related to
904   surgical planning or seeking life advice, the model generates relevant responses, demonstrating its adapt-
905   ability beyond the training data.
906
      In summary, the M3D-LaMed model exhibits robust generalization capabilities for OOD problems. This
907
      proficiency is attributed to our approach of performing lightweight LoRA fine-tuning on the LLM rather
908
      than full-parameter fine-tuning, which preserves the LLM’s original understanding and knowledge. By
909   leveraging the inherent capabilities of the LLM and fine-tuning on new multi-modal datasets, our MLLM
910   demonstrates enhanced professional and generalization capabilities. Consequently, developing a medical
911   MLLM grounded in a robust LLM foundation proves to be indispensable.
