009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper deals with distributed optimization problems that use compressed
012            communication to achieve efficient performance and mitigate the communication
013            bottleneck. We propose a family of compression schemes in which operators trans-
014            form vectors fed to their input according to a Markov chain, i.e., the stochasticity of
015            the compressors depends on previous iterations. Intuitively, this should accelerate
016
               the convergence of optimization methods, as considering previous iterations seems
               more natural and robust. The compressors are implemented in the vanilla Quantized
017
               Stochastic Gradient Descent (QSGD) algorithm. To further improve efficiency
018
               and convergence rate, we apply the momentum acceleration method. We prove
019            convergence results for our algorithms with Markovian compressors and show
020            theoretically that the accelerated method converges faster than the basic version.
021            The analysis covers non-convex, Polyak-Lojasiewicz (PL), and strongly convex
022            cases. Experiments are conducted to demonstrate the applicability of the results
023            to distributed data-parallel optimization problems. Practical results demonstrate
024            the superiority of methods utilizing our compressors design over several existing
025            optimization algorithms.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028   The optimization problem is currently a key issue in many practical applications, such as optimization
029   in neural network training, resource allocation in computational systems, and parameter tuning in
030   algorithmic trading strategies.
031   In addition, a variety of algorithms for optimization on a single device, such as SGD Robbins
032   & Monro (1951), Adam Kingma & Ba (2014), Lion Yazdani & Jolai (2016), have emerged and
033   been subjected to theoretical analysis. However, in the contemporary landscape of deep learning,
034   there is an increasing trend towards adopting intricate and expansive models that pose significant
035   training challenges. Prominent among these challenges are advanced deep learning frameworks for
036   image analysis, sophisticated natural language processing structures akin to transformers Vaswani
037   et al. (2017), and complex reinforcement learning methodologies designed for autonomous system
038
      operations Kiran et al. (2021). As a result, the training of such models has become impractical for
      execution on a single device due to their requirement for extensive data sets for training, which are
039
      unfeasible to store on a single device. Consequently, optimization algorithms have been specifically
040
      developed for distributed training Verbraeken et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021). These methods utilize
041   a large number of devices, with each one processing distinct data subsets and participating in an
042   effective data exchange mechanism, thereby aiding in the training of these computationally intensive
043   models. Thus, the problem of classical ( optimization evolves into
                                                                       ) a distributed optimization form:

      This bottleneck can significantly impede the efficiency of the system, particularly in scenarios involv-
055   ing extensive data exchange across distributed networks. The challenge intensifies in environments
056   where the bandwidth is limited, requiring solutions to mitigate the impact of data transmission delays
057   and ensure seamless data flow.
058   The primary solution at present is the compression of transmitted information Bekkerman et al.
059   (2011); Chilimbi et al. (2014); Alistarh et al. (2017), wherein not a whole package is sent, but rather
060   a selected subset. This method involves strategically selecting and compressing the most informative
061   segments of data for transmission. By doing this way, it significantly reduces the volume of data that
062   needs to be communicated across the network, thereby alleviating the communication bottleneck.
063   In recent times, a number of methods employing compression have been conceived and scrutinized
064   Mishchenko et al. (2019); Gorbunov et al. (2021a); Richtárik et al. (2021). However, a lot of studies
065   have utilized unbiased compression operators due to their simplicity and amenability to theoretical
066   analysis. Such compression techniques, including methods as random sparsification and value
067   rounding Nesterov (2012a); Alistarh et al. (2017); Horvath et al. (2022); Beznosikov et al. (2023a),
068   fail to consider the integration of information conveyed in prior iterations. We hence highlight a
069
      potential research gap regarding the usage of previously transmitted data in compression operators
      and optimization algorithms.
070
071   This omission raises the following research questions that we address in the paper:
072            • Is it possible to design compression operators that take into account information
073            about what and how we forwarded in previous iterations?
074            • What methods can we integrate this kind of compression operators into? How
075            does it affect the convergence rate of the methods, both in theory and in practice?
076            • Can the methods be made even more efficient, e.g., by using additional momentum
077            acceleration techniques?
078   In our paper, we focus on compression-based methods that take into account information collected
079   across multiple preceding iterations, employing what are termed as Markovian compression operators.
080   To the best of our knowledge, this approach emerges as novel and unexplored in the existing literature.
081
      1.1   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
082
083   New type of compression operators. We introduce a novel type of compressors that utilizes stochas-
084
      ticity transmitted over several previous iterations. We refer to this type of compressors as Markovian,
      because the states of these compressors can be viewed as a Markov chain. We examine two in-
085
      vented examples of such compressors: BanLast(K, m) (Definition 5) and KAWASAKI(K, b, π∆ , m)
086
      (Definition 6). The first new compressor operates on a more intuitive basis: it works as random
087   sparsification, but prohibits the transmission of coordinates that were sent in the previous K iterations.
088   The latter functions in terms of probabilities: it reduces the likelihood of transmitting coordinates that
089   appeared in previous iterations. The KAWASAKI(K, b, π∆ , m) compressor is more flexible and, in
090   fact, modify the idea BanLast(K, m), but it introduces two hyperparameters that will be discussed
091   later in Section 2.1.
092   New algorithms. The compression operators described above give rise to new methods that utilize
093   them. In this context, our paper outlines a general framework based on Alistarh et al. (2017) for
094   distributed gradient descent algorithms that employ Markovian compression operators (MQSGD, see
095   Algorithm 1). Subsequently, to make this basic algorithm faster we apply the multiple momentum
096   technique Nesterov (2012a) and obtain the accelerated method AMQSGD. The formulation of such
097   an algorithm is detailed in Algorithm 2. The basic and accelerated methods are explored both
098   theoretically and experimentally throughout the paper. Furthermore, experiments utilizing Markovian
099
      operators in the DIANA Mishchenko et al. (2019) and SGD with momentum algorithms are conducted
      in Section 3.
100
101   Strongly convex and non-convex cases. Motivated by various applications primarily from machine
102
      learning, we provide the theoretical analysis in the strongly convex (Theorem 3) and non-convex / PL-
      condition (Theorem 2) cases of the target function f . Notably, we provide proper analysis for both
103
      setups with specific cases, which is rarely present in the field.
104
105
      Numerical experiments. We conduct experiments with Markovian compressors in a data-parallel
      setup for several optimization problems and datasets. In particular, we analyze the proposed MQSGD
106
      and AMQSGD, as well as the DIANA and SGD optimizers for distributed optimization. In all setups,
107
      we observe an acceleration of convergence for methods employing the BanLast and KAWASAKI
      compressors compared to the baseline random sparsification.

      1.2   R ELATED WORK
109
110
      Compressed communications. The use of compressed communications is a fairly well-known idea
      in distributed learning Seide et al. (2014). As soon as the main property of compressed messages
111
      is that they are much easier to transfer, it can be reached in different ways, such as by quantizing
112
      the entries of the input vector Alistarh et al. (2017); Mayekar & Tyagi (2019); Gandikota et al.
113   (2020); Horvath et al. (2022), or by sparsifying it Richtárik & Takáč (2016); Alistarh et al. (2018), or
114   even by combining these ideas Albasyoni et al. (2020); Beznosikov et al. (2023a). However, all of
115   the compression operators could be roughly Condat et al. (2023) separated into two large groups:
116   unbiased and biased.
117   The first group is much easier to analyze and is therefore more broadly represented in the literature.
118   The basic method with unbiased compression was presented in Alistarh et al. (2017). Later this
119   algorithms were modified using variance reduction technique with compression of gradient differences
120   Mishchenko et al. (2019); Horváth et al. (2019); Gorbunov et al. (2021a) in order to improve the
121   theoretical convergence guarantees. One can also note the works Gorbunov et al. (2019) and Khaled
122   et al. (2020), where the authors developed a general theory for SGD-type methods with unbiased
123   compression.
124   On the other hand, our understanding of distributed optimization with biased compressors is more
125   complicated. In particular, biased compression implies the use of error compensation techniques
126   Stich et al. (2018). Distributed SGD with biased compression and linear rate of convergence in a
127   multi-node setting was first introduced in Beznosikov et al. (2023a). In the meantime, other error
128
      compensation techniques are being actively developed, Lin et al. (2022); Richtárik et al. (2021). The
      last approach called EF21 was later studied in Fatkhullin et al. (2021), Gruntkowska et al. (2023).
129
130   Markovian stochasticity. Another recent trend in the literature is to design algorithms that use
131
      Markovian stochastic processes instead of i.i.d. random variables in various ways. For instance, Duchi
      et al. (2012) introduced a version of the Mirror Descent algorithm that yields optimal convergence
132
      rates for non-smooth and convex problems. Later, Doan et al. (2020a); Dorfman & Levy (2023);
133
      Beznosikov et al. (2023b) studied first-order methods in the Markovian noise setting. Alternatively,
134   token algorithms Hendrikx (2022); Ayache et al. (2022) are also a popular area of research in
135   Markovian stochasticity. In particular, Even (2023) obtained optimal rates of convergence, and Sun
136   et al. (2022); Mao et al. (2019); Doan et al. (2020b) looked at the token algorithm from the angle
137   of the Lagrangian duality and from variants of the ADMM method. At the same time, there exist
138   particular results, e.g., Bresler et al. (2020), which provide a lower bound for the particular finite sum
139   problems in the Markovian setting.
140   Despite all of the above, to the best of our knowledge, there are currently no works that combine
141   compressed data communications and Markovian stochasticity of the compressors.

187   2.1     M ARKOVIAN COMPRESSORS

      In this section, we introduce Markovian compressors that take into account the information transmitted
189   in previous K operations. It is assumed that these compressors function within an iterative algorithm
190   aimed at minimizing the problem (1), wherein a distinct discrete variable, denoted as the step t, is
191   involved. Consequently, due to the dependence of the compressors on previous states, they exhibit a
192   reliance on the step t. Let us narrow down the class of compressors to be discussed in this paper.
193   Definition 4 (Random sparsification). Qt (x) is a random sparsification compressor, if it operates on
194   the vector x ∈ Rd as Qt (x) = m  d

196   The classical Randm operator fits Definition 4, in particular, for this compressor subsets νt are
197
      generated uniformly at each step t, therefore it is unbiased, i.e., Et [Qt (x)] = x for all t. In this paper,
      we do not generate νt independently, but according to some Markov chain, i.e., compressors start to
198
      take into account past iterations. We formulate this idea as an assumption.

200
      is a random sparsification compressor (Definition 4) and {νt }t≥0 are realizations of some ergodic

532   4 C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we propose a family of compression schemes, which takes into account previous
534   iterations of algorithm and transform the input vector according to a Markov chain. We develop two
535   sparsification methods BanLast (Definition 5) and KAWASAKI (Definition 6) based on this idea.
536   These compressors are implemented in QSGD (Algorithm 1) and accelerated QSGD (Algorithm
537   2). We provide convergence rates under different assumptions on the objective function (Theorems
538   2 and 3). In experiments, we show that our compression methods outperform the baselines in the
539   deep neural network optimisation problem. Future research may consider the implementation of our
      Markovian compressors in other optimization methods, e.g. using the variance reduction techniques.

       We conduct experiments on classification with logistic regression on four datasets: Mushrooms,
2338
       A9A, W8A, MNIST. We apply the following optimization algorithms: proposed MQSGD and its
2339   accelerated version AMQSGD, and also use Markovian compressors with popular DIANA Mishchenko
2340   et al. (2019) algorithm. In all of our experiments, we do not utilize the steps of the optimizer, but
2341   rather the information that is transmitted by each worker at the current timestamp t. This implies that
2342   there are n workers, with each worker sending m coordinates at each iteration of the optimization
2343   step. Consequently, the x-axis displays numbers of the form mn · 1, mn · 2, . . . , mn · t, . . . , mn · T .
2344   This allows us to understand the performance of compressors with varying values of m and n.
2345   We use convex logistic regression loss with a regularization term λ = 0.05. Each dataset is split
2346   horizontally (by rows) equally between N = 10 clients. The feature dimension is denoted as d in the
2347   figures, varying from hundreds to almost a thousand between datasets. The underlying sparsification
2348   compressors in Rand-10% for all logistic regression experiments. Learning rate initial value and decay
2349   rate are fine-tuned for each problem and compressor. Additionally, Markovian-specific parameters
2350   such as history size K, forgetting rate b are also fine-tuned. Table 2 provides hyperparameters grid
2351   for the tuning. We obtain optimal solution x∗ for each problem with scipy.optimize method in
2352   order to use this value for the graphics.

2582   As opposed to data-parallel setting, model parallelism is paradigm which splits the model (typically a
2583   deep neural network) to a pipeline of layers between workers. Such distributed scenario is especially
2584   relevant for large language models (LLM), which consist of billions of trainable parameters. As
2585   communication is a typical bottleneck in such systems Diskin et al. (2021), various compression
2586   techniques are applied to layer activations and their respective gradients that are transferred between
2587   adjacent pipeline workers. Such techniques include quantization and sparsification Dettmers et al.
2588
       (2022); Bian et al. (2023), as well as low-rank compression Song et al. (2023) techniques.
2589   We perform training of Resnet-18 He et al. (2016) convolutional neural network on CIFAR-10
2590   dataset Krizhevsky et al. (2009). We split the ResNet onto 4 workers by resnet blocks, simulated
2591   on a single device with compression of activations and their respective gradients in the places of
       communication. We apply Markovian compressors only to gradients in model-parallel setup, using

2616
       In this series of experiments, we examine a distributed approach to fine-tuning language models using
2617
       LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). This method is based on freezing the model weights that are pre-trained on a
2618   large dataset, and add a low rank adapter with matrices A ∈ Rn×r and B ∈ Rr×m to some selected
2619   layers Wold ∈ Rn×m of this model, such that Wnew = Wold + A · B. Since in practice the parameter
2620   r is chosen to be much smaller than n and m, the new model has much fewer trainable parameters
2621   and can be efficiently trained on downsteram tasks.
2622
       In our experiments, we apply LoRA adapters with fixed rank r = 8 to the attention layers of the
2623
       DeBERTaV3-base model (He et al., 2021). The downsteram task is the classical GLUE benchmark
2624   for natural language understanding (Wang et al., 2019). We consider only random sparsification
2625   compressors (Definition 4) with 25% compression rate, due to the large computational cost of this
2626   experiment. Figure 15 shows learning curves for training with N = 10 clients. Our Markovian
2627   compressors appears to have best convergence against independent Randm compressor.
