009                                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                As multimodal large language models (MLLMs) continue to demonstrate increas-
012                ingly competitive performance across a broad spectrum of tasks, more intricate
013                and comprehensive benchmarks have been developed to assess these cutting-edge
014                models. These benchmarks introduce new challenges to core capabilities such
015                as perception, reasoning, and planning. However, existing multimodal bench-
016
                   marks fall short in providing a focused evaluation of multi-step planning based
                   on spatial relationships in images. To bridge this gap, we present ING-VP,
017
                   the first INteractive Game-based Vision Planning benchmark, specifically de-
018
                   signed to evaluate the spatial imagination and multi-step reasoning abilities of
019                MLLMs. ING-VP features 6 distinct games, encompassing 300 levels, each with
020                6 unique configurations. A single model engages in over 60,000 rounds of in-
021                teraction. The benchmark framework allows for multiple comparison settings,
022                including image-only vs. text-only inputs, single-step vs. multi-step reasoning,
023                and with-history vs. without-history conditions, offering valuable insights into
024                the model’s capabilities. We evaluated numerous state-of-the-art MLLMs, with
025                the highest-performing model, Claude-3.5 Sonnet, achieving a best accuracy of
026                only 8.00%, far below the human accuracy of 65.66%. This work aims to provide
027
                   a specialized evaluation framework to drive advancements in MLLMs’ capacity
                   for complex spatial reasoning and planning. The code is publicly available at
028
                   https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ING-VP-E49A.

046   1    I NTRODUCTION
047
048   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language
049   processing, generation, and even textual complex reasoning and planning (Zhao et al., 2023). Building
050
      upon this powerful foundation of LLMs, integrating visual inputs has led to the development of even
      more powerful models (OpenAI, 2024; Anil et al., 2023a), a.k.a multimodal large language models
051
      (MLLMs).
052
053   Despite demonstrating impressive performance in handling most general multimodal tasks, the
      effectiveness of MLLM in multimodal reasoning and planning still remains unclear. Moreover, recent


                                                                                     1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      studies (Lu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024) indicate that vision-language training might degrade the
055   textual capabilities of MLLMs, suggesting that MLLMs built upon LLMs could be impaired when
056   adapted to multimodal reasoning and planning tasks. Consequently, there is an urgent need for a
057   test that incorporates multimodal complex reasoning and planning cases to guide the subsequent
058   enhancements of MLLMs.
059
      To address this issue, existing studies generally utilize visual question answering (VQA) (Antol
060
      et al., 2015; Kafle & Kanan, 2017) and game-based evaluations (Wu et al., 2023; Bellemare et al.,
061   2013) to assess the visual reasoning capabilities of MLLMs. In general, VQA necessitates a verified
062   ground-truth answer that relies on human annotations. But acquiring these annotations is both
063   costly and time-consuming. Moreover, the absence of interaction and planning in typical VQA tasks
064   poses difficulties in evaluating the reasoning and planning capabilities of advanced MLLMs. The
065   tasks presented in these benchmarks are overly simplistic (Yue et al., 2023) or only test reasoning
066   within domain-specific knowledge (Yue et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a), which mainly evaluates
067   the LLM knowledge of MLLMs rather than the perception, reasoning, and planning of MLLMs.
068   Therefore, recent studies (Xu et al., 2024; Chia et al., 2024) prompt MLLMs to interact with digital
069
      game environments, which are measured by game outcomes and scores, leading to the game-based
      evaluation. Unlike VQA tasks, these methods can evaluate the multi-step reasoning capabilities
070
      and even spatial imagination of MLLMs, which is crucial function of human cognition, allowing
071
      us to interact with realistic environments (Wu et al., 2024). Despite the effectiveness, these works
072   are typically restricted to individual games with complex rules, involve time-consuming evaluation
073   episodes, and fail to effectively assess the models’ generalization capabilities in multimodal planning.
074   Considering these challenges, our goal is to develop a generalizable and efficient benchmark to
075   evaluate the multi-step planning abilities of MLLMs, providing insights for subsequent improvements
076   of MLLMs with complex multi-step reasoning.
077
      To fill this gap, in this paper, we introduce the INteractive Game-based Vision Planning benchmark
078   (ING-VP), meticulously focusing on evaluating the spatial imagination and multi-step reasoning
079   abilities of MLLMs. Figure 1 shows games, evaluation settings, and the interactive process in our
080   ING-VP. To construct our ING-VP, we initially collect six games featuring easily understandable
081   rules. In each game, we collect 50 levels, each comprising both an image and a text representation of
082   the current state, providing vision and textual inputs for MLLMs, as illustrated in Figure 2. To assess
083   the spatial imagination and planning capabilities of MLLMs, we establish six experimental settings,
084   which prompt the models to perform single-step and multi-step reasoning, with or without historical
085   interaction. During the evaluation, we employ MLLMs to interact within the environment until the
086
      game is completed. To evaluate model performance comprehensively, beyond merely determining
      whether a model can finish a game, we also use the model’s action efficiency and the remaining steps
087
      to complete the game as evaluation metrics.
088
089   With our ING-VP, we test 15 open- and closed-source MLLMs and analyze their performance on
090   our test cases. We first support the benchmark designed to evaluate the multi-step reasoning and
091   spatial imagination capabilities of MLLMs — ING-VP bench. Then we analyze these capabilities of
092
      current open- and closed-source MLLMs, despite a performance gap, the leading open-source model,
      InternVL2-Llama3-76B, achieves an accuracy of 2.50%, ranking just behind Claude-3.5 Sonnet,
093
      GPT-4o, and Gemini-1.5 Pro. Notably, its performance significantly surpasses that of GPT-4o mini,
094
      which stands at 1.05%, and GPT-4v, which records a mere 0.32%. We also conduct a detailed analysis
095   of these models’ performance, the evidence shows that:
096
097          • The inability to process the relative positions of elements is one of the primary issues with
098            MLLM perception.
099          • Even the most advanced MLLMs have very limited planning capabilities, far below the
100            performance of ordinary humans on these simple tasks.
101
             • Current models tend to generate instructions that are much longer than necessary to complete
102            the levels. While this can improve accuracy on simple levels, it also indirectly reveals that
103            MLLMs are “uncertain” about the correct solution.
104
105   While most tasks in the ING-VP benchmark are straightforward for humans, they pose significant
106   challenges for MLLMs, even the top-performing model, Claude-3.5 Sonnet, achieving an average
107   accuracy of just 3.37%. We reveal that current MLLMs generally lack spatial imagination and
      multi-step planning abilities, and offer a new perspective on the capability requirements for MLLMs.

133   2     R ELATED W ORK
134
135   Multimodal Large Language Models. LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023b) have
136   demonstrated their ability of generating human-like texts to understand and respond to complex
137   instructional queries. The successes of LLMs has elicited the burgeoning proliferation of multi-modal
138   LLMs (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023b),
139   which is designed to process and integrate multiple types of data. The primary attempt Flamingo
140   (Alayrac et al., 2022) endows visual-language models with in-context few-shot learning capabilities
141
      by trained on large-scale interleaved text-image data. BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023b) designs a Q-Former
      architecture to align the visual-textual knowledge during the pre-training phase. LLaVA (Liu et al.,
142
      2024) collect GPT-4 generated multimodal language-image instruction-following data and train a
143
      general-purpose visual-language assistant. Beyond multimodal understanding, EMU-2 (Sun et al.,
144   2024) and LaVIT (Jin et al., 2023b) take one step further and act as generative multimodal model to
145   support visual prompting and object-grounded generation.
146
147
      MLLM Benchmarks. The development of MLLMs has highlighted the critical need of benchmarks
      for thorough evaluations. Although traditional visual-language tasks (e.g., visual question answering
148
      (Antol et al., 2015; Kafle & Kanan, 2017) and image captioning (Lin et al., 2014; Plummer et al.,
149
      2015)) can be used as evaluation benchmarks, they are too strict and require the exact match with
150   the ground-truth answers. To this end, LVLM-eHub (Xu et al., 2023) and LAMM (Yin et al., 2024)
151   reformulate exiting public datasets as evaluation samples and employ human annotators or GPT
152   to assess the quality. MME (Li et al., 2024), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023b) and SEED-Bench (Li
153   et al., 2024) construct multiple-choice questions to mitigate the subjectivity and instability of GPT
154   evaluation. MMMU (Yue et al., 2024) evaluate the advanced perception and reasoning of MLLMs on
155   specific domains (e.g., science, business).
156   Game-based Evaluations. Digital games are acknowledged as essential in the pursuit of artificial
157   general intelligence since they present complex challenges requiring advanced reasoning and cognitive
158   skills. These challenges make digital games an ideal benchmark for evaluating the capabilities of
159   MLLMs (Wu et al., 2023; Bellemare et al., 2013; Hu et al., 2024; Sweetser, 2024; Xu et al., 2024)
160   including the environment perception (Hong et al., 2023; Akoury et al., 2023), memory construction
161   (Zhu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Ding et al., 2023; Park et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a), reasoning
      (Liu et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023a; Qian et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022) and decision-making

      3.1    OVERVIEW OF ING-VP
195
      We introduce ING-VP benchmark, a new interactive game-based vision planning benchmark designed
196
      to measure the multi-step reasoning and spatial imagination capabilities of MLLMs. The benchmark
197   encompasses 6 distinct settings, 6 games, and 50 levels per game, the core mechanisms are depicted
198   in Figure 1. To mitigate data leakage and ensure problem solvability, the majority of our levels
199   are algorithmically generated and verified. Representative examples of each game are illustrated in
200   Figure 2. Details of the data collection process are provided in the Appendix B.
201
      ING-VP features 6 games that are conceptually simple yet cognitively challenging: Sokoban, Maze,
202
      Sudoku, 8-queens, Tower of Hanoi, and 15-puzzle. The simplicity lies in the easily comprehensible
203   rules and the ability to encapsulate complete level information within a single image, facilitating
204   comprehensive reasoning. The challenge stems from the requirement for models to precisely capture
205   core visual elements and their spatial relationships, necessitating multi-step reasoning to successfully
206   complete each level. We meticulously craft 6 reasoning settings, enabling researchers to systematically
207   identify the strengths and limitations of target models through comparative analysis of performance
208   across these settings.
209
210   3.2    S IX I NFERENCE S ETTINGS
211
212   One-step: Image and Text-only Settings In the One-step with Image setting, we provide the
213   model solely with an image depicting the initial game state and prompt it to generate comprehensive
214   instructions for level completion. The One-step Text-only setting follows an identical approach,
215   with the key distinction being the replacement of the image input with its corresponding textual
      representation.


                                                                      4

216
      Multi-step: Image and Text-only Settings (without History) In the Multi-step with Image setting,
217   we provide the model with an image of the current game state at each inference round. After the
218   model outputs a single-step instruction, this instruction is fed into the game as input, causing the game
219   state to change and generate a new image. This new image then serves as the model’s input for the
220   next step. The Multi-step Text-only setting follows the same process, but uses textual representations
221   as the model’s input.
222
      Multi-step: Image and Text-only Settings (with History) The key distinction in these settings is the
223   inclusion of the model’s historical outputs as part of the prompt in each interaction. Additionally, for
224   Sokoban, Sudoku, and N-queens, we add an undo option, allowing the model to freely revert to any
225   previous state. This enhancement applies to both the Image and Text-only variants of the Multi-step
226   setting.
227
228   3.3   G AME S ELECTION
229
230   We chose six games that are widely recognized, have straightforward rules, and operate in a determin-
231   istic environment, making them ideal representatives for our study. In a deterministic environment,
232   the outcome of every action taken by an agent is predictable and certain. Such an environment can
233
      be formally defined using a Markov Decision Process (MDP). The model employs a strategy π to
      determine the next action at based on the current state st and all previous actions a0:t−1 , represented
234
      as:
235
236
237                                             at = π(st , a0:t−1 )                                       (1)
238
      The planning process of MLLMs can be expressed as:
239
240
241                                             S ′ = π(S, A, G, n)                                        (2)
242
243   Where S ′ is the future sequence of states, which terminates upon achieving the goal G or exhausting
244
      the available moves n; S is the current sequence of states; A represents the current sequence of
      actions.

      MLLMs. We consider a comprehensive suite of mainstream large multimodal models. Closed-source
256
      models include GPT-4o, GPT-4o Mini, GPT-4v, GPT-4 Turbo, Claude-3.5 Sonnet, Claude-3 Opus,
257
      and Gemini-1.5 Pro. Open-source models consist of CogVLM2-19B, DeepSeek-VL, Internvl-Chat-
258   v1.5, Internvl2-8B, Internvl2-26B, Internvl2-40B, InternVL2-Llama3-76B, and MiniCPM-V2.6. We
259   utilize each model’s official API for closed-source systems or the publicly available checkpoint for
260   open-source implementations, More information of these models can be found in the Appendix A.
261
      Evaluation. We present a systematic interactive environment for evaluating all MLLMs, where
262
      models interact with the game environment until either completing the task or exhausting the allotted
263
      steps. We constrain the model’s output action instructions to JSON format through prompts and
264   extract them using regular expressions. The correctly extracted instructions are then used as input
265   for the game environment. After the game state changes, the new state is fed back to the model
266   for the next round of inference. We employ three metrics: accuracy, completion degree, and action
267   efficiency. (1) Accuracy is our main metric, it measures whether the model can complete the task
268   within the specified number of steps. (2) Completion degree is determined by the final state of the
269   game environment after interaction with the model. The closer the final state is to the cleared state,
      the higher the score; if it deviates, the score decreases accordingly. (3) Action efficiency represents

      5   T WO T HINKING ABOUT PLANNING
466
      A holistic approach may outperform a divide-and-conquer strategy. When humans are tasked
467
      with completing a planning problem, whether in a single or multi-step process, it typically involves
468
      three key phases: understanding the goal, devising a plan, and breaking down the steps. Large
469   models should operate similarly, yet when presented with the same game level, their outputs differ
470   significantly between one-step and multi-step settings, as highlighted in Table 2. Notably, even the
471   initial steps diverge between the two approaches. To explore the planning capabilities of the model
472   further, we employ two methods to adjust the multi-step output:
473
474          • Step-wise Best of N (BoN): The model generates ten candidate responses at each step, with
475            the most frequent answer selected as the final output.
476          • Forced Planning: The model is required to complete its entire plan before producing a final
477            answer, akin to the one-step setting.
478
479   FIgure 6 illustrates an example of these methods in action, despite these adjustments, the multi-step
480   approach failed to match the performance of the one-step setting. This suggests that, for the large
481   models, even when given identical image, one-step and multi-step tasks are fundamentally different,
482
      with the former better eliciting the model’s planning capabilities.
483   Small changes in the prompt phrasing can substantially influence the model’s planning effec-
484   tiveness. A thorough comparison of single-step and multi-step outputs reveals not only differences
485   but also distinct tendencies. For instance, in Maze and Sokoban games, Claude-3.5 Sonnet favors ”U
      (Up)” and ”D (Down)” in the one-step mode, whereas it prefers ”L (Left)” and ”R (Right)” in the

      6    C ONCLUSION
505
506
      In this work, we introduce ING-VP, an interactive game-based vision planning benchmark designed
      to evaluate the spatial imagination and planning capabilities of MLLMs. Our experimental results
507
      reveal that even the most advanced MLLMs struggle to achieve satisfactory performance on game
508
      tasks that humans find trivial. This underperformance stems from multiple factors: existing models
509   often fail to generate accurate perceptions of images, and they face even greater challenges in making
510   inferences and plans based on their understanding. We believe that ING-VP is of noteworthy to
511   the community’s deeper understanding of MLLMs, and can also advance MLLMs’ capabilities in
512   comprehension and planning within visual contexts.
513
514
      L IMITATIONS
515
516
      Despite its strengths, ING-VP has certain limitations. We deliberately omit difficulty grading settings.
517
      Including simpler levels would significantly increase the likelihood of models completing tasks by
518   chance after sufficient steps, potentially compromising the reliability of our results. Conversely,
519   incorporating more challenging levels would yield little insight, given that MLLMs already struggle
520   with current difficulty levels, and could negatively impact inference efficiency. Furthermore, ING-VP
521   does not exhaustively cover all possible game types. Instead, we focus on selecting well-known
522   and representative games to ensure relevance and broad applicability. Finally, to address efficiency
523   concerns, we do not use images of previous states as input in the multi-step with history setting.
524   These considerations provide clear directions for future enhancements to our benchmark.

782   Sokoban. It involves pushing crates onto designated storage locations within a warehouse maze. We

785   Maze. The Maze game challenges players to navigate from a starting point to a target through a
786   network of paths. We employ a Depth-First Search (DFS) algorithm to automatically generate 50
787   solvable levels, each with an 11x11 grid size. We also constrain the solution length to a maximum of
788   8 steps.
789
      8-Queens. The 8-Queens puzzle challenges people to place eight queens on an 8x8 chessboard such
790   that no two queens threaten each other. N-Queens is a special game due to its standard formulation:
791   models could potentially solve it without visual input, relying solely on memorized patterns from
792   training data. To ensure that visual reasoning is essential, we modify the puzzle by manually placing
793   the first queen in a different position for each level. The image presented to the MLLMs shows this
794   initial configuration, requiring them to reason from this starting point to complete the puzzle.
795
      Sudoku. Sudoku is a logic-based number placement puzzle that requires filling a 9x9 grid such that
796   each row, column, and 3x3 subgrid contains all digits from 1 to 9 without repetition. A well-formed
797   Sudoku puzzle with a unique solution requires a minimum of 17 initial clues. For our benchmark,
798   we curate a set of 50 puzzles with each puzzle contain 71 clues from a Kaggle dataset 2 , ensuring
799   each puzzle meets this criterion. We then manually generate corresponding images for each level to
800   maintain consistency with our benchmark’s visual reasoning focus.
801
      Hanoi The Tower of Hanoi is a classic mathematical puzzle that involves transferring a stack of disks
802   of varying diameters from one rod to another, adhering to the constraint that a larger disk must never
803   be placed atop a smaller one. In our implementation, each problem instance consists of four rods and
804   five disks, with an optimal solution requiring a minimum of 8 moves.
