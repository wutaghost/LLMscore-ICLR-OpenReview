011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion-based generative models achieve SOTA results in mode coverage and
014            generation quality but suffer from inefficient sampling. Recently introduced dif-
015            fusion distillation techniques approach this issue by transforming the original
016            multi-step model into a one-step generator with approximately the same output
017            distribution. Among these methods, Distribution Matching Distillation (DMD)
018            offers a suitable framework for training general-form one-step generators, appli-
019
               cable beyond unconditional generation. In this paper, we propose a modification
               of DMD, called Regularized Distribution Matching Distillation (RDMD), which
020
               applies to the unpaired image-to-image (I2I) translation problem. To achieve this,
021
               we regularize the generator objective from DMD by adding the transport cost
022            between its input and output. We validate the method’s applicability in theory by
023            establishing its connection with optimal transport. Moreover, we demonstrate its
024            empirical performance in application to several translation tasks, including 2D
025            examples and I2I between different image datasets, where it performs on par or
026            better than multi-step diffusion baselines.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   One of the global problems of contemporary generative modeling consists of solving the so-called
033
      generative learning trilemma (Xiao et al., 2021). It states that a perfect generative model should
      possess three desirable properties: high generation quality, mode coverage/diversity of samples
034
      and efficient inference. Today, most model families tend to have only 2 of the 3. Generative
035
      Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) have fast inference and produce high-quality
036   samples but tend to underrepresent some modes of the data set (Metz et al., 2016; Arjovsky et al.,
037   2017). Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Rezende et al., 2014) efficiently
038   produce diverse samples while suffering from insufficient generation quality. Finally, diffusion-based
039   generative models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Karras et al.,
040   2022) achieve SOTA generative metrics and visual quality yet require running a high-cost multi-step
041   inference procedure.
042   Satisfying these three properties is essential in numerous generative computer vision tasks beyond
043   unconditional generation. One is image-to-image (I2I) translation (Isola et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2017),
044   which consists of learning a mapping between two distributions that preserves the cross-domain
045   properties of an input object while appropriately changing its source-domain features to match the
046   target. Most examples, like transforming cats into dogs (Choi et al., 2020) or human faces into
047   anime (Korotin et al., 2022) belong to the unpaired I2I because they do not assume ground truth
048   pairs of objects in the data set. As in unconditional generation, unpaired I2I methods were previously
049   centered around GANs (Huang et al., 2018; Park et al., 2020; Choi et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2022),
050
      but now tend to be competed and surpassed by diffusion-based counterparts (Choi et al., 2021; Meng
      et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022; Wu & De la Torre, 2023). Most of these methods build on top of the
051
      original diffusion sampling procedure and tend to have high generation time as a consequence.
052
053   Since diffusion models succeed in both desirable qualitative properties of the trilemma, one could
      theoretically obtain samples of the desired quality level given sufficient computational resources. It


                                                         1

054
      makes the acceleration of diffusion models an appealing approach to satisfy all of the aforementioned
055   requirements, including efficient inference.
056
057   Recently introduced diffusion distillation techniques (Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023b; Sauer et al.,
058
      2023) address this challenge by compressing diffusion models into one-step students with (hopefully)
      similar qualitative and quantitative properties. Among them, Distribution Matching Distillation
059
      (DMD) (Yin et al., 2023; Nguyen & Tran, 2023) offers an expressive and general framework for
060
      training free-form generators based on techniques initially introduced for text-to-3D (Poole et al.,
061   2022; Wang et al., 2024). Free-form here means that the method does not make any assumptions
062   about the generator’s structure and distribution at the input. This crucial observation opens a large
063   space for its applications beyond the noise → data problems.
064
      In this work, we introduce the modification of DMD, called Regularized Distribution Matching
065
      Distillation (RDMD), that applies to the unpaired I2I problems. To achieve this, we replace the
066
      generator’s input noise with the source data samples to further translate them into the target. We
067   maintain correspondence between the generator’s input and output by regularizing the objective with
068   the transport cost between them. As our main contributions, we
069
070         1. Propose a one-step diffusion-based method for unpaired I2I;
071         2. Theoretically verify it by establishing its connection with optimal transport (Villani et al.,
072            2009; Peyré et al., 2019);
073
            3. Ablate its qualitative properties and demonstrate its generation quality on 2D and image-to-
074            image examples, where it obtains comparable or better results than the multi-step counter-
075            parts.

      2.3   U NPAIRED I2I AND OPTIMAL TRANSPORT
163
164   The problem of unpaired I2I consists of learning a mapping G between the source distribution pS and
165   the target distribution pT given the corresponding independent data sets of samples. When optimized,
166   the mapping should appropriately adapt G(x) to the target distribution pT , while preserving the
167   input’s cross-domain features. However, at first glance, it is unclear what the preservation of cross-
168   domain properties should look like.
169   One way to formalize this is by introducing the notion of a "transportation cost" c(·, ·) between the
170   generator’s input and output and stating that it should not be too large on average. In a practical I2I
171   setting, we can choose c(·, ·) as any reasonable distance between images or their features that we aim
172   to preserve, such as pixel-wise distance or the difference between embeddings.
173
      Monge optimal transport (OT) problem (Villani et al., 2009; Santambrogio, 2015) follows this
174   reasoning and aims at finding the mapping with the least average transport cost among all the
175   mappings that fit the target pT :
176
                                     inf EpS (x) c(x, G(x)) | G(x) ∼ pT ,

                                                                                                 (8)
177                                  G
178   which can be seen as a mathematical formalization of the I2I task.
179
      Under mild constraints, when pS and pT have densities, the optimal transport map G∗ is bijective,
180
      differentiable and has a differentiable inverse, thus satisfying the change of variables formula
181
      pS (x) = pT (G∗ (x))| det (∇G∗ (x)) |. This highly non-linear change of variables condition provides
182   insight into why optimizing Eq. 8 directly is notoriously challenging.

      3.2   A NALYSIS OF THE METHOD
241
242   The optimization problem in Eq. 10 can be seen as the soft-constrained optimal transport, which
243   balances between satisfying the output distribution constraint and preserving the original image
244   properties. Moreover, if one takes λ ≈ 0, the objective essentially becomes equivalent to the Monge
245   problem (Eq. 8). It can be seen by replacing the λ coefficient before the transport cost with the 1/λ
246   coefficient before the KL divergence. For small λ, it is almost equal to +∞ whenever the generator
247   output and the target distributions differ, making the corresponding problem hard-constrained and,
248   therefore, equivalent to the original optimal transport problem. Based on this observation, we prove
249   Theorem 1. Let c(x, y) be the quadratic cost 2 ∥x − y∥2 and Gλ be the theoretical optimum in the
250   problem 10. Then, under mild regularity conditions, it converges in probability (with respect to pS )
251   to the optimal transport map G∗ , i.e.
252                                                      pS
253                                                Gλ −−−→ G∗ .                                             (12)
                                                        λ→0
254
255
      The detailed proof can be found in Appendix A. Informally, it means that the optimal transport
256   map can be approximated by the RDMD generator, trained on Eq. 11, given a small regularization
257   coefficient, enough capacity of the architecture, and convergence of the optimization algorithm.
258
259
      It is important to consider this result from a different perspective. It is ideologically similar to the L2
      regularization for over-parameterized least squares regression. The original least squares, in this case,
260
      have a manifold of solutions. At the same time, by adding L2 weight penalty and taking the limit as
261
      the regularization coefficient goes to zero, one obtains a solution with the least norm based on the
262   Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse (Moore, 1920; Penrose, 1955). In our case, numerous maps may be
263   optimal in the original DMD procedure, since it only requires matching the distribution at output.
264   However, taking λ ≈ 0 results in a feasible solution with almost optimal transport cost. We illustrate
265   this by comparing the loss surface with and without regularization on a toy problem in Figure 1.

      4    R ELATED WORK
271
272
273
274
275   In this section, we give an overview of the existing methods for solving unpaired I2I including
276   GANs, diffusion-based methods, and methods based on optimal transport. We also cover diffusion
277   distillation, which is one of the core concepts in the paper.
278   GANs were the prevalent paradigm in the unpaired I2I for a long time. Among other methods,
279   CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017) and the concurrent DualGAN (Yi et al., 2017), DiscoGAN (Kim et al.,
280   2017) utilized the cycle-consistency paradigm, consisting in training the transfer network along with
281   its inverse and optimizing the consistency term along with the adversarial loss. It gave rise to the
282   whole family of two-sided methods, including UNIT (Liu et al., 2017) and MUNIT (Huang et al.,
283   2018) that divide the encoding into style-space and content-space and SCAN (Li et al., 2018) that
284   splits the procedure into coarse and fine stages. The one-sided GAN-based methods aim to train
285   I2I without learning the inverse for better computational efficiency. DistanceGAN (Benaim & Wolf,
286
      2017) achieves it by learning to preserve the distance between pairs of samples, GCGAN (Fu et al.,
      2019) imposes geometrical consistency constraints, and CUT (Park et al., 2020) uses the contrastive
287
      loss to maximize the patch-wise mutual information between input and output.
288
289   Diffusion-based I2I models mostly build on modifying the diffusion process using the source image.
290   SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021) initializes the reverse diffusion process for target distribution with the
291   noisy source picture instead of the pure noise to maintain similarity. Many methods guide (Ho &
292
      Salimans, 2022; Epstein et al., 2023) the target diffusion process. ILVR (Choi et al., 2021) adds
      the correction that enforces the current noisy sample to resemble the source. EGSDE (Zhao et al.,
293
      2022) trains a classifier between domains and encourages dissimilarity between the embeddings,
294
      corresponding to the source image and the current diffusion process state. At the same time, it
295   enforces a small distance between their downsampled versions, which allows for a balance between
296   faithfulness and realism. The other diffusion-based approaches include two-sided methods based on
297   the concatenation of two diffusion models (DDIB (Su et al., 2022) and CycleDiff (Wu & De la Torre,
298   2023)).
299
      Optimal transport (Villani et al., 2009; Peyré et al., 2019) is another useful framework for the
300
      unpaired I2I. Methods based on it usually reformulate the OT problem (Eq. 8) and its modifications
301   as Entropic OT (EOT) (Cuturi, 2013) or Schrödinger Bridge (SB) (Föllmer, 1988) to be acces-
302   sible in practice. In particular, NOT (Korotin et al., 2022), ENOT (Gushchin et al., 2024a), and
303   NSB (Kim et al., 2023a) use the Lagrangian multipliers formulation of the distribution matching
304   constraint, which results in simulation-based adversarial training. The other methods obtain (partially)
305   simulation-free techniques by iteratively refining the stochastic process between two distributions.
306   De Bortoli et al. (2021); Vargas et al. (2021) define this refinement as learning of the time-reversal
307   with the corresponding initial distribution (source or target). The newer methods are based on Flow
308   Matching (Lipman et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022) and the corre-
309
      sponding Rectification (Liu et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023) procedure. While being
      theoretically sound, most of these methods work well for smaller dimensions (Korotin et al., 2023)
310
      but suffer from computationally hard training in large-scale scenarios.
311
312   Diffusion distillation techniques are mainly divided into two families. First family of methods
313   suggests using the pre-trained diffusion model as a (multi-step) noise→ image mapper and learning
314   it. This includes optimizing the regression loss between the outputs (Salimans & Ho, 2022) or
315
      learning the integrator of the corresponding ODE (Gu et al., 2023a; Song et al., 2023; Kim et al.,
      2023b), including ODEs with guidance (Meng et al., 2023). Second family of methods considers
316
      diffusion models as a source of "knowledge" that can push an arbitrary model toward matching the
317
      distributional constraint. It is commonly formalized as optimizing the Integrated KL divergence (Luo
318   et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2023; 2024; Nguyen & Tran, 2023) by training an additional "fake" diffusion
319   model on the generator’s output distribution. Instead of the KL divergence, one can push similarity
320   of the corresponding scores (Zhou et al., 2024) or moments (Salimans et al., 2024). Notably, these
321   methods do not have any specific restrictions on the model structure, which allows their wide usage
322   (e.g. in text-to-3D (Poole et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024)). Importantly, it allows us to push the
323   generator towards the target distribution in the I2I setting, combined with the input-output transport
      cost regularization.

      This section presents the experimental results on several unpaired translation tasks. Section 5.1 is

      Dσ (x), conditioned on the noise level σ, and optimize Equation 3 to train the target diffusion model.

362   map Gθ . In particular, we study its impact on the transport cost and fitness to the target distribution
363   pT . In the experiment, both source and target distributions are represented with 5000 independent
364   samples. We use the same small MLP-based architecture (Shi et al., 2024) for all the networks.
365
      The main results are presented in Figure 2. The standard DMD (λ = 0.0) learns a transport map with
366   several intersections when demonstrated as the set of lines between the inputs and the outputs. This
367   observation means that the learned map is not OT, because it is not cycle-monotone (McCann, 1995).
368   Increasing λ yields fewer intersections, which can be used as a proxy evidence of optimality. At the
369   same time, the generator output distribution becomes farther and farther from the desired target. The
370   results show the importance of choosing the appropriate λ to obtain a better trade-off between the
371   two properties. Here, the regularization coefficient λ = 0.2 offers a good trade-off by having small
372   intersections and producing output distribution close to the target.

522   6   D ISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
523
524   In this paper, we propose RDMD, the novel one-step diffusion-based algorithm for the unpaired I2I
525   task. This algorithm is a modification of the DMD method for diffusion distillation. The main novelty
526   is the introduction of the transport cost regularization between the input and the output of the model,
527   which allows to control the trade-off between faithfulness and visual quality.
528   From the theoretical standpoint, we prove that at low regularization coefficients, the theoretical
529   optimum of the introduced objective is close to the optimal transport map (Theorem 1). Our
530   experiments in Section 5.1 demonstrate how the choice of regularization coefficient affects the trained
531   mapping and allows us to build the general intuition. In Sections 5.2 and 5.3 we compare our
532   method with the diffusion-based baselines in pixel and latent space and obtain better results given fair
533   restrictions on the transport cost. Given fixed realism in terms of FID, our model generally achieves
534   better faithfulness compared to the baselines, despite requiring only one function evaluation.
535   In terms of limitations, we admit that our theory works in the asymptotic regime, while one could
536   derive more precise non-limit bounds. Our experimental results are limited in terms of achieving
537   the lowest baselines’ FID values (e.g. in Cat→Wild experiment we achieve 6.9, while one of the
538   multi-step baselines, DDIB, achieves 2.71). We see making few-step modification as a potential way
539   to mitigate this difference. Furthermore, the desired feature of the method would be switching among
      different regularization coefficients without re-training.

      A    T HEORY
800
801
      In this section, we aim at proving the main theoretical result of the work: solution of the soft-
      constrained RDMD objective converges to the solution of the hard-constrained Monge problem. Our
802
      proof is largely based on the work of Liero et al. (2018). It introduces the family of entropy-transport
803
      problems, consisting in optimizing the transport cost with soft constraints based on the divergence
804   between the map’s output distribution and the target. There are, however, differences between the
805   problems, that prevent us from reducing the functional in Eq. 10 to the entropy-transport problems.
806   First, authors consider the case of finite non-negative measures, while we stick to the probability
807   distributions. Second, the family of Csiszár f -divergences (Csiszár, 1967), used by Liero et al. (2018),
808   seemingly does not contain the integral ensemble of KL divergences, used in Eq. 10. Finally, we
809   illustrate the proof in a simpler particular setting for the narrative purposes. Nevertheless, the used
      ideas are very similar.

       B    A BLATION OF THE INITIALIZATION PARAMETER
1220
1221
       In this section, we further explore the design space of our method by investigating the effect of the
1222
       fixed generator input noise parameter σ on the resulting quality. To this end, we take the colored
1223
       version of the MNIST (LeCun, 1998) data set and perform translation between the digits "2" and "3"
1224   initializing from various σ. We use a small UNet architecture from Gushchin et al. (2024a).
1225
1226   The parameter σ is residual from the pre-trained diffusion architecture and is, therefore, fixed
1227
       throughout training and evaluation. However, the target denoiser network tries to convert the expected
       noisy input into the corresponding sample from the output distribution. Consequently, one may
1228
       expect that at a suitable noise level, the generator may change the input’s details to make them look
1229
       appropriate for the target while preserving the original structural properties.
1230
1231   We demonstrate this effect on various noise levels in Figure 6. Here we observe that the small sigmas
1232   lead to the mapping close to the identity, whereas the large sigmas lead to almost constant blurry
1233
       images, corresponding to the average "3" of the data set. However, there is a segment [1.0, 10.0]
       of levels that gives a moderate-quality mapping in terms of both faithfulness and realism, which
1234
       makes it a suitable initial point. Note that the FID-L2 plot is not monotone at high L2 values due
1235
       to the overall poor quality of the generator, i.e. it outputs bad-quality pictures slightly related to the
1236   source. We further investigate optimal σ choice by going through a 2D grid of the hyperparameters
1237   (σ, λ) and aim to see if it is possible to choose the uniform best noise level. In Figure 6 we report
1238   the faithfulness-quality trade-off concerning various σ. We observe that there is almost monotone
1239   dependence on σ on the segment [1.0, 40.0]: here the σ = 1.0 gives almost uniformly best results in
1240   terms of both metrics. Similar results are obtained by the values 5.0, 10.0 which have fair quality
1241   visual results at initialization. Therefore, we conclude that it is best to choose the least parameter σ
       among the parameters with appropriate visuals at the initial point.

1653   In this section, we compare RDMD with the baselines that perform image-to-image translation based
1654   on solving different formulations of the optimal transport problem. Among them, OTCS (Gu et al.,
1655   2023b) defines a coupling over source and target domains and trains a conditional diffusion model
1656   between them. UOTM (Choi et al., 2024b) and DIOTM (Choi et al., 2024a) originate from OTM (Fan
1657   et al., 2021) and solve different minimax versions of the OT problem (first corresponds to the
1658   unbalanced OT formulation; second relies on insights from the dynamic formulation). Additionally,
1659
       we consider currently best working models based on Schrödinger bridges: DSBM (Shi et al., 2024)
       and ASBM (Gushchin et al., 2024b). We exclude similar methods as NOT (Korotin et al., 2022)
1660
       and OTM (Fan et al., 2021), because Choi et al. (2024b) and Choi et al. (2024a) show that their
1661
       performance is inferior to UOTM and DIOTM.
1662

1664   the 64 × 64 AFHQv2 Wild→Cat translation problem. We choose this problem because it is frequently
1665
       used by the methods we compare with (besides ASBM). Since UOTM was not originally validated
       on I2I experiments, we take the original implementation, modify it for this scenario, and run with the
1666
       default hyperparameters, suggested for larger-scale experiments. We also run the implementation
1667
       of DIOTM, published in OpenReview, to measure similarity metrics and FID in (train vs train) and
1668   (test vs train) scenarios. We train OTCS on Wild->Cat with the hyperparameters used for the main
1669   unpaired I2I experiment from the paper (unpaired CelebA deblurring). We also report (test vs train)
1670   FID, measured by the authors (marked by *), which is calculated by sampling 10 output images for
1671   each source. For a more fair comparison with our one-to-one implementation of RDMD, we adapt
1672   this calculation by sampling 10 augmentations for each souce test sample (original, flipped, and 4
1673   random crops for original and flipped) and report the obtained value as FID (text ×10). We take the
       DSBM metrics from De Bortoli et al. (2024, Table 1).

       From the quantitative results we observe that the method performs strictly better than all of the
