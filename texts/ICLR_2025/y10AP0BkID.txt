009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Using parts of existing models to rebuild new models, commonly termed as
012            example-based modeling, is a classical methodology in the realm of computer
013            graphics. Previous works mostly focus on shape composition, making them
014            very hard to use for realistic composition of 3D objects captured from real-
015            world scenes. This leads to combining multiple NeRFs into a single 3D scene
016            to achieve seamless appearance blending. However, the current SeamlessNeRF
017            method struggles to achieve interactive editing and harmonious stitching for real-
018            world scenes due to its gradient-based strategy and grid-based representation. To
019
               this end, we present an example-based modeling method that combines multiple
               Gaussian fields in a point-based representation using sample-guided synthesis.
020
               Specifically, as for composition, we create a GUI to segment and transform multi-
021
               ple fields in real time, easily obtaining a semantically meaningful composition of
022            models represented by 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS). For texture blending, due
023            to the discrete and irregular nature of 3DGS, straightforwardly applying gradi-
024            ent propagation as SeamlssNeRF is not supported. Thus, a novel sampling-based
025            cloning method is proposed to harmonize the blending while preserving the orig-
026            inal rich texture and content. Our workflow consists of three steps: 1) real-time
027            segmentation and transformation of a Gaussian model using a well-tailored GUI,
028            2) KNN analysis to identify boundary points in the intersecting area between the
029            source and target models, and 3) two-phase optimization of the target model using
030
               sampling-based cloning and gradient constraints. Extensive experimental results
               validate that our approach significantly outperforms previous works in terms of
031
               realistic synthesis, demonstrating its practicality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   As we all know, 3D scenes typically contain multiple 3D objects composed of various parts.
038   Example-based modeling Funkhouser et al. (2004) is a technique that involves combining differ-
039
      ent parts from different objects to create new ones. This is a common tool in Computer Graphics
      (CG) modeling, where objects are designed in a non-realistic CG fashion. In this paper, we consider
040
      realistic example-based modeling, where all parts are captured from the real world, as shown in
041
      Fig. 2. This task becomes prominent with the emergence of Neural Radiance Fields, which enables
042   photorealistic 3D reconstruction and rendering.
043
044   Among the various approaches designed for 3D modeling from multiple neural fields, a portion
045
      of the research Gao et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023b) is devoted to the inverse rendering process to
      achieve consistent lighting and shadowing. But these methods rarely consider a situation where
046
      the harmonious and seamless effect is required for merging or unifying two or more neural fields.
047
      SeamlessNeRF Gong et al. (2023) is the first work to tackle seamless merging, attempting to address
048   the consistency problem by propagating gradients on synthesis cases. Nonetheless, due to its implicit
049   grid-based representation, SeamlessNeRF can neither achieve fine-grained editing (e.g. the face in
050   the Santa case in Fig. 1) under real-world cases nor provide an interactive workflow in real-time.
051   Additionally, its gradient-based strategy can produce significant artifacts (see Fig. 9) and fails to
052   propagate structural characteristics when the condition becomes more complex (e.g., the bottle in
053   the left-upper corner in Fig. 2). Therefore, achieving a harmonious and photorealistic stitching result
      on real-world data remains an unsolved challenge that needs further exploration.

054
      To address the limitations mentioned above, we propose a new method for interactive editing and
055   stitching multiple parts using explicit shape representation in 3D Gaussian Splatting. Our method
056   has two significant advantages. First, its point-based representation enables fine-grained editing,
057   allowing for detailed appearance optimization and the removal of artifacts. Second, its rasterizer
058   pipeline provides a real-time interactive editing environment. Due to the discrete and irregular nature
059   of 3D-GS, it is not feasible to conduct gradient propagation as SeamlessNeRF. Thus, we introduce
060   a novel sampling-based optimization strategy that can seamlessly propagate not only color tones
061   but also structural characteristics. Our evaluation benchmarks are primarily derived from real-world
062   scenes, demonstrating our superior ability to handle complex cases.
063   More specifically, our pipeline takes multiple scenes as input, containing source and target objects
064   represented by 3DGS. We then carefully segment these objects and apply rigid transformations in
065   order to create a semantically meaningful composite in 3D space. An intersection boundary region
066   between the objects is also identified before blending. The next is the key step in our process which
067   aims to optimize the appearance of the target objects so that their texture and color match those of the
068   source object. We achieve this by using a two-phase optimization scheme: the first phase involves
069
      sampling-based cloning (S-phase), and the second phase involves clustering-based tuning (T-phase).
      During the S-phase, the target field is optimized using a heuristic sampling strategy that considers
070
      the structural characteristics at the boundary. Additionally, an efficient 2D gradient constraint is
071
      applied to preserve the original texture content of the target field. However, optimizing solely with
072   S-phase may lead to the appearance of artifacts or unintended color features that do not fit with the
073   overall composite. Therefore, we address this issue with T-phase, where we utilize a pre-calculated
074   feature palette derived from the source field through aggregation and clustering. Subsequently, this
075   palette is applied to tune the target field. It is important to note that the two-phase optimization is a
076   joint procedure, where losses from the S-phase are always maintained while losses from the T-phase
077   are added later during optimization.
078   In summary, our method makes the following contributions:
079
080   • The first work to use 3D-GS for realistic and seamless part compositing, enabling real-world
081
        example-based modeling.
      • A novel sampling-based optimization strategy is proposed, with which not only the texture color
082
        but also the structural characteristics can be propagated seamlessly.
083
      • A user-friendly GUI is carefully designed to support an interactive workflow of the modeling
084     process in real time.

      2.1   E XAMPLE - BASED S EAMLESS E DITING
090
      Seamless editing, particularly in the context of example-based image and texture synthesis, is a
091   well-studied editing technique in computer graphics and image processing. As for textures, example-
092   based texture synthesis Wei et al. (2009); Efros & Leung (1999) intends to seamlessly create textures
093   at any size from exemplars, which has been widely employed in contemporary graphics pipelines
094   and game engines. In 2D image synthesis, patch-based synthesis techniques have been widely re-
095   searched to seamlessly combine visually inconsistent images Pérez et al. (2023); Darabi et al. (2012).
096   Meanwhile, Kwatra et al. (2005) introduced “Texture Optimization,” which transfers photographic
097   textures to a target image for example-based synthesis. To facilitate structural image editing tasks,
098   “PatchMatch” Barnes et al. (2009) found approximate nearest-neighbor correspondences between
099
      patches in images for seamless image region reshuffling. In terms of seamless editing in 3D ob-
      jects, Rocchini et al. (1999) and Dessein et al. (2014) propose methods for stitching and blending
100
      textures on 3D objects, respectively, while Yu et al. (2004) use the Poisson equation to implicitly
101
      modify the original mesh geometry via gradient field manipulation. Additionally, example-based
102   modeling can also generate novel models from parts of existing models Funkhouser et al. (2004),
103   allowing untrained users to create interesting and detailed 3D designs, such as city building Merrell
104   (2007), things arrangements Fisher et al. (2012), mesh segmentation Katz et al. (2005), and merging
105   Kreavoy et al. (2007). Recently, deep learning methods have leveraged generative models to gen-
106   erate diverse instances from a single exemplar Wu & Zheng (2022); Li et al. (2023b) or a cluster
107   of examples Zhang et al. (2023). Definitely, the example-based methodology is a valuable tool for
      creating diverse and novel content, which can reduce the workload for the artists or can be leveraged

130   2.2      N EURAL S CENE C OMPOSITION
131
      Neural scene composition primarily involves the synthesis of multiple neural objects represented by
132
      neural fields, such as free-viewport video Lin et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023),
133
      autonomous driving Ost et al. (2021); Kundu et al. (2022); Tancik et al. (2022); Fu et al. (2022); Zhou
134   et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023) and scene understanding Kerr et al. (2023); Shuai et al. (2022); Yang
135   et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2022). And for those composition tasks with multiple pre-trained models,
136   mesh scaffold Yang et al. (2022); Yariv et al. (2023) or texture extraction Tang et al. (2023b); Chen
137   et al. (2023b) from the neural field are preferred to achieve higher render speed or rather fine-grained
138   control. This type of work acts as a “bridge” between neural and traditional representations in order
139   to improve performance using the classical graphics pipeline. A small portion of the work focuses
140   on creating a mixed render pipeline for neural 3D scene composition tasks, combining traditional
141   render techniques like ray tracing Qiao et al. (2023), shadow mapping Gao et al. (2024), and ambient
142
      occlusion Gao et al. (2023). There are also a few works that focus on creating a compositional scene
      with generative models like diffusion models Po & Wetzstein (2023).
143
144   None of those works except Neural Imposter Liu et al. (2023a) and SeamlessNeRF Gong et al.
145   (2023) focus on example-based modeling by stitching multiple part NeRFs. However, part objects
146   in Neural Imposter are just placed together without any appearance blending, which cannot support a
147   general case of 3D modeling. SeamlessNeRF achieved harmonious results on a small-scale synthesis
148
      dataset, making it the first work to discuss seamless example-based modeling with neural techniques
      today. However, SeamlessNeRF cannot handle real-world cases when the condition becomes more
149
      complex, nor can it perform interactive editing, which is commonly required in example-based
150
      modeling. On the contrary, our approach overcomes these limitations, performs well in real-world
151   scenarios, and supports interactive editing using Gaussian fields.

      2.3      3D G AUSSIANS
154
155
      3D Gaussian Splatting Kerbl et al. (2023) is a point-based rendering method that has recently gained
156   popularity Yang et al. (2024b); Huang et al. (2024); Liang et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2023a); Chen
157   et al. (2023a); Yang et al. (2024a); Li et al. (2024) due to its realistic rendering and significantly
158   faster training time than NeRFs. Compared to the implicit representation of NeRF, 3DGS is more
159   advantageous for editing tasks. The superior advance lies in the fact that, unlike previous work that
160   embedded an object in a certain neural field (e.g., learnable grid or MLP network), once clusters of
161   Gaussians are optimized, they can be easily fused together and fed into the rasterizer. The 3DGS
      pipeline was born with an intrinsic property suitable for composition.

      3.3   S AMPLING - BASED C LONING
271
272   We propose sampling-based cloning as our “S-phase” in optimization. The core idea is how to
273   seamlessly propagate the style in boundary through the remaining points in the target field while
274   preserving its rich content. In contrast to a regular grid suitable with a gradient-based strategy in
275   SeamlessNeRF Gong et al. (2023), Gaussian points are irregularly and discretely distributed in 3D
276   space. As a result, alternative approaches need to be explored. A straightforward idea is that given a
277   point in target field T , one can calculate the feature difference between that point and its neighbors in
278
      T , resembling “Laplacian coordinates”. Then, one can use that “difference” as the regularizer while
      minimizing Lfeature . However, this naive approach may fail even before propagation begins (see
279
      Fig. 4). Furthermore, the boundary’s structural characteristics (such as the bottle-bell intersection
280
      in the right-upper corner of Fig. 9) necessitate seamless cloning, which significantly improves the
281   stitching quality.
282
283   Hence, we propose an effective sampling strategy to explicitly propagate features for each remaining
284
      point outside the boundary. The core idea lies in the way of searching several “driven points” for a
      candidate. The color of the candidate is driven by those points. For each a ∈ T −∂B, the optimizing

       4.1           Q UALITATIVE C OMPARISON
407
408    We compare our method to SeamlessNeRF Gong et al. (2023), the first and most recent work that
409    approaches our goal. Fig. 9 depicts three comparison cases. In the first case (clay & bread), Seam-
410    lessNeRF failed to achieve high-level geometry editability and struggled with artifacts caused by
411    implicit representation. In the second case (bottle and bell), SeamlessNeRF failed to maintain a har-
412    monious seamless effect due to applying the gradient-based strategy on the complex boundary. In
413    the third case, SeamlessNeRF failed to propagate sufficient color tones due to the complex gradients
414    in the boundary. In addition, we show that the 2D-guided style-transfer method Nguyen-Phuoc et al.
415
       (2022) cannot produce a seamless stitching effect, as shown in Fig. 10. On the contrary, ours can
       handle all of these situations while producing harmonious results.

474   5     C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
475
476   We have developed a highly efficient and effective interactive framework for creating realistic 3D
477   models. The method involves stitching Gaussian components seamlessly to create a harmonious 3D
478   model that is an accurate representation of the real world. Our approach has been tested on real-
479   world datasets and has proved to be capable of handling complex cases with a user-friendly interface.
480   This presents a promising avenue for example-based modeling directly from the real world.
481
482   Limitations and Future Work. Currently, our work is unable to transform Gaussian models in
483   a non-rigid manner, which may make it difficult to develop more imaginative cases. To enable a
484   more flexible composition, we can use deformation methods such as ARAP Igarashi et al. (2005) in
485   the future. Furthermore, achieving a consistent lighting effect can help improve composition quality
      under intense lighting.
