011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           Explainable molecular property prediction is essential for various scientific fields,
014           such as drug discovery and material science. Despite delivering intrinsic explain-
015           ability, linear models struggle with capturing complex, non-linear patterns. Large
016           language models (LLMs), on the other hand, yield accurate predictions through
017
              powerful inference capabilities yet fail to provide chemically meaningful explana-
              tions for their predictions. This work proposes a novel framework, called MoleX,
018
              which leverages LLM knowledge to build a simple yet powerful linear model for
019
              accurate molecular property prediction with faithful explanations. The core of
020           MoleX is to model complicated molecular structure-property relationships using
021           a simple linear model, augmented by LLM knowledge and a crafted calibration
022           strategy. Specifically, to extract the maximum amount of task-relevant knowledge
023           from LLM embeddings, we employ information bottleneck-inspired fine-tuning
024           and sparsity-inducing dimensionality reduction. These informative embeddings
025           are then used to fit a linear model for explainable inference. Moreover, we intro-
026           duce residual calibration to address prediction errors stemming from linear mod-
027           els’ insufficient expressiveness of complex LLM embeddings, thus recovering the
028
              LLM’s predictive power and boosting overall accuracy. Theoretically, we provide
              a mathematical foundation to justify MoleX’s explainability. Extensive experi-
029
              ments demonstrate that MoleX outperforms existing methods in molecular prop-
030
              erty prediction, establishing a new milestone in predictive performance, explain-
031           ability, and efficiency. In particular, MoleX enables CPU inference and accelerates
032           large-scale dataset processing, achieving comparable performance 300× faster
033           with 100,000 fewer parameters than LLMs. Additionally, the calibration improves
034           model performance by up to 12.7% without compromising explainability. The
035           source code is available at https://github.com/MoleX2024/MoleX.

048
      in text-based molecules, LLMs show promising capabilities in providing accurate molecular prop-
049   erty predictions (Ahmad et al., 2022). Nevertheless, the black-box nature of LLMs hinders the
050   understanding of their decision-making mechanisms. Inevitably, this opacity prevents people from
051   deriving reliable predictions and insights from these models (Wu et al., 2023a).

140   2   R ELATED W ORK
141
142   Explainable Molecular Property Prediction. Given that molecules can be naturally represented
143   as graphs, a collection of explainable GNNs have been proposed to explain the relationship between
144   molecular structures and properties (Lin et al., 2021; Pope et al., 2019). However, these atom or
145   bond-level explanations are not chemically meaningful to interpret their sophisticated relationships.
146   Besides, through learning chemical semantics, the transformer-based LLMs can effectively capture
147
      interactions among substructures (Wang et al., 2024) and thus demonstrated their potential in under-
      standing text-based molecules (Ross et al., 2022; Chithrananda et al., 2020). However, the opaque
148
      decision-making process of LLMs obscures their operating principles, risking unfaithful predictions
149
      with severe consequences, especially in high-stakes domains like drug discovery (Chen et al., 2024).
150
151   Explainability Methods for LLMs. To obtain trustworthy output, various techniques were in-
152   troduced to unveil the LLM’s explainability. The gradient-based explanations analyze the feature
153
      importance by computing output partial derivatives with respect to input (Sundararajan et al., 2017).
      These methods, nevertheless, lack robustness in their explanations due to sensitivity to data per-
154
      turbations (Kindermans et al., 2019; Adebayo et al., 2018). The attention-based explanations use
155
      attention weights to interpret outputs (Hoover et al., 2020). Yet, recent studies challenge their reli-
156   ability as attention weights may not consistently reflect true feature importance (Jain and Wallace,
157   2019; Serrano and Smith, 2019). The perturbation-based explanations elucidate model behaviors by
158   observing output changes in response to input alterations (Ribeiro et al., 2016). However, these ex-
159   planations are unstable due to the randomness of the perturbations (Agarwal et al., 2021). To resolve
160   these issues, we extract informative embeddings from the LLM to fit a linear model for inference.
161   This approach leverages both the LLM’s knowledge and the linear model’s explainability, offering
      reliable substructure-level explanations.

      overall model’s explainability.

336   Quantifiable Functional Group Contributions. As described in section 3, we measure the func-
337   tional group xj ’s contributions to molecular property y using n-gram coefficients. The molecular
338   property y distributes its entire semantic information into individual functional groups xj . Due to the
339   linearity and additivity between xj and y, the scalar coefficient wj corresponding to xj in the linear
340   model weighs xj ’s contributions to y in terms of chemical semantics. By taking the dot product of
341   wj and the embedding of xj , we obtain a projection length of the functional group in the direction
342
      of weight vector, thus quantifying the impact of that functional group on the molecular property.
      Quantitatively, the larger the absolute value of an n-gram coefficient, the greater the contribution
343
      of the corresponding functional group to property. This metric provides a rigorous interpretation of
344
      feature contributions, ensuring unbiasedness and significance through OLS estimation (see our proof
345   in appendix A.1). Using this method, we identify important functional groups from the LLM’s com-
346   plex embedding space. Furthermore, by incorporating n-gram coefficients and identified functional
347   groups into the molecular graph, we can determine whether identified functional groups bond with
348   each other and infer interactions among them. Based on this, MoleX reveals chemically meaningful
349   substructures along with their interactions to faithfully explain molecular property predictions.
