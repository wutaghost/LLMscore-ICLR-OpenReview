009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformer models have revolutionized natural language processing, achieving
012            state-of-the-art performance and demonstrating remarkable scalability. However,
013            their memory demands, particularly due to maintaining full context in memory,
014            pose significant challenges for inference. In this paper, we present FlashEVA,
015            an efficient implementation of EVA (Efficient Attention via Control Variates),
016            and demonstrate how to finetune transformers to adapt to FlashEVA attention.
017            Our method enables fine-tuning of Transformer models with as few as 1.5B to-
018            kens while preserving effectiveness across various downstream tasks. Notably,
019
               FlashEVA achieves up to 6.7x higher throughput and 5x lower peak GPU mem-
               ory usage during inference compared to standard Transformer implementations.
020
               Despite these improvements, we observe limitations in retrieval-focused tasks.
021
               Our implementation offers control over the trade-off between throughput and ac-
022            curacy through adjustable hyperparameters, providing flexibility for diverse use
023            cases. This work represents a significant step towards more efficient and adapt-
024            able Transformer-based models for inference.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Transformer models have become ubiquitous in the field of natural language processing, achieving
030   state-of-the-art performance across a wide range of tasks (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
031   2019; Radford et al., 2019; Dong et al., 2018). Their success can be attributed to their ability to
032   scale effectively and the possibility of parallel training, which has led to significant improvements in
033   model capabilities (Kaplan et al., 2020; Gadre et al., 2024). However, these advancements come at
034   a cost: Transformers have high memory requirements during inference, particularly due to the need
035   for an ever-increasing cache to keep the full history in context (Pope et al., 2022).
036   This memory constraint poses a significant challenge, especially as new use cases emerge that de-
037   mand higher throughput. Such applications include inference on long contexts (e.g. Q&A over long
038   documents or large codebases) (Rozière et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2022; Shaham et al.,
039   2022), agentic workflows that require exploring multiple trajectories in parallel (Yang et al., 2024a;
040   Yao et al., 2023b), as well as workflows combining search algorithms with LLMs (Chen et al., 2023;
041   Yao et al., 2023a; Wang & Zhou, 2024)
042   As these demands grow, it becomes increasingly important to address the limitations of Transformer
043   models. A crucial consideration is the desire to leverage the existing pre-trained Transformer mod-
044   els, avoiding the need for resource-intensive retraining from scratch. There are different approaches
045   that aim to address these issues:
046
047          • Distributed KV cache storage, as shown by Liu et al. (2023a), can be achieved without
048            communication overhead, due to the fact that the attention operation can be computed in a
049            blockwise fashion (Dao et al., 2022). This allows the computation of different blocks to be
050
               done on separate devices and can in principle support an infinite context length provided
               enough GPUs are available.
051
052          • KV cache compression techniques aim to compress the existing KV cache Zhao et al.
053            (2024); Yue et al. (2024), or discard uninformative tokens from the cache (Jiang et al., 2023;
               Han et al., 2024) to minimize the memory cost without sacrificing model performance.

054
             • State-Space Models (SSMs) are an alternative class of models that have shown to be com-
055            petitive with transformers on small to medium scale (Gu & Dao; Yang et al., 2023; Qin
056            et al., 2024b; Peng et al., 2023). Recent works Wang et al. (2024); Bick et al. (2024)
057            have shown that transformers can be efficiently distilled into Mamba, keeping most of the
058            downstream performance.
059
             • Linearized attention approaches aim to replace the Softmax attention in the transformer
060
               with linearized variants, which promises to reduce the computational and memory require-
061
               ments of the attention (Zheng et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2021). Previous
062            works attempting to adapt pretrained transformers into linearized transformers have not
063            scaled or maintained model performance on downstream tasks (Chen et al., 2024; Mao,
064            2022; Kasai et al., 2021a).
065
066   In this paper, we propose to revisit and enhance the Efficient Attention via Control Variates (EVA)
067   method (Zheng et al., 2023) to address these challenges. We present an efficient implementation of
068   EVA attention using custom CUDA and Triton kernels, which allows us to maintain the benefits of
069   Transformer models while reducing their memory and computational footprint.
070   Our approach demonstrates that Transformers can be fine-tuned with as little as 1.5 billion tokens,
071   recovering most of the original performance on downstream tasks. Nevertheless, the performance
072   still suffers on retrieval focused tasks, similar to previous approaches with linearized attention and
073   state space models (Waleffe et al., 2024).
074
      While FlashEVA attention still requires to keep a cache of (compressed) past context, this overhead
075   is less than the memory overhead for computing the prefix during model inference, and is thus does
076   not represent a limitation for the inference. Notably, compared to vanilla transformers, our method
077   achieves significant improvements in inference scenarios of particular interest, such as generating
078   content with long prompts, handling extended generations, and maximizing parallel throughput. We
079   report up to 6.7x inference throughput increase on long sequence generation, and up to 5x reduc-
080   tion in peak GPU memory usage on long sequence generation. Additionally, EVA attention allows
081   for trading off memory and model performance in a principled manner, through two hyperparam-
082   eters, allowing up to 50% lower memory usage with 0.5% accuracy impact on downstream task
083
      performance.
084   The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: In Section 2, we review the theoretical founda-
085   tions of EVA attention and introduce our efficient implementation, which we call FlashEVA. Section
086   3 details our experimental setup, including model architecture, training procedures, and evaluation
087   metrics. Section 4 presents our results, comparing FlashEVA’s performance to existing methods
088   across various tasks and inference scenarios. We also provide an in-depth analysis of the trade-offs
089
      between speed, accuracy, and memory usage. Finally, Section 5 concludes the paper, summarizing
      our findings and discussing potential avenues for future research in efficient attention mechanisms
090
      for large language models. An extended review of related works, as well as ablation studies are in
091
      the Appendix.

094   2     BACKGROUND
095
096   This section provides a comprehensive review of key attention mechanisms, establishing the foun-
097   dation for our work. We begin by revisiting the Softmax attention mechanism and elucidating its
098   relationship to Random Feature Attention. Subsequently, we examine the Efficient Vision Attention
099   (EVA) framework, which reinterprets Randomized Attention through the lens of control variates.
100   Finally, we highlight the practical implementation challenges associated with EVA attention and
101   demonstrate how these limitations can be addressed by reformulating the approach as Softmax at-
102   tention over a modified set of keys and values.

      Note, in the rest of the paper, we refer to the k̃c and β̂c (ω) as RFA keys and values, or simply

220   tional challenges. It necessitates attending separately to past keys and random-feature based keys,
221   requiring a custom attention mask not supported by the standard FlashAttention implementation.
222   To address this, we adapt the FlashAttention Triton kernels2 to accommodate the required attention
223   mask.
224   This adaptation facilitates a significant enhancement to the original EVA attention mechanism.
225   Specifically, it enables the implementation of a sliding window for local attention, which has the
226   potential to improve model performance by providing an effectively larger context window for the
227   deeper attention layers in the model.

230   3   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
231
232
233
      Our main experiments leverage the open-source Pythia checkpoints (Biderman et al., 2023) for fine-
      tuning. This choice provides a consistent family of models at various scales, enabling us to evaluate
234
      our method’s scalability while maintaining architectural and pretraining consistency.
235
236   Pythia was pretrained on the Pile (Gao et al., 2020), an 825 GB corpus of English text designed for
237   language model pretraining. To minimize discrepancies between pretraining and fine-tuning setups
238   that could impact the efficiency of adaptation to a different attention mechanism, we utilize the same
239
      dataset for model fine-tuning. Due to copyright disputes rendering the original dataset unavailable,
      we employ the uncopyrighted version accessible on HuggingFace3 .
240
241   We fine-tune the models for 50k steps with a sequence length of 2048 and a total batch size of 16,
242   amounting to approximately 1.6B tokens. For larger models, we initially warm up the attention
243   layers for 2k steps (using a constant learning rate of 3e − 4) before continuing the fine-tuning of all
244   weights for an additional 48k steps (using a one-cycle cosine decay schedule). This approach miti-
245
      gates the risk of large gradients disrupting the pre-learned weights and enhances training stability.
246   To accelerate training, we employ custom-implemented Triton kernels for FlashEVA, torch compile,
247   and mixed precision. As these optimizations can cause instabilities in larger models during the initial
248   stages of training due to large gradients, we conduct the first 2k steps without mixed precision or
249   custom Triton kernels.
250   We adapt the Pythia model to incorporate gated output projection in the attention layer, similar to Sun
251   et al. (2023) and Chen et al. (2024). To stabilize training, we clip the values and reduce the variance
252   of the random weights in EVA attention. Additionally, we employ RoPE positional encoding (Su
253   et al., 2023) during fine-tuning with FlashEVA, applied to all tokens prior to the random feature
254   projections. Detailed ablation studies on various model and training choices are presented in the
255   Appendix.
256   For downstream model performance evaluation, we utilize the LM evaluation harness (Gao et al.,
257   2024) to conduct reproducible assessments. Following prior work (Chen et al., 2024; Biderman
258   et al., 2023), we select six tasks: PIQA (Bisk et al., 2019), WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2019),
259   WSC (Sakaguchi et al., 2019), ARC-C and ARC-E (Clark et al., 2018), and LogiQA (Liu et al.,
260   2020). To further assess the model’s retrieval capabilities, we include two additional tasks: FDA
261   (Arora et al., 2023) and SWDE (Arora et al., 2024a).
262   To assess inference throughput, we consider a fixed prefix/prompt of 2048 tokens, similar to Gu &
263   Dao, with varying numbers of generated tokens ranging from 1024 to 10240. To measure maximum
264   throughput, we explore batch sizes up to 32. For the EVA hyperparameters inference performance
265   trade-off analysis, we constrain the setting to a prefix of 2048 tokens and 1024 generated tokens.

300   The experimental results demonstrate that FlashEVA effectively recovers the performance of Pythia
301   on the majority of downstream tasks across various model sizes, ranging from 70M to 1B parame-
302   ters. Notably, the average performance across six downstream tasks is nearly identical to the Pythia
303   baseline, despite utilizing only 1.6B tokens for finetuning. Furthermore, FlashEVA consistently out-
304   performs DiJiang across all model sizes, suggesting that the random feature approach employed by
305   FlashEVA provides a more effective approximation of Softmax attention.
306   However, an exception is observed in retrieval-focused tasks, where the pairwise interaction facil-
307   itated by full Softmax attention appears to be crucial. In these tasks, while FlashEVA marginally
308   outperforms DiJiang, it exhibits significantly lower performance compared to both the Softmax
309   and sliding window attention baselines. This suboptimal performance can be attributed to the ab-
310   sence of a sliding window mechanism in FlashEVA’s architecture. Although we have implemented
311   a FlashEVA variant incorporating sliding window attention (detailed in the Appendix), it was not
312   employed in the current experiments due to training instabilities on larger models, likely stemming
313   from the custom Triton kernel implementation.
314   Interestingly, the sliding window attention baseline exhibits remarkably robust performance, recov-
315   ering a substantial portion of Softmax attention’s capabilities, with a more pronounced performance
316   gap in retrieval tasks (but higher than FlashEVA or DiJiang). This phenomenon may be partially
317   explained by the limited finetuning of 1.6B tokens, which, while sufficient for adaptation to sliding
318   window attention, may be suboptimal for larger models to fully leverage the FlashEVA or DiJiang
319   attention mechanisms. It is noteworthy that FlashEVA outperforms sliding window attention on
320
      smaller models, with this performance differential diminishing as model size increases. Addition-
      ally, the sliding window in the attention effectively expands the context window of deeper attention
321
      layers, resulting in a more expressive model.

347

348   design incorporating a cache that scales with sequence length, our empirical observations reveal that
349   peak memory consumption occurs during prefix computation. In practical scenarios, FlashEVA thus
350   exhibits peak memory usage comparable to Sliding Window Attention, while DiJiang Attention

352   peak memory usage, up to 5-fold—compared to the vanilla Transformer.

354   marginally underperforming Sliding Window Attention. This performance differential can be at-
355   tributed to two factors: (1) the reduced number of sequential operations required for token compu-
356   tation in Sliding Window Attention, and (2) the generally memory-bound nature of inference tasks.
357   Nevertheless, FlashEVA demonstrates substantial throughput improvements over the vanilla Trans-
358   former, achieving a 2-fold increase when generating 1024 tokens and up to a 6.7-fold improvement
359   for 10240 token generation tasks.

        4.3                                    F LASH EVA INFERENCE PERFORMANCE TRADEOFF
379
380    (Flash)EVA introduces two key hyperparameters that influence its performance and computational
381    requirements: the local attention window size and the number of keys/values compressed into a
382    single random feature-based token. Generally, larger local attention windows or reduced token
383    compression result in increased computational costs.
384    Our findings demonstrate that FlashEVA can substantially reduce peak memory usage while main-
385    taining competitive performance. Certain configurations decrease peak memory by up to 50% with
386    only a 0.5% reduction in average performance on downstream tasks (excluding retrieval-focused
387    tasks). Moreover, we observe up to a 50% increase in total inference throughput with minimal
388    performance degradation. It is noteworthy that configurations with larger local attention windows
389    exhibit superior performance on retrieval-focused tasks. However, we do not include these tasks in
390    the reported average accuracy, hypothesizing that hybrid models may offer a more favorable trade-
391    off for retrieval task performance.
392    Interestingly, the configuration used for our main results (local window size 256, RFA chunk size 16)
393    is suboptimal in terms of inference performance. Our analysis suggests that configurations (256, 8)
394    or (512, 8) may offer a more optimal balance between performance and computational efficiency.

415
416    We benchmark the time to run a forward and backward pass of the (Flash)EVA attention layer and
417
       compare it to FlashAttention2 kernels. We evaluate at different sequence lengths, adjusting batch

418
       and another 128 random-feature based tokens.
419
420    Despite its theoretical promise of improved computational complexity, EVA attention encounters
421    challenges similar to previous linear attention implementations, where practical performance gains
422    fail to materialize, particularly when compared to the efficient FlashAttention implementation. Fla-
423
       shEVA attention does achieve speedups over FlashAttention2 for longer sequences due to its lower
       computational complexity, up to 2.2x on for the 16k sequence length. However, for shorter se-
424
       quences, the overhead associated with random feature computation results in slower performance.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
757
758
      Linear transformers Linear transformers were introduced by (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) where
759   they also showed their formulation as RNNs. Following works Peng et al. (2021); Zheng et al.
760   (2022); Qin et al. (2022); Choromanski et al. (2022), focused on improving the features maps used
761   in linear transformers to close the gap to Vanilla transformer. Subsequent works added also architec-
762   tural improvements, such as output gating of the attention Qin et al. (2024a); Sun et al. (2023); Hua
763   et al. (2022), and (learned) decay of the hidden state Yang et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2023); Peng et al.
764   (2023). Despite closing the performance gap to transformers on most tasks, there are still funda-
765   mental limitations of linear transformers, especially when it comes to retrieval tasks and in context
766   learning (Arora et al., 2024b; Merrill et al., 2024; Akyürek et al., 2024). Finally, (Dao & Gu, 2024)
767
      also showed that there exists a connection between linear transformers and specific subset of SSM
      models.
768
769   State Space Models Deep State Space Models were introduced as an efficient architecture to model
770   long sequences. S4, DSS showed promising results on long range synthetic tasks. Later models such
771   as GSS (Mehta et al., 2022), BiGS (Wang et al., 2023), and H3 (Fu et al., 2023) introduced gating as
772   a way to increase the expressivity of the model (i.e. increase the interactivity between tokens). The
773
      newest generation of models (Mamba (Gu & Dao), GLA Yang et al. (2023), DeltaNet (Yang et al.,
      2024b), HGRNN (Qin et al., 2024b), xLSTM(Beck et al., 2024)) introduce input dependent SSM
774
      parameters, which does not allow the SSM to be expressed as a convolution over the input, however,
775
      it can still be computed efficiently with parallel scan. These models are achieving comparable
776   performance to Transformers on language modeling tasks, and crucially, have significantly higher
777   throughput, thanks to the fact, that they do not require to store the full past context on inference, but
778   rather only the hidden state.
779
      Hybrid Models Currently, the biggest gap in performance between SSMs/Linear transformer mod-
780
      els and Transformer models is the performance on (in context) retrieval tasks (Wen et al., 2024;
781
      Jelassi et al., 2024). Consequently, many of the recent models propose to use a combination of
782   attention and recurrent layers in the same model Zancato et al. (2024). Griffin (De et al., 2024)
783   uses a combination of Linear Recurrent Units (Orvieto et al., 2023) with sliding window attention
784   layers, matching the performance of Llama-2, while achieving lower latency and higher throughput
785   on inference in models of size up to 14B parameters. Similarly, Dao & Gu (2024); Waleffe et al.
786   (2024); Glorioso et al. (2024) use a combination of Mamba layers and Attention layers to achieve
787   same or better performance than transformer based baselines.
788   Distilling transformers into RNNs This idea has been initially investigated by Kasai et al. (2021b),
789   where they replaced the Sofmax in the attention with learnable feature maps for the queries and keys,
790   composed of a one layer MLP with ReLU activation and then finetuned the pretrained transformer
791   model. They obtained substantial memory savings and inference speedup, however, the performance
792   lagged the pretrained transformer performance on language modelling and machine translation tasks.
793   (Zhang et al., 2024) improved on this by finetuning the transformer into a linearized transformers,
794   where an additional loss terms is used to match the linearized attention to the softmax attention.
795   However, this method required having access to teh full attention matrix of the base transformer
796
      during training, which is computationally expensive. Instead, recent works (Bick et al., 2024; Wang
      et al., 2024) apply more involved distillation methods to distill Transformers into (hybrid) Mamba
797
      based models with as few as 3B tokens. They show competitive performance to Transformer on
798
      downstream tasks on models up to 7B parameters.
799
800   KV cache compression The autoregressive nature of language models requires storing the full past
801   context to produce the next new token. To speed up inference, caching key-value states (KV-Cache)
802
      in memory is a simple yet effective technique Pope et al. (2022), however, this necessitates a large
      amount of memory. Consequently, there are several lines of research that looked into compress-
803
      ing KV cache. Quantization based techniques Zhao et al. (2024); Yue et al. (2024); Zhang et al.
804
      (2023); Liu et al. (2023b) aim to reduce the memory footprint needed by each token in the cache.
805   Eviction based techniques seek ways to keep only a subset of tokens in the KV cache without sac-
806   rificing model performance significantly Jiang et al. (2024; 2023); Han et al. (2024). Additionally,
807   some works looked at compressing the cache via low rank projection of the tokens. Unlike these
808   approaches, our method compresses the cache through summarizing chunks of tokens into single
809   tokens as part of the attention mechanism itself.
