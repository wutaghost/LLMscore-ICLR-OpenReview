009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative models, particularly diffusion models, have achieved remarkable suc-
012            cess in density estimation for multimodal data, drawing significant interest from the
013            reinforcement learning (RL) community, especially in policy modeling in continu-
014            ous action spaces. However, existing works exhibit significant variations in training
015            schemes and RL optimization objectives, and some methods are only applicable to
016            diffusion models. In this study, we compare and analyze various generative policy
017            training and deployment techniques, identifying and validating effective designs for
018
               generative policy algorithms. Specifically, we revisit existing training objectives
               and classify them into two categories, each linked to a simpler approach. The
019
               first approach, Generative Model Policy Optimization (GMPO), employs a native
020
               advantage-weighted regression formulation as the training objective, which is sig-
021            nificantly simpler than previous methods. The second approach, Generative Model
022            Policy Gradient (GMPG), offers a numerically stable implementation of the native
023            policy gradient method. We introduce a standardized experimental framework
024            named GenerativeRL. Our experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods
025            achieve state-of-the-art performance on various offline-RL datasets, offering a
026            unified and practical guideline for training and deploying generative policies.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Generative models, such as flow models and diffusion models, have demonstrated remarkable
032   capabilities in modeling multi-modal data across diverse applications, including image, video, and
033
      audio generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Mittal et al., 2021), and protein structure
      prediction (Abramson et al., 2024). Their expressive power stems from constructing continuous
034
      and invertible mappings between probability distributions, enabling the transformation of simple
035
      distributions like standard Gaussians into complex target distributions. Generative policies, which
036   are RL policy models based on generative models, have become a focus of study within the RL
037   community (Janner et al., 2022; Chi et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024). They offer a principled approach
038   to modeling expressive and nuanced action distributions, particularly important in robotics tasks with
039   continuous and high-dimensional action spaces.
040
      Despite the success of recent studies in offline-RL (Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
041
      2023; Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023), these studies often employ complex training schemes and lack
042   systematic investigation. This hinders understanding of key factors in training generative models for
043   policy modeling, leading to unnecessary dependencies, training inefficiencies, and higher inference
044   costs. Some work focuses primarily on diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020),
045   which has limited applicability to other generative models, such as flow models (Lipman et al., 2023;
046   Liu et al., 2023; Pooladian et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Tong et al., 2024).
047
      This motivates us to develop simple yet effective training schemes for both diffusion and other
048   emerging generative models, leveraging their advancements for the RL community’s benefit. We
049   revisit previous works on generative policy optimization in Table 1, and then categorize these works,
050   and propose two training schemes: Generative Model Policy Optimization (GMPO) and Generative
051   Model Policy Gradient (GMPG). GMPO is an advantage-weighted regression method with a stable
052   training process that does not require pretraining the generative model before optimal policy extraction.
053   This makes it more efficient and easier to train, featuring a shorter training schedule and a wider
      range of model applications while maintaining comparable performance to previous works. GMPG

054
      is a policy gradient-based method in an RL-native formulation. We provide a numerically stable
055   implementation for continuous-time generative models as an RL policy. It has proven effective
056   in optimal policy extraction, especially for suboptimal policies. We evaluate the performance of
057   diffusion and flow models using these two training schemes in an offline reinforcement learning
058   setting with the D4RL dataset (Fu et al., 2021) and RL Unplugged (Gulcehre et al., 2020). Our results
059   demonstrate that the proposed schemes offer comparable or better performance than previous works
060   in most cases. We provide multiple ablations, covering model types, temperature settings, solver
061   schemes, and sampling time steps for training, to validate the effectiveness of our proposed methods.
062   To facilitate consistent comparisons and analyses, we introduce a standardized experimental frame-
063   work designed to combine the strengths of generative models and reinforcement learning by decou-
064   pling the generative model from the RL components. This decoupling enables consistent comparisons
065   and analyses of different generative models within the same RL context, a capability lacking in
066   previous works. Four key properties distinguish our framework from existing ones: unified API,

068   In conclusion, the main contributions of our work include:
069
070   • Proposing two simple yet effective RL-native training approaches for generative policies, GMPO
071     and GMPG, which are compatible for both diffusion and flow models.
072
      • Evaluating the performance of different generative models with the two training schemes on offline-

075
      • Providing a unified framework for combining the power of generative models and RL seamlessly,
076
        easy for conducting a fair and RL-native evaluations, named GenerativeRL.

082   energy-based generative policy for learning continuous actions from continuous states. Haarnoja et al.
083   (2018a) integrates hierarchical policies with discrete layers into the Soft Actor-Critic (Haarnoja et al.,
084   2018b) framework, which is called SACLSP and utilizes normalizing flows. Janner et al. (2022) first
085
      incorporate diffusion models into RL named as diffuser, acting as optimal trajectory planners by
      linking generative models with value function guidance being applied rudimentary. Chi et al. (2023)
086
      explores diffusion models for policy modeling in robotics, which is the concept of diffusion policy
087
      is first introduced. Chen et al. (2023) demonstrates effective policy learning in offline Q-learning
088   using a diffusion model as support called SfBC. Wang et al. (2023) introduces Diffusion-QL and
089   achieves policy regularization by alternating training between a Q-function-guided diffusion model
090   and a diffusion-supported Q-function. Lu et al. (2023) introduces Q-guided Policy Optimization
091   (QGPO) and derives the exact formulation of energy guidance for energy-conditioned diffusion
092   models, enabling precise Q-guidance for optimal policy. Hansen-Estruch et al. (2023) introduces
093   Implicit Diffusion Q-learning (IDQL) which uncovers the implicit policy form after Implicit Q-
094   learning, emphasizing the importance of sampling from both the behavior policy and the diffusion
095   model. Chen et al. (2024) uses the score function of a pre-trained diffusion model as a regularizer,
096
      optimizing a Gaussian policy to maximize the Q-function by combining the multimodal properties
      of diffusion models with the fast inference of Gaussian policies, which is called Score Regularized
097
      Policy Optimization (SRPO). Similar methods can also be applied to models trained using the flow
098
      matching (Zheng et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2024). More details about the generative policy analyzed
099   in this paper are provided in Appendix C.

      3.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
121
      Reinforcement learning (RL) addresses sequential decision-making tasks typically modeled as a
122   Markov decision process (MDP), defined by the tuple (S, A, p, r, γ). Here, S is the state space, A is
123   the action space, p(st+1 |st , at ) represents the transition dynamics, r(st , at ) is the reward function,
124   and γ is the discount factor. At each time step t, the agent, in state st , takes an action at ∈ A
125   according to the policy π(at |st ). The agent then receives a reward rt and transitions to a new state
126   st+1 based on the transition dynamics p(st+1 |st , at ). The goal of the agent is to learn a policy
127   π : S → A that maximizes the expected cumulative reward over time,        Pusing only previously collected
                                                                                 ∞
128   data. This objective can be expressed as: Rs0 ,a0 = Es0 ,a0 ,s1 ,a1 ,... [ t=0 γ t rt ] .
129   In offline reinforcement learning (offline-RL), the agent has access to a fixed dataset Dµ of historical
130   interaction trajectories {st , at , rt , st+1 }, collected by a behavior policy µ(at |st ), which is often
131   suboptimal. Offline-RL is challenging because the agent cannot collect new data to correct its
132   mistakes, unlike in online-RL, where exploration is possible.
133
      Suppose the value of action a at state s is modeled by a Q-function Q(s, a) ≈ Rs,a , which estimates
134   the expected return of taking action a at state s and following policy π thereafter. The value of state s
135   is modeled by a V-function V (s) = Ea∼π(·|s) [Q(s, a)].

      computational efficiency and expressiveness.

334   5     F RAMEWORK
335
336   Based on the research needs analyzed in previous sections, we create GenerativeRL for verifying and
337
      comparing various generative models and reinforcement learning algorithms. The key distinction of
      GenerativeRL is its standardized implementation and unified API for generative models, allowing
338
      researchers to access these models at the configuration level without dealing with complex details.
339
      When compared to existing frameworks like CleanDiffuser (Dong et al., 2024), GenerativeRL differs
340   in its design principles:
341
342   • Unified API: It offers a simple API that maximizes compatibility with different kinds of generative
343     models, avoiding immature algorithms.
344   • Flexible Data Formats: It ensures consistent data formats for long-term use, supporting inputs and
345     outputs as PyTorch tensors, tensordicts (Bou et al., 2023), and treetensors (Contributors, 2021),
346     given the prevalence of dict-type data in RL.
347   • Auto-Grad Support: Designed to support Neural ODEs, it facilitates gradient-based inference via
348     ODE or SDE, which is useful for many RL policies.
349
      • Diverse Model Integration: It integrates various generative models, including flow and bridge
350
        models, treating diffusion models as a special case.
351
352   Thus, GenerativeRL seamlessly incorporates both diffusion and flow models for various RL algo-
353   rithms, while decoupling the generative model from RL components. This allows for consistent
354   comparisons and analyses of different generative models within the same RL context. See usage
355   examples in Appendix E.1 and framework structure in Appendix E.2.

399   Table 3 shows the performance of various generative policy algorithms on D4RL environments,
400   including SfBC (Chen et al., 2023), Diffusion-QL (Wang et al., 2023), QGPO (Lu et al., 2023),
401   IDQL (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023), and SRPO (Chen et al., 2024). Table 4 presents the performance
402   of generative policies on the RL Unplugged DeepMind Control Suite datasets (Gulcehre et al., 2020).
403   For reference, we include the performance of two classical offline RL algorithms: RABM (Siegel
404   et al., 2020) and D4PG (Barth-Maron et al., 2018), the latter being the algorithm for which most of
405
      this dataset was collected. The average performance across these tasks demonstrates that GMPO
      and GMPG effectively solve challenging continuous control tasks, achieving competitive results
406
      compared with other state-of-the-art algorithms.
407
408   Hansen-Estruch et al. (2023) claims that using highly expressive models with importance weighted
409   objectives can be problematic as such models can increase the likelihood of all training points
410   regardless of their weight. And they find using advantage-weighted regression in the DDPM objective
411
      to not help performance, so they recommend sampling from behavior policy and filter out the high
      Q-value actions with the softmax importance sampling. However, our practice overturns the above
412
      claims and found that generative policy trained with advantage-weighted regression even from a
413
      scratch initialization can gain comparably equivalent performance to IDQL using resampling tricks,
414   as shown in Table 3. Since utilizing the same Implicit Q-learning method, GMPO and GMPG
415   both successfully extract optimal policies from the Q function. GMPO, a simpler variant of QGPO,
416   achieves comparable performance on these datasets. This indicates that the advantage-weighted
417   regression loss, a common component of both methods, is crucial for successful training.
418
      Unlike Diffusion-QL, which separates the KL divergence into a simulation-free score matching
419
      loss but remains Q guidance through simulation, GMPG computes both Q guidance and the KL
420   divergence directly through simulation, achieving similar performance. Despite using a significantly
421   larger number of sampling steps (T = 1000) compared to Diffusion-QL (T = 5), GMPG does not
422   exhibit computational difficulties in our implementation. This effectively addresses the limitations
423   highlighted by Wang et al. (2023), where the maximum T they could afford was 20, and they opted
424   for T = 5 to balance performance and computational cost.
425
      In addition, our experiments challenged the intuition that a complete 1000-step inference inherently
426   leads to high computational costs, even with Neural ODEs. Our results demonstrate that with
427   direct gradient guidance, no extra computational cost is required for optimization. For example, in
428   halfcheetah-medium-v2-GMPG-GVP, optimal performance is achieved in 50-100 steps, taking 5-10
429   hours on an A100 GPU. Similarly, for halfcheetah-medium-v2-GMPO-GVP, optimal performance
430   occurs at 240K-480K steps, also requiring 5-10 hours. Despite slower calculations for a 1000-step
431   inference for one gradient step, it uses fewer training batches, resulting in similar overall time costs,
      ensuring no computation is wasted during training.

467
      gradient method more aggressively leverages Q guidance and is less constrained by the behavior
468
      policy, allowing optimization into regions with less data support — a beneficial exploration strategy
469   in medium and medium-replay data, though it results in slightly poorer performance with expert data

471   improvement, while GMPG exhibits fluctuating performance during training with small batch sizes,

523   7     C ONCLUSION
524
525
526   In this paper, we provide a comprehensive study of generative policies and propose two unified
527   and RL-native training schemes, GMPO and GMPG, that are effective and straightforward for both
528   diffusion and flow models. GMPO benefits from a stable training process and does not require
529   pretraining the generative model before optimal policy extraction, making it more efficient and easier
530
      to train. GMPG is a native policy gradient-based method for continuous-time generative models,
      and we provide a numerically stable implementation for the RL community. Our experiment results
531
      demonstrate that the proposed training schemes offer comparable or better performance than previous
532
      works in most cases.
533
534   To ensure consistent comparisons and analyses, we introduce a unified framework for reinforcement
535   learning algorithms that leverages the expressive power of generative models. This standardized
536
      experimental framework decouples the generative model from the RL components, allowing for
      consistent evaluation of different generative models within the same RL context.
537
538   Overall, this work simplifies and unifies the training of generative models for policy modeling,
539   providing practical guidelines for training and deploying generative policies in reinforcement learning.
      Additionally, we discuss existing limitations and valuable topics for future work in Appendix F.

878
      to be mode-covering, preferring to cover all modes of the target distribution, including those with
879
      low probability mass. In contrast, training with the reverse KL divergence tends to be mode-seeking,
880   concentrating on modes with high probability mass while potentially ignoring others.
881

883
      which may mitigate these tendencies, the two training objectives still have different implications and
      may lead to different performance in practice. We provide a detailed term-by-term illustration of
884
      these two methods and their variants in Appendix A.2.1 and A.2.2, respectively.

1028   Continuous normalizing flows (CNFs), introduced by Chen et al. (2018) and Grathwohl et al. (2019),
1029   are the first continuous-time generative models capable of modeling complex target distributions.
1030   However, their simulation-based maximum likelihood training process is unstable, often resulting in
1031   poor performance and distorted generative paths, as illustrated by Finlay et al. (2020).
1032   To address these issues, recent works (Lipman et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Pooladian et al. (2023);
1033   Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden (2023); Tong et al. (2024)) propose CNFs with simulation-free objectives,
1034   similar to diffusion models that use designed and fixed diffusion paths as regression objectives. These
1035   approaches are more stable during training and retain the advantage that the source distribution can
1036   be arbitrary, unlike diffusion models which require a Gaussian source distribution.

1480   E     F RAMEWORK
1481
1482   GenerativeRL is implemented using native PyTorch 2.0 and utilizes numerical solvers like torchdif-
1483   feq (Chen et al., 2018) and Torchdyn (Poli et al., 2021) for ordinary differential equations. Unlike
1484
       frameworks such as Hugging Face Diffusers (von Platen et al., 2022) that generate final outputs,
       in reinforcement learning (RL), generative models act as policies, world models, or other compo-
1485
       nents. This requires a differentiable sampling process with computable and differentiable likelihoods.
1486
       GenerativeRL addresses this by supporting sampling x ∼ p(·|c) and computing log p(x|c) with or
1487   without automatic differentiation, uniformly across all continuous-time generative models.
1488
1489
       E.1    U SAGE E XAMPLE
1490
1491   Figure 4 illustrates a usage example of GenerativeRL. The framework is user-friendly and designed
1492   for ease of use by reinforcement learning researchers. All models, training, and sampling processes
1493   are defined in a single script, simplifying understanding and modifications. Experiment configurations
1494   are recorded automatically, facilitating result reproduction.
1495
       Modular settings allow users to switch between different generative models, neural network compo-
1496   nents, and special configurations easily. Once the model is defined, training and sampling require only
1497   a few lines of code. Users can switch between training objectives and inference strategies seamlessly.
1498   Most functions for generative models support batch processing and automatic differentiation, ensuring
1499   smooth integration into reinforcement learning algorithms.
1500
1501   E.2    F RAMEWORK S TRUCTURE
1502
1503   The framework structure is illustrated in Figure 5. It consists of three main components: reinforcement
1504   learning algorithms, generative models, and neural network components. The reinforcement learning
1505   algorithms include those based on generative models, such as QGPO and SRPO. The generative
1506   models, including diffusion, flow, and bridge models, are used to model data distributions and serve
1507   as key components in RL algorithms. The neural network components comprise commonly used
1508
       layers like DiT and U-Net, which are essential for building generative models. Users can customize
       the neural network components and generative models to fit their specific needs. The framework is
1509
       designed to be compatible with future RL algorithms and can be easily extended for different RL
1510
       tasks.

1529   F    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1530
1531   Our work has several limitations:
1532
1533   • We focused on policy extraction using an optimal Q-value function trained with IQL, without
1534     considering suboptimal Q-value functions or improving Q-value estimation using generative models.
1535
         This remains an open question for actor-critic methods utilizing generative models as policies.
1536   • Generative models excel in high-dimensional data generation but may perform similarly to discrim-
1537     inative models in low-dimensional contexts. Since most RL environments are low-dimensional,
1538     future work should explore environments like Evogym (Bhatia et al., 2021), where generative
1539
         policies can leverage larger action spaces.
1540   • We did not address scenarios where generative policies are deployed online with real-time data
1541     generation. Future studies should assess the stability of policy optimization in dynamic data
1542     contexts, evaluate performance, and consider the additional costs of using generative policies.
1543
1544   G    S OCIETAL I MPACTS
1545
1546   This work uses generative models as policy models in reinforcement learning, applicable in real-world
1547   applications such as robots and autonomous vehicles. Given uncertainties in Q-value function training
1548   and reward labeling, potential negative societal impacts exist, including the risk of aligning generative
1549   models with harmful Q-value functions. Therefore, caution is essential throughout the training and
1550   deployment process, from data collection and reward labeling to Q-value function training and the
1551   application of generative policies. Additionally, we must prevent the abuse of this technology, such
1552
       as using it for illegal activities or unethical purposes.
