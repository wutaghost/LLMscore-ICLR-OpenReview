011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Deception ranges from minor mischief to serious fraud, often leading to signifi-
014            cant psychological and financial harm. Effective deception detection is crucial to
015            mitigate these risks and preserve societal trust. Cognitive load is a useful indicator
016
               for detecting deception, as lying causes individuals to experience greater mental
               strain. While prior research leveraged cognitive load features, typically measured
017
               through physiological signals such as pupil dilation, these methods often require
018
               specialized equipment and can be subject to human bias. These limitations hinder
019            the scalability and automation of deception detection systems. Thus, we propose
020            a novel deception detection framework that automatically extracts cognitive load
021            features from audio-visual data, eliminating the need for specialized hardware or
022            subjective human input. Our approach integrates these features into the deception
023            detection pipeline, enhancing its robustness. Moreover, we introduce a focal loss
024            to address the inherent complexity of deception detection. This objective function
025            enables the model to focus on harder-to-detect instances of deception, thereby im-
026            proving the performance. Our approach achieves state-of-the-art results on bench-
027
               mark audio-visual datasets, demonstrating improvements in automated deception
               detection. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of both our cognitive
028
               load feature extraction and the proposed objective function in advancing the field.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deception, the act of leading someone to believe false information as true, has been a subject of eth-
034
      ical debate since ancient times. For millennia, lying has been viewed as a moral issue–St. Augustine
035
      regarded every lie as a sin, while philosophers such as Aristotle and Kant expressed similarly strong
036   stances against deception (Zuckerman, 1981). In contemporary society, deception poses significant
037   challenges. It affects critical areas such as judicial proceedings, security protocols, and public trust,
038   with far-reaching consequences, including fraud and other societal harm. As deception is pervasive
039   in everyday life (Wu et al., 2018) and often serves as a social tool (Guerrero et al., 2017), there is
040   a growing demand for reliable, automated methods of deception detection across domains such as
041   airport security, criminal investigations, job interviews, and marketing (Pérez-Rosas et al., 2015b).
042   However, the human ability to detect deception is limited. Individuals overestimate their ability to
043   identify deception in others while underestimating their capacity to deceive (Elaad, 2003). Empirical
044   studies suggest that the average person detects deception with approximately 54% accuracy—barely
045   better than chance (Bond Jr & DePaulo, 2006). This limitation leads to substantial research to
046   develop more accurate and efficient deception detection techniques.
047
      Recent deception detection work examined verbal and non-verbal cues to differentiate between truth-
048   tellers and liars. Verbal deception detection strategies include interrogation techniques, the role of
049   communication mode in deception, and structured interview approaches such as the PEACE model
050   (Bull et al., 2019; Hartwig et al., 2011; Strömwall & Anders Granhag, 2003). Non-verbal research
051   tended to focus on behavioral patterns during repeated questioning, the impact of speech disfluency
052   and gestures, and training methods to improve detection accuracy through non-verbal cues (Fiedler
053   & Walka, 1993; Granhag & Strömwall, 2002; King et al., 2020). Most prior research underscored
      the effectiveness of verbal and non-verbal indicators in identifying deception.

054
      A key reason differentiating liars from truth-tellers is the cognitive load associated with deception.
055   Lying imposes higher cognitive demands than truth-telling, which can manifest in both verbal and
056   non-verbal behaviors (DePaulo et al., 2003b). Prior research explored the link between cognitive
057   load and deception detection, suggesting that liars experience greater cognitive strain, which can be
058   leveraged to improve detection accuracy (Bird et al., 2019; Blandón-Gitlin et al., 2014; Van’t Veer
059   et al., 2014; Wielgopolan & Imbir, 2023). To measure cognitive load, previous research exam-
060   ined physiological signals such as pupil dilation, blink rates, body movements, and response times
061   (Abouelenien et al., 2016; Constâncio et al., 2023; Elkins et al., 2012; Raiman et al., 2011; Walczyk
062   et al., 2012). Despite the promise of these approaches, practical limitations remain—reliance on spe-
063
      cialized equipment, inconsistent physiological interpretations, and challenges in real-time analysis
      restrict their scalability and portability (Joseph, 2013; Vanneste et al., 2021; Weber et al., 2021).
064
065   Inspired by the potential of cognitive load for deception detection, we propose a novel approach that
066   incorporates audio-visual cognitive load features into a fully automated deception detection frame-
067   work. Our proposed framework, AVDDCL (Audio-Visual Deception Detection with Cognitive
068   Load), extracts cognitive load features directly from audio-visual data, overcoming the limitations
069
      of traditional methods that rely on specialized equipment or manual annotations. By automating the
      detection process, our method offers a scalable and efficient solution for real-world applications.
070
071   To the best of our knowledge, this is one of the beginning steps in utilizing audio-visual cognitive
072   load features for deception detection. Our contributions are presented as follows:
073
074          • We introduce a novel framework that integrates audio-visual cognitive load features into
075            deception detection, moving beyond conventional physiological and behavioral analyses.
076            This approach offers a more scalable, automated, and comprehensive solution.
077
078
             • We introduce the focal loss to address the specific challenges of deception detection, where
               differentiating between truth and deception is inherently difficult. By focusing on harder-
079
               to-detect cases, our model improves overall detection accuracy.
080
081          • Our proposed approach establishes a new state-of-the-art performance benchmark on the
082            DOLOS dataset (Guo et al., 2023), demonstrating significant improvements in both accu-
083            racy and robustness for fully automated deception detection systems.

086   2     R ELATED W ORKS
087
088   2.1   D ECEPTION D ETECTION A PPROACHES
089
090   Deception detection is extensively investigated through various approaches, ranging from analyzing
091   physiological patterns to verbal and non-verbal cues. Early deception detection methods mainly
092   relied on physiological indicators such as heart rate, blood pressure, and skin conductivity, em-
093   ploying polygraph-based techniques. Despite their widespread use, polygraph tests faced consistent
094   criticism among scientists due to concerns over their validity (Meijer & Verschuere, 2014).
095
      Following the limitations of physiological methods, the research mainstream shifted toward non-
096   verbal cues, emphasizing facial expressions and behavioral indicators. Ekman’s work on deception
097   detection through facial expressions became foundational (Ekman, 2009), while subsequent studies
098   explored the use of micro-expressions (Wu et al., 2018) and body movement analysis (Van der Zee
099   et al., 2019). Another line of research focuses on gaze tracking and eye interaction as deception
100   indicators (Kumar et al., 2021; Mirsadikov & George, 2023). However, micro-expressions could
101   not significantly enhance deception detection accuracy, leading to contradictory conclusions about
102   their effectiveness (Jordan et al., 2019). Several methods often required advanced and non-portable
103   equipment, limiting their practical applications in real-world settings (Dinges et al., 2023).
104   Recently, advancements in deep learning have propelled detection research beyond single-modality
105   approaches, encouraging multi-modality fusion. Several prior research integrated diverse informa-
106   tion sources (channels), including audio, visual, and EEG signals, into deep learning frameworks for
107   deception detection (Şen et al., 2020). Others combined physiological responses, thermal sensing,
      and linguistic features to achieve multimodal deception detection (Abouelenien et al., 2014).

      2.2   D ECEPTION D ETECTION WITH C OGNITIVE L OAD
109
110   Previous research suggested that liars experience more significant cognitive load and nervousness
111   than truth-tellers, as they exert additional mental effort to appear credible (Vrij, 2008). In many
112   situations, telling the truth is cognitively less demanding than lying, especially in face-to-face in-
113   teractions, where fabricating a lie requires more mental resources than simply recounting the truth
114   (Van’t Veer et al., 2014; Vrij, 2008). Lying imposes a cognitive burden, making it more effortful
115   than truth-telling (Van’t Veer et al., 2014), and liars often exhibit detectable signs of increased cog-
116
      nitive load, such as subtle behavioral cues (Blandón-Gitlin et al., 2014). Notably, the effectiveness
      of deception detection methods is often linked to the degree of cognitive load experienced by the
117
      individual, with higher cognitive load improving detection accuracy (Wielgopolan & Imbir, 2023).
118
119   Several scholars utilized physiological indicators of cognitive load to improve deception detection.
120   For example, eye blinking and pupil dilation are closely associated with cognitive load (Stern et al.,
121   1984), making them reliable markers for detecting deception through eye-tracking technologies and
122
      pupil diameter measurements (Labibah et al., 2018). Other scholars reinforced this finding, demon-
      strating that increased pupil dilation during deception was a significant indicator of cognitive load
123
      (Pasquali et al., 2020). Moreover, eye movement, response time, and eloquence were effective de-
124
      ception indicators, as they were strongly tied to cognitive load (Gonzalez-Billandon et al., 2019).
125   Micro-expressions on the face, particularly under cognitive strain, were also examined to enhance
126   deception detection accuracy, with research suggesting that imposing cognitive demands during in-
127   terviews could significantly improve the identification of deceptive behaviors (Monaro et al., 2022).
128
      Although physiological methods have the potential for assessing cognitive load in deception de-
129
      tection, their practical application is limited by the need for specialized and non-portable equip-
130
      ment (Weber et al., 2021). Furthermore, individual variability in physiological responses introduces
131   challenges in data interpretation (Joseph, 2013), and the complexity of real-time assessment com-
132   plicates practical deployment (Vanneste et al., 2021).
133
134
      To overcome these limitations, the automatic extraction of cognitive load features presents a promis-
      ing alternative. For example, the AVCAffe dataset (Sarkar et al., 2023) employs audio-visual data
135
      to analyze cognitive load and affective states. Building on this foundation, we introduce the first
136
      automated method to extract cognitive load indicators from audio-visual data specifically for decep-
137   tion detection, filling a significant gap in the current literature. Considering cognitive load features
138   as intermediate tasks, we propose a novel multimodal framework that improves deception detection
139   performance while overcoming the constraints related to traditional physiological methods. It pro-
140   vides us a foundation for scalable, practical applications in real-world deception detection scenarios.

      3.2     C OGNITIVE L OAD F EATURE P RE - TRAINING
221
222   To extract cognitive load features, we employ the pre-trained AVPEF network, specifically designed
223   to capture the critical role of temporal information in cognitive load assessment (Liu et al., 2023;
224   Li et al., 2025; Puma et al., 2018). The network’s UT-Adapter effectively incorporates temporal dy-
225   namics, while its parameter efficiency ensures it remains computationally feasible, even in resource-
226   constrained environments. For pre-training, we utilize the AVCAffe dataset (Sarkar et al., 2023),
227   which includes 58,112 short video segments averaging 6.74 seconds each, totaling approximately
228   108.72 hours of data from 106 participants. These segments were extracted from longer task-based
229
      recordings using a silence detection algorithm (Robert et al., 2018) to capture affective and cognitive
      load attributes with high temporal granularity. This approach enables a more precise and scalable
230
      analysis of cognitive load features, which is essential for advancing deception detection.
231
232   The dataset provides task-based self-reported labels for arousal, valence, and cognitive load at-
233   tributes. Cognitive scores, based on NASA-TLX (Hart, 2006), are rated on a 0-21 scale across
234   categories such as mental demand, physical demand, temporal demand, performance, effort, and
235
      frustration. Scores above 10 are classified as high, and others as low. In the AVCAffe dataset, frus-
      tration, physical demand, and performance were excluded due to minimal variance, with the focus
236
      placed instead on mental demand, effort, and temporal demand.
237
238
      3.2.1    P RE - TRAINED AVPEF
239
240   We pre-trained the AVPEF network to extract cognitive load features across three key dimensions:
241   mental demand, effort, and temporal demand. During pre-training, we integrated linear layers to
242   predict each of these dimensions from the audio-visual dataset, training them separately to capture
243   specific aspects of cognitive load. To ensure robust model evaluation and prevent information leak-
244
      age, we divided the dataset into 86 participants for training and 20 for validation, carefully stratified
      by age, gender, and ethnicity, with no overlap in recording sessions. The AVPEF module effectively
245
      learns distinct audio-visual patterns associated with each cognitive load dimension. For the final de-
246
      ception detection task, we removed the linear layers, retaining only the pre-trained AVPEF network
247   for feature extraction, ensuring a streamlined and effective analysis pipeline.

      3.3.2    O BJECTIVE F UNCTION
271
272   In deception detection, deception is inherently more challenging to detect than truth, as it involves
273   greater cognitive load, leading to inconsistencies and complex behavioral patterns, which are often
274   masked by strategic actions, making accurate identification difficult (DePaulo et al., 2003a; Ekman,
275   2009; Vrij, 2008; Vrij & Granhag, 2012; Zuckerman, 1981). This contrast underscores the need for
276
      a sophisticated approach capable of capturing the subtle differences between truth and deception.
277   In light of the inherent difficulty in distinguishing deception, we employ focal loss as the objective
278   function to optimize model performance. Focal loss mitigates the impact of easily classified in-
279   stances, allowing the model to prioritize more difficult cases, such as deception, which often involve
280   subtle, complex patterns (Ross & Dollár, 2017). The focal loss is as follows:

284   Here, pi is the model’s predicted probability for the true class. γ is the focusing parameter that
285
      controls the contribution of hard-to-classify deceptive samples. This function enhances the model’s
      ability to focus on deceptive instances that are often masked by strategic behaviors and varying
286
      cognitive loads. By incorporating focal loss, we ensure that our model captures subtle distinctions
287
      between truth and deception, improving classification performance on deceptive data.

      4.1     DATASET
293
      DOLOS (Guo et al., 2023). The DOLOS dataset is a large game show deception detection dataset,
294   featuring rich deceptive conversations. This dataset is collected from a British reality comedy game
295   show. It includes 1,675 video clips featuring 213 participants (141 male and 72 female). For each
296   episode, video clips are extracted based on specific criteria: participants must speak only relevant
297   content (i.e., telling the truth or lies) clearly without significant background noise, and their faces
298   must be visible without occlusion. From 84 episodes, 1,675 clips, ranging from 2 to 19 seconds,
299   were chosen. However, due to some clips becoming unavailable, the final dataset contains 1,656
300   clips. Each clip is annotated with a MUMIN coding scheme, focusing on non-verbal deceptive cues.
301   Real Life Trail (Pérez-Rosas et al., 2015a). The Real Life Trail (RLT) dataset includes testimonies
302   from defendants or witnesses in real court trials, consisting of 121 video clips with 61 deceptive
303   and 60 truthful segments. The average duration of the clips is 28.0 seconds, with deceptive and
304   truthful clips averaging 27.7 and 28.3 seconds, respectively. The speakers consist of 21 female and
305   35 male speakers, with ages ranging from 16 to 60 years. This comprehensive collection offers
306   a robust resource for studying the nuances of deceptive and truthful behavior in high-stakes, real-
307   world scenarios, providing a valuable benchmark for evaluating deception detection methodologies.
308
      Due to availability constraints, 110 out of the 121 clips were used in this study.
309   Box of Lies (Soldner et al., 2019). The Box of Lies (BOL) dataset, derived from ‘The Tonight Show
310   Starring Jimmy Fallon,’ features 25 video clips totaling 2 hours and 24 minutes. Each clip averages
311   6 minutes, with about three game rounds involving different guests and Jimmy Fallon. The dataset
312   contains 1,049 utterances, of which 862 are deceptive and 187 truthful, with annotations focusing
313   on verbal and non-verbal behaviors, including facial expressions and conversational cues, based on
314
      the MUMIN coding scheme. Details on statistical analysis can be found in Appendix A.4

343
      To investigate the impact and combinations of cognitive load features on deception detection, de-
344   noted as Ffinal , seven different feature sets are used: mental demand (M), effort (E), temporal demand
345   (T), mental demand + effort (M + E), mental demand + temporal demand (M + T), effort + tempo-
346   ral demand(E + T), and mental demand + effort + temporal demand (M + E + T). These features
347   are concatenated with the final output of the deception detection AVPEF network for experimental
348   evaluation. The dimensions of each feature combination are standardized to 256, consistent with the
349   dimensionality of the AVPEF network’s feature.
350   For cognitive load feature extraction, we conduct experiments using the AVCAffe dataset, following
351   the data-splitting strategy described in 3.2.1 to ensure no information leakage between training and
352   validation sets ensuring that no information leakage occurred between the training and validation
353   sets. The model was trained with a learning rate of 3e-4, a batch size of 8, and cross-entropy loss as
354   the objective function. Four encoders were used, and training was carried out for 20 epochs with the
355   Adam optimizer. An early stopping mechanism based on the F1-score was applied, halting training
356   if no improvement was observed for 5 consecutive epochs.
357   For the deception detection framework, the average values of ACC, F1-score, and AUC are measured
358   based on the 3-folds defined by the train-test protocol in the DOLOS dataset. The experiments are
359   conducted over 20 epochs using the Adam optimizer, with an initial learning rate set to 1e-4. The
360   batch size is 16, and focal loss (FL) with γ = 2 is used as the objective function. Additionally,
361   the model architecture includes 4 encoders with a dropout rate of 0.5. The learning rate is adjusted
362   using the StepLR scheduler, where the learning rate is halved every 5 epochs. All experiments are
363   performed in a Python 3.8.19 and PyTorch 1.13.1 environment.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
501
502
      Existing studies emphasize that fully automated decision-making systems, while significantly
503   efficient, can raise concerns considering fairness and reliability when human judgment is ex-
504   cluded (Kern et al., 2022). It is important in high-stakes scenarios, such as law enforcement or
505   medical diagnosis, where automation bias can lead to over-reliance on or disregard for system out-
506   puts (Belavadi et al., 2020). In these contexts, false positives can carry substantial risks, underlining
507   the need for balanced and transparent systems. Misuse of automated systems may also erode public
508   trust, enable manipulation, spread misinformation, or perpetuate discrimination against marginalized
509   groups (Biçer & Dibeklioğlu, 2023). Therefore, rigorous ethical considerations are crucial before
510   implementing such systems. Incorporating human-in-the-loop systems is essential to contributing to
511
      fairness and efficiency, leveraging human judgment alongside automated capabilities (Cummings,
      2017; Khan et al., 2021)
512
513   Integrating human cognitive and psychological factors into system design can greatly enhance the
514   trustworthiness of models (Cummings, 2017; Mosier & Skitka, 2018). Building on this principle, we
515   integrate cognitive load-based features to address automation bias and ensure more reliable decision-
516   making. However, unresolved biases in datasets and demographic factors induced during training
517
      pose challenges to generalization across diverse contexts. The lack of real-world datasets, such as the
      Real-Life Trial dataset, exacerbates domain-specific overfitting, limiting the model’s applicability to
518
      both high-stakes and low-stakes deception scenarios (King & Neal, 2024). In addition, demographic
519
      factors like gender, age, and cultural differences significantly influence deception cues, impacting
520   model performance (Abouelenien et al., 2018; Levitan et al., 2016; Naven et al., 2020).
521
522   Future research should focus on creating more diverse datasets and enhancing domain generalization
523
      to improve cross-context performance. Incorporating human-in-the-loop systems will be key to
      ensuring fairness and reliability in automated deception detection. Improving explainability through
524
      interpretable decision-making processes is also essential for building trust in high-stakes contexts.
525
      Bias mitigation strategies, such as fairness-aware training and demographic balancing, are crucial for
526   ensuring equitable outcomes. Moreover, making the parameters α and γ in focal loss adaptive and
527   learnable could further enhance the model’s ability to generalize across diverse, real-time contexts.
528   Furthermore, optimizing models for real-time applications through pruning and quantization can
529   minimize latency and computational demands while balancing efficiency and reliability for robust
530   performance in real-world settings.
531
532   6   C ONCLUSION
533
534   We propose the AVDDCL framework, a novel approach to automated deception detection using
535   cognitive load features extracted from audio-visual data. Our findings show that incorporating mul-
536   tiple cognitive load dimensions significantly improves model performance, supporting the idea that
537   cognitive load and its dimensions are key to detecting deception. The use of focal loss further en-
538   hances the model by targeting difficult instances, boosting accuracy and robustness. This scalable,
539   automated solution eliminates the need for specialized equipment or human annotations, enabling
      more reliable and practical real-world applications.

783   A.1   D ETAILS ABOUT UT-E NCODER
784
785   The UT-Encoder (UTE) refers to the entire architecture that incorporates the W2V2 or ViT encoder
786   alongside UT-Adapters. UT-Adapters are placed in parallel with MHSA and feed-forward layer
787   (FFD) in each encoder of W2V2 and ViT. The encoder components, including MSHA, Multi-Layer
788   Perceptron(MLP), and normalization layers, remain frozen, while only the UT-Adapter parameters
789   are learnable. Each UT-Adapter consists of a series of linear layers and 1D-convolutional layers.
790
      The architecture of the UT-Adapter is as follows:
791
792                            U (X) = L2 (P (C (P (L1 (X; W1 )) ; WC )) ; W2 )                         (6)
793
      Here, L1 and L2 represent the Linear 1 and Linear 2 layers, respectively, and P and C denote
794
      the permutation and 1D-convolutional Layers. The weights W1 ∈ RD×128 and W2 ∈ R128×D
795
      are trainable parameters of the linear layers L1 and L2 , while WC is the trainable weight for the
796   1D-convolutional layer with a kernel size of 3. Specifically, L1 projects the input X ∈ RL×D to
797   X ∈ RL×128 . The Permutation layer P shifts the data from X ∈ RL×128 to X ∈ R128×L . The
798   convolutional layer C is then applied along the temporal dimension to capture temporal dynamics.
799   After the convolution operation, the Permutation layer and L2 project the data back from X ∈
800   R128×L to X ∈ RL×D .
801
      UTE block effectively utilizes the UT-Adapter to capture local temporal information, while the
802   MHSA and MLP modules focus on learning global temporal and spatial attention. This architecture
803   allows the model to balance parameter efficiency and performance.

      A.4     DATA S TATISTICS
865
866   To ensure a fair and rigorous evaluation of AVDDCL’s performance, we employed a 5-fold cross-
867   validation strategy for both the RLT and BOL datasets. This approach ensured robust results by
868   minimizing the effects of data splits and allowed for consistent comparison across different experi-
869   mental settings.
870
      The original BOL dataset required specific preprocessing steps to align with the structure of the RLT
871   dataset and address its inherent class imbalance. First, the BOL dataset, which initially featured
872   utterance-based labeling, was adjusted by grouping utterances into “rounds” to reduce discrepancies
873   with the video-based labeling format of the RLT dataset. This grouping reflected the gameplay
874   structure observed in each video. Second, the significant imbalance between the ’deception’ and
875   ’truth’ classes in the BOL dataset, with an overrepresentation of deceptive samples, was addressed
876   by applying random under-sampling of the ’deception’ class. This balancing ensured that the model
877   could effectively learn both classes without bias.

891
892   A.5.1    V IDEO P RE - PROCESSING
893
      For all datasets, the video preprocessing pipeline involved a series of steps to extract meaningful
894
      facial regions from video frames while maintaining temporal consistency.
895
896   Frames were sampled uniformly from each video at a fixed rate of 20 frames per second (FPS).
897   This was implemented using OpenCV, where the native FPS of each video was determined using the
898
      cv2.VideoCapture function and its CAP PROP FPS property. For videos with an FPS greater
      than 20, the interval between sampled frames was calculated as frame interval = max(1,
899
      int(fps / 20)), ensuring an even distribution of frames. If the FPS was lower than 20, no
900
      frames were skipped to preserve the temporal resolution.
901
902   Once frames were extracted, facial regions were detected using MTCNN. The MTCNN model pro-
903   vided bounding box coordinates and facial keypoints such as the positions of the eyes, nose, and
904
      mouth. For frames containing multiple faces, a tracking mechanism was employed to assign unique
      IDs to each detected face. This mechanism compared bounding box positions and used a Euclidean
905
      distance threshold of 40 pixels between nose keypoints to determine if a detected face matched
906
      an existing ID. Across all frames in a video, the face with the highest frequency of detection was
907   selected as the primary face for further processing.
908
909   The selected facial regions were cropped using the bounding box coordinates provided by MTCNN
910
      and resized to a fixed resolution of 160×160 pixels using OpenCV’s cv2.resize function. Bilin-
      ear interpolation was applied during resizing to preserve facial details.
911
912   Finally, 64 frames were uniformly sampled from each video to ensure consistent representation
913   across datasets.

915   A.5.2    AUDIO P RE - PROCESSING
916
917   The audio preprocessing pipeline was designed to extract and refine speech signals from the videos
      while minimizing background noise.

      The first step involved extracting audio tracks from each video using the MoviePy library. The
919   VideoFileClip class was used to load each video, and its audio component was accessed and
920   exported as a .wav file. This ensured that the audio data retained the original fidelity of the record-
921   ing. Videos without audio tracks were logged and excluded from further processing to maintain
922   pipeline integrity.

924

926   processed using the two-stem configuration, which focuses on isolating speech content from other
927   sound elements.
928
      By combining noise separation and structured organization, the preprocessing pipeline provided
929
      clean and consistent speech signals for subsequent feature extraction and analysis. This ensured that
930
      the audio data was of high quality and aligned with the temporal structure of the video data.
