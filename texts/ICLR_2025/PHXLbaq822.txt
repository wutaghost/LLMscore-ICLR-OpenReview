009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Alignment of Large Language Models (LLMs) intends to make LLMs behave to
012
               satisfy human preferences and values. Widely used methods, e.g., Reinforcement
013            Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), usually involve the additional training
014            of LLMs with a reward model or the dataset reflecting human preferences. How-
015            ever, these training-based methods cannot quickly adapt to different preferences.
016            Recent methods leverage search during the decoding process to align LLMs with
017            preferences. However, these methods ignore the influence of prompts on the de-
018            coding distribution, thus hindering the performance. In this work, we propose
019            HCFR, a Hierarchical Coarse-to-Fine Refinement for efficient LLM alignment.
020            Specifically, HCFR includes a two-stage refinement: i) coarse refinement which
021
               rephrases the prompts from users through self-refinement, and ii) fine refinement
               which leverages the search methods, e.g., Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), for
022
               the responses with the guidance of a pre-trained reward model. Experimental re-
023
               sults on HH-RLHF and UltraChat demonstrate that HCFR can significantly out-
024            perform existing methods, e.g., ARGS, CARDS, and Rejection sampling, in terms
025            of performance and efficiency, i.e., achieving a 71.3% win-tie rate in GPT-4 eval-
026            uations while reducing time consumption by 42%.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional capabilities in various natural lan-
033   guage processing tasks (Wei et al., 2022a; Bubeck et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). Considering
034   their potential for broad societal impact, it is crucial that responses generated by LLMs align with
035   human preferences, such as being harmless, precise, and conforming to social norms and morals
036
      (Weidinger et al., 2021; Ganguli et al., 2022; Deshpande et al., 2023). Preference alignment has
      emerged as a critical area of study (Leike et al., 2018; Dai et al., 2023), aiming to ensure that LLMs
037
      generate texts aligned with human preferences. Techniques such as Reinforcement Learning from
038
      Human Feedback (RLHF) have shown promising performance in improving the alignment of LLM
039   outputs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b). However, aligning LLMs through fine-tuning meth-
040   ods poses significant challenges (Casper et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023b). Gradient-based updates
041   over billions of parameters in large models demand immense computational resources. Moreover,
042   when new alignment requirements arise, it becomes difficult to rapidly adjust the model’s output to
043   align with these new preferences (Kaplan et al., 2020; Minaee et al., 2024).
044
      To address the mentioned challenges in alignment via fine-tuning, alignment via decoding has re-
045   cently emerged as a promising solution (Khanov et al., 2024; Deng & Raffel, 2023). The objective
046   of alignment via decoding is to iteratively refine the decoding distribution of LLMs to generate
047   responses aligned with human preferences without parameter updates. Specifically, it employs an
048   outcome-based reward model trained on preference data to evaluate the value of tokens or seman-
049   tic segments sampled during the decoding process. By selecting optimal tokens or segments, the
050   method gradually refines the decoding distribution (Bai et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2024d; Li et al.,
051   2024; Khanov et al., 2024). Alignment via decoding reformulates the alignment task as a distribu-
052   tion refinement process guided by the reward model. When new preferences need to be aligned,
053
      only the reward model requires modification, which is rapid and resource-efficient (Mudgal et al.,
      2023; Wang et al., 2024a).

      In this paper, we propose Hierarchical Coarse-to-Fine              ments through fine-tuning refinements.
074   Refinement (HCFR), a novel decoding-time alignment
075   method that simultaneously considers both the prompt and the response to alignment via decod-
076   ing. As illustrated in Figure 1, HCFR begins with a high-level stage that rephrases the user input
077   prompt into a structured and precise goal representation, effectively performing a coarse refinement
078   of the decoding distribution. This refined prompt then serves as the basis for subsequent fine-grained
079   adjustment at the response level. Importantly, both the coarse and fine-grained stages estimate the
080
      value of each refined prompt and response segment through the evaluation of the entire generated re-
      sponse. This hierarchical refinement framework significantly improves the efficiency and precision
081
      of the alignment process, achieving a better balance between computational cost and performance.
082
      Our contributions are threefold: (1) Coarse Refinement: HCFR leverages the self-refinement capa-
083   bilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) to rephrase the user input prompt, thereby setting a new
084   goal to guide the subsequent fine refinement process. (2) Fine Refinement: In the fine refinement
085   stage, inspired by tree search techniques (Silver et al., 2017; Feng et al., 2023), HCFR samples and
086   approximates the value of response segments by rolling out complete responses and assessing their
087   reward scores. Through iterative selection of optimal segments, HCFR continuously refines the
088   distribution until a complete response is generated. (3) Experimental Evaluation: Our method out-
089   performs multiple decoding-time alignment methods across various tasks and models. Compared
090   to state-of-the-art methods like CARDS, it achieves a 69.3% win-tie rate in GPT-4 evaluations.
091
      Notably, as the computational budget increases, our method’s performance continues to improve,
      demonstrating strong scalability, while other methods plateau or decline.

      4.1       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
293
294   Models and Decoding Settings. We conduct our experiments using two variants of the Llama3
295   model (Dubey et al., 2024). The first variant, Llama3-SFT 1 , is fine-tuned but has not undergone
296   alignment to human preferences. The second variant, Llama3-DPO 2 , is fine-tuned using the Direct
297   Preference Optimization (DPO) method to better align its outputs with human preferences. In all
298   experiments, we employ the same reward model to guide the decoding process (Wang et al., 2024c).
299
      Our method and baseline parameters are detailed in A.1
300   Evaluation. We conduct our evaluation using two datasets: the HH-RLHF dataset (Bai et al.,
301   2022a), which contains human annotations on the helpfulness and harmfulness of model-generated
302   responses, and the UltraChat dataset (Ding et al., 2023), which covers a broader set of preference,
303   such as coherence, verbosity, and thoughtfulness.
304   We perform alignment evaluations using the average reward score, which reflects how closely the
305   model’s responses align with the attributes in the reward model . We also report efficiency evaluation
306   by reporting the average time (per 100 responses). Additionally, we adopt a GPT-4-based evaluation
307   approach for comparing the quality of responses, following instructions detailed in Appendix A.3.
308
      Baselines. We compare our method against two categories of decoding-time alignment approaches:
309   (1) Decoding without a Reward Model, These methods rely solely on the model’s logits and self-
310   reflection capabilities, including Beam Search (Freitag & Al-Onaizan, 2017), which employs to-
311   ken selection based on model probabilities, and RAIN (Li et al., 2023), which incorporates self-
312   reflection by evaluating and selecting segments using the LLM’s internal assessment. (2) Decoding
313   with a Reward Model: These methods leverage reward models to guide generation. Rejection sam-
314   pling (Xiong et al., 2023; Khaki et al., 2024) samples multiple responses and selects the one with
315   the highest reward score. ARGS (Khanov et al., 2024) and CARDS (Li et al., 2024) score responses
316   at the token (ARGS) or segment (CARDS) level, selecting the highest-reward option. BFS-V (Feng
317
      et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024), retains top-k candidates based on reward scores.

443   5     R ELATED W ORKS
444
445
446   Alignment via Fine-tuning. While Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong
447   performance across diverse language tasks, they can still generate unexpected outputs, including in-
448
      accurate, biased, or harmful responses (Bai et al., 2022a; Zhao et al., 2024). Reinforcement Learning
      from Human Feedback (RLHF) addresses these challenges by aligning model behavior with com-
449
      plex human preferences (Yang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023).
450
      However, recent studies highlight that RLHF incurs substantial computational cost and presents
451   challenges in efficiently adapting to evolving preference shifts (Wang et al., 2023; Casper et al.,
452   2023; Singhal et al., 2023).
453
454
      Alignment via Decoding. Aligning language models during the decoding process enables them to
      dynamically adapt to diverse preferences through the utilization of varying value functions (Mudgal
455
      et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Khanov et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). A preva-
456
      lent methodology involves leveraging a reward model to steer the decoding trajectory by selectively
457   choosing from sampled tokens or segments, thereby iteratively constructing the final response (Kim
458   et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). RAIN (Li et al., 2023) utilizes prompts to guide
459   the LLM in conducting self-evaluation of segments. Furthermore, in-context learning has been
460   identified as a potent alignment mechanism, effectively regulating the LLM’s output by tailoring
461   the prompt design (Yao et al., 2022; Chia et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023a). For example, (Deng
462   et al., 2023) adopts instruction-based methodologies to facilitate self-correction. Although these
463   strategies are effective in enhancing alignment, they often introduce considerable computational
464   overhead (Snell et al., 2024; Sahoo et al., 2024). Our proposed method addresses this challenge by
465
      implementing a hierarchical decoding framework that better balances the trade-off between compu-
      tational efficiency and alignment quality.

469   6     C ONCLUSION
470
471
      In this paper, we propose a Hierarchical Coarse-to-Fine Refinement (HCFR) approach for efficient
472
      alignment through decoding. We analyze the necessity of a coarse-to-fine refinement process from
473
      an information-theoretic perspective. Our method leverages the self-refinement capabilities of large
474   language models (LLMs) by utilizing rephrasing to modify the decoding distribution at a macro
475   level, followed by fine refinement based on an optimally refined prompt. Both stages employ an
476   MCTS-like strategy for value approximation. This approach effectively alleviates the trade-off be-
477   tween performance and computational cost while maintaining strong scalability. We hope HCFR
478   can bring the new perspective to unify prompt engineering, e.g., CoT (Wei et al., 2022b), and the
479   responses for efficient alignment to be deployed to align the LLM agents (Wang et al., 2024b) which
480   usually involve multiple processing steps of texts with single or multiple LLMs.
481   Limitations. Currently, our proposed method, HCFR, cannot be applied at a large scale due to the
482   additional computational overhead introduced by expanding refined prompts and response segments.
483   Moreover, the fine refinement stage does not further adjust the refined prompts generated in the
484   earlier coarse stage. Future research will focus on expanding the scope of decoding refinement
485   while maintaining control over the computational budget, along with developing more precise value
      approximations to enhance the overall effectiveness of the method.
