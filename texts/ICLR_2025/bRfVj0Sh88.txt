009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The success of deep learning in various complex tasks relies heavily on large
012            amounts of annotated data, which can be prohibitively expensive to acquire. Tech-
013            niques such as reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) and direct
014            preference optimization (DPO) have emerged as methods for fine-tuning models
015            by leveraging human preferences, but they come with significant costs, especially
016            when applied to large-scale language models (LLMs). Recent efforts to reduce
017            these costs have focused on active preference optimization, which uses certainty-
018
               based selection to minimize the annotation burden. However, the two-step process
               of selecting uncertain input prompts and then acquiring completions can lead to sub-
019
               optimal pairings, potentially limiting model learning capacity. This paper suggests
020
               that divAPO eliminates suboptimal pairings that are typical of two-step methods
021            and enhances learning capacity by selecting the most informative preference pairs
022            in a single phase, taking into account both data distribution probabilities and pref-
023            erence model certainty. Through experiments on complicated Language tasks, we
024            demonstrate that our method achieves significant performance improvements over
025            existing approaches.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The success of deep learning in a variety of intricate tasks is significantly influenced by the availability
031   of extensive, well-annotated data, which can be prohibitively expensive to acquire in practice.
032   Reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) and direct preference
033
      optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) are two techniques that have emerged in recent years as
      approaches to fine-tuning models by leveraging human preferences. Nevertheless, these methods
034
      result in substantial expenses, particularly when fine-tuning large models such as GPT (Brown, 2020)
035
      or diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), which necessitate a significant number of responses from human
036   participants with domain expertise, as well as large-scale language models (LLMs). In addition, the
037   generation of numerous responses for a single input prompt and their subsequent ranking are typical
038   characteristics of preference labels, necessitating a substantial quantity of responses (Ouyang et al.,
039   2022; Jiang et al., 2024; Ziegler et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020). Nevertheless, it is imperative to identify
040   methods to optimize the data acquisition process, as the cost of preference labeling from all of these
041   responses can be substantial.
042   In this regard, to reduce these costs, recent research has explored the integration of active learning
043   (AL) strategies, such as active preference learning (APL) (Muldrew et al., 2024). APL suggests a
044   certainty-based selection strategy for correcting the overconfident prediction of DPO. According
045   to Figure 1 (a), this method involves a two-step selection process. First, predictive uncertainty
046   calculates the average entropy score of the output text token probability to identify informative input
047   prompts. Then, preference model certainty—which is defined using the implicit rewards gap of the
048   preference model, like DPO—acquires corresponding completions. These strategies aim to minimize
049   the annotation burden by intelligently selecting the most informative input prompt and completion
050
      pairs for labeling.
051   Although this strategy effectively selects uncertain input prompts, it fails to consider the potential
052   informativeness of the input prompt and completion pairings from a fine-tuning perspective, which
053   could restrict the model’s overall learning capacity. Furthermore, no assurance selecting an input
      prompt based on its uncertainty will produce a high-quality or well-informative completion pair,

082   In this paper, we present divAPO, a novel active learning strategy that is intended to enhance
083   preference learning by simultaneously evaluating the informativeness of the pairs and the diversity of
084
      input prompt stimuli. Our approach considers both the data distribution probabilities and preference
      model certainty regarding the completions pairs, allowing for a more comprehensive selection
085
      of informative samples. Our method employs an input prompt-completions one-step selection
086
      strategy, eliminating the sub-optimal candidate selection issue that arises when either input prompt
087   or completions is chosen first. We were able to select data more diversely by approximating the
088   distribution of input prompts and completions and incorporating the model’s assurance to modify this
089   distribution. By employing this approach, we were able to identify an optimal subset that effectively
090   balances diversity and informativeness, thereby improving the overall learning process. Furthermore,
091   we underscore the significance of input prompt diversity, as the repetitive sampling of similar data
092   points resulting from multiple overlapping input prompts and completion configurations within the
093   dataset can result from the selection of uncertain data alone.
094   We validate our approach through extensive experiments on various tasks, including IMDB movie
095   review generation, TL;DR text summarization, and Anthropic HH (helpfulness-harmlessness) single-
096   turn dialogue. Using large-scale, open-source LLMs with approximately 1–3 billion parameters,
097   our results demonstrate a significant performance improvement, with an increase up to 9.21%
098   over baseline methods. These findings underscore the effectiveness of our proposed method in
099   leveraging preference feedback for AL, contributing to both the theoretical understanding and
100   practical application of AL in the context of modern machine learning tasks. The source code is
101
      available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/divAPO-8A96.

105   2.1     P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
106
107   The rapid advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have highlighted the importance of
      aligning models with intricate human preferences (Jiang et al., 2024). Preference optimization (PO)


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      has emerged as a promising technique in fine-tuning these models, leading to a compelling model such
109   as ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022). One of the pioneer approaches in this domain is Reinforcement
110   Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) that involves a two-stage process:
111   1) learning an auxiliary reward model on human preference datasets, and 2) fine-tuning LLMs to
112   align the reward with RL objectives, such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman, 2017),
113   P3O (Wu et al., 2023), and REINFORCE (Ahmadian et al., 2024).
114
      As many studies have reported that RLHF often suffers from high variance and sensitivity to hy-
115   perparameters, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) has emerged as an
116   alternative by not requiring explicit reward modeling or RL training. DPO simplifies the training
117   process by directly maximizing the output likelihood of preferred data examples while reducing
118   that of non-preferred examples. Several variants of supervised loss of preference optimization have
119   been studied (Azar et al., 2024). SLiC-HF (Zhao et al., 2023) introduces a hinge-loss objective to
120   further separate preferred and non-preferred samples. IPO (Azar et al., 2024) replaces the traditional
121   sigmoid-based objective with a mean squared error (MSE) loss to enhance training stability. Despite
122   their promising results, the sample selection problem for constructing better preference datasets
123
      remains underexplored, yet is crucial for further refining alignment processes.
124
125   2.2   ACTIVE L EANING FOR P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
126
127   Active Learning in NLP. AL is a well-established problem with a primary goal to maximize model
128   performance with minimal labeling costs by identifying the most informative samples and labeling
129   them with human oracle (Zhang et al., 2022; Cohn et al., 1994; Houlsby et al., 2011; Settles, 2009).
130   In NLP, there are two main traditional AL strategies commonly used (Zhang et al., 2022). One
131   strategy is based on the informativeness of data instances, utilizing factors such as the uncertainty
132
      and disagreement of the models (Engelson & Dagan, 1996; Huang et al., 2024; Siddhant & Lipton,
      2018). The other strategy is based on the representativeness of data instances (McCallum et al., 1998;
133
      Settles & Craven, 2008; Zhao et al., 2020), which focuses on selecting representative samples that
134
      can effectively capture the diversity of the underlying data distribution (Bloodgood & Callison-Burch,
135   2014; Eck et al., 2005; Zeng et al., 2019). These two directions are particularly necessary for
136   complex tasks including text generation or summarization, where the selected samples need to be
137   both informative (Zhao et al., 2020; Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Gidiotis & Tsoumakas, 2023) and
138   representative (Sener & Savarese, 2017; Tsvigun et al., 2023; Perlitz et al., 2023). However, most
139   AL methods have traditionally focused on supervised learning scenarios, where only a single label is
140   required for annotating each data example, making them hard to directly apply to the PO framework,
141   which usually requires a pair of answers (or labels) to incorporate the human preference.
142   Active Preference Optimization. Recently, a few approaches have attempted to study active learning
143   for preference optimization. APL (Muldrew et al., 2024) particularly involves a two-step sample
144   selection process based on the sample uncertainty and DPO reward; it first roughly selects samples
145   with a high LLM output entropy, and then refines the choices by further selecting samples with a
146   high DPO reward gap between a pair of answers. While APL shows enhanced sample efficiency with
147   the two-step uncertainty-perspective selection, it lacks studies of the effect of diversity and does not
148   validate generalizability over various PO approaches, such as SLiC-HF.

      4.3   G REEDY S ELECTION A LGORITHM
271
272   Given that our objective function, which incorporates DEP and the preference model certainty, is
273   NP-hard, solving it directly is computationally intractable. As a result, we employ a greedy selection
274   algorithm to approximate the optimal solution. The objective function satisfies the properties of
275   monotonicity and submodularity, which ensures that the return of the function increases monotonically
276   as new examples are added to the subset. Moreover, the marginal benefit of adding a new example
277   diminishes as the subset grows. These properties justify the use of a greedy algorithm, as the solution
278
      is guaranteed to be within a constant factor of the optimal solution, as demonstrated in A.
279   In Theorem 4.2, we guarantee the selected subset S obtained by our greedy solution achieves a
280   (1 − 1/e)-approximation of the optimum.
281   Theorem 4.2. Since Eq. (4), denoted as OBJ, is a monotone, submodular, and non-negative function
282   on x, the greedy solution provides a set with a (1 − 1/e)-approximation of the optimum. Formally,

      Time Complexity Analysis. The time complexity of the divAPO strategy can be understood by
317
      breaking down its key components. The primary computational steps include embedding extraction,
318   reward calculation, and diverse point selection using a clustering-based approach. First, embeddings
319   are calculated for all samples, which requires O(m · d), where m is the number of unlabeled data
320   points and d is the dimensionality of embeddings. Next, rewards for each sample are computed
321   with complexity O(m). To calculate distance estimated probability, we use the p-norm distance
322   between embeddings, with complexity O(m · k · d), where k is the number of querys. Overall, the
323   dominant factor is the clustering process, yielding an overall time complexity of O(m · k · d) per
      query iteration.

355    consists of 25K movie reviews, and the task focuses on continuously generating positive reviews
356    from a short initial review. TL;DR contains 117K Reddit posts, and the task is to generate summaries
357
       of those posts. HH-RLHF consists of 170K dialogues between humans and automated assistants, and
       the task aims to increase the assistant answer’s helpfulness while decreasing its harmlessness.
358
359    Algorithms. We compare divAPO with a random sample selection, Uniform, and three conventional
360    AL approaches for supervised learning, Entropy (Kadavath et al., 2022), Certainty (Muldrew et al.,
361    2024), and Coreset (Sener & Savarese, 2017), and one AL approach for preference optimization,
362    APL (Muldrew et al., 2024). Entropy selects samples with a high average entropy score of the output
363
       text token probability. Certainty selects the pair completions where the model’s implicit reward
       value is high. Coreset selects a subset of examples that maximize the distance coverage to the entire
364
       training set in the embedding space of SentenceBert (Reimers, 2019). APL performs sample selection
365
       by a two-step uncertainty-based filtering, that first roughly selects samples with a high LLM output
366    entropy, and refines the choices by further selecting samples with a high DPO reward gap.
367
368    Implementation Details. We validate our experiment using two promising preference optimization
369
       approaches, DPO and SLiC-HF. For the initial backbone LLM, we employ GPT-2 (Radford et al.,
       2019) for IMDB dataset, Pythia-1B (Biderman et al., 2023) for TL;DR dataset, and Pythia-2.8B for
370
       HH-RLHF dataset. All the backbone LLMs are trained by SFT first, and then used for preference
371
       optimization. For the experiments, we followed the hyperparameter setup used in (Muldrew et al.,
372    2024), utilizing the ADAM optimizer with a learning rate 1e-06. We trained 30 epochs for IMDB
373    data and 50 epochs for TL;DR and HH-rlhf data. A batch size of 32 was employed, and each
374    query round included a query size of 128 samples with a total of 6 rounds. Additionally, we set the
375    hyperparameter β to 0.2. To calculate embeddings for divAPO, we use the same sentence embedding
376    model, all-MiniLM-L6-v2 SentenceBERT, with CoreSet. We repeated every experiment 5 times and
377    reported the average value. All experiments were conducted using a 48GB A6000 GPU. Further
       implementation details can be found in Appendix B.

477
478
      In this work, we introduced divAPO, a novel active learning strategy that addresses the limitations of
479
      existing approaches in preference optimization. By simultaneously considering the distance estimated
480   probability and preference model certainty, our method overcomes the shortcomings of traditional two-
481   step selection processes, which often lead to suboptimal data pairings. Our one-step selection strategy
482   ensures that the model learns from a more diverse and informative subset of data, thereby enhancing
483   its overall learning capacity. Extensive experiments on multiple datasets, including IMDB, TL;DR,
484   and HH-rlhf, demonstrated the effectiveness of our approach, with performance improvements of
485   up to 9.21% compared to baseline methods. These findings underscore the importance of balancing
      diversity and preference model certainty in active learning for preference-based optimization.

790    Using GPT as Oracle.
791
       In traditional active learning settings, an oracle is often a human labeler who annotates unlabeled
792    data. For many tasks, this involves providing a clear, singular label, which the oracle then uses to
793    guide the model. However, in tasks requiring preference judgments, where labels are given based on
794    relative evaluation, an immediate and adaptable oracle is needed to handle the latest model-generated
795    responses. Given the impracticality of conducting objective experiments with multiple human judges
796    for every evaluation, we leverage the language model APIs provided by OpenAI for fine-tuning
797    and evaluation processes. Research from the DPO paper indicates that OpenAI’s GPT-4, when
798    appropriately prompted, aligns closely with human judgments, demonstrating strong agreement.
799    Cost-efficient GPT model. One notable drawback of using GPT-4 as the oracle model is its high cost
800    and latency. In response, several alternative models such as GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4-turbo, GPT-4o,
801    and GPT-4o-mini have been proposed since the publication of the DPO paper. To determine the most
802    suitable model, we conducted a consistency test using 64 prompts and their associated completions to
803    generate preference labels twice. As depicted in Figure 5, GPT-4o, and GPT-4o-mini exhibit over 90%
804    consistency across 64 data points, with GPT-4 and GPT-4o-mini also demonstrating high response
805    similarity exceeding 90%. Consequently, for a more cost-effective active learning framework, we
806
       opted to use GPT-4o-mini as the oracle model.
807    Prompt Design. Utilizing GPT models as oracles necessitates carefully designed prompts tailored
808    to each specific task. Different prompts are employed depending on the nature of the task, as
809    detailed in the appendix. Prompts are evaluated based on their effectiveness in reflecting human
       preferences, grammatical correctness, functional performance, and consistency of responses, with

878   Limitation. While the proposed method offers a novel approach to Active Learning with Preference
879   Optimization, there are several limitations to consider. First, the method’s effectiveness is highly
880   dependent on the quality and diversity of the preference feedback. In cases where the feedback
881   is biased or incomplete, the model’s learning capacity may be restricted, potentially resulting in
882
      suboptimal performance. Second, the 1-step selection strategy, while efficient, may struggle to scale
      effectively in extremely large datasets due to computational constraints, particularly when calculating
883
      distance-estimated probabilities (DEP) and reward gaps across massive data pairs. Additionally,
884
      while our approach maximizes learning capacity for a specific task, it may not generalize well to
885   tasks with highly distinct structures or feedback modalities, limiting its broader applicability.
886
887   Potential Negative Societal Impact. The widespread application of Active Learning with Preference
888
      Optimization could have unintended societal consequences. By prioritizing human preferences,
      models trained using this method might inadvertently reinforce biases present in the feedback data,
889
      especially in cases where feedback reflects socially biased or exclusionary viewpoints. If such models
890
      are deployed in sensitive applications, such as recommendation systems or content moderation,
891   they could perpetuate or exacerbate inequalities, including discrimination based on race, gender,
892   or socioeconomic status. Furthermore, reliance on human feedback at scale may introduce ethical
893   concerns regarding labor exploitation, particularly in crowdsourced settings where annotators are
894   compensated at low wages. These societal impacts highlight the need for careful consideration of
895   feedback sources and post-hoc auditing of models trained using AL-PO methods to mitigate harmful
896   biases and ensure ethical deployment.
