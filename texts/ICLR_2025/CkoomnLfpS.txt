009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               The rapid advancements in using neural networks as implicit data representations
012
               have attracted significant interest in developing machine learning methods that ana-
013
               lyze and process the weight spaces of other neural networks. However, efficiently
014            handling these high-dimensional weight spaces remains challenging. Existing
015            methods often overlook the sequential nature of layer-by-layer processing in neural
016            network inference. In this work, we propose a novel approach using dynamic
017            graphs to represent neural network parameters, capturing the temporal dynamics of
018            inference. Our Dynamic Neural Graph Encoder (DNG-Encoder) processes these
019            graphs, preserving the sequential nature of neural processing. Additionally, we also
020            leverage DNG-Encoder to develop INR2JLS for facilitate downstream applications,
021            such as classifying INRs. Our approach demonstrates significant improvements
022
               across multiple tasks, surpassing the state-of-the-art INR classification accuracy
               by approximately 10% on the CIFAR-100-INR. The source code has been made
023
               available in the supplementary materials.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Deep neural networks have demonstrated superb capability in addressing real-world problems in
030
      fields such as computer vision, natural language processing, and the natural sciences. While it is
031   generally used for learning patterns from data, recent studies have expanded its scope by treating
032   neural networks themselves as inputs, enabling tasks such as opt imizing networks Metz et al.
033   (2022), predicting the labels of the data encoded in implicit neural representations Dupont et al.
034   (2022), and generating or modifying their weights to alter functionality Schürholt et al. (2022).
035   However, processing these weight spaces presents considerable challenges due to their complex,
036   high-dimensional nature.
037   To address the difficulty, some existing methods propose to narrow the effective weight space using
038   a restricted training process (Bauer et al. (2023); Dupont et al. (2021); De Luigi et al. (2023)).
039   However, this neglects the crucial permutation symmetry property of the neural network weights
040   , i.e., neurons within a layer can be rearranged without altering the network’s function (Hecht-
041   Nielsen (1989)). Overlooking the permutation symmetry can significantly increase the search space
042   for optimal parameters of processing network, resulting in reduced generalization and unsatisfied
043   performance. By observing this, recent works (Navon et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024b;a)) build
044   permutation equivariant weight-space models named neural functionals. Unfortunately, these methods
045
      need manual adaptation for each new architecture, and a single model can only handle one fixed
      architecture. To encourage process heterogeneous architectures, Kofinas et al. (2024); Lim et al.
046
      (2024) introduce to model neural network weights as graph, which links neural network parameters
047
      similarly to a computation graph. This methods, while innovative, predominantly employ static
048   graphs. This static representation allows GNNs to process the entire graph in a single pass. However,
049   such an approach overlooks a critical aspect of neural network behavior during inference: the
050   sequential nature of layer-by-layer processing. Neural networks, by design, perform inferences in a
051   temporally ordered manner, where each layer’s output serves as the input for the subsequent layer.
052   This sequential dependency suggests that a more natural and effective modeling of neural network
053   parameters could be achieved through dynamic graphs. Unlike static graphs, dynamic graphs evolve
      over time, capturing the temporal dynamics inherent in the forward pass process.

      Motivated by these observations, we propose a novel method that represents neural network parame-
055   ters as dynamic graphs, namely dynamic neural graph. Leveraging this dynamic graph, we introduce
056   the Dynamic Neural Graph Encoder (DNG-Encoder), a recurrent-like graph neural network designed
057   to process dynamic neural graphs. This approach mirrors the forward propagation mechanism of
058   neural networks, preserving the temporal characteristics of the data flow through the layers. To
059   facilitate downstream applications, we use the DNG-Encoder to develop INR2JLS, a method that
060   learns a joint latent space between deep weights and the original data. This approach provides a more
061   informative latent space compared to previous methods that focused solely on INR weights.
062   Our contribution can be summarized as follows. First, we introduce the concept of dynamic neural
063   graphs for modeling neural network parameters, capturing the temporal dynamics of the forward
064   pass. Second, we develop a novel RNN-based graph neural network to process these dynamic graphs,
065   effectively imitating the sequential nature of neural network inference. Third, we propose INR2JLS,
066   a technique that maps INR weights into a joint latent space that can benefit developing downstream
067   application. Finally, we show through extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of our
068   method across three tasks. Notably, the performance of our method improves over the state-of-the-art
069
      by 9% and 10% on CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 for classifying INR weights.

075   A recent study (Kofinas et al. (2024)) introduce a novel representation of neural networks, named
076   neural graphs, which ensures invariance to neuron symmetries. For example, in an L-layer multilayer
077   perceptron (MLP) M, the weight matrices are denoted as {W1 , W2 , ..., WL }, and the biases are
078   denoted as {b1 , b2 , ..., bL }. Each weight matrix Wl and bias bl respectively have dimensions
079   dl × dl−1 and dl . M can be converted to a neural graph G = (V, E), where V = {v0 , v1 , ..., vL }
080
      denotes the set of nodes features, and E = {e1 , e2 , ..., eL } represents the set of edges features.
               l                 l   l−1
081   vl ∈ Rd ×dv and el ∈ Rd ×d ×de are the nodes and edges of the l-th layer in G, respectively, where
082   dv and de represent the dimensions of the node feature and the edge feature, respectively. In addition,
083
      vl and el corresponds to neurons at the l-th layer of M and connections between neurons at the
      l-th layer and the (l − 1)-th layer of M, respectively. Typically, edge feature matrices contain the
084
      weights of M, while nodes are constructed using biases. Additionally, the feature matrices may not
085
      necessarily be the original weights and biases. Some studies (Kofinas et al. (2024) Zhou et al. (2024b)
086   Zhou et al. (2024a)) have demonstrated improved performance by using frequency representations
087   of bl and Wl , such as Random Fourier Features (RFFs) (Rahimi & Recht (2007)). It is worth
088   mentioning that once the neural graph is defined, its structure remains unchanged throughout the
089   analysis or application. According to the definitions of various graphs (West et al. (2001)), this type
090   of neural graph can also be referred to as a static neural graph.

183   To address the above limitations of static neural graphs, we propose converting the input neural
184   network into dynamic graphs. This conversion incorporates the inherent temporal processing charac-
185   teristics of neural networks directly into the graph structure, enabling subsequent models to effectively

231   dimensions differ from those of MLP weights. To address this problem, Kofinas et al. (2024) propose

234

237   between nodes of adjacent layers, vl−1 and vl , by a number of cl−1 × cl × hl × wl edges, with each
238   edge corresponding to a scalar in Wl and each pair of nodes is connected by hl × wl edges. To
239
      maintain consistency in the number of edges between each pair of nodes within the dynamic neural
      graph, we perform padding by adding additional zero edges between a pair of nodes to reach the
240
      maximum of hl × wl .

364   eralization of trained networks. Current data augmentation techniques for deep space processing
365   either encode augmented images into implicit neural representations (INRs) (Kofinas et al. (2024);
366   Zhou et al. (2024a)) or directly modify the INR weights (Shamsian et al. (2024)). With the pro-
367   posed INR2JLS, we introduce a distinct augmentation method that can be easily implemented in
368   our framework. Specifically, we generate different views of the original images using the decoder.
369   By encouraging the INR2JLS to generate diverse views of the image Iimg , the model can learn
370
      representations Faug that are more robust and invariant to such transformations. A more detailed
      discussion can be found in Appendix H.4.

394   6.1    C LASSIFYING INR S WITH INR2JLS
395
396   Experiment Setup. There are mainly two steps in order to use our proposed framework for INR classi-
397   fication. First, we utilize the INR2JLS framework introduced in Section 5, which uses DNG-Encoder
398   proposed in Section 4.1 along with a proposed augmentation strategy to generate a permutation-
399   invariant implicit feature map, Faug , that captures diverse semantic information from images via
400   reconstruction. Our augmentation involves five transformations, including clockwise rotations of 90,
401
      180 and 270 degrees, as well as horizontal and vertical flips. This increases the channels of Faug to
402   six times of a single latent feature F, represented as Faug ∈ Rhs ×ws ×6d .
403   Second, we keep the DNG-Encoder and the Latent Generator fixed and add additional classification
404   CNN that takes Faug as inputs. During training, given a set of INR weights and their corresponding
405   label, the DNG-Encoder processes the INR weights to produce Faug . Then Faug is used as input to
406   the classification CNN, and we optimize the CNN using cross-entropy loss to learn a mapping from
407   latent space to label space. It is worth emphasizing that during the training for classifying INRs, we
408   do not directly optimize our encoder to encourage it extracting label-relevant features from input
409
      INRs. Instead, we employ a pre-trained encoder, which has learned in a self-supervised manner. In
      this case, the performance of classification can indicate the quality of the learned latent space.
410
411   We conduct the classification task on the public datasets introduced by Zhou et al. (2024a), i.e.,
412   MNIST INRs dataset, FashionMNIST INRs dataset, and CIFAR-10 INRs dataset. Besides, to
413   further compare our method with the state-of-the-arts, we perform the INR classification on the more
414   challenging CIFAR-100 INRs dataset. Each image in all datasets contains INRs with 10 views.
415   Comparison Results. Table 1 presents a comparison between our method and several state-of-the-art
416   approaches on the test sets of the four aforementioned datasets. Our method consistently exhibits
417   superior performance compared to all other approaches. Notably, as the difficulty of the dataset

528   8     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we have introduced a novel method to model neural network weights as dynamic graphs.
531   To process dynamic neural graphs, we propose Dynamic Neural Graph Encoder (DNG-Encoder)
532   to handle the temporal dynamics intrinsic to neural network inference, maintaining the sequential
533   flow of data through the layers. We further enhance our model’s utility with INR2JLS, which maps
534   INR weights into a joint latent space, providing a more informative and robust representation for
535   downstream tasks. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.
536   Limitation and Future Work. Despite the significant improvements on classifying INRs using our
537   method, its performance still lags behind that of CNNs on analogous image-space tasks. Developing
538   a more powerful variant of temporal GNNs could potentially lead to further improvements. Moreover,
539   since our experiments were primarily conducted on 2D images, extending the proposed approach to
      handle neural radiance fields would significantly broaden its potential applications.

      Implicit Neural Representations. Recent works employ a neural network as a continuous function
      to implicitly represent the objects or shapes (Park et al. (2019)Genova et al. (2019a)Genova et al.
671
      (2019b)Michalkiewicz et al. (2019)Gropp et al. (2020)). This function takes an input (often a point
672
      coordinate) and outputs the corresponding feature value or property. SIREN (Sitzmann et al. (2020))
673   is a continuous implicit neural representation that utilizes sine as a periodic activation function. It
674   excels at fitting complex signals, including natural images and 3D shapes. In our experiments, the
675   INRs in the datasets we used with the form of SIRENs.
676
      Dynamic Graphs. Dynamic graphs can be categorized into two main types: continuous-time dynamic
677
      graphs (CTDGs) and discrete-time dynamic graphs (DTDGs). A DTDG represents snapshots of a
678
      dynamic graph captured at regular time intervals, where each snapshot represents the graph structure
679   at a specific timestamp and can be treated as a static graph. A CTDG can be represented by an
680   initial state of a dynamic graph (essentially a static graph) and a sequence of events occurring
681   at different timestamps. To address the complex structure and temporal information in dynamic
682   graphs, substantial research has focused on the dynamic graph neural network. A common approach
683   involves employing an encoder-decoder structure (Kazemi et al. (2020)). Here, the encoder is
684   responsible for learning node embeddings, while the decoder utilizes these embeddings to perform
685   downstream tasks. There has been a lot of work to handle DTDG and CTDG using neural networks,
686   and one line of notable approach is leveraging recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to capture temporal
687
      dependencies and dynamics within the graph structure, such as methods for processing DTDG
      (Seo et al. (2018)Narayan & Roe (2018)Chen & Wang (2018)Chen et al. (2022), and methods for
688
      processing CTDG Rossi et al.Kumar et al. (2018)Kumar et al. (2019)Trivedi et al. (2017)Trivedi et al.
689
      (2019)).
690
691   Learning in Deep Weight Spaces. The intricate and high-dimensional nature of weight spaces
692   in implicit neural representations (INRs) presents substantial challenges for extracting meaningful
693
      information about the encoded data. To tackle these challenges, some pioneers have focused on
      narrowing the effective weight space through constrained training processes (Bauer et al. (2023);
694
      Dupont et al. (2021); De Luigi et al. (2023)). However, Navon et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024a) argue
695
      that these methods overlook the permutation symmetry property of neural network weights. Ignoring
696   this symmetry can expand the search space for optimal parameters, leading to decreased generalization
697   and performance. To address this issue, they introduced permutation equivariant weight-space models.

       G.1       A DDITIONAL C OMPONENTS IN CNN S AS DYNAMIC N EURAL G RAPHS
1091
1092   In Section 3.2, we outlined how to convert the fundamental modules of CNNs - the convolutional
1093   layers and the linear layers - into modules within the dynamic neural graph. In modern CNNs, besides
1094   convolutional and linear layers, there are often additional components like the flattening layer and
1095   residual connections (He et al. (2016)). To ensure our dynamic neural graph framework is applicable
1096   to a wide range of CNN architectures, we define how to convert flatten layers and residual connections
1097
       to the modules in dynamic neural graphs.
1098   Flattening layer. The flattening layer in CNNs is used to convert multi-dimensional feature maps
1099   into a single feature vector that can be accepted by fully connected layers for subsequent operations.
1100   The dimensions of the feature map output by a convolutional layer at the l-th layer of a CNN are
1101   hlf t × wfl t × cl , where hlf t and wfl t represent the spatial dimensions of the feature map, cl denotes
1102   the number of channels of the feature map. Assuming we flatten the feature map to a single feature
1103   vector of dimension dl , where dl = hlf t × wfl t × cl . A linear layer is then utilized at the (l + 1)-th
1104   layer of the CNN to perform a linear transformation on this flattened feature vector, yielding a vector
1105
       of dimension dl+1 .
1106   In the dynamic neural graph, cl corresponds to the number of nodes in vl , and dl+1 corresponds to
1107   the number of nodes in vl+1 . We can conceptualize the function of the flattening layer as generating
1108   hlf t × wfl t virtual vertices within each node in vl , and they are subsequently connected to nodes
1109   vl+1 . Virtual vertices do not contain any feature information, they are only used to indicate the
1110   connection relationship between their carriers vl and nodes vl+1 . Each connection between a virtual
1111   vertex and a node in vl+1 corresponds to a single weight scalar in the weight matrix W l+1 of the
1112   linear layer at the (l + 1)-th layer. In essence, this implies that each node in vl is linked to a node

1321   H.4    DATA AUGMENTATION FOR INR2JLS
1322
1323   Data augmentation for images often introduces various image transformations during training. This
1324
       allows the model to learn features and patterns under diverse conditions, enhancing its adaptability
       to input images and improving overall model generalization. In our proposed data augmentation
1325
       method for the INR2JLS framework, we aim to enrich the feature map outputted by the DNG-encoder
1326
       with a broader range of features and patterns present in the original image. This enhancement
1327   subsequently boosts the generalization ability of the classification model based on the feature map.
1328   Specifically, we rotate and flip the image corresponding to each INR, generating multiple images for
1329   each INR. Our model then learns a feature map Faug with increased channels from the given INR
1330   to reconstruct multiple images. For a given input INR, we generate more latent vectors by defining
1331   more distinct spatial vectors to fuse with the same node memory s(tL ), and then permute these new
1332   latent vectors to generate a feature map Faug with more channels. The rotation and flipping of each
1333   image in the dataset essentially alter the spatial arrangement of the pixels of the image, reflecting
1334   a same spatial arrangement of the input coordinates of each INR. After obtaining s(tL ) containing
1335
       semantic information of the INR, we use different spatial vectors to simulate various permutations of
       input coordinates, and fuse them with s(tL ) in the latent space to decode the image. This process
1336
       simulates the INR use different permutations of input coordinates to output images with different
1337
       pixel arrangements. While the feature map Faug can reconstruct distinct images to some extent,
1338   they inherently encapsulate a variety of features and patterns from these images. Consequently,
1339   the classifier can achieve better generalization when performing classification tasks based on Faug .
1340   Figure 7 shows how to employ data augmentation in the INR2JLS framework.
1341
       We also proposed a data augmentation method of adding noise to the image to compare with the
1342
       above proposed method of rotating and flipping the image. For the method of rotating and flipping
1343
       the image, we apply five transformations on the image, including clockwise rotations of 90, 180 and
1344   270 degrees, as well as horizontal and vertical flips, which consistent with the settings in Section 6.1.
1345   For the method of adding noise, we add Gaussian noise with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of
1346   0.06 the original image. Table 9 shows the performance improvement of the two methods for the
1347   classification task. It can be found that adding noise has almost no significant improvement in the
1348   performance of the model, and even get worse performance on the MNIST dataset. The rotation and
1349   flip method can improve the performance for all datasets, especially for the more complex CIFAR-10
       and CIFAR-100 datasets.

1380   we do not need to explicitly embed the activation functions into the graphs. To further support our
1381   approach, we conduct an experimental analysis by adding learnable non-linear embeddings to our
