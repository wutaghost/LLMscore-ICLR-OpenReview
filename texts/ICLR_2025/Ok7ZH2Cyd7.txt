009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep reinforcement learning research has been the center of remarkable scientific
012            progress for the past decade. From winning one of the most challenging games to
013            algorithmic advancements that allowed solving problems without even explicitly
014            knowing the rules of the task at hand reinforcement learning research progress
015            has been the epicenter of many breakthrough ideas. In this paper, we analyze the
016            methodological issues in deep reinforcement learning. We introduce the theoretical
017            foundations of the underlying causes outlining that the asymptotic performance
018
               of deep reinforcement learning algorithms does not have a monotone relationship
               to the performance in the regimes where data becomes scarce. The extensive
019
               large-scale empirical analysis provided in our paper discovers that a major line of
020
               deep reinforcement learning research under the canonical methodological choices
021            resulted in suboptimal conclusions.
022
023
024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Founded on rigorous theoretical guarantees (Sutton, 1984; Watkins, 1989; Barto & Singh, 1990;
027   Barto et al., 1995), reinforcement learning research achieved high acceleration upon the proposal of
028   the initial study on approximating the state-action value function via deep neural networks (Mnih
029   et al., 2015). Following this initial study a line of highly successful deep reinforcement learning
030   algorithms have been proposed (Hasselt et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016; Hessel et al., 2018; 2021;
031   Kapturowski et al.; Zhu et al., 2024) from focusing on different architectural ideas to employing
032   estimators targeting overestimation, all of which were designed and tested in the high-data regime
033   (i.e. two hundred million frame training). An alternative recent line of research with an extensive
034
      amount of publications focused on pushing the performance bounds of deep reinforcement learning
      policies in the low-data regime, i.e. with one hundred thousand environment interaction training.
035
      Many different ideas in current reinforcement learning research, from model-based reinforcement
036
      learning to increasing sample efficiency with observation regularization, gained acceleration in several
037   research directions based on policy performance comparisons demonstrated in the Arcade Learning
038   Environment 100K benchmark.
039
040
      In this paper, we focus on the canonical methodological choices made in deep reinforcement learning
      research and demonstrate that there is a significant overlooked underlying premise driving this line
041
      of research without being explicitly discussed: that the performance profiles of deep reinforcement
042
      learning algorithms have a monotonic relationship with different sample-complexity regimes. This
043   implicit assumption, that is commonly shared amongst a large collection of low-data regime studies,
044   shapes how the canonical methodological choices are made in deep reinforcement learning research
045   and represents a prominent misdirection in scientific progress. The suboptimal conclusions obtained
046   from these canonical choices shape future research directions with incorrect reasoning. These
047   methodological decisions fueled by the incorrect conclusions, further influence the overall research
048   efforts directed towards certain ideas for several years following. Thus, in our paper we target these
049   underlying premises and aim to answer the following questions:
050
051
             • What are the canonical methodological choices made in deep reinforcement learning that
               fundamentally affects the conclusions made?
052
053          • What is the foundational relationship between sample complexity and the algorithmic
               performance from the data-scarce regime to the asymptotic regime?

           D ISTRIBUTION
240
      The instances of the implicit assumption that the performance profile of an algorithm in the high-data
241
      regime will translate to the low-data regime monotonically appear in almost all of the studies con-
242
      ducted in the low-data regime. In particular, we see that when this line of work was being conducted
243   the best performing algorithm in the high-data regime was based on learning the state action value
244   distribution. Hence, there are many cases in the literature (e.g. DRQ, OTR, DER, CURL, SimPLE,
245   Efficient-Zero) where all the newly proposed algorithms in the low-data regime are being compared
246   to an algorithm that learns the state-action value distribution, under the implicit assumption that the
247   algorithm that learns the state-action value distribution must achieve the current best performance in
248   the low-data regime. The large scale experiments provided in Section 5 demonstrate the impact of
249   this implicit assumption in the low-data regime deep reinforcement learning algorithm design. In
250   particular, the results reported in Section 5 prove that the performance profile of an algorithm in the
251
      high-data regime does not monotonically transfer to the low-data regime. Due to this extensive focus
      throughout the literature on low-data regime comparisons to algorithms that learn the state action
252
      value distribution, we provide additional theoretical justification for the empirically observed sample
253
      complexity results in the low to high-data regime in deep reinforcement learning.
254
255   To obtain theoretical insight into the larger sample complexity exhibited by learning the state-action
256   value distribution we consider the fundamental comparison between learning the distribution of
257
      a random variable X versus only learning the mean E[X ]. In the base algorithm that learns the
      state-action value distribution the goal is to learn a distribution over state-action values that has
258
      finite support. It is well-known that learning a discrete distribution to error ϵ in total variation
259
      distance requires more samples than estimating the mean to within error epsilon (see Proposition
260   B.1). Although this fact implies that learning the state-action value distribution has an intrinsically
261   higher sample complexity than that of standard Q-learning, it does not provide insights into the
262   comparison of an error of ϵ in the mean with an error of ϵ in total variation distance. Hence, the
263   following proposition demonstrates a precise justification of the comparison: whenever there are two
264   different actions where the true mean state-action values are within ϵ, an approximation error of ϵ in
265   total variation distance for the state-action value distribution of one of the actions can be sufficient to
266   reverse the order of the means.

504   Datasets are Created and Founded on Implicit Assumptions: Thus far we have discussed the
505   pivotal role of implicit assumptions on the algorithmic comparisons and developing baselines in
506   deep reinforcement learning. However, this issue further extends back to even how the entire Arcade
507
      Learning Environment 100K benchmark was established. The ALE 100K was initially created to
      allow researchers to work on a subset of games instead of full set of games used in the high-data

      regime (Kaiser et al., 2020), and this benchmark is currently used by any algorithm developed for the

513   the issues we explicitly discover and analyze in our paper are not limited to baselines but further
514   extend to entire benchmarks that we evaluate reinforcement learning algorithms on.

516   forcement learning research that is based on these implicit assumptions, give incorrect signals on
517   why and what makes these algorithms work when designed for the low-data regime, and hence affect
518   future research directions while misdirecting research efforts from ideas that could have worked in
519   the algorithm design process.

523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
526   In this paper we aimed to answer the following questions: (i) What are the canonical methodological
527   choices that fundamentally effects the progress in deep reinforcement learning research, (ii) What
528   is the underlying theoretical relationship between the performance profiles and sample complexity
529   regimes?, and (iii) Do the performance profiles of deep reinforcement learning algorithms designed
530   for certain data regimes translate monotonically to a different sample complexity region? To be able
531
      to answer these questions we provide theoretical analysis on the sample complexity of the baseline
      deep reinforcement learning algorithms. We conduct extensive experiments both in the low-data
532
      regime 100K Arcade Learning Environment and high-data regime baseline 200 million frame training.
533
      Our analysis reveals that under the canonical methodological choices a major line of research resulted
534   in suboptimal conclusions. In particular, both theoretical and empirical analysis provided in our
535   paper demonstrate that the performance profiles of deep reinforcement learning algorithms do not
536   have a monotonic relationship across sample complexity regimes. The underlying assumption of
537   the monotonic relationship of the performance characteristics and the sample complexity regimes
538   that is currently present in many recent state-of-the-art works led these studies to result in incorrect
539   conclusions. Our paper demonstrates that several baseline Q algorithms perform better than a line of
      recent algorithms claimed to be the state-of-the-art.
