011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Forecasting models are pivotal in a data-driven world with vast volumes of time
014            series data that appear as a compound of vast Linear and Nonlinear patterns. Recent
015
               deep time series forecasting models struggle to utilize seasonal and trend decom-
               position to separate the entangled components. Such a strategy only explicitly
016
               extracts simple linear patterns like trends, leaving the other linear modes and vast
017
               unexplored nonlinear patterns to the residual. Their flawed linear and nonlinear
018            feature extraction models and shallow-level decomposition limit their adaptation to
019            the diverse patterns present in real-world scenarios. Given this, we innovate Recur-
020            sive Residual Decomposition by introducing explicit extraction of both linear and
021            nonlinear patterns. This deeper-level decomposition framework, which is named
022            LiNo, captures linear patterns using a Li block which can be a moving average
023            kernel, and models nonlinear patterns using a No block which can be a Transformer
024            encoder. The extraction of these two patterns is performed alternatively and recur-
025            sively. To achieve the full potential of LiNo, we develop the current simple linear
026
               pattern extractor to a general learnable autoregressive model, and design a novel
               No block that can handle all essential nonlinear patterns. Remarkably, the proposed
027
               LiNo achieves state-of-the-art on thirteen real-world benchmarks under univariate
028
               and multivariate forecasting scenarios. Experiments show that current forecasting
029            models can deliver more robust and precise results through this advanced Recursive
030            Residual Decomposition. We hope this work could offer insight into designing
031            more effective forecasting models. Code is available at this anonymous repository:
032            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LiNo-8225/.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Time series forecasting (TSF) is a long-established task (Lim & Zohren, 2021; Wang et al., 2024b),
038   with a wide range of applications (Zhou et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2022a; Piao et al., 2024). Notably,
039
      numerous deep learning methods have been employed to address the TSF problem, utilizing ar-
      chitectures such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Elman, 1990; Lin et al., 2023), Temporal
040
      Convolutional Networks (TCNs) (Donghao & Xue, 2024; Wu et al., 2023), Multilayer Perceptron
041
      (MLP) (Liu et al., 2023; Challu et al., 2023) and Transformers (Liu et al., 2022b; Kitaev et al.,
042   2020). Recent advancements in the time series forecasting community have suggested that seasonal
043   (nonlinear) and trend (linear) decomposition can enhance forecasting performance (Zhang et al.,
044   2022b; Wu et al., 2021). This is typically performed once, with the trend component being extracted
045   using methods such as the simple moving average kernel (MOV) (Zhou et al., 2022b; Wang et al.,
046   2023), the exponential smoothing function (ESF) (Woo et al., 2022), or a learnable 1D convolution
047   kernel (LD) (Yu et al., 2024). The seasonal component is then obtained by subtracting the trend part
048   from the original time series.
049   Real-world time series data often exhibit more complex structures, combining multiple levels of
050   linear and nonlinear characteristics (Stock & Watson, 1998; Dama & Sinoquet, 2021; Agrawal &
051   Adhikari, 2013). This suggests that they are the result of the additive combination of various linear
052   and nonlinear components, as illustrated in Figure 1. Simple seasonal and trend decomposition
053   faces three main challenges when deployed in real-world time series data. Firstly, these methods
      rely on simple techniques (MOV, LD, ESF). MOV and LD employed a fixed window size, which

073   cannot fully extract all linear patterns, while the non-learnability of ESF limits its performance.
074   They can only extract simple linear features such as trends, while other linear features like cyclic
075   patterns and autoregression (Chatfield & Xing, 2019) remain underutilized. Secondly, we argue the
076   obtained seasonal part is actually a residual consisting of all the unextracted linear and nonlinear

      mixtures (Bengio et al., 2013; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015). Another problem is the design of current
079
      nonlinear models, which mainly focus on one or two types of nonlinear patterns (temporal variations,
080
      frequency information, inter-series dependencies, etc). Such designs cannot satisfy our requirements
081   in the real world. Thirdly, their shallow-level decomposition is incompatible with the multi-level
082   characteristics of real-world time series. In contrast, numerous studies have shown that deeper-level
083   decomposition can lead to better time series analysis (Huang et al., 1998; Rilling et al., 2003).
084   Therefore, it is crucial to develop advanced decomposition methods capable of multi-granularity
085   separation of various modes, and better linear and nonlinear pattern extractors to provide a more
086   nuanced understanding of the signal’s structure and potentially improve forecasting performance.
087   To address the challenges above, we propose adopting Recursive Residual Decomposition (RRD), a
088   method used in Empirical Mode Decomposition (EMD) (Huang et al., 1998; Rilling et al., 2003), to
089   decompose a time series into multiple patterns. This process is performed recursively. Each pattern is
090   extracted based on the residual obtained by subtracting previously extracted patterns from the original
091   signal, utilizing Intrinsic Mode Functions (IMF) to identify similar characteristics. Specifically, We
092   can employ a Li block (MOV, LD, ESF, etc.) as the IMF for extracting linear patterns and a No
093   block (Transformer, RNN, etc.) as the IMF for extracting nonlinear patterns. Then, the RRD is
094   performed on a deeper level. We denote the proposed overall framework as LiNo. To fully realize
095
      RRD’s potential, we propose an advanced LiNo, Specifically, we enhance the Li block to a general
      learnable autoregressive model with a full receptive field and propose a novel No block capable of
096
      modeling essential nonlinear features, such as temporal variations, period information, and inter-series
097
      dependencies in multivariate forecasting.
098
099   In summary, our contributions can be delineated as follows.
100
              • We advance current shallow linear and nonlinear decomposition by innovating Recursive
101
                Residual Decomposition.
102
              • Proposed No block demonstrates better nonlinear pattern extraction ability than current
103
                SOTA nonlinear models, such as iTransformer and TSMixer.
104
105           • LiNo consistently delivers top-tier performance in multivariate and univariate forecasting
106
                scenarios, demonstrating robust resilience against noise disturbances.
107           • The significant improvement by more nuanced and deeper linear and nonlinear decomposi-
                tion provides insight for designing more effective and robust forecasting models.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Advancement in recent time series forecasting. Time series forecasting is a critical area of
111   research that finds applications in both industry and academia. With the powerful representation
112   capability of neural networks, deep forecasting models have undergone a rapid development (Wang
113   et al., 2024b; Lim & Zohren, 2021; Torres et al., 2021). Recent research endeavors have focused
114   on segmenting the sequence into a series of patches (Nie et al., 2023; Zhang & Yan, 2023), better
115   modeling the relationships between variables (Ng et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024), the dynamic changes
116   within a sequence (Wu et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023), or both (Yu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a). Some
117   works strive for more efficient forecasting solutions (Lin et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). Other efforts
118   aim to revitalize existing architectures, such as RNN (Lin et al., 2023), Transformer (Liu et al., 2024b),
119
      TCN (Donghao & Xue, 2024), with new ideas, or to explore the potential of outstanding architectures
      from other domains, such as MLP-Mixer (Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a), Mamba (Ahamed &
120
      Cheng, 2024; Wang et al., 2024c), Graph Neural Network (Yi et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2022), even
121
      Large Language Models (Jin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Pan et al., 2024; Bian et al., 2024; Gruver
122   et al., 2024), for application in time series forecasting. Notably, some efforts also begin to ponder the
123   role of self-attention in time series forecasting (Ilbert et al., 2024).
124
125   Importance of linear and nonlinear patterns. Deep time series models that are dedicated to
126   model nonlinear patterns such as non-stationarity (Liu et al., 2022c), time-variant and time-invariant
127   features (Liu et al., 2023), and frequency bias (Piao et al., 2024) have delivered outstanding perfor-
128   mance in various domains. In contrast, recent advances have proved the importance of attention to
129   linear patterns in time series (Toner & Darlow, 2024). For instance, DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023) and
130   RLinear (Li et al., 2023a) achieve results comparable to, or even surpassing, many intricately designed
131   nonlinear models in certain scenarios, using only simple linear layers. FITS (Xu et al., 2024) even
132   achieves SOTA performance with merely 10k parameters. This suggests that balanced consideration
133
      of both linear and nonlinear patterns can be crucial for enhancing predictive performance in time
      series forecasting, which is unexplored in the current simple seasonal and trend decomposition.
134
135
      Decomposition based on Residual. Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) first explored a rudimentary
136
      seasonal (Nonlinear) and trend (Linear) decomposition based on residual. The trend part is extracted
137
      using linear models like a moving average kernel, and the seasonal part is the residual of subtracting
138   the input feature using the trend part. The earlier and more pioneering residual-based decomposition
139   in deep learning time series was explored in N-BEATS (Oreshkin et al., 2020). N-BEATS is built
140   using stacked residual blocks, where each block refines the forecast by predicting based on the
141   residual errors of previous predictions. Instead of directly modeling the time series components (e.g.,
142   trend or seasonality), the model iteratively adjusts the residuals at each step.
143
      Our LiNo develops the decomposition strategy in Autoformer by explicitly capturing both seasonal
144   (Nonlinear) and trend (Linear) components and performing at a deeper level. Though LiNo and
145   N-BEATS share some similarities, such as both employing the concept of Recursive Residual
146   Decomposition (RRD), they are fundamentally different. First, N-BEATS does not explicitly capture
147   linear and nonlinear separately. Second, as a univariate model, N-BEATS cannot handle more
148   complex multivariate time series. Third, the role of RRD differs in each model: while N-BEATS
149   uses RRD to refine the final prediction based on residual errors from previous predictions, LiNo
150   employs RRD to capture more nuanced linear and nonlinear patterns. To empirically compare their
151   differences, we designed a model based on the N-BEATS framework, where the internal components
152
      are identical to those of the proposed LiNo. In the subsequent experiments, we will denote this model
      as Mu. A more detailed comparison and analysis of LiNo and N-BEATS is left in Appendix F.

202   3.2       L I N O P IPELINE
203
      The structure of the LiNo framework is illustrated in Figure 2 (Middle). Initially, we extract the
204
      whole series embedding, which is then processed through N LiNo blocks to forecast future values of
205
      the time series. In this subsection, we will explain the whole series embedding, Li block, and No
206   block, step by step.
207
208   Whole series embedding. Following iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024b), we first map time series data
209   X ∈ RC×T from the original space to a new space to get Xembed ∈ RC×D using a simple linear
210   projection, where D denotes the dimension of the layer. Such a design can better preserve the unique
211   patterns of each variate.
212
213   Li block. The fixed window size of the MOV and learnable 1D convolution kernel (LD), and the
214   non-learnability of the exponential smoothing function (ESF), prevent them from fully extracting all
215   linear patterns. So we introduce a learnable autoregressive model (AR) with a full receptive field to
      replace them, where MOV, LD, and ESF are its subsets.

235   No block. Typical nonlinear time series characteristics include temporal variations, frequency infor-
236   mation, inter-series dependencies, etc. While all of these factors are crucial for precise forecasting,
237   current nonlinear models can only address one (Wu et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022a;
238   Piao et al., 2024) or two (Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023) of these aspects.
239   Therefore, we designed a No block that simultaneously handles all these characteristics.
240   As in Figure 2 (left), given a feature RiL ∈ RC×D , both temporal variation patterns and frequency
241   information are accessible through linear projection. Hence, we start by applying a linear projection
242   in the time domain to obtain temporal variation patterns NiT ∈ RC×D in a classical manner. Corre-
243   spondingly, we apply a linear projection in the frequency domain, which is transformed from RiL
244   using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), following FITS (Xu et al., 2024). After that, the frequency
245   domain representation is converted back, using the Inverse Fast Fourier Transform (IFFT) to obtain
246   frequency information patterns NiF ∈ RC×D . To leverage the complementary strengths of both
247   domains, features extracted from both the time and frequency domains are fused and activated by:
248   NiT F = Tanh(NiT + NiF ).
249   To model inter-series dependencies, we first normalize NiT F across the channel dimension using
250   a softmax function, as in Figure 2 (upper left). We then compute a weighted mean by multiplying
251   the softmax weights with NiT F and summing over the channel dimension. This weighted mean is
252   repeated to match and concatenate with NiT F . The concatenated result is passed through an MLP to
253   obtain inter-series dependencies information NiC . We note that a recent work, SOFTS (Han et al.,
254   2024), has adopted a similar approach to ours for modeling inter-series dependencies. A detailed
255   comparative analysis is provided in Appendix G.
256   To integrate temporal variations, frequency information, and inter-series dependencies, we first apply
257   Layer Normalization to the sum of NiT F and NiC , resulting in NiT F C . A subsequent MLP is applied,
258   and the result is added back to NiT F C , followed by a final Layer Normalization to produce the
259   overall nonlinear pattern Ni . The nonlinear prediction Pino ∈ RC×F for this level is then obtained by
260   mapping the extracted nonlinear part Ni . Finally, RiN = RiL − Ni is passed to the next LiNo block,
261   where Hi+1 = RiN .

469
470    Forecasting performance comparison. We use iTransformer, a leading transformer-based time
471    series forecasting model, as the backbone. We evaluate three model designs: ‘Raw’ (classical design),
472
      ‘Mu’ (recursive splitting of representations for prediction, design used in N-BEATS (Oreshkin et al.,
       2020)), and ‘LiNo’ (our proposed framework with further recursive splitting into linear and nonlinear
473
       patterns). The forecasting performance in Table 6 (b) demonstrates the effectiveness of the LiNo
474
       framework. Compared to ‘Raw’, ‘Mu’ reduces the MSE on ETTm2, ECL, and Weather by 1.3%,
475    0.28%, and 1.06%, respectively. LiNo further reduces the MSE by 2.96%, 6.34%, and 6.72%. These
476    results indicate that while ‘Mu’ improves forecasting performance, it remains suboptimal due to the
477    entanglement of linear and nonlinear predictions. LiNo effectively separates these patterns, achieving
478    more accurate results.

484   where α ∈ {0%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 100%} is the noise intensity coefficient, and noise ∈ RB×T ×N is
485   Gaussian noise with mean 0 and standard deviation 1. The noisy input X̂ was used during training. A
      more robust forecasting model will be less affected by this noise.

      5    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      The commonly used seasonal and trend decomposition (STD) in previous methods still rely on
532
      flawed linear pattern extractors. Their lack of separating the nonlinear component from residual
533
      and shallow-level decomposition severely hinders its modeling capability. This work advances
534   them by incorporating a more general linear extraction model and introducing a novel and powerful
535   nonlinear extraction model into RRD. By performing RRD at a deeper and more nuanced level, we
536   achieve a more refined decomposition, leading to more accurate and robust forecasting results. The
537   proposed No block excels in capturing nonlinear features. Experiments across multiple benchmarks
538   demonstrated LiNo’s superior performance in both univariate and multivariate forecasting tasks,
539   offering improved accuracy and stability. These findings could offer opportunities to design more
      robust and precise forecasting models.

       D.2    V ISUALIZATION OF W EIGHT OF L I BLOCKS AND N O BLOCKS
1038
       We present visualizations of the weight of Li blocks and No blocks obtained across 13 multivariate
1039   forecasting benchmarks to help better understand the proposed LiNo. We set the input length T = 96,
1040   prediction length F = 96, and number of LiNo blocks (layers) N = 3. Results are in Figure 19– 31.
1041
1042
       The method used for getting the weight follows the approach outlined in Analysis of linear
       model (Toner & Darlow, 2024). Plotting the learned matrices as in Figure 19 requires us to first
1043
1044
       convert each trained model into the form f (⃗x) = A⃗x + ⃗b. To do this we note that f (⃗0) = A⃗0 + ⃗b = ⃗b.
       Thus, the bias can be found by passing the zero vector into the trained model. We can determine A
1045
       in a similar manner. Let e⃗i denote the i-th coordinate vector, that is e⃗i is the vector which is 1 at
1046
       position i and zero elsewhere. Then f (⃗ei ) = A⃗ ei + ⃗b = A·,i + ⃗b where A·,i is the i-th column of
1047
       A. Hence, given that we have already computed the bias term, we may derive A simply by passing
1048
       through each coordinate vector e⃗i and subtracting ⃗b. Then, we repeat this process separately to each
1049
       Li block and No block to get their weight, since they each output a forecasting result.
1050
1051   Take the ETTh1 dataset as an example, we observe in Figure 7 that each block (Li or No block)
1052   produces significantly different weights. This indicates that each block focuses on different patterns.
1053
       The three No blocks all generate weights with noticeable periodicity, while the three Li blocks’
       weights are more concentrated on the most recent points in the input series. These interesting findings
1054
       help us better understand how neural networks behave when extracting features from time series.
