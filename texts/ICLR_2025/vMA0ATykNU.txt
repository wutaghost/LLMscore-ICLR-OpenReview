011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Trajectory prediction is crucial for practical applications, encompassing naviga-
014            tion for autonomous vehicles and the implementation of safety systems based on
015            the Internet of Vehicles (IoV). Most existing methods significantly rely on com-
016            prehensive map information, employing robust rule constraints to incrementally
017            predict trajectories within the driver’s local decision-making context. However,
018
               in environments characterized by weak rule enforcement, such as urban intersec-
               tions, these approaches neglect the disparity between the driver’s global inten-
019
               tions and current behaviors.Recognizing the characteristics of intersection traf-
020
               fic flow—macroscopically organized yet microscopically disordered, exhibiting
021            highly heterogeneous conditions—this paper presents a novel model termed Long-
022            short Range Aggregation for Trajectory Prediction in Intersections (LSTR). This
023            model anchors the vehicle’s local decision-making process to long-range inten-
024            tions. Specifically, LSTR predicts the vehicle’s destination via a global intention
025            inference module and models its long-range driving intentions through cluster-
026            ing to extract macroscopic traffic flow patterns. This long-range intention subse-
027            quently informs the short-range local interaction behaviors captured by the local
028            behavior decision module. Ultimately, the fused features from these two modules
029
               are analyzed using a multi-modal decoder to interpret the various motion pat-
               terns, resulting in the trajectory prediction outcomes.We rigorously validate the
030
               proposed framework across multiple intersection scenarios utilizing real-world
031
               datasets, including inD, roundD, and a subset of WOMD. Experimental results
032            indicate that our model surpasses several benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Trajectory prediction is crucial for autonomous driving and intelligent transportation, providing fore-
039
      sight for vehicle planning, safety, and traffic management. Existing methods have achieved success
      in highway and urban road applications Cong et al. (2023); Shi et al. (2024); Sun et al. (2022),
040
      often relying on lane-level high-definition maps to spatially constrain trajectories and make locally
041
      optimal decisions. These methods can be seen as ”greedy algorithms under strong constraints.”
042   However, in weakly constrained environments like urban intersections, they underperform due to
043   neglecting multi-level flow dynamics, leading to a disconnect between global intentions and local
044   decisions.
045
      The multi-tiered flow dynamics at intersections can be modeled as a superposition of macroscopic
046
      and microscopic traffic flows Zhao et al. (2023).Microscopic traffic flow involves complex interac-
047
      tions and behaviors of intersection users, characterized by disorder and heterogeneity. Each user
048   navigates the intersection with different strategies based on real-time conditions, the behavior of
049   others, and individual decision-making, such as alternating vehicle passage or pedestrians crossing
050   unexpectedly. This dynamism, driven by individual choices and implicit interactions, makes micro-
051   scopic traffic flow inherently difficult to predict. Macroscopic traffic flow at intersections is defined
052   by a weakly regulated pattern, with the primary constraint being the ”route choice”—the selection
053   of entry and exit routes. Despite the complexity of microscopic behavior, macroscopic flow reflects
      collective tendencies, providing essential global prior information for trajectory prediction.

075   Accordingly, intersection trajectory prediction can be divided into two core challenges: local in-
076   teraction scene perception and long-range global intention prediction. Local interaction scene per-
077   ception primarily focuses on the dynamic coupling relationships within microscopic traffic flow.
078   By capturing real-time interactions among traffic participants, it seeks to comprehend complex and
079   heterogeneous traffic behaviors, thereby accurately modeling the mutual influence between individ-
080
      ual vehicles. Global intention prediction, in contrast, centers on analyzing the global trends within
      macroscopic traffic flow. It relies on the relatively weak ”route choice pattern” rule to discern and
081
      infer the overall driving intention of vehicles at intersections.
082
083   In this work, we present a novel trajectory prediction method for urban intersections, integrating
084   local interaction modeling and global intention prediction within a Transformer-based framework.
085   Our approach introduces two key innovations:(1) We propose a Local Behavior Decision Module
086
      (LBDM) to model local interactions. Temporal dependencies are captured through the Temporal
      Neighborhood Aggregation module, and short-range motion patterns are refined using the Coherent
087
      Occupancy Prediction (COP) head. This enables parallel prediction of future trajectories, effec-
088
      tively capturing local dynamics.(2) We propose a Global Intention Inference Module (GIIM), which
089   predicts the destination, captures the ”route choice pattern,” and anchors global intentions with lo-
090   cal decisions. These features are filtered and passed to the decoder, combining mode-specific and
091   temporal encoding for path search, enabling accurate and interpretable predictions in complex in-
092   tersections.(3) Using these techniques, the LSTR framework outperforms map-prior algorithms. In
093   experiments on the inD, rounD, and WOMD datasets, where LSTR improves b-minFDE6 on inD by
094   4.5 and minADE6 on rounD by 4.2 over the second-best method.

      Trajectory prediction based on HD maps. Trajectory prediction is a well-researched area in au-
      tonomous driving Huang et al. (2023)Teeti et al. (2022), involving the estimation of future trajecto-
100
      ries based on historical agent states and environmental information. HD maps play a critical role by
101
      providing detailed road structure, lane configurations, and traffic signs, significantly improving pre-
102   diction accuracy Wang et al. (2023)Sharma et al. (2024). Methods leveraging HD maps are catego-
103   rized into traditional kinematic models and deep learning approaches.Kinematic models use physics
104   and kinematics to predict vehicle trajectories, considering position, velocity, acceleration, and road
105   constraints Lefkopoulos et al. (2020)Okamoto et al. (2017)Wang et al. (2019)Zhang et al. (2017).
106   While these models are interpretable and perform well in short-term predictions (up to one second),
107   they struggle with complex maneuvers in dynamic environments like urban intersections due to
      simplifying assumptions.Recently, deep learning models have advanced trajectory prediction using

108
      HD maps. Early methods Casas et al. (2020)Marchetti et al. (2020)Park et al. (2020)Zhang et al.
109   (2020) encoded maps and agents into images using CNNs, followed by fully connected layers for
110   prediction. Approaches like VectorNet Gao et al. (2020) and LaneGCN Liang et al. (2020) represent
111   road structures and agent motion in graph networks to capture agent-map interactions. Target-driven
112   models such as TNT Zhao et al. (2021) and DenseTNT Gu et al. (2021a) generate map-based candi-
113   date targets to regress trajectories. The MTR series Liu et al. (2021)Shi et al. (2024) uses learnable
114   motion queries to search for strategies within lane-level constraints.While deep learning methods
115   reliant on HD maps perform well, they falter in environments like urban intersections where map
116   data is sparse, and vehicle strategies are influenced by global intentions and unstructured informa-
117
      tion (e.g., traffic flow, interactive behaviors, implicit rules). Greedy algorithms fail to model global
      strategies effectively, distorting predictions. Our approach explicitly models agents’ global inten-
118
      tions through endpoint clustering, using this to constrain local interactions and ensure consistency
119
      between local decisions and global planning.
120
121   Transformer-based Vehicle Motion Forecasting. Transformers are a neural network architec-
122   ture leveraging the attention mechanism, originally developed for machine translation in NLP, and
123
      have outperformed recurrent neural networks Braşoveanu & Andonie (2020). Recently, they have
      proven highly effective for trajectory analysis Quintanar et al. (2021). Huang et al. Huang et al.
124
      (2022) proposed a Transformer-based multimodal trajectory prediction model using multi-head at-
125
      tention to capture relationships between agents. SceneTransformer Ngiam et al. (2021) integrates
126   features from agent interactions and road maps spatially and temporally, while LaneTransformer
127   Wang et al. (2023) extends this with Attention-Based Block Aggregation for higher-order interac-
128   tions.In summary, Transformers show great potential for trajectory prediction by capturing complex
129   dependencies and interactions. They offer scalability, transfer learning, and the ability to manage
130   multiple agents. Our LSTR approach builds on this by introducing a Transformer architecture that
131   incorporates auxiliary tasks in both the local behavior decision module and global intent inference
132   module. This design tightly constrains global intentions and local behaviors, enabling accurate and
133   interpretable trajectory predictions in complex intersections.

      3.3   M ULTI - MODAL D ECODER
271
272   The objective of this module is to predict K multimodal trajectories for each target vehicle, complet-
273   ing the trajectory prediction task. The model has learned global intentions from previous modules,
274   and vehicles with similar intentions have similar trajectories but differ in temporal shifts. To account
275   for this, we introduce time-sensitive, learnable position embeddings to search for local trajectories
276   across different modes. These embeddings are added to the global intention pseudo-labels FP and
277   the Key and Value features of the target vehicles.
278   The multi-modal decoder first selects the K mode labels FP′ most similar to FP and adds them to the
279   Query after position encoding to predict K future modes. Key and Value consist of two parts: the
280   vehicle’s pseudo-label FP , re-featurized and concatenated with the historical trajectory feature FA ,
281   and the map features selected based on the predicted exit êi and entry si . After position encoding,
282   these are cross-attended with the Query, and the fused map and trajectory features are passed through
283   an MLP into the prediction head. The prediction head outputs K trajectories, each modeled by a 2D
284
      Gaussian distribution.
285   The predicted trajectory mean, standard deviation, and correlation coefficient capture trajectory un-
286   certainty. The model is trained using negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss, optimizing the probability

610   A.2    DATASET P ROCESSING
611
612   Downsampling trajectory data speeds up prototyping and experimentation, but excessively low sam-
613   pling rates may distort trajectories. To balance this, we downsampled the inD and RounD datasets
614   to 10 Hz (0.1-second intervals) while preserving the original information. Analysis revealed com-
615   ponents exceeding the new Nyquist frequency (5 Hz), so a 7th-order Chebyshev Type I filter was
616
      applied before downsampling to remove high-frequency components.
617   For the inD dataset, 8,233 vehicle trajectories from four intersections were split into training, val-
618   idation, and test sets (0.8:0.1:0.1). We predicted future trajectories using a 3-second observation
619   window and a 5-second prediction horizon. For the RounD dataset, all 13,509 vehicle trajectories
620   from three roundabouts were used for training, with the same data splits and observation/prediction
621   windows.
622   To validate generalizability, we used 60 intersection scenarios from the Waymo Open Motion
623   Dataset, comprising about 580,000 data points from 6,640 vehicles. Time steps were 0.1 seconds
624   apart, and the data was split (0.8:0.1:0.1), using the same observation and prediction windows as
625   above.
