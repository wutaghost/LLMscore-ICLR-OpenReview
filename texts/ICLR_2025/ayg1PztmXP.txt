009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training) uses contrastive learning from
012            noise image-text pairs to excel at recognizing a wide array of candidates, yet its
013            focus on broad associations hinders the precision in distinguishing subtle differ-
014            ences among fine-grained items. Conversely, Multimodal Large Language Models
015            (MLLMs) excel at classifying fine-grained categories, thanks to their substantial
016
               knowledge from pre-training on web-level corpora. However, the performance of
               MLLMs declines with an increase in category numbers, primarily due to grow-
017
               ing complexity and constraints of limited context window size. To synergize the
018
               strengths of both approaches and enhance the few-shot/zero-shot recognition abil-
019            ities for datasets characterized by extensive and fine-grained vocabularies, this pa-
020            per introduces RAR, a Retrieving And Ranking augmented method for MLLMs.
021            We initially establish a multi-modal retriever based on CLIP to create and store
022            explicit memory for different categories beyond the immediate context window.
023            During inference, RAR retrieves the top-k similar results from the memory and
024            uses MLLMs to rank and make the final predictions. Our proposed approach not
025            only addresses the inherent limitations in fine-grained recognition but also pre-
026            serves the model’s comprehensive knowledge base, significantly boosting accu-
027
               racy across a range of vision-language recognition tasks. Notably, our approach
               demonstrates a significant improvement in performance on 5 fine-grained visual
028
               recognition benchmarks, 11 few-shot image recognition datasets, and the 2 object
029
               detection datasets under the zero-shot recognition setting.

      The CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training) (Radford et al., 2021) model and its diverse
036   variants (Sun et al., 2023a; Dong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b) provide flexible and robust perfor-
037   mance across a wide array of visual-language understanding tasks. Despite its successes, we observe
038   that CLIP’s performance begins to wane when faced with datasets characterized by vast vocabularies
039   or fine-grained categories. As shown in the upper left of Fig. 1, the decline is largely attributable to
040   the inherent ambiguity of language descriptions and the challenges posed by synonyms, which can
041   confound the model’s ability to distinguish between closely related but distinct classes.
042   Parallel to these developments, Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have emerged as
043   a powerful class of generative models, exemplified by the likes of GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023) and
044   analogous advancements (Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Ye
045   et al., 2023; Awadalla et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Chen
046   et al., 2023). MLLMs, pre-trained on extensive corpora with substantial knowledge, demonstrate
047   remarkable proficiency in identifying fine-grained categories when the total number of candidates
048   remains manageable. Nevertheless, MLLMs’ efficacy is similarly compromised in scenarios involv-
049   ing extensive vocabularies and fine-grained categorizations (upper left of Fig. 1).
050   To address these challenges, we propose augmenting standard MLLMs with our RAR, a retrieving-
051   and-ranking augmented technique. Our RAR enables models to dynamically incorporate external
052   knowledge into the processing and generation workflows. By augmenting MLLMs with external
053   knowledge sources, we address challenges related to language ambiguity, synonym handling, and the
      limitations imposed by limited context windows when dealing with vast vocabularies. Our method

104
105   Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) understands images and
106   texts by contrastive learning from a vast amount of visual data paired with natural language descrip-
107   tions. CLIP has robust capabilities in downstream tasks including image-text retrieval (Yasunaga
      et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Glass et al., 2022), zero-shot classification (Zhou et al., 2022a; Gao et al.,


                                                                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2023), and open-vocabulary perception (Gu et al., 2022; Zang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022b). Fol-
109   lowing CLIP, many subsequent vision-language models (Jia et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a;b; Zhong
110   et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Sun et al., 2023b; Yang et al.,
111   2023b; Lüddecke & Ecker, 2022) are proposed to further improve the vision-language understand-
112   ing abilities. There are also works done to improve CLIP in zero-shot perception tasks (Subramanian
113   et al., 2022; Shtedritski et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023a). However,
114   simple dot-product between two unimodality features can lead to sub-optimal results for fine-grained
115   classification. In this paper, we demonstrate that CLIP faces challenges in making accurate zero-
116   shot predictions for fine-grained classes, and how our proposed method can effectively re-rank these
117
      predictions to improve the accuracy.
118   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) such as GPT4V (OpenAI, 2023), represent a sig-
119   nificant evolution in the landscape of Large Language Models (LLMs) by integrating visual images
120   as input tokens alongside textual information. The integration is facilitated through the use of an
121   additional vision encoder (Radford et al., 2021) and a bridging mechanism (Zhu et al., 2023; Liu
122   et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Awadalla et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
123
      2023; Bai et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023). MLLMs significantly enhance the
      interaction between humans and AI in more natural and intuitive ways and demonstrate remark-
124
      able capabilities in understanding and generating multi-modal content. Despite their prowess, our
125
      research uncovers a nuanced limitation: MLLMs tend to underperform in tasks requiring vast vocab-
126   ularies, where distinguishing subtle differences among different categories is crucial. However, we
127   prove that MLLMs exhibit a strong ability to excel in the re-ranking of top results obtained through
128   vision-language models such as CLIP. Fine-R (Liu et al., 2024b) first delves into leveraging MLLMs
129   for fine-grained perception tasks by prompt design for better descriptions and attributes. We find a
130   new way to prompt it with possible candidates to help screening and achieve better performance.

185   3.2   M ULTIMODAL R ETRIEVER
186
187
      The multimodal retriever is essentially responsible for querying a large multi-modal external mem-
      ory or database to find information relevant to the input query or context. In the process of multi-
188
      modal retriever, the main challenge lies in efficiently encoding and storing a large volume of im-
189
      ages/text embeddings for quick, accurate retrieval. Recognizing the main challenge, as shown in
190   Fig. 2, we have developed a multi-modal retriever that creates and stores multimodal embeddings,
191   with a focus on optimizing retrieval speed through index construction techniques.
192
193
      Extracting the Multi-modal Embeddings. We use the CLIP model discussed in Sec. 3.1 to extract
      the multi-modal embeddings. Given a data sample (xi , ci ) from the dataset D containing the image
194
      xi and class name ci , we use the CLIP image encoder Φimg to extract the image embedding eimg ∈ Rd
195
      and the CLIP text encoder Φtext to extract the text embedding etext ∈ Rd . The symbol d refers to the
196
      feature dimension (e.g., d = 512 for CLIP ViT-B/16). The image and text embeddings are stored
197
      in the memory M for retrieval (will discuss in Sec. 3.3). In some zero-shot settings, the image
198   embedding is not available and we merely store the text embedding into the memory.
199
200   Fast Retrieval Optimization. The brute force search is the common method for designing the re-
201
      triever, which requires iteration over all vectors in the memory M to compute similarity scores (e.g.,
      cosine similarity) and subsequently identify the top-k results. Although the brute force method is
202
      inherently straightforward, its efficiency markedly diminishes as the dataset escalates to the magni-
203
      tude of millions of embeddings. To enhance the speed of retrieval, we implement an index system
204   that uses the HNSW(Hierarchical Navigable Small World) algorithm (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018).
205   The adoption of the HNSW methodology facilitates a significant dimensionality reduction, thereby
206   enabling the construction of a more condensed index. Specifically, vectors in a Rd space of dimen-
207   sion d are transformed into a reduced d9 dimensional space. This reduction in dimensionality plays
208   a pivotal role in enhancing the speed of the retrieval process.
209
      Pre-processing for Detection Datasets. In object detection datasets, our methodology for extract-
210   ing image embeddings eimg is slightly different from the approach discussed previously. As pre-
211   sented in Fig. 3, we apply two additional pre-processing steps: cropping and blurring. Some pre-
212   vious works have proposed similar methods in CLIP like (Yang et al., 2023b; Lüddecke & Ecker,
213   2022). In the object detection dataset, an image typically contains multiple objects of varying sizes.
214   Some objects may dominate a large portion of the image, whereas others occupy minimal space.
215   Accordingly, our object detection procedure begins with cropping the image regions based on pro-
      posal bounding box coordinates, subsequently resizing the cropped region to a fixed proportion.

238   3.3   I NFERENCE WITH R ETRIEVING A ND R ANKING
239
240   After successfully constructing memory M by using our multimodal retriever, our next step is to
241   integrate the memory with the retrieval process and use MLLMs to rank the retrieval results and
242
      enhance the performance in few-shot/zero-shot perception tasks.
243   For example, in the inference stage of the few-shot image classification task, we first use the visual
244   encoder Φimg to process the input image and obtain the corresponding image embedding ê. The
245   visual encoder is identical to the encoder used in our multi-modal retriever. The image embedding
246   ê is then navigated through the previously constructed memory index and ranked by similarity to
247   identify the top-k related images. Consequently, memory M yields the names of the retrieved top-k
248
      categories, denoted as {c1 , c2 , c3 , ..., ck }. The top-k retrieved results serve as a preliminary filter,
      narrowing down the vast possibilities to those most likely relevant, based on historical data and the
249
      semantic closeness of stored labels to the image content.
250
251   Since these cropped sub-images are usually small, CLIP’s ability to extract features from these low-
252   resolution images is limited. Therefore, in the object detection task, we do not perform image-to-
253   image retrieval but use CLIP’s inherent image-text interaction capabilities to conduct image-to-text
254
      retrieval. Finally, we also obtain the top-k category information with the highest similarity.
255   Following the retrieval phase, the retrieved category labels alongside image embedding ê are inte-
256   grated and sent to the MLLMs through our ranking prompt. The MLLMs, combining the internal
257   knowledge and the retrieved information, make the final prediction of the image category. Our
258   proposed inference process, using both the retrieval results from our memory bank and subsequent
259   ranking by the MLLM, ensures a more accurate and contextually aware classification prediction.
260
      Our design represents a significant advancement in few-shot image classification, enabling our sys-
      tem to handle a wide variety of images and categories with high precision and flexibility.
261
262   Ranking Prompt Format. Fig. 4 presents our ranking prompt format. The process be-
263   gins with the prompt ‘Sort the optional categories: [class a, class b,
264   class c, class d, class e]’, which is dynamically generated to include the top-k class
265   names retrieved from our multimodal retriever. Our method uses the MLLM’s ability to rank these
266
      retrieved class names. Unlike traditional approaches that might rely solely on the initial retrieval
      order, our MLLM employs advanced linguistic and semantic analysis to assess the contextual ap-
267
      propriateness of each class name with the input image.
268

290   To create our fine-tuning data, we use the CLIP image encoder Φimg to extract the embeddings of
291   two disjoint subsets of images Da and Db , both drawn from the FGVC-Aircraft dataset. We provide
292   the ablation studies in Sec. 4.5 about using different datasets to construct the fine-tuning data. Our
293   observation reveals that the MLLM demonstrates robustness to the choice of fine-tuning datasets,
294
      with only marginal differences in performance outcomes.
295   For each image in Db , we apply the k-NN clustering algorithm to find the top 20 most similar
296   images in Da including their categories. Afterward, we select 16 sets from these 20 images, each
297   set comprising k images, and retain those groups that contain images of the same category as Db .
298   We then shuffled the category labels for these sets. Using the prompts shown in Fig. 4, we create a
299   dataset comprising roughly 30,000 entries, with the original sequence of categories serving as the
300
      ground-truth label. In summary, we build the fine-tuning data aiming to bolster the MLLM’s ranking
      performance.
301
302   In-Context Learning for Ranking. In-context learning presents a valuable alternative to fine-tuning
303   with ranking examples, particularly due to its flexibility and lower requirement for specialized data
304   preparation. While fine-tuning with ranking examples has proven to be highly effective, it neces-
305   sitates a substantial amount of curated data and computational resources for training. In contrast,
306
      in-context learning uses the model’s existing knowledge by providing it with specific examples di-
      rectly within the input prompt, guiding the model to understand and execute the task of ranking
307
      without the need for explicit re-training. Here we elaborate on the application of in-context learning
308
      with MLLMs to rank the retrieved results. To effectively guide the MLLMs in comprehending the
309   ranking task, we use the prompt format similar to Fig. 4 and integrate a specific ranking example
310   into the prompts. Please refer to the Sec. B for our structured in-context learning prompt. Please
311   refer to Sec. 4.5 for the ablation studies of discussing the difference between using fine-tuning or
312   in-context learning for ranking.

529   5      C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we highlight the potential of combining retrieving and ranking with multi-modal large
532   language models to revolutionize perception tasks such as fine-grained recognition, zero-shot im-
533   age recognition, and few-shot object recognition. Motivated by the limited zero-shot/few-shot of
534   CLIP and MLLMs on fine-grained datasets, our RAR designs the pipeline that uses MLLM to rank
535   the retrieved results. Our proposed approach can be seamlessly integrated into various MLLMs
536   for real-world applications where the variety and volume of categories continuously expand. Our
537   method opens up new avenues for research in augmenting the MLLM’s abilities with the retrieving-
538   augmented solution and could be beneficial for other tasks such as reasoning and generation in future
539
      works.

      A     DATASET S TATISTICS
768
769
      In this section, we delve deeper into the specifics of the fourteen classification and two detection
770
      datasets employed in our research. The classification datasets encompass a wide range, from general
771   categories that cover a broad spectrum of common objects to fine-grained types that focus on more
772   specific, detailed distinctions within a particular category. The detection datasets, on the other hand,
773   are extensive, encompassing tens of thousands of object categories. These datasets are designed to
774   challenge the model’s ability to identify and categorize objects from a vast array of possible classes.
775   The long-tail nature of these datasets poses a significant challenge for our RAR model.
776
777   A.1   C LASSIFICATION DATASETS
778
779   In the experimental part, we use a total of fourteen image classification datasets, including seven
780   fine-grained classification datasets and seven common classification datasets. Fine-grained image
781   classification datasets include: Bird-200 (Wah et al., 2011), Stanford Cars (Krause et al., 2013),
782   Dog-120 (Khosla et al., 2011), Oxford Flowers (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008), Oxford Pets (Parkhi
783   et al., 2012), FGVC-Aircraft (Maji et al., 2013), and Food101 (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008).

      Common image classification datasets include: ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), Caltech101 (Fei-Fei

785
      DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014), and UCF-101 (Soomro et al., 2012). We present all the utilized datasets
786
      in Fig. 6. And in Tab. 6, we list the statistics and sources of these datasets in detail.

788   In our fine-grained visual recognition experiments, we employed the following datasets: Bird-200,
789   Stanford Cars, Dog-120, Flowers-102, and Oxford pets. In each dataset, we selected 3 images from
790
      the training set to construct our memory and conducted tests on the corresponding validation sets.
      In our few-shot image classification experiments, we used the FGVC-Aircraft dataset to build fine-
791
      tune data and tested our RAR model across eleven classification datasets: Stanford Cars, Flower-

      102, Oxford Pets, Food101, ImageNet, Caltech101, RAF-DB, Sun397, Eurosat, DTD, and UCF-

912   Semantic Similarity (sACC).
913   Evaluation Metrics. In the fine-grained visual recognition section, we use two synergistic met-
914   rics: Clustering Accuracy (cACC) and Semantic Similarity (sACC) to evaluate our method, follow-
915   ing (Liu et al., 2024b). Clustering Accuracy (cACC) mainly assesses the accuracy of clustering
916   images within the same category, without considering the semantic relatedness of category labels.
917   Complementing this, Semantic Similarity (sACC) measures the similarity between the names of
      categories in the clusters and the ground truth.

1116   More Evaluation Results. In our few-shot image classification experiments, we employed the
1117   CLIP B/16 model to extract embeddings from n images in each category, which were then stored in
1118   memory for testing the accuracy of n-shot experiments. To accelerate retrieval speed, we initially
1119   use the HNSW algorithm to transform the original 576-dimensional vectors into 64-dimensional
1120   indices before storing the image embeddings in memory. HNSW is a commonly used Approximate
1121   Nearest Neighbor (ANN) algorithm, primarily aimed at quickly finding the k nearest elements to
1122   a query in a large set of candidates. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, we included
1123   results from 1-shot, 2-shot, and 16-shot experiments in the supplementary materials, alongside the

1127   centage point increase, and significantly outperforming the performance of the LLaVa model it-
1128   self. This outcome demonstrates the excellence of RAR in image classification tasks (including
