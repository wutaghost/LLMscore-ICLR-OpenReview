009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces ROUTE F INDER, a comprehensive foundation model frame-
012            work to tackle different Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) variants. Our core idea is
013            that a foundation model for VRPs should be able to represent variants by treating
014            each as a subset of a generalized problem equipped with different attributes. We
015            propose a unified VRP environment capable of efficiently handling any attribute
016            combination. The ROUTE F INDER model leverages a modern transformer-based
017            encoder and global attribute embeddings to improve task representation. Addi-
018            tionally, we introduce two reinforcement learning techniques to enhance multi-
019
               task performance: mixed batch training, which enables training on different vari-
               ants at once, and multi-variant reward normalization to balance different reward
020
               scales. Finally, we propose efficient adapter layers that enable fine-tuning for new
021
               variants with unseen attributes. Extensive experiments on 48 VRP variants show
022            ROUTE F INDER achieves competitive results. Our code is openly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Vehicle Routing Problems (VRPs) are an important class of Combinatorial Optimization (CO) prob-
028
      lems that have received much attention in Operations Research (OR) and Computer Science. Since
029
      the VRP is an NP-hard problem, finding an optimal solution by exhaustively exploring the solu-
030   tion space is often computationally expensive and impractical for large instances. Instead, heuristic
031   methods that quickly generate good (but possibly suboptimal) solutions are commonly used. The OR
032   community has developed many heuristics over the year, including the well-known Lin-Kernighan-
033   Helsgaun (LKH) heuristic (Helsgaun, 2017), Fast Iterated Local Optimization (FILO) (Accorsi &
034   Vigo, 2021; 2024) and Hybrid Genetic Search (HGS) (Vidal, 2022; Wouda et al., 2024). While these
035   algorithms are state-of-the-art on a range of VRP variants, they often require careful consideration of
036   the problem specifics, algorithm parameters, and computational resources to achieve the best results,
037   and thus require considerable expert knowledge to be applied in practice.
038   Recently, Neural Combinatorial Optimization (NCO) approaches have been developed to solve CO
039   problems. By leveraging deep learning, these approaches seek to learn and generalize from data,
040   potentially providing more flexible and scalable solutions (Kool et al., 2019; Hottung & Tierney,
041   2019; Kwon et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022; Berto et al., 2024; Hottung et al., 2024). In this way,
042   optimization problems essentially become data science problems, making them more accessible.
043   Similar to how the developments in natural language processing have resulted in Large Language
044   Models (LLMs), research efforts in solving CO problems through machine learning are also trending
045   toward foundation models (Liu et al., 2024c; Ye et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2024).
046   However, despite the recent progress made in learning VRP variants, there is a lack of a unified
047   approach that can effectively tackle a wide range of tasks without needing high-quality labeled
048   datasets, which is crucial for real-world impact. Such an approach would additionally provide a
049   platform for effectively finetuning unseen variants (Lin et al., 2024). A foundation model for VRPs
050
      would have important implications in terms of cost savings for companies and organizations as it
      can be easily adapted to new business requirements (constraints) outside of the training distribution.
051
052   In this work, we introduce ROUTE F INDER, a comprehensive foundation model framework for solv-
053   ing VRPs. We summarize our key contributions, including problem formulation, modeling, training,
      and finetuning, as follows:

071   2   R ELATED W ORKS
072
073
      Neural combinatorial optimization for VRPs NCO has emerged as a pivotal solution approach
074
      for VRPs and other CO problems, leveraging advancements in machine learning and neural network
075
      architectures (Bengio et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2021; Mazyavkina et al., 2021; Bogyrbayeva et al.,
076   2022). The seminal work of Vinyals et al. (2015) using pointer networks paved the way to apply
077   these techniques to CO problems, where they now routinely find near-optimal solutions for VRPs
078   through further developments by Bello et al. (2016) and Nazari et al. (2018). Subsequent inno-
079   vations, including the transformer-based encoder with self-attention of Kool et al. (2019), POMO
080   (Kwon et al., 2020) and Sym-NCO (Kim et al., 2022), have significantly enhanced solution genera-
081   tion and improvement strategies for VRPs. These advancements have been complemented by novel
082   training algorithms, including learning with (partial) problem re-encoding at each step (Bdeir et al.,
083   2022; Drakulic et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024a;b) and population-based approaches (Grinsztajn et al.,
084
      2024; Hottung et al., 2024; Chalumeau et al., 2024).
085   Despite this progress, challenges remain in the form of requiring manual tuning for inductive bias,
086   the need for problem-specific models, and lack of generalization, which impact deployment and gen-
087   eralizability (Liu et al., 2023; Thyssens et al., 2023). The field has also explored non-autoregressive
088   solution construction methods that allow for better generalization, such as predicting promising
089   edges (Joshi et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2021; Kool et al., 2022; Sun & Yang, 2024), improvement meth-
090
      ods iteratively refining solutions through local adjustments or sequential rewriting (Hottung & Tier-
      ney, 2019; Ma et al., 2021; 2022; 2024), and test-time adaptation methods (Hottung et al., 2021;
091
      Choo et al., 2022) which allow for solution improvement given larger time budgets. Recent works
092
      additionally explore alternative ways of solving VRPs, such as learning heuristics for Ant Colony
093   Optimization (Ye et al., 2024b; Kim et al., 2024) and divide-and-conquer methods (Kim et al., 2021;
094   Li et al., 2021; Hou et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2024c; Chen et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024).
095
096
097   Multi-task learning for VRPs In this work, we develop a unified VRP solver that can be gen-
098   eralized to any number of VRP variants. This issue of generalization has garnered much attention
099
      recently. Wang & Yu (2023) introduces a multi-armed bandit method that solves several VRP vari-
      ants with limited training budgets. Lin et al. (2024) proposes training a backbone model (i.e., deep
100
      layers) for VRPs that can then be adapted via low-dimensional layers such as linear projections to
101
      fine-tune different problems efficiently. Drakulic et al. (2024) propose a multi-task model for CO
102   problems trained via supervised learning, akin to LLMs. Jiang et al. (2024a) introduce UNCO, a
103   method to transfer different problems to the embedding space via textual description through an
104   LLM; however, UNCO still falls short in terms of performance compared to state-of-the-art NCO
105   methods. Most related to this work are the works of Liu et al. (2024a) and Zhou et al. (2024), which
106   use attribute composition (Ruis et al., 2021) to achieve (zero-shot) generalization on several VRP
107   variants. Liu et al. (2024a) builds on the Reinforcement-Learning-based POMO (Kwon et al., 2020),
      on top of which Zhou et al. (2024) employ a mixture-of-experts model to improve generalization.

      N ODE ATTRIBUTES
142   Demand and Vehicle Capacity (C) [q ∈ [0, Q]]: Every customer i ∈ Nc has a linehaul demand
143   qi that needs to be served using vehicles with a homogeneous fixed capacity Q > 0. The total
144   customer demand in the vehicle must not exceed its capacity at any point of the route.
145
146   Backhauls (B) [p ∈ [0, Q]]: Backhauls generalize demand to also account for return shipments.
147   Customers are either linehaul or backhaul customers. Linehaul customers require delivery of a
148   demand qi that needs to be transported from the depot to customer i (as in the CVRP), whereas
149   backhaul customers need a pickup of an amount pi that is transported from the client back to the
150
      depot. It is possible for vehicles to serve a combination of linehaul and backhaul customers in a
      single route, but then any linehaul customers must precede the backhaul customers in the route. An
151
      application with returnable bottles is presented in Ropke & Pisinger (2006): full bottles need to be
152
      delivered from the depot to customers, while empty bottles are returned to the depot via backhaul.
153
154   Time Windows (TW) [e, s, l ∈ [0, T ]3 ]: Every customer i ∈ Nc has a time window [ei , li ] during
155   which service must begin. Service takes si time. The depot has a time window [e0 , l0 ] = [0, T ], and
156   a service duration of s0 = 0. Vehicles must reach node i before the end of its time window at li , but
157   any early arrivals must wait at the node location until time ei before service may start.
158
159   Multi-depot (MD) [m > 1]: Generalizes single-depot (m = 1) variants as CVRP with multiple
160   starting nodes m > 1 from which vehicles can their start their tour. Each vehicle must return to its
161   start depot. This variant requires decisions about depot-customer assignments, making the problem
      more realistic for organizations operating from multiple facilities (Karakatič & Podgorelec, 2015).

182   each action determines the next step in the solution based on the current partial sequence. This is
183
      achieved using a decoder gθ . The encoding and decoding process can be formalized as follows:

      4.1   U NIFIED VRP E NVIRONMENT
209
210   In previous works proposing multi-task learning across VRP variants, like MTPOMO (Liu et al.,
211   2024a) and MVMoE (Zhou et al., 2024), the training scheme samples an instance variant (CVRP,
212   VRPTW, etc.) out of the set of available variants during training. Every instance within that batch,
213   therefore, is of the same problem category. This can, however, bias the optimization at each gradient
214   step toward a specific task, potentially hindering stable and effective training for a foundation model.
215   We thus propose to learn across problems throughout training and include problem instances of
      various attributes within each training batch.

271
272   Global attributes as outlined in Section 3.1 are essential for modeling VRPs; for instance, given
273   an open (O) attribute, the solver may find optimal routes that do not necessarily loop back to the
274   starting depot. Previous multi-task learning models for VRPs (Liu et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2024)
275   project such features on the shallow decoder as dynamic features. However, such a design can be
276
      suboptimal since the deep transformer layers carry out most of the learning and, importantly, can
      enable effective attribute mixing, which is essential in understanding a (new) problem. We thus
277
      design Global Attribute Embeddings for effective problem representation, which incorporate prob-
278
      lem variants and help the deep layers understand which problem is being faced. Global attributes

348   4.4   E FFICIENT A DAPTER L AYERS : F INETUNING TO U NSEEN ATTRIBUTES
349
350
      Previous multi-task learning works (Liu et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2024) train in an environment
351
      of single-attribute VRP variants and, using compositionality (Ruis et al., 2021), achieve promis-
352
      ing results on zero-shot generalization to VRP variants combining these individual attributes. In
353   ROUTE F INDER, we go a step further and investigate how to efficiently generalize our pre-trained
354   foundation model to variants with unseen attributes outside of the training set. Lin et al. (2024)
355   propose pretraining a backbone model, on top of which specific Adapter Layers (AL) can be ap-
356   plied for more efficient finetuning to new problems – with the rationale being that the backbone
357   (i.e., the encoder layers) may capture transferable knowledge. However, doing so excludes previ-
358   ous information accumulated in the projection layers from the raw attribute features to the hidden
359   space, complicating optimization. For instance, if the first two out of k dimensions encoded the Eu-
360   clidean locations of nodes as (x, y), re-initializing a new adapter layer from scratch will eliminate
361
      such transferable knowledge. Therefore, we propose Efficient Adapter Layers (EAL), an effective
      approach to learning few-shots for VRP foundation models.
362
363   Consider a linear projection layer W ∈ Rk×d as the original weight matrix for the projection from
364   the raw attribute to latent space, where k is the number of attributes and d is the hidden dimension.
365   In this work, for simplicity, we consider unbiased linear projections to the latent space. This can be
366   readily extended to general affine projections using a bias term. To accommodate l new attributes,
367   EAL augments W with zeros. The new matrix W′ ∈ R(k+l)×d can be written as:

      5         E XPERIMENTS
379
380   In this section, we empirically demonstrate the state-of-the-art performance of ROUTE F INDER in
381   extensive experiments1 . We address the following research questions:
382
383    (RQ1) Does ROUTE F INDER outperform state-of-the-art foundation models for routing problems
384          on many different VRP variants?
385    (RQ2) How do the novel components of ROUTE F INDER contribute to its performance?
386    (RQ3) Is the proposed EAL effective in ROUTE F INDER finetuning to unseen VRP variants?
387
388   Hardware All training runs are conducted on NVIDIA A100 GPUs and take between 9 to 48
389   hours per model. Evaluation runs are conducted on an AMD Ryzen Threadripper 3960X 24-core
390   CPU with a single RTX 3090 GPU.
391
392   Baselines Traditional solvers: We use PyVRP (Wouda et al., 2024), an open-source, state-of-
393   the-art heuristic VRP solver built on top of HGS-CVRP (Vidal, 2022). PyVRP can solve all VRP
394   variants considered in this study. We also use Google’s OR-Tools (Perron & Furnon, 2023), an
395
      open-source exact and heuristic solver that relies on constraint programming and is commonly used
      in the ML community for its versatility to solve a large number of VRP variants. We use OR-Tools’
396
      guided local search procedure in this work. Both baseline methods solve each instance on a single
397
      CPU core with a time limit of 10 and 20 seconds for instances with 50 and 100 nodes, respectively.
398   We parallelize traditional solvers across 16 CPU cores as in Kool et al. (2019); Zhou et al. (2024).
399
400   Neural solvers: We consider recent multi-task learning baselines for the VRP, including the recent
401
      MTPOMO (Liu et al., 2024a), which is based on POMO (Kwon et al., 2020), and MVMoE (Zhou
      et al., 2024), which introduces mixture-of-experts (Fedus et al., 2022) to improve the model per-
402
      formance. ROUTE F INDER variants, denoted as RF in the tables, are trained with all components
403
      proposed in the methodology section. We use Reward Normalization with division through the ex-
404   ponentially smoothed mean with α = 0.25. We consider three versions of ROUTE F INDER: one
405   version considering the (MT)POMO encoder (RF-POMO), one with the MVMoE model with four
406   experts and hierarchical gating (RF-MoE), and one with our modern Transformer-based Encoder
407   (RF-TE). Further details are available in Appendix B.
408
409   Training We follow the setup in Kwon et al. (2020) and the recent works on MTPOMO (Liu et al.,
410   2024a) and MVMoE (Zhou et al., 2024). Each model is trained for 300 epochs, each containing
411   100, 000 instances generated on the fly. We use the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) with a
412   learning rate of 3×10−4 and batch size of 256. At epochs 270 and 295, the learning rate is multiplied
413
      by 0.1. Note that our setup differs from the one in Liu et al. (2024a) and Zhou et al. (2024) in that
      we do not artificially restrict the variants with single attributes (such as only (B) or (TW)), but train
414
      on all available data – similarly to how LLMs are trained on all available data, which is readily
415
      available through our unified VRP environment (more details in Appendix A).
416
417
      Evaluation For all ML approaches, we roll out greedy solutions using multi-starts and 8× sym-
418   metric dihedral augmentations of Kwon et al. (2020), resulting in n × 8 solutions per instance.

      6      C ONCLUSION
525
      In this work, we presented ROUTE F INDER, a comprehensive framework to develop foundation mod-
526
      els for VRPs. We introduced a unified VRP environment to represent any combination of attributes.
527
      We proposed a new Transformer Encoder and Global Attribute Embeddings to enhance learning
528   representations of diverse VRPs. We introduced Mixed Batch Training and Multi-variant Reward
529   Normalization to allow for effective training with RL in a multi-task setting with different tasks
530   and reward scales. Finally, we introduced Efficient Adapter Layers, a lightweight and powerful
531   technique to finetune ROUTE F INDER to unseen attributes. Our extensive evaluations on 24 VRP
532   variants showed ROUTE F INDER outperforms SOTA neural baselines for VRPs.
533
      ROUTE F INDER represents an early attempt to learn a foundation model across problem variants.
534   While demonstrating strong generalization, it does so at a slight expense in solution quality com-
535   pared to techniques trained on specific problem variants, at least for in-distribution results, as also
536   noted by prior works (Liu et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2024). For future work, we intend to extend
537   ROUTE F INDER to support further variants of the vast VRP literature. We also intend to improve
538   the model performance to eventually outperform state-of-the-art traditional OR solvers – exciting
539   directions include decomposition methods (Ye et al., 2024c; Zheng et al., 2024) and end-to-end
      construction and improvement (Kong et al., 2024).

828   A.1    DATA GENERATION
829
830   We now explain the individual steps in the data generation process we use for our modular VRP
831   environment, including the node attributes and global attributes. While throughout the main part of
832   this paper, we have focused on routing problems with a single depot, our unified environment can
833   actually handle problems with multiple depots, where we define m as the number of depots. For
834   comparability to the neural baselines, the main experiments were run on single-depot problems, but
835
      we report results for multi-depot problems (Appendix D.3).
836
837   Locations We generate m + n locations randomly with xi and yi ∼ U (0, 1), ∀i ∈ {0, ..., m + n −
838
      1}, where [xi , yi ], i ∈ {0, ..., m − 1} denote the m depots and [xi , yi ], i ∈ {m, ..., m + n − 1}, the
      n customer nodes. Note that this setting can be expanded to consider more realistic distributions as
839

960   Distance limit (L) The distance limit is sampled from a uniform distribution to ensure meaningful
961   and feasible constraints. Specifically, we sample L from U (2 · max(d0i ), lmax )), where d0i is the
962   distance from the depot to customer i, and lmax = 3.0 is a predefined upper bound. This approach
963
      ensures that L is always greater than the round trip to the farthest customer (2 · max(d0i )), making
      all customers reachable, while also allowing for variation in the constraint tightness. For the multi-
964
      depot case we replace max(d0i ) with minj (maxi (dij )), i ∈ {m, ..., m + n}, j ∈ {0, ..., m}, i.e.,
965
      we first get the maximum distance from any customer node to each of the depots and then take
966   the minimum out of those distances. By taking the maximum in the first step we ensure that all
967   customers are reachable, and by taking the minimum across depots, we make the problem more
968   challenging, because even though all nodes can in principle be serviced, some may only be serviced
969   by one (or a subset) of the available depots. This sampling method produces more variation than
970   previous works Liu et al. (2024a); Zhou et al. (2024) (where there was virtually no difference in
971   solutions of (L) and non-(L) variants), as it guarantees feasible instances while still providing a
      range of challenging scenarios.

979
980    To determine available actions for the Unified VRP environment formulation, the constraints for the
981    individual problems have to be combined in the action mask (action_mask in the code following
982    RL4CO, where True means that the action is feasible (Berto et al., 2024)). We build a logical
983
       test structure, essentially separating the checks in the action mask according to the individual VRP
       problem types and then bringing them all together again. The individual action_mask checks are
984
       the following:
985
986
       a) Can reach in time: depending on the current time and the travel distance to every node not yet
987       visited, can we reach that node before its service time window ends? tcurr + dij < lj , where tcurr
988       is the current time.
989
       b) Does not exceed distance limit: depending on the current length of the route, if we travel to any
990
          available node, will we exceed the total distance limit for the route? lcurr + dij < L, where lcurr
991       is the current length.
992
       c) Can reach depot: there are two types of constraints from time windows (TW) and distance limit
993
          (L):
994
995          • If we need to ensure we can reach the depot in time, i.e., the current time plus traveling time
996
               to the depot must be smaller than the system end time: max(tcurr + dij , ej ) + sj + dj0 <
               tmax .
997
998          • If we need to ensure we can reach the depot without exceeding the distance limit, i.e., the
999
               current distance plus the traveling distance to the depot must be smaller than the distance
               limit: lcurr + dij + dj0 < L.
1000
1001      For the multi-depot case we replace dj0 in both these constraints with djk , where k ∈ {0, ..., m −
1002      1} indexes the depot the current route started from. For open routes, this will always be set to
1003
          True, i.e., this constraint does not apply.
1004   d) Demand constraints for backhaul problems:
1005         • Checks for all backhauls problems:
1006            – Does the linehaul demand exceed vehicle capacity if we add a node’s demand to the
1007                current vehicle? ccurr + qj < C, where ccurr is the used capacity.
1008            – Does the backhaul demand exceed vehicle capacity if we add a node’s demand to the
1009                current vehicle? ccurr + pj < C, where ccurr is the used capacity.
1010         • Checks for traditional backhaul settings:
1011            – Carrying backhaul: if we are already picking up backhaul demands, we cannot service
1012                any linehaul demands on this route anymore.
1013            – If we are not carrying backhaul demands yet, are there any unserved linehaul demands
1014                left?
1015            – If there are no linehaul demands left or we are already carrying backhauls, are there still
1016                unserved backhaul demands?
1017         • Checks for mixed backhaul settings:
1018            – Cannot service linehaul demands: depending on the backhaul demands currently loaded
1019                in the vehicle, do we have space left for further linehaul demands?
1020           We additionally remark that our definition of backhauls follows the generally accepted def-
1021           inition in the OR community, originally due to Goetschalckx & Jacobs-Blecha (1989). This
1022           definition differs from the routing problems with backhaul considered in several recent pa-
1023           pers in the machine learning (e.g., Liu et al. (2024a); Zhou et al. (2024)), who define back-
1024           haul customers as having a negative demand of the same commodity used for linehaul, and
1025           do not consider the precedence constraint that all linehaul must be completed before back-
               haul may start on the route. The problem setting with a single commodity is not commonly


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
               studied in the OR literature since it implies pickups may be used for deliveries at later cus-
1027           tomers, while the relaxation of the precedence constraint is more properly referred to as a
1028           mixed backhaul problem (Koç & Laporte, 2018).
1029
       e) Already visited: every customer node needs to be visited exactly once.
1030
1031
       We bring together checks a) to e) and introduce an additional check for the depot: if we are currently
1032   in the depot and there are still unserved customers, we cannot select the depot as the next action to
1033   ensure the model cannot get stuck during decoding. For the multi-depot case we further extend this
1034   check. If we are currently in a depot and there are unserved customers, we cannot visit any depot.
1035   If no further customers can be serviced, all depots are available actions again. However, if we are
1036   currently in a depot and no customers can be served from this depot, we mask it out so as to service
1037   the remaining customers from the remaining depots that can actually service them.
1038   Combining these checks in this way allows us to meticulously check for individual VRP settings
1039   while at the same time maintaining the necessary flexibility the unified environment formulation
1040   requires.

1170   Wkg h, Wvg h, Wvp h are precomputed once as cache. We note that Eq. (18) is usually referred to as
1171   the pointer mechanism (Vinyals et al., 2015).
1172
1173   Logits processing Finally, logits z are transformed into a probability distribution:
1174                                         p = Softmax (C · tanh(z))                                   (19)
1175   where logits for infeasible actions can be masked, and C is the tanh clipping that serves in improving
1176   the exploration, which we set to 10 according to Bello et al. (2016).
1177
1178   Action selection During training, we use the POMO multistart sampling. For the multi-
1179   depot case we force the first action to start from all depots in the instance. For the single-depot
1180   case we force the first action to start with every customer node to maximize diversity. Note that if
1181   num_starts is not divisible by the number of depots m, the resulting tensor will not have an equal
1182   number of indices for each depot, i.e., the number of starts will not be distributed evenly across the
1183   depots, as we use the modulo operator for the assignment.
1184   During testing, we also employ multistart but with greedy selection (i.e., selecting the maxi-
1185   mum probability). Prior to the selection, a dihedral augmentation is also performed prior to encoding
1186   instance x in the encoder, which enables exploring 8× as many solutions with 4 rotations × 2 flips.
1187   We note that additional augmentations and techniques can be performed during inference, which
       can further boost evaluation performance (Kim et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Choo et al., 2022; Luo

       C.3   A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSION
1297
1298
1299   Motivation Foundation models have been successful in several areas in recent years, including
1300   large language models (Achiam et al., 2023), computer vision (Kirillov et al., 2023) as well as other
1301   domains such as biology (Abramson et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024). However, foundation models
1302   for discrete decision-making, such as CO and our target VRPs, are still under-explored as an area
1303   - one reason being the lack of large, high-quality open datasets that can effectively be employed to
1304   train such models - which motivates our use of RL. Such foundation models may not only obtain
1305
       solutions faster than traditional OR counterparts but also avoid the requirement of possibly decades
       of research and resources to tackle a single task, while a foundation model may automatically learn
1306
       heuristics without supervision.
1307
1308
       Generalist, or specialized? Another open question is the idea of generality behind the model. In
1309   ROUTE F INDER, we argue that a model might not need to be extremely complex and be specialized
1310   for a specific application (such as routing). One such reason is that with larger model capabilities
1311   comes larger size and inference time, which is crucial for real-world deployment. An interesting
1312   future direction would be to attempt to generalize a model as a "foundation model for CO", for
1313   instance, based on a general formulation (Boisvert et al., 2024), and see whether the additional
1314   training and inference costs are worth a (possible) boost in optimality gaps and generalization ability.
1315   Such a model may be able to attain a better few-shot generalization to totally unseen attributes, either
1316   with adapter layers (Lin et al., 2024) or with our proposed EAL. However, we believe that tailored,
1317
       specialized foundation models as ROUTE F INDER for VRPs may be more practical and efficient. We
       note that an orthogonal direction to ours is the use of LLMs as hyper-heuristics (Romera-Paredes
1318
       et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Ye et al., 2024a), which starts from a generalist LLM agent to generate
1319
       algorithms that can be used to improve the optimization of CO problems as VRPs. However, such
1320   models are not used at inference time due to the inefficiency of using billions of parameters that are
1321   not tailored for the problem at hand.
1322
1323   Going forward in specialized foundation models for VRPs, there are several challenges yet to be
1324   addressed. One such challenge is the still sub-par performance compared to state-of-the-art solvers
1325   (Wouda & Lan, 2023; Wouda et al., 2024), which may be offset on a larger scale by several means,
1326   including decompositions. Another way to attain better performance would be to integrate with local
1327   search (Ye et al., 2024b; Kim et al., 2024) and hybridize constructive (the current policy paradigm)
1328   with improvement methods (Ma et al., 2021; 2024) to guarantee monotonic improvements given
1329
       larger time budgets. Finally, given the robust cross-task performance even compared to single-task
       models, we believe expanding to more VRP variants (and their attribute distributions) may further
1330
       improve overall performance.
