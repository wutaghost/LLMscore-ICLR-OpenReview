009                                                   Abstract
010
011              Reasoning encompasses two typical types: deductive reasoning and inductive
012              reasoning. Despite extensive research into the reasoning capabilities of Large
013              Language Models (LLMs), most studies have failed to rigorously differentiate
014              between inductive and deductive reasoning, leading to a blending of the two. This
015              raises an essential question: In LLM reasoning, which poses a greater challenge -
016              deductive or inductive reasoning? While the deductive reasoning capabilities of
017              LLMs, (i.e. their capacity to follow instructions in reasoning tasks), have received
018
                 considerable attention, their abilities in true inductive reasoning remain largely
                 unexplored due to the inseparability of the two types of reasoning in most of the tasks.
019
                 To investigate the true inductive reasoning capabilities of LLMs, we propose a novel
020
                 framework, SolverLearner. This framework enables LLMs to learn the underlying
021              function (i.e., 𝑦 = 𝑓𝑤 (𝑥)), that maps input data points (𝑥) to their corresponding
022              output values (𝑦), using only in-context examples. By focusing on inductive
023              reasoning and separating it from LLM-based deductive reasoning, we can isolate
024              and investigate inductive reasoning of LLMs in its pure form via SolverLearner.
025              Our observations reveal that LLMs demonstrate remarkable inductive reasoning
026              capabilities through SolverLearner, achieving near-perfect performance with ACC
027              of 1 in most cases. Surprisingly, despite their strong inductive reasoning abilities,
028              LLMs tend to relatively lack deductive reasoning capabilities, particularly in tasks
029
                 involving “counterfactual” reasoning.

      1     Introduction
033
      Recent years have witnessed notable progress in Natural Language Processing (NLP) with the devel-
034
      opment of Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) and ChatGPT (OpenAI,
035
      2023). While these models exhibit impressive reasoning abilities across various tasks, they face
036   challenges in certain domains. For example, a recent study (Wu et al., 2023) has shown that while
037   LLMs excel in conventional tasks (e.g., base-10 arithmetic), they often experience a notable decline
038   in accuracy when dealing “counterfactual” reasoning tasks that deviate from the conventional cases
039   seen during pre-training (e.g., base-9 arithmetic). It remains unclear whether they are capable of
040   fundamental reasoning, or just approximate retrieval.
041
      In light of this, our paper seeks to investigate the reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Reasoning can
042   encompasses two types: deductive reasoning and inductive reasoning, as depicted in Fig. 1. Deductive
043   reasoning starts with a general hypothesis and proceeds to derive specific conclusions about individual
044   instances while inductive reasoning involves formulating broad generalizations or principles from
045   a set of instance observations. Despite extensive research into the reasoning capabilities of LLMs,
046   most studies have not clearly differentiated between inductive and deductive reasoning. For instance,
047   arithmetic reasoning task primarily focuses on comprehending and applying mathematical concepts to
048   solve arithmetic problems, aligning more with deductive reasoning. Yet, when employing in-context
049   learning for arithmetic reasoning tasks, where the model is prompted with a few 〈input, output〉
050
      examples, the observed improvements are often attributed to their inductive reasoning capacity. This
      fusion of reasoning types poses a critical question: Which is the more significant limitation in
051
      LLM reasoning, deductive or inductive reasoning?
052
053   To explore this question, it’s crucial to differentiate between deductive and inductive reasoning.
      Current methods that investigate deductive and inductive reasoning often rely on disparate datasets,

107   functions for inference. Specifically, functions are applied through external interpreters, such as code

      We evaluate the performance of several LLMs across various tasks. LLMs consistently demonstrate re-
109   markable inductive reasoning capabilities through SolverLearner, achieving near-perfect performance
110   with ACC of 1 in most cases. Surprisingly, despite their strong inductive reasoning abilities, LLMs
111   tend to exhibit weaker deductive capabilities, particularly in terms of “counterfactual” reasoning. This
112   finding, though unexpected, aligns with the previous research. Numerous studies indicate that LLMs
113   lack robust deductive capabilities; for instance, they often struggle with numerical computations. In
114   a zero-shot scenario, the ability of an LLM to correctly execute tasks by applying principles (i.e.
115   deductive reasoning) heavily relies on the frequency with which the model was exposed to the tasks
116   during its pre-training phase (Wu et al., 2023). Conversely, there is significant evidence highlighting
117
      the importance of inductive reasoning for LLMs, such as the effectiveness of few-shot in-context
      learning in enhancing their reasoning abilities. Our study systematically separate the sources of
118
      LLM reasoning capabilities and identify the bottlenecks they face. By gaining a more comprehensive
119
      understanding of how LLMs reason, we can better identify key areas for improvement. For instance,
120   when designing agent systems, we should leverage the strong inductive capabilities of LLMs while
121   delegating the deductive tasks to external tools.

      2    Task Definition
125
      Our research is focused on a relatively unexplored question: Which presents a greater challenge to
126
      LLMs - deductive reasoning or inductive reasoning? To explore this, we designed a set of comparative
127
      experiments that apply a uniform task across various contexts, each emphasizing either deductive
128   or inductive reasoning. The primary distinction between the deductive and inductive settings is
129   whether we explicitly present input-output mappings to the models. Informally, we can describe these
130   mappings as a function 𝑓𝑤 : 𝑋 → 𝑌 , where an input 𝑥 ∈ 𝑋 is transformed into an output 𝑦 ∈ 𝑌 . We
131   distinguish between the deductive and inductive settings as follows:
132
133           • Deductive setting: we provide the models with direct input-output mappings (i.e., 𝑓𝑤 ).
134           • Inductive setting: we offer the models a few examples (i.e., (𝑥, 𝑦) pairs) while intentionally
135             leaving out input-output mappings (i.e., 𝑓𝑤 ).
136
      For example, consider arithmetic tasks, where the base system is the input-output mapping function.
137
      The two approaches on the left side of Fig. 1 (i.e., method (a) and (b)) follow the deductive setting,
138
      illustrating the case where the arithmetic base is explicitly provided. In contrast, the two methods
139   (i.e., method (c) and (d)) on right side of Fig. 1 adhere to the inductive setting, depicting the scenario
140   characterized by the absence of a specified arithmetic base, while a few input-output examples are
141   provided for guidance.

      3.1   Framework
181
182   In this subsection, we will take the arithmetic task as a case study to demonstrate the entire process.
183
184
      Function Proposal: Given the in-context examples, the primary goal of LLMs is to learn a function
      that can map input data points (𝑥) to their corresponding output values (𝑦). This process of learning
185
      the mapping between inputs and outputs is akin to inductive reasoning, while employing the learned
186
      function to address unseen queries aligns with deductive reasoning. In order to separate inductive
187   reasoning from deductive reasoning, the execution of the learned function should be completely
188   detached from LLMs. To achieve this separation, external tools such as code interpreters serve as
189   efficient way to execute these functions independently. By encapsulating the learned function within
190   Python code, we can effectively detach the duty of deductive reasoning from LLMs, assigning it solely
191   to these external executors. For instance, in function proposal stage for an arithmetic task, we have:
192
      “You are an expert mathematician and programmer. You are asked to add two numbers, the base of
193    which is unknown. Below are some provided examples: The result for 76+76 is 174.
194    Please identify the underlying pattern to determine the base being used and implement a solver()
195    function to achieve the goal.
196    def solver(n1: str, n2: str) -> str:
197    # Let’s write a Python program step by step
198    # Each input is a number represented as a string.
199    # The function computes the sum of these numbers and returns it as a string. ”
200   Function Execution: In the second phase, functions are executed through external code interpreters
201   to solve the test cases for evaluation purposes. These code interpreters act as “oracle” deductive
202   reasoners, fully preventing the LLM from involving deductive reasoning. This ensures that the final
203   results reflect only the inductive reasoning capability of the LLM. To further decouple the LLM’s
204   influence in this phase, test cases are generated using a template without involving the LLM. More
205   details can be found in Appendix A.1.3.

207   4     Tasks
208
209
      In this section, we provide a brief overview of the tasks under consideration. Our focus is on
210   investigating the reasoning abilities of LLMs in both deductive and inductive reasoning scenarios. To
211   ensure a robust evaluation, we carefully select tasks that lend themselves well to comparison. Firstly,
212   to prevent LLMs from reciting tasks seen frequently during pre-training, which could artificially
213   inflate performance in deductive reasoning, a significant portion of the tasks falls into the category of
214   “counterfactual reasoning” tasks. Secondly, in the context of inductive reasoning, where only a few
215   in-context examples are available without the mapping function, our objective is to learn the function
      that maps inputs to outputs based on this restricted dataset. To achieve this, we choose tasks that are

      well-constrained, ensuring the existence of a single, unique function capable of fitting this limited
217   data. Detailed descriptions of each task and the prompts used can be found in Appendix A.1 and A.2.
218
219   Arithmetic In this study, we focus on the two-digit addition task previously explored in the work
220
      of (Wu et al., 2023). We investigate multiple numerical bases, specifically base-8, 9, 10, 11, and
      16 where base 10 corresponds to the commonly observed case during pretraining. In the context of
221
      deductive reasoning, the base is explicitly provided without any accompanying in-context examples,
222
      and the LLM is expected to perform the addition computation by relying on its inherent deductive
223   reasoning abilities. Conversely, in the context of inductive reasoning, instead of explicitly providing
224   the base information to LLMs, we provide LLMs solely with few-shot examples and require them
225   to induce the base through these examples and subsequently generate a function to solve arithmetic
226   problems.
227
      Basic Syntactic Reasoning In this setting, we concentrate on tasks related to syntactic recognition
228
      previously explored by (Wu et al., 2023). Our objective is to evaluate LLMs using artificially
229   constructed English sentences that vary from the conventional subject-verb-object (SVO) word order.
230   For deductive reasoning, we directly provide the new word order to LLMs without any contextual
231   examples, challenging them to identify the subject, verb, and object within this artificial language. In
232   contrast, for inductive reasoning, we do not give explicit instructions on the changes in word order.
233   Instead, we introduce sentence pairs where one sentence follows the standard word order, and the
234   other follows a modified sequence. Through this setting, LLMs are expected to learn the specific
235   changes made to the word order and then apply this learned rule to identify the subject, verb, and
236   object within new sentences.
237   Spatial Reasoning In this task, we investigate the spatial reasoning previously investigated by (Wu
238   et al., 2023). Our specific focus is on modifying the direction-unit vector mapping and determining the
239   object coordinates in this revised system. We explore multiple systems, starting with the commonly
240   observed case during pretraining, where up corresponds to north, down to south, left to west, and right
241   to east. This is compared to coordinate systems with swapped, rotated, and randomly permuted axes.
242   For deductive reasoning, we directly provide the direction-unit vector mapping without any contextual
243
      examples, requiring LLMs to compute the object coordinates within these systems. Conversely, in the
      context of inductive reasoning, instead of directly explaining the changes made to the direction-unit
244
      vector mapping to LLMs, we present LLMs with a few example shots and challenge them to infer the
245
      changes made to the mapping. They are then expected to apply this learned function to determine the
246   object coordinates in the system.
247
248   Cipher Decryption Under this scenario, we explore an innovative task that we have created,
249
      concentrating on the decryption of strings encrypted using specific cipher systems. We have
      incorporated three particular cipher systems for this exploration: the Alphabetically Sorting Cipher
250
      the Caesar Cipher and the Morse Cipher. For deductive reasoning, we directly inform LLMs about
251
      the cipher system being used, yet we do not offer any contextual examples. The objective for LLMs is
252   to decode strings according to these cipher systems. Conversely, in the inductive reasoning scenario,
253   our task involves providing LLMs with several examples, each consisting of an encrypted string and
254   its decrypted version. The main challenge for the models in this scenario is first to identify what
255   cipher system was used and then to apply that cipher system to decrypt an unseen string.

405   6            Related Works
406
407   6.1              In-Context Learning
408
409
      GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) has demonstrated its effectiveness in learning from a few demonstration
      examples and solve previously unseen tasks without requiring updates to its model parameters (Wei
410
      et al., 2022a). This remarkable capability is commonly referred to as the “in-context learning ability”
411
      of language models. It implies that the LLMs can leverage its existing knowledge and generalize from
412   a few demonstration examples to solve new, related tasks (Dong et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Rubin
413   et al., 2021; Gonen et al., 2022). Some notable works include chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei
414   et al., 2022b), which elicits reasoning with intermediate steps in few-shot exemplars. Built upon
415   the CoT framework, several works expand CoT by organizing and processing thoughts using more
416   complex structures, such as trees (Yao et al., 2023) and graphs (Besta et al., 2023) or breaking a
417   problem into sub problems and then proceeds to solve each one independently (Zhou et al., 2022).
418   While these studies have effectively improved the reasoning capability of LLMs, they have failed to
419   clearly distinguish between inductive and deductive reasoning, let alone investigate which represents
420
      a more critical limitation for LLM reasoning capabilities: deductive reasoning or inductive reasoning.
421
422   6.2              Exploring LLMs’ Reasoning Skills
423
      Despite the impressive achievements of LLMs in various reasoning tasks, the underlying mechanisms
424
      of their reasoning capabilities remain a subject of debate. The question of whether LLMs genuinely
425
      reason in a manner akin to human cognitive processes or merely simulate aspects of reasoning
426   without true comprehension is still open (Huang & Chang, 2022). For instance, Kojima et al. have
427   suggested that LLMs exhibit commendable zero-shot reasoning abilities, implying that these models
428   can draw logical conclusions in scenarios they have not been explicitly trained on (Kojima et al., 2022).
429   However, some researchers cast doubt on the reasoning capability of LLMs. While approaches like
430   the chain-of-thought method may mimic human-like thought processes, it remains uncertain whether
431   LLMs are genuinely engaging in reasoning or simply following patterns learned during training (Wei
      et al., 2022b; Valmeekam et al., 2022). Additionally, there’s a debate regarding whether LLMs are


                                                                                                            8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      symbolic reasoners (Tang et al., 2023) or possess strong abstract reasoning capabilities (Gendron
433   et al., 2023). In light of these seemingly contradictory conclusions, our research aims to investigate
434   deeper into the reasoning capabilities of LLMs. We intend to dissect the nuances of inductive and
435   deductive reasoning within the context of LLMs, identifying which form of reasoning presents a more
436   significant challenge to their reasoning abilities.
437
438   6.3   Equipping LLMs with External Tools
439
440   Large Language Models (LLMs) have made significant progress in utilizing tools through frameworks
441   like Program of Thoughts (PoT) (Chen et al., 2022), CREATOR (Qian et al., 2023) and LATM (Cai
442   et al., 2023), which allow LLMs to create tools using documentation and code. Logic-LM (Pan
443
      et al., 2023) integrates LLMs with symbolic solvers to improve logical problem-solving, However,
      these approaches focus exclusively on deductive reasoning, aiming to enable LLMs to derive correct
444
      answers for specific questions without incorporating the capacity for inductive reasoning to infer
445
      underlying mapping function shared by few-shot examples. In contrast, our primary objective is not
446   to propose a new framework for using tools to enhance the problem-solving capabilities of LLMs.
447   Instead, we aim to differentiate between deductive and inductive reasoning within LLMs and explore
448   which presents a greater challenge to their reasoning abilities.
449
450
451
      7     Limitations
452
      LLMs cannot perform inductive reasoning over all the tasks In our inductive learning setting,
453
      LLMs are provided with only a limited number of contextual examples. The goal is to infer the
454
      function that accurately maps inputs to outputs based solely on this constrained dataset. In order to
455   solve this problem, it is significant that we can find a unique function satisfied given these examples.
456   For instance, a linear function can be precisely determined given just two data points, as it has
457   a singular solution. However, attempting to deduce a quadratic curve from two points poses an
458   insurmountable challenge due to the existence of infinite functions capable of passing through those
459   specific points. Additionally, LLMs might struggle to discern the correct mapping function when
460   the search space of the problem expands excessively. Consider the case of arithmetic tasks; without
461   limiting the search space to finding a suitable base that aligns with the observations, the task becomes
462   overwhelmingly complex. This is because the search space could encompass any conceivable rule
463
      that accommodates the observations.
464   The effectiveness of LLMs’ inductive reasoning capability is heavily reliant on the foundational
465   model While GPT-4 consistently showcase impressive inductive reasoning abilities through Solver-
466   Learner and achieve perfect performance with ACC of 1 across all the tasks, GPT-3.5 struggle to
467   learn the correct input-output mapping function in several cases. This observation suggests that the
468   inductive reasoning potential of LLMs is significantly constrained by the underlying model.
469   Chain of Thought (COT) has not been incorporated into the comparison Chain of Thought
470   (COT) (Wei et al., 2022b) is a significant prompting technique designed for use with LLMs. Rather
471   than providing a direct answer, COT elicits reasoning with intermediate steps in few-shot exemplars.
472   This method was not incorporated into our comparison as it is viewed as a technique to improve the
473   deductive reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Although COT has proven to be effective across various
474   tasks, numerous studies highlight a significant performance gap that COT still needs to bridge to
475
      achieve flawless execution.
476
477   8     Conclusion
478
479   This study aims to explore a less-investigated aspect of LLMs: within LLM reasoning, which presents
480   a greater challenge — deductive or inductive reasoning? To investigate the inductive reasoning
481   capacities of LLMs, we introduce a novel framework called SolverLearner. By concentrating on
482   inductive reasoning while setting aside LLM-based deductive reasoning, SolverLearner can scrutinize
483
      the pure form of inductive reasoning in LLMs. Our findings unveil remarkable inductive reasoning
      prowers in LLMs through SolverLearner, achieving near-perfect performance with an ACC of 1 in
484
      most cases. Surprisingly, despite their strong inductive reasoning abilities, LLMs often exhibit weaker
485
      deductive capabilities, particularly in tasks involving “counterfactual” scenarios.

597   A.1     Full Setups
598
599   SolverLearner is a prompting based reasoning approach, and we only need to perform inference with

602   A.1.1    Settings for Each Task
603
604   Arithmetic The arithmetic dataset introduced in Wu et al.’s paper (Wu et al., 2023) comprises 1,000
605   randomly selected addition expressions, each involving two-digit numbers. These expressions are
606   drawn from bases 8, 9, 10, 11, and 16, with separate sampling for each base. Importantly, all the
607   expressions have been carefully chosen to yield distinct results when evaluated in their respective
608   bases, thereby distinguishing them from one another during the process of rule learning.
609   Basic Syntactic Reasoning In accordance with the methodology outlined in Wu et al.’s work (Wu
610   et al., 2023), we have generated a set of 100 simple three-word sentences (e.g., “bob likes bananas”)
611   with five different word order variations (e.g., “bananas bob likes” in OSV format). Subsequently, we
612   tasked LLMs with learning how to manipulate sentence order. It’s noteworthy that we took great care
613   in selecting words to ensure that each word in a sentence can only fulfill one specific role, such as
614   subject, object, or verb. For instance, we ensured that sentences like “bob likes anna” were excluded,
615   as both “bob” and “anna” could potentially serve as both subjects and objects, violating this constraint.
616   Spatial Reasoning The spatial reasoning dataset introduced in Wu et al.’s paper (Wu et al., 2023)
617   consists of 100 rooms that were randomly selected, and each room contains three distinct objects.
618   The spatial directions within these rooms are represented using unit vectors. For instance, north is
619   represented as (0, 1), south as (0, -1), east as (1, 0), and west as (-1, 0), with a y-axis pointing upward
620   serving as the default orientation. In our study, we have modified the mapping between directions
621   and unit vectors and tasked LLMs with learning this new direction-to-unit vector relationship. We
622   explore two direction-swapped scenarios (north-south and east-west), three rotated scenarios (by 90°,
623
      180°, and 270°), and a randomly permuted scenario. The primary metric we report is instance-level
      accuracy, which necessitates that all three objects within a room must be correctly positioned in order
624
      to be considered accurate.
625
626   Cipher Decryption We’ve generated a collection of 100 pairs of strings (e.g., “Mrxuqhb -> Journey”
627   for Caesar Cipher) for each of three cipher systems, including the Alphabetically Sorting Cipher the
628   Caesar Cipher and the Morse Cipher. Each pair comprises an encrypted string (e.g., “Mrxuqhb”) and
629
      its corresponding decrypted version (e.g., “Journey”). By providing LLMs with several examples,
      each containing an encrypted string alongside its corresponding decrypted counterpart, the primary
630
      task is to accurately determine the cipher system employed in an open-world context.
631
632
633   A.1.2    Few shot Example Generation
634
635   The preparation of examples for few-shot learning follows a straightforward process. We divide all the
636   data into a training set and a test set, from which few-shot examples are extracted from the training set.
637   These few-shot examples are automatically prepared by associating queries with their corresponding
638   ground truth answers using a pre-defined template.
639
640
      A.1.3    Test Case Generation
641
642   In the function execution phase, the test cases are generated using a template without involving LLM.
643   In particular, the test cases are drawn from the test data files, containing all the queries along with
644   their correct answers (e.g., “76+76 = 174”). When the LLM is used for generating code, we specify
645   a function interface, such as def solver(n1: str, n2: str) -> str. Then, using the query examples
646   provided, like “76+76 = 174”, we create test cases by applying this function interface to the query
647   (e.g., solver(76,76)), thereby eliminating any reliance on LLM for this process. This method ensures
      that our test case generation is 100% correct.

691   A.5    Ablation studies
692
693   LLMs struggle as executors when applying learned functions. To better demonstrate the deductive
694   capacity of LLM, we present both GPT-3.5 and Python with identical code and task them with
695
      applying the code to deduce the same set of queries. As shown in Table 13, while the Python
      interpreter can be considered an oracle, delivering flawless performance, it proves challenging for
696
      LLMs to accurately execute the code.
697
698   LLMs can learn the function with very few examples when the inductive reasoning problem is
699   well defined. To examine the impact of the number of few-shot examples on the inductive reasoning
700   capability of LLMs, we vary the number of in-context examples within [1,2,4,8,16] and assess
701
      performance on the spatial reasoning task using GPT-3.5 as presented in Table 14. We observe that
      even with very few examples, GPT-3.5 can still learn the mapping function if it is learnable.
