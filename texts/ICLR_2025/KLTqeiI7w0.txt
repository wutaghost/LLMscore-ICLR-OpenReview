009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Performing language-conditioned bimanual manipulation tasks is of great impor-
012            tance for many applications ranging from household service to industrial assem-
013            bly. However, teleoperating dual-arm demonstrations is expensive due to the high-
014            dimensional action space, which poses challenges for conventional methods to
015            handle general bimanual manipulation tasks. In contrast, single-arm policy has
016
               recently demonstrated impressive generalizability across a wide range of tasks be-
               cause of scaled model parameters and training data, which can provide sharable
017
               manipulation knowledge for dual-arm systems. To this end, we propose a plug-
018
               and-play method named AnyBimanual, which transfers pretrained single-arm
019            policy to multi-task bimanual manipulation policy with limited bimanual demon-
020            strations. Specifically, we first introduce a skill manager to dynamically schedule
021            the discovered skill primitives from pretrained single-arm policy for bimanual ma-
022            nipulation tasks, which combines skill primitives with embodiment-specific com-
023            pensation. To mitigate the observation discrepancy between single-arm and dual-
024            arm systems, we present a voxel editor to generate spatial soft masks for visual
025            embedding of the workspace, which aims to align visual input of single-arm pol-
026            icy model for each arm with those during pretraining stage. Extensive results on
027
               13 simulated and real-world tasks indicate the superiority of AnyBimanual with
               an improvement of 12.67% on average success rate compared with previous state-
028
               of-the-art methods.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Dual-arm systems play an important role in robotic manipulation due to the high capacity of com-
034   pleting diverse tasks in household service (Zhang et al., 2024), robotic surgery (Hu et al., 2023) ,
035   and component assembly in factories (Buhl et al., 2019). Compared to single-arm systems, dual-arm
036   systems enlarge the workspace and are able to handle more complex manipulation tasks by stabiliz-
037   ing the target with one arm and interact with that using another arm (Grannen et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
038   2024). Even for the tasks that single-arm policies can handle, dual-arm systems are often more
039
      efficient because multiple action steps can be simultaneously accomplished (Grotz et al., 2024).
      Since modern robotic applications require the robot to interact with different tasks and objects, it is
040
      desirable to design a generalizable policy model for bimanual manipulation.
041
042   To enhance the generalization ability of the manipulation agent, prior works present to leverage the
043   high-level reasoning and semantic understanding capabilities of foundation models like Large Lan-
044   guage Models (LLMs) and Vision Language Models (VLMs) to breakdown tasks into executable
045
      sub-tasks that can be solved by external low-level controllers (Huang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024b;
      Liu et al., 2023; Joublin et al., 2023; Gbagbe et al., 2024), which thus struggles with contact-rich
046
      tasks that requires complex and precised low-level motion. To generalize to contact-rich tasks, re-
047
      cent methods (Kim et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024; Ahn & et al., 2024) tend to learn robotic founda-
048   tion models directly from large-scale teleoperation data (Khazatsky & et al., 2024; Collaboration &
049   et al., 2024; Walke et al., 2024), which has shown impressive generalizability across various single-
050   arm tasks. However, dual-arm demonstrations are extremely expensive to acquire in real-world,
051   which often need specialized teleoperation systems with additional sensors and fine-grained cali-
052   bration with high human laborer cost (Zhao et al., 2023a; Fu et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024; Cheng
053   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). To address this challenge, recent methods aim to
      simplify learning budget by exploiting human inductive bias like parameterized atomic movements

      Generalizable Bimanual Manipulation. Generalizable Bimanual manipulation agent is able to
      handle a large variety of tasks by predicting a trajectory of dual-arm operation, which is with signif-
116
      icant potential in complex applications from household service (Zhang et al., 2024), robotic surgery
117
      (Hu et al., 2023), to component assembly in factories (Buhl et al., 2019). To achieve multi-task
118   learning, earlier studies attempted to leverage the emerged general understanding and reasoning ca-
119   pacities of pretrained foundation models like LLMs (Touvron & et al., 2023) and VLMs (Chen &
120   et al., 2023), where the foundation model was prompted to generate high-level plan for low-level ex-
121   ecutors. For example, VoxPoser (Huang et al., 2023) utilized LLMs and VLMs to specify 3D affor-
122   dance and constraint map via code generation, where a sampling-based motion planner is employed
123   as the low-level executor. However, the performance of directly leveraging foundation models in a
124   training-free manner is bottlenecked by the predefined low-level executor, which struggles to gen-
125   eralize to more contact-rich tasks like straightening a rope that are highly-desired in real-world ap-
126
      plications. To overcome this challenge, robotic foundation models (Kim et al., 2024) that pretrained
      on large-scale real-world demonstrations were proposed under the single-arm setting, which have
127
      shown high generalizabilty across everyday manipulation tasks. However, bimanual tasks demand
128
      precise coordination of two high degree-of-freedom arms, making the teleoperation of demonstra-
129   tions for training generalizable policies also costly. Although some recent approaches (Zhao et al.,
130   2023a; Fu et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024; Chuang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) have developed
131   more specialized teleoperation systems to reduce these costs, scaling up demonstrations for high
132   generalization ability remains a challenge. To address the limited availability of demonstrations, al-
133   ternative methods (Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024; Grannen et al., 2023) proposed to simplify the
134   learning of bimanual policies by decoupling the dual-arm system into a stabilizing arm and an acting
135   arm. Nevertheless, these methods often rely on predefined roles for each arm, which precludes their
136   applicability to tasks requiring more flexible collaboration patterns. In contrast to these approaches,
137
      our work presents a novel method that transfers generalizable single-arm policies to bimanual tasks,
      which eliminates the necessity for explicit inductive bias like role specification.
138
139   Skill-based Methods. Skill learning (Zhang et al., 2023) is the process where intelligent agents
140   acquire new abilities that are transferable across different tasks, which is of great significance for
141   cross-task generalization. Thus, skill learning is being attractive in enhancing the generalizability
142   of different models, such as game agents (Wang et al., 2023), robotic manipulation (Liang et al.,
143
      2024), and autonomous driving (Fu et al., 2023). The initial attempt to utilize skill learning was
      orchestrating a set of predefined skill (Munawar et al., 2018), which hindered their scalability to
144
      unseen tasks. To overcome this limitation, (Liang et al., 2024; Chitnis et al., 2020b) proposed to
145
      learn shareable skill primitives from data. For example, skill diffuser (Liang et al., 2024) introduced
146   a hierarchical planning framework that integrating learnable skill embedding into conditional tra-
147   jectory generation, which realized accurate execution of diverse compositional tasks. In the field
148   of bimanual manipulation, skill learning was dominated by handcrafted primitives. For example,
149   (Batinica et al., 2017; Colomé & Torras, 2020; Franzese et al., 2022; Grannen et al., 2020; Avigal
150   et al., 2022; Ganapathi et al., 2021; Amadio et al., 2019; Yin & Chen, 2014; Fu et al., 2022; Chitnis
151   et al., 2020a; Xie et al., 2020; Ha & Song, 2021) proposed to utilize parameterized atomic move-
152   ments to shrink the high dimensionality of the bimanual action space, which has shown impressive
153   performance on templated bimanual manipulation tasks. While the predefined atomic movements
154
      did boost the success rate on specific tasks, they are often difficult for even human users to specify,
      which restricts the deployment scenarios of these methods. In this paper, we propose to leveraging
155
      learnable implicit skill primitives to represent the learned commonsense of the pretrained single-arm
156
      policy, so that the knowledge can be transferred across different levels of tasks.

201   3.2     OVERALL P IPELINE
202
203   The overall pipeline of our AnyBimanual method is shown in Figure 2. For the language branch, we
204   employed a pretrained text encoder (Radford et al., 2021) to parse the bimanual instruction to lan-
205   guage embeddings with high-level semantics, where the skill manager schedules the skill primitives
206   with composition and compensation to boost the language embeddings. Therefore, the pre-trained
207
      single-arm policy model can be prompted to generate feasible manipulation policy for each arm with
      high generalization ability across tasks with the sharable manipulation knowledge. For the visual
208
      branch, we lift and voxelize the RGB-D input to the voxel space as visual observation, and a 3D
209
      sparse voxel encoder is utilized to encode and tokenize the voxel observation for informative volu-
210   metric representation. The voxel editor generates a soft spatial mask to align visual representation of
211   single-arm policy model with that during pretraining, so that the observation discrepancy between
212   single-arm and dual-arm systems can be minimized for policy transferability enhancement. Finally,
213   we employ two pretrained single-arm models to predict the left and right robot actions based on the
214   visual representations and text embeddings, where the pretrained single-arm policy can be multi-
215   modal transformer-based policy (Shridhar et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024) or diffusion-based policy
      (Ke et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024).

218   In order to transfer single-arm manipulation policy to dual-arm setting without generalizability
219   drops, we propose a skill manager to decompose the action policies from single-arm foundation
220   models into skill primitives and integrates primitives for dual-arm systems. However, the given of-
221   fline expert demonstrations D does not contain any explicit intermediate skill primitives or sub-task
222   boundaries, but only low-level end-effector poses and a high-level natural language instruction are
223   provided. Therefore, we design a automatic skill discovery method in an unsupervised manner to
224
      learn primitive skills and their schema from offline bimanual manipulation datasets during training.
      In the test phase, the skill manager predicts different weighted combinations of discovered primitive
225
      skills to orchestrate each arm given high-level language instruction, which enables effective transfer
226
      of pre-trained single-arm policy to diverse bimanual manipulation tasks.

275   Voxel Editor. Despite the skill manager enables generalization in the language modality, the dis-
276   tributional shift from the single-arm to dual-arm workspace in terms of the visual context still may
277   harm the model performance. To mitigate the observation discrepancy, we present a voxel edi-
278   tor q that predict two spatial soft masks at each step t to edit the voxel space so that the decom-
279   posed subspace of single-arm policy model for each arm aligns with those during pretraining stage:
280   (v̂tleft , v̂tright ) = q(vt , l, pt ). The decomposed observation represents the locality of the workspace,
281   which is then augmented by the bimanual observation to form the final visual embedding:
282
283                              vtleft = (v̂tleft ⊙ vt ) ⊕ vt ,   vtright = (v̂tright ⊙ vt ) ⊕ vt ,              (3)
284   where ⊙ is the element-wise multiplication, and ⊕ refers to the concatenation operator. As a result,
285   the augmented visual representations for each arm contains both embodiment-specific information
286   and the global context, which is then passed through the two single-arm policies to decode the
287   optimal bimanual action.
288
      Learning Aligned Spatial Representation. Our goal is to mitigate the visual domain gap between
289
      the single-arm and dual-arm setting, so that the pretrained commonsense knowledge in single-arm
290   policy can be transferred with high adaptation ability. Since we can not access the single-arm pre-
291   training data in common usages, we instead impose a mutually exclusive prior to the voxel editor
292   that encourages it to implicitly divide the dual-arm workspace into single-arm workspaces. This
293   prior is regularized by optimizing a Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence regularization term:
294
295                             Lvoxel = −DKL (v̂tleft ∥v̂tright )/2 − DKL (v̂tright ∥v̂tleft )/2                 (4)
296
      where DKL means the Kullback-Leibler (KL)-divergence operator. To provide further explaination,
297
      bimanual manipulation tasks often involves asynchronous collaboration that requires the left and
298   right arm attends to different areas of the whole workspace to act as different roles, such as stabi-
299   lizing and acting. Based on this observation, the mutually exclusive division of the entire bimaual
300   workspace will naturally separates one arm and its target from the other, which highly resembles
301   the single-arm configuration. Hence by maximizing the divergence between the two soft masks, the
302   voxel input of the bimanual manipulation agent can be disentangled into single-arm visual represen-
303   tations that align with those in the pretraining phase effectively.

342   expert demonstrations. For agent observation, we employed six cameras (i.e. front, left, right, wrist
343   left, wrist right, overhead) with a resolution of 256 × 256 to cover the entire workspace and two
344   robot arms. For behavior cloning in the training phase, we provide 100 demonstrations for each
345   task, which are generated by a oracle script expert. In the test phase, we evaluate 25 episodes per
346   task in the testing set to mitigate bias from noise.
347   Real Robot. The real-world setup for our experiments involves two Universal Robots UR5e ma-
348   nipulators equipped with Robotiq 2F-85 grippers, controlled using two Xbox gamepads as 6-DoF
349   controllers for collecting demonstrations. An RGB-D Realsense camera mounted on a tripod pro-
350   vides 640 × 480 resolution images at 30 Hz, simulating a human viewpoint during bimanual tasks.
351   Camera-to-arm calibration is achieved using the easy handeye package and ARUCO markers on the
352   end-effectors. We collect 20 real-world human demonstrations during training, while the evaluations
353   are conducted using a Nvidia RTX 4080 GPU. For more setup details, see Section 4.5.We present
354
      two qualitative examples of the action sequences in Figure 4.
355   Baselines. We compare our AnyBimanual with the state-for-the-art approaches, including
356   PerAct2 (Grotz et al., 2024), which is a strengthened version from the well-known single-arm policy
357   PerAct. To exclude the influence of model parameter, we also implement a baseline that incorporates
358   two pre-trained multi-task PerAct (Shridhar et al., 2022) model. Additionally, we include PerAct-
359   LF in our comparisons, which employs a leader-follower Grotz et al. (2024) architecture using two
360
      Perceiver Actor networks. The evaluation metric is the task completion rate or success rate, which
      is defined as the percentage of episodes where the agent successfully completes the goal specified
361
      by natural language within a budget of 25 steps.
362
363   Implementation Details. Following the common training recipe (Shridhar et al., 2022; Zhao et al.,
364   2023b), we use the SE(3) augmentation for the expert demonstrations in the training set to improve
365   the generalizability of the agents. Specifically, we augment each training sample by perturbing the
366
      3D point cloud with [±0.125m, ±0.125m, ±0.125m] translation, and rotate it around the z-axis
      by [0◦ , 0◦ , 45◦ ]. All compared methods are trained on two NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPUs for 100k
367
      iterations with a batch size of 2. We use the LAMB optimizer (You et al., 2020) with in a constant

      learning rate of 5 × 10−4 to update the model parameters.

531
      In this paper, we have introduced AnyBimanual, a novel framework designed to transfer pretrained
532
      single-arm manipulation policies to multi-task bimanual manipulation with limited dual-arm demon-
533
      strations. We develop a skill manager to dynamically schedule skill primitives discovered from
534   single-arm policies, enabling their effective adaptation for bimanual tasks with the addition of
535   embodiment-specific compensations. To address the observation discrepancies between single-arm
536   and bimanual systems, we propose a voxel editor that generates spatial soft masks, aligning the vi-
537   sual embeddings of each arm with those used during the pretraining stage of the single-arm policy
538   model. Extensive experiments across 13 simulated and real-world tasks demonstrate the superiority
539   of AnyBimanual. The limitations of our approach primarily arise from the need for careful visual
      alignment between single-arm and dual-arm systems to ensure robust policy transfer across tasks.

      model’s robustness. Each task undergoes training for a full day on a single Nvidia RTX 3090 GPU
848

849
