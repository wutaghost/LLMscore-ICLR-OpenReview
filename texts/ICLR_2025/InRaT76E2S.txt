009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalization in deep learning is often associated with the sharpness of the min-
012            ima encountered during training. We introduce a novel, deterministic, and com-
013            putationally efficient method called activation decay, designed to flatten sharp
014            minima and improve generalization across a wide range of tasks. Derived from
015            Gaussian smoothing, activation decay operates by regularizing the activations of
016
               critical network layers, effectively reducing sharpness and improving robustness.
               Unlike stochastic techniques such as dropout or the more computationally ex-
017
               pensive Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM), our approach requires no addi-
018
               tional computational overhead, making it particularly suited for large-scale mod-
019            els. We further demonstrate that activation decay can be seamlessly combined
020            with other regularization techniques, offering enhanced regularization without in-
021            creasing training complexity. Extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, ImageNet,
022            and natural language processing (NLP) tasks validate our approach, showing con-
023            sistent improvements in generalization and robustness to label noise.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Generalization in deep learning models remains a fundamental challenge, particularly as models
028
      grow in complexity and are tasked with learning from increasingly large datasets. The nature of the
029
      minima in the loss landscape has often been cited as a key factor influencing generalization. Models
030   that converge to sharp minima tend to be highly sensitive to small perturbations, resulting in poor
031   performance on unseen data. In contrast, models that converge to flatter minima have often been
032   associated with improved robustness and generalization (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). While
033   sharpness-based measures have been shown to correlate strongly with generalization under certain
034   settings (Jiang et al., 2020), it is important to note that flatness is not a universal guarantee of better
035   generalization, as it is sensitive to parameterization and re-scaling effects in deep, overparameterized
036   networks (Zhang et al., 2017). Despite these nuances, regularization techniques aimed at minimizing
037   sharpness or curvature remain effective heuristics in guiding models toward solutions that generalize
038
      well in practice.
039   A widely used regularization method is weight decay, or ℓ2 regularization on weights, which pe-
040   nalizes the magnitude of the model’s weights by adding a term proportional to the squared norm of
041   the parameters to the loss function. Weight decay penalizes large parameter values, smoothing the
042   loss landscape and reducing the complexity of the learned model. By controlling the magnitude of
043   the weights, weight decay helps the model find flatter minima, improving generalization (Krogh &
044
      Hertz, 1992).
045   Bishop (1995) first established the connection between noise injection and deterministic regular-
046   ization, demonstrating how noise injection can be cast as a form of regularization. In more recent
047   work, Wei et al. (2020); Orvieto et al. (2022) further explored this connection by analyzing how
048   specific forms of noise injection, such as anticorrelated noise and dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014),
049   can encourage flatter minima and improve generalization. While noise injection introduces stochas-
050
      ticity during training, making models more robust to perturbations and guiding them toward flatter
      minima, those methods introduce randomness into the optimization process, which can lead to per-
051
      formance variability across different runs.
052
053   Another important method in the field of regularization is Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM)
      (Foret et al., 2021), which directly targets sharpness by introducing a min-max optimization frame-

054
      work. SAM aims to minimize the worst-case sharpness in a neighborhood of the model’s parameters,
055   encouraging convergence to flatter minima. While effective, SAM’s computational cost and memory
056   overhead are significantly higher than simpler methods like weight decay and noise injection. Zhang
057   et al. (2018) show that batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) and residual connections (He
058   et al., 2016) enhance backpropagation by improving the local Hessian’s spectrum leading to better
059   gradient flow.
060
      Beyond simply smoothing the loss landscape, methods like SAM provide robustness to label noise
061   by guiding models to converge to flatter minima, where the sensitivity to noisy labels is reduced
062   (Baek et al., 2024). Similarly, smooth networks, such as Lipschitz networks, demonstrate robustness
063   to adversarial attacks (Tsuzuku et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2019), as controlling the Lipschitz constant
064   ensures that small perturbations in the input lead to bounded changes in the output. Importantly, the
065   Lipschitz constant of a network also plays a critical role in generalization bounds (Bartlett et al.,
066   2017), as it governs how sensitive the model is to variations in the data, further linking generalization
067   performance with robustness to both label noise and adversarial perturbations.
068   The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
069
070          • We provide a theoretical framework that establishes a clear relationship between noise vari-
071            ance and the spectral norm of the Hessian. Specifically, we present two results: Corollary 1
072            quantifies the reduction in Hessian curvature due to Gaussian smoothing near a local min-
073            imum, offering guarantees on how smoothing influences the loss landscape and improves
074            generalization. Theorem 1 demonstrates how the curvature of the final layer and loss, along
075            with the Lipschitz constants of the intermediate layers, regularizes the Hessian by decom-
076            posing the individual contributions of each layer to the overall Hessian.
077
078          • We propose a simple and deterministic method based on Gaussian smoothing applied to
079            the final layer weights in Theorem 2. This method significantly reduces the computational
080
               overhead while retaining the smoothing benefits with regard to flat minima. We call that
               method activation decay (AD) as it can be cast as a ℓ2 -norm regularization on activations.
081
082
             • We validate our approach through extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, ImageNet, and
083
               natural language processing (NLP) benchmarks in a multi-task setting. Our method consis-
084
               tently improves generalization, demonstrates robustness to label noise (on CIFAR-10), and
085            outperforms SAM and dropout on NLP tasks. Additionally, it can be seamlessly combined
086            with SAM without additional computational overhead.
087
088   The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews the related work on regularization techniques in
089   neural networks to promote flat minima, focusing on approaches such as noise-based regularization,
090   ℓ2 regularization, and their impact on generalization. Section 3 presents our theoretical framework,
091   detailing the relationship between Gaussian smoothing, Hessian curvature, and ℓ2 regularization,
092   including new insights into how layer-wise regularization impacts the overall loss landscape. Sec-
093   tion 4 describes our experimental setup, discusses the results, and demonstrates the effectiveness of
094   our approach across multiple tasks.

      2.1   F LAT MINIMA
109
110   Generalization, the ability of a model to perform well on unseen data, is often associated with the
111   flatness of the minima found during training, as highlighted by empirical studies (Keskar et al., 2016;
112   Chaudhari et al., 2019), where sharp minima are linked to poorer generalization. The relationship
113   between loss landscapes, generalization, and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) has been a central
114   topic in machine learning research for years (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). For instance, it has
115   been shown that in overparameterized models, local minima of the loss function are often close to
116   the global minima (Choromanska et al., 2015). Additionally, Xing et al. (2018) demonstrate that
117
      SGD exhibits an implicit bias, favoring regions of the loss landscape resembling a valley.
118   A pivotal study by Keskar et al. (2016) shows that large-batch training tends to converge to sharp
119   minima, which correlates with worse generalization compared to the flatter minima achieved with
120   small-batch training. However, Dinh et al. (2017) point out that common sharpness metrics, such as
121   the spectral norm of the Hessian of L(θ), are sensitive to re-scaling. In our analysis, the networks
122   are not adversarially reparameterized, ensuring a fair comparison. It is important to note that flatter
123
      minima do not always guarantee better generalization, as counterexamples exist where sharp minima
      generalize well and flat minima perform poorly (Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2022; Zhang et al.,
124
      2017). Nevertheless, Jiang et al. (2020) show that sharpness-based metrics often outperform other
125
      complexity metrics for evaluating generalization.
126
127   Finally, as discussed in Section 2.3, Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM), introduced by Foret
128   et al. (2021), has become a prominent and deeply studied method in this area.
129
130   2.2   N OISE INJECTION
131
132   Noise injection methods add stochasticity during training, either to activations or weights. It im-
133   proves robustness and prevents overfitting. The most widely known is probably the dropout (Srivas-
134   tava et al., 2014), which randomly drops units during training, reducing co-adaptation of neurons
135   and improving generalization. However, its stochastic nature introduces additional variance in per-
136
      formance, requiring careful tuning to prevent underfitting, particularly when applied to deep models
      (Liu et al., 2023). The increased training variability may also result in unstable training dynamics
137
      under certain conditions. Perturbed Gradient Descent (PGD) (Jin et al., 2017) introduces noise into
138
      the weight updates to help models escape saddle points and better explore the optimization land-
139   scape. Building on PGD, anticorrelated noise (Orvieto et al., 2022) modifies the noise structure
140   and proves it promotes convergence to flatter minima by controlling the curvature of the loss land-
141   scape. The resulting optimization program can be summarized with the smoothed loss: Lσ (θ) =
142   E∆∼N (0,σ2 I) [L(θ + ∆)] . The resulting optimization landscape becomes smoother, reducing the
143   impact of sharp regions. However, tuning the noise parameter σ is critical, as improper choices can
144   lead to instability with exploding variance or insufficient regularization.
145
146   2.3   L OSS SMOOTHING AND REGULARIZATION
147
148   While noise-based regularization techniques have been widely adopted, they often introduce un-
149   desirable variance into the training process, which can hinder performance. The work of Bishop
150   (1995) interprets Gaussian noise on inputs as Tikhonov regularization on parameters also known as
151   weight decay, establishing a connection between noise injection and deterministic loss smoothing.
152   This encourages smaller weights and promotes simpler solutions that generalize better (Krogh &
153   Hertz, 1992). Such regularization flattens the loss landscape, leading to convergence toward wider,
154
      flatter minima that correlate with improved generalization (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). The
                                                               2
155   regularized loss function can be expressed as: L(θ) + σ2 ∥θ∥22 .
156   Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2021), a more recent approach, penalizes sharp
157   minima directly. SAM minimizes a robust smoothed objective: minθ max∆∈B(0,ρ) L(θ + ∆),
158   where B(0, ρ) represents a ball of radius ρ around zero, and ∆ is the perturbation applied to the pa-
159   rameters. By introducing an adversarial component into the optimization process, SAM effectively
160   reduces sharpness in the loss landscape, which improves generalization across many tasks. However,
161   SAM’s computational cost is significantly higher due to multiple forward-backward passes required
      for the perturbed loss evaluation.

169   3     ACTIVATION DECAY: CONTROLLING THE H ESSIAN NORM TO IMPROVE
170         GENERALIZATION
171
172   In this section, we provide a theoretical framework to quantify how smoothing techniques, such as
173   Gaussian noise injection, affect the curvature of the loss landscape and contribute to better general-
174   ization. Specifically, we examine how these techniques influence the spectral norm of the Hessian,
175   a key measure of sharpness in the loss function. By doing so, we establish a connection between
176   noise-based regularization and the flattening of minima. This is followed by an analysis of how con-
177   straining the spectral norm weight and the Hessian of individual layers can further control the overall
178   curvature, offering a tractable approach to loss smoothing. Finally, we discuss a practical method of
179   smoothing the loss through activation decay, providing theoretical guarantees for its effectiveness in
180
      reducing sharpness. All proofs are provided in the Appendix.
181

      4.3   A DAPTING TO L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
419
420   Multi-task learning (MTL) Zhang et al. (2021) is a paradigm in machine learning where a model
421   is trained to perform multiple tasks simultaneously, leveraging shared representations across tasks.
422   MTL also offers the benefit of reducing computational costs and latency, enabling the model to
423   handle multiple tasks in a single inference step, rather than performing separate inferences for each
424   task.
425
      With the advent of large-scale pretrained models, multi-task learning has become increasingly pop-
426   ular in NLP. Pretrained on large corpora, these models can capture a wide range of patterns and
427   dependencies in text data. Leveraging this general knowledge during fine-tuning while specializing
428   in specific tasks leads to new state-of-the-art performances on a variety of downstream tasks. This
429   task-specific adaptation during fine-tuning, is needed for earlier Large Language Models (LLMs) as
430   BERT Devlin et al. (2019) and RoBERTa Liu et al. (2019) and has recently been reduced to few-shot
431   learning with models like GPT-3 Brown et al. (2020) and T5 Raffel et al. (2020) and even extended
      to zero-shot settings by Wei et al. (2022); Sanh et al. (2022) in the context of very large models.


                                                                                    8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      However, fine-tuning multiple tasks can lead to overfitting on individual tasks, which can degrade
433   the model’s performance on other tasks, calling for specific design choices, adding extra task-
434   specific parameters (adapters), or using specific prompts (see Stickland & Murray (2019); Wang
435   et al. (2023)). Our AD method promotes flat minima to improve generalization across tasks. The
436   goal is to prevent overfitting to any specific task while retaining the benefits of pretraining on various
437   tasks. Our flat minima regularization helps preserve the generalization capabilities of the pretrained
438   model, ensuring robust performance across all tasks during fine-tuning. As detailed in Section 2.1
439   flat minima and generalization are often tied together and the experimental results presented in Ta-
440   bles 6 and 3 show our regularization helps conserve performance across diverse tasks by promoting
441
      smooth optimization landscapes during fine-tuning.
442
443   Fine tuning on distinct tasks In this experiment, we evaluate the performance of a multi-task
444
      NLP model using the RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), and T5 (Raffel et al.,
      2020) architectures to handle distinct tasks. The multi-task setup allows the model to process these
445
      tasks simultaneously, optimizing latency and resource consumption. We experimentally show that
446
      in the context of fine-tuning and few-shot learning, our Activation Decay (AD) method helps LLMs
447   to generalize better across tasks, leading to improved performance compared to the baseline models.
448
449   Each task is described as follows: (i) Sentiment Analysis: a binary classification task (posi-
450
      tive/negative) on the IMDb dataset, using classification accuracy as the metric; (ii) NER: named
      entity recognition on the Snips and CoNLL datasets, evaluated using F1-score, precision, and recall
451
      metrics; (iii) Intent Classification: intent detection on the Snips dataset, evaluated with classification
452
      accuracy; (iv) Entailment Classification (SNLI): a binary classification task predicting whether a
453   sentence entails another, based on the SNLI dataset, with classification accuracy as the metric; (v)
454   POS Tagging: part-of-speech tagging on the CoNLL dataset, evaluated using F1-score, precision,
455   and recall metrics; (vi) Query Correctness: a binary classification task assessing the correctness of
456   queries, evaluated with classification accuracy.
457
      The backbone models used for all tasks are BERT (bert-base) and RoBERTa (roberta-base), with
458
      dropout probability set to 0. The model’s performance is evaluated using a custom smoothed loss
459   function with σ = 0.05, and the results are compared with SAM regularization at different ρ values.
460   Weight decay is present by default in the training configuration of the backbone. We use standard
461   training configuration from HuggingFace corresponding models and trainers.
462
      Table 2 presents evaluation results for fine-tuning BERT on seven NLP tasks, comparing the baseline
463
      model with standard dropout (p = 0.1), SAM regularization with standard values (ρ = 0.01), and
464
      Activation Decay (σ = 0.05). The best values for SAM were selected from ρ = 0.01, 0.05, 0.1,
465   and Activation Decay from σ = 0.01, 0.05, 0.1. Metrics include classification accuracy, F1-score,
466   precision, and recall. Activation Decay consistently outperforms both the baseline and SAM across
467   most tasks. The same results for RoBERTa are reported in Table 5 in the Appendix, showing the
468   efficiency of AD.

473   updated version published by (OpenAI, 2023), which expanded the dataset to include 14 languages

475   periments, fine-tuning the small (60 M), base (220 M), and large (770 M) variants of the model

477
      setting, consistently outperforms the standard dropout p = 0.1 a common baseline for fine-tuning
      baseline across all model sizes, demonstrating its effectiveness for large models. This result high-
478
      lights the importance of our approach in improving accuracy, particularly in large-scale multitask
479
      environments. All code and implementation details will be made available upon acceptance of the

531

532   Gaussian smoothing to regularize critical activations, activation decay flattens minima and enhances
533   robustness without incurring additional computational costs. Our method provides a determinis-
534   tic alternative to stochastic regularization techniques like dropout and SAM, maintaining efficiency
535   while achieving comparable or superior performance. Additionally, its ability to integrate and com-
536   bine seamlessly with existing regularization methods like weight decay makes it a versatile and
537   practical tool for large-scale models.
