009                                              A BSTRACT
010
               We present a novel framework for online learning in Stackelberg general-sum
011
               games, where two agents, the leader and follower, engage in sequential turn-based
012
               interactions. At the core of this approach is a learned diffeomorphism that maps
013            the joint action space to a smooth Riemannian manifold, referred to as the Stack-
014            elberg manifold. This mapping, facilitated by neural normalizing flows, ensures
015            the formation of tractable isoplanar subspaces, enabling efficient techniques for
016            online learning. By assuming linearity between the agents’ reward functions on
017            the Stackelberg manifold, our construct allows the application of standard bandit
018            algorithms. We then provide a rigorous theoretical basis for regret minimization
019            on convex manifolds and establish finite-time bounds on simple regret for learn-
020            ing Stackelberg equilibria. This integration of manifold learning into game the-
021
               ory uncovers a previously unrecognized potential for neural normalizing flows as
               an effective tool for multi-agent learning. We present empirical results demon-
022
               strating the effectiveness of our approach compared to standard baselines, with
023
               applications spanning domains such as cybersecurity and economic supply chain
024            optimization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
029
030
      A Stackelberg game consists of a sequential decision-making process involving two agents, a leader
      and a follower. This framework, introduced in Stackelberg 1934 models hierarchical strategic inter-
031
      actions where the leader moves first, anticipating the follower’s best response, and then the follower
032
      reacts accordingly. These games have become central to understanding interactions in various fields,
033   from economics to societal security, providing a formal method for analyzing situations where one
034   party commits to a strategy before the other, affecting the subsequent decision-making process and
035   reward outcomes. Over time, Stackelberg games have evolved to address more complex environ-
036   ments, incorporating factors like imperfect information and no-regret learning of system parameters.
037   The solution to such a game typically revolves around finding a Stackelberg equilibrium, where the
038   leader optimizes his strategy assuming or knowing the follower type, which affects how she opti-
039   mizes her utility based on the leader’s action. (Kar et al. 2015; Korzhyk, Conitzer, and Parr 2010).
040   Several challenges arise in the practical applications of Stackelberg games. One key issue is the
041   uncertainty regarding the follower’s type or rationality (or sub-rationality). In many real-world sce-
042   narios, the follower might not be fully rational or the leader might have incomplete knowledge of the
043   follower’s preferences, leading to uncertainty in the leader’s decision-making process. Additionally,
044   imperfect information regarding reward outcomes adds another layer of complexity, as the leader
045
      may not have accurate knowledge of the payoffs associated with various strategies. These uncer-
      tainties have been addressed in domains such as security, where randomized strategies and robust
046
      optimization approaches are deployed to mitigate risks arising from incomplete information and un-
047
      predictable follower behaviour (Jiang et al. 2013; Kar et al. 2015; Debarun Kar et al. 2017). For
048   instance, in deployed systems like ARMOR at LAX or PROTECT at U.S. ports, leaders must make
049   security decisions under uncertainty, balancing multiple risks (Jain et al. 2011; Shieh et al. 2012).
050   Stackelberg games also feature prominently in supply chain optimization settings, where there exist
051   areas of uncertainty, such as demand manifestation (L. Liu and Rong 2024) (Cesa-Bianchi et al.
052   2023). Stackelberg game models have also found applications in novel areas like conversational
053   agents using large language models (LLMs), where one agent (the model) anticipates the user’s
      behaviour and adjusts its responses accordingly (Nguyen et al. 2014).

      For non-cooperative multi-agent games that exhibit additive noise, sublinear regret can be achieved
055   via gradient based optimization methods, such as AdaGrad (Duchi, Hazan, and Singer 2011), in the
056   face of Gaussian noise but this is often subject to constraints on the magnitude of the noise (Hsieh
057   et al. 2023). Nevertheless, in these games, the problem settings are extended to an unlimited number
058   of players - with regret performance degrading as the number of players increases. We investigate
059   the problem setting of a two-player Stackelberg game, with a tractable best response function -
060   commonplace in economics and adversarial machine learning in general (Wang et al. 2024; Zhou
061   and Kantarcioglu 2016).
062   Problem Setting: We consider a two-player Stackelberg game where player A leads and player B
063   responds. Stackelberg games are sequential, meaning that the players take turns and, the follower can
064   best respond to the leader’s action, given information available to him. The best response of player
065   B lies on a manifold within a subspace of the joint action space A × B. We define this Stackelberg
066   game setting in the framework of optimal transport, where the structure of the best response function
067
      B(·) gleans simplifications to the solution methodology to obtain Stackelberg regret. This research
      focuses on applying multi-armed bandit (MAB) methods, particularly in Stackelberg equilibrium
068
      settings, to achieve sublinear regret. It explores the game theory, utilizing geometric topologies to
069
      better understand agent behaviour and simplify computations.
070
071
      Key Contributions: We introduce a novel algorithm that significantly advances the understanding
      of Stackelberg learning under imperfect information, akin to the problem settings covered in Balcan
072
      et al. 2015 and Haghtalab et al. 2022, presenting a systematic framework for how equilibrium can be
073
      efficiently solved in this problem setting. Central to our contribution is the construction of a feature
074   map using neural normalizing flows, which transforms the ambient joint action space into a more
075   tractable embedding, we define as the Stackelberg manifold. By leveraging the geodesic proper-
076   ties of this manifold, our approach allows for more efficient computation of Stackelberg equilibria
077   with respect to no-regret learning, particularly in the presence of parameter uncertainty. In addition
078   to this, we offer a rigorous theoretical foundation for optimizing Stackelberg games on spherical
079   manifolds. This framework is validated via empirical simulations, stemming from applications in
080   supply chain management and cybersecurity, demonstrating that our method outperforms standard
081   baselines, offering improvements in both computational efficiency and regret minimization.

084   In a Stackelberg game, two players take turns executing their actions. Player A is the leader, she
085   acts first with action a selected from her action space A. Player B is the follower, he acts second
086
      with action b ∈ B. The follower acts in response to the leader’s action, and both players earn a joint
      payoff as function of their actions.

088   2.1     R EPEATED S TACKELBERG G AMES
089   In a repeated Stackelberg game, the leader chooses actions at ∈ A, and the follower reacts with
090   actions bt ∈ B at each round t = 1, 2, . . . , T . The leader’s strategy πA (·|Ht ) is a probability
091   distribution over the action space A which selects at based on past joint actions up to time t, i.e.,
092   Ht := {(aτ , bτ )|τ < t}. Similarly, the follower’s strategy πB (·|Ht ) is a conditional probability
093   distribution over B which determines bt given the full history, i.e., Ht := Ht ∪ {at }.
094   Best Response Strategy of the Follower: To be specific, the follower selects his best response
095   strategy at round t by maximizing his expected reward function µB (a, b) : A×B → R given that the
096   leader has played action at . Since, we assume that the reward function solely depends on the most
097   recent pairs of actions, the follower’s best strategy is first order Markov, i.e., πB (·|Ht ) = πB (·|at ).
098   Formally, the follower’s best response at round t is given by,

      2.2     T HE S TACKELBERG M ANIFOLD
116
117   To address the complexity of solving for Stackelberg equilibrium under uncertainty, we propose the
118   idea of mapping actions from the ambient space onto a manifold Φ leading to several key advan-
119   tages. Simplifying the problem by mapping to a geometric structure, such as a unit sphere, allows for
120
      significantly faster numerical computation while optimizing directly on an intuitive intrinsic geom-
      etry, reducing redundancies and provides ease with respect to enforcing constraints. Additionally,
121
      smoothness on such a structure enables computational advantages through methods like Riemannian
122
      gradient descent (Bonnabel 2013), which exploits differentiability for efficient optimization.
123
124
      This concept of mapping the data from the ambient space, in our case defined by the joint action
      space A × B, onto a latent space Φ has been explored in several prior works. For a well defined
125
      manifold, typically the approach is to learn a diffeomorphism between the ambient data space, and
126
      the objective manifold, which is a subspace of the ambient data space (D. J. Rezende, Papamakarios,
127   et al. 2020) (Gemici, D. Rezende, and Mohamed 2016). Suppose the manifold is not given, or there
128   lies flexibility in defining the structure of such a manifold, the certain manifold learning techniques
129   could be devised (Brehmer and Cranmer 2020). These approaches typically define invertible, or
130   pesudo-invertible, probability density maps between the ambient data space, the latent space, and
131   the manifold space.

302   a convex manifold. The convex manifold is a manifold where the geodesic between any two points
303   on the manifold is contained within or forms a geodesically convex set (Def. C.1). Essentially, in a
304   convex manifold, every geodesic between two points is contained within the manifold, adhering to
305   the geodesic convexity property. The formal definitions can be found in Appendix C.1.

520   techniques for tractable solutions. (We specify the details and additional experiments in Appendix

      6    C ONCLUSION
523
524   This work establishes a foundational connection between Stackelberg games and normalizing neural
525   flows, marking a significant advancement in the study of equilibrium learning and manifold learning.
526
      By utilizing normalizing flows to map joint action spaces onto Riemannian manifolds, particularly
      spherical ones, we offer a novel, theoretically grounded framework with formal guarantees on simple
527
      regret. This approach represents the first application of normalizing flows in game-theoretic settings,
528
      specifically Stackelberg games, thereby opening new avenues for learning on convex manifolds.
529   Our empirical results, grounded in realistic simulation scenarios, highlight promising improvements
530   in both computational efficiency and regret minimization, underscoring the broad potential of this
531   methodology across multiple domains in economics and engineering. Despite potential challenges
532   related to numerical accuracy for the neural flow network, this integration of manifold learning into
533   game theory nevertheless exhibits strong implications for online learning, positioning neural flows
534   as a promising tool for both machine learning and strategic decision-making.

812   B.1   B I - LEVEL O PTIMIZATION S TRUCTURE
813   Bi-level Optimization Structure: The optimization problems represented by Eqs. (3.4) and (3.5)
814   exhibit the structure of a bi-level optimization problem (Balling and Sobieszczanski-Sobieski 1995;
815   Beck, Ljubić, and Schmidt 2023; Sinha, Malo, and Deb 2017). Generally, a bilevel optimiza-
816   tion problem comprises an upper-level optimization task with an embedded lower-level problem,
817   where the solution to the upper-level problem depends on the solution to the lower-level one. Two
818   conventional methods have been employed to address the bilevel optimization problem. The first
819   leverages the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions to exploit the optimality of the lower-level
820
      problem (see Appendix B.2). The second employs gradient-based algorithms like gradient ascent
      (discussed in Appendix B.3). Both approaches, however, have notable limitations. KKT conditions
821
      assume strong convexity or pseudo-convexity, making them unsuitable for many non-convex set-
822
      tings, while gradient-based methods, in addition to being computationally inefficient, often struggle
823   or converge poorly when weak-convexity is not guaranteed. Moreover, these methods typically as-
824   sume optimization under perfect information, whereas we focus on learning-based frameworks with
825   uncertainty due to sampling.
