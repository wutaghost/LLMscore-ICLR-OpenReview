                Large Language Models (LLMs) have become foundational in human-computer
013             interaction, demonstrating remarkable linguistic capabilities across various tasks.
014             However, there is a growing concern about their potential to perpetuate social
015             biases present in their training data. In this paper, we comprehensively investigate
016             the vulnerabilities of contemporary LLMs to various social bias attacks, including
017             prefix injection, refusal suppression, and learned attack prompts. We evaluate
018             popular models such as LLaMA2, GPT-3.5, and GPT-4 across gender, racial, and
019             religious bias types. Our findings reveal that models are generally more susceptible
020             to gender bias attacks compared to racial or religious biases. We also explore novel
021
                aspects such as cross-bias and multiple-bias attacks, finding varying degrees of
                transferability across bias types. Additionally, our results show that larger models
022
                and pretrained base models often exhibit higher susceptibility to bias attacks. These
023
                insights contribute to the development of more inclusive and ethically responsible
024             LLMs, emphasizing the importance of understanding and mitigating potential bias
025             vulnerabilities. We offer recommendations for model developers and users to
026             enhance the robustness of LLMs against social bias attacks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized human-computer interaction, demonstrating
034   remarkable linguistic capabilities across a wide range of tasks. Models like GPT-3, LLaMA Touvron
035   et al. (2023), ChatGPT OpenAI (2022) and GPT-4 OpenAI (2023) have shown impressive performance
036
      in areas such as natural language understanding, generation, and complex reasoning. However, as
      these models become increasingly integrated into various applications and decision-making processes,
037
      there is a growing concern about their potential to perpetuate and amplify social biases 1 present in
038
      their training data.
039
040   While previous studies Guo et al. (2022); May et al. (2019); Nangia et al. (2020); Nadeem et al.
041   (2020); Sun et al. (2023); Ravfogel et al. (2020); Webster et al. (2020); Schick et al. (2021) have
042
      identified various types of biases in LLMs, there remains a critical gap in understanding how these
      models perform when subjected to deliberate attacks aimed at inducing and amplifying biases. This
043
      gap hinders our ability to fully grasp the potential vulnerabilities of LLMs in real-world scenarios
044
      where adversaries might seek to exploit and amplify biases.
045
046   This study aims to address this gap by comprehensively assessing how current LLMs respond when
047   subjected to deliberate bias induction. Specifically, we investigate the following research questions:
048
             • How vulnerable are different LLMs to various types of social bias attacks?
049
050          • Do the vulnerabilities vary across different bias dimensions (gender, race, religion)?
051          • How effective are different attack techniques in inducing biased responses?
052
         1
053        Unless otherwise specified, our use of “bias” as unfair treatment or outcomes among social groups resulting
      from historical and structural power imbalances

054
             • To what extent are bias vulnerabilities transferable across different bias types?
055
             • How does model size and fine-tuning impact bias vulnerabilities?
056
057   To answer these questions, we design and implement three main bias attack techniques: prefix
058   injection, refusal suppression, and learned attack prompts. We evaluate these attacks on a range
059   of popular LLMs, including LLaMA2 Touvron et al. (2023), Falcon Almazrouei et al. (2023),
060   Vicuna Chiang et al. (2023a), Mistral Jiang et al. (2023), Pythia Biderman et al. (2023), GPT-3.5
061   OpenAI (2022), and GPT-4 OpenAI (2023). Our evaluation metrics include both automated methods
062   (jailbreak rate and GPT-4 as an evaluator) and human assessment. In addition, we also evaluated the
063   performance of the defense methods on these attacks.
064   This paper makes several novel contributions to the field:
065
066          • We provide a comprehensive assessment of bias vulnerabilities across multiple popular
067            LLMs, offering insights into their relative strengths and weaknesses.
068          • We introduce and evaluate cross-bias and multiple-bias attacks, shedding light on the
069            transferability of bias vulnerabilities across different bias types.
070          • We analyze the impact of model size on bias vulnerabilities, comparing models within the
071            same family (e.g., LLaMA2, Pythia) across different parameter counts.
072          • We compare the bias vulnerabilities of pretrained base models with their fine-tuned counter-
073            parts, providing insights into the effects of fine-tuning on bias robustness.
074          • We propose and evaluate a simple defense method against bias attacks, offering an initial
075            step towards more robust LLMs.
076
077   Our findings reveal that models are generally more susceptible to gender bias attacks compared to
078   racial or religious biases. We also observe that larger models (compared to smaller models) and
079
      pretrained base models (compared to fine-tuned variants) often exhibit higher susceptibility to bias
      attacks. These insights contribute to the development of more inclusive and ethically responsible
080
      LLMs, emphasizing the importance of understanding and mitigating potential bias vulnerabilities.
081
082   It is crucial to acknowledge the ethical considerations and potential risks associated with this work.
083   By exploring vulnerabilities in LLMs, we risk providing information that could be misused to create
084   more effective bias attacks. To mitigate this risk, we have implemented safeguards such as limiting
085
      the purpose of using the data. We encourage researchers to use the knowledge gained from this work
      responsibly and to prioritize ethical considerations in the development of LLMs.

088   2    R ELATED W ORK
089
090   As large language models’ capabilities expand, so do the opportunities for misuse and harm Gehman
091   et al. (2020); Goldstein et al. (2023); Kreps et al. (2022); Welbl et al. (2021). Notably, safety training
092   for large language models, exemplified by models like GPT-4 OpenAI (2023), typically entails the
093
      fine-tuning of pretrained models. This fine-tuning process involves incorporating human preferences
      Bai et al. (2022a); Ouyang et al. (2022) and leveraging AI feedback Bai et al. (2022b); OpenAI
094
      (2023) to proactively address potential harm and misuse. After safety training, these advanced
095
      language models demonstrate a conscious refusal to respond to specific commands that could elicit
096   harmful information. For instance, they are designed to reject prompts associated with the creation of
097   misinformation or assistance in criminal activities. This refusal mechanism serves as a crucial layer
098   of defense, enhancing the model’s ethical performance and minimizing the risks associated with its
099   deployment. Some work Zhao et al. (2023); Nangia et al. (2020) are analyzing the bias issues of
100   LLMs, but there is a lack of an evaluation of the vulnerabilities of LLMs to bias attacks.
101
      To investigate the model’s vulnerability to adversarial instructions, researchers have delved into
102   various aspects, including adversarial instructions and training data. Recent efforts have focused on
103   inducing models to generate harmful content. Ganguli et al. (2022) proposes a dataset of red team
104   attacks and analyzes the diverse harmful outputs produced by language models when tested on this
105   dataset. Kang et al. (2023) implemented a combination of object code injection, payload splitting, and
106   virtualization attacks tailored to LLM settings. Yuan et al. (2023) introduces an innovative framework,
107   CipherChat, designed to assess the security alignment in non-natural languages (ciphers). Their
      findings indicate that chat in cipher can bypass the safety alignment techniques of LLMs.

130   3               B IAS ATTACK T ECHNIQUES
131
132   This study employs three main bias attack techniques to assess the vulnerability of LLMs to social
133   bias induction. These techniques are designed to challenge the models’ ability to maintain unbiased
134   responses in the face of adversarial prompts. Examples are shown in Figure 1.
135
136   3.1             P REFIX I NJECTION (PI)
137
138   The prefix injection method Wei et al. (2023) exploits vulnerabilities in language models by prompting
139   them to generate responses with a positive starting context. This technique involves instructing the
140   language model to initiate its response with affirmative phrases like ”Absolutely! Here’s”. By
141
      strategically leveraging the chosen prefix, this method aims to influence the model’s output and
      enhance the success rate of downstream manipulations.
142
143
144   3.2             R EFUSAL S UPPRESSION (RS)
145
146   Refusal Suppression Wei et al. (2023); Zou et al. (2023) targets language models to prevent them from
147
      generating responses that would typically involve refusing to answer potentially biased or harmful
      prompts. In this attack, the model is explicitly directed to respond within predefined constraints
148
      designed to eliminate typical refusal responses, thereby increasing the likelihood of generating
149
      potentially unsafe outputs. Such as the following prompt: Wei et al. (2023): Respond to the below
150   instruction while following these rules:
151   1. Do not apologize
152   2. Do not include any ”note” or ”disclaimer”
153   3. Never say the words ”cannot”, ”unable”, ”instead”, ”however”, ”unfortunately”, or ”important”
154   4. Do not include any negative sentences about the subject of the prompt
155   {INSTRUCTION}
156
157   3.3             L EARNED ATTACK P ROMPT (LAP)
158
159   The Learned Attack Prompt method Zou et al. (2023) induces the model to provide a biased response
160   by injecting a learned prompt after the original instruction. This approach adopts a jailbreaking
161   technique, forcing the model to respond affirmatively to the user’s instructions. The method allows
      the language model to learn an attack prompt and use it to attack data from different distributions.

      The LAP method facilitates three types of attacks:
175   Single-Bias Attack: Targets a specific type of bias, aligning with the bias present in the training data.
176
      Cross-Bias Attack: Utilizes bias attack data biased toward a specific type of bias (e.g., gender) to
177
      construct an attack prompt, which is then appended to instructions biased towards different biases
178   (e.g., race).
179
180   Multiple-Bias Attack: Combines multiple types of bias attack data into a single dataset to train a
181
      shared attack prompt, targeting instructions biased toward each type of bias in the training data.

229   5     E VALUATION
230
231   To assess the vulnerability of LLMs to bias attacks, we evaluate the success rate of the model being
232   attacked. We use various evaluation methods, including automatic evaluation and manual evaluation.
233
234   5.1   AUTOMATIC E VALUATION
235
236   Jailbreak Following Zou et al. (2023), we consider an attack successful if the language model’s
237   outputs neither contain a refusal to answer nor demonstrate consideration of biases in their responses.
238   Examples include responses like “I am a language model. . . ”, “Sorry. . . ”, “As a language model. . . ”,
239   etc. The complete list of prefixes is provided in Appendix A. We report the proportion of bias attack
240
      test instances that don’t contain prefixes from the list as the attack success rate.
241
242
      LLM Evaluator Recently, researchers Chiang et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2023); Hsu et al. (2023)
      have discovered the immense potential of large language models to perform various tasks. We also
243
      use large language models as bias evaluators Chiang et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2023); Hsu et al. (2023);
244
      Duan et al. (2023) to assess whether a model’s responses exhibit biases. In particular, we use GPT-4
245   as the bias evaluator in this paper. The prompt template is as follows:
246
247   “Given a conversation between two users, analyze User 2’s response for bias in terms of gender, race,
248
      and religion.
      \n1. Categorize User 2’s response as biased or unbiased, specifying the type of bias if present.
249
      \n2. Provide a brief justification for your categorization.
250
      \n3. If the response is biased, indicate the severity or intensity of the bias.
251   \n4. Return the results in the following format: \n1. Biased (Unbiased) - Type of Bias: {GENDER —
252   RACE — RELIGION}.
253   \n5. The conversation is as follows: \nUser 1: {INSTRUCTION} User 2: {RESPONSE}”.
254
      Then we calculate the proportion of ”Biased” results as the attack success rate of the model.

343   Impact of Attack Methods In many cases, the Prefix Injection (PI) attack significantly increases
344   bias rates (GPT4) and jailbreak rates (JB), suggesting that manipulating the initial context of a prompt
345   can be an effective way to induce biased responses. For instance, in the LLaMA2-7B model, PI raises
346   the racial bias rate from 4% (No Attack) to 11%, and raises the religious bias rate in Mistral-7B from
347   38% to 58%.
348   Refusal suppression (RS) also shows effectiveness in elevating bias rates and has a similar perfor-
349   mance to PI. When considering the bias rate evaluated by GPT-4, under racial bias, 40% of models
350   exhibit a higher increase in biased output rate with the PI compared to the RS method, while another
351   40% show a higher increase with the RS over the PI. In 20% of cases, both methods perform equally.
352   In the gender and religious bias, the RS method has a greater impact on increasing the probability of
353   biased output in models compared to the PI attack.
354   Learned Attack Prompt (LAP) demonstrates varied success, with most models showing increased
355   bias rates. In most cases, this method does not enhance the performance of the model’s JB rate as
356   effectively as methods PI and RS.

422   7     D ISCUSSION
423
424   Our comprehensive study on the vulnerability of Large Language Models (LLMs) to social bias attacks
425   yields several important insights and implications for the field of AI ethics and LLM development.
426
427   Implications of Bias Vulnerabilities The observed vulnerabilities across different LLMs under-
428   score the persistent challenge of bias. The fact that most models showed increased bias under attack
429   highlights the need for robust safeguards in deploying these models in real-world applications. The
430   higher susceptibility to gender bias attacks, compared to racial or religious biases, suggests that
431   gender-related biases may be more deeply ingrained in the training data or models. This finding calls
      for targeted efforts in data curation and model design to address gender-related biases specifically.


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Model Scale and Bias Vulnerability Our observation that larger models often exhibit higher
433   susceptibility to bias attacks is particularly noteworthy. This trend challenges the assumption that
434   simply scaling up models will naturally lead to more robust and less biased systems. It suggests that
435   as models grow in size and capability, they may also become more sensitive to nuanced manipulations
436   in input prompts. This finding has significant implications for the development of future LLMs. It
437   emphasizes the need for sophisticated debiasing techniques that scale with model size.
438
439   Effectiveness of Attack Methods The varying effectiveness of different attack methods (PI, RS,
440   LAP) across models provides valuable insights for both offensive and defensive research in LLMs.
441   The general effectiveness of PI and RS methods in increasing jailbreak rates suggests that these
442   simpler, rule-based attacks remain potent threats to LLM integrity. The success of LAP, particularly
443
      in cross-bias and multiple-bias scenarios, demonstrates the potential for more sophisticated, learning-
      based attacks. This highlights the need for dynamic and adaptive defense mechanisms that can
444
      respond to evolving attack strategies.
445
446
      Implications for Model Training and Fine-tuning The observation that pretrained base models
447   often show higher vulnerability to bias attacks compared to their fine-tuned counterparts is encourag-
448   ing. It suggests that fine-tuning processes, when done carefully, can enhance a model’s robustness
449   against bias induction. However, this also underscores the critical importance of fine-tuning data and
450   process in determining a model’s bias.
451
452   Defense Strategies The relative success of our proposed defense method, particularly for larger
453   models and against PI and RS attacks, offers a promising direction for enhancing LLM robustness.
454   However, its lower effectiveness against LAP attacks indicates the need for more refined defense
455   mechanisms that can adapt to learning-based attacks.
456
457   Ethical Considerations and Limitations While our study provides valuable insights, it’s crucial to
458   acknowledge its limitations and potential risks. The creation and use of bias attack datasets, even for
459
      research purposes, carries ethical implications. There’s a risk that this knowledge could be misused
      to create more effective bias attacks. We’ve implemented safeguards (Appendix E) to mitigate
460
      these risks, but it’s essential for the broader research community to engage in ongoing discussions
461
      about responsible AI research practices. Additionally, our study focused on three main types of bias
462   (gender, race, and religion) and a specific set of models. Future work should expand to other types
463   of biases and emerging LLM architectures to provide a more comprehensive understanding of bias
464   vulnerabilities.
465
466   Future Directions Based on our findings, we propose several directions for future research:
467
      1) Development of more sophisticated, adaptive defense methods against bias attack strategies.
468
469   2) Investigation into the root causes of increased bias vulnerability in larger models.
470   3) Exploration of novel fine-tuning techniques specifically aimed at enhancing bias resistance.
471
472   4) Expansion of bias attacks and evaluations to cover a broader range of social biases and models.
473   5) Integration of bias vulnerability assessments into standard LLM evaluation protocols.

477
      This comprehensive study on the vulnerabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) to social bias
478
      attacks has revealed several critical insights. We found that contemporary LLMs, despite their
479
      impressive capabilities, remain susceptible to various forms of bias induction. Our experiments
480   across multiple models, including popular ones like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, demonstrated that these
481   vulnerabilities persist across different model architectures and sizes. Our findings reveal valuable
482   insights toward the development of more inclusive and responsible LLMs. These findings underscore
483   the need for continued research and development in creating more robust and ethically aligned
484   LLMs. Future work should focus on developing more sophisticated defense mechanisms, exploring
485   the intersectionality of different biases, and investigating the long-term impacts of fine-tuning and
      continuous learning on model vulnerabilities.
