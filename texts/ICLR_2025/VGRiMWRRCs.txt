011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The lack of group robustness has emerged as a critical concern in machine
014            learning, as conventional methods like Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) can
015
               achieve high overall accuracy while yielding low worst-group accuracy in minor-
               ity groups. This issue often stems from spurious correlations—non-essential fea-
016
               tures that models exploit as shortcuts—which can compromise deep learning mod-
017
               els in high-stakes applications. Previous works have found that simply retraining
018            classifiers with reweighted datasets or rebalanced samples could significantly im-
019            prove robustness. However, existing methods lack a unified framework, as they
020            often exhibit inconsistent performance across datasets, and sometimes rely heav-
021            ily on hyperparameter tuning, making them impractical for real-world datasets. In
022            this work, we first argue that existing methods can be unified as one Empirical
023            Bayesian framework, where a priori of group information is not specified. We
024            then propose our method Learn from Known Unknowns under this framework by
025            quantifying the epistemic uncertainty of biased ERM models and introducing a
026
               selective reweighting technique for retraining. Our empirical results demonstrate
               that this approach improves group robustness across diverse datasets and reduces
027
               reliance on hyperparameter tuning, offering a more efficient and scalable solution
028
               to spurious correlations.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Machine learning models are notoriously sensitive to spurious correlations—brittle associations be-
034   tween prediction targets and non-essential features of the input, such as background, texture, or
035   secondary objects (Geirhos et al., 2019; Stock & Cisse, 2018; Baker et al., 2018; Beery et al.,
036   2018; Sagawa et al., 2020). These correlations can lead to models achieving high average accu-
037   racy but failing on specific groups, especially when those groups differ from the majority in ways
038   that the model has incorrectly learned to associate with the target label. This problem is generally
039
      described as group robustness in machine learning. A prominent example is in the healthcare do-
      main, where convolutional neural networks trained for pneumonia detection mistakenly relied on
040
      hospital-specific metal tokens in chest X-rays rather than focusing on the pathological features of
041
      the disease (Zech et al., 2018). This problem is exacerbated by imbalanced group representation
042   in the training data and the use of standard Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) (Vapnik, 1999),
043   which can cause models to be overly dependent on these spurious features as predictive shortcuts.
044   As a result, model robustness and interpretability are severely compromised in critical, real-world
045   applications.
046
      To mitigate the effects of spurious correlations, a plethora of approaches have been developed. It
047
      has been shown that simply retraining classifiers with a reweighted dataset or learned failures from a
048   biased classifier can significantly improve group robustness (Liu et al., 2021; Nam et al., 2020a; Yao
049   et al., 2022). A notable line of work is Deep Feature Reweighting (DFR) (Kirichenko et al., 2023;
050   Izmailov et al., 2022) and its variants like SELF (LaBonte et al., 2024b), which achieve state-of-the-
051   art group robustness by retraining the last layer of a model using a group-balanced held-out dataset.
052   While effective in reducing worst-group errors, these methods still face significant practical chal-
053   lenges. Specifically, obtaining accurate group labels requires expert knowledge and labor-intensive
      annotations, which is infeasible for large-scale datasets. Therefore, most recent works (Qiu et al.,


                                                        1

054
      2023; Li et al., 2024) have moved the focus to improving group robustness without any group annota-
055   tions. However, class-balancing techniques during fine-tuning have shown inconsistent performance
056   across datasets (LaBonte et al., 2024a), highlighting the urgent need for a better understanding of
057   this problem and more adaptive methods that learn the latent group information by themselves.
058
      To address these challenges, we propose to unify existing methods under an Empirical Bayes frame-
059
      work, which provides a principled approach to estimate and adjust for unknown group biases (Rob-
060
      bins, 1992; Efron, 2024). In this framework, we estimate its beliefs on group information as latent
061   variables based on observed training data. Methods like JTT (Liu et al., 2021), LfF (Nam et al.,
062   2020a), LISA (Yao et al., 2022), and SELF (LaBonte et al., 2024b) can be interpreted within this
063   framework, where they attempt to mitigate spurious correlations by indirectly inferring group la-
064   bels and reweighting the data. However, because the prior distributions over group assignments
065   are not specified or heuristically chosen, these methods may not achieve the optimal solution under
066   the framework. They often rely heavily on hyperparameter tuning and exhibit inconsistent perfor-
067   mance across different datasets, indicating that they do not fully exploit the posterior distribution
068   over group assignments. This underscores the limitations of current approaches within the Empir-
069
      ical Bayes framework and highlights the need for a more principled method that can effectively
      utilize posterior information to improve robustness.
070
071   Motivated by this, we explore the integration of uncertainty quantification into the proposed Em-
072   pirical Bayes framework to achieve a near-optimal solution under it. Uncertainty quantification,
073   particularly epistemic uncertainty, provides valuable information about the model’s confidence in
074   its predictions due to limited knowledge or data (Kendall & Gal, 2017; Hüllermeier & Waegeman,
075
      2021). By quantifying epistemic uncertainty, we can better identify instances where the model is
      likely relying on spurious correlations, which can inform a more effective reweighting strategy.
076
      Recent advances in evidential deep learning offer scalable methods for estimating epistemic uncer-
077
      tainty without the need for specifying priors (Sensoy et al., 2018; Amini et al., 2020), making them
078   well-suited for our approach within the Empirical Bayes framework.
079
080   To this end, we build on these insights by leveraging epistemic uncertainty to address the limitations
081
      of current group robustness techniques. Specifically, we propose a novel selective reweighting tech-
      nique that quantifies the epistemic uncertainty of biased ERM models and uses this information to
082
      create a self-adaptive held-out set for retraining. Our approach not only enhances model robustness
083
      across various datasets but also reduces reliance on hyperparameter tuning, offering a more scalable
084   and generalizable solution to addressing spurious correlations.
085
086   In summary, we make the following contributions:
087
             • We unify current popular group robustness methods under the Empirical Bayes framework,
088            where the group information is not given or from a held-out set.
089
             • Based on this framework, we propose the method Learn from Known Unknowns that
090
               achieves optimal performance by inferring the posterior distribution through epistemic un-
091            certainty quantification on the ERM model.
092
             • We demonstrate through empirical results that our method improves robustness across di-
093
               verse datasets using the uncertainty-informed group estimation and reduces dependence on
094            hyperparameter tuning.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
098
099
      Group Robustness Methods. Group robustness methods focus on training models that perform
      consistently across predefined groups within a dataset, especially when some groups are underrep-
100
      resented or prone to spurious correlations. Traditional methods like Group Distributionally Robust
101
      Optimization (Group DRO) (Sagawa et al., 2020) utilize explicit group annotations to minimize
102   worst-case group loss, while Deep Feature Reweighting (DFR) (Kirichenko et al., 2023) retrains
103   the final layer using a group-balanced dataset to reduce reliance on spurious features. In scenarios
104   without group labels, approaches such as Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) (Arjovsky et al., 2019)
105   and Learning from Failure (LfF) (Nam et al., 2020a) attempt to learn invariant representations or use
106   failure patterns to mitigate spurious correlations. However, these methods often depend on strong
107   assumptions or heuristics that may not generalize well, highlighting the need for adaptive techniques
      that can construct reliable fine-tuning sets without group annotations (LaBonte et al., 2024a). Our


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      work addresses this gap by leveraging epistemic uncertainty to identify samples affected by spurious
109   correlations, enabling a self-adaptive retraining process.
110
111   Empirical Bayes Methods. Empirical Bayes (EB) methods combine frequentist and Bayesian
112   ideas by estimating priors directly from data, allowing for adaptive inference without a fully spec-
113   ified prior (Robbins, 1992). Widely used in areas like shrinkage estimation (James & Stein, 1992)
114   and false discovery rates (Efron, 2024), EB methods excel in settings where the distribution of some
115   random variables are not obtainable and latent. Thus, it must be inferred from observations. One
116   example would be the James-Stein estimator (James & Stein, 1992) which exemplifies the EB prin-
117   ciple by shrinking estimates toward a common mean to reduce variance. In group robustness, EB
118
      provides a natural framework for learning group-specific parameters without explicit group labels.
      Methods like JTT (Liu et al., 2021) and LfF (Nam et al., 2020a) implicitly use empirical Bayes-like
119
      principles, updating group information based on model failures. Our work formally gives an Empir-
120
      ical Bayes Framework to summarize previous methods and extends this perspective by leveraging
121   epistemic uncertainty to guide group-balanced training set reconstruction, improving robustness to
122   spurious correlations across groups without relying on additional annotations.
123
124   Uncertainty Quantification. Uncertainty quantification is crucial for assessing the reliability of
125   deep learning models, particularly in safety-critical applications. It is typically divided into aleatoric
126   uncertainty, arising from inherent data noise, and epistemic uncertainty, stemming from limited
127   knowledge about model parameters (Kendall & Gal, 2017). Methods like Bayesian Neural Net-
128   works (Blundell et al., 2015), Monte Carlo Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), and Deep Ensem-
129   bles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) estimate uncertainty with multiple model parameters and can
130   be computationally intensive. Evidential deep learning (Sensoy et al., 2018) offers an efficient yet
131
      effective alternative by modeling evidence as parameters of a Dirichlet distribution, capturing both
      uncertainties without the need for sampling or ensembles. While uncertainty quantification has been
132
      applied to model calibration, active learning, and out-of-distribution detection, its use in improving
133
      group robustness is less explored. Building on these insights, our method employs epistemic uncer-
134   tainty to selectively reweight samples during retraining, reducing the model’s reliance on spurious
135   features without requiring explicit group labels or extensive hyperparameter tuning.

      Building upon the insights from the Empirical Bayesian framework presented in the previous sec-
      tion, we explore a probabilistic approach to estimate p̂(g | x, θ) instead of relying solely on rule-
257
      based methods. To this end, we propose a novel method, Learn from Known Unknowns, which
258
      leverages model uncertainty to estimate latent group probabilities and addresses selection biases in-
259   herent in training data. We then utilize these uncertainty-informed empirical priors to retrain the
260   ERM model under the Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) through reweighting. By adopting an
261   Empirical Bayesian perspective, the proposed method improves robustness across all groups with-
262   out requiring explicit group labels.

      5     E XPERIMENTS
345
346   We evaluate Learn from Known Unknowns on a range of existing benchmarks and provide additional
347   results to further support our motivation and methodology designs.

349   5.1   DATASETS
350
351   We first describe five datasets that we use in the experiments. These datasets are known for ex-
352   hibiting poor worst-group performance due to spurious correlations, as highlighted in prior works.
353   Our evaluation focuses on group robustness across both vision and language tasks: (1) Colored
354   MNIST (Arjovsky et al., 2019) is a variant of the MNIST dataset for image classification where
355   the digit images are colored to introduce spurious correlations between the color and the class label.
356
      (2) Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2020) is an image classification dataset with waterbird and landbird
      classes selected from the CUB dataset Welinder et al. (2010). The spurious attribute is the water
357
      or land backgrounds from the Places dataset Zhou et al. (2017), where more landbirds are present
358
      on land backgrounds than waterbirds, and vice versa. (3) CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) is an image
359   classification with celebrity faces. The task is to identify whether the celebrity is blonde or not.
360   The spurious attribute is gender where the majority group is blonde women in the training set. (4)
361   MultiNLI (Williams et al., 2017) is a large-scale natural language inference dataset that contains
362   sentence pairs across multiple genres, annotated with entailment labels (entailment, contradiction,
363   or neutral). The spurious attribute involves syntactic or lexical patterns that correlate with specific
364   labels. (5) CivilComments (Borkan et al., 2019) is a text classification dataset sourced from online
365   comments. The task is to predict whether a comment is toxic or non-toxic. The spurious attributes
366   include demographic identity terms (e.g., gender, race) that correlate with the label.
367
368   5.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
369

526   6    C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we introduce a new Empirical Bayesian perspective to enhance the understanding of
529   group robustness. Our framework demonstrates that leveraging existing observations from both the
530   ERM model and the dataset enables effective estimation of latent group information. Building on
531   this, we propose an uncertainty-informed group robustness method, Learn from Known Unknowns,
532   which utilizes epistemic uncertainty from biased ERM models to inform the retraining process.
533   Extensive experiments demonstrate the efficacy of our approach, achieving compelling worst-group
534
      performance across diverse datasets. We believe this work provides valuable insights into developing
      more adaptive and scalable methods to improve group robustness in real-world applications.
