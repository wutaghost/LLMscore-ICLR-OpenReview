009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this study, we introduce the Explicitly Constrained Agent (EC-Agent), a novel
012            approach designed to enhance the task-solving capabilities of Large Language
013            Models (LLMs). Unlike existing multi-agent systems that depend on agents evalu-
014            ating tasks from different perspectives, EC-Agent explicitly imposes task-oriented
015            constraints for LLMs. Our observations are two-fold: first, assigning agents to
016            sub-tasks with defined responsibilities implicitly sets constraints; second, these
017            multi-agent systems often struggle with accurately assigning agents to sub-tasks,
018
               leading to overlapping duties and potential misguidance. In contrast, our single-
               agent system, driven by explicit methods and constraints, provides LLMs with
019
               detailed prompts, resulting in more precise responses. EC-Agent consists of two
020
               stages: a Reasoning Stage and a Summary Stage. 1) In the Reasoning Stage,
021            three modules are proposed: Explicit Method, Explicit Constraint, and Execution.
022            Specifically, LLMs utilize the Explicit Method and Constraint modules to ana-
023            lyze the task type and specific rules, generating multiple suitable methods and
024            constraints. Subsequently, the Execution module combines these methods and
025            constraints to produce and output possible solutions. 2) In the Summary Stage,
026            LLMs evaluate the multiple reasoning processes and results from the previous
027            step. They rectify any inconsistencies, summarize the information, and output the
028            final result. Experimental results demonstrate that EC-Agent outperforms previous
029
               methods across a variety of tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      The widespread applications of Large Language Models (LLMs) has led to transformative advances
034
      in various domains. However, the potential of LLMs is still under-exploited, especially for high-
035
      complexity tasks such as creative writing and high-level work plan design according to Qin et al.
036   (2023), where the LLMs suffer a lot from issues related to the strength of knowledge and reasoning
037   caused by problems such as hallucination (Bang et al., 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023), lack of slow
038   thinking (Sloman, 1996; Lin et al., 2023a) and so on.
039
      Recent research has attempted to address these challenges by introducing structured intermediate
040
      steps and leveraging the ability of multi-persona systems. The Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al.,
041
      2023) approach improves LLM performance by breaking down the task into smaller, easier handle
042   steps, leading to more thorough reasoning at each stage. Similarly, the Tree of Thought (ToT) (Yao
043   et al., 2023a) approach uses a branching strategy to explore multiple potential solutions at the same
044   time, thereby increasing the chances of finding the right and best result. In addition, the Self-Planner
045   Prompt (SPP) (Wang et al., 2023b) study illustrates the effectiveness of dynamically allocating agents
046   in the LLM based on specific task inputs and user-defined requirements. This approach enables
047   the model to generate more rational and contextually appropriate outputs by adapting the reasoning
048   process to the task’s unique demands.
049   However, our observation is that multi-agent systems implicitly set constraints based on the task and
050   rely on agents to evaluate the task from different perspectives. While these systems aim to improve
051   instruction generation by introducing agents with distinct roles and responsibilities, they face practical
052   challenges. Accurately generating executable plans for the LLM and allocating agents to specialized
053   sub-tasks and defining their responsibilities can be difficult, potentially leading to overlapping duties.
      Furthermore, the effectiveness of these solutions depends on the roles of the generated agents and

071   To address these issues, we propose the EC-Agent, a novel approach that leverages a single-agent
072   system driven by explicit method and constraints. By providing LLMs with prompts that combine
073   explicit method and constraints, our approach provides more accurate and efficient reasoning during
074   task-solving.
075   To be more specific, EC-Agent involves two stages: the Reasoning Stage and the Summary Stage. The
076   Reasoning Stage consists of three modules: the explicit method generation module (EM), the explicit
077   constraints generation module (EC), and the execution module. The input task is first analyzed to
078   infer a suitable method for solving it, along with explicit and implicit constraints, ensuring the model
079   accurately identifies the necessary conditions for task solutions. Following this, EC-Agent guides
080   the LLMs in combining the task description with the generated method and explicit constraints to
081
      formulate a comprehensive execution plan. This plan, which includes a detailed multi-step work
      strategy, significantly enhances the LLMs’ reasoning accuracy. In the Summary Stage, EC-Agent
082
      reviews the outputs of the execution process. It prompts the LLMs to rectify and summarize the
083
      results based on the initial task description, method, and constraints, ensuring a high-quality final
084   solution.
085
086   We further conduct experiments on various tasks, including ‘Trivia Creative Writing,’ ‘Logic Grid
087
      Puzzles,’ ‘Codenames Collaborative,’ and ‘Code Generation.’ EC-Agent improves the performance
      of LLMs compared with standard prompting and outperforms previous methods.
088
089   In summary, EC-Agent is an intelligent agent driven by explicit task constraints and planning. It
090   effectively solves general tasks while avoiding some pitfalls associated with multi-role agent systems.
091   Our main contributions are as follows:
092
            1. EC-Agent combines the Reasoning Stage and the Summary Stage in a structured pipeline
093
               that is simple yet ensures thorough and systematic task-solving.
094
095         2. By incorporating explicit method and constraints, EC-Agent provides clear and direct
096            guidance to LLMs, improving the accuracy and reliability of their outputs.
097         3. Experimental results show that EC-Agent improves LLM performance compared to standard
098            prompting and outperforms previous methods with performance margins on various tasks.

123

129   self-refine based on results. Wu et al. (2023) allows users to create and manage multiple autonomous
130   agents to collaboratively complete complex tasks. Wang et al. (2023b) proposes a reasoning method

      different ability to evaluate and improve reasoning results multiple times. It endows LLMs with

      of an environment for external feedback. However, these methods depend on the accuracy of the LLM

      in generating agents. Hallucinations by the LLM, such as creating mismatched agents or making

172   3     M ETHOD
173
174   In this section, we formally introduce EC-Agent, which consists of two stages: Reasoning and
175   Summary, as demonstrated in Figure 1. We begin by revisiting the definition of task-solving for
176   LLM agents, followed by a detailed explanation of EC-Agent, including its key components and the
177   complete pipeline.
178   Given an input instruction x and a model M, if we denote the final output to be y, then the Standard
179   Prompting (Equ. 1) and Chain-Of-Thought (Equ. 2) can be formulated as:
180
181
182
                                                      y = M(x)                                                  (1)
183
                                            y = M(pcot |x|{z1 , z2 , .., zn })                                  (2)
184
185   where pcot is the CoT prompt and z1 , z2 , ...zn are the implicit intermediate steps.
186   In contrast, our EC-Agent can be divided into two stages: ‘Reasoning’ and ‘Summary.’ In the
187   ‘Reasoning’ stage, EC-Agent aims to identify and generate suitable explicit methods and explicit
188   constraints from user input x. Specifically, it generates multiple methods suitable for solving the
189   task, represented as F(x) = (f1 , f2 , f3 , ...), along with a set of constraints C(x) = (c1 , c2 , c3 , ...),
190   all based on user input x. Next, results are generated based on F(x) and C(x), denoted as
191   E(f, c) = (e1 , e2 , e3 , ...en ), where (e1 , e2 , e3 , ...en ) represents the implicit execution sequence.
192   In the ‘Summary’ stage, it includes both a review section and a summary section. The review section,
193
      denoted as A(e, c) = (a1 , a2 , a3 , ...an ), is used to examine and adjust the reasoning process as well
      as the validity of the results. The summary section, denoted as S(a, c), integrates all reasoning
194
      processes and answers, and based on the task and constraints, generates the final result. The system
195
      can be represented as:
196
197                              y = M(pec |x|F(x)|C(x)|E(f, c)|A(e, c)|S(a, c))                                (3)
198
199   We now provide details for each stage of EC-Agent. Detailed prompts of each module can be found
200
      in Appendix A.
201
202   3.1     R EASONING S TAGE
203
204   The first stage of EC-Agent is reasoning, where it can generate multiple possible solutions given
205
      user input x. At the core of this stage are the creation of methods and constraints with respect to
      the tasks. This is then followed by an execution step to develop the solutions. We now elaborate on
206
      the detailed functionality of the three main components in reasoning: Explicit Method Generation,
207
      Explicit Constraints Generation, and Execution.
208
209
      3.1.1    E XPLICIT M ETHOD G ENERATION
210
211   A core module of EC-Agent is the generation of explicit methods. In the first step of the reasoning
212   stage, EC-Agent guides the LLM to search for and generate one or more methods suitable for the
213   given task based on the input task information. These methods are then output in an explicit form
214   as execution plans. During this phase, we prompt the LLM to generate general methods rather than
215   those specifically tailored to the task requirements. This process is represented as F(x), and the
      generated results are denoted as (f1 , f2 , f3 , ...).


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      3.1.2     E XPLICIT C ONSTRAINTS G ENERATION
217
218   Another core module of EC-Agent is the generation of explicit constraints. Previous work has
219   shown that multi-agent systems benefit from evaluating outputs based on the roles of the agents. We
220   posit that providing constraints to LLMs during problem-solving can achieve similar performance
221   enhancements, with explicit constraints offering direct and efficient guidance. Specifically, these
222   explicit constraints are derived from two aspects:
223
224           1. Explicit Requirements from the Input Task: This includes specific instructions embedded
225              within the task, such as "5 or 10 common-sense questions in Trivia Creative Writing tasks"
226              or "room numbers with person preferences in Logic Grid Puzzle tasks."
227           2. Implicit Constraints Related to the Task: This includes inherent requirements necessary for
228              the task’s execution, such as "validity checks for input values in code generation tasks."
229
230   This process is represented as C(x), and its output results are as (c1 , c2 , c3 , ...).
231
232
233
      3.1.3     E XECUTION
234
      Based on the generated F(x) and C(x), EC-Agent further guides the LLMs to perform reasoning
235   execution. This involves synthesizing the current task description with the generated methods and
236   constraints, and then outputting the reasoning results, denote as E(f, c).
237
238
      To enhance the robustness of EC-Agent, we employ a multi-sampling approach. This approach allows
      the LLMs to execute the Reasoning Stage multiple times, generating several results for the task. This
239
      increases the likelihood of producing feasible and accurate solutions. To maintain a balance between
240
      efficiency and cost, we use 2 samples in our experiments.
241
242
243   3.2     S UMMARY S TAGE
244
245   Following the execution results from the Reasoning stage, the Summary stage is employed to produce
246
      the final solution. This stage consists of two main steps: Review and Conclusion. In the Review
      step, each execution plan’s results are evaluated independently to ensure correctness, consistency,
247
      and adherence to the constraints. In the Conclusion step, the LLMs synthesize all solutions and their
248
      evaluations, summarizing the outcomes into a coherent and accurate final result.
249
250
251   3.2.1     R EVIEW
252
       In this step, a list of ‘common errors for the task’ is generated by combining the ‘task,’ ‘method,’ and
253
      ‘constraints.’ This is followed by a reflection and correction of the input "reasoning information and
254    results," and the corrected "reasoning process and results" are re-output, denoted as A(e, c).
255
256
257
      3.2.2     C ONCLUSION
258
      The second step is ‘Conclusion,’ denoted as S(a, c). We instruct the LLMs to summarize and select
259   based on the task description and all reasoning records (each containing a complete reasoning process
260   and outcome), discarding alternative answers with flawed logical reasoning, ultimately providing the
261   final result. Throughout this process, we observe that the LLMs pay more attention to differences and
262   the logic in the reasoning process. Based on this information, they summarize and select, leading to a
263   performance improvement.

403   4.5   A BLATION S TUDIES
404
405   We further conducted ablation studies on different modules of EC-Agent to evaluate the impact of
406
      each component on overall performance. The results of these evaluations are presented in Table 6.
407
408   Explicit Method. As shown in “EM” row in Table 6, Guiding the LLM to apply explicit methods in
409
      the reasoning process can efficiently enhances outcomes. This aligns with human cognitive processes,
      where a clear understanding of the task and selecting appropriate methods are crucial for successful
410
      completion.
411
412
      Explicit Constraints. In our approach, we enhance the performance of LLMs by replacing the
413
      generation of dynamic role agents with explicit conditional constraints. The impact of the EC module
414
      on the results can be seen in the row titled ‘EC’ in Table 6. We also observer that the integration of
415   explicit conditional constraints offers key advantages:
416
417         1. It eliminates the uncertainty and incompleteness in dynamic role generation.
418
            2. It directs LLMs to focus more precisely on the task during the reasoning process. (Refer to
419
               Task 3 in Appendix B and Table 6)
420
421
      Multi Samples. Additionally, we also tested the hypothesis: “Can the performance of problem-
422
      solving be improved by allowing the LLM to perform task planning multiple times under limited
423   model ability?” by using a Multi Samples strategy. The results showed that while the model’s
424   reasoning performance benefits from additional repetitions of task planning, the associated reasoning
425   cost also increases. (Refer to ‘EM + EC + Samples’ row in Table 6).
426
427   Result Review. Finally, we also evaluated the effects of ‘self-summarization and reflection’ on
428   single and multiple reasoning results across various tests. The results indicated that the effects
429   of ‘summarization and reflection’ varied across different tasks, depending on the method used to
430   determine task outcomes. When task outcomes can be assessed through strict logical reasoning, this
431   approach can enhance reasoning results; conversely, its effectiveness is weaker when the assessment
      method is less rigorous. (Refer to ‘EM + EC + Review’ row in Table 6).

475   to that of the ‘EM + EC’ combination (‘EM + EC’ is more focused on the specific requirements
476   of the tasks). Therefore, the ‘EM + EC’ combination offers the better cost-effectiveness (including
477   support for task diversity, reasoning results, reasoning efficiency, and computational cost). If the
478   better performance is needed, running the complete EC-Agent Pipeline is recommended.

483   In conclusion, our study demonstrates that integrating explicit conditional constraints enhances LLMs
484   performance by aligning model reasoning more closely with structured task execution method. Our
485   findings also suggest that integration of explicit constraints and optimized task planning could pave
      the way for more robust and efficient LLMs applications in complex problem-solving scenarios.
