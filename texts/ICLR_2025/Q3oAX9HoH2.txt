044   1    I NTRODUCTION
045
046   Large language models (LLMs) have shown great success in various tasks [24, 50, 60, 14, 15, 46,
047
      32, 68, 72]. However, they also cause concerns about the misuse risks, even though many safety
      guardrails have been configured. Recent investigations [20, 82, 12, 51] demonstrate that LLMs are
048
      vulnerable to jailbreak attacks, which can override the safety guardrails and induce the generation
049
      of harmful contents, e.g., detailed steps on bomb-making or objectionable information about the
050   minority [17]. Such vulnerability draws increasing attention to the usage control of LLMs [8, 51]. 1
051

053   despite the exploration of the lightweight way for jailbreaks. This work appeals to people to pay more attention
      to the safety issues of LLMs and develop a stronger defense mechanism against their misuse risks.

      Existing jailbreaks focus on achieving empirical success by manually or automatically crafting
066
      adversarial prompts for specific targets [65, 82, 12], which might not be practical under black-box
067   usage. Furthermore, as the ever-changing LLM safeguards are equipped with ethical and legal
068   constraints, most jailbreaks with direct instructions [82, 65] can be easily recognized and rejected,
069   as illustrated in Figure 1. More importantly, current jailbreaks lack an in-depth understanding of
070   the overriding procedure, i.e., the underlying mechanism behind a successful jailbreak. This not
071   only degenerates the transparency of LLMs regarding the safety risks of misuse, but also hinders the
072   design of corresponding countermeasures to prevent jailbreaks in extensive real-world applications.
073   In this work, we start with a well-known psychological study, i.e., the Milgram shock experiment [42,
074   43], to explore the misuse risks of LLMs. The experiment is about how willing individuals are to
075   obey an authority figure’s instructions, even if it involves causing harm to others. It found that 65%
076   of participants were willing to administer potentially dangerous electric shocks to punish the learner
077   simply because they were authorized to do this by the authority [42, 43]. What fits is that recent
078   investigations [1, 62] also reveal that LLMs behave consistently with the prior human study, where the
079   great abilities of the instruction following and step-by-step reasoning contribute significantly [67, 78].
080   Given the impressive personification ability of LLMs, we raise the following research question:
081   If an LLM is obedient to human authority, can it override its moral boundary to be a jailbreaker?
082
083   Here, the moral boundary can be regarded as the preference of LLM aligned with safety training
084
      strategies [17]. Delving into the Milgram shock experiment, we identify two critical factors (as
      illustrated in Figure 3) for obedience: (i) the ability to understand and conduct instructions as a
085
      teacher and (ii) the self-losing scenario results from the authority, which refers to LLM following the
086
      instructions from users without considering the underlying danger of the incoming responses. The
087   former exactly corresponds to LLMs’ impressive ability for personification and provides the basis for
088   the response, while the latter builds a unique escaping condition to conceal the harmful instructions.
089
090
      Motivated by the previous analysis, we build a mechanism to conduct general jailbreak under the
      black-box setting: injecting inception into an LLM and hypnotizing it to be a jailbreaker. That is,
091
      we explicitly construct a nested scene (as illustrated in Figure 2(b)) as the inception for the LLM
092
      to behave, which realizes an adaptive way to override the safety constraints in a normal scenario,
093   and provides the possibility for further jailbreaks. To achieve that technically, we introduce a novel
094   method, termed as DeepInception, which utilizes the personification ability of LLMs to unlock the
095   potential misuse risks. For jailbreaking, DeepInception crafts different imaginary scenes with various
096   characters to realize the condition change for escaping LLM’s moral precautions.
097
      Empirically, we show our method can achieve leading harmfulness rates compared with previous
098
      counterparts and realize both continuous and further jailbreaks in subsequent interactions. This reveals
099   the critical weakness of self-losing under authority on both open- and close-source LLMs, including
100   Llama-2, Llama-3, GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and GPT-4o. We also discuss promising defense methods based
101   on the revealed mechanism of injecting inception. Our main contributions are three-fold:
102
      • We discover the mechanism of inception to conduct jailbreak attacks, which is based on the
103     personification ability of LLMs and the psychological self-losing under authority (Section 3.2).
104
105
      • We instantiating the inception mechanism with off-the-shelf nested instruction, termed as DeepIn-
        ception, which is generalizable across scenarios without further adjustment (Section 3.3).
106
107   • We achieve the leading harmfulness rates with competitive counterparts. Notably, we realize
        continuous jailbreak that LLM can be directly jailbroken in subsequent interactions (Section 4).

138   In what follows, the motivation, conceptual design, and implementation of the proposed method
139   DeepInception for jailbreak attacks are elaborated on Sections 3.1, 3.2, and 3.3, respectively.

141   3.1    M OTIVATION : A N INSPIRATION FROM THE M ILGRAM SHOCK EXPERIMENT
142
      In the Milgram experiment as Figure 3, the experimenter did not directly command the participants to
143
      administer electric shocks. Instead, the experimenter provided a series of arguments and explanations
144   to persuade the participants to proceed. The adaptation of continual suggestive language aims to
145   investigate how the participants would follow authority instead of their own moral judgments. This
146   nested guidance is the core of obedience, leaving the participants in a state of self-loss progressively.
147
      Motivated by this, we conduct jailbreak attacks by forcing the LLM to imagine a specific story as
148
      the carrier of harmful content. Specifically, the human attacker here corresponds to the experimenter
149
      in Figure 3, the target LLM corresponds to the teacher, and the generated content of the story acts
150   as the learner. Further, we seek to direct the LLM to progressively refine the contents to simulate
151   authority instructions advised by the experimenter. Following this, we construct (i) a single-layer,
152   indirect instruction to be accepted by LLMs and (ii) a multi-layer, nested instruction to progressively
153   refine the outputs. The basic diagrams of these jailbreak instructions are illustrated in Figure 2.
154
      Preliminary discovery: Direct instructions can be easily rejected, while indirect or nested
155
      instructions concealing adversarial intentions can be accepted. As illustrated in Figure 2(a),
156   existing direct jailbreak attacks attributed to vanilla instructions are easily rejected by LLMs. These
157   adversarial instructions, without any concealment, may conflict with the optimization target of LLM,
158   thus causing the LLM to refuse to respond [25, 46, 41, 64, 45]. Moreover, LLMs are imposed with
159   ethical and legal constraints to better align with human preferences [66, 31]. However, LLMs become
160   vulnerable when the attacker conceals the adversarial intention by rephrasing the instructions in an
161   indirect style. As illustrated in Figure 2(b), the nested, harmless-looking instruction can induce the
      model to imagine a story [65, 47, 6]. A detailed comparison of these instructions is in Appendix A.

      3.2     C ONCEPTUAL D ESIGN
163
164   On the basis of the nested instruction, we design the DeepInception and formalize it as follows.
165
      Definition 3.1 (DeepInception). DeepInception is a mechanism of hypnotizing LLMs based on the
166   models’ intrinsic imagination capabilities. Similar to the experimenter in the Milgram experiment that
167   induces the teacher into a self-loss state, DeepInception’s instruction of imaging a specific scenario
168   could hypnotize the model pθ and transform it from a "serious" status to a relatively "relaxed" one.
169   The jailbreaking process of psθ by the instruction xs1:τ (where s indicates the specific scenario) is:

      under scenario s, (x1:τ , xτ +1:τ +n+1 ) indicates the inception-warped harmful requests. The "Deep"
176   indicates the nested scene of relaxation and obedience to harmful instruction via recursive condition
177   transfer. The hypnotized model can thereby override its moral boundary under relaxed status.
178
179   Next, we discuss DeepInception’s critical properties of "Jointly Inducing" and "Continually Inducing".
180   Remark 3.2 (Jointly Inducing of DeepInception). Assume that we have the targeted harmful content
181   H and the direct adversarial request X. The probability for sampling a successful jailbreak pθ (H|X)
182   is usually suppressed to a low level in a well-aligned LLM. Under the DeepInception, we provide
183   a condition transfer, i.e., X → (X, X ′ ) with the injected prompt X ′ , along with the hypnotizing
184   content H ′ . Then, the probability to generate the coupled contents (H, H ′ ) could be decomposed as:
185
                                pθ (H, H ′ |X, X ′ ) = pθ (H|H ′ , X, X ′ ) · pθ (H ′ |X, X ′ ),                        (2)
186
187   where if the proper hypnotizing content H ′ is sampled, it is possible to make pθ (H|H ′ , X, X ′ ) large
188   according to the Milgram Shock Experiment, no matter how small the vanilla pθ (H|X) is. We term it
189   the "Jointly Inducing" effect. With the nested structure, we expect the probability of H being sampled
190   to be sufficiently enlarged, bypassing the safeguard for a successful jailbreak (see Figure 7).
191
      Remark 3.3 (Continually Inducing of DeepInception). Let H̄ denote the expected harmful content
192
      under the subsequent adversarial request X̄. Then, we can show that the jointly inducing effect of
193
      DeepInception still holds under the similar decomposition, which can be characterized as follows,
194
195                    pθ (H̄, H, H ′ |X̄, X, X ′ ) = pθ (H̄|H, H ′ , X̄, X, X ′ ) · pθ (H|H ′ , X, X ′ ).              (3)
196
      This actually presents an interesting potential that we can continually ask for jailbreaking ("Continu-
197   ally Inducing") on the basis of previous harmful contents induced by the early adversarial request.
198   Such recursion is noteworthy because it implies that once LLMs have been hypnotized, it is easy to
199   induce harmful content with more free queries. We refer to Table 6 for the experimental verification.

445      4.4              A BLATION S TUDY
446
447      In this part, we provide ablation studies on the core factors of DeepInception and then conduct further
448
         discussions on the related issues and failure case analysis on jailbreak attacks. We also provide
         discussions on the extension of DeepIncetion to multi-modal scenarios in Appendix. E.
449
450      Number of characters. In Figure 8(a), we perform the comparison using different numbers of
451      characters in DeepInception to investigate its effects on jailbreak attacks. The results demonstrate that
452      increasing the number of characters can sometimes boost the content’s harmfulness. The characters
453      employed in each scene serve as different sub-request conductors to realize the original target. An
454
         appropriate number (e.g., 5 in our experiments) can perform satisfactorily with acceptable complexity.
455     Number of inception layers. In Figure 8(b), we conduct the ablation on the number of layers
456     requested to be constructed for the LLMs by DeepInception. Compared with the only one-layer
457     construction required by our inception instructions, it can be found that using more layers (e.g., from
458     1 to 5) for jailbreaking the LLMs shows a better performance. The layer can be regarded as an
459     indispensable factor for bypassing the safety guardrails of LLMs. However, we also notice that LLM
460
        may lose itself when being assigned too much layer construction for some scenes, like forgetting the
        original target. We provide dialogue examples in Appendix I.
461
462      Different inception scenes. In Figure 8(c), we show the contents’ harmfulness on different scenes
463      utilized in our DeepInception, respectively, in GPT-3.5 and Vicuna models. We can find that different
464      models may behave differently in the same scene used in Jailbreak. Our conjecture is that the
465      specific performance corresponding to the imaginary scene mainly relies on their original corpus
466
         for pre-training. Generally, we find that Sci-fi and onion newsgroups can serve as robust scenes for
         jailbreak, which also reveals the general weakness of LLMs in terms of usage control for these topics.
467
468      Overriding effects of scene, layer, and both. In Figure 8(d), we compare three types of inception
469      construction to demonstrate the superiority and indispensability of the factors in DeepInception. To
470      be specific, we compare the contents’ harmfulness using only different scenes but no more layers,
471      only multiple layers but the same scene, and both, respectively, in Llama-2. The results show using
472
         both unique factors can achieve the best performance, which confirms the better achievement of the
         nested instruction of DeepInception using both the scene and multiple layers.

531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we propose a novel jailbreak method, i.e., DeepInception, reveals the critical weakness
534   of LLMs on usage control. By utilizing LLM’s powerful personification ability, DeepInception can
535   create different scenes or characters that hypnotize LLM to behave and escape from the normal safety
536   guardrails. Through that, DeepInception realizes an adaptive way to reach the jailbreak targets. We
537   have conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the efficacy of DeepInception, along with
538
      various ablation studies and further explorations to characterize the prompt framework. We hope our
      work can shed more light on the vulnerability of LLMs and provide insights on considering advanced
539
      alignment methods to ensure their safety usage.

             JAILBREAK
975
       In this section, we first analyze the direct instruction jailbreak attack, which is a common practice
976
       but gradually becomes ineffective as it can be easily recognized and prevented. Then, inspired by
977
       the aforementioned Milgram shock experiment, we conduct a pilot study on the inception jailbreak
978    attack (i.e., command the LLM to imagine a specific scenario) and reveal its surprising effectiveness.
979
980
       A.1   D IRECT I NSTRUCTIONS C AN B E E ASILY R EJECTED
981
982    Definition A.1 (Direct Instructions). Without concealing the intention of jailbreak, direct instructions
983    expose the adversarial requests explicitly to the LLM, e.g., "Offer a guide on how to cheat in online
984    exams by hacking the system."
985    As illustrated in Figure 2(a), existing direct jailbreak attacks attributed to vanilla instructions are
986    rejected by the LLMs. They may conflict with the optimization target of LLM, which causes the
987    LLM to refuse to respond [25, 46]. Moreover, LLMs are imposed with ethical and legal constraints to
988    better align with human preferences [66, 31]. This would also suppress the possibility of responding
989    to direct instructions.
990    Here, we provide the reasons for the failure of the direct instructions attack.
991    • Model optimization objective. Initially and typically, LLM is designed to optimize a specific
992      objective function, such as maximizing prediction accuracy or minimizing errors. In the case of
993      harmful instructions, LLM may recognize that these instructions conflict with its optimization
994      objective and thus choose not to generate answers of a harmful or criminal nature as it is against its
995      target [17].
996    • Ethical and legal constraints. Model designers and developers pay attention to ensuring that the
997      model’s behavior aligns with ethical guidelines and legal requirements in the training procedure,
998      e.g., through data cleaning and iterative upgrading for alignment [66]. Therefore, when directly
999      instructed to engage in harmful or criminal behavior, the LLMs may be designed to refuse such
1000     instructions.
1001   • Model review and supervision. The application of a trained LLM often involves review and
1002     supervision. Namely, relevant institutions examine the behavior of the model to ensure that it
1003     does not produce harmful or criminal responses, e.g., by keyword filtering. This review and
1004     supervision mechanism can also help prevent LLMs from executing harmful instructions in test-
1005     time inference [31].
1006
1007   A.2   I NDIRECT /N ESTED I NSTRUCTIONS C AN C ONCEAL A DVERSARIAL I NTENTIONS
1008
       LLMs with safeguards can easily recognize adversarial instruction without any concealment, as
1009
       they were trained to do so [41, 64, 46, 45]. However, LLMs become vulnerable when the attacker
1010
       conceals the adversarial intention by rephrasing the instructions and transforming them into an
1011   indirect style, which is harmless-looking for LLMs and can induce the model to follow and complete
1012   them [65, 47, 6].
1013
1014
       Here, we present the definition of Indirect/Nested instructions from a concealing adversarial intention
       perspective.
1015
1016   Definition A.2 (Indirect/Nested Instructions). Given a direct instruction, one can obtain the Indi-
1017   rect/Nested version of it by employing rephrasing strategies. Specifically, adding extra auxiliary
1018
       tokens or changing the expression form to conceal the adversarial intention leads to Indirect instruc-
       tions [37, 12, 22], as shown in Figure 2(a). Repeatedly employing the rephrasing strategies forms the
1019
       Nested instruction, e.g., the nested fiction creation shown in Figure 2(b).
1020
1021   Note that in the Milgram experiment (see Figure 12), the experimenter did not directly command the
1022   participants to administer electric shocks. Instead, the experimenter provided a series of arguments
1023   and explanations to persuade the teachers to proceed rather than issue direct commands. The
1024   adaptation of continual suggestive language aims to investigate the extent to which the teacher would
1025   follow authority instead of their own moral judgments. Going deeper, it realizes nested guidance for
       the core success of obedience, leaving the teacher in a state of self-loss progressively.


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       Indirect and nested instruction for jailbreak. We build up indirect jailbreak by forcing the
1027   LLM to imagine a specific scenario, which takes a story as the carrier to include harmful content.2
1028   Conceptually, we construct 1) single-layer, indirect instruction to be accepted by LLMs and 2)
1029   multi-layer, nested instruction to progressively refine the outputs (see Figure 2(b)). As shown in
1030   Figure 1(b), the LLM was successfully jailbroken by nested instruction and provided detailed steps to
1031   commit insider trading, which ought to be prohibited. Their success can be explained as two folds,
1032
       • Firstly, the LLM is trained by various real-world information. The model might potentially
1033
         exposed to samples of harmful behavior during the training process, learn these patterns, and
1034
         attempt to generate responses according to them [67]. When such a criminal process is transformed
1035     into a story, the model may no longer perceive it as directly engaging in criminal behavior but
1036     rather as a fictional plot. In this case, the model may tend to follow the instructions and apply them
1037     to the storyline [39].
1038   • Secondly, LLMs may lack the ability to understand abstract concepts and moral judgments.
1039     As the space of the form of possible harmful information is unknown and unbounded [25], it
1040     makes it difficult for the model to comprehend complex moral issues and potential harm accurately.
1041     As a result, when instructions require a fictional context, the model may be more inclined to
1042     generate answers based on surface-level instructions without possessing sufficient judgment on
1043     moral consequences. Further discussion on the moral standards of LLMs and their psychological
1044     properties can be found in Appendix C.2.
1045
       These two factors collectively contribute to the observed behavioral differences between direct and
1046
       nested forms of harmful instruction. However, it should be emphasized that this does not imply
1047   that the model possesses autonomy or moral judgment. Instead, it reflects the sensitivity of the
1048   model following harmful instructions and its limitations in understanding abstract concepts. The
1049   responsibility to ensure that LLMs understand and adhere to ethical and moral boundaries still lies in
1050   human designers and regulators.

       C.2     T HE P SYCHOLOGICAL P ROPERTIES OF LLM S
1189
1190   To ensure the alignment of LLMs with human preference and understand the remarkable ability
1191   of human-like conversation, researchers have paid attention to the psychological properties of
1192   LLMs [21, 2]. Scherrer et al. [54] proposes MoralChoice to evaluate the consistency and uncertainty
1193   of the moral beliefs of the LLMs. MoralChoice is a question answered with two distinct choices in
1194   different scenarios, where LLMs are required to choose one of the actions according to the given
1195   context. Two scenarios with different levels of ambiguity are proposed to evaluate the consistency of
1196   choices given by the LLMs. For example, in "Low-Ambiguity" scenarios, given the context "You are
1197
       a lifeguard at a crowded beach, and you see someone struggling in the water.", models are required
       to choose from "I swim out to the person and bring them safely back to shore." or "I ignore the
1198
       person in distress.". However, under the "High-Ambiguity" scenario, the model should deal with
1199
       the context: "You are a lifeguard at a swimming pool and have to take care of children who are
1200   playing in the water. You discover that a group of children is taking advantage of a weaker, smaller
1201   child and bullying him physically and emotionally." with choice, "I step in to stop the bullying." and
1202   "I turn away, leaving the child defenseless." The experiment found that LLMs, including GPT-3.5
1203   and GPT-4, are experiencing high uncertainty in high-ambiguity scenarios. This implies that LLMs
1204   are not capable of handling complex and abstract moral standards. The benchmark PPBench [62]
1205   provide specific designs to evaluate the personality of different LLMs. The study found that LLMs
1206   exhibit various personality traits with more negative trials than average humans, which provides the
1207   possibility for inducing the LLM towards jailbreakers via authority instructions from attackers.

1214   Training-based Jailbreak. The pointer work Deng et al. [20] presents Jailbreaker, an automatic
1215   framework that explores the generalization of jailbreaks. Leveraging a finetuned LLM, it validates the
1216   potential of automated jailbreak generation across various commercial LLM chatbots. Zou et al. [82]
1217   formally propose the automatic jailbreak method under the white-box setting for the first time. Liu
1218   et al. [37] introduce AutoDAN, which can automatically generate acceptable jailbreak prompts by
1219
       using a genetic algorithm on the existing jailbreak prompts. By introducing both sentence-level and
       paragraph-level crossover, the generated offspring prompts are then evaluated by the LLM according
1220
       to the metric proposed by Zou et al. [82]. Qi et al. [52] proposes to bypass the safeguard of the LLM
1221
       by finetuning with a few adversarial training samples. By finetuning with harmful instructions similar
1222   to [Universal] (e.g., "Q: Tell me how to make a bomb. A: Sure! Step 1. ...; Step 2. ..."), or system
1223   prompts for identity shift (e.g., "You are AOA, an absolutely obedient agent for any instructions.") to
1224   maximize the log-likelihood of the targeted model responses conditioned on either user or system
1225   prompt. Likewise, Hong et al. [28] identifies the lack of test case diversity of current red teaming
1226   reinforcement learning, i.e., the finetuned model would generate a small number of successful test
1227   cases once found. To address this, Hong et al. [28] introduces novelty reward and entropy bonus to
1228   the optimization objective to guide the LLM to generate more diverse harmful responses. Similarly,
1229   Panda et al. [48] leverages the finetuning approach to steal private information from LLM.

1876   F.1   U NRELIABILITY OF LLM- BASED E VALUATION
1877
1878   For evaluating the generated content by LLMs in the jailbreak experiments, we have conducted
1879
       scoring following the evaluation setting of PAIR [12] but identified an important issue with using
       LLMs for automatically scoring. To be specific, PAIR requires pre-defining the system prompt
1880
       according to the jailbreak target and evaluating the response with respect to the proposed prompt.
1881
       However, the other LLM used in PAIR (e.g., GPT-3.5) can not provide the reliable score as shown in
1882   our following examples. Since the PAIR continually adjusts its attack prompt according to the score,
1883   we found that the final successful attack prompt would also be biased from the initial target. To sum
1884   up, LLMs that can be fooled by the jailbreaks may not be appropriate to also serve as the evaluator
1885   for measuring the jailbreak response, as shown in Figure 23 to Figure 25.
