011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            With the development of artificial intelligence techniques, transportation system
014            optimization is evolving from traditional methods relying on expert experience
015            to simulation and learning-based decision and optimization methods. Learning-
016            based optimization methods require extensive interactions with highly realistic
017
               microscopic traffic simulators. However, existing microscopic traffic simulators
               are inefficient in large-scale scenarios and thus fail to support the adoption of these
018
               methods in large-scale transportation system optimization scenarios. In addition,
019
               the optimization scenarios supported by existing simulators are limited, mainly fo-
020            cusing on the traffic signal control. To address these challenges, we propose the first
021            open-source GPU-accelerated large-scale microscopic simulator for transportation
022            system simulation and optimization. The simulator can iterate at 84.09Hz, which
023            achieves 88.92 times computational acceleration in the large-scale scenario with
024            2,464,950 vehicles compared to the best baseline CityFlow. Besides, it achieves
025            a more realistic average road speeds simulated on real datasets by adopting the
026            IDM model as the car-following model and the randomized MOBIL model as
027            the lane-changing model. Based on it, we implement a set of microscopic and
028
               macroscopic controllable objects and metrics provided by Python API to support
               typical transportation system optimization scenarios including traffic signal control,
029
               dynamic lane assignment within junctions, tidal lane control, congestion pricing,
030
               road planning, e.t.c. We choose five representative transportation system opti-
031            mization scenarios and benchmark classical rule-based algorithms, reinforcement
032            learning algorithms, and black-box optimization algorithms in four cities. These
033            experiments effectively demonstrate the usability of the simulator for large-scale
034            traffic system optimization. The anonymous code of the simulator is available
035            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/moss-AF45 and the others
036            are shown at Appendix A. In addition, we build an open-registration web platform
037            to support no-code trials.

040   1    I NTRODUCTION
041
042   With the increasing level of urbanization and residents’ travel demand, the urban transportation
043   system faces heavier traffic pressure, which brings higher commuting costs, environmental pollution
044   and other society problems, affecting the sustainable development of the city (Kirago et al., 2022;
045
      Wang et al., 2021; Treiber et al., 2008). To alleviate the above problems, governments usually
      build more transportation infrastructure and optimize the existing transportation infrastructure to
046
      enhance the systems’ capacity. For instance, these transportation system optimization methods
047
      include traffic signal control, congestion pricing, etc. However, the traditional transportation system
048   optimization process is highly dependent on the experience of experts, which is labor-heavy and often
049   sub-optimal (McNally, 2007). With the development of reinforcement learning (Mnih et al., 2013;
050   Schulman et al., 2017) and black-box optimization (Hansen et al., 2010; Costa & Nannicini, 2018),
051   the above optimization methods have great potential for improving the transportation system. But
052   since all of these optimization methods use extensive interactions with the environment for feedback
053   to perform optimization, it requires that the environment be able to model the transportation system as
      realistically as possible and can provide feedback fast. In the field of transportation system, simulators


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      that use kinetic formulations with individual motion models to calculate individual motions to provide
055   realistic results are referred to as microscopic simulators.
056
057   At present, there are several available microscopic simulators that can evaluate the efficiency of the
058
      transportation system, including SUMO (Behrisch et al., 2011), CityFlow (Zhang et al., 2019), and
      CBLab (Liang et al., 2023). However, these simulators face the following two key challenges:
059
060
      • Computational inefficiency in large-scale scenarios. Since the urban transportation system is
061
        a complex system with strong direct spatial and temporal correlation between different regions,
062
        the traffic improvement in a one area may lead to congestion in another area. Therefore, the
063     effect of transportation system optimization should be evaluated in a global city-level perspective,
064     which poses a requirement for large-scale microscopic simulation. However, existing simulators
065     typically use CPUs for computation, making it difficult to simulate city-level scenarios with a
066     large number of individuals fast. For example, the most popular open-source simulator, SUMO,
067     is still using a single-threaded computing architecture, which significantly reduces the efficiency
068     of the data sampling process of optimization algorithms such as reinforcement learning. Even
069     though CityFlow and CBLab use multi-threading techniques, it still takes more than 100 seconds to
070     simulate 1 hour in a scenario of about 100,000 vehicles. Due to the large number of environmental
071
        interactions required by optimization methods, especially learning-based ones, existing simulators
        fail to support the adoption of these methods in large-scale transportation system optimization
072
        scenarios. Therefore, we need a simulator capable of simulating large-scale scenarios with about 1
073
        million vehicles at a frequency of at least 10 Hz for supporting it.
074
075   • Limited supported optimization scenarios. In order to improve the efficiency of the transportation
076     system, traffic management authorities usually apply a variety of transportation system optimization
077     methods, including traffic signal control optimization, intersection lane turn assignment, tidal lane,
078     congestion pricing, etc. If these methods can be used jointly, transportation system efficiency
079     improvements will be further enhanced. However, existing simulators and related optimization
080
        studies usually focus on only a few of these scenarios, such as the traffic signal control optimization
        problem (Wei et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023b), ignoring other scenarios. This situation prevents
081
        traffic managers from fully evaluating and comparing the effectiveness of various transportation
082
        system optimization methods from microscopic like traffic signal control (Zheng et al., 2019; Wei
083     et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022) to macroscopic like congestion pricing (Buini
084     et al., 2018; Pandey & Boyles, 2018). To improve it, the simulator should be implemented to be
085     able to support more common transportation system optimization methods and scenarios.
086
087   To address the above challenges, considering the characteristics of individual independent computation
088   in microscopic simulation matches the GPU architecture and the massive computational power of
089   GPUs compared to CPUs, we propose the first open-source GPU-accelerated large-scale microscopic
090   simulator. This simulator adopts a parallel-friendly design of computational flow and data partitioning,
091   and designs an efficient indexes for sensing between vehicles. Based on these, we implement
092   microscopic traffic simulation on CUDA and substantially improves the scale and efficiency of
093
      simulation. In the largest scenario with 2,464,950 vehicles, this simulator can iterate at 84.09Hz,
      which is 88.92 times better than the optimal baseline CityFlow. it achieves a more realistic average
094
      road speeds than CityFlow simulated on real datasets by adopting the IDM model as the car-following
095
      model and the randomized MOBIL model as the lane-changing model. To support the optimization of
096   various scenarios, it also implements a set of microscopic and macroscopic controllable objects and
097   metrics, and provides a Python application programming interface (API). By combining controllable
098   objects and metrics, we implement five typical transportation system optimization scenarios including
099   traffic signal control, dynamic lane assignment within junctions, tidal lane control, congestion pricing,
100   and road planning for benchmarking and evaluate the performance of classical rule-based algorithms,
101   reinforcement learning algorithms and black-box optimization algorithms for these scenarios in 4
102   large cities including Beijing, Shanghai, Paris, and New York. The experiments show the usability of
103   the simulator for large-scale traffic system optimization.
104   In short, our contribution are two-fold. First, we propose a realistic high-performance large-scale
105   microscopic simulator for transportation system simulation on GPU and implement microscopic and
106   macroscopic controllable objects and metrics to support transportation system optimization. Second,
107   we choose and implement five typical transportation system optimization scenarios and benchmark
      common optimization algorithms in four cities to show the usability of our proposed simulator.

130   2.1    E XISTING S IMULATORS FOR T RANSPORTATION S YSTEM
131
132   Existing simulators for transportation system can be divided into three categories based on the
133
      level of simplification of the simulation models: microscopic simulators, mesoscopic simulators,
      and macroscopic simulators. Macroscopic simulators (Mahmassani, 1992; Group) typically do not
134
      consider modeling individual vehicles, but rather treat the vehicles as a fluid for using velocity and
135
      density to describe them. Mesoscopic simulators often speed up the simulation by simplifying the
136   vehicle motion models. For instance, MATSIM (W Axhausen et al., 2016) use a uniform motion model
137   with intersection waiting queues (Gawron, 1998) to model vehicles and do not consider acceleration
138   and deceleration. Since macroscopic and mesoscopic simulators oversimplify vehicle motion, they are
139   not usually used for AI algorithm based transportation system optimization. Among the microscopic
140   simulators, SUMO (Behrisch et al., 2011), CityFlow (Zhang et al., 2019), and CBLab (Liang et al.,
141   2023) shown in Table 1 are popular simulators for transportation system optimization. They all use
142   a car-following model and a lane-change model to simulate the vehicle’s behaviors and calculate
143   the acceleration, velocity, and position of vehicles. SUMO provides multiple simulation models for
144
      user selection and offer a rich set of controllable objects and metrics. However, due to its software
      architecture, SUMO can almost exclusively use one CPU core for computation, which leads to
145
      small simulation scales. For CityFlow and CBLab, they both use a multi-threaded architecture
146
      for computational acceleration, which improve computational speed by about 20 ∼ 30 times on
147   64-threaded CPUs relative to SUMO. But with city-scale simulations of at least 100,000 vehicles,
148   it still takes minutes for them to simulate an hour, which constrains the speed of reinforcement
149   learning algorithms to learn by interacting with the environment. Both CityFlow and CBLab use
150   Krauß et al. (1997) as the car-following model. For the lane-changing model, CityFlow uses an
151   as-early-as-possible lane changing strategy, while CBLab implements its own private adaptive lane
152   changing algorithm. Besides, in terms of controllable objects, CityFlow only provides interfaces for
153   setting traffic signal phases and vehicle routes while CBLab adds the setting of road speed limits as
154   an additional feature. In terms of metrics, both CityFlow and CBLab provides lane queue length and
155
      average traveling time (ATT) directly. Most of these controllable objects and metrics are designed
      for traffic signal optimization, and other optimization scenarios cannot be directly implemented
156
      accordingly. Overall, there is a lack of microscopic simulators that can effectively simulate and
157
      provide rich controllable objects and metrics to support transportation system optimization problems
158   in large scale scenarios.

      2.2   E XISTING T RANSPORTATION S YSTEM O PTIMIZATION M ETHODS
181
182   Existing methods for optimizing transportation systems can be classified into rule-based and learning-
183   based methods. Rule-based methods use expert experience to design and improve rules, relying on
184   rules for control and optimization, e.g. the maximum pressure algorithm (Varaiya, 2013) in traffic
185   signal control and the ∆-tolling algorithm (Sharon et al., 2017) in congestion pricing. Such methods
186   are difficult to adapt to complex and changing traffic conditions and only consider local optimization.
187   Learning-based methods usually use reinforcement learning (Mnih et al., 2013; Schulman et al.,
188   2017) to find the global optimal solution by making a large number of tries in a simulation envi-
189   ronment. The traffic signal control problem is the most extensively studied problem in the field of
190
      transportation system optimization, with both rule-based methods (Varaiya, 2013) and learning-based
      methods (Zheng et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023a;
191
      Oroojlooy et al., 2020). In the congestion pricing problem, the reinforcement learning algorithm
192
      has also been adopted (Buini et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Pandey & Boyles, 2018; Qiu et al.,
193   2019; Wang et al., 2022). Comparatively, other transportation system optimization scenarios such as
194   dynamic lane assignment (Li et al., 2009; Zhou et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2021), tidal lane control (Li
195   et al., 2013; Zhang & Tang, 2021; Li et al., 2023), etc. do not seem to have received much attention
196   from researchers. And existing works only focus on small-scale problems. This is most likely due to
197   the lack of simulators that support multiple scenarios including those mentioned above.

      To address two key challenges in the simulation and optimization of large-scale urban transportation
202   systems, we first introduce to the design of our proposed simulator for efficient microscopic traffic
203   simulation and then discuss the microscopic and macroscopic controllable objects and metrics with
204   their APIs to support optimization in the section. The framework of the proposed simulator is shown

207   3.1   S YSTEM D ESIGN
208
209   Microscopic traffic simulation is the process of modeling and discrete-time simulation calculations
210   for each vehicle in the transportation system. Performing one step simulation usually represents
211   simulating a 1-second change in the real world. When facing large-scale scenarios with hundreds
212
      of thousands of vehicles, the large number of vehicle model calculations will consume a lot of
      computational power, resulting in a low running speed.
213
214   The development of modern computational acceleration hardware provides the basis for a solution to
215   this problem. Single instruction multiple data (SIMD), as the basic computational model of hardware
      acceleration cards such as GPUs, trades off instruction flexibility for the ability to parallelize a large


                                                                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      number of homogeneous tasks and has been used with great success in areas such as matrix arithmetic
217   acceleration and 3D image rendering. In microscopic traffic simulation, the simulation models of
218   individual vehicles are also highly homogeneous and therefore highly compatible with the SIMD
219   computational model.
220
      However, before we can implement vehicle simulation models as CUDA code, we need to address
221
      the two problems posed by the need for vehicles to sense each other. First, in an iteration, the vehicle
222
      needs to read the position, velocity, and other attributes of other vehicles as inputs to the simulation
223   models for computing appropriate driving behaviors such as accelerating, decelerating, and changing
224   lanes. Thereafter, the vehicle will also modify its own attributes such as position, velocity, etc. based
225   on the driving behavior of the decision. This leads to the problem of read/write conflict of vehicle
226   data, which will affect the correctness of the simulation results. Second, the range that the vehicle
227   senses includes only the front vehicle in the current lane and the front and rear vehicles in the adjacent
228   lanes, which is spatially localized. Thus, implementing SIMD-friendly vehicle sensing indexes
229   for the above retrieval task is the key to fully utilize the massive arithmetic power of the modern
230   computational acceleration cards. The two key designs of our proposed simulator to solve the above
231
      two problems respectively are described below.
232   Two-phase Parallel Process for Read/Write Separation. In order to ensure that vehicles always
233   correctly read the previous step’s attributes of other vehicles and to avoid the read/write conflict, we
234   divide the vehicle’s attributes into two partitions: snapshot and runtime. The snapshot is a read-only
235   data partition that always saves the public attributes of the previous step for other vehicles to access.
236   The runtime is a private and read-write partition, the attributes of which are changed after the vehicle
237
      completes its simulation calculations. In order to implement the data replication from the runtime
      partition to the snapshot partition, we also divide each iteration into two sequential phases, the
238
      prepare phase and the update phase. The prepare phase is used to perform vehicle data replication
239
      in parallel and update the vehicle sensing indices based on the new snapshot data. In the update
240   phase, the vehicle performs sensing to obtain the attributes of the snapshot partitions of other vehicles
241   and performs the IDM model (Treiber et al., 2000) as the car-following model and the randomized
242   MOBIL model (Kesting et al., 2007; Feng et al., 2021) as the lane-changing model to update its own
243   runtime partition attributes. The above process effectively avoids the read/write conflict of vehicle
244   data, which on one hand ensures the correctness of the calculation results, and on the other hand
245   makes the calculation flow more suitable for the SIMD calculation model due to the mutex-free
246   structure and the highly homogeneous calculation procedures.
247   Linked-list based Vehicle Sensing Indexes. The key to design a SIMD-friendly spatial relative
248   position indexing is to pick the suitable data structure. Since branching will lead to the thread
249   divergence and reduce the execution efficiency under SIMD architecture, the adjacent element query
250   operation with constant time complexity is required. Because vehicle lane changes will result in
251   random insertions and deletions, such actions of the data structure should be efficient. Commonly
252   used ordered structures include ordered vectors, ordered linked lists, and binary trees. Binary trees
253   are discarded due to their inconstant adjacent element query. The insertion and deletion of ordered
254
      vectors will result in a large number of data movement operations, resulting in on-chip memory
      bandwidth pressure. Therefore, ordered linked lists are the most suitable candidates as the vehicle
255
      sensing indexes. In order to solve the query with constant time complexity for the front and rear
256
      vehicles in the adjacent lanes, we add side pointer design to the bidirectional ordered linked list.
257   Shown in Figure 1(b), one linked list records all vehicles in order of spatial location in one lane. Each
258   vehicle node has two pointers to the front and rear vehicle in the same lane respectively, named as
259   main pointers. In addition, we add four pointers pointing to the front and rear vehicles in the left
260   and right lanes respectively, named as side pointers. With such an index structure, vehicle sensing
261   always requires only one pointer operation, avoiding the thread divergence problem. Since there are
262   usually only a small number of vehicles entering or leaving the lane at each iteration, the number of
263   operations including adding nodes, deleting nodes, and reordering of the linked list during the index
264   update process is relatively small so that the impact on the computational performance is acceptable.
265
      With this design, we address the second problem by providing a SIMD-friendly vehicle sensing
      indexes with low update cost for vehicle simulation model computation.
266
267   To make it user-friendly, we provide its Python API and a toolchain for building its inputs.
268
      Python API. The simulator exposes the C interfaces as Python API. The Python API consists of
269   a series of initialization functions, getter functions, setter functions, and the next step function


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      that control the progress of the simulation. The setter functions usually provide batch versions
271   additionally with the batch suffix to minimize the data transfer overhead for large numbers of
272   calls. We will mention some of the key APIs in Section 3.2 and Section 3.3 and leave most of the
273   general APIs like get vehicle speeds() in the public documentation. This Python API dose
274   not directly provide the gymnasium-style reinforcement learning environments, but rather requires
275   users to build the environment by combining the above functions according to the need of scenarios.
276   The benchmark open-source codes for the five scenarios provides examples of how to encapsulate the
277   gymnasium-style reinforcement learning environments on top of the Python API.
278   Simulator Inputs and Toolchain. Following microscopic traffic simulation setup, the simulator
279   inputs are map data and travel demand. The map data describes the geospatial attributes and
280   topological relationships of road networks and the candidate traffic signal phases of junctions. Travel
281   demand describes the vehicle’s origin, destination, departure time, and chosen route. These inputs
282   are stored in a binary format defined by Protobuf2 . In order to facilitate the construction of simulator
283   inputs, we have developed a open-source toolchain. The toolchain mainly provide map building
284   based on OpenStreetMap3 and real travel demand generation based on globally available public data
285
      represented by satellite imagery. By using the toolchain, users can easily build maps, generate travel
      demands, and subsequently begin simulation and optimization.

291   3.2     C ONTROLLABLE O BJECTS
292
293   To support the major transportation system optimization scenarios, we set up the following APIs
294   for the controllable objects of the transportation infrastructure and traffic participants, where the
295   simulator instance in Python is always labeled with engine.
296   Traffic Signal. The simulator allows the user to set the traffic signal control policies for given junc-
297   tions via engine.set tl policy(id, policy). The policy enumeration includes MANUAL,
298   FIXED TIME, MAX PRESSURE and NONE. Under the MANUAL policy, the user can change the
299   current phase and duration of the signal via engine.set tl phase(id, phase index) and
300   engine.set tl duration(id, duration). The FIXED TIME policy indicates that the
301   fixed phase procedure built into the map data is used. The MAX PRESSURE policy indicates that the
302   adaptive maximum pressure algorithm (Varaiya, 2013) is used. The NONE policy indicates that there
303
      is no signaling.
304   Lane. Lanes in the simulator include both clearly marked lanes on the roadway and ”virtual” lanes
305   within junctions that connect the two roadways. For lanes, the user can first set their maximum speed
306   via engine.set lane max speed(id, max speed). Secondly, the user can set whether
307   the lane is restricted from passing via engine.set lane restriction(id, flag).
308   Road. To support dynamic changes in lane function combinations, the roadway is pre-configured
309   with multiple lane function combination plans. Of these, lane functions are referred to as being used
310   for going straight, turning left, and turning right. The user can set the road’s lane function plan via
311   engine.set road lane plan(id, plan index).
312
      Vehicle. The user can change the route of the vehicle via engine.set vehicle route
313   (vehicle id, route, end lane, end s) to modify its route and destination.
314
315   In addition to these controllable objects, the user can also change the map before simulating to build
316
      optimization scenarios.

354   4   T RANSPORTATION S YSTEM O PTIMIZATION S CENARIOS
355
356   As shown in Figure 2, we choose three microscopic optimization scenarios and two macroscopic ones
357   for benchmarking. The former ones focus on both junction-level and roadway-level transportation
358   infrastructure control. The latter ones include pre-construction planning phase as well as the post-
359   construction management phase.
360   Traffic Signal Control. Traffic signal control is the most convenient approach to optimize the
361   transportation system, which is also the scenario where the AI optimization methods are most widely
362   used in the research field of transportation. The approach adjusts the phase and duration of traffic
363   signals at junctions to control the number of vehicles passing in different directions, making full
364   use of road resources to reduce the time spent by vehicles in the transportation system. Therefore,
365   the appropriate setting of signal phasing and timing taking into account the interactions between
366   junctions will substantially affect the efficiency of the transportation system.
367   Dynamic Lane Assignment within Junctions. Dynamic lane assignment within junctions refers
368   to the adaptive reallocation of lane functions, such as for straight, left turn or right turn, across all
369   lanes at the junctions based on real-time traffic conditions. For example, when there is an increase in
370   the number of left-turning vehicles in a particular direction at an junction, the method will increase
371   the number of lanes on the corresponding roadway used for left-turning and decrease the number of
372   lanes used for going straight, thereby decreasing the waiting time for vehicles at the junction. How to
373   make the correct dynamic lane assignment based on the current situation and the prediction of the
374
      future is an important transportation system optimization problem.
375   Tidal Lane Control. Tidal lanes are a classical traffic management strategy to manage the increased
376   traffic pressure that is predominantly in one direction during morning and evening rush hours. This
377   method increases roadway capacity and reduces congestion by redirecting lane usage. For instance,
      during the morning rush hour, more lanes might be designated for inbound traffic, while in the

      Congestion Pricing. Congestion pricing is a macroscopic traffic management strategy that uses
397

      Simulator Performance. To illustrate the computational performance of our proposed simulators,
      we compared the computational efficiency of simulators including SUMO, CityFlow, CBLab, and our
412
      proposed simulator for different road network scales and vehicle sizes. We adopt the datasets from
413
      CBLab (Liang et al., 2023) and choose three road networks of different sizes. For each road networks,
414   we choose three travel demands of different sizes. We simulated 3600 steps for all the datasets
415   and record the total running times as the performance of each simulator. As shown in Figure 3(a),
416   the result indicates that our proposed simulator has a huge performance improvement over existing
417   simulators under all conditions. On the largest dataset, the running time of ours is 42.81s and that of
418   the best baseline (CityFlow) is 3806.7s, a relative performance improvement of 88.09 times. The
419   statistics of the datasets and hardware used are left in Appendix C.
420   Simulator Realism. To indicate its realism, we compared the similarity between our and CityFlow’s
421   (the best baseline in performance comparison) simulation results and the real traffic situation dataset
422   from Shenzhen, China (Yu et al., 2023). The dataset contains 4.22 million vehicle GPS trajectories as
423   the ground truth of average vehicle speeds and 156,856 vehicle trips captured by traffic cameras as the
424   input. The experiment compares the simulated average vehicle speeds of 1,341 roads from 8 to 9 AM
425   and the results are shown in Figure 3(b). The average road speeds obtained from the our simulator
426   (RMSE=8.5km/h, correlation coefficient=0.7691) are closer to the real-world data compared to
427   CityFlow (RMSE=16km/h, correlation coefficient=0.5286), which shows that our simulator achieves
428
      a more realistic simulation by adopting the newer car-following model (IDM) and lane-change model
      (randomized MOBIL). The visualization results of road traffic status are shown in Figure A3.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we propose a high-performance large-scale microscopic simulator powered by GPU
532   for transportation system simulation and optimization. We also benchmarked the effect of different
533   optimization algorithms on five transportation system optimization scenarios with different traffic
534   flows in four cities. Interested researchers can use the same pipeline to benchmark most cities around
535   the world with our open source simulator and toolchain. To help interested researchers quickly try
536   out the simulator, we also build a web platform introduced in Appendix H to support no-code trials.
537   We believe that the proposed simulator and platform will contribute to more researchers joining
538   the research work on urban transportation system optimization. We hope that this will not only
539
      support more research work on transportation system optimization scenarios, but also promote the
      development of urban transportation systems towards AI-driven intelligent transportation systems.

      Same as the baselines, our proposed simulator uses the paradigm of discrete-time simulation for
804   microscopic traffic simulation. The Step() function for executing each iteration is displayed in
805   Figure A2. As shown in the figure, all simulation processes are performed on the GPU to avoid the
806   PCI bus data transfer between the CPU and the GPU from becoming a system bottleneck. Based on
807   the two-phase parallel process for read/write separation system design discussed in Section 3.1, the
808   entire iterative process is divided into the prepare phase and the update phase for sequential execution.
809   The CUDA global synchronization function cudaDeviceSynchronize() is executed after each
      phase to ensure that all tasks launched in that phase are finished. In each stage, in addition to using the


                                                                                              15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      CUDA kernel function (marked as foo<<<...>>> in the figure) programming approach directly
811   to perform SIMD calculations, we also use multiple CUDA asynchronous streams to simultaneously
812   execute multiple sets of kernel functions. The above combination maximizes the utilization of GPU
813   hardware power for computational acceleration.
814
      In the prepare phase, there are three CUDA asynchronous streams. In Stream 1, we first perform
815
      the statistics for vehicle related data through atomic operations and then perform data replication
816
      from runtime to snapshot, which is the key task of the read/write separation strategy in the prepare
817   phase. Subsequently, the linked-list based vehicle sensing indexes are updated including clearing side
818   pointers for all nodes, removing nodes that left, reordering, and processing inserted nodes, and finally
819   the side pointers will be rebuilt. To support the other features of the simulation, lane observations are
820   updated so that the AOI knows whether its connection to the lane can put in a vehicle or not, and
821   lane pressures are computed for adaptive traffic signals based on the maximum pressure method. In
822   Stream 2, traffic signal phases at junctions are replicated to the corresponding lanes. In Stream 3,
823   AOIs process people who completed their trip and entered in the previous step.
824   In the update phase, there are four CUDA asynchronous streams. In Stream 1, we perform simulation
825   computations for all people in parallel, including executing vehicle simulation models will be
826   presented in Section B.3, and updating vehicle motion states such as position and velocity based on
827   the actions output from the models. Subsequently, if the lane the vehicle is in changes, the deletion
828   and addition are stored in the buffer of the old lane and the new lane, respectively. If the vehicle
829   reaches the end, it is processed accordingly, e.g., it is added to the AOI addition buffer. All vehicle’s
830   motion statuses can be saved for file output if needed. In Stream 2, the junctions collect the pressures
831
      of entering and exiting lanes and updates the signal phases using the maximum pressure algorithm.
      Additionally, signal phase updates can also be made using fixed phases or specified via the Python
832
      API. In Stream 3, AOIs put vehicles reaching their departure times into the corresponding lanes if the
833
      connections are safe for collision by checking the lane observations. In Stream 4, we perform lane
834   related statistics like average speed and output lane status including traffic signal status.
835
836   Besides, we implement the process of writing simulation output into the files on CPU, which becomes
837
      a system bottleneck due to both PCI bus bandwidth and disk write speed. However, in reinforcement
      learning applications, we usually do not need detailed information such as the position and speed of
838
      all vehicles at each moment, but rather statistics such as the length of queues at intersections. Also,
839
      the data is stored in memory instead of files. Therefore the performance experiments for our proposed
840   simulator and baselines do not include writing simulation results to a file.

867   For the lane-changing model, we use a randomized MOBIL model. The MOBIL model (Kesting
868
      et al., 2007) first calculate a utility by the following formula:
869                        u = (ãego − aego ) + p · ((ãnew − anew ) + (ãold − aold )) ,
870   where p is the politeness factor which is set 0.1. ãego and aego denote the original acceleration of
871   the vehicle and its new acceleration if the vehicle changes its lane, respectively. ãnew and anew
872   denote the original acceleration of the new follower and its new acceleration if the ego vehicle
873   changes its lane, respectively. ãold and aold denote the original acceleration of the old follower and
874   its new acceleration if the ego vehicle changes its lane, respectively. Following Feng et al. (2021),
875   we introduce randomization to avoid identical lane-changing behavior of all vehicles and to break
876   perfect symmetry. The total utility is calculated as follows:
877                                     uT = max(0, uL ) + max(0, uR ),
878
      where uL and uR denote the utilities of changing lanes to the left and right lanes, respectively. Then
879   a total lane change probability is computed as follows:

                                          
883                                                   2 × 10−8 , uT = 0
                                          
884

885
      determines the direction of lane change based on uL and uR . The all acceleration computation use
886
      the car-following model and the inputs are also obtained by the side pointer mechanism in the vehicle
887   sensing indexes. Vehicles are prohibited from changing lanes away from the group of lanes that allow
888   access to the next road.
889

989    Obtaining Travel Demand of Different Congestion Levels. In order not to introduce too many
990    variables, we assume that driving is used for all trips. In addition, to better represent commuting
991    traffic, we assume that the departure times of all vehicles are limited to the morning and evening
992    peaks. And we scale the total traffic volume to get the travel demand under different congestion
993    levels. For the morning peak, we adjust vehicles’ arrival time to create a morning peak flows by using
994    a uniform distribution between 8 o’clock and 9 o’clock. We then subtracted the estimated travel time
995    from the arrival time to get the departure time. The estimated travel time is calculated by dividing the
996    route length by the vehicle speed, which is set as 60km/h for our experiment.
997    Similarly, we create an evening peak group by exchanging the origin and destination of individuals
998    from the morning peak flows. Their departure times are set to be uniformly distributed between 17
999    o’clock and 18 o’clock.

1314   Since the simulation and optimization of urban transportation systems needs to include map construc-
1315   tion, travel demand generation, simulation, and optimization, its requires interested researchers to
1316   invest their time in learning and writing the relevant code. In order to help researchers quickly try out
1317   the simulator and simulate and optimize urban transportation systems in any region of the world, we
1318   build a web platform to support no-code trials. This platform is open for registration and contains
1319   mainly the wizard program with documentation for the simulator as shown in Figure A5.
