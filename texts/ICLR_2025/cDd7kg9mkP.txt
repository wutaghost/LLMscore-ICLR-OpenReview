011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this work, we bridge the gap between wearable sensor technology and person-
014            alized AI assistants by enabling Large Language Models (LLMs) to understand
015            time-series tasks like human activity recognition (HAR). Despite the strong rea-
016
               soning and generalization capabilities of LLMs, leveraging them for sensor data
               tasks remains largely unexplored. This gap stems from challenges like the lack of
017
               semantic context in time-series data, computational limitations, and LLMs’ diffi-
018
               culty processing numerical inputs. To address these issues, we introduce Sensor-
019            LLM, a two-stage framework to unlock LLMs’ potential for sensor data tasks. In
020            the Sensor-Language Alignment Stage, we introduce special tokens for each sen-
021            sor channel and automatically generate trend-descriptive text to align sensor data
022            with textual inputs, enabling SensorLLM to capture numerical changes, channel-
023            specific information, and sensor data of varying lengths—capabilities that exist-
024            ing LLMs typically struggle with, all without the need for human annotations.
025            Next, in Task-Aware Tuning Stage, we refine the model for HAR classification
026            using the frozen LLM and alignment module, achieving performance on par with
027
               or surpassing state-of-the-art models. We further demonstrate that SensorLLM
               evolves into an effective sensor learner, reasoner, and classifier through Sensor-
028
               Language Alignment, enabling it to generalize across diverse datasets for HAR
029
               tasks. We strongly believe our work lays the stepstone for future time-series
030            and text alignment research, offering a path toward foundation models for sensor
031            data. Our codes are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/
032            r/sensorllm_code-E0FC.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Human Activity Recognition (HAR) is a time-series (TS) classification task that involves mapping
038   sensor data, such as accelerometer and gyroscope signals, to human activities. Models such as
039
      LSTM (Guan & Plötz, 2017; Hammerla et al., 2016) and DeepConvLSTM (Ordóñez & Roggen,
      2016) have gained popularity in HAR due to their ability to learn high-level features. However, these
040
      models are typically task-specific and struggle to scale across varying sensor configurations and
041
      activity sets. In contrast, Large Language Models (LLMs) (Han et al., 2021) have shown remarkable
042   success in integrating diverse data types (Wu et al., 2023b; Yin et al., 2023), including text and
043   images. In this work, we explore how LLMs can unlock new possibilities for HAR by improving
044   the interpretation of sensor data and offering a more scalable, flexible solution to this challenging
045   task.
046
      To enable LLMs to understand effectively and process sensor data (Jin et al., 2023), there are two pri-
047
      mary approaches. The first approach involves pretraining or fine-tuning LLMs on sensor data (Zhou
048   et al., 2023b), optimizing them to handle time-series inputs directly. However, this method presents
049   significant challenges, particularly related to computational resource requirements and data limita-
050   tions. Pretraining LLMs on such data demands immense computational resources, and the limited
051   availability of labeled sensor data, especially for rare activities, leads to class imbalance, making
052   the training process difficult and inefficient. The second approach to enabling LLMs to understand
053   sensor data focuses on using zero-shot and few-shot prompting, where sensor data is transformed
      into textual formats that LLMs can process without retraining (Ji et al., 2024). However, this in-

069   troduces several challenges. One major issue is the numerical and temporal nature of data, which
070   poses challenges due to how LLMs tokenize numerical inputs (Nate Gruver & Wilson, 2023). LLM
071   tokenizers, designed for text, are not optimized for numerical values. They struggle with recogniz-
072   ing patterns and contextual nuances, treating consecutive values as independent tokens and ignoring
073   temporal correlations. Sensor data, characterized by its continuous and extensive nature, often ex-
074   ceeds the maximum sequence length, resulting in truncation and potential loss of vital information.
075   Another challenge lies in converting sensor data into text-like embeddings while preserving high-
076
      level time-series features, as this transformation risks losing the temporal relationships crucial for
      complex activity recognition (Spathis & Kawsar, 2024). Handling multi-channel sensor data fur-
077
      ther complicates this process, as encoding multi-dimensional information in a way that LLMs can
078
      interpret effectively is difficult cause the input for LLMs is univariate. Moreover, designing effec-
079   tive prompts that enable LLMs to interpret sensor readings, detect trends, and classify activities is
080   complex, especially when dealing with intricate time-series patterns (Liu et al., 2023b).
081
082
      To address these challenges, we introduce a novel approach for aligning sensor data with natural
      language, allowing LLMs to interactively analyze sensor data through text-based commands (see
083
      Figure 1) without requiring any modifications to the LLM itself. A significant challenge in this
084
      context is managing the annotation bottleneck, which is further exacerbated by the inherent hetero-
085   geneity and complexity of sensor data. Unlike image-text pairs that can be more straightforwardly
086   aligned, sensor data comprises multiple modalities with diverse characteristics, making the inter-
087   pretation and alignment process considerably more difficult. Previous approaches (Jin et al., 2024a;
088   Sun et al., 2024b) have often used condensed text prototypes for alignment. While these methods
089   provide a solution, they face limitations in interpretability, as the mapping between numerical time-
090   series data and abstract text prototypes is not always intuitive. Moreover, such methods often require
091   multiple experiments to select appropriate text prototypes, adding complexity to the process.
092   In this work, we propose a novel approach that automatically generates descriptive text from time-
093   series data, removing the need for manual annotations. These descriptions are generated using
094   statistical analyses or simple descriptions of numerical trends. We employ pre-defined templates to
095   automate the generation of descriptive texts, offering a scalable, precise, and interpretable solution
096   for aligning time-series data with text in our SensorLLM framework. SensorLLM introduces a two-
097   stage framework process that has been proven effective across various domains (Xu et al., 2024;
098   Huang et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024). In Sensor-Language Alignment Stage, we generate automatic
099
      question-answer pairs to align sensor data with text, providing a scalable and interpretable solution.
      To preserve the temporal features of the sensor data, we use a pretrained encoder to generate sensor
100
      embeddings, which are then mapped into a space that the LLM can interpret. This avoids the issues
101
      caused by using a text-specific embedding model used by LLM. Furthermore, we introduce special
102   tokens for each sensor channel, enabling the LLM to capture and interpret multi-channel data ef-
103   fectively. This ensures that both time-series characteristics and sensor-specific information are fully
104   aligned with the LLM’s interpretative capabilities. In Task-Aware Tuning Stage, the aligned data is
105   leveraged to address downstream tasks HAR, utilizing the LLM’s reasoning abilities while keeping
106   its parameters frozen. This alignment framework enhances the capabilities of LLMs beyond their
107   original pretraining. Our work demonstrates the effectiveness of SensorLLM, addressing the ques-
      tions raised by Tan et al. (2024) regarding the broader applicability of LLMs for time-series data.

      In this section, we explore recent works that utilize LLMs for human-centric tasks, such as human
129
      activity recognition and mobile health sensing. Current approaches can be categorized based on two
130   main aspects: (1) how they integrate numerical time-series data with LLMs, and (2) the specific end
131   task, whether it is time-series forecasting, classification, etc. Zhang et al. (2024) provides a com-
132   prehensive review of the existing methodologies that leverage LLM capabilities in processing time-
133   series data. Several works investigated using LLM for numerical data by treating time-series signals
134   as raw text. They used the same tokenization and embedding models as text such as PromptCast
135   (Xue & Salim, 2023) and LLMTime (Gruver et al., 2024). However, despite LLM’s versatility, they
136   are inherently designed for processing sequential text data and not for heterogeneous time-series nu-
137   merical data. The challenge lies in enabling LLMs to interpret and learn patterns within multimodal
138
      time-series data effectively. As highlighted by Spathis & Kawsar (2024), one significant barrier is
      the absence of suitable tokenizers for numerical data. LLMs typically employ tokenizers that seg-
139
      ment text into smaller units, but these tokenizers are not optimized for representing numerical values.
140
      Consequently, they may struggle to recognise repetitive patterns and context, often treating consec-
141   utive values as independent tokens and overlooking the crucial temporal relationships inherent in
142   time-series data. To extract more meaningful insights from numerical data before feeding them to
143   LLM, several works proposed the use of time-series encoders and tried to align the embedding space
144   with text embedding models. Liu et al. (2024a) aligns ECG data with corresponding textual descrip-
145   tions using contrastive learning techniques. Similar approaches have been investigated for aligning
146   motion/IoT sensors with text (Zhou et al., 2023c; Haresamudram et al., 2024; Moon et al., 2023;
147   Xia et al., 2024). Please refer to Appendix A.1 for a more in-depth review of existing works.

      3   M ETHODS
151
152   In this work, we propose a novel approach to align sensor data with descriptive text using automati-
153
      cally generated question-answer pairs. The aim of this work is to develop a multimodal foundational
      model with reasoning capabilities to address challenges in analyzing wearable sensor data. As shown
154
      in Figure 2, our SensorLLM comprises three core components: 1) a pretrained LLM, 2) a pretrained
155
      TS embedder, and 3) an alignment module MLP. The three core modules cooperate in a two-stage
156   framework. In the Sensor-Language Alignment Stage, a generative model aligns sensor readings
157   with text based on user instructions (Liu et al., 2023a) and questions. In Task-Aware Tuning Stage,
158   a classification model is used to perform HAR and maintain an end-to-end alignment. Notably, only
159   the parameters of the MLP in both stages, as well as the classifier in the task-aware tuning stage, are
160   updated during training, while the backbone LLM and TS embedder remain frozen. Consequently,
161   only 5.67% of the parameters (535.9M) in sensor-language alignment stage and 0.12% (10.5M) in
      task-aware tuning stage are trainable, ensuring a highly efficient and lightweight training process.

180   3.1       S ENSOR -T EXT DATA G ENERATION
181
182   A key challenge in aligning time-series data with text for LLM-based tasks is effectively bridging
183   these two modalities. Unlike other multimodal tasks, time-series data typically lacks rich seman-
184   tic textual information beyond class labels, making manual annotation impractical (Deldari et al.,
185   2024; Haresamudram et al., 2024). While prior works have used predefined text prototypes for
186   alignment (Sun et al., 2024a; Jin et al., 2024a), we aim to guide the model toward a more human-
187
      intended understanding of the data. We argue that time-series data inherently contains semantic
      information that can be expressed through descriptive text, from simple numerical trends to more
188
      complex insights derived from statistical analysis. Our approach focuses on automatically generat-
189
      ing descriptive text based on observed trends and fluctuations in the input data. To achieve this, we
190   use a variety of predefined templates to generate diverse question-answer (QA) pairs that describe
191   trend changes in the sensor data, ensuring both accuracy and scalability, while avoiding the potential
192   errors of manual annotation. These templates are randomly combined to produce diverse QA pairs.
193   For example:
194
195          (1) The time-series data represents readings taken from a <S>
196              sensor between < ts > and < te > seconds.
197          (2) To sum up, the data exhibited a <T> trend for a cumulative
198              period of < tt > seconds.
199
200   where T and S represent placeholders for a specific trend and sensor type, respectively, and t corre-
201   spond to numerical values. These automatically generated QA pairs offer a scalable and interpretable
202   method for aligning sensor data and text in the latent space, facilitating more effective understanding
203   and interaction with LLMs. See Appendix A.2 for detailed templates.

      As shown in Figure 2 (a), Sensor-Language Alignment stage employs a generative model designed to
      create multi-modal sentences that combine single-channel sensor readings with textual descriptions.
208
      The sensor data is represented as a matrix X ∈ RC×T , where C indicates the number of sensor
209
      channels, and T represents the total number of time steps in the data sequence. Each individual
210   channel’s data, denoted as Xc for channel c, is processed independently to maintain the uniqueness
211   of information captured by each channel. The data is then divided into non-overlapping segments,
212   XcS , where S represents the total number of segments. Each segment xs is assigned a random length
213   l within a predefined range. The variation in the length of segments allows the model to effectively
214   learn from a variety of temporal patterns and trend variations across different segments. By learning
215   from segments with different lengths, the generative model becomes more adept at handling the
      diverse characteristics of sensor data, ensuring that both long-term trends and short-term fluctuations

      3.3   TASK -AWARE T UNING
249
250   As shown in Figure 2 (b), the Task-Aware Tuning stage employs a classification model to align
251   the multimodal sensor-text embeddings with human activity labels. This stage further integrates
252   multi-channel sensor readings with activity labels for HAR. The input sensor data X is divided into
253   segments of window size L with 50% overlap (Li et al., 2018), forming segments XS ∈ RS×C×L ,
254   where S is the number of segments and C is the number of channels. The pretrained alignment
255   module from first stage is used to map sensor data to activity labels, learning the relationship between
256   temporal patterns and human activities while preserving inter-channel dependencies.
257   Input Embedding. For each sensor channel c, we first retrieve the combined sensor embeddings ôcs .
258   These embeddings are then concatenated across all sensor channels, along with their corresponding
259   statistical information (mean and variance) as prompts, to form the final input embedding ẑ =
260   ô1s ⊕ ô2s ⊕ · · · ⊕ ôC
                              s ⊕ ẑstat , where ẑstat represents the statistical information of each channel, and
261   C is the total number of channels. This ensures the model integrates both temporal and statistical
262   characteristics of the sensor data, providing a comprehensive input for HAR.
263   Loss Function. The input token sequence is passed through the LLM to extract a latent represen-
264   tation H ∈ RK×D , where K is the number of tokens and D is the embedding dimension. Due
265   to causal masking, the model pools the final hidden state by selecting the representation of the last
266   token, h = HK , which encodes information from all preceding tokens. This pooled vector, h, is
267   then passed through a fully connected layer, which maps it to a vector of size M , where M is the
268   number of activity classes. The softmax function is applied to obtain the predicted class probabili-
269   ties ŷi , which represent the likelihood of each activity class. The model then is optimized using the
                                                  PM −1
      cross-entropy loss, defined as: Lcls = − i=0 yi log ŷi , where yi is the ground truth label.

      reason across temporal-numerical (i.e., time-series) data, and in performing HAR tasks. All experi-

313   training and testing subjects across both stages. This setup guarantees that the test data used in Task-

316   semantic information from the input data. We chose Chronos as the TS embedder for our experi-
317   ments, as it has not been pre-trained on any sensor data, making it an ideal candidate for evaluating
318   the robustness of our approach in learning from raw sensor inputs.

      Metrics. We evaluate the effectiveness of SensorLLM in understanding and generating trend de-
343
      scriptions from sensor data, compared to the robust GPT-4o1 . This evaluation primarily focuses on
344   assessing the success of our Sensor-Language Alignment. Using a predefined prompt A.3, GPT-4o
345   generates answers based on the input sensor data, following our text template. To comprehensively
346   assess performance, we employed three different evaluation methods.
347
348            • NLP Metric Evaluation: We use standard NLP metrics including BLEU-1 (Papineni et al.,
349              2002), ROUGE-1, ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004), and METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005) to mea-
350              sure surface-level similarity and n-gram overlap. Besides, we also report Sentent-BERT
351              (SBERT) (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) and SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021), which compute the
352              semantic similarity between sentence embeddings of the model output and ground truth.
353
                 For a fair comparison, we used SimCSE’s tokenizer across all metrics, except for SBERT,
                 to better handle numerical content like timestamps.
354
355            • GPT-4o Evaluation: We task GPT-4o with evaluating the generated text based on its align-
356              ment with ground truth. GPT-4o assessed the correctness of the trend descriptions and rated
357              each model’s output on a scale of 1 to 5 (with 5 being the highest) with accompanying ex-
358
                 planations based on predefined criteria. As one of the most advanced LLMs, GPT-4o’s
                 semantic evaluation ensures a more accurate assessment of how well the models capture
359
                 trend-related information.
360
361            • Human Evaluation: Five human experts in the field of time-series, including PhD stu-
362              dents, postdoctoral researchers, and academics, are engaged to evaluate the generated trend
363
                 descriptions. Following the same scoring criteria as GPT-4o, they assess the accuracy and
                 quality of the generated answers, providing a human-centered perspective on the model’s
364
                 outputs.

409   experiments were run five times with the following parameters: 8 training epochs, a batch size of 4,

      Baselines. To evaluate our model, we benchmark it against 11 baselines. The first set includes state-
413
      of-the-art time-series (TS) models: Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), Informer (Zhou et al., 2021),
414   NS-Transformer (Liu et al., 2022), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023a), and
415   iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024c). The second set consists of state-of-the-art HAR models: DeepCon-
416   vLSTM (Ordóñez & Roggen, 2016), DeepConvLSTM + Attention (DeepConvLSTMAttn) (Mura-
417   hari & Plötz, 2018), and Attend (Abedin et al., 2021). For a fairer comparison, we also include
418   Chronos+MLP and GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2023b) as baselines. GPT4TS is a recent work that fine-
419   tunes the GPT-2 backbone (Radford et al., 2019) for time-series tasks. Detailed introduction of the
420   baselines can be found in the Appendix A.7.
421   Results. Table 2 presents the F1-macro results (%) for the task-aware tuning stage, averaged over
422   five random repetitions. SensorLLM achieves the best performance on four out of five datasets
423   (USC-HAD, PAMAP2, MHealth, and CAPTURE-24) and achieves the second-best performance
424   on the UCI-HAR dataset. These results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in handling
425   diverse sensor data and outperforming or matching state-of-the-art baselines across most datasets.
426   Specifically, SensorLLM achieves a notable improvement on the CAPTURE-24 dataset, surpassing
427   all baselines by a significant margin with a mean F1-macro score of 48.6%, which is 5.0% higher
428   than Attend (43.6%). On USC-HAD, SensorLLM achieves the highest score of 61.2%, outperform-
429   ing Attend, the second-best baseline, by 1.0%. Similarly, on PAMAP2, SensorLLM achieves a mean
430   F1-macro score of 86.2%, exceeding Attend (84.6%) by 1.6%. On MHealth, SensorLLM sets a new
431   state-of-the-art with a mean score of 89.4%, surpassing Attend (83.4%) by 6.0%. These results
      highlight SensorLLM’s ability to consistently outperform existing methods across diverse datasets.

524   7   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
525
526   In this study, we introduced SensorLLM, a novel multimodal framework for aligning sensor data
527
      with automatically generated text at a human-perception level, unlike previous machine-level align-
      ment methods. SensorLLM captures and interprets complex sensor data patterns, outperforming or
528
      matching state-of-the-art models in HAR tasks. Experiments across diverse datasets demonstrate its
529
      robustness and flexibility in handling variable-length sequences, different input channels, and addi-
530   tional textual information such as metadata or statistical analysis. Cross-dataset experiments further
531   highlight its strong generalizability without dataset-specific alignment. This work lays a founda-
532   tion for a versatile Sensor-Text MLLM framework, with potential applications in chain-of-thought
533   reasoning, sensor data generation, and zero-shot or few-shot learning. We have open-sourced our
534   code and data generation methods to support future research in aligning time-series and text data,
535   especially in domains with limited textual labels.

813   A.1   M ORE RELATED WORK
814
815   Deep learning in human activity recognition. Over the last decade, HAR has transitioned from
816   hand-crafted feature extraction to deep learning models capable of automatic feature learning. Early
817   work by Kwapisz et al. (2011) utilized machine learning techniques, such as decision trees and
818   MLPs, to classify activities using features extracted from wearable sensor data. Later, Haresamu-
819   dram et al. (2019) demonstrated that optimized feature extraction within the Activity Recognition
820   Chain (ARC) could rival or outperform end-to-end deep learning models. Deep learning models,
821
      particularly CNNs and LSTMs, have since become dominant in HAR. Bevilacqua et al. (2019) de-
      veloped a CNN-based model for HAR, while Ha & Choi (2016) introduced CNN-pf and CNN-pff
822
      architectures that apply partial and full weight sharing for better feature extraction. Other notable
823
      works include Perception-Net Kasnesis et al. (2019), which leverages 2D convolutions for multi-
824   modal sensor data, and InnoHAR (Xu et al., 2019), which combines Inception CNN and GRUs
825   for multiscale temporal feature learning. A dual-stream network utilizing convolutional layers and
826   LSTM units, known as ConvLSTM, was employed by Yuki et al. (2018) to analyze complex tem-
827   poral hierarchies with streams handling different time lengths. The combination of attention mech-
828   anisms with recurrent networks to enhance the computation of weights for hidden state outputs has
829   also been demonstrated by DeepConvLSTM Kasnesis et al. (2019) in capturing spatial-temporal
830   features.
831   Large Language Models for Time-Series Forecasting. LLMs have achieved remarkable success
832   in text-related tasks, and their utility has expanded into time-series forecasting. Xue & Salim (2023)
833   presents PromptCast, which redefines time-series forecasting as a natural language generation task
834   by transforming numerical inputs into textual prompts, enabling pre-trained language models to
835   handle forecasting tasks with superior generalization in zero-shot settings. Gruver et al. (2023)
836   explores encoding time-series as numerical strings, allowing LLMs like GPT-3 and LLaMA-2 to
837   perform zero-shot forecasting, matching or surpassing the performance of specialized models, while
838
      highlighting challenges in uncertainty calibration due to model modifications like RLHF. Zhou et al.
      (2023a) demonstrates that pre-trained language and image models, such as a Frozen Pretrained
839
      Transformer (FPT), can be adapted for diverse time-series tasks like classification, forecasting, and
840
      anomaly detection, leveraging self-attention mechanisms to bridge the gap between different data
841   types and achieving state-of-the-art performance across various tasks. Jin et al. (2024b) highlights
842   the transformative potential of LLMs for time-series analysis by integrating language models with
843   traditional analytical methods. Jin et al. (2024a) introduces a reprogramming framework that aligns
844   time-series data with natural language processing capabilities, enabling LLMs to perform time-
845   series forecasting without altering the core model structure. Cao et al. (2024) presents TEMPO,
846   a generative transformer framework based on prompt tuning, which adapts pre-trained models for
847   time-series forecasting by decomposing trends, seasonality, and residual information. Sun et al.
848   (2024a) proposes TEST, an innovative embedding technique that integrates time-series data with
849
      LLMs through instance-wise, feature-wise, and text-prototype-aligned contrast, yielding improved
      or comparable results across various applications. Chang et al. (2024) develops a framework that
850
      enhances pre-trained LLMs for multivariate time-series forecasting through a two-stage fine-tuning
851
      process and a novel multi-scale temporal aggregation method, outperforming traditional models in
852   both full-shot and few-shot scenarios. Finally, Liu et al. (2024b) introduces UniTime, a unified
853   model that leverages language instructions and a Language-TS Transformer to handle multivariate
854   time series across different domains, demonstrating enhanced forecasting performance and zero-shot
855   transferability.
856
      LLMs for Human Activity Recognition. While LLMs like ChatGPT have demonstrated remark-
857
      able performance in various NLP tasks, their effectiveness in HAR remains limited due to challenges
858   in interpreting sensor data. These models often struggle to distinguish between activities that share
859   similar objects, requiring more advanced prompt engineering to highlight activity-specific details.
860   Xia et al. (2023) proposed an unsupervised approach to HAR using ChatGPT, leveraging two-stage
861   prompts to infer activities from object sequences without manual descriptions. The method demon-
862   strates superior performance on three benchmark datasets, marking a significant advancement in
863   applying language models to activity recognition tasks. Similarly, Ji et al. (2024) explored LLMs
      for zero-shot HAR using raw IMU data, showing that GPT-4 can outperform both traditional and

999    Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024) is an innovative framework for pretrained probabilistic time series
1000   models. It tokenizes time series data into a fixed vocabulary through scaling and quantization, then
1001   trains T5-based (Raffel et al., 2020) language models on these tokenized sequences using cross-
1002   entropy loss. Pretrained on a diverse corpus of public and synthetic datasets, Chronos models
1003   demonstrate remarkable performance, outperforming other methods on familiar datasets and show-
1004
       ing competitive zero-shot capabilities on unseen tasks. This approach leverages data from vari-
       ous domains to enhance forecasting accuracy, potentially simplifying time series analysis pipelines
1005
       across different fields.

       In this section, we describe the various evaluation metrics used to assess the performance of Sensor-
1047
       LLM in generating trend descriptions from sensor data. Each metric offers a distinct perspective on
1048
       model performance, ranging from surface-level textual similarity to more complex semantic align-
1049   ment.
1050
1051   BLEU-1 (Papineni et al., 2002). BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) is a precision-based
1052
       metric commonly used to evaluate machine-generated text by comparing it to reference texts.
       BLEU-1 focuses on unigram (single-word) overlap, assessing the lexical similarity between the
1053
       generated and reference text. While useful for measuring word-level matches, BLEU-1 does not
1054
       capture deeper semantic meaning, making it most effective for surface-level alignment.
1055
1056   ROUGE-1 and ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004). ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Eval-
1057   uation) evaluates the recall-oriented overlap between generated text and reference text. ROUGE-1
1058
       focuses on unigram recall, similar to BLEU-1 but emphasizing how much of the reference text
       is captured. ROUGE-L measures the longest common subsequence, assessing both precision and
1059
       recall in terms of structure and content overlap, though it does not evaluate semantic accuracy.
1060
1061   METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005). METEOR (Metric for Evaluation of Translation with Ex-
1062   plicit Ordering)combines precision and recall, with additional alignment techniques such as stem-
1063   ming and synonym matching. Unlike BLEU and ROUGE, METEOR accounts for some degree
1064
       of semantic similarity. However, its emphasis is still on word-level alignment rather than factual
       accuracy or meaning.
1065
1066   SBERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019). SBERT (Sentence-BERT) 2 is a metric that generates sen-
1067   tence embeddings using the BERT architecture. It computes cosine similarity between embeddings
1068   of the generated and reference texts, providing a deeper assessment of semantic similarity beyond
1069   lexical matches.
1070   SimCSE (Gao et al., 2021). SimCSE (Simple Contrastive Sentence Embedding) 3 introduces a
1071   contrastive learning approach to fine-tune language models for sentence embeddings. By applying
1072   different dropout masks to the same sentence, it generates positive examples, encouraging similar
1073   embeddings for semantically identical sentences while distinguishing different ones.
1074   GPT-4o Evaluation. In addition to the NLP metrics, we also employed GPT-4o as a human-like
1075   evaluator. Given its strong reasoning and comprehension abilities, GPT-4o was tasked with scoring
1076   the generated text based on its alignment with the ground truth. GPT-4o evaluated the correctness,
1077   completeness, and coherence of the trend descriptions and assigned a score from 1 to 5, accompanied

1207   In our evaluation, we use the F1-macro score to assess the model’s performance across datasets. F1-
1208   macro is particularly suitable for datasets with imbalanced label distributions, which is common in

1210   have fewer samples. Unlike the micro F1 score, which emphasizes the performance on frequent
1211

       and then averaging them.
