011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Driven by the goal of data-driven analysis on the large-scale cohort, a large lan-
014            guage model(LLM) has solidified itself as a critical focus of artificial intelligence
015
               medical research today. However, such efforts have coalesced around a small
               group of evidence, leaving behind the vast majority of factors collected in the
016
               cohort investigation. What does it take to break the more than 70 factors while
017
               ensuring responsible, high-quality prediction, all while keeping medical consid-
018            erations in mind? In No Factor Left Behind, we first took on this challenge by
019            numerical interpretable evidence contextualizing the need for Premature rupture
020            of membranes (PROM) risk assessment through exploratory interviews with do-
021            main experts. Then, we created datasets and models aimed at narrowing the per-
022            formance gap between low and high-frequency factors. More specifically, we
023            developed a model based on factor-value pairs trained on data obtained with ro-
024            bust and effective data mining techniques tailored for low-frequency factors. We
025            propose multiple architectural and training improvements to counteract overfit-
026
               ting while training on 70 factors. Critically, we interpreted the risk of PROM over
               7000 cohort participants’ directions using numerical interpretable evidence with
027
               precise values of factors combined with human evaluation covering all factors in
028
               the dataset to assess medical safety. Our model achieves a performance of 79%
029            accuracy (78 factors) and 96% accuracy(40 factors) with risk assessment at the
030            screening level, laying the novel insight for realizing a general medical cohort
031            analysis method in the era of LLMs.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   There may not exist another domain like medical cohort analysis that requires both a high level of
037   expert knowledge and substantial human resources while acquiring expert-interpreted data is quite
038   expensive. Medical cohort studies involve the systematic collection and analysis of vast amounts of
039
      heterogeneous data, encompassing clinical measurements, demographic information, genetic data,
      lifestyle factors, and more. The integration and interpretation of these diverse factors are crucial
040
      for understanding disease mechanisms, predicting patient outcomes, and personalizing treatment
041
      strategies. Traditionally, medical cohort analyses have focused on a limited set of well-established
042   factors, often driven by prior clinical knowledge or the availability of high-frequency data. While
043   this approach has yielded significant insights, it inherently overlooks a multitude of potentially rel-
044   evant factors that may have low prevalence or are less studied. Ignoring these factors can lead to
045   incomplete models that fail to capture the complexity of medical conditions, potentially missing
046   critical predictors of patient outcomes.
047
      Recent advancements in artificial intelligence, particularly the development of large language mod-
048   els (LLMs), have opened new avenues for data-driven analysis in healthcare. LLMs excel at han-
049   dling large-scale, high-dimensional data and can uncover complex patterns that traditional statistical
050   methods might miss. Currently, medical LLMs have made significant strides in enhancing clinical
051   decision support, medical documentation, and patient interaction. Models like Biollama and Clini-
052   calBERT have demonstrated improved performance in tasks such as disease classification, symptom
053   extraction, and electronic health record (EHR) analysis (Kraljevic et al., 2021; Saab et al., 2024; Wu
      et al., 2023). Additionally, specialized LLMs are increasingly being integrated into diagnostic tools,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      enabling more accurate and timely predictions (Qin et al., 2023). These advancements underscore
055   the potential of LLMs to transform healthcare by providing deeper insights and supporting more
056   informed medical decisions. However, the application of LLMs in medical cohort analysis has pre-
057   dominantly concentrated on a narrow set of evidence, leaving the vast majority of collected factors
058   underutilized. This imbalance not only limits the predictive power of models but also restricts the
059   discovery of novel insights that could emerge from a more comprehensive analysis.
060
      In this paper, we aim to leverage the powerful pre-trained large language models like llama3.1 series
061   and Phi3.5 MoE with expressive medical prompts to make efficient domain transfers from natural
062   language to medical language for risk assessment. To this end, we first explore how to manually
063   design effective medical prompts by using hierarchical prompt with Chain of thought(CoT), and
064   show that such well-designed prompts can significantly improve the domain transfer risk assessment
065   compared to the default factors names and values. Intuitively, the common factors’ names in text
066   prompts, such as education level, sleeping time, and clinical measurements, are different aspects
067   of participants, and therefore, by clustering factors to these expressive attributes in the prompts,
068   the LLMs can selectively learn to align features’ meaning with value in the prompts rather than
069
      aimlessly learning.
070   Furthermore, to improve the efficiency and avoid the laborious manual annotations, we propose
071   several approaches, i.e., masked language model (MLM) auto-prompt generation with numerical
072   feature interaction map, factors’ knowledge specific auto-prompt generation or a hybrid of both, to
073   automatically generate medical prompts that make the LLMs perform on par with the model with
074   manually elaborated prompts. The MLM-driven approach mainly focuses on extracting expert-level
075
      knowledge from pre-trained language models specialized in the medical cohort domain. In contrast,
      the cohort-specific prompt generation, based on the Table question answering (TableQA) system,
076
      allows the flexibility in designing prompts to include cohort-specific attribute information rather
077
      than using a single fixed prompt for all participants during inference.
078
079   We evaluate our approaches on a wide range of existing open-source models across different arch,
080   context window, and parameter sizes. The models with our well-designed medical cohort prompts
081
      exhibit significant superiority over those with default prompts in terms of zero-shot and few-shot
      performance, some surpassing the supervised model trained with full data. Moreover, our fine-tuned
082
      models outperform the traditional supervised baselines by a significant margin across almost all
083
      models.

      transfer learning between natural and medical language domains, prompt design in language models,

      table question answering (TableQA), retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and the integration of

094   2.1   T RANSFER L EARNING B ETWEEN NATURAL AND M EDICAL L ANGUAGE D OMAINS
095
096   Transfer learning has become a prevalent strategy for training deep neural networks in domains
097   with out-of-distribution data, such as the medical field. In natural language processing (NLP), mod-
098   els pre-trained on large-scale general-domain corpora are fine-tuned on domain-specific datasets to
099
      adapt to specialized vocabulary and concepts. This approach is particularly valuable in the medical
      domain, where annotated data is scarce and expensive to obtain due to the need for expert interpre-
100
      tation. Several studies have explored the transfer of linguistic knowledge from natural to medical
101
      language domains. For instance, BioBERT (Lee et al., 2019) and ClinicalBERT (Alsentzer et al.,
102   2019) are adaptations of BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), pre-trained on biomedical and clinical text
103   corpora, respectively (Singhal et al., 2022). These models have shown significant improvements
104   in various medical NLP tasks, including named entity recognition, relation extraction, and ques-
105   tion answering(Zhao et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022c;b). However, most of these models focus on
106   high-frequency medical terms and conditions, potentially overlooking low-frequency but clinically
107   significant factors. Our work addresses this gap by developing models capable of integrating a
      broader range of factors from medical cohorts.

      2.2   P ROMPT D ESIGN
109
110   Knowledge-intensive domains like medicine require language models to comprehend and generate
111   domain-specific content accurately. Prompting techniques have emerged as a way to guide lan-
112   guage models in generating desired outputs by framing tasks as text-completion problems. Effective,
113   prompt design is crucial for eliciting the correct information from language models, especially when
114   dealing with specialized knowledge. Recent advancements have introduced methods like prompt
115   tuning (Lester et al., 2021) and instruction-based learning (Mishra et al., 2022), which fine-tune
116
      language models with minimal additional parameters or adapt them using natural language instruc-
      tions. In the medical domain, prompt design helps models interpret complex clinical queries and
117
      generate responses that are both accurate and contextually appropriate (Wang et al., 2024; Zaghir
118
      et al., 2024). Our approach leverages prompt design to enhance the interpretability and reliability of
119   risk assessments, ensuring that all factors in the cohort are considered.
120
121
      2.3   TABLE Q UESTION A NSWERING
122
123   TableQA involves interpreting structured data and answering queries based on the information con-
124   tained within tables. Large language models have shown promise in comprehending and analyzing
125   tabular data, which is crucial for medical cohort analysis, where patient data is often stored in tabular
126   form. Models like TaBERT (Yin et al., 2020) and TAPAS (Herzig et al., 2020) have been developed
127   to jointly encode tables and text, enabling them to perform tasks like table-based question answering
128   and fact verification (Zhang et al., 2024; Zha et al., 2023) . These models integrate the structural
129
      information of tables with textual data, allowing for more nuanced understanding. However, privacy
      concerns in medical data limit the use of proprietary models. Our work builds upon open-source
130
      LLMs to process cohort data effectively meeting the biosafety and privacy constraints while provid-
131
      ing a practical method for medical cohort data analysis.
132
133
      2.4   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION (RAG)
134
135   RAG is a methodology that enhances language models by providing them with direct access to
136   external knowledge bases during the generation process. By retrieving relevant information, models
137   can produce outputs that are more accurate and informative, especially in domains where up-to-date
138   or specialized knowledge is essential (Li et al., 2024). In the context of medical cohort analysis,
139   RAG can help reduce hallucinations—instances where the model generates incorrect or nonsensical
140   information and enhance reasoning abilities in risk assessments. Lewis et al. demonstrated that
141   incorporating retrieval mechanisms allows models to generate more factual responses (Lewis et al.,
142
      2021). Our study builds upon RAG by embedding information from the cohort population and
      participant factors, thereby improving the model’s ability to consider all relevant factors and produce
143
      more reliable risk assessments.
144
145
      2.5   T OOLFORMER
146
147   The Toolformer technique enables large language models to leverage external tools through self-
148   supervised learning (Schick et al., 2023). By training the model to determine which APIs to call,
149   when to call them, and how to integrate the results, Toolformer extends the capabilities of LLMs
150   beyond text generation. Our study utilizes these advancements by training an LLM to incorporate
151   machine learning-based information into natural language risk assessments (Lundberg & Lee, 2017).
152   This approach enhances both the robustness and interpretability of the screening process without
153   the need for expert annotation, thereby streamlining the analysis and making it more scalable. By
154   integrating external computational tools, the model can perform complex calculations and access
155
      up-to-date data, which is critical for accurate medical assessments.

168   3.1   PRELIMINARIES
169
170
      Unifying tabular data and language pre-training norms have emerged as a powerful approach to im-
      prove LLM performance in various table-related tasks, showcasing promising cross-domain transfer
171
      capabilities. Inspired by the success of incorporating language supervision in visual recognition,
172
      TableLLM adopts a similar philosophy by integrating textual prompts with tabular data. For in-
173   stance, when dealing with spreadsheet-embedded tabular data, TableLLM receives both the table
174   header and a subset of rows alongside a text prompt specifying the desired manipulation operation.
175   This prompt can take the form: Prompt = ”[Operation]-[Subcategory] Instruction”, where [Opera-
176   tion] denotes the main operation type (e.g., Query, Update, Merge, Chart) and [Subcategory] speci-
177   fies the sub-operation (e.g., Filter, Aggregate, Sort). This integration allows TableLLM to leverage
178   the rich semantic information embedded within natural language instructions to effectively under-
179   stand and execute complex tabular data manipulations. It is not hard to see that the data-text inputs
180   have been sufficiently aligned, so one could provide an auxiliary prompt input to guide the LLMs
181
      to reasoning the factors’ value and association more easily. Given that, we believe a well-designed
      prompt could largely enhance the performance of the pre-trained models on the table-related tasks,
182
      especially in an unfamiliar domain like the medical cohort

531
      This paper comprehensively studies how to leverage the large-scale large-language models pre-
      trained on general language tasks to the medical cohorts. We present that well-designed medical
532
      prompts containing domain-specific knowledge are the key to bridging the gap between domains.
533
      Therefore, we propose several approaches to generate medical prompts manually or automatically.
534   While the manual approach tremendously improves the zero-shot performance compared to the
535   default prompts with object names, the automatic approaches allow us to generate expert knowledge
536   augmented and cohort-specific prompts on a large scale. Extensive experiments are conducted on
537   the 11 different medical models across various aspects, showing that the prompts generated by our
538   approaches can improve the transfer performance, and our fine-tuned models surpass the supervised
539   baselines by a large margin. This superior domain transfer performance also prompts us to explore
      more cohort-efficient language algorithms to benefit medical cohort understanding.
