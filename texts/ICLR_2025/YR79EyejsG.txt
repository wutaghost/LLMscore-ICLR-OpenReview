009                                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Real-world environments require robots to continuously acquire new skills while
012            retaining previously learned abilities, all without the need for clearly defined task
013            boundaries. Storing all past data to prevent forgetting is impractical due to storage
014            and privacy concerns. To address this, we propose a method that efficiently re-
015            stores a robot’s proficiency in previously learned tasks over its lifespan. Using an
016            Episodic Memory (EM), our approach enables experience replay during training
017
               and retrieval during testing for local fine-tuning, allowing rapid adaptation to pre-
               viously encountered problems. Additionally, we introduce a selective weighting
018
               mechanism that emphasizes the most challenging segments of retrieved demon-
019
               strations, focusing local adaptation where it is most needed. This framework offers
020            a scalable solution for lifelong learning without explicit task identifiers or implicit
021            task boundaries, combining retrieval-based adaptation with selective weighting to
022            enhance robot performance in open-ended scenarios.

049   Lifelong learning seeks to endow neural networks with the ability to continually acquire new skills
050   while retaining previously learned knowledge. This balance between stability and plasticity is cru-
051   cial as models face sequences of tasks over time. While significant progress has been made in apply-
052   ing lifelong learning to domains such as computer vision (Huang et al., 2024; Du et al., 2024) and
053   natural language processing (Shi et al., 2024; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023), the challenges are more
      pronounced in robotics. Robots are expected to adaptively learn and solve unseen tasks throughout


                                                                                 1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      their operational lifespan (Thrun & Mitchell, 1995). Their interactions with dynamic environments
055   introduce complexities absent in static data domains; a single misstep in task execution can result in
056   complete failure. Moreover, robotics is constrained by limited data availability due to the expense
057   and complexity of real-world interactions (Zhu et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023). These factors not
058   only intensify the difficulty of continual learning in robotics but also demand more robust lifelong
059   learning capabilities.
060
      Lifelong robot learning typically requires robots to learn a sequence of tasks, each distinguished
061   by domain, scenario, scene, or task goals (Liu et al., 2024; 2023). Existing lifelong robot learning
062   algorithms often rely heavily on task IDs or boundaries. However, in dynamic real-world settings, it
063   is impractical to predefine tasks or assign specific IDs, as robots are likely to encounter a vast array
064   of unpredictable situations, with tasks that may be subdivided into smaller components of varying
065   granularity. Therefore, approaches that depend on specific task identifications with clear boundaries
066   are unrealistic and unscalable (Koh et al., 2021).
067   To address these challenges, we propose a novel task-unaware lifelong robot learning framework,
068   which enables robots to continually learn and adapt without knowing task IDs or boundaries. We
069   employ our method in manipulation scenarios based on the LIBERO benchmark (Liu et al., 2024).
070   Our approach leverages pre-trained models to generate consistent embeddings across different tasks
071   and training phases, thereby mitigating the embedding drift that often occurs in sequential learning
072   scenarios (Liu et al., 2023; Kawaharazuka et al., 2024). We adopt Experience Replay (ER) baseline
073   (de Masson D’Autume et al., 2019) to rehearse samples from previous tasks, helping to maintain
074   learned skills and reduce forgetting.
075   Despite these measures, some degree of forgetting remains inevitable due to the multitasking nature
076   of lifelong learning and the robot’s limited access to previous demonstrations. Drawing inspiration
077   from human learning processes — where individuals revisit knowledge they once knew but have for-
078   gotten details — we introduce an efficient local adaptation mechanism. Humans often perform quick
079   reviews using limited resources, allowing them to efficiently regain proficiency without relearning
080   all aspects of the task (Sara, 2000). Similarly, when the robot encounters a forgotten scenario, our
081
      mechanism enables it to swiftly adapt and regain skills through fast fine-tuning, using the same
      episodic memory employed for experience replay during training.
082
083   Given the indistinct task boundaries, we leverage retrieval-based mechanisms (Du et al., 2023; van
084   Dijk et al., 2024; de Masson D’Autume et al., 2019) to retrieve data most similar to the testing
085   scenario based on vision and language input similarities. To adapt the model effectively — especially
086   focusing on the most challenging phases where the robot’s performance deviates — we first perform
087
      a few episodes of rollouts to obtain ”feedback” on the model’s performance before local adaptation:
      these rollouts are then used for automatic selective weighting by comparing them with the retrieved
088
      demonstrations without human intervention (Spencer et al., 2022; Mandlekar et al., 2020). The
089
      weighted samples facilitate the local adaptation phase, thereby improving performance.
090
091   In summary, the key contributions of our solution are:
092
             • Task-unaware Retrieval-Based Local Adaptation: During testing, the robot retrieves
093
               relevant past demonstrations from episodic memory to locally adapt the neural network,
094
               enabling it to quickly regain proficiency on previously encountered but forgotten scenarios
095            without relying on explicit task IDs nor implicit task boundaries.
096
097
             • Selective Weighting Mechanism: A weighting mechanism emphasizes the most challeng-
               ing segments of the retrieved demonstrations, optimizing real-time adaptation.
098
099          • Paradigm for Memory-Based Lifelong Robot Learning: We demonstrate that our ap-
100            proach can be applied to different memory-based robotic lifelong learning algorithms dur-
101
               ing test time, serving as a paradigm for skill restoration.

105   2.1   L IFELONG ROBOT L EARNING
106
107   Robots operating in continually changing environments need the ability to learn and adapt on-the-
      fly (Thrun, 1995). In recent years, lifelong robot learning has been applied to SLAM (Yin et al.,

      2023; Gao et al., 2022; Vödisch et al., 2022), navigation (Kim et al., 2024), and task and motion
109   planning scenarios (Mendez-Mendez et al., 2023). In the context of continual learning from demon-
110   strations, robots can 1) acquire skills from non-technical users (Grollman & Jenkins, 2007), or 2)
111
      adapt to user-specific task preferences — Chen et al. (2023) proposed strategy mixture approach to
112
      efficiently model new incoming demonstrations, enhancing adaptability.
113
114   Furthermore, methods have been developed to improve manipulation capabilities over a robot’s
115   lifespan. Some approaches leverage previous data to facilitate forward transfer but suffer from
116
      catastrophic forgetting (Xie & Finn, 2022). Others maintain an expandable skill set to accommodate
      an increasing number of manipulation tasks (Parakh et al., 2024), or continually update models of
117
      manipulable objects for effective reuse (Lu et al., 2022). Large language models have been utilized to
118
      improve knowledge transfer (Bärmann et al., 2023; Tziafas & Kasaei, 2024; Wang et al., 2023), and
119   hypernetworks with neural ODEs have been employed to remember long trajectories (Auddy et al.,
120   2023) incrementally. Additionally, (Yang et al., 2022) evaluates how typical supervised lifelong
121   learning methods can be applied in reinforcement learning scenarios for robotic tasks.
122
      To standardize the investigation of lifelong decision-making and bridge research gaps, Liu et al.
123
      (2024) introduced LIBERO, a benchmarking platform for lifelong robot manipulation where robots
124
      learn multiple atomic manipulation tasks sequentially. Recent works exploring lifelong robot learn-
125   ing based on it include (Liu et al., 2023), which assigns a specific task identity to each task, and
126   (Wan et al., 2024), which requires a pre-training phase to build an initial skill set before continual
127   learning. However, catastrophic forgetting for lifelong robot learning remains an open challenge,
128   especially when task IDs and boundaries are not available.
129
130   2.2   TASK - UNAWARE C ONTINUAL L EARNING
131
132   Despite the success of continual learning with clearly labeled task sequences, there still remains
133   a gap in progress within settings where algorithm is unaware of task boundaries both in training
134   and inference, an online situation more reflective of real-world scenarios. Many attempts (Lee
135   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Ardywibowo et al., 2022) focus on learning specialized parame-
136   ters using expanding network structures. Memory-based algorithms remain effective by prioritiz-
137
      ing informative samples (Sun et al., 2022), removing less important training samples (Koh et al.,
      2021), improving decision boundaries (Shim et al., 2021), and increasing gradient diversity (Aljundi
138
      et al., 2019b). Methods aiming to exploit replay buffer (He et al., 2020; Mai et al., 2021; Caccia
139
      et al., 2021) have also demonstrated notable success. Moreover, researchers (Aljundi et al., 2019a)
140
      tackled the issue of implicit task boundaries in regularization-based lifelong learning methods
141
      (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Aljundi et al., 2018; Zenke et al., 2017) by introducing an online learning
142
      approach that consolidates knowledge upon detecting a loss ”plateau”. However, these approaches
143
      have not been explored in robotic applications that involve decision-making and physical interac-
144
      tions with the environment.
145

147   2.3   I NFORMATION R ETRIEVAL FOR ROBOTICS
148
      Information retrieval techniques have been used to optimize robotic behaviors by retrieving relevant
149
      actions from memory in novel tasks (Du et al., 2023). For example, path following based on image
150
      retrieval improves visual navigation (van Dijk et al., 2024), and incremental learning helps humanoid
151   robots adapt to new environments by recalling past behaviors (Bärmann et al., 2023). Retrieval has
152   also enabled skill transfer from videos (Papagiannis et al., 2024) and affordance transfer for zero-
153   shot manipulation (Kuang et al., 2024), allowing robots to manipulate objects without prior training.

191
192   Unlike previous approaches (Liu et al., 2023; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018)
193   that rely on explicit task identifiers or implicit task boundaries, such rigid categorization becomes
194   impractical in open-ended environments. To model these realistic conditions, we define a set of
195   tasks T = {Tk }, k = 1, 2, . . . , T , where each task is represented by Tk encompassing various envi-
196   ronmental settings (e.g., objects positions, robot initial states) and language descriptions (e.g., ”pick
197   the bottle and put it into basket”, ”Please place the bottle into basket”). From Tk , we sample spe-
198   cific environmental settings and language descriptions to generate a concrete scenario Snk ∼ p(Tk ),
199   which serves as the basis for collecting demonstrations τnk . Multiple demonstrations form the train-
200   ing dataset Dk = {τnk }, n = 1, 2, . . . , N for task Tk .
201
202   Notably, multiple tasks may share overlapping distributions in either environmental settings or lan-
203   guage descriptions, and our lifelong learning approach operates without access to task identifiers or
204   boundaries between different tasks Tk . This natural setting closely mirrors real-world conditions,
205   where it is difficult to determine which task generated a given scenario. This ambiguity underpins
206   the proposed method’s task-unaware design: instead of relying on task identification, it focuses on
207   retrieving relevant information.
208   Our robot utilizes a visuomotor policy learned through behavior cloning to execute manipulation
209   tasks by mapping sensory inputs and task descriptions to motor actions. The policy is trained
210   by minimizing the discrepancy between the predicted actions and the expert actions derived from
211   demonstrations. Specifically, we optimize the following loss function across a sequence of tasks T
212   with Dk . Notably, Dk is not fully accessible for k < K due to the use of experience replay from
213   Episodic Memory M, where K denotes the current task:

229   In this section, we outline our proposed method depicted in Figure 1, with corresponding pseudocode
230   in Algorithm 1. To effectively interact with complex physical environments, the network integrates
231   multiple input modalities, including visual inputs from workspace and wrist cameras, proprioceptive
232   inputs of joint and gripper states, and task descriptions.
233   Instead of training all modules jointly in an end-to-end manner, we employ pretrained visual and lan-
234   guage encoders that leverage prior semantic knowledge. Pretrained encoders enhance performance
235   on downstream manipulation tasks (Liu et al., 2023) and are well-suited to differentiate between var-
236   ious scenarios and tasks without relying on explicit task identifiers or clear task boundaries. Their
237   consistent representations when new tasks continue to come is essential for managing multitask
238   problems and retrieving relevant data to support our proposed local adaptation during test time.
239   When learning new tasks, the robot preserves previously acquired skills by replaying prior manipu-
240   lation demonstrations stored in an episodic memory M, which contains a small subset of previous
241   task demonstrations (Chaudhry et al., 2019). Trained with the combined data from the latest sce-
242   narios and episodic memory M, the model can acquire new skills while mitigating catastrophic
243   forgetting of old tasks, thereby maintaining a balance between stability and plasticity (Wang et al.,
244   2024). Figure 2 illustrates the network architecture, and implementation details are provided in
245
      Section A.2.

      During deployment, we first retrieve the most relevant demonstrations from episodic memory M
249
      based on similarity to the current scenario. Due to the unclear task boundaries, some tasks share
250   similar visual observations but differ in their task objectives, while others have similar goals but
251   involve different backgrounds, objects, etc. To account for these variations, we compare both visual
252   inputs from the workspace camera (Du et al., 2023) and task descriptions (de Masson D’Autume
253   et al., 2019) using L2 distances of their embeddings. The retrieval process follows a simple rule:

257   of the scene observation from the workspace camera, and Dl depicts the distance between the task

      5.1.2     BASELINES
350
351   We evaluate our proposed method against the following baseline approaches:
352
353           1. Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017): A regularization-based
354              approach that constrains updates to the network’s parameters to prevent catastrophic for-
355
                 getting of previously learned tasks.
356           2. Experience Replay (ER) (Chaudhry et al., 2019): A core component of our training setup,
357              ER utilizes stored episodic memory to replay past demonstrations, helping the model main-
358
                 tain previously acquired skills and mitigate forgetting. As a baseline, we evaluate the stan-
                 dalone performance of ER without additional retrieval-based adaptation techniques.
359
360           3. Average Gradient Episodic Memory (AGEM) (Hu et al., 2020): Employs a memory
361              buffer to constrain gradients during the training of new tasks, ensuring that updates do not
362
                 interfere with performance on earlier tasks.
363           4. AGEM with Weighted Local Adaptation (AGEM-WLA): An extension of AGEM that
364              incorporates weighted local adaptation during the testing phase, enhancing the model’s
365              ability to adapt to specific tasks based on retrieved demonstrations. This allows us to assess
366
                 the generalizability of our proposed method as a paradigm framework on other memory-
                 based lifelong learning approaches.
367
368           5. PackNet (Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018): An architecture-based lifelong learning algorithm
369              that iteratively prunes the network after training each task, preserving essential nodes while
370
                 removing less critical connections to accommodate subsequent tasks. However, its pruning
                 and post-training phases rely heavily on clearly defined task boundaries, making PackNet
371
                 a reference baseline when task boundaries are well-defined.

383   5.1.4    M ODEL , T RAINING , AND E VALUATION
384
385   As illustrated in Figure. 2, our model utilizes pretrained encoders for visual and language inputs:
386
      R3M (Nair et al., 2022) for visual encoding, Sentence Similarity model (SS Model) (SentenceSimi-
      larity, 2024) for language embeddings, and a trainable MLP-based network to encode proprioceptive
387
      inputs. Embeddings from ten consecutive time steps are processed through a transformer-based tem-
388
      poral encoder, with the resulting output passed to a GMM-based policy head for action sampling.
389   Specifically, R3M, a ResNet-based model trained on egocentric videos using contrastive learning,
390   captures temporal dynamics and semantic features from scenes, while Sentence Similarity Model
391   captures semantic relationships in task descriptions, enabling the model to differentiate between
392   various natural language instructions.
393
      The model first undergoes a lifelong learning phase, where it is trained sequentially on 10 or 20
394
      tasks, depending on the specific benchmark, with each task trained for 50 epochs. After training on
395   each task, only random 20% of the demonstrations from that task are stored in the episodic memory
396   M, which is used for experience replay to maintain learned knowledge. Every 10 epochs, we check
397   the model’s performance and save the version that achieves the highest Success Rate to prevent
398   over-fitting.
399
      After training on all tasks sequentially, we conduct reviewing and testing on various scenarios sam-
400
      pled from each task for comprehensive analysis. During the reviewing stage, we firstly evaluate
401   potential forgetting by having the agent perform 10 rollout episodes on the deployment scenario
402   Sdeploy —referred to as a quiz phase. We then retrieve the most similar demonstrations from M and
403   fine-tune the model for only 20 epochs using the retrieved demonstrations with selective weighting.
404   Finally, we evaluate the adapted model for 20 episodes—the final testing phase—to assess perfor-
405   mance improvements. All training, local adaptation, and testing in the benchmarks are conducted
406   using three random seeds (1, 21, and 42) to reduce the impact of randomness.

408   5.2     R ESULTS
409
410   5.2.1    C OMPARISON WITH BASELINES
411
412   To address Question 2, we compared our proposed method, Weighted Local Adaptation (ER-WLA),
413   with all baseline approaches. As shown in Table 1, ER-WLA consistently outperforms baselines
414   of EWC, AGEM, ER, and AGEM-WLA, which do not rely on explicit task IDs. By incorporating
415   local adaptation during test time — our method mirrors how humans review and reinforce knowledge
416
      when it is partially forgotten — the continually learning robot could also regain its proficiency on
      previous tasks.
417
418   In contrast, PackNet serves as a reference method, as it requires well-defined task bound-
419   aries. However, as the number of tasks increases, the network’s trainable capacity under Pack-
420   Net diminishes, leaving less flexibility for future tasks. This limitation becomes evident in the
421   libero different scenes benchmark, which includes 20 tasks. PackNet’s success rate drops
422
      significantly for later tasks, resulting in poor overall performance and highlighting its constraints on
      plasticity compared with our proposed ER-WLA approach.
423
424   Additionally, when we applied WLA to the AGEM baseline (resulting in AGEM-WLA), it also im-
425   proved its performance, demonstrating the effectiveness of our method as a paradigm for memory-
426   based lifelong robot learning methods. These findings also support our conclusions regarding Ques-
427   tion 4.

906
907   In this section, we introduce our Selective Weighting mechanism in detail.
908
909   A.4.1    D ETAILED H EURISTICS AND I MPLEMENTATIONS
910
911   To assign weights to retrieved demonstrations, we analyze the image embedding distance between
912   demonstration and failed rollout trajectories. Typically, the embedding distance increases as the
913   failed rollout diverges from the demonstration. We selectively add weights for the frames in the re-
914
      trieved demonstration using the Embedding Distance Curve (EDC), derived from the Embedding
      Distance Matrix (EDM), as illustrated in Figure 9.
915
916   Due to the multi-modal nature of robotic actions and visual observation noise, raw embedding dis-
917   tances can be erratic. To mitigate this, we smooth the EDC using a moving average window. Despite
      smoothing, the curve may remain jittery, making it difficult to pinpoint a single divergence point.

953   Therefore, we identify a range of frames, termed the Separation Segment, where the distances
954   increase, indicating vulnerable steps that lead to task failure.
955   We apply two thresholds to identify the segment: a lower threshold at 18 and an upper threshold
956   at 13 of the maximum observed distance in EDC. We locate frames where the smoothed embedding
957   distance falls within this range, focusing on the last occurrence to account for initial divergences that
958   may later converge. We then extend this segment by 15 frames before and after to mitigate noise
959   effects.
960
      For each frame within the Separation Segment, we increment the corresponding weight in the ini-
961
      tially uniform weight vector by 0.3. This process is repeated for up to five failed rollouts per re-
962   trieved demonstration. After processing all demonstrations, we clip the weights to a maximum of
963   2 and normalize the weight vector to maintain consistent loss scaling and ensure stable gradient
964   updates. During adaptation, the resulting weights (wt,n ) are integrated into the loss function as de-
965   scribed in Equation equation 3. This selective weighting emphasizes critical samples while reducing
966   the influence of less relevant ones, thereby enhancing the model’s learning efficiency.
