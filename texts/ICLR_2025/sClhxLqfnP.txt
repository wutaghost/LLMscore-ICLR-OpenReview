035                                              A BSTRACT
036
037
              The generation of high-quality 3D car assets is essential for various applications,
038
              including video games, autonomous driving, and virtual reality. Current 3D gen-
039           eration methods utilizing NeRF or 3D-GS as representations for 3D objects, gen-
040           erate a Lambertian object under fixed lighting and lack separated modelings for
041           material and global illumination. As a result, the generated assets are unsuit-
042           able for relighting under varying lighting conditions, limiting their applicability in
043           downstream tasks. To address this challenge, we propose a novel relightable 3D
044           object generative framework that automates the creation of 3D car assets, enabling
045           the swift and accurate reconstruction of a vehicle’s geometry, texture, and mate-
046           rial properties from a single input image. Our approach begins with introducing
047
              a large-scale synthetic car dataset comprising over 1,000 high-precision 3D vehi-
              cle models. We represent 3D objects using global illumination and relightable 3D
048
              Gaussian primitives integrating with BRDF parameters. Building on this represen-
049
              tation, we introduce a feed-forward model that takes images as input and outputs
050           both relightable 3D Gaussians and global illumination parameters. Experimen-
051           tal results demonstrate that our method produces photorealistic 3D car assets that
052           can be seamlessly integrated into road scenes with different illuminations, which
053           offers substantial practical benefits for industrial applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Generating high-quality 3D car assets plays a vital role in various applications like digital city,
058   autonomous driving, and virtual reality, among others. Traditionally, the 3D modeling of vehicle
059   assets is hand-crafted by skilled artists using Computer-Aided Design (CAD) software. The modeled
060   cars are then integrated into rendering engines such as Blender Blender (2019) or Unreal Engine Epic
061   Games for photorealistic rendering. However, establishing such a 3D asset can be a cumbersome
062   task, especially when there is a need to model 3D assets based on images of real-world cars. In such
063   cases, the modeling artists are required to manually design and adjust the shape, texture and material
064
      of cars in detail to match the reference image accurately. To alleviate the burden on artists and
      facilitate complex car modeling, in this work, we focus on generating 3D car assets automatically
065
      with a data-driven method. Specifically, given an input image of a random 3D car, we aim to swiftly
066
      and precisely reconstruct its 3D geometry, texture, and material like roughness and metallic. This
067   allows applications like relighting and holds substantial promise for industrial design.
068
069   In recent years, there has been a revolution in 3D generation with the rise of diffusion models.
070
      Some works Poole et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023); Ornek et al. (2023) integrate NeRF Milden-
      hall et al. (2021) or 3D GS Kerbl et al. (2023) with diffusion models to generate 3D objects using
071
      text prompts. However, these optimized-based methods typically face challenges like slow gener-
072
      ation speed and unstable training processes. Other methods Shi et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023a;
073   2024) extend 2D diffusion models to generate consistent multi-view images by introducing view-
074   point awareness. For example, Wonder3dLong et al. (2024) generates consistent multi-view RGB
075   images with corresponding normal maps via a cross-domain diffusion model, with the input of a
076   reference image and camera parameters. However, existing multi-view image generation models
077   only map a single image to multiple views, necessitating supplementary modules like NeuSWang
078   et al. (2021) to reconstruct the 3D shape and texture. These object-specific models often demand a
079   lot of training time, ranging from minutes to hours. Recently, several works Tang et al. (2024a); Xu
080   et al. (2024); Tochilkin et al. (2024); Hong et al. (2023) have leveraged transformer architecture for
081
      a feed-forward 3D generation that takes images as input and outputs 3D representation like NeRF
      or 3D-GS. These methods offer rapid 3D model generation through fast feed-forward inference and
082
      provide more precise control over the generated outputs.
083
084   Despite these advancements, the 3D generation of vehicles still struggles with issues such as insuffi-
085   cient detail and inconsistent geometry. These limitations are primarily due to the lack of large-scale
086   car datasets that could support the learning of generalizable priors for vehicles. Besides, current 3D
087
      generation methods primarily focus on the geometry and texture of objects, often neglecting material
      modeling. In these methods, objects’ shading colors are typically baked into the surface, showing
088
      only the lighting effect under specific illumination. As a result, the generated objects are unsuitable
089
      for relighting with different illumination and require manual material assignment for downstream
090   applications, thereby limiting their practicality for industrial 3D asset generation.
091
092   To address the aforementioned problems, we propose a large-scale synthetic car dataset and a novel
093
      relightable 3D object generation pipeline, offering efficient solutions for rapid and precise 3D car
      asset creation. First, we collect and construct a synthesized vehicle dataset named Carverse, which
094
      contains over 1,000 high-precision 3D vehicle models. These models are rendered with different
095
      materials and under different lighting to obtain multi-view images and camera poses. With the
096   dataset, we introduce a novel relightable 3D-GS generative model (RGM) that: (1) We model a
097   3D scene with global illumination and a set of relightable 3D Gaussians, where each Gaussian
098   primitive is associated with a normal direction and BRDF parameters including albedo, roughness,
099   and metallic. (2) we adopt a U-Net Transformer as an image-conditioned model, which outputs
100   both the relightable 3D-GS and global illumination parameters. With this pipeline, we successfully
101   reconstruct the geometry, appearance, and material of a 3D car from an image, enabling the realistic
102   insertion of the car into different scenes. We further present an autonomous driving scene simulation
103   pipeline to demonstrate the practicality of RGM.
104   Fig. 1 provides qualitative results of the reconstructed cars with normal and material maps. Exper-
105   iments demonstrate that our method outperforms previous approaches both quantitatively and qual-
106   itatively. Additionally, we showcase the effectiveness of relighting and integrating the generated
107   car assets into road scenes. The results achieve photo-realistic performance, providing significant
      benefits for a wide range of AR/VR applications.

110   2.1   G ENERATIVE M ODEL
111
112   The goal of generative model is to produce new samples with similar statistical characteristics to
113   the training data by learning the underlying distribution. In recent years, there has been a revolution
114   in generative model, with groundbreaking networks named diffusion models Sohl-Dickstein et al.
115   (2015). Diffusion models have demonstrated significant success in image synthesis Rombach et al.
116   (2022); Zhang et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023); Podell et al. (2023) by generating new samples through
117
      the gradual denoising of a normally distributed variable. They have also inspired numerous works to
      generate 3D assets. Many works Poole et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2023); Ornek et al.
118
      (2023) integrate NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021) or 3D Gaussian Kerbl et al. (2023) with diffusion
119
      models to generate 3D objects using text prompts. Additionally, several works Shi et al. (2023);
120   Tang et al. (2024b); Liu et al. (2023a; 2024) extend 2D diffusion models to generate consistent
121   multi-view images by introducing viewpoint awareness. Moreover, some works Richardson et al.
122   (2023); Chen et al. (2023a); Youwang et al. (2023); Zeng (2023) focus on generating texture for
123   3D assets by extending text-conditional generative models. However, these works typically faces
124   challenges like slow generation speed and unstable optimization process. Recently, servral works
125   Tang et al. (2024a); Xu et al. (2024); Tochilkin et al. (2024); Hong et al. (2023) utilize transformer
126   architectures for feed-forward processes, converting images into 3D representations such as NeRF
127   or 3D-GS . These approaches enable rapid 3D model generation through efficient feed-forward
128
      inference. Nevertheless, these methods primarily concentrate on reconstructing shape and texture,
      which restricts their utility in practical industrial applications. In this study, we investigate a fast 3D
129
      generation pipeline capable of producing high-quality 3D car assets as well as material properties.
130
131
      2.2   3D G AUSSIAN S PLATTING
132
133   3D Gaussian splatting (3DGS) employs 3D Gaussian primitives to represent the 3D scene and al-
134   pha blending to render the image. In comparison to the NeRF, the 3DGS method exhibits supe-
135   rior rendering quality and speed. A number of previous studies have addressed the misrendering
136   issues caused by viewpoint changes during 3DGS rendering. Mip-splattingYu et al. (2024) and SA-
137   GSSong et al. (2024) address the frequency inconsistency of Gaussian primitives when the camera
138   is zoom-in or zoom-out by employing different 3D or 2D filters. Other works enhance the render-
139   ing quality by modifying the form of Gaussian primitives. For example, 2DGSHuang et al. (2024)
140
      compresses Gaussian primitives into 2D facets, enhancing the Gaussian geometry and facilitating
      superior normal vector rendering. 3d-HGSLi et al. (2024) disassembles the Gaussian into a half-
141
      Gaussian, enhancing its scene representation. Furthermore, certain worksLu et al. (2024); Ren et al.
142
      (2024); Yan et al. (2024b); Yang et al. (2024) endeavor to restrict the use of GS to achieve more
143   compact scene structures. Besides, some works have employed various deep learning modules (e.g.,
144   GS in the DarkYe et al. (2024b), Deblur-GSChen & Liu (2024)) to address the issue of noisy im-
145   ages. Others have utilized GS to model large-scale streetscapes (e.g., Street GSYan et al. (2024a),
146   AutosplatKhan et al. (2024)). Moreover, some works employ the modeling of physical properties in
147   3DGS through the representation of BRDF functions, as exemplified by Relightable GSGao et al.
148   (2023) and 3D Gaussian Splatting with Deferred ReflectionYe et al. (2024a). In this study, we also
149   use 3DGS to model a 3D car and integrate it with physical attributes to model the vehicle under
150   arbitrary lighting conditions and to enable light editing of the vehicle.

195   3.1    C ARVERSE DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
196
197   As a crucial component of autonomous driving simulation, the manual creation of vehicle models by
198   artists is an exceptionally time-consuming process. In this paper, we present a data-driven pipeline
199   for automatic vehicle generation, which necessitates a large-scale, high-quality vehicle dataset. Ex-
200   isting datasets are often limited by low quality and a restricted variety of vehicles. To overcome
201
      these challenges, we introduce Carverse, a high-quality synthetic dataset designed to support re-
      search in vehicle reconstruction and related tasks. Carverse is constructed using Blender’s physics
202
      engine Blender (2019) and the ray-tracing Blender Cycles rendering engine, enabling the generation
203
      of photorealistic images with diverse materials and global illumination settings.
204
205   To develop a comprehensive and realistic dataset, we sourced over 1,000 high-quality 3D vehicle
206   models and 3,000 distinct HDR maps from online repositories. The dataset includes a wide range of
207
      vehicle types, such as sedans, sports cars, vans, and trucks. The 3D vehicle models were divided into
      training and testing sets with 1,006 as the train set and 50 as the test set. To ensure dataset diversity,
208
      each model was subjected to random z-axis rotations and minor flipping along the XY plane prior to
209
      rendering. Additionally, HDR maps, vehicle colors, and materials were randomly varied to simulate
210   different illumination and surface properties. Each vehicle in the training set was rendered 5 times
211   with randomly assigned textures and lighting conditions, yielding a total of 5,030 samples across
212   the 1,006 vehicles. For each sample, we captured ground truth data from 100 distinct camera poses,
213   including monocular RGB renderings, camera pose information, normal maps, and material maps
214   such as albedo, roughness, and metallic. In the test set, 50 vehicle samples were rendered, with one
215   image used as input and 30 random views for benchmarking, along with a mesh file for geometry
      evaluation. Fig. 2 illustrates the dataset construction pipeline and showcases sample diversity.

      3.4    A PPLICATIONS FOR AUTONOMOUS D RIVING S IMULATION
369
      To demonstrate the practicality of RGM, we further present a 3D-GS-based pipeline for autonomous
370
      driving scene simulation that seamlessly integrates a vehicle into a dynamic road scene with pho-
371
      torealistic rendering. Given a set of posed images of the scene, we first employ a dynamic 3D-GS
372   Chen et al. (2023b) to reconstruct the road scenes. Subsequently, we adopt DiffusionLight Phongth-
373   awee et al. (2024), a diffusion-based lighting estimation module, to generate the environment map
374   from an image of the scene. Using an input image of the vehicle, RGM infers and generates the ve-
375   hicle’s Relightable GS, which is subsequently relighted using the environment map. The relighted
376   GS is then spatially aligned to place the vehicle on the road, with its trajectory defined. Finally, the
377   relighted GS is integrated into the road scene’s dynamic 3D-GS, enabling the realistic rendering of
      vehicles within various road environments.

476   5      C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In conclusion, in this paper we successfully tackle the challenges in 3D vehicle generation, such as
479
      limited datasets and lack of modeling of material properties for relighting. By introducing the Car-
480   verse dataset, containing over 1,000 high-precision 3D vehicle models, we support robust learning
481   and generalization. Additionally, our novel relightable 3D-GS generation framework named RGM
482   enables the efficient creation of detailed 3D car models by modeling global illumination and rep-
483   resenting 3D scenes through Gaussian primitives that capture geometry, appearance, and material
484   properties. This approach allows for realistic relighting under different illumination conditions, ad-
485   vancing 3D asset generation for AR/VR applications and industrial design by improving both speed
      and quality in automatic 3D car modeling from a single image.
