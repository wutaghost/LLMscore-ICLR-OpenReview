009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks operating on non-Euclidean geometries, such as Rieman-
012            nian manifolds, have recently demonstrated impressive performance across var-
013
               ious machine-learning applications. Motivated by the success of the attention
               mechanism, several works have extended it to different geometries. However,
014
               existing Riemannian attention methods are mostly designed in an ad hoc manner,
015
               i.e., tailored to a selected few geometries. Recent studies, on the other hand, show
016            that several matrix manifolds, such as Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD), Sym-
017            metric Positive Semi-Definite (SPSD), and Grassmannian manifolds, admit gyro
018            structures, offering a principled way to build Riemannian networks. Inspired by
019            this, we propose a Gyro Attention (GyroAtt) framework over general gyro spaces,
020            applicable to various matrix manifolds. Empirically, we manifest our framework
021            on three gyro structures in the SPD manifold, three in the SPSD manifold, and one
022            in the Grassmannian manifold. Extensive experiments on four electroencephalog-
023            raphy (EEG) datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
024
025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026   Recently, Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) over Riemannian manifolds, known as Riemannian neu-
027   ral networks, have garnered increasing attention in various applications (Huang & Van Gool, 2017;
028   Gulcehre et al., 2018; Brooks et al., 2019; Shimizu et al., 2021; Kobler et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023;
029   Wang et al., 2024b; Nguyen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024e). Commonly encoun-
030   tered manifolds include vector manifolds, such as hyperbolic (Ungar, 2005b) and spherical spaces
031   (Thurston, 1997), and matrix manifolds, such as Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) (Arsigny et al.,
032   2005), Symmetric Positive Semi-Definite (SPSD) (Bonnabel & Sepulchre, 2010; Bonnabel et al.,
033
      2013), and Grassmannian manifolds (Absil et al., 2004). Among these non-Euclidean spaces, hy-
      perbolic manifolds stand out due to the rich algebraic structure of gyrovector spaces (Ungar, 2002;
034
      2005b; 2014), which enables principled and convenient extensions of Euclidean deep learning to
035
      hyperbolic manifolds (Gulcehre et al., 2018; Shimizu et al., 2021; Bdeir et al., 2024). In contrast,
036   matrix manifolds provide a compelling balance between structural richness and computational fea-
037   sibility (Cruceru et al., 2021). Consequently, neural networks on matrix manifolds have emerged as
038   appealing alternatives to their hyperbolic counterparts in various applications (Kim, 2020; Nguyen,
039   2022b; Nguyen & Yang, 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Ju et al., 2024). Recently, Kim (2020); Nguyen
040   (2022a;b); Nguyen & Yang (2023) have demonstrated that several matrix manifolds, including SPD,
041   SPSD, and Grassmannian, admit gyrovector space structures, enabling the extension of several fun-
042   damental building blocks to matrix manifolds (Nguyen et al., 2024).
043   Inspired by the great success of the attention mechanism in DNNs (Vaswani et al., 2017; Hu et al.,
044   2018; Dosovitskiy, 2020), researchers have developed attention operations on different geometries.
045   Gulcehre et al. (2018) introduced an attention mechanism for hyperbolic spaces based on the hy-
046   perboloid and Klein models, while Pan et al. (2022) extended the attention mechanism to SPD
047   manifolds under Log-Euclidean Metric (LEM). Subsequently, Wang et al. (2024a) further adapted it
048   to Grassmannian manifolds using an extrinsic approach under the projective perspective. However,
049   these designs are tailored for specific manifolds and metrics, limiting their applicability.
050   As self-attention serves as the prototype of other attention variants, this paper focuses on self-
051   attention. Given that several matrix manifolds admit gyrovector structures, we propose a general
052   framework for attention over gyrovector spaces, called GyroAtt. Unlike previous Riemannian at-
053   tention approaches, which are tailored to specific geometries (Gulcehre et al., 2018; Pan et al.,
      2022; Wang et al., 2024a), GyroAtt can be applied across different matrix geometries. Additionally,

      GyroAtt naturally generalizes several basic attention blocks to manifolds, including linear transfor-

      2.1 G YROGROUPS AND G YROVECTOR SPACES
085   Gyrogroups and gyrovector spaces generalize groups and vector spaces, offering a powerful frame-
086   work to analyze non-Euclidean geometries. Below, we formally present their definitions.
087   Definition 2.1 (Gyrogroups (Ungar, 2014)). A gyrogroup is a generalization of groups. Let G be
088   a nonempty set with a binary operation ⊕ and an identity element E ∈ G. Then, a pair (G, ⊕) is a
089   gyrogroup if it satisfies the following axioms:
090   (G1) There exists an identity element E ∈ G such that for all A ∈ G, E ⊕ A = A.
091
092   (G2) For each A ∈ G, there exists a left inverse ⊖A ∈ G satisfying ⊖A ⊕ A = E.
093   (G3) For all A, B, C ∈ G, there exists an automorphism gyr[A, B](·) : G → G, satisfying
094
095                              A ⊕ (B ⊕ C) = (A ⊕ B) ⊕ gyr[A, B](C).                                  (1)
096   Here, the map gyr[A, B](·) is called the gyroautomorphism, or the gyration of G generated by A, B.
097
098   (G4) For all A, B ∈ G, The map gyr[A, B] generated by each A, B satisfies the left loop property:
099
      gyr[A, B] = gyr[A ⊕ B, B].
100   Definition 2.2 (Gyrocommutative Gyrogroups Ungar (2014)). A gyrogroup (G, ⊕) is gyrocom-
101   mutative if it satisfies the gyrocommutative law: A ⊕ B = gyr[A, B](B ⊕ A) for all A, B ∈ G.
102   The following definition of gyrovector spaces is derived from Nguyen (2022b, Def. 2.3), which is
103   slightly different from in Ungar (2014, Def. 3.2).
104
      Definition 2.3 (Gyrovector Spaces (Nguyen, 2022b)). A gyrocommutative gyrogroup (G, ⊕)
105
      equipped with a scalar multiplication ⊗ : R × G → G is a gyrovector space if the following axioms
106   are satisfied:
107
      (V1) 1 ⊗ A = A, 0 ⊗ A = t ⊗ E = E, and (−1) ⊗ A = ⊖A.

223

224            Rn → Rm , which preserves the vector structure as a homomorphism over vector spaces:
225

227

229        2). Attention calculation. This computes the correlation- or similarity-based attention be-
230            tween Qi and Kj for each pair {vi , vj }.
231

531   7    C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we propose the GyroAtt framework, which extends the Euclidean attention mechanism
533
      to general gyrovector spaces in a principled manner. Specifically, we adopt gyro homomorphisms,
534   geodesic-based attention, and WFM as counterparts to the transformation, attention, and aggregation
535   operations in Euclidean attention. Notably, we identify the concrete non-trivial expressions of gyro
536   homomorphisms on different matrix gyro spaces. The principled construction of GyroAtt enables
537   a direct assessment of the impact of geometry on a given task while keeping the neural network
538   architecture constant. Extensive experiments on four EEG datasets demonstrate the efficacy and
539   flexibility of our approach. For future avenues, we will implement our GyroAtt framework on other
      concrete gyro spaces.

      conditions (Zhang et al., 2021). However, EEG signals exhibit a low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and
818   low specificity, complicating meaningful information extraction (Johnson, 2006; Hine et al., 2017).

834   seconds. Each subject completed five sessions, with five trials per stimulation frequency in each

837   BCI-ERN. This dataset involves 26 subjects in a P300-based spelling task to measure ERN. EEG
838

839

840
841   BNCI2014001. This dataset comprises EEG recordings from 9 subjects performing four motor
842   imagery tasks: imagining movements of the left hand, right hand, both feet, and tongue. Each
843   subject participated in two sessions on different days, each containing six runs. Each run included
844   48 trials—12 per class—totaling 288 trials per session.
845   BNCI2015001. EEG signals were recorded from electrodes centered around positions C3, Cz, and
846   C4, according to the International 10-20 System. Data were collected using a g.GAMMAsys active

848   and 100 Hz and a notch filter at 50 Hz.

      B.2 EEG P REPROCESSING
850
851   For the BNCI2014001 and BNCI2015001 datasets, we followed the preprocessing steps described
852   by Kobler et al. (2022). Using the Python packages moabb and mne, we resampled the EEG signals
853
      to 250/256 Hz, applied temporal filters to extract oscillatory activity in the 4–36 Hz range, and
      extracted short segments ( ≤ 3 seconds) associated with class labels.
854
855   For the MAMEM-SSVEP-II dataset, we adhered to the preprocessing protocol of Pan et al. (2022).
856   The steps included: (1) band-pass filtering between 1–50 Hz; (2) selecting eight channels (PO7,
857   PO3, PO, PO4, PO8, O1, Oz, and O2) located in the occipital area corresponding to the visual
858   cortex; and (3) segmenting each trial into four 1-second segments from 1s to 5s after cue onset.
859
      This resulted in 500 trials of 1-second, 8-channel SSVEP signals per subject, with each input EEG
      segment comprising 125 time points.
860
861   For the BCI-ERN dataset, we followed the preprocessing procedure outlined by Pan et al. (2022).
862   The steps involved: (1) downsampling the signals from 600 Hz to 128 Hz; (2) applying a band-
863   pass filter between 1–40 Hz. After preprocessing, each trial consisted of 56 channels with 160 time
      points.

      Table A3 provides a summary of the specific network architectures of GyroAtt-SPSD across the four
      datasets. The network structures for GyroAtt-Gr (Tab. A5) and GyroAtt-SPD (Tab. A4) are identical
892
      to that of GyroAtt-SPSD. We just introduce GyroAtt-SPSD as an example.
893
894   For the MAMEM-SSVEP-II and BCI-ERN datasets, the initial convolutional block consists of a
895   convolutional layer, followed by batch normalization and an ELU activation function. The subse-
896   quent convolutional block performs depthwise spatial convolution. A pointwise convolution, batch
897
      normalization, and another ELU activation follow this. In the MAMEM-SSVEP-II dataset, features
      are split into two non-overlapping segments, followed by covariance pooling. For the BCI-ERN
898
      dataset, the second convolutional block is repeated in two additional blocks. The outputs from these
899
      blocks are concatenated along the channel dimension. The data is then split along the channel di-
900   mension, and covariance pooling is applied, resulting in three covariance matrices.
901
902   For BNCI2014001 and BNCI2015001 datasets, the initial convolutional layer employs 4 or 5 filters
903   with a kernel size of (1, 25), performing temporal convolution while maintaining the same size
904
      through padding. The second convolutional layer applies spatial convolution with a kernel size of
      (22, 1) to integrate information from different channels. The output sequences undergo temporal
905
