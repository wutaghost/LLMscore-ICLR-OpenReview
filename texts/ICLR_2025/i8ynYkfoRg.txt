009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning (FL) is widely adopted as a secure and reliable distributed ma-
012            chine learning system for it allows participants to retain their training data locally,
013            transmitting only model updates, such as gradients or parameters. However, the
014            transmission process to the server can still lead to privacy leakage, as the updated
015            information may be exploited to launch various privacy attacks. In this work, we
016
               present a key observation that the middle layer outputs, referred to as data repre-
               sentations, can exhibit independence in value distribution across different types of
017
               data. This enables us to capture the intrinsic relationship between data represen-
018
               tations and private data, and inspires us to propose a Model Entanglement(ME)
019            strategy aimed at enhancing privacy preserving by obfuscating the data represen-
020            tations of private models in a fine-grained manner, while improving the balance
021            between privacy preservation and model accuracy. We compare our approach
022            to the baseline FedAvg and two state-of-the-art defense methods. Our method
023            demonstrates strong defense capabilities against mainstream privacy attacks, only
024            reducing the global model accuracy by less than 0.7% and training efficiency of
025            6.8% respectively on the widely used dataset, excelling in both accuracy and pri-
026            vacy preserving.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep learning, particularly through the use of deep neural networks, has seen widespread adoption
031   due to its exceptional performance, which is also heavily dependent on large volumes of high-quality
032   training data. Currently, the widely adopted distributed learning algorithm known as Federated
033   Learning (FL)McMahan et al. (2017) allows a central server to handle broadcasting and computation
034   for the participating client nodes. This iterative process facilitates collaborative model refinement
035   while preserving individual data privacy.
036   Although Federated Learning effectively mitigates the direct transmission of training data to enhance
037   privacy protection, it does not provide security guarantees for clients’ private local contributions. In
038   related work, Zhu et al.Zhu & Han (2020); Geiping et al. (2020) demonstrated that even in scenarios
039   where gradients are shared, adversaries could reconstruct training data. The scenario of sharing
040   model parameters introduces additional privacy risks. Carlini et al. (2022); Li & Zhang (2021)
041   utilized model outputs to conduct membership inference attacks, focusing solely on the structure of
042   black-box models. These attacks aim to determine whether private data corresponds to the model’s
043   training data, effectively identifying membership.
044   Essentially, whether through reconstruction attacks or inference attacks, the core privacy threat stems
045   from extracting the intrinsic relationship between the model and the underlying data. The evolving
046   landscape of privacy attacks highlights the crucial need for developing robust strategies to protect
047   sensitive information when sharing model parameters in distributed learning scenarios.
048   Currently, mainstream defense approaches against these attacks include Differential Privacy (DP),
049   Secure Multi-party Computation (SMC), and Data Compression (DC). DP enhances privacy by
050   introducing perturbations to shared data, though it often compromises model accuracy. SMC en-
051   crypts user data, allowing servers to aggregate encrypted information while preventing malicious
052   eavesdropping, but it faces challenges related to key distribution and high computational demands.
053   Encryption-based methods also require extensive matrix operations, leading to increased computa-
      tional load and potential communication delays. While less common, DC similarly struggles with

054
      balancing privacy defense and accuracy. Despite their strengths and limitations, ongoing research
055   is essential to further optimize these methods for privacy in distributed learning. Therefore, it is
056   imperative to propose a more comprehensive solution that fully leverages the collaborative nature of
057   client training to achieve robust client-level privacy preservation.
058
      Our preliminary experiments yielded a key observation: when training data distributions differ,
059
      the model parameters and updates reflect these distributional variations, particularly in non-
060
      independent and identically distributed (Non-IID) datasets. This phenomenon may be linked to
061   the model’s capacity to memorize data, which is a key factor in privacy attacks. Based on the above
062   observations, this paper first investigates the intrinsic relationship between intermediate outputs of
063   the model and the model updates(i.e, gradient information) with the privacy. Accordingly, a pri-
064   vacy measurement mechanism based on node importance is proposed. The results of privacy risk
065   assessment are used as guidance to design a parameter replacement algorithm, which is then applied
066   within a federated learning framework. This leads to the development of a federated multi-client
067   collaborative privacy preserving framework with a safeguard client.

      2.1   P RIVACY ATTACK
072
073   Membership Inference Attack(MIA): MIA enables an attacker to determine whether a sample
074   (x, y) belongs to the training dataset of a target machine learning model by constructing a binary
075   classifier which output 1 or 0. Ye et al. (2022) analyzed various attacks and attributed the vulnera-
076   bility of data points to different levels of memorization, or overfitting to conditional memorization.
077   Recent research has explored MIA’s significance in real-world scenarios. For instance, Chen et al.
078   (2022) proposed a practical MIA against the industrial Internet of Things, relaxing key assumptions
079   made by prior MIAs that were impractical in industrial settings. Zarifzadeh et al. (2024) introduced
080   RMIA which is a refined MIA with lower overhead and uses fine-grained modeling of null hypothe-
081
      ses in likelihood ratio tests and achieves greater robustness and accuracy than existing methods.
082   Model Inversion Attack: Under the model inversion attack, the attacker attempts to restore the
083   training data of the model with limited knowledge. Recently works, such as Zhu & Han (2020);
084   Geiping et al. (2020); Zhao et al. (2020) had continuously improved this attack, making it more effi-
085   cient. The research in Wang et al. (2022) analyzed how weight distribution affects the training data
086   recovery from gradient and proposed the algorithm exploited the variance of gradients. Additionally,
087
      Nguyen et al. (2023) addressed the suboptimal loss functions and poor quality of reconstructed sam-
      ples by introducing regularization terms to improve the loss function’s convexity, thereby enhancing
088
      the accuracy of recovered samples.
089
090
091   2.2   P RIVACY P RESERVING T ECHNOLOGY
092
093
      Secure Multi-party Computation: MPC aims to compute private inputs from all parties through
      a secure function and return the result. Bonawitz et al. (2017) proposed HybridAlpha, a multi-
094
      party training method based on functional encryption, which introduced a trusted third party for
095
      verification. Zhang et al. (2020) developed Batchcrypt, a homomorphic encryption-based secure ag-
096   gregation scheme for cross-organizational settings, which reduces communication overhead, though
097   its performance significantly degrades with larger models compared to plaintext schemes. However,
098   MPC methods incur substantial computational overhead and conceal model updates from the server,
099   making them vulnerable to Byzantine and Poisoning attacks Wu et al. (2020); Chang et al. (2020).
100   Chen et al. (2023) addressed issues related to detecting malicious parameters and the strong reliance
101   on IID distributions in federated learning and MPC.
102   Differential Privacy: DP algorithms can resist corresponding privacy attacks by adding noise to the
103   federated learning framework. The work Wei et al. (2020) introduced noise to local updates before
104   the aggregation to defend against privacy attacks, optimizing the selection of the best K clients to
105   balance privacy and efficiency.Zhu et al. (2022) proposed a fine-grained method to allocate noise
106   according to the importance value of layers in order to remain high model performance. In address
107   precision degradation, Wang et al. (2023) introduced the idea of dynamic defense in DP federated
      learning. However, existing DP strategies require clients to conform to a specific statistical distri-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      bution collectively, so that their mutual noisy effects are neutralized after aggregation. In scenarios
109   with fewer clients, the added noise significantly impacts individual client accuracy.
110
111   Data Compression: DC or pruning model updates (Tsuzuku et al. (2018)) is a practical approach
112
      to alleviate the connection between updates and private data. Zhu & Han (2020) achieved the effect
      of resisting DLG attacks by gradient information compresion and sparsification. However, this
113
      method requires manual setting of the compression ratio, and requires a higher compression rate for
114
      a better mitigation effect. To better address system heterogeneity and adapt to dynamic edge-client
115   changes within the federated learning framework, some methods propose adaptive control of local
116   updates for model compression. Miao et al. (2022) used compressive sensing and noise processing
117   with a privacy budget, reducing the computational cost of differential privacy for large models.
118   Similarly, Xu et al. (2023) optimized local updates based on client bandwidth and computational
119   resources. However, these methods focus more on system convergence rather than demonstrating
120   data compression’s effectiveness in privacy preservation.

362
363   In this section, we conduct simulation experiments to verify the superiority of our proposed frame-
364   work in terms of training accuracy and its effectiveness against privacy attacks.
365
      Datasets. We select two widely used datasets, MNIST and CIFAR-10, to evaluate the effectiveness
366   of our defense approach in real-world scenarios. The MNIST dataset is a benchmark for machine
367   learning tasks, containing 70,000 gray scale images of size 28 × 28. The CIFAR-10 dataset is
368   a widely recognized benchmark for image recognition tasks. It consists of 32 × 32 RGB images
369   across 10 classes, including animals and vehicles. We adopt the non-IID partitioning of the dataset,
370   consistent with our hypothesis that data held by different clients exhibit varying label distributions.
371   The dataset is divided into 10 disjoint parts, each allocated to one of the 10 clients in the FL scenario.
372   One partition is designated as the auxiliary dataset and used to train the guard client, ensuring that
373   its data distribution remains uncorrelated with the other private clients.
374   Hyperparameters configurations. We employ the LeNet-5 model architecture following Zhu &
375   Han (2020). For the MNIST dataset, the learning rate is set to 0.01, with 10 local epochs, 50 global
376   epochs, and a batch size of 256. For CIFAR-10, the learning rate is 0.001, with 20 local epochs,
377   50 global epochs, and a batch size of 64. The model is optimized using the SGD optimizer and
      Cross-Entropy loss function.

522   In this work, we present the observation that the data representations of a model’s intermediate
523   outputs are independent of one another. Building on this insight, we propose a replacement algo-
524   rithm that leverages data representations with varying distributions for entanglement, applying it to
525   federated learning scenarios. This approach ensures model accuracy while enhancing resistance to
526   privacy attacks. Specifically, we introduce the guard client whose updated information is used to
527   replace and fuse as the base with private client data before uploading to the server, thereby optimiz-
528   ing collaboration among clients within the federated learning framework. Furthermore, we provide
529   a theoretical analysis and discussion supporting the use of data representations for replacement. Fi-
530
      nally, we conduct extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of our method in both global
      model accuracy and resisting attacks. The results demonstrate that our approach offers significant
531
      improvements in terms of accuracy, privacy preserving, and efficiency compared to other widely-

      used privacy-preserving techniques.
