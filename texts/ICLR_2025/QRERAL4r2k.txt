011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We address the problem of spatially guided text-to-motion synthesis. While there
014            has been work to incorporate spatial constraints in text-to-motion diffusion mod-
015            els, existing methods still face significant challenges in generating motions that
016            align with the conditional controls. To this end, we propose Cycle Consistent Dif-
017
               fusion, a novel approach that improves controllable generation by explicitly opti-
               mizing frame-level cycle consistency between generated motions and conditional
018
               controls. Specifically, for an input conditional control, we ensure that the output
019
               motion and the input spatial constraint are forced to be consistent. A straightfor-
020            ward implementation though consistent with the input often does not match fine-
021            grained control signals. To this end, we introduce a novel test-time optimization
022            framework that directs our pre-trained cycle consistent diffusion model towards
023            user-defined sparse constraints. We demonstrate approximately 5 to 10 percent
024            improvement in controllability of motion synthesis on the HumanML3D dataset,
025            while significantly reducing foot skating artifacts.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Controlled Human Motion synthesis is essential for several applications ranging from gaming to
031   robotics. The problem is challenging due to the immense space of possible human motions and the
032   cost of capturing high-quality data. Recently, the emergence and improvements of diffusion models
033
      (Tevet et al., 2023b), along with the introduction of large-scale motion datasets such as AMASS
      (Mahmood et al., 2019) and the concomitant text-labeled motion datasets (Guo et al., 2022b) have
034
      lead to significant strides in text-to-motion generation. However, several commands cannot be en-
035
      tirely provided using text descriptions, and thus the provision of only text as the control signal is
036   insufficient for several applications such as fine-grained human interaction synthesis. Often, an an-
037   imator wants to provide a sparse spatial control signal along with a text input (Starke et al., 2019;
038   Clavet, 2016). For example, an animator may wish for the precise end-effector of a character to
039   terminate a specific location or for the character to sit at a specific location in space. In this work,
040   we focus on the problem of incorporating spatial control signals over any joint at any given time into
041   text-conditioned human motion generation, as shown in Fig. 1.
042   This problem poses significantly more challenges. While text provides an abstract signal that may be
043   satisfied by multiple generated sequences, spatial signals provide more difficult constraints. For the
044   objective to be adequately satisfied, the synthesized motion must match the precise spatial constraint
045   provided by the animator, whereas such fine-grained alignment requirements are absent for text-
046   guided synthesis. While there have been studies on incorporating spatial constraints (Xie et al.,
047   2024; Karunratanakul et al., 2023a; Shafir et al., 2024) in diffusion-based motion synthesis methods,
048   they either rely on approximate guidance to guide diffusion models towards motions that satisfy
049   constraints or they require inpainting at every denoising step which in turn requires a very dense
050
      control signal. As such, their performance for sparse spatial constraints remains unsatisfactory.
051   To this end, we propose a novel solution that casts the problem of motion synthesis as a simultaneous
052   sampling and optimization problem. We design a novel objective that directs spatially constrained
053   pre-trained diffusion motion models toward satisfying user-defined sparse joint constraints. Our
      solution draws inspiration from ideas of test-time alignment introduced in research related to the

      When used with existing motion diffusion architectures, such a test-time optimization often leads
084   to degenerate solutions. To address this problem, we design a novel, Cycle Consistent, Spatially
085   Constrained Diffusion Model that generates motions in accord with animator-provided spatial con-
086   straints. The idea is that if we translate motion from the control domain to the synthesized domain
087   and back, we should arrive where we started. We leverage this insight to explicitly design a loss that
088   encourages such consistency during the synthesis process.
089
      A proper solution design adopting this idea is critical as a naive implementation typically ignores the
090   text prompt while fully satisfying the spatial constraint, i.e., the diffusion model satisfies the reward
091   - in our case, the spatial constraint - but ignores the original prompt. This is a common observation
092   in diffusion sampling, called ’reward hacking’ Tang et al. (2024). To address this, we introduce a
093   novel loss function in the context of human motion that penalizes motions in the low support region
094   of the original Gaussian noise and thus prevents reward-hacking.
095
      Our full framework leads to 5 − 10 percentage point improvements in terms of foot-skate ratio
096   and control error over existing state-of-the-art spatially controlled motion synthesis methods on the
097   HumanML3D dataset. We further demonstrate that when coupled with path planning, our idea can
098   be used to generate long-term human motion in diverse 3D scenes. By using some user-provided
099   spatial locations in a 3d scene as key points to direct motion, we synthesize diverse motions such as
100   walking with raised hands and twirling chained together in 3D scenes. To the best of our knowledge,
101   our paper is the first to demonstrate the use of diffusion models for the synthesis of chained, diverse
102   motion with fine-grained control in 3D scenes.
103   To summarize, our contributions are 1) We propose a novel algorithm HuMouS for controlled motion
104   synthesis that leads to state-of-the-art results in spatially constrained text-to-motion synthesis. 2) We
105   introduce the idea of a cycle-consistent spatially constrained diffusion model for controlled motion
106   synthesis. 3) We demonstrate that when coupled with path-planning and incorporating some sparse
107   user-provided constraints, our framework allows for synthesizing chained diverse motions in large
      3D scenes.

111   Diffusion Models Diffusion-based probabilistic generative models (DPMs) are a class of generative
112
      models learned by progressive denoising of the input data, (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al.,
      2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021b). Diffusion models have been successfully shown
113
      to produce state-of-the-art results in a range of diverse tasks: such as image generation (Ramesh
114
      et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022), image-conditioned editing (Meng et al.,
115   2022; Choi et al., 2021; Brooks et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2022; Balaji et al., 2022), super-resolution
116   (Saharia et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022), 3D shape generation (Poole et al., 2022; Watson et al., 2022),
117   speech synthesis (Kong et al., 2021; Popov et al., 2021), video generation (Ho et al., 2022b;a),
118   controlled image synthesis (Zhang et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2023) depth estimation (Saxena et al.,
119   2023) and reinforcement learning (Janner et al., 2022). Our method is inspired by Controlnet++
120   (Li et al., 2024) which produces SoTA results for text-to-image synthesis by introducing the idea of
121   cycle consistency. In contrast, our method focuses on human motion synthesis.
122   Controlling Diffusion Models Several methods have been proposed to introduce conditioning fac-
123   tors into the denoising process of diffusion models such as inpainting, (Chung et al., 2022; Choi
124   et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022), classifier-based guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Chung et al.,
125   2022), and classifier-free guidance (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022;
126   Ho & Salimans, 2022). It has also been shown possible to embed images into the latent codes of the
127   diffusion model by hacking the denoising process (Meng et al., 2022), optimizing for latent codes
128   (Wallace et al., 2023) (Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024). More recently, performing a sampling-

239   3.2   S PATIALLY C ONSTRAINED D IFFUSION M ODEL
240
241   Our goal is to train a spatially constrained diffusion model which synthesizes motion in accordance
242   with a user-provided spatial constraint cs and text-prompt ct . While the user is free to provide
243   spatial constraints corresponding to any frame in the motion sequence or to any joint in any of the
244   frames we ensure that these constraints are represented in a standard format cs ∈ RN ×D to ensure
245   alignment with the motion representation 3.1.
246   In order to modify the diffusion approximation function for it it incorporates spatial constraints cs
247   as well D(xt , t, ct , cs ; θ), we use a spatial module P which learns to parse the 3D sparse locations
248   provided by the user. Specifically, it is a trainable copy of the Transformer encoder in the motion
249   diffusion model that learns to enforce the spatial constraints. In addition to the spatial constraint cs ,
250   this module also takes the text constraint ct as input.
251   The main transformer, instead of only using self-attention during the forward pass, unlike the orig-
252   inal MDM formulation, also incorporates a cross-attention layer. After every self-attention layer
253   that processes the noisy motion xt , we use a cross-attention block with the output of the spatial
254   block P (cs , ct ). To effectively handle the sparse control signals in time, we mask out the features
255   at frames where there are no valid control signals,
256   Inspired by (Zhang et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024), the spatial module is initialized with zeros, so that
257   at the beginning, it has numerically insignificant output. As the training goes on, the spatial module
258   learns the spatial constraints and adds the learned feature corrections to the corresponding layers in
259   the motion diffusion model to amend the generated motions implicitly.

      the last Q frames and is left blank for the middle J frames. Using this information, we perform a
      refinement step that minimizes
410

412                           Lreward = ||mtar ⊙ T (G(cjoin , z, T )) − ctar ||2 )                    (10)
413
414
      The mask mtar is defined such it is blank for the middle J frames and full for the known Q frames.
415
      In essence we aim to synthesize a motion sequence where the first Q and last Q frames match the
416   motion synthesized in the previous step but the diffusion prior is asked to inpaint the Q frames in
417   the middle.
418
419
      The steps outlined above are repeated for all the P − 1 transitions to finally synthesize a long
      chained motion sequence that respects the spatial, textual constraints defined by the user along with
420
      the constraints of the 3D scene. Please note that we do not claim to generate SoTA human motion in
421
      3D scenes but are trying to show that diffusion allows for the synthesis of diverse chained motions
422   in large 3d Scenes which to the best of our knowledge has not been shown before.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We have presented a novel method for spatially constrained text-to-motion synthesis. We introduce
534   the idea of cycle consistency in the context of human motion and show that it leads to improved per-
535   formance. We also introduce the idea of latent space manipulation with a novel test-time optimiza-
536   tion algorithm that directs pre-trained spatially constrained diffusion models toward user-defined
537   preferences. We have further demonstrated that when coupled with path planning and some user-
538   provided sparse key points, our framework can synthesize long-term human motion in 3D scenes.
539   We hope our work will inspire further research in the field of text-to-motion synthesis and contribute
      to advancements in computer animation.
