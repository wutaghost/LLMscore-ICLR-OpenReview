009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent findings by Cohen et al. (2021) demonstrate that during the training of neural
012            networks with full batch gradient descent at a step size of η, the sharpness—defined
013            as the largest eigenvalue of the full batch Hessian—consistently stabilizes at 2/η.
014            These results have significant implications for generalization and convergence.
015            Unfortunately, this was observed not to be the case of mini batch stochastic gradient
016            descent (SGD), thus limiting the broader applicability of these findings. We show
017            that SGD trains in a different regime we call Edge of Stochastic Stability. In this
018
               regime, what hovers at 2/η is, instead, the average over the batches of the largest
               eigenvalue of the Hessian of the mini batch loss—which is always bigger than
019
               the sharpness. This implies that the sharpness is generally lower when training
020
               with smaller batches or bigger learning rate, providing a basis for the observed
021            implicit regularization effect of SGD towards flatter minima and a number of well
022            established empirical phenomena.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Training algorithms are a key ingredient to the success of deep learning. Stochastic gradient descent
028   (SGD) (Robbins & Monro, 1951), a stochastic variant of gradient descent (GD), has been effective
029   in finding parameters that yield good test performance despite the complicated nonlinear nature of
030   neural networks.
031   Full batch GD and its adaptive versions have been shown to optimize in regime of instability Xing
032   et al. (2018); Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2020); Cohen et al. (2021; 2022). Precisely, if the step size is η > 0,
033   the highest eigenvalue of the full batch Hessian, also called full batch sharpness–which we will denote
034   as FullBS–grows until 2/η in a first phase of training called progressive sharpening and hovers around
035   that value, subject to small oscillations. This regime is called Edge of Stability. This instability does
036   not damage convergence when training neural networks. This is surprising as that would be the case
037
      of quadratics where if the curvature is higher than 2/η the optimization diverges.
038   This regime, called Edge of Stability by Cohen et al. (2021), implies that for a fixed learning rate, full
039   batch sharpness is inherently limited by 2/η, and constitutes a mode of instability.
040
041   The work on EoS is about full batch methods. The picture for full batch algorithms seems
042   (empirically) clear, both for GD Cohen et al. (2021); Damian et al. (2023) and its adaptive and
043   accelerated version Cohen et al. (2022). Unfortunately, neural networks are typically trained with the
044   mini batch versions of these gradient based methods, and how Cohen et al. (2021) noticed and stated
045
      in the limitations of their work, what they observed is not the case of mini batch training. Precisely,
      to quote Cohen et al. (2021):
046
047
048       [. . .] while the sharpness does not flatline at any value during SGD (as it does during gradient
049       descent), the trajectory of the sharpness is heavily influenced by the step size and batch size
050       (Jastrzebski et al., Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2019; 2020)), which cannot be explained by existing
051       optimization theory. Indeed, there are indications that the “Edge of Stability” intuition might
052       generalize somehow to SGD, just in a way that does not center around the (full batch) sharpness.

136   SGD finding flatter minima. Our result is in nature a result about improved flatness by mini batch
137
      training. Indeed, the it explains why we can expect a smallest size for the eigenvalues of the full
      batch Hessian when training with a small batch. There has been a long line of work in this direction
138
      since Keskar et al. (2016) showed that SGD with smaller batch size finds minima with a smaller
139
      Hessian. A more recent paper, Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2021), shows empirically that big learning rate SGD
140   has an effect similar to penalizing the trace of the Fisher matrix, in image classification tasks. In
141   similar settings, the Fisher matrix has been shown to approximate the Hessian during the training;
142   in particular, there is an overlap between the top eigenspaces of the Hessian and its eigenspaces
143   Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2018); Martens (2020); Thomas et al. (2020). Furthermore, Jastrz˛ebski et al. (2021)
144   shows that, in practice, penalizing it consistently improves generalization, reduces memorization,
145   and regularizes the trace of the final Hessian. Moreover, the advantages of penalizing the trace of
146   the Fisher matrix are even stronger when in the presence of noisy labels. There exist multiple other
147   studies along these lines, and in particular corroborate our findings.

      that direction (?). Training algorithms aiming to find a flat minimum were developed and shown to

      perform well on a variety of tasks (??).

      4     T HE 2 P HASES OF P ROGRESSIVE S HARPENING
259
260   Progressive sharpening (PS) was defined by Cohen et al. (2021) as "When training neural networks,
261
      it seems to be a general rule that so long as the sharpness is small enough for gradient descent to
      be stable (< 2/λ, for vanilla gradient descent), gradient descent has an overwhelming tendency to
262
      continually increase the sharpness." This concept, first posited as a general rule by Jastrz˛ebski et al.
263
      (2020) in their Assumption 4, builds upon empirical findings from earlier studies Jastrz˛ebski et al.
264   (2018; 2019). We analyze and document here the phases of progressive sharpening. Not only we
265   overwhelmingly empirically confirm the presence of this phase, but we provide a clear framework
266   to understand this phase: we reveal that the PS phase consists of two smaller subphases. This
267   particularly highlights the differences between full batch and mini batch stochastic gradient descent
268   (SGD): progressive sharpening is not about the FullBS but it is inherently about the MiniBS. As
269   expected, the description of full batch PS of Cohen et al. (2021) turns out to be a particular edge-case
      of our characterization.

299
300   Contrary to full batch case, where the entire progressive sharpening happens in the first phase, mini

303
      case of full batch GD we would expect this phase transition to happen as the MiniBS approaches
      the EoSS. The trajectories, however, deviate from the full batch GD and gradient flow dynamics
304
      much earlier than when EoSS plateaus. In this intermediate phase between the initial progressive
305
      sharpening and the EoSS plateau, the rate of sharpness increase varies with batch size. The dynamics
306   in this phase is inherently about the progressive sharpening of the MiniBS, as we can see in Figure

311   responds to lr changes according to the theories established for FullBS by the aforementioned works.
312   The observed slower PS rate for smaller batch sizes corroborates mathematical theories from the
313   implicit regularization literature that applies to big learning rate (Damian et al. (2021); Smith et al.
314   (2021); Beneventano (2023)). After the completion of the first PS phase, the loss has typically reduced
315   substantially and the manifold of minima is nearly reached (see also section 4.2), the implicit bias of
316   mini batch SGD—which generally opposes the PS tendency seen in gradient flow—becomes more
317   pronounced, particularly at smaller batch sizes.

      7     I MPLICATION : N OISE INJECTED FULL BATCH GD ̸= MINI BATCH SGD
461
462   7.1   T HE M INI BATCH L ANDSCAPE M ATTERS
463
464   Theoretical analyses of neural network optimization dynamics traditionally adopt two perspectives:
465   (i) Online Case: Each sample serves as an independent, noised, unbiased estimator of the gradient
466   from the theoretical loss. Here, stochastic gradient descent (SGD) is treated as performing noisy
467   gradient descent on the actual expected risk derived from the theoretical distribution of the sample.
468   (ii) Offline Case: A fixed finite dataset defines an empirical deterministic full batch landscape, with
469
      SGD executing noisy gradient descent upon this landscape.
470   Both scenarios presume the existence of a "real landscape" through which SGD navigates as a noisy,
471   first-order method. If this was the case of the training dynamics of neural networks, then the quantity
472   that would matter would be the FullBS, not influenced by the noise, while the gradients would show
473   high levels of noise. However, an essential insight from our studies is that the specific landscape
474   traversed at each step—the "mini batch landscape"—is crucial, rather than the generalized landscape
475
      (either full batch or theoretical). This distinction becomes evident when considering the variability of
      mini batch Hessians, their higher dimensional kernel, and their average sharpness set at 2/η. If the
476
      sharpness chosen per batch is excessively high, it directs SGD trajectories sharply, highlighting that
477
      the average step taken across the mini batch landscape is more critical than the step on the average
478   landscape.
479
480
      7.2   C HALLENGING SDE S AS A M ODEL FOR SGD
481
482   Our findings reveal that the dynamics of mini batch SGD with small batch sizes exhibit significant
483   qualitative differences from those modeled by stochastic optimization trajectories on both full batch
484   and theoretical landscapes. For instance, when employing continuous SDEs on these landscapes,
485   the observed full batch Hessian showcases substantial discrepancies in: (i) The configuration of
      eigenvectors, as well as the magnitude and quantity of positive eigenvalues compared to those derived


                                                         9
486
      from mini batch processes. (ii) The orientation of these eigenvectors relative to the descent direction,
487   which differs markedly from that observed in mini batch scenarios. This variation underscores a
488   critical limitation of continuous SDE models in accurately predicting or replicating the nuanced
489   behaviors of mini batch SGD, particularly when the batch size is small. The inherent differences
490   highlighted by these observations strongly suggest that continuous time models may struggle to
491   provide reliable insights into the pathways and outcomes of mini batch SGD unless implemented
492   with considerably larger batch sizes.
493   The existing literature has already established that SDEs may not adequately model the dynamics
494   of SGD. Notably, Yaida (2018) identified that the SDE approximation limits used are often ill-
495   posed, indicating fundamental issues in their application to SGD dynamics. Later, Li et al. (2021)
496   demonstrated that SDEs do not mimic SGD behaviors except under very specific conditions, such
497   as extremely small learning rates in scale-invariant neural networks where variance dominates the
498   gradient influence. On top of all the above, HaoChen et al. (2020) showed that various forms of
499   noise—like those from Langevin dynamics or label noise—lead to convergence at different minima,
500   suggesting diverse dynamics that SDE models may struggle to capture. Finally, Damian et al. (2021);
501
      Li et al. (2022); Shalova et al. (2024) have shown that the implicit regularization effects often
      attributed to diffusion are actually more likely to result from drifts caused by higher-order terms in
502
      Taylor expansions. In light of these findings, some recent theoretical efforts have begun to address the
503
      discrete nature and account for the different batch-specific Hessians, .e.g., Roberts (2021); Smith et al.
504   (2021); Beneventano (2023), contrasting with much of the earlier literature that does not adequately
505   capture these aspects.
506
507
      However, the problems of continuous analysis as highlighted in the literature so fare were probabilistic
      in nature. While advancements in more sophisticated probabilistic tools and distributions, as sug-
508
      gested, e.g., by Ziyin et al. (2023), may offer potential solutions to them, the challenge of accurately
509
      incorporating the variability of batch-specific Hessians into the analyzes remains significant. This
510   complexity underscores a critical gap in our understanding and modeling of SGD dynamics, raising
511   questions about the feasibility of accurately predicting SGD behavior using existing analyzes.

      8    C ONCLUSIONS
515
516
      This study advances our understanding of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) dynamics in training
      neural networks, particularly underlining the distinct behavior of mini batch SGD compared to full
517
      batch gradient descent. We introduce the concept of the Edge of Stochastic Stability (EoS), where
518
      the average largest eigenvalue of the mini batch Hessians—mini batch sharpness—stabilizes around
519   2/η unlike the full batch sharpness observed in previous studies. SGD’s mini batch sharpness is
520   consistently higher than full batch sharpness, underscoring its role in the implicit regularization that
521   leads to flatter minima. Moreover, we properly characterize the behavior of SGD in the initial phase
522   of the training, partly making more precise previous observations.
523
      In summary, this work clarifies in what way mini-batch SGD does not align with full batch gradient
524
      descent behaviors. It challenges existing paradigms and encourages the development of new theories
525   and methods that are more congruent with the stochastic nature of practical deep learning.

544
545
      In the literature, it was generally assumed that, with a number of assumptions, SGD produces
      gradients that are unbiased estimates of the full gradients. After such an assumption, the analysis
546
      continues on the “true” loss landscape (and the empirical loss landscape is a good approximation
547
      of which). Now, in our analysis, we are also focusing on the loss landscape that is formed when
548   we consider a the loss restricted to the instances of the mini batch. On one hand, this landscape is
549   “temporary” and changes drastically from batch to batch, thus making it unsuitable for any sort of
550   multi-step analysis. [This might consitture the reason why this loss landscape is rarely considered.]
551   On the other hand, this mini batch loss landscape is the one that dictates the concrete step, and
552   therefore constitutes an important object to consider for one-step analysis
553

      9.2   T HE MINI BATCH S HARPNESS
556
      Now, although the aforementioned assumption that SGD produces an unbiased estimate of the
557
      empirical (and thus “true”) is by definition true, this is not the case for the hessian of the loss. In
558
      particular, the aspect of the hessian that we care about is its highest eigenvalue, i.e. sharpness. Now,
559   it turns out that the highest eigenvalue of the mini batch Hessian (the mini batch sharpness, MBS)
560   doesn’t provide an unbiased estimator of the highest eigenvalue of the full-batch Hessian (FBS).
561   In particular, the expectation of MBS over the batches might be higher than the FBS: [the usual
562   calculation goes here, side-by-side, comparing FBS and MBS] Notice that the first term is dominating,
563   and would thus determine the sharpness of the network. [we need to add justification of why this is
564   the case] Notice that the first term is a an average of (scaled) projections onto the gradients of the
565   network. Now, notice that the operator norm of this operator (and therefore the sharpness of the loss)
566   is non-increasing as we increase the batch size. This is simply follows from the fact that the norm of
567
      the sum is not greater than the sum of the norms. In one extreme case, if those gradients are perfectly

568

569
      batch size. In the extreme case of perfectly orthogonal vectors, the norm of the operator would drop

571   as we increase the batch size.

813   features of the full-batch notion of Edge of Stability are that

816            threshold.
817         2. The steps are such that the second and third order terms of the Taylor expansion of the loss
818            L(θ) evolution cancel out. Indeed, θ1 after one step of full batch GD with step size η > 0
819            from θ0 is such that

824
            3. The FullBS during this phase implicitly defines the sharpness of the minimum found by
825            gradient descent.
826
            4. This phenomenon is unexpected in the understood cases of optimization of quadratics.
827
828   Mini batch SGD with fixed step size is known to oscillate because of its inherent noise even in
829   the case of quadratics at convergence. Precisely, at convergence, it oscillate around a minimum
830   with size of the oscillations η for the case with replacement or η 2 for the case without replacement,
831   see Mishchenko et al. (2020) and references therein. SGD thus exhibits the properties 1. and 3.
832   naturally, even for quandratics, not because of an instability due to the landscape, but because of
833   an instability due to the noise. The only previous work connecting EoS and SGD present to our
834   knowledge describe an effect which contains this natural effect due to SGD noise. The effect we
835   discover and the generalization to mini batches that we give for edge of stability instead is about an
836
      instability due to the landscape, and thus about the properties 2. and 4. above. Edge of stability is
      about having a quantity that is around 2/η; it is also about the canceling out in the equation above,
837
      but it is not only about the oscillations per se. We thus believe that this is the generalization of Edge
838
      of Stability for the mini batch setting that is interesting for understanding the training dynamics of
839   deep learning and that naturally generalizes the one given by Cohen et al. (2021) for full batch GD
840   and by Cohen et al. (2022) for full batch Adam. Indeed, EoSS reduces to EoS in the full batch case
841   and our empirical observations are inherently of how our relevant notion of sharpness acclimates to
842   learning rate and batch size. The only other work on extending the EoS for mini batch SGD is Lee &
843   Jang (2023), but there the authors are rather presenting a non-consistent correction term to the GD
844   EoS to get the EoS for SGD, without establishing its limiting factor or the influence on the dynamics
845   of SGD.
