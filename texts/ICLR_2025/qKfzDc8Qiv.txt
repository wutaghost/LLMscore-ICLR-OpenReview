011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Evaluating the robustness of deep neural networks (DNNs) is crucial in safety-
014            critical areas, driving research into methods that accurately measure and enhance
015            their resilience against adversarial attacks, specifically from a statistical perspec-
016            tive due to scalability issues faced by deterministic methods. Existing approaches
017
               based on independent sampling usually fail to directly capture such instances due
               to their rarity. Hence in this work, we treat the existence of adversarial examples
018
               as a rare event, and propose an innovative statistical framework for assessing the
019
               adversarial robustness of DNNs, called REPP. Our approach redefines the prob-
020            lem of calculating the occurrence of adversarial examples as the exponential of
021            the mixture of a Poisson random variable and some potential geometric random
022            variables. We adapt the point process with a Minimum Variance Unbiased Esti-
023            mator (MVUE) to accurately estimate the likelihood of encountering adversarial
024            examples, with an upper bound of the true probability with high confidence. Un-
025            like existing rare-event methods based on Multi-level Splitting, REPP does not
026            require the inherent level concept or the continuity condition of the cumulative
027            distribution function (CDF) within DNNs. This adaptation allows for practical
028
               application across both computer vision and natural language processing tasks.
               Experimental results demonstrate that our method is more flexible and effective,
029
               offering a more reliable robustness evaluation than existing statistical approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      As the core methodology of the recent advancements in artificial intelligence, Deep Neural Networks
035
      (DNNs) have revolutionized various fields from autonomous driving (Caesar et al., 2020; Hu et al.,
036
      2023) to natural language processing (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019; Vaswani et al., 2017; Touvron
037   et al., 2023). However, despite their impressive performance, DNNs are known to be vulnerable
038   to adversarial examples, which contain maliciously crafted noises (Goodfellow et al., 2015). The
039   difference between benign examples and their adversarial counterparts is often trivial from a human
040   perspective, yet the latter could disrupt DNNs, resulting in significant performance drops (Wang
041   et al., 2022; Goyal et al., 2023). Therefore, adversarial examples pose critical challenges to the
042   reliability and robustness of DNN-based systems, especially in safety-critical applications. A large
043   amount of effort has been put into certifying the adversarial robustness of DNNs (Huang et al.,
044   2020). Most of those works study this topic under a rigorous white-box setting that requires access
045
      to the DNNs’ weights and backpropagation process (Li et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2020; Singh et al.,
      2019a;b; Xiang et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2021). However, the rapid increase in the scale of recent
046
      DNNs, especially Large Language Models (LLMs) (Kaplan et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023), raises
047
      notable challenges for previous white-box verification solutions. While some pioneering studies
048   have started to explore verifying adversarial robustness in black-box settings, these methods, though
049   applicable to large-scale DNNs, rely on specific assumptions about the target models’ continuity or
050   output distribution (Cohen et al., 2019; Ruan et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023).
051   Some of these works adopt statistical verification, differing from traditional deterministic methods.
052   In many real-world scenarios, guaranteed safety is not always feasible. For example, communication
053   networks cannot ensure no message loss. This makes deterministic verification overly pessimistic,
      whereas statistical methods allow systems to handle occasional message loss more effectively.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Therefore, we focus on quantifying the probability of encountering adversarial examples from a sta-
055   tistical perspective. While it is possible to use a crude Monte Carlo estimator to sample adversarial
056   examples, it is computationally inefficient due to the high dimensionality of the perturbation space
057   and its rarity. As a result, it may require an extremely high number of samples to observe even a few
058   adversarial examples. Thus, in this work, adversarial examples are considered as rare events within
059   the input space because they are not commonly encountered in typical usage and require specific
060   conditions to be met. Recently, an advanced statistical method, named multi-level splitting (Kahn &
061   Harris, 1951; Au & Beck, 2001; Cérou et al., 2012), is developed for estimating extreme probabili-
062   ties of some rare events. In particular, Webb et al. (2018) adopt the adaptive version of multi-level
063
      splitting methods to assess the robustness of DNNs, which is known as Adaptive Multi-Level Split-
      ting (AMLS). However, AMLS requires a continuous cumulative distribution function (cdf ) within
064
      DNNs, the existence of each level, and the well-approximated condition distribution for each level.
065
066   In this paper, we break the continuous and level limitations of AMLS and propose a novel statisti-
067   cal framework, termed REPP, for performing the Robustness Evaluation of neural networks based
068   on Point Process (Walter, 2015). The probability of the occurrence of an adversarial example is
069
      redefined as the exponential of a parameter following the Poisson distribution, and its estimation
      eventually falls into a counting problem of random variables. By incorporating geometric random
070
      variables, we can provide precise statistical estimations for the probability regardless of the presence
071
      of discontinuities. The contribution of this work is summarized as follows:
072
073          • For the first time, we redefine the occurrence of adversarial examples as the exponential of a
074            Poisson parameter, enabling us to adapt a Minimum Variance Unbiased Estimator (MVUE)
075            to accurately estimate the likelihood of encountering adversarial examples. Compared with
076            statistical methods depending on independent sampling, REPP offers a more reliable and
077            meaningful estimation at the same confidence level, especially for rare events.
078          • Unlike existing statistical methods such as Multi-level Splitting, our framework REPP
079            breaks the condition of level concept and ensures better flexibility under different condi-
080            tions, regardless of whether the cdf of the rare event (i.e., the output of the neural network)
081            is discontinuous or not, enabling wider applicability, especially in NLP domain. Addition-
082            ally, REPP reduces both the number of queries and the simulations required for estimation.
083          • Experiments conducted across various cases in the computer vision and natural language
084            processing domains demonstrate its flexibility and effectiveness on several datasets for dif-
085            ferent DNN models, even including the large-scale ViT classifiers and the emerging LLMs.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
088
089
      Verification is a key component of robustness evaluation, using deterministic or statistical methods.
090
091   Deterministic verification approaches Based on a given input and any specified perturbation, a
092   typical approach is converting a verification problem into a series of constraints, which can sub-
093
      sequently be tackled by various program solvers (Katz et al., 2017; Amir et al., 2021) but faces
      ‘timeout’ and scalability issues. Therefore, utilizing a layer-by-layer approximation or relaxation
094
      to derive a valid lower bound for the reachability problem is a common approach used in incom-
095
      plete methods (Zhang et al., 2018; Boopathy et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2019b; Salman et al., 2019).
096   In addition to the aforementioned white-box methods, which require access to model parameters,
097   black-box methods relying on global optimization have also been developed to verify the adversar-
098   ial robustness of DNNs (Ruan et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023). Current deterministic verification
099   methods often face scalability challenges due to high input dimensionality or the size of the neural
100   network. Additionally, they typically require Lipschitz continuity constraints, which can be restric-
101   tive. More importantly, the safety requirements are not always feasible or applicable in real-world
102   scenarios, making deterministic verification potentially unduly pessimistic since it focuses on the
103   worst-case scenarios only. This has led to recent developments in statistical verification methods.
104   Statistical verification approaches Unlike deterministic verification approaches, statistics-based
105   robustness analysis can either provide probabilistic guarantees on the consistency of the classifier’s
106   output given a perturbation or quantify the probability of encountering a counterexample. In the first
107   thread, randomized smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020) has recently become a popu-
      lar framework for providing probabilistic guarantees on the robustness of DNNs, ensuring consistent


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      outputs within a certain radius with high probability. Weng et al. (2019) offers a certificate of neural
109   network robustness under random noise conditions. It extends traditional worst-case scenarios to
110   a probabilistic setting using existing worst-case certification frameworks. However, it requires that
111   the perturbation noise follows a specific distribution, such as Gaussian or Sub-Gaussian distributions
112   with bounded support. RoMA (Levy & Katz, 2022) is proposed as a method for measuring robust-
113   ness against adversarial examples, under the assumption that the highest incorrect confidence scores
114   are normally distributed. Another research focus on achieving statistical robustness involves bound-
115   ing the risk of encountering counterexamples through random sampling perturbations. By specify-
116   ing user-defined confidence levels and acceptable error margins, different concentration inequalities
117
      (e.g., Chernoff (Baluta et al., 2021), Chernoff-Cramer (Pautov et al., 2022), Hoeffding (Huang et al.,
      2021), and Adaptive Hoeffding (Zhang et al., 2022)) can be applied to derive the results with suffi-
118
      cient number of samples. However, these methods rely on independent naive Monte Carlo, which
119
      may fail when a valid adversarial example cannot be sampled, even after a large number of attempts,
120   such as 1010 samples, as demonstrated in our following experiments. In particular, Baluta et al.
121   (2021) introduces the concept of ‘adversarial density’ to quantify the likelihood of adversarial ex-
122   amples within a given perturbation range, which is exactly the one we aim to estimate, where a base
123   classifier with lower adversarial density will benefit randomized smoothing defense. The closest
124   works to this paper are (Webb et al., 2018; Tit et al., 2021), which apply multi-level sampling tech-
125   niques for rare events to directly estimate the ratio of adversarial examples in a black-box manner.

168

169   is very low. This can potentially result in no occurrences being sampled or require an extremely large
170   number of samples to accurately estimate their probability. To address this as a rare event, Webb
171   et al. (2018) proposed the use of the Adaptive Multi-Level Splitting (AMLS) technique (Guyader
172   et al., 2011) for estimating these properties, while Tit et al. (2021) introduced the Last Particle
173   method, a variant of AMLS. AMLS has been proven to be unbiased (Bréhier et al., 2015) under
174   three conditions: 1) the cdf of Y is continuous; 2) Each level exists; 3) Well-approximation of
175   the condition distribution for each level. While the third condition is typically considered satisfied
176   by MCMC, AMLS can only address the integral case in Eq. (2) and fail to handle the discontinuity.
177

      does not rely on the level setting in AMLS or the continuous cdf constraint, and provide an upper
178
      bound probability with high confidence to deal with the absence of the event.

331
      To conduct the hypothesis test introduced above, all we need to do is sampling along the non-
332
      decreasing random walk through MCMC. However, we first need to determine the number of
333   MC simulations based on the specific purpose of the test. If the goal is verification, a smaller
334   N like 10-20 is sufficient as it only affects the variance of the estimator; however if the goal is a
335   precise probabilistic estimation, a much larger N is required. If we do not know whether the true
336   probability we want to estimate is 0 or not, a straightforward trick is to run REPP with smaller N
337   firstly as a probe and then run with large N if a more precise estimation is required. More detailed
338   implementations of the proposed REPP can be found in Appendix B.1.

      5.2     ROBUSTNESS E VALUATION ON THE D ISCONTINUOUS C ASES
470
471   The discontinuity of the cdf of Y typically arises from two sources: the discrete nature of the input
472   and the output metric itself. In the following sections, we aim to demonstrate that our approach can
473   effectively handle such discontinuities, providing precise results within NLP domains. Appendix D
474   provides additional results for handling specifically designed discontinuities in the image domain.
475
476   5.2.1    S ENTIMENT C LASSIFICATION
477
      In this part, we show that our REPP can also work on the discontinuous case, where the text data
478
      in natural language processing is a natural discrete input. The discontinuity comes from the lim-
479
      ited combination state of the input, it may be less noticeable due to the embedding in the text, but
480   the state of the Y is also constrained by the number of possible states in the input. Here we uti-
481   lize the Stanford Sentiment Treebank 2 (SST-2) dataset (Socher et al., 2013), a widely recognized
482   benchmark for sentiment analysis in natural language processing (NLP), to test our approach. It
483   contains movie reviews from Rotten Tomatoes, labeled as positive or negative. In our experiment,
484   we use the validation set of SST-2, which contains 872 movie reviews. The open-source BERT
485   model bert-base-uncased-SST-2 (Devlin et al., 2018) classifies each review as positive or
      negative. In this case, P[Y ≥ 0] becomes the probability of occurrence of adversarial examples


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      introduced by replacing the synonyms in the original text input. In the experiment, we filter the
487   samples based on the following criteria: We first apply a popular attack method TextFooler (Jin
488   et al., 2020) on the validation set, which involves word replacement from a synonym set. The max-
489   imum number of synonym candidates is set to 10 and we filter out cases where the total number of
490   word combinations exceeds 5,000,000. We also limit the search space to remain close to the original
491   input (unlike iterative synonym replacement used in TextAttack (Morris et al., 2020). This approach
492   allows us to replace synonyms only in the original text, ensuring that the exhaustive search yields the
493   ground truth of I. We then focus on samples where Textfooler fails to find the adversarial examples,
494   but their probabilities are not zero. Specifically, we only consider samples that can be verified by
495
      exhaustive search, even though TextFooler fails to find an adversarial counterpart. Ultimately, we
      obtained 14 valid samples based on the setting mentioned above. Therefore, we can directly com-
496
      pare the results with the ground-truth probabilities to illustrate the effectiveness of REPP, rather than
497
      relying on naive Monte Carlo (MC) sampling as the baseline used in the previous section. During
498   the non-decreasing random walk, we employ Gibbs sampling (Gelfand & Smith, 1990) with step 40
499   to approximate the conditional distribution, sampling each dimension successively. Figure 3b also
500   displays the index of these samples in the SST-2 validation set along the x-axis. We can see that our
501   REPPN =2600,M =40 estimation exactly matches the ground truth.

      6      C ONCLUSION
533
534   In conclusion, we proposed a novel statistical framework, termed REPP, for assessing the robustness
535   of DNNs based on Point Process. The probability of the occurrence of an adversarial example is
536   redefined as the exponential of a parameter following the Poisson law, together with the geometric
537   random variables, we can handle the discontinuous variables and provide a precise estimation for the
538   probability no matter whether the existence of the discontinuity. Experiments are conducted across
539   CV and NLP domains in various scenarios, demonstrating its flexibility and effectiveness compared
      to other statistical approaches that rely on independent sampling or required continuous conditions.

717   The Poisson process (Kingman, 1992) is a cornerstone of stochastic processes, widely utilized in
718   reliability engineering and the analysis of rare events. In reliability engineering, it effectively mod-

720   happen at a constant average rate. This makes it invaluable for predicting the number of failures
721   within a given period, helping engineers assess system reliability, and planning maintenance sched-
722   ules for critical infrastructure. Besides, the Poisson process is also an ideal model for capturing the
723   low probability and randomness associated with specific rare events.

725   Poisson distribution (Grimmett & Stirzaker, 2020):

769   B.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF REPP
770
771   Based on the previous analysis, all we need to do to correctly estimate the probability of the discov-
772   ery of an adversarial example is sampling along the non-decreasing random walk. Specifically,
773   MCMC methods are employed to perform conditional simulations µ(x|Y > y) for any y ∈ R,
774   such that multiple non-decreasing random walks can be obtained simultaneously. In each iteration,
775   we perform conditional sampling and count the number of failures of random searching adversarial
776   examples until all the routes have found the adversarial case, i.e., all of them surpass 0 and become
777   some adversarial examples, such that we can obtain estimators of a probability p = P[Y > 0] as
778
      a robustness evaluation. Although we aim to estimate the probability of the occurrence of the ad-
      versarial example, during the non-decreasing random walk, we can get some successful adversarial
779
      examples, as a byproduct. In other words, our proposed REPP can be seen as a kind of black-box
780
      adversarial attack with generating N adversarial example and also its probability of existence. The
781   overall pseudo-code can be seen in Alg. 1.
782
783   In practice, to avoid local maxima (as we are sampling from −∞ to 0) and improve the convergence
784
      of the Markov chain, it would be better not to start from the current state Xcurrent with Ycurrent =
      s(Xcurrent ). Therefore, we apply the following strategy: We randomly pick up a starting point X ∗
785
      from the database db, where each element consists of a tuple {X ∗ , Ylast , Y ∗ }. Ylast represents the
786
      level from which Y ∗ is generated. To improve coverage, we aim to sample a starting point that
787   already follows the target distribution, noting that X ∗ can be a valid starting point if and only if
788   Ylast ≤ Ycurrent ≤ Y ∗ is satisfied. After each iteration of the MCMC conditional simulation, we
789   update the database db only when the slowest chain can be enhanced by sampling (see line 22 in
790   Alg. 1, where there is at least one sample will be following the target distribution for the worst
791   chain). We only update those samples where Ycurrent < 0 such that preventing the valid points in the
792   database from vanishing.
793
      In addition, REPP also provides an anytime (in each iteration) probability estimation Î, and an extra
794
      Ī, as an upper bound of I with high probability 1 − α, when Ī is smaller than a predefined threshold
795   τ = 10−50 , we say that with 1 − α probability the true probability I will be smaller than Ī, hereby
796   approximates the absence of the adversarial examples.

      Multilevel splitting Kahn & Harris (1951), also called Subset Simulation Au & Beck (2001) or
911

914   for estimating this property. Multi-level splitting breaks down the prediction of rare events into
915   simpler, more manageable tasks. In this method, they set up a sequence of intermediate thresholds,
916   L0 , L1 , L2 , . . . , LK , with −∞ = L0 < L1 < L2 < . . . < LK = 0, to create a bridge from our
917   initial model p(·) to the target distribution µ(· |Y ≥ 0). In each level, the conditional pk (·) is defined

1046   E    E SSENTIAL D IFFERENCES TO OTHER S TATISTICAL V ERIFICATION
1047
            A PPROACHES
1048
1049
1050   Here we further discuss the essential differences between our proposed REPP framework and other
1051   SOTA statistical verification methods. Unlike randomized smoothing providing a probabilistic guar-
1052   antee as a defense approach, we aim to directly estimate the probability of encountering adversarial
1053   examples. The most relevant literature includes methods either based on naive MC sampling with
1054   different concentration inequalities Huang et al. (2021); Baluta et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2022) or
1055   from a rare-event perspective Webb et al. (2018); Tit et al. (2021).
1056   Approaches based on naive Monte Carlo (MC) independent sampling can provide statistical evalu-
1057   ation regardless of whether the CDF is continuous or not. However, they fail to produce meaningful
1058   statistics for particularly rare events, even with a sufficiently large number of samples. This has
1059   been validated in our experiments, as described in Sec. 5.1.2 and Sec. 5.1.3. On the other hand, Ap-
1060   proaches based on rare-event like Adaptive Multi-Level Splitting (AMLS) (Webb et al., 2018) and
1061   Last Particle Algorithm (LPA) (Tit et al., 2021), their common limitation compared to our REPP
1062
       is that they fail to handle discontinuous cases, additionally, even for continuous cases, they are not
       able to provide a high confidence for their results like us. LPA is a special case of AMLS, where
1063
       in each iteration, only the worst particle is replaced by one of the better ones. It shares the same
1064
       minimum variance with REPP theoretically but lacks the ability to perform sampling in parallel. In
1065   other words, the proposed REPP is an advanced version of LPA that supports parallel sampling and
1066   extends its flexibility to handle discontinuous cases.
1067
1068
       Normally the statistical approach consumes a fixed number of calls to the DNN models. In total, the
       maximum number of calls for estimating the probability I scales as O(log(1/I)) for LPA and REPP,
1069
       which is in stark contrast to independent sampling, where the number of calls scales proportionally
1070
       to O(1/I). Therefore, they are specifically designed for rare events that naive Monte Carlo sampling
1071   cannot effectively handle.
