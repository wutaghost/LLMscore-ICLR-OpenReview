008
009
                   Auto-evaluation is crucial for assessing response quality and offering feedback for
010
                   model development. Recent studies have explored training large language models
011                (LLMs) as generative judges to both evaluate model responses and generate natu-
012                ral language critiques. However, existing models have been trained almost exclu-
013                sively with supervised fine-tuning (SFT), often only on a small number of datasets,
014                resulting in poor generalization across different evaluation settings and tasks. In
015                this paper, we investigate how learning from both positive and negative data with
016                direct preference optimization (DPO) enhances the evaluation capabilities of LLM
017                judges across three evaluation tasks: pairwise, single ratings, and binary classifi-
018                cation. We achieve this by creating three forms of DPO data from a diverse col-
019
                   lection of human and synthetic judgements on contemporary model outputs, with
                   the goal of training our model to generate meaningful critiques, make accurate
020
                   judgements, and understand what constitutes good and bad responses for a given
021
                   user input. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, we train judge models
022                of three sizes: 8B parameters, 12B, and 70B, and conduct a comprehensive study
023                over 13 benchmarks (7 pairwise, 4 single rating, and 2 classification), measur-
024                ing agreement with human and GPT-4 annotations. Our models exhibit the best
025                aggregate performance, with even our 8B model outperforming strong baselines
026                like GPT-4o and specialized judge models, such as OffsetBias-8B, Auto-J-13B,
027                Prometheus-2-8x7B, and Skywork-Critic-70B, in pairwise benchmarks. Further
028                analysis shows that our judge model robustly counters biases such as position and
029                length bias, flexibly adapts to practitioner-specified evaluation protocols, and pro-
030
                   vides helpful language feedback for improving downstream generator models.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Auto-evaluation plays an important role for assessing response quality and providing feedback for
035   improving large language models (LLMs) since human evaluation is expensive and unscalable. Due
036   to their impressive language understanding and generative capabilities, LLMs themselves have been
037   leveraged in recent studies as generative judges to not only evaluate outputs from other models, but
038   also provide free-text critiques as feedback for model alignment (Akyürek et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
039   2023; Hu et al., 2024a). Auto-evaluation using LLMs has evolved quickly, moving from prompting
040   high-performing LLMs, like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), to training specialized judge models, which are
041   explicitly purposed to provide judgements given an original input instruction and model response(s).
042
      The typical approach for training judge models involves collecting labeled data with ground-truth
      judgement annotated by either humans or powerful LLMs, then training with supervised fine-tuning
043
      (SFT) (Vu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Kim et al., 2024b). However, SFT alone is known to
044
      be suboptimal, as it only allows LLMs to learn from positive examples with correct judgements
045   without learning to avoid generating incorrect judgements (Song et al., 2020; Pang et al., 2024).
046
047   In this work, we investigate learning from both positive and negative evaluations with direct pref-
048
      erence optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) to enhance the evaluation capabilities of gener-
      ative judges. To collect preference pairs, we prompt an LLM to perform chain-of-thought (CoT)
049
      evaluation of other models’ outputs for different evaluation tasks, covering single rating, pairwise
050
      comparison and classification (Training Tasks (a) - (c) in Fig. 1). We then categorize the gener-
051   ated evaluations into positive and negative evaluations based on whether the final judgements match
052   ground-truth labels for DPO training. To enhance the judge’s ability to identify strong or weak

077   which only use a small number of datasets to target specific judgement domains (Shiwen et al., 2024;

080

      models to better generalize to various evaluation tasks, as we demonstrate with our experiments.
081
082   We train judge models of three sizes: 8B, 12B, and 70B parameters. In contrast to existing judges
083   (Table 1), our judges are trained using three unique types of pairwise DPO data to perform multi-
084   faceted evaluation (pairwise, single rating, classification). This is done without single rating-specific
085   changes to the DPO loss, as in the single rating only Themis (Hu et al., 2024b) or iterative training,
086
      as in pairwise only Self-taught-evaluator (Wang et al., 2024c). The breadth of our evaluation tasks is
      matched only by non-publicly released FLAMe (Vu et al., 2024). We conduct a comprehensive set
087
      of experiments, covering 13 different benchmarks across the three evaluation tasks to evaluate judge
088
      capabilities in various domains, such as safety, reasoning, and instruction following. The results
089   validate both effectiveness and generalizability of our training and data curation methods, as many
090   of our evaluation tasks are unseen during training. Our largest model achieves the best aggregate
091   performance (84.25 pairwise accuracy, 0.76 single rating Pearson correlation, 85.60 classification
092   accuracy), surpassing GPT-4o (76.78 pairwise, 0.75 single rating, 85.47 classification) and other
093   strong judge models. Additional analysis shows that: (1) Our judges can robustly counter common
094   biases such as position and length bias, (2) Our judges accommodate a variety of prompting tech-
095   niques, offering use-case specific practitioner flexibility, and (3) Our judges can act as a powerful
096   reward models for model development by providing AI feedback and revising poor model responses.

135   Of prior work, only FLAMe (Vu et al., 2024) is trained explicitly on these three tasks. For each task,
136   an evaluation rubric is also provided as input to the judge to specify what aspects (e.g., helpfulness,
137   safety, or in general) are considered for evaluating the responses. We compile an array of training
138   datasets into these three tasks. For each dataset, we hand-craft an evaluation protocol p that describes
139   the evaluation task (single, pairwise or classification) and the evaluation rubric, following original
140   directions given to human annotators when available. All these datasets are formatted as a sequence-
141   to-sequence task. Ultimately, we aim to train a unified generative judge that can perform different
142   evaluation tasks based on the protocol and the input in the prompt. In contrast to prior work like
143
      Prometheus (Kim et al., 2023; 2024b), the level of detail in our evaluation rubrics can vary from
      fine-grained, instance-specific guidelines to very general fixed criteria (e.g., “Ensure the response
144
      follows user instructions”), offering practitioners flexibility in how they prompt our judge models.
145
146   Previous studies focus on supervised fine-tuning (SFT), where the generative judge is trained on pos-
147   itive evaluation examples with correct judgements, annotated by either humans or powerful LLMs
148   like GPT-4. However, SFT for boosting the reasoning capability of an LLM can be suboptimal for
149
      the following reasons. First, the judge only learns to imitate the reasoning form from the positive
      examples but not necessarily the underlying reasoning skills for deriving the right judgement (Dai
150
      et al., 2024). Second, since the model does not explicitly learn to avoid generating the negative
151
      examples with incorrect judgements, the probability of negative examples could also increase along
152   the positive examples during SFT as shown in Pang et al. (2024).

155   3    M ETHOD
156
157   The above observations motivate us to construct both positive and negative examples for training
158   our generative judge via preference optimization instead of pure SFT. Notably, we propose 3 types
159   of positive and negative examples to improve the capability of our generative judges from different
160   perspectives, as illustrated as Preference Pairs in Fig. 1. These include: 1) Chain-of-Thought Cri-
161   tique, which not only aims to improve the reasoning capability, but also serves as an explanation for
      the judge’s decision, 2) Standard Judgement, which aims to provide direct supervision for producing

      the correct judgement, and 3) Response Deduction, which aims to further enhance the understanding
163   of good/bad responses in hindsight. The preference data construction process is shown in Fig. 2.
164
165   3.1: Chain-of-Thought Critique. Given an evaluation protocol p, a task input i and a response r
166
      from another model to be evaluated (or a response pair {ra , rb } for pairwise comparison) as input
      x ∈ X , our judge is trained to generate a free-text evaluation y = {c, j} ∈ Y. The evaluation con-
167
      sists of (1) a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) critique c that provides a detailed analysis of the response(s)
168
      and (2) a final judgement j, which could be a single score, a preference over {ra , rb }, or a classi-
169   fication result. To construct the positive and negative examples DCoT = {x, y w , y l } for judgement
170   preference optimization, we first prompt a teacher LLM MTeacher to generate candidate evaluations
171   y = {c, j} for a diverse set of training data. Then based on whether the judgement j matches the
172   ground-truth annotation, we categorize the candidates into positive and negative examples. Through
173   preference optimization, our generative judge learns to increase the probability of good reasoning
174   traces while decreasing that of bad reasoning traces.
175   3.2: Standard Judgement. In addition to training our judge models to produce explanations in the
176   form of critiques, we want to ensure our judges produce the correct final judgement. However, in
177   the CoT critiques, only a few important tokens determine the final judgement while the remaining
178   tokens improve flow of speech and coherence, as exemplified in Fig. 3. Thus, the relatively long
179   output sequence may dilute the training signal for these crucial tokens (Chen et al., 2024), leading to
180   poor judgement supervision and sub-optimal alignment with human preferences. To mitigate this,
181   we also train our model to generate standard judgements without the CoT critiques. To construct
182   the positive and negative examples DStd = {x, y w , y l }, we simply remove the CoT critique part of y
183
      from DCoT and modify the evaluation protocol p in x accordingly to reflect this output requirement.
      By learning from such standard judgement preference, we provide a more direct training signal on
184
      the representation of our generative judge. In § 5.3, we show that this task is critical for the judge to
185
      initiate correct reasoning even when conducting CoT-type evaluation.

223   4.1: Training Data and Details. To build a multifaceted judge model that generalizes across various
224
      evaluation tasks, we curate our training data to cover a wide range of evaluation tasks (single rating/-
      pairwise/classification) that evaluate different aspects (general quality, factuality, helpfulness, safety,
225
      etc.) of model responses to various types of instructions (general user queries, reasoning, math or
226
      coding problems). In doing so, we invest effort in compiling annotated data from a wide variety of
227   data sources, preserving the evaluation granularity for each data source. This stands in contrast to
228   other judge models, which rely on a few datasets with many samples, like HelpSteer (Wang et al.,
229   2023e), WildChat (Zhao et al., 2024) or UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023). Our training data sources
230   from both human- and model-generated annotations. For human annotated datasets, we take inspi-
231   ration from the datasets proposed by Vu et al. (2024). However, we focus on datasets with modern
232   (2023 and beyond) LLM responses, as older datasets likely contain lower quality responses from
233   less capable models, with correspondingly stale annotations. We supplement human-annotated data
234   with synthetically generated data to endow our judge models with specific capabilities (e.g., fol-
235
      lowing fine-grained rubrics in evaluation), utilizing datasets similar to those used by other judge
      models (Kim et al., 2024b; 2023; Park et al., 2024; Shiwen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c).
236
237   A majority of available datasets do not provide the CoT critiques, as such free-text explanations
238   are more expensive to collect compared to the final judgements. However, our approach does not
239   require annotated CoT critiques, allowing us to make use of these high-quality annotated judge-
240   ments. As described in § 3, we prompt Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct as a strong teacher model to obtain
241
      high-quality preference data DCoT . Standard judgement preference DStd is obtained by removing the
      CoT critiques from DCoT . For obtaining DDed , we prompt a weaker model Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct to
242
      generate the deduced responses as the negative examples. In total, we collect 680K preference pairs,
243
      with a 70%:15%:15% ratio for DCoT , DStd and DDed 2 . We then train three models using the train-
244   ing loss in Eq. 1: Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, NeMo-Instruct-12B, and Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct, yielding
245   Our model 8B, Our model 12B, Our model 70B (Names redacted for review), respectively.

      4.2: Evaluation Datasets. We adopt a comprehensive evaluation suite, comprising seven pair-
      wise comparison benchmarks, four single rating benchmarks, and two classification benchmarks.
248
      This suite of benchmarks is meant to broadly evaluate how judge models make decisions in dif-
249
      ferent use cases (e.g., general chat quality, summary quality, safety). For pairwise comparisons,
250   we evaluate on RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024), InstruSum (Liu et al., 2023c), Auto-J (Eval-P
251   test set with ties) (Li et al., 2023a), HHH (Askell et al., 2021), LFQA (Xu et al., 2023), EvalBi-
252   asBench (Park et al., 2024), and PreferenceBench (Kim et al., 2024b). These benchmarks span
253   both general (e.g., Auto-J assesses across eight major groups, including creative writing, rewriting,
254   and coding) and specific (e.g., InstruSum focuses on instruction-following for summarization), with
255   PreferenceBench assessing the fine-grained evaluation ability of judge models via detailed rubrics
256   and reference answers. For single rating, we evaluate on BiGGen-Bench model outputs (Kim et al.,
257   2024a), FLASK (Ye et al., 2023b), MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024), and FeedbackBench (Kim et al.,
258
      2023). For classification, we evaluate on LLM-AggreFact (Tang et al., 2024)3 and InfoBench (Ex-
      pert split) (Qin et al., 2024). For a more detailed dataset overviews, see Appendix A.

290   to perform classification by outputting “Yes” or “No” in natural language. We make this choice due
291   to the pointwise nature of single rating and classification tasks. Additionally, we select OpenAI’s
292   GPT-4o and GPT-4o-mini as proprietary baselines.
293   For fair comparison, we utilize the original prompt templates of generative judge baselines, making
294   minimal changes to accommodate new tasks or information (e.g., accommodating rubrics in evalua-
295   tion or allowing for pairwise comparison ties). For proprietary and instruct models, unless the bench-
296   mark has provided a template (Auto-J and Prometheus), we utilize the default pairwise prompt from
297   RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) and the default single rating prompt from Prometheus (Kim
298   et al., 2023). For single rating tasks, our models use a fixed prompt for all benchmarks, as all of
299   the benchmarks include specialized scoring rubrics and reference answers5 . For pairwise compari-
300   son benchmarks, which lack exact scoring rubrics, we craft specific protocols for each benchmark,
301
      primarily to highlight the flexibility our models afford practitioners due to the careful curation of
      training samples. Such specific prompting is not the source of performance gains over baselines: we
302
      explore two other prompting strategies that are uniform across all pairwise benchmarks in § 5.4 and
303
      find negligible differences in performance, with mild performance gains in some cases.
304
305   For pairwise comparison and classification benchmarks, we report the agreement between judges
306   and human annotators (i.e., accuracy), and for single rating benchmarks, we report Pearson correla-
307
      tion coefficient between judge and human ratings. We adopt the default evaluation setup for Reward-
      Bench. For all other pairwise comparison benchmarks, because existing judges exhibit positional
308
      bias (Wang et al., 2023b), where judgements are not consistent when the order of the two responses
309
      is swapped, we adopt an evaluation setup that measures consistency: we run each benchmark twice,
310   exchanging the order of responses in the second run. We report the best performance of these two
311   runs in § 5 and analyze the consistency rate of judge models in § 5.2. For datasets with multiple cat-
312   egories (e.g., EvalBiasBench and HHH), we report microaverage. For all non-proprietary models,
313   we set the sampling temperature to 0, top-p to 1, and limit the number of output tokens to 1024. For
314   OpenAI models, we utilize the default API parameters (temperature of 0.7, top-p of 1).

      Recent analysis (Park et al., 2024) has identified six types of biases that judge models are vulnerable
      to, and proposed EvalBiasBench, a meta-evaluation benchmark with bias-specific test samples. In
410
      particular, the higher accuracy a judge achieves on each subset of EvalBiasBench, the more immune
411
      a judge is to that type of bias; see Appendix A for descriptions of the biases. To analyze model
412   biases, we evaluate Our models and other common LLM-as-judge models for bias on EvalBiasBench
413   and also report the average consistency across the non-RewardBench benchmarks, which measures
414   if the model is capable of returning the same judgement choice if the order of responses is swapped
415   in a pairwise comparison. Our results are presented in Table 5. On EvalBiasBench, our models
416   outperform powerful models such as GPT-4o, trailing only Llama-3-OffsetBias, the Skywork-Critic
417   models, and Self-taught-evaluator. Llama-3-OffsetBias was specifically trained with an emphasis
418   on bias mititgation, whereas Skywork-Critic and Self-taught-evaluator both employ self-teaching
419   techniques that closely resemble how EvalBiasBench data was generated. Despite this, our model
420
      is competitive across a range of bias categories, but is relatively weak when it comes to handling
      empty references. For positional bias, our models surpass all comparable baselines by substantial
421
      margins, with an average consistency of 91.41% for our largest model and 89.00% for our smallest
422
      model. All three of our models demonstrate more consistent pairwise comparison judgements than
423   the next best models, beating GPT-4o-mini, Skywork-Critic-8B, and Llama-3-OffsetBias by at least
424   5.37, 3.21, and 7.40 absolute percentage points, respectively. Skywork-Critic-70B was the only
425   other model to break the 89% consistency barrier, but trails Our model 70B by 2.25%.

455   As our training data includes a diverse variety of protocols, instructions, and rubrics, we are able

459   benchmarks. First, we use only our prompt for RewardBench (see Appendix B.4) for all pairwise

464
      model 12B) when using fixed prompt templates. This demonstrates flexibility Our models offer to
465
      practitioners: If one has task-specific criteria, our models can accommodate such criteria in evalu-
466   ation. On the other hand, if no such criteria exist, our models can reliably judge responses using

468   judge models using both our RewardBench and PRePair prompt templates in Appendix C.8.

471
472   In this study, we demonstrate how downstream models can learn from the feedback provided by our
473   generative judge for model development. We investigate two settings in particular. In the first setting,
474   Our model 70B is used as a reward model to score the generations sampled from a downstream
475   model (Llama-3-8B-Instruct) for UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023), a dataset with a diverse set of
476   prompts, using a 5-point Likert scale with additive prompting (Yuan et al., 2024). Then, for each
477
      data point, we treat the highest-scoring response as the positive response and the lowest-scoring

478
      method with baselines using classification-based reward models PairRM (Jiang et al., 2023) and
479

484   also prompt the untuned Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct to refine the responses for comparison. We assess
485   the resulting models on the open-ended instruction-following benchmark AlpacaEval-2 (Li et al.,

      6   R ELATED W ORK
492
493
      The rapid acceleration in LLM development has necessitated more efficient and cost-effective ways
      of assessing the quality of model outputs than collecting human preferences. Powerful LLMs, such
494
      as GPT-4o and Claude, naturally yielded a line of research that explored the ability of such models
495
      to act as automated evaluators by precise prompting (Wang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023b; Fu
496   et al., 2024; Chiang & Lee, 2023). While promising, such approaches have several fundamental
497   drawbacks. First, these models exhibit an array of biases (Park et al., 2024; Koo et al., 2023), such
498   as favoring their own model outputs (Liu et al., 2023b; Bai et al., 2024; Panickssery et al., 2024),
499   being sensitive to the position of responses in pairwise comparisons (Li et al., 2023a; Wang et al.,
500   2023b). Second, the most capable LLMs are often closed-source, requiring API calls to an ever-
501   changing model backend.
502   As a result, there has been increased interest in training judge models specifically to perform eval-
503   uation. PandaLM (Wang et al., 2023d) explored fine-tuning models based on GPT-3.5 judgements,
504   while MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024) led to the small-scale experiments training on human pref-
505   erences. Auto-J (Li et al., 2023a) expanded upon this work by diversifying the training data and
506   using GPT-4 to generate explanations to accompany preference labels. Recent work on generative
507   judges has focused on training data, with one line of work espousing training entirely on synthetic
508   data, via either self-teaching with Self-taught evaluator (Wang et al., 2024c) or synthesized directly
509   from larger teacher models as is done with Prometheus (Kim et al., 2023; 2024b;a). In contrast,
510
      FLAMe (Vu et al., 2024) has emphasized using entirely human-annotated preference data. While
      using high-quality models to produce training samples scales well, such models are not immune to
511
      the biases (Park et al., 2024) or hallucinations (Gudibande et al., 2023). On the other hand, a large
512
      portion of the FLAMe training data relies on human annotations on older, less powerful model re-
513   sponses, introducing an issue of scale drift (Myford & Wolfe, 2009; Harik et al., 2009) of whether a
514   5/5 response before the LLM era still a 5/5 response.
515
516
      Our work, in contrast, uses DPO and modern data to produce a family of multifaceted judge mod-
      els. Preference optimization has been used to enhance critique generation (McAleese et al., 2024),
517
      where a standard RLHF workflow is used to train CriticGPT to critique model-written code. While
518
      CriticGPT is not trained to perform evaluation tasks such as pairwise comparisons, its ability to
519   meaningfully critique both code and out-of-distribution chat data hints that the standard RLHF
520   workflow could be effective for training judges. Other works that explore preference optimization
521   are Self-taught-evaluator (Wang et al., 2024c) and Themis (Hu et al., 2024b). Self-taught-evaluator,
522   a concurrent work, employs iterative SFT and DPO using a self-teaching framework. This training
523   procedure requires multiple (5+) rounds of data generation and DPO training, using only preference
524   pairs of preferred/rejected critiques and judgements. In contrast, our work uses one round of DPO
525   training with three training tasks, both simplifying the training procedure and diversifying evaluation
526   capabilities that are learned, leading to more well-rounded performance. Themis (Hu et al., 2024b)
527
      trains a judge model to provide just single ratings by modifying the DPO loss with an additive
      margin with magnitude depending on the difference between the preferred/rejected ratings. While
528
      natural for single rating evaluation, it is unclear how to make analogous changes to accommodate
529
      non-metric judgements, such as pairwise comparisons.

533
534   In this work, we present a family of multifaceted judges, trained with three distinct forms of pairwise
535   DPO data, to perform three different evaluation tasks. Our experiments show that our models are
536
      high performing across a variety of tasks and benchmarks, with our 70B model exhibiting the best
      aggregate performance and our 8B model outperforming GPT-4o on multiple benchmarks. Further
537
      analysis shows the importance of each our training tasks, the robustness of our judges to common
538
      biases, and how our judges can be effective in downstream model improvement.

      A    E VALUATION DATASET DETAILS .
814
815
      We evaluate performance on the following seven pairwise comparison datasets.
816
      • RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024). RewardBench assesses reward-modeling capabilities with
817     a focus on four categories: Chat, Chat Hard, Safety, and Reasoning (math and coding).
818
819
      • InstruSum (Liu et al., 2023c). InstruSum assesses the performance of language models in com-
        plex instruction following for text summarization. Their test set is comprised of human responses
820
        to pairwise comparisons formed from 11 different LLM outputs.
821
822   • Auto-J (Eval-P set) (Li et al., 2023a). Auto-J assesses the generative capabilities of language
823
        models across eight major groups, including creative writing, code, and rewriting. This test set
        consists of pairwise comparisons (ties allowed) between outputs sourced from 58 different models.
824
825   • HHH (Askell et al., 2021). HHH consists of human annotated pairwise comparisons meant to
826     assess the safety of models along four axes: helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, and other.
827   • LFQA (Xu et al., 2023). LFQA evaluates models on their ability to answer questions with high
828     degrees of complexity, often necessitating longer, well-reasoned responses. This benchmark con-
829     sists of pairwise comparisons between GPT-3.5 responses and human written responses answered
830     by experts across seven domains.
831   • EvalBiasBench (Park et al., 2024). EvalBiasBench is a meta-evaluation benchmark for evalu-
832     ating how biased an LLM-judge model is in 6 different categories: length, concreteness, empty
833     reference, content continuation, nested instruction, and familiar knowledge.
834   • PreferenceBench (Kim et al., 2024b). PreferenceBench is an in-domain test set for the
835     Prometheus 2 models, which aims to assess the fine-grained evaluation ability of judge models
836     via rubrics and reference answers.
837
838   We evaluate performance on the following four single rating benchmarks.
839
      • BiGGen Bench (Kim et al., 2024a). BiGGen Bench evaluates nine distinct generation capabil-
840
        ities (e.g., instruction following, reasoning, tool usage, etc.) across 77 tasks, providing model
841
        outputs and scores for 103 different language models. We utilize the human evaluation test set.
842
843   • FLASK (Ye et al., 2023b). FLASK contains human and GPT-4 scores, along with fine-grained
844
        rubrics, for responses from four different models.
845   • MT Bench (Zheng et al., 2024). MT Bench consists of GPT-4 scored responses from four differ-
846     ent models.
847   • FeedbackBench (Kim et al., 2023). FeedbackBench is an in-domain test set for the Prometheus
848     models, which acts as a fine-grained evaluation benchmark with rubrics and reference answers.
849
850   For classification, we use the following two benchmarks.
851
      • LLM-AggreFact (Pre-August 9, 2024 update) (Tang et al., 2024). LLM-AggreFact is a large-
852
        scale benchmark that sources questions from 10 attribution benchmarks. Here, the judge model
853
        is given a document and is asked to verify if the claim, which is produced by either a model or a
854     human, is supported by the document.
855
      • InfoBench (Expert split) (Qin et al., 2024). InfoBench evaluates the instruction following capa-
856
        bilities of five different language models via multiple yes/no questions for each response. Because
857
        the responses and questions contain specialized content, we evaluate on the expert annotations
858     for questions for which all experts responded with the same response. This filtering yielded 930
859     unique yes/no questions.
860
861   It is important to ensure that judge models are robust to common biases. Here, we provide a brief
862   description of each of the six biases the EvalBiasBench benchmark (Park et al., 2024). To evaluate
863   for bias, EvalBiasBench constructs pairs of responses where one response is correct, and the other
      is incorrect, but constructed in a way such that a biased judge may be tricked into believing it to be


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      correct. Bias is then measured in terms of accuracy on the evaluation set, where less biased models
865   are able to more accurately identify the correct response.
866
867   The six biases that are measured by EvalBiasBench are as follows:
868
869   • Length bias: judges prefer longer responses, even if the response does not precisely follow the
870
        user’s instructions.
871   • Concreteness bias: judges prefer responses that are more concrete, such as citing precise percent-
872     ages or figures, even if they are wrong or irrelevant.
873
874   • Empty reference bias: Sometimes the input instruction provided by a user is incomplete (Off-
875     setBias authors provide an example of a user requesting a summary of an article, but forgetting
876     to provide an article). Weaker models are susceptible to hallucinating responses based on imag-
877
        ined input content, whereas strong models ask for clarification. Judges tend to prefer hallucinated
        model responses rather than responses that ask for clarification.
878
879   • Content continuation bias: judges prefer responses that continue generating related content to user
880     requests, rather than those that faithfully execute user instructions.
881
882   • Nested instruction bias: If the user instruction includes an input (e.g., an article) that includes
883     an instruction, then the judge may evaluate responses based on how well they satisfy the nested
884
        response rather than the original user instruction.
885   • Familiar knowledge bias: Judge models may prefer responses that contain common information
886     (e.g., idiomatic sayings or common facts) rather than responses that precisely follow the user’s
887     instructions.

1644   C.7    H OW GOOD ARE JUDGE CRITIQUES ? [A DDED N OVEMBER 2024.]
1645
1646   Our benchmarking efforts largely focus on evaluating the correctness of the final judgement, mea-
1647
       sured by agreement with existing human annotations. However, prior work has identified scenarios
       where, while the final judgement may produced may be consistent with human annotations, the
1648
       critique may be inconsistent or hallucinated (Sun et al., 2024).
1649
1650   In this section, we measure analyze the quality of the critiques produced by our judges and other
1651   explanation-generating baselines. To do so, we adopt the MetaCritique (Sun et al., 2024) framework.
1652   MetaCritique evaluates critiques in a question-answer setup: Judge models are provided with a user
1653
       question, a model response, and asked to determine if the response is correct or not, along with a
       critique of the response. Critiques are evaluated along two axes: (1) factuality and (2) completeness
1654
       (compared to a critique generated by GPT-4). To do so, atomic information units (AIUs), or simple
1655
       true/false statements, are generated via GPT-4 given the user question, model response, and judge
1656   critique. The critique is then judged based on how many AIUs it has correctly satisfied. For example,
1657   an example of a generated AIU is “The model-generated answer is incorrect and irrelevant to the
1658   input question,” and the critique is checked to see if it identifies the model response as incorrect.
1659
       To measure factuality, AIUs are extracted from judge critiques, then GPT-4 is used to determine if the
1660
       critique satisfies each AIU, with the Meta-Precision metric measuring the fraction of AIUs satisfied.
1661
       To measure completeness, AIUs are extracted from a reference critique produced by GPT-4, and
1662   GPT-4 is once again used to determine if the judge-generated critique satisfies each reference AIU.
1663   The Meta-Recall metric measures the fraction of reference AIUs satisfied. To aggregate both scores,
1664   Meta-F1 score is computed by taking the harmonic mean of Meta-Precision and Meta-Recall, and
1665   serves as an aggregate measure of critique quality.
1666
       Because of the question-and-answer (Q&A) nature of the evaluation, we prompt our models to con-
1667
       duct classification evaluation, where we present the judge with the Q&A pair and ask the model to
1668   produce a critique and a binary yes/no label for correctness. We additionally evaluate Self-taught-
1669   evaluator-Llama-3.1-70B and Themis-8B. For Self-taught-evaluator, we prompt the judge to per-
1670   form the same binary classification task as our judge models. For Themis, we prompt the judge to
1671   perform single rating evaluation (rate the response based on the user’s question) and classification,
1672   and report both results. While the classification approach is more natural for this setting, Themis
1673   was trained exclusively to perform single rating evaluation, and as such, we experiment with both.
       We report performance in Table 11, using reported numbers from the MetaCritique leaderboard for
