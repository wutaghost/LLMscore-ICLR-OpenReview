011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have been effectively used for many computer
014            vision tasks, including image classification. In this paper, we present a simple
015
               yet effective approach for zero-shot image classification using multimodal LLMs.
               By employing multimodal LLMs, we generate comprehensive textual representa-
016
               tions from input images. These textual representations are then utilized to gener-
017
               ate fixed-dimensional features in a cross-modal embedding space. Subsequently,
018            these features are fused together to perform zero-shot classification using a lin-
019            ear classifier. Our method does not require prompt engineering for each dataset;
020            instead, we use a single, straightforward, set of prompts across all datasets. We
021            evaluated our method on several datasets, and our results demonstrate its remark-
022            able effectiveness, surpassing benchmark accuracy on multiple datasets. On aver-
023            age over ten benchmarks, our method achieved an accuracy gain of 4.1 percentage
024            points, with an increase of 6.8 percentage points on the ImageNet dataset, com-
025            pared to prior methods. Our findings highlight the potential of multimodal LLMs
026
               to enhance computer vision tasks such as zero-shot image classification, offering
               a significant improvement over traditional methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Zero-shot image classification aims to categorize images into classes unseen during training, pre-
032   senting a significant challenge in computer vision. Recent approaches leverage the power of large
033   language models (LLMs) like GPT-4 (Brown et al. (2020)) to generate prompts for target classes,
034   often in conjunction with vision-language models such as CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)) to embed
035   images and text in a common space. Open-vocabulary models like CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)) and
036   VirTex (Desai & Johnson (2021)) have shown promise in this area due to their ability to generalize
037   to unseen classes. These models learn to match images with captions from vast amounts of image-
038   text data, allowing for dynamic classification without retraining. Early works like DeViSE (Frome
039
      et al. (2013)) pioneered the concept of a joint embedding space for images and text, enabling gen-
      eralization to unseen classes. Approaches like CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)), based on contrastive
040
      learning, and ALIGN (Jia et al. (2021)), employing a two-stage framework, further refined the align-
041
      ment of image and text representations. More recent approaches for zero-shot image classification
042   (e.g., Pratt et al. (2023); Menon & Vondrick (2023)) have utilized LLMs to generate prompts (i.e.,
043   captions or descriptions) for the target classes to further improve the classification accuracy.
044
045
      However, relying solely on visual features of the input images during inference can limit accuracy,
      as these features may not be sufficient to fully capture the nuances present in textual descriptions.
046
      To address this, we propose a novel method that leverages the capabilities of multimodal LLMs to
047
      generate rich textual representations of the input images. Multimodal LLMs, such as GPT-4 (Brown
048   et al. (2020)) and Gemini (Gemini Team Google (2023)), have demonstrated remarkable abilities
049   in various tasks. They are capable of processing and integrating information from various sources
050   like text, images, and audio. This allows them to perform tasks that were previously impossible,
051   such as generating detailed image descriptions, answering complex visual questions, and even cre-
052   ating realistic images from text. Inspired by these advancements, we utilize a straightforward set
053   of prompts to generate detailed textual descriptions of the input images, eliminating the need for
      complex prompt engineering seen in previous works (e.g., Radford et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2023)).

089   2       M ETHOD
090
091   Given an input image, X, containing object(s) belonging to a single class label from a finite set of
092   class labels, {li }mi=1 , our objective is to classify X without any dataset-specific training process for
093   image classification. The overview of our method is illustrated in Figure 2. As shown in Figure 2, our
094   approach relies on a cross-modal embedding encoder models (image encoder, fi , and text encoder,
095   ft ), trained to learn joint representations of images and text, as demonstrated in prior works, such as
096   Radford et al. (2021); Frome et al. (2013); Jia et al. (2021). Additionally, we utilize a multimodal
097   LLM, g, which is pre-trained on a large corpus of multimodal data. This model, g, is designed to
098   generate responses that align with both textual and visual inputs, effectively integrating information
099
      from both modalities for enhanced predictions (e.g., Gemini Team Google (2023)).

240   3     E XPERIMENTS
241
242   In our experiments, we employed Gemini Pro (Gemini Team Google (2023)) as our multimodal
243   LLM, g, for generating image descriptions and initial predictions. We utilized CLIP (ViT-L/14)
244   (Radford et al. (2021)) as our cross-modal embedding encoder models, fi and ft , to encode input
245   testing images, image descriptions, and initial predictions generated by Gemini Pro.
246
      We explored four different versions of class label features, M, in Equation 1. Specifically, we
247
      used CLIP to encode the following representations of class labels: 1) class label names, 2) the
248   text template ‘‘A photo of {class label}’’, where {class label} denotes each class
249   label in each dataset, and 3) class descriptions, similar to those generated by CuPL (Pratt et al.
250   (2023)), and 4) a combination of the aforementioned three options—akin to the fusion of our input
251   features, we combined the three encoded features of each class and computed their average feature.
252   The class descriptions were produced by prompting Gemini Pro to describe each class label in the
253   dataset 50 times, resulting in 50 different class descriptions for each class. Subsequently, we utilized
254   CLIP to encode the 50 class descriptions for each class and compute the average encoded feature
255   vector to represent each class label. The class description features are generated once as described
256
      in Section 2. The exact prompts used to generate image descriptions, initial predictions, and class
      description are detailed in Appendix A.

451   5     L IMITATIONS
452
453   Our method introduces a new approach by leveraging multimodal LLMs to enhance the accuracy
454   of zero-shot image classification. However, it is important to acknowledge that there are still some
455   limitations inherent in our proposed method. Since our method relies on multiple queries to a mul-
456   timodal LLM to generate the required features (i.e., DF and PF), there may be potential constraints
457
      when running on devices with limited computational power, and it may consume more time com-
      pared to other methods. Nevertheless, we believe that advancements in LLMs will lead to models
458
      that can run efficiently on lower computational power. This would enable broader accessibility and
459
      applicability of such models, such as Gemini (Gemini Team Google (2023)), GPT (Brown et al.
460   (2020)), and LLaMA (Touvron et al. (2023)).
461
462   Our method fails in some cases. Figure 6 shows examples of failure cases, where our method
463
      misclassify the input image. While the initial prediction and image description features generally
      enhance classification accuracy, as demonstrated in Table 2, they can sometimes lead to misclassi-
464
      fications. In the first example in Figure 6, both the image description and initial prediction suggest
465
      that the image show a ‘lobster’, whereas it actually shows a ‘crayfish’. Similarly, in the
466   second example, the image description lacks specific features of the cat, while the initial prediction
467   suggests the ‘Bengal’ class label, whereas the actual class label is ‘Egyptian Mau’.

470   6     C ONCLUSION
471
472   In this work, we introduced a zero-shot image classification method that relies on multimodal large
473   language models (LLMs). Our approach involves using a multimodal LLM to describe the input
474
      testing image and make an initial class prediction based on input testing image and the target class
      label names. Subsequently, we fuse the encoded features of the image description, initial LLM’s
475
      prediction, and input testing image to retrieve similar encoded features to class labels from the target
476
      dataset. Our method is straightforward and easy to implement, resulting in significant improvements
477   in zero-shot classification accuracy when compared with prior methods in this domain.

706    6   def create_classifer(class_names, k=50):
       7     ’’’Constructs zero-shot image classifier.
707    8     Args:
708    9       class_names: A list of class names.
      10       k: Number of class descriptions to be generated by the LLM.
709   11     Returns:
710   12       A zero-shot image classification model.
      13     ’’’
711   14     assert k >= len(class_ps)
712   15     assert k % len(class_ps) == 0
      16     weights = []
713   17     for class_name in class_names:
714   18       class_name_feature = encoder.encode_text(class_name)
      19       template_feature = encoder.encode_text(f"A photo of {class_name}")
715   20       llm_class_description = tf.zeros((1, encoder.output_feature_length))
716   21       for _ in range(k // len(class_ps)):
      22         for class_p in class_ps:
717   23           llm_class_feature = llm.process(class_p.format(class_name), temperature=0.99)
718   24           llm_class_description += encoder.encode_text(llm_class_feature)
      25       llm_class_description /= k
719   26       class_feature = class_name_feature + template_feature + llm_class_description
720   27       normalized_class_feature = class_feature / tf.norm(class_feature)
      28       weights.append(tf.squeeze(normalized_class_feature))
721   29     model = {"weights": tf.transpose(tf.convert_to_tensor(weights)),
722   30              "class_names": class_names}
      31     return model

724   33
      34   def classify(image, classifier):
725   35     ’’’Performs zero-shot image classification.
726   36     Args:
      37       image: Input testing image.
727   38       classifier: A zero-shot classification model generated by create_classifier function.
728   39     Returns:
      40       Predicted class name.
729   41     ’’’
730   42     image_feature = encoder.encode_image(image)
      43     image_feature /= tf.norm(image_feature)
731   44     initial_prediction = llm.process([classification_p, image], temperature=0)
732   45     prediction_feature = encoder.encode_text(initial_prediction)
      46     prediction_feature /= tf.norm(prediction_feature)
733   47     image_description = llm.process([description_p, image], temperature=0)
734   48     description_feature = encoder.encode_text(image_description)
      49     description_feature /= tf.norm(description_feature)
735   50     query_feature = image_feature + prediction_feature + description_feature
736   51     query_feature /= tf.norm(query_feature)
      52     index = tf.argmax(tf.linalg.matmul(query_feature, classifier["weights"]))
737   53     return classifier["class_names"][index.numpy().squeeze()]

789   Our work introduces a method for zero-shot image classification that leverages the power of mul-
790   timodal large language models (LLMs) not only during the classifier model construction phase but
791   also at inference time. We achieve this by generating comprehensive textual representations directly
792   from input images. These representations are then combined with the input images for classification,
793
      resulting in a significant enhancement in accuracy.
794   Importantly, our approach eliminates the need for dataset-specific prompt engineering, as commonly
795   required in prior approaches, thereby simplifying the implementation process and enhancing acces-
796   sibility – effectively acting as a plug-and-play solution. By removing the requirement for dataset-
797   specific customization, our method offers a straightforward and user-friendly approach to zero-shot
798   image classification, making it more accessible to a broader range of users.
799   By demonstrating its effectiveness across diverse datasets,we illustrate the utility of our method
800   for robust and generalizable real-world computer vision systems reliant on image classification,
801   eliminating the need for dataset-specific training, tuning, or prompt engineering. This approach
802   holds promise for simplifying the deployment of image classification systems and advancing the
803   field of computer vision.
