008
009
010
               We propose M ODEL S WARMS, a collaborative search algorithm to adapt LLMs
               via swarm intelligence, the collective behavior guiding individual systems. Specif-
011
               ically, M ODEL S WARMS starts with a pool of LLM experts and a utility func-
012
               tion. Guided by the best-found checkpoints across models, diverse LLM experts
013            collaboratively move in the weight space and optimize a utility function repre-
014            senting model adaptation objectives. Compared to existing model composition
015            approaches, M ODEL S WARMS offers tuning-free model adaptation, works in low-
016            data regimes with as few as 200 examples, and does not require assumptions about
017            specific experts in the swarm or how they should be composed. Extensive exper-
018            iments demonstrate that M ODEL S WARMS could flexibly adapt LLM experts to
019            a single task, multi-task domains, reward models, as well as diverse human in-
020            terests, improving over 12 model composition baselines by up to 21.0% across
021
               tasks and contexts. Further analysis reveals that LLM experts discover previously
               unseen capabilities in initial checkpoints and that M ODEL S WARMS enable the
022
               weak-to-strong transition of experts through the collaborative search process.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026   Advancing beyond efforts to train a single, universal large language model (LLM) (Brown et al.,
027   2020; Gemini Team et al., 2023) that shares parameters across all languages and tasks, recent work
028   has increasingly recognized the importance of modularity through multi-LLM collaboration, where
029   diverse models interact and complement each other in various ways (Shen et al., 2024c; Feng et al.,
030   2024a; Chan et al., 2024; Du et al., 2024). For example, mixture-of-experts (MoE) relies on the rout-
031   ing of queries to various neural sub-components, leveraging the specialized expertise of one model
032   (Masoudnia & Ebrahimpour, 2014; Roller et al., 2021; Pfeiffer et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024). Rout-
033
      ing to domain-specific experts demonstrates great potential, while no new model/expert is produced
      in the MoE process. However, challenging real-world tasks often require flexible composition and
034
      adaptation to new domains and/or capabilities that go beyond the scope of an existing expert.
035
036   Two lines of work aim to extend multi-LLM collaboration beyond routing to compose and produce
037   new adapted models. 1) Learn-to-fuse designs trainable components to “glue” experts together into
038   a merged model, then fine-tunes the model with supervised objectives to produce compositional
039
      experts (Jiang et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024b; Bansal et al., 2024). These approaches often rely
      on large training sets to tune the learnable parts from scratch and hardly offer the modularity of
040
      seamlessly adding/removing experts. 2) Model arithmetic composes LLM experts by conducting
041
      arithmetic operations on model weights and/or token probabilities (Ilharco et al., 2023; Yu et al.,
042   2024; Yadav et al., 2024; Mavromatis et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). These approaches often come
043   with strong assumptions about the available experts and how the desired adaptation should be de-
044   composed (e.g., lion indoors = lion outdoors + (dog indoors - dog outdoors) (Ilharco et al., 2023)).
045   As such, a flexible approach that does not rely on excessive tuning data or strong assumptions about
046   existing models is crucial for adapting diverse LLM experts for wide-ranging purposes.
047
      To this end, we propose M ODEL S WARMS, where multiple LLM experts collaboratively search for
048   new adapted models in the weight space. Inspired by Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) (Kennedy
049   & Eberhart, 1995), M ODEL S WARMS views each LLM expert as a “particle” and defines LLM
050   adaptation as the collaborative movement of particles governed by a utility function representing an
051   adaptation objective. Specifically, to model the proactive search of LLMs instead of passive merg-
052   ing, each expert particle starts with a location (model weights) and a velocity (direction in the weight
053   space). The velocity is iteratively impacted by inertia (the tendency to keep current velocity), per-
      sonal best (the best-found location of a given particle, best/worst meaning the best/worst-performing

      M ODEL S WARMS achieves superior performance across four distinct LLM adaptation objectives:
074   • Single task: Optimizing over as few as 200 instances, M ODEL S WARMS outperforms 12 model
075     composition baselines by 13.3% across 9 datasets spanning knowledge, reasoning, and safety.
076
      • Multi-task domain: Jointly optimizing multiple tasks in medical, legal, scientific, and cultural
077
        domains, M ODEL S WARMS often produces Pareto-optimal experts than optimizing a single task.
078
079
      • Reward model: Optimizing reward model scores of general and conflicting preferences, M ODEL
        S WARMS offers steerable experts that outperform baselines by up to 14.6% in controllability.
080
081   • Human interest: On 16 topics evaluated by humans (e.g., electric vehicles and PhD applications),
082
        Model Swarms produces experts on par or better than existing models in 85% of cases.
083
      Empirical analyses reveal that the diversity of starting experts is crucial, models display emerging
084   capabilities not seen in initial checkpoints, and surprisingly, the best ending particle often did not
085   start as the best. M ODEL S WARMS could be accelerated with dropout-like strategies and seamlessly
086   extended to token probability arithmetic for experts with different model architectures. We envision
087   M ODEL S WARMS as a versatile framework to reimagine the potential of diverse open models.

089   2   M ETHODOLOGY
090
      We propose M ODEL S WARMS, a collaborative search algorithm to adapt LLM experts via swarm
091
      intelligence. We present an overview of M ODEL S WARMS in Figure 1 and Algorithm 1.
092
093   M ODEL S WARMS assumes the access to various LLM experts {xi }ni=1 , which could be full models
094   or LoRA adapters (Hu et al., 2022) fine-tuned on diverse tasks and domains publicly available on
095   model-sharing platforms (Wolf et al., 2019). It also requires a utility function f : x → R, mapping
096
      each expert onto a scalar value that should be optimized for model adaptation. Utility functions
      could be dataset performance, reward model scores, or human preferences (Section 3).
097
098   Inspired by Particle Swarm Optimization (Kennedy & Eberhart, 1995) and evolutionary algorithms
099   in general (Bäck & Schwefel, 1993), M ODEL S WARMS employs several terminologies:
100
      • Each LLM expert, or “particle” in the model swarm, has a location represented by model weights;
101
102   • Each particle has a velocity, a direction in the model weight space that should move towards next;
103   • Personal best pi : the best-found location of xi based on utility function f in its search history;
104   • Global best and worst g and gw : the best/worst location in all of {xi }ni=1 ’s search history.
105
106   The location and velocity of particles enable the proactive search of LLM experts instead of passive
107   merging, while the personal/global best checkpoints help keep track of good locations and neigh-
      borhoods in the weight space to further explore.

133   Step 0. Initialize To expand the pool of starting experts/particles {xi }ni=1 , M ODEL S WARMS
134   employs pairwise crossover with linear interpolation. Concretely, we randomly select two ex-
135
      perts xa and xb from {xi }ni=1 and sample t ∼ U(0, 1), a new starting particle is obtained by
136
      xnew = txa + (1 − t)xb . Repeat this process for N − n times to expand {xi }ni=1 into {xi }N    i=1 .
      Expanding the starting particles allows for more trial-and-error bandwidth in the search process.
137
138   For each particle xi , we initialize its velocity as pointing to a random particle vi =
                                ∗
139   random({xj }N j=1 ) − xi . We initialize its personal best as its current location pi = xi and de-
140   termine the global best/worst as g = arg maxx f (x) and gw = arg minx f (x), x ∈ {xi }ni=1 .
141
142   Step 1. Velocity Update The movement of LLM experts is mainly governed by velocity v, direc-
143   tions in the weight space. We posit that the weight neighborhoods of good model checkpoints might
144   be promising to explore (Eilertsen et al., 2020), thus the velocity of particles vi is iteratively drawn
145
      by personal best pi , global best g, and repelled by global worst gw . Concretely:
146                         1
                    vi ←       rv φv vi + rp φp (pi − xi ) + rg φg (g − xi ) − rw φw (gw − xi )
147                         C
148   where C = rv φv + rp φp + rg φg + rw φw is a normalization term. To dissect this formula:
149
150   • The new velocity is the weighted average of four factors: vi , the particle keeps some of its current
151     velocity (i.e. inertia); (pi − xi ), it is drawn towards its personal best; (g − xi ), drawn towards
152
        the global best; −(gw − xi ), repelled from the global worst. Inertia enables each expert to chart
        an independent search path, personal/global best terms encourage experts to explore good weight
153
        neighborhoods, while the global worst term repels experts to stay clear of bad model checkpoints.
154
155   • Hyperparameters – inertia φv , cognitive coefficient φp , social coefficient φg , repel coefficient φw ,
156
        all ∈ [0, 1] – are configurable and govern how much the search process is impacted by pi , g, and
        gw . In particular, inertia φv has a unique control over exploration, where lower φv means more
157
        exploration (less impacted by current velocity and more by other models) and vice versa.
158
159   • Walk randomness factors rv , rp , rg , rw ∼ U(0, 1) ensure that the search is not deterministic,
160
        boosting particle exploration and are crucial in the collaborative search process (Table 6).

      Step 2. Weight Update Based on velocity v, the weights/locations of LLM experts are updated
163   by taking a step towards v: xi ← xi + λvi , where λ is the step length hyperparameter. The updated
164   particles are then evaluated on the utility function f to update g, gw , and {pi }N
                                                                                        i=1 , if necessary.
165
166
      Since M ODEL S WARMS explicitly encourage randomness and exploration, particles might some-
      times fail to find desirable locations and stray away: this exploration is made possible by randomness
167
      factors rv , rp , rg , rw ∼ U(0, 1), where the impact of personal/global bests are randomly discounted
168
      to favor exploration rather than overly quick convergence. We propose to restart undesirable parti-
169   cles and give them another chance: concretely, if for particle i the personal best pi didn’t change
170   in cr iterations, where cr is a hyperparameter, we put the particle back to its personal-best location
171   with xi ← pi and vi ← 0, essentially granting the particle another chance with a relatively good
172   starting point. In this way, M ODEL S WARMS strikes a balance between exploration and robustness.
173
174   Step 3. End of Iteration If the global best g hasn’t changed in c iterations (patience hyperparam-
175   eter) or the maximum iteration of K is achieved, the search process ends. Otherwise the step length
176   λ is reduced by a hyperparameter factor φλ , λ ← λ × φλ , and goes back to step 1. In the end, the
177   global best expert g is returned as the product of M ODEL S WARMS.

180   Models and Implementation We implement a prototype of M ODEL S WARMS with G EMMA -
181   7B (google/gemma-7b-it) (Gemma Team et al., 2024) in the main paper, while we also employ
182
      other LLMs such as M ISTRAL -7B (Jiang et al., 2023a) in Table 8. We create a pool of 10 initial
      experts/particles by fine-tuning G EMMA -7B separately on the 10 SFT data domains‡ in Tulu-v2
183
      (Ivison et al., 2023) with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022). We fine-tune for 5 epochs with a starting learning
184
      rate of 2e-4 and effective batch size of 32 by default. For M ODEL S WARMS searches, we employ
185   N = 20, φλ = 0.95, p = 10, pr = 5, K = 50, while running grid search over other hyperparameters
186   and report the best-found expert based on utility function f .
187
188   Baselines We compare with 12 model composition baselines in three categories.
189
190   • Trivial composition, 1) Best Single expert, essentially arg maxx f (x) for x ∈ {xi }ni=1 ; 2) Data
191
        Merge, where the 10 SFT data domains in Tulu-v2 are merged to train one single expert; 3) Pre-
        diction Merge, where the predictions of {xi }ni=1 are ensembled via plurality vote (if applicable).
192
193   • Static composition, where the composed expert is independent of the adaptation task/utility func-
194     tion f . We evaluate Uniform Soup (Wortsman et al., 2022a), Slerp, Dare-Ties (Yu et al., 2024;
195
        Yadav et al., 2024), and Model Stocks (Jang et al., 2024).
196   • Dynamic composition, where the composed expert changes based on the utility function f . We
197     evaluate Greedy Soup (Wortsman et al., 2022a), Pack of LLMs (Mavromatis et al., 2024), cBTM
198
        (Gururangan et al., 2023), EvolMerge (Akiba et al., 2024), and LoraHub (Huang et al., 2023).
        These approaches are also guided by the utility function f like M ODEL S WARMS.

      Data and Evaluation We investigate whether M ODEL S WARMS could adapt LLM experts via
201
      collaborative search on four types of adaptation objectives and the corresponding utility functions.
202
203   • Single task: we employ 9 datasets spanning knowledge (MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021),
204     MMLU-pro (Wang et al., 2024e), Hellaswag (Zellers et al., 2019)), reasoning (GSM8k (Cobbe
205     et al., 2021), Knowledge Crosswords (Ding et al., 2024), NLGraph (Wang et al., 2024a; Zhang
206     et al., 2024b)), and safety (TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022), RealToxicityPrompts (Gehman et al.,
207     2020), AbstainQA (Feng et al., 2024a)). We by default randomly sample 200 and 1000 samples
208
        as the validation/test sets: the utility function f is defined as performance on the validation set.
209   • Multi-task domain: in addition to optimizing for one task, models should also be adaptable to
210     an application domain comprising of multiple tasks. We employ 4 such domains and 2 tasks in
211
        each domain, specifically medical (MedQA (Jin et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024b) and MedMCQA
        (Pal et al., 2022)), legal (hearsay and citation prediction classification in LegalBench (Guha et al.,
212
        2024)), scientific (SciFact (Wadden et al., 2020) and the STEM subset of MMLU-pro (Wang et al.,
213
        2024e)), and culture (the country-based and value-based subtasks of Normad (Rao et al., 2024)).
214     The utility function f is defined as the harmonic mean of performance on the two tasks.

494
495   Composing Diverse LLM Experts In addition to developing gargantuan general-purpose LLMs,
496   increasing research focus on the composition of multiple models. Mixture-of-experts (MoE) models
497   (Jiang et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024) and methods (Roller et al., 2021; Lewis et al., 2021; Kudugunta
498
      et al., 2021; Pfeiffer et al., 2022; Du et al., 2022; Gururangan et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2024b) are
      one of the most noted paradigms in composing models, where different approaches vary on parallel
499
      neural components (Zhang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), routing mechanisms (Zhou et al., 2022;
500
      Dai et al., 2022), and expert partition (Gururangan et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2023). More recently,
501   learn-to-fuse approaches propose to “glue” experts together with trainable modules (Bansal et al.,
502   2024), adapters (Wang et al., 2024b), or even LLMs (Jiang et al., 2023b): these approaches often
503   need substantial supervised data and might not be modular to seamlessly add/remove experts. In
504   addition, static model arithmetic approaches propose to compose experts by performing arithmetic
505   on model weights and token probabilities to reconcile sign differences (Yu et al., 2024; Yadav et al.,
506   2024), simulate tuning effects (Liu et al., 2024), and induce compositional capabilities (Ilharco et al.,
507   2023), and more (Davari & Belilovsky, 2023; Jang et al., 2024; Deep et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
508   2024). In comparison, dynamic model arithmetic proposes to merge models guided by an objective
509
      function, employing perplexity heuristics (Mavromatis et al., 2024), evolutionary methods (Akiba
      et al., 2024), and more (Wortsman et al., 2022a; Huang et al., 2023; Gururangan et al., 2023). Most
510
      of these model arithmetic approaches often rely on strong assumptions about the experts how they
511
      should be composed (e.g. lion indoors = lion outdoors + (dog indoors - dog outdoors) (Ilharco et al.,
512   2023)). In contrast, M ODEL S WARMS presents a modular, assumption-free, and flexible approach to
513   compose and adapt diverse LLM experts guided by as few as 200 data instances.
514
515
      Evolutionary Algorithms and LLMs M ODEL S WARMS is in part inspired by particle swarm
516
      optimization (PSO) (Kennedy & Eberhart, 1995), an evolutionary algorithm (EA) solving optimiza-
517   tion problems. This echoes a recent and contemporary uptake of EAs, especially genetic algorithms
518   (GAs) in ML/LLMs (Zhao et al., 2023; Lange et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2024; Lange
519   et al., 2024). EvolMerge (Akiba et al., 2024) seeks to compose a math LLM and a Japanese LLM
520   through discovering better weight/layer and data flows guided by genetic algorithms. PromptBreeder
521   (Fernando et al., 2024) seeks to search for specialized LLM prompts by maintaining a prompt pop-
522   ulation and conducting LLM-based crossover and mutation to produce better prompts, resembling
523   GA processes. EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2024a) also follows similar concepts of applying GAs for
524   prompt optimization. We see two key differences between M ODEL S WARMS and this line of ex-
525
      isting research: most methods focus on improvements in prompt/data engineering (Fernando et al.,
      2024; Guo et al., 2024a), while M ODEL S WARMS seek to adapt LLMs by changing model weights
526
      and inducing new expert capabilities (Figure 2), which is more fundamental and offers greater head-
527
      room for improvement; existing EA applications mostly employed genetic algorithms that necessi-
528   tate much hand-crafted rules (Lambora et al., 2019) (how should two prompts/models crossover to
529   produce new ones, how to mutate, etc.), while M ODEL S WARMS is inspired by swarm intelligence
530   that come with little to no manual engineering in the composition and collaboration of models.

537   explore to optimize utility functions representing various adaptation objectives. Extensive exper-
538
      iments demonstrate that M ODEL S WARMS outperforms three categories of 12 model composition
      baselines by up to 21.0% across four types of model adaptation. Further analysis reveals that M ODEL
539
      S WARMS help discover new skills in the collaborative search process and bring out the best and im-
      plicit expertise of weak models for weak-to-strong expert transition.

      L IMITATIONS AND E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      M ODEL S WARMS assumes access to a pool of initial experts for collaborative search to adapt lan-
543   guage models. On one hand, it might be challenging to select the right pool of LLMs while we
544   present evidence that the diversity of initial experts is crucial to M ODEL S WARMS’ successes (Fig-
545   ure 4); On another hand, M ODEL S WARMS require the update of all experts at each iteration, which
546   might be computationally challenging. We provide time/space complexity analysis in Appendix
547   B and present a preliminary dropout-like acceleration scheme in Figure 9. M ODEL S WARMS is
548   uniquely suited to low-data contexts where only a few hundred examples are readily available to
549   serve as the utility function f .
550   M ODEL S WARMS aims to adapt language models based on their existing expertise rather than mem-
551   orizing new information that was never seen in the training of these experts. While theoretically by
552   changing model weights experts could pick up new information, our preliminary experiments with
553   perplexity as the utility function, a proxy for memorization, indicates that M ODEL S WARMS could
554   not reliably optimize perplexity. We envision that temporal updates could be enabled by employing
555   retrieval augmentation (Chen et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023c; Shi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024f)
556   over unseen documents in conjunction with M ODEL S WARMS.
557   M ODEL S WARMS by default operate on the model weight space, enabling the collaborative search
558   and movement of LLM experts in terms of model parameters. While this paradigm is incompati-
559   ble with a pool of experts with heterogeneous model architectures, we propose token swarms and
560   demonstrate its preliminary success in Figure 5. We highlight the trade-off between weight swarms
561   and token swarms: weight swarms induces more fundamental change of model capabilities through
562   weight changes, but it would require all experts to share the same architecture; token swarms is
563
      much more flexible in expert architectures, but only changes the composition of token probabili-
      ties without touching on the model’s parametric capabilities. We expect a full implementation and
564
      adaptations to the token swarms variant as important future work.
565
566   Unsuccessful M ODEL S WARMS searches might be confined to a local minimum without broad
567   exploration of the desirable weight space. While 1) we take several measures in Algorithm 1 to
568   mitigate this (random starting velocity, walk randomness factors, etc.), 2) we observe strong em-
569
      pirical performance of M ODEL S WARMS and consistent improvement to the global best g, and
      3) we visualize the movement of particles in Figure 8 demonstrating its convergence quality, one
570
      way to mitigate this concern is by annealing/adding noise to go beyond the local search: chang-
571
      ing rv , rp , rg , rw ∼ U(0, 1) to rv , rp , rg , rw ∼ U(−0.2, 1) so that models have a small chance of
572   moving towards the reverse direction and potentially jump out of local minimums.
573
574   We would like to highlight the dual-use risk of M ODEL S WARMS: thanks to its flexible adaptation
575
      strategy by using a model-to-scalar utility function f , it also leads to malicious use cases by having
      malicious f s. Some examples could include optimizing the reverse reward model scores, optimizing
576
      for lower scores on RealToxicityPrompts (Gehman et al., 2020), optimizing for certain social and
577
      political biases (Feng et al., 2023), and more. We argue for the responsible use of the M ODEL
578   S WARMS methodology as well as the responsible release of adapted experts.

       A    D ISCUSSION
1027
1028
       Three Key Strengths of M ODEL S WARMS 1) training-free: by training-free we mean that the
1029   composition of models in M ODEL S WARMS doesn’t require specific training objectives, loss func-
1030   tion, gradient descent, or back propagation. This alleviates data dependency: by using as few as 200
1031   examples M ODEL S WARMS could produce better adapted experts, while that is only a bit over 3
1032   batches for training-based approaches with a typical effective batch size of 64. 2) automatic dis-
1033   covery or assumption-free: instead of dictating the composition of models in A=B+C-D formulas,
1034   M ODEL S WARMS automatically discover better adapted experts through swarm intelligence without
1035   making assumptions about experts and how they should be composed. 3) any adaptation objective:
1036   the collaborative search is only guided by a particle-to-scalar utility function f which could be any
1037
       thing: dataset performance, reward model scores, human interests, and more.
1038
1039
       M ODEL S WARMS and Optimization Research M ODEL S WARMS is in part inspired by particle
       swarm optimization, one algorithm in the very rich literature of optimization research. We don’t
1040
       claim that PSO is the only and best applicable algorithm in the modern LLM world: on the contrary,
1041
       we invite follow-up works that critically examine how classic optimization techniques, especially
1042   for non-convex problems without strong guarantees, could be revived in today’s context.
1043
1044   Non-Neural Reward Models In Figure 4 we demonstrate that M ODEL S WARMS could adapt to
1045   preferences represented by neural reward models. However, any model-to-scalar utility function f
1046   could work and non-neural RMs are definitely possible: optimizing engagement in social media
1047   posts, optimizing click-through rates in online ads, optimizing charity donations when advertising
1048   a righteous case. We see many positive (and also negative) possibilities when employing M ODEL
1049   S WARMS in conjunction with non-neural RMs in social-economic contexts.
1050
1051   Long vs. Short In Figure 4 we demonstrate that M ODEL S WARMS could steerably adapt to either
1052   verbose RM or concise RM, offering use agency and controllability in model behavior. We discuss
1053   the distinctions with two other potential solutions: 1) setting max new tokens, which might result in
1054
       cutoffs in generated texts; 2) penalizing [EOS] tokens, which might tamper with token probabilities
       and harm generation quality. For a more on-the-fly steerability, we suggest to separately conduct
1055
       M ODEL S WARMS for two conflicting objectives, then employ an interpolation of the two models
1056
       with a user-controlled scaler from 0 to 1.
1057
1058
       Resilience to Malicious Experts There is discussion in multi-agent research about the impact of
1059   malicious agents (Huang et al., 2024). However, M ODEL S WARMS is robust to malicious experts
1060   since the only time a model has influence on others is when it becomes the global best g, while an
1061   intentionally “bad” model has no chance of becoming g on the “good” utility function f .
1062
1063   M ODEL S WARMS and Multi-Agent Systems The role of all “experts” in M ODEL S WARMS is
1064   homogeneous, i.e. they pursue the same goal/adapt to the same objective as represented by utility
1065   function f . In multi-agent systems (Rame et al., 2022; Zaman et al., 2023; Ainsworth et al., 2023;
1066   Chan et al., 2024; Talebirad & Nadiri, 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a; Abdelnabi
1067   et al., 2024; Kannan et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024b; Sun et al., 2024; Han et al.,
1068   2024; Ishibashi & Nishimura, 2024; Wang et al., 2024d; Zhao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c;
1069
       Hong et al., 2024; Smit et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a;b), the agents often have different roles
       to jointly complete a task, albeit those roles are more or less hand-crafted and especially through
1070
       prompting. We envision future work on adapting M ODEL S WARMS and automatically discovering
1071
       heterogeneous and collaborative agents that jointly serve a purpose.

1074   man et al., 2022b; Davari & Belilovsky, 2023; Deep et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024;
1075   Rame et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Ramé et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Tang et al., 2024; Wang
1076   et al., 2024c; Du & Kaelbling, 2024): searching in the sense that models are proactively moving
1077   in the search space for better experts instead of passively being squashed together, merging in the
1078   sense that each resulting model is implicitly an expert taking input from other models and changing
1079   its weights accordingly. Contrary to the often “many-to-one” paradigm in model merging research
       where there is only one merged model, M ODEL S WARMS is a “many-to-many” operation that yields

1301   Baseline Details We employ 12 baselines in total:
1302
1303   • Best single expert: among the 10 initial experts, the expert that performed best on utility function
1304     f is evaluated and reported.
1305   • Data merge: instead of separately training 10 initial experts, we combine the SFT data and train 1
1306     expert, then evaluate and report its performance.
1307   • Prediction merge: each initial expert generates a prediction, then the final answer is determined
1308     through majority vote. Note this is not applicable to open-ended generation tasks such as Real-
1309     ToxicityPrompts or tasks where the correct behavior vary across models such as AbstainQA.
1310
       • Uniform soup (Wortsman et al., 2022a): the weights of the 10 initial experts are uniformed aver-
1311     aged together into a new model.
1312
       • Slerp: spherical interpolation of the top-2 experts as evaluated by f based on the implementation
1313
         of Goddard et al. (2024) with default hyperparameters.
1314
1315   • Dare-ties: sparsifies task vectors to reduce interference (Yu et al., 2024) with the sign consensus
1316
         algorithm (Yadav et al., 2024) based on the implementation of Goddard et al. (2024). We run this
         algorithm on the top-2, top-3, top-4, or top-5 models as evaluated by f and employ the best-found
1317
         expert.
1318
1319   • Model stocks: employ geometric properties of models to determine linear interpolation weights
1320     (Jang et al., 2024). We run this algorithm on the top-3, top-4, or top-5 models as evaluated by f
1321
         and employ the best-found expert based on the implementation of Goddard et al. (2024).
1322   • Greedy soup: starting from the expert with the best f scores, iteratively add the next-best expert
1323     into the soup of uniform averaging, retains the added expert if the soup becomes better and discard
1324     if not, until every expert is considered (Wortsman et al., 2022a).
1325   • Pack of LLMs: the linear interpolation weights of models is decided by perplexity on inference
1326     queries (Mavromatis et al., 2024). We run a hyperparameter search for 200 times with temperature
1327     from 0.1, 0.2, to 1 and report the best-found expert by f .
1328   • cBTM: the ensemble weights of experts are decided by an embedding model’s embedding of
1329     inference query and expert training data (Gururangan et al., 2023). We employ RO BERTA - BASE
1330     as the embedding model to fuse the top-2, top-3, top-4, or top-5 expert and report the best-found
1331     expert.
1332   • EvolMerge: employing genetic algorithm to combine models based on data/layer flow engineering
1333     (Akiba et al., 2024). We run for 200 times randomly crossover the layers of the top-2 experts
1334     through linear interpolation to produce new models, while we keep a maximum population size
1335     of 50, retain 10 best-found at every iteration, a max iteration of 5 and report the best-found expert.
1336   • LoraHub: dynamic LoRA composition by employing genetic algorithm to optimize the linear
1337     interpolation weights of different LoRA modules (Huang et al., 2023). We run for 200 times by
1338     employing a population size of 50, 10 max iterations, α = 0 or α = 0.05, and report the best-
1339     found expert.
