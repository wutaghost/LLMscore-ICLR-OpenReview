011                                           Abstract
012
013           Black-box algorithms are designed to optimize functions without relying
014           on their underlying analytical structure or gradient information, making
015           them essential when gradients are inaccessible or difficult to compute. Tra-
016           ditional methods for solving black-box optimization (BBO) problems pre-
017           dominantly rely on non-parametric models and struggle to scale to large
018           input spaces. Conversely, parametric methods that model the function
019           with neural estimators and obtain gradient signals via backpropagation
020           may suffer from significant gradient errors. A recent alternative, Explicit
021
              Gradient Learning (EGL), which directly learns the gradient using a first-
              order Taylor approximation, has demonstrated superior performance over
022
              both parametric and non-parametric methods. In this work, we propose
023
              two novel gradient learning variants to address the robustness challenges
024           posed by high-dimensional, complex, and highly non-linear problems. Op-
025           timistic Gradient Learning (OGL) introduces a bias toward lower regions
026           in the function landscape, while Higher-order Gradient Learning (HGL)
027           incorporates second-order Taylor corrections to improve gradient accuracy.
028           We combine these approaches into the unified OHGL algorithm, achiev-
029           ing state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on the synthetic COCO suite.
030             Additionally, we demonstrate OHGL’s applicability to high-dimensional
031           real-world machine learning (ML) tasks such as adversarial training and
032
              code generation. Our results highlight OHGL’s ability to generate stronger
              candidates, offering a valuable tool for ML researchers and practitioners
033
              tackling high-dimensional, non-linear optimization challenges.

037   1   Introduction
038
039
      Black-box optimization (BBO) is the process of searching for optimal solutions within a
040
      system’s input domain without access to its internal structure or analytical properties Audet
041
      et al. (2017c). Unlike gradient-based optimization methods that rely on the calculation of
042   analytical gradients, BBO algorithms query the system solely through input-output pairs,
043   operating agnostically to the underlying function. This feature distinguishes BBO from
044   traditional ML tasks, such as neural network training, where optimization typically involves
045   backpropagation-based gradient computation.
046
      Many real-world physical systems naturally fit into the BBO framework because their ana-
047
      lytical behavior is difficult or impossible to model explicitly. In these cases, BBO algorithms
048   have achieved remarkable success in diverse fields, such as ambulance deployment Zhen et al.
049   (2014), robotic motor control Gehring et al. (2014); Prabhu et al. (2018), parameter tuning
050   Olof (2018); Rimon et al. (2024), and signal processing Liu et al. (2020), among others
051   Alarie et al. (2021). BBO applications extend beyond physical systems; many ML problems
052   exhibit a black-box nature when the true gradient is absent. Examples include hyperparam-
053   eter tuning Bischl et al. (2023), contextual bandit problems Bouneffouf et al. (2020), and
      large language model training with human feedback Bai et al. (2022), to name a few.

      As the scale of data continues to grow, the dimensionality of the problem space increases in
055   tandem. This trend is particularly evident in ML, where model architectures, embedding and
056   latent representation sizes, and the number of hyperparameters are continually expanding.
057   High-dimensional optimization challenges traditional BBO algorithms, which often require
058   the number of collected samples at each step, ns , to scale proportionally with the problem
059   size, N . Parametric neural models, however, have shown that reusing past samples can
060   effectively reduce the required sample size such that ns ≪ N Sarafian et al. (2020); Lu et al.
061   (2023). Building on this, we propose a sampling profiler that further reduces the number of
062   samples needed at each step while maintaining performance.
063   While problem dimensions increase, the cost of evaluating intermediate solutions remains a
064   critical constraint, especially in real-world settings where interaction with the environment is
065   expensive or in ML tasks where larger models counterbalance gains in computational power.
066   Therefore, modern BBO algorithms must not only reduce evaluation steps but also converge
067   more quickly Hansen et al. (2010). Achieving this requires algorithms capable of more
068   accurately predicting optimization directions, either through better gradient approximation
069
      Anil et al. (2020); Lesage-Landry et al. (2020) or momentum-based strategies to handle non-
      convexity and noise. In this paper, we propose two key improvements to Explicit Gradient
070
      Learning (EGL): (1) Optimistic Gradient Learning (OGL), a weighted gradient estimator
071
      that biases toward promising solutions and (2) Higher-Order Gradient Learning (HGL),
072   which incorporates Hessian corrections to yield more accurate gradient approximations.
073
074   We combine the strengths of OGL and HGL to a unified algorithm termed OHGL which
075
      exhibits four key advantages:
076
           • Robustness: OHGL consistently outperforms baseline algorithms across a diverse
077
             range of benchmark problems, including synthetic test suites and real-world ML
078          applications. Its ability to handle noisy and non-convex environments.
079
           • Gradient Precision: By integrating the second-order information via Hessian
080
             corrections, OHGL achieves significantly more accurate gradient approximations
081
             than standard EGL.
082
083
           • Convergence Rate: OHGL demonstrates faster convergence rate.
084        • Utilizing the sampling profiler and the optimistic approach, OGL is able to solve
085          high-dimensional problems with smaller budget and converge faster than
086          baseline algorithms.
087
      Related works: Black-box optimization (BBO) algorithms have a long history, with vari-
088
      ous approaches developed over the years. Some of the foundational techniques include grid
089
      search, coordinate search Audet et al. (2017e), simulated annealing Busetti (2003), and
090   direct search methods like Generalized Pattern Search and Mesh Adaptive direct search Au-
091   det et al. (2017a), Gradient-less descent Golovin et al. (2019), and ZOO Chen et al. (2017).
092   These approaches iteratively evaluate potential solutions and decide whether to continue
093   in the same direction. However, they resample for every step and don’t use the sampled
094   budget from previous iterations, wasting a lot of budget.
095
      Another prominent family of BBO algorithms is the genetic algorithm family Back (1996).
096   This includes methods such as Covariance Matrix Adaptation (CMA) Hansen (2016) and
097   Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) Clerc (2010). These algorithms simulate the process of
098   natural evolution, where a population of solutions evolves through mutation and selection
099   Audet et al. (2017b). They are considered state-of-the-art (SOTA) in optimization due
100   to their effectiveness in tackling complex problems. However, they come with significant
101   drawbacks, particularly the need for extensive fine-tuning of parameters like generation
102   size and mutation rates. CMA, for example, struggles in higher-dimensional environments
103   and requires careful adjustment of hyperparameters and guidance to perform optimally
104
      Loshchilov et al. (2013); Tang (2021). In this work, we propose a simpler method to enhance
      the performance of CMA, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
105
106   Then there are model-based methods Audet et al. (2017d), which attempt to emulate the
107   behavior of the function using a surrogate model. These models provide important analytical
      information, such as gradients Bertsekas (2015), to guide the optimization process and help

108
      find a minimum. Within this class, we can further distinguish two sub-classes. To address
109   the issue of dimensionality, Explicit Gradient Learning (EGL) was proposed by Sarafian
110   et al. (2020). While many model-based methods focus on learning the function’s structure to
111   derive analytical insights (e.g., Indirect Gradient Learning or IGL Lillicrap (2015); Sarafian
112   et al. (2020)), EGL directly learns the gradient information. EGL uses Taylor’s theorem to
113   estimate the gradient. The authors also emphasize the importance of utilizing a trust region
114   to handle black-box optimization problems. However, EGL has some drawbacks: it often
115   uses the available budget inefficiently, disregarding both the complexity and dimensionality
116   of the environment. Additionally, the datasets created by EGL can be naive, leading to
117
      over-fitting or improper network learning. This work tackles these issues by showing the
      importance of proper algorithm calibration and optimization.
118
119   Recent work also highlights the limitations of common assumptions in BBO algorithms,
120   such as continuity or Gaussian distributions of functions, which can hinder optimization.
121   For instance, OPT-GAN Tang (2021), a generative model, seeks to bypass these assumptions
122   by learning a function’s distribution and generating better candidate solutions based on that
123
      knowledge.
124   The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 covers the algorithm’s theoretical back-
125   ground and mathematical foundations. Sections 3 and 4 present our two enhanced variants
126   of the gradient learning algorithm: OGL and HGL, these are followed by section 5 where we
127   present the full algorithm OHGL. Section 6 provides experimental results on the synthetic
128   COCO test suite and Section 7 highlights 2 real-world high-dimensional applications and
129
      potential uses. Finally, section 8 concludes and suggests future research directions. Our
      code, experiments, and environment setup are available in the supplementary material.

      6.2    Hyperparameter Tolerance
379
380   In this part, we evaluate the robustness of our algorithm to hyperparameter tuning. Our
381   objective is to demonstrate that variations in key hyperparameters have minimal impact on
382   the algorithm’s overall performance. To assess this, we conducted systematic experiments,
383   modifying several hyperparameters and analyzing their effects. Table 2 (and Table 6 in the
384   appendix) reports the coefficient of variation (CV), defined as CV = σµ , across different
385   hyperparameter sweeps, highlighting the algorithm’s stability under varying conditions.
386   Our findings indicate that certain hyperparameters—such as the epsilon factor, shrink fac-
387   tor, and value normalizer learning rate (LR)—exhibit cumulative effects during training.
388   While small variations in these parameters may not have an immediate impact, their influ-
389   ence can accumulate over time, potentially leading to significant performance changes. In
390   A.11.2, we establish the relationship between the step size and the epsilon factor necessary
391   for ensuring progress toward a better optimal solution. When selecting these parameters,
392   this relationship must be considered. Additionally, the shrink factor for the trust region
393
      should be chosen relative to the budget, enabling the algorithm to explore the maximum
      number of sub-problems. This underscores the importance of fine-tuning these parameters
394
      for optimal results. Conversely, we found that the structural configuration of the neural
395
      network, including the number and size of layers, had minimal effect on performance. This
396   suggests that the algorithm’s reliance on Taylor loss enables effective learning even with
397   relatively simple network architectures, implying that increasing model complexity does not
398   necessarily yield substantial improvements.

      7.1    Adversarial Attacks
409
410   As powerful vision models like ResNet Targ et al. (2016) and Vision Transformers (ViT)
411   Han et al. (2022) grow in prominence, adversarial attacks have become a significant con-
412   cern. These attacks subtly modify inputs, causing models to misclassify them, while the
413   perturbation remains imperceptible to both human vision and other classifiers Tang et al.
414   (2019). Formally, an adversarial attack is defined as:
415                                x∗a = arg min d(x, xa )   s.t.f (x) ̸= f (xa )                       (8)
416                                           x

417   Where f is the classifier and d is some distance metric between elements.
418
      Recent studies have extended adversarial attacks to domains like AI-text detection Sadasivan
419
      et al. (2024) and automotive sensors Mahima et al. (2024). These attacks prevent tracking
420   and detection, posing risks to both users and pedestrians. Adversarial attacks are classified
421   into black-box and white-box methods. Black-box attacks only require query access, while
422   white-box methods use model gradients to craft perturbations Machado et al. (2021); Cao
423   et al. (2019). Despite some black-box methods relying on surrogate models Dong et al.
424   (2018); Xiao et al. (2018); Madry et al. (2017); Goodfellow et al. (2014), approaches like
425   AutoZOOM Tu et al. (2019) generate random samples to approximate gradient estimation,
426   though they are computationally expensive. Other methods use GAN networks to search
427   latent spaces for adversarial examples Liu et al. (2021); Sarkar et al. (2017). Still, they
428
      depend on existing GANs and their latent space diversity.
429   Our Enhanced Gradient Learning method offers a true black-box approach with precise
430   perturbation control, avoiding gradient back-propagation. OGL directly optimizes pertur-
431   bations to maintain low distortion while fooling the model, handling high-dimensional spaces
      with over 30,000 parameters.

514   8    Conclusion
515
516   In this work, we introduced several key enhancements to the EGL algorithm, resulting
517   in OHGL, a powerful black-box optimization tool capable of addressing various complex
518   problems. We demonstrated improvements such as second-order gradient approximation,
519   optimistic gradients, and trust region enhancements, which collectively led to faster conver-
520   gence and more robust performance, particularly in high-dimensional and intricate tasks.
521   Our experiments showcased OHGL’s superiority over state-of-the-art methods, especially
522   in tasks where computational efficiency and precision are paramount, such as adversarial
523   attacks on vision models and optimizing code generation. OHGL’s ability to navigate com-
524   plex optimization spaces while minimizing computational cost demonstrates its utility across
525   various applications.
526   Looking ahead, Future research should explore new applications of black-box optimization
527   algorithms like OHGL. While OHGL has shown promise in high-dimensional tasks, its effi-
528   ciency could be further improved. Our research utilized dimension-reduction methods such
529   as Hu et al. (2021). Similar techniques (Zhao et al. (2024); Anil et al. (2020)) can help
530   calculate the Hessian more efficiently.
531
      In conclusion, OHGL represents a significant advancement in BBO algorithm design, pro-
532   viding a robust framework for solving complex optimization problems. However, the true
533   potential of BBO algorithms lies in their broader applicability, and future research should
534   focus on unlocking new avenues for their use, especially in generative models and other
535   cutting-edge areas.

810      def count_triplets(n):
811          """
         This function calculates the number of triplets (a, b, c) of positive integers such that:
812      1. a*b + a*c + b*c <= n
813      2. a + b + c <= x
         where the order of triplets matters (e.g., (1, 1, 2) and (1, 2, 1) are different triplets)
814      and a, b, c must be strictly greater than 0.
815          """
         count = 0
816      # Iterate over a
817      for a in range(1, x + 1):
             # Iterate over b
818          for b in range(1, x - a + 1):
819              # Find the maximum c based on the condition a + b + c <= x
                 max_c = min(x - a - b, (n - a * b) // (a + b)) # upper bound for c
820              # Use list comprehension to count valid triplets
821              count += sum(1 for c in range(1, max_c + 1) if a * b + a * c + b * c <= n)
822      return count

834

853   A.5   Trust Region Management
854
855
      After finding the optimal solution inside the trust region, EGL shifts and scales down (i.e.
856
      shrinks) the trust region so that the optimal solution is centered at its origin. In case the
857
      decent path is long so that the minimum point is far away from the current trust region,
858   this shrinking has the potential to slow down the learning rate and impede convergence.
859   To prevent unnecessary shrinking of the trust region, we distinguish between two conver-
860   gence types: interior convergence: in this case, we shrink the trust region while moving
861   its center and boundary convergence where we only shift the center without applying
862   shrinking (When the algorithm reaches the edges of the TR). This adjustment prevents
863   fast convergence of the step size to zero before being able to sufficiently explore the input
      domain.

904   A.8   CMA Versions
905
906   The trust region is key in the EGL algorithm (see Figures 1). We map between the search
907
      space (Ω) and trust region (Ω∗ ) using the tanh function, which provides smooth transi-
      tions but exhibits logarithmic behavior at the edges, slowing large steps. While helpful for
908
      problems with many local minima, this behavior restricts CMA’s ability to take large steps,
909
      especially in high dimensions (see Figure 14). We developed two CMA variants: one with
910   linear TR (L-CMA) and one with tanh TR(T-CMA). The linear TR allowed larger steps,
911   improving exploration, while the tanh TR limited performance.
