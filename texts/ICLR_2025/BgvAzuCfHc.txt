009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The Lennard-Jones potential, initially developed to model molecular interactions,
012            is characterized by a repulsive force at short distances to prevent over-clustering
013            and an attractive force at longer distances to maintain balanced proximity, resem-
014            bling the equilibrium-seeking behavior of particles in natural systems. This offers
015            a potential pathway for more orderly entropy reduction in higher-order features.
016            This paper introduces a self-supervised approach for feature re-representation, uti-
017            lizing a Lennard-Jones potential loss to constrain the gradient directions between
018            positive and negative features in computer vision tasks. Unlike supervised learn-
019            ing directly driven by downstream tasks or contrastive learning with multi-label
020
               data pairs and multi-feature extractors, the proposed loss term integrates with ex-
               isting task-specific losses by directly constraining gradient directions, thereby en-
021
               hancing the feature learning process. Extensive theoretical analysis and experi-
022
               mental results demonstrate that, across various domains, datasets, network archi-
023            tectures, and tasks, models incorporating the Lennard-Jones potential loss signif-
024            icantly outperform baseline models without this auxiliary loss in both accuracy
025            and robustness. This approach highlights the potential of physics-inspired loss
026            functions to improve deep learning optimization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   The second law of thermodynamics (Clausius, 1850) posits that in an isolated system, without exter-
033
      nal interference, the system will naturally progress toward a state of disorder and equilibrium. Simi-
      larly, in deep learning, loss functions act as external forces, guiding randomly initialized, disordered
034
      parameters toward a more organized and structured state, thereby enhancing model performance.
035
      Task-specific loss functions, like cross-entropy loss (Shannon, 1948), typically target the final-layer
036   features for downstream tasks. While effective at optimizing task performance, these loss func-
037   tions often overlook the structural refinement of the feature space, which is crucial for enhancing
038   generalization.
039
      Recently, contrastive loss functions (Hadsell et al., 2006) have improved feature space structure
040
      by maximizing similarity between similar samples and minimizing similarity between dissimilar
041
      ones. Notable examples include the single-modal dual-encoder model in MoCo (He et al., 2020b)
042   and multi-modal learning in CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). However, these methods tend to require
043   substantial additional resources, such as data augmentation, multiple feature extractors, or annotated
044   paired data, which complicate models and increase computational costs (Chen et al., 2020a; Grill
045   et al., 2020a).
046
      In parallel to how physical laws govern the transformation of systems from disorder to order, deep
047
      learning optimization aims to evolve chaotic initial features into a low-entropy ordered state. Early
048   research (Rackauckas et al., 2020; Karniadakis et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2018;
049   Rackauckas et al., 2020) explored this idea by integrating physical principles into the optimization
050   process. Energy-based (LeCun et al., 2006; Ngiam et al., 2011) and entropy-based (Jaynes, 1957;
051   Grandvalet & Bengio, 2005) methods, inspired by physics, offer alternative perspectives for optimiz-
052   ing feature spaces. Energy-based models cluster similar samples by minimizing energy states, while
053   entropy-based methods increase the entropy of feature distributions to avoid over-fitting. These
      physics-inspired approaches have shown promise in both self-supervised and unsupervised tasks by

      Building on this foundation, we propose Lennard-Jones (LJ) Loss, an auxiliary loss function aimed

      at optimizing the feature space. Inspired by the Lennard-Jones potential (Lennard-Jones, 1924) in
081   physics, LJ Loss employs a dual mechanism of ”attraction” and ”repulsion” within the feature space.
082   When semantic distances between samples are small, LJ Loss applies a repulsive force to avoid over-
083   clustering, while larger distances trigger an attractive force to reduce feature dispersion. As shown
084   in Figure 1, LJ Loss better balances intra-class compactness and inter-class separation compared
085   to traditional Euclidean distance-based methods, without adding inference overhead. Extensive ex-
086   periments on various computer vision tasks demonstrate substantial performance improvements,
087   especially in cases with small sample sizes and complex intra-class distributions. These findings
088   underscore the potential of LJ Loss in enhancing feature learning and optimization, and suggest
089
      promising future research directions. The main contributions of this paper are:
090          • Propose a gradient direction-constrained loss function based on the Lennard-Jones poten-
091            tial, optimizing feature distributions by regulating sample positions and gradient directions.
092
             • Demonstrate significant performance improvements across various computer vision tasks,
093            including image classification, multi-view learning, and semantic segmentation.
094
             • Analyze feature categories and limitations of LJLoss, providing theoretical insights and
095
               proofs to establish a new foundation for optimizing feature representations.
096
097   Through these contributions, we demonstrate that a gradient direction-constrained loss based on the
098   Lennard-Jones potential enhances deep learning model performance, promoting the integration of
099   physical models in the development of advanced loss functions.

103   2.1   C OMPUTER V ISION M ODELS
104
105   Deep learning models have made significant advancements in computer vision tasks (LeCun et al.,
106   1998; Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; He et al., 2016a; Tan & Le, 2019;
107   Dosovitskiy et al., 2021b; Liu et al., 2021), including image classification, object detection, and se-
      mantic segmentation, primarily by learning rich and discriminative feature representations. Classic


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      architectures like the ResNet (He et al., 2016b) and its variants utilize deep convolutional neural
109   networks (CNNs) to hierarchically extract features from images, aiming to distinguish between dif-
110   ferent classes (inter-class variation) while minimizing differences within the same class (intra-class
111   variation). These models, however, typically lack mechanisms to handle more subtle intra-class
112   variations — the differences between instances of the same class that are not necessarily captured
113   by maximizing inter-class variance alone. For example, in image and point cloud processing, mod-
114   els can effectively separate different object categories but may struggle with differentiating nuanced
115   intra-class differences, such as variations in pose, illumination, or style among objects of the same
116   category. This limitation suggests the need for more refined approaches that consider both inter-class
117
      and intra-class relationships within the feature space.
118
119   2.2   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
120
121   Contrastive learning has become a powerful approach for self-supervised representation learn-
122   ing (Chen et al., 2020b; He et al., 2020a; Grill et al., 2020b; Khosla et al., 2020), where the ob-
123   jective is to learn meaningful features without direct supervision by maximizing agreement between
124
      positive pairs and minimizing agreement between negative pairs. This method relies on explicitly
      defining what constitutes a ”positive” sample (typically, different augmented views of the same in-
125
      stance) and a ”negative” sample (different instances altogether), often requiring manual or heuristic-
126
      based labeling of these pairs. While contrastive learning excels at learning inter-class distinctions
127   by pulling positive samples closer together and pushing negative samples apart in the feature space,
128   it inherently lacks mechanisms to handle intra-class consistency.
129
130
      The fundamental limitation of contrastive learning lies in its focus on distinguishing samples based
      on pre-defined positive and negative pairs. This binary relationship does not inherently consider the
131
      nuanced variations that may exist within a single class, such as subtle differences in shape, texture, or
132
      style among samples that share the same label. Consequently, contrastive learning methods are not
133   designed to ensure that the features extracted within a single class maintain semantic coherence and
134   meaningful alignment. This gap limits their effectiveness in tasks that require a finer understanding
135   of intra-class structure and variability, especially when dealing with complex or highly variable data
136   distributions.

      2.3   A PPLICATIONS OF T HEORETICAL P HYSICS IN D EEP L EARNING
139
140
      In recent years, theoretical physics has inspired several innovative applications in deep learning,
141   providing new methods for model design and optimization. For example, concepts from statistical
142   mechanics, such as energy minimization, have been used to develop energy-based models that better
143   capture underlying data distributions and improve robustness against adversarial attacks. The Ising
144   model (Ising, 1925), commonly used in physics to describe ferromagnetism, has been adapted to
145   analyze and optimize neural networks, drawing parallels between the dependencies among neurons
146   and the interactions of magnetic spins. Additionally, ideas from quantum mechanics, such as the
147   Feynman path integral (Feynman, 1948), have influenced new approaches to probabilistic reasoning
148   and uncertainty estimation in machine learning. These applications demonstrate that insights from
149
      physics can offer fresh perspectives on deep learning, particularly in enhancing model robustness
      and interpretability. However, most of these applications do not directly address the structuring of
150
      feature spaces or the refinement of relationships between individual samples within those spaces,
151
      leaving room for new approaches that leverage physical principles in more targeted ways.

      3.2   G LOBAL AND L OCAL F EATURES D EFINITION
193
194   Global features refer to the comprehensive information extracted from all elements of an input sam-
195   ple, reflecting the overall characteristics of the sample. Unlike local features, global features are not
196   restricted to specific regions or subsets of the input. Instead, they encapsulate high-level informa-
197   tion derived from the entire sample, which can be obtained through various means, such as fully
198   connected layers, pooling operations, or attention mechanisms. In a more generalized form, global
199   features can be expressed as:
200
201                                     G = f (X) = g(x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ),
202
203   where X is the input sample, xi represents each element or region in the sample, and g(·) is a general
204   operation (such as pooling, attention, or a combination thereof) that aggregates information from the
205   entire input, with no assumption that it must be an average.
206   Local features, in contrast, focus on a subset of the input sample and are typically extracted by
207   earlier layers of a neural network. Convolution is a common method for generating local features
208   (see Appendix B), as its receptive field generally cannot cover the entire sample. For example, in
209   the case of convolutional layers, the extraction of local features can be described mathematically as:

256   4     M ETHODOLOGY OF LJ L OSS F UNCTION
257
258   Inspiring by the Lennard-Jones potential, the proposed loss function models molecular interactions,
259   to address limitations in existing contrastive learning approaches. Specifically, the LJ Loss func-
260   tion introduces both inter-class repulsive forces and intra-class attractive forces, promoting well-
261   separated inter-class features while ensuring compact intra-class distributions. Unlike contrastive
262
      learning, the LJ Loss function does not rely on predefined positive and negative pairs but instead
      constructs interaction forces based on the distances between sample representations.
263

476   6                      C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATION
477
478
      This paper theoretically and experimentally demonstrates the feasibility of applying physical laws
479
      to feature re-representation under known initial and final states. The improvements are reflected
480   in comprehensive performance, mitigating the impact of over-parameterization and constraining the
481   model’s tendency toward overfitting. Introducing the Lennard-Jones potential as a loss function does
482   not add significant burden to the model and often accelerates convergence, saving time. However,
483   its computational cost grows quadratically with the number of feature channels. Additionally, the
484   globality and parameter combinations, while theoretically sound, may impose overly strict or irrele-
485   vant physical constraints. Just as the Lennard-Jones equation is unsuitable for all systems, it cannot
      handle highly skewed potential wells. Future work will aim to address these limitations.

      A    W HY L ENNARD -J ONES P OTENTIAL OVER OTHER P HYSICAL E QUATIONS
649
650
      We selected the Lennard-Jones (LJ) potential over other physical equations because it effectively
651
      guides the feature space optimization toward the intended objective of balancing intra-class com-
652   pactness and inter-class separation. Other physical equations, such as Coulomb’s law (Coulomb,
653   1785) and gravitational models (Newton, 1687), are not suitable for achieving this goal due to their
654   inherent limitations in describing the relationships between features in a manner conducive to our
655   optimization process. Moreover, these alternatives introduce challenges in quantifying properties
656   like mass or charge, which do not have direct analogs in deep learning feature representations.
657
      For instance, Coulomb’s law is expressed as:
658
659
                                                           ke q1 q2
660                                                  F =
661                                                          r2
662   where q1 and q2 represent electric charges, and r is the distance between them. Coulomb’s force is
663   restricted to binary interactions—either attractive or repulsive, depending on the charge signs. In the
664   context of deep learning, feature representations do not have a natural equivalent to electric charge,
665   making it difficult to apply this equation in a meaningful way. Furthermore, Coulomb’s law, with its
666   inverse-square relationship, can result in extreme gradient magnitudes when applied to optimization,
667   leading to potential instability, such as gradient explosion or vanishing.
668   Similarly, the gravitational force equation:
669
670
                                                           Gm1 m2
671                                                  F =
672

673   where m1 and m2 denote masses, suffers from similar limitations. The gravitational force only
674   describes attraction between masses, lacking any repulsive counterpart. This makes it unsuitable
675   for controlling both intra-class compactness and inter-class separation, as required in feature space
676   optimization. Additionally, mass, like charge, is not a well-defined property in deep learning models,
677   complicating its application. The lack of repulsion in the gravitational model would result in features
678   clustering too tightly, which is undesirable for maintaining separation between distinct classes.
679   In contrast, the Lennard-Jones potential (Lennard-Jones, 1924) is expressed as:

689   we seek to maintain class separation while ensuring compact intra-class representations. Addition-

881   D.1    OVERFITTING : E XPLANATION AND D EFINITION
882
      Overfitting occurs when a machine learning model becomes overly complex, fitting noise and acci-
883
      dental patterns in the training data, which results in poor generalization performance. To understand
884
      the essence of overfitting, it is necessary to clarify the concept of hypothesis space.
885
886   The hypothesis space H is the set of all functions the model can choose from. Given a dataset
887   D = {(xi , yi )}Ni=1 , which is drawn from an unknown true distribution P (x, y), the goal of machine
888
      learning is to find a hypothesis h ∈ H that minimizes the loss over the true distribution:
889
890                                       L(h) = E(x,y)∼P [L(h(x), y)]
891
892   In practice, we only have access to a limited training dataset Dtrain , and thus the loss minimization
893
      is performed over this finite dataset:
894
                                                              N
895                                                      1 X
896

                                                         N i=1
897
898   When the hypothesis space H is too large or complex, the model may select a hypothesis hoverfit that
899   fits the training data perfectly but generalizes poorly. In this case, the training loss approaches zero:
900
901
                                                  Ltrain (hoverfit ) → 0
902
903   However, the test loss remains large:
904
905
                                           Ltest (hoverfit ) ≫ Ltrain (hoverfit )
906
907   The concept of overfitting can be further understood through the Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) dimen-
908   sion (Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 1971), which measures the capacity of a model by quantifying its
909   ability to shatter data points in any distribution. A higher VC dimension implies that the model has
910   more capacity to fit arbitrary patterns in the training data. When the model’s capacity, represented
911   by the VC dimension, exceeds the inherent complexity of the data distribution, the model tends to
912   overfit. Mathematically, the loss can be decomposed using the bias-variance trade-off:
913
914                                           E[L] = Bias2 + Var + σ 2
915
916   where Bias refers to the model’s error due to simplifying assumptions, Var is the model’s sensitivity
917   to fluctuations in the training set, and σ 2 represents the irreducible noise. In an overfitted model, the
      variance term dominates, leading to poor generalization performance.

      Overfitting also depends on the size of the dataset. On smaller datasets, a model may lack sufficient
919   data to capture the true distribution, leading to overfitting as the model fits the noise in the limited
920   samples. On the other hand, larger models on the same dataset can overfit because they have the
921   capacity to fit overly complex patterns (prior hypotheses) that may not exist in the data distribution.
