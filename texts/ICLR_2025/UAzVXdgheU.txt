011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Transferring the world knowledge encoded in pretrained models through knowl-
014            edge distillation is an effective approach to improve the performance of small,
015
               task-specific production models. However, the effectiveness of such knowledge
               transfer drops greatly for strong models that are pretrained in a large scale. In this
016
               paper, we explore methods to preprocess strong pretrained models to improve the
017
               effectiveness of its knowledge transfer. From a mutual information perspective
018            of distillation effectiveness, we propose to incorporate mutual information-aware
019            optimization into the fine-tuning of strong pretrained models. For small or highly-
020            imbalanced downstream datasets where such optimization is less effective, we
021            further propose to heuristically reweight the MLP blocks, which is inspired by
022            our observation that top MLP blocks often cause the loss of mutual information.
023            Our method enables small student models to benefit from those pretrained models
024            among the strongest.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large-size models pretrained on large-scale general-domain data have achieved great successes in
029
      many real-world applications. These models are believed to encode world knowledge (Peng et al.,
030   2023) and are able to generalize to specific tasks with proper adaptation. Nevertheless, the shear size
031   of these models brings about significant amount of serving cost, which impedes their product-level
032   deployment. A straightforward way to utilize these strong pretrained models without inducing
033   additional serving cost is through knowledge distillation, where one hopes that the encoded world
034   knowledge that is relevant to the specific task can be effectively transferred to existing small models,
035   typically with a much smaller size.
036   The off-the-shelf paradigms to distill pretrained models on specific downstream tasks often consist
037   of two stages. First, the pretrained model is adapted to the downstream task through fine-tuning,
038   where the pretrained model is specialized and competitive performance can be achieved. Second, the
039   fine-tuned pretrained model is distilled into desired product-level small models, through matching the
040   predictions or intermediate features between two models.
041
      However, such a paradigm may not be ideal as existing results have shown that knowledge distillation
042   may be less effective on strong models (Wang et al., 2022), either when model size is scaled up (Cho
043   & Hariharan, 2019) or when advanced training strategies are employed (Müller et al., 2019). Indeed,
044   we observed that distilling strong pretrained models results in non-competitive small models, often
045   not better than those distilled from much weaker pretrained models. To mitigate this issue, a wide
046   spectrum of knowledge distillation algorithms have been proposed, for example, by employing
047   mid-size models as assistants to bridge the capacity gap (Mirzadeh et al., 2019), by matching the
048   intra-class relation of the predictions (Huang et al., 2022), or by pruning the distillation signals from
049   those difficult classes (Zhu et al., 2022).
050   In this paper, we argue that effective knowledge transfer from strong pretrained models requires not
051   only advanced distillation algorithms, but also proper preprocessing of these pretrained models before
052   distillation. After all, the problem that a student cannot learn well from the teacher may not entirely
053   indicate that the student’s learning strategy is ineffective, but more likely suggests that the teacher is
      not properly aligned for instruction.

054
      To tackle this problem, we motivate from the existing understanding of the effectiveness of knowl-
055   edge distillation, where it has been empirically observed (Müller et al., 2019) and theoretically
056   argued (Wang et al., 2022) that the mutual information between the input data and the distillation tar-
057   gets is critical for knowledge distillation. We observed that the relative ineffectiveness of knowledge
058   transfer from strong pretrained models can also be attributed to the low mutual information. Inspired
059   by this, we explore simple and efficient methods to improve the mutual information during fine-tuning.
060   We found that Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2020), an optimization technique
061   widely used to improve model generalization, if properly tuned, can be employed to effectively
062   improve mutual information and the distillation effectiveness as well.
063   When the downstream dataset is small, the above mutual information-aware optimization may not be
064   effective. We thus explore methods that directly process the model modules to improve the mutual
065   information. We focus on the Transformer architecture. By gradually pruning MLP and self-attention
066   blocks from top to bottom, we found that the depletion of mutual information in strong pretrained
067   models can be largely attributed to top MLP blocks. We further found that top MLP blocks deplete
068   mutual information is mainly the result of their high expertness, which roughly measures how sparse
069
      the MLP block’s neuron activations are for a subset of similar input examples. We prove that high
      expertness of an MLP block naturally bottlenecks its mutual information. As an extreme case for
070
      an intuition, when a subset of inputs only activate a single neuron, the block outputs converge to
071
      the same representation, which obscures any information within this set of inputs. Based on this
072   observation, we propose to simply downweight the top MLP blocks of strong pretrained models to
073   improve the mutual information.
074
075
      We combine both SAM and our simple block reweighting heuristics to improve the effectiveness
      of knowledge distillation for strong pretrained models. On a variety of tasks, model architectures,
076
      and fine-tuning methods, we observe consistent gain of the small student model’s performance upon
077
      distillation. Furthermore, when scaling up the size of the pretrained model and the scale of the
078   pretraining data, we observe that the small student model continues to benefit from the increasing
079   performance of the strong teacher models.

082   2    R ELATED W ORK
083
084   Understand the difficulty of distilling strong models. Existing works have shown that strong
085   models as knowledge distillation teachers may be less effective, or even consistently hurt the small
086   model’s performance compared to weak models (Cho & Hariharan, 2019; Huang et al., 2022).
087
      Specifically, early-stopped checkpoints of the teacher model, or even the snapshot ensemble (Huang
      et al., 2017) of these checkpoints, may be beneficial to the student performance (Wang et al.,
088
      2022). Dao et al. (2021) proved that strong teacher can overfit the training set, thus may deviate
089
      its probabilistic predictions from the Bayes class probabilities of the data distribution. Zhu et al.
090   (2022) found that, compared to vanilla training, distilling from strong teachers may hurt the student’s
091   performance on certain classes, which they refer to as “undistillable classes”.
092
093
      Student-oriented teacher training. The main focus of this paper is to distill strong pretrained
      models on specific downstream tasks. Distillation in this scenario can be particularly challenging,
094
      potentially due to not only the gap between teacher-student model capacity, but also the gap between
095
      the pretrained and downstream datasets. We are thus particularly interested in how to process the
096   pretrained model to improve its distillation effectiveness.
097
098   Existing works have been focusing on training teachers for better student performance, albeit in the
099
      standard full training setup instead of pretraining-fine-tuning setup. Dao et al. (2021) proposes to
      conduct cross-fitting when training the teacher. Specifically, the training data is split into several folds,
100
      where the teacher predictions on each fold are generated by the model trained only on out-of-fold
101
      data. Park et al. (2021) jointly trains the teacher model and student’s model blocks, aiming to impose
102   regularizations toward the student performance. Dong et al. (2022) explores the necessary conditions
103   for the teacher model to learn Bayes probabilistic predictions and proposes to incorporate additional
104   regularization into the teacher training, which is shown to improve the student performance. For
105   multi-generalization distillation specifically, Yang et al. (2019) proposes to penalize the difference
106   between the probabilistic prediction at the true class and these at other semantically relevant classes
107   to encourage the learning of secondary probabilities in teacher training, which can improve the
      performance of models in later generations. However, these methods may not be readily applied to

      the distillation of large pretrained models, either due to significant computation overhead, or strict
109   constraints on the teacher’s model architecture.

247   As shown by Figure 4, we find that as more self-attention blocks are pruned, the mutual information

      more MLP blocks are pruned, there exists a turning point. The mutual information first increases

254   Reweighting MLP blocks to increase mutual information. The above observation offers us a
255
      simple heuristic to improve the mutual information and thus the distillation effectiveness of strong
      pretrained models. Specifically, we propose to simply downweight the output of the MLP block in
256
      each layer to reduce its negative impact on the mutual information, while keep the attention block
257
      intact, namely

276
277   It has been widely observed that the neuron activations of MLP blocks are highly sparse (Zhang
278   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022). Here neuron activations refer to the intermediate outputs of the MLP
279   block after the ReLU activation function. Such sparsity can be as low as only ∼ 1% of the activations
280   are non-zeros for each input to MLP, and is more significant for top MLP blocks and for strong
281   pretrained models (Li et al., 2022). Existing works have successfully utilized such sparsity to convert
282
      dense pretrained models to MoE models (Zhang et al., 2021), which consist of MoE MLP blocks in
      replace of standard MLP blocks. An MoE MLP block is different from a standard MLP block in that
283
      it partitions the block parameters into several subsets, which are known as experts. Given an input,
284
      only one or a few experts will activate, which thus reduces the computation cost. Formally, we define
285   an MoE MLP block as follows 2 .

      5.2   H IGH EXPERTNESS CORRELATES WITH LOW MUTUAL INFORMATION
346   We show that top MLP blocks of strong pretrained models cause low mutual information likely
347   because of their high expertness.
348
349
      Intuition of why expertness leads to low mutual information. The intuition here is that, if the
      outputs of a sub-population of input examples are contributed by the few neurons in an expert, they
350
      will tend to be similar and thus it would be difficult to distinguish the input examples based on these
351
      outputs. In this case, these outputs will contain little information in additional to the fact that they are
352   from that particular sub-population. Here the distinguishability of the input examples from outputs
353   can be described by mutual information as mentioned before. And how few the experts would be
354   sufficient to compute the outputs is well measured by expertness.
355
      An analytical result.      We further provide an analytical result to show expertness of the MLP
356
      blocks may cause low mutual information. We consider an MoE MLP block that consists of M
357
      experts, where each expert selectively activates a few neurons based on the sub-population that the
358   input example belongs to. We are interested in the mutual information between the output of the MoE
359   MLP and the input X, which can be upper bounded as follows.
360
      Proposition 5.4 (Mutual information of an MoE MLP). Let Z be a random variable that denotes
361
      the index of the expert that will be used for input X and Z ∼ [M ]. Equivalently, Z will also denote
362   the index of the sub-population of X that will share the same expert. We will have

365                                                            z∈[M ]                                           z∈[M ]
366   where b is the maximum entropy (i.e., bits) of a single neuron activation.
367
368   Here the first “≤” leverages the data processing inequality (DPI) since computation of the MoE MLP

      7       C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
525
      In this paper, we explore simple and efficient methods to improve the knowledge distillation ef-
526
      fectiveness of strong pretrained models. By observing that mutual information is essential for
527
      effective knowledge distillation, we propose to incorporate mutual information-aware optimization
528   into fine-tuning. We alos localize the mutual information problem of pretrained models to mostly
529   the top MLP blocks. We show the spontaneous expertness of these MLP blocks will greatly reduce
530   the mutual information and impact the knowledge distillation effectiveness. We propose to further
531   downweight these particular MLP blocks before employing strong pretrained models as teacher,
532   which can significantly boost the effectiveness of the knowledge distillation in a widely variety of
533   tasks and settings.
534   Our proposed method may also be suitable for use as a curriculum control method to gradually ramp
535   up the difficulty of the teacher, which may be more effective or efficient than those conventional
536   curriculum learning methods, for example, learning from small to large models sequentially, or from
537   early to late checkpoints sequentially. However, due to limited computation resources, we would like
538   to leave this exploration as a future work.

907   C.2   V ISUALIZATION OF NEURONS
908
909   We visualize the neurons in the top MLP blocks and show that they encode specific “skills” that are
910   highly predictive of the class label, which echoes with the observation that top MLP blocks may have
911   high expertness.
912   To visualize representative neurons, we conduct criticality analysis (Zhang et al., 2019; Maini et al.,
913   2023) to reveal the dependence of model outputs on specific neurons. Specifically, on each example xi ,
914   we record the relative change of the “[CLS]” token’s embedding (i.e., pre-logits representation) when
915   setting an individual neuron h to zero. We then use the maximum change over all examples to denote
916   the criticality of a specific neuron to the model output, namely σ(h) = maxi∈[N ] kFih←0 −Fi k/kFi k,
917   where Fi denotes the “[CLS]” embedding of the i-th input example, and N is the total number of
      input examples.


                                                                17
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
       In Figure 7, we visualize 10 neurons in the top MLP block that influence the model outputs the
919    most based on the criticality analysis. One can observe that these neurons encode complete and
920    concrete object features. For each neuron, we also show the downstream training example that is
921    most influenced by it 9 . One can observe that the input example can almost be exactly matched by the
922    visualization of the corresponding neuron . This implies that each of these neurons may encode the
923    knowledge that is sufficient to recognize the a similar class of examples in the downstream dataset on
924    its own. In contrast, for bottom MLP blocks and for weak models, we rarely observe any concrete
925    objects in the knowledge encoded by their neurons, as shown in Figures 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13. Such
926    phenomena are also observed in different downstream datasets (Figures 14, 15).
927    These evidences suggest that top MLP blocks may indeed behave as MoE, where each neuron singly
928    or a few neurons collectively act as an expert, and the partition of the experts may largely based on
929    the class label.
