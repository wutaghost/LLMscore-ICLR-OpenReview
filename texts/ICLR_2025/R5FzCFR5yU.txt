011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            This work demonstrates that automatic differentiation has strong limitations when
014            employed to compute physical derivatives in a general physics-informed frame-
015            work, therefore limiting the range of applications that these methods can address.
016
               A hybrid approach is proposed, combining deep learning and traditional numer-
               ical solvers such as the finite element method, to address the shortcomings of
017
               automatic differentiation. This novel approach enables the exact imposition of
018
               Dirichlet boundary conditions in a seamless manner, and more complex, non ana-
019            lytical problems can be solved. Finally, enriched inputs can be used by the model
020            to help convergence. The proposed approach is flexible and can be incorporated
021            into any physics-informed model. Our hybrid gradient computation proposal is
022            also up to two orders of magnitude faster than automatic differentiation, as its
023            numerical cost is independent of the complexity of the trained model. Several
024            numerical applications are provided to illustrate the discussion.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Solving Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) with deep-learning based approaches has received a
030
      growing interest over the past few years, mainly due to the early promising results of Raissi et al.
      (2019). Their dataless, physics-informed approach, allows to leverage the physical knowledge of
031
      a problem to train deep learning models, without having access to numerically costly accurate
032
      simulations of the problem.
033
034
035   With this growing interest, many variations of the physics-informed framework have emerged.
036
      The main improvements are focused on the model’s architecture, the loss construction and the
      optimization procedure during training. However, few works address the computation of residuals
037
      by automatic differentiation, and how some improvements can be made in this direction. In the
038
      following, it is argued that this step constitutes a bottleneck of the physics-informed framework and
039   that an hybrid numerical approach could be beneficial, both in terms of global performance and
040   generalization capabilities. More precisely, it is demonstrated in Section 3 that the computation of
041   physical derivatives can lead to wrong results in many settings, for instance when the PDE coeffi-
042   cients do not have an analytic form, or when enriched input data is fed to the deep learning model.
043   In light of these constraints, in Section 4, we propose a new framework, where physical derivatives
044   are computed by numerical operators. A comparison between our new approach and the traditional
045   Physics-Informed framework is presented in Section 5. Finally, we present a numerical experiment
046   in Section 6 to test our hybrid approach for the strong inclusion of Dirichlet boundary conditions on
047
      a challenging geometry. We compare our results with the existing Automatic-Differentiation based
      PINNs, both in terms of accuracy and computation time.

105   purposes, some works have been focusing on enriching the model, through the loss function (Yu
106   et al., 2022) or by changing the learning task to learn operators instead of functions to a single
107   problem (Wang et al., 2021b; Podina et al., 2023).

120   the geometry and the mesh, and can be reused for other limit conditions and PDEs.

      Automatic differentiation (AD) plays now a key role in machine learning and deep learning. There
      are many ways of implementing this set of techniques, and each deep learning framework, such as
124
      Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019), Tensorflow (Abadi et al., 2015) or JAX (Frostig et al., 2018) has its
125
      own specificities. Here, we only describe briefly the reverse mode AD, since it is the most common
126   in machine learning settings. For a broader overview of AD and its applications, see Baydin et al.
127   (2018).
128
129
      AD is based on the fact that computational operations are obtained by combinations of a finite
130
      number of elementary, differentiable operations, for which the analytical derivatives are known. In
131
      reverse mode AD, computations are decomposed into two phases: a forward and a backward phase.
132   In the forward phase, every computation made is recorded hierarchically, inside a computational
133   graph. This register allows to pile up all the dependencies between the variables. In the backward
134   phase, the registered operations are run through backward, from the outputs to the inputs. At each
135   step, the derivative of the associated elementary operation is computed in a formal way, and evalu-
136   ated at the corresponding value. Next, with the chain rule, the derivatives are multiplied, to obtain as
137   a global result the derivative of the outputs, with respect to every input. This allows for any gradient
138   computation, for instance to compute the derivative of a neural network’s output with respect to its
139   parameters. Since most of the optimization processes in deep learning are gradient-based and are
140
      variations of the gradient descent procedure, AD has proven to be very efficient and succesful in
      these applications.

145   3.1   N ON - ANALYTICAL PDE COEFFICIENTS
146
147   While many applications of physics-informed models have been published recently, the vast majority
148   of the cases are academic, analytical PDEs, where the target solution and the PDE coefficients have a
149   known, analytical expression. However, for many real-life cases in Physics or engineering, the PDE
150   coefficients are not of this type, and can only be tabulated. For instance, this may be the case for
151   thermal and electrical conductivity for diffusion processes, viscosity in fluid mechanics, or material
152
      properties in solid mechanics. When this situation arises, the computation of the PDE residuals
      by AD is compromised. As an example, consider the following one-dimensional static diffusion
153
      equation:
154
155
156                                              −(α(x)u′ )′ = 0.                                         (6)
157
158   Suppose the diffusion coefficient α is given by tabulated data, and is not known analytically. Con-
159   sequently, the diffusion term of (6) will be computed as −α(x)u′′ , since the spatial derivative of α
160   cannot be computed by the AD framework. Therefore, in this case, the PDE residuals computed by
161   AD will not be accurate, preventing the model from converging to the true solution. This limitation
      is illustrated in Section 5.

      3.2   S TRONG IMPOSITION OF D IRICHLET BOUNDARY CONDITIONS
163
164   Respecting boundary and limit conditions is a challenge for Physics-Informed models. Many works
165   have focused on weak or strong imposition of boundary conditions, first for simple geometries (La-
166   garis et al., 1998a), then for more complex ones (Leake & Mortari, 2020; van der Meer et al.,
167   2022; Berg & Nyström, 2018; Wang et al., 2021a; Sheng & Yang, 2022; Sukumar & Srivastava,
168   2022). While these methods can give accurate results, they often require additional preprocessing
169   and computations. Moreover, weak imposition approaches do not guarantee an exact satisfaction of
170
      these boundary conditions. On the other hand, classical numerical approaches can usually impose
      Dirichlet boundary conditions exactly, by modifying the linear system of equations to account for
171
      these conditions. A similar approach, within the physics-informed framework, would be to proceed
172
      as follows.
173
174
175   Suppose one wants to solve the following PDE, with Dirichlet boundary conditions:
176
177
                                                  N (u) = f        in Ω,                                   (7)
178
179
                                                    u = uD         on ∂Ω.                                  (8)
180
181
      Here, Ω can be any arbitrary open and connex domain of Rd , d ≥ 1, and uD is the Dirichlet
      boundary condition. Suppose a neural network Mθ is used to approximate the solution u. A simple,
182
      straightforward way of strongly imposing the boundary conditions is to simply modify the neural
183
      network’s outputs when on the boundary. The predicted solution û of the PDE (7) - (8) would
184   therefore simply be:
185

187

188   Here, 1∂Ω is the characteristic function of the set ∂Ω.

      While this very simple trick guarantees that the boundary conditions are fulfilled, to the best of our
      knowledge, no work has been published using this approach for Physics-Informed Neural Networks.
      Once again, the reason comes from the inability of AD to include spatial fields (in this case, 1∂Ω ,
192
193
      and eventually uD if the Dirichlet conditions do not have an analytical expression) that have not
194   been recorded inside of its computational graph. Therefore, the gradient of û will be computed by
195   AD as:
196

198                                          auto. diff.

      While this computation is correct on the interior of the domain, this leads to singularities close to the
200
      boundary, and the Dirichlet condition (8) will not be taken into account in the training. In particular,
201
      for a PDE where the zero function (or any constant function) would be a solution to (7), but not
202   to (8), an AD-based training would converge to such solution. For an example of this behavior, along
203   with a comparison with our proposed framework, which is described in Section 4, see Section 5.2.
204   Conceptually, our approach intends to demonstrate that the model does not need to learn explicitly
205   the boundary conditions. Rather, by strongly applying the BC as external unknowns, the model can
206   expand its capacity to generalization as it should learn how to propagate the information throughout
207   the domain.
208   Other conditions, such as Neumann or Robin conditions, may not be addressed as directly as what
209   has been proposed here. For these conditions, a variational approach could be considered, in the
210   same fashion as Samaniego et al. (2020). For periodic conditions, a preprocessing step could be
211   implemented. For a more thorough discussion on this point, see Wang et al. (2023).

213   3.3   E NRICHED INPUTS TO THE MODEL
214
215   Since the first works on the physics-informed framework, many of its aspects have been challenged
      and optimized to yield better results on various academic application cases. However, to the best of

      our knowledge, there has been little to no discussion on optimizing the inputs of such models. The
217   enriched physical knowledge of the problems have been applied to the models’ architecture and
218   inside the loss function to optimize the training, but no discussion have been conducted on using
219   this knowledge to enrich the models’ input fields. This limitation of the framework is significant
220   in terms of both global performance and generalization capabilities. Providing information on
221   changes, to the source term or boundary conditions for example, as input to the model would be
222   beneficial, enabling it to expand its range of application without requiring re-training for every
223   modification of the initial problem. This limitation is strongly linked with the failure of automatic
224   differentiation pointed out in Theorem 3.2, since any additional input to the model leads to wrong
225
      evaluations of spatial derivatives, therefore to wrong estimations of PDE residuals.

515   7   C ONCLUSION AND PERSPECTIVES
516
517
      In this paper, a novel framework, hybrid numerical PINNs, has been presented. This general
518   procedure is flexible and can be combined with any improvement regarding the model’s archi-
519   tecture or the training step. Our hybrid approach allows to strongly impose Dirichlet boundary
520   conditions on arbitrary shapes, with no preprocessing complexity. This new framework overcomes
521   the limitations of automatic differentiation, paving the way for further enhancements to physics-
522   informed applications. The new capabilities have been demonstrated on several numerical problems.
523
524   Important future directions include the extension of this setting to more complex problems, for
525   instance with more challenging boundary conditions and equations, and its application with state-
526   of-the-art models such as Fourier Neural Operators or Graph Networks.
