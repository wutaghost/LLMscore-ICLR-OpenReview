009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Estimating mutual information (MI) from data is a fundamental task in machine
012            learning and data science, yet it remains highly challenging even with state-of-
013            the-art estimators. This work proposes a new distribution-free MI estimator based
014            on reference distributions. Unlike existing works that only discern between the
015            joint distribution and the marginal distribution, which can easily overfit in high-
016            MI settings, our method compares them with extra reference distributions. These
017            artificial distributions share the same marginals as the original distributions but
018            have known dependence structures, providing additional signals for more accu-
019
               rate dependency modelling. Experiments on synthetic tasks with non-Gaussian,
               high-dimensional data and real-world applications including Bayesian experimen-
020
               tal design and self-supervised learning demonstrate the potential of our approach.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Mutual information (MI), defined as the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the joint probability
027
      and the product of marginals, is a fundamental measure of the dependence between random vari-
      ables. Unlike correlation, MI captures non-linear statistical dependencies between high-dimensional
028
      variables, making it a robust measure of dependence in diverse cases [1]. Consequently, MI has been
029
      widely applied across diverse fields, such as representation learning [2; 3; 4], Bayesian experimental
030   design [5; 6], information bottleneck [7; 8], domain generalization [9; 10], and causality [11; 12].
031
032   Despite its widespread applications, estimating MI in high-dimensional spaces from raw data is
033
      highly challenging. Early methods work in a non-parametric fashion, utilizing techniques like bin-
      ning or kernel density estimation [13; 14; 15; 16; 17]. As non-parameteric methods typically do not
034
      scale well with sample size or data dimensionality [18], recent studies have focused on utilizing pow-
035
      erful parametric models such as deep neural networks in MI estimation [19; 2; 20; 21; 22; 23; 24; 25].
036   In these estimators, a neural network (often known as the critic) is trained to discern or classify sam-
037   ples from the joint distribution and the marginal distribution. Upon convergence, it can be shown
038   that the learned network corresponds to an estimate of the density ratio between the joint distribu-
039   tion and the product of marginals [19; 2; 20; 21; 22], or that the loss function of these estimators
040   directly optimize a variational lower bound of MI [19; 2], thereby offering an estimate of MI. Com-
041   pared to non-parametric methods, estimating MI through neural network training not only handles
042   large sample sizes more effectively but also scales better with high-dimensional data. Thereby, it is
043   increasingly gaining popularity in both machine learning and statistics communities.
044   Although neural network-based MI estimators have achieved success in numerous tasks, they are
045   recently found to suffer from the so-called high-discrepancy issue, particularly when the underlying
046   mutual information is high or the data dimensionality is high. In such scenarios, distinguishing sam-
047   ples from the joint and the marginal distributions becomes considerably easier, leading the network
048   to easily overfit with limited data. This curse of high-discrepancy has been empirically observed in
049   multiple applications [26; 22; 27] and has also been formally analyzed in theoretical studies [28; 29].
050   In this work, we propose Mutual Information Multinomial Estimation (MIME), a novel approach
051   tailored for estimating mutual information accurately in high-discrepancy settings, drawing upon
052   recent advances in multi-classes ratio estimation [22] and vector copula theory [30]. Unlike many
053   existing methods that solely focus on discerning samples between the joint and the marginal distri-
      butions, which can easily overfit in high-discrepancy scenarios, MIME further compares them with

      a set of reference distributions whose marginal distributions are the same as the original data distri-

059   • We propose a new mutual information estimator tailored for high-MI cases, combining the

061   • We systematically compare our estimator against state-of-the-art neural estimators on not only
062     high-dimensional, non-Gaussian synthetic dataset with known MI but also real-world applications.

093   found more robust than DV-like estimators [3]; however, the MI estimated is not a lower bound.
094
      It is worth noting that these two lines of methods are closely related to each other. On one hand, it
095   can be shown that the optimal f in DV and NWJ estimators is, up to some constant, the log density
096   ratio log p(x, y)/p(x)p(y). Conversely, the estimated ratio r̂ in ratio-estimating method can in turn
097   be used in the DV representation to obtain a lower bound estimate of MI. Regarding the learning
098   process, ratio-estimating method can be seen as optimizing a lower bound of the Jensen-Shannon
099   divergence between p(x, y) and p(x)p(y), whereas DV and NWJ methods optimize KL divergence.
100
      Curse of high discrepancy. Despite their advantages over non-parametric methods, distribution-
101   free estimators often suffer from the so-called high-discrepancy issue in practice. High-discrepancy
102   issue typically happens when the dimensionality of data is high and/or the underlying MI is high,
103   which results in a large KL between p(x, y) and p(x)p(y). However, it is shown that asymptoti-
104   cally, any distribution-free estimator of the KL divergence between two distributions p and q will
105   inevitably suffer from an estimation variance that is exponential to the ground truth value [29; 28]:
106                                            ˆ
                                           V[KL[p∥q]]   ≈ O(eKL[p∥q] )                                   (4)
107
      which implies that methods based on KL divergence estimation (e.g. DV, NWJ) will be impractical

      if the underlying MI is large or the intrinsic dimensionality of the data is high, both of which can
109   lead to a large KL. The situation becomes even worse when we learn estimators with mini-batches.
110   This issue raises concerns about the reliability of DV- and NWJ-like estimators in high-MI settings.
111
112
      We highlight that the impact of the high discrepancy issue is beyond KL divergence-based estima-
      tors. Recent works have confirmed that high-discrepancy issue also exists in ratio-based estimator
113
      (3), where the classifier in the estimator tends to overfit when samples x, y ∼ p(x, y) and samples
114
      x, y ∼ p(x)p(y) are overly easy to discern [21; 36; 27; 22; 37]. One intuitive explanation is that
115   when the gap between p(x, y) and p(x)p(y) is large, there exist many classifiers that can achieve
116   almost perfect accuracy but have differently estimated ratios, leading to inaccurate estimate of MI.

      3    M ETHOD
120
121
      In this work, we propose a new MI estimator aiming to address the aforementioned high-discrepancy
      issue in distribution-free MI estimation. The key idea is, unlike existing works that solely compare
122
      the joint distribution p(x, y) with the product of the marginal distributions p(x)p(y), which easily
123
      causes overfitting, we also compare them with additional reference distributions which share the
124   same marginals as the original distribution but have various dependence structures. These additional
125   comparisons provide more fine-grained signals for accurately modelling the dependence structures
126   of p(x, y) and p(x)p(y), allowing us to better model the dependence between X and Y .
127

128
      nomial classification problem, where we not only distinguish samples from p(x, y), p(x)p(y) but
129
      also samples from specific-designed reference distributions. Specifically, consider classifying sam-
130   ples x, y from four distributions p1 (x, y), p2 (x, y), p3 (x, y), p4 (x, y) with a classifier h. This can be
131   done by training h with the following objective function:

      This estimation strategy can be seen as a new paradigm for combining discriminative (distribution-

165   Choices of reference distributions. We next discuss the design of reference distributions. While
166
      any distributions can be used in principle, we focus on those that are marginal preserving, i.e. the
      marginals q(x) and q(y) of the reference distribution q(x, y) should closely approximate those of
167
      the original distribution: q(x) ≈ p(x), q(y) ≈ p(y). This property is motivated by the fact that MI is
168
      irrelevant to marginals, so a good reference should only differ from p(x, y) in dependence structure;
169   When asking the network h to distinguish among the various distributions in the above framework,
170   these marginal-preserving references enforce the network to learning a useful representation to dis-
171   tinguish the small changes in dependence structures, leading to accurate MI estimate.
172

233   5   R ELATED WORKS
234
235   Alleviating high-discrepancy issue in statistical divergence estimation. Several methods have
236   been developed to address the high discrepancy issues in the estimation of statistical divergence the
237   estimation of density ratio. One method is to reduce the estimation variance by clipping the output
238   of the estimator [29], which can be seen as trading low variance with additional bias. Another line
239   of methods solves the problem by the divide-and-conquer principle [21; 42], where the statistical
240   divergence/density ratio between some intermediate distributions are first estimated, followed by an
241   aggregation step to recover the original statistical divergence/density ratio from these intermediate
242   estimations. Due to the reduced distributional discrepancy, each of these intermediate estimates
243
      can be expected to be accurate. However, divide-and-conquer methods are found to suffer from the
      distribution-shift issue during the aggregation step, which can lead to estimation inaccuracy. Unlike
244
      these methods, our method does not incur extra estimation bias and is free from distribution shift.
245
246   Multi-classes ratio/MI estimate. Similar to our work, some recent works also utilize a set of refer-
247   ence distributions q to improve density ratio estimate [43; 22], and the use of reference distributions
248   in MI estimation have also been explored in [44; 45]. A crucial difference between our method
249
      and these approaches is that the reference distributions in our method are marginal-preserving i.e.
      their marginal distributions q(x) and q(y) are close to the original marginal distributions. As afore-
250
      mentioned, such marginal-preserving property is important as it enforces the network to focus on
251
      modelling the changes in dependence structures rather than modelling the marginal distributions,
252   which are irrelevant for MI estimation. This importance will be verified in the experiment section.
253
254   Generative methods for mutual information estimation. In addition to the above distribution-
255
      free methods for MI estimate, there also exist a set of MI estimators [46; 28; 47; 48; 38] based
      on generative modelling. These methods work by learning a generative model (e.g. flow-based
256
      model [40; 41] or diffusion model [38]) to approximate the density or the score of the data distribu-
257
      tion rather than comparing between different densities, thereby are free from the high-discrepancy
258   issue. However, the performance of such estimators is dominated by the quality of the learned gen-
259   erative models, and generative modelling as done by e.g. flow-based model is well-known hard in
260   high-dimensional cases [49; 50; 51; 41] and can suffer from model mis-specification. Our method
261   also uses generative models, but only as informed reference distributions, which is less sensitive to
262   model mis-specification and the quality of the learned generative models.
263
      Vector Gaussian copula for MI estimate. We discuss a recent work [48] highly relevant to our
264   method. This work first Gaussianizes the two marginals via two distint flow models, then approx-
265   imates the joint distribution of the (marginally) Gaussianized data by a Gaussian distribution, from
266   which mutual information is calculated. This process can be seen as modelling the data distribution
267   as a vector Gaussian copula model (8) and directly computing MI from this model. This way of
268   MI calculation is accurate if the underlying data distribution can be well-approximated by a vector
269   Gaussian copula, but can be inaccurate in the case of model mis-specification. Rather than fully
      relying on the vector Gaussian copula approximation, which is risky, we use it as references solely.

398   We next consider the application of our method in Bayesian experimental design (BED), an im-
399   portant task in science and engineering. In BED, we wish to design experiments so that the data
400   collected in the experiment will be maximally informative for understanding the underlying process
401   of the experiment. For example, we may wish to find out the best policy design d for epidemic
402   disease surveillance and intervention, so that we can infer the properties θ of the epidemic as ac-
403   curate as possible with minimal observed data X. Such a task can be mathematically described as
404   optimizing the conditional mutual information I(X; θ|d) between the experiment outcomes X (e.g.
405   the observed dynamics of the infectious people in the population) and the properties of interest θ
406

      6.4       P ROTEIN SEQUENCE ANALYSIS
503
504   Our last task involves quantifying dependence in protein language model (PLM) embeddings [68].
505   The goal is to investigate to what extent do the PLM embeddings contain information about protein-
506   protein interactions, which are essential for the study of their functional property. This information
507   is quantified by the mutual information between the embeddings of different protein sequences.
508
      Setup. Following the setups in [55], we consider two types of protein-protein interactions: kinase-
509   target and ligand-receptor interactions, using annotated data from the OmniPath database [36]. For
510   kinase-target interaction, we consider kinase and target sequence embeddings as random variables
511   K ∈ R1024 , T ∈ R1024 with joint distribution p(K, T ), and are interested in evaluating I(K; T ).
512   Similarly, we are interested in evaluating I(L; R) for ligand-receptor interactions. All data are
513   preprocessed using the auto-encoder approach in [55] to reduce their dimensionality to 32 before MI
514   estimation. As in previous experiments, we ensure that all MI estimates are lower bound estimates.
515   Results. For this task, our method estimates I(K; T ) ≈ 0.8nats and I(L; R) ≈ 2.8nats. These
516   results indicate that protein sequences as encoded by the PLM embeddings contain information
517   about both interactions, with the information about ligand-receptor interactions much higher than
518   kinase-target interactions — a result consistent with recent studies [55]. Methods like MINE largely
519   underestimate this information and wrongly suggest independence for kinase-target interaction.

      7       C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we propose a new method for mutual information (MI) estimation in high-MI settings.
524   A main challenge in this task is the high-discrepancy issue, which causes direct comparison between
525   the joint distribution and the marginal distribution inaccurate. We address this issue by addition-
526   ally comparing these distributions with their vector copula approximations, which share the same
527   marginal distributions as the original data distribution and have Gaussian dependence structures.
528   Experiments on diverse tasks demonstrate that such additional comparisons provide fine-grained
529   signals for accurately modelling the true dependence in data, which leads to better MI estimate.
530   One limitation of the proposed method is it relies on a reasonably well-trained generative model
531   (a vector Gaussian copula) to serve as an informed reference distribution. While this model can
532   generally be learned effectively for structured data with moderate dimensions, it can be challenging
533   in the case of high-dimensional, non-structured data (e.g. images). This suggests that our method
534   may be more suited for analyzing structured data, such as embeddings of a protein language model or
535   measurements from biological experiments, rather than directly applied to high-dimensional images.
536   Another limitation is the additional computation cost raised by the use of generative models.
537   For future work, we consider exploring other choices of generative models, such as those in [38], as
538   powerful references and baselines, as well as extending our analysis to other real-world datasets [69].

941   B.2     I MPLEMENTATION OF THE MAPPING f, g IN VECTOR G AUSSIAN COPULA
942
      The detailed implementation of the bijective mappings f and g are task-dependent. Specifically,
943
944   • Non-representation learning tasks. For this type of tasks, we implement the two mappings f, g
945     by a continuous flow model trained by flow matching [41]. This flow model is implemented as a 4
946     layer MLP with 1024 hidden units per each layer and softplus non-linearity. This MLP is trained
947     by Adam [70] with a learning rate of 5 × 10− 4 and early stopping. We use this implementation for
948     all the experiments in Section 6.1 (synthetic data) and Section 6.2 (Bayesian experiment design).
949   • Representation learning tasks. For this type of tasks, we implement f, g as element-wise func-
950     tions. In such case, the model degenerates to a Gaussian copula model. Sampling in Gaussian
951     copula can be done by first sampling ϵ ∼ N (ϵ; 0, Σ), then computing the rank of ϵd in the popu-
952     lation: rd = ⌈nΦ(ϵd )⌉. Here Φ(·) is the CDF of standard normal distribution and n is the sample
953     size of population. After that, we set xd as the rd -th smallest element in the population of Xd . The
954     estimation of Σ can be done by inverting this process, which takes ≤ 30ms when n = 10, 000.

1049   B.7     N EURAL NETWORK ARCHITECTURE AND OPTIMIZER
1050
       Network architecture. For fair comparison, we use the same network architecture for the critic
1051   f (x, y) in all the MI estimators considered. This network takes the form of a MLP with 3 hidden
1052   layers, each of which has 500 neurons. A densenet architecture [71] is used for the network, where
1053   we concatenate the input of the first layer (i.e., x and y) and the representation of the penultimate
1054   layer before feeding them to the last layer. Leaky ReLU [72] is used as the activation function for
1055   all hidden layers.
1056
       Optimizer. We train all networks by Adam [70] with its default settings, where the learning rate
1057   is set to be 5 × 10−4 and the batch size is set to be 512. Early stopping and a slight weight decay
1058   (1 × 10−6 ) are applied to avoid overfitting. No dropout or batch normalization is used.
