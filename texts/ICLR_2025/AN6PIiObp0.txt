009                                          Abstract
010
011           In Large Language Model (LLM) development, Reinforcement Learning
012           from Human Feedback (RLHF) is crucial for aligning models with human
013           values and preferences. RLHF traditionally relies on the Kullback-Leibler
014           (KL) divergence between the current policy and a frozen initial policy as a
015           reference, which is added as a penalty in policy optimization algorithms like
016           Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). While this constraint prevents models
017
              from deviating too far from the initial checkpoint, it limits exploration of
              the reward landscape, reducing the model’s ability to discover higher-quality
018
              solutions. As a result, policy optimization is often trapped in a narrow
019
              region of the parameter space, leading to suboptimal alignment and per-
020           formance. This paper presents SALSA (Soup-based Alignment Learning
021           for Stronger Adaptation), a novel approach designed to overcome these
022           limitations by creating a more flexible and better located reference model
023           through weight-space averaging of two independent supervised fine-tuned
024           (SFT) models. This model soup allows for larger deviation in KL divergence
025           and exploring a promising region of the solution space without sacrificing
026           stability. By leveraging this more robust reference model, SALSA fosters bet-
027           ter exploration, achieving higher rewards and improving model robustness,
028
              out-of-distribution generalization, and performance. We validate the effec-
              tiveness of SALSA through extensive experiments on popular open models
029
              (Llama2-7B, Mistral-7B, and Gemma-2B) across various benchmarks (MT-
030
              Bench, Arena-Hard, UltraFeedback), where it consistently surpasses PPO
031           by fostering deeper exploration and achieving superior alignment in LLMs.

034   1   Introduction
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) by
037   demonstrating remarkable capabilities in understanding and generating human language.
038   These models, powered by vast amounts of data and advanced neural architectures, have set
039
      new benchmarks in various NLP tasks, from machine translation to conversational agents.
      Despite these advancements, aligning LLMs with human values and preferences remains a
      significant challenge. Misalignment can lead to undesirable behaviors, including generating
040
041
      biased or inappropriate content, which undermines the reliability and safety of these models
042   (Kenton et al., 2021; Bender et al., 2021; Bommasani et al., 2021; Gehman et al., 2020).
043
044   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has become a promising technique
045
      for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences. By fine-tuning LLMs
      based on human feedback, RLHF guides models towards more human-aligned behaviors,
      improving truthfulness, helpfulness, and harmlessness while maintaining the generation of
046
047
      high-probability, correct answers (Christiano et al., 2017). Reward-based RLHF methods
048   utilize a reward model to determine the reward for a given (prompt, response) pair. The
049   policy model is then optimized by maximizing the average reward through reinforcement
050   learning algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). A
051   crucial aspect of RLHF is the use of a reference model to compute the Kullback-Leibler (KL)
052   divergence penalty, which prevents the fine-tuning process from deviating too far from the
053   original model (Ziegler et al., 2019). This approach ensures that the policy remains close to
      the initial model, reducing the risk of generating nonsensical responses.

054
      While effective, reliance on a single reference model can be limiting. The KL penalty term
055   constrains the policy model to stay close to the initial supervised fine-tuning (SFT) model,
056   restricting its ability to fully explore the solution space for higher-reward models. This
057   constraint can lead to suboptimal alignment and a lack of robustness in the training process,
058   increasing the risk of generating nonsensical outputs. Ensuring that the reference model
059   is already positioned in a robust space can mitigate this issue, allowing for more confident
060   exploration without compromising output quality.
061   To address this limitation, we propose SALSA. It integrates a “model soup” as the reference
062   model within the RLHF framework. A model soup is constructed by performing weight-
063   space averaging of multiple independently supervised fine-tuned models that demonstrate
064   comparable performance. This method leverages the principle that fine-tuned models
065   from the same pre-trained initialization often reside in a shared low-error basin in the
066   loss landscape, enabling effective weight interpolation without compromising accuracy. As
067   evidenced by Wortsman et al. (2022), this approach results in significant improvements in
068   both in-distribution and out-of-distribution generalization. The key advantage of model
069
      soup lies in its ability to harness the complementary strengths of diverse models, reducing
      variance and improving robustness, while maintaining computational efficiency. This makes
070
      the “model soup” reference model a superior choice for improving the stability and reliability
071
      of the RLHF training process compared to relying on a single reference model.
072
073   In this paper, we demonstrate the effectiveness of SALSA through comprehensive experi-
074   ments. We apply SALSA to Llama2-7B, Mistral-7B, and Gemma-2B and benchmark
075
      the results against standard evaluation datasets, including MT-Bench, Arena-Hard, and
      UltraFeedback—the latter being used for RLHF training in our experiments. Our findings
076
      reveal that weight space averaging is a straightforward yet effective approach for aligning
077
      LLMs with human preferences, and enhancing their performance on real-world-like datasets.
078
079   In particular our contributions are following:
080
081   • We demonstrate that the reward in the region near the model soup is inherently superior
082     to that of the original SFT model. The improvement in reward is a newly observed
083
        phenomenon and is complementary to the improvement in accuracy of model soups. We
        further show having model soups as reference point of RLHF results in higher reward
        outcomes. (Section 4.2).
084
085
086   • Drawing from these observations, we propose SALSA, a novel approach for implementing
087     RLHF that utilizes the model soup as the reference model.
088
089   • We perform a comprehensive evaluation across diverse benchmarks and models, demon-
090
        strating that SALSA consistently outperforms PPO (Section 4.3).

      2.1   Model Soup
100
101
      Model soups build on the findings of (Neyshabur et al., 2020), which showed that inde-
102   pendently fine-tuned models often lie within the same loss basin, allowing their weights
103   to be successfully combined through interpolation. (Wortsman et al., 2022) extended this,
104   demonstrating that averaging fine-tuned model weights can improve performance compared
105   to using a single model. Unlike traditional ensembling, model soups require no extra memory
106   or inference time, while still enhancing robustness. This method has shown consistent per-
107   formance gains across NLP and image classification tasks (Wortsman et al., 2022; Izmailov
      et al., 2018).

124   2.2      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)
125
126   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved significant success across tasks, thanks to
127   fine-tuning methods like Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning from
128   Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a; Bai et al., 2022; Anil
129
      et al., 2023). RLHF has been crucial for aligning models with human preferences, enabling
      outputs that are more helpful and aligned with societal values (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon
      et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023).
130
131
132   RLHF methods are divided into reward-based and reward-free approaches. Reward-based
133   methods rely on training a reward model to guide policy optimization, typically with Proximal
134   Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2023). PPO is widely used for
135
      its balance of stability and exploration (Ouyang et al., 2022). Studies have also examined the
      importance of hyperparameter tuning and reward model quality to avoid overoptimization
      (Casper et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023a). Reward-free methods, such as Direct Preference
136
137
      Optimization (DPO), bypass the reward model by directly optimizing human preference data
138   (Rafailov et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023). While DPO simplifies training and
139   performs well on tasks like instruction following (Touvron et al., 2023a), it struggles with
140   out-of-distribution data, and its generalization capabilities are limited (Yuan et al., 2024; Xu
141   et al., 2024).
      In this paper, we focus on Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) for its demonstrated
142
143
      robustness in managing large models and diverse tasks. Studies such as (Xu et al., 2024)
144   highlight the limitations of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), which tends to find
145   biased solutions when confronted with out-of-distribution responses and is highly sensitive to
146   distribution shifts. In contrast, PPO effectively mitigates these challenges through the use
147   of reward models and KL divergence regularization (Ouyang et al., 2022; Schulman et al.,
148   2017). This enables PPO to consistently outperform DPO in tasks like dialogue and code
149   generation, proving its reliability across alignment challenges (Xu et al., 2024).
150   Recent RLHF research has introduced novel approaches to improve upon traditional methods,
151   particularly in addressing the limitations of using reference models. SimPO (Meng et al.,
152   2024) eliminates the need for a reference model by optimizing the average log probability
153   of sequences with a reward-free approach. This reduces computational costs and improves
154   memory efficiency over traditional DPO, achieving better performance across benchmarks like
155   AlpacaEval and Arena-Hard. SimPO underscores the limitations of static reference models in
156   DPO and advocates for scalable, reference-free approaches. Conversely, (Gorbatovski et al.,
157   2024) introduces a dynamic reference model that evolves throughout training using Trust
158
      Region methods. This dynamic approach allows the reference model to adapt alongside the
      policy, preventing constraints imposed by outdated checkpoints and enabling more effective
      generalization and alignment with human preferences.
159
160
161   The aforementioned papers have identified that the reference model may limit the optimization
      process in DPO, and they propose innovative solutions to address this issue. Similarly, we


                                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      aim to solve this problem within the PPO framework. We adopt a model soup approach,
163   which averages the weights of fine-tuned models. This method allows the optimization
164   process in PPO to explore a broader solution space, offering greater flexibility and enhancing
165   alignment performance.

      3     Method
168
169
      In this section we present our method, SALSA (Soup-based Alignment Learning for Stronger
170   Adaptation). We begin with a brief overview of Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), and
171   model soup, followed by a detailed description of our approach.
172
173   3.1   RLHF
174
175   In our main experiments, we focus on reward-based RLHF, in particular Proximal Policy
176   Optimization (PPO). The conventional framework for reward-based RLHF consists of several
177   key stages as follows.
178   Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). The initial stage of alignment involves supervised
179   fine-tuning, where a pre-trained language model is refined using a high-quality instruction
180   dataset.
181
      Reward Model. Reward-based RLHF involves training a reward model, which is typically
182
      initialized from the SFT model. In this process, the logit layer of the SFT model is replaced
183   by a new linear layer. This linear layer takes the embedding of the last token and outputs a
184   scalar reward. Higher rewards indicate better samples. For a given prompt x and a pair of
185   responses yw (chosen) and yl (rejected), the loss function is optimized as:
186
                                 L(Rθ ) = − log σ(Rθ (x, yw ) − Rθ (x, yl )),                     (1)
187
188   where Rθ (·, ·) is the reward model, θ denotes its parameters, σ indicates the sigmoid function.
189   Policy Training. The last phase of RLHF is dedicated to training the policy model, which
190   is initialized from a reference model, typically the SFT model. Based on a recent study (Xu
191   et al., 2024), PPO performs better in distribution shifts and results in superior alignment
192   with human preferences across challenging tasks like code generation and dialogue systems.
193   Therefore, we selected PPO as the training algorithm. The goal is to optimize the policy
194   model to maximize the reward for a given prompt x and its generated response y, while
195
      also minimizing the KL divergence between the policy model and the reference model. The
      overall loss function for this stage is given by:
196
197                       LPPO (πθ ) = −R(x, y) + βKL (πθ (y | x) k πref (y | x))                 (2)
198
      where πθ is the policy model, πref is the reference model, and R(., .) is the trained reward
199
      model.
200
201   3.2 Model Soup
202   The concept of a model soup, introduced in Wortsman et al. (2022), aims to enhance model
203   performance by averaging the weights of multiple pre-trained networks. This technique
204   combines the parameters of independently trained models to produce a more robust outcome,
205   leveraging the strengths of each. There are three primary strategies for constructing a model
206   soup: uniform, greedy, and learned. In our approach, we employ its most basic strategy, i.e.,
207   the uniform method, where the parameters of separate SFTs are averaged. Investigating
208   other mixing strategies for RLHF and comparing them is left as future work. Formally, we
209
      construct our soup model using two SFT models: πref , which serves as the initialization of
      the policy model in the PPO framework, and πother , an additional SFT model trained on
210
      the same data with a different random seed. Their weights denoted as θ is averaged using a
211
      coefficient α:
212                                   θsoup = (1 − α)θref + αθother                             (3)
213
214   In our experimental setup, as shown in Figure 4a, setting α to 0.5 produces the best results.
215   This will be further discussed in the next section, where we also explain our proposed method,
      SALSA.

      3.3   SALSA
217
218   The KL term in equation 2 ensures that the model remains closely aligned with the reference
219   model. This term is essential because optimizing solely for the reward can result in the
220   generation of nonsensical outputs. However, it also imposes a constraint by preventing
221   the model from deviating significantly from the initial reference model. To address this
222   limitation, we propose replacing the KL term in 2, with the following loss function:
                                                                                                 (4)
223
                        LSALSA (πθ ) = −R(x, y) + βKL (πθ (y | x) k πsoup (y | x))
224
225
      As mentioned in Equation 3, πsoup refers to a model soup, which is the result of averaging
226
      two independently trained supervised fine-tuned models (SFTs), including the reference
227
      model. Since the policy model πθ is initialized from the reference model πref , substituting
228   the KL term in equation 2 with the KL term in 4 which is the model soup of πref and πother
229   allows the policy model to search around averaged model, thereby enabling exploration of
230   a broader and more promising parameter space. The primary distinction between the loss
231   terms in equations 2 and 4 is that the soup model is used in place of the reference model.
232   This substitution keeps our approach straightforward to implement while proving highly
233   effective. Our experiments demonstrate improved performance, resulting in higher win rates
234   over PPO across three models and three datasets. These consistent results indicate SALSA’s
235   effectiveness in various settings.

239   4.1   Experimental setups
240
241   In this section, we outline our experimental setup. We use three models: Llama2-7B (Touvron
242   et al., 2023b), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), and Gemma-2B (Team et al., 2024). For
243   Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), we employ the UltraChat-200k dataset (Ding et al., 2023).
244   The UltraFeedback dataset (Cui et al., 2023) is utilized for both training the reward model
245   and optimizing preferences. All experiments across the three stages (SFT, reward model,
246
      RLHF) are conducted using the TRL library by HuggingFace (von Werra et al., 2020). For
      both PPO and SALSA, we use the KL coefficient β that achieves the highest win rate.
      Further details on the hyperparameter settings are available in Appendix A.
247
248
249   Evaluation Benchmarks. We evaluate the effectiveness of our method using three well-
250   established instruction-following benchmarks: MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024), Arena-Hard
251   v0.1 (Li et al., 2024), and UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023) test dataset. MT-Bench comprises
252
      80 questions across 8 categories, while Arena-Hard is an enhanced version of MT-Bench,
      featuring 500 well-defined technical problem-solving questions.
253
254   In our evaluation process, we used the datasets described in the previous section to generate
255   samples. Pairwise comparisons were then conducted using GPT-4-Turbo as the judge model,
256   following the ”LLM-as-a-judge” methodology. The prompt used for our judge model is
257   provided in Figure 7 in the Appendix. For evaluation, we utilized the FastChat repository
258
      (Zheng et al., 2023b). For the MT-Bench questions, which involve two rounds, we generated
      and evaluated outputs for each round.

362   4.4   Ablation Study
363
364   We explored using alternative reference points along the line between πref and πother by
365   varying the α value in Equation 3. For each α, we adjusted the KL divergence to achieve the
366   optimal win rate over PPO. This systematic adjustment allowed us to observe how the win
367
      rate varies with α, as shown in Figure 4a which reveals a clear trend: the adjusted win rate
      increases as α approaches 0.5, peaking at this midpoint before declining at higher values.
368

369
      only 43.07% over PPO. This outcome arises because, although the model explores a wide
370   range between πref and πother , it ultimately converges on a lower-reward region near πother .
371   These findings support our hypotheses on reward dynamics (Section 4.2) and the effects of
372   KL divergence (Section A.4).

      5   Conclusion
433
434
      This paper presents SALSA, a novel method for improving alignment in large language
435   models by leveraging a model soup as a reference in the RLHF framework. By utilizing
436   weight-space averaging of fine-tuned models as the reference, SALSA facilitates more effective
437   exploration during policy optimization, leading to stronger performance in in-distribution
438   and more resilience in out-of-distribution regimes. We showed that model soup resides in
439   a higher reward region even before the PPO process, enabling SALSA to search for higher
440   potential model. Furthermore we showed using model soup as a reference model allows
441   for larger deviation in KL enabling search in a larger region. Experimental results across
442   multiple benchmarks consistently show that SALSA outperforms PPO, yielding higher win
443
      rates, increased average rewards, and improved alignment with human preferences. We
      further extended our work to show averaging over more SFT models results even in higher
      win rates and robustness.
444
445
446   There are many avenues to extend this work: applying model soups to other forms of
447   learning from human feedback like DPO is a very interesting future work. Systematically
448   exploring other forms of ensembling different models as reference, and model averaging with
449
      a non-uniform or adaptive weights is another valuable line of work. Finding out remedies for
      KL-Hacks when using SALSA is another direction for theoretical and emperical research.

651
      A.1   Supervised fine-tuning setup
652
      We use a learning rate of 2.5e − 5, warmup ratio of 0.03, and batch size of 8 for Llama2-7B
653
      and Mistral-7B, and 16 for Gemma-2B. For each of these models, we train two supervised fine-
654   tuned models with different random seeds. Flash attention is applied to conserve resources,
655   and the maximum sequence length is set to 2048 tokens for all models.
656
657
658   A.2   Reward model training
659
660
      The reward model training uses a learning rate of 2e − 5, with a batch size of 8. We apply a
      weight decay of 0.001 and use the AdamW optimizer. The learning rate schedule follows a
661
      linear decay policy.
662
663
664   A.3   RLHF setup
665
666   For the RLHF stage, we use different learning rates: 2e − 6 for Llama2-7B and Mistral-7B,
667   and 1e − 7 for the Gemma-2B model. The batch size is set to 8, with 2 epochs of training.
668   We set the initial KL divergence coefficient to 0.2 for the PPO experiments and 0.01 for the
669
      SALSA experiments.
670
671   A.4   KL divergence
672
673   The model soup resides on the line between two SFT models. As previous work by Wortsman
674   et al. (2022) has shown, each point along the line between two fine-tuned models can
675   improve the accuracy compared to the individual fine-tuned models. With this intuition, we
676   hypothesize that there is a broader search space around the model soup that can still be
677
      beneficial for RLHF. Essentially, instead of searching for a model that does not diverge from
      a single SFT model, we can find models that do not diverge from the line between two SFT
678
      models. This allows for higher KL-divergence from the model soup. To verify this hypothesis,
679
      we tried using a small KL-divergence coefficient of β = 0.01 for PPO as well (in comparison
680   to β = 0.2 in the optimal setting). Figure 6 illustrates that while PPO converged to a state
681   producing gibberish output, SALSA, using the same β value, achieved its optimal win rate.
682
683
      Furthermore, high KL coefficients cannot be applied when using SALSA because the response
      length tends to converge to zero. Specifically, if the response length y for a given prompt x is
684
      zero, the KL divergence between the trained policy and the frozen policy (whether reference
685
      or soup model) also becomes zero. As a result, the policy can gravitates toward regions
686   where the response length is zero, a phenomenon we refer to as the KL-Hack. Investigating
687   this issue is an interesting future research direction.
