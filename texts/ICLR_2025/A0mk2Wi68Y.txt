011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            There has been a recent push of research on Transformer-based models for long-
014            term time series forecasting, even though they are inherently difficult to inter-
015            pret and explain. While there is a large body of work on interpretability methods
016
               for various domains and architectures, the interpretability of Transformer-based
               forecasting models remains largely unexplored. To address this gap, we develop
017
               a framework based on Concept Bottleneck Models to enforce interpretability of
018
               time series Transformers. We modify the training objective to encourage a model
019            to develop representations similar to predefined interpretable concepts. In our ex-
020            periments, we enforce similarity using Centered Kernel Alignment, and the pre-
021            defined concepts include time features and an interpretable, autoregressive surro-
022            gate model (AR). We apply the framework to the Autoformer model, and present
023            an in-depth analysis for a variety of benchmark tasks. We find that the model
024            performance remains mostly unaffected, while the model shows much improved
025            interpretability. Additionally, interpretable concepts become local, which makes
026            the trained model easily intervenable. As a proof of concept, we demonstrate a
027
               successful intervention in the scenario of a time shift in the data, which eliminates
               the need to retrain.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Transformers have shown great success for various types of sequential data, including the modalities
      of language (Devlin (2018), Brown (2020)), images (Dosovitskiy et al. (2021), Liu et al. (2021)), and
034
      speech (Baevski et al. (2020), Gulati et al. (2020)). Their ability to capture long-term dependencies
035
      has triggered much interest in applying them to time-series forecasting, for which sequential data is
036   central, and in particular to the challenging task of long-term time series forecasting. Transformer-
037   based architectures, indeed, often show superior performance in this domain (Zhou et al., 2021;
038   2022; Wu et al., 2021; Ni et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), for an overview see Wen et al. (2023).
039
      However, due to their deep and complex architecture, Transformers are difficult to interpret, which
040
      is especially important in high-stakes domains such as finance and energy demand prediction. There
041
      is a large body of work in the field of Explainable AI to make neural networks more interpretable,
042   including the approach of Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020). This approach
043   relies on the idea of constraining the model such that it predicts human-interpretable concepts first
044   (i.e., the concept bottleneck), and then uses only these concepts to make the final prediction. In order
045   to operationalise this idea, however, concept annotations are needed during training. To overcome
046   this limitation, various approaches have been proposed to learn the concepts themselves using multi-
047   modal models (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Oikarinen et al., 2023). CBMs and their variants have
048   become popular in different domains, especially computer vision. Nonetheless, as of yet, their
049   application to the time series domain is left underexplored.
050   In this paper, we propose a domain-agnostic training framework to make any time series Transformer
051   into a Concept Bottleneck Model using time-series specific, yet domain-agnostic concepts, as shown
052   in Figure 1. The first concept is a simple linear surrogate model which is trained on forecasting the
053   same data. Due to its linear nature, the model’s predictions are easier to interpret. The second
      concept is timestamp information provided with the time series data. A key aspect of our training

094   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
095
096   This paper combines and builds upon foundational works from different domains, including Con-
097
      cept Bottleneck Models (CBMs), knowledge transfer with Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) and
      time series Transformers. CBMs have been applied to the time series domain before (Ferfoglia
098
      et al., 2024), but not with the same interpretable concepts. Likewise, the similarity index CKA has
099
      been used before to transfer knowledge between models (Tian et al., 2023), yet, to the best of our
100   knowledge, it has not been used to construct a CBM. Our work aims to bridge this gap, and thereby
101   provides some unique contributions, such as the use of a surrogate model as an interpretable concept.
102
103   2.1     C ONCEPT B OTTLENECK M ODELS
104
105   Concept Bottleneck Models (CBM; Koh et al., 2020) have emerged in the domain of computer vision
106   as promising interpretable models (Poeta et al., 2023). The concept bottlenecks constrain the model
107   to first predict interpretable concepts, and then use only these concepts in the final downstream
      task. They are shown to be useful in multiple applications, such as model debugging and human


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      intervention on decisions. The bottleneck allows for explaining which information the model is
109   using and when it makes an error due to incorrect concept predictions.
110
111   However, in standard CBMs, concept annotations are needed during training to learn the bottle-
112
      neck. To release this restriction, variants have been proposed to learn the concept bottleneck itself
      too. Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) obtain concept annotations from other datasets or from natural lan-
113
      guage descriptions of concepts via multimodal models, and Oikarinen et al. (2023) and Yang et al.
114
      (2023) first obtain the concept set using GPT-3 and then use a multimodal model to obtain scores for
115   different combinations of input images and concepts. Shang et al. propose incremental concept dis-
116   covery, such that missing concepts to any concept bank can be identified. However, concept labels
117   do not necessarily contain all information needed to accurately perform the downstream task, and
118   can therefore decrease the task accuracy (Mahinpei et al., 2021). Therefore, Zarlenga et al. (2022)
119   propose Concept Embedding Models, where concepts are represented as a supervised vector, such
120   that richer and more meaningful concept semantics can be captured. Barbiero et al. (2023) propose
121   the Deep Concept Reasoner, which builds symbolic rule structures, using similar high-dimensional
122   concept embeddings, such that the decision process based on the high-dimensional concepts is also
123
      interpretable.
124   CBMs can suffer from information leakage, where the model makes use of additional information
125   in the concept space rather than the concept information itself (Mahinpei et al. (2021), Havasi et al.
126   (2022)). This can occur when the model is jointly trained on the concept prediction and down-
127   stream task, and if it uses soft concepts (numerical representations with values between 0 and 1).
128   Information leakage compromises the interpretability and intervenability in soft CBMs, but it can
129
      be addressed when introducing a side-channel (Havasi et al., 2022) or by alignment of the model’s
      representation with an underlying data generation process using disentangled representation learning
130
      (Marconato et al., 2022).
131
132   CBMs and their variants are usually applied to the domain of computer vision, and less frequently
133   to the domain of natural language (Tan et al., 2024), graphs (Barbiero et al., 2023) or tabular data
134   (Zarlenga et al., 2022). In principle, the methodology can be applied to the domain of time series
135
      as well, but defining high-level, meaningful concepts is challenging. Ferfoglia et al. (2024) use
      Signal Temporal Logic (STL) formulas as concept embeddings for time series of cyber-physical
136
      systems (recorded by sensors). STL is a formalism to describe the characteristics of time series
137
      data and contains the temporal operators ‘eventually’, ‘globally’ and ‘until’, besides the classical
138   propositional logic operators. STL formulas can be converted into a natural language description
139   such as “the temperature should never exceed a certain threshold for more than a specified duration”,
140   and can therefore be human-interpretable concepts. The authors use these concepts as bottleneck for
141   anomaly detection in time series.

146   2.2   K NOWLEDGE T RANSFER WITH C ENTERED K ERNEL A LIGNMENT
147
148
      Inspired by neuroscience, Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) measures the similarity between dif-
149
      ferent representations from neural networks (Kornblith et al., 2019). CKA captures intuitive notions
150   of similarity between representations. It can be used to identify (1) correspondences between layers
151   in the same network trained from different initializations, (2) correspondences in layers across dif-
152   ferent architectures and (3) across models trained on different datasets. To obtain the score, firstly,
153   the similarity between every pair of examples in each representation separately is measured using a
154   pre-defined kernel, and then the obtained similarity structures are compared. We refer to Kornblith
155   et al. (2019) for more details.
156   The CKA score can be used to transfer knowledge between different models when included in the
157   loss function, which is shown by Tian et al. (2023). In this work, the authors study Knowledge
158   Distillation between a teacher and student model, and incorporate CKA into the loss function to
159   transfer feature representation knowledge from the pretrained model (teacher) to the incremental
160   learning model (student). The goal is to encourage the incremental learning model not to forget
161   previously learned knowledge, while continuously learning new knowledge, so that it is able to
      adapt to a dynamic environment (Parisi et al., 2019).

      2.3   AUTOFORMER
163
164   The Autoformer is a Transformer-based model for long-term time series forecasting, as introduced
165   by Wu et al. (2021). The model’s encoder-decoder architecture is inspired from time series decom-
166   position, and contains two major modifications to the original Transformer architecture. Firstly, the
167   Autoformer contains decomposition blocks, such that the long-term trend information can be sepa-
168   rated from the seasonal information. Secondly, Autoformer employs an auto-correlation mechanism
169   instead of self-attention, such that similarities can be measured on subseries 1 . An overview of the
170
      architecture from the original paper is provided in Appendix A.
171   More specifically, the Autoformer can be regarded as a function f : RI×d ×RI×4 ×RO×4 → RO×d ,
172   where I is the number of input time steps, O is the number of future time steps, and d is the number
173   of variables in the time series. The first and the second component of the input correspond to values
174   denoted as X ∈ RI×d , and timestamps denoted as T ∈ RI×4 , respectively. The additional four
175   dimensions of the latter represent four time features at each time step which are hour-of-day, day-
176
      of-week, day-of-month, and day-of-year. The third component of the model input corresponds to the
      future time stamps, for which the output values will be forecasted. Note that this set-up is identical
177
      to the original paper, however, we explicitly introduce a notation for the timestamps to later define
178
      the CKA scores and the intervention.
179
180   The Autoformer consists of an encoder and a decoder, which are both constructed from one or

      by masking the other components with zero (close to the mean).

      component shows a periodicity to the actual hour-of-day feature. The third component is not trained

430   to intervene locally in these concepts to exclusively employ the model with data from its training
431   distribution. To show such benefit of our framework, we evaluate the trained model on data with
      shifted timestamps and compare it with performing an intervention on the shifted concept.

      Interestingly, the two types of bottlenecks show different results under the intervention. Where the

513
      mance for all time shifts in the feed-forward bottleneck. In fact, the performance of the intervention

      is even close to the original performance in the feed-forward bottleneck. These results indicate that
515   the choice of bottleneck location is relevant, and suggest that the concept of time might be repre-
516   sented in a more complex manner in the attention heads. Presumably, intervening in an individual
517   head within the multi-headed auto-correlation mechanism provides more unforeseen consequences
518   than intervening in the slice of a linear layer due to the increased complexity. All in all, the results
519   show that the (feed-forward) bottleneck Autoformer can be effectively employed on the shifted data
520   without re-training, given that the shift is known, and it is shifted in the interpretable concepts.

522   5              D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
523
524   In this work, we propose a training framework based on Concept Bottleneck Models to enforce
525   interpretability of time series Transformers. We introduce a new loss function based on the similarity
526   score CKA of the model’s representations and interpretable concepts. We apply the framework to
527   the Autoformer model using six different datasets. Our results indicate that the overall performance
528   remains unaffected, while the model’s components become more interpretable. Additionally, it
529   becomes possible to perform a local intervention when employing the model after a temporal data
530   shift. The scope of our study has been limited to a single Transformer model, and an interesting
531   direction for future research would be to extend this framework to other Transformer models and
532
      modalities. Additionally, future work could focus on optimizing the number and type of interpretable
      concepts in the bottleneck. We hope our work contributes to a deeper understanding of (time series)
533
      Transformers and their behavior in different domains. In particular, recent progress in the field of
534
      mechanistic interpretability is based on the observation that the residual stream of the Transformer
535   encourages modular solutions, which enables localized concepts or specialized circuitry to perform
536   a specific task. Instead of relying on post-hoc localization of these concepts, our paper presents a
537   demonstration that we can encourage locality of concepts, without a significant loss in performance.

       F    H YPER -PARAMETER S ENSITIVITY
973
974
       To verify the sensitivity to hyperparameter α in the loss function, we train the Autoformer with a
975    feed-forward bottleneck on different values for α, where the bottleneck contains a free component
976    (c = 3) and the model is trained on the electricity dataset. The results are given in Table 5. Interest-
977    ingly, a high value for α provides the (slightly) best performance (i.e., α = 0.7), which corresponds
978    to a high importance for the bottleneck components to be similar to the interpretable concepts. This
979    verifies that training for interpretability does not necessarily hurt the performance, at least not in this
980    set-up.

       H     A PPLICATION OF F RAMEWORK TO VANILLA T RANSFORMER
1243
1244
       To demonstrate the generality of the concept bottleneck framework, we apply it to an additional
1245   Transformer architecture, namely the vanilla Transformer (the original architecture from which all
1246   Transformer models, including all time series Transformers, are derived). We train it using the same
1247   six benchmark datasets and perform a similar, but less extensive, analysis as done for the Autoformer
1248   model.
1249
1250   H.1   P ERFORMANCE A NALYSIS
1251
1252   The performance of the vanilla Transformer model with and without bottleneck is given in Table 6.
1253   We train the bottleneck with a ‘free’ component (the side channel), i.e., with c = 3. The original
1254   Transformer paper does not provide scores for these benchmark forecasting datasets, therefore we
1255
       cannot provide the ‘Original’ scores, as done for the Autoformer. The results show that the vanilla
       Transformer perform, unsurprisingly, worse than the Autoformer, and for most datasets also worse
1256
       than the linear AR model. However, most relevant, for our purposes, is that across the datasets using
1257
       a concept bottleneck does not hurt the overall performance of the vanilla Transformer.

1447
1448   We train an Autoformer model on the synthetic dataset using the concept bottleneck framework.
1449   Each concept in the bottleneck is defined as one of the underlying functions (i.e., f1 , f2 or f3 ),
1450   for which the ground-truth is known by construction. The model contains three encoder layers,
1451   with three attention heads per layer. We apply the bottleneck to the attention heads of the second
1452   encoder layer. Additionally, we train the bottleneck using different values for hyperparameter α,
1453   which controls the weight of the CKA loss in the total loss function (see Section 3.1).
1454   As expected, we find for all values α < 1 that the model is able to forecast the dataset well, see
1455   Figure 23. Note that a low forecasting error cannot be expected for α = 1, because in this edge
1456   case the loss function does not contain any forecasting error. Remarkably, for all other cases, the
1457   performance of the Autoformer seems to improve as α increases. This suggests that properly chosen
       concepts improve the performance of the model, at least when the ground-truth underlying functions

       J        E FFECT OF AR AS S URROGATE M ODEL
1567
1568
       Interestingly, the AR model outperforms the Autoformer for some datasets (see Table 1). This raises
1569   the question whether the AR surrogate model makes up for any loss in performance introduced by
1570   the concept bottleneck.
1571
1572
       To test this, we train an Autoformer without the AR concept. Specifically, we include the time
       concept and a free component in the feed-forward bottleneck. Here, the free component refers to a
1573
       component in the bottleneck that is not included in the CKA loss (see Section 3.2).
1574
1575   The performance on the electricity data for this model is (MSE: 0.206, MAE: 0.321), which is
1576   seemingly identical to the original performance of (MSE: 0.207, MAE: 0.320). This suggests that it
1577   is not the AR head that makes up for the loss in performance. The CKA plots, see Figure 26, verify
1578
       that there is no component in the minimal set-up (without AR) that is very similar to the AR model,
       unlike in the original set-up. So, these results show that the AR model does not add performance to
1579
       the bottleneck model, merely interpretability.
1580
1581   Additionally, we refer the reader to Appendix I, where we perform more experiments on training the
1582   bottleneck without the AR surrogate model.
