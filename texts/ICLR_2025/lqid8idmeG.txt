002   Free Speech or Free of Distortion?

009                                                Abstract
010
011               User-generated content (UGC) on social media platforms is vulnerable
012               to incitements and manipulations, necessitating effective regulations. To
013               address these challenges, those platforms often deploy automated content
014               moderators tasked with evaluating the harmfulness of UGC and filtering
015               out content that violates established guidelines. However, such moderation
016
                  inevitably gives rise to strategic responses from users, who strive to express
                  themselves within the confines of guidelines. Such phenomenons call for a
017
                  careful balance between: 1. ensuring freedom of speech — by minimizing the
018
                  restriction of expression; and 2. reducing social distortion — measured by the
019               total amount of content manipulation. We tackle the problem of optimizing
020               this balance through the lens of mechanism design, aiming at optimizing the
021               trade-off between minimizing social distortion and maximizing free speech.
022               Although determining the optimal trade-off is NP-hard, we propose practical
023               methods to approximate the optimal solution. Additionally, we provide
024               generalization guarantees that determine the amount of finite offline data
025
                  required to effectively approximate the optimal moderator.

      1       Introduction
029
      The internet supports a global ecosystem of social interaction. Many people use social
030
      media to connect with others, engage with news content, share information, and entertain
031   themselves. However, in recent years, the nature of this content and these interactions has
032   raised concerns among policymakers. Social media can be exploited for extremist causes,
033   as well as to spread misinformation and fake news. For example, social bots were used to
034   disrupt the 2016 U.S. presidential election. Another troubling issue is cyberbullying, which
035   especially targets well-known individuals. Some European governments have been trying
036   to curb fake news and hate speech by regulating social media platforms. However, these
037   measures risk suppressing free speech. In this work, we explore the challenge of balancing
038
      free speech with the regulation of social media.
039   More specifically, we consider scenarios where users engage with a harmful social trend to
040   gain more attention on the platform. In these instances, misleading or problematic topics
041   or hashtags gain traction, and the challenge for platforms is to prevent the spread of such
042   rumors. We study a setting where the principal’s goal is to design guidelines to minimize
043
      users’ engagement with such harmful social trends. Our objective is to develop content
      moderators1 so that users are discouraged from following harmful social trends as much
044
      as possible. Simultaneously, we aim to protect individuals’ freedom of speech as much as
045   possible by avoiding unnecessary content removal.
046
047   Inspired by the recent literature on designing machine learning algorithms in the presence
048
      of strategic behavior (e.g. Hardt et al. (2016)), we formulate this problem as follows. In a
      distributional setting, first, the principal commits to a content moderator f . Users observe
049
      the deployed content moderator and a social trend e and best-respond by changing their
050   original content from x to z so that their utility is maximized. We define the users’ utility
051   function as their reward at the manipulated state z minus the cost of manipulation from x
052   to z. Their reward function depends on two factors, first, the manipulated state z is marked
053

      as benign by the moderator; otherwise, their content gets removed by the moderator and
055   the user receives zero utility. Second, assuming their manipulated content z remains on the
056   platform, how well it aligns with the trend e. However, unlike the strategic classification
057   problem where the goal is to design classifiers that have high accuracy considering the
058   strategic response of the users, our goal is to first discourage the users from manipulating
059   their content since we assume that the social trend that they try to follow is harmful, and
060   second, protect the users’ freedom of speech as much as possible.

062   1.1   Our Results and Techniques:
063
064   Optimization problem. We model this problem as a constrained optimization problem,
065
      where the objective is to minimize the average social distortion which is the average distance
      between the original and manipulated contents of the users. We argue that this is equivalent
066
      to maximizing another objective which we call social distortion mitigation. For each user,
067   social distortion mitigation captures the distance between their ideal manipulation state z ′
068   assuming there are no moderators in place, and their final manipulated state z ∗ . The key
069   idea here is that the user decides to move to z ∗ instead of z ′ when z ′ gets removed by the
070   moderator. The overall constrained optimization problem is to maximize the average social
071   distortion mitigation subject to removing a bounded number of manipulated contents had
072   the users manipulated to their ideal location z ′ .
073   Sample complexity results. For any filter class H, we derive sample complexity results that
074   guarantee if a sufficiently large set of samples S are drawn from an underlying distribution D,
075   then for any filter h ∈ H, the average social distortion and the fraction of filtered-out examples
076   on S and D are approximately the same. Our sample complexity results are in terms of the
077   VC-dimension of the filter class H, and the Pseduo-dimension of their corresponding social
078   distortion mitigation function class (Theorem 1). Furthermore, we bound the VC-dimension
079
      of the filter class H, and the Pseduo-dimension of their corresponding social distortion
      mitigation function class for some specific classes of filters, i.e. linear filters, classes of
080
      piece-wise linear functions, and some specific kernels (Proposition 3).
081
082   Computational hardness results. We demonstrate that even for a class of linear filters,
083   given a set of agents, finding a linear filter that minimizes average social distortion while
084
      filtering out at most k contents is NP-hard (Theorem 2). To establish the NP-hardness
      result, we show that another closely related combinatorial problem is NP-hard: Given a set
085
      of points, finding a hyperplane that maximizes the number of points on it while allowing at
086   most k points on the positive side of the hyperplane is NP-hard. By allowing the maximum
087   number of points on the hyperplane, intuitively, we are minimizing the social distortion since
088   social distortion for each content only decreases as it approaches the boundary of the filter.
089
      Experiments. We consider the computation of the optimal linear filter in the offline
090
      setting, where the platform has access to a set of clean data2 . Despite the computational
091   hardness established earlier, we propose an empirical approach to approximately compute
092   the optimal filter by introducing a soft version of the freedom of speech violation constraint.
093   By reformulating the constrained optimization problem as an empirical loss minimization
094   under a smoothed, quasi-convex surrogate loss, we show that the platform can achieve any
095   desired trade-off between minimizing social distortion and preserving freedom of speech.

130   2    Problem Setting
131
132   Let X ⊂ Rd denote the feature space of each user’s generated content (UGC). Throughout
133   the paper we assume that X is convex and compact. Our problem formulation is built upon
134   the interplay between a set of of n users on a social media platform and an automated
135
      content moderator M, which we elaborate on in the following.
136   User Representation: A user indexed by i is represented by a tuple ui = (xi , ci ), where
137   xi ∈ X is the feature vector of ui ’s generated content representing her original intention of
138   expression and ci denotes the manipulation cost. We consider the case when the user wants
139   to tweak the original message xi to zi to better align with a global ongoing social trend e,
      at a marginal cost ci . We outline our model in detail as follows.
140
141   Convex Content Moderator:
142
      The role of a content moderator M is to regulate published content, ensuring it adheres to
143   platform guidelines. Without loss of generality, M can be regarded as an indicator function
144   I[f (x; w) ≤ 0], where 0 indicates that the content is flagged as problematic and should be
145   filtered, while 1 indicates it is benign. For simplicity, we define the content moderator as the
146   function f (x; w) : Rd → R, parameterized by w, and refer to the set x ∈ X : f (x; w) ≤ 0 as
147   the benign region associated with f . The output of f can be interpreted as a harmfulness
148   score for each content. In this work, we focus on moderators f that induce convex benign
149
      regions3 . This assumption is justified by the natural property that if two pieces of content,
      x1 and x2 , are both benign, their linear combination λx1 + (1 − λ)x2 should also be benign
150
      in the feature space. This property directly translates into the convexity of the benign region.
151
152   User’s Strategic Response: With the components outlined above, we can formulate a
153   utility function to capture the potential strategic behavior of a user and predict her response

      characterizes the user’s utility when modifying her published content from x to z:

202   by x. For UGC near the filter boundary, moderation can mitigate distortion by incentivizing
203   users to align their content with platform guidelines. Thus, the platform can intuitively
204   reduce overall social distortion by encouraging more UGC to move closer to this boundary.
205   However, this strategy comes with a trade-off: the risk of filtering out certain UGC, potentially
206   infringing on users’ freedom of expression. This presents a key challenge for the platform—how
207   to balance reducing social distortion with preserving free speech. In the following section,
208
      we formalize this problem and explore its complexity and possible solutions.

210   3   The Social Distortion, Freedom of Speech, and their
211       Trade-off
212
213   In this section, we formally introduce the concept of social distortion and explain why content
214   moderation can reduce social distortion but at the potential cost of infringing on freedom of
215   speech, thereby creating a concrete challenge of balancing these two considerations. From
      Proposition 1, we observe two significant effects of deploying a content moderator f : first, it


                                                            4
216
      discourages users from excessively following social trends e, which is beneficial; second, it
217   may flag some UGC as harmful, potentially leading to user churn. The first effect can be
218   quantified using the social distortion metric, which measures the displacement of users who
219   were initially on the benign side, under the strategic environment shaped by the moderator
220   f . The second effect can be assessed by the proportion of users who remain on the platform,
221   serving as an index for freedom of speech, as users who leave the platform due to their
222   content being flagged harmful experience a form of expression suppression.

229   The social distortion function D, defined in Eq. (3), quantifies the manipulation effort of a
230   user’s content x as the squared L2 distance between the original feature x and the user’s
231   best response under a moderator f . This measures how much the user’s strategic adaptation
232   diverges from her true expressive intent x. Importantly, our definition of social distortion
233   applies only to users whose initial features x are not filtered by f (i.e., f (x) ≤ 0). This is
234   because the strategic behavior of users with f (x) > 0 does not contribute to the distortion
235
      negatively. As Proposition 1 suggests, users with f (x) > 0 are either filtered out—meaning
      their content is not distorted—or they adjust x to align with platform guidelines, which
236
      is considered a beneficial manipulation and thus should not be counted as distortion. In
237   contrast, for users with f (x) ≤ 0, their strategic behavior often reflects a shift toward
238   following a harmful social trend e, diverging from their original expressive ideas, which
239   constitutes social distortion.

      7          Conclusion
533
      We addressed the challenge of designing content moderators that reduce engagement with
534
      harmful social trends while preserving freedom of speech. By modeling the problem as a
535   constrained optimization task, we introduced the concept of social distortion mitigation and
536   provided generalization guarantees based on the VC-dimension and Pseudo-dimension of the
537   filter function class. While we established the computational hardness of finding optimal
538   linear filters, we provide an empirically efficient approximation approach that enables the
539   platform to achieve any desirable trade-offs. Our findings highlight the need for efficient
      algorithms and further exploration of more flexible filtering mechanisms.
