                                                  A BSTRACT
011
               In recent years, compression of large language models (LLMs) has emerged as an
012            important problem to enable language model deployment on resource-constrained
013            devices, reduce computational costs, and mitigate the environmental footprint of
014            large-scale AI infrastructure. In this paper, we lay down the foundations of LLM
015            quantization from a convex optimization perspective and propose a quantization
016            technique that builds on this foundation for optimum quantization outcomes. Our
017            quantization framework, CVXQ, scales to models containing hundreds of billions
018            of weight parameters and provides users with the ﬂexibility to compress models
019            to any speciﬁed model size, post-training. A reference implementation of CVXQ
020
               can be obtained from.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have become a versatile framework for solving a large number of
025
      problems in natural language processing, from text translation and summarization to conversational
026
      AI and automatic generation of radiology reports. While LLMs have surpassed traditional methods
027   in many of these tasks, they can involve tens or hundreds of billions of weight parameters (!), and
028   this makes their deployment onto devices with limited resources challenging—model weights and
029   activations far exceed the available on-chip memory so that activations need to be loaded from and
030   saved to oﬀ-chip memory throughout inference, rendering LLM inference memory-bound (Yuan et
031   al., 2024). Xis greatly hinders the usability of LLMs particularly in time-sensitive applications and
032   exacerbates the environmental footprint of large-scale AI infrastructure required by LLMs.
033   One way to reduce the memory requirements of large models for inference is by compressing (that
034   (is, simplifying) the representation of the model weights and activations after training. Xis can be
035   achieved via weight pruning, quantization of activations and weights, or PCA-type dimensionality
036   reduction of weight matrices. Out of these, quantization of weights and activation has proven to be
037   particularly useful for compressing models to very low bit depths or arbitrary user-speciﬁed model
038   sizes (Dettmers et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022; Frantar et al., 2022; Frantar & Alistarh, 2022; Kim et
039   al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Guan et al., 2024). Using state-of-the-art quantization
040
      techniques, it is now possible to compress 10–100 billion-parameter LLMs to 3–4 bits per weight
      on average with a negligible loss of model accuracy (Chee et al., 2024; Frantar et al., 2022; Lin et
041
      al., 2024), facilitating LLM inference on a single consumer-grade GPU for example.
042
043   Although signiﬁcant advances have been made in LLM quantization recently, current approaches to
044   model quantization still lead to considerably reduced model accuracy at low bit depths, with many
045   methods ﬁne-tuning model weights during quantization (Frantar et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024; Chee
046
      et al., 2024). Xis makes such quantization methods less suitable for the quantization of activations
      during inference, where ﬁne-tuning would lead to unacceptable delays in the inference pipeline.
047
048   Given the symmetry between weights and hidden states in matrix multiplications, achieving fast and
049   accurate quantization of both weights and activations can be crucial for enhancing computational
050   eﬃciency and prediction accuracy of LLMs, as well as for informing hardware design. Xis work
051
      aims to address gaps in the current model compression literature and advance compression methods
      further to enable accurate and eﬃcient inference on quantized LLMs.
052
053   In this paper—the ﬁrst of a three-part series—we tackle the problem of LLM compression using the


                                                        1

054   framework of convex optimization. We begin with the problem of weight quantization and analyze
055   how a model’s weights should be quantized to maximize quantized model accuracy for a given bit
056   size. We then propose a stochastic gradient descent-type algorithm to solve this problem exactly and
057   eﬃciently, post-training—in minutes for billion-parameter models and in a few hours for 10–100-
058   billion-parameter models. Compared with the recent OPTQ family of quantization methods (Frantar
059   et al., 2022; Frantar & Alistarh, 2022; Huang et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; van Baalen et al., 2024)
060   in which weights are ﬁne-tuned during quantization, our approach spends virtually zero time on the
061
      actual quantization once the optimum bit depths have been determined. Xis makes our framework
      also suited for quantizing intermediate activations, which can further reduce the memory footprint
062
      of batched inference. Using our bespoke mixed precision CUDA kernel (see Appendix A), we also
063
      accelerate matrix-vector multiply relative to the ﬂoating-point matrix-vector multiply of cuBLAS.

066   2    P REVIOUS W ORK
067
068   Early work on neural network model quantization can be attributed to Vanhoucke et al. (2011), who
069
      demonstrated that 8-bit integer arithmetic can be used for network training and inference without
      incurring a signiﬁcant loss of accuracy. More generally, quantization-aware training (QAT) (Zhou
070
      et al., 2017; Jacob et al., 2018; D. Zhang et al., 2018; Esser et al., 2019; Y. Choi et al., 2017; Wang
071
      et al., 2019) integrates the quantization process into training by allowing the model to adapt to the
072   reduced precision in weights (Esser et al., 2019; Jacob et al., 2018; D. Zhang et al., 2018; Zhou et
073   al., 2017) and activations (Y. Choi et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019) by determining the optimum bit
074   depth (Wang et al., 2019; D. Zhang et al., 2018) and step size (Esser et al., 2019) using back-prop
075   to facilitate the gradient to ﬂow through quantization operators. One shortcoming of QAT methods
076   is that model training needs to be repeated for diﬀerent quantized model sizes and accuracy, which
077   makes them less suitable for quantizing larger models such as LLMs.
078   More recent quantization techniques for language and vision models aim to facilitate compression
079   of already trained models for rapid deployment without further training (Dong et al., 2019; Chen et
080   al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022; Frantar et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2023; Xiao et
081   al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024). Xese approaches
082   quantize model weights to 3–4 or 8 bits for integer-arithmetic-only inference (Jacob et al., 2018)
083   using mixed bit depth quantization (Wang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021) or by a separate handling
084   of outlier channels (Zhao et al., 2019) to improve the accuracy of the quantized model. Loss-aware
085
      quantization techniques (Hou & Kwok, 2018; Nahshan et al., 2020; Qu et al., 2020) seek to minimize
      accuracy loss in quantized models by calibrating quantization and biases on calibration data. Data-
086
      free quantization methods (Nagel et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020; K. Choi et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2023)
087
      attempt to remove the need for real calibration data by matching the distribution of weights instead
088   (Nagel et al., 2019) or using synthetic data in place of real calibration data (K. Choi et al., 2021).
089
090   For LLM compression in particular, an extension to the Optimum Brain Surgeon (OBS) algorithm
091
      (Hassibi & Stork, 1992) known as GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) was proposed for the quantization of
      1–100 billion parameter models. Further recent extensions to GPTQ (Dettmers et al., 2023; Lee et
092
      al., 2024) incorporate the handling of sensitive weights by scaling or simply by retaining the original
093
      weight values similarly to (Lin et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023). Here, we use convex optimization for
094   ﬁne-granularity mixed-precision weight quantization, overcoming the combinatorial nature of
095   determining the optimal bit depth (0, 1, …, 8 bits) per channel to better attend to channel sensitivity.

477
      Formulating weight quantization as a convex optimization problem, as we have done here, bestows

518   Surgeon algorithm (Hassibi & Stork, 1992), which is a convex formulation for weight pruning, not
519   quantization (see Appendix F). As a result, these methods inherit the need for ﬁne-tuning as part of
520   the quantization process, making them less suitable for the quantization of activations at inference
521
      time, where ﬁne-tuning would lead to unacceptable delays in the inference pipeline.
522   Our experimental results indicate that an accurate characterization of the quantization problem can
523   indeed lead to better compression outcomes. While the smaller OPT-125M model is too limited for
524   practical use in many situations, its relative incompressibility helps contrast the performance of the
525   diﬀerent weight quantization methods themselves (Table 1). With larger models like OPT-66B and
526   Llama 2-66B, most approaches (including RTN) perform similarly, suggesting that larger language
527
      models are more compressible in general. At ﬁrst glance, RTN may seem suﬃcient for quantizing
      larger models. However, RTN-quantized models lead to severely reduced accuracy on downstream
528
      tasks such as GSM8K (Table 4 (a)), which highlights the importance of validating the accuracy of
529
      quantized models across multiple tasks and datasets (Jaiswal et al., 2024). Increasing the number of
530   calibration examples (from 128 to 1024) did not noticeably aﬀect the quantized model’s perplexity
531   on C4 (± 0.01), which agrees with ﬁndings from previous reports (Hubara et al., 2021).
532
      Our CUDA matmul kernel (Appendix A) accelerates matrix-vector multiplication by dequantizing
533
      mixed precision weights to ﬂoating point representation dynamically and multiplying with a ﬂoating
534
      point activation vector. For the 12288 × 49152 weight matrix of OPT-175B at 3 bits per weight on
535   average, our kernel provides a 3.8x speed up over FP16 matrix-vector multiplication using cuBLAS
536   matmul on Nvidia A6000. Accelerated matrix-vector multiplication, along with our low complexity
537   quantization approach (once the optimal bit depths have been determined) allows us to apply CVXQ
538   also to activation quantization, where quantization eﬃciency becomes paramount. Joint quantization
539   of activation and weights is discussed in Part 2.
