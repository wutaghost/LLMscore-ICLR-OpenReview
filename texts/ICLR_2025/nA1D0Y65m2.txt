011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite the remarkable empirical performance of distributional reinforcement
014            learning (RL), its theoretical advantages over classical RL are still not fully
015
               understood. Starting with Categorical Distributional RL (CDRL), we propose
               that the potential superiority of distributional RL can be attributed to a derived
016
               distribution-matching regularization by applying a return density function decom-
017
               position technique. This less-studied regularization in the distributional RL con-
018            text aims to capture additional knowledge of return distribution beyond only its
019            expectation, contributing to an augmented reward signal in policy optimization.
020            In contrast to the standard entropy regularization in MaxEnt RL, which explicitly
021            encourages exploration by promoting diverse actions, the regularization derived
022            from CDRL implicitly updates policies to align the learned policy with environ-
023            mental uncertainty. Finally, extensive experiments substantiate the significance of
024            this uncertainty-aware regularization derived from distributional RL on the em-
025            pirical benefits over classical RL. Our study offers a new perspective from the
026
               exploration to explain the benefits of adopting distributional learning in RL.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The fundamental characteristics of classical reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms, such as Q-
031   learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018; Watkins & Dayan, 1992), relies on estimating the expectation
032   of discounted cumulative rewards that an agent observes while interacting with the environment.
033   In contrast to the expectation-based RL, a novel branch of algorithms, termed distributional RL,
034   seeks to estimate the entire distribution of total returns and has achieved state-of-the-art performance
035   across a diverse array of environments (Bellemare et al., 2017a; Dabney et al., 2018b;a; Yang et al.,
036   2019; Zhou et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2020; Wenliang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024b). Meanwhile,
037   distributional RL inherits enhanced capabilities in areas, such as risk-sensitive control (Dabney et al.,
038   2018a; Lim & Malik, 2022; Chen et al., 2024), offline learning (Wu et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2021),
039
      policy exploration (Cho et al., 2023; Mavrin et al., 2019; Rowland et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2024b),
      robustness (Sun et al., 2023; Sui et al., 2023), optimization (Sun et al., 2024a; Rowland et al., 2023;
040
      Kuang et al., 2023), and statistical inference (Zhang et al., 2023).
041
042   Motivation: Interpreting the Benefits of Being (Categorical) Distributional in RL. Despite the
043   impressive empirical success of various distributional RL algorithms, our comprehension of their
044   advantages in RL, especially within the general function approximation framework and practical
045
      implementations, remains incomplete. Early work (Lyle et al., 2019) demonstrated that in many re-
      alizations of tabular and linear approximation settings, distributional RL behaves similarly to classic
046
      RL, suggesting that its benefits are mainly realized in the non-linear approximation setting. Al-
047
      though their findings offer profound insights, their analysis, based on a coupled update method,
048   overlooks several factors, such as the optimization effect under various losses. The statistical ben-
049   efits of quantile temporal difference (QTD), employed in quantile distributional RL algorithms like
050   QR-DQN (Dabney et al., 2018b), were highlighted in (Rowland et al., 2024; 2023), which posited
051   that the robust estimation of QTD fosters the benefits in stochastic environments. The foundational
052   theoretical aspects of CDRL were first discussed in (Rowland et al., 2018); however, the empirical
053   superiority of adopting categorical distributional remains under-explored. Furthermore, recent stud-
      ies (Wang et al., 2023; 2024) elucidate the benefits of distributional RL by introducing the novel

054
      small-loss and second-order PAC bounds, demonstrating enhanced sample efficiency in specific
055   cases, such as those with small achievable costs. Yet, their findings are not directly based on typi-
056   cal distributional RL algorithms commonly used in practice, such as C51 (Bellemare et al., 2017a)
057   or QR-DQN. Therefore, it is imperative to close this gap between the theoretical explanation and
058   practical deployment for distributional RL algorithms.
059
      Contributions. In this paper, we interpret the potential advantages of distributional learning in RL
060
      over classical RL, specifically focusing on CDRL, the pioneering family within distributional RL.
061   We examine these benefits through the lens of regularized exploration effect, offering a distinct per-
062   spective relative to existing literature. Our investigation begins with the decomposition of CDRL’s
063   objective function into an expectation-based term and a distribution-matching regularization, fa-
064   cilitated by our proposed return density decomposition technique. This regularization acts as an
065   augmented reward in the actor-critic framework, encouraging policies to explore states and actions
066   whose current return distribution estimates lag far behind the target ones, determined by environ-
067   mental uncertainty. This derived regularization from the objective function of distributional learning
068   promotes an uncertainty-aware exploration effect, diverging from the commonly used exploration
069
      for diverse actions in MaxEnt RL (Williams & Peng, 1991; Haarnoja et al., 2018a;b). Addition-
      ally, we propose a theoretically grounded algorithm called Distribution-Entropy-Regularized Actor
070
      Critic, interpolating between expectation-based and distributional RL. Empirical evidence under-
071
      scores the pivotal role of the uncertainty-aware entropy regularization in CDRL’s empirical success
072   over expectation-based RL on both Atari games and MuJoCo environments. We further elucidate
073   the distinct roles that the uncertainty-aware entropy in distributional RL and the explicit vanilla en-
074   tropy in MaxEnt RL play by exploring their mutual impacts on learning performance. This opens
075   new avenues for future research in this domain. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
076
077          • We propose a return density decomposition technique to decompose the objective function
078            in CDRL. We argue that the derived regularization can promote uncertainty-aware explo-
079
               ration, which interprets the benefits of adopting distributional learning in RL.
080          • Within the actor-critic framework, we compare the cross-entropy-based uncertainty-aware
081            regularization from distributional RL and vanilla entropy regularization in MaxEnt RL. A
082
               byproduct interpretable algorithm is further introduced, interpolating between classical and
               distributional RL.
083
084          • Empirically, we verify the uncertainty-aware regularization effect on the performance ad-
085            vantage of distributional RL and explore the mutual impacts of two types of regularization.
086
      Outline. We provide the related work and background knowledge in Sections 2 and 3, respectively.
087
      We begin by interpreting the benefits of distributional learning as uncertainty-aware exploration in
088   value-based CDRL in Section 4. We further study this exploration benefit within the policy gradient
089   framework in Section 5, where we directly compare it with the vanilla entropy regularization in
090   MaxEnt RL. Extensive experiments demonstrate the regularized exploration benefit of distributional
091   RL and its mutual impact with vanilla entropy regularization in MaxEnt RL in Section 6.

      Distributional Learning via Categorical Representation. Categorical learning has been widely
096   employed, with advantages in representation (Pan et al., 2019; Jang et al., 2016) and optimiza-
097   tion (Imani & White, 2018; Sun et al., 2024a). The empirical superiority of categorical distribution
098   learning has increasingly gained attention in various RL tasks (Farebrother et al., 2024), even beyond
099   the classical category of CDRL. Thus, a pressing need exists to examine the theoretical foundations
100   of categorical distributional learning, particularly in the RL context. The perspective of uncertainty-
101   aware regularization-based exploration that our research introduces adds a significant theoretical
102   understanding of the benefits of being categorical distributional in RL.
103   Exploration in RL in the Entropy Principle. As a general and effective mechanism, the en-
104   tropy principle has been extensively studied to enhance the exploration in RL. Classical algo-
105   rithms are established upon the MaxEnt RL framework (Williams & Peng, 1991), including soft
106   Q-learning (Haarnoja et al., 2017), Soft Actor Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018a) and their vari-
107   ants (Han & Sung, 2021). The key characteristic in MaxEnt RL is to directly incorporate the en-
      tropy term regarding the policy in the objective function, while other works introduce the variation

      in decision-making in distinct ways. These works include (Mavrin et al., 2019), which utilizes the
109   variance of return distribution to promote the exploration, and (Lee et al., 2021), which relies on the
110   ensemble technique. By contrast, we show that distributional learning in RL implicitly encourages a
111   distinct uncertainty-aware exploration driven by optimizing the derived cross-entropy-based regular-
112   ization that measures the discrepancy between the agent’s uncertain estimate and the environment.

284   MaxEnt RL motivate us to probe their underlying connection, especially in exploration.

513   Entropy (VE) in MaxEnt RL or Uncertainty-aware Entropy (UE) in CDRL play distinct roles in the
514   policy learning when used simultaneously, including mutual improvement or potential interference.
515   DSAC stands for Distributional SAC, as initially introduced by (Ma et al., 2020). We perform an ab-

526
      butional RL updates the policy to explore states and the associated actions whose current return
527   distribution estimate lags far behind the correct one determined by the environment uncertainty.

530   7     D ISCUSSIONS AND C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we interpret the benefits of CDRL over classical RL as uncertainty-aware regularization
533   derived through the return density decomposition. In contrast to encouraging diverse actions for
534   the exploration in MaxEnt RL, the uncertainty-aware regularization in CDRL promotes to explore
535   states where the environment uncertainty is largely underestimated. This novel perspective from the
536
      exploration explains the benefits of (categorical) distributional learning in RL.
537   Limitations and Future Work. The uncertainty-aware regularization with the exploration effect is
538   founded on CDRL. However, it remains elusive whether extending the uncertainty-aware exploration
539   in CDRL to general distributional RL is feasible, given that the analytical techniques in other classes,
      such as QR-DQN, are highly different from CDRL. We leave this extension as future work.

       In summary, the first decomposition method decomposes the quantile-base distributional loss into
1654
       the mean-related and residual terms. After a mild approximation, the mean-related term can be
1655   simplified as a least-squared loss equipped with an expected quantiles estimator. Combining the
1656   equivalence regarding the limiting behavior of the expected quantiles, the mean -related term is thus
1657   approximately equivalent to the standard least-squared loss used in classical RL, asymptotically
1658   satisfying the mean-preserving property in distributional dynamic programming. Moreover, the
1659   residual term is able to capture the return distribution information beyond its expectation. In the
1660   context of uncertain-aware regularized exploration in our paper, the residual term plays the similar
1661   role of the cross-entropy-based regularization derived in Proposition 2 of CDRL.
