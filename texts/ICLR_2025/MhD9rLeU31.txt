011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013
               We present a comprehensive benchmark to evaluate the performance of deep
014            learning models on limit order book (LOB) data. Our work makes four significant
015            contributions: (i) We evaluate existing LOB models on a proprietary futures LOB
016            dataset to examine the transferability of LOB model performance between various
017            assets; (ii) We are the first to benchmark existing LOB models on the mid-price
018            return forecasting (MPRF) task. (iii) We present the first benchmark study to
019            evaluate SOTA time series forecasting models on the MPRF task to bridge the two
020            fields of general-purpose time series forecasting and LOB time series forecasting;
021            and (iv) we propose an architecture of convolutional Cross-Variate Mixing Layers
022
               (CVML) as an add-on to any deep learning multivariate time series model to
               significantly enhance MPRF performance on LOB data. Our empirical results
023
               highlight the value of our benchmark results on our proprietary futures LOB dataset,
024
               demonstrating a performance gap between the commonly used open-source stock
025            LOB dataset and our futures dataset. Furthermore, the results demonstrate that
026            LOB-aware model design is essential for achieving optimal prediction performance
027            on LOB datasets. Most importantly, our results show that our proposed CVML
028            architecture brings about an average improvement of 244.9% to various time series
029            models’ mid-price return forecasting performance.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The Limit Order Book (LOB) serves as the order-matching engine for all exchanges, providing
035   the most granular market time series data for analysis (Weber, 1999; Ntakaris et al., 2018). As
036   a universal data format across markets and assets (e.g., stocks and futures), LOB contains high-
037   resolution macroeconomic information crucial for asset price predictions (Chan et al., 2005; Harris &
038
      Panchapagesan, 2005; Large, 2007; Avellaneda & Stoikov, 2008; Roşu, 2009; Eisler et al., 2012).
039   Two primary research tracks in LOB data analysis are Mid-Price Trend Prediction (MPTP) and
040   Mid-Price Return Forecasting (MPRF). Inspired by the success of deep learning in domains like
041   natural language processing (Vaswani et al., 2023) and computer vision (He et al., 2016), researchers
042   have proposed deep learning models for LOB analysis and general time series predictions (Zeng
043   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024).
044   Despite these advancements, the field lacks comprehensive benchmark studies comparing model
045   performance. While Prata et al. (2024) conducted a benchmark study for MPTP, it was limited to
046   stock datasets, excluding other asset types. For MPRF, which encompasses both LOB modeling
047   and time series prediction, no benchmark exists that evaluates deep learning-based LOB models and
048   state-of-the-art time series forecasting models on LOB data. This work aims to address these gaps in
049   both MPTP and MPRF tasks.
050   To address these limitations, we conduct a comprehensive study on both MPTP and MPRF tasks. For
051   MPTP, we evaluate state-of-the-art LOB models using the open-source FI-2010 stock dataset and
052   our proprietary futures dataset, CHF-2023, measuring each model’s F1 scores across five different
053   prediction horizons. We also conduct an ablation study to assess the predictive power of various LOB
      feature types, including basic LOB features, time-insensitive features, and time-sensitive features.

054
      For the MPRF task, we evaluate both LOB-specific models and state-of-the-art time series forecasting
055   models on FI-2010. We assess their forecasting performance using three metrics: Mean-Square Error
056   (MSE), Coefficient of Determination (R2 ), and Pearson Correlation (Corr). Additionally, we propose
057   a novel neural architecture featuring cross-variate mixing layers, called CVML, designed to enhance
058   the forecasting performance of existing time series models on LOB data.
059
      Our experiments reveal several key findings. For MPTP, we observe inconsistent model performance
060
      rankings between the stock and futures LOB datasets, with models generally performing worse
061   on the futures data. This suggests limited generalizability of current LOB model architectures and
062   highlights the distinct underlying characteristics of LOB data from different assets. Our ablation study
063   confirms that each feature subset contributes unique predictive power, underscoring the importance
064   of comprehensive feature selection in LOB modeling. For MPRF, our results demonstrate that LOB
065   models incorporating LOB-specific inductive bias significantly outperform general-purpose time
066   series forecasting models. The latter, lacking LOB-specific design considerations, show minimal
067   forecasting power when directly applied to LOB datasets. Our proposed CVML module significantly
068   improves the forecasting capabilities of all benchmarked time series models, extending their predictive
069
      power to the more complex and noisy LOB time series data.
070   Contributions. We summarize our contributions as follows:
071
      • We evaluate existing LOB models on the MPTP task using a proprietary futures LOB dataset,

        CHF-2023, to assess the transferability of models designed for stock LOB data across asset classes.

      • We present the first benchmark on the MPRF task in the literature.

      • We pioneer benchmarking state-of-the-art time series forecasting models on the MPRF task,
        bridging the gap between general-purpose and LOB-specific time series forecasting.

      • We propose a novel Cross-Variate Mixing Layer (CVML) as an add-on to existing time series

086   2 R ELATED W ORKS
087   Price Trend Prediction Surveys. Several comprehensive benchmark surveys have examined deep
088   learning applications in price trend prediction (Ozbayoglu et al., 2020; Sezer et al., 2020; Jiang, 2021),
089   each with a distinct focus. Jiang (2021) emphasize reproducibility, analyzing model architectures,
090   evaluation metrics, and implementations in stock price and market index prediction studies from
091   2017 to 2019. A follow-up study by Kumbure et al. (2022) extend this analysis to datasets and input
092   variables commonly used in stock market predictions. Hu et al. (2021) review 86 papers on stock and
093
      foreign exchange price prediction, while other surveys (Rundo et al., 2019; Mintarya et al., 2023)
      compare machine learning and deep learning methods in stock market prediction, concluding that
094
      deep learning approaches generally offer superior accuracy. Nti et al. (2020) broaden the scope
095
      beyond technical analysis, reviewing 122 papers from 2007 to 2018 covering technical, fundamental,
096   and combined analyses. Additionally, Shah et al. (2019) evaluate the real-world applicability of
097   models through backtesting performance. Notably, these surveys do not include benchmarks for
098   prediction models on Limit Order Book (LOB) data. The most relevant work is a benchmark study
099   by Prata et al. (2024) on mid-price trend prediction models using LOB data. However, our work
100   addresses three key limitations of their study: We use a proprietary dataset with a time range 200 times
101   larger than the open-source dataset they employed. We benchmark models on both stock and futures
102   datasets, whereas they focused solely on stocks. We extend our analysis to include the mid-price
103   forecasting problem (a regression task), benchmarking both LOB models and state-of-the-art time
104
      series forecasting models, in addition to the mid-price trend prediction task (a classification problem)
      they addressed. These enhancements allow our study to provide a more comprehensive and diverse
105
      evaluation of LOB-based prediction models across different assets and problem types.
106
107   Time Series Forecasting Models. The success of deep learning in natural language processing
      and computer vision has significantly influenced time series forecasting, with deep learning models

      becoming predominant in this field. Transformer-based architectures, in particular, have emerged
109   as the leading approach for multivariate time series forecasting (Nie et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023).
110   However, recent developments have shown that models based on linear layers (Zeng et al., 2023;
111   Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Oreshkin et al., 2020; Challu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022) can
112   achieve comparable performance to transformer-based models. While convolutional neural networks
113   (O’shea & Nash, 2015; Wu et al., 2023; Franceschi et al., 2019) and recurrent networks (Hochreiter
114   & Schmidhuber, 1997; Lai et al., 2018; Franceschi et al., 2019) have also been applied to time
115   series forecasting, their performance generally lags behind that of transformers and linear-based
116   architectures. Notably, there has been limited overlap between general time series forecasting and
117
      Limit Order Book (LOB) time series analysis. To date, no comprehensive benchmarking of state-of-
      the-art time series forecasting models on the complex LOB time series data has been conducted. To
118
      address this gap, our paper selects four state-of-the-art time series forecasting models, encompassing
119
      both transformer-based and linear architectures. Our aim is to bridge the divide between general-
120   purpose time series forecasting and the more specialized field of LOB time series forecasting,
121   providing insights into the applicability and performance of these models on LOB data.

123   3    BACKGROUND
124   Limit Order Book (LOB). Global exchanges use matching engines to pair orders from bid and ask
125   sides of market participants. The Limit Order Book is the essential data structure organizing these
126   orders, reflecting market supply and demand. Three common order types exist: 1) Market orders
127   are requests to buy or sell a specified number of shares at the best available price, usually executed
128   immediately. 2) Limit orders are requests to buy or sell a specified number of shares at a specified
129   price, often queued for matching due to price constraints. 3) Cancel orders are requests to withdraw
130   previously submitted limit orders.

174   4       E XPERIMENTS
175
      We conduct all experiments using 3 seeds to minimize the effect of random initialization.
176
177   Datasets: 1) FI-2010 (Ntakaris et al., 2018)1 : This Limit Order Book (LOB) dataset includes 10
178   trading days of data from five Finnish companies on the NASDAQ Nordic stock market. It was
179   designed to evaluate machine learning models’ performance on stock price trend prediction. 2)
180
      CHF-2023: To address FI-2010’s limitations (single asset type and short time span), we use this
      proprietary futures LOB dataset. It covers 5 years of LOB data for SC (Crude Oil), one of China’s
181
      most liquid futures contracts. The raw data2 is the most granular LOB dataset available for the
182
      Chinese futures market, offering 500ms resolution snapshots with five-level bid and ask information.
183   We use both datasets in MPTP to study LOB models’ generalizability to different assets. We only use
184   FI-2010 in MPRF for efficient experimentation.
185
186
      Evaluation Metrics: For MPTP, we use the F1 scores. For MPRF, we use Mean Squared Error
      (MSE), Pearson Correlation Coefficient (Corr), and Coefficient of Determination (R2 ). MSE quan-
187
      tifies the average squared difference between predicted and actual returns, providing a measure of
188
      prediction accuracy. The Pearson Correlation Coefficient assesses the linear relationship between
189   predicted and actual returns, indicating the direction and strength of their association. Lastly, R2
190   represents the proportion of variance in the target variable explained by our model. The implementa-
191   tion of MSE and R2 are from Scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011). The implementation of Pearson
192   Correlation Coefficient is from SciPy (Virtanen et al., 2020).
193
      Hyperparameter Search. For MPTP, we use the hyperparameters from the original paper of the
194
      models. For MPRF, we perform a grid search. Details are in Appendix B.

351   Notably, complex Transformer-based models including PatchTST and iTransformer underperform
352   compared to simpler LOB-specific architectures based-on MLPs or LSTMs. This discrepancy
353   suggests that without LOB-aware architectural design, conventional time series models struggle to
354   generate accurate predictions on LOB data due to its low signal-to-noise ratio. This observation
355   indicates that the sophisticated temporal modeling capabilities of state-of-the-art time series models
356   may be impeded by the noisy temporal dynamics and intricate cross-variate correlations inherent
357
      in LOB data. Based on this hypothesis, we propose a novel module called Cross-Variate Mixing
      Layers (CVML) to enhance the signal-to-noise ratio of LOB time series. CVML serves as an add-on
358
      layer preceding the time series modeling layers in standard time series prediction models. It accepts
359
      raw LOB data, mixes the features (or different variates from a time series perspective), and outputs
360   an intermediate multivariate time series as input for subsequent modeling layers. CVML integrates
361   seamlessly with existing time series models without requiring further modifications and can be
362   trained end-to-end. CVML has five Conv1D layers, each with a kernel size of 2 and ⌈N/2⌉ output
363   channels, where N is the number of input features. This design leverages convolution kernels to
364   extract cross-variate features and capture correlations. Additionally, we implement increasing dilation
365   in the kernel for each successive layer to enhance temporal signal extraction.
366   To demonstrate CVML’s efficacy, we prepend it to four time series models without altering their
367   core architectures. Results show significant improvements in forecast performance across all metrics
368   (MSE, Corr, and R2 ), as shown in Table 6. Averaging across four models and five horizons, CVML
369   achieves a 7.4% improvement in MSE, 101.6% in Pearson Correlation (Corr), and 244.9% in R2 .
370   This highlights CVML’s potential as a powerful add-on for enhancing general time series models
371   on complex LOB data, bridging the gap between specialized LOB models and general-purpose
372   forecasting approaches.

504      Two notable spike periods are identified: September 2021 to January 2022: Coinciding with crude oil
505      price increases due to supply disruptions and production constraints (United States International Trade
506      Commission, 2021). May 2023 to June 2023: Corresponding to crude oil price fluctuations following
507      the EU’s import ban on Russian crude oil and products (French, 2024). This analysis highlights
508
         the unique characteristics and challenges inherent in the futures dataset compared to FI-2010. The
         inclusion of futures data in our benchmark provides a more comprehensive evaluation, capturing
509
         market dynamics not present in stock-only datasets. This diversity in data sources enhances the
510
         robustness and applicability of our benchmark study to real-world financial forecasting scenarios.

          6                  C ONCLUSION
513
514     We present a comprehensive benchmark of neural network architectures’ predictive performance
515
        on limit order book (LOB) datasets. Our analysis encompasses two critical LOB prediction tasks:
        mid-price trend prediction (MPTP) and mid-price return forecasting (MPRF). We evaluate models
516
        using both an open-source stock LOB dataset and a proprietary futures LOB dataset, comparing
517
        specialized LOB models against state-of-the-art general-purpose time series forecasting models. Our
518     research yields four conclusions:
519
520         • Feature Importance. All three sets of LOB features, basic, time-insensitive, and time-sensitive,
521           demonstrate significant predictive power for the MPTP task, underscoring the importance of
522           comprehensive feature selection in LOB modeling.
523         • Asset-Specific Challenges. The futures LOB dataset exhibits unique characteristics that pose novel
524           challenges for LOB models initially designed on stock data. It highlights the need for asset-specific
525           considerations in model development.
526         • Model Specialization. LOB-specific models significantly outperform general-purpose state-of-
527           the-art time series models on LOB data for the MPRF task. It shows a performance degradation
528           of general-purpose state-of-the-art time series models on the low signal-to-noise ratio LOB time
529
              series data.
530         • CVML Enhancement. Our proposed convolution-based cross-variate mixing layers (CVML)
531           substantially improve the predictive performance of general-purpose time series models (by 101.6%
532
              in Correlation and 244.9% in R2 ) without requiring modifications to the core time series model
              architectures. These findings underscore the complexity of LOB data analysis and the potential for
533
              architectural innovations to bridge the performance gap between specialized and general-purpose
534
              models, enabling more effective modeling of the complex dynamics present in LOB data.

707   A.1   M ODELS FOR M ID - PRICE T REND P REDICTION
708
709
      • MLP, LSTM, CNN1, CNN2, CNNLSTM. Tsantekidis et al. (2017b) used Multilayer Perceptron
710     (MLP) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) to predict future mid-price movements. The same
711     authors (Tsantekidis et al., 2017a) also proposed a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) model
712     (CNN1), which employs a standard CNN architecture with convolutional layers followed by fully
713     connected layers. In 2020, the same research team (Tsantekidis et al., 2020) proposed another
714     CNN model (CNN2) and a CNNLSTM model based on the described LSTM and CNN2. The
715     key improvement of CNN2 is the use of causal convolutions with ”full” padding, ensuring all
716     convolutional layers produce outputs with the same number of time steps. This matches labels to
717     their correct time steps and prevents future data from influencing past predictions. The CNNLSTM
718
        model merges CNN2 and LSTM, using CNN2 for feature extraction and passing the features to the
        LSTM module for classification. In our experiments, we adjusted the CNN kernel size to match
719
        the different number of features in each dataset.
720
721   • DAIN. Passalis et al. (2019) introduce Deep Adaptive Input Normalization (DAIN) for time
722
        series forecasting. The main innovation of the method is learning to adaptively normalize input
        data during model training via two feed-forward layers, an adaptive shifting layer and scaling
723
        layer, instead of using fixed schemes like z-score normalization. Besides, they use a gating
724
        layer to suppress irrelevant features. DAIN explores three possible neural network architectures:
725     MLP, CNN, and RNN. We choose the MLP architecture as it demonstrates the highest empirical
726     performance.
727
      • CTABL, BINCTABL. Tran et al. (2018) propose the Temporal-Attention-Augmented Bilinear
728     Layer (TABL) model. The Bilinear Layer (BL) performs two linear transformations on the input
729     data along the feature and temporal dimensions to capture how stock prices interact at a given point
730     in time and how prices at an index progress over time. However, within a BL, it is unclear how
731     different time instance representations interact. TABL addresses this by incorporating a temporal
732     attention mechanism into the BL model to learn the importance of each time instance relative to oth-
733     ers. The input time series is first passed through the feature dimension transformation, then through
734     the temporal attention mechanism, and finally through the temporal dimension transformation. In
735     our experiments, we use the C(TABL) variant of TABL for its superior empirical performance
736
        over A(TABL) and B(TABL). Tran et al. (2021) improve upon TABL with BINCTABL, which
        incorporates a Bilinear Normalization (BiN) strategy that normalizes the data along the temporal
737
        and feature dimensions with learnable parameters to scale the normalization. The authors compare
738
        BiN as a simpler and more intuitive approach compared to DAIN when using TABL networks.
739
740
      • DEEPLOB. Zhang et al. (2019) propose Deep Convolutional Neural Networks for Limit Order
        Books (DeepLOB). The authors employ a mid-price-based smooth data labeling method to reduce
741
        noise and eliminate minor oscillations. DEEPLOB combines convolutional layers and an Inception
742
        Module to extract features from the noisy financial data, then uses a Long Short-Term Memory
743     (LSTM) layer to capture longer time dependencies among the extracted features. In our experiments,
744     we adjust the kernel size of the last convolution layer for different datasets.
745
      • DEEPLOBATT. Zhang & Zohren (2021) introduce DeepLOB-Attention, an encoder-decoder
746     model built upon their previous work, DEEPLOB. DEEPLOBATT utilizes DEEPLOB as the
747     encoder and an Attention model (Luong et al., 2015) as the decoder. The authors investigated
748     both Attention and Seq2Seq as the decoder in their paper. Since DEEPLOBATT outperforms
749     DEEPLOB-Seq2Seq in nearly all experiments, we choose the DEEPLOBATT model in our
750     experiments and adjust the kernel size of the convolutional block for different datasets according
751     to the feature dimensions.
752   • TLONBoF. Passalis et al. (2020) propose Temporal Logistic Neural Bag-of-Features (TLo-NBoF)
753     (2020), a refined Bag-of-Features (BoF) formulation to better capture the dynamics of time series
754     data compared to existing BoF methods. They achieved this by introducing a novel adaptive
755     scaling mechanism and replacing the Gaussian density estimation of regular BoF with a logistic
        kernel. The input time series is first passed through a 1-D convolution layer to extract relationships


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
        between time instances. Then, the TLONBoF formulation uses a learnable logistic kernel and
757     codebook to aggregate the feature vectors into short-term, mid-term, and long-term histograms
758     that capture the overall behaviors of the input time series. This adaptive method removes the need
759     for sophisticated initialization methods and facilitates smoother hyperparameter tuning.
760
      • DLA. Guo & Chen (2022) propose a Dual-Stage Temporal Attention-Based Deep Learning
761     Architecture (DLA). It uses a dual-stage temporal attention mechanism to repeatedly highlight the
762     most important time instances in input time series data. In the first stage, temporal attention is
763     performed on the input data to learn the importance of each time instance relative to the others. The
764     output of the attention is passed through a stacked Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) network to further
765     enhance the representational state of the input. In the second stage, temporal attention weights
766     are adaptively assigned to the hidden states of the GRU network. The model’s performance is
767     benchmarked against other models in the literature, such as CTABL (Tran et al., 2018), DEEPLOB
768     (Zhang et al., 2019), and TLONBoF (Passalis et al., 2020).
769   • TRANSLOB. Wallbridge (2020) proposes TransLOB, a new model architecture for LOB data that
770     uses Transformer blocks (Vaswani et al., 2023). The TRANSLOB architecture consists of a causal
771     convolution module and a causal transformer block to stay consistent with the nature of time-series
772
        data. The convolutional module comprises 5 1-D convolution layers with increasing dilation to
        capture relationships between short-term and long-term time instances. The transformer block
773
        includes 2 masked self-attention encoders to determine important time instance representations.
774
        The performance of TRANSLOB is benchmarked against other state-of-the-art models such as
775     CTABL, DEEPLOB, and CNN-LSTM.

777   A.2   M ODELS FOR M ID - PRICE F ORECASTING
778
      Besides the LOB Models including MLP, CNN1, LSTM, BINCTABL, DAIN, and TRANSLOB
779   mentioned above, we also include 4 state-of-the-art time series forecasting models.
780
781   • PatchTST. Nie et al. proposes PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022), a multivariate time series forecasting
782     model based on vanilla Transformer architecture. It has two major novelties. First, it uses the
783     same model to predict each variate of a multivariate time series model independently, making it
784
        technically a univariate time series model. Second, to better capture time series’ local pattern, it
        models the time series in patch-wise (each patch includes multiple consecutive time steps) rather
785
        than timestep-wise.
786
787
      • iTransformer. To better capture variate-centric information rather than only temporal dimension,
        Liu et al. (2023) proposes iTransformer. It uses the vanilla transformer components in a novel
788
        way where it first embeds each series of a multivariate time series into a token and then applies
789
        attention across all the tokens representing different series.
790
791
      • DLinear. Besides transformer-based models, linear models are also showing top performance
        in time series forecasting. Zeng et al. (2023) questions Transformer architecture’s capability
792
        to properly capture temporal correlations in time series data. They propose a simple baseline
793
        architecture, DLinear. It is a one-layer linear model with a decomposition component. It first
794     decomposes the raw time series input into a trend component and a seasonal component. Then,
795     two separate one-layer linear layers are applied on these two components. Lastly, two components
796     sum up in to the final prediction. DLinear outperformans most of the Transformer-based time
797     series prediction model and achieve top-tier time series forecasting performance.
798   • TimeMixer. Along the line of better modeling temporal correlations from the time series data,
799     Wang et al. (2024) proposes TimeMixer. It is a linear-layer-based model. Its major novelty is
800     to first preprocess the raw input time series into multiple time series of different resolutions by
801     downsampling. Then, they mix pairs of time series of different resolutions by adding one of the
802     time series to a transformation of the other. The transformation consists of two linear layers and an
803     intermediate GELU activation function. Lastly, they use a linear layer to regress from the mixed
804     time series to produce the final prediction.

882   C.1    S YNTHETIC T IME S ERIES DATA G ENERATION

884   linear interactions. The data generation process follows a hierarchical structure where intermediate
885   variables influence the final target variable (electricity price). There are totally six varieties: electricity
886   price, load, production, temperature, supply margin, price volatility. These varieties have cross-
887   variate correlations. For example, an increasing load of electricity leads to an increasing electricity
888   price. An increasing production of electricity leads to a decreasing electricity price. When the
