011                                                      A BSTRACT
012
013                Reinforcement Learning (RL) suffers from sample inefficiency in sparse reward
014                domains, and the problem is further pronounced in case of stochastic transitions.
015                To improve the sample efficiency, reward shaping is a well-studied approach to
016
                   introduce intrinsic rewards that can help the RL agent converge to an optimal
                   policy faster. However, designing a useful reward shaping function for all desirable
017
                   states in the Markov Decision Process (MDP) is challenging, even for domain
018
                   experts. Given that Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive
019                performance across a magnitude of natural language tasks, we aim to answer the
020                following question: Can we obtain heuristics using LLMs for constructing a reward
021                shaping function that can boost an RL agent’s sample efficiency? To this end, we
022                aim to leverage off-the-shelf LLMs to generate a plan for an abstraction of the
023                underlying MDP. We further use this LLM-generated plan as a heuristic to construct
024                the reward shaping signal for the downstream RL agent. By characterizing the
025                type of abstraction based on the MDP horizon length, we analyze the quality
026                of heuristics when generated using an LLM, with and without a verifier in the
027
                   loop. Our experiments across multiple domains with varying horizon length and
                   number of sub-goals from the BabyAI environment suite, Household, Mario, and,
028
                   Minecraft domain, show 1) the advantages and limitations of querying LLMs with
029
                   and without a verifier to generate a reward shaping heuristic, and, 2) a significant
030                improvement in the sample efficiency of PPO, A2C, and Q-learning when guided
031                by the LLM-generated heuristics.

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Sample inefficiency of training Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents in sparse reward domains1
037   has been a long-standing challenge (Ng et al., 1999; Laud & DeJong, 2003; Marthi, 2007; Grzes &
038   Kudenko, 2008; Devlin & Kudenko, 2011). The number of environment interactions take a much
039   severe hit if the domain further consists of stochastic transitions (Grześ, 2017; Ben-Porat et al., 2024).
040   In an effort to improve this sample efficiency, reward shaping has been proven to be effective, which
041   provides intrinsic rewards as a better training signal over just the sparse extrinsic (environment)
042   rewards (Laud & DeJong, 2003; Marthi, 2007; Devlin & Kudenko, 2011).
043   Underlying reward shaping techniques (Ng et al., 1999; Devlin et al., 2011; Devlin & Kudenko, 2012;
044   Gao & Toni, 2015; Eck et al., 2016), there is an inherent assumption made on how this reward shaping
045   function can be constructed. One of the straightforward ways is for a domain expert to hand-engineer
046   the reward shaping function, which can be cognitively demanding and additionally lead to a cognitive
047   bias in the engineered rewards (Wu et al., 2024; Lightman et al., 2023). Yet another popular approach
048   relies on learning the intrinsic rewards via Inverse RL (IRL) (Russell, 1998; Abbeel & Ng, 2004;
049   Russell & Norvig, 2016), where the human records an expert demonstration for solving the task.
050   Lately, Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable performance spanning a wide variety
051   of natural language-based tasks (Kocoń et al., 2023; Gilardi et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023) which can
052   be attributed to the enormous and diverse data that they have been trained on. While some tasks have
053

055   that are either highly domain-specific or require higher degrees of generalizability, require fine-tuning
056   (Li et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). However, several recent studies have shown the performance

060   show some promise when tasked with solving a sufficiently relaxed version of the original problem
061   (Nirmal et al., 2024), or assisting in obtaining the final solution (Kambhampati et al., 2024). Keeping
062   this trade-off in mind for our use case, we aim to answer: Can we obtain heuristics using LLMs for
063
      constructing a reward shaping function that can boost an RL agent’s sample efficiency?
064   From the limited exploratory works that currently lie at utilizing LLMs for guiding RL (Liang et al.,
065   2023; Du et al., 2023; Carta et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2019; Kwon et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Ma
066   et al., 2023), LLMs have particularly been effective in providing either high-level (hierarchical) policy
067   guidance (Jiang et al., 2019; Liang et al., 2023) or the reward function (Kwon et al., 2023; Ma et al.,
068   2023), which may only be feasible for tasks where there has been sufficient background knowledge
069
      or data that could have possibly been part of the language model’s training data. However, treating
      LLMs as sources of approximate common-sensical knowledge, our intuition is that we can expect
070
      them to generate a heuristic that can be useful for the downstream task (Cheng et al., 2021). To this
071
      end, we take inspiration from the problem abstraction methods, which have been extensively studied
072   in the planning and RL literature (Dietterich et al., 1998; Sutton et al., 1999; Lane & Kaelbling, 2002;
073   Kattenbelt et al., 2010; Kulkarni et al., 2016; Gopalan et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2019; Nashed et al.,
074   2021), and query LLMs to obtain a solution for a sufficiently relaxed abstract problem.
075
      For a given RL problem, the desired heuristic can be obtained at both, the low-level, and a high-level
076
      (symbolic) action space. For example, for the Household environment shown in Figure 5, low-
077
      level actions comprise → up, down, left, right ↑, whereas high-level (symbolic) actions
078   comprise → pickup key, open door ↑, etc. Furthermore, the choice of abstraction depends
079   on the nature of the underlying problem, since LLMs operate in the text space. One straightforward
080   possibility is to consider a deterministic abstraction of the underlying stochastic Markov Decision
081   Process (MDP) (Yoon et al., 2008). While the abstract MDP in this case will still require a solution
082   using the low-level action space, it may only be useful for the RL agent if LLMs can easily find
083   a goal-reaching plan for that deterministic problem. Hence, we first investigate the usefulness of
084   directly prompting off-the-shelf LLMs for the deterministic abstraction of short horizon stochastic
085   MDP problems, which tend to yield incomplete plans that are consistently unable to reach the goal.
086
      Furthermore, our experiments show the ineffectiveness of using these incomplete plans as heuristics
      for reward shaping the RL agents. While a deterministic abstraction on the low-level action space
087
      still remains a challenging planning problem for LLMs, a yet another possibility particularly for
088
      long-horizon problems, is to construct a hierarchical abstraction of the underlying MDP which
089   consists of a high-level (symbolic) action space (Jiang et al., 2019; Liang et al., 2023). A plan in this
090   hierarchical abstraction will include some or all the sub-goals the agent has to achieve in the correct
091   order to reach the goal, that can further be used to design our reward shaping function accordingly.
092   Note, that our work does not intend to pose a dichotomy between a deterministic and a hierarchical
093   MDP setup, but aims to study these two possible MDP abstractions for a downstream RL task which
094   has sparse-reward and stochastic transitions.
095   While LLMs may still not be able to yield valid (executable) plans each time for the abstract problem,
096   we further investigate the performance when LLMs are used with a verifier in the loop. The presence
097   of such a verifier can help generate a valid goal-reaching plan. The idea of such verifier-augmented
098   LLM setups has also been recently seen for planning and reasoning problems(Kambhampati et al.,
099   2024; Gundawar et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Finally, we
100   leverage the LLM and LLM+verifier generated plans as a basis for constructing the reward shaping
101   function for the downstream RL sparse reward task. Through this work, we aim to understand the
102   possible role of LLMs as heuristic generators for downstream RL problems, and discuss important
103
      trade-offs that need to be considered for choosing the right abstraction and constructing a verifier.
104   The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
105
106         1. In the context of investigating LLMs’ utility in generating heuristics, we study two different
107            types of MDP abstractions - deterministic and hierarchical, for short and long-horizon
               problems respectively.

125   2    R ELATED W ORK
126
127
      Sparse Reward RL and LLM-based Guidance: The seminal foundational work by (Ng et al.,
128   1999) provided policy invariance guarantees using Potential-based Reward Shaping (PBRS) for
129   boosting the sample efficiency of RL agents, followed by further theoretical investigations by (Laud
130   & DeJong, 2003; Wiewiora, 2003). (Pathak et al., 2017) also showed the advantages of using intrinsic
131   rewards for training RL agents. Reward shaping methods have been studied under several dimensions,
132   including but not limited to automatic reward learning (Grzes & Kudenko, 2008; Marthi, 2007),
133   (Zhang et al., 2024; Srivastava et al., 2024), multi-agent domains (Devlin & Kudenko, 2011; Sun
134   et al., 2018), meta-learning (Zou et al., 2019), etc. Primarily, the sources of obtaining and/or learning
135   intrinsic rewards includes domain experts hand-engineering the rewards (Wu et al., 2024; Lightman
136
      et al., 2023), via providing feedback (Lee et al., 2023), or via providing expert demonstrations (Argall
      et al., 2009). More recently, Large Language Models have been utilized to give feedback on RL
137
      agent’s environment interaction (Du et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024)
138
      or directly provide the reward function for the RL agent’s task.
139
140
      LLMs for Planning and Search: There is a research divide in the current literature regarding
141
      the planning, reasoning and verification abilities of Large Language Models. While popular works
142
      claiming LLM reasoning abilities have proposed several prompting methods (Wei et al., 2022;
143   Yao et al., 2023; Long, 2023; Yao et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024), there have been independent
144   investigations refuting such claims using LLMs for solving deterministic planning and classical
145   reasoning problems (Valmeekam et al., 2023; Stechly et al., 2024; Verma et al., 2024). While LLMs
146   are themselves not reliable for providing accurate feedback (Stechly et al., 2024), other than in natural
147   language tasks (Yao et al., 2023), recent works have augmented LLMs with task-specific verifiers (for
148   example, a Python compiler that can check LLM-generated code for syntactic correctness) that can
149   evaluate the validity (not necessarily correctness) of the LLM-generated output and provide feedback
150   to the LLM accordingly (Kambhampati et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Liang et al.,
151
      2023). These augmented LLM frameworks have been shown to be useful for improving the overall
      task performance when prompting LLMs. We further utilize these plans to construct the reward
152
      shaping function for the downstream RL agent.

157   We consider a finite horizon Markov Decision Process (MDP) M defined by the tuple (S, A, P,
158   R, ω). Here, S represents the set of all possible states, A represents the set of all possible actions,
159   P : S ↓ A ↔ S is the stochastic state transition function where P(s→ |s, a) is the transition probability
160   for s, s→ ↗ S and a ↗ A, R : S ↓ A ↓ S ↔ R is the reward function, and ω is the discount
161   factor. In our case, we consider an MDP M with sparse rewards, i.e., R = 1 for g ↗ S and
      R = 0 otherwise, where g is the goal or termination state. The objective of the agent is to learn a

166   Deterministic Abstraction: For short-horizon problem setting, we consider the MDP M→ modified
167   from the underlying stochastic sparse-reward MDP M such that, M→ can be defined using the tuple
168   (S, A, T → , R, ω), where T → : S ↓ A ↔ S is the deterministic transition function. We aim to obtain a
169   guide plan εg in M→ using the LLM-environment interaction, that can further be used as a heuristic

171
172   Hierarchical Abstraction: For long-horizon problem setting, we consider the abstract MDP M→→
173
      modified from the underlying stochastic sparse-reward MDP M such that, M→→ can be defined in
      the form of declarative action-centered representation of a planning task. Specifically, we consider a
174
      STRIPS-style planning problem (Fikes & Nilsson, 1971), where the planning model can be defined
175
      in the form P = →F, A, I, G↑. F is a set of propositional state variables or fluents defining the
176   hierarchical state space. Similar to (Guan et al., 2022), we assume access to a function H : S ↓ F ↔
177   {0, 1} that maps MDP states to hierarchical fluents, such that H(s, f ) is set to true for fluent f if f
178   exists in the MDP state s ↗ S. A is the set of action definitions, where action a ↗ A is defined as
179   a = →preca , adda , dela ↑; where preca are set of preconditions in the form of binary features that
180   need to be true in a state to execute action a, and, adda and dela are the add and delete effects that
181   capture the set of binary features set to true and false, respectively, when action a is executed. For
182   this work, since A represents the high-level (symbolic) actions for us, we will refer to these actions as
183   Ah to distinguish them from the underlying MDP’s low-level action space represented by A. Finally,
184
      I is the initial state of the agent and G ↘ F is the goal specification. A solution to the planning
      problem P is the set of correctly-ordered actions which will act as the guide plan εg . Similar to the
185
      deterministic abstraction case, εg can further be used to construct as a heuristic to construct a reward
186
      shaping function for the underlying RL problem for learning εω .
187
188
      Problem Statement: In this work, we first aim to obtain the guide plan εg by querying a LLM
189   which can act as the heuristic to construct a reward shaping function for the underlying stochastic
190   sparse-reward MDP M. Once we have obtained εg , we adopt the Potential-based Reward Shaping
191   approach, as proposed in (Ng et al., 1999). In the case of deterministic abstraction, the goal is to obtain
192   εg using a LLM where εg is a sequence of (state, action) pairs where the state and actions are same as
193   the underlying MDP M. Since εg consists of (state, action) pairs which are same as the underlying
194   MDP M, we construct a (state, action)-based reward shaping function F = !(s→ , a→ ) ≃ !(s, a) for
195   s, s→ ↗ S and a, a→ ↗ A (Wiewiora et al., 2003) and ! is the potential function which gives a potential
196   to any (s, a) if a (state, action) pair exists in εg . In the latter case of hierarchical abstraction, the goal
197
      is to obtain εg using a LLM where εg is a sequence of PDDL actions, which represent the different
      sub-goals that exist in the underlying MDP M. Hence, similar to (Grzes & Kudenko, 2008), we
198
      adopt the state-based potential function F = !(s→ ) ≃ !(s) for s, s→ ↗ S where the state potential is
199
      given by the number of sub-goal (or landmark) fluents that have been satisfied in εg .

      4.2     AUGMENTING LLM S WITH A V ERIFIER
252
253   In this subsection, we discuss the verifier-augmented LLM framework which is used to generate a
254   valid solution, i.e., εg , for the abstract MDP. This plan is further used to construct a reward shaping
255   function, resulting in a sample efficiency boost for the RL agent that learns a policy on the original
256   stochastic, sparse reward problem.
257
258   4.2.1    V ERIFIER C ONSTRUCTION
259
260   For the deterministic MDP M→ , we will again use the BabyAI DoorKey environment, as shown
261
      in Figure 1, as the example to discuss the details of how we construct the verifier. It is important
      to note that we eventually require a set of actions from this verifier, that are feasible at any given
262
      state of the environment that the agent can be in. We will refer to this set of valid actions as {Av |s}
263
      and set of invalid actions as {Av→ |s} for s ↗ S. Hence, for the BabyAI DoorKey environment, we
264   begin with computing the agent’s position (x,y) at the given state (si ). At any given step of the
265   LLM-environment interaction, we can verify each action that the agent can and can not take for a
266   given state, i.e., either a ↗ {Av |si } or a ↗ {Av→ |si }. For example, in the state (si ) shown in Figure
267   1, the agent has already picked up the key but can only move forward if the door is open. Hence, the
268   model-based verifier, given the agent’s current position (x,y) and the set of valid actions {Av } taken
269   until step i, computes the set of actions that are feasible in the current state, i.e., turn left, turn
      right, and toggle (open door). Next, given the LLM’s guessed action, i.e., move forward,

      the verifier finds the action to be infeasible and generates a back-prompt that is appended to the
271   LLM’s original prompt. We further discuss prompt construction details in Section 4.2.2.

273   For the hierarchical abstraction case, consider the abstract MDP M→→ for the same BabyAI DoorKey en-
274
      vironment shown in Figure 1. In this case, our PDDL actions can be → pickup_key, open_door,
      reach_goal ↑, representing the sub-goals that the underlying MDP agent needs to achieve. If the

281   4.2.2    P ROMPT C ONSTRUCTION
282
283   At any given step i in the LLM’s interaction with the environment for the deterministic abstraction case,
284   we once again construct the LLM prompt with three components. The first is the Task Description
285   followed by the Observation Description that is now generated independently at every step after
286   the previous LLM-generated valid action is executed, and finally include the Query Description
287
      asking the LLM to guess the next low-level action. We refer to this as the step-prompt for our
      discussion. Once the LLM returns a valid action as a guess to the step-prompt, i.e. εLLM (si ) = a
288
      for a ↗ {Av |si }, we execute the action in the environment and continue the interaction. Consider the
289
      case where the LLM has guessed an invalid action, i.e. εLLM (si ) = a for a ↗ {Av→ |si }. In this case,
290   we do not execute the action in the environment, but rather, construct the back-prompt by appending
291   the feedback given by the model-based verifier to the current prompt. To the existing step-prompt,
292   we add this verifier feedback that lists all the invalid actions ({Av→ |si }) guessed by the LLM for
293   the current state si and prompt the LLM again to choose a different action that is not in {Av→ |si }.
294   Once the LLM guesses a valid action, we break out of this back-prompting loop and continue our
295   environment interaction as mentioned above (see Appendix D.2 for the complete prompt).
296   For the hierarchical abstraction case, we prompt the LLM with the PDDL domain and problem
297   specification in the Observation Description, and query it to generate only the next high-level action
298   (↗ Ah ) in the Query Description. Additionally, we provide the LLM-generated plan so far, i.e.,
299   εgi↓1 consisting of the valid actions till step i ≃ 1. Similar to the deterministic abstraction case, we
300   back-prompt the LLM if it outputs an invalid action, and continue the interaction till it finds a valid
301   plan (see Appendix D.2 for the complete prompt).

303   4.3     R EWARD S HAPING USING εg
304
305   Potential-based Reward Shaping (PBRS) (Ng et al., 1999) allows for injecting intrinsic rewards for
306   training an RL agent on a sparse reward problem while guaranteeing the policy invariance property.
307   Once we have obtained εg by querying the LLM with and without a verifier, we utilize it to construct
308
      our reward shaping function F. Using the reward shaping function F, we assign potentials to the (s, a)
      pairs in the LLM-generated plan for the deterministic case, and to the states (s) in the LLM-generated
309
      plan for the hierarchical case. Formally, we adopt the definition which utilizes a shaping reward
310
      function F such that our updated reward function can be defined as: R→ (s, a) = R(s, a) + F(s, a)
311   for s ↗ S, a ↗ A in the former case, and as R→ (s) = R(s) + F(s) in the latter. We refer the readers
312   to Section 5 for details on F for both types of abstractions.
313
314
      In the exploration phase, the RL agent stores the environment interactions, i.e., (s, a, s→ , r) tuples, in a
      dataset buffer D. Given F, we update the buffer D with the shaped rewards such that (s, a, s→ , r) ↔
315
      (s, a, s→ , r→ ) where r→ is the shaped reward. Our RL agent then continues the learning over this updated
316
      dataset buffer D→ . We present the complete pipeline in Figure 1 and Algorithm 1 in Appendix C.

      5.4   D ISCUSSION
487
488   In this sub-section, we aim to draw insights from our experiments for both RQ1 and RQ2. Specifically,
489   we discuss the trade-offs a) between the utility and the ease of constructing a deterministic or a
490   hierarchical abstraction, and b) between the quality of heuristic obtained and effort to construct a
491   verifier for querying LLMs.
492
493
      Deterministic or Hierarchical Abstraction? Recall, that the primary difference between the two
494
      types of abstractions lies in the state and action space representations. The deterministic abstract MDP
495
      M→ preserves the same state and low-level action space representation as the underlying stochastic
496   sparse-reward MDP, while the hierarchical abstraction only considers the high-level sub-goals over
497   the underlying MDP task. The utility of each of the two types stems from how well LLMs perform
498   to generate the abstract plan. Our analysis from RQ1.1 shows that LLMs are unable to consistently
499   generate a solution when we represent the underlying MDP problem as part of a text prompt. Also,
500   there is an added burden due to the prompt engineering effort required in the deterministic abstraction
501   case due to the preserved state and action spaces. However, as we can provide shaped rewards to each
502   (state, action) pair in the goal-reaching plan generated for the deterministic MDP, the reward shaping
503   signal for the downstream RL agent is much more fine-grained. In the hierarchical abstraction case,
504
      even when LLMs are able to find a partial solution in terms of correctly ordered sub-goals, we are able
      to provide intrinsic rewards to states where those sub-goal fluents are true making LLM-generated
505
      heuristics useful even in the absence of a verifier. As pointed out by (Kambhampati et al., 2024),
506
      while we could use PDDL planners to get this abstract plan, these planners are useful in a narrow set
507   of domains whereas LLMs can be useful in many more generalizable scenarios. Moreover, we believe
508   that extending our reward shaping work to partially-observable state space and continuous action
509   space environments can be a valuable future extension. For example, our hierarchical abstraction can
510   be easily utilized for continuous action spaces. Similar to the discrete action space experiments shown
511   for Mario, Minecraft, and the Household environment in our work, the RL agent can learn a policy
512   that follows the correct sequence of subgoals using shaped rewards in a continuous environment too.
513   To conclude, we note that while the reward shaping signal may not be as fine-grained, hierarchical
514   abstraction allows for obtaining better reward shaping heuristics using LLMs.
515
516   How useful is having a verifier in the loop? For either type of the abstraction, the presence of a
517   verifier allows LLMs to use a generate-test-verify loop for generating an improved plan. However,
518   note that the verifier assumes the knowledge of the environment’s domain model such that it can
519   check for any valid or invalid actions at any given step of the LLM-environment interaction. This
520   requirement is slightly relaxed as the LLMs can be useful translators in obtaining a noisy approximate
521   domain model when provided with natural language instructions (Guan et al., 2023). Hence, in the
522   case of hierarchical abstraction, the verifier is much simpler than that in deterministic abstraction as
523
      it only needs to check if the preconditions of the LLM-generated sub-goal are satisfied. To conclude,
      we note that if obtaining the domain model is not expensive, having a verifier in the loop can improve
524
      the quality of heuristic obtained. Future works can delve into alternative methods of constructing
525
      these verifiers.

531
      Designing reward shaping methods to inject intrinsic rewards useful for training Reinforcement
      Learning agents can be challenging, even for domain experts. Lately, Large Language Models have
532
      shown remarkable success in a variety of natural language tasks, while also encountering limitations
533
      when it comes to prompting them for planning and reasoning problems. Keeping these limitations
534   in mind, we aim to investigate the utility of LLMs to generate heuristics for guiding RL training in
535   sparse reward tasks. We study a deterministic and a hierarchical abstraction of the underlying MDP
536   for short and long-horizon environments, respectively. Using LLM and LLM+verifier-generated
537   plans, we construct a reward shaping function for the underlying MDP. Our results indicate a boost
538   in the sample efficiency of downstream RL training across BabyAI suite, Household, Mario and
539   Minecraft domains. Finally, we discuss the trade-offs associated with the choice of abstraction and
      the presence of a verifier for obtaining heuristics that can guide RL agents.
