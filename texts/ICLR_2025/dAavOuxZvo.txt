009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Diffusion probabilistic models learn to remove noise that is artificially added to
012            the data during training. Novel data, like images, may then be generated from
013            Gaussian noise through a sequence of denoising operations. While this Markov
014            process implicitly defines a joint distribution over noise-free data, it is not simple
015            to condition the generative process on masked or partial images. A number of
016            heuristic sampling procedures have been proposed for solving inverse problems
017            with diffusion priors, but these approaches do not directly approximate the true
018            conditional distribution imposed by inference queries, and are often ineffective
019            for large masked regions. Moreover, many of these baselines cannot be applied to
020
               latent diffusion models which use image encodings for efficiency. We instead de-
               velop a hierarchical variational inference algorithm that analytically marginalizes
021
               missing features, and uses a rigorous variational bound to optimize a non-Gaussian
022
               Markov approximation of the true diffusion posterior. Through extensive experi-
023            ments with both pixel-based and latent diffusion models of images, we show that
024            our VIPaint method significantly outperforms previous approaches in both the
025            plausibility and diversity of imputations, and is easily generalized to other inverse
026            problems like deblurring and superresolution.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020b; Song et al., 2021b; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Song & Er-
033
      mon, 2019) and hierarchical variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Child, 2021; Vahdat & Kautz, 2020;
      Sønderby et al., 2016) are generative models in which a sequence of latent variables encode a rich
034
      data representation. For diffusion models, this latent structure is defined by a diffusion process that
035
      corrupts data over “time” via additive Gaussian noise. While each step of hierarchical VAE training
036   requires end-to-end inference of all latent variables, diffusion models estimate stochastic gradients
037   by sampling a few timesteps, and learning to incrementally denoise corrupted data. Given a learned
038   denoising network, synthetic data is generated by sequentially refining Gaussian noise for hundreds
039   or thousands of time steps, producing deep generative models that have advanced the state-of-the-art
040   in natural image generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Kingma et al., 2021a; Karras et al., 2022).
041
      Diffusion models for high-dimensional data like images are computationally intensive. Efficiency
042   may be improved by leveraging an autoencoder (Kingma & Welling, 2019; Rombach et al., 2022b;
043   Vahdat et al., 2021) to map data to a lower-dimensional encoding, and then training a diffusion model
044   for the lower-dimensional codes. This dimensionality reduction enables tractable but expressive
045   models for images with millions of pixels. The effectiveness of latent diffusion models (LDMs) has
046   made them a new standard for natural image generation, and they are thus our focus here.
047
      Motivated by the foundational information captured by diffusion models of images, numerous al-
048   gorithms have incorporated a pre-trained diffusion model as a prior for image editing (Meng et al.,
049   2021), inpainting (Song et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2023b; Kawar et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022b;
050   Lugmayr et al., 2022; Cardoso et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023; Trippe et al., 2023; Dou & Song,
051   2024), or other inverse problems (Kadkhodaie & Simoncelli, 2021; Song et al., 2023; Graikos et al.,
052   2022; Mardani et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2023). Many of these prior methods are specialized to in-
053   painting with pixel-based diffusion models, where every data dimension is either perfectly observed
      or completely missing, and are not easily adapted to state-of-the-art LDMs.

      Most algorithms for inpainting with diffusion models employ an iterative refinement procedure, like
069   that used to generate unconditional samples, and then guide their predictions towards the partially
070   observed image via various approximations and heuristics. But by sequentially annealing from
071   independent Gaussian noise to noise-free images, these approaches produce myopic samples that do
072   not adequately incorporate information from observed pixels, and fail to correct errors introduced in
073   earlier stages of the “reverse-time” diffusion. More recent work has extended these approaches to
074   enable image editing (Avrahami et al., 2022) or inpainting (Rout et al., 2023; Corneanu et al., 2024;
075   Chung et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024) with LDMs, but continues to suffer similar inaccuracies.
076   In this work, we present VIPaint, a novel application of variational inference (VI) (Wainwright &
077   Jordan, 2008; Blei et al., 2017) that efficiently optimizes a hierarchical, Markovian, non-Gaussian
078   approximation to the true LDM posterior. VI has achieved excellent image restoration results with
079   a wide range of priors, including mixtures (Fergus et al., 2006; Ji et al., 2017) and hierarchical
080   VAEs (Agarwal et al., 2023), but there is little work exploring its integration with state-of-the-art
081   LDMs. While Red-Diff (Mardani et al., 2023) applies VI to approximate the posterior of pixel-based
082   DMs, its local approximation of the noise-free image posterior is difficult to optimize, requiring an-
083
      nealing heuristics that we demonstrate are sensitive to local optima. Our VIPaint method instead
      defines a hierarchical posterior that strategically accounts for a subset of noise levels, enabling the
084
      inference of both high-level semantics and low-level details from observed pixels simultaneously
085
      (see Fig. 1). We efficiently infer variational parameters via non-amortized optimization for each
086   inpainting query, avoiding the need to collect a training set of corrupted images (Liu et al., 2024;
087   Corneanu et al., 2024), expensively fine-tune generative models (Avrahami et al., 2022) or vari-
088   ational posteriors (Feng et al., 2023) for each query, or retrain large-scale conditional diffusion
089   models (Rombach et al., 2022b; Saharia et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022a).
090
      We begin by reviewing properties of (latent) diffusion models in Sec. 2, and prior work on inferring
091
      images via pre-trained diffusion models in Sec. 3. Sec. 4 then develops the VIPaint algorithm,
092   which first fits a hierarchical posterior that best aligns with the observations, and then samples
093   from this approximate posterior to produce diverse reconstruction hypotheses. Results in Sec. 5
094   on inpainting, and the Appendix on other inverse problems, then show substantial qualitative and
095   quantitative improvements in capturing multimodal uncertainty for both pixel-based and latent DMs.

      In many real-life scenarios, we encounter partial observations y derived from an underlying x. Typ-
      ically, these observations are modeled as y = f (x) + v, where f represents a known linear degrada-
167
      tion model and v is Gaussian noise with v ⇠ N (0, v2 ). For instance, in an image inpainting task, y
168
      might represent a masked imaged y = x m, where m is a binary mask indicating missing pixels.
169
170   In cases where the degradation of x is significant, exactly recovering x from y is challenging, be-
171   cause many x could produce the same observation y. To express the resulting posterior p(x | y)
172
      given a DM prior, we can adapt the Markov generative process in Eq. (2) as follows:
173

177   Exactly evaluating this predictive distribution is infeasible due to the non-linear noise prediction
178   (and decoder) network, and the intractable posteriors of latent codes p(zt 1 | zt , y) for all t.

      Blended methods like Song et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023a) define a procedural, heuristic ap-
180
      proximation to the posterior and is tailored for image inpainting. They first generate unconditional
181
      samples zt 1 from the prior using the learned noise prediction network, and then incorporate y
182   by replacing the corresponding dimensions with the observed measurements. RePaint Lugmayr
183   et al. (2022) attempts to reduce visual inconsistencies caused by blending via a resampling strat-
184   egy. A “time travel” operation is introduced, where images from the current time step zt 1 are first
185   blended with the noisy version of the observed image yt 1 , and then used to generate images in the
186   (t 1) + r, (r      1) time step by applying a one-step forward process and following the Blended
187   denoising process.

189   Sampling Methods. Motivated by the goal of addressing more general inverse problems,
190   Diffusion Posterior Sampling (DPS) (Chung et al., 2023) uses Bayes’ Rule to sample from
191   p✓ (zt 1 |zt , y) / p✓ (zt 1 |zt )p✓ (y|zt 1 ). Instead of directly blending or replacing images with
192
      noisy versions of the observation, DPS uses the gradient of the likelihood log p✓ (y|zt ) to guide the
      generative process at every denoising step t. Since computing rzt log p(y|zt 1 ) is intractable due
193
      to the integral over all possible configurations of zt0 for t0 < t 1, DPS approximates p(y|zt 1 )
194
      using a one-step denoised prediction x̂ using Eq. (3). The likelihood p(y|x) = N (f (x), v2 ) can
195   then be evaluated using these approximate predictions. To obtain the gradient of the likelihood term,
196   DPS require backpropagating gradients through the denoising network used to predict x̂.
197
198
      Specializing to image inpainting, CoPaint (Zhang et al., 2023) augments the likelihood with another
      regularization term to generate samples zt 1 that prevent taking large update steps away from the
199
      previous sample zt , in an attempt to produce more coherent images. Further, it proposes CoPaint-TT,
200
      which additionally uses the time-travel trick to reduce discontinuities in sampled images.
201
202   Originally designed for pixel-space diffusion models, it is difficult to adopt these works directly
203   to latent diffusion models. Posterior Sampling with Latent Diffusion (PSLD) (Rout et al., 2023)
204
      first showed that employing DPS directly on latent space diffusion models produces blurry images.
      It proposes to add another “gluing” term to the measurement likelihood which penalizes samples
205
      zt that do not lie in the encoder-decoder shared embedding space. However, this may produce
206
      artifacts in the presence of measurement noise (see Song et al. (2024)). To address this issue, recent
207   concurrent work on the ReSample (Song et al., 2024) method divides the timesteps in the latent
208   space into 3 subspaces, and optimizes samples zt in the mid-subspace to encourage samples that are
209   more consistent with observations. Other work (Yu et al., 2023) highlights a 3-stage approach where
210   data consistency can be enforced in the latter 2 stages which are closer to t = 0.

      4   VIPAINT: VARIATIONAL I NFERENCE OF D IFFUSIONS FOR I NPAINTING
260
261   Given a pre-trained diffusion model, our framework defines a structured family of distributions on
262   the latent variable z, which is optimized for each partial observation. This simple and general
263   approach can be applied both to models which diffuse in the pixel-space, or latent space.
264   Rather than optimizing a posterior or latent samples at a single noise level and annealing through
265   noise levels, our VIPaint method constructs a joint posterior that is Markovian across a subset of
266   zt , introducing a hierarchical structure to our inference. The parameters of this posterior are
267   optimized separately for each query to steer our proposed variational distribution to be semantically
268   aligned with the observation, while searching for consistent images within the prior higher ranged
269   ([Te , T ]) latent space z. Once fit, the second stage involves sampling from this posterior and utilizes
      DPS gradient updates in the low ranged ([0, Ts ]) latent space, see Fig. 3.

535   6     C ONCLUSION
536   We present VIPaint, a simple and a general approach to adapt diffusion models for image inpaint-
537   ing and other inverse problems. We take widely used (latent) diffusion generative models, allocate
538   variational parameters for the latent codes of each partial observation, and fit the parameters stochas-
539   tically to optimize the induced variational bound. The simple but flexible structure of our bounds
      allows VIPaint to outperform previous sampling and variational methods when uncertainty is high.
