011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Precipitation nowcasting is a vital spatio-temporal prediction task essential for
014            various meteorological applications, but it faces significant challenges due to the
015            chaotic property of precipitation systems. Mainstream methods primarily rely on
016
               radar data for echo extrapolation, but over longer lead times, radar echoes mainly
               exhibit translation, failing to capture precipitation generation and dissipation pro-
017
               cesses. This results in blurry predictions, attenuation of high-value echoes, and
018
               positional inaccuracies issues. In the other hand, deterministic models using MSE
019            loss often produce blurry forecasts, while probabilistic models struggle with lo-
020            calization accuracy. To address these challenges, we propose a multi-source data
021            fusion framework, which integrates satellite and radar data, with former effec-
022            tively complementing limitations of latter. In this framework, we leverages global
023            motion fields to capture echo dynamics and introduces a residual diffusion mech-
024            anism to reduce memory usage by non-residual features. Various spatio-temporal
025            models (e.g. RNN-based, CNN-based, and ConvRNN-based models) can seam-
026            lessly integrated into this framework. Extensive experiments on a Jiangsu dataset
027
               demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly
               in short-term forecasts. The code and models will be released.

037   processes complicates the accurate prediction of precipitation at both large scales and fine resolu-

083   inaccuracies issues, particularly in extended forecasts Leinonen et al. (2023).
084   Over the past few years, applying deep learning techniques to precipitation nowcasting has
085   gained significant traction, with models such as the RNN-based PredRNN++ Wang et al. (2018),

092   More recently, advancements in diffusion models have been investigated for their potential to en-
093   hance precipitation forecasting. Models like PredDiff Blücher et al. (2022), MCVD Voleti et al.
094   (2022), Diffcast Yu et al. (2024), and SRNDiff Ling et al. (2024) integrate diffusion mechanisms
095   to better capture the uncertainty and stochastic nature of precipitation processes. Despite these im-
096   provements, even advanced diffusion models face challenges in balancing the trade-offs between
097   detail preservation and forecast accuracy, particularly under complex meteorological conditions.
098   To address these challenges, we propose a novel, flexible, and unified end-to-end framework dubbed
099   as UniDiff, specifically designed for precipitation nowcasting. UniDiff seamlessly integrates radar
100   and satellite data via an attention-guided multi-source fusion, effectively capturing and merging
101   the complementary strengths of diverse data. Furthermore, the combined features are processed
102   by a spatio-temporal sequence prediction module to model the primary motion patterns of the pre-
103   cipitation system and perform coarse forecasting. To further enhance forecast precision, UniDiff
104
      incorporates a diffusion residual component based on the RST-Unet architecture. This component
      leverages both deterministic models, known for their ability to capture broad spatiotemporal dy-
105
      namics, and probabilistic diffusion models, which provide fine-grained accuracy, thereby generating
106
      highly refined and accurate precipitation predictions.

126   2.1   D EEP L EARNING -BASED P RECIPITATION N OWCASTING
127
      Deep learning has garnered significant attention in the domain of weather pattern analysis, offering
128
      new possibilities for precipitation nowcasting. Initially, RNN/LSTM-based methods were exten-
129   sively utilized to tackle weather forecasting challenges Salman et al. (2018). Shi et al. advanced this
130   area by integrating convolutional operations within recurrent architectures, leading to the develop-
131   ment of the Convolutional LSTM (ConvLSTM) model Shi et al. (2015). In this model, convolutional
132   layers replaced fully connected layers for LSTM state transitions, capturing spatiotemporal depen-
133   dencies more effectively. Subsequently, Shi et al. introduced the Trajectory GRU (TrajGRU) model,
134   which incorporates a subnetwork to dynamically learn location-variant structures for recurrent con-
135   nections, achieving superior predictive accuracy on the HKO-7 precipitation benchmark Shi et al.
136   (2017).
137   Further advancements in this field include the Predictive Recurrent Neural Network (PredRNN)
138   Wang et al. (2017) and its enhanced version, PredRNN++ Wang et al. (2018). These models intro-
139   duced novel mechanisms, such as the Gradient Highway Unit and Causal LSTM, to address gradient
140   propagation challenges, thereby improving spatiotemporal prediction performance on both synthetic
141   and real-world datasets. Following these developments, various ConvLSTM variants, such as MIM
142   Wang et al. (2019), PFST Luo et al. (2021), and ATMConvGRU Yu et al. (2022), have been pro-
143
      posed. However, these RNN-based methods continue to struggle with gradient vanishing issues and
      require memory-intensive computations, particularly in handling long sequences Che et al. (2022).
144
145   In recent years, research has shifted towards exploring architectures that offer more efficient training
146   and reduced computational demands. For example, Han et al. Han et al. (2020) proposed a CNN-
147   based method that reframed the convective storm nowcasting problem as a classification task. Build-
148   ing on this, a UNet-based fully convolutional network (FCN) model was introduced for precipitation
149
      nowcasting Han et al. (2021), demonstrating that even simple FCN architectures can achieve per-
      formance comparable to ConvLSTM. SmaAt-UNet Trebing et al. (2021), which integrates attention
150
      modules and depthwise-separable convolutions, further enhanced predictive accuracy on real-world
151
      datasets, such as those from the Netherlands. Additionally, non-recurrent architectures like SimVP
152   Gao et al. (2022) and PhyDNet Guen & Thome (2020) have been explored, leveraging encoding-
153   decoding processes to make predictions. Furthermore, hybrid models combining CNNs and Trans-
154   formers, such as Rainformer Bai et al. (2022) and AA-TransUNet Yimin & Mehrkanoon (2020),
155   have shown promise in precipitation nowcasting. Despite these advances, a common challenge per-
156   sists: deterministic models tend to produce blurred predictions, particularly during high-intensity
157   precipitation events.

159   2.2   C ONDITIONAL D IFFUSION IN P RECIPITATION N OWCASTING
160
161   Diffusion models have emerged as a pivotal framework in generative modeling, driven by their
      unique approach of progressively diffusing and reconstructing noise. The foundational concepts of

162
      diffusion models were first introduced in Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015), but it was the introduction
163   of Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) Ho et al. (2020) that brought widespread
164   attention to this field. These models function by iteratively denoising Gaussian noise to learn a
165   target distribution, allowing for content generation conditioned on various inputs, such as labels,
166   text, or image features. The denoising network is trained to minimize the error defined by:
167
168                                     h         √      √                i
169                                    E |ϵ − ϵθ ( ᾱx0 + 1 − ᾱϵ, t, c)|2 ,                                      (1)

171   where X0 denotes the noise-free image, ϵ ∼ N (0, 1) represents Gaussian noise, α is a time-
172   dependent function, and c signifies the conditioning information. The training process is centered
173   on accurately predicting the noise added to the system, while the reverse process reconstructs the
174   original image X0 from the noise distribution N (0, 1) through iterative denoising.
175   As diffusion models have matured, they have proven to be invaluable tools in short-term precipita-
176   tion forecasting, particularly due to their denoising capabilities, which allow for the reconstruction
177   of precise target images or sequences from noisy inputs under various conditions. For instance, the
178   SRNDiff model Ling et al. (2024) integrates a conditional encoder to extract features from radar im-
179   ages, which are subsequently processed by a denoising network to produce high-resolution precipi-
180   tation forecasts. This end-to-end training approach has been shown to improve prediction accuracy,
181   especially in scenarios involving moderate to heavy rainfall. Expanding on this, the ExtDM model
182
      Zhang et al. (2024) introduces a distribution extrapolation mechanism, predicting future frames by
      extending the distribution of current frame features. Although initially designed for video prediction,
183
      this methodology’s emphasis on temporal consistency makes it highly applicable to precipitation
184
      forecasting.
185
186   Moreover, the PredDiff model Blücher et al. (2022) combines condition-guided diffusion with
187   domain-specific knowledge, ensuring that the forecasts not only align with historical data but also
188
      adhere to physical principles, thereby enhancing the reliability of predictions in extreme weather
      conditions. The Generative Diffusion Ensemble (GDE) model Asperti et al. (2023) further show-
189
      cases the potential of diffusion models in handling high levels of uncertainty in weather forecasting.
190
      By generating multiple forecast scenarios based on conditional guidance, and refining these through
191   post-processing, GDE underscores the ability to synthesize a range of potential outcomes, leading
192   to more accurate and consistent precipitation predictions.

203   R = {r1 , r2 , . . . , rn } and S = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sn }, consisting of n radar and satellite images, respec-
204   tively, with consistent spatial and temporal resolutions (10 min and 1 km). The goal of the UniDiff
205   is to predict a sequence of m future radar echo frames Ŷ = {ŷ1 , ŷ2 , . . . , ŷm }, formulated as:

213   Fusion Module, which integrates spatiotemporal features from radar and satellite data; (2) a base-

      The dataset utilized in this study comprises radar and satellite data collected over three years, from

363

366   For the purposes of this experiment, we leverage radar echo data in conjunction with infrared chan-
367   nel C13 from the Himawari-8 satellite. The dataset is divided into training and testing sets based
368
      on temporal segmentation: the training set includes data from August 2019 to August 2021 (en-

369
      2019 (a total of 2 months). Following preprocessing steps, such as denoising and interpolation, a
370
      sliding window approach is employed to segment the dataset into distinct events, with each event
371   comprising 6 frames of radar and satellite inputs, followed by 6 frames of radar outputs.
372
373   To focus specifically on significant precipitation events, the dataset is filtered by computing the mean
374
      radar reflectivity across the 6 input frames. Events where the mean value exceeds a threshold of 1
      dBZ are selected for further analysis. As a result, the training set contains 5859 precipitation events,
375
      while the testing set includes 1068 precipitation events.

530
531
      To assess the importance of individual components within the UniDiff framework, we performed
      ablation studies by systematically removing key modules, specifically the satellite data input (Our
532
      w/o satellite), the attention-guided multi-source fusion module (Our w/o MSFusion), and the ST-
533
      Guided UNet (Our w/o RST-Unet). The results, presented in Tables 1 and 2, reveal that the removal
534   of any of these components significantly degrades the model’s performance, particularly in CSI and
535   HSS metrics. The absence of the MSFusion module, which refers to the attention-guided multi-
536   source fusion process (replaced by simple element-wise addition of the two data sources), resulted
537   in the most substantial drop in performance, highlighting its critical role in effectively integrating
538   radar and satellite data to enhance prediction accuracy. These ablation studies confirm that each
539   component within the UniDiff framework is essential for achieving the high levels of accuracy and
      robustness observed in our experiments.

      5   C ONCLUSION
541
542
      In this paper, we introduced UniDiff, an innovative end-to-end framework tailored for precipitation
543   nowcasting, which harnesses the power of multi-source data integration and diffusion-based refine-
544   ment. By effectively fusing radar and satellite data through an attention-guided multi-source fusion
545   mechanism, UniDiff capitalizes on the complementary strengths of these heterogeneous data sources
546   to enhance the accuracy and robustness of precipitation predictions. The framework’s coarse-to-
547   fine refinement strategy, utilizing an RST-Unet-based diffusion residual component, bridges the
548   gap between deterministic and probabilistic approaches, enabling the model to capture broad spa-
549   tiotemporal dynamics while preserving fine-grained details. This interactive process ensures that
550   the coarse predictions are progressively refined, guided by conditional cues from the multi-source
551
      fusion, resulting in superior forecasting performance. Extensive evaluations on the Jiangsu dataset
      have demonstrated that UniDiff outperforms existing state-of-the-art models, particularly in main-
552
      taining higher accuracy as the lead time increases.
