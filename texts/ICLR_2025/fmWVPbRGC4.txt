009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Much of the research on the interpretability of deep neural networks has focused on
012            studying the visual features that maximally activate individual neurons. However,
013            recent work has cast doubts on the usefulness of such local representations for
014            understanding the behavior of deep neural networks because individual neurons
015            tend to respond to multiple unrelated visual patterns, a phenomenon referred to
016            as “superposition”. A promising alternative to disentangle these complex patterns
017
               is learning sparsely distributed vector representations from entire network layers,
               as the resulting basis vectors seemingly encode single identifiable visual patterns
018
               consistently. Thus, one would expect the resulting code to align better with human-
019
               perceivable visual patterns, but supporting evidence remains, at best, anecdotal. To
020            fill this gap, we conducted three large-scale psychophysics experiments collected
021            from a pool of 560 participants. Our findings provide (i) strong evidence that
022            features obtained from sparse distributed representations are easier to interpret by
023            human observers and (ii) that this effect is more pronounced in the deepest layers
024            of a neural network. Complementary analyses also reveal that (iii) features derived
025            from sparse distributed representations contribute more to the model´s decision.
026            Overall, our results highlight that distributed representations constitute a superior
027            basis for interpretability, underscoring a need for the field to move beyond the
028            interpretation of local neural codes in favor of sparsely distributed ones.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      One of the goals of explainable AI (XAI) in computer vision is to identify the visual features and
      characterize the representations used by deep neural networks (DNNs) to categorize images (Ribeiro
034
      et al., 2016; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Smilkov et al., 2017; Petsiuk et al., 2018; Selvaraju et al.,
035
      2017; Linsley et al., 2019; Fel et al., 2021; 2023c;a; Novello et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2016; Bau et al.,
036   2017; Cammarata et al., 2020b; Kim et al., 2018; Ghorbani et al., 2019). In general, identifying these
037   features requires uncovering the visual patterns that drive the activation of units within a network.
038
039
      This goal is shared with the study of biological vision, where there is extensive research over the last
      several decades focused on identifying the “preferred stimulus” of individual neurons in the visual
040
      cortex (Hubel & Wiesel, 1959; Lettvin et al., 1959; Tsunoda et al., 2001; Wang et al., 1996; Pasupathy
041
      & Connor, 2001; Quiroga, 2005). This approach to visual neuroscience reflected the dominant theory
042   at the time, known as the “grandmother (cell)” theory , which postulates that information in the
043   visual system is stored locally – at the level of single neurons – and that the visual system contains
044   specific neurons that respond to particular objects or people (including one’s own grandmother).
045   Early XAI advances inspired by neuroscience research similarly focused on understanding local
046   representations (Zhou et al., 2016; Bau et al., 2017). This led to the development of more sophisticated
047   optimization methods to synthesize maximally activating images for individual neurons (Erhan et al.,
048   2009; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014a; Yosinski et al., 2015; Olah et al., 2017; 2020; Nguyen et al., 2016a;b;
049   Cammarata et al., 2020b).
050   This parallelism between XAI and neuroscience extends beyond vision as recent work has found
051   neurons in multi-modal systems that respond to very high-level concepts beyond simple image
052   appearance, including hand-drawing and text (Goh et al., 2021). Interestingly, the authors identified a
053   “Halle Berry” neuron in CLIP, reminiscent of the neuroscience finding reported two decades ago in
      the human brain (Quiroga, 2005).

075
      At the same time, a paradigm shift is taking place in neuroscience, where the study of neural
076   populations is quickly superseding the study of single neurons (for a review see Ebitz & Hayden
077   (2021)) because the neural code is believed to be sparse and distributed rather than local (Haxby et al.,
078   2001; Quiroga et al., 2008; 2013). Interestingly, a similar shift is emerging in XAI, because local
079   representations are known to suffer from the “superposition” problem (Arora et al., 2018; Cheung
080   et al., 2019; Olah et al., 2020; Elhage et al., 2022; Fel et al., 2023b): the number of features captured
081   by DNNs might be larger than the number of neurons. Therefore, the neurons’ activations might
082   be driven by multiple unrelated features. To address this challenge, the XAI community has started
083   using dictionary learning methods (Fel et al., 2023c;b; Bricken et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024) to
084
      project the activations of DNNs onto a new basis of vectors, each activated by a single distinct feature.
      From an interpretability perspective, representations driven by single features are more desirable
085
      because they are expected to be easier to understand by a human, i.e., a unit responding to a single
086
      visual pattern –compared to multiple patterns– is inherently easier to interpret. An implicit hypothesis
087   is that applying dictionary learning methods to network activations helps break down complex visual
088   patterns into simpler ones corresponding to single features (Fig. 1.a), which, in turn, might be
089   easier for humans to interpret (Fig. 1.b). Scaling up standard interpretability evaluations to study
090   representations poses significant challenges (Colin et al., 2022). A practical alternative is to assess
091   the ambiguity—or perplexity—of the visual features derived from these representations (Borowski
092   et al., 2021) (see 3.2.1 for a more thorough elaboration on this point).
093   Despite the growing consensus that distributed representations constitute a stronger basis for inter-
094   pretability compared to local ones (single neurons), empirical evidence remains scarce. This paper
095   aims to fill this gap. Specifically, we contend that a representation can be considered superior if the
096   features derived from it are more intelligible, i.e., easier for humans to make sense of while being
097   demonstrably used by the model in its decision-making process. By means of computational and
098   psychophysics experiments, we set out to find which of the local vs. distributed representations better
099   meets these two conditions.
100   In sum, the main contributions of this paper are as follows:
101
102          • We conduct three large-scale psychophysics experiments for a total of 15,720 responses
103            from a pool of 560 participants, to evaluate the visual ambiguity of the features derived
104            from local vs distributed representations (see Fig 1). In the process, we identify a potential
105            semantic bias in the experimental protocols commonly used in the field (Borowski et al.,
106
               2021; Zimmermann et al., 2023), and provide an approach to at least partially mitigate it.
107          • Our findings provide strong evidence that (i) features derived from distributed represen-
               tations are significantly easier for humans to interpret than features derived from local


                                                         2
108
               representations, and (ii) this effect is even more pronounced in the deepest layers of a neural
109            network.
110
             • Additionally, we observe that (iii) models rely significantly more on features derived from
111
               distributed representations compared to those derived from local representations. Overall,
112
               our results suggest that distributed representations provide a substantially better foundation
113            for the interpretability of models than local representations.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
117
      From local to distributed representations in XAI. Early research on explainable AI (XAI)
118
      in computer vision developed attribution methods (Zeiler et al., 2011; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014a;b;
119
      Sundararajan et al., 2017; Smilkov et al., 2017; Fong & Vedaldi, 2017; Ancona et al., 2018; Shrikumar
120   et al., 2017; Chattopadhay et al., 2018; Fel et al., 2021; Novello et al., 2022) to understand specific
121   model predictions. These methods predominantly aimed to identify ”where” are the most important
122   pixels of an image, given a specific model prediction. Unfortunately, these methods fell short in
123   explaining the ”what” (Kim et al., 2018; 2022; Taesiri et al., 2022; Colin et al., 2022), i.e., the
124   visual features that the models rely on to make their predictions. As a result, new XAI methods,
125   including Feature Visualization approaches (Nguyen et al., 2016b; Olah et al., 2017), were developed
126   to provide insights into the features that neurons or model layers respond to. These efforts revealed
127   that the neurons’ activations can be driven by visually distinct features (Nguyen et al., 2016b; 2019;
128
      Cammarata et al., 2020a; Bricken et al., 2023). A similar behavior was observed in other fields, and
      notably in Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Elhage et al., 2022).
129
130   The neurons’ tendency to respond to multiple unrelated visual elements indicates that single neurons
131   might not align well with the model’s internal representations. This phenomenon, known as super-
132   position (Arora et al., 2018; Cheung et al., 2019; Olah et al., 2020; Elhage et al., 2022; Fel et al.,
133   2023b) or feature collapse (Fel et al., 2023c), suggests that there could be significantly more features
134
      than neurons in a model. Consequently, interpreting neurons might be no more meaningful than
      interpreting arbitrary directions in the feature space. To achieve effective model interpretation, it
135
      is, therefore, essential to identify an interpretable basis that facilitates the extraction of meaningful
136
      features. This observation has led to increased interest, over the past five years, towards examining
137   deep learning models by considering the distributed nature of latent space representations. More
138   specifically, it spurred the development of concept extraction methods (Ghorbani et al., 2019; Zhang
139   et al., 2021; Fel et al., 2023c;b; Graziani et al., 2023; Vielhaben et al., 2023) that leverage dictionary
140   learning, especially overcomplete dictionaries. In NLP, sparse autoencoders (SAEs) (Elhage et al.,
141   2022; Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023; Tamkin et al., 2023) are extensively studied
142   and regarded as a promising direction for discovering interpretable bases. In this paper, we are
143   interested in comparing the suitability of local vs distributed representations to serve as a basis for
144   the interpretability of deep learning models in computer vision.
145
146   Human-evaluation of interpretability. Since the ultimate goal of XAI is to make complex models
147
      understandable to humans, it is essential to incorporate human evaluation in measuring interpretability
      through psychophysics experiments. Human evaluation serves as a critical benchmark for assessing
148
      whether the explanations provided by XAI systems are comprehensible, useful, and actionable for
149
      end-users. This human-centric approach ensures that interpretability methods are not just theoretically
150   sound but also practically effective in real-world applications.
151
152   To the best of our knowledge, Borowski et al. (2021) were the first to quantify the interpretability
153
      of features through psychophysics experiments. Their approach involved visualizing unit responses
      by contrasting maximally and minimally activating stimuli. Specifically, Borowski et al. (2021)
154
      focused on studying features from local representations (i.e., single unit activations) of an Inception
155
      V1 (Szegedy et al., 2015) trained on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009; Russakovsky et al., 2015). In their
156   experiments, participants were shown maximally and minimally activating images from the ImageNet
157   ILSVRC 2012 validation set (Russakovsky et al., 2015) to illustrate a specific feature. They were then
158   asked to select which of two query images also activated the feature (see Fig. 2 for an example trial).
159   As a proxy for interpretability, they measured the visual coherence of maximally activating stimuli of
160   a given feature. It is maximal when the visual feature is unambiguous. The main conclusion from
161   these experiments is that, from a human-centric perspective, maximally activating natural images
      are more effective for studying features than synthetically generated feature visualizations (Olah


                                                          3
162
      et al., 2017). Zimmermann et al. (2021) proposed a variation of this task to investigate if humans
163   gained causal insights from those visualizations. They tested the participants’ ability to predict
164   the effect of an intervention, such as occluding a patch of the image, on the activation of the unit.
165   Both by means of a large-scale crowdsourced psychophysics experiment and measurements with
166   experts, they found that synthetic feature visualizations (Olah et al., 2017) helped humans perform
167   the task successfully. However, these visualizations did not provide a significant advantage over
168   other visualizations, such as exemplary images. Finally, Zimmermann et al. (2023) extended the
169   work of Borowski et al. (2021) to a broader set of deep learning architectures used for computer
170   vision tasks, including a ResNet50 (He et al., 2016). Their main conclusion is that increasing the
171
      scale of the model does enhance the interpretability of the features. While both our work and that
      of Zimmermann et al. (2023) build upon the experimental protocol of Borowski et al. (2021), they
172
      focus on scaling Borowski et al. (2021)’s insights, whereas we adapt the protocol to generate insights
173
      into a different and novel research question: comparing the suitability of features derived from local
174   vs distributed representations for interpretability.
175
176   Interestingly, in a similar vein but within the context of NLP, (Bricken et al., 2023) compare the
177
      interpretability of 162 features extracted from both local and distributed representations. One of
      the authors scored each feature using samples drawn uniformly across the spectrum of activation.
178
      Their findings indicate that features obtained from distributed representations are substantially more
179
      interpretable than those from single neurons. The research presented in this paper differs from this
180   work in the application domain, the experimental design, single participant in their study vs 15,720
181   responses from a pool of 560 participants in our study.

189   3.1   T ECHNICAL M ETHODS
190
191   Model All the experiments described in this paper were performed on a ResNet50 (He et al., 2016)
192   from the Torchvision (Marcel & Rodriguez, 2010) library, pre-trained on ImageNet-1k (Deng et al.,
193
      2009).
194
195   Sparse Distributed Representations To compute sparse distributed representations for the dis-
196
      tributed condition in our psychophysics experiments (see Section 3.2), we employed CRAFT (Fel
      et al., 2023c), an off-the-shelf dictionary learning method. Specifically, given a model f : X → A
197
      that maps from an input space X ⊆ Rd to an activation space A ⊆ Rp (e.g., any layer of the network),
198
      we compute the activations A = f (X) ∈ Rn×p , where X = [x1 , . . . , xn ]⊤ ∈ Rn×d represents a
199   set of n input data points. Each row ai ≥ 0 of A contains the non-negative activations for a given
200   data point xi , due to the use of ReLU activations. To obtain the sparse distributed representations,
201   Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) is applied to approximate A as:
202
203                                  (Z⋆ , D⋆ ) = arg min     A − ZD⊤ F ,
                                                  Z≥0, D≥0
204
205   where ∥·∥F denotes the Frobenius norm. Here, Z ∈ Rn×k are the codes (or concept coefficients),
206   and D ∈ Rp×k forms the dictionary (or concept bank). Both Z and D are constrained to have non-
207   negative entries and tend to be sparse due to the properties of NMF. The dictionary matrix D provides
208   a new set of basis vectors (concepts) aligned with the activation patterns of the neural network, while
209   Z contains the coefficients representing the original activations A in terms of these concepts. CRAFT
210   is particularly well suited for our purposes as it has been shown to extract interpretable features from
211   deep neural networks (Fel et al., 2023c).
212

      activation along this dimension to zero for xi , resulting in modified activations a′i . These modified

      3.2     P SYCHOPHYSICS S TUDIES
249
250   3.2.1    E XPERIMENTAL PROTOCOL
251
252   Interpretability is a human-centric attribute. Hence, to shed light on how well local vs distributed
253   representations serve as a basis for the interpretability of deep neural networks, we performed
254   three large-scale online psychophysics experiments. The three experiments focus on evaluating
255
      the interpretability of features extracted from both types of representations. In practice, given the
      challenges of scaling up standard interpretability evaluations to study representations (Colin et al.,
256
      2022), we adapt the experimental protocol of Borowski et al. (2021) to measure the ambiguity, or
257
      perplexity, of the visual feature as a proxy for its interpretability. More precisely, given a feature and
258   a set of images that illustrate it—e.g., maximally activating images—, this protocol measures how
259   visually coherent humans find this set of images. The more visually coherent it is, the more likely
260   they are to correctly identify another member of this set, namely the correct query image. Hence, in
261   our results, we report the visual coherence of a feature as the proportion of participants that correctly
262   identified the query image. This visual coherence should be maximal when the feature represents a
263   single visual pattern that people can interpret.
264   Each participant was assigned to one of two conditions: local or distributed representation. After
265   successfully finishing a practice session composed of 9 trials, participants were asked to sequentially
266   perform 40 different trials of the same task, where each trial corresponded to a different network unit.
267   For each selected network unit and trial, participants were shown on the left and right-hand side of
268   the screen two panels of 9 reference images each, separated by 2 images in the center, which were
269   denoted as queries, as illustrated in Figure 2. The task across all experiments consisted of selecting
      the query image that participants believed shared the same visual elements as the 9 reference images


                                                          5
270
      displayed on the right panel. The selection of the reference images shown in each panel differed
271   depending on the experiment, as explained below.
272
273   3.2.2    N EURON AND S TIMULI S ELECTION
274
275   Neuron selection In line with the literature (Zimmermann et al., 2023), we aimed to obtain a
276   representative sample of units within the different layers of the ResNet50. As a starting point, we
277   selected the 80 units reported in (Zimmermann et al., 2023) for a ResNet50 (He et al., 2016) model.
278
      Given that CRAFT requires positive activations, we replaced the neurons from the convolutional
      layers (which could have non-positive activations) to their equivalent in the following batchnorm
279
      layer (layer1.1.conv1 neuron 53 -> layer1.1.bn1 neuron 53). As a result, we selected 80 neurons
280
      distributed across 43 layers that are grouped in four blocks. Thus, the names of the layers used in
281   this paper (layer1 through layer4) are directly obtained from the modules of the ResNet50 Pytorch
282   implementation.
283
284   Stimuli selection for the local representation condition For each of the selected neurons, we
285   identified 2, 900 images from the validation set of ImageNet ILSVRC 2012 (Russakovsky et al.,
286   2015) obtained as follows: the 2, 500 most strongly activating images and the 400 least strongly
287   activating images. Following the procedure described by Borowski et al. (2021), we illustrated a
288   visual feature through both maximally activating (images that possess the feature, see Fig. 2 right
289   panel) and minimally activating (images that do not possess the feature, see Fig. 2 left panel) stimuli.
290
      Again, like in previous work (Borowski et al., 2021), for the former, we selected a random sample of
      9 images from the top 150 images; for the latter, we uniformly sampled 9 images from the bottom
291
      20 images. We created 10 different trials per feature following this procedure to ensure image
292
      independence in the results. When participants were assigned to a feature, we randomly sampled one
293   of those 10 trials to illustrate the feature.
294
295   Stimuli selection for the distributed representation condition Our hypothesis is that applying
296   dictionary learning methods to local representation allows us to disentangle the superposition of
297   features that might drive the activation of a neuron. Hence, to test this hypothesis, all the distributed
298   representations studied in this work are obtained from the local representation. In practice, for
299   each neuron in the local condition, we selected the top 300 maximally activating images, i.e., those
300   that most strongly led to the activation of the neuron. We used CRAFT to identify a new basis of
301   distributed vectors through which their activations can be expressed. Following the recommended
302
      procedure in (Fel et al., 2023c), we constrained the dimensionality of this new basis to d = 10. From
      these 10 directions, we selected the one that was the most frequently the most activated across the
303
      300 images. Once a direction was chosen, we ranked the 2, 900 images according to their activation
304
      in that direction to obtain the images that illustrate a visual feature in the distributed representation.

307
308   In this section, we present the details of each of the three psychophysics experiments conducted in
309   this work.
310
311   Experiment I This experiment is an adaption of the methodology proposed by (Borowski et al.,
312   2021) with two conditions: local vs distributed representations. An illustration of a trial belonging to
313
      Experiment I can be found in Figure 2. In this experiment, we performed a total of 1,600 trials (80
      neurons × 2 conditions × 10 trials per unit).
314
315
      Experiment II The main objective of Experiment II was to control for potential semantic con-
316
      founding variables present in Experiment I. If the reference images that possess/do not possess the
317
      feature of interest belong to very different semantic groups (classes), then it could be possible to solve
318   the task through simple semantic grouping. Figure 3 illustrates this phenomenon: in the example
319   depicted in the figure, it is easier to solve the trial by inferring that the feature of interest is not about
320   a monkey than it is to infer the actual visual feature present in the reference images. The goal of
321   Experiment II is to control for this potential semantic confound as follows: given a set of reference
322   images that possess the feature of interest, we extract their semantic labels from ImageNet and aim to
323   find within the 400 minimally activating images a set of 10 images that share the same distribution
      of semantic labels. We define four levels of semantic similarity (level 0 to level 3). In level 0, the

396   4       R ESULTS
397
398   In this section, we summarize the results obtained from analyzing the responses from the 138, 133,
399   and 122 participants who successfully completed Experiments I, II, and III, as well as the results
400   from our feature importance analysis.
401   Unless stated otherwise, all of our behavioral data analyses consist of generalized logistic mixed-
402   effects regressions (GLMER), with trial accuracy (1 vs. 0) as the dependent variable. The random-
403   effects structure included both a by-participant and a by-unit 2 random intercept, as well as a by-unit
404   random slope for the condition variable. We used the lme4 (Bates et al., 2015) package in R (R
405   Core Team, 2021) to fit the models and lmerTest to obtain p-values for the fixed effects, with an
406   α-level of 0.05 for statistical significance.
407
408   Reproduction of previous results. Given that the experimental protocol used in Experiment I,
409   with the local representation condition, is the same as the one described in (Zimmermann et al.,
410   2023), we first evaluate to which degree our results corroborate previous research. For the ResNet50,
411   Zimmermann et al. (Zimmermann et al., 2023) report an average task performance of 83.0% ± 2.03 .
412   In our experiment, we obtain an average performance of 78.8% ± 1.5. Given the results’ similarity
413
      and that the selected units are not exactly the same (as described in Section 3), we conclude that
      Experiment I reproduces previously reported findings regarding local representations. This result
414
      also serves as an external validation of our experimental protocol.
415
416
417   Human performance is superior in the distributed representation condition. Based on our main
418   hypothesis that a distributed representation constitutes a better basis for interpretability than a local
419
      representation, we predicted that participants would be better at selecting the maximally activating

490   5    D ISCUSSION
491
492   Conclusion In this work, we have investigated and compared the suitability of local and distributed
493
      representations to serve as a basis for the interpretability of deep neural networks. We contend that
      the best-suited basis possesses a dictionary of features that is (a) more aligned with the set of features
494
      that human observers can interpret while (b) being demonstrably more important for decision-making
495
      by the model. Through psychophysics experiments, we consistently find that features derived from
496   distributed representations are easier for humans to interpret, particularly when features are derived
497   from deeper layers in the network. Equally important, we find that the model relies significantly
498   more on those features to make its decisions. To the best of our knowledge, our results provide
499   the first empirical evidence of the superiority of distributed representations over local ones for the
500   interpretability of deep neural networks.
501
502   Limitations Our study is not exempt from limitations. First, the methodology proposed
503   by Borowski et al. (2021) and followed by Zimmermann et al. (2023) utilizes the entire ImageNet
504   validation set with the goal of studying a broad range of stimuli and thereby increasing the likelihood
505   of identifying stimuli that are representative of neurons’ selectivity. While such motivation is sound, it
506
      poses a significant challenge when performing psychophysics experiments: neurons that are selective
      to class-specific features (e.g., fish scale) will be maximally activated when presented with stimuli
507
      that belong to the corresponding classes (e.g., fish) and minimally activated when provided with
508
      stimuli that belong to other classes (e.g., dogs). In those cases, the task can be solved trivially using
509   semantics. The design of Experiment II reflects an initial attempt to mitigate this confounding factor.
510   However, both quantitative results and a manual exploration of the trials by the authors hint at only a
511   partial success of the adopted methodology to address this challenge. We leave to future research a
512   further refinement of the experimental protocol.
513
      Second, while the original work (Borowski et al., 2021) this manuscript builds on aimed at measuring
514
      the interpretability of features, we have been more conservative with the terminology employed
515   in this paper as we believe that the current experimental protocol only measures a loose proxy
516   of interpretability. Interpretability, or understanding, of a complex visual pattern usually requires
517   procedural learning with feedback (Ashby & Maddox, 2005). Given that, in practice, this protocol
518   measures a score based on a set of images presented in a single trial, it seems unreasonable to expect
519   this score to capture interpretability. In contrast, it seems more reasonable to expect this score to
520   characterize something more visual, like the ambiguity of the features, i.e., how complex it might
521   be to interpret the features if one had to. Such ambiguity can be inferred, at least in part, by the
522   visual coherence of the set of images. Zimmermann et al. have indeed shown that responses from
523
      participants can be accurately predicted based on visual coherency—or perceptual similarity—of a
      trial.
524
525   While we believe that these metrics are useful proxies and that features achieving a high score at
526   them are desirable for interpretability, we want to emphasize that we do not claim that it means that
527   they are interpretable. Perceptual similarity differs from interpretability; being able to group images
528   correctly does not necessarily imply a deep understanding of the underlying factors driving those
529
      groupings or the ability to explain why the images belong together based on intrinsic features or
      causal relationships.
530
531   Nevertheless, we believe that the adopted experimental protocol serves as a solid foundation for
532   developing a scalable and robust paradigm to measure the interpretability of features in deep learning
533   models. We leave to future work the development of such a paradigm.

      D    S UPPLEMENTARY INFORMATION ABOUT EXPERIMENT III
865
866
      Experimental protocol Given that the adopted experimental protocol was originally designed to
867   evaluate the informativeness of feature visualizations, we devised Experiment III to shed light on
868   the value that a state-of-the-art feature visualization method (MACO) would add to the task. We
869   subscribe to the idea that feature visualizations are more suitable to complement natural images
870   than to replace them. Thus, Experiment III implements a protocol similar to the mixed condition
871   reported by Zimmermann et al. (Zimmermann et al., 2021). In practice, Experiment III was based on
872   Experiment II, but in each trial, 4 of the natural images displayed both on the left and right panels
873   were replaced by 4 feature visualizations, as depicted in Figure 6.
874
875   Method To synthesize feature visualizations for Experiment III (see Section 3.2), we used
876   MACO (Fel et al., 2023a), inspired by the method by Olah et al. (Olah et al., 2017). MACO
877
      generates feature visualizations with a natural Fourier amplitude spectrum by fixing the amplitude
      spectrum to the empirical mean derived from natural images. Specifically, MACO optimizes directly
878
      in the Fourier domain F(x) by modifying the phase φ of the target image while keeping the magni-
879
      tude r of the spectrum fixed. This constraint on the magnitude helps prevent high-frequency artifacts
880   and ensures that the resulting images remain visually coherent. Formally, let r denote the average
881   amplitude spectrum computed across the ImageNet dataset, and let d be a target concept direction to
882   be maximized. MACO solves the following optimization problem:
883                                                                                      ⋆

                    φ⋆ = arg max f F −1 r ◦ eiφ · d , and x⋆ = F −1 r ◦ eiφ .

884
                             φ
885
              ⋆
886   where x is the feature visualization obtained after optimization, F denotes 2-D Discrete Fourier
887   Transform (DFT) on X , F −1 its inverse and ◦ represents element-wise multiplication. Additionally,
888   MACO introduces an attribution-based transparency mask to highlight spatially important regions in
889   the visualizations, enhancing interpretability.
890
891   The value of feature visualization remains unclear. Finally, Figure 7 depicts the results obtained
892
      from Experiment III, which combined natural images with synthetic images corresponding to feature
      visualizations using MACO. Similarly to (Zimmermann et al., 2021), we do not find that mixing
893
      stimuli from feature visualization with natural images helped participants perform better at the task.
894
      In fact, participants performed overall significantly worse in Experiment III than in Experiment II:
895   βdepth:Exp3 = −0.63, SE = 0.19, z = −3.3, p < 0.001. Interestingly, the worst performance was
896   observed in the local condition for units located in the deepest layers of the network, with a drop in
897   performance from 80.2% in Experiment I to 68.3% in Experiment III. Furthermore, the difference
898   in the per-layer task performance obtained in layer 4 in each condition is 8.2%, 17.1%, and 18.9%,
899   respectively. We leave to future work the investigation into the specific factors that contributed to
900   this decline in performance. Potential avenues for future research include exploring the cognitive
901   load imposed by mixed stimuli and identifying optimal conditions under which feature visualization
902   might enhance rather than hinder task performance.

       G.1    C OMPLEXITY
1142
       Kolmogorov complexity. The first hypothesis we test is whether features from distributed repre-
1143   sentation are easier to interpret than features from local conditions because they are less complex.
1144   While we do not have access to the feature per se, we have access to the images that possess the
1145   features as a proxy. While measuring the complexity of images is still an open research question,
1146   there are works that show that the Kolmogorov complexity of images (Li et al., 2004) correlate well
1147   with human-derived ratings for the complexity of natural images (Forsythe et al., 2008; Forsythe,
1148   2009). In practice, we follow previous works (Li et al., 2004; de Rooij & Vitányi, 2006) and use a
1149   standard compression technique (JPEG) to approximate the Kolmogorov complexity of images. The
1150   hypothesis is that the more an image can be compressed, the less complex the features that compose
1151
       the images.
1152
1153   Methodology For a given target feature, we conducted T trials T ∈ [1, 10], based on how many
1154
       trials can be semantically controlled). In each trial, we had access to 10 images that possess the target
       feature. We measured the average Kolmogorov complexity of these 10 images by recording their
1155
       compressed file sizes after JPEG compression. We then averaged these complexities across all trials
1156
       for each feature, resulting in a measure of Kolmogorov complexity for that feature. This methodology
1157   is applied to every feature.
1158
1159
       Results. We did not find a significant correlation between the Kolmogorov complexity of the images
1160   and the visual coherency scores from Experiments II (ResNet50) (Fig. 13 and IV (VGG16) (Fig 14).
1161   This suggests that while complexity might be a factor, the superiority of features from distributed
1162   representations cannot be explained by complexity alone.

       and change in structural information.
