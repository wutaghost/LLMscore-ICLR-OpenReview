009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Few-Shot Action Recognition (FSAR) is a challenging task that requires recogniz-
012
               ing novel action categories with a few labeled videos. Recent works typically ap-
013
               ply semantically coarse category names as auxiliary contexts to guide the learning
014            of discriminative visual features. However, such context provided by the action
015            names is too limited to provide sufficient background knowledge for capturing
016            novel spatial and temporal concepts in actions. In this paper, we propose D I ST,
017            an innovative Decomposition-incorporation framework for FSAR that makes use
018            of decoupled Spatial and Temporal knowledge provided by large language models
019            to learn expressive multi-granularity prototypes. In the decomposition stage, we
020            decouple vanilla action names into diverse spatio-temporal attribute descriptions
021            (i.e., action-related knowledge). Such commonsense knowledge complements se-
022
               mantic contexts from spatial and temporal perspectives. In the incorporation stage,
               we propose Spatial/Temporal Knowledge Compensators (SKC/PKC) to discover
023
               discriminative object- and frame-level prototypes, respectively. In SKC, object-
024
               level prototypes adaptively aggregate important patch tokens under the guidance
025            of spatial knowledge. Moreover, in TKC, frame-level prototypes utilize temporal
026            attributes to assist in inter-frame temporal relation modeling, further understand-
027            ing diverse temporal patterns in videos. These learned prototypes at varying levels
028            of granularity thus provide transparency in capturing fine-grained spatial details
029            and dynamic temporal information, so as to enable accurate recognition of both
030            appearance-centric and motion-centric actions. Experimental results show D I ST
031            achieves state-of-the-art results on four standard FSAR datasets (i.e., Kinetics,
032            UCF101, HMDB51 and SSv2-small). Full code will be released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   With deep learning advancements [1, 2, 3, 4], significant progress has been made in the field of ac-
039
      tion recognition [5, 6, 7, 8, 9] recently. However, this success relies heavily on a large amount of
      manually-labeled samples, which are time-consuming and expensive to acquire. To alleviate the
040
      data-hunger issue, considerable works [10, 11, 12, 13, 14] have turned their attention to few-shot ac-
041
      tion recognition (FSAR), where there exist base action classes (seen) with a large volume of training
042   examples, and novel action classes (unseen) with unlabeled samples. FSAR learns feature represen-
043   tation from base action classes, and then evaluates its generalization ability on novel action classes.
044
045
      Modern FSAR solutions [15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 21] are largely built upon metric-based meta-
      learning paradigm [22], where the model learns class (prototype) representation and performs
046
      prototype-query matching with respect to predefined or learned distance metrics. Among them,
047
      the top-leading methods [11, 15, 23, 13] directly extract class-related spatio-temporal feature repre-
048   sentation from raw visual signals. Though impressive, these methods lack a basic grasp of explicit
049   action knowledge, struggling to learn new concepts in action classes, particularly under data-limited
050   conditions. Recent works [24, 21, 25] transfer knowledge from pre-trained vision-language mod-
051   els [26, 27] (e.g., CLIP [27]) to enhance FSAR model capability. However, these methods typically
052   apply semantically coarse or ambiguous category names as auxiliary context information to com-
053   pensate for visual features. Such context provided by the action names is too limited to provide
      enough background knowledge for video action understanding [28, 29, 30].

069   To address this issue, we study how to effectively collect and leverage action-related commonsense
070
      knowledge provided by large language models (LLMs), compensating for visual information and
      thus enhancing the few-shot learning capacity. The LLM serves as a knowledge base to provide
071
      action-related background commonsense descriptions from complementary spatial and temporal
072
      perspectives, which we refer to as decoupled “spatio-temporal prior knowledge”. Compared to
073   vanilla categories, spatio-temporal prior knowledge i) makes up for missing context information
074   to achieve semantic completeness, and ii) transforms unseen categories into known commonsense
075   descriptions, easily interpreted by pre-trained language models.
076
      In light of the above, we develop a novel decomposition-incorporation framework for FSAR: D I ST,
077
      which firstly decomposes vanilla category names into diverse spatio-temporal attribute descriptions,
078
      and then incorporates decoupled commonsense knowledge and visual features to guide the learn-
079   ing of object-level and frame-level prototypes. As shown in Fig. 1, for the decomposition stage,
080   we make use of LLMs, to generate action-related commonsense descriptions (i.e., external contex-
081   tual information and different steps of action) from coarse category names. Such comprehensive
082   descriptions complement semantic contexts from spatial and temporal perspectives. In the incor-
083   poration stage, we propose Spatial/Temporal Knowledge Compensators (SKC/TKC), which incor-
084   porate decoupled prior knowledge and visual features to form discriminative object-level (spatial)
085   and frame-level (temporal) prototypes, respectively. Specifically, SKC aggregates important patches
086   into compact object prototypes by patch-level cross-attention within each frame, and further guides
087
      object-level prototype learning with the assistance of spatial prior knowledge. These object-level
      prototypes filter out video noise and focus on informative image patches, which refer to the most
088
      class-related ones and correspond to key entities. Meanwhile, TKC captures the temporal rela-
089
      tionships between frame-level prototypes through inter-frame interaction, and then enables these
090   prototypes to aggregate essential semantic information from temporal prior knowledge. The learned
091   different-level prototypes can capture fine-grained spatial details and dynamic temporal information,
092   respectively, so as to yield more accurate action recognition results.
093
      Overall, our contributions are summarized as follows: ❶ We pioneer the early exploration in mak-
094
      ing use of action-related prior knowledge for FSAR, and to achieve this end, we construct back-
095
      ground commonsense descriptions provided by LLMs in a spatiotemporal-decoupled manner. ❷
096   We propose a novel decomposition-incorporation framework that decouples category names into di-
097   verse spatial and temporal prior knowledge, and then incorporates them and visual features to learn
098   object-level and frame-level prototypes in a dual way. ❸ We design Spatial/Temporal Knowledge
099   Compensators (SKC/TKC) that inject decoupled prior knowledge into different-level prototypes to
100   capture fine-grained spatial details and dynamic temporal information.
101
      To the best of our knowledge, we make the pioneering effort to explore the application of diverse
102   spatio-temporal prior knowledge in FSAR, aiming to effectively provide semantic contexts for the
103   learning of different-level prototypes from multiple perspectives. Different from simply combining
104   rich LLM-generated descriptions (i.e., only one sentence about actions) and frame-level features in
105   a single branch, our framework conducts customized feature interaction for different branches to
106   incorporate i) patch-level features and spatial knowledge; and ii) frame-level features and temporal
107   knowledge, respectively (see Table 5c in §4.4). Such a framework can discover fine-grained spa-
      tial patterns and dynamic temporal patterns, hence producing more accurate few-shot results. To

115   2   R ELATED W ORK
116
117   Few-shot Image Classification. The objective of few-shot image classification [35, 36] is to recog-
118   nize new categories with a small number of annotated samples. Existing methods can be roughly
119   categorized into three groups: i) Augmentation-based methods [2, 37, 38] exploit various augmenta-
120   tion strategies to alleviate the data scarcity dilemma, mainly including spatial deformation [39] and
121   feature augmentation [40, 41]; ii) Optimization-based methods [42, 43, 44, 45] learn optimization
122   states, like model initialization [42, 43] or step sizes [44, 45], to update models with a few gradient
123   steps; and iii) Metric-based methods [46, 47, 22, 48, 49, 50] learn a class representation (prototype)
124
      by averaging embeddings belonging to the same class, and predict query (i.e., test sample) labels
      with respect to predefined [46, 47, 22, 48] or learned [49, 50] distance metric. Our work is more
125
      closely related to the metric-based methods [47, 22], whereas we focus on few-shot action recog-
126
      nition – a more challenging task that requires handling videos encompassing a wealth of temporal
127   information due to common and distinct patterns in nearby frames [51].
128
129   Few-shot Action Recognition (FSAR). FSAR is a challenging task with the goal of recognizing
130
      previously unseen action classes (i.e., query class) with a few labeled videos. Existing FSAR meth-
      ods [52, 53, 54, 13, 15] mainly belong to the metric-based meta-learning paradigm [47], which
131
      learns class (prototype) representation and performs prototype-query matching based on the learned
132
      distance metrics. These methods are mainly devoted to feature representation learning [15, 13] and
133   matching strategy exploration[52, 55, 56, 11, 18, 16, 13]. As a primary step, feature representation
134   learning helps models to learn expressive spatio-temporal features for further matching process.
135   Recent appoarches [15, 13] model temporal features through temporal attention operations [15] or
136   more detailed temporal-patch and temporal-channel interaction [13], and further exploit low-level
137   spatial features by patch-level information interaction within each frame or across frames [57]. Some
138   others make use of video features in a whole task (i.e., episode) to extract relevant discriminative
139   patterns [13, 16, 58] by a graph neural network [13] or attention relation modeling [16]. For match-
140   ing strategy exploration, early works [52, 55, 56] aggregate the frame features into a single video
141
      representation for video-level feature matching. Though straightforward, these methods suffer from
      suboptimal performance due to neglecting the temporal cues in videos. To address this limitation,
142
      the following approaches [11, 18, 16, 13, 23] devise various temporal alignment metrics for frame-
143
      level feature matching, e.g., frame-level alignment [11, 16], segment-level alignment [18], and even
144   frame-to-segment alignment [23] that is also common in realistic video matching.
145
146   Recent works [24, 59, 21] transfer knowledge from pre-trained vision-language models (e.g.,
147
      CLIP [27])toenhance FSAR model capability. Though promising, they heavily rely on semantically
      coarse or ambiguous category names as semantic source to provide action-related context. Such
148
      context is insufficient to offer enough background knowledge for video understanding. In contrast,
149
      our D I ST represents the first effort in FSAR to decouple class names into diverse spatio-temporal
150   attribute descriptions (i.e., action-relevant knowledge) to complement semantic contexts. More sig-
151   nificantly,such acquired decoupled knowledge is further injected into visual features to learn object-
152   and frame-level prototypes in a dual way, so as to enable more accurate action recognition.
153
      Few-shot Learning with Semantic Information. Recent works on few-shot learning [60, 61, 62]
154
      integrate semantic information (provided by class labels) and visual information (extracted from
155
      visual observations) to represent a novel class. Based on the levels at which modality information
156   fusion occurs, these methods can be roughly categorized into three groups: i) Prototype-level meth-
157   ods [63, 64] model class (prototype) representation as a combination of visual prototypes and se-
158   mantic prototypes obtained through word embeddings of class labels by attention mechanism [64] or
159   adaptive fusion mechanism [63]; ii) Classifier-level methods [60] enable classifiers to predict novel
160   categories by incorporating auxiliary semantic information acquired from a graph convolutional
161   network [65]; and iii) Extractor-level methods [66, 62] consider semantic information as prompts to
      tune the feature extractor, allowing the feature extractor to better focus on class-specific features.

193   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
194
      The goal of FSAR is to classify unlabeled test videos given a few (e.g., one or five) samples per class.
195
      Under the few-shot setting, the model learns a feature representation on training classes Dbase and is
196   evaluated on testing classes Dnovel to emphasize its generalization ability on novel categories, where
197   Dbase ∩ Dnovel = ∅. In the training stage, follwing [67, 16, 13], we train a few-shot learning model
198   in an episodic way. Here, each episode (i.e., a standard M -way K-shot episode task) is formed
199   by sampling M categories from Dbase . The M -way K-shot task consists of K labeled videos per
200   class as the support set S, and a fraction of the rest samples from M classes as the query set Q. The
201   episodic training for FSAR is achieved by minimizing, for each episode, the loss of the prediction on
202   samples in the query set, given the support set. In the inference stage, we randomly sample episode
203   tasks from Dnovel for evaluation, and report average results over multiple episode tasks.
204
205   3.2   OVERALL F RAMEWORK
206
207
      We introduce D I ST, which collects and leverages action-related commonsense knowledge provided
      by LLMs to guide the learning of object- and frame-level prototypes for FSAR (Fig. 2). D I ST con-
208
      sists of visual/text encoders and two knowledge compensators. The model takes the RGB frame
209
      sequence of length T and corresponding action names as input. We first utilize the visual encoder
210   of CLIP [27] to get frame-level (i.e., class token in each frame) and patch-level feature representa-
211   tion, i.e., F ∈ RT ×C and X ∈ RT ×P ×C , where P is the number of tokens in each frame. Besides,
212   we prompt LLM with corresponding action names to generate decoupled spatial and temporal com-
213   monsense descriptions (§3.3). These descriptions are fed into frozen text encoder of CLIP to obtain
214   spatial and temporal attribute features, i.e., Qs ∈ RG×C and Qt ∈ RL×C . We further design two
215   complementary modules to make use of decoupled spatio-temporal attributes: 1) Spatial Knowledge
      Compensator (SKC) (§3.4) injects spatial attributes into patch-level features to explicitly learn com-

217   sator (TKC) (§3.5) incorporates temporal attributes and frame-level features to inform frame-level

219   matching scores to obtain the merged query prediction.

221   3.3   D ECOUPLED S PATIO - TEMPORAL ATTRIBUTE G ENERATION
222
223   Spatial Attribute Generation. Naive category names provided limited commonsense knowledge to
224   focus on action-related spatial contexts. Thus we make use of prior knowledge in LLMs [68, 69]
225   to generate detailed and informative spatial attributes for each category, i.e., action-related object
226   instances and environment. Specifically, taking the action category “drink” as an example, to obtain
227
      spatial attribute descriptions,we prompt ChatGPT [69] by “Given action label {drink}, please gen-
      erate {G} most related objects for each class.”, where G is empirically set to 6. This prompt returns
228
      a set A with G spatial attribute descriptions, such as “container; mouth; hand; ...”. Then we encode
229
      these spatial attributes via frozen CLIP text encoder to get spatial attribute features Qs ∈ RG×C .
230
231   Temporal Attribute Generation. Prior researches [24, 59] apply semantically coarse category
232   names as auxiliary information to guide temporal feature learning. However, such context provided
233
      by action names is too limited to provide enough temporal context for action recognition. Thus, we
      propose to utilize the abundant prior knowledge in LLMs [68, 69] to expand the coarse action names.
234
      Temporal attribute descriptions generated by LLM are a collection of multiple atomic actions, which
235
      describe the temporal evolution of an action. Concretely, taking the action category “drink” as an
236   example, to obtain temporal attribute descriptions, we prompt ChatGPT [69] by “Given action label
237   {drink}, please describe {L} states of each action in simple and short words.”, where we empirically
238   set L to 3. This prompt always returns a set B with L temporal attribute descriptions, such as “Hold
239   container; Bring container to mouth; Put container; ...”, which decompose one action class into
240   multiple atomic actions in a step-by-step manner. Then we adopt the off-the-shelf text encoder of
241   CLIP to encode these descriptions and obtain temporal attribute features Qt ∈ RL×C .

243   3.4   S PATIAL K NOWLEDGE C OMPENSATOR
244
245
      Previous methods exploit spatial features by patch-level information interaction within each frame or
      across frames [15, 57]. However, this leads to two issues: 1) Too many irrelevant patch tokens bring
246
      redundant information, interfering with further spatial feature matching; 2) They fail to focus on
247
      important objects without the guidance of spatial prior knowledge. Therefore, we investigate how to
248   better incorporate spatial attributes (§3.3) and patch-level visual features into compact object-level
249   prototypes to highlight potential target objects. To this end, as showcased in Fig. 3, proposed Spatial
250   Knowledge Compensator (SKC) summarizes discriminative spatial patterns via aggregating patch-
251   level features into compact object-level prototypes (i.e., patch aggregation), and further delivers the
252   union of spatial attribute knowledge and such object-level prototypes to enhance learned spatial
253   patterns (i.e., attribute injection).
254   Patch Aggregation. We first introduce a set of learnable object-level prototypes to aggregate image
255   content and highlight potential target objects. The prototypes are randomly initialized embeddings
256   and represented as Po ∈ RN ×C , where N is the number of object prototypes. Firstly, a self-
257   attention layer is adopted for the N object prototypes to interact with each other in each frame.
258   Then, these prototypes aim to adaptively aggregate action-related or object-related key patches in
259   a sparse manner by patch-level cross-attention within each frame. Specifically, for patch tokens

368
      vious methods [24, 21]. By default, the number of spatial attributes G and temporal attributes L

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we propose a novel yet effective D I ST framework for FSAR, which is the first work
534   to grasp spatiotemporal-decoupled prior knowledge from LLM to compensate for visual features.
535   In particular, we design Spatial/Temporal Knowledge Compensators to learn object- and frame-
536   level prototypes, so as to capture fine-grained spatial details and dynamic semantics. Experimental
537   results demonstrate that our D I ST achieves state-of-the-art performance on four standard bench-
538   marks. However, this is the first cursory exploration of leveraging spatiotemporal-decoupled prior
539   knowledge in FSAR. Though the first step is not always elegant, exploring additional attempts to
      leverage richer prior knowledge provided by LLMs promises intriguing prospects for the future.

872   Algorithm 1 Pseudo-code of object-level and frame-level prototype matching in a PyTorch-like
873
      style.
874       # q_obj_p: query object prototype (B_q x T x N x C)
875       # s_obj_p: support object prototype (B_s x T x N x C)
876       # q_frm_p: query frame prototype (B_q x T x C)
          # s_frm_p: support frame prototype (B_s x T x C)
877       # B_q: number of query videos
878       # B_s: number of support videos
879       # T: number of frames
          # N: number of object prototypes in each frame
880       # OTAM: temporal metric
881
882       def matching(q_obj_p, s_obj_p, q_frm_p, s_frm_p):
              # Object-level prototype matching
883           q_obj_p = q_obj_p.reshape(B_q x T x N, C) # (B_q x T x N) x C
884           s_obj_p = s_obj_p.reshape(B_s x T x N, C) # (B_s x T x N) x C
885           obj_sim = cos_sim(q_obj_p, s_obj_p) # (B_q x T x N) x (B_s x T x N)
              obj_dist = 1 - obj_sim # (B_q x T x N) x (B_s x T x N)
886           obj_dist = rearrange(obj_dist) # (B_q x T) x (B_s x T) x N x N
887           obj_fdist = obj_dist.min(3)[0].sum(2) + obj_dist.min(2)[0].sum(2) #
888                (B_q x T) x (B_s x T)
              obj_fdist = rearrange(obj_fdist) # B_q x B_s x T x T
889           obj_logits = obj_fdist.min(3)[0].mean(2) + obj_fdist.min(2)[0].mean
890               (2) # B_q x B_s
891
                # Frame-level prototype matching
892             q_frm_p = q_frm_p.reshape(B_q x T, C) # (B_q x T) x C
893             s_frm_p = s_obj_p.reshape(B_s x T, C) # (B_s x T) x C
894             frm_sim = cos_sim(q_frm_p, s_frm_p) (B_q x T) x (B_s x T)
                frm_dist = 1 - frm_sim # (B_q x T) x (B_s x T)
895             frm_dist = rearrange(frm_dist) # B_q x B_s x T x T
896             frm_logits = OTAM(frm_dist) # B_q x B_s
897
                return obj_logits, frm_logits

       E.1    L IMITATION A NALYSIS
1009
1010   D I ST relies on large language models to generate high-quality spatial and temporal prompts, which
1011   affect the final performance. In addition, relying solely on a fixed number of spatial and temporal
1012   prompts may not be optimal for every category. An adaptive approach, customizing the number of
1013   prompts for each category, would likely be more effective. In the future, we will also explore a more
1014   unified and effective way to inject spatial and temporal prompts into visual features.

1016   E.2    S OCIAL I MPACT
1017
       This work proposes a novel framework to leverage spatiotemporal-decoupled prior knowledge from
1018
       LLM to compensate for visual features, so as to achieve more accurate few-shot matching. Our
1019
       framework has demonstrated its effectiveness over multiple common benchmarks. On the positive
1020   side, the approach advances few-shot action recognition accuracy, and is valuable for real-world
1021   applications in the automated understanding of human actions from videos [73, 74], e.g., human-
1022   robotic interaction in elderly care facilities [74], behavior analysis for nursing procedures [73]. For
1023   potential negative social impact, our D I ST struggles to understand human actions across varying
1024   domains, which is a common limitation shared by all few-shot action recognition methods [13, 16,
1025   15]. Hence in the future, we will broaden the few-shot action recognition capability to generalize
       across varying domains.
