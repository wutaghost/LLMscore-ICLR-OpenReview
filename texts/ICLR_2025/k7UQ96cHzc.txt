009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            While softmax cross entropy loss is the standard objective for supervised classifi-
012            cation, it primarily focuses on the ground truth classes, ignoring the relationships
013            between the non-target, complementary classes. This leaves valuable information
014            unexploited during optimization. In this work, we set explicit non-zero target
015            distributions for the complementary classes, in order to address this limitation.
016            Specifically, for each class, we define an anti-class, which consists of everything
017            that is not part of the target class—this includes all complementary classes as well
018
               as out-of-distribution samples, and in general any instance that does not belong
               to the true class. Various distributions can be used as a target for the anti-classes.
019
               For example, by setting a uniform one-cold encoded distribution over the com-
020
               plementary classes as a target for each anti-class, we encourage the model to
021            equally distribute activations across all non-target classes. This approach promotes
022            a symmetric geometric structure of classes in the final feature space, increases
023            the degree of neural collapse during training, addresses the independence deficit
024            problem of neural networks and improves generalization. Our extensive evaluation
025            demonstrates that our proposed framework consistently results in performance
026            gains across multiple settings, including classification, open-set recognition, and
027            out-of-distribution detection.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep learning has seen amazing progress over the past decade across multiple domains such as
033
      vision, speech, and text (He et al., 2015b). The optimization criterion universally used for supervised
      classification tasks is the cross entropy loss, which aims at minimizing the discrepancy between the
034
      predicted probability distribution across classes and the ground truth probability distribution, typically
035
      represented as a one-hot encoding of the true label (Goodfellow et al., 2016). Minimizing the cross
036   entropy loss maximizes the likelihood of correctly identifying disjoint classes under a given set of
037   parameters of a statistical learning model, such as a neural network, making it an intuitive choice,
038   with strong empirical results over the years.
039
      Despite its theoretical and practical appeal, cross entropy loss is not without its limitations. Neural
040
      networks optimized with cross entropy are known to result in overconfident predictions (Guo et al.,
041
      2017), (Nguyen et al., 2015). This problem becomes more evident in open set classification scenarios,
042   where a model must be able to also recognize unknown (i.e., not seen in training) classes at inference
043   time (Scheirer et al., 2013). Current neural networks struggle to recognize out-of-distribution (OOD)
044   samples (Bendale & Boult, 2015), and often misclassify them to one of the known classes with high
045   confidence, which calls into question their trustworthiness. In a recent work, Feng et al. (2024)
046   also identified an independence deficit in neural networks, where the prediction confidence of some
047   classes is redundantly determined by others through simple linear relationships, even when the classes
048   are semantically unrelated. This entanglement can lead to overfitting and poor generalization, as the
049   network neglects learning distinct class representations.
050   In spite of these limitations, neural networks can generalize remarkably well, and understanding the
051   underlying reasons has been the focus of extensive research (Zhang et al., 2021), (Yang et al., 2020).
052   In this effort, recent work has uncovered the intriguing empirical phenomenon of Neural Collapse
053   (NC), which arises during the terminal stage of deep neural network training (Papyan et al., 2020),
      (Zhu et al., 2024). In NC, class features collapse to their respective means, class means become

      maximally separated in a Simplex Equiangular Tight Frame (ETF), and the classifier aligns with
055   these class means, leading to a highly structured decision boundary. NC has been linked to improved
056   robustness and benign overfitting (Bartlett et al., 2020), (Ma et al., 2018), with connections to the
057   information bottleneck theory (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015), which suggests that networks focus on
058   essential information, while discarding unnecessary variability.
059
      While independence deficit and neural collapse are distinct and even contradicting behaviors—one
060
      reflecting a redundancy in class independence and the other signifying maximally separated and
061   independent class representations— cross entropy loss does not provide a mechanism to explicitly
062   control these phenomena. Instead, their emergence during training is often incidental, with networks
063   passively exhibiting one behavior or the other, depending on the dataset size, class distribution,
064   task difficulty and model complexity rather than actively optimizing for either. In this work, we
065   rethink the classification objective by introducing the concept of an anti-class, which consists of
066   the complementary set of a class, encompassing all that the class is not. With anti-classes, we can
067   explicitly control the relationships between the complementary classes, and therefore the structure of
068   the classes in the final feature manifold.
069   We propose using a one-cold encoding, where the true class is labeled with 0, while the rest of the
070   classes are labeled with non-zero targets. Inspired by the geometric properties of the Simplex ETF, we
071   mainly focus on a uniform one-cold encoding, where each complementary class is labeled with 1, as
072   illustrated in Figure 1a. This encoding reflects that all complementary classes of a ground truth class
073   belong equally to its anti-class. (see Figure 1b). Through this simple label transformation, each class
074   is encouraged to maintain a symmetric relationship with all other classes, similar to the symmetry
075
      in the Simplex ETF, where class centroids are equinorm and equiangular. This approach forces
      the network to develop distinct and independent representations for each class, preventing reliance
076
      on linear correlations with other classes. We argue that this enhances the model’s generalization
077
      capability.

098
099           • We introduce the concept of anti-classes, providing a novel approach to modeling classifica-

101           • We propose using one-cold cross entropy (OCCE) to enable the model to learn target anti-

106

107

      2.1   A NTI - CLASS FORMULATION
111
112   Let’s consider a predefined set of N known classes. Each class k can be represented as a subspace in
113   a d-dimensional space, denoted by Ck ⊆ Rd . For every class k, we can define the complementary
114   subspace to Ck , which we denote as C¯k = Rd \ Ck and refer to as the anti-class subspace of k. The
115   anti-class subspace C¯k contains every instance that does not belong to class k, including instances
116   from other known or unknown classes, out-of-distribution instances, random noise or adversarial
117
      examples. Intuitively, directly modeling the anti-class subspace C¯k seems a much harder problem
      than modeling Ck , as the latter is clearly more restricted. Moreover, in mutually exclusive classes,
118
      an instance that belongs to Ck inherently belongs to the anti-class of all other known classes C¯j̸=k .
119
      Consequently, in multi-class classification every vector p ∈ Rd belongs to at least N − 1 anti-classes,
120
      and potentially to all N of them, if the vector does not represent an instance of a known class. We can
121
      encode the anti-class labels using a one-cold encoded label l̄, where the ground truth class is labeled
122   with 0 (hence the term cold), and every complement class is labeled with a non-zero value, indicating
123   its membership to the anti-class. If we adopt hard assignments, the vector l̄ can be defined as:
124
125                                   ¯li = 0 if i = ground truth class,                                   (1)
126                                           1 if i = complementary class.

128   2.2   O RDINARY SUPERVISED CLASSIFICATION
129
130   In a standard supervised learning scenario, we deal with a set of n labeled instances D =
131   {(x0 , l0 ), . . . , (xn , ln )} over an initial feature space X ⊆ Rd and a discrete label space L =
132
      {1, . . . , N }, where d is the dimensionality of X and N the number of classes. An instance x which
      belongs to class k is sampled from the subspace Ck . Given a model with parameters θ, the objective
133
      of an ordinary classification task is to find the mapping f : X → L, where f (x; θ) = l, correctly
134
      mapping each input x to its corresponding label l. Standard classification training minimizes the
135   cross entropy loss for each instance, comparing the model’s predicted probability distribution ŷ with
136   the one-hot encoded label y. The cross entropy loss is defined as:
137
138                                                                     exp(zi )
139                         LCE = −y⊤ log(ŷ),       where      ŷi = PC            .                     (2)

      In equation 2, only the log-probability of the ground truth labels is explicitly minimized, while the
142
      probabilities of the complementary classes are indirectly influenced through the softmax normal-
143   ization. This approach overlooks the relationships between the complementary classes. Such a
144   limitation can be problematic, particularly for handling anomalous samples like adversarial examples
145   or out-of-distribution inputs. Ideally, for these cases, a model should produce a close to uniform
146   probability distribution across all classes, indicating maximum uncertainty. However, achieving this
147   uniform distribution requires equal activations z for all classes, which is not explicitly encouraged by
148   cross entropy loss. Furthermore, it does not enforce any specific geometric structure of the classes in
149   the feature space, leading to undesirable phenomena such as minority collapse (Fang et al., 2021),
150   where in imbalanced training the features of minority classes collapse towards identical clusters, or
151
      independence deficit (Feng et al., 2024), where spurious correlations between classes emerge.

153   2.3   L EARNING WITH ANTI - CLASSES
154
      Deriving the OCCE Loss. To address these challenges, we shift our focus to anti-classes. Since
155
      one-cold encoded labels are not valid probability distributions, standard cross entropy loss cannot
156   be directly applied. A trivial solution would be to convert the task into a multi-label problem and
157   predict each anti-class probability independently using binary cross entropy loss. However, this
158   approach is redundant, as binary cross entropy treats labels 0 and 1 identically, learning each class
159   independently without controlling the relationships between complementary classes. To resolve this,
160   we introduce competition between anti-classes by applying a softmax function. While in one-hot
161   encoding, this results in a winner-takes-all scenario (Goodfellow et al., 2016), with anti-classes, the
      target probability distribution can reflect how much an instance belongs to each anti-class. In this

      Although theoretical analyses show that neural networks trained with CE loss converge to a Simplex
      ETF under ideal optimization conditions (Lu & Steinerberger, 2022), in practice, the extent of neural
241
      collapse observed varies. This variation is influenced by factors such as the number of classes, their
242
      label distribution, the available data, and the complexity of the model. Empirically, neural collapse
243   tends to occur only in the later stages of training when CE loss approaches zero, implying that the
244   activations for all complementary classes converge towards zero, achieving nearly equal values. With
245   uniform OCCE, this behavior can be explicitly enforced from earlier stages of training, leading to
246   faster convergence toward this desired structure.

      Neural Collapse. To study the effect of uniform OCCE loss on the occurrence of neural collapse
263
      during optimization, we measure the amount of neural collapse during training using the four proposed
264   NC metrics by Zhu et al. (2024). N C 1 measures the ratio between the within-class covariance ΣW
265   and the between-class covariance ΣB , N C 2 quantifies the alignment of the learned classifier with a
266   Simplex ETF structure, N C 3 evaluates the duality between the classifiers W and the centered class-
267   means H, while N C 4 measures how the bias term b compensates for a nonzero global mean of the
268   features hG , reflecting the degree of collapse in the bias term (refer to A.9 for detailed computations).
269   Details on the calculation of these metrics are provided in Appendix. In Figure 4, we report the
      measurements of these NC metrics during standard training of a ResNet18 architecture. Larger γ

284   Indepedence Deficit. Uniform OCCE encourages each class to be equidistant from all the other
285   classes, enforcing decoupled classes with linearly independent representations. In order to show
286   the effect on independence deficit we follow the procedure from Feng et al. (2024) to estimate
287
      the intrinsic dimension of the penultimate classification layer required to reconstruct the original
      classification accuracy. We compute the covariance matrix of the representations in that layer, extract
288
      its principal components, and project onto subspaces spanned by the top-K eigenvectors.
289
290   Figure 5a shows that with standard cross entropy loss, 80%, 90%, and 99% of the original classifi-
291   cation accuracy can be reconstructed using only 22, 37, and 64 dimensions, respectively, from the
292   original 100-dimensional activation layer on CIFAR-100. However, when using uniform OCCE
293
      and increasing the value of γ, the reconstructed accuracy shows a more linear relationship with the
      dimensionality of the subspace. This suggests that uniform OCCE decouples the classes and enforces
294
      linear independence in the activation layer. For instance, with γ = 10, reconstructing 80%, 90%, and
295
      99% of the accuracy requires 73, 89, and 98 dimensions, respectively. Intuitively, each class ci is
296   identified by the corresponding activation zi , rather than permitting predictions for ci through linear
297   combinations of other activations zj , j ̸= i, directly addressing the independence deficit.

      Impact on Generalization. In Figure 5c, we observe that increasing the value of γ consistently

      reduces the generalization gap between train and test accuracy. However, as γ becomes large, it

329   Base Training. We train the following architectures: a ResNet18v2 (He et al., 2016), a MobileNetv2
330   (Sandler et al., 2018) and a DenseNet121 (Huang et al., 2016) on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009) and
331   TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015) datasets, containing 100 and 200 classes respectively. We follow a
332   standard hyperparameter configuration across all experiments from He et al. (2015a), which involves
333   employing an SGD optimizer with momentum of 0.9, mini-bacthes of 128 samples, weight decay
334   of 0.0001, no dropout and a total of 150 epochs for CIFAR-100 and 90 epochs for TinyImageNet.

      The learning rate starts from the base value of 0.1 and is divided by 0.1 at 50 and 100 epochs for
      CIFAR-100, and at 30, 60 and 80 epochs for TinyImageNet. For augmentation, we follow a universal
336
      approach of normalization, random horizontal flipping and random cropping in the train set, while
337
      only normalizing and center cropping the images in the test set.

      4    R ELATED W ORK
487
488   Complement Entropy. Complement Objective Training (COT) (Chen et al., 2019) alternatively
489   optimizes the primary cross entropy loss and a complement objective, to maximize the Shannon’s
490   entropy of complementary classes, including two updates per batch, which increases training time
491   by 1.6-fold. Later, Kim et al. (2021) unified these objectives into Complementary Cross Entropy
492
      (CCE) loss. OCCE, unlike COT and CCE which are narrowed down to Shannon’s entropy in the
      complementary classes, optimizes a specified anti-class distribution. As a result, OCCE can be
493
      naturally integrated with soft labels and knowledge distillation (see A.1.1).
494
495   RCE and Label Smoothing. Szegedy et al. (2015) introduced label smoothing to mitigate the
496   overconfidence of neural networks, by assigning small targets to the complementary classes. However,
497   this introduces global bias to the correct class. Pang et al. (2017) introduced RCE training procedure
498   as a solution to this problem. RCE is an extreme case of label smoothing, when λ → inf, and can be
499   seen as the stand-alone uniform OCCE objective in our framework, while our proposed approach
500   combines class and anti-class learning, outperforming both RCE and label smoothing (see Table 1).
501   ETF Simplex Classifiers. Inspired from neural collapse, in Zhu et al. (2024), the authors fix the
502   last layer classifier to be a Simplex ETF, demonstrating time and memory gains without performance
503   loss. Furthermore, Simplex ETF structure has been used to mitigate minority collapse in imbalanced
504   training (Yang et al., 2024b) and few-shot incremental learning (Yang et al., 2023). OCCE diversifies
505   from these approaches, because it induces the Simplex ETF structure in an end-to-end learnable
506   manner, rather than predefining the structure of the classifier.
507
      Hierarchical Classification. Hierarchical classification methods have been proposed to structure
508
      class activations by leveraging parent-child relationships in predefined hierarchies (Wu et al., 2020;
509   Valmadre, 2022; Liang & Davis, 2023). While these approaches address hierarchical relationships
510   (vertical in a hierarchy tree), our method focuses on the flat structure, emphasizing relationships
511   between classes at the same hierarchy level. The proposed method can be employed at multiple
512   hierarchical levels in a similar fashion.
513
514
      Focal Approaches. Focal Loss (Lin et al., 2017) scales the loss of well-classified samples with
      (1 − ŷc )γ , where ŷc is the predicted probability for the correct class, and γ > 0 emphasizes hard
515
      samples. Variants like Cyclic Focal Loss (Smith, 2022) and AdaFocal (Ghosh et al., 2024) dynamically
516
      adjust γ. These methods focus on instance-level difficulty, but they assign zero probabilities to
517   complementary classes, effectively ignoring their structure. Introducing target anti-class distributions
518   operates orthogonally to focal approaches and can be readily combined with them.
519
520   Supervised Contrastive Learning. Supervised Contrastive Learning (SupCon) (Khosla et al.,
521   2020) extends cross entropy to the feature space by pulling positive pairs closer and pushing negative
522   pairs farther apart. Even though it normalizes distances among both positive and negative pairs in
523
      the softmax denominator to prevent collapse, SupCon does not explicitly optimize the relationships
      among the complementary classes, treating them only as a normalization mechanism. This represents
524
      a fundamental difference from our proposed approach.
525
526   Reciprocal Points Learning. Reciprocal Points Learning (RPL) models "otherness" by positioning
527   reciprocal points in latent space (Chen et al., 2020). Adversarial RPL (ARPL) reduces open space
528   risk using an adversarial margin constraint (Chen et al., 2022), while ARPL + CS adds adversarial
529   samples equidistant from all reciprocal points to simulate unseen classes. OCCE objective aligns the
530   activations of the complementary classes for each instance, rather than relying on a single anti-class
531
      prototype for each class in the latent space.
532
533
      5    C ONCLUSIONS
534   In this paper, we highlight the importance of controlling the relationships between complementary
535   classes. To this end, we introduce the concept of anti-classes as a complementary approach to
536   modeling classification problems. By representing anti-classes using one-cold encoding, we optimize
537   neural networks to directly learn target anti-class probability distributions through one-cold cross
538   entropy loss. While we mainly focus on a uniform one-cold encoding, inspired by the phenomenon of
539   neural collapse, our proposed framework can be naturally extended to non-uniform target distributions
      to address limitations, for example to account for inter-class relationships (see A.1.1).

704   A.1     L IMITATIONS OF U NIFORM OCCE
705
      In our main experimental evaluation, we demonstrate that adding the uniform OCCE objective
706   consistently improves performance across various settings and learning scenarios. However, this
707   raises a natural question: should the network treat all non-target classes equally, ignoring their
708   inherent similarities while enforcing uniformity and the Simplex ETF structure? To address this
709   question, we explore assigning soft target anti-class distributions, that consider class similarities. Our
710   results indicate further improvements on top of uniform OCCE, proving that our proposed framework
711   can also efficiently support non-uniform distributions.
712
      A.1.1      E XTENDING TO NON - UNIFORM ANTI - CLASS DISTRIBUTIONS
713
714   We experiment with two types of self distillation scenarios, to provide softer anti-class distributions:
715   1. Instance-based and 2. Class-based self-distillation.
716
      Instance-based self-distillation. The new target anti-class probability distribution ȳ′ for each
717   instance is defined as a weighted combination of the uniform one-cold encoding and the network’s
718   previous predictions, as:
                                            ′
719                                       ȳi = (1 − α) · ȳi + α · ȳˆi ,                           (9)
720
      where α ∈ [0, 1] controls the contribution of the self-distillation. When α = 0, the target reduces to
721
      the uniform one-cold encoding, while α = 1 fully relies on the network’s previous predictions.
722
723   Class-based self-distillation. In this variation, the target anti-class probability distribution ȳ
724   incorporates class-specific self-distillation. During each epoch, we calculate the average predicted
725                            ˆ across the entire dataset for each class. This results in a class-specific
      anti-class probabilities ȳ
726   weighted matrix, C, where we set the diagonal elements Ci,i = 0 and normalize row-wise. Here
727   Ci,j represents the normalized similarity of class i to each complementary class j. The new target
728   distribution for a sample belonging to class i is a weighted combination of the uniform one-cold
729
      encoding and the class-specific weights:
                                               ′
730                                          ȳj = (1 − α) · ȳj + α · Ci,j ,                                    (10)
731
732   where α ∈ [0, 1] controls the contribution of the class-specific self-distillation. When α = 0, the tar-
733   get reverts to the uniform one-cold encoding, while α = 1 incorporates only the class-specific weights.
734
735   We train ResNet18v2 on MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet to compare the per-
736   formance of cross-entropy (CE), reverse cross-entropy (RCE), our proposed approach with uniform
737   OCCE, and our proposed approaches using soft anti-class distributions (in Table 5). Specifically, we
738
      employ instance-based and class-based knowledge distillation techniques in the latter. The results
      demonstrate that while uniform OCCE consistently improves performance across all datasets, the
739
      incorporation of soft anti-class distributions provides additional improvements on top of uniform
740
      OCCE. This highlights the flexibility of OCCE, which extends beyond the uniform case to address
741   for specific optimization objectives.

      A.1.2    J USTIFICATION OF UNIFORM OCCE
757
758
      Although the uniform OCCE loss does not account for the inherent relationships between classes and
      enforces uniformity, it still delivers consistent improvements. In this section, we analyze the uniform
759
      OCCE loss from a theoretical perspective to justify its effectiveness.
760
761   Fisher Discriminant Ratio (FDR). Uniform OCCE minimizes the Fisher discriminant ratio,
762   defined as FDR = tr(SB )/tr(SW ), where SB and SW are the between-class and within-class scatter
763   matrices, respectively. By minimizing within-class scatter (SW ) and maximizing between-class
764   scatter (SB ), the loss improves class separability in the feature space.
765
      Information-Theoretic Perspective. Uniform OCCE encourages the class means to form a Simplex
766
      ETF, aligning with principles of optimal coding. Observations are modeled as h = µc + z, where
767
      z ∼ N (0, σ 2 I) and µc represents the class means. The Simplex ETF structure maximizes the
768   minimum pairwise distance between class means, reducing misclassification under Gaussian noise
769   and maximizing the mutual information between the class index c and the signal h.
770
771   Maximum Entropy Principle. Uniform OCCE aligns with the maximum entropy principle by as-
772   signing uniform probabilities ( N 1−1 ) to non-target classes, which enforces the least biased distribution
773   consistent with the constraints.

      A.3    E MPIRICAL EVALUATION OF ACTIVATIONS
849
850   While the previous theoretical analysis describes the asymptotic behavior of activations under ideal
851   conditions, in practice, the activations of a neural network often behave differently due to various
852   factors. These include the influence of regularization methods like weight decay, which penalize large
853
      weights, and the complexity of the optimization process. Here, we provide an empirical evaluation of
      the distribution of activations in a converged ResNet-18 network.
854
855
      In Figure 11, we present the activation distributions for both the correct classes and the complementary
      classes across three different training losses: a cross entropy (CE) trained network (first column), a
856
      CE+OCCE with γ = 1 trained network (second column), and an OCCE-only trained network (third
857
      column). It is evident that while the cross entropy loss results in Gaussian-like distributions for the
858   activations of the complementary classes, similar to those of the correct classes, the introduction
859   of the OCCE objective alters this behavior. Specifically, with uniform OCCE, all activations of the
860   complementary classes converge to the same value. This uniformity is the primary mechanism that
861   drives neural collapse. For each instance, the activations corresponding to its complementary classes
862   must be equal, indicating that the instance is equidistant from all their class means. Consequently, this
863   forces the instances within each class to collapse toward their respective class means, while ensuring
      that the class means themselves are equidistant in the final feature manifold.

      A.5     C ONNECTION BETWEEN OCCE AND L OW R ANK R EPRESENTATIONS
919
920   Empirically, we observe that uniform OCCE influences the numerical rank of each layer. Early layers
921   exhibit higher ranks with larger γ values, indicating more information preservation compared to CE.
922   In contrast, the final layers have significantly lower ranks, suggesting that uniform OCCE promotes
923
      sparse, linearly independent representations for each class in the last layer.
924   We compute the rank of a layer function fi by calculating its Jacobian matrix Jf = (∂fi /∂xj )i,j ∈
925   Rd×n for each layer i over the validation set, where d is the layer’s output dimension and n is the
926   input dimension (Feng et al., 2024). The numerical rank is estimated by counting the singular values
927   larger than a threshold ϵ = 1.19 × 10−7 . Figures 13 (left, linear scale) and (right, log scale) show the
928
      rank progression, with the linear scale revealing trends in higher ranks, and the log scale highlighting
      differences in lower ranks.
