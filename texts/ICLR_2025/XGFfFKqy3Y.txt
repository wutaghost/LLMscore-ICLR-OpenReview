009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models (DMs) are a class of generative models that allow sampling
012            from a distribution learned over a training set. When applied to solving inverse
013            imaging problems (IPs), the reverse sampling steps of DMs are typically modi-
014            fied to approximately sample from a measurement-conditioned distribution in the
015            image space. However, these modifications may be unsuitable for certain settings
016            (such as in the presence of measurement noise) and non-linear tasks, as they of-
017            ten struggle to correct errors from earlier sampling steps and generally require
018
               a large number of optimization and/or sampling steps. To address these chal-
               lenges, we state three conditions for achieving measurement-consistent diffusion
019
               trajectories. Building on these conditions, we propose a new optimization-based
020
               sampling method that not only enforces the standard data manifold measurement
021            consistency and forward diffusion consistency, as seen in previous studies, but
022            also incorporates step-wise and network-regularized backward diffusion consis-
023            tency that maintains a diffusion trajectory by optimizing over the input of the pre-
024            trained model at every sampling step. By enforcing these conditions, either im-
025            plicitly or explicitly, our sampler requires significantly fewer reverse steps. There-
026            fore, we refer to our accelerated method as Step-wise Triple-Consistent Sampling
027            (SITCOM). Compared to existing state-of-the-art baseline methods, under dif-
028            ferent levels of measurement noise, our extensive experiments across five linear
029
               and three non-linear image restoration tasks demonstrate that SITCOM achieves
               competitive or superior results in terms of standard image similarity metrics while
030
               requiring a reduced run-time across all considered tasks.

036

038   applications, the primary goal is to recover an unknown image or signal x ∈ Rn from measurements
039   or degraded image y ∈ Rm , which are often corrupted by noise. Mathematically, the unknown
040   signal and the measurements are related as

131   a Gaussian distribution to the posterior distribution of the clean image given the measurements.
132   This approach involves solving an optimization problem using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to
133   minimize a data-fitting term while maximizing the likelihood of the reconstructed image under the
134   denoising diffusion prior (as a regularizer). However, the SGD process requires multiple iterations,
135   each involving evaluations of the pre-trained DM on a different noisy image at some randomly
136   selected time. While RED-diff reduces the run-time, their qualitative results are not competitive on
137   several image restoration tasks.
138   Recently, Decoupling Consistency with Diffusion Purification (DCDP) (Li et al., 2024) proposed
139   separating diffusion sampling steps from measurement consistency by using DMs as diffusion pu-
140   rifiers (Nie et al., 2022; Alkhouri et al., 2024), with the goal of reducing the run-time. However,
141   for every task, DCDP requires tuning the number of forward diffusion steps for purification for
142   each sampling step. Shortly after, Decoupled Annealing Posterior Sampling (DAPS) (Zhang et al.,
143   2024) introduced another decoupled approach, incorporating gradient descent noise annealing via
144   Langevin dynamics. DAPS, similar to DPS, also requires a large number of sampling and opti-
145
      mization steps. Under measurement noise, DCDP achieves SOTA run-time across various linear
      restoration tasks, while DAPS sets the SOTA in restoration quality. Both will serve as primary
146
      baselines in our experiments.

279
280   We now summarize the three key conditions that apply at each sampling step.
281
      C1 Measurement Consistency: The reconstruction x̂′0 is consistent with the measurements. This
282
283
      means that A(x̂′0 ) ≈ y.
284   C2    Backward Consistency: The reconstruction x̂′0 is a denoised image produced by the Tweedie-
285   network denoiser f . More generally, we define the backward consistency to include any form of DM
286   network regularization (e.g., using the DM probability-flow (PF) ODE (Karras et al., 2022)) applied
287   to x̂′0 .
288
289    C3 Forward Consistency: The pre-trained DM network ϵθ is provided with in-distribution inputs

290   with high probability. To ensure this, we apply the resampling formula in Equation (8) and enforce
291   that v̂t remains close to xt .
292   We note that the three considered consistencies are step-wise, meaning they are enforced at every
293   sampling step. This approach contrasts with enforcing these consistencies solely on the final recon-
294   struction at t = 0, which represents a significantly weaker requirement.
295
      C1-C3 aim to ensure that all intermediate reconstructions x̂′0 (xt ) (with t > 0) are as accurate as
296   possible, allowing us to effectively reduce the number of sampling steps.
297
298   Previous works, such as (Song et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024), enforce measurement consistency

      by applying A(x̂0 ) = y exactly, whereas DPS (Chung et al., 2023b) does not ensure consistency
      along the diffusion trajectory.

413   The computational requirements of SITCOM are determined by (i) the number of sampling steps
414   N and (ii) the number of gradient steps K required for each sampling iteration. Given the pro-
415   posed stopping criterion, this results in at most N K Number of Function Evaluations (NFEs) of the
416   pre-trained model (forward pass), N K backward passes through the pre-trained model, and N K
417   applications each for the forward operator and its adjoint to solve the optimization problem in Equa-
418
      tion (S1 ). With early stopping, the computational cost is lower. For example, for a linear operator
      A with dimensions m × n, the cost of applying it (or its adjoint) to a vector is O(mn). For a net-
419
      work with width M and depth L, the cost for making a forward pass is O(LM 2 ). The gradients are
420
      computed w.r.t. the input of the DM network, requiring an additional backward pass. Consequently,
421   this procedure is significantly more efficient than network training, where the network weights are
422   updated instead of the input.

      3.6    R ELATION WITH E XISTING A PPROACHES
425
426   While SITCOM and DPS (Chung et al., 2023b) both use Tweedie’s formula, there are two major
427   differences. First, DPS does not enforce backward consistency. Specifically, it only considers one
428   gradient descent step of the optimization in Equation (S1 ), whereas our method perform multiple
429   steps, initializing with xt . Second, DPS does not enforce the forward diffusion consistency, namely,
430   it does not use resampling Equation (S3 ). This means that DPS does not enforce a step-wise C1-
431   C3. Both SITCOM and the works in (Song et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024) are optimization-
      based methods that modify the sampling steps to enforce measurement consistency, and both involve


                                                               8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      mapping back to time t − 1 (as in step 3 of SITCOM). However, there is a major difference between
433   them: The optimization variable in these works is the estimated image at time t (the output of the
434   DM network), whereas in SITCOM, it is the noisy image at time t (the input of the network). This
435   means that these studies enforce C1 and C3, but not C2.
436
      It is worth noting that while some previous works, such as RED-diff (Wang et al., 2024) and DMPlug
437
      (Mardani et al., 2023), also utilize the implicit bias of the network, they adopt the full diffusion
438
      process as a regularizer, applied only once. In contrast, our method uses the neural network as the
439   regularizer at each iteration and focuses specifically on reducing the number of sampling steps for a
440   given level of accuracy.

531
      In this paper, we proposed three conditions to achieve measurement- and diffusion-consistent trajec-
532
      tories for linear and non-linear inverse imaging problems using diffusion models (DMs) as priors.
533
      These conditions form the basis of our unique optimization-based sampling method, which opti-
534   mizes the input of the diffusion model at each step. This approach allows for greater control over
535   the diffusion process and enhances data consistency with the given measurements. Through exten-
536   sive experiments across eight image restoration tasks, we evaluated the effectiveness of our method.
537   The results showed that our sampler consistently delivers improved or comparable quantitative per-
538   formance against state-of-the-art baselines, even with measurement noise. Notably, our method is
539   efficient, requiring significantly less run-time than leading baselines, making it practical for real-
      world applications.

752   B    L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
753
754   In SITCOM, the stopping criterion parameter is set slightly higher than the level of measurement
755   noise, determined by σy . As a result, our method requires access to (or estimation of) the measure-
      ment noise prior to the restoration process. Knowledge of noise level is also assumed in other works


                                                            14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      such as DAPS (Zhang et al., 2024). In practice, classical approaches, such as (Liu et al., 2006; Chen
757   et al., 2015), can be used to estimate the noise.
758
759   Additionally, the stated conditions and proposed sampler are limited to the non-blind setting, as
760
      SITCOM assumes full access to the forward model, unlike works such as (Chung et al., 2023a),
      which perform both image restoration and forward model estimation.
761
762   For future work, in addition to addressing the aforementioned limitations, we aim to extend SIT-
763   COM to the latent space and explore its applicability in medical image reconstruction.
