009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose an Autoregressive (AR) Moving-average (MA) attention structure that
012            can adapt to various linear attention mechanisms, enhancing their ability to cap-
013            ture long-range and local temporal patterns in time series. In this paper, we first
014            demonstrate that, for the time series forecasting (TSF) task, the previously over-
015            looked decoder-only autoregressive Transformer model can achieve results com-
016            parable to the best baselines when appropriate tokenization and training methods
017            are applied. Moreover, inspired by the ARMA model from statistics and recent
018
               advances in linear attention, we introduce the full ARMA structure into existing
               autoregressive attention mechanisms. By using an indirect MA weight generation
019
               method, we incorporate the MA term while maintaining the time complexity and
020
               parameter size of the underlying efficient attention models. We further explore
021            how indirect parameter generation can produce implicit MA weights that align
022            with the modeling requirements for local temporal impacts. Experimental results
023            show that incorporating the ARMA structure consistently improves the perfor-
024            mance of various AR attentions on TSF tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results.
025            The code implementation is available at the following link.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, autoregressive (AR) decoder-only Transformer-based models (Vaswani, 2017; Rad-
031   ford, 2018) have been widely used in sequence modeling tasks across fields such as NLP (Brown
032   et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023), CV (Chen et al., 2020; Esser et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2022),
033
      and audio (Borsos et al., 2023). This structure is well-suited for various sequential generation and
      prediction tasks. However, in typical sequence modeling tasks like time series forecasting (TSF),
034
      there has been less exploration of this architecture compared to other structures. Most of the best-
035
      performing recent TSF models are encoder-only Transformers (Liu et al., 2024a; Nie et al., 2022),
036   MLPs (Das et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), or even linear models (Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
037   The few relevant discussions mainly focus on using pretrained autoregressive LLMs or similar struc-
038   tures for few-shot and zero-shot prediction (Gruver et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024; Liu
039   et al., 2024b), with little research directly evaluating their TSF performance in end-to-end training.
040   Therefore, this paper will first briefly demonstrate that with appropriate tokenization and training
041   methods, a basic AR Transformer is enough to achieve results comparable to the state-of-the-art
042   (SOTA) baselines, as shown in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2.
043   Recently, efficient linear AR attention variants have been explored and developed (Katharopoulos
044   et al., 2020; Hua et al., 2022), reducing the time complexity of standard softmax attention from
045   O(N 2 ) to O(N ). Researchers have found that adding a gating decay factor or a similar exponential
046   moving average (EMA) structure to AR structure, as in gated linear attention (Ma et al., 2022; Yang
047   et al., 2024), enhances linear attention’s ability to model local patterns and improves performance.
048   The success of these approaches inspired us to introduce a more comprehensive full autoregressive
049   moving-average (ARMA) structure into existing AR attention mechanisms and explore the perfor-
050
      mance of the ARMA Transformers in TSF.
051   In TSF models, EMA, connecting back to the historic work of Holt-Winters (Winters, 1960; Holt,
052   2004), focuses on smoothed local data, which improves the modeling of short-term fluctuations but
053   reduces the ability to capture long-term information. In contrast, ARMA, connecting back to the
      historic work of Box-Jenkins (Box et al., 1974), a classic structure in TSF, considers both historical


                                                         1

054
      data and the cumulative impact of prediction errors. This allows it to handle and decouple long-term
055   and short-term effects, significantly improving forecasting performance on data with complicated
056   temporal patterns.
057
058
      We propose the ARMA attention mechanism, which integrates a moving-average (MA) term into
      various existing AR attention mechanisms. Our method improves the TSF performance of AR
059
      Transformers without significantly increasing computational costs, maintaining O(N ) time com-
060
      plexity and original parameter size. We design an indirect MA weight generation to obtain the
061   MA output without explicitly computing the MA attention matrix, preserving efficiency of linear
062   attentions. We explore specific techniques for generating implicit MA weights to ensure proper
063   decoupling and handling of short-term effects. Extensive experiments and visualization analyses
064   demonstrate that ARMA balances long- and short-term dependencies, significantly improving AR
065   Transformers and achieving state-of-the-art TSF results.
066
      The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
067
068   a) We demonstrate that, with appropriate tokenization and preprocessing methods, an AR Trans-
069   former is enough to achieve the level of existing SOTA baselines. Furthermore, the introduction of
070
      ARMA attention enables the decoder-only Transformer to outperform SOTA baselines.
071   b) We propose the ARMA attention mechanism, which introduces an MA term into existing AR
072   attention without increasing time complexity or parameter size. By adding the MA term to vari-
073   ous AR attention mechanisms, the resulting ARMA Transformers significantly improve forecasting
074   performance compared to their AR counterparts.
075   c) We design an indirect MA weight generation method that is computationally efficient while en-
076   suring that the implicit MA weights effectively capture the important short-term effects in TSF,
077   allowing the AR term to focus more on long-term and cyclic patterns.

087   The objective is to learn a mapping function f : RLI ×C → RLP ×C that predicts the future values

      2.7    S ELECTION OF ϕ(·) AND CHARACTERISTICS OF IMPLICIT MA WEIGHTS
251
252   The MA term models short-term effects and local temporal relationships, so we want the implicit Θ
253   to follow a pattern where elements near the diagonal have larger absolute values, and those farther
254   away gradually decrease. The expanded form of Θ is given by Θ = B · (I − B)−1 = B + B2 +
255   B3 +· · ·. The elements along the diagonal direction in B continually accumulate as products into the
256   elements below them in Θ. Since B is strictly lower triangular, the elements of the subdiagonal in Θ
257   remain constant, while the elements further down progressively accumulate additional terms formed
258
      by the product of different β· elements above. Assuming β· follows a distribution and simplifying
      by setting each β· to the distribution mean b, the elements of Θ can be expressed as:

260                                    θij = b(1 + b)i−j−1 , where i > j                                (5)

339   Baselines We built AR Transformers using the five attention mechanisms from Table 1 and added
340   MA terms to create ARMA attention for comparison in TSF tasks. Additionally, we included five
341   recent SOTA baselines: FITS (Xu et al., 2024), iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024a), CATS (Lu et al.,
342   2024), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022), and DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023). We also used a simple channel-
343   dependent encoder-only Transformer, modified by repeating the last input value (like NLinear) to
344   address distribution shift. This model already surpasses older architectures like Autoformer (Wu
345   et al., 2021) and Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), so we excluded these from our comparison.
346   In the main experiments, both AR and ARMA Transformers use a consistent setup: m √       = 3 Trans-
347   former layers, 8 heads, and model dimension determined by a empirical method d = 16 C, where
348   C is the number of series. We evaluate their performance using one-step prediction for each test
349   datapoint, aligned with the baselines. Baseline hyperparameters are set to the reported values from
350   their original papers. For more details on hyperparameters and implementation, see §A.2.
351
      LI , LP , and fair comparison AR/ARMA Transformers maintain stable performance across dif-
352
      ferent input lengths LI and forecasting horizons LP . In the main experiments, we set LI = 512
353   and test with LP ∈ {12, 24, 48, 96}. We did not select longer LP values for the main experiments
354   because the token length in our AR/ARMA Transformer is determined by LI /LP . For longer hori-
355   zons like LP = 720, AR/ARMA Transformers need a larger LI (e.g., 4096). If LI = 512 is used, it
356   results in only one token, reducing the model to a simple MLP, which is feasible but fails to demon-
357   strate the strengths of the AR/ARMA structure. However, increasing LI for baselines would hurt
358   their performance, as LI = 512 is within the optimal range for them. To ensure fairness, we use
359   these settings for the main experiments and later include additional experiments extending LI to
360   4096 and LP to 720.
361   We ran all models on all datasets for the four different LP . In the main text, we report the average
362   test set MSE for each model across different LP on each dataset and provide the full results in §A.3.

      Main TSF results Table 2 highlights the significant performance gains from extending AR Trans-
364
      formers to the ARMA structure. All ARMA attention mechanisms outperform their AR counterparts
365   in both average test MSE and ranking, with linear and standard attention showing the best results.
366
367   Performance of linear attention Linear attention outperforms softmax attention in TSF, suggest-
368
      ing that simpler attention patterns and non-normalized input shortcuts (without denominator) can
      improve generalization on time-varying distributions. This aligns with earlier findings where linear
369
      models can outperform more complex Transformers in TSF (Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
370
371   Performance of gated linear attention ARMA brought the greatest improvement to gated linear
372   attention. In gated AR models, the decay factor helps the AR term focus on important local patterns,
373   but it weakens the ability to capture long-term or stable cyclic patterns. By introducing the MA
374
      term, local effects are absorbed, allowing the decay factor to function properly in the AR forgetting
      mechanism, leading to significant performance gains.
375
376   Performance of fixed attention Fixed attention, which lacks dynamic parameter generation, per-
377   forms worse than other attention. However, its significant improvement with MA terms shows that
      ARMA enhances the model’s ability structurally to capture comprehensive sequence patterns.

404   longer LI often leads to overfitting to long-term patterns, ignoring more important local effects
405   (Zeng et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a). However, the next-step prediction and varying

407   step, improving generalization. As shown in Table 4, increasing LI from 512 to 4096 improves
408   AR/ARMA performance, demonstrating scalability and the ability to properly leverage long-term
409   effects. Also, the ARMA structure consistently boosts AR model performance across different LI .

516   5              C ONCLUSION , LIMITATION , AND FUTURE WORKS
517
518   We propose the ARMA attention mechanism, which integrates an MA term into existing AR atten-
519   tion using a novel indirect MA weight generation method. This approach maintains the same time
520   complexity and parameter size while ensuring the validity of the implicit MA weights. Experiments
521   demonstrate that ARMA attention successfully decouples and handles long-term and short-term ef-
522   fects. The ARMA Transformer, enhanced with the MA term, outperforms their AR counterparts and
523   achieves state-of-the-art results, offering consistent improvements in training with minimal added
524   computational cost.
525   One limitation is that we have not explored combining the channel-independent ARMA Transformer
526   with multivariate forecasting models to improve its handling of inter-series relationships. For future
527   work, ARMA attention could be applied to general sequence modeling tasks beyond TSF. Testing on
528   larger-scale datasets, such as using ARMA Transformers for large-scale NLP pretraining, is another
529   promising direction.

      A.2   H YPER - PARAMETER SETTINGS AND IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
757
758   For the hyper-parameter settings of the AR/ARMA Transformer, we use m = 3 Transformer lay-
759   ers, 8 heads, and√set the hidden dimension d based on the number of series C, using the empirical
760   formula d = 16 C. We use 4d as the hidden dimension for the feedforward MLP in the Trans-
761   former layer. A dropout rate of 0.1 is applied to both the AR term and MA term. We initialize the
762   weights of all linear layers and embedding layers using the GPT-2 weight initialization method, with
763   a normal distribution and a standard deviation of 0.02. For the output projection
                                                                                      √ layers in the atten-
764   tion and MLP, we additionally scale the standard deviation by a factor of 1/ m, aligned with the
765
      GPT-2 setting. Normalization layer is applied both before the input to the Transformer and after the
      Transformer output. We experimented with both standard LayerNorm and RMSNorm as the normal-
766
      ization layer, finding no significant performance differences, so we opted for RMSNorm for lower
767
      computational cost. For token input projection, we use a linear layer to project the LP -dimensional
768   token to a d-dimensional input vector. In the output projection, we do not tie the weights between
769   the input and output linear layers. A learnable position embedding that maps the integer labels from
770   1 to N (the input sequence length) to the corresponding d-dimensional position vectors is used. At
771   the beginning of the model, we apply RevIN to input series SI , subtracting the mean and dividing
772   by the standard deviation for each series. Before outputting the final result, we multiply by the stan-
773   dard deviation and add the mean back. All input series are processed independently and in parallel,
774   merging different series dimensions into the batch size for parallel computation. The random seed
775   used in all the experiments is 2024.
776   All training tasks in this paper can be conducted using a single Nvidia RTX 4090 GPU. The batch
777   size is set to 32. For larger datasets, such as Traffic and PEMS07, we use a batch size of 16 or 8,
778   with 2-step or 4-step gradient accumulation to ensure the effective batch size for parameter updates
779   remains 32. During training, AR/ARMA Transformers are trained using the next-step prediction ob-
780   jective with MSE loss. We use the AdamW optimizer with betas=(0.9, 0.95) and weight decay=0.1,
781   following the GPT-2 settings. For a fair comparison, the same optimizer is used for training baseline
782
      models. It is important to note that the baseline models trained with this AdamW setup show signif-
      icantly better TSF performance compared to those trained with the default Adam optimizer settings.
783
      As a result, the baseline performance presented in this paper may exceed the results reported in
784
      their original papers. Since this study focuses on long-term last token prediction results, we apply
785   an additional weight factor to the training loss for the last token, multiplying it by N . However,
786   this weighting only slightly affects performance on smaller datasets with fewer data points, such as
787   ETTs, and has little to no effect on larger datasets. Given the minimal impact of this method, the
788   original next-token MSE loss is sufficient for most datasets, without requiring further modifications.
789
      We use the same train-validation-test set splitting ratio as in previous studies by Zeng et al. (2023);
790
      Nie et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2024a). We also follow the same dataset standardization methods used
791   in these studies. During training, we evaluate the validation and test losses at the end of each epoch,
792   with an early-stopping patience set to 12 epochs. The maximum number of training epochs is 100.
793   We apply a linear warm-up for the learning rate, increasing it from 0.00006 to 0.0006 over the first
794   5 epochs, and gradually decreasing it in the subsequent epochs.
