                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Training modern AI models has become increasingly expensive, and updating
010
               these base models can significantly alter the behavior of applications or services
011            built on them, due to changes to internal feature representations. In retrieval
012            systems, this involves re-extracting feature vectors for all gallery data. This process
013            can be computationally expensive and time-consuming, especially for large-scale
014            gallery sets. To address this issue, backward compatible learning was introduced,
015            allowing direct comparison between the representations of the base model and the
016            updated ones. Existing backward compatible methods introduce additional losses
017            or specific network architecture changes, which require the availability of base
018            models, thereby limiting compatibility with models trained independently. In this
019
               paper, we show that any independently trained model can be made compatible with
               any other by simply using features derived from softmax outputs. We leverage
020
               the geometric properties of the softmax function, which projects vectors into the
021
               Probability Simplex, preserving the alignment of softmax outputs across model
022            updates and verifying the definition of compatibility. A similar property is observed
023            when using a feature representation derived from logits. They distribute in a
024            simplex configuration, but with a wider spread in the feature distribution than
025            softmax outputs, leading to a more robust and transferable representation. Our
026            framework achieves state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks, where
027            either the number of training classes extends across multiple steps or the base
028            model is updated with advanced network architectures, showing that any publicly
029            available pretrained model are compatible without requiring any additional training
030
               or adaptation. Our code will be made available upon acceptance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Training modern AI models has become increasingly expensive, limiting accessibility to a few
035
      well-resourced organizations (Wolf et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2021; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
036   2023; Anthropic, 2024). Despite reductions in model parameters and computing costs (Dubey et al.,
037   2024), training from scratch, fine-tuning and inference (i.e., test time) (Wang et al., 2024; OpenAI,
038   2024) remain economically challenging, particularly for smaller teams. As a result, these models are
039   increasingly offered as services through APIs, a trend that is likely to continue due to persistent high
040   costs and the significant benefits of scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020). Offering models as services
041   not only simplifies the development of new applications but also enables their widespread use across
042   various fields. However, updating these base models can completely transform the behavior of the
043   applications or services built upon them (Raffel, 2023).
044   Several factors may drive updates to the base model (Raffel, 2023; Yadav et al., 2024), including
045   evolving training strategies (Biondi et al., 2024; Echterhoff et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2020), advance-
046   ments in architectures (Touvron et al., 2023), the availability of higher-quality datasets (Gunasekar
047   et al., 2023), the expansion of training classes, or extended training periods (Biderman et al., 2023;
048   Raffel, 2023). These advancements encapsulate rapid progress in a unified model, simplifying usage
049   as models, datasets, and computational infrastructures grow in size and complexity (Bommasani
050
      et al., 2021; Sorscher et al., 2022).
051   A common scenario involves developers focusing on performance improvements in model updates,
052   potentially compromising compatibility with earlier model versions. Concurrently, end users, such
053   as drivers of semi-autonomous cars, often develop a mental model of the machine learning model’s
      capabilities (Bansal et al., 2019a). As the software updates, these human-users must continually

054
      adjust their mental model of its functionality and capabilities, a task that is not only challenging
055   and potentially dissatisfying but also unsafe (Bansal et al., 2019b). To minimize user adjustments,
056   both current and new software versions run in the background to compare decisions before the final
057   deployment (Templeton, 2019). Similarly, image retrieval services might experience unexpected
058   changes in image rankings following a model update, requiring users to adapt when previously
059   top-ranked retrieved images no longer appear first (Shen et al., 2020). In the same vein, recent
060   observations have shown similar issues with large language models (LLMs) (Echterhoff et al., 2024),
061   which directly raises concerns about AI safety and alignment. This is particularly critical when
062   decisions and textual outputs from these agents are translated into actions (Amodei et al., 2016; Ngo
063
      et al., 2024).
064   This general issue of updating a base model seamlessly while ensuring compatibility has been
065   independently explored with research emphasis varying based on the type of compatibility required
066   with earlier models: 1) if through the ultimate layer, it targets the problem of “negative flips”— ensure
067   that the new model only mimics the old model when it is correct. (Milani Fard et al., 2016; Yan
068   et al., 2021); 2) if through the penultimate layer, it involves learning “backward-compatible” feature
069
      representations that can be interchangeably used across models (Shen et al., 2020; Biondi et al.,
      2023); and 3) if through the whole architecture it targets merging by averaging earlier models using
070
      the concept of “linear mode connectivity” (Ainsworth et al., 2023; Wortsman et al., 2022; Matena &
071
      Raffel, 2022; Frankle et al., 2020).
072
073   Although repositories of pre-trained foundation models are publicly available (Wolf et al., 2020),
074   merging large models still requires not only the same architecture but also access to weights behind
075
      an API and substantial compute resources (Yadav et al., 2024). Backward compatibility, as defined
      in (Shen et al., 2020) originally developed to avoid image re-indexing in retrieval, is crucial in
076
      overcoming these challenges by ensuring models remain effective on targeted tasks and compatible
077
      with established input and output (Raffel, 2023). However, most existing studies on backward
078   compatibility require the availability of previous or base models, thereby limiting compatibility with
079   models trained independently (Shen et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2021; Duggal et al., 2021). Recent
080   studies have begun to address this issue (Biondi et al., 2023; 2024). Although some theoretical results
081   support these methods, implementation requires agreement on using a specific fixed classifier among
082   parties before training the models (Pernici et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021). This agreement is often
083   challenging due to competition-related issue between organizations, and the fixed classifier requires
084   substantial pre-allocation for future classes.
085   In this paper, we demonstrate that any independently trained model can be made compatible with
086   any other, and can remain compatible when expanded with new classes. This is achieved by using
087   feature representations derived from softmax outputs. The softmax function projects the feature
088   space into the Probability Simplex, a geometric configuration in which the vertices—corresponding
089   to the standard unit vector of the feature space—are maximally equidistant. Although the Probability
090   Simplex evolves with the introduction of new classes, we show that a projection matrix can be defined
091   to preserve alignment across model updates, resulting in stationary representations that verify the
092
      definition of compatibility in Shen et al. (2020). A similar property is observed when using feature
      representations derived from logits. We demonstrate that, during training, they distribute in the same
093
      simplex configuration of the softmax outputs. However, they present a wider spread in the feature
094
      distribution than softmax outputs, leading to a more robust and transferable representation.
095
096   Our framework achieves state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks, where either the
097   number of training classes grows over multiple steps or the base model is upgraded with more
098
      advanced architectures. This shows that any publicly available pretrained model can be seamlessly
      made compatible without requiring additional training or adaptation.
099
100   Our main contributions are threefold:
101
102         1. We introduce a novel approach to model compatibility that requires no training and utilizes
103            independently trained models.
104         2. Theoretical support for the methodology is provided, confirming its validity and broad
105            applicability.
106         3. We demonstrate significant empirical improvements, particularly in scenarios with frequent
107            model updates.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      The aim of compatible training is to learn representations that can be used interchangeably when
111
      updating a model, thereby avoiding the re-indexing of the gallery set in retrieval. This basic formu-
112   lation, originally proposed in Shen et al. (2020), is in principle a practical method for evaluating
113   model updates, even when the base or updated models are not explicitly used for retrieval but operate
114   as input-output black boxes. Backward Compatible Training (BCT) was introduced by Shen et al.
115   (2020), employing the previous classifier as a fixed reference during the training of the new model, so
116   that new feature vector can align to their old class prototypes. Following works introduced additional
117   regularization loss functions (Meng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; 2022; Pan et al., 2023) to align
118   the updated representation with the old one. In particular, in Meng et al. (2021) (LCE) a combination
119   of loss functions is used to align class means between different model upgrades and to achieve more
120
      compact intra-class distributions while learning new data, directly optimizing one of the inequalities
      in the definition of compatibility given by Shen et al. (2020). An adversarial learning discriminator
121
      was introduced in BCT by Pan et al. (2023) (AdvBCT) to minimize the distribution disparity between
122
      features from the old and new models.
123
124   Due to the regularization constraints imposed to achieve compatibility, the performance of the new
125   backward-compatible model often does not reach that of a newly independently trained model (Zhou
126
      et al., 2023). To address this issue, other methods achieved compatibility between independently
      trained models using mapping-based approaches (Chen et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Meng et al.,
127
      2021; Ramanujan et al., 2022; Jaeckle et al., 2023). However, learning these mapping functions on
128
      top of the models introduces additional computational overhead, which can become infeasible with
129   large datasets, and involves the composition of these modules when the model is updated several
130   times. In a similar vein, in Zhou et al. (2023); Ricci et al. (2024), at each model update, the feature
131   space of the model is expanded to obtain compatibility with the previous model while learning the
132   new information in the expanded part of the feature space. Although these methods can leverage the
133   discriminative power of a newly independently trained model, their implementations require specific
134   network architecture changes.
135   The solution proposed by Biondi et al. (2023) (CoReS) to achieve compatibility between indepen-
136   dently trained models involves learning according to a pre-allocated d-Simplex fixed classifier. This
137   approach learns stationary representations, with features remaining aligned with their fixed class
138   prototypes, while new classes are incorporated into pre-allocated regions of the feature space. Our
139   formulation leverages one-hot encoded label vectors as a fixed reference, implicitly supporting
140   stationarity in the alignment of features derived from softmax outputs. The implicit use of one-hot
141   encoded label vectors as a fixed reference obviates the need for a unified fixed classifier among parties
142   before training the models, given that the softmax function is inherently universal across these parties.

518

519

520
521   Limitations. PSP and LSP require that the ordering of classes does not change across model
522   updates or, in the case of extended training classes, follows an extension within a nested dataset
523   structure that does not alter the original sequence. Although this condition may seem restrictive, it can
524
      be mitigated if the permutations of the class order is known. A further limitation of our framework is
      that the representation size increases linearly with the number of classes (as can be seen from Eq. 2
525
      and Eq. 6), as in the case of other methods (Biondi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Biondi et al., 2024)
526
      that leverage feature representations configured as Simplex. This results in feature vectors with high
527   dimensions, which may lead to increased memory requirements. Although the representation benefits
528   from top-k sparsification for dimensionality reduction, as empirically confirmed (Appendix K), this
529   promising and important aspect merits further investigation in future studies.
530
531   5    C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      This paper showed that independently trained DNNs can be made compatible simply using feature
534   representations derived from softmax outputs and logits. We demonstrated that these representations,
535   configured as regular simplex, remain aligned across multiple model updates through projections. This
536   results into stationary representations, which satisfy both the criteria of the definition of compatibility,
537   recently shown to be not strictly feasible. Experiments confirmed the theoretical results, showing that
538   our framework achieves state-of-the-art compatibility performance, particularly in scenarios with
539   frequent model updates. Notably, our methodology achieves compatibility between publicly available
      pretrained models without requiring any additional training or adaptation.

1058   verifies the compatibility as in Def. 1. Fig. 5 shows this behavior while varying the classes d. If
1059   there is not new information to improves the model’s discrimination capability, this leads to equal
1060   concentration in the von Mises-Fisher (vMF) distribution of the updated model’s class features,
1061   corresponding to the condition η = 1. As the mean resultant length md (κ) does not change with the
1062   model update, the inequalities of Def. 1 are satisfied (it correspond to the trivial solution discussed by

       Shen et al. (2020)).
