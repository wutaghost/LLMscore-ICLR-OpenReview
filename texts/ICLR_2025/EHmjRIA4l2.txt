009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a modular and compositional approach to learning human-aligned
012            world models via state-action hierarchies. Our approach is inspired by sensory-
013            motor hierarchies in the mammalian brain. We model complex state transition
014            dynamics as a sequence of simpler dynamics, which in turn can be modeled using
015            even simpler dynamics, and so on, endowing the approach with rich composi-
016
               tionality. We introduce Composer, a practical method for learning complex world
               models that leverages hypernetworks and abstract states for generating lower-level
017
               transition functions on-the-fly. We first show that state abstractions in Composer
018
               emerge naturally in simple environments as a consequence of training. Incor-
019            porating a variant of contrastive learning allows Composer to scale to more com-
020            plex environments while ensuring that the learned abstractions are human aligned.
021            Additionally, learning a higher-level transition function between learned abstract
022            states leads to a hierarchy of transition functions for modeling complex dynamics.
023            We apply Composer to compositional navigation problems and show its capability
024            for rapid planning and transfer to novel scenarios. In both traditional grid-world
025            navigation problems as well as in the more complex Habitat vision-based naviga-
026            tion domain, a Composer-based agent learns to model the state-action dynamics
027
               within and between different rooms using a hierarchy of transition functions and
               leverage this hierarchy for efficient downstream planning. Our results suggest that
028
               Composer offers a promising framework for learning the complex dynamics of
029
               real-world environments using a compositional and interpretable approach.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Composing existing skills and knowledge to creatively generate solutions for new and complex
035   problems is a fundamental attribute of human intelligence. Advances in generative AI and large lan-
036   guage models are beginning to demonstrate attributes of human-like intelligence but fail at simple
037   tasks like multiplying a few small numbers (Bubeck et al. (2023); Bender et al. (2021); Schmidhuber
038   (1991a)) that rely on application of compositional knowledge and reasoning. Similar observations
039
      hold true for traditional reinforcement learning (RL) and embodied AI agents (Lake et al. (2016)).
      Recent developments in hierarchical reinforcement learning, supported by novel architectures, have
040
      resolved several such challenges in the field (Hafner et al., 2022; Levy et al., 2017; Kulkarni et al.,
041
      2016). However, a significant gap remains in effectively utilizing structured architectures to ex-
042   ploit compositionality and enable rapid transfer of dynamics and skills. Additionally, while Vision
043   Language Models have made labeling visual data faster and more affordable (Radford et al., 2021;
044   Deitke et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), there is still limited research on how these labels can enhance
045   compositional learning in embodied agents.
046
      This prompts a key question: What fundamental computational principles in biological neural net-
047
      works enable compositionality for solving novel problems? To rigorously answer this question, we
048   look towards recent advances in computational neuroscience. Predictive coding theories have con-
049   sistently garnered increasing attention as computational models of how the brain perceives and acts
050   in the real world (Rao & Ballard (1999); Friston & Kiebel (2009); Keller & Mrsic-Flogel (2018);
051   Jiang & Rao (2022b)). In predictive coding, different areas of the neocortex together implement a
052   hierarchical generative model of the world. Feedback connections from a higher to a lower level
053   predict lower-level responses, and the prediction errors propagate via feedforward connections to
      update higher-level estimates. While the original formulation of predictive coding ignored actions,

085

086

087   and solve them via modulated transition dynamics (a.k.a firing patterns) specific to the problem.
088
089   Motivated by these insights, we develop in Section 2 the Composer algorithm for learning a hier-
090
      archy of transition functions and state abstractions. Composer uses only random trajectory rollouts
      of an agent and their prediction errors to naturally learn abstractions (Fig. 1). We argue that unlike
091
      traditional state and action abstractions, Composer learns to abstract the transition dynamics of en-
092
      vironments and reuses them in similar scenarios. We confirm our hypothesis with experiments and
093   present them here. In Section 3 we build on the preliminary insights from the previous section and
094   scale Composer to more complex environments with interpretable notion of abstractions. Finally, in
095   Section 4 we present results for interesting applications with Composer, like hierarchical planning
096   and novel scene generation. Details including additional results, code snippets and derivations are
097   presented in the supplementary section. To summarize, the main contributions of this paper are:
098

245   The real test for Composer is when it is scaled to realistic, pixel based, ego-centric environments. We
246   choose Habitat 2.0 for their fast and efficient rendering, flexibility in environment configuration and
247   their highly realistic suite of embodied AI tasks (Szot et al., 2021). Habitat’s noisy and sometimes
248   partially occluded images provide a very realistic scenario for benchmarking our approach.

      3.1   W HAT DOES C OMPOSER LEARN IN H ABITAT 2.0?
251
252   There are two fundamental difficulties when scaling our originally proposed algorithm seen in Sec-
253   tion 2. First, inferring abstractions from prediction errors, and then subsequently training the model
254   in a alternating descent fashion is very inefficient and slow. The entire process requires a minimum
255   of two backpropagation step and often more if we choose to infer for multiple steps. Modern auto-
256   differentiation libraries like pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and parallelized GPU operations render
257   the proposed Composer algorithm much slower than the state-of-the art hierarchical RL approaches.
258   Second, and more importantly, early experiments show that it is not trivial to learn well defined and
259   well separated abstractions in complex, and noisy RGBD images. As seen in Figure 4, with increase
260   in complexity of the sequential data, the ability to discern different abstract dynamics diminishes. In
261   Figure 4(C), the agent was allowed to explore the entire environment without supervision. We see
262   that even though there are regions of densely clustered points for a given room, it is hard to decipher
263   anything meaningful and reliable when several complex dynamics are involved. This problem is
264   compounded by the fact that it is not obviously clear if there is an inherent difficulty in learning
265
      dynamics for this environment or if the model is utterly distracted by spurious variations, a feature
      of visual pixel based reconstruction (Stone et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023).
266
267   To address the first issue, we introduce an encoder RNN to directly estimate abstract states s(2)
268   from lower-level variables. We can now avoid an extra inference step which required propagating
269   error gradients across the model. To address the second issue, we introduce supervised contrastive
      learning in the next subsection.

301   3.2   S UPERVISED C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
302
303
      To overcome the non-trivial problem of learning well defined and well separable abstractions, we
      turn towards the human brain. We as humans, do not necessarily learn every abstraction bottom-up
304
      from scratch. More often than not, we strongly rely on signals and labels used by others and quickly
305
      adopt them to our own internal models. This is especially true when exploring a novel scene or a
306   problem. Humans try their best to use existing ideas and concepts to derive new solutions (Lake
307   et al., 2016).
308
309
      Motivated by this fact, we look towards learning from labels. Recent advances in Vision Language
      Models, have made it incredible cheap to generate and gather labeled images and videos (Radford
310
      et al., 2021; Deitke et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Supervised Contrastive Learning is a variant of
311
      SimCLR that uses labels to learn robust and powerful representations of visual data (Khosla et al.,
312   2021; Chen et al., 2020). Given some anchor indices I, if P (i) are the set of positive examples for
313   anchor i (samples from same room in Habitat, for example) and A(i) is all samples excluding the
314   ith , the supervised contrastive loss for a batch of inputs is defined as:

503   5    C ONCLUSION
504
505   This paper presents a new method called Composer for learning transition dynamics for complex
506   real-world environments based on a structured heirarchical model. The method is inspired by the
507   theory of the mammalian cortex, and learns a hierarchy of transition functions using self-supervised
508   learning based on prediction errors and hypernetworks. We applied the model to both traditional
509   grid worlds and the more complex Habitat domain and showed that higher-level latent codes that
510   generate transition dynamics for different environments form different clusters in the latent space.
511   Furthermore, this continuous latent space exhibits smooth transformations of transition functions,
512
      allowing Composer to generate dynamics for new environments in a compositional manner. We in-
      troduce abstract actions to allow transition functions to be learned for higher-level latent state spaces,
513
      giving rise to hierarchical world models. We also introduce contrastive learning with very sparse la-
514
      bels to regularize the learnt abstractions and align them towards human interpretable representations.
515   Our results demonstrate the efficacy of higher-level planning using Composer by exploiting learned
516   hierarchical world models and local reference frames. Our ongoing and future work is focused on
517   scaling Composer to larger-scale environments and RL benchmarks, and leveraging the model’s
518   compositional structure and ability to generate new transition functions on the fly to achieve fast
519   transfer across environments.

      A.1   R ELATED W ORK
759
760   The most closely related work is the Active Predictive Coding model (Rao et al. (2023)) and re-
761   lated papers on predictive coding Rao & Ballard (1999); Jiang & Rao (2022a). Other related work
762   pertains to various components and aspects of our model including: (1) Top-down Modulation, (2)
763   Hierarchical World Models (3) Hierarchical Policy (4) Reference Frames.
764
      Top-Down Modulation refers to an abstract state, conditioning a function that is usually working
765   at a constrained spatio-temporal scale. Intuitively, the latent vector abstracts critical parts of the
766   lower-level transition or policy function, allowing re-use of learned dynamics or policies in novel
767   scenarios. For example, a child learns to lift a coffee mug and has no problem transferring that
768   experience to picking up a jar or pitcher with a handle. There is evidence from neuroscience that the
769   cortex may use top-down gain modulation to facilitate such transfer of learned behaviors (Ferguson
770   & Cardin (2020)). We propose variants of hypernetworks (Ha et al. (2016); Galanti & Wolf (2020))
771   as potential candidates to implement such abstractions in the Composer model.
772   Hierarchical Transition Models: Ha & Schmidhuber (2018) introduced world models into model-
773   based RL. Since then, powerful variants of world models have been proposed for modeling in-
774   creasingly complex environment dynamics (Hafner et al. (2020; 2022); Micheli et al. (2023)). Yet,
775   these world models are limited in scope when exposed to novel environments. Graph schemas have
776   gained in popularity in recent years as potential computational mechanisms for emulating abstrac-
777   tion, transfer and planning in the brain (Guntupalli et al. (2023); Moser et al. (2017); Whittington
778   et al. (2021)). Our model, which is inspired by the brain’s hierarchical architecture, employs hier-
779
      archical world models that learn abstractions of transition functions limited in space and time, and
      further learns to transition in the new abstract space with access to only unsupervised prediction
780
      errors.
781
782   Hierarchical Policies: Hierarchical Reinforcement Leaning and action abstractions have have a
783   long history in RL (Sutton et al. (1999), Barto & Mahadevan (2003), Schmidhuber (1990; 1991a;b)).
784   With the introduction of deep neural networks (LeCun et al. (2015); Schmidhuber (2014)), many
785
      variants of hierarchical and deep reinforcement learning have been developed (Bacon et al. (2017);
      Hafner et al. (2022); Kulkarni et al. (2016)). Abel (2022) provides an extensive discussion of abstract
786
      states and actions. In Composer, a higher-level abstract action vector is generated by the higher-level
787
      policy, and this action vector in turn generates, via a hypernetwork, a low-level policy function;
788   details in Section 3.4, see also (Rao et al. (2023))).
789
790   Reference Frames: Our approach decomposes a complex problem into transition functions and
791
      policies that operate hierarchically within local reference frames. This allows an agent to ignore
      task-irrelevant state and action information at each level, resulting in considerable efficiencies in
792
      training and transfer. The importance of reference frames in intelligence and reasoning has been
793
      highlighted recently by Hawkins (2021) based on evidence from neuroscience involving “grid cells”
794   and spatial reference frames in the cortex and hippocampus (O’Keefe & Dostrovsky (1971); Moser
795   et al. (2017)). Previous work in AI on hard attention models (Mnih et al. (2014)) can be regarded as
796   single-level versions of our approach which learns hierarchical reference frames (Section 2).
