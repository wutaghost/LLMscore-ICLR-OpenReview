                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Fine-tuning pretrained (large) Transformer backbones efficiently for downstream
010
               tasks has been extensively explored using both Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
011            (PEFT) methods, such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) and its variants, as well
012            as more recent Representation-Efficient Fine-Tuning (ReFT) approaches. In both
013            of these formulations, fine-tuning weights for selected pretrained layers are treated
014            as model parameters that are directly learned from the downstream task data, often
015            making them layer-specific. While these methods simultaneously aim for memory
016            efficiency, some approaches, such as VeRA (Vector-based Random matrix Adapta-
017            tion), may not achieve this consistently in practice. In this paper, we propose a novel
018            approach for generating fine-tuning weights through a configurable layer-sharing
019
               mechanism, termed Generative parameter-effIcient Fine-Tuning (GIFT). GIFT
               uses a simple parameterization scheme involving two linear layers (without bias
020
               terms) to enable efficient fine-tuning. This method bridges the gap between PEFT
021
               and ReFT, ensuring both parameter and memory efficiency. GIFT can be viewed
022            as a variant of LoRA with parameters shared across layers, conditioned on the
023            pretrained weights, with significantly fewer trainable parameters. Through exten-
024            sive experiments, we demonstrate that our GIFT consistently achieves superior
025            performance and parameter efficiency compared to baselines on commonsense and
026            arithmetic reasoning tasks, instruction tuning with the Llama family of models, and
027            visual recognition benchmarks with Vision Transformers. Notably, GIFT achieves
028            a 5.7% absolute increase in average accuracy with a 14x reduction in trainable
029            parameters compared to LoRA on the Commonsense170k dataset using Llama-3
030
               (8B), and a 5.4% increase in win rate with a 4x reduction in parameters using
               Llama-2 (7B) during instruction tuning. Our method also attains a slightly
031
               higher win rate for instruction tuning than GPT-3.5 (Turbo 1106).

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Fine-tuning pretrained deep neural networks (DNNs) as feature backbones for downstream tasks
037   has been an important and challenging research topic. In recent years, large feature backbones
038   with open weights such as Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; AI@Meta, 2024), termed foundation
039   models (Bommasani et al., 2021), have become ubiquitous. Training such models from scratch is
040   impossible with limited resources, and fine-tuning them entirely may also be costly. This raises
041   questions about which parts of a pretrained model to fine-tune (often as a hyperparameter), and how
042   they should be trained (entailing rigorous formulations).
043   Efficient fine-tuning in terms of parameters, compute and memory/storage has been extensively
044   explored using both Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods, such as Low-Rank Adaptation
045   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) and its variants (Zhang et al., 2023b; Dettmers et al., 2023; Lialin et al.,
046   2023; Jie & Deng, 2023; Kopiczko et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), as well as more
047   recent Representation Fine-Tuning (ReFT) (Wu et al., 2024b) approaches. LoRA learns weight-
048   residuals in the low-rank form (i.e., Al and B l in Fig. 1 (a)). Motivated from a causal intervention
049   mechanism (Geiger et al., 2024), ReFT steers the pretrained model towards a task by editing the
050
      representations of a few selected tokens in a low-dimensional subspace, showing strong performance
      at a lower parameter cost compared to LoRA, albeit at a slight increase in inference cost as the
051
      learnable parameters cannot be merged into the pretrained model like LoRA.
052
053   In both, LoRA-based and ReFT-based formulations, fine-tuning parameters for selected pretrained
      layers are treated as model parameters that are directly learned from the downstream task data, often

139   2.1   O UR P ROPOSED GIFT
140   Denote by ΩL×dout ×din the pretrained weights of L selected layers in fine-tuning (e.g., all the Query
141   layers). Following the common practice towards efficiency, we enforce a low-rank structure for the
142   weight-generator network G(·; Θ) in Eqn. 4. We have,
143

144                  G(ΩL×dout ×din ; Θ) = Linear g Linear ΩL×dout ×din ; ϕ ; θ ; ψ ,                    (6)

      where,
147   • Linear(Ω; ϕ) projects the input dimension to a lower dimension (or rank) r with learnable weights
148     ϕ ∈ Rdin ×r without bias terms. Denote by Ω1 ∈ RL×dout ×r the output of this layer.
149   • Linear(·; ψ) is an output dimension-recovery projection with learnable weights ψ ∈ Rr×din and
150     no bias term. It outputs the learned weight-residuals, ∆Ω ∈ RL×dout ×ddin .
151   • g(·; θ) is the low-dimensional generator network, which can be realized by any suitable network
152     specifications. We consider the following schema in this paper:
153
        – Transformer: We treat Ω1 as a batch of L sequences each consisting of dout tokens in r-dim
          space. We then apply a single Transformer block (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
154
        – MLP-Mixers: Similar to vanilla Transformers, we apply a single MLP-Mixer (Tolstikhin et al.,
155
          2021) block.
156     – Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs): e.g., g(Ω1 ; θ) = Linear(GELU(Linear(Ω1 ; θ1 )); θ2 ), where
157       θ1 ∈ Rm·r×r+m·r and θ2 ∈ Rr×m·r+r consist of weights and bias terms of the two linear layers
158       with the MLP latent dimension ratio m (e.g., m = 2).
159     – Element-wise non-linearity functions without learnable parameters (i.e., θ = ∅): e.g., g(Ω1 ) =
160       Sigmoid(Ω1 ) or g(Ω1 ) = GELU(Ω1 ).
161     – The identity operation: g(Ω1 ; θ) = Identity(Ω1 ) = Ω1 with no learnable parameters θ = ∅,
          which leads to the simple two-layer linear parameterization of GIFT (Eqn. 5).

      2.3   GIFT A IMS TO “BALANCE ” P RETRAINING AND F INE -T UNING
199
200   Pretrained Transformer backbones encode diverse knowledge from large-scale pretraining datasets
201   within their weights. Fine-tuning them for a downstream task aims to incorporate new information
202   from the task-specific training data and utilize the information present in the pretrained weights to the
203   fullest extent. To achieve this, the fine-tuned weights can be directly conditioned on the pretrained
204   weights, such that the new information is learned conditionally from the information in the pretrained
205
      weights. While LoRA and it’s variants use a residual structure to address this, the residual weights
      are not directly conditioned on the pretrained weights, but rather learned via back-propagation (chain
206
      rule) updates. One of the simplest functions that can achieve this explicit conditioning is a linear
207
      transformation of the pretrained weights, as leveraged in Eqn. 7. Hence, the fine-tuned weights can
208   also be expressed in the space of the pretrained weights Wdout ×din via Wdout ×din · Θdin ×din .
209
210   When pretrained Transformer backbones are sufficiently expressive, as is typically assumed in
211
      efficient fine-tuning, simpler parameterization methods like GIFT should be more generalizable and
      better under the principle of Occam’s razor. Our ablation studies in Section 4.2 show the effectiveness
212
      of the linear parametrization over other schemes.

411   4.1      S HARING THE WEIGHT RESIDUALS IN L O RA
412   As mentioned in Section 2, GIFT generates layer-specific weight residuals and fine-tuned weights
413   even though the learnable parameters are shared across layers. We verify that this approach proposed
414   in our GIFT is beneficial over simply sharing the residual weights in LoRA across the layers of
415   the same type, (i.e., ∆W l = B · A ∀l ∈ L). Table 5 show that this strategy leads to much lower
416   performance than GIFT. This shows that the fine-tuned weights indeed need to be layer specific, and
417   the generative approach in GIFT can achieve this while still maintaining the parameter efficiency
418   of shared weights. This also suggests that methods like (Renduchintala et al., 2024), which impose
419
      further restrictions by sharing the residuals across Query, Key and Value components may not scale
      to more complex datasets.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
441   Parameter Efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT). The goal of PEFT methods is to reduce the compu-
442   tational resources (memory footprint, wall time, etc.) required for fine-tuning large models such
443   as Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
444   Prompt-based methods either append prompts to the input tokens (Lester et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022),
445   or the intermediate layers (Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023c). Early work on
446
      PEFT used sequential/parallel learnable adapters added after the Multi-Head Self Attention and/or
      FFN blocks (Houlsby et al., 2019; Bapna & Firat, 2019; Pfeiffer et al., 2021; 2020; Rücklé et al.,
447
      2021; Mahabadi et al., 2021a; Chen et al., 2022). LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) and its variants (Zhang
448
      et al., 2023b; Dettmers et al., 2023; Lialin et al., 2023; Jie & Deng, 2023; Kopiczko et al., 2023;
449   Gao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) learn residuals to the pretrained weight matrices in the form of
450   low-rank factorization, removing the added inference cost in adapter based methods. Tied LoRA
451   (Renduchintala et al., 2024) shares the residual weights across layers, and also across Query, Key and
452   Value components. BitFit (Zaken et al., 2022) fine-tunes all the bias terms in a pretrained backbone.
453   MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022) edits a pretrained model by learning fine-tuning weights from the
454   gradient inputs with a low-rank MLP parameterization.
455   Hypernetworks. Ha et al. (2016) introduced Hypernetworks, i.e., neural networks that generate
456   the parameters for other neural networks, in language modeling tasks by generating the weights
457   of an LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). Hypernetworks have previously been applied for
458   few-shot classification (Zhao et al., 2020; Zhmoginov et al., 2022), transfer learning (Requeima et al.,
459   2019) and continual learning (von Oswald et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2022). Similar to our proposed
460   approach, (Requeima et al., 2019) learns to adapt a global feature extractor through an adaptation
461   network. In a few shot continual learning setup, (Vladymyrov et al., 2023) uses a hyper-Transformer
462   to generate the parameters for a separate Convolutional Neural Network (ConvNet), which use as
463
      inputs both a support set of images of the current task and the ConvNet parameters generated for the
      previous tasks. HyperFormer++ (Mahabadi et al., 2021b) uses a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to
464
      generate the parameters from layer embedding and a latent vector for Adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019)
465
      introduced across layers of a pretrained model in a multitask setting. Unlike (Mahabadi et al., 2021b),
466   we directly use the weights of the frozen pretrained model, thus eliminating the need for embeddings.
467
468   Neural Functionals: Our approach is related to neural functionals that aim to learn deep neural
469
      networks acting on the weights of other neural networks. For toy problems, equivariant architectures
      have been explored for tasks like classifying implicit neural representations (Navon et al., 2023; Zhou
470
      et al., 2023a;b; Kofinas et al., 2024), adapting model architectures to new domains (Navon et al.,
471
      2023), predicting model generalization performance (Zhou et al., 2023a;b; Kofinas et al., 2024; Lim
472   et al., 2023), and learned optimizers (Zhou et al., 2024). However, our work is the first to explore
473   fine-tuning of a model using it’s own weights. We do not use equivariant architectures, but note that
474   this direction of work is orthogonal to ours, and can be further explored in the future.
475
476   6    C ONCLUSION
477
      We present Generative Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (GIFT) for adapting pretrained Transformer
478
      backbones on downstream tasks. Our GIFT learns to generate the fine-tuning weight-residuals for
479
      layers selected in fine-tuning directly from their frozen pretrained weights using a neural network.
480   We show that a simple design - where GIFT consists of two linear layers without bias terms - can
481   achieve strong performance, which provides a novel angle for formulating PEFT methods. We further
482   show that the simple GIFT bridges PEFT and ReFT methods. We conduct experiments across various
483   tasks, including Natural Language Generation (instruction following, commonsense reasoning, and
484   arithmetic reasoning), Natural Language Understanding, and Visual Recognition. GIFT outperforms
485   previous PEFT methods while using approximately 14 times fewer parameters and surpasses previous
      ReFT approaches with half the parameters.
