009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Performance evaluation is a critical but compute-intensive procedure in neural
012
               architecture search (NAS). To alleviate evaluation costs, performance predictors
013
               have been widely adopted to predict architecture performance directly. Recent
014            studies have introduced ranking loss functions into predictors to focus on the
015            architecture rankings instead of absolute accuracy, thus enhancing the ranking
016            ability of performance predictors. Despite the successful application of ranking
017            loss functions, the lack of comprehensive measure metrics and different experi-
018            mental configurations make a fair comparison among these loss functions a huge
019            challenge. Additionally, some well-known ranking loss functions have not been
020            thoroughly examined in the context of performance predictors. In this paper, we
021            conduct the first study for 11 ranking loss functions containing the existing and
022
               the novel ones by comparing their effectiveness in performance predictors under
               various settings. We find that: (i) The choice of ranking loss function has a ma-
023
               jor influence on the performance of predictors; (ii) the quality of the architectures
024
               searched by the predictor-based NAS methods is closely correlated with the pre-
025            dictor’s performance on top-centered rank metrics, rather than traditional metrics
026            like Kendall Tau. We believe these results and insights can serve as recommenda-
027            tions for the optimal loss function to employ in predictors across various search
028            spaces and experimental conditions.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Neural architecture search (NAS) aims to automate the design of neural architectures (Elsken et al.,
035   2019) and has been widely applied in various domains (Liu et al., 2018a; Pham et al., 2018; Ghi-
036   asi et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020). One bottleneck of early NAS is the expensive performance
037   evaluation stage, which involves training architectures from scratch (Zoph & Le, 2016; Real et al.,
038   2017). To tackle this issue, performance predictors have been proposed to estimate the final perfor-
039
      mance of unseen architectures (Luo et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021). Specifically,
      performance predictors can learn the mapping from architecture design to the corresponding per-
040
      formance using only a small number of trained architectures, thus greatly reducing computational
041
      costs. However, it remains challenging to accurately estimate the absolute performance of a given
042   architecture at the budget of limited training samples (Xu et al., 2021). To overcome this challenge,
043   recent advancements have proposed a paradigm shift from architecture performance prediction to
044   architecture ranking prediction by replacing the widely-used mean square error (MSE) loss in pre-
045   dictors with pairwise and listwise ranking loss functions (Xu et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2024; Hwang
046   et al., 2024). This transition allows predictors to easily rank architectures in the correct order and
047   thus facilitate NAS to discover the top-performing architectures.
048   Although ranking loss functions endow predictors with a better ranking ability, a fair comparison
049   among them is still lacking currently. The results in prior works were mostly obtained under differ-
050   ent experimental conditions where predictor types, search spaces, data splits, and hyperparameter
051   tuning vary a lot from each other. Besides, only a few ranking losses have been applied to perfor-
052   mance predictors previously because most losses were originally designed for information retrieval.
053   Meanwhile, the relationship between the predictors’ performance in rank-based metrics and their
      results in NAS experiments has not been well investigated.

054
      This naturally raises the following questions: how do pointwise, pairwise and listwise ranking losses
055   compare to each other under consistent conditions? Furthermore, could a ranking loss with excellent
056   performance on ranked-based metrics indicate promising searching results for predictor-based NAS
057   methods?
058
      We answer the questions above by studying 11 ranking loss functions across five search spaces:
059
      NAS-Bench-101 (Ying et al., 2019), NAS-Bench-201 (Dong & Yang, 2019), TransNAS-Bench-
060
      101 (Duan et al., 2021), NAS-Bench-NLP (Klyuchnikov et al., 2022) and DARTS (Liu et al.,
061   2018b). These ranking losses can be categorized into pointwise, pairwise, listwise, and weighted
062   ones, with five of them used in previous predictors and six implemented newly by ourselves. To
063   ensure a fair comparison between ranking losses, we employ a uniform predictor based on a graph
064   convolution network (GCN) encoder in our experiments. The ranking ability of different ranking
065   losses is assessed from three dimensions: training portion, test portion, and rank-based metrics.
066   We adopt Kendall Tau (Kendall, 1938) and top-centered metrics such as Rel@K and N@K to test
067   their overall ranking performance along with their performance in identifying top-ranked architec-
068   tures. Then, we evaluate the ability of each ranking loss to facilitate two predictor-based NAS
069
      frameworks: predictor-guided random search (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012) and predictor-guided evo-
      lutionary search (Real et al., 2019). The relationship between the performance of ranking loss on
070
      rank-based metrics and the performance of predictor-based methods is clearly reflected in our ex-
071
      perimental results.
072
073   Our findings indicate that the choice of ranking loss greatly affects the performance of predictors.
074   For example, ListMLE (Xia et al., 2008) achieves a Kendall Tau of 0.55 for the Autoencoder task
075
      in TransNAS-Bench-101-Micro while MSE loss performs poorly with a score of 0.13. We also
      change the loss function in more complicated predictors for a more suitable one and find significant
076
      improvements in ranking performance. In addition, we find that a ranking loss performing well on
077
      top-centered metrics is more likely to aid predictor-based NAS in discovering promising architec-
078   tures. In other words, we disprove the intuition that a predictor with high Kendall Tau is certain to
079   help find well-performing architectures in NAS experiments. We show that a high Kendall Tau only
080   indicates good overall ranking performance instead of a strong ability to recognize top-performing
081   architectures. The latter matters more for NAS because predictor-based NAS methods mainly focus
082   on discovering the optimal architecture in the search space.
083
      We summarize our main contributions as follows:
084
085          • We provide the first comprehensive study for a series of pointwise, pairwise, listwise, and
086            weighted ranking loss in performance predictors for NAS across various settings.
087          • We analyze our experimental results and find two key insights: (i) the importance of ranking
088            loss in performance predictors; (ii) the correlation between performance on top-centered
089            rank metrics and the quality of the architecture discovered by predictor-based NAS.
090          • We release a framework containing 11 ranking loss functions across 13 NAS tasks, which
091            can serve as recommendations for predictors under different conditions. Note that six of
092            them are adapted from applications in other domains.

094   2   R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Performance Predictor for NAS. Performance predictors have recently emerged to boost the evalu-
097   ation efficacy of NAS. Predictors can estimate the final performance of unseen architectures directly,
098   allowing NAS methods to navigate the search space more efficiently. To improve the accuracy and
099   generalization of predictors, many studies focus on extracting diverse features from architectures by
100   opting for complicated models(Wen et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2023), optimizing architecture encod-
101   ing(Ning et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2021) and incorporating tailored self-supervised tasks(Jing et al.,
102   2022; Zheng et al., 2024). However, the design of loss functions for performance predictors plays
103   an equally significant role but is still rarely explored.
104   Ranking Loss. Ranking losses, which stem from learning to rank in the field of information re-
105   trieval, can be generally categorized into three types: pointwise, pairwise, and listwise (Liu et al.,
106   2009). Pointwise methods treat the ranking problem as a regression task and directly predict the
107   exact relevance degree of each document. Pointwise ranking loss is optimized by minimizing the
      regret such as the square error (Cossock & Zhang, 2006) on the training data. Pairwise methods


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      are closer to the nature of ‘ranking’. They focus on the relative ranking for a pair of documents
109   and predict which one is preferred within each data pair. Aiming to reduce the number of incor-
110   rectly ranked pairs as much as possible, many pairwise methods are proposed based on support
111   vector machines (Herbrich et al., 2000; Joachims, 2002), boosting Freund et al. (2003), neural net-
112   works (Burges et al., 2005), and other machine learning models (Tsai et al., 2007; Zheng et al.,
113   2007). Also, MP loss (Cortes et al., 2007) is proposed to keep the magnitude of the labeled doc-
114   uments when ranking the document pairs. Listwise methods consider the entire set of documents
115   and ranked output a list that is most likely to be the ground truth. They can be roughly divided into
116   two sub-categories: measure-specific (Taylor et al., 2008; Chakrabarti et al., 2008) and measure-
117
      irrelevant (Xia et al., 2008; Cao et al., 2007) loss functions. Additionally, some weighted ranking
      losses (Burges et al., 2006; Weston et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2018) are proposed to better recognize
118
      the top-rank documents. Despite the success of the ranking loss, it was originally designed for infor-
119
      mation retrieval or other fields. The large difference between document label and architecture label
120   makes it nontrivial to adapt the ranking loss to the architecture ranking prediction task. To address
121   this, we implement several well-known ranking losses tailored for performance predictors.
122
123
      Intersection Between Ranking Loss and Predictors. Most existing performance predictors pri-
      marily utilize MSE loss, which belongs to pointwise ranking methods (Luo et al., 2018; Liu et al.,
124
      2021; Lu et al., 2023). However, predicting the relative ranking between architectures is more crit-
125
      ical and effective than estimating the absolute accuracy of architectures (Xu et al., 2021). Recent
126   predictors have introduced pairwise and listwise ranking loss functions to enhance their ranking abil-
127   ity. ReNAS (Xu et al., 2021) and FlowerFormer (Hwang et al., 2024) separately employ the hinge
128   ranking loss and the margin ranking loss to preserve the rankings between different architectures.
129   DCLP (Zheng et al., 2024) adopts ListMLE (Xia et al., 2008), a listwise method, to achieve better
130   overall ranking performance. Although ranking loss enables the predictors to rank architectures bet-
131   ter, no prior works conducted a comprehensive and fair comparison between them under consistent
132   settings to the best of our knowledge.

135
136
      In this section, we first introduce the basics of different categories of ranking loss functions. Then
137
      we discuss the evaluation metrics used to measure the ranking ability of performance predictors.
138   The detailed descriptions for 11 ranking loss functions are included in Appendix A.1.

      3.1   R ANKING LOSS F UNCTIONS
141
142
      In this paper, we compare four different categories of 11 ranking loss: pointwise ranking loss, pair-
143   wise ranking loss, listwise ranking loss and weighted ranking loss. Note that weighted ranking loss
144   is mostly developed from pairwise and listwise ones. Formally, given N architecture-performance
145   pairs {(xi , yi )}N                                                                                  N
                        i=1 , the predictor takes architectures as input and output predicted values {yˆi }i=1 .
146
      Pointwise Ranking Loss. MSE loss, the traditional option for performance predictors, is chosen to
147
      represent the pointwise ranking loss in our experiments. For each input architecture xi , the predictor
148
      aims to minimize the square loss (yi − yˆi )2 between predicted scores and ground truth. After that,
149   the architecture ranking is calculated based on the predicted scores.
150
151   Pairwise Ranking Loss. Four pairwise ranking loss functions are integrated into performance pre-
152
      dictors. They are hinge ranking (HR) loss, margin ranking (MR) loss, bayesian personalized ranking
      (BPR) loss (Rendle et al., 2009) and magnitude preserving (MP) loss (Cortes et al., 2007). For each
153
      input architecture pair (xi , xj ), the predictor is encouraged to predict relative ranking order cor-
154
      rectly. The overall ranking is based on the massive relative ranking pairs. Among them, MP loss is
155   special because it penalizes not only the mis-ranked pairs but also the correct ones if the magnitude
156   of the prediction value is large.
157
158
      Listwise Ranking Loss. ListNet (Cao et al., 2007) and ListMLE (Xia et al., 2008) losses are tested
      in our experiments. They optimize the entire architecture list {xi }N
                                                                          i=1 to approach the correct ranking
159
      order and directly output a predicted ranking list for architectures.
160
161   Weighted Ranking Loss. Four weighted ranking loss functions are employed in our experiments
      which assign more weights to the top-ranked architectures when computed. We first develop the HR


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      loss for two weighted variants (short for WHR 1 and WHR 2) using different assignment manners.
163   WHR 2 assigns more weights to the top-ranked architectures than WHR 1. Additionally, we intro-
164   duce the well-known weighted approximate-rank pairwise (WARP) (Weston et al., 2011) loss and
165   LambdaLoss (Wang et al., 2018) that assign weights in different manners.

167
168   3.2   R ANK - BASED M ETRICS
169
170   In this paper, we employ the following rank-based metrics to evaluate the predictors’ overall ranking
171   performance and performance in identifying top-performing architectures.
172   Kendall Tau (Kendall, 1938) (τ ). τ can reflect the ordinal association between a predicted score and
173   the absolute accuracy of architectures for each task. We have τ ∈ [−1, 1] and the ranking correlation
174   is stronger if τ is closer to 1. We opt for Kendall Tau as the representative ranking correlation metric
175   for the sake of its popularity in the field of performance predictors (Ning et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
176   2021; Lu et al., 2023; Hwang et al., 2024).
177
      Weighted Kendall Tau (Vigna, 2015) (τ w ). Compared to vanilla τ , τ w assigns weights to architec-
178   tures with top performance. There is a hyperbolic drop-off in architecture importance according to
179   the descending order of accuracy. Predictors will earn a higher τ w if the top-performing architec-
180   tures are better ranked.
181
      N@K. N@K denotes the true rank of the architecture that has the highest accuracy among the
182
      predicted top K ones. It is used to evaluate predictors because the relative rankings between archi-
183
      tectures with poor performance is of little importance (Ning et al., 2020). If a promising architecture
184   is predicted to top K, the performance in identifying top-performing architectures is good.
185
186   Rel@K. Distinguished from N@K, Rel@K is sensitive to the actual value of architecture. Rel@K
187
      computes the ratio of the accuracy of architecture AK to that of the best one Amax . We have
      Rel@K∈ (0, 1]. In view of its success in other ranking problems (Li et al., 2021), this metric is also
188
      suitable for evaluating the effectiveness of the predictor in NAS because the majority of previous
189
      works only report the accuracy of the best architectures instead of the rank (Wen et al., 2020; Liu
190   et al., 2021; Jing et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024).

      4     E MPIRICAL E VALUATION OF R ANKING L OSS F UNCTIONS
194
195
196   We divide our experiments into two parts: evaluating the performance of each ranking loss in a
197   variety of rank-based metrics (Section 4.1) and evaluating the effectiveness of predictor-based NAS
198
      methods (Section 4.2). We start by describing the experimental settings.
199   Experimental Settings. The experiments are conducted in five popular search spaces: NAS-Bench-
200   101 (Ying et al., 2019), NAS-Bench-201 (Dong & Yang, 2019), TransNAS-Bench-101 (Duan et al.,
201   2021), NAS-Bench-NLP (Klyuchnikov et al., 2022) and DARTS (Liu et al., 2018b). Note that
202   TransNAS-Bench-101 includes the macro and the micro search space. Most predictors are only
203   evaluated on the latter while our experiments include the results on both. Detailed information
204
      about these search spaces will be elaborated in Section B.1. Although performance predictors can
      benefit from ranking loss functions, their effectiveness is heavily dependent on model selection, data
205
      scale and hyperparameter tuning. To achieve a fair comparison, we opt for a uniform performance
206
      predictor which is composed of a four-layer GCN encoder and a three-layer MLP in our experiments.
207   The detailed hyperparameter configuration is included in Appendix B.2.

531
532
      In this paper, we provide the first comprehensive study for 11 ranking loss functions in performance
533
      predictors, including pointwise, pairwise, listwise and weighted ranking loss. We compare their per-
534   formance in ranking correlation metrics and top-centered metrics under various settings. Meanwhile,
535   we combine these ranking loss functions into predictor-based NAS methods to search for promising
536   architectures. We find ranking loss with excellent performance on top-centered rank metrics can
537   help find architectures with high quality. In addition, our experiments show that a well-designed
538   ranking loss can greatly improve the performance of existing predictors and predictor-based NAS
539   methods. In the future, we will study some complex ranking loss functions which are composed of
      several single losses to further enhance performance predictors.

      A.1   D ETAILED D ESCRIPTIONS OF R ANKING L OSS F UNCTIONS
719
720   Now we give detailed descriptions of 11 different ranking loss functions that we used.
721
722
      Mean Square Error (MSE). MSE loss is the most widely used loss function in existing perfor-
      mance predictors (Wen et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023). They first
723
      predict absolute architecture performance precisely and then rank architectures according to the
724
      predicted values. We directly use the official implementation from the Pytorch package.
725
726   Bayesian Personalized Ranking (BPR). BPR loss (Rendle et al., 2009) is very popular in the
727   field of recommendation system and was first combined with the performance predictor by GMAE-
728
      NAS (Jing et al., 2022). For each sample in a batch, BPR compares its predicted score with other
      samples which have higher true accuracy. BPR optimizes the relative ranking between architecture
729
      pairs, encouraging the predictor to capture the difference between high-rank and low-rank architec-
730
      tures for better ranking ability. We use the implementation from the open source of GMAE-NAS.
731
732   ListMLE. ListMLE loss (Xia et al., 2008) is a classic listwise ranking loss in information retrieval
733   and was introduced into performance predictor by DCLP (Zheng et al., 2024). ListMLE loss aims
734
      to maximize the likelihood of the correct architecture ranking, making the predictor better estimate
      the ranking order more accurately. We use the implementation from the open source of DCLP.
735
736   Margin Rank (MR). MR loss was used in FlowerFormer (Hwang et al., 2024) to optimize the
737   ranking of architecture pairs. It promotes correct ranking by comparing pairs of architecture scores
738   and maintaining a minimum margin between the high-rank architecture and the low-rank one. We
739   directly use the official implementation from the Pytorch package.
740   Hinge Rank (HR). HR loss can be categorized into pairwise ranking and was applied to perfor-
741   mance predictor by ReNAS (Xu et al., 2021) to predict relativistic architecture performance. A
742   hinge function is used to calculate the loss. The loss will be 0 only when the architecture pair is
743   correctly ranked and the score difference is within the margin. We use the implementation from the
744   open source of ReNAS.
745   Weighted Hinge Rank (WHR1 & WHR2). We design two versions of weighted hinge ranking loss
746   functions based on Hinge Rank by assigning more weights to architectures with better performance.
747   Given a pair of architecture scores si and sj , the loss function is calculated by LHR × wi × wj ,
748   where wi and wj are the weights for two architectures. For WHR1, the weight of each architecture
749   X is set to (N − rank(X) + 1)/N , where rank(X) denotes the true ranking of X. As for WHR2,
750   the weight of each architecture X is formulated as exp(sX ) − 1. WHR2 assigns a higher weight to
751   top-performing architectures compared to WHR1. We implement two WHR loss functions on the
752
      foundation of Hinge Rank.
753   ListNet. ListNet loss (Xia et al., 2008) is another classic listwise ranking loss that converts both
754   the predicted scores and true scores into probability distributions with the Softmax function and
755   calculates the cross-entropy between them to enhance the model’s ranking ability. We develop the
      original code of ListNet to fit the task of architecture performance.


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Weighted Approximate-Rank Pairwise (WARP). WARP loss (Weston et al., 2011) was originally
757   designed for image annotation. To calculate the loss, pairs of items are randomly sampled until a
758   wrongly ranked pair is found and the predicted scores of the two incorrect items. A weight will
759   be assigned to the loss according to the times it takes to find a wrongly ranked pair. Based on this
760   idea, we tailor the WARP loss for the performance predictor. For each architecture in the batch,
761   we randomly choose others to construct pairs until a pair is ranked incorrectly. If the incorrect pair
762   appears soon, we think the predictor is poor at ranking and will assign a large weight to this sample
763   when calculating the loss. Instead, if we need more samples to find a wrong pair, the performance of
764   the predictor is already good and the loss should be small. We borrow the core idea of the original
765
      WARP and implement a new version for architecture performance ranking.
766   Magnitude Preserving (MP). MP loss (Cortes et al., 2007) combines the idea of MSE loss and
767   pairwise ranking loss and preserves the magnitude of ground truth. MP loss aims to make the
768   difference between the predicted scores approach that between the true accuracy within each data
769   pair for better ranking ability. So the correctly ranked pairs will still be penalized. We develop the
770   original code of MP and apply it to predict architecture performance.
771   LambdaLoss. LambdaLoss (Wang et al., 2018) is a probabilistic framework for ranking metric
772   optimization advanced from the classic LambdaRank (Burges et al., 2006), which contains different
773   metric-driven loss functions for the model. To enhance the model’s ability to identify top-performing
774   architectures, we design the loss function based on the one that was originally used to optimize
775   the model performance in Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG), a top-rank centered
776   metric. We use the implementation from the AllRank framework (Pobrotyn et al., 2020).

780
781   Now we provide detailed algorithms of the two predictor-based NAS frameworks that we employed
782   in Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2.
783
784
785   Algorithm 1: Predictor-guided Random Search Framework
786   Initialize: Sample a few architectures randomly from search space S to construct an
787    initialization space S0 , train them for ground-truth performance and add them to history.
788   while |history| + |S0 | < N do
789       Train predictor using architectures in history ;
790       Sample M candidate architectures at random from S − history ;
791
          Utilize predictor to predict the performance of each candidate architecture ;
          Update history by adding architectures with top-K predicted values ;
792
      return Architecture A′ with best ground-truth performance in history
793

      Algorithm 2: Predictor-guided Evolutionary Search Framework
798
799
      Initialize : Sample a few architectures randomly from search space S to construct an
       initialization space S0 , train them for ground-truth performance and add them to history and
800
       population P .
801
      while |history| + |S0 | < N do
802       Train the predictor using architectures in history ;
803       Select T well-performing architectures in P through Binary Tournament Selection ;
804       Mutate the selected architectures and generate M candidate architectures ;
805       Utilize predictor to predict the performance of each candidate architecture ;
806       Update history and P by adding architectures with top-K predicted values ;
807       Remove the oldest architecture from P ;
808   return Architecture A′ with best ground-truth performance in history

      B.1     S EARCH S PACES
813
814   We discuss the search spaces we used in the following part.
815
      NAS-Bench-101. NAS-Bench-101 (Ying et al., 2019) is a cell-based search space composed of over
816
      423k architectures. An operation is represented by a node in the cell. Each architecture cell includes
817   at most seven nodes and nine edges. The search space provides the accuracy of architectures on the
818   CIFAR-10 dataset.
819
820
      NAS-Bench-201. NAS-Bench-201 (Dong & Yang, 2019) is also a cell-based search space which
      contains 15625 architectures. The operation type is represented by the edge on NAS-Bench-201.
821
      There exist four nodes and six edges in the architecture cell. NAS-Bench-201 provides the accuracy
822
      of architectures on the CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and ImageNet16-120 datasets.
823
824   TransNAS-Bench-101-Micro & Macro. TransNAS-Bench-101 (Duan et al., 2021) is composed of
825   two parts: a micro (cell-based) search space containing 4096 architectures and a macro (skeleton-
826
      based) search space containing 3256 architectures. The constitution of the architecture cell in the
      micro search space is the same as NAS-Bench-201. As for the macro space, we use the same
827
      encoding in Arch-Graph (Huang et al., 2022). The search space provides architecture performance
828
      across diverse tasks including segmentation, regression, pixel-level prediction and self-supervised
829   tasks. In our experiments, we test our methods on four tasks: Class-Object, Class-Scene, Jigsaw and
830   Autoencoder.
831
832
      NAS-Bench-NLP. NAS-Bench-NLP contains 14332 architectures and a cell-based search space.
      There are at most 24 nodes and 26 edges in each cell. Different from the other search spaces, this
833
      one aims to design well-preforming architectures for NLP tasks. NAS-Bench-NLP provides test
834
      results of the architectures which are trained on Penn Tree Bank dataset (Kombrink et al., 2011) for
835   50 epochs.
836
837   DARTS. DARTS (Liu et al., 2018b) is an open-domain cell-based search space without available
838
      architecture performance. It is the largest search space in our experiments which contains around
      1018 architectures. Each architecture consists of a normal cell and a reduction cell, which contains
839
      seven nodes and eight edges in a single cell.
