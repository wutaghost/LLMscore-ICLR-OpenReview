009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series foundation models (TSFMs) promise to be powerful tools for a wide
012            range of applications. However, their internal representations and learned con-
013            cepts are still not well understood. In this study, we investigate the structure
014            and redundancy of representations across various TSFMs, examining the self-
015            similarity of model layers within and across different model sizes. This analy-
016
               sis reveals block-like redundancy in the representations, which can be utilized for
               informed pruning to improve inference speed and efficiency. Additionally, we ex-
017
               plore the concepts learned by these models—such as periodicity and trends—and
018
               how these can be manipulated through latent space steering to influence model
019            behavior. Our experiments show that steering interventions can introduce new
020            features, e.g., adding periodicity or trends to signals that initially lacked them.
021            These findings underscore the value of representational analysis for optimizing
022            models and demonstrate how conceptual steering offers new possibilities for more
023            controlled and efficient time series analysis with TSFMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Foundation models have taken significant strides in modeling both textual (Brown et al., 2020)
028
      and visual (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) data, and have made complex language and image processing
029
      accessible to non-experts. These models are pre-trained on massive internet-scale datasets and can
030   be used to solve multiple tasks across a variety of domains, with little to no adaptation. Recently, a
031   growing body of work (Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023; Goswami et al., 2024; Rasul et al.,
032   2024; Das et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024; Ansari et al., 2024) has extended the benefits of this
033   paradigm to time series data, a modality prevalent in fields such as finance (Taylor, 2008), healthcare
034   (Goswami et al., 2021), and climate science (Schneider & Dickinson, 1974).
035
      Time series foundation models (TSFMs) have shown promising performance on multiple modeling
036   tasks such as forecasting, classification, anomaly detection, and imputation, across a wide range of
037   domains, and in settings with varying amounts of data and supervision. However, the underlying
038   mechanisms and learned representations of TSFMs remain largely unexplored. Little is known about
039   the characteristics of learned representations, the kinds of concepts that these models are learning,
040   and how can these concepts could be manipulated to influence model outputs. A deeper understand-
041   ing of the inner workings of TSFMs is key to enhancing their performance and trustworthiness. We
042   address these knowledge gaps, by systematically probing these models and intervening in them.
043   We begin by analyzing TSFMs from two complementary perspectives: (1) representational simi-
044   larity (Sec. 3.1), and conceptual understanding (Sec. 3.2). Our first set of experiments are aimed
045   at answering the fundamental question: Are two TSFMs learning the same thing? We assess this
046   using the similarity of representations learned by these models. The second set of experiments fo-
047   cus on identifying “which” human-interpretable concepts TSFMs learn, and “where” these concepts
048   emerge. Our results in Sec. 4 show find that TSFMs learn redundant representations, and intuitive
049   concepts such as base patterns (e.g., constant vs. sinusoidal waves), amplitudes, periodicity, and
050
      trends; often in specific layers and patches.
051   We leverage these insights to improve TSFMs and their trustworthiness in two ways: (1) In Sec. 3.1,
052   we exploit their redundant representations to perform layer-wise pruning, which reduces model
053   size, accelerates inference while preserving accuracy. (2) In the following Sec. 3.2, we steer model
      predictions along specific conceptual directions (e.g., introducing an upward trend in forecasts) using

      synthetic time series as a way to imbue domain expertise into model predictions without explicit
055   training. Our work is the first step towards understanding the inner workings of TSFMs and utilizing

      Time Series Foundation Models (TSFMs). TSFMs are versatile neural networks pre-trained on
      vast amounts of time series data, and have shown remarkable capabilities in producing accurate
063
      predictions even in zero-shot settings. Recently, a number of TSFMs have been proposed (Garza &
064
      Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024; Goswami et al.,
065   2024; Ansari et al., 2024; Ekambaram et al., 2024; Talukder et al., 2024). While most TSFMs are
066   based on variations of the Transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017), they exhibit notable differences
067   in tokenization strategies, pre-training datasets, and the specific tasks they are designed to address.
068   While our methods are broadly applicable to Transformer-based TSFMs, we will primarily focus
069   on analyzing MOMENT Goswami et al. (2024), Chronos Ansari et al. (2024), and MOIRAI Woo
070   et al. (2024), three representative TSFMs that are fully open-source and offer distinct approaches to
071   time series tokenization (patch vs. discrete), architecture (encoder-only vs. encoder-decoder), and
072   pre-training objectives (imputation vs. forecasting).
073   Analyzing Representations of Deep Learning Models. Deep learning models often operate as
074   black boxes, making their internal mechanisms and learned representations difficult to understand.
075   One approach to gaining insights into these models is by comparing their intermediate represen-
076   tations. Similarity metrics can be used to determine the similarity or dissimilarity of represen-
077   tations at different stages of a model, revealing the hierarchy, homogeneity, and redundancy of
078   learned features. Previous studies focused on quantifying the similarity of neural network repre-
079
      sentations Raghu et al. (2017); Kornblith et al. (2019). Raghu et al. (2021) demonstrated the use
      of these metrics for analyzing and comparing representations in vision transformers and CNNs,
080
      providing valuable insights into their functioning. Nguyen et al. (2021) investigated the impact of
081
      model size and training data ratios on similarity patterns by varying the depth and width of different
082   models. They also explored model pruning based on similarity. Building on these these studies, our
083   work presents the first comprehensive analysis of representations learned by TSFMs, offering useful
084   insights into their internal workings.
085
      Identifying and Manipulating Learned Concepts in Pre-trained Models. Understanding the in-
086
      ternal representations learned by pre-trained models has been an active area of research, particularly
087
      in the context of LLMs and vision models. Previous studies have explored whether individual neu-
088   rons or directions in a model’s latent space correspond to specific features or concepts (Dalvi et al.,
089   2019; Goh et al., 2021; Gurnee et al., 2023; Elhage et al., 2022). These investigations often focus
090   on identifying linear representations, where features are encoded as linear combinations of neuron
091   activations. Recent work has also employed various probing techniques to classify and interpret
092   these internal representations, addressing aspects such as truthfulness and model robustness (Azaria
093   & Mitchell, 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Burns et al., 2023; Marks & Tegmark, 2023). While many of
094   these studies rely on meticulously curated datasets to probe language and vision models, we demon-
095   strate that for time series models, synthetic data generated using simple mechanisms can effectively
096
      identify, localize, and probe the concepts learned by these models.

102   We study TSFMs using two complementary analysis and intervention methods. In Section 3.1, we
103   examine learned representations through the lens of similarity, uncovering the redundancy inherent
104   in TSFM representations. We leverage this redundancy to prune multiple layers of pre-trained mod-
105   els, thereby improving their efficiency without compromising accuracy. In Section 3.2, we identify
106   the specific concepts learned by TSFMs, localizing them to specific hidden states. Furthermore,
107   we explore the ability to steer model predictions along these conceptual directions, enabling us to
      influence model outputs in targeted ways.

290   Deriving Steering Matrices for Model Steering. Once we have identified that a feature is linearly
291   represented in the latent space of the M, we can use steering interventions to manipulate the latent
292   space and generate time series that reflect intended concepts. For instance, to introduce periodicity to
293   a constant time series, we can utilize a steering matrix S, as illustrated in Figure 2. By strategically
294   intervening in M using this steering matrix, we can bias its outputs towards predicting periodic
295   time series. To construct a steering matrix, we first derive steering vectors Si ∈ RN ×D , for each
296   layer i. These vectors represent the change that activations in layer i must undergo such that M
297
      produces periodic outputs. Si is simply the difference between the median activation matrix of
      the constant time series Mic , from that of sinusoids Mis . We stack these vectors for all layers to
298
      derive the steering matrix. This matrix allows us to simultaneously intervene across multiple tokens
299
      and layers during inference, which we found to be more effective than single-token interventions.
300   During inference, to steer model predictions, at each layer i, we update its hidden representation as
301   follows: hi ← hi + λSi , where λ ∈ R is a scalar that controls the strength of the intervention.

303   3.2.3     R ESEARCH Q UESTIONS AND E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
304
305   Through our experiments, we aim to answer the following research questions: (RQ4) Do TSFMs
306   represent concepts associated with specific data-generating functions distinctly in the latent space?
307
      (RQ5) Are these learned concepts localized to specific layers and tokens within TSFMs? (RQ6)
      Can we leverage these learned concepts to bias model predictions towards intended outcomes? For
308
      example, can we add periodicity or an upward trend to a constant time series? (RQ7) Is it possible
309
      to combine multiple steering interventions to manipulate model predictions towards complex com-
310   positions of various concepts? For instance, can we steer a model to add both trend and periodicity
311   to a constant signal?
312
313
      To address these research questions, we will leverage the techniques outlined in previous sections.
      We also explore two alternative modalities of intervention: (1) deriving steering vectors using the
314
      mean of hidden activations rather than the median, and (2) steering a single token versus all tokens
315
      throughout the model.

      TSFMs learn Intuitive Linear Concepts. Our concept localization results in Fig. 8 show that
      certain concepts represented by MOMENT-Large are linearly separable and that this separability
467
      is not consistent but rather emerges at specific layers in the model. We also found intuitive differ-
468
      ences in the locations where these concepts are learned. We observed that certain concepts, such
469   as distinguishing between constant and sinusoidal patterns, require careful examination of the entire
470   time series. In contrast, differentiating between increasing and decreasing trends can be achieved
471   by focusing on the initial and final patches. However, we did not identify specific locations where
472   models learn to distinguish between time series of different amplitudes. This may be attributed to
473   the normalization of input time series, a common practice in many TSFMs, including MOMENT.
474
475
476   We can effectively steer TSFM predictions. Our concept steering interventions effectively trans-
477
      form the latent space of TSFMs, resulting in model predictions that align with the intended concepts,
      as demonstrated in Fig. 9. We successfully introduced periodicity and trend concepts to constant
478
      time series and demonstrated the ability to combine multiple steering vectors to create more com-
479
      plex patterns. By combining steering vectors representing increasing trends and sinusoidal patterns,
480   we were able to steer model predictions towards a combination of these features. To evaluate the
481   effectiveness of steering in the latent space, we analyzed the impact of our interventions on hid-
482   den representation, by projecting in a two-dimensional space using Principal Component Analysis
483   (PCA). We found that steering in the latent space is reflected in these lower-dimensional represen-
484   tations, as illustrated in Fig. 12. Notably, the PCA reduction often captured the concept direction as
485   one of the principal components. This can be attributed to the careful design of our synthetic data
      generation process.

      Interestingly, the method of obtaining the steering matrix, either by computing the mean or median
487   across embedding concept classes, has no notable effect on the steered output as shown in Fig. 13.
488   However, applying concept steering interventions across all tokens is necessary to achieve the in-
489   tended steered concept output compared to applying concept steering interventions to a single token.
490   Moreover, the λ parameter can have considerable effect on steered output. For Chronos, steering
491   required tuning the parameter λ ≈ 0.1 for effective performance, whereas MOMENT maintained
492   effective steering with λ = 1.

      5   D ISCUSSION
496
497   We explored two complementary approaches to probing and intervening in TSFMs. We gained
498   valuable insights into their internal mechanisms and identified opportunities for improvement. For
499
      instance, our analysis revealed redundancy in learned representations. We leveraged this represen-
      tational redundancy, inherent to large over-parameterized TSFMs, to devise a simple block-wise
500
      pruning strategy. This strategy effectively reduced the size and computational cost of these models
501
      without compromising performance, demonstrating the potential for distilling smaller, more efficient
502   models from larger TSFMs.
503
504   We also explored ways to influence model predictions along conceptual directions using steering
505
      matrices. Concept steering has many practical applications, including the ability to correct predic-
      tion errors in pre-trained models that may arise from out-of-distribution inference, reflecting the
506
      effects of exogenous factors on model predictions or inducing prior knowledge not fully captured
507
      during training. Additionally, steering provides a method to reduce computational costs by min-
508   imizing the need for fine-tuning. It can also be used to introduce inductive biases from various
509   domains into the model. This is particularly valuable for pre-trained models that may lack exposure
510   to specific concepts due to limited or restricted training data. Such inductive biases can be useful
511   in domains like healthcare, where, e.g., knowledge of the excitatory or inhibitory effects of treat-
512   ments can guide pre-trained model predictions about whether a patient’s vital signs should increase
513   or decrease in response to specific interventions. Moreover, concept steering can be used for data
514   generation, improving data augmentation techniques and creating more diverse datasets to imxprove
515   TSFM performance across various tasks. Prior work has already shown that data augmentation tech-
516
      niques can improve TSFM model generalization by enhancing model robustness and exposing it to
      a wider variety of patterns (Ansari et al., 2024).
517
518   Finally, our findings also underscore the importance of synthetic data in studying and steering
519   TSFMs. As opposed to meticulously curated datasets used to probe large language and vision mod-
520   els, we demonstrate that for time series models, synthetic data generated using simple mechanisms
521   can effectively identify, localize, and probe the linear concepts learned by these models.
522
523   Limitations and Future Work. This paper provides insights into how a few basic patterns are
524   linearly represented in time series foundation models. Future work must evaluate whether time
525   series foundation models can learn more complex patterns present in real-world time series and
526   whether steering matrices estimated using synthetic data can be used to steer predictions of out-
527
      of-distribution, real-world time series. While our methods are broadly applicable to different
      transformer-based foundation models, future research should explore other architectures such as
528
      state space models (Gu & Dao, 2023) and stacked multi-layer perceptrons (Ekambaram et al., 2024).
529
      Moreover, future studies should evaluate whether our findings hold for other time series foundation
530   models and tasks such as anomaly detection, and classification. Beyond time series, we hope that
531   our work inspires the use of synthetic data to steer large language and vision models as well.
532
533
534   E THICS S TATEMENT
535
536   While our pruned TSFMs demonstrate promising performance, it is crucial to exercise caution when
537   using them, especially in high-stakes applications such as healthcare. Before deploying these mod-
538   els for critical decision-making, we strongly recommend fine-tuning and evaluating them on task-
539   specific, in-domain datasets using relevant metrics. The ability to steer model predictions offers
      numerous benefits but also raises concerns regarding potential biases. We urge users to exercise

541   cations of manipulating model outputs.

794   The term “block” refers to groups of consecutive layers within a transformer that exhibit high rep-
795   resentational similarity. Consistent with prior work Phang et al. (2021), we use “layer” to refer to
796   individual transformer encoder or decoder blocks consistent with prior work, while ”block” refers to
797   a higher-level structure made up of multiple such layers that share similar representations Nguyen
798   et al. (2021).
799   As an example, consider Figure 1 which illustrates the pairwise similarity between the 24 layers of
800   MOMENT-Large. In this figure, lighter colors (yellow) represent higher representational similarity,
801   as measured by Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) Kornblith et al. (2019). The identified blocks are
802   outlined with red bounding boxes. For example, Block 1 comprises layers 1–5, Block 2 comprises
803   layers 9–18, and Block 3 comprises layers 19–23.
804   Our pruning algorithm is summarized in Algorithm 1. Our pruning method involves retaining the
805   first and last layers of each block while zeroing out the weights of the intermediate layers. This
806   approach preserves the structural integrity of the block while leveraging the skip connections within
807   transformer blocks to ensure that signals and gradients continue to flow through the network. In the
808   case of MOMENT-Large’s Block 3, composed of layers 19–23, this means that layers 19 and 23
809   are retained, while the weights of layers 20, 21, and 22 are zeroed out. We have added a dedicated
      section on pruning in Appendix C to further clarify this process.

890   D     O N S TEERING AND THE PARAMETER λ
891
892   Below we provide some guidance on selecting good values of λ and insights into its properties:
893
      Selection and Impact of the Steering Strength Parameter λ
894
895          • Optimal Range: Based on our empirical experiments, we found that the steering strength
896            parameter λ is most effective for interventions when its value lies within the interval
897            [0.1, 2.0].
898          • Lower Bound Considerations: Values of λ < 0.1 often result in insufficient perturbation
899            of the activation patterns, leading to suboptimal intervention effects that may not manifest
900            visibly in the model’s output.
901          • Upper Bound Effects: Setting λ > 2.0 induces excessive perturbations that push acti-
902            vations beyond their typical distribution bounds, potentially resulting in degenerate or se-
903            mantically meaningless outputs. In the PCA/latent space visualizations, these cases simply
904            appear as more distant points along the steering direction.
905
906   Directional Properties
907
             • Reversibility: Multiplying the steering vector by -1 effectively reverses the direction of
908
               intervention, enabling bidirectional control (e.g., transforming concept A → B into B →
909
               A).
910
911
             • Example application: For a steering vector trained to increase signal magnitude, apply-
               ing its negative counterpart (-λS) produces controlled signal decrease, demonstrating the
912
               symmetric nature of the steering operation.
913
914   Practical Guidelines
915
916          • Initial Calibration: We recommend starting with λ = 1.0 and adjusting based on the
917            observed intervention strength. In most cases value λ = 1.0 works well and does not need
               tuning.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
             • Task and Model-Specific Tuning: If λ = 1.0 does not yield satisfactory results, the op-
919            timal value requires tuning based on both the specific steering objective and target model,
920            necessitating empirical calibration to achieve the desired intervention strength.
921
             • Monitoring: When applying steering interventions, practitioners should monitor both the
922
               immediate output and latent space representations to ensure meaningful transformations
923            while maintaining output coherence.
