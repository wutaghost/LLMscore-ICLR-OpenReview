009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Evaluating large vision-language models (LVLMs) is very expensive, due to the
012            high computational costs and the wide variety of tasks. The good news is that if
013            we already have some observed scores, we may be able to infer unknown ones.
014            In this study, we propose a new framework for predicting unknown performance
015            scores based on observed ones from other LVLMs or tasks. We first formulate the
016
               performance prediction as a matrix completion task. Specifically, we construct a
               sparse performance matrix R, where each entry Rmn represents the performance
017
               score of the m-th model on the n-th dataset. By applying probabilistic matrix
018
               factorization (PMF) with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), we can complete
019            the performance matrix, that is, predict unknown scores. Additionally, we esti-
020            mate the uncertainty of performance prediction based on MCMC. Practitioners
021            can evaluate their models on untested tasks with higher uncertainty first, quickly
022            reducing errors in performance prediction. We further introduce several improve-
023            ments to enhance PMF for scenarios with sparse observed performance scores.
024            In experiments, we systematically evaluate 108 LVLMs on 176 datasets from 36
025            benchmarks, constructing training and testing sets for validating our framework.
026            Our experiments demonstrate the accuracy of PMF in predicting unknown scores,
027
               the reliability of uncertainty estimates in ordering evaluations, and the effective-
               ness of our enhancements for handling sparse data.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   It is expensive to evaluate large vision-language models (LVLMs). First, large-scale models result
033   in significant computational or API calling costs and memory usage. Additionally, since a single
034   LVLM can handle a wide range of tasks, comprehensively understanding model performance on
035   different tasks becomes more challenging. As a result, hundreds of benchmarks have been proposed
036   to assess the strengths and weaknesses of LVLMs (Li & Lu, 2024). Zhang et al. (2024b) report that
037   it takes hundreds of hours to evaluate one model on around 50 tasks in LMMs-Eval, and evaluation
038
      even exceeds 1,400 hours on models of 100B parameters or more.
039   Fortunately, we have already observed performance scores from some of these models on some
040   tasks, for instance, from the official reports of released models and datasets. For new models, scores
041   can also be readily obtained with limited compute by running on a small number of tasks. If these
042   observed scores can be used to predict unknown ones, we could avoid unnecessary evaluations and
043   effectively reduce costs. Recent works (Polo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b) require running the
044
      same model on the same task to predict model performance, and most of them ignore the potential
      of leveraging observed performance data from other models or tasks.
045
046   In this study, we propose a new framework for predicting unknown performance scores based on
047   observed ones from other LVLMs or tasks. We first formulate this as a matrix completion prob-
048   lem. Specifically, we construct a sparse performance matrix R where each entry Rmn represents
049   the performance score of the m-th model on the n-th dataset. By applying probabilistic matrix fac-
050
      torization (PMF) with Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), we can predict unknown performance
      scores based on observed entries in the matrix. A summary of the framework is shown in Fig. 1.
051
052   A bonus of our framework is active evaluation, which aims to select a subset of model-dataset pairs
053   to evaluate in order to minimize prediction errors across the entire performance matrix. Given a
      PMF model on a very sparse performance matrix, we calculate prediction uncertainty from MCMC

073   A challenge is that PMF tends to predict the average score for models and datasets with very few
074   observed scores, resulting in poor prediction results (Mnih & Salakhutdinov, 2007). To address this,

078   & Mnih, 2008) with an LKJ prior (Lewandowski et al., 2009) on the variance. Third, we also

080   example, if we know a model uses CLIP as a vision encoder, the information may help predict the
081
      model’s performance, especially when we observe only a few performance scores of the model.
082   In experiments, we conduct a systematic evaluation of 108 LVLMs across 176 distinct datasets de-
083   rived from 36 existing benchmarks, based on four prior works (Duan et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
084   2024b; Liang et al., 2024; Karamcheti et al., 2024). We evaluate open-source models such as
085   LLaVA-v1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023), mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2023), and
086   MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), as well as closed-source models including GPT-4o, GPT-4 (Achiam
087

      knowledge-dense VQA (Yue et al., 2024), hallucination (Li et al., 2023b), medicine (He et al., 2020),
088
      emotion recognition (Goodfellow et al., 2013), and others. To reduce computational and API costs,
089
      we subsample some datasets, following the practice in Liang et al. (2024).
090
091   Using the results from 108 LVLMs across 176 datasets, we construct a 108 × 176 performance ma-
092   trix, with some entries masked for testing. We empirically demonstrate that PMF accurately predicts
093
      masked scores and consistently outperforms baselines as long as more than 10% entries in the per-
      formance matrix are observed. We also show that selecting high-uncertainty model-dataset pairs for
094
      evaluation significantly reduces prediction errors compared to random selection. Additionally, our
095
      improvements effectively alleviate the sparse data issue of PMF.
096
097   In summary, this paper covers three main points. First, we formulate a problem of predicting the
098   unknown performance of LVLMs across tasks. Second, we apply the well-established PMF algo-
099
      rithm to this problem, show the application of active evaluation, and propose several strategies to
      mitigate the sparse data issue. Third, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of 108 LVLMs across
100
      176 datasets, constructing training and testing sets for further experiments.

123   2.2   I MPROVE E VALUATION E FFICIENCY
124
125   Recent works introduce unified frameworks to assess models across multiple benchmarks using a
126   single codebase, such as VLMEvalKit (Duan et al., 2024), LMMs-Eval (Zhang et al., 2024b), and
127   HEMM (Liang et al., 2024). Our study builds on these efforts by consolidating their evaluation
128
      frameworks and integrating models in Prismatic VLMs series.
129   Predicting unknown model performance can reduce the evaluation cost. Recent works select a core-
130   set of samples from a large benchmark, for evaluating LLMs (Polo et al., 2024; Perlitz et al., 2023)
131   and LVLMs (Zhang et al., 2024b; Zhu et al., 2024). The performance of a specific model on the core-
132   set is used to estimate its performance on the full benchmark. Besides, prior studies estimate model
133   performance on an unlabeled test set based on distribution shift (Deng & Zheng, 2021), confidence
134
      scores (Guillory et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024), or LLM feedback (Zheng et al., 2023). Instead of
      running models on a coreset or an unlabeled set, our framework predicts unknown performance by
135
      utilizing the correlation between model performances across benchmarks.
136
137   Another related direction is adaptive testing (Rodriguez et al., 2021; Prabhu et al., 2024). Given a
138   new model, only a subset of samples is selected based on sample difficulty for evaluating the new
139   model. While their work focuses on sample-level testing with a single metric, our approach operates
140
      at the dataset level, using six different metrics. Furthermore, instead of relying on statistically
      inferred sample difficulty, we propose a method to rank model-dataset pairs for evaluation based on
141
      uncertainty in performance prediction from MCMC.

144   2.3   P ROBABILISTIC M ATRIX FACTORIZATION
145
      PMF (Mnih & Salakhutdinov, 2007) is a technique widely applied in recommender systems. Given
146
      part of the ratings that users provide for items, the goal is to model the observed ratings and predict
147
      the missing ones. PMF achieves this by decomposing the observed rating matrix into two lower-
148   dimensional matrices, representing the latent features of users and items. A rating is modeled as a
149   Gaussian distribution centered around the dot product of the user’s and item’s feature vectors.
150
151
      One major challenge with PMF is that, if users rate very few items, their predicted ratings will be
      near the average for those items. Bayesian PMF (BPMF) (Salakhutdinov & Mnih, 2008) addresses
152
      this by placing distributions over the priors of the latent user and item features, making it more
153
      effective in handling sparse data. Additionally, Constrained PMF (Mnih & Salakhutdinov, 2007)
154   introduces a latent similarity constraint matrix to further refine the user feature vectors.

      3.1      R EVISIT P ROBABILISTIC M ATRIX FACTORIZATION
183
184   Let R be an M × N matrix representing model performance scores on datasets, where M is the
185   number of models and N is the number of datasets. For simplicity, we initially assume a single
186   performance metric, though in reality, benchmarks often employ multiple metrics. In such cases, R
187   becomes an M × N × S tensor, where S represents the total number of metrics. We will address
188   this more complex scenario in the following sections.
189   In practice, only a subset of the elements in R are observed, meaning we evaluate only a portion
190   of the model-dataset pairs and aim to estimate the remaining performance scores. Specifically, we
191   define a matrix O ∈ {0, 1}M ×N , where Omn = 1 if Rmn is observed, and 0 otherwise.
192
      To model the observed matrix and estimate the unknown values, we employ PMF (Mnih & Salakhut-
193
      dinov, 2007), as illustrated by the probabilistic graphical model in Fig. 2(A). PMF decomposes R
194
      into two low-dimensional matrices, U ∈ RM ×D and V ∈ RN ×D , where D is the latent dimen-
195   sion. Here, Um,: and Vn,: are the latent feature vectors for the m-th model and the n-th dataset,
196   respectively, and we refer to them as Um and Vn . These latent vectors are modeled as multivari-
197   ate Gaussian distributions, and the observed ratings are assumed to follow a Gaussian distribution
198   centered at the dot product of the latent feature vectors:

      3.2   ACTIVE E VALUATION
217
218   MCMC allows us to estimate score distributions and readily obtain uncertainty estimates for each
219   unknown score, enabling us to prioritize evaluation experiments. For example, if we are uncertain
220   about GPT-4’s performance on a 3D understanding but confident about LLaVA’s performance on
221   object recognition, we can prioritize evaluating GPT-4 on the 3D task when our resources are limited.
222   In our method, we begin by applying PMF to model a sparse performance matrix. Using MCMC,
223   we get hundreds of estimations of each unknown score and calculate the standard deviation of es-
224   timations as a measure of uncertainty. The unobserved scores are ranked by their uncertainties.
225   High-uncertainty scores are replaced with ground truth, simulating evaluation process in practice.
226   We rerun PMF with updated observed data, calculate uncertainty, and determine the next set of eval-
227   uations. This process is repeated until our resource budget is exhausted or all scores are observed.
228
229   3.3   M ULTIPLE M ETRICS
230
231
      Previously, we assumed that each dataset has only one scoring metric, but this is not the case in
      practice. For example, yes-or-no questions can be evaluated using accuracy, precision, recall, and
232
      F1 score, while open-ended questions may use metrics like BART score (Yuan et al., 2021) and
233
      BERT score (Zhang et al., 2019). Model performances are represented by a tensor R ∈ RM ×N ×S ,
234   where S is the total number of metrics. Empirically, we find that using PMF to model and predict
235   each metric independently works well when sufficient data is available. However, when observed
236   data is sparse, incorporating relationships between metrics will be helpful.
237
      To address this, we extend our PMF model into a simple Probabilistic Tensor Factorization (PTF),
238
      where we decompose the 3D tensor R into the product of two low-rank matrices and a 1D vector.

      3.5   M ODEL AND DATASET P ROFILES
271
272   The final enhancement to our framework is the incorporation of additional information about the
273   models and datasets. For example, knowing that two LVLMs use CLIP as the vision encoder, or that
274   LLaVA-v1.5 and LLaVA-NeXT are developed by the same team, suggests potential relationships in
275   their performances. Inspired by Constrained PMF (Mnih & Salakhutdinov, 2007), we incorporate
276   extra information as model and dataset profiles, to improve performance prediction.
277   Let H ∈ RM ×K and G ∈ RN ×J represent the model and dataset profiles, where Hm,: encodes
278   K properties of the m-th model (e.g., vision encoder type), and Gn,: encodes J properties of the
279   n-th dataset. We introduce Gaussian-distributed variables Y ∈ RK×D and X ∈ RJ×D to learn the
280   effects of these profiles. The latent feature vectors are now the sum of the original vectors and the
281   profile features, following Constrained PMF (Mnih & Salakhutdinov, 2007).

288   Oracle Profiles. To explore the upper bound of model and dataset similarities, we use the full R
289   matrix to cluster models and datasets. For each model, we take Ri,: (its performance across all
290   datasets) as a vector and apply the K-Means algorithm to cluster all models. We select the optimal
291   number of clusters using the elbow method. Similarly, for each dataset, we cluster R:,j in the same
292   way. We convert the cluster assignments into one-hot vectors to serve as profiles.
293   Custom Profiles. Since oracle profiles rely on complete performance data, they are not practical
294   for real-world use. To overcome this, we define custom profiles that can be applied in practice. For
295   models, we include features such as the number of parameters in the LLM backbone, vision encoder
296   type (one-hot), and the LVLM family (one-hot), illustrated in the supplementary material (Table 4).
297   Additionally, we cluster datasets based on latent representations obtained from various models and
298   get one-hot encoded dataset profiles. We explore three different approaches to generate these latent

      D2. using CLIP to encode images and BGE-M3 to encode questions in a dataset (following Zhang
      et al. (2024b)), then averaging the embeddings on the dataset; and D3. using LLaVA-7B to encode
301
      both images and text, then averaging the embeddings for the dataset.

      4.1   E VALUATING M ODELS ON B ENCHMARKS
310
      Prior works have developed general pipelines for evaluating LVLMs across a wide range of bench-
311
      marks (Duan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Liang et al., 2024). Building on these code repos-
312   itories, we evaluate 108 LVLMs on 36 benchmarks. The open-source models we cover include
313   LLaVA-v1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a), LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2023a), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023),
314   mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2023), and Prismatic VLMs (Karamcheti et al., 2024). We also evaluate
315   closed-source models such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Gemini-1.5 (Reid et al., 2024).
316
      The benchmarks span a variety of domains, including general VQA (SEED-2), knowledge-dense
317
      VQA (MMMU), hallucination (POPE), medical question answering (PathVQA), and emotion recog-
318   nition (FaceEmotion). Some large-scale benchmarks, such as SEED-2 (Li et al., 2023a) and
319   MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), cover multiple tasks. To conduct a fine-grain analysis, we split these
320   benchmarks into task-specific datasets, resulting in 176 datasets in total. Following HEMM (Liang
321   et al., 2024), we subsample some datasets to reduce computational and API calling costs of LVLMs.
322   For each dataset, we calculate a main metric for PMF (either accuracy or BARTScore), and several
323   other metrics, leading to a total of six metrics for PTF modeling. Full details of datasets and models
      are provided in the supplementary material (Section A).

      4.2   E STIMATING U NKNOWN P ERFORMANCES
345
346   We mask P % of the elements in the score matrix R, use the observed portion to normalize R,
347   and train the PMF model using MCMC sampling. The model reconstructs the matrix R̂, and we
348   evaluate the performance by comparing the estimated values with the ground truth for the masked
349   elements. For MCMC, we employ the NUTS sampling method, tuning with 500 samples in the
350   burn-in stage and drawing 100 samples. Empirical results show that 100 samples are sufficient for
351   stable estimation. The reconstructed matrix R̂ is taken as the mean prediction from MCMC.
352
      We use Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) as the primary metric to evaluate PMF performance.
353
      Additional metrics such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and the coefficient of determination (R2 )
354   are reported in the supplementary material (Section B).
355
356   We compare our method against two baselines: (1) Global Mean: predicting the global mean for un-
357
      observed scores; (2) Mean of Means: for each unobserved score, we average the mean performance
      of the model, the mean performance on the dataset, and the global mean.
358
359   Results. As shown in Fig. 3(A-C), PMF significantly outperforms the baselines when the test ratio is
360   lower than 90%. This suggests that when only a portion of the scores is available, PMF can infer the
361   unobserved scores with high accuracy. Additionally, as demonstrated in Fig. 3(D-F), the estimated
362   scores strongly correlate with the actual scores.
363   However, as the amount of observed data decreases, PMF’s performance declines as can be expected.
364   In extreme cases where the test ratio exceeds 90%, with limited information about model or dataset
365   performance, PMF can perform worse than predicting the means. We will address this issue in the
366   following sections with our proposed enhancement techniques.

      4.4                              E NHANCING PMF
407
408   We apply three enhancement techniques to our PMF model and evaluate their effectiveness across
409   different test ratios. To minimize experimental variance, we perform each experiment 10 times with
410   different random seeds and report the average performance at each test ratio.
411
      Results. As seen in Table 1, the multi-score method PTF can get better performance when the matrix
412   is very sparse. When there is enough data, separately modeling PMF with each score works very
413   well and is comparable to PTF. For BART and BERT scores, PMF even outperforms PTF. This is
414   likely because PTF assumes a linear relationship between scores. When this assumption does not
415   hold, such as in the case of BART and BERT scores, it can negatively impact model performance.
416   When the test ratio is high, PTF demonstrates better performance.
417
      Fig. 5 illustrates the impact of the other two enhancement techniques. As shown, Bayesian PTF
418   offers only negligible improvements over standard PTF when there is enough observed data, but it
419   is particularly beneficial in sparse conditions. In Fig. 5(B), our custom profiles also show improve-
420   ments when data is limited, though there remains a gap between our custom profiles and the oracle
421   profiles. Additionally, Fig. 5(C) highlights that adding profiles not only enhances PTF’s overall per-
422   formance but also reduces instability, as seen by smaller error bars. Model profiles show significant
423   performance gains, whereas dataset profiles contribute only marginally. Better methods for encoding
424   and utilizing dataset information need further exploration.

      6      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      In this study, we evaluate 108 models on 176 datasets across 36 benchmarks. Our framework esti-
532
      mates unknown LVLM performances across tasks using PMF, prioritizes evaluations based on un-
533
      certainty, and introduce some enhancements to address the sparse data issue. Our study could lead
534   to significant savings in development time and computation costs. We highlight several limitations.
535   First, recent advances show that in-context learning or generating multiple responses can improve
536   LVLM performance on the same dataset. Modeling these different evaluation settings (e.g., 5-shot)
537   could extend our framework. Second, some model-dataset pairs with high uncertainties might offer
538   limited value for improving performance prediction on other datasets, so better heuristics for active
539   evaluation could be developed. Third, our method cannot answer what new benchmarks are needed,
      which we believe is an interesting future direction.

      We provide a comprehensive overview of the datasets and LVLMs used in our study. Detailed dataset
