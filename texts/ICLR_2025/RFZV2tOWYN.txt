009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models, widely used for image and video generation, face a significant
012            limitation: the risk of memorizing and reproducing training data during inference,
013            potentially generating unauthorized copyrighted content. While prior research has
014            focused on image diffusion models (IDMs), video diffusion models (VDMs) remain
015            underexplored. To address this, we introduce new metrics specifically designed to
016            separately assess content and motion memorization in VDMs. By applying these
017            metrics, we systematically analyze memorization in various pretrained VDMs,
018
               including text-conditional and unconditional models on various datasets, revealing
               that memorization is widespread across both video and image datasets. Finally, we
019
               propose effective detection strategies for both content and motion memorization,
020
               offering a foundational approach for improving privacy in VDMs.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Following the surge in popularity of image diffusion models (IDMs), which surpassed GANs (Good-
026   fellow et al., 2014) in image synthesis quality (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021), video diffusion models
027   (VDMs) (OpenAI, 2024; Runway, 2024) have emerged as a significant advancement and attracted a
028   lot of attention in both the research community and public attention due to their ability to generate
029   novel and realistic videos. However, a critical limitation has been identified in IDMs that raises
030   questions about the novelty of their outputs, where training images can be extracted by a pretrained
031   IDM and regurgitated during generation (Carlini et al., 2023). Such memorized outputs have been
032   criticized as “digital forgery”(Somepalli et al., 2023a), and several lawsuits(Saveri & Matthew, 2023)
033
      have been filed against companies for generating copyrighted artworks with these models.
034   Significant research has been conducted to understand and mitigate this memorization issue, iden-
035   tifying causes (Somepalli et al., 2023b) and proposing remedies (Daras et al., 2023; Wen et al.,
036   2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Ren et al., 2024). These efforts have achieved notable success in IDMs.
037   However, there is a lack of thorough investigation into memorization in VDMs, particularly in the
038   influential text-conditional (T2V) models. The investigation of memorization in VDMs is arguably
039
      even more critical than in IDMs, as videos consist of multiple temporally consistent frames. This
      adds an additional dimension to consider, and alongside content memorization, motion memorization
040
      also needs to be addressed. Motion patterns are highly distinctive, much like biometrics, and their
041
      memorization raises significant privacy concerns. Previous work (Kale et al., 2004; Klempous, 2009;
042   Nixon et al., 2010) has shown that unique physical traits, such as a person’s gait, are sufficient for
043   identification. Additionally, recent studies (Nair et al., 2023a;b) demonstrate that motion data can
044   be used to identify individuals in virtual reality (VR) environments. As a result, addressing motion
045   memorization is just as crucial as tackling content memorization, especially given the growing use
046   of VDMs and their broad range of downstream applications. A recent study (Rahman et al., 2024)
047   attempted to explore memorization in VDMs. However, its definitions and evaluations of both content
048   and motion memorization are narrow in scope and lack broad applicability, which has hindered its
049   wider adoption in the research community.
050   To address these limitations, we have made the following key contributions:
051
052         1. Redefinition of content and motion memorization (Sec. 2). We propose more practical
053            definitions for both content and motion memorization with a focus on privacy preservation.
               Specifically, we redefine content memorization as a frame-level phenomenon independent

      2     D EFINING AND MEASURING VIDEO MEMORIZATION
109
110
      Previous work (Rahman et al., 2024) highlights the need to analyze motion memorization in video
111   diffusion models (VDMs) in addition to content memorization, which has been well-studied in
112   image diffusion models (IDMs). They proposed two memorization definitions: content and motion
113   memorization and motion memorization, along with evaluation methods. However, we identify
114   several limitations in their definitions and metrics that may hinder broader adoption. To address this,
115   we propose more practical definitions and more generalized, disentangled metrics for evaluating
116   memorization in VDMs.
117
118   2.1     C ONTENT MEMORIZATION
119
120   2.1.1     R ESEARCH GAP
121   Content memorization has been extensively studied in IDMs, where it is defined as ‘reconstructive
122   memory’, which refers to the reproduction of an object that appears identically in a training image,
123   neglecting minor variations in appearance that could result from data augmentation, with allowances
124   for transformations like shifting, scaling, or cropping (Somepalli et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024a).
125   SSCD (Pizzi et al., 2022) is the standard pre-trained model used to extract embeddings from images,
126   and cosine similarity between SSCD embeddings quantifies content memorization.
127   The only work that has investigated content memorization in VDMs (Rahman et al., 2024), however,
128   does not separately define content memorization as a disentangled definition. Instead, it combines
129   content and motion memorization into a single definition, where a generated video is considered
130   memorized if every frame perfectly replicates a training video in both content and motion. They cor-
131   respondingly measure such content and motion memorization by adapting SSCD in a corresponding
132   way, where they compute SSCD embeddings for each frame, concatenate them, and then calculate
133   the cosine similarity of the resulting vectors, termed Video SSCD (VSSCD):
134

138   embedding for a frame.
139
140   However, this approach has several limitations:
141
              1. No definition or metric for content-only memorization. The current definition and
142
                 VSSCD metric overlook cases where content is memorized, but the motion is not, narrowing
143
                 the range of possible use cases and leading to incomplete evaluations.
144
145
              2. Lack of frame-level memorization detection. Privacy concerns arise if even a single frame
                 of a generated video memorizes a training frame. Current methods only capture video-level
146
                 memorization and miss the more practical frame-level risks.
147
148           3. No assessment of image training dataset memorization. Many VDMs, like those built on
149
                 IDMs such as Stable Diffusion, are pretrained on large-scale image datasets. This creates
                 a risk of generated videos memorizing these image datasets, yet the current definitions
150
                 and metrics only consider video-to-video comparisons. We argue for a more generalized
151
                 approach that unifies memorization assessments across both video and image domains.
152
153
              4. No quantitative evaluation of the metric. Rahman et al. (2024) only demonstrates the
                 effectiveness of VSSCD by showing limited true positive examples with high VSSCD scores.
154
                 However, it has not been evaluated using standard classification measures such as F1-score &
155
                 AUC. Our analysis reveals many failure cases, motivating the need for an improved metric.

160   pendent of motion memorization. A generated video is considered memorized if any single frame
161   replicates a training video frame or a training image (in the case of single-frame data), following the
      rationale of ‘reconstructive memory’ defined in image memorization studies.

191   2.2     M OTION MEMORIZATION
192
193   2.2.1      R ESEARCH GAP
194
      Motion memorization is not applicable for image generations and has not been extensively explored in
195   video diffusion models (VDMs), and has only been indirectly studied by Rahman et al. (2024). Their
196   approach focuses narrowly on image-conditional video generation (I2V), where they define motion
197   memorization as the inability of the model to generate novel motion patterns when conditioned
198   on augmented versions of an initial training video frame. The rationale behind their method is
199   that a VDM capable of understanding motion should produce consistent high-fidelity videos when
200   conditioned on both the original and augmented initial frames (e.g., flipping, cropping).
201   Instead of using a direct metric to evaluate motion memorization, they propose an indirect approach:
202   comparing the fidelity of generated videos when conditioned on the original and varied versions of the
203   initial frame. They found that conditioning on the original frame resulted in high-fidelity outputs due
204   to perfect memorization of both content and motion, while conditioning on variants led to artifacts
205   attributed to the model’s memorization of motion patterns from the training videos.
206
      However, this definition and evaluation have several limitations:
207
208           1. Limited applicability: The method applies only to I2V scenarios and cannot be extended
209              to other types of generations, such as unconditional or text-conditional.
210           2. Ambiguity in definition and evaluation: The reliance on fidelity as a proxy for motion
211              memorization introduces ambiguity, as high fidelity can also result from content memoriza-
212              tion or other factors unrelated to motion. This approach fails to isolate the contribution of
213              memorized motion patterns and draw reliable conclusions about motion memorization.
214           3. Inefficiency: Their method is resource-intensive, requiring numerous video generations
215              to compute the FVD for fidelity assessment. The lack of a one-to-one similarity metric
                 between individual generation-training pairs further reduces practicality and efficiency.

      2.2.2     P ROPOSED DEFINITION AND METRIC
248
249   To address these shortcomings, we propose a more general and direct definition of motion memoriza-
250   tion that applies to all types of video generations, not just I2V. Our approach evaluates motion alone,
251   disentangling it from other elements like content or fidelity. We define motion memorization as the
252   occurrence of high similarity in the optical flow of consecutive frames between a generated and a
253   training video. Crucially, we disregard natural motions, such as camera panning or static frames, that
254   pose no privacy risks. To formalize this, we introduce a novel metric called Optical Flow Similarity
255

281   2.2.3     NATURAL M OTION F ILTERING (NMF)
282
283   To further refine our approach, we introduce Natural Motion Filtering (NMF) to filter out motions
284   that should not be classified as memorized due to natural occurrence. NMF targets two specific cases:
285
              1. Camera panning: This effect involves uniform motion across all pixels in the same direction.
286
                 We compute the entropy of the flow directions to quantify the diversity of pixel movement.
287              Low entropy indicates uniform motion, which we consider natural and not memorized.
288              Specifically, we compute the angle θi of each optical flow vector fi and create a histogram
289              of these angles. The entropy H is then calculated as:
290                                                      X
291
                                                H=−          p(θi ) log p(θi )                            (5)
292              where p(θi ) is the probability of a flow vector having direction θi . If H falls below a
293              threshold, the motion is classified as camera panning and is ignored.
294           2. Static frames: Frames with minimal movement are considered static. We calculate the
295              average magnitude of the optical flow vectors, and if the magnitude is below a specified
296              threshold, the frame is categorized as static and excluded from further analysis.
297
298   By applying NMF, we ensure that only meaningful, privacy-sensitive motion patterns are evaluated
299
      as potential memorized motions, filtering out natural and non-threatening movements.

      3     M EMORIZATION IN VIDEO DIFFUSION MODELS (VDM S )
325
326
327   Previous work (Rahman et al., 2024) only investigated generated videos sourced from project websites
328
      of several VDMs and identified instances of their memorization of the training data. However, the
      frequency and extent of these memorization issues remain unclear. To address this, we leverage
329
      insights into the causes of memorization in diffusion models and apply our proposed metrics for
330
      quantifying memorization. This allows us to systematically extract memorized cases and report
331   statistics on the degree and frequency of memorization across multiple VDMs.
332
333   In terms of the scope, previous work focused only on memorization issues in unconditional and
334
      image-conditional VDMs using small datasets such as UCF-101 (Soomro et al., 2012) and Kinetics-
      600 (Carreira & Zisserman, 2018). However, for text-to-video (T2V) generation, their study lacked a
335
      comprehensive investigation of various VDMs trained on larger datasets like WebVid-10M (Bain
336
      et al., 2021), which serves as the foundation for many VDMs used in video generation (Singer et al.,
337   2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023) and video editing (Guo et al., 2023a;b; Zhang et al.,
338   2024). To address this gap, we analyzed the WebVid-10M dataset and conducted the first investigation
339   into the memorization behavior of several open-source VDMs (Wang et al., 2023a;b; Chen et al.,
340   2023; 2024b) trained on this dataset.
341
      Additionally, their scope only addresses the VDMs’ memorization of video training data, while
342
      neglecting the memorization of image training data. Specifically, Rahman et al. (2024) reported that
343   VDMs extended from pretrained text-to-image (T2I) models can benefit from their inherent capability
344   to generate creative image outputs, which can reduce memorization on video datasets. However, we
345   have demonstrated that this benefit comes at the cost of increased memorization of the image data
346   inherent in the pretrained T2I backbone. This reveals an overlooked trade-off where reducing video
347   dataset memorization leads to the unintended consequence of memorizing image data.
348

355

      of 1,000 generations were memorized instances. As RaMViD was not pretrained on any T2V models,
411
      we did not analyze its memorization of image datasets. Qualitative examples can be found in Fig. 4.

413
414   4     P OTENTIAL REMEDIES
415
416   4.1   M OTIVATION
417
      The only previously proposed solution for mitigating memorization in VDMs involves using a text-to-
418
      image diffusion model as a backbone, then fine-tuning it on a video dataset to reduce memorization of
419
      video data (Rahman et al., 2024). However, as shown in Sec. 3, this approach leads to the unintended
420   consequence of the model memorizing image data, thus reducing the overall effectiveness of this
421   strategy.
422
423
      As a preliminary investigation into memorization in VDMs, we draw inspiration from the IDM
      memorization domain, which offers several potential strategies. One such approach is to retrain
424
      VDMs on de-duplicated datasets, as duplication has been identified as a major factor leading to
425
      overfitting and memorization. This suggests that future pre-training of VDMs should prioritize
426   training on de-duplicated data or using pre-trained backbones that have been trained on such data.
427   However, two challenges arise: First, duplication is not the only cause of memorization. For
428   example, text captions in different expressions may share similar meanings, which could still trigger
429   memorization when the user prompt is overly specific. Second, retraining on de-duplicated data is
430   computationally expensive and inefficient. Another approach involves adapting mitigation strategies
431   from the image domain to VDMs, such as adding randomness to input prompts (Somepalli et al.,
      2023b), employing prompt engineering (Wen et al., 2024), or leveraging guidance strategies during


                                                       8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      inference (Chen et al., 2024a). However, these strategies typically come at the cost of utility, such as
433   lower quality or reduced text alignment in the generated outputs.
434
435   In contrast, we argue that inference-time detection strategies are more practical and impactful. These
436
      strategies can effectively and efficiently reduce memorized generations by allowing for early exits
      during inference upon detecting memorization. In terms of effectiveness, inference-time detection
437
      strategies do not modify the inference process itself but rely on memory-efficient cache signals to
438
      detect memorization, thereby fully preserving output utility. In terms of efficiency, these strategies
439   avoid the computational infeasibility of searching through entire training datasets for detection, which
440   is challenging for high-performing models trained on vast amounts of data. Instead, inference-time
441   detection strategies can perform detection during the inference process, which typically takes only
442   tens of seconds. Moreover, our proposed detection method can achieve accurate detection within
443   1-step and 10-step inference processes that only take seconds.
444
      Thus, developing an efficient inference-time detection strategy for both types of memorization in
445   VDMs could significantly contribute to the field. To this end, we adapt the image memorization
446   detection strategy (Wen et al., 2024) to the video domain, incorporating the temporal dimension to
447   enable the detection of both content and motion memorization. This adaptation serves as a solid
448   foundation for future improvements.
449
450   4.2     D ETECTION STRATEGIES FOR BOTH CONTENT AND MOTION MEMORIZATION
451
452   In the IDM memorization domain, a recent work (Wen et al., 2024) introduces a method for memo-
453   rization detection using the magnitude of text-conditional predictions, expressed as:
454
                                          mt = ∥ϵθ (xt , ep ) − ϵθ (xt , eϕ )∥2                               (6)
455
456   This method builds on the observation that, under identical initialization, outputs conditioned on dif-
457   ferent text prompts tend to exhibit similar semantic properties, resulting in relatively small magnitude
458   values throughout the inference process. However, for prompts ep that are prone to memorization, the
459   text condition ϵθ (xt , ep ) consistently steers the generation toward memorized outcomes, regardless
460   of initialization, thereby producing significantly larger magnitude values. Thus, a larger magnitude
461   serves as a signal for potential memorization.
462   We extend this detection strategy to VDMs to enable both content and motion memorization detection,
463   adapting it to account for the temporal dimension inherent in videos. In IDM, the magnitude is a
464   3-dimensional tensor with the shape [C, H, W ], where C is the number of channels, and H and W
465   represent the height and width of the latent noise prediction ϵθ (xt , ep ) and ϵθ (xt , eϕ ). In VDMs, this
466   expands to a 4-dimensional tensor with the shape [C, F, H, W ], where F is the number of frames in
467   the video.
468
469   4.2.1    C ONTENT M AGNITUDE
470
471   For content magnitude, we follow the same intuition behind our content memorization metric
472   (GSSCD) that emphasizes frame-wise memorization, where frame-wise similarities are computed,
473   and the maximum value is used as the overall score. Similarly, we compute the frame-wise magnitude
474   for each of the F frames, resulting in F magnitudes. The maximum magnitude is then taken as the
475
      content magnitude:
476                        mcontent = max{∥ϵθ (xit , ep ) − ϵθ (xit , eϕ )∥2 : i = 1, . . . , F }             (7)
477
478   This maintains coherence and simplicity while also resulting in effective detecting content memoriza-
479   tion.
480
481
      4.2.2    M OTION M AGNITUDE
482
483   For motion magnitude, our approach leverages the temporal dimension by analyzing frame transitions.
484   The frame transition is defined as the difference between each consecutive pair of frames. Given
485   F frames, this results in F − 1 transitions. To compute the motion magnitude, we compare the
      transitions between the text-conditional prediction and the unconditional prediction:


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
487
                       mmotion = max{∥∆ϵθ (xit , ep ) − ∆ϵθ (xit , eϕ )∥2 : i = 1, . . . , F − 1}              (8)
488
489   where ∆ϵθ (xit , ep ) = ϵθ (xi+1             i
                                   t , ep ) − ϵθ (xt , ep ) represents the frame transition at step i. This method
490   captures whether the transitions between frames in the text-conditional generation are “abnormal"
491   compared to the unconditional prediction, thus signaling motion memorization that overfits the
492   motion in the training videos.
493
      Remark on the alternative approach of using optical flow. We also explored using optical flow to
494
      quantify motion transitions. This involved decoding intermediate results of ϵθ (xit , ep ) and ϵθ (xi+1
                                                                                                          t , ep )
495
      pairs for each frame i from each inference step t back into image space using the diffusion model’s
496   decoder, followed by computing optical flow with RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020). However, this
497   approach yielded unsatisfactory results, as the decoded images were likely out-of-distribution for
498   RAFT compared to the dataset it was trained on. This mismatch led to inaccurate flow estimations,
499   making this method inferior to our proposed approach. Another limitation of using RAFT in this case
500   is the significant additional computational cost, as the RAFT model would need to be invoked multiple
501   times during every inference step. By directly leveraging the latent space through frame transitions,
502   our motion magnitude method remains simple yet effective for detecting motion memorization.

528   5    C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we address the critical issue of memorization in video diffusion models (VDMs), a
531
      largely overlooked area compared to image diffusion models (IDMs). We introduced new, privacy-
      focused definitions for both content and motion memorization, as well as tailored evaluation metrics
532
      that separately assess these types of memorization in VDMs. Armed with such metrics, we are the
533
      first to systematically extract training data from large video and image datasets and report quantitative
534   results on the extent of memorization in various open-source VDMs. Our findings demonstrate that
535   memorization is a widespread issue, not only in video datasets but also in image datasets used to
536   train text-to-image (T2I) backbones, which has been previously underestimated in the literature.
537   Furthermore, we propose effective remedies by adapting image memorization detection strategies to
538   the video domain. These strategies, incorporating the temporal dimension, offer efficient detection of
539   both content and motion memorization, providing a robust foundation for future improvements in
      privacy preservation within VDMs, particularly as their usage continues to grow.

666   A.1    R ELATED WORK
667
668   Video diffusion models. Video diffusion models (VDMs) extend the diffusion framework, initially
669   developed for image generation, to model the temporal dynamics in videos. Building on the success
670   of image diffusion models (IDMs) such as Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs)(Ho
671
      et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) and Stable Diffusion(Rombach et al., 2022), VDMs tackle
      the added complexity of maintaining temporal consistency across frames. Early works, like Video
672
      Diffusion Models (Ho et al., 2022), adapted the diffusion process by incorporating 3D convolutional
673
      architectures to handle both spatial and temporal dimensions. Recent VDM advancements such as
674   Make-A-Video (Singer et al., 2022), which leverage pretrained text-to-image (T2I) backbones like
675   Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), have pushed the field forward, expanding the application
676   of VDMs to conditioned video generation from text prompts. Other models, such as Runway Gen-
677   1 (Esser et al., 2023), also push boundaries by incorporating text, images, and videos as conditional
678   inputs to better control generated content. The popularity of VDMs has further surged with advance-
679   ments such as Sora (OpenAI, 2024), Gen-3 (Runway, 2024), and discord-based servers (Discord,
680   2023), which have garnered significant attention on social media. Despite this increasing interest, the
681   phenomenon of memorization in VDMs remains largely underexplored.
682   Memorization in diffusion models. Memorization in image diffusion models (IDMs) has been
683   extensively studied. Pioneer works such as Carlini et al. (2023) and Somepalli et al. (2023a)
684   successfully extracted training data from IDMs, including DDPMs and Stable Diffusion, using
685   datasets like CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) and LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022). This motivates the
686   subsequent works (Somepalli et al., 2023b; Daras et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2024; Chen
687   et al., 2024a; Ren et al., 2024) to work on analyzing the causes and proposing remedies. Recently,
688
      Rahman et al. (2024) explored memorization in VDMs, but the study’s definitions and evaluations
      of content and motion memorization are limited in scope and lack broad applicability. Additionally,
689
      their analysis was based on individual instances of generated videos extracted from project websites,
690
      rather than a systematic study of training data and the extent of memorization. Furthermore, the study
691   did not explore memorization in text-to-video (T2V) models trained on widely used datasets like
692   WebVid-10M.
693
694
      In this work, we address these limitations by conducting a thorough investigation on several open-
      source VDMs, quantifying the percentage of memorized generations and average similarity scores
695
      for both content and motion memorization across both video and image datasets.
