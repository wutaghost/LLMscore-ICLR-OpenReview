009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The recent advancement of large foundation models (FMs) has increased the
012            demand for fine-tuning these models on large-scale and cross-domain datasets.
013            To address this, federated fine-tuning has emerged as a solution, allowing models
014            to be fine-tuned on distributed datasets across multiple devices while ensuring
015            data privacy. However, the substantial parameter size of FMs and the multi-
016            round communication required by traditional federated fine-tuning algorithms
017            result in prohibitively high communication costs, challenging the practicality of
018            federated fine-tuning. In this paper, we are the first to reveal, both theoretically
019
               and empirically, that the traditional multi-round aggregation algorithms may not
               be necessary for federated fine-tuning large FMs. Our experiments reveal that
020
               a single round of communication (i.e., one-shot federated fine-tuning) yields a
021
               global model performance comparable to that achieved through multiple rounds
022            of communication. Through rigorous mathematical and empirical analyses, we
023            demonstrate that large FMs, due to their extensive parameter sizes and pre-training
024            on general tasks, achieve significantly lower training loss in one-shot federated
025            fine-tuning compared to smaller models. Our extensive experiments show that
026            one-shot federated fine-tuning not only reduces communication costs but also
027            enables asynchronous aggregation, enhances privacy, and maintains performance
028            consistency with multi-round federated fine-tuning for models larger than 1 billion
029            parameters, on text generation and text-to-image generation tasks. Our findings
030
               have the potential to revolutionize federated fine-tuning in practice, enhancing
               efficiency, reducing costs, and expanding accessibility for large-scale models. This
031
               breakthrough paves the way for broader adoption and application of federated
032
               fine-tuning across various domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Cutting-edge foundation models (FMs) demonstrate remarkable versatility across various domains.
039
      Notably, large language models (LLMs) like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemma (Team et al., 2024),
      and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023b) excel in tasks such as translation, question answering (QA), chat
040
      assistant, and math. Similarly, stable diffusion models can generate diverse images based on textual
041
      descriptions. Achieving such versatility requires fine-tuning these FMs on cross-domain datasets.
042   However, this process faces significant challenges in real-world scenarios due to the valuable datasets
043   residing on devices owned by organizations or individuals, raising privacy concerns. To address
044   these privacy issues, researchers have proposed using federated learning (FL) (Zhang et al., 2021)
045   for distributed fine-tuning of FMs, a process known as federated fine-tuning. Federated fine-tuning
046   allows distributed clients to collaboratively fine-tune a global FM on specific tasks without disclosing
047   their private data.
048   Traditional FL requires multiple communication rounds between clients and the server to ensure
049   the global model convergence (McMahan et al., 2017). However, the substantial parameter size
050   of FMs (typically in billions) results in significant communication overhead. Many devices lack
051   the capability to repeatedly communicate model parameters of this scale. While previous works
052   adopt parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods such as low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al.,
053   2021) to reduce the number of trainable and communicated parameters, the high communication
      requirements of federated fine-tuning remain a practical limitation.

073   Unexpectedly, our recent experiments have discovered an emergent capability of FMs that could
074   fundamentally shift the approach to federated fine-tuning. We find that with sufficient local fine-tuning
075   epochs, a single communication round is all it needs to effectively fine-tune FMs, which is called
076   one-shot federated fine-tuning (Guha et al., 2019). Figure 1 highlights the performance comparisons
077   between one-shot FL and traditional multi-round FL, maintaining the same total number of local
078   steps. While one-shot FL underperforms multi-round FL for smaller models (e.g., ResNet-18 and
079   LSTM), it achieves comparable performance for larger FMs (e.g., GPT-2, Llama, etc). This unique
080   discovery challenges the conventional belief that multiple communication rounds are essential for
081
      federated fine-tuning. Instead, we demonstrate that FMs can achieve convergence with just a single
      aggregation of well-fine-tuned local models. This paper explores this innovative finding, providing
082
      theoretical analysis and compelling empirical evidence to validate the effectiveness of one-shot FL
083
      for fine-tuning FMs.
084
085   The introduction of one-shot federated fine-tuning brings transformative benefits. First, it dramat-
086   ically reduces communication costs. One-Shot FL slashes communication overhead by a factor
087
      of T1 , where T represents the number of communication rounds in traditional federated fine-tuning.
      This reduction is a game-changer for devices with limited bandwidth. Second, one-shot FL enables
088
      seamless asynchronous training. This flexibility removes the bottleneck of server waiting times,
089
      ensuring uninterrupted training regardless of client connectivity or resource limitations. The process
090   becomes far more robust and efficient. Third, one-shot FL offers enhanced security against prevalent
091   client-side federated learning attacks. Attacks like client-side model inversion and gradient inversion,
092   which depend on multiple global model updates, are rendered ineffective. This significantly bolsters
093   the integrity of the training process.
094
      Our key contributions are listed as follows:
095
096          • Novel Discovery: To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to discover that one
097            communication round is sufficient for federated fine-tuning large FMs.
098          • Theoretical Analysis: We theoretically demonstrate the relationship between the global
099            difference of one-shot federated fine-tuning and model smoothness, fine-tuning model
100            update, number of fine-tuning rounds, and the second norm of original model parameters.
101            Our analysis, backed by experiments, suggests that the lower difference between one-shot
102            and multi-round federated fine-tuning of larger FMs is related to the inherent smoothness of
103            FMs and the smaller parameter updates during fine-tuning.
104          • Experimental Validation: We conduct extensive experiments on six FMs and three tasks,
105            demonstrating that one-shot federated fine-tuning achieves performance comparable to
106            multi-round federated fine-tuning, particularly for models with over 1 billion parameters.
107            Experimental results also surprisingly show that LoRA outperforms full fine-tuning in the
               context of one-shot federated fine-tuning.

136   Local datasets in FL are typically heterogeneous, leading to differences in local objectives. Therefore,
137   FL usually converges more slowly than centralized machine learning. This slow convergence
138   necessitates a large number of global communication rounds and local steps to achieve satisfactory
139   performance. For example, experiment results in (Reddi et al., 2020) show that the ResNet-18 model
140   requires more than 2000 and 4000 communication rounds to converge on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky
141
      et al., 2009) and CIFAR-100 respectively. Even for simple natural language processing tasks such
      as Shakespeare, an RNN model needs more than 50 rounds to converge. The requirement for
142
      multi-round communication rounds introduces several significant drawbacks. First, clients must
143
      frequently exchange model parameters with the server, which can be prohibitively expensive in
144   certain constrained scenarios or on devices with limited resources. Second, repeated invocation of
145   computational resources for training increases the overall computational overhead. Additionally,
146   the multi-round communication approach leads to excessive energy consumption, synchronization
147   difficulties, and challenges in maintaining privacy protection. Thus, optimizing FL algorithms to
148   minimize the number of communication rounds is an essential research direction in FL.
149
      Federated Fine-Tuning Foundation Models. Foundation models (FMs) (Zhou et al., 2023) refer to
150   pre-trained deep learning models with a vast number of parameters, typically in the order of billions.
151   These FMs are trained on broad data at scale and are adaptable to a wide range of downstream
152   tasks when fine-tuned on domain-specific datasets (Bommasani et al., 2021). Since domain-specific
153   datasets are often distributed across multiple devices, FL offers an important paradigm for fine-tuning
154   FMs while preserving data privacy.
155
      Federated fine-tuning also adopts the same FedAvg algorithm in Eq. 1 and Eq. 2 to aggregate the
156   local model updates. The key difference lies in the model parameter size. The parameter size of
157   large FMs is usually hundreds of times greater than that of small models, resulting in a significant
158   increase in the computation resources and communication overhead required for federated fine-tuning.
159   Given the network communication capabilities of commonly used devices, performing multi-round
160   synchronized communication of large model parameters between servers and clients is virtually
161   impossible. Although parameter-efficient fine-tuning algorithms like LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) have
      been adopted, the communication overhead remains excessively high, hindering practical application.

      One-Shot Federated Learning. To reduce communication overhead in FL, recent works have
163   focused on one-shot FL (Jhunjhunwala et al., 2024; Guha et al., 2019; Gong et al., 2021; Li et al.,
164   2020; Zhou et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024), which uses a single communication round to obtain the
165   global model. These algorithms often employ knowledge distillation or neuron-matching methods
166   to optimize the global model. However, these approaches require additional data or computation.
167   Knowledge distillation often necessitates auxiliary public datasets or external generative models,
168   and neuron matching requires additional computation on both clients and the server. Despite these
169   additional resource requirements, the performance of one-shot FL has historically been inferior to
170   standard multi-round FL. For instance, experiments in (Jhunjhunwala et al., 2024) show that one-shot
171
      FL achieves only 50% accuracy on the CIFAR-10 dataset, which is 20% lower than the accuracy
      achieved with 5-round FL.
172
173   However, our recent experiments have uncovered greater potential for one-shot FL in federated fine-
174   tuning large FMs. As shown in Figure 1, one-shot FL for large models does not show a significant
175   performance gap compared to multi-round FL, which is commonly observed with smaller models. In
176   fact, when the total number of local steps is the same, the performance of large models fine-tuned
177
      by one-shot FL is comparable to that of multi-round FL. Additionally, in fine-tuning larger models
      such as Llama-13b, one-shot FL even performed slightly better than multi-round FL. These results,
178
      along with the experiment results in Section 4, suggest that traditional multi-round FL algorithms like
179
      FedAvg may no longer be necessary for federated fine-tuning large FMs. Large FMs can effectively
180   learn downstream tasks from distributed clients with just a single communication round, opening up
181   new possibilities for federated fine-tuning applications.
182
183
      Although we have observed consistently good performance with one-shot federated fine-tuning,
      the reasons for the divergent results between one-shot FL in fine-tuning large models and training
184
      small models remain unexplored. In the next section, we will delve into this phenomenon through
185
      theoretical analysis.

340   Models and Datasets. To demonstrate the performance of FMs of different sizes on one-shot
341   federated fine-tuning, we selected multiple models ranging in parameter size from 1b to 13b for
342   experiments. The language models we experimented with range in parameter size from smallest to
343   largest as follows: TinyLlama (1.1b) (Zhang et al., 2024b), Gemma-2b (Team et al., 2024), Llama-7b,

351   the MMLU dataset to 10 clients and the Wizard dataset to another 10 clients, and lets the 20 clients
352   fine-tune the FM. For the baseline, we use a multi-round FedAvg algorithm on both LoRA and full

      fine-tuning. For our one-shot federated fine-tuning, we only perform a single communication round.
      To ensure fairness, we keep the total number of local steps the same between multi-round and one-shot
354
      federated fine-tuning. e.g., , if the setting in multi-round federated fine-tuning is 3 communication

      rounds, 1 local epoch in each round, the setting in one-shot should be 1 communication round, 3 local

425   5      D ISCUSSION
426
427   One-Shot Federated Fine-Tuning Saves Communication Cost. In FL, the server needs to send
428   the model parameters to all the selected clients and receive the model updates from the clients in each
429   communication round. Thus, the total number of communicated parameters in multi-round should be
430   2mT S, where S is the model size. In one-shot federated fine-tuning, the server and the clients only
431   perform one-round communication, so the number of communicated parameters is only 2mS. This
      reduction in communication overhead is significant, especially in the federated fine-tuning of large


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      FMs. For instance, the Llama-13b model has approximately 50GB parameters, i.e., S = 50GB. In
433   our experiments, the 3-round federated fine-tuning on Llama-13b needs to communicate 3000GB data
434   between the server and the clients, which may be unaffordable in scenarios with tight communication
435   budgets. However, one-shot federated fine-tuning reduces this amount to 1000GB. This substantial
436   reduction in communication makes federated fine-tuning of large FMs more practical and affordable
437   in real-life scenarios.

      One-Shot Federated Fine-Tuning Naturally Mitigates Client-Side Privacy Threatens. In a
465   traditional FL algorithm, clients repeatedly receive new global model parameters each round, which
466   could lead to client-side privacy issues. Malicious clients can exploit model inversion (Fredrikson
467   et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2020) and gradient inversion attacks (Huang et al., 2021) to recover private
468   training samples or user inputs from other clients (Wei et al., 2023). These attacks heavily rely on
469   access to the global model parameters and certain data distribution information. However, in one-shot
470   FL, the server can choose not to send back global parameters to the clients and only provide an API
471   of the fine-tuned model. By doing this, it can eliminate the possibility of client-side privacy attacks.

      6   C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we tackle the critical issue of high communication costs that limit the practical
477
      application of federated fine-tuning. Through a series of experiments, we demonstrate that multi-
      round communication is not necessary for fine-tuning FMs, as one-shot federated fine-tuning achieves
478
      comparable performance. We then provide a theoretical analysis to explain why one-shot federated
479
      fine-tuning is effective for large FMs and validate our findings with empirical evidence. Our extensive
480   experiments show that one-shot federated fine-tuning performs on par with multi-round federated fine-
481   tuning across 5 different models and 3 diverse tasks. This method significantly reduces communication
482   overhead, making federated fine-tuning more feasible and efficient, especially for large-scale models.
483   Moreover, one-shot federated fine-tuning supports asynchronous local updates and enhances security
484   by minimizing data exposure during the training process. These findings make it possible to harness
485   the power of large FMs in environments with limited communication resources, thereby broadening
      the accessibility and utility of advanced AI technologies.

629   A    R ELATED W ORK
630
631   One-Shot Federated Learning. One-shot federated learning refers to learning the parameters of the
632   global model in a single round of communication between clients and the server (Guha et al., 2019).
633   There are two main strategies for optimizing one-shot FL, neuron matching and knowledge distillation.
634   Neuron matching is based on the permutation symmetry of neural networks (Ainsworth et al., 2022),
635   which means that client model parameters can be aligned according to a common ordering and then
636
      be averaged. Previous works use algorithms such as the Fisher information matrix (Jhunjhunwala
      et al., 2024) and permutation matrix (Wang et al., 2020a) to match the local model parameters. The
637
      knowledge distillation methods aim at distilling knowledge from well-trained local models through
638
      public data (Gong et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Heinbaugh et al., 2022). Some works also use distilled
639   data to transfer knowledge between clients and the server (Zhou et al., 2020). Recent works adopt
640   generative models to help generate substitute data for the local dataset on the server (Yang et al.,
641   2024; Zhang et al., 2022).
642
643   Federated Fine-Tuning. Federated fine-tuning (Orescanin et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2021) aims to
644   fine-tune FMs by cross-domain on-device datasets while preserving data privacy. Recent works use
645   PEFT methods such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) in federated fine-tuning (Zhang et al., 2024a) to save
646   communication and computation costs. Federated fine-tuning also faces similar research problems as
647   FL. Current works have discussed the non-IID problem (Cho et al., 2024) and personalized federated
      fine-tuning (Wagner et al., 2024).

      E    L IMITATION
745
      This work has two main limitations. (1) The paper is limited in federated fine-tuning tasks since we
746
      lack the computation resources to conduct federated pre-training experiments. (2) Since common
747   stable diffusion models do not vary significantly in parameter size, this work does not observe the
748   performance of different-sized stable diffusion models in one-shot federated fine-tuning. The impact
749   of model parameter size on one-shot federated fine-tuning in text-to-image generation tasks still
750   needs to be explored.
