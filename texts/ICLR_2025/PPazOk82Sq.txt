009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The capacity of deep learning models is often large enough to both learn the un-
012            derlying statistical signal and overfit to noise in the training set. This noise memo-
013            rization can be harmful especially for data with a low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR),
014            leading to poor generalization. Inspired by prior observations that label noise
015            provides implicit regularization that improves generalization, in this work, we in-
016            vestigate whether introducing label noise to the gradient updates can enhance the
017            test performance of neural network (NN) in the low SNR regime. Specifically,
018            we consider the learning of a two-layer NN with a simple label noise gradient
019            descent (GD) algorithm, in an idealized signal-noise data setting. We prove that
020
               adding label noise during training suppresses noise memorization, preventing it
               from dominating the learning process; consequently, label noise GD enjoys rapid
021
               signal growth while the overfitting remains controlled, thereby achieving good
022
               generalization despite the low SNR. In contrast, we also show that NN trained
023            with standard GD tends to overfit to noise in the same low SNR setting and estab-
024            lish a non-vanishing lower bound on its test error, thus demonstrating the benefit
025            of label noise injection in gradient-based training.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The success of deep learning across various domains (LeCun et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2016; Brown,
032   2020) is often attributed to their ability to extract useful features (Girshick et al., 2014; Devlin,
033
      2018) via gradient-based training (Damian et al., 2022; Ba et al., 2022). One desirable property
      of gradient-based feature learning is the algorithmic regularization that prioritizes learning of the
034
      underlying signal instead of overfitting to noise: real-world data contains noise due to mislabeling,
035
      data corruption, or inherent ambiguity, yet despite having the capacity to memorize noise, neural
036   networks (NNs) trained by gradient descent (GD) tend to identify informative features and “low-
037   complexity” solutions that generalize (Zhang et al., 2021; Rahaman et al., 2019). To understand this
038   behavior, recent theoretical works considered data models that partition the features into signal and
039   noise components (Ghorbani et al., 2020; Ben Arous et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024), and studied
040   the performance of gradient-based training under different signal-to-noise conditions.
041
      Among existing theoretical settings, the signal-noise model proposed in Allen-Zhu & Li (2020);
042   Cao et al. (2022) has been extensively studied in the feature learning theory literature. In this model,
043   input features are constructed by combining a label-dependent signal with label-independent noise.
044   The signal represents meaningful patterns relevant to the predictive task while the noise component
045   captures background features unrelated to the learning task. This idealized setting has shed light on
046   how various algorithms, neural network architectures, and other factors influence optimization and
047   generalization of neural networks, depending on the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) (Frei et al., 2022;
048   Zou et al., 2023; Jelassi & Li, 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022).
049   In the signal-noise model, it is known that the SNR dictates a transition from benign overfitting
050   to harmful overfitting. In the high SNR regime, gradient-based feature learning prioritizes signal
051   learning over noise memorization; hence upon convergence, the trained NN recovers the signal and
052   generalizes to unseen data despite some degree of noise memorization, a phenomenon known as
053   benign overfitting (Bartlett et al., 2020; Tsigler & Bartlett, 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Sanyal et al.,
      2020; Shamir, 2023). In contrast, when the SNR is low, noise memorization dominates the training

054
      dynamics, and the network fails to identify useful features before the training loss becomes small,
055   leading to harmful overfitting (Cao et al., 2022; Kou et al., 2023b).
056
057   Motivated by this observation, recent works have explored algorithmic modifications that either en-
058
      hance signal learning or suppress noise memorization, to improve generalization in the challenging
      low SNR regime. Huang et al. (2023) showed that the smoothing effect of graph convolution in
059
      graph neural networks mitigates overfitting to noise; however, this approach requires the graph to be
060
      sufficiently dense and exhibits high homophily. Chen et al. (2024) found that the sharpness-aware
061   minimization (SAM) method (Foret et al., 2020) prevents noise memorization in early stages of
062   training, thereby promoting effective feature learning; this being said, SAM has higher computa-
063   tional cost than standard GD due to the two forward and backward passes per step, and it involves
064   more complex hyperparameter tuning. The goal of this work is to address the following question.
065
066         Is there a simple modification of GD with no computational overhead that achieves small
067              generalization error in low SNR settings where standard GD fails to generalize?
068
069   1.1   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
070
071   We provide an affirmative answer to the question above by introducing random label noise to
072   the training dynamics as a form of regularization, inspired by the label noise stochastic gradient
073
      descent (SGD) method (Blanc et al., 2020; HaoChen et al., 2021; Shallue et al., 2019; Szegedy
      et al., 2016). Specifically, we consider the learning of a two-layer convolutional neural network in
074
      a binary classification problem studied in Cao et al. (2022), and show that by randomly flipping
075
      the labels of a small proportion of training samples at each iteration, noise memorization can be
076   suppressed despite the low SNR, whereas signal learning experiences a period of fast growth. As a
077   result, neural network trained by label noise GD attains good generalization performance in regimes
078   where standard GD fails, as summarized in the following informal theorem:

090
091
      • Improved Generalization due to Label Noise. The theorem provides an upper bound on the test
092     error of label noise GD, as well as a lower bound on the error of standard GD. This demonstrates
093     that incorporating label noise into the gradient descent updates improves generalization in the low
094     SNR regime. We note that our conditions on label noise GD learnability are weaker than those
095     required for SAM as specified in Chen et al. (2024), even though our studied algorithm is arguably

098     ization of the training dynamics of label noise GD on a two-layer convolutional NN with squared
099     ReLU activation. A key observation in our analysis is that label noise introduces regularization
100     to the noise memorization process, preventing it from growing beyond a constant level; mean-
101     while, signal learning continues to exhibit a rapid growth rate, allowing the model to identify the
102     informative features and avoid harmful overfitting in low SNR regimes.

110   primarily explored in the context of linear regression or shallow neural networks, particularly in re-
111   gression settings (Blanc et al., 2020; Damian et al., 2021; HaoChen et al., 2021; Huh & Rebeschini,
112   2024; Li et al., 2021; Vivien et al., 2022; Takakura & Suzuki, 2024); these studies have highlighted

114   illustrated the implicit regularization of label noise in mean-field neural networks, while Li et al.
115   (2021); Damian et al. (2021) proved that label noise introduces bias towards flat minima. In con-
116   trast to these existing literature, our work focuses on the binary classification setting specified by the
117
      signal-noise model, providing a quantitative analysis of the training dynamics and the generalization
      benefits of label noise GD in the low SNR regime.

      ious contexts, including (i) optimization algorithms, such as Adam (Zou et al., 2021), momentum

126   (Cao et al., 2022; Kou et al., 2023b), vision transformer (Jelassi et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a), graph
127   neural network (Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Our work is in line with Chen et al. (2022);
128   Huang et al. (2023), with the goal of showing that a simple algorithmic modification (label noise

515   6    C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATION
516
517   We presented a theoretical analysis of gradient-based feature learning in the challenging low SNR
518
      regime. Our main contribution is to demonstrate that label noise gradient descent (GD) can effec-
      tively enhance signal learning while suppressing noise memorization; this implicit regularization
519
      mechanism enables label noise GD to generalize in low SNR settings where standard GD suffers
520
      from harmful overfitting. Our theoretical findings are supported by experiments on synthetic data.
521
522
      Limitations. We highlight a few limitations and future directions. Our current analysis applies to
523   a specific choice of activation function (squared ReLU) and architecture (two-layer convolutional
524   neural network); it would be interesting to extend this framework to more complex architectures
525   such as deeper neural networks. Additionally, analyzing label noise GD under other optimization
526   schemes, such as stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and adaptive optimizers like Adam, could pro-
527   vide a deeper understanding of the implicit regularization effects in practical settings.
