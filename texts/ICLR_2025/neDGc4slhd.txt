009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This study aims to analyze the global structure of the functional subgraph of DNNs
012            using tools from topological data analysis (TDA), namely persistent homology (PH)
013            and the Betti curve similarity. Using these methods we present an empirical study
014            on the application of TDA to DNNs in order to gain a better understanding of their
015            architecture and to provide a framework for a similarity measure between DNNs.
016            The study is conducted by training several convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
017            on disjoint subsets of the ImageNet dataset and then by analyzing the structure
018            of their functional graphs across datasets using the Betti curve similarity. Results
019
               show that the Betti curve similarity is able to distinguish between different DNN
               models across datasets and can be a tool for detecting a departure from previous
020
               internal representations of those datasets, providing a new method for the analysis
021
               of DNNs and a potential path forward for their theoretical development.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      With the seemingly ubiquitous implementation of deep neural network (DNN) algorithms in modern
027
      applications, it has become increasingly important for scientists and practitioners of deep learning,
028
      to develop methods for the analysis and scrutability of these algorithms. There have already been
029   various attempts, and small triumphs, with tools such as SHAP values, LIME and XNN (Agarwal
030   and Das, 2020), to name a few, but a complete framework for the scrutability of DNNs has yet to
031   emerge. The sheer size of these DNNs is one of the major reasons why they remain inscrutable, and
032   recent trends seem to indicate that DNNs will only become larger, thus exacerbating this problem.
033
      For these particular reasons we show that a candidate tool for analyzing the global structure of DNNs
034
      is persistent homology (PH) and its corresponding summary statistic, the Betti curve. Both of these
035   originate from topological data analysis (TDA), a branch of abstract topology composed of tools for
036   computing the global structure of data. To demonstrate their uses in deep learning, we modify and
037   add upon work by Corneanu et al. (2019) by analyzing the functional graphs of convolutional neural
038   networks (CNNs) and comparing those graphs across time, i.e. epochs, and datasets. This is done by
039   first training several distinct CNNs on disjoint datasets, extracting their activations on the respective
040   testing data, reducing the activation data via a k-means++ algorithm, processing this reduced data
041   using persistent homology, and finally comparing our results using their respective Betti curves, as
042   seen in Figure 1.
043

      2   M ETHODS
045
046
      The proposed analysis begins with training a series of CNNs on disjoint subsets of the ILSVRC2017
047
      dataset (Russakovsky et al., 2015), commonly known as ImageNet. The global structure of the CNNs’
048   functional graphs across datasets and epochs are then analyzed using PH and the Betti curve similarity.
049   For reproducibility, the details of the data, the CNN models, and the TDA tools that are used in the
050   study along with the random seed are provided. The code used for the study is largely a modification
051   of the previous work by Corneanu et al. (2019), which provides the scaffolding for the models, data
052   loaders, training, and activation extraction. Our modifications are available here at GitHub and the
053   data is available for download at ImageNet. All of the packages used in the study are listed in the
      REQUIREMENTS . TXT file in the GitHub repository.

074   2.1   DATA
075
076   The machine learning task studied here is image classification over ten categories. In order to compare
077   CNN models across subsets of data and epochs, a dataset is required that is large enough to be divided
078   into several subsets (in our case 30 in order to provide a statistically significant sample size without
079   incurring excessive computational overhead) of training and test instances. For its size, availability
080   and ease of use, ImageNet is a natural choice.
081   The original ImageNet training dataset consists of 1.2 million images, each of which is labeled
082   with one of 1000 categories. The original test dataset consists of 50, 000 images, each of which is
083   also labeled with one of its respective categories and where each category contains 50 images. The
084   training dataset is balanced so that each category is represented by the same number of instances, in
085   this case 732 (this being the number of samples in the category with the fewest number of images).
086
      Thirty disjoint subsets of ten categories each are randomly selected using seed 1234; these subsets
087   are then held constant throughout the entire experiment in order to compare the CNN models across
088   the different subsets. Because the data set contains images of varying heights and widths, every
089   image is resized to be 64 × 64 pixels. All images are then standardized by subtracting the mean and
090   dividing by the standard deviation of the respective training subset. This ensures that the results are
091   consistent with standard practice and that no data leakage occurs between the training and test sets.
092   At training time, the subsets are augmented by randomly flipping the images horizontally and adding
093   random color jitter in order to help prevent overfitting and improve the generalization of the models.

      2.3   N ON - LINEAR D IMENSIONALITY R EDUCTION
137
138   In order to make the analysis more computationally feasible, the number of neuron activations from
139   each of the layers of the CNNs is reduced using a PyTorch GPU accelerated k-Means++ algorithm
140   (Omer, 2020). This reduction allows the construction and PH computation of the functional graphs in
141   a reasonable amount of time. k-Means++ was chosen as the reduction technique due to its non-linear
142   nature and its previous success in reducing the dimensionality of point clouds for PH analysis (Malott
143   and Wilsey, 2019).
144   In order to construct the functional graphs of the CNNs for a given subset, neuron activations are
145   extracted from each of the layers of the network for each of the images in the test set by passing
146   the images (transformed by the corresponding training set transform) through the CNNs and then
147   extracting the neuron activations from each of the layers. The activations are then stored in an array
148   of size M × N , where M is the number of images in the test set and N is the aggregated number
149   of neuron activations from each layer. Any neuron activations whose variance is zero are discarded,
150   since these activations do not contribute to the global structure of the functional graph due to the fact
151
      that the correlation between them and other neuron activations is always zero. The neuron activations
      are then prepared for the k-Means++ algorithm through standardization.
152
153   To reduce the number of the neuron activations for each model, the k-Means++ algorithm was used to
154   cluster the neuron activations into 1000 clusters1 . Dimensionality reduction is effected by replacing
155   each neuron activation in a cluster with the neuron activation which is closest to the cluster’s centroid.
156   These neuron activations are then used as the reduced set for the PH analysis, which allows us to
157
      construct the functional graphs of the CNNs. Analysis of the silhouette scores for the clusters for
      each of the models show that the clusters were poorly separated, which in turn shows that the means
158
      of the neuron activations are not well-separated.

      This reduction in the number of the neuron activations introduces some approximation error into the

165   activations is not as important as the global structure, since the local structure is more representative
166   of overfit in the model (Corneanu et al., 2019). Therefore, we believe that the k-Means++ reduction
167   is a suitable method for the analysis of the global structure of the functional graphs of the CNNs,
168   which is the main focus of our study.

170   2.4   F INITE M ETRIC S PACES AND FUNCTIONAL GRAPHS
171
172   A finite metric space is a finite set of points X equipped with a function dX : X ×X → R+ that
173   satisfies the properties of a metric, i.e., non-negativity, symmetry, and the triangle inequality. Since
174   the set of points is finite, we can also completely describe the metric by a distance matrix DX where
175
      (DX )i,j = dX (xi , xj ) for all xi , xj ∈ X. In this way, we can represent the finite metric space as a
      weighted graph, where the vertices are the points of the metric space and the edge weights are the
176
      distances between the points.
177
178   We formalize the functional graph of a DNN as a finite metric space, where the points are given by
179   the neuron activations of the DNN and the distance between two activations is given by
180
                                                            q
181
                                          dρ (ai , aj ) =       1 − |ρ(ai , aj )|                             (1)
182
183
184   where ρ(ai , aj ) is the correlation between the neuron activations ai and aj . We note that the distance
185
      function dρ satisfies all properties of a metric except for positivity, since dρ equaling 0 does not imply
      that the inputs are the same (López De Prado, 2016). Further, dρ is satisfied by several different
186
      correlation functions, such as the Pearson and the Spearman correlation. For our study, we use
187
      the Spearman correlation as our correlation function ρ, since it is able to capture both linear and
188   non-linear relationships and does not require that the neuron activations be normally distributed
189   (Kutner, 2005).
190
191
      In order to construct the weighted graph representing the functional graph of a given network net,
      we first took its reduced set of neuron activations from 2.3 and constructed its distance matrix Dnet
192
      using equation 1. With the distance matrix Dnet we then calculated the persistent homology of the
193
      functional graph of net .

      2.5   T OPOLOGICAL DATA A NALYSIS
196
197
      Topological data analysis is a framework for analyzing the underlying topological space of a given
198   dataset. It comprises a suite of tools from abstract topology used to construct and count the combi-
199   natorial objects which model the structure of topological spaces. In our case, we use the Giotto-tda
200   library (Tauzin et al., 2020) to calculate the PH of the functional graphs of the CNNs and extract the
201   Betti curves from their persistence diagrams. The Betti curves are then used to calculate the Betti
202   curve similarity between the CNN models across the different subsets and epochs. We provide a
203   quick overview of terminology and concepts from TDA from which we derive the necessary tools
204   (Edelsbrunner and Harer, 2010).
205   Let d be a positive integer and let {x0 , x1 , . . . , xn } ⊂ Rd be a finite set of points. An n-simplex is
206   the convex hull of n+1 affinely independent points, often denoted by σ = [x0 , x1 , . . . , xn ] where
207   the dimension of σ is n. A face of σ is any of the simplices of equal or lesser dimension that are
208   contained in σ and is often denoted by τ ≤ σ. The boundary of σ is the union of all proper faces of
209   σ where a proper face is simply a face of strictly lesser dimension, denoted τ < σ. A simplicial
210   complex K then, is a finite collection of simplices such that if σ ∈ K and τ ≤ σ, then τ ∈ K, and if
211   σ1 , σ2 ∈ K, then σ1 ∩ σ2 is a face of both or is empty.
212   Let K be a simplicial complex and let Cn (K) be the free abelian group generated
                                                                               Pn      by the n-simplices
213   of K. The objects of Cn (K) are called n-chains and are formal sums c = i=1 ai σi where ai ∈ Z2
214   and σi is an n-simplex of K. For anyPtwo elements c1 , c2 ∈ Cn (K), addition is defined similarly to
                                             n
215   that of polynomials, i.e., c1 + c2 = i=1 (ai + bi )σi . Thus, the n-chains of Cn (K) form a vector
      space over Z2 and are known as chain groups.

226   Given a simplicial complex K and a dimension p, the p-th boundary operator ∂p is used to define
227   what are known as cycles and boundaries of the chain group Cp (K), written Zp (K) and Bp (K),
228   respectively.
229   A p-chain c ∈ Cp (K) is a cycle if ∂p c = 0 and is a boundary if there exists a (p + 1)-chain
230   b ∈ Cp+1 (K) such that ∂p+1 b = c. This means then that Zp (K) = ker ∂p and Bp (K) = im ∂p+1 ,
231   making them both subspaces of Cp (K). Further, due to properties of the boundary operator, it turns
232   out that Bp (K) ⊆ Zp (K); therefore, the quotient space

236   is defined, and is known as the p-th homology group of K. It is essentially the span of the p-cycles
237   which are also not boundaries, and it is used to describe the topological structure of the complex.

239   adding in simplices a few at a time. This is known as a filtration of the complex and must satisfy

245   Given a simplicial complex K and a filtration ∅ = K0 ⊆ K1 ⊆ · · · ⊆ Kn = K, the persistent
246   homology of K is a measure of the scale of the topological features throughout the filtration, and
247

      indices i ≤ j, the p-th persistent homology group of K is defined as

253   The Betti numbers of a simplicial complex are used to count the number of p-dimensional generators
254
      of space and therefore give a unique summary (up to isomorphism) of its topological structure. It is
      with these that we construct our Betti curves which allow us to compare networks.
255
256   The Vietoris-Rips complex Vϵ , is a simplicial complex that is used to approximate the topology of
257   a finite metric space by constructing simplices from its points. Given a finite metric space (X, d),
258   the simplices of Vϵ (X) are the subsets of X whose diameter is less than or equal to the filtration
259   parameter ϵ, and where the diameter is defined to be the maximum distance between any two points
260
      in the subset. The complex is then constructed from these simplices.
261   This is done by starting with the points of X as our 0-simplices and connecting them with edges if the
262   pairwise distance between them is less than or equal to ϵ/2. We get higher and higher dimensional
263   simplices by continuing to increase ϵ and adding more pairwise intersections between the points.
264   Creating new simplices however comes at a cost, as the youngest simplices are the first to be removed
265   while the eldest live on. This is known as the Elder Rule and we say that a simplex is born at the
266
      filtration parameter ϵi and dies at ϵj if it is added to the complex at ϵi and removed at ϵj .
267   For our study we use the multi-threaded Vietoris-Rips complex implementation from Giotto-tda to
268   compute the PH of the finite metric space of each of our functional graphs Dnet . This particular
269   implementation has been shown to be efficient and scalable for large datasets (Tauzin et al., 2020),
      even outperforming certain C++ and GPU accelerated implementations.

363   3.1   F UNCTIONAL S IMILARITY ACROSS T IME
364
365   Comparing the average unnormalized similarity over time reveals that temporal similarity is quite
366   low at the beginning of training and then typically increases as the models learn the features of the
367   dataset. For example, in Figure 4 the similarity between the ResNet-18 model at epoch 0 and the
368   same model at epoch 60 is quite low, indicating that the global structure of the functional graphs of
369
      the network changes over the course of training. A large shift in similarity from epoch 0 to epoch 10
      is also evident, where the accuracy of the model is increasing most rapidly. Both of these are to be
370
      expected as the network learns the features of the dataset, as seen in Figure 2. Further, in Figure 4,
371
      the convergence of the network’s functional graph towards some global structure can be observed, as
372   the similarity between adjacent epochs is increasing. The same phenomenon appears over the other
373   persistent homology dimensions as well (Figure 17). Also of interest is the fact that, on average, the
374   similarity between the models seems to be increasing when compared at the same epoch. This is
375   especially true in the zeroth and first persistent homology dimensions, and can be seen in Figure 5 in
376   which AlexNet and VGG-16 are compared, hinting that the global structures of the functional graphs
377   of the models are becoming more similar as the models are trained and that perhaps on average the

407   3.2   F UNCTIONAL S IMILARITY ACROSS DATA
408
409   Comparing the average unnormalized similarities across the different subsets of testing data, reveals
410   that the models’ functional graphs are quite dissimilar, i.e., the models’ representation of the subsets
411   are not the same. For certain models and subsets, the similarity was quite low, indicating that the
412   representation for that particular subset was quite different from the others and that the models seem
413   to be representing the features of the dataset in different ways. For example, in Figure 6 it can be
414
      seen that the similarity between the ResNet-18 model and the VGG-16 model for subset 11 is very
      low. Further inspection of the subset itself reveals that the classes in the subset are very distinct
415
      (see subsubsection A.1.5) as compared to others (e.g., subset 25 with three classes of dog), and the
416
      accuracy of the models on subset 11 shown in Figure 7 reveals that ResNet-18 outperforms VGG-16
417   by approximately 5% on the testing set . It can be further observed that for subset 27, a subset with
418   similar classes in terms of morphism, the similarity between the models ResNet-18, VGG-16 and
419   AlexNet was quite high, while they all differ considerably from the extended LeNet model as seen in
420   Figure 8. Looking at the accuracy of the models on this subset however, would not readily reveal this
421   difference, as the models’ performance in terms of accuracy are all distinct, with the extended LeNet
422   model performing most poorly, with AlexNet coming in second worst, as seen in Figure 9. Therefore,
423   it can be concluded that statistically the models are creating distinct internal representations of the
424   testing data across subsets. This is somewhat surprising since the models are not fundamentally
425
      different, being simply CNNs of varying sizes, but this also evidences that a simple change to the
      architecture topology, namely the residual connections in ResNet, can make a large difference for
426
      certain datasets.
