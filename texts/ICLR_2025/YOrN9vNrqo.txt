009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Preference Optimization (PO) has proven an effective step for aligning language
012            models to human-desired behaviors. Current variants, following the offline Direct
013            Preference Optimization objective, have focused on a strict setting where all to-
014            kens are contributing signals of KL divergence and rewards to the loss function.
015            However, human preference is not affected by each word in a sequence equally but
016            is often dependent on specific words or phrases, e.g. existence of toxic terms leads
017            to non-preferred responses. Based on this observation, we argue that not all tokens
018
               should be weighted equally during PO and propose a flexible objective termed
               SparsePO, that aims to automatically learn to weight the KL divergence and re-
019
               ward corresponding to each token during PO training. We propose two different
020
               variants of weight-masks that can either be derived from the reference model it-
021            self or learned on the fly. Notably, our method induces sparsity in the learned
022            masks, allowing the model to learn how to best weight reward and KL divergence
023            contributions at the token level, learning an optimal level of mask sparsity. Ex-
024            tensive experiments on multiple domains, including sentiment control, dialogue,
025            text summarization and text-to-code generation, illustrate that our approach as-
026            signs meaningful weights to tokens according to the target task, generates more
027            responses with the desired preference and improves reasoning tasks by up to 2
028            percentage points compared to other token- and response-level PO methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The rise of employing Large Language Models (LLMs) as conversational agents has increased the
      importance of aligning them with human preferences. Preference Optimization (PO), i.e. the train-
034
      ing paradigm that aims to steer models to a desired behavior (typically related to human perception),
035
      is considered the last and most important step in the pipeline of LLM training for producing accurate,
036   harmless and controllable models. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF; Chris-
037   tiano et al. (2017)) was the primary method for obtaining such a behavior. However, due to it’s
038   inherent complexity it has been overpowered by Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov
039   et al., 2023), a simpler, offline approach that produces a policy model that fits the preference data
040   without the need for reinforcement learning.
041
      DPO performs at the sequence level, optimizing rewards and measuring KL divergence for complete
042   responses. However, various studies have shown that signals from specific tokens are primarily
043   responsible for learning desired behaviors, both during pre-training (Lin et al., 2024) and preference
044   optimization (Yang et al., 2024). In particular, in domains where the preference is determined by
045   a specific aspect (e.g. sentiment, toxicity) or when the decision relies on certain subsequences (Pal
046   et al., 2024), it is necessary to consider more fine-grained updates. To further illustrate this point,
047   Figure 1 shows that DPO is already learning implicitly to assign different token-level rewards, with
048   higher values on a few tokens with positive/negative polarity (e.g. pretty, weak). However, noting
049   the various lone tokens with high rewards, DPO’s reward distribution seems inconsistent, and we
050
      posit that it would benefit from a more explicit signal.
051   Aligned with prior work, we argue that not all tokens are important in preference optimization. We
052   further propose that in order to have more diverse responses, and flexible optimization, we should
053   allow only certain tokens to be close to the reference model so that the rest are able to grow beyond
      it–dismissing the need for measuring KL divergence on all tokens. As such, in this work we pro-

084   2.1   P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
085
086   The purpose of aligning models with human preferences is to steer model behavior to produce
087   human-acceptable responses. To realize that, we assume training data in the form of static, paired
088   preferences. A prompt x is associated with two responses, chosen yc and rejected yr , so that yc
                                                                             (i) (i)
089   is preferred over yr (yc ≻ yr |x), resulting in a dataset D = {x(i) , yc , yr }N i=1 . Such responses
090   and their rankings are typically collected either by humans or automatically from other models (Xu
091
      et al., 2024). In PO, we aim to train a model to generate responses closer to yc than yr .
092   In the standard Reinforcement from Human Feedback (RLHF) pipeline (Ziegler et al., 2019) this is
093   realized in a sequence of steps. Firstly, we perform supervised fine-tuning on the task for which we
094   would like to learn preferences, to shift the distribution of the language model in-domain with the PO
095   data. Then, a reward model is trained, responsible for assigning a higher score (reward) to chosen
096   responses and lower scores to rejected ones. Given a policy network π (i.e., the model that we aim
097
      to optimize), responses are sampled and then scored by the reward model. The policy training aims
      to maximize the rewards associated with chosen responses and minimize those of rejected ones,
098
      subject to a KL constraint with a reference model πref . The constraint prevents the policy π from
099
      deviating too much from the distribution that the reward model has learned, as well as avoids reward
100   hacking. The above process is translated into the following objective.

157   2.3   M ASK C OMPUTATION
158
159   In the previous section we showed how we can control the contribution of rewards and KL diver-
160   gence of each token through the introduction of weights in the loss function. Next, we introduce
161   two strategies to obtain these weights from the reference model, one that is derived directly from its
      internal activations and another that is learned in parallel during preference optimization.


                                                                   3

162
      Model Activation-based Mask (M A PO)
163
164   Inspired by mechanistic interpretability approaches (Huben et al., 2023), we leverage the rich in-
165   formation captured per token in the activations of the reference model and aggregate them into
                                                                 ′
166   token-level weighting masks, as follows. Let atg ∈ Rd be the output of activation function g(∗) in
167   network πref , and ātg its average value across dimensions for time step t. Note that atg is exposed to
168   information from y <t due to the autoregressive nature of generation. We obtain [ã1g , .., ãTg ], where
169   ãtg = (ātg − mean(āg ))/std(āg ) is the standardization of ā across sequence y. Then, we define
170   activation-based mask m(y <t ) = mean{ãtg |∀g ∈ πref }, i.e. the average ãtg for all activations in the
171   reference model. In practice, we aggregate outputs from feed-forward layers, residual connections,
172   and attention layers, across all layers in πref . Finally, we set mu (y <t ) = md (y <t ) = m(y <t ), i.e.
173   a common mask for the rewards and KL terms given.
174   Learnable Sparse Mask (S PARSE PO)
175
176
      In our second variant, mask m(y <t ) is computed using learnable parameters. Specifically, we learn
      one feed-forward network (FFN) with ReLU activation for each model layer, and aggregate repre-
177
      sentations from all layers with a linear layer.1 A single layer mask is computed as follows:
178
179                              m(l) (y <t ) = ReLU H(l) (y t ) · w(l) + b(l) ,
180
181   where H(l) ∈ RN ×d corresponds to the reference model hidden representation for layer l for N
182   tokens and w(l) ∈ Rd , b(l) are the l-layer learned parameters. Consequently, when learning multiple
183   masks per layer, they are combined as
184

                        m(y <t ) = ReLU Concat m(1) (y <t ), ..., m(L) (y <t ) · wo ,
185
186   with wo ∈ RL the output merging vector.

188   The ReLU activation function produces a sparsity in the masks, the degree of which is dependent on
189
      the target preference data and the reference model. The mask values (independent of strategy) are
      utilized solely during PO training and are ignored during model inference.

192   3     E XPERIMENTS
193
194   In this section, the effectiveness of SparsePO is investigated in both proxy-preference and human-
195   preference setups. Proxy-preference setups are analyzed through sentiment control, summariza-
196   tion, and code generation, whereas human-preference setup is analyzed through single-turn dialogue
197
      tasks. We refer the reader to Appendix B for further details on experimental setup.

199   3.1    M ODEL C OMPARISON
200
      We compare the performance of SparsePO against supervised fine-tuning over preferred responses
201
      (SFT, serving both as a baseline and the starting point of the PO variants) and performant PO strate-
202   gies that model preference at the sequence and token levels. At the sequence level, we compare
203   against DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), which aims to mitigate KL divergence; SimPO (Meng et al.,
204   2024), which aims to maximize the probability difference between chosen and rejected responses;
205   and DPOP (Pal et al., 2024), which adds a penalty term to the DPO loss to encourage high prob-
206   ability scores of the preferred completions. At the token level, we compare against TDPO v1 and
207   v2 (Zeng et al., 2024), which adds token-level KL divergence as a regularization term. Unless
208   stated otherwise, we investigate SparsePO setups learning a common mask for reward and KL terms
209   (mu = md ) as well as learning different ones (mu ̸= md ).

      These results indicate that although SparsePO is weighting more tokens as important for preference,
452
      in the code domain and in particular code execution, this requirement cannot be easily satisfied. In
453
      fact, code sequences are heavily structured and every ‘word’ is intricately reliant on all other ‘words’
454   in the sequence, i.e. there is little information that may be considered redundant. As such, a weighing
455   scheme (such as in SparsePO) will effectively ignore parts of the sequence that can be crucial; this
456   is further supported from qualitive analysis presented in Figure 22 in the Appendix. Since the goal
457   of the task is to improve functional correctness (whether a programs runs correctly or not) ignoring
458   any ‘word’ in a code sequence will most certainly lead to a functionally incorrect solution. This
459   is in contrast to natural language, where some words are naturally more important for preference
460   than others. This includes the standard Preference Optimization goals of reducing toxicity or style
461   adaptation, but it extends on reasoning tasks as well when that reasoning is happening through
462
      natural language. This also explains SparsePO’s benefits to the MATH benchmark, as performance
      there is enabled by Natural Language instructions through chain-of-thought reasoning.

468   4   R ELATED W ORK
469
470   Since the introduction of DPO, several methods have been developed to mitigate the various short-
471   comings of the method, mostly by introducing further constrains to the loss function. Identity Prefer-
472   ence Optimization (Gheshlaghi Azar et al., 2024, IPO) was proposed to primarily tackle overfitting,
473   that does not rely on the Bradley-Terry modulation assumption. Ethayarajh et al. (2024) introduced
474   KTO, that takes advantage of that Kahneman-Tversky model of human utility. The method drops
475   the requirement for preference pairs and is dependent only on a binary signal of whether a re-
476   sponse is acceptable or not. To control response length and dismiss the need for a reference model,
477
      SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) uses the average log probability of the sequence (instead of the sum)
      while also requiring the difference between responses to be at least equal to a margin. Another
478
      method that does not require a reference model or prior supervised fine-tuning, is ORPO (Hong
479
      et al., 2024), that optimizes the odds ratio together with cross-entropy. On a similar vein, Amini et al.
480   (2024) argues that not all preference pairs are considered equal, requiring the preferred responses to
481   have a likelihood larger than an offset value from the dispreferred ones, based on the score assigned
482   to each response from an external reward model. Other methods that incorporate margins between
483   probability differences include DPO-Positive (Pal et al., 2024), where the log probability of the pre-
484   ferred response for the policy needs to be higher than that of the reference model. The method is
485   particularly effective when the edit distance between responses is low, e.g in math data. Wu et al.
      (2024) specifically aimed at a dynamic optimization of the β value for each batch, proposing β-DPO.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Closer to our approach, there is a family of methods that focus on token-level rather than sequence-
487   level optimization. In TDPO (Zeng et al., 2024), the sequence-level DPO objective is converted into
488   token-level, which results in the KL divergence to act as a regularization term, optimized together
489   with the original objective. The new loss leads to more controllable KL values throughout the course
490   of training. Inverse-Q*(Xia et al., 2024) optimizes the same objective as PPO assigning token-
491   level reward feedback via an estimated policy. Similarly, Token-level Continuous Rewards (Yoon
492   et al., 2024, TLCR) incorporate a discriminator trained to distinguish positive and negative tokens
493   (obtained from GPT-4 judgments). The confidence of the discriminator is used to assign continuous
494   rewards to each token considering the context. Similarly to our motivation, in Selective PO (Yang
495
      et al., 2024, SePO), not all tokens are considered equal. An oracle model is trained first to identify
      which tokens are important in chosen and rejected responses (based on their reward values). These
496
      tokens are then used to train DPO again, while the rest are zeroed out. In contrast to the above
497
      methods, we aim for maximum flexibility. Our approach does not require an external LLM to model
498   rewards and our proposed masks are learned on the fly, effectively assigning higher rewards to tokens
499   that are important to the target preference. In addition, SparsePO induces the necessary sparsity in
500   the masks automatically with a single stage of training.

      5     D ISCUSSION
504
505
      Based on the controlled experiments we conducted in the previous section, here we briefly discuss
      our overall findings. Firstly, based on the sentiment control analysis, SparsePO allows larger KL
506
      divergence at little to no cost in expected ground-truth reward. The β value is able to control sparsity
507
      in both masks, across domains, with values between 0.6 to 4 leading to mid-range sparsity levels.
508   Depending on the domain and target preference proxy, we found that higher sparsity was present in
509   sentiment control, highlighting a certain triviality of the task as the SFT model seems able to already
510   identify words that are important for the target preference. On the other end, for code generation
511   and summarization, lower sparsity between 0.2 and 0.4 seemed best in terms of alignment accuracy
512   as executability and summary correctness are less well-defined preference proxies. For helpfulness
513   control, optimal sparsity was found instead between 0.6 and 0.8, possibly as existence of toxic terms
514   immediately renders response dispreferred. We would argue that the mask works in tandem with beta
515   and we observed that the range of betas that are effective with SparsePO is generally higher than
516
      DPO (with best values between 0.4-1).9
517   From our analysis over DPO, TDPO and their variants, it is important to note that, although restrict-
518   ing divergence at the response or token-level proves effective at maintaining the model in-domain,
519   this does not guarantee better ground-truth rewards or better downstream task performance. For
520   cases in which the preference proxy is complex, such as ‘helpfulness’, ‘summary quality’ or ‘ex-
521   ecutability’, this plain control can even hinder performance. In contrast, we devise a training pro-
522
      cedure in which a model can learn to enhance or suppress the reward and KL divergence for each
      token independently. Our qualitative analysis shows that indeed for trivial tasks tokens important
523
      towards the preference get high rewards and low KL divergence, meaning they need to be close to
524
      the reference predictions to maintain preference.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   We introduced Sparse Token-level Preference Optimization (SparsePO), a novel LM alignment strat-
530   egy that learns to weight the reward and KL divergence for each particular token in a response during
531   PO training. We proposed two masking strategies, obtaining model activation-based masks from the
532   reference model and learning mask representations either commonly for both reward and divergence
533
      terms or independently. By allowing masks to be learned along with preference, we observed that
      they converged to a non-trivial level of sparsity which can be controlled with well-studied hyper-
534
      parameters in preference optimization, while being dependent on target preference proxy. Exten-
535
      sive experiments across several tasks and domains, reveal that our method consistently outperforms
536   strong baselines that model preference at the response and token-level, while assigning higher re-
537   wards and lower KL values to tokens that are important for inferring target preference. SparsePO can
538   be easily extended to use other masking strategies and can be combined with other PO variations.

      the methodology in Aharoni et al. (2023) and keep instances with a reference summary–document
952   entailment probability higher than 0.6, given by SummaCZS Laban et al. (2022).10 For ROUGE,
953   we report results using stemming; for BERTScore, we use RoBERTa large (Liu et al., 2019) as un-
954   derlying model with sentence-level IDF importance weighting, for which the scores were calculated
955   over the training set. EDNA scores we calculated using the SummaCZS score.
956
      Regarding win-rate calculation, we uniformly sample 100 prompts from the entire test set
957
      and sample 5 completions using nucleus sampling (p = 0.95) and temperatures T =
958   {0, 0.25, 0.50, 0.75, 1.0}. Then, we elicit quality judgements from GPT4 (gpt-4-turbo) using the
959   prompt in Figure 8, comparing reference summaries against system responses. The order of re-

1333   Mask behavior and response correctness. Next, we analyze the behavior of the mask in scenarios
1334   where the ‘correctness’ of the task can be verified deterministically, taking as test cases the tasks
1335
       text-to-code generation and mathematical reasoning. Both of these tasks require that a response is
       ‘correct’, however with a crucial difference. In current math benchmarks (e.g. MATH) correctness
1336
       is evaluated as obtaining the correct final answer, regardless of the correctness of intermediate rea-
1337
       soning steps. Hence, a model has more liberty in generating a response consisting of steps and the
1338   final answer, i.e. if a response contains incorrect intermediate steps but the correct final answer, it
1339   will be deemed as correct. However, in our text-to-code setup, it is crucial that the response not
1340   only executes but also that it returns the correct answer for all test units. In this case, an incorrect
1341   intermediate logical step in the response, even if executable, will prompt an incorrect answer (or fail
1342   to run).
