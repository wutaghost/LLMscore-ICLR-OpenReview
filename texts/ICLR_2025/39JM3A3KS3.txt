009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               On-policy Reinforcement Learning (RL) offers desirable features such as stable
012            learning, fewer policy updates, and the ability to evaluate a policy’s return dur-
013            ing training. While recent efforts have focused on off-policy methods, achieving
014            significant advancements, Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) remains the go-to
015            algorithm for on-policy RL due to its apparent simplicity and effectiveness. How-
016            ever, despite its apparent simplicity, PPO is highly sensitive to hyperparameters
017            and depends on subtle and poorly documented tweaks that can make or break its
018            success–hindering its applicability in complex problems. In this paper, we revisit
019            on-policy deep RL with a focus on improving PPO, by introducing principled
020
               solutions that enhance its performance while eliminating the need for extensive
               hyperparameter tuning and implementation-level optimizations. Our effort leads
021
               to PPO+, a methodical adaptation of the PPO algorithm that adheres closer to
022
               its theoretical foundations. PPO+ sets a new state-of-the-art for on-policy RL on
023            MuJoCo control problems while maintaining a straightforward trick-free imple-
024            mentation. Beyond just performance, our findings offer a fresh perspective on
025            on-policy RL that could reignite interest in these approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   A fundamental distinction in Reinforcement Learning (RL) lies between on-policy and off-policy
032   methods (Sutton and Barto, 2018). On-policy methods, such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
033
      (Schulman et al., 2017b) and Trust-Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) (Schulman et al., 2015),
      directly optimize the expected reward under the current policy’s state-action distribution, improving
034
      the policy while actively interacting with the environment. This leads to stable learning and safer
035
      exploration since the policy stays close to the data distribution it learns from, though at the cost of
036   potentially reduced sample-efficiency. In contrast, off-policy methods optimize the expected reward
037   under a different distribution—often using an exploration or behavior policy. By leveraging data
038   generated from different policies, off-policy methods can reuse past experiences, boosting sample-
039   efficiency. This flexibility supports more aggressive exploration, making off-policy methods more
040   suitable when data collection is expensive or restricted.
041
      Recent advancements in off-policy approaches, such as Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al.,
042   2018) and Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradients (TD3) (Fujimoto et al., 2018), have
043   significantly improved continuous control on complex tasks. However, on-policy algorithms have
044   not kept pace in terms of asymptotic performance and sample-efficiency. While PPO remains a dom-
045   inant choice in on-policy RL, delivering impressive results across a range of applications (Berner
046   et al., 2019; Andrychowicz et al., 2020b; Mirhoseini et al., 2021; Rudin et al., 2022), it is hindered
047   by the complexity of its inherent mechanisms, including trust-region optimization, multiple loss
048   functions, and various implementation-specific optimizations, making it highly sensitive to hyper-
049   parameter tuning (Andrychowicz et al., 2020a; Huang et al., 2022).
050   Moreover, common practices in the use of PPO have crucial shortcomings. For example, despite
051   the empirically demonstrated success of maximum entropy RL (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Bhatt et al.,
052   2024) and theoretical works suggesting it can enhance the convergence of policy gradient methods
053   (Mei et al., 2020; Cen et al., 2024), its application for on-policy deep RL remains underexplored.
      Additionally, common on-policy algorithms that utilize the policy gradient theorem frequently over-

      look the discount factor in the state distribution. This omission is technically incorrect and can result
055   in degenerate learning behaviors in certain environments (Thomas, 2014; Nota and Thomas, 2019).
056
057   These challenges, combined with the inherent complexity of current on-policy deep RL methods,
058
      motivate us to pursue simpler and more sample-efficient alternatives. In this paper, we intro-
      duce PPO+, a principled enhancement of the PPO algorithm that introduce targeted solutions to
059

064   PPO optimization problem under the maximum entropy RL perspective for enhanced exploration.
065   Finally, we address a key limitation of biased policy gradient estimates caused by improper dis-
066   counting, which can adversely impact the performance of policy-gradient methods.
067   We show that PPO+ achieves state-of-the-art performance among on-policy methods for continuous
068   control while maintaining a simple and trick-free implementation and being closely aligned with the
069   theoretical foundations of on-policy RL.

      4.2    O FF -P OLICY C RITIC L EARNING
252
253   Temporal-Difference (TD) learning, as outlined by Sutton and Barto (2018), offers a methodology
254   for learning the value function using only system transitions, as expressed in Equation (2.4). This
255   algorithm is a cornerstone in the field of RL, with extensive research dedicated to understanding its
256   properties. It is well known that when TD is applied to a tabular value function representation, it
257   converges to the true value function (Dayan, 1992; Jaakkola et al., 1993). Conversely, on-policy TD
258   learning approaches using linear function approximation have been proven to converge to a fixed
259   point in the vicinity of the projection of the true value function (Tsitsiklis and Van Roy, 1996).
260   However, divergence may occur with standard TD learning when states are sampled off-policy and
261   linear function approximation is used (Baird, 1995). This issue has prompted the creation of several
262   alternative algorithms specifically engineered to guarantee convergence under off-policy sampling
263   (Kolter, 2011; Diddigi et al., 2019). In light of the lack of convergence guarantees for simple off-
264   policy TD learning, the desirable properties of on-policy TD learning have inspired the development
265   of deep RL on-policy methods that learn a critic Qπ using exclusively the data generated by π,
266
      forgoing the use of a replay buffer to store previous transitions (Schulman et al., 2015; 2017b).

268   off-policy data to train critics in both online algorithms (Lillicrap, 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Fu-

368     In Figure 3, we present an ablation study for PPO+ examining our three key design choices: (1)
369
        the application of the true discounted policy gradient; (2) the use of off-policy data for training the
        critic; and (3) the use of the maximum entropy objective. Our results demonstrate that restricting
370
        the critic’s training to on-policy data significantly degrades performance, even impeding learning in
371
        tasks such as Humanoid-v5. Overall, we find that training the critic on larger datasets, even with off-
372     policy data, is generally advantageous compared to limiting the training to a smaller pool of freshly
373     generated data. This is in contrast to the common practice of restricting training the critic to on-
374     policy data for on-policy gradient methods. Interestingly, while the use of off-policy data is already
375     well explored in deep off-policy actor-critic methods like Lillicrap (2015); Haarnoja et al. (2018);
376     Fujimoto et al. (2018), it is not clear whether this choice majorly benefit the actor or the critic. This
377     work suggests that at least the critic has a great benefit from the use of off-policy data, showing that
        deep neural networks can overcome the state aliasing inherent to off-policy TD learning.

          benefit from the extra conservatism.

420       slightly different one), as reported in Andrychowicz et al. (2020a).

442   6   R ELATED W ORKS
443
444   Numerous studies underscore the sensitivity of current deep on-policy methods to hyperparameters
445
      and implementation details (Huang et al., 2022; Andrychowicz et al., 2020a), urging the community
      to simplify and close the gap between theory and practical implementation.
446
447   Ilyas et al. (2018) observe that the behavior of deep PG algorithms differs greatly from its motivating
448   frameworks. Specifically, learned value estimators frequently fail to fit the true value function, and
449   there is a poor correlation between gradient estimates and the ‘true’ gradient. In Nota and Thomas
450   (2019), the authors demonstrate that the undiscounted policy gradient does not correspond to the
451
      gradient of any objective function. They also identify instances where this empirical gradient can
      be suboptimal for both discounted and undiscounted policy return. (Thomas, 2014) introduce the
452
      γ t method used to discount the gradient in our work, (Che et al., 2023b) refine this method by
453
      creating an estimator with lower variance. Aside a few exceptions (Tosatto et al., 2020; 2022a;b; Che
454   et al., 2023a), proper discounting remains uncommon in the deep RL literature. Several works have
455   explored training critics using off-policy data (Degris et al., 2012; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Fujimoto
456   et al., 2018), with Bhatt et al. (2024) being one of the first to streamline the critic learning process
457   by eliminating the need for target networks, which were initially popularized by Mnih (2013).
458
      Entropy regularization plays a pivotal role in numerous deep RL algorithms (Haarnoja et al., 2018;
459
      Bhatt et al., 2024). In fact, the entropy of the policy acts as a regularizer shaping the objective
460   landscape (Ahmed et al., 2019). The prevalent strategy regularizes the policy evaluation phase by
461   supplementing the standard RL task objective with an entropy term. This method guides policies
462   towards regions of higher expected trajectory entropy, a scheme often referred to as maximum en-
463   tropy RL (Ziebart, 2010; Haarnoja et al., 2018), which is recognized for enhancing the exploration
464   capabilities and robustness of policies by fostering stochasticity. Recent studies on policy gradient
465   methods have highlighted the efficacy of maximum entropy RL in speeding up convergence (Mei
466   et al., 2020; Ahmed et al., 2019; Cen et al., 2024).
467
468   7   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
469
470   In this work, we introduced PPO+, a methodical enhancement of the Proximal Policy Optimization
471   (PPO) algorithm. PPO+ rigorously adheres to the theoretical foundations of on-policy Reinforce-
472   ment Learning (RL) while maintaining a simple, trick-free implementation. PPO+ introduces three
473   key improvements over PPO, namely training the critic using off-policy data while maintaining the
474   on-policy policy gradient formulation, using the true discounted policy gradient, and employing
475   maximum entropy exploration. Moreover, by focusing on the quality of the critic approximation,
476   and consequently the surrogate objective estimator, PPO+ avoids complex critic learning schemes
477
      and implementation-level optimizations. In practice, PPO+ eliminates the use intricate critic learn-
      ing schemes used in common practices and obtains state-of-the-art performance for deep on-policy
478
      RL methods in MuJoCo locomotion problems. Thank to its simplicity and rigorous formulation, we
479
      believe that PPO+ offers an accessible and solid ground for future research on on-policy deep RL.
480   Limitations. Despite its strengths, PPO+ does not match the performance of its off-policy coun-
481   terparts, e.g., SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) or TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018). Nevertheless, we hope
482   that the simplicity of PPO+ and the insights provided in our work will inspire further interest in
483   on-policy methods. Potential directions for improvement include better strategies for correcting the
484   off-policy distribution to improve critic learning, which could potentially be integrated into actor
485   updates. Other directions may focus on improving critic learning itself, such as exploring validation
      criteria (Kallel et al., 2024) or improving the neuroplasticity of the critic (Nikishin et al., 2022).
