011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013                AI alignment is a pivotal issue concerning AI control and safety. It should con-
014                sider not only value-neutral human preferences but also moral and ethical con-
015                siderations. In this study, we introduced FairMindSim, which simulates the moral
016                dilemma through a series of unfair scenarios. We used LLM agents to simulate hu-
017
                   man behavior, ensuring alignment across various stages. To explore the various so-
                   cioeconomic motivations, which we refer to as beliefs, that drive both humans and
018
                   LLM agents as bystanders to intervene in unjust situations involving others, and
019
                   how these beliefs interact to influence individual behavior, we incorporated knowl-
020                edge from relevant sociological fields and proposed the Belief-Reward Alignment
021                Behavior Evolution Model (BREM) based on the recursive reward model (RRM).
022                Our findings indicate that, behaviorally, GPT-4o exhibits a stronger sense of so-
023                cial justice, while humans display a richer range of emotions. Additionally, we
024                discussed the potential impact of emotions on behavior. This study provides a
025                theoretical foundation for applications in aligning LLMs with altruistic values.1

028       1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030       As large language models (LLMs), also known as foundational models, increasingly engage in lan-
031       guage comprehension and content generation tasks that resemble human capabilities, a critical and
032       scientifically challenging question emerges: How can we ensure that these models’ capabilities and
033       behaviors align with human values, intentions, and ethical principles, thereby maintaining security
034       and trust in human-AI collaborative processes Bengio et al. (2024)? These concerns have spurred
035       research efforts in the field of AI alignment Bostrom (2013); Ord (2020); Bucknall & Dori-Hacohen
036       (2022), which strives to develop AI systems that act in accordance with human intentions and val-
037
          ues. This challenge extends across various domains, including economics, psychology Demszky
          et al. (2023), sociology Liu et al. (2024), and education. Additionally, human values often play a
038
          critical role in AI alignment, which we refer to as value alignment Gabriel (2020), but due to the in-
039
          herently abstract and uncertain nature of human values MacIntyre (2013), they also pose additional
040       challenges.
041
042       Recently, one significant avenue of research has focused on examining the cognitive and reasoning
043
          competencies of large language models (LLMs), benchmarking these capabilities against human
          intelligence using frameworks such as Theory of Mind Strachan et al. (2024), Turing tests Mei et al.
044
          (2024), and strategic behavior assessments Sreedhar & Chilton (2024). Another prominent research
045
          direction involves the realistic simulation of social systems. Researchers have proposed various
046       research topics in this area Critch & Krueger (2020). This encompasses rule-based agent-based
047       modeling Bonabeau (2002), deep learning-based simulation Sert et al. (2020), and simulations that
048       incorporate LLMs Li et al. (2024); Shen et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024). These simulation methods
049       have a wide range of downstream applications, including impact assessment and multi-agent social
050       learning. In the field of social sciences, a growing body of research uses agents to simulate human
051       behavior in contexts such as economic and trust games Zhao et al. (2024); Horton (2023); Xie
052       et al. (2024). While most studies presume similarities between human behaviors and those of LLM
053

      agents Manning et al. (2024), some research explicitly explores these similarities through interactive
055   dialogues with LLM agents Peters & Matz (2024).
056
057   It has been suggested that alignment research should develop within an ecosystem Drexler (2019).
058
      Current research in this area is focused on multi-agent interactions Wang et al. (2021); Xu et al.
      (2023b) and self-evolution in generally capable LLM-based agents Xi et al. (2024). These studies

      demonstrate a strong reasoning ability, potentially mimicking an ability to understand social contexts

063
064
      Beyond simple black-box testing, several important questions remain unanswered Zhu et al. (2024),
      such as whether agent values are aligned with human values in their interactions with the environ-
065
      ment, and whether these values are evolving. Addressing these questions is crucial for the trust-
066
      worthiness and alignment of AI systems Ngo et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2023a). Moreover, in this
067   ecosystem evolution, the alignment of human ethical and social values with LLM agents remains a
068   black-box question.
069
070
      In this work, considering the complexity of the real-world environment Hagendorff (2024), and
      combining the relative clarity of the definition of fairness compared to other human values, we
071
      constructed FairMindSim, which combines a traditional economics game Fehr & Gächter (2002) to
072
      simulate the moral dilemma through a series of unfair scenarios. In this case, we used the personality
073   and other information collected from the human participants in reality to define the LLM agents
074   to achieve personality alignment Zhang et al. (2024a); Huang et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2024).
075   To explore the various socioeconomic motivations Wardle & Steptoe (2003), which we refer to as
076   beliefs, that interact to influence individual altruistic behavior. And we incorporated knowledge
077   from relevant sociological fields and proposed the Belief-Reward Alignment Behavior Evolution
078   Model (BREM) based on the recursive reward model (RRM). The results indicate that GPT-4o
079   demonstrates better performance in fairness and justice compared to humans. Additionally, human
080   behavior in this scenario is influenced by emotions. The contributions of this work are summarized
081
      as follows:
082
      • Value Alignment Perspective: In terms of value alignment, we explored the issue of moral dilem-
083     mas faced by LLMs from the perspective of social psychology. It also provides corresponding
084     theoretical support for the intersection of AI and sociology.
085
      • Simulation of Moral Dilemmas: Under the Moral Dilemma, we developed FairMindSim, a simu-
086
        lation of unfair events, to compare the differences in behavior and emotion between humans and
087     LLM agents, adhering to psychological ethical standards.
088
      • Based on the RRM and integrating relevant psychological theories, we proposed the BREM model
089
        to explore the relationship between belief evolution and decision-making, comparing belief dif-
090
        ferences between humans and LLM agents, and discussing the influence of emotion.
091
092
      • Results showed that GPT-4o exhibits a higher sense of social morality, such as fairness and justice,
        whereas humans display more complex emotional stability that can affect decision-making.

097   2.1   E THICAL AND S OCIAL VALUES IN AI
098
099
      As artificial intelligence systems become increasingly integrated into various aspects of daily life,
      the importance of embedding ethical and social values in AI has grown significantly. These values
100
      guide AI systems in making decisions that align with human norms, ensuring their actions are ben-
101
      eficial and respectful of societal standards Shneiderman (2020). Ethicality, refers to a system’s un-
102   wavering commitment to uphold human norms and values within its decision-making and actions Ji
103   et al. (2023). To address the challenges of integrating ethical considerations into AI, researchers are
104   turning to the realistic simulation of social systems Fukuda-Parr & Gibbons (2021). This approach
105   enables a deeper understanding of how AI can interact with complex social dynamics and adapt to
106   the nuanced expectations of human society. By studying these simulations, developers can create AI
107   systems that not only perform tasks efficiently but also respect and reinforce the ethical frameworks
      that underpin human communities Paraman & Anamalah (2023).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      In the construction of simulated societies, LLM agents play a crucial role Ziems et al. (2024). These
109   agents, powered by AI algorithms designed to emulate human behaviors and communication modal-
110   ities, simulate individual actions and interactions within social environments Esposito (2017); Ha-
111   gendorff & Fabi (2023). Within this dynamic system, each agent acts both as an observer and par-
112   ticipant, navigating through well-defined settings that mimic the social interplay of the real world.
113   LLM agents demonstrate autonomy and complexity, capable of emulating human cognition Binz
114   & Schulz (2023), emotions Wang et al. (2023), and social behaviors Hagendorff (2023), including
115   communication, decision-making, and cooperation O’Gara (2023) and competition within groups.
116   For example, in simulated societal contexts, agents can engage in organized collaboration to ef-
117
      fectively solve problems and optimize task execution Seeber et al. (2020); Ramchurn et al. (2016).
      Additionally, they are capable of establishing and maintaining networks of interpersonal relation-
118
      ships, disseminating information through social networks, and influencing opinions and emotions
119
      within the group Mou et al. (2024).
120
121   Moreover, LLM agents can be deployed to explore ethical decision-making and game theory, sim-
122   ulating individual and collective choices in moral dilemmas and how these choices shape societal
123
      norms and values Zhang et al. (2023). Integrating the Altruistic punishment paradigm into LLM
      agents is key to developing AI systems that understand and enhance human cooperation Leng &
124
      Yuan (2023). By simulating human social behaviors and norms, LLMs can identify and address un-
125
      fairness Xi et al. (2023), promoting justice and equity in social interactions. These simulations offer
126   essential data for developing social policies that promote fairness and cooperation, while guiding AI
127   in ethical decisions. In human-AI collaboration, agents using altruistic punishment ensure fairness.
128   By learning from these mechanisms, LLMs can aid AI ethics governance as compliance guardians.

134   Alignment research does not live in a vacuum but in an ecosystem Drexler (2019); Sumers et al.
135   (2023), and we simulate an ecosystem by designing FairMindSim to explore human and llm in value
136   alignment by designing a multi-round traditional economics game where the entire ecosystem is a
137   series of unfair scenarios. In the FairMindSim as shown in Figure 1, We simulate a small ecosystem
138   which “Player1” is responsible for allocating funds each round, while “Player2” is a passive observer
139   without actual actions. “Player3” (played by a human participant or another LLM agent) observes
140   the allocation and responds to “Player1”s decisions based on their own standards of fairness. The
141
      specific algorithm is described in the Appendix Algorithm 1.
142   An agent architecture is constructed by endowing LLM with the necessary functionalities required
143   for simulating core users. On the left side of the Figure 1, the core user agent architecture based on
144   LLM is presented. Driven by LLM, the agent is equipped with a profiling module, memory module,
145   and decision-making module.
146
147
              1. Profiling Module - Describes the user’s profile using the corresponding agent’s individual
                 information, including age, gender, autism spectrum quotient scores, and anxiety scores, to
148
                 portrait personality and behavior.
149
150
              2. Memory Module - Utilizes the memory module to manage the agent’s memory.
151           3. Decision-Making Module - Answers questions related to psychological scales and executes
152              decisions for the current round.

      A simulated environment of an economic game theory experiment is constructed. In each round,
      the core user agent decides based on (1) the agent’s profile information; (2) the agent’s memory;

      3.2     B ELIEF -R EWARD A LIGNMENT B EHAVIOR E VOLUTION

250   In the context of FariMindSim, when system rewards conflict with social values, leading to the
251   ”ethical dilemma”, we disentangled the construction of the system’s objective from evaluating its
252   behavior Ibarz et al. (2018). Based on the concept of recursive reward modeling Leike et al. (2018);
253   Hubinger (2020), we proposed the Belief-Reward Alignment Behavior Evolution Model (BREM),
254   as shown in Figure 3. This model is used to study and simulate how individuals or systems maxi-
255   mize rewards by leveraging beliefs in dynamic environments. It continuously adjusts behaviors to
256   achieve better alignment and optimization between beliefs and rewards. In this process, individu-
257   als continuously update their beliefs about the state of the environment and their own behaviors by
258
      receiving and processing new information. Over time, the model achieves a dynamic balance and
      optimization of beliefs, behaviors, and reward systems, allowing us to analyze the differences in
259
      self-belief strength and belief variations among different types of individuals. In this scenario, we
260
      refer to factors that are not related to rewards but still impact subsequent behavior as beliefs Rouault
261   et al. (2019), specifically the beliefs of fairness and justice.

502   5   C ONCLUSION
503
504   Under the ethical dilemma, we simulate an ecosystem by designing FairMindSim to explore value
505   alignment between humans and LLMs. This is achieved through a multi-round traditional economics
506   game where the entire ecosystem consists of a series of unfair scenarios. In the realm of social val-
507   ues, we investigated altruism. The LLM agents were fully aligned with humans in various aspects
508
      of the experiment, such as behavioral and emotional measures. We incorporated knowledge from
      relevant sociological fields and proposed the Belief-Reward Alignment Behavior Evolution Model
509
      (BREM), based on the recursive reward model (RRM), to explore the beliefs of humans and LLM
510
      agents. It was found that GPT-4o demonstrates a higher sense of fairness and justice in unfair sce-
511   narios and does not change over time, whereas human beliefs vary across different unfair scenarios
512   and evolve to become more stable. Additionally, human emotions are more diverse compared to
513   those of LLMs.

      6   D ISCUSSION
517
518
      Regarding beliefs, most discussions about LLMs’ beliefs focus on competence-related beliefs Zhang
      et al. (2024b); Zhu et al. (2024). In the field of social sciences, factors that are unrelated to rewards
519
      but still influence subsequent behavior are called beliefs Schultz (2006). These include beliefs in
520
      integrity and honesty, respect for others, cooperation, compassion, and charity. Also included are
521   beliefs in fairness and justice. In terms of value alignment, we believe that discussions about aligning
522   these beliefs should begin with specific task design and involve collaboration with fields such as
523   sociology. We simultaneously considered the impact of emotions on decision-making and found
524   that humans are more influenced by emotions in their behaviour. This is one of the contributions of
525   our work, aiming to provide a reference for the integration of AI and sociology.

813   A.1   E THICAL AND S OCIAL VALUES IN H UMAN
814
815   In human societies, ethical and social values shape our behavioral norms and decision-making
816   frameworks Crossan et al. (2013). These values not only influence individual moral judgments and
817   choices but also play a pivotal role in broader social cooperation and group dynamics Tyler et al.
818   (1996). In promoting social cooperation and fairness, the concept of ”altruistic punishment” reveals
819   the profound impact of ethics and values Grimalda et al. (2016). Altruistic punishment refers to the
820
      phenomenon where individuals uphold social norms by punishing others, although the punishment
      is costly for them and yields no material gain Fehr & Gächter (2002). Altruistic punishment occu-
821
      pies an extremely important position in the evolutionary development of human cooperation Bowles
822
      & Gintis (2004). Within teams and organizations, altruistic punishment can promote cooperation on
823   a broader scale, even in situations that appear disadvantageous in the short term Gurerk et al. (2006).
824   Understanding the mechanisms of altruistic punishment can aid in developing more effective social
825   and economic policies that enhance fairness and cooperation Fehr & Rockenbach (2003).Although
826   altruistic punishment may seem irrational at the individual level, it plays a significant role in main-
827   taining social cooperation and fairness. Gaining a deeper understanding of its mechanisms and
828   impacts is crucial for building a more harmonious society and formulating effective policies.

902
903   In this experimental game, there are three players: player1, player2, and you, player3. The primary
904   objective of the game is to study decision-making behavior and emotional responses to different
905   allocation schemes of monetary resources. player1 has the authority to decide the allocation of a
906   certain amount of money between themselves and player2. player3, which is your role, observes
907
      the allocation outcome and has the power to make judgments on that allocation. Your emotional
      reactions to the allocation and judgments are assessed using using the emotion grid method described
908
      by Heffner.The game unfolds over 20 trials, each presenting a unique allocation situation devised by
909
      player1. You, as player3, will experience various emotional states in response to these allocations,
910   which you will report on before and after making your judgments. Your decisions can either ’Accept’
911   the allocation, granting you a monetary reward or ’Reject’ by reducing player1’s income to zero
912   and affecting your own reward based on specific conditions of the experiment. This setup aims
913   to analyze the complex interplay between fairness perception, emotional impact, and subsequent
914   decision-making.
