009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we explore the use of untrained message passing layers in graph neural
012            networks for link prediction. The untrained message passing layers we consider are
013            derived from widely used graph neural network architectures by removing trainable
014            parameters and nonlinearities in their respective message passing layers. Exper-
015            imentally we find that untrained message passing layers can lead to competitive
016
               and even superior link prediction performance compared to fully trained message
               passing layers while being more efficient and naturally interpretable, especially in
017
               the presence of high-dimensional features. We also provide a theoretical analysis
018
               of untrained message passing layers in the context of link prediction and show that
019            the inner product of features produced by untrained message passing layers relate
020            to common neighbour and path-based topological measures which are widely used
021            for link prediction. As such, untrained message passing layers offer a more efficient
022            and interpretable alternative to trained message passing layers in link prediction
023            tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) are a powerful class of machine learning models that can learn from
028
      graph-structured data, such as social networks, molecular graphs, and knowledge graphs. GNNs
029
      have emerged as an important tool in the machine learning landscape, due to their ability to model
030   complex relationships and dependencies within data and have found applications in a variety of fields
031   where data exhibits a complex topology that can be captured as a graph. This is shown by a multitude
032   of studies, including Basu et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Fu et al. (2022); Jaini et al. (2021); Puny
033   et al. (2021), which highlight the versatility and adaptability of GNNs for machine learning tasks
034   across a range of fields.
035
      One of the key concepts underlying GNNs is Message Passing (MP) (Gilmer et al., 2017), which
036   operates by propagating and aggregating information between nodes in the graph, using message and
037   update functions possibly with learnable parameters. However, designing effective GNNs can be
038   challenging, as they can suffer from issues such as over-smoothing or over-parameterization, and
039   because training GNNs can be computationally demanding. In order to address these shortcomings
040   recent efforts have concentrated on finding simplified architectures that are both more interpretable
041   and easier to optimize.
042   With our work, we aim to complement existing works on simplified and untrained MP architectures,
043   previously formulated in the context of node classification, from the perspective of link prediction
044   (Zhang & Chen, 2018). Link prediction is an important task in graph ML with many applications such
045   as recommender systems, spam mail detection, drug re-purposing, and many more (Zhang & Chen,
046   2018). Current state of the art LP methods (Wang et al., 2023) in general rely on a combination of
047   GNNs and structural features hence formulating effective, efficient and interpretable MP architectures
048   has the potential to further improve current LP methods in these regards. Therefore we focus our
049   analysis on MP layers rather than trying to formulate a novel end-to-end LP method.
050   In this work, we explore the use of untrained message passing layers for link prediction in graph
051   datasets with high dimensional features. By formulating Untrained Message Passing (UTMP) layers,
052   we follow an approach similar to that of Simplified Graph Convolutional Networks introduced by
053   Wu et al. (2019). This approach simplifies GNN architectures by removing trainable parameters
      and nonlinearities resulting in an architecture that can be clearly separated into two components:


                                                         1

054
      an untrained message passing/feature propagation steps followed by a linear classifier. In addition
055   to these we also consider fully untrained architectures based on simple inner products of features
056   obtained after l iterations of UTMP layers as a baseline and find that these features produced by
057   UTMP layers are already highly informative leading to surprisingly high link prediction performances.
058
      We base our analysis on untrained versions of four widely used MP architectures, namely Graph
059
      Convolutional Networks (GCN)(Kipf & Welling, 2016a), SAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), GraphConv
060
      (Morris et al., 2019) and GIN (Xu et al., 2018). We test these untrained message passing layers on
061   a variety of datasets that cover a wide range of sizes, node features, and topological characteristics
062   ensuring a comprehensive evaluation of the models. We test UTMP layers on a variety of datasets
063   that cover a wide range of sizes, node features, and topological characteristics ensuring a compre-
064   hensive evaluation of the UTMP layers. Mirroring the results reported by Wu et al. (2019) for node
065   classification we find that UTMP layers in many cases outperform their fully trained counterparts in
066   LP tasks while being highly interpretable and easier to optimize.
067   We also show that link prediction provides a complementary perspective for the theoretical analysis of
068   UTMP layers (Zhou et al., 2022; Chamberlain et al., 2022). In our theoretical analysis we establish a
069   direct connection between features produced by UTMP layers and various path based node similarity
070   measures. Path based measures capture the indirect connection strength between node pairs nodes in
071   the absence of a direct link connecting the nodes. Consequently, path based measures and methods
072   have been widely used in traditional link prediction methods (Martı́nez et al., 2016; Kumar et al.,
073   2020) and also play a key role in many state of the art methods (Zhu et al., 2021; Zhang & Chen,
074   2018).
075   Our theoretical analysis relies on the assumption that initial node features are orthonormal which
076   covers widely used initialization schemes such as as one-hot encodings and high dimensional
077   random features, and also holds approximately for many empirical data sets with high dimensional
078   features. Hence, our theoretical findings also provide new insights into the effectiveness of the widely
079   used initialization schemes of one-hot encodings and high dimensional random features in graph
080   representation learning. More generally our results show that untrained versions of message passing
081
      layers are highly amenable to theoretical analysis and hence could potentially serve as an general
      ansatz for the theoretical analysis of GNNs including settings beyond link prediction.
082
083   The main contributions of the paper are as follows:
084
085          • We show that untrained versions of widely used MP layers often outperform their fully
086
               trained counterparts in LP tasks.
087          • We establish a direct connection between MPNNs and path based node similarity measures
088            both of which are widely used in LP methods.
089          • Our theoretical analysis further provides insights in to the effectiveness of widely used node
090            initialization schemes such as one-hot-encodings and random features.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094
      Our work is motivated by recent works that investigate simplified and untrained GNNs from different
095
      perspectives. We formulate UTMP layers following the approach of Wu et al. (2019) which simplifies
096   GNNs by successively removing trainable parameters from layers and nonlinearities between consec-
097   utive layers. Wu et al. (2019) also provide a theoretical analysis of simplified models in the context
098   of node classification reducing the model to a fixed low-pass filter followed by a linear classifier. The
099   paper also empirically evaluates the simplified architectures on various downstream applications and
100   shows that simplified architectures do not negatively impact accuracy while being computationally
101   more efficient than their fully trained counterparts.
102   Other works have focused on finding untrained subnetworks. For instance Huang et al. (2022)
103   explores the existence of untrained subnetworks in GNNs that can match the performance of fully
104   trained dense networks at initialization, without any optimization of the weights. The paper leverages
105   sparsity as the core tool to find such subnetworks and shows that they can substantially mitigate
106   the over-smoothing problem, hence enabling deeper GNNs. The paper also shows that the sparse
107   untrained subnetworks have appealing performance in out-of-distribution detection and robustness
      to input perturbations. Similarly, in Böker et al. (2023) the authors demonstrate that GNNs with


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      randomly initialized weights, without training, can achieve competitive performance compared to
109   their trained counterparts focusing on the problem of graph classification. In Dong et al. (2023) the
110   authors show that certain common neighbour measures can be approximated by MPNNs initialised
111   with random weights and node features without training. Other more recent works on untrained
112   GNNs include Dong et al. (2024) where the authors propose a training free linear GNN model for
113   semi supervised node classification in text attributed graphs and Sato (2024) that defines training free
114   GNNs for transductive node classification based on using training labels as features.
115   Link prediction is widely studied problem with a multitude of available methods. UTMP layers are
116   related to a both GNN based methods such as Variational Graph Autoencoders (V-GAE) (Kipf &
117   Welling, 2016b) and more traditional methods that are rely on path and random walk based measures
118   for link prediction (Kumar et al., 2020). On the other hand state of the art methods such as SEAL
119   (Zhang & Chen, 2018), NBFnet (Zhu et al., 2021), BUDDY (Chamberlain et al., 2022), Neo-GNN
120   (Yun et al., 2021) and NCNC (Wang et al., 2023) in general rely on combining GNNs and structural
121   features. Some methods such as SEAL (Zhang & Chen, 2018) and WalkPool (Pan et al., 2021) are
122   based on extracting and performing MP on local subgraphs around target links effectively framing
123
      link prediction as a graph classification problem. Although subgraph extraction based methods can
      out perform purely GNN based methods in link prediction tasks the subgraph extraction process can
124
      be resource intensive for large networks negatively affecting the scalability of these methods. NBFnet
125
      (Zhu et al., 2021) is another state-of-the-art method that is motivated by the Bellman-Ford algorithm.
126   NBFnet is based on learning representations of paths between target nodes and aggregation functions
127   for these representations. While NBFnet scales more favorably compared to subgraph extraction
128   based methods it still needs to compute representations for large numbers of paths to predict links
129   and hence has worse scaling behavior compared to purely GNN based link prediction methods (Zhu
130   et al., 2021). Neo-GNN (Yun et al., 2021) and BUDDY (Chamberlain et al., 2022) circumvent these
131   difficulties by using pairwise similarity measures between higher order neighbourhoods of nodes,
132   which are then used together with GNN based node features for LP. Notably BUDDY includes
133   a feature propagation step that can be seen as a special case of UTMP (see UTSAGE in Section
134
      3.1). In Wang et al. (2023) the authors propose a GNN based approach that in addition to using
      node level features also aggregates features of common neighbours for link prediction and further
135
      propose Neural Common Neighbour Completion (NCNC) to counteract the negative effects of graph
136
      incompleteness on LP performance.
137
138   As detailed above GNNs are widely employed as sub-components in LP methods. Hence rather than
139   proposing a new method for link prediction we consider the advantages of using UTMP layers over
140
      their trained counterparts in existing LP methods. Moreover, our theoretical results establish a link
      between MP based approaches and structural features by showing that features resulting from UTMP
141
      implicitly encode neighbourhood information that underlies many widely used common neighbour
142
      and path based structural features.
143
144   From a theoretical perspective, our results also relate to recent works on the effectiveness of random
145   node initializations and one-hot encodings. For instance Sato et al. (2021) and Abboud et al. (2020)
146
      focus on the effect of random node initializations of the expressivity of GNNs in the context of graph
      classification while Cui et al. (2022) explores various encodings for the task including node and graph
147
      classification. Our analysis complements these works from the point of view of LP and establishes a
148
      link between features derived from random and one hot initializations and, path based topological
149   features.
150
151   Finally, in our theoretical analysis we rely on the fact that collections of high dimensional vectors
152
      tend to be mutually orthogonal. This is a widely know fact that has wide raging applications in
      ML more broadly given the pervasive use of high dimensional vector representations in modern ML
153
      methods (Kanerva, 2009; 2018).

326   Triadic closure, also known as transitivity, refers to the tendency for nodes in real-world networks to
327   form connections if they share (many) common neighbors. As such triadic closure has been widely
328   studied as a mechanism that drives link formation in complex real-world networks (Rapoport, 1953;
329   Holland & Leinhardt, 1971). Moreover, node similarity measures that build on triadic closure in
330   social networks have been used for similarity-based link prediction algorithms (Lü & Zhou, 2011).

      4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
370
      To ensure a fair comparison among models we maintain the same overall architectures across all
371
      experiments and MP layers. For trainable message passing layers, each layer is followed by an
372   Exponential Linear Unit (ELU) and the optimal number of layers for models is determined via
373   hyperparameter search. Upon completion of the message passing layers, we introduce a final linear
374   layer for both trained and simplified models. We also consider untrained (UT) models that do not
375   include this final linear layer and directly take the inner product between the propagated features of
376   the source and target nodes resulting in a parameter-free and hence fully untrained model. Since the
377   simplified architectures consist of UTMP layers followed by a trainable linear layer, the consideration
      of UT models which do not include the linear layer also covers all possible ablation studies.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
406
      In the following section, we discuss the results of our experiments for link prediction in graphs
407
      with node attributes (i.e. in graphs where nodes have additional features) and non-attributed graphs
408   separately. This diverse selection of data sets allows us to thoroughly evaluate the capabilities of the
409   models for graphs from different application scenarios, with different sizes, and different topological
410   characteristics.
411
      Results for attributed graphs are given in Table 1 where we find that the simplified model SGCN
412
      performs best on 5 out of 7 datasets in terms of Hits@100. Moreover, we find that simple GAE
413
      type architectures based on UTMP layers can outperform more sophisticated state-of-the-art models
414   such as SEAL, NBFnet and Neo-GNN (See Table 9 in the Appendix). In general, our experiments
415   show that simplified models tend to perform better than or on par with their fully trained counterparts
416   on almost all datasets in terms of Hits@100. This demonstrates that the raw features produced by
417   UTMP layers, which the simplified models are trained on, are already highly informative for link
418   prediction in accordance with our theoretical results. Note that, the fully untrained (UT) models can
419   be computed efficiently via sparse matrix multiplication.
420   We observe that replacing trained GCN layers with their untrained counterpart also improves the
421   LP performance of NCNC. Indeed, some of the results reported in Wang et al. (2023) seem to be
422   obtained using UTGCN layers.
423
      In the case of non-attributed graphs (Table 2) we observe that models based on UTMP layers achieve
424
      the highest score on 6 out of 8 datasets, with the exceptions being NS and Router datasets. Moreover,
425
      we find that the fully untrained UTGCN model performs best on the ’Celegans’, ’PB’, ’USAir’, ’E-
426   coli’ which can be attributed to the reduced dimension of the learned features that come with the linear
427   layers present in the simplified and fully trained models. Furthermore, as we used one hot encodings
428   as initial node features for the unattributed datasets orthonormality is satisfied exactly and therefore
429   there is a one-to-one correspondence between the UT models and path based topological measures.
430   We also find that using UTGCN layers in NCNC instead of trained GCN layers also improves link
431   prediction performance on 6 out of 7 datasets. Additional results for NCNC experiments can be

      Finally, we also examine the effect of increasing the number of UTMP layers using fully untrained
447   (UT) models. Our results in Fig.1 indicate that, in general, UTGCN and UTSAGE maintain their
448   performance as the number of layers is increased whereas the performance of UTGIN decreases
449   sharply with more layers. This behavior can be attributed to the lack of degree based normalization in
450   the formulation of GIN (see Sec.3.3) which leads UTGIN to be dominated by longer paths, and hence
451   longer distance correlations, as the number of layers increases. In general however we find that UTMP
452   layers do not suffer from over-squashing when equipped with proper degree based normalisation
453   which can be attributed to the absence of nonlinearities and mixing between feature dimensions in
454   UTMP layers.

472   5    C ONCLUSION
473
474   In this work, we explored the application of graph neural networks with untrained message passing
475   layers for link prediction. Interestingly, our experimental evaluation of twelve data sets shows that
476   simplifying GNNs architectures by eliminating trainable parameters and nonlinearities not only
477   enhances the link prediction performance of GNNs, but also improves their interpretability and
478   training efficiency. As such untrained message passing layers offer a computationally efficient
479   alternative to their fully trained counterparts that naturally scales to large graphs. To complement our
480   experimental results, we offered a theoretical perspective on untrained message passing, analytically
481   establishing links between features generated by untrained message passing layers and path-based
482
      topological measures. We found that the link prediction offers a complementary perspective for
      analysing MPNNs and provides insights into the topological features captured by widely used
483
      initialization schemes such as random features and one-hot encodings.
484
485   In future work, we hope to extend our study to other classes of graphs, such as directed, signed,
      weighted, and temporal networks. The conceptual simplicity of untrained message passing layers


                                                                                                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      might also be a useful guide in designing new graph neural network architectures or adapting existing
487   architectures to directed or temporal networks. We thus believe that our work is of interest both for
488   the community of researchers developing new machine learning methods, as well as for practitioners
489   seeking to deploy efficient and resource-saving models in real-world scenarios.

      B    E XPERIMENTAL SETUP AND H YPERPARAMETER CHOICES
675
676   For each model, the optimal values of the learning rate, the number of layers, and hidden dimensions
677
      are determined through an exhaustive search over the values given in Table 4). The optimal hyperpa-
      rameters values for attributed and non-attributed datasets are given in Table 5 and Table 6, respectively.
678
      We implement a three-fold cross-validation procedure to select the optimal hyperparameter values.
679
680   We use Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) as an optimization function and employ binary cross entropy
681   with logits as our loss function. All datasets are preprocessed by normalizing the node features
682   and randomly splitting them. For non-attributed datasets, 10% of the data is allocated to the test
683
      set, 5% to the validation set Zhang & Chen (2018), and the remaining data is used for the training
      set. In contrast, for attributed datasets, the split is 20% for the test set, 10% for the validation set,
684
      and the remainder for the training set Wang et al. (2023).Each model configuration is run 10 times,
685
      with the results averaged over these runs. Our training and testing procedures are based on the
686   methodology outlined in pyGteam (2021), where we perform a new round of negative edge sampling
687   for each training epoch. We limit the maximum number of epochs to 10,000 and also incorporate
688   an early stopping mechanism in our training process by terminating training whenever there is no
689   improvement in the validation set results over a span of 250 epochs.
690
      All hyperparameter searches and experiments were conducted on a workstation with AMD Ryzen
691
      Threadripper PRO 5965WX 24-Cores with 256 GB of memory and two Nvidia GeForce RTX 3090
692   Super GPU, and also AMD Ryzen 9 7900X 12-Cores with 64 GB of memory and an Nvidia GeForce
693   RTX 4080 GPU.

730   D      RUNTIME A NALYSIS AND T RAINING E FFICENCY
731
732   Efficiency of SMPNNs While we allocated a very generous limit of 10,000 epochs for training
733   models in the main paper to ensure models can reach their best possible performance in order to
734   compare the computational efficiency of the simplified models to their fully trained counterparts we
735   also consider an experimental setting where we restrict the maximum number of training epochs
736
      to 100. We find that simplified models achieved convergence even for larger learning rates and
      considerably faster than their fully trained models. Even when constrained to 100 training epochs
737
      simplified models maintain scores that are almost identical to those presented in Table 8, while fully
738
      trained architectures suffer from the increased learning rates and require in general more epochs to
739   converge. This leads to training efficiency gains similar to those reported by Wu et al. (2019) in the
740   case of node classification.
741
742
      In Table 8, it is evident that the simplified models consistently outperform the fully trained models
      across all datasets by a considerable margin. Furthermore, as demonstrated in Table 1, the fully
743
      trained models nearly achieve their peak accuracy within just 100 epochs, indicating that extended
744
      training offers minimal additional benefit. This also implies that the Simplified models are more
745   efficient in terms of both time and resources required for training.
746
747   The hyperpameter space used for the computational efficiency experiments is the same as in Table5,
748
      except that we only use 100 epochs.

      This characteristic of UT models leads to a substantial reduction in both time consumption and

      electricity costs.
