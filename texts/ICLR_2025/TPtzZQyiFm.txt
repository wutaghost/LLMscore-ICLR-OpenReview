                                                A BSTRACT
013
014
              Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have demonstrated remarkable
015
              capabilities in processing and generating content across multiple data modalities.
016
              However, a significant drawback of MLLMs is their reliance on static training
017           data, leading to outdated information and limited contextual awareness. This
018           static nature hampers their ability to provide accurate and up-to-date responses,
019           particularly in dynamic or rapidly evolving contexts. Though integrating Mul-
020           timodal Retrieval-augmented Generation (Multimodal RAG) offers a promising
021           solution, the system would inevitably encounter the multi-granularity noisy cor-
022           respondence (MNC) problem, which hinders accurate retrieval and generation.
023           In this work, we propose RagVL, a novel framework with knowledge-enhanced
024           reranking and noise-injected training, to address these limitations. We instruction-
025
              tune the MLLM with a simple yet effective instruction template to induce its
              ranking ability and serve it as a reranker to precisely filter the top-k retrieved
026
              images. For generation, we inject visual noise during training at the data and to-
027
              ken levels to enhance the generator’s robustness. Extensive experiments on four
028           datasets verify the effectiveness of our method. Code and models are available at
029           https://anonymous.4open.science/r/RagVL-F694.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As an attempt towards Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), Large Language Models (LLMs) have
035   made significant strides in language understanding and human-like text generation (Brown et al.,
036   2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). However, true AGI requires more than just lin-
037   guistic capabilities. It necessitates a comprehensive understanding and interaction with the world,
038   encompassing multiple modalities beyond text. Thus, the recent progress of Multimodal Large Lan-
039
      guage Models (MLLMs) in handling multimodal information has attracted the community. By pro-
      cessing and generating content across different modalities, MLLMs aim to create a more holistic and
040
      nuanced understanding of the world, closer to how humans perceive and interpret information. This
041
      integration of modalities enables MLLMs to perform tasks that require contextual understanding
042   from multiple data sources, such as Visual Question Answering (VQA) (Goyal et al., 2017; Hudson
043   & Manning, 2019; Marino et al., 2019), Table Question Answering (Lu et al., 2022), Text-to-image
044   Generation (Ramesh et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Aghajanyan et al., 2022), etc.
045
      Nevertheless, the promising performance of language models primarily relies on the knowledge
046
      implicitly stored in their massive parameters, leading to several issues such as long-tail knowl-
047
      edge gaps (Asai et al., 2024), generating hallucinations (Ye & Durrett, 2022), and poor model
048   interpretability. To better adapt to knowledge-intensive tasks and real-world scenarios, Retrieval-
049   augmented Language Models (RALM) (Lewis et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2023; Izacard & Grave, 2020;
050   Karpukhin et al., 2020) employ a dense retriever to retrieve up-to-date knowledge from external
051   memories for grounded generation. Similarly, Multimodal Retrieval-augmented Generation (Multi-
052   modal RAG) enhances MLLMs by dynamically retrieving relevant information from external multi-
053   modal data sources before generation. This allows the models to incorporate real-time, contextually
      accurate visual information, significantly improving the factuality and accuracy of their outputs.

072   As illustrated in Figure 1, to answer the information-seeking query, the model must retrieve and
073   reason over external visual knowledge, which differs from traditional VQA on the left and is non-
074   trivial. To solve this, MuRAG (Chen et al., 2022) makes the first endeavor to extend RAG to multiple
075
      modalities. It is built upon ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) and pre-trained
      to encode image-text pairs for both answer generation and retrieval. MuRAG embeds items into an
076
      external memory and handles queries for retrieving multimodal knowledge from the same memory.
077
078   However, integrating multimodal RAG would inevitably introduce the multi-granularity noisy cor-
079   respondence problem (MNC) (Huang et al., 2021). As shown in Figure 1, MNC refers to the noise
080   at two different granularities: (I) Coarse-grained noise (query-caption). During the retrieval stage,
081
      coarse-grained captions result in retrieving similar but negative images (e.g., ‘Uxmal Gobernador
      Uxmal,Yucatan, Mexico Governer’s Palace, seen from House of the Old Woman’ and ‘Palacio del
082
      Gobernador-Uxmal-Yucatan-Mexico0277 Palace of the Governor in Uxmal’). (II) Fine-grained
083
      noise (query-image). The retriever and generator must distinguish fine-grained visual elements to
084   formulate the responses. Any discrepancies between the images and the question can introduce
085   noise, thereby compromising the accuracy of the results. In this scenario, the classical CLIP (Rad-
086   ford et al., 2021) struggles to match the query with the image during the retrieval phase (see in
087   Table 1). Also, identifying the correct correspondence amidst the fine-grained noise to provide an
088   answer to the query is a challenge.
089
      To this end, we propose RagVL, a novel framework with knowledge-enhanced reranking and noise-
090   injected training, to mitigate MNC in multimodal RAG. In the retrieval stage, we instruction-tune
091   the MLLM with a simple yet effective instruction template to induce its ranking ability. Given that
092   MLLMs are inherently capable of understanding cross-modal information, we employ the fine-tuned
093   model as a reranker to evaluate the relevance between the query and the image, which precisely
094   selects top-N candidates that are more related to the query semantically. Subsequently, we apply an
095   adaptive threshold to filter the candidates, collaborating with the reranker to alleviate the fine-grained
096   mismatches. To further mitigate the impact of fine-grained mismatches during the generation phase,
097   we introduce noise at both data and token levels in the training process. Specifically, at the data level,
098
      we perform negative sampling for single-image input questions within the single/multiple-image
      interleaved dataset, supplementing them with references from hard negative images. At the token
099
      level, we introduce additional visual uncertainty to images through Gaussian noise and reassign
100
      training loss weights by comparing the logits of the distorted and original inputs.
101
102   In a nutshell, the main contributions of this work are as follows:
103
104
      • We achieve effective and robust multimodal retrieval-augmented generation with a three-stage
        pipeline. Additionally, we address the inherent multi-granularity noisy correspondence (MNC)
105
        problem in multimodal retrieval-augmented generation.
106
107   • We introduce the knowledge-enhanced reranking and noise-injected training technique to mitigate
        the coarse-grained and fine-grained noise from MNC.

113
114   2.1   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
115
      Recent advances in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have demonstrated impressive
116
      performances in handling multi-format information (Driess et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Achiam
117
      et al., 2023). MLLMs are generally built upon existing Large Language Models (LLMs) and inte-
118   grating visual information as input tokens by utilizing an additional vision encoder (e.g. CLIP) and
119   a bridging connector (e.g. MLP). For instance, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b;a) adopts one/two linear
120   MLP to project visual tokens and align the feature dimension with word embeddings, while BLIP-
121   2 (Li et al., 2023) leverages a group of learnable query tokens to extract information in a query-based
122   manner. By connecting the visual and textual modalities, MLLMs significantly enhance human-AI
123   interaction and demonstrate remarkable capabilities in understanding and generating multimodal
124   content. Despite these advances, MLLMs tend to underperform in knowledge-intensive tasks (e.g.
125   WebQA and MultimodalQA (Talmor et al., 2021)) that require seeking up-to-date information. Since
126
      the knowledge stored in their massive parameters is currently limited, it is crucial for MLLMs to re-
      sort to external memories for grounded generation.
127
128
129   2.2   M ULTIMODAL R ETRIEVAL - AUGMENTED G ENERATION
130
      Enhancing language models by incorporating relevant information from diverse knowledge sources
131
      has been shown to improve performance across various NLP tasks (Borgeaud et al., 2022; Lewis
132   et al., 2020). DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020) trains the retriever using in-batch documents and sam-
133   ples negative examples for contrastive learning, allowing the pre-trained retriever to excel in open-
134   domain question answering. REALM (Guu et al., 2020) and RAG (Lewis et al., 2020) treat the re-
135   trieved passages as latent variables and train the retriever-generator system jointly, leading to more
136   effective retrieval-augmented generation models. Inspired by textual RAG, Plug-and-play (Tiong
137   et al., 2022) retrieves relevant image patches using GradCAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) to local-
138   ize relevant parts based on the query. MuRAG (Chen et al., 2022) proposes the first multimodal
139   retrieval-augmented Transformer, which accesses an external non-parametric multimodal memory
140
      to augment language generation. To better connect candidates and model their relations during re-
      trieval, SKURG (Yang et al., 2023) employs an Entity-centered Fusion Encoder to align sources
141
      from different modalities and determines the number of retrieval steps adaptively using a unified
142
      Retrieval-generation Decoder. However, none of these works specifically focus on MNC in multi-
143   modal RAG, which is the primary focus of our research. Experimental results show that the proposed
144   knowledge-enhanced reranking and noise-injected training effectively improves multimodal RAG.

149   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
150
      The traditional Retrieval-augmented Language Model (RALM) acquires knowledge from the exter-
151
      nal memory M and utilizes the knowledge in grounded outputs to promote accurate and explainable
152
      generation. The retriever R first retrieves the top-K most relevant contexts C = {c1 , · · · , ck } from
153   M for the given question q. Subsequently, the autoregressive language model generates answers
154   based on these retrieved contexts. Under the multimodal setting, the retriever needs to compare the
155   textual queries with the multimodal documents and find the best matches for the generator G. In this
156   paper, we focus on retrieving the visual-related contexts to study open-world multimodal question
157   answering.

193   3.3    I NDUCING R ANKING A BILITY OF MLLM S
194
195   CLIP stands out across a wide range of multimodal representations and retrieval tasks as a power-
196   ful and highly transferable model. However, when encountering long-tail distribution or domain-
197   specific terms, CLIP fails to match the proper pairs across text and images. It results in lower
198   accuracy and higher demand of k value to increase the recall rate of supporting materials, which is
199   time- and resource-consuming. More importantly, simply aligning different modalities is insufficient
200   for real-time multimodal queries, leading to severe performance degradation in knowledge-intensive
201
      situations (see in Table 1). To mitigate this, we resort to MLLMs for their capabilities of semantic
      understanding. In general, MLLMs are pre-trained on vast image-text pairs for feature alignment and
202
      fine-tuned on language-image instruction-tuning datasets for instruction following. With this pre-
203
      injected multimodal knowledge, they are inherently capable of understanding semantically relevant
204   contents across both visual and textual modalities at a deeper level, while CLIP merely computes
205   similarity between vectors. Therefore, to mitigate the bottleneck challenge of multimodal RAG, we
206   introduce the flexible knowledge-enhanced reranking to induce the ranking ability of MLLMs.
207
208
      Ranking Data Construction To enhance the ranking capability of MLLMs, we construct the
209
      instruction-following data based on each multimodal QA dataset. We treat each query and the
210   ground truth images as relevant, while the hard negative images as irrelevant. We construct two
211   types of ranking tasks and require the model to generate ‘Yes’ for the relevant pairs and ‘No’ for the
212   irrelevant pairs. Intuitively, the caption-aware style brings more additional knowledge for the model
213   to distinguish the relevance between the image and query. Therefore, we train the reranker with the
214   caption-aware ranking task. In addition, the instruction tuning for ranking can be either blended
215   into the supervised fine-tuning of downstream tasks or conducted separately. See the details of the
      instruction template in Table 9.

      Adaptive Threshold Since the reranked images might still have low relevance p to the query, they
229   can negatively affect answer generation, potentially performing worse than not including the images.
230   To further improve the retrieval accuracy, we apply an adaptive threshold η to filter out candidates
231   when p < η. We set two types of thresholds: the natural threshold and the adaptive threshold.
232   The natural threshold refers to η = 0.5, which is the natural boundary for our binary classification
233   ranking. For more precise retrieval, we experiment on the validation set and utilize the intersection
234   point of the interpolated curve of exact match and mismatch as the adaptive threshold. In this way,
235   the model can avoid the distraction from irrelevant images. By forcing the MLLM to jointly consider
236   the query, caption, and image, the simple yet effective question template stimulates and enhances the
237
      model’s ranking ability with multimodal knowledge, thereby supporting the trustworthy generation.

239   3.4   N OISE - INJECTED T RAINING
240
      Compared to providing a fixed number of images each time, the task with single/multiple images
241
      interleaved is more aligned with real-world scenarios. However, it also presents challenges in deter-
242   mining the optimal number of images to provide each time and in extracting relevant information
243   rather than distracting information from the provided images. Though the reranker performs well in
244   selecting relevant images, irrelevant ones still inevitably disturb the accurate generation.
245
      Inspired by VCD (Leng et al., 2024), visual uncertainty amplifies language priors, and contrasting
246
      the logits obtained from the enhanced language priors with the original logits can better highlight
247
      visual relevance. In light of this, we propose enhancing the model’s robustness by injecting vi-
248   sual noise during training, both at the data level and token level: (I) For single-image/multi-image
249   interleaved datasets, we sample randomly from the hard negatives to ensure that each instruction-
250   following data has the same amount of image input. (II) We introduce additional visual uncertainty
251   by applying a Gaussian noise mask and contrasting the logits to reweight the loss for each token.
252
253   Noise-injected Data Construction For datasets that may require both single and multiple image
254   inputs, we standardize the number of image inputs for each sample in the instruction-following data
255   to the maximum number needed for any question. In the case of WebQA, where each question
256   requires 1-2 images for answering, we randomly sample 1 image from the hard negatives as an
257   injected noise for the single-image query. The model is required to distinguish between relevant and
258
      irrelevant visual information, which strengthens its capability of visual understanding.
259
260
      Noise-injected Logits Contrasting Although injecting noise into the dataset can help the model
      better adapt to noisy environments, it can also be a double-edged sword, making the training process
261
      more unpredictable. Instead of the simple Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) loss, we need a
262
      more robust objective (Xiao et al., 2024) to guide the model to learn the correlation between visual
263   tokens and textual (query) tokens accurately. Thus, we resort to reweight the training loss and first
264   employ the forward diffusion process (Ho et al., 2020) to distort the image:

289   Datasets and Evaluation Metrics For evaluation, we consider the image-related subsets of two

291
      seeking query-answer pairs. Since the test set labels from both datasets are not publicly available, the

292
      sets are sourced from the original validation sets. Each query is associated with a set of hard negative
293
      distractors so that two evaluation setups can be used, namely distractor and full-wiki. However, we

295   pipeline. Additionally, we conduct more experiments on Flickr30K (Young et al., 2014) and MS-

299   4.2    E VALUATION ON M ULTIMODAL K NOWLEDGE - SEEKING
300
301   Results of Retrieval Table 1 shows the performance of knowledge-enhanced rerankers on Mulit-
302   modalQA and WebQA. The experimental results show that the retriever performs weakly regarding
303
      precise recall (R@1 and R@2) on both datasets, making it difficult for accurate multimodal retrieval-
      augmented generation. Since the captions from the two datasets are basically names of objects or
304
      places, it isn’t easy to adapt to the scenarios using vanilla contrastive learning, as proven in the
305
      table. After inducing the ranking abilities of MLLMs, our proposed method effectively improves
306   the retrieval performance by a large margin. Specifically, with five MLLMs, our method consis-
307   tently improves R@2 on WebQA by an average of 40%. The results are significantly improved after
308   reranking the Top-K candidates from four different retrievers. Notably, on MultimodalQA, it reaches
309   the upper limit of Recall@20 (98.26%) from CLIP on LLaVA-v1.5-13B and InternVL2-1/2B. These
310   demonstrate the superior performance of our proposed method in multimodal knowledge retrieval.
311
312   Generalizabilities of Caption-aware Instruction Tuning To further validate the generalizability
313   of our method, on one hand, we test the reranker fine-tuned on the WebQA training set on MMQA.
314   As shown in Figure 3a, the reranker trained on WebQA exhibits competitive performance in terms of
315
      R@1, R@5, and R@10, and even matches the original reranker’s performance with InternVL2-1/2B.
      On the other hand, we randomly select different portions of data from WebQA to train InternVL2-2B
316
      in a low-resource setting, and obtain the probability distribution of the reranker outputting ‘Yes’ for
317
      correctly recalled images. Figure 3b shows the robust performance of our proposed method under the
318   low-resource settings. With only 2.5% of the original data, the reranker significantly outperforms the
319   strong retriever baseline, InternVL-G, in terms of R@2. As the data scale increases, the probability
320   of correctly recalling images also improves, stabilizing around 20%, and the recall metrics follow
321   a similar trend. In summary, these two points fully demonstrate the strong generalizability of our
322   proposed method across datasets as well as in low-resource settings within the same dataset, making
323   it easily adaptable to more scenarios. We provide an evaluation on LLaVA-v1.5-13B and make
      further discussion in Appendix F.

      5 C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we present a robust framework for enhancing Multimodal Large Language Models
532
      (MLLMs) through knowledge-enhanced reranking and noise-injected training to tackle the multi-
533
      granularity noisy correspondence (MNC) problem in multimodal retrieval-augmented generation.
534   Our comprehensive approach addresses both coarse-grained and fine-grained noise, significantly
535   improving retrieval accuracy and generation robustness. The results from our extensive exper-
536   iments on the WebQA and MultimodalQA datasets demonstrate the superiority of our proposed
537   method, especially in scenarios requiring fine-grained visual understanding and robust generation.
538   By instruction-tuning MLLMs for reranking and injecting visual noise during training, we enhance
539   the model’s capability to handle real-world noisy data and improve its overall performance in mul-
      timodal tasks.

       H    C OMPARISON OF C ONTRASTIVE L OGITS C ALCULATION IN VCD AND
991         R AG VL
992
993    Although both our method and VCD use contrastive logit calculation, there are fundamental differ-
994    ences in their implementation and motivation. Our approach employs contrastive logit calculation
995
       during fine-tuning, rather than inference. VCD, by contrast, applies this calculation exclusively
       during inference and does not involve fine-tuning. Additionally, we introduce two types of noise
996
       during training: token-level noise and data-level noise (negatively sampled images). VCD only in-
997
       corporates token-level noise during inference. By injecting noise at both levels during training, we
998    leverage the ∆logits as visual correlation weights to reassign the loss for each token, guiding the
999    model to focus on relevant visual elements. Importantly, inference in our method involves standard
1000   decoding, not contrastive decoding.
1001
       We draw inspiration from VCD, where visual uncertainty amplifies language priors, and contrasting
1002
       the logits obtained from enhanced language priors with the original logits better highlights visual
1003
       relevance. However, our motivation extends beyond mitigating irrelevant factors from a single re-
1004   trieved image to addressing those arising from multiple images. In contrast, VCD focuses on better
1005   attending to visual tokens within a single ground truth image. Thus, the motivations underlying our
1006   use of contrastive logits differ fundamentally from those of VCD.
