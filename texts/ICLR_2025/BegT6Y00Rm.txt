009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Predicting the behavior of AI-driven agents is particularly challenging without a
012            preexisting model. In our paper, we address this by treating AI agents as stochastic
013            nonlinear dynamical systems and adopting a probabilistic perspective to predict
014            their statistical behavior using the Fokker-Planck equation. We formulate the
015            approximation of the density transfer operator as an entropy minimization problem,
016            which can be solved by leveraging the Markovian property and decomposing
017            its spectrum. Our data-driven methodology simultaneously approximates the
018
               Markov operator to perform prediction of the evolution of the agents and also
               predicts the terminal probability density of AI agents, such as robotic systems
019
               and generative models. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our prediction model
020
               through extensive experiments on practical systems driven by AI algorithms.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Autonomous agents operate in dynamic environments, making decisions based on continuous feed-
026   back that enables them to learn and adapt over time. Therefore, studying the behavior and alignment
027   of these AI-driven agents is critical for several reasons. Analyzing their actions can help prevent
028   behaviors that conflict with human values and ethical standards [Rossi & Mattei (2019), Doshi
029   & Gmytrasiewicz (2005)]. Furthermore, understanding their behavior is essential for enhancing
030   their efficiency and reliability, particularly in safety-critical applications such as autonomous robots
031   [Pourmehr & Dadkhah (2012)]. These intelligent models are typically complex, high-dimensional,
032   and only partially observable over short time intervals. This complexity raises the question of
033
      which properties can be efficiently quantified to truly understand their capabilities. The design and
      understanding of AI components embedded within these agents depend crucially on analyzing the
034
      interplay between AI-driven decision-making and the physical behavior of the closed-loop system.
035
      This capability is foundational for users to perceive, predict, and interact effectively with intelligent
036   systems. It also provides the theoretical and technical basis for practical tasks such as decision-making
037   and reinforcement learning [Levine et al. (2016), Ganzfried & Sandholm (2011)].
038
039
      Given these challenges, it is important to develop methods capable of harnessing critical information
      to identify the behavior of AI components in closed-loop systems. In Déletang et al. (2021) and
040
      Roy et al. (2022), the authors try to model the AI agent as a system that was pre-trained using
041
      reinforcement learning and the environment is a partially-observable Markov decision process. The
042   authors in Dipta et al. (2022) present a card game and leverage the game theory framework to
043   analyze learning agents’ characteristics with environmental changes. Moreover, Lee & Popović
044   (2010) try capture a rich set of behavior variations by determining the appropriate reward function in
045   the reinforcement learning framework. For a comprehensive review of literature on modeling and
046   predicting AI agents behavior, we refer readers to Albrecht & Stone (2018). Among these emerging
047   methodologies, there has been a notable increase in modeling these behaviors as nonlinear dynamical
048   systems [Narendra & Parthasarathy (1992); Beer (1995); Suttle et al. (2024); Ijspeert et al. (2002)].
049   Originating from studies in partial differential equations (PDEs) and fluid mechanics, techniques such
050
      as Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) and its generalizations have also demonstrated significant
      capability in revealing the underlying evolutionary laws of AI agents [ Schmid (2022), Brunton et al.
051
      (2021)].
052
053   Despite these advances made in the above mentioned works chaotic behavior resulting from nonlin-
      earity and stochasticity encountered in practical problems pose significant challenges to deterministic

054
      modeling and identification. However, despite the substantial uncertainties, and sensitivities to initial
055   conditions affecting AI agents, their statistical behavior and properties can be surprisingly regular.
056   Specifically, when the closed-loop dynamics of AI agents are time-invariant, the density evolution
057   of the agents’ states follows a Markovian property [Risken (1996), Gardiner (2009), Hoehn et al.
058   (2005)].
059
      These observations motivate analyzing this problem from a statistical perspective. In particular,
060
      modeling the evolution trajectories of the state via a stochastic process and then studying the transfer
061   and propagation of the probability density functions, has gathered a lot of attention. This approach,
062   drawing from statistical mechanics, assumes that the agents trajectories are independent and governed
063   by the same Fokker-Planck equation, which characterizes the statistical behavior of agents based
064   on their underlying dynamics, showing significant advantages in handling systems with complex,
065   high-dimensional nonlinear chaotic dynamics and noise perturbations [Risken (1996); Lasota &
066   Mackey (2013)]. Another motivation for analyzing the evolution of densities arises from generative
067   AI. The characteristics of the sampling probability density are often intricate. Traditional statistical
068   models frequently fall short due to the high dimensionality of the data and the inherent complexities
069
      of the sampling process. Generative models, such as those based on denoising diffusion processes
      [Ho et al. (2020)] or iterative reward-based sampling methods using transformers [Kingma (2013)],
070
      introduce complex stochastic dynamics. These dynamics are challenging to analyze at the level of
071
      individual samples. Instead, adopting a macroscopic perspective and focusing on the evolution of
072   probability densities induced by these models enables the study of the aggregate behavior of complex
073   models and a better understanding of their underlying mechanisms.
074
075
      The application of probabilistic models to learn and predict the statistical behavior of complex AI
      agents has gained increasing attention in areas such as autonomous driving, motion planning, and
076
      human-robot interaction [Lefèvre et al. (2014); Trautman et al. (2015); Bai et al. (2015); Rasouli et al.
077
      (2017)]. However, algorithms based on this probabilistic perspective, particularly those studying the
078   propagation of density processes, remain underexplored. Several open challenges persist in advancing
079   this field, especially in developing unified frameworks that bridge the gap between domain-specific
080   methodologies and general modeling approaches [Baarslag et al. (2016)].
081
      In this work, we focus on virtual or physical agents with stochastic dynamics, which are controlled or
082
      operated by AI algorithms. We model the evolution of their state distributions as a Markov chain
083
      (or process). Unlike existing approaches that primarily predict agent evolution, our work adopts a
084   macroscopic/statistical mechanics perspective. Our aim is to develop algorithms capable of predicting
085   not only the propagation of future densities but also the stationary distribution of the Markov process,
086   which corresponds to the controlled objective applied to the AI agents.
087
      Specifically, we utilize the spectral decomposition theorem [Lasota & Mackey (2013)] for Markov
088
      operators to decompose the propagation of the Markov transfer operator into a transient decaying term
089
      and projections onto a set of cyclical bases representing the asymptotic behavior. As demonstrated
090   in our analysis, this approach significantly simplifies the learning and representation of Markov
091   processes, while also facilitating the prediction of the agents’ future behavior from a macroscopic
092   perspective.

094   1.1     R ELATED W ORK
095
096   The behavior prediction of AI-driven agents has been a growing focus in various domains, including
097   robotics, generative modeling, and reinforcement learning. A number of approaches have been
098   proposed, but they differ significantly in methodology, interpretability, and scope. Below, we situate
099   our work within these efforts, identifying gaps that our approach aims to address.
100
101
      1.1.1    S TATISTICAL M ODELING OF AI AGENTS
102   Modeling AI agents as stochastic systems has been explored in works like Goswami et al. (2018), who
103   use constrained Ulam Dynamic Mode Decomposition to approximate Perron-Frobenius operators
104   for deterministic and stochastic systems. Norton et al. (2018) proposes finite volume methods for
105   numerical approximation, which form the foundation for deep learning-based operator approximation
106   methods, such as DeepONets and Fourier Neural Operators [Li et al. (2021); De Ryck & Mishra
107   (2022)]. However, these methods prioritize scalability and numerical efficiency, often at the cost of
      interpretability and robustness when applied to systems with high-dimensional chaotic dynamics.

      Our approach addresses this gap by adopting a spectral decomposition framework, which not only
109   approximates the Markov operator but also provides interpretable insights into the evolution and
110   asymptotic behavior of densities.
111
112   1.1.2    B EHAVIOR P REDICTION AND R EACHABILITY A NALYSIS
113
114   Several works have examined the behavior of AI agents through reachability analysis. Meng et al.
115   (2022) directly learn state transfer functions, while Everett et al. (2021) and Zhang et al. (2023)
116
      analyze neural network-controlled systems to estimate their reachable states. These approaches focus
      on trajectory-specific predictions, which can become computationally expensive for systems with
117
      high-dimensional state spaces or stochastic dynamics.
118
119   Our method departs from these by adopting a macroscopic perspective: rather than tracking individual
120   trajectories, we model the evolution of probability densities. This provides computational advantages
121   and insights into the statistical behavior of systems without requiring exhaustive trajectory-level
122
      analysis.
123
124
      1.1.3    G ENERATIVE M ODELING AND D IFFUSION P ROCESSES
125   Generative models have also seen increasing adoption in behavior prediction, particularly through
126   diffusion-based approaches [Song et al. (2020); Ho et al. (2020)]. These methods focus on sampling
127   from complex distributions by iteratively refining noisy samples, but they do not explicitly model the
128   density evolution over time. This makes it challenging to analyze the long-term statistical behavior of
129   these models.
130   In contrast, our work incorporates diffusion modeling within a broader framework of Markov
131   transfer operators, enabling both the prediction of density evolution and the estimation of stationary
132   distributions.
133
134   1.1.4    L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING A PPROACHES
135
136   Despite their contributions, many existing methods are either limited in scope or fail to generalize
137
      across diverse application domains. For instance:
138
              • Reachability methods often require exhaustive computation of individual trajectories, which
139             is infeasible for high-dimensional stochastic systems.
140
              • Generative models excel at producing samples but do not explicitly address density evolution,
141
                limiting their applicability for long-term behavior prediction.
142
143           • Operator approximation methods prioritize computational efficiency but lack interpretability
144
                or alignment with asymptotic properties.

146   1.2     O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
147
      Our main contributions are detailed as follows:
148
149           • AI-driven agents behave in unpredictable ways due to machine learning black boxes. We
150             look at this through the lens of propagation of probability densities and the stochastic transfer
151             operator. AI agents are trained with data that has inherent biases. This, coupled with the
152             structure of machine learning models, can potentially alter the alignment of the model. To
153             verify the alignment of the model, we predict the asymptotic behavior of the model by
154             analyzing the terminal stationary density of the AI agents.
155           • We propose PISA, a novel and scalable algorithm that can simultaneously predict the
156             evolution of the densities of AI agents and estimate their terminal density. Our algorithm is
157             motivated by the spectral decomposition theorem [Lasota & Mackey (2013)] and provides a
158             theoretical backing for its performance. PISA simultaneously approximates the action of
159             the Markov transfer operator from the trajectory data of agents and predicts their asymptotic
160
                behavior.
161           • In our proposed algorithm PISA, the model complexity is indexed by the number of basis
                functions. The number of basis functions is a tunable parameter that can be altered according

               to the user’s needs. We provide a theoretical guarantee of the existence of the optimal solution
163            to our operator estimation problem.

             • We numerically verify the effectiveness of PISA in a variety of practical cases and compare it

      Remark 1 It is important to note that the Markov transfer operator P completely defines the
217   evolution of the density of the system. Hence, our goal is to analyze the behavior of the AI-driven
218   agents given by (2), through the estimation of the action of P . Our goal is to also estimate the
219   asymptotic behavior of AI-driven agents as several systems (2) exhibit stationary states asymptotically.
220   For example, robotics systems are designed to stabilize certain points in the domain. Another example
221   is a diffusion model which is trained to sample from unknown target distributions. For systems
222   that exhibit stationary states, there exists an invariant density ρ∗ [Lasota & Mackey (2013)] for the
223   Markov operator such that (5) holds. Here agents following (2) reach ρ∗ asymptotically. We seek to
224   estimate the terminal density ρ∗ as it provides a convenient method to assess the alignment of the

229

230

231   convenience of analysis. To give an example, the state evolution of Markov decision processes (MDPs)
232   with any fixed (stationary) control policy applied, forms a Markov process [Bertsekas (2012)].

558   5     C ONCLUSION AND B ROADER D ISCUSSION
559
560   While this paper introduces the PISA framework for predicting the behavior of AI-driven agents
561   and demonstrates its advantages over existing methods, there remain several broader considerations
562   regarding its applicability, interpretability, and limitations in the context of understanding AI systems.
563
564   5.1   C OMPARISON WITH OTHER M ETHODS
565
      PISA leverages spectral decomposition to approximate the Markov transfer operator, providing a
566
      theoretically grounded approach for both short-term prediction and asymptotic behavior estimation.
567
      Compared to alternative methods, such as direct neural network-based approximations (e.g., Meng
568   et al. (2022)) or dynamic mode decomposition approaches (e.g., Zhao & Jiang (2023b)), PISA shows
569   superior long-term prediction accuracy. However, these advantages are subject to the following
570   trade-offs:
571
572          • Interpretability: The spectral decomposition framework provides a clearer interpretative
573            advantage by explicitly decomposing density evolution into orthogonal components. In
574
               contrast, purely neural network-based methods, while often achieving strong performance,
               lack this interpretability due to their black-box nature.
575
576          • Prediction Accuracy: While PISA outperforms existing methods in long-term predictions,
577            its accuracy is sensitive to the number of basis functions (l) used in the decomposition. This
578
               dependency introduces a trade-off between computational complexity and predictive fidelity,
               which needs careful tuning based on the specific task.
579
580          • Flexibility in Non-Markovian Systems: PISA assumes a Markovian setting for density
581
               evolution. Though it can perform well in approximately Markovian or partially observable
               systems (as shown in the UCY pedestrian dataset), its performance may degrade when the
582
               dynamics deviate significantly from this assumption.
583
584
      5.2   K ERNEL D ENSITY E STIMATION AND B ROADER L IMITATIONS
585
586   The conclusion section highlights KDE as a potential limitation due to its dependency on kernel
587   choice and bandwidth parameters. While KDE is computationally efficient and widely applicable, it
588   introduces the following challenges:
589
590          • Scalability in High Dimensions: KDE struggles in high-dimensional settings due to the
591            curse of dimensionality, which can limit its applicability to complex systems with large state
592
               spaces.
593          • Sensitivity to Parameters: The choice of kernel bandwidth directly impacts the quality of
               density estimates, necessitating careful tuning that may not generalize across tasks.


                                                          11
      Under review


594
      Alternative density estimation methods, such as normalizing flows or diffusion-based models, could
595   be integrated into the PISA framework to mitigate these issues. However, such methods may increase
596   computational overhead and reduce interpretability.
597
598   5.3   U NDERSTANDING AI S YSTEMS IN B ROADER C ONTEXT
599
600   The stated goal of this work is to provide tools to understand AI systems. While PISA contributes to
601   this understanding by predicting density evolution and terminal behavior, several broader challenges
602   remain:
603
604
             • Alignment with System-Level Interpretability: The focus on density evolution offers
               macroscopic insights but may not fully address questions about individual trajectory behav-
605
               iors or their implications for safety and alignment in AI systems.
606
607          • Trade-offs Between Generality and Specificity: PISA’s general approach enables its appli-
608
               cation across diverse domains, but domain-specific adaptations (e.g., customized kernels,
               task-tailored loss terms) might be needed to achieve maximum predictive accuracy and
609
               insight.
610
611          • Broader Metrics for Evaluation: While KL divergence is used as the primary metric for
612
               performance evaluation, incorporating additional metrics, such as sensitivity to outliers,
               robustness to noise, or interpretability indices, could offer a more comprehensive assessment
613
               of the framework’s effectiveness.

      5.4   F UTURE D IRECTIONS

617   To fully situate PISA in the broader landscape of methods for understanding AI systems, future
618   research could:

624          • Develop methods to quantify and compare interpretability and alignment performance across
625            different prediction frameworks.

             • Incorporate task-specific constraints, such as physical feasibility or safety guarantees, di-

      A    O PERATOR THEORY AND STATISTICAL MECHANICS
811
812
      The study of density evolution from a macroscopic perspective is rooted in the principles of statistical
813   mechanics, which bridge the microscopic behavior of individual components and the macroscopic
814   properties of complex systems. Foundational works, such as those by Lasota & Mackey (2013)
815   and Risken (1996), have established frameworks for examining how distributions of states evolve
816   over time in systems governed by stochastic dynamics. This perspective is particularly valuable in
817   systems with high-dimensional, nonlinear dynamics, such as AI-driven agents, where direct analysis
818   of individual trajectories is impractical.
819   The macroscopic approach shifts focus from tracking each particle or agent to analyzing the aggregate
820   behavior of a population. By modeling the evolution of probability densities, this framework
821   enables predictions about the statistical behavior of the system as a whole, revealing regularities that
822   emerge despite underlying stochasticity and chaos. A crucial simplification in this framework is the
823   assumption of independence among trajectories.
824
825
      Assumption 1 (Ash (2012)) (Independent Particles Approximation) In our basic problem setting, we
      assume that there are N trajectories indexed by n and each trajectory Xn = [χn0 , χn1 , χn2 , · · · , χnK ] ∈
