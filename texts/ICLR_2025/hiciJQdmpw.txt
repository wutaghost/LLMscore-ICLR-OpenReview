009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep generative models have achieved substantial success in protein design. A
012            prevalent approach for de novo protein design involves initially designing a pro-
013            tein backbone structure using deep generative models, such as diffusion and flow
014            models, followed by using a separate inverse folding model to design the corre-
015            ponding sequence. Recently, co-design methods, which aim to jointly generate the
016
               structure and sequence of a protein, have attracted considerable attention. Despite
               of this, co-designing sequences and structures of long proteins remains challeng-
017
               ing. The complexity of this high-dimensional multimodal generative modeling
018
               makes sampling of diffusion and flow models prone to accumulated errors, of-
019            ten leading to non-designable regions. To tackle this challenge, we introduce a
020            contrastive guided sampling algorithm with dual multimodal flows to sample both
021            sequences and structures of highly designable proteins. The contrastive guidance
022            uses the lower-quality flow to help the higher-quality flow avoid non-designable
023            regions by gently steering it during sampling. Our method achieves designabil-
024            ity of 80% for length-400 proteins and 37% for length-500 proteins, significantly
025            outperforming previous approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Proteins are fundamental molecules essential to biology. The ability to design novel proteins (Huang
030
      et al., 2016) presents a promising pathway for the development of advanced therapeutics (Silva et al.,
031   2019), biomaterials (King et al., 2012), biocatalysis (Röthlisberger et al., 2008), among other appli-
032   cations. Protein engineering has traditionally relied on significant expertise and intensive experi-
033   mental efforts. This impedes the advancement of novel biotechnologies. Computational approaches
034   incorporating deep learning have transformed the paradigm, markedly accelerating the process of de
035   novo protein design (Ding et al., 2022).
036
      Noticeably, deep generative models, such as diffusion (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) and flow
037   (Lipman et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022) models, have been extensively utilized in de novo protein de-
038   sign, yielding promising results. Following the fundamental biological principle that structure deter-
039   mines function, numerous efforts have been focused on generating protein backbone structures, i.e.,
040   protein backbone design. Various protein presentations have been explored in this field, including
041   Cα only (Trippe et al., 2022), backbone torsion angles (Wu et al., 2024), and residue frame repre-
042   sentation (Yim et al., 2023b; Lin & Alquraishi, 2023) which is adopted from AlphaFold2 (Jumper
043   et al., 2021) proposed for protein structure prediction. Among these, residue frame representation
044   has demonstrated the best performance (Watson et al., 2023) and has been adopted in recent stud-
045
      ies of protein backbone design. Due to the lengthy reverse generative process of diffusion models
      causing slow inference speed, researchers have shifted to flow models for faster and higher-quality
046
      protein backbone generation (Yim et al., 2023a; Bose et al., 2023). Given these well-developed pro-
047
      tein backbone design models along with inverse folding models (Dauparas et al., 2022; Hsu et al.,
048   2022), de novo protein design can be achieved by initially constructing a protein backbone structure
049   and subsequently designing sequences based on this structure. The inverse folding models (Dau-
050   paras et al., 2022) and folding models (Jumper et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022) can be utilized together
051   as tools for in silico evaluation of protein backbone design by comparing the generated structures and
052   the refolded ones. Another line of research focuses on protein sequence generation, which mod-
053   els the distribution of protein sequences while ignoring structures. To bypass the complexities of
      protein design pipelines with multiple models and more effectively capture the intricate relationship


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      between protein sequences and structures, some researchers have introduced the concept of pro-
055   tein sequence-structure co-design (Shi et al., 2022; Campbell et al., 2024), which aims to jointly
056   generate continuous protein structures alongside corresponding discrete amino acid sequences. Rep-
057   resentative works include: Protein Generator (Lisanza et al., 2023; 2024), a sequence space diffusion
058   model based on RoseTTAfold (Baek et al., 2021) that simultaneously generates protein sequences
059   and structures; ProtPardelle (Chu et al., 2024), an all-atom Euclidean diffusion model paired with
060   an iterative sequence prediction mechanism; Multiflow (Campbell et al., 2024), a multimodal flow
061   model; ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024), a frontier multimodal generative language model that reasons
062   over the sequence, structure, and function of proteins. These works introduce a new paradigm and
063
      highlight a promising direction for future research.
064   Despite significant achievements de novo protein design based on generative models has achieved,
065   designing long proteins remains challenging. This complexity arises from the vast search space and
066   the inherently challenging nature of high-dimensional generative modeling, as seen in other areas
067   such as image and text generation. Researchers in fields like image and text generation attempt
068   to overcome this challenge by scaling up both the training dataset size and the model parameters
069
      (Achiam et al., 2023; Rombach et al., 2022). Acquiring large-scale, high-quality protein structure
      datasets is challenging, resulting in a lack of effective solutions to the high-dimensional challenges in
070
      the field of protein design. The performance of diffusion and flow models are attributed to sampling
071
      errors (Xu et al., 2023), which mainly come from two aspects: discretization errors of SDE/ODE
072   samplers and the approximation error of the learned neural network relative to the ground truth drift
073   (e.g., the score function in diffusion models and the vector field in flow models). The errors can be
074   accumulated and further amplified along the sampling process (Li & van der Schaar, 2023). Under
075   the shadow of the aforementioned issues, even the most advanced protein design models are not
076   consistently able to generate high-quality proteins.
077
      We assume that it is not possible to perfectly learn the joint distribution of protein sequence and
078   structure from limited high-quality data due to its intricate, high-dimensional nature and complex
079   structure. By aiming to improve protein sequence-structure co-design from the perspective of sam-
080   pling rather than training, we propose an approach based on dual flows with contrastive guidance
081   for generating high-designable proteins. This approach calibrates the drift (i.e., vector field) of one
082   flow with that of another during the sampling process. More specifically, we initially pretrain a mul-
083   timodal flow model following Campbell et al. (2024) and slightly fine-tune it on self-synthesized
084   data and selective self-synthesized data to obtain two flows differing in quality. Note that neither
085   of the dual flows is perfect. Leveraging the contrast of the dual flows, we can calibrate the vector
086
      field of higher-quality flow by steering it away from the regions of non-designable samples pointed
      out by the lower-quality flow. We provide both theoretical (albeit non-formal) justification and intu-
087
      itive understanding of our work. We implement the contrastive guidance on Euclidean space, SO(3)
088
      manifold, and categorical probability space for both the sequences and structures of proteins. With
089   all these together, our method achieves designability of 80% for length-400 proteins and 37% for
090   length-500 proteins, outperforming previous approaches by a significant margin.

211   3.2     M OTIVATION AND D ERIVATION OF D UAL F LOWS
212
213   While the base multimodal flow model is powerful in modeling distributions over short proteins,
214   it struggles with generating long proteins effectively. The reasons are multifaceted: approximation
215   errors (i.e., model estimation errors) and discretization errors of the ODE solver (i.e., the sampler),
      among others (Xu et al., 2023). These errors accumulate throughout the sampling process. Due to


                                                                                    4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      the high-dimensional nature of the problem when generating long proteins, these errors can further
217   deteriorate, potentially causing the sampling trajectories to become lost in the vast probability space
218   and ultimately leading to regions of non-designable samples.
219
220
      The designable proteins have intrinsic properties and correlation of structures and sequences, which
      the neural network cannot easily and perfectly learn. Thus, we properly assume that the flow models
221
      that correspond to real data distribution q(T) are always unavailable.
222
223   We further assume that the synthesized proteins genrated by the pretrained base model as introduced
224   in Sec. 3.1 follows the distribution p̃(T) = p(T) exp(−ζ(T)), where ζ(·) is a function for which
225   we do not know the explicit formula. Note R that the above assumption implies certain constraints on
226
      ζ(·) since p̃(T) is self-normalized, i.e., p(T) exp(−ζ(T)) = 1, thereby satisfying the property
      required to be a probability density function.
227
228   We curate two small datasets using the self-synthesized proteins generated by the base flow model.
229   Specifically, we use the pretrained base model to generate a small set consisting of both designable
230   and non-designable proteins as the first dataset and then select the designable proteins from the
231   first dataset to construct the second dataset. We slightly fine-tune the pretrained base model on
232
      the two datasets separately to derive the dual flow models, denoted as F LOW-LQ (low quality)
      and F LOW-HQ (high quality). We denote the induced distribution by F LOW-LQ and F LOW-HQ
233
      as pL (T) and pH (T), respectively. Here we assume pL (T) = p(T) exp(−ζL (T)) and pH (T) =
234
      p(T) exp(−ζH (T)). Since training generative models on self-synthesized data usually leads to a
235   decrease in quality (Alemohammad et al., 2023), pL and pH show worse performance in expectation
236   than native proteins in terms of designability. Nonetheless, due to the the fact that the above two self-
237   synthesized datasets explicitly differ in data quality, we could safely assume that, for any designable
238   protein (denoted as TH ), we have that pH (TH ) > pL (TH ), and for any non-designable protein
239   (denoted as TL ), we have that pH (TL ) < pL (TL ). Although we have no knowledge about the
240   explicit formula about ζL (·) and ζH (·), the above claims still indicate that ζL (TH ) > ζH (TH ) and
241   ζL (TL ) < ζH (TL ).
242   This inspires us to sample highly designable proteins utilizing the difference between these two
243   flows. Our goal is to sample following the probability defined by

         4.2             M AIN R ESULTS
509
510      The evaluation metrics on length-400 proteins of all the baselines and our method are reported in
511      Table. 1. Our method performs the best on scTM, scRMSD, Des.*, and Des. over all the meth-
512      ods. This demonstrates our hypothesis introduced in Sec. 3.2 and the effectiveness of contrastive
513      guidance. Notably, our dual flow approach outperforms its counterpart (also its base model) by a
514      significant margin in designability-related metrics. Our method shows similar diversity and novelty
515      to its counterpart, Multiflow, though these two metrics of our method slightly fall behind the other
516      methods. This also meets our expectation since classifier-free guidance for diffusion and flow mod-
517
         els (Ho & Salimans, 2022; Zheng et al., 2023) also suffers from similar issues. Our methods also
         show competetive performance in evaluation metrics related to sequence quality, which indicates
518
         that our contrastive guidance in categorical distribution can effectively improve the plausibility of
519
         the generated sequence. The results on length-500 proteins are reported in Table. 2. Many methods
520      totally fail under this setting. Our method still performs the best over all the methods, even though
521      F LOW-HQ and F LOW-LQ are not fine-tuned on length-500 proteins as introduced in Sec. 4.1. This
522      demonstrates the generalizability of our approach. See generated examples in App. B.
