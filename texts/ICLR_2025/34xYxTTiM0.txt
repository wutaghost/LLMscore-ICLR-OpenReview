009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Model calibration aims to align confidence with prediction correctness. The Cross-
012            Entropy (CE) loss is widely used for calibrator training, which enforces the model
013            to increase confidence on the ground truth class. However, we find the CE loss
014            has intrinsic limitations. For example, for a narrow misclassification, a calibrator
015            trained by the CE loss often produces high confidence on the wrongly predicted
016
               class (e.g., a test sample is wrongly classified and its softmax score on the ground
               truth class is around 0.4), which is undesirable. In this paper, we propose a new
017
               post-hoc calibration objective derived from the aim of calibration. Intuitively, the
018
               proposed objective function asks that the calibrator decrease model confidence on
019            wrongly predicted samples and increase confidence on correctly predicted samples.
020            Because a sample itself has insufficient ability to indicate correctness, we use its
021            transformed versions (e.g., rotated, greyscaled, and color-jittered) during calibrator
022            training. Trained on an in-distribution validation set and tested with isolated,
023            individual test samples, our method achieves competitive calibration performance
024            on both in-distribution and out-of-distribution test sets compared with the state of
025            the art. Further, our analysis points out the difference between our method and
026            commonly used objectives such as CE loss and Mean Square Error (MSE) loss,
027
               where the latters sometimes deviates from the calibration aim.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Model calibration is an important technique to enhance the reliability of machine learning systems.
032   Generally, it aims to align predictive uncertainty (a.k.a. confidence) with prediction accuracy. We are
033   interested in post-hoc accuracy preserving calibrators that scale the model output to make it calibrated
034   (Guo et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2020; Tomani et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2020; Kull et al., 2019).
035
      Existing methods typically use Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) to train a calibrator for the
036   classification task, such as the Mean Square Error (MSE) loss (Tomani et al., 2022; 2023; Zhang
037   et al., 2020) and the Cross-Entropy (CE) loss (Guo et al., 2017; Zou et al., 2023). Although these
038   approaches demonstrate efficacy in reducing calibration errors such as Expected Calibration Error
039   (ECE) and Brier scores, they lack theoretical guarantee that the calibration error is minimized when
040   MLE converges. In Fig. 1, for an image which is incorrectly classified and has a relatively high
041   probability on the ground truth class, calibrators trained by the CE or MSE loss would give high
042   confidence on the wrongly predicted class. It means a user may trust this prediction to be true. As to
043   be revealed in Sec. 3.4, the inherent problem of CE and MSE loss functions limits them in calibrating
044
      such test cases.
045   In this paper, we derive a concrete interpretation of the goal of calibration which is then directly
046   translated into a novel loss function that aligns with the newly interpreted goal. Specifically, we start
047   from the general definition of calibration and its error (Guo et al., 2017), and then, under a finite
048   test set, represent the error in a discretized form. Minimizing this discretized error gives us a very
049   interesting and intuitive calibration goal: A correct prediction should have possibly high confidence
050
      and a wrong prediction should have possibly low confidence. Theoretically, this optimization goal
      can naturally reduce the overlap of confidence values between correct and incorrect predictions.
051
052   We translate this goal into a loss function that enforces high confidence (i.e., 1) for correctly classified
053   samples, and low confidence (i.e., C1 , where C is the number of classes) for wrongly classified
      ones, named Correctness-Aware (CA) loss. Nevertheless, it is non-trivial to identify classification

073
      Our method allows a calibrator to be trained on the in-distribution validation set and directly applied
074
      to individual test samples during inference. We demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed strategy
075   on various test sets. In both in-distribution (IND) and out-of-distribution (OOD) test sets, our method
076   is clearly superior to uncalibrated models and competitive compared with state-of-the-art calibrators.
077   Moreover, our method demonstrates the potential to better separate correct and incorrect test samples
078   using their calibrated confidence. Below we summarize the main contributions.
079
080   вАҐ Theoretically, we derive the concrete goal of model calibration that has a clear semantic meaning.
081     This allows us to design a new calibration loss function: correctly classified samples should have
082     high confidence, while incorrectly classified ones with low confidence.
083   вАҐ To indicate prediction correctness, we use the softmax prediction scores of transformed versions of
084     the original image as calibrator inputs.
085   вАҐ Our method achieves competitive calibration performance on various IND and OOD datasets.
086
087
      вАҐ We diagnose commonly used calibration loss functions including the CE and MSE loss: they are
        often limited under test samples of certain characteristics.

090   2               R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Loss functions used in post-hoc calibration. Existing post-hoc calibrators typically use the Maxi-
093   mum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) for optimization (Jung et al., 2023; Tao et al., 2023). For example,
094   Mukhoti et al. (2020) use the CE loss, while Kumar et al. (2019) use the MSE loss. Additionally,
095   Mukhoti et al. (2020) uses the focal loss, a variant to CE, which enhances learning on wrong predic-
096   tions. Guo et al. (2017) directly optimizes the Expected Calibration Error (ECE). This paper identifies
097   inherent problems with MLE for calibration and derives a new loss function that better aligns with
098
      calibration goal.
099   OOD calibration deals with distribution shifts in test sets (Tomani et al., 2021). A useful practice is
100   modifying the calibration set to cover OOD scenarios (Tomani et al., 2021; Krishnan & Tickoo, 2020),
101   but these methods are usually designed for specific OOD scenarios which may lead to compromised
102   IND calibration performance. Another approach adapts to test data distribution, whether OOD or
103   IND, using domain adaptation (Wang et al., 2020), calibration sets from multiple domains (Gong
104
      et al., 2021), or improving calibration sets by estimating test set difficulty (Zou et al., 2023). In
      comparison, our calibrator is trained on the IND validation set only, and does not need test batches
105
      for update, but still demonstrates improved and competitive OOD calibration performance.
106
107   Predicting classification correctness has not been widely studied. Among the few, Xia & Bouganis
      (2023) investigate a three-way classification problem: classify a test sample into correct prediction,

257
258   From the intuition of the CA loss in Eq. (7), its optimization requires the post-hoc calibrator to be
259   aware of the correctness of each test sample. Empirically, we find the test sample itself offer limited

      3.4    C OMPARISON BETWEEN CA LOSS AND MLE
325
326   Maximum Likelihood Estimate (MLE) is widely used for calibration training (Kumar et al., 2019),
327   under concrete formats such as the Cross-Entropy (CE) or Mean Square Error (MSE) losses. This
328   section goes through their connections and differences with the CA loss. Appendix B shows more
329   discussions.
330   For correct predictions, MLE (e.g., CE or MSE loss) has similar effect with the CA loss. MLE
331   enforces the softmax probability of the ground-truth class to be close to 1. For correct predictions,
332   the softmax probability of the ground-truth class equals the sample confidence (maximum probability
333   in the softmax vector). Under this scenario, MLE aligns with both the calibration objective and the
334   CA loss: the confidence of correct predictions should be possibly high.
335
      For wrong predictions, MLE sometimes deviates from the calibration goal while CA is the-
336   oretically consistent. In Fig. 3, we visualize the loss surface of the CE, MSE, and CA loss w.r.t.
337   temperature and softmax probability on the ground truth class (first row), from which we use examples
338   of two typical calibration training samples for more intuitive illustration (both first and second rows).
339   Particularly, optimal temperature (x-axis) is achieved when the respective loss (y axis) is minimum
340   (the second row is easier to read).
341
      For an absolutely wrong sample (green curves in both rows), whose probability of the wrongly
342   predicted class is far greater than that on the ground-truth class, the optimization direction of MLE is
343   similar to CA: the loss curve keeps decreasing and finally a large temperature or a low confidence is
344   obtained. In fact, under this scenario, calibration objective requires the probability of the ground-truth
345   class to increase and probability of the wrongly predicted class to decrease. This is consistent with
346   the objective of MLE: to increase probability on the ground-truth class.
347
      For a narrowly wrong sample (yellow curves in both rows), whose probability on the wrongly pre-
348   dicted class is much closer to that on the ground-truth class, the optimization direction of MLE is
349   very different from or even opposite to CA. Take the yellow curves in the second row of Fig. 3 as
350   example. The CE loss, to become smaller, leads to a small temperature, meaning a large confidence,
351   which is undesirable for this wrongly predicted sample. For MSE, its minimum is achieved when
352   temperature is around 1.0, which does not change the temperature and confidence much. This again
353   is undesirable. In comparison, the CA loss keep decreasing when temperature increases so will
354   eventually give a large temperature or a small confidence for this type of samples. This is consistent
355   with the calibration objective.
356   Empirically, we find that such narrowly wrong predictions take up 2%-8% of the calibration set
357   (ImageNet validation).4 This would negatively impact training efficacy of MLE. Moreover, during
358   inference, if a test set has many such narrowly wrong predictions, MLE will also be negatively
359   impacted because of its unsuitable in dealing with such samples during training. This would explain
360   why our system is superior to and on par with state of the art on OOD and IND test sets, respectively
361   (refer to Sec. 4). In both IND and OOD scenarios, our calibrated models are much better.

670   B      F URTHER D ISCUSSION
671
672   How could the CA loss improve the ECE metric? ECE bins confidence and calculates the difference
673   between confidence and accuracy of samples in each bin. In the extreme case where bin size is
674   infinitely small, each bin will contain only one sample (assuming no image duplicates), meaning
675   accuracy of each bin is either 100% or 0%. In this scenario, the CA loss will push correct (wrong)
676   predictions to the high (low) confidence, which always reduces ECE. When bin size gradually
677
      becomes larger, improvement brought by CA loss will be less definite but still visible.
678   Sample-adaptive temperature used in Joy et al. (2023); Balanya et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023)
679   and our method has different properties from global temperature Guo et al. (2017). Because global
680   temperature does not change the order of samples ranked by their confidence, it cannot improve
681   the ability of confidence to separate correct and wrong predictions. Sample-adaptive temperature
682   at least has such potential (but under specific design). On the other hand, trained with the CE loss,
683
      the sample-adaptive temperature is demonstrated to produce a competitive calibrator for IND test
      sets Joy et al. (2023); Balanya et al. (2022). But issues with CE and the lack of using additional
684
      information limit its effectiveness for OOD data. In comparison, our method is competitive on both
685
      IND and OOD test sets.
686
687   Why the CA loss sometimes still have empirical failures? A calibrator perfectly optimized by the
688   CA loss will give 0 ECE, because all the correctly (wrongly) classified samples will have confidence
689
      of 1 (0). In practice, however, the bottleneck is to tell prediction correctness. We use augmented
      images but it might not be an optimal solution. In future we will explore new methods for correctness
690
      prediction.
691
692   Correctness prediction performance as a potential calibration metric. Given a confidence value,
693   better separability between correct and wrong predictions leads to safer decision making for users,
694   because less mistakes are made. This paper uses area under the ROC curve (AUROC) to measure the
695
      performance of predicting classification correctness. As shown in Sec. D.2, using a calibration method
      does not always mean user makes less mistakes during decision making. Considering the strong tie
696
      between this 2-way classification problem and model calibration (Eq. (1)), we think AUROC can be
697
      an additional evaluation metric for model calibration.
698
699   Impact of k. We use the indices of top-k confidences to locate and select the k-dim Softmax vectors
700   from the transformed images. In Fig. 6, we find that for various values of k our method improves over
701
      uncalibrated models. Moreover, k > 5 does not bring much improvement. Considering computational
      cost, we use k = 4. Note k is chosen on the ImageNet-A test set and applied on all the other test sets.
