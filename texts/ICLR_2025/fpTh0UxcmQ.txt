011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Textual and topological information is significant for link prediction (LP) in text-
014            attributed graphs (TAGs). Recent link prediction methods have focused on im-
015
               proving the performance of capturing structural features by Graph Convolutional
               Networks (GCNs). The importance of enhancing text encodings, powered by the
016
               advanced Pre-trained Language Models (PLM) has been underestimated. In this
017
               work, we analyse and emphasise the importance of PLMs and propose a novel
018            PLM-nested GCN design. We developed an extensive benchmark to compare
019            current competitive link prediction methods and PLM-based methods in a unified
020            experimental setting and systematically investigate the representation power of the
021            text encoders in the link prediction task. Based on our investigation, we introduce
022            LMGJ OINT — a memory-efficient fine-tuning method. The key design features
023            include: residual connection of textual proximity, a combination of structural and
024            textual embeddings and a cache embedding training strategy. Our empirical analy-
025            sis shows that these design elements improve MRR by up to 19.75% over previous
026
               state-of-the-art methods and achieve competitive performance across a wide range
               of models and datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Link Prediction (LP) aims to predict the likelihood of a connection between two nodes in a graph,
032   encompassing various real-world applications, including protein-protein interaction prediction (Szk-
033   larczyk et al., 2018), recommendation systems (Huang et al., 2005) and knowledge graph completion
034   (Hu et al., 2020b). While early LP relied on handcrafted graph heuristics (Adamic & Adar, 2003),
035   more advanced approaches follow a two-stage framework: (1) an encoder maps graph information
036   into node embeddings and (2) then a decoder assesses pairwise embedding similarity to predict
037   connection likelihood.
038   Among encoder designs, Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) are the dominant paradigm, de-
039   pending on both node and structural features. In previous benchmarks such as Cora (McCallum
040   et al., 2000) and PubMed (Sen et al., 2008b), node features have often relied on shallow text em-
041   beddings such as Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013). However, these embeddings struggle to capture
042   context-aware information and complex semantic relationships, which are crucial for link prediction.
043   Despite their limitations, these shallow embeddings are widely used in standard benchmarks (Hu
044   et al., 2021), which has led to several issues: (1) they are often practically irreproducible, making it
045   difficult to replicate them on new datasets; (2) the reliance on a specific text embedding has resulted
046   in architecture over-optimization in current algorithm development; and (3) the decoupling of the text
047   embedding process and the GCN design hinders seamless end-to-end training, thereby reducing the
048   overall effectiveness of the approach.
049   Text-attributed graphs (TAGs) help overcome these limitations by offering rich semantic content.
050   They enable the characterization of individual node properties using powerful Pretrained Language
051   Models (PLMs). Additionally, TAGs allow for the seamless integration of learnable text embeddings
052   with GCN-based structural aggregation. However, existing works on TAGs primarily focus on node
053   classification (Duan et al., 2024; He et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2024b) or suffer
      from limited comparisons due to a lack of strong baselines (Wang et al., 2023; Yun et al., 2021;

252   via summing the weighted neighboring features iteratively. Such a local smoothing mechanism helps
253   generate more representative embedding when the homophily assumption holds Luan et al. (2023).

310   5.1   G RAPH - BASED M ETHODS
311
312   This section evaluates graph-based methods which can be categorized into four groups: 1) Graph
313   Heuristic: Local heuristic leverages modified shared neighborhoods, including Common Neighbor
314   (CN), Adamic-Adar (AA), Resource Allocation (RA). Other global heuristics such as Katz, Shortest
315
      Path and symmetric PageRank(Adamic & Adar, 2003; Page et al., 1999) consider all paths between

316
      methods to model neighborhood contexts via random walks, including DeepWalk, Node2Vec and
317
      LINE (Perozzi et al., 2014; Tang et al., 2015; Grover & Leskovec, 2016). 3) Aggregation-based
318   Methods: GCNs aggregate information recursively from first-hop neighbors (Kipf & Welling, 2016;
319   Velickovic et al., 2017). SAGE embeds self and neighboring nodes separately to handle heterophily
320   (Hamilton et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2020). GIN achieves the same expressivity as Weisfeiler-Lehman
321   test (Xu et al., 2018a) by employing injective transformation and we perform a dot product with an
322   MLP layer to handle the final embeddings. 4) GCN4LP: SEAL, BUDDY and ELPH introduces
323   different labeling techniques and structure embeddings to address the automorphic nodes problem
      (Zhang et al., 2020; Zhang & Chen, 2018; Chamberlain et al., 2023). The latest GCNs augment

330   5.2   PLM- BASED M ETHODS
331
332   We introduce and transfer four extended benchmarked frameworks from node classification task, each
333   with progressively increasing resource requirements. This section provides a detailed overview of the
334   PLMs/LLMs used and their corresponding embedding configurations. We investigate the following
335   configurations: (1) PLM/LLM as a Fixed Inference Model (PLM/LLM-Inf): The PLM/LLM
336   operates in a frozen state, generating static embeddings that are then fed into a Multi-Layer Perceptron
337   (MLP) for binary classification. Specifically, the final hidden states of the PLMs are utilized as text
338
      embeddings. (2) Fine-Tuned PLMs (FT-PLM): Extending the PLM/LLM-Inf setup, fine-tuning
      is introduced by training the last few encoder layers alongside the MLP (Chen et al., 2024a). (3)
339
      PLM/LLM-Inf-GCN: This configuration first generates text embeddings from the PLMs as node
340
      features, after which a GCN is trained on the updated node features, but without further training on
341   PLM/LLM. (4) FT-PLM-GCN: Building upon (3), this approach optimizes all parameters, including
342   those in the last encoder layers of the PLM, the GCN and the MLP (Yang et al., 2021). Configurations
343   (1) and (2) are classified as text-only methods. To sum up, we benchmark cascade (3) and nested
344   architecture (4) adopted from prior work in node classification.
345
      Selection of PLM/LLMs In this paper, we define PLMs as those models practical for inference
346
      and fine-tuning within typical academic budgets, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and LLMs
347   refer to models requiring substantial computational resources to fine-tune such as thousands of
348   GPUs or TPUs, exemplified by Llama-3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024). We utilize both encoder-only and
349   decoder-as-embedder (BehnamGhader et al., 2024) models for text embedding, including (1) BERT,
350   a lightweight deep text embedding model pretrained in a self-supervised manner (Devlin et al., 2019);
351   (2) e5-large-v2 (Wang et al., 2022), Sentence-Transformers MiniLM-L6-v2 (Reimers & Gurevych,
352   2019a) and MPNet(Song et al., 2020), pretrained using a contrastive learning approach. Additionally,
353   we incorporate Meta-LLaMA-3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024),a decoder-only LLM, which we include
354   as a case study for text embedding at scale. To ensure consistency and comparability with existing
355
      studies, we include shallow embedding methods such as bag-of-words (Harris, 1954) and Word2Vec
      (Mikolov et al., 2013). We leverage the [EOS] token in LLaMA3 and the [CLS] token in sentence
356
      embedding models as node features and fine tune them with full-parameter tuning strategy. Further
357
      details on fine-tuning and embedding strategy are provided in Appendix B.3. To sum up, we include
358   BERT, e5-large-v2, MiniLM-L6-v2, MPNet and Meta-LlaMA-3-8B, bag-of-words and Word2Vec
359   as text encoder with 4 experiment settings.

361   5.3   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
362
363
      Metrics choice To ensure consistency in previous works Hu et al. (2021), we benchmark all ap-
      proaches on the Cora, PubMed and Arxiv_2023. Furthermore, our evaluation is extended to all
364
      nine datasets with metrics including Hits@50, Hits@100, MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank) and AUC.
365
      Hits@K quantifies the ratio of positive edges ranked within the top K positions, while MRR evaluates
366   the model’s ability to rank positive above negative ones. AUC assesses the model’s ability to score

453   Does GCN4LP Maintain Its Superiority? Currently promising GCN4LP methods—such as SEAL,
454   BUDDY and NeoGNN, do NOT show a significant advantage over GCNs in this setting. Nonetheless,

      In PLM-Inf-GCN category, we observed that NCNC’s performance could be improved simply by
459
      replacing the original node feature with PLM-based text embeddings. This signifies the significant
460   potential of PLM-based text embeddings to enhance performance in link prediction tasks.
461
462   Cascade PLM-GCN vs. Nested PLM-GCN. To answer Q3, we evaluate the impact of fine-tuning
463
      within cascade and nested frameworks by comparing NCNC and PLM-Inf-NCNC, PLM-Inf-MLP and
      FT-PLM-MLP, GCN and FT-PLM-GCN. We observe limited improvement in Hits@K, accompanied
464
      by a notable decline in MRR. It indicates that incorporating context-aware PLM text encoding can
465
      enhance representation quality concerning Hits@K. However, the performance decay in MRR is
466   consistent with prior findings by Chen et al. (2024b), which suggest that fine-tuning without specific
467   design considerations can sometimes result in negative performance gains. In summary, these results
468   indicate that optimizing PLMs independently of topology-based methods does not lead to consistent
469   improvements across all metrics. This underscores the importance of developing an effective nested
470   architecture to fully leverage the strengths of both approaches.

497   7   C ONCLUSION
498
499   This work tackles the under-explored area of joint PLM-GCN architecture design for link prediction
500   by benchmarking PLM and GCN-based methods on extensive datasets. In our benchmark, we focused
501   on discussing the impact of fine-tuning modules and text embeddings within various PLM-GCN
502   architectures. We introduce the LMGJ OINT, a simple yet powerful nested framework that combines
503   the strengths of both GCN4LP and PLM-based methods while avoiding their weaknesses. Extensive
504   and rigorous experiments demonstrate that LMGJ OINT consistently improves performance across all
505
      the metrics on various datasets, with minimal hyperparameter tuning required. We expect it to benefit
      PLM-GCN models and other graph learning tasks, including node classification and regression.
