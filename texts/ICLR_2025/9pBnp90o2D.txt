009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We investigate the distance function implicitly learned by message passing neu-
012            ral networks (MPNNs) on specific tasks. Our goal is to capture the functional
013            distance that is implicitly learned by an MPNN for a given task. This contrasts
014            previous work which relates MPNN distances on arbitrary tasks to structural dis-
015            tances that ignore the task at hand. To this end, we distill the distance between
016
               MPNN embeddings into an interpretable graph distance. Our distance is an opti-
               mal transport on the Weisfeiler Leman Labeling Tree (WILT), whose edge weights
017
               reveal subgraphs that strongly influence the distance between MPNN embeddings.
018
               Moreover, it generalizes the metrics of two well-known graph kernels and is com-
019            putable in linear time. Through extensive experiments, we show that MPNNs
020            define the relative position of embeddings by focusing on a small number of sub-
021            graphs known by domain experts to be functionally important.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Message passing graph neural networks (MPNNs) have been reported to achieve high predictive
027   performance in various domains (Zhou et al., 2020). To understand these performance gains, many
028
      studies have focused on the expressive power of MPNNs (Morris et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Maron
      et al., 2019). However, the binary nature of expressive power excludes any analysis of the distance
029
      between graph embeddings, which is considered to be a key to the predictive power of MPNNs (Liu
030
      et al., 2022b; Li & Leskovec, 2022; Morris et al., 2024). Recently, there has been growing interest
031   in the analysis of MPNN (generalization) performance using structural distances between graphs
032   Böker et al. (2024); Franks et al. (2024) that consider graph topology but ignore the target function
033   to be learned. One can then derive generalization bounds under assumptions on the margin between
034   classes or on Lipschitz constants of the target function. Both assumptions do often not hold on real
035   data and MPNN architectures used in practice. In this work, we instead investigate the distance
036   dMPNN implicitly obtained from an MPNN and its relation to a functional distance dfunc defined on
037   the target values of the learning task.
038   Specifically, we ask: What properties does the distance dMPNN learned by a well-performing MPNN
039   have in practice that can explain the high performance? While previous studies (Chuang & Jegelka,
040   2022; Böker et al., 2024) focused on the alignment between dMPNN and a non-task-tailored struc-
041   tural graph distance dstruc , we have found that it is not critical to the predictive performance of
042   MPNNs. Rather, even if an MPNN was trained with classical cross-entropy loss, dMPNN respects
043   the task-relevant functional distance dfunc and the alignment between both is highly correlated with
044   the predictive performance of MPNNs. Then, we move to our second question: How do MPNNs
045
      learn such a metric structure? Since MPNNs essentially consider graphs as multisets of Weisfeiler
      Leman (WL) subgraphs, we propose a method to identify WL subgraphs whose presence in a graph
046
      significantly affects its relative position to other graphs in the MPNN embedding space. Specifi-
047
      cally, we distill MPNNs into a weighted Weisfeiler Leman Labeling Tree (WILT) while preserving
048   dMPNN . The WILT yields an optimal transport distance on a tree ground metric, which we prove
049   to be a trainable generalization of the graph distances of existing high-performance kernels (Kriege
050   et al., 2016; Togninalli et al., 2019). We show experimentally that the WILTing tree distance fits
051   MPNN distances well. Examination of the resulting edge parameters on WILT after distillation
052   shows that only a small number of WL subgraphs determine dMPNN . In a qualitative experiment, the
053   subgraphs that strongly influence dMPNN are those that are known to be functionally important by
      domain knowledge. In short, our contributions are:

146   3    I S THE MPNN E MBEDDING D ISTANCE C RITICAL TO P ERFORMANCE ?
147
148   Our first question is what properties dMPNN of well performing MPNNs have in practice that can
149   explain their high performance. This section investigates whether the alignment between dMPNN and
150   the task-relevant pseudometric dfunc is such a property. Specifically, we address questions below:
151
152   Q1.1 Does training MPNN increase the alignment between dMPNN and the task-relevant dfunc ?
153   Q1.2 Does a strong alignment between dMPNN and dfunc indicate high performance of the MPNN?
154
      Note that the alignment between dMPNN and task-irrelevant structural graph pseudometrics dstruc has
155
      been considered a key to MPNN performance in previous studies (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022; Böker
156   et al., 2024; Franks et al., 2024). However, we found that this property is not consistently improved
157   by training and does not correlate with performance. (See Appendix E for detailed analyses).
158

      learn dMPNN that respects dfunc ? Since MPNN embeddings are aggregations of WL color embed-
217   dings, we can infer that MPNNs learn during training which WL colors are important for captur-
218   ing the task-relevant functional graph distance dfunc . This determines the relative position between
219   MPNN embeddings based on the existence of such WL colors in graphs. To identify WL colors that
220   strongly influence dMPNN , we propose to distill dMPNN into our new graph pseudometric dWILT , which
221   has tunable weights and is based on the WL colors of the input graphs. dWILT is an optimal transport
222   distance on the Weisfeiler Leman Labeling Tree (WILT) and generalizes two existing distances of
223   high-performing graph kernels (Kriege et al., 2016; Togninalli et al., 2019). After distillation, the
224   parameters of dWILT allow us to identify WL colors that are considered important by the MPNN.

246   shows two equivalent characterizations of dWILT as an optimal transport distance and as a weighted

250

252

      6    C ONCLUSIONS
467
468
      We analyzed the metric properties of the embedding space of MPNNs. We found that the alignment
469   with the functional pseudometric improves during training and is a key to high predictive perfor-
470   mance. In contrast, the alignment with the structural psudometrics, which has been studied inten-
471   sively in previous works, does not improve and is not correlated with performance. To understand
472   how MPNNs learn and reflect the functional distance between graphs, we propose a theoretically
473   sound and efficiently computable new pseudometric on graphs using WILT. By examining the edge
474   weights of the distilled WILT, we found that only a tiny fraction of the entire WL colors influence
475   dMPNN . The identified colors correspond to subgraphs that are known to be functionally important
476   from domain knowledge.
477   While we investigated MPNNs specifically, there is a hierarchy of more and more expressive GNNs
478   that are bounded in expressiveness by corresponding WL test variants. In this paper, we have defined
479   WILT on the hierarchy of 1-WL labels. Still, it is straightforward to extend the proposed WILT
480   metric to color hierarchies obtained from higher-order WL variants (Morris et al., 2023; Geerts &
481   Reutter, 2022) or extended message passing schemes (Frasca et al., 2022; Graziani et al., 2024).
482   While beyond the scope of this work, higher-order WILTing trees may prove useful in interpreting
483
      a range of GNNs. However, as the number of trainable WILT weights scales with the number of
      colors, the practical relevance of higher-order WILTs remains an open question. Using WILT for a
484
      purpose other than understanding GNNs is also interesting. For example, by training WILT’s edge
485
      parameters from scratch, we might be able to build a high-performance graph kernel.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
595
596
      A graph is a data structure composed of nodes and the edges connecting them, capable of repre-
597
      senting various entities such as molecules and social networks. Due to the high flexibility of graph
598   structures, it is difficult to apply deep neural networks from non-graph domains, such as convolu-
599   tional neural networks (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), to graph data. Thus, graph-specific architectures
600   called graph neural networks have been studied for about two decades since their initial proposal
601   (Gori et al., 2005). Message passing graph neural networks, in particular, achieve high predictive
602   performance in various tasks, including node or graph classification and link prediction.
603
      To understand the high performance of MPNNs, many studies have focused on their expressive
604   power (Morris et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Maron et al., 2019). Expressive power refers to the ability
605   of a permutation invariant function to embed nonisomorphic graphs into distinct representations.
606   Formally, a message passing graph embedding function f is said to be at least as expressive as
607   another function g if the following holds:
608
609
                                ∀G, H ∈ G : f (G) = f (H) =⇒ g(G) = g(H),
610   where G is the set of all pairwise nonisomorphic graphs. However, the binary expressive power
611   cannot capture the similarity between graphs, so it alone is said to be insufficient to explain the
612   performance of MPNNs. Recently, it has become increasingly recognized that the geometry of the
613   embedding space in MPNNs, not just their combinatorial expressiveness, is crucial (Li & Leskovec,
614   2022; Morris et al., 2024). For instance, many of the graph contrastive learning methods implic-
615   itly assume that good metric structure in the embedding space will lead to the high performance
616   of MPNNs (Liu et al., 2022b). Chuang & Jegelka (2022) theoretically showed that the distance
617   between MPNN embeddings can be upper bounded by their proposed task-irrelevant structural dis-
618
      tance, called the tree mover’s distance, paving the way for the theoretical analyses of MPNN gen-
      eralization ability or robustness. Böker et al. (2024) proved the equivalence between the MPNN
619
      embedding distance and other structural distances, but their analyses dealt only with dense graphs
620
      and required the consideration of all MPNNs with some Lipschitz constant. Our study also focuses
621   on the geometry of the embedding space, but we investigate one MPNN trained on practical sparse
622   graphs.
623
624
      This study is also related to GNN interpretability (Liu et al., 2022a; Yuan et al., 2022). The inter-
      pretability of GNNs is important because people may be reluctant to apply them to real-world prob-
625
      lems where safety or privacy are important if the mechanism behind their predictions is unknown.
626
      Furthermore, higher interpretability of well-performing models may lead to a new understanding of
627   scientific phenomena when applied to scientific domains such as chemistry or biology. Most of ex-
628   isting interpretation methods are instance-level, which identify input features in a given input graph
629   that are important for its prediction. However, instance-level methods cannot explain the global be-
630   havior of GNNs. Recently, some studies have proposed a way to understand the global behavior of
631   GNNs by distilling them into highly interpretable models. The resulting model can be a GNN with
632   higher interpretability (Müller et al., 2024), or a logical formula (Azzolin et al., 2023; Köhler &
633   Heindorf, 2024; Pluska et al., 2024). Our study also distills MPNNs into highly interpretable WILT
634   for global-level understanding, but the difference is that ours aims to interpret the metric structure
635
      of the MPNN embedding space, while previous studies focused on generating explanations for each
      label class. In addition, our method can be applied to regression tasks on graphs, while previous
636
      studies cannot.

1117   The closer the RMSE is to zero, the better the alignment is. Zero RMSE means perfect alignment.
1118   That is, dMPNN is a constant multiple of dstruc . Note that we use different evaluation criteria to
1119
       measure the alignment between dMPNN and dfunc (Definition 3) or dstruc (Definition 12). There are
       multiple reasons for this. First, the RMSE is in principle designed for non-binary dstruc . Therefore,
1120
       RMSE(dMPNN , dfunc ) is not a meaningful value when dfunc is a binary function, which is the case
1121
       when the task is classification. Second, the computation of ALIk (dMPNN , dstruc ) is computationally
1122   too expensive. We explain this in terms of how many graph pairs we need to compute the distance
1123   for. Both the RMSE and ALIk require the calculation of the distance between |D|2 pairs in the
1124   original definition. This is too demanding, especially when dstruc is dGED , which is NP-hard to
1125   compute. Therefore, in practice, we approximate the RMSE with 1000 randomly selected pairs
1126   from D2 . This kind of approximation is difficult for ALIk . To approximate ALIk , we first choose a
                                                                  2
1127   subset Dsub of D, and then compute dstruc of all pairs in Dsub . Even if we set |Dsub | = 100, which is
1128   quite small, we still need about 10 times more computation than the RMSE.
