008
009
               Next-token prediction is conventionally done using decoder-only Transformers
010
               with causal attention, as this approach allows for efficient reuse of keys and val-
011
               ues. What if we were not compute-limited, should we still use decoder-only
012            Transformers? In this work, we introduce Encoder-only Next Token Prediction
013            (ENTP). We use small scale experiments to explore the differences between ENTP
014            and decoders, highlighting potential advantages of ENTP in setting with un-
015            bounded compute. We introduce the Count3 task and show, both theoretically
016            and experimentally, that while ENTP can perform this task easily, a decoder-only
017            Transformer cannot. Finally, we empirically demonstrate ENTP’s superior perfor-
018            mance across various synthetic tasks, such as length generalization and in-context
019            learning. learning.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Traditionally, auto-regressive language modeling has relied on decoder-only Transformers (Vaswani
025   et al., 2017) with causal attention, trained using the next-token prediction objective. Causal attention
026
      ensures that each token can only attend to previous tokens, preventing future tokens from influencing
      past outputs. This mechanism makes training and inference more efficient, as past keys and values
027
      do not need to be recomputed for each token. This efficiency enables the scaling of decoder-only
028
      Transformers, such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024), up to billions
029   of parameters using current hardware.
030
031   However, causal attention also introduces artificial constraints. Given tokens x1 , x2 , ..., xn , the con-
032
      textual embedding of xj (where j < n) can only attend to embeddings of earlier tokens, even
      when predicting xn+1 . While this constraint ensures a strict causal structure, it may not always be
033
      necessary or beneficial. We investigate what happens when we remove this constraint, while still
034
      maintaining causality externally.
035
036   Specifically, we look at Encoder-only Transformers, which are typically used for tasks like classifi-
037   cation, and do not impose this causality constraint. Though traditionally not used for auto-regressive
038
      tasks, encoder-only architectures can be adapted for next-token prediction. When computing the
      output at the current time step, an encoder-only Transformer, or any sequence model, can be made
039
      causal by only providing inputs up to and including the current time step. Therefore, in this work, we
040
      investigate the idea of using encoder-only Transformers for next-token prediction. We summarize
041   our findings below.
042
043
      Functions expressible with Decoder-only and Encoder-only Transformers. We demonstrate
044   that the sets of functions expressible by decoder-only and encoder-only Transformers are not com-
045   parable, which goes against intuition that the expressivity of encoders would subsume that of de-
046   coders. Rather, there exist functions expressible with decoder-only Transformers that are not ex-
047   pressible with encoder-only Transformers, and vice versa, as well as functions expressible by both
048   architectures.
049
050   Complexity of Decoder-only and Encoder-only Transformers. Based on the minimum time and
051   space complexities, we give a description of the functions that can be performed by decoder-only and
052   encoder-only Transformers. We propose an auto-regressive task that can be performed by encoder-
053   only Transformers, and cannot be performed by decoder-only Transformers (given that some mild
      assumptions hold). We validate our hypothesis with small experiments using small decoder-only

      Expressive Power of Transformers. There have been various literature exploring the expressive
      power of Transformers. From the lens of universal approximation, Yun et al. (2020) showed that any
085
      continuous sequence-to-sequence function over a compact set can be approximated arbitrary close
086
      by a Transformer (of finite albeit very large size). Other works approach expressiveness from the
087   perspective of computability and complexity such as Pérez et al. (2021) which showed Transformers
088   are Turing complete and Merrill et al. (2022); Merrill & Sabharwal (2024); Li et al. (2024) which
089   use circuit complexity to characterize the languages recognizable by Transformers of fixed depth.
090   Giannou et al. (2023) presents a framework for Transformers as universal computers by placing
091   them in a loop. Communication complexity has also been used to show the impossibility of one-layer
092   Transformers of expressing certain functions e.g. induction head, without the model size being linear
093   in the length of the input (Sanford et al., 2024b;a). We modify functions introduced in (Sanford et al.,
094   2024b) to compare the expressive power decoder-only Transformers against ENTP. We note that the
095
      existing bounds (Sanford et al., 2024b;a) are highly related, but they do not directly imply the relative
      expressive power of encoder and decoder (and specifically of the same fixed model size).
096
097
098   Transformer Architectures for Next Token Prediction. Transformers have become the de facto
099
      backbones for next-token prediction tasks, leading to several variants such as encoder-decoder,
      causal decoder-only, and prefix decoder-only models. In the encoder-decoder model (Lewis et al.,
100
      2019; Chung et al., 2024), similar to the vanilla Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), the encoder
101
      transforms the input tokens into conditioning features, and the decoder auto-regressively predicts
102   the target tokens by using cross-attention over the encoded representation and causal attention over
103   the output tokens. In contrast, the causal decoder-only model (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al.,
104   2023) uses only the Transformer decoder and applies causal attention to all tokens to perform next-
105   token prediction, ensuring that each token attends only to previous tokens. The prefix decoder-only
106   model (Raffel et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021) is similar to the causal decoder-only model but differs
107   in that it applies non-causal attention (i.e., full self-attention) to the input sequence (see Figure 8 for
      visualizations of the attention patterns in these variants).

      With the development of these models, recent studies have investigated the performance of each
109   variant across various tasks. Notably, Wang et al. (2022) examined the zero-shot generalization
110   performance of each model along with various objectives, and Ding et al. (2024) analyzed the per-
111   formance of causal decoder-only and prefix decoder-only models in in-context learning. However,
112   despite these diverse studies, there is a lack of research on encoder-only models that do not im-
113   pose the constraint of causal attention for every next-token prediction. Therefore, in this work,
114   we analyze the characteristics of encoder-only next token prediction (ENTP), comparing them with
115   decoder-only models.

119   Sequence-to-Token Functions and Autoregression. Given a vocabulary V (which we tradi-
120   tionally think of as some finite set, but could in general be any arbitrary perhaps uncountable
121   set e.g. Rd ), we can define a sequence over V as (x1 , . . . , xn ) where x1 , . . . , xn ∈ V. Let
122   V ∗ = {(x1 , . . . , xn ) : n ∈ N; xi ∈ V } be the set of all sequences generated by V . Then, we
123   say that f : V ∗ → V is a sequence-to-token function.
124   We can view a causal model as a map from an input sequence to an output sequence with the
125   causality constraint that the i’th ouput token depends only on the first i input tokens. Mathematically,
126   we enforce this causality contraint by characterizing our causal model, Tf : V ∗ → V ∗ , with some
127   sequence-to-token function f where on input sequence (x1 , . . . , xn ) we have that
128
                       Tf (x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ) := (f (x1 ), f (x1 , x2 ), . . . , f (x1 , x2 , . . . , xn )).   (1)
129
130   Observe that a sequence-to-token function f can be used auto-regressively to generate tokens from
131   some initial sequence (x1 , . . . , xn ) via the following update rule:
132
                                                xn+i := f (x1 , . . . , xn+i−1 ).                                  (2)
133
134   This can also be viewed as a special case of the causal model, where the input sequence is chosen
135   so that
136
                                  Tf (x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ) = (x2 , x3 , . . . , xn+1 ).           (3)
137   Hence, if we are trying to learn a causal or auto-regressive model, it suffices to learn the sequence
138   function that generates it. Thus in this paper, we focus on the type of sequence functions that
139   encoders versus decoders can learn and express.
140
141   Encoders and Decoders. We will use the letters E and D respectively to refer to encoders and
142   decoders. In this paper, both models refer to variants of the Transformer architecture introduced in
143   Vaswani et al. (2017), where the only difference lies in the masking used on the attention scores
144   (decoder uses a causal mask while encoder allows full attention, as illustrated in Figure 1). The
145   model size of a Transformer is determined by two parameters:
146
             • L: number of Transformer blocks.
147
148          • D: embedding dimension.

      We then have TE and TD as the causal models of E and D when used as sequence functions fE

      Given that we can learn causal functions (defined as causal model in preliminary) using either
174
      encoders and decoders, the natural question to ask is how the expressive power of each model is
175   related, i.e. can the encoder express more causal functions than a decoder of the same model size?
176   Or perhaps they express the exact same class of causal functions? Towards answering this ques-
177   tion, one trivial observation is that one-layer decoders and encoders are equivalent (formal proof in
178   Appendix A.3) which directly implies the existence of causal functions2 that both architecture can
179   model exactly.
180
      Now, what about causal functions that a decoder can model but not encoder, or vice versa — that
181   encoder can model but not decoder? These functions exist too, as we show in the following two
182   theorems:
183
      Theorem 1. For any L ≥ 2 and D ≥ 1, there exists a position-free decoder D       e that has L-layers and
184   embedding dimension D, such that for any encoder E, there exists some input sequence (x1 , x2 , . . . )
185   with x1 , x2 , · · · ∈ RD , and TDe (x1 , x2 , . . . ) ̸= TE (x1 , x2 , . . . ).
186
187   Theorem 2. For any L ≥ 2 and D ≥ 1, there exists a position-free encoder Ee that has L-layers and
188
      embedding dimension D, such that for any decoder D with positional embeddings satisfying p1 ̸=
      p2 , there exists some input sequence (x1 , x2 , . . . ) with x1 , x2 , · · · ∈ RD , and TEe(x1 , x2 , . . . ) ̸=
189
      TD (x1 , x2 , . . . ).
190
191   The above two theorems are existential in nature. Informally, Theorem 1 says that if we consider
192   causal model defined over the entirety of RD as its vocabulary, we can find some decoder, for
193   which any encoder will differ from it on some input sequence. Theorem 2 makes a similar (albeit
194   weaker statement) in the other direction; namely the existence of a causal function computable by
195   an encoder, but not by any decoder that uses “non-trivial” positional embeddings (e.g. embeddings
196   for different positions are unique). Detailed proof of both theorems are deferred to Appendix A.
197   Of course, the setting and assumptions of the above two statements are not necessarily very real-
198   istic. For one, they focus on general class of causal models rather than only auto-regressive ones.
199   Furthermore, the results only pertain to exact realization and say nothing about approximation. The
200   assumption of unbounded domain is also not realistic as in practice decoders are trained and used
201   over a finite domain of tokens, each with some fixed embeddings. And specific to Theorem 2, no
202   claim is made about decoders that do not use positional embeddings. But despite the limitations,
203   these theorems give an indication that the expressive power of encoder and decoder model are dif-
204
      ferent — despite the almost identical description modulo the attention mask. Changing the mask on
      the attention scores causes significant changes to the properties of the model. Thus, in the following
205
      sections we propose an auto-regressive tasks and run experiments comparing encoders and decoders
206
      that corroborates this view.

      required computational complexity. We give an informal comparison of encoders and decoders in
217   terms of their required time and space complexities — both over the entire sequence and for each
218   additional token. We propose Count3, which is closely related to Match3 (Sanford et al., 2024b),
219   to highlight the “gap” between the complexity of encoders and decoders. Count3 is feasible for an
220   encoder but challenging for a decoder due to its limited computation complexity.
221
222   Time Complexity Comparison. Decoder-only Transformers, using KV-Cache, take O(n) time
223   to generate each token, and O(n2 ) time to generate an entire sequence. Because an ENTP has to
224   compute the entire attention matrix for every token, it takes O(n2 ) time to generate each token. Thus,
225   it takes O(n3 ) time to generate the entire sequence. While this implies that ENTP is more compute-
226   intensive (i.e., ENTP will be slower than decoder-only Transformer), this also implies that ENTP
227
      can express more compute-intensive functions than decoders. Specifically, since the total amount of
      compute that decoders use for generating n tokens is O(n2 ), they cannot run any algorithm whose
228
      runtime is ω(n2 ) (strictly greater than quadratic time).
229
230
      Space Complexity Comparison. Both encoder-only and decoder-only use O(n) space complex-
231
      ity to generate an entire sequence. Although the standard implementation of attention uses O(n2 )
232
      space, attention can be implemented using only O(n) space. For details of algorithmic implementa-
233   tions of attention using O(D) memory, refer to Algorithm 3 in the appendix. Thus, we need a more
234   detailed approach to find a difference between the space complexity of the two models.
235
236
      Towards this end, we classify memory used for the computation over the current token as either
      precomputed or additional. Precomputed memory stores values from computation over past tokens.
237
      Values stored in precomputed memory persist, and are used for computation over current and future
238
      tokens, e.g. the keys and values of previous tokens for a decoder. Additional memory stores values
239   that depend on the current token, e.g. keys and values of the current token.
240
241   When generating the nth token, a decoder uses O(n) precomputed memory to store keys and values
242
      of previous tokens and O(1) additional memory to compute results over the current token. An
      encoder computes everything from scratch for each token, so it uses O(n) additional memory and
243
      no precomputed memory. Under this view, there is a space complexity gap between encoder and
244
      decoder.

519   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521   In this work, we present theoretical and novel experimental results suggesting that, assuming com-
522   pute is unlimited, decoder-only Transformers are not the ideal model for sequence modeling. We
523   show that ENTP is more expressive without compromising generalization. Using Theorem 1 and 2,
524   we find that the classes of functions encoder-only and decoder-only Transformers can exactly learn
525   are different. We introduce the Count3 task and demonstrate, both theoretically (assuming Conjec-
526   ture 1) and experimentally, that while encoders can perform this task easily, decoders cannot. We
527
      also find that encoders outperform decoders on a variety of auto-regressive tasks, including length
      generalization and in-context learning. However, the expressivity gained by removing the causal
528
      mask comes at the expense of efficiency.
529
530
      Future Work. Several open avenues for further research remain in ENTP theory, such as proving
531
      Lemma 1 without relying on Conjecture 1 (or providing a counterexample) and generalizing Count3
532   to a broader class of tasks where encoders perform well but decoders do not. Further investigation
533   into length generalization across tasks beyond addition is valuable to confirm the findings of this
534   paper. Another natural extension of our work would be to explore whether we can further enhance
535   expressivity past ENTP. In a more practical direction, as both training and inference with ENTP are
536   compute-intensive, future work may focus on reducing computational costs. For instance, could we
537   first train a decoder and then efficiently convert it to ENTP? Is it possible to combine decoders with
538   ENTP? More broadly, are there better methods for efficiently training ENTP models?
