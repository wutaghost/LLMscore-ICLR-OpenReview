009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011
               Many papers have shown that attention heads work in conjunction with each other to perform com-
012
               plex tasks. It’s frequently assumed that communication between attention heads is via the addition
013
               of specific features to token residuals. In this work we seek to isolate and identify the features used
014            to effect communication and coordination among attention heads in GPT-2 small. Our key leverage
015            on the problem is to show that these features are very often sparsely coded in the singular vectors of
016            attention head matrices. We characterize the dimensionality and occurrence of these signals across
017            the attention heads in GPT-2 small when used for the Indirect Object Identification (IOI) task. The
018            sparse encoding of signals, as provided by attention head singular vectors, allows for efficient sepa-
019            ration of signals from the residual background and straightforward identification of communication
020            paths between attention heads. We explore the effectiveness of this approach by tracing portions of
021            the circuits used in the IOI task. Our traces reveal considerable detail not present in previous studies,
022
               shedding light on the nature of redundant paths present in GPT-2. And our traces go beyond previous
               work by identifying features used to communicate between attention heads when performing IOI.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent work has made progress interpreting emergent algorithms used by language models in terms of circuits (Olah
029
      et al., 2020). Much of the work in model interpretability views a model as a computational graph (Geiger et al., 2021),
030   and a circuit as a subgraph having a distinct function (Conmy et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Marks et al., 2024). In
031   language models, the computation graph is typically realized through communication between model components via
032   the residual stream (Elhage et al., 2021).
033
      In this context, an important subtask is tracing a circuit (Wang et al., 2023; Lieberum et al., 2023; Conmy et al.,
034
      2023) – identifying causal communication paths between model components that are significant for model function.
035
      Focusing on just communication between attention heads, one approach to circuit tracing might be to track information
036   flow. For example, given an attention head computing a score for a particular pair of tokens, one might ask which
037   upstream heads modify those tokens in a way that functionally changes the downstream attention head’s output. A
038   straightforward attack on this question would involve direct inspection of residuals and model component inputs and
039   outputs at various places within the model. Unfortunately and not surprisingly, direct inspection reveals that the great
040   majority of upstream attention heads meet this criterion. Each downstream attention head examines a pair of query
041   and key subspaces, and most upstream heads write at least some component into each downstream head’s query or
042   key subspace. The problem this causes is that, absent interventions using counterfactual inputs, it is not clear which of
043   those contributions are making changes that are significant in terms of model function.
044   Many previous studies have approached this problem by averaging over a large set of inputs, comparing test cases with
045   counterfactual examples, and by using interventions such as patching (Zhang & Nanda, 2024; Goldowsky-Dill et al.,
046   2023). This approach has many successes, but it can be hard to isolate specific communicating pairs of attention heads
047   and hard to identify exactly what components of the residual are mediating the communication.
048   In this paper we explore a different strategy. We ask whether leverage can be gained on this problem by looking for
049   low dimensional components of each upstream head’s contributions. To do so, we ask: is there a change of basis
050   associated with any given attention head, such that components of its input in the new basis make sparse contributions
051   to the attention head’s output? If so, those components may represent the most significant parts of the input in terms of
052   model function. Our main result is to find a change of basis for the attention head’s inputs, such that attention scores
053   are sparsely constructed in the new basis. We show that this basis comes from the singular value decomposition of the
      QK matrix of the attention head.

054
      Our testbed is GPT-2 small applied to the Indirection Object Identification (IOI) task (Wang et al., 2023). In this
055   setting we show that in the new basis, tracing inputs to attention heads back to sources serves generally identifies a
056   small set of upstream attention heads whose outputs are sufficient to explain the downstream attention head’s output.
057   Further, the small set identified generally includes upstream heads that are known to be functionally associated with the
058   downstream head. We also show that, in the new basis, the representations of tokens can be correlated with semantic
059   features. We exploit these properties to build a communication graph of the attention heads of GPT-2 for the IOI
060   task. By construction, each edge represents a causal direct effect between an upstream head and the attention score
061   output from the downstream head; more interestingly, we demonstrate that the edges in this graph generally identify a
062   communication path that has causal effect on the ability of GPT-2 to perform the IOI task.
063   In sum, our contributions are twofold. First, we draw attention to the fact that attention scores are typically sparsely
064   decomposable given the right basis. This has significant implications for interpretability of model activations. Note that
065   this is very different from the uses of SVD to analyze model matrices in previous work; we are nowt concerned with
066   static analysis of model matrices themselves. Second, we demonstrate that by leveraging the sparse decomposition to
067   denoise inputs to heads, we can effectively and efficiently trace functionally significant causal communication paths
068   between attention heads.

070   2   R ELATED W ORK
071
072   The dominant style of circuit tracing is via patching (Zhang & Nanda, 2024; Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023). That strat-
073   egy has shown considerable success (Wang et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023; Hanna et al., 2023; Lieberum et al., 2023)
074   but is time-consuming, generally requires the creation of a counterfactual dataset to provide task-neutral activation
075   patches, may miss alternative pathways (Makelov et al., 2023; Mueller, 2024), and has been shown to produce indirect
076   downstream effects that can even result in compensatory self-repair (McGrath et al., 2023; Rushing & Nanda, 2024).
077
      In this work, we trace circuits using only a single forward pass over the data, eliminating the need for counterfactuals
078   and avoiding the problems of self-repair after patching. The authors in (Ferrando & Voita, 2024) trace circuits in a
079   single forward pass, and argue that the approach is much faster than patching, and avoids dependence on counter-
080   factual examples and the risk of self-repair. We derive the same benefits, but unlike that paper we leverage spectral
081   decomposition of attention head matrices to identify the signals flowing between heads.
082
      Like distributed alignment search (Geiger et al., 2024) we adopt the view that placing a neural representation in an
083
      alternative basis can reveal interpretable dimensions (Smolensky, 1986). However, unlike that work, we do not require
084   a gradient descent process to find the new basis, but rather extract it directly from attention head matrices. Likewise,
085   using sparse autoencoders (SAEs) the authors in (Gurnee et al., 2023; Marks et al., 2024) construct interpretable
086   dimensions from internal representations; our approach is complementary to the use of SAEs and the relationship
087   between the representations we extract and those obtained from SAEs is a valuable direction for further study.
088
      We demonstrate circuit tracing for the IOI task in GPT-2 small, which has become a ‘model organism’ for tracing
089
      studies (Wang et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023; Ferrando & Voita, 2024). Like previous studies, one portion of our
090   validation consists of recovering known circuits; however we go beyond recovery of those known circuits in a number
091   of ways, most importantly by identifying signals used for communication between heads.
092
093
      The use of SVD in our study is quite different from its previous application to transformers. SVD of attention matrices
      has been used to reduce the time and space complexity of the attention mechanism (Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024)
094
      and improve reasoning performance (Sharma et al., 2024), often leveraging a low-rank property of attention matrices.
095
      Our work does not rely on attention matrices showing low-rank properties. We use SVD as a tool to decompose the
096   computation of attention; the leverage we obtain comes from the resulting sparsity of the terms in the attention score
097   computation. Likewise, previous work has shown interpretability of the singular vectors of OV matrices and MLP
098   weights — though not of QK matrices (Millidge & Black). We show here evidence that it is the representations of
099   tokens in the bases provided by the singular vectors of QK matrices that show intepretability.

208   To demonstrate the utility of singular vector tracing we apply it to a specific setting: the behavior of GPT-2 small
209
      when performing indirect object identification (IOI). The IOI problem was introduced in (Wang et al., 2023), and that

214   together perform the IOI task. We refer the reader to (Wang et al., 2023) for additional details.

227   The IOI dataset that we use consists of 256 example prompts with 106 different names. We are using the same 15

230   Singular vector tracing as described in §4.2 can be applied at every attention head with respect to every token pair.
231   However in our experiments we limit our analysis to cases where the attention head is primarily attending to a single
232   source token for a given destination token. This strategy is similar to analyses in prior work (Wang et al., 2023; Conmy

352   5.3   S INGULAR V ECTOR T RACE OF GPT-2 ON IOI
353
354   Next we construct a singular vector trace using the concepts from §4.2. We start at a particular attention head and
355   token pair. If the head is firing on the token pair (as discussed in §4.3) we obtain the subspaces U and V and associated
356
      projectors PU and PV . We then look at each upstream attention head’s output, and separate from it the signal it contains
      for the downstream head. The properly adjusted magnitude of this signal constitutes the upstream head’s contribution
357
      to the downstream head’s attention score via the corresponding token, as computed via (7).
358
359   Empirically we find that the contributions from most upstream heads are small, with only a few upstream heads making
360   large contributions to the downstream attention score. We filter out the small contributions, which are unlikely to have
361   significant impact on model performance. To do this for a given downstream firing, we adopt the simple rule of
362
      choosing the smallest set of upstream heads whose contributions sum to at least 70% of the sum of all contributions.
363   Using this rule, for each token we identify the upstream heads with significant contribution through that token. An
364   edge in the resulting trace graph is defined by the upstream head, the downstream head, the two tokens on which
365   the downstream head is firing, and the choice of which token is being written into by the upstream head. For each
366   upstream head we then ask whether it is firing on that token as a destination. If so, the process repeats from that head
367   and token pair. The process is presented in detail as Algorithm 1 in the Appendix.
368   We ran singular vector tracing on GPT-2 small using 256 prompts from the IOI dataset described in §4.3. We started
369   the trace at the three name mover heads (9, 6), (9, 9) and (10, 0), as they were identified as having direct effect on
370   model performance in (Wang et al., 2023). The resulting trace is shown in Figure 5; we refer to the graph in the figure
371   as G. There are two kinds of edges in G: communication edges from heads to tokens, and attention edges from tokens
372   to heads. In the figure, blue edges are toward tokens that are source tokens downstream, and red edges likewise are
373   destinations. The width of the edge is the accumulated contribution of the edge over the 256 prompts. Darker nodes
374
      fired more often in our traces, and we have placed green borders around nodes that appeared in (Wang et al., 2023).
      Edges that appear very few times (less than 65 times over the 256 prompts) are omitted.

      Possible Mechanisms. The results in §§5.1 and 5.2 support the sparse decomposition hypothesis. In this section we
489   discuss some possible mechanisms behind this phenomenon. First, a motivation for decomposing attention matrices
490   using SVD comes from the following fact:
491
492
      Lemma 1 Given vectors x and y, among all rank-1 matrices having unit Frobenius norm, the matrix D that maximizes
                        ⊤
                    x y
493   x⊤ Dy is D = ∥x∥ ∥y∥ .
494
      The proof is straightforward and provided in the Appendix. This suggests that model training could have the following
495   effect. If an attention head needs to attend to particular vectors x and y, it needs to output a large value for x̃⊤ Ωỹ.
496   In that case, model training could result in construction of Ω in which one term of the SVD, say uk σk vk⊤ , has uk ≈
497   x̃/∥x̃∥, vk ≈ ỹ/∥ỹ∥, and with σk reflecting the importance of this term in the overall computation of the attention
498   score. As an illustration, we note that the results in §5.2 and the Appendix show that in GPT-2 the singular vectors of
499   some Ω matrices are correlated with word features that are relevant for the IOI task.
500
      Consider a hypothetical case in which the sets of vectors to which the attention head needs to attend, say {x̃i } and {yi },
501   happen to each form orthogonal sets. Then to achieve maximum discrimination power in distinguishing corresponding
502   pairs, the singular vectors of Ω, that is {ui } and {vi } respectively, should be aligned with the corresponding vectors
503   in {x̃i } and {ỹi }. To move from vectors to features, we refer to the linear representation hypothesis (Mikolov et al.,
504   2013; Gurnee & Tegmark, 2024; Park et al., 2023) which suggests that concepts, including high-level concepts, are
505   often represented linearly in the model.
506
      To move closer to realistic cases, we note that the authors in (Elhage et al., 2022) make observations, based on
507   experimental evidence and geometric considerations, about features constructed by neural models. They argue that
508   models will tend to represent correlated feature sets in a manner such that, considered in isolation, the sets are nearly
509   orthogonal. They term this the use of “local, almost-orthogonal bases.” In our case, if an attention head is attending to
510   vector sets {x̃i } and {ỹi } that are important when performing a specific task, then we may hypothesize that training
511   will construct the sets to be “nearly-orthogonal,” meaning that cosine similarities among the vectors in each set would
512   typically be small.In this case, the resulting sets of singular vectors {uk }, {vk } are more likely to sparsely encode the
513   {x̃i } and {ỹi } than exist in one-to-one correspondence.
514   Given the above argument, for cases where the linear representation hypothesis holds, we expect that an attention head
515   is testing for a pair of low-dimensional subspaces in the inputs xi and xj . In that case, we expect that subsets of the
516   singular vectors of Ω will be constructed during training so as to ‘match’ those subspaces. In this context, a sparse
517   encoding allows the attention head to attend to more than r different subspaces, expanding the number of concepts that
518   the attention head can recognize. In the Appendix we discuss situations where the sparse decomposition hypothesis
519   may not hold.
520
521   Limitations and Future Work. There are a number of limitations of our study and directions for future work. First,
522   the method as used here does not explore alternative pathways that may affect model output (Makelov et al., 2023).
523
      It also does not directly assess adaptive computations in the model (Marks et al., 2024; Rushing & Nanda, 2024) nor
      does it construct minimal circuits in the sense of (Wang et al., 2023). However, we believe that it offers an alternative
524
      toolbox that can be extended to help investigate those issues, in part by exposing the signals passing between specific
525
      pairs of attention heads. And as in (Wang et al., 2023), we focus on understanding the interaction between attention
526   heads. We believe that extending our framework to include the contributions of MLPs is an important direction for
527   future work.
528
529
      Further, we have not explored in depth the nature of the signals themselves. Indirect evidence, such as the difference
      in effect between local and global ablations, suggests that there are some similarities between signals used on different
530
      edges of the network. Exploration of the nature of signals and their relationships is an intriguing direction for future
531
      work.
532
533
      Conclusions. Transformer-based models are largely considered closed boxes whose internals are difficult to interpret
534   in domain-level terms (Alishahi et al., 2019). By helping elucidate the circuits used by a language model in performing
535   a given task, we hope to improve our ability to assess, validate, control, and improve model functions. In this paper we
536   draw attention to a powerful tool for analyzing circuits: the fact that attention scores are typically sparsely constructed.
537   This effect leads directly to the ability to identify signals used by attention heads to effect inter-head communication.
538   We show that these low-dimensional signals have causal effect on attention head computations, and generally on the
539   ability of GPT-2 to perform the IOI task. We believe that further exploration of signals holds promise for even deeper
      understanding of the internals of language models.

679   Handling Homogeneous Coordinates. The Ω matrix as defined in (2) expects inputs in homogeneous coordinates
680
      in which the last component of the input vector is 1. This allows the bilinear form (3) to incorporate the linear
      and constant terms in (1). When projecting a residual to isolate a signal component, we are only concerned with
681
      the dimensions represented in the residual. We are not concerned with the additional (d + 1)th dimension, as that
682
      dimension represents the linear and constant terms that will be added by the downstream attention head. Hence, to
683   compute the projection of a residual x in the subspace associated with P , we first form x̃ by extending x with a
684   (d + 1)th component having value 1, then compute s̃ = P x̃, then drop the (d + 1)th component of s̃ leaving s.
685
686   Slices Used When a Head is Not Firing. In Figure 8 we show analogous plots to Figure 2, except that we choose
687   cases where the attention heads are not firing. Interestingly, a consistent and small set of orthogonal slices is used in
688   each head when it is not firing. However, comparing to Figure 2, we see that an entirely different set of orthogonal
689   slices are in use when the head is not firing. We note that across our measurements we find a very small number of
690   slices are responsible for an attention head’s score when it is not firing.

693   tration, we consider the (9, 9) attention head, a name mover. The projection of a token’s residual into the V subspace
694   of this attention head isolates the signal used by the head to recognize the IO token.
695   To make this a uniform measure we can apply across multiple tokens, we create a consensus estimate of a single
696   subspace V across all firings of (9, 9). We do this by selecting only the orthogonal slices that appear in at least 100
697   firings. This yields 12 sets of singular vectors which we can use to construct PV . We then measure ∥PV x∥ for all
698   token residuals x at the input of layer 9. The average of the norms for each token is plotted in Figure 9. Tokens are
699   sorted by the magnitude of the signal ∥PV x∥ and colored according to whether they are names (in red) or not (in blue).

758   xi . This determines the direction for vk (and if A′ij > 0, then vk will lie in the direction of positive inner product
759                                                                            ℓa

762   As in (Wang et al., 2023), we focus on understanding the interaction between attention heads. We believe that extend-
763   ing our framework to include the contributions of MLPs is an important direction for future work. Furthermore, in the
764   analyses in this paper we do not consider cases in which heads attend to the first token – that is, when attention head
765
      has a large value of Aij for j = 0. Because the attention weights for each target token form a probability distribution,
      when a destination token should not be meaningfully modified, attention heads normally put their weight on the first
766
      token. This role for token 0 has been noted in previous work (Nanda).3
767
768   Finally, as noted in §4.3, to properly attribute contributions of upstream heads to downstream inputs, we need to
769   take into account the effect of the (downstream) layer norm. The layer norm operation can be decomposed into four
770   steps: centering, normalizing, scaling, and translation. Centering, scaling, and translation are affine maps, which
771
      means that they can be folded into different parts of the model with mathematical equivalence. The TransformerLens
      library handles the centering step by setting each weight matrix that writes into the residual stream to have zero mean.
772
      Moreover, it folds the scaling and translation operations into the weights of the next downstream layer.4 The result is
773
      that centering, scaling, and translation make changes to the matrices used to compute Ω as shown in (2). The remaining
774   step is the normalizing step. This step does not change the direction of the residual; it only affects the magnitude of
775   the contribution calculation (7). Since for any contribution calculation, we are considering a specific addition to the
776   residual oi , we can simply scale its contribution by the same scaling factor used for the corresponding token xi when
777   it is input to the downstream layer.

       First we show that any rank-1 matrix having unit Frobenius norm can be expressed as the outer product of two unit-
       norm vectors. Consider a rank-1 matrix X having unit Frobenius norm. Since X is rank-1, we can write X = xy⊤ .

1100   Now construct X̃ = ∥x∥   ∥y∥ . By construction X̃ is both rank-1 and unit norm. Matrices X and X̃ differ by a constant
                 1
1101   factor ∥x∥∥y∥ . However, since they have the same norm, we must have ∥x∥∥y∥ = 1, and so X can be expressed as
1102   the outer product of two unit vectors.
