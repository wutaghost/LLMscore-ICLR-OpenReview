                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) with low-rank adaptations (LoRAs)
               has become common practice, often yielding numerous copies of the same LLM
012
               differing only in their LoRA updates. This paradigm presents challenges for sys-
013
               tems that serve real-time responses to queries that each involve a different LoRA.
014            Prior works optimize the design of such systems but still require continuous load-
015            ing and offloading of LoRAs, as it is infeasible to store thousands of LoRAs in
016            GPU memory. To mitigate this issue, we investigate the efficacy of model com-
017            pression when serving LoRAs. We propose a method for joint compression of
018            LoRAs into a shared basis paired with LoRA-specific scaling matrices. We ex-
019            tend our algorithm to learn clusters of LoRAs that are more amenable to joint
020            compression, allowing it to scale gracefully to large LoRA collections. Our ex-
021            periments with up to 500 LoRAs demonstrate that compressed LoRAs preserve
022
               performance while offering major throughput gains in realistic serving scenarios
               with over a thousand LoRAs, maintaining 80% of the throughput of serving a
023
               single LoRA.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The myriad uses for foundation models (FMs) have led to a proliferation of specialized models, each
029
      fine-tuned to perform a downstream task. To avoid fine-tuning foundation models with billions of
030   parameters, more parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) algorithms were proposed. An especially
031   successful PEFT method is low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), which learns low-rank
032   additive changes to neural network matrices. Because of the low-rank parameterization, these ma-
033   trices (called adapter weights) contain orders-of-magnitude fewer parameters than the base model.
034   Still, LoRA can achieve performance on par with full fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021).
035
      LoRA’s popularity has triggered a growing need to serve large collections of LoRA adapters at
036   scale. For example, proprietary and open-source LLM providers offer fine-tuning services (OpenAI,
037   2024; TogetherAI, 2024; Predibase, 2024) with user bases likely in thousands or even hundreds of
038   thousands. As each user wants to use their own fine-tuned version of the LLM, simply serving a
039   dedicated fine-tuned LLM per user becomes infeasible. To this end, S-LoRA (Sheng et al., 2023)
040   considers a system where only the base LLM is placed on an inference server and individual LoRA
041   adapters are switched as needed at inference time. S-LoRA optimizes the system’s inner workings
042   via custom CUDA kernels and memory management to increase the throughput when serving multi-
043   ple LoRAs. Such multi-LoRA system design has also been adopted in vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023), a
044
      state-of-the-art LLM serving engine. Despite the optimized system design, serving LoRAs still has
      a fundamental limitation: when the number of adapters is large, they need to be constantly loaded
045
      and offloaded from GPU memory to accommodate incoming requests, degrading throughput.
046
047   The problem of accommodating multiple LoRA adapters is also apparent when placing LLMs on
048   edge devices, where smaller LLMs are fine-tuned for various tasks and the adapters are swapped
049   depending on the task at hand (Gunter et al., 2024). In this case, the amount of adapters is smaller,
050
      e.g., a few dozen (Gunter et al., 2024), but the memory constraints are also tighter due to the limited
      capacity of edge devices.
051
052   In this work, we consider the problem of compressing a collection of LoRAs. We have two key
053   objectives: (1) preserving the performance of the original LoRAs and (2) improving the throughput
      of serving many LoRAs. We formulate LoRA compression as a reconstruction problem, where the

      We summarize our main contributions below:
076   • We formulate the problem of compressing a collection of LoRAs and propose a joint compression
077     scheme based on joint diagonalization.
078   • For large numbers of LoRAs, we scale the joint compression scheme by proposing a clustering
079     algorithm where each cluster is jointly compressed to minimize the overall reconstruction error.
080   • We establish theoretical guarantees for the reconstruction error central to our compression formu-
081
        lation and verify the relation between reconstruction loss and performance empirically.
      • We train a collection of 500 high-quality LoRAs for Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang
082
        et al., 2023a) on 500 natural instruction tasks (Wang et al., 2022) and demonstrate that our com-
083
        pression techniques preserve the performance of the original LoRAs. We will release the 500
084     LoRAs to facilitate future work on LoRA compression as well as the code for our method.
085   • We incorporate LoRA compression into a state-of-the-art LLM serving system and demonstrate
086     that it is possible to serve over 1000 LoRAs across thousands of asynchronous requests with
087     throughput comparable to serving a single LoRA.

092   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has become prevalent for updating foundation models thanks
093   to the need for efficiency in training and communication (Lialin et al., 2023). Many PEFT methods
094   have been proposed, e.g. (Houlsby et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022) and LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) became
095   the standard, partially due to the ease of switching between LoRAs in inference time.
096   Several works improve LoRA (Liu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), sometimes with algebraic meth-
097   ods like SVD (Meng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023b) or by leveraging its statis-
098   tical properties (Zhu et al., 2024; Zeng & Lee, 2024). Relatively few, however, accelerate inference
099   times. S-LoRA (Sheng et al., 2023) provides an efficient means of switching between LoRAs. (Wen
100   & Chaudhuri, 2024) adapts training to reduce batch multiplications, accelerating inference. Our
101   method achieves a similar outcome (see Appendix D) without changing the LoRA formulation or
102   requiring that LoRAs be trained in a dedicated way; future improvements to LoRA will also benefit
103   from this aspect of our work (e.g., Meng et al. (2024)).
104   Punica (Chen et al., 2023) introduces Segmented Gather Matrix-Vector Multiplication (SGMV) to
105   optimize multi-LoRA serving by parallelizing feature-weight multiplications in batches and group-
106   ing requests that utilize the same LoRA model. Our approach, by contrast, emphasizes parameter
107   reduction as a means to efficiently serve multiple LoRAs, providing an orthogonal strategy that
      can be seamlessly integrated with Punica’s methods to further enhance performance. In our vLLM

      experiments, we leveraged the Punica kernel for multi-LoRA implementation, demonstrating the
109   application of our method in conjunction with Punica’s optimizations.

111   There are many efforts to compress models (Cheng et al., 2017; Gholami et al., 2022; Sharma et al.,
112
      2024; Li et al., 2018)—including some specifically for LoRAs—to accelerate inference. Predom-
      inantly, pruning and sparsification methods delete weights (Yadav et al., 2023a), and quantization
113
      methods reduce the weights’ precision (Dettmers et al., 2024). Some works compress weights to
114
      reduce model size but typically require decompression and hence do not save GPU memory (Her-
115   shcovitch et al., 2024). Similarly to our work, while most methods increase speed at the cost of
116   performance, a few note increased performance and generalization after compression (Yadav et al.,
117   2023a; Nadjahi et al., 2023; Hershcovitch et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2024).
118
      Our work also relates to model merging (Choshen et al., 2022; Wortsman et al., 2022; Matena &
119
      Raffel, 2021) and mixture of experts methods (Muqeeth et al., 2024; Yadav et al., 2024). These
120
      methods reuse models trained by others (Choshen et al., 2023; Raffel, 2023), serving them together
121   as one compressed model. Despite this similarity, these methods create a single general model that
122   acts on any input, while our model allows for more performant per-task solutions.

125   3     R ANK -BASED L O RA C OMPRESSION
126
127
      LoRA updates are parameterized by pairs of matrices A, B, whose product BA updates the fixed
128
      weight matrices W0 ∈ RdB ×dA of a neural network foundation model. Given an input x to a layer,
129
      the output of the LoRA-updated model at this layer is (W0 + BA)x.
130
131   In formulating our compression algorithms, we consider a collection of given LoRA adapters
132   {(Ai , Bi )}ni=1 that we would like to serve. We let ri refer to the rank of the LoRA adapter-pair
133   (Ai , Bi ), i.e., Bi ∈ RdB ×ri , Ai ∈ Rri ×dA .
134   While our compression technique has access only to a collection of {(Ai , Bi )}ni=1 pairs, in our
135   experiments we will assess the efficacy of compression by comparing how the compressed matrices
136   perform relative to the uncompressed LoRAs on typical data. For this reason, although in this section
137   we optimize a Frobenius norm reconstruction error relative to the product Bi Ai , in reality this is a
138   proxy for the nonlinear and complex way that compression errors in the adapters impact transformer
139   performance. Our experimental evaluation will thus focus on the performance of the compressed
140
      LoRAs against the uncompressed versions on real data in §6.
141   Our compression methods significantly reduce the overall number of parameters. Reducing param-
142   eters through compression theoretically accelerates storage and serving processes for a collection of
143   LoRAs. This reduction, however, alters the computational dynamics during inference, so parameter
144   reduction alone does not immediately imply faster throughput. In light of the complexities of GPU
145   optimization, we experimentally assess the throughput under realistic conditions in §6.3.
146
147   3.1   J OINT D IAGONALIZATION
148
149   For compression to scale to large numbers of LoRAs, the compressed number of parameters should
150   not scale linearly with n. Hence seeking to compress each LoRA individually (e.g., via SVD as
151   detailed in the experimental baselines) is inherently limited.
152
      To address this, we suggest a Joint Diagonalization (JD) method, which optimizes a shared basis
153
      onto which we can project the set of n LoRAs. This will allow structure to be shared, implicitly
154
      grouping and/or merging the collection of LoRAs.
155
156   In this model, each LoRA product Bi Ai is factorized into the form U Σi V , where U and V are
157   shared across all LoRAs and Σi is specific to each LoRA. In this formulation, every Σi shares the
158
      same rank r. This allows U and V to be pre-loaded onto the GPU, with Σi loaded when necessary
      for each batch. The matrices Σi can be either diagonal or small square matrices, accelerating the
159
      forward pass compared to conventional multi-LoRA serving configurations.

197   3.2   C LUSTERING
198
      As the number of LoRAs n grows and becomes more diverse, the rank r needed for Joint Diago-
199
      nalization to achieve good performance will tend to increase. This increases the size and number
200
      of parameters of each Σi that needs to be stored, especially for JD-Full which will require O(nr2 )
201   storage for these matrices. If the necessary r is growing proportionally to n, then this storage will
202   eventually become the bottleneck.
203
204
      To resolve this limitation with very large n, we propose to group the n LoRAs into |Cj | clusters Cj .
      Each cluster is given its own rank r JD compression, and the clusters are chosen such that the overall
205
      reconstruction error is minimized. Specifically, the overall objective is

211   algorithm details are in Appendix A.3. Typically, the goal with large n is to have |Cj | grow with

276   Since the loss space of transformers is highly complex, increasing weight reconstruction error does
277   not necessarily imply degrading LLM performance. Interestingly, in Figure 3 below, we see that

281

282

283   tendency towards averaging all or some of the LoRA signals directly connects to the concept of
284   merging LoRAs, whose empirical success (Shah et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024) could explain the
285   success of our procedure despite the nonlinearity of transformers.

287

288
      significantly lower than the reconstruction error for random noise, indicating that there is a major
289   shared component between the LoRAs that is being successfully retained.

450   not improve baseline throughput when serving a smaller number of LoRAs and should not be used
451   in such cases. For example, rank 16 JD-Full improves baseline throughput with 4 and 8 LoRAs, but
452   will underperform with more LoRAs, while 7 cluster rank 64 JD-Full does not improve throughput
453
      with 64 or fewer LoRAs, but when serving 1000+ LoRAs it improves the throughput significantly

454

      4 and 1024, as we showed in Figure 1. Specific compression settings for each LoRA collection size

      6.4   R ECOMMENDATIONS
475
476   JD-Full is generally preferred over JD-Diag, although for smaller numbers of LoRAs (less than
477   100), the performance difference is negligible. While JD-Full alone is effective up to 100 LoRAs,
478   incorporating clustering at scales of 500 LoRAs significantly enhances performance.
479
      We recommend the following procedure for hyperparameter selection. For 100 or fewer LoRAs,
480
      JD-Full can be utilized independently without substantial degradation, using a rank approximately
481   equal to (number of LoRAs/2) + 7. Beyond 100 LoRAs, clustering becomes increasingly critical.
482   A robust method for any number of LoRAs up to 500 involves employing JD-Full with clustering.
483   Specifically, select a LoRA module from the middle of the network, apply a compression rank of 16,
484

      and experiment with an exponentially increasing number of clusters. Compute the reconstruction
487   error for each setting on this module across all LoRAs—a computationally efficient process. Choose
488   the minimal number of clusters that achieves a reconstruction loss below 0.5, and then use these
489   settings to compress all LoRA modules. An example of this procedure applied to 500 LoRAs is

      We note that tuning hyperparameters as discussed above using reconstruction loss as a validation
492
      metric is especially convenient since it can be done efficiently on CPU without having to perform
493   expensive LLM evaluation. As our experiments demonstrate, compression settings that achieve be-
494   low 0.5 reconstruction loss reliably translate into preserving 99% or more of the LoRA performance,
495   sometimes even outperforming the original LoRAs.
496
      For inference, this procedure is executed as a preprocessing step before deploying our inference
497
      server. As new LoRAs are submitted, they are initially served uncompressed. A background cron
498
      job re-runs the compression algorithm on the CPU every six hours, and upon completion, updates
499   the served LoRA parameters with the compressed versions.

501   7   D ISCUSSION
502
503   This study introduces approaches to LoRA compression, addressing significant challenges facing
504   foundation models and large language models. Our contributions include theoretical formulations,
505   empirical validation, and practical implementations that enhance the understanding and application
506   of LLMs in scalable environments.
507   The implications of our findings are manifold. Our theoretical guarantees for reconstruction error
508   not only increase confidence in the use of compressed models but also lay a groundwork for future
509   explorations in this area. Demonstrating that our compression techniques can preserve up to 100%
510   of the original LoRAs’ performance highlights the effectiveness of our methods. Furthermore, in-
511   tegrating LoRA compression into state-of-the-art LLM serving systems demonstrates the potential
512   for resource optimization, with throughput for thousands of LoRAs nearing that of a single LoRA.
513   The promising results of our study suggest several future research directions. First, further com-
514   pression may be possible via quantization. Our joint-diagonalization compression and quantization
515   are independent axes of approaching the problem and exploring a combined solution can be fruitful.
516   Second, when scaling to hundreds of thousands of LoRAs, joint compression, while effective, will
517   not be sufficient to fit all LoRAs onto the GPU, thus requiring a procedure to schedule the requests.
518   Our clustering variant offers opportunities to develop an efficient scheduling mechanism that takes
519
      into account the cluster assignments of LoRAs corresponding to the incoming requests.
520   In conclusion, our research significantly advances the deployment of LLMs by providing robust,
521   scalable, and efficient compression solutions. The ability of compressed LoRAs to maintain high
522   performance while facilitating substantial resource savings opens new avenues for the broader appli-
523   cation and adoption of LLMs across various industries. We encourage the community to build upon
524   our findings and the shared LoRAs to further explore and enhance the utility of these technologies.

       D    AVOIDING BATCHED M ATRIX M ULTIPLICATION (BMM)
988
989
       Fast LoRA (Wen & Chaudhuri, 2024) aims to alleviate the batched matrix multiplication (BMM)
       bottleneck when serving many LoRAs. They propose an adapter parameterization that replaces ad-
990
       dition with elementwise multiplication, avoiding BMM and improving LoRA throughput at lower
991
       ranks. Our JD LoRA formulation also circumvents or heavily reduces the impact of BMM as dis-
992    cussed below, and both individual and joint compression methods can be applied to Fast LoRAs.
993
994    In the envisioned deployment scenario, a service provider hosts a large collection of LoRAs. Upon
995
       receiving a request, each user specifies both the input data and the desired LoRA identifier. The
       provider then processes the base model augmented with the specified LoRA for each user’s data. As
996
       a provider is batching a collection of requests for GPU parallelization, they can expect to frequently
997
       have more than one unique LoRA identifier per batch.
998
999    Traditionally, a specific LoRA is integrated into the base model by transforming W0 → W0 + Bi Ai .
1000   Serving multiple LoRAs conventionally would necessitate maintaining and executing a separate
1001
       copy of the base model for each LoRA, bringing substantial computational overhead. Alternatively,
       the computation for W0 x and Bi Ai x can be performed independently and subsequently merged.
1002
       This strategy necessitates only a single instance of W0 x computation and storage of LoRA-specific
1003
       parameters rather than the entire base model.
1004
1005   Consider the batch processing of BAx, where boldface indicates that Bi , Ai are stacked into tensors
1006   of dimensions (b × m × r) and (b × r × n) respectively, with batched data x shaped (b × l × n):
1007                          Ax ↔ (b × r × n) × (b × l × n) → (b × l × r) bmm
1008
                          B(Ax) ↔ (b × m × r) × (b × l × r) → (b × l × m) bmm.
1009
1010   Here, “bmm” denotes batched matrix multiplication, a known bottleneck in both throughput and
1011   latency. Consider the corresponding operations for our joint compression scheme, U ΣV ⊤ x:
1012
                               V ⊤ x ↔ (r̃ × n) × (b × l × n) → (b × l × r̃) broadcasted

       matrices are diagonal. If not, given that r̃ ≪ m, n, any required batched matrix multiplication
