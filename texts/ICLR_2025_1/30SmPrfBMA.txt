036                                                       A BSTRACT
037
038           To solve the problem of generating complex motions, we introduce GCML
039           (Grounding Complex Motions using a Large Language Model). This method sup-
040           ports complex texts and scenes as inputs, such as mopping the floor in a cluttered
041           room. Such everyday actions are challenging for current motion generation mod-
042           els for two main reasons. First, such complex actions are rarely found in exist-
043           ing HSI datasets, which places high demands on the generalization capabilities
044           of current data-driven models. Second, these actions are composed of multiple
045
              stages, with considerable variation, making it difficult for models to understand
              and generate the appropriate motions. Current methods in the HSI field can con-
046
              trol the generation of simple actions under multiple constraints, such as walking
047
              joyfully toward a door, but they cannot handle the complexity of tasks like the
048           one described above. By incorporating a Large Language Model and a 3D Visual
049           Grounding Model into the HSI domain, our approach can decompose a complex
050           user prompt into a sequence of simpler subtasks and identify interaction targets
051           and obstacles within the scene. Based on these subtask descriptions and spa-
052           tial control information, the Motion Generation Model generates a sequence of
053           full-body motions, which are then combined into a long motion sequence that
              aligns with both the user’s input and the scene semantics. Experimental results

062   1   I NTRODUCTION
063
064   Research within Human-Scene Interaction (HSI) has advanced considerably in terms of modeling
065   the sophisticated interaction between people and their environments (Zhao et al., 2023); (Zhang
066   et al., 2022). It has also succeeded in synthesizing high-fidelity human motion in various
067   scenes (Hassan et al., 2021); (Zhang & Tang, 2022); (Wang et al., 2022a). However, two major
068   challenges exist:
069   (1) Generative models depend heavily on large amounts of high-quality paired data. The col-
070   lection of HSI datasets and their annotations is a time-consuming and labor-intensive task. Although
071   many efforts have been made (Hassan et al., 2019); (Wang et al., 2022b);(Jiang et al., 2024), chal-
072   lenges like limited diversity in actions, overly simplistic descriptions, and inadequate dataset sizes
073   still exist. On the other hand, existing datasets that only contain human motion, like AMASS (Mah-
074   mood et al., 2019) and HumanML3D (Plappert et al., 2016); (Guo et al., 2022b), are quite substantial
075
      in terms of data scale and motion description. Diffusion-based methods trained on these datasets are
      capable of generating high-quality human motions (Tevet et al., 2023) based on textual descriptions.
076
      Recent works (Karunratanakul et al., 2023); (Xie et al., 2023) also enable users to manipulate the
077
      style of generated actions via textual prompts and enhance precision using spatial constraints. In-
078   spired by this, we propose tackling scene awareness and motion generation separately. This strategy
079   helps mitigate the shortage of high-quality paired HSI data by maximizing the use of existing human
080   motion and 3D visual grounding datasets.
081
      (2) Only Common and simple motions can be generated. Due to the lack of diversity in HSI
082
      datasets, the majority of current research that generates human motions in scenes tends to focus
083
      on producing common and simple movements such as walking, lying, and sitting (Wang et al.,
084   2022b); (Jiang et al., 2024); (Wang et al., 2024). However, industries with a need for human motion
085   generation often require more than these elementary motions. For instance, video game characters
086   should navigate to specified locations and interact with particular targets, while animated movie
087   characters need to perform everyday tasks such as brushing their teeth, cooking, or watering plants.
088   Such complex motions are rare in paired datasets, thus current data-driven methods for human mo-
089   tion generation typically fail to produce them. However, complex motions are often composed of
090   simpler ones, and a practical solution to this challenge is to utilize Large Language Models for task
091   decomposition and reasoning (Huang et al., 2023b); (Lin et al., 2024).
092   To alleviate these limitations, we propose a novel method called Grounding Complex Motions using
093   Large Language Models (GCML), depicted in Figure 1. Our method can generate complex actions
094   like washing hands or cooking meals, accommodating complex scene and textual input as well
095   as producing human motions of long sequences. Furthermore, by incorporating Large Language
096   Models and 3D Visual Grounding Models, our approach creatively generates motions consistent
097   with textual descriptions based on the objects and their layout within a scene. For example, a hungry
098
      person in a scene, upon not finding available food, will attempt to open a refrigerator in search of
      food.
099
100   Our method operates by taking both text and scene inputs. The text is first processed by the Task
101   Planner, which breaks it down into multiple sub-tasks, each corresponding to a simple motion se-
102   quence. Next, the Sub-task Executor identifies the target objects within the scene for interaction and
103   generates control descriptions for each sub-task with the help of the 3D Visual Grounding Model.
104
      These control descriptions and spatial data are sent to the Motion Generation Model, which refines
      them into whole-body motion sequences for each sub-task. Finally, these sequences are combined
105
      into a complete, unified motion.
106
107   We have thoroughly evaluated GCML on a variety of benchmarks. Experimental results on the HU-
      MANISE dataset reveal that even for simple tasks, without relying on HUMANISE training data, the

109   the generalization evaluation set proposed by (Wang et al., 2024), our method outperforms afford-

      Our key contributions are as follows:
114
115
             • We introduce a new task along with a corresponding evaluation set: Complex Motion Gen-
116            eration. The gaming and animation industries increasingly require not just simple actions
117            like walking or sitting, but also the ability to think and interact with surrounding objects
118            like a human. We hope this new task will inspire the development of more methods for

126            achieves comparable performance in simple motion generation, while in complex motion

132   2.1   C ONDITIONAL H UMAN M OTION G ENERATION
133
      Human-Scene Interaction can be seen as the generation of human motions conditioned on both lan-
134
      guage descriptions and scene context. A wide range of conditions have been explored for controlled
135   motion generation, such as past motions (Yuan & Kitani, 2020); (Cao et al., 2020); (Xie et al., 2021),
136   music (Tseng et al., 2023); (Li et al., 2021), text (Petrovich et al., 2023); (Guo et al., 2022c); (Tevet
137   et al., 2022); (Chen et al., 2023); (Kim et al., 2023), objects (Ghosh et al., 2023); (Kulkarni et al.,
138   2024); (Xu et al., 2023), and scenes (Huang et al., 2023a); (Wang et al., 2022b);. Furthermore, recent
139   work has added spatial constraints to text-guided motion generation (Text2Motion). MDM (Tevet
140   et al., 2023) and priorMDM (Shafir et al., 2023) use motion inbetweening during the diffusion de-
141   noising process to replace key control frames, allowing for human motion generation that fits spa-
142   tial constraints without sacrificing motion quality. GMD (Karunratanakul et al., 2023) introduces
143
      a two-stage motion diffusion model to handle sparse control signals, reducing jitter in controlled
      frames. Omnicontrol (Xie et al., 2023), employing a ControlNet-inspired approach, applies spatial
144
      constraints during motion generation and enables control over any key joint.
145
146   In the domain of Human Scene Interaction, language, and scene context are the two primary condi-
147   tioning factors. HUMANISE (Wang et al., 2022b) introduced a comprehensive dataset and devel-
148   oped a cVAE-based model capable of generating motions that respond to both textual descriptions
149
      and scene interactions. TRUMANS (Jiang et al., 2024) proposed an even larger human-scene inter-
      action dataset and used autoregressive conditional diffusion to generate HSI motions of any length.
150
      AffordMotion (Wang et al., 2024) employed scene affordances as intermediate representations, com-
151
      bining scene embeddings and language-guided motion generation in a two-stage approach. How-
152   ever, these methods are limited by their dependence on training data, generating only the common
153   motion types seen in the datasets. Our work resolves this by separating scene understanding from
154   motion generation, thus mitigating the reliance on specific datasets.

      2.3    3D V ISUAL G ROUNDING
194
195   3D scene understanding has been extensively researched, particularly in the fields of vision and
196   robotics. Core tasks include 3D object classification (Wu et al., 2015) , 3D object detection and lo-
197   calization (Caesar et al., 2020); (Chen et al., 2020), 3D semantic and instance segmentation (Behley
198   et al., 2019); (Liao et al., 2022), and 3D affordance prediction (Deng et al., 2021). A recent ap-
199   proach, OpenScene (Peng et al., 2023), extracts CLIP features for each 3D point, supporting open-
200   vocabulary queries and segmentation based on any text input, making it a versatile tool for scene
201   understanding in our work.
202   In Human Scene Interaction tasks, few approaches utilize existing 3D scene understanding models to
203   assist in generating motions that align with scene semantics. Instead, data-driven methods typically
204   train models by mapping specific 3D structures to actions, but this introduces two major limitations:
205   the reliance on the quantity and quality of paired data limits performance, and biases in the data may
206   restrict certain interactions. For example, interactions with chairs are typically confined to sitting,
207   ignoring other potential uses. Utilizing existing 3D Visual Grounding models provides distinct
208   advantages in overcoming these issues.

      tion Generation Model produces full-body motion sequences for each subtask based on the control

227   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION AND N OTATIONS
228
229   The generation of complex motions can be viewed as a recursive process, where complex motions
230   are composed of simpler ones that follow the same input and output structure. Consequently, the
231   task of generating complex motions can be divided into generating multiple simple ones and linking
232   them together. Therefore, a method that can generate complex motions is inherently capable of
233   generating simple motions as well.
234   Specifically, the input to our task consists of a user text prompt T and a scene S, with the output being
235   a human motion sequence H. The user prompt T is a text description of the motion to be generated,
236   while S ∈ RN ×6 represents a point cloud of N points, each with RGB color information. The
237   output motion sequence {Hi }N    i=1 is a series of human pose parameters over N frames. We use the
238   SMPL-X model (Pavlakos et al., 2019) to represent the human body’s pose and shape. The SMPL-
239   X body mesh M ∈ R10475×3 is parameterized as M = F (t, r, β, p), where t ∈ R3 is the global
240
      translation, r ∈ R6 is the continuous representation of global orientation, β ∈ R10 defines the body
      shape, and p ∈ RJ×3 represents joint rotations in axis-angle format. F is the differentiable linear
241
      blend skinning function. In most cases, we do not generate the full set of SMPL-X shape parameters
242
      directly; instead, we first generate the 3D world coordinates of 22 key body joints P ∈ R22×3 ,
243   which are then used to infer the complete SMPL-X parameters. The process can be summarized by
244   the following formula:

250   3.2   LLM P LANNER AND S UBTASK E XECUTOR
251
252   Similar to VoxPoser (Huang et al., 2023b), our method leverages a Language Model Program (LMP)
253   to use a Large Language Model in generating human motion. Each LMP is responsible for a distinct
254   function—such as breaking down tasks or invoking perception modules—and can call upon other
255   LMPs as needed. The Large Language Model employed in our approach is GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
256   2023) from OpenAI.
257
      Figure 3 provides an example of the generation process. First, we instruct the Large Language Model
258   to learn from the provided code examples. For each LMP, we offer around 5-10 query samples along
259   with their corresponding responses.
260
261
      Following the sequence of calls between LMPs, the user’s input motion description is first sent
      to the Task Planner, where complex or abstract tasks are broken down into simpler ones for the
262
      Subtask Executor. These tasks typically involve actions like walking to a location, interacting with
263
      an object, or performing a motion while standing still. The Subtask Executor creates human-centric
264   motion descriptions based on the subtask, while object interaction information is provided by the
265   3D Visual Grounding model. Scene traversability data is directly obtained from the scene’s point
266   cloud. Spatial data is organized using a voxel map, with the Target Map and Avoidance Map values
267   combined to produce a cost map C ∈ R100×100×100 , where lower values correspond to interaction
268   targets and higher values indicate obstacles. Finally, using the following formula, we generate the
269   trajectories of key human joints (such as the pelvis and hand), ensuring they avoid obstacles and
      reach the interaction target.

      3.3   M OTION G ENERATION M ODEL
311
312   Our Motion Generation Model is built upon OmniControl (Xie et al., 2023), a diffusion-based gener-
313   ative model that conditions on both text and spatial keypoint positions. OmniControl was trained on
314   the HumanML3D dataset and supports control over any joints at any time. We adapted it to generate
315   motion sequences of arbitrary length.
316
      In the previous steps, we obtained joint control data h ∈ RN ×22×3 . At each step of diffusion, we
317
      calculate the L2 distance between the generated joints and the control data h, using the gradient to
318   guide the model in generating sub-motion sequences that align with both the action description T
319   and the spatial control signal h. To ensure smooth transitions between sub-sequences, we use the
320   motion inbetweening method from MDM (Tevet et al., 2023). In each diffusion step, we replace
321   the first frame of the generated motion with the last frame from the previous sequence. We also
322   compute a transformation matrix based on the movement from the first to the last frame of the
323   previously generated sequence. This matrix is applied to the subsequent sequences, merging the
      sub-task sequences into a coherent long motion sequence.

      3.4   3D V ISUAL G ROUNDING M ODEL
325
326   In the above generation process, we did not elaborate on how the positions of required objects were
327   obtained from the scene. While many well-established methods exist for detecting and localizing
328   3D objects (Caesar et al., 2020); (Chen et al., 2020), in the scenarios described in this paper, we
329   sometimes need to detect unconventional objects (e.g., “something eatable” in Figure 3). For this
330   reason, we chose OpenScene (Peng et al., 2023) as our scene perception module due to its support
331   for open vocabulary queries. Once OpenScene assigns object categories to each point in the scene’s
332
      point cloud, we use the DBSCAN (Khan et al., 2014) clustering algorithm to filter out noise and
      pinpoint object instance locations. Additionally, OpenScene’s ability to handle open-vocabulary
333
      queries allows us to detect object parts as well. For example, when interacting with a door, we
334
      can specifically locate the door handle rather than the entire door, which helps create more realistic
335   motions.

      3.5   C OMPLEX M OTION E VALUATION S ET
338
339   In addition to controlling the behavior and style of the virtual human, we are also interested in
340   understanding how they would respond to environmental changes and perform everyday tasks. The
341   former requires the virtual human to simulate human thinking and react appropriately to external
342   stimuli, while the latter involves decomposing actions into subtasks that are easier to execute. These
343   complex actions are difficult to generate, even though they are quite common in everyday life. To
344   evaluate model’s ability to generate these intricate motions, we established the Complex Motion
345
      Evaluation Set.
346   We gathered 16 scenes from ScanNet (Dai et al., 2017) and Replica (Straub et al., 2019) as en-
347   vironments for virtual human activities, annotating these scenes with their offsets from the origin.
348   We then provided the scene information and multi-view RGB images to a vision-language model
349   (VLM). By asking, “What could an advanced virtual human do in this scene?” we generated a se-
350   ries of possible behaviors. After part-of-speech tagging, these behaviors were transformed into HSI
351
      descriptions that guide interactions between the virtual human and the environment. Compared to
      manually designing interactions, this automated approach saves labor and ensures that the generated
352
      descriptions are free from personal bias.
353
354   The generative model is tasked with producing complex human motions based on the scene mesh
355   and the aforementioned HSI descriptions. Its performance is evaluated using relevant metrics and
356   a human perceptual study. The HUMANISE dataset, Generalization Evaluation Set, and our newly
357
      introduced Complex Motion Evaluation Set all use scenes and text as conditional inputs, generating
      human motions that align with the semantic meanings of both the text and the scenes. Table 1
358
      presents examples cases from each dataset. They show increasing difficulty levels and pose greater
359
      challenges for motion generation methods.

371

372   cols of (Wang et al., 2022b) and (Zhang et al., 2020). Specifically, we used goal distance to measure

374   scene, and non-collision to quantify the proportion of actions that did not collide with the scene.
375   In the afford-motion Generalization Evaluation Set and our Complex Motion Evaluation Set, we
376   applied the same physical metrics but excluded goal distance, as these tasks did not have a clearly
377   defined target object for interaction. Additionally, in alignment with afford-motion evaluation, we
      utilized the metrics proposed by (Guo et al., 2022a) to assess the quality of the generated motions.

533
534   This paper introduces GCML, a novel method for generating complex human motions guided by
535   textual descriptions within a scene. By utilizing a Large Language Model for task decomposition
536   and subtask execution, and a 3D Visual Grounding Model for scene perception, our method produces
537   complete complex motion frames. We validated the effectiveness of our method across multiple
538   datasets, with experimental results showing that our approach performs well in generating simple
539   human motions. Moreover, on our newly introduced test set for complex human motion generation,
      our method consistently outperformed existing methods across all evaluation metrics.
