009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the help of large-scale pre-training, generative Vision-Language Models
012            (VLMs) have acquired general-purpose capabilities. As downstream applications
013            diversify, it is imperative for VLMs to learn and adapt continuously without experi-
014            encing catastrophic forgetting or necessitating complete retraining. In this work,
015            we analyze the forgetting behavior of VLMs and propose a solution to enhance
016
               their incremental learning abilities. We introduce a Task Codebook within VLMs,
               enabling efficient retrieval of task-specific parameters for model adaptation. Our
017
               evaluation encompasses a diverse set of tasks spanning a wide range of visual
018
               domains and textual instructions. Experiments demonstrate that our approach
019            effectively mitigates forgetting, even under highly demanding task sequences.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
      Recent advancements in generative vision-language models OpenAI (2023); Alayrac et al. (2022);
024   Chen et al. (2023c); Wang et al. (2022a); Google (2024) (VLMs) have demonstrated remarkable
025   success. These models leverage extensive pre-training corpora to acquire substantial world knowl-
026   edge, facilitating adaptation to downstream tasks. This paper investigates the incremental learning
027   capabilities of such models. Our objective is to incrementally adapt generative VLMs to multiple
028   tasks, rather than maintaining separate models specialized for each task.
029
      A significant challenge in incremental learning is catastrophic forgetting McCloskey & Cohen (1989),
030   where models rapidly lose previously learned knowledge. An ideal incrementally trained model
031   should mitigate forgetting while retaining the ability to acquire new knowledge. Existing research has
032   primarily focused on class-incremental learning for classification, assuming sequential availability of
033   class subsets.
034
      While class-incremental learning is an important research direction, general-purpose VLMs span
035
      a much wider set of applications than simple classification. It is thus more realistic to consider a
036   diverse set of applications ranging from classification over detection to question answering. This
037   poses a greater challenge for the incremental learning setting, as the model now needs to learn new
038   applications instead of expanding a single application’s coverage. More recent studies have examined
039   catastrophic forgetting in vision-language models Garg et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2023); Zhang et al.
040   (2023). However, these methods often assume task similarity (e.g., different VQA tasks) He et al.
041   (2023), limiting their practical applicability.
042   We propose a new incremental learning method for generative VLMs that improves their ability to
043   learn and adapt to various, and potentially very different, tasks over time. To that end, we introduce a
044   task codebook that stores adapters specialized for each encountered task. When presented with a new
045   task, the model learns to access its most relevant modules to enhance its performance. This design
046   allows our model to effectively learn diverse tasks without needing to know at inference time which
047   task it is being asked to do, all while avoiding the common issue of catastrophic forgetting Compared
048   to existing prompt-based solutions Wang et al. (2022c), we find that our method is more flexible and
049   performs well across a wide set of incremental-learning settings.
050   To comprehensively evaluate the incremental learning capabilities of generative VLMs, we present a
051   novel benchmark comprising diverse tasks, such as captioning Young et al. (2014); Gurari et al. (2020),
052   VQA Goyal et al. (2017); Marino et al. (2019), OCR-enhanced captioning and VQA Sidorov et al.
053   (2020); Wang et al. (2021), open-vocabulary classification Wang et al. (2022a), object detection Lin
      et al. (2014), referring expression generation Kazemzadeh et al. (2014), grounding Kazemzadeh

054
      et al. (2014) and multi-modal instruction tuning Liu et al. (2023). This diverse set of tasks enables a
055   thorough assessment of incremental learning abilities under varying conditions, including different
056   classes, datasets, and applications.
057
058
      Our contributions in this paper can be summarized as follows:
059          • We propose a novel adaptation strategy for generative models with a task codebook that
060            allows lookup and dynamic routing at inference time. This approach demonstrates efficacy
061            in scenarios involving sequential tasks of different natures.
062          • We propose a new multi-modal incremental learning benchmark for generative models,
063            spanning 36 datasets and 8 applications.
064
             • We provide a comprehensive evaluation of our method under various scenarios, demonstrat-
               ing our method’s superiority over existing baselines across a wide range of tasks.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
068   Incremental learning. In this task, the assumption is that the training set of a dataset is not available
069   all at once. Data from previous timestamps are discarded as new data becomes available. Existing
070   studies mainly focus on single modality such as images Hsu et al. (2018); Van de Ven & Tolias
071   (2019) or texts Ke et al. (2023). For incremental learning in the vision domain, typical incremental
072   learning setups include: task-incremental learning Hsu et al. (2018); Van de Ven & Tolias (2019),
073   class-incremental learning Hsu et al. (2018); Van de Ven & Tolias (2019), domain-incremental
074
      learning Hsu et al. (2018); Van de Ven & Tolias (2019), task-agnostic incremental learning Aljundi
      et al. (2019a), and online continual learning Aljundi et al. (2019b).
075
076   Class-incremental learning. Existing approaches address this challenge through regularization
077   techniques such as knowledge distillation Li & Hoiem (2017), model expansion Rusu et al. (2016);
078   Wang et al. (2017) or representations Yan et al. (2021) and weight consolidation Aljundi et al. (2018);
079
      Chaudhry et al. (2018); Kirkpatrick et al. (2017). Alternatively, replay-based methods retain a
      small sample of data from previous classes Rebuffi et al. (2017); Chaudhry et al. (2019); Iscen et al.
080
      (2020). More recent techniques introduce prompt retrieval for class-incremental learning Wang et al.
081
      (2022c;b); Khan et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2023) without requiring a replay buffer.
082
083
      Multi-modal incremental learning. Multi-modal incremental learning has seen recent interest due
      to the advancement of multi-modal models Radford et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022a); Li et al. (2023).
084
      Some of the research has focused on dual-encoder models, e.g. CLIP Radford et al. (2021), examining
085
      their performance on evolving data distributions Garg et al. (2023) and knowledge preservation Zheng
086   et al. (2023). Other studies focus on incremental learning for the Visual-Question Answering (VQA)
087   tasks, either by formulating it as a classification problem Qian et al. (2023); Srinivasan et al. (2022) or
088   a generative task Zhang et al. (2023); He et al. (2023). In this paper, we adopt a more practical setting
089   with varied tasks and leverage generative models like GIT Wang et al. (2022a) for their potential
090   across diverse settings.
091   Codebook learning. Codebook learning is an effective method for converting continuous features
092   into semantically rich, discrete codes. This technique has found widespread application in the field of
093   self-supervised representation learning Caron et al. (2020; 2021) and reconstruction Van Den Oord
094   et al. (2017); Ramesh et al. (2021). Inspired by recent advancements in retrieval mechanisms that
095   facilitate incremental learning in classifiers Prabhu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022c;b), this study
096   introduces the concept of a task codebook. Our approach enables sequential task learning in a
097   non-interfering manner, effectively addressing the challenge of catastrophic forgetting.

      3.2   TASK C ODEBOOK I NCREMENTAL A DAPTATION (TCIA)
160   Incremental learning often employs buffer replay, using either past input data Chaudhry et al. (2019)
161   or past model weights Li & Hoiem (2017) to mitigate catastrophic forgetting. However, storing
      extensive data or numerous model replicas becomes infeasible as task and model scales increase. In


                                                                                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      this paper, we introduce a novel task codebook method using task-specific keys and values. The task
163   keys are efficiently retrieved using a nearest neighbor lookup mechanism and dynamically updated.
164   Each retrieved task key is paired with a task value, which is then employed to efficiently condition the
165   remaining layers of the model Lester et al. (2021); Li & Liang (2021); Houlsby et al. (2019) to the
166   considered task. This design choice effectively integrates our task lookup module within the decoder,
167   enabling the deeper generative layers to adapt to a diverse set of tasks. An overview of our method is
168   presented in Fig. 1.
169   Task codebook. Formally, we introduce a task codebook at the lth decoder layer of the generative
170   model. This codebook contains a key-value pair (et , vt ) for each task t of the task mixture T. The
171   task-specific keys et ∈ Rd are used to recognize which task to be dealt with while the task-specific
172   values vt are employed to adapt the last Nl = L − l layers of the model to the considered task.
173   Task-specific keys: recognizing tasks with lookups. Intuitively, we want a task-specific key et to
174   be able to recognize which task t we are currently solving, making it play the role of a prototype for
175   task t. A simple yet effective strategy to do so is to use an average of the input embeddings coming
176   from the considered task t. Specifically, at training time, we dynamically update the relevant task
177   keys with an exponential moving average (EMA) of the sequence representation at the lth decoder
178   layer, denoted by xl , coming from examples sampled from Dt . Formally, we have for each task
179
      t: et = m · et + (1 − m) · xl where xl is the embedding of an example x = (xv , xi ) ∼ (Vt , It )
      sampled from task t and m is a decay rate. An alternative is to learn the task keys via gradient descent
180
      using an additional loss term as in L2P Wang et al. (2022c). Our simple EMA design offers faster
181
      adaptation and results in improved performance compared to learning the keys (see Sec. 5.5). We
182   empirically find that max-pooling exhibits robust advantages across all learning setups when it comes
183   to the choice of the sequence representation (see also Sec. 5.5).
184
185
      At inference time, we simply select a relevant task index t for the evaluated input x by performing
      a nearest-neighbor lookup, defined as t = argmaxi γ(ei , Xl0 ) where γ is a similarity metric. In
186
      practice, we use cosine similarity.
187
188   Task-specific values: parameter-efficient adaptation. In order to achieve efficient adaptation to
189
      each task and minimal parameter overhead, we integrate the task-specific values vt as parameters
      within an adapter module Houlsby et al. (2019). Specifically, the task-specific values vt represent
190
      a set of Nl lightweight bottleneck multi-layer perceptron (MLP) parameters introduced after the
191
      attention and feedforward layers of each of the last Nl transformer decoder blocks. We refer to this
192   way of adapting the decoder using task-specific MLPs by the adapter strategy and denote the resulting
193   model by TCIAA . As illustrated in the right panel of Fig. 1, we also evaluate alternative strategies
194   denoted by TCIAP (resp. TCIAPr ) where task-specific values vt refer instead to a prompt Lester
195   et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022c) (resp. to a prefix Li & Liang (2021)). We find in our experiments
196   (see Tab. 1) that while these two variants perform competitively and strongly allievate forgetting,
197   our variant using adapters, i.e. TCIAA , leads to the best version of our task codebook incremental
198   adaptation framework.

311
312   We conduct dataset-incremental learning across 4 diverse applications. These applications are all
313   comprised of different datasets as detailed in Tab. 7.
314   Baseline comparison. We see in Tab. 1 that TCIA models outperform all the considered baselines
315   in the sequential task learning scenario. First, as expected, the naive baseline of training the model
316   directly on sequential tasks yields severe forgetting: for example we observe −59.1 points of
317   performance drop on captioning compared to single-tasking for finetuning. Second, we see that the

      that our design choices are favorable. Details of incremental learning scores across training steps

      are provided in the Fig. 6. Overall, our TCIA models improve over strong baselines for incremental

      5.5     A BLATION S TUDY
406
407   In this section, we thoroughly analyze TCIA to assess the impact of each component. To complement
408   the end-task incremental learning score, we introduce an additional metric, AccL , which directly
409   quantifies the lookup module’s performance by measuring the accuracy of selecting the correct task
410
      key during inference.
411   Task key representation. We study the impact of different task key representations on codebook
412   lookup accuracy (Tabs. 5i and 5ii) and end-task incremental learning score (Tab. 5iii). We find that
413   using max-pooling over the sequence feature yields robust lookup accuracy over different incremental
414   learning setups. This echos studies on text classification Chen (2015); Conneau et al. (2017) finding
415   that max-pooling excels at capturing salient information for tasks like sentiment analysis where
416   the presence of certain keywords is informative. Notably, we find that [CLS] and [EOS] are
417   complementary-[CLS] excels at dataset-incremental learning while [EOS] excels at instruction-
418
      incremental learning. Although their concatenation offers higher lookup accuracy hence end-task
      score, it still lags behind from simply using max-pooling. See Tab. 17 for additional results on other
419
      setups.
420
421   Task codebook depth. We incorporate the task codebook into the l-th decoder layer, effectively
422   adapting the subsequent N − l layers. We use a GiT decoder with N = 6 layers and investigate the
423   impact of task codebook depth by varying l in Tabs. 5i and 5ii. Results indicate that positioning the
424   codebook at the shallowest layer (l = 0) performs more robustly against an intermediate placement
425   (l ≥ 1) when using max-pooling as task key representation. For models with the task codebook
426
      placed at deeper layers, the end-task score decline is primarily attributed to low AccL , suggesting
      that the model struggles to identify the appropriate task key at inference.
427
428   Update strategy. We discuss two alternatives to update the task-specific keys described in Sec-
429   tion 3.2. As shown in Tab. 6i, employing Exponential Moving Average (EMA) with m = 0.99
430   for task code updates yields a higher performance for both captioning and VQA. In comparison to
431   learning the keys via gradient backpropagation as in L2P approach Wang et al. (2022c), we observe
      improvements of +2.4 points and +1.1 points on captioning and VQA, respectively.

      6         C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we expand the setting of incremental learning to the scope of VLMs. With a high
528   diversity of tasks, datasets and modalities, we model a realistic setting of adapting large multi-modal
529   models for various downstream applications while minimizing forgetting. We introduce a new
530   simple incremental learning method, TCIA, that improves over prior proposed methods and baselines,
531
      especially for complex tasks. We extensively evaluate our new incremental learning method on 36
      datasets and 8 applications. We hope that our paper will help the community to advance incremental
532
      learning research for large multi-modal models.

918
      Captioning requires the model to provide a concise description of visual content. The following
919   datasets with short captions are considered: COCO Captions Lin et al. (2014) with the split defined
920   in Karpathy & Fei-Fei (2015), Flickr30K Young et al. (2014), TextCaps Sidorov et al. (2020),
921   VizWiz-Cap Gurari et al. (2020), Screen2Words Wang et al. (2021), and BLIP-LCS Li et al. (2022).
922   To account for the increasing length and quality of captions generated by multi-modal instruction-
923   following models, the ShareGPT4V-PT Chen et al. (2023a) dataset is also included, which features
924   significantly longer and more descriptive captions.
925
926   Visual Question Answering (VQA) aims at generating textual answers based on both a textual
927   question and visual context. For our benchmark, we use datasets where the visual context con-
928   sists of natural images Chen et al. (2023b): VQAv2 Goyal et al. (2017), OKVQA Marino et al.
929
      (2019), TextVQA Singh et al. (2019), VizWiz-QA Gurari et al. (2018), ST-VQA Biten et al. (2019),
      GQA Hudson & Manning (2019), TDIUC Kafle & Kanan (2017), and TallyQA Acharya et al. (2019).
930
931
      OCR-enhanced captioning and VQA (OCR-CV) leverage the output of an upstream OCR model
932
      to solve captioning and VQA applications. We consider the following datasets: TextCaps Sidorov
933   et al. (2020), Screen2Words Wang et al. (2021), VizWiz-QAGurari et al. (2018), OCR-VQA Mishra
934   et al. (2019), ChartQA Masry et al. (2022), DocVQA Mathew et al. (2021), TextVQA Singh et al.
935   (2019), AI2D Kembhavi et al. (2016) and InfographicsVQA Mathew et al. (2022). In these datasets,
936   OCR text tokens are extracted by an upstream OCR system Chen et al. (2023b) and provided as
937   additional text inputs.
938
939   Open-vocabulary classification categorizes object-centric images into their corresponding classes.
940   In our benchmark, we redefine classification as a generative task. Instead of training a set of
941   classifier weights Chaudhry et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2022c), we use a generative model with
942   open-world vocabulary to directly generate class names Wang et al. (2022a). The considered datasets
943
      are CIFAR10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009), CIFAR100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009), MNIST LeCun et al.
      (1998), notMNIST Bulatov (2011), FashionMNIST Xiao et al. (2017), SVHN Netzer et al. (2011),
944
      ImageNet-R Wang et al. (2022b), and Flowers Nilsback & Zisserman (2008).
945
946
      Spatial recognition assesses the model’s ability to comprehend and generate output based on
947   spatial coordinates of the image, typically represented as bounding boxes. Following the approach of
948   Pix2Seq Chen et al. (2021; 2023c;b), we directly incorporate discretized bounding boxes into the text
949   input by concatenating them with the relevant object or concept. We evaluate the model for three
950   distinct applications: i) Detection, where the bounding boxes for all objects are regarded as the target
951   Y, ii) Referring Expression Generation, where the bounding box for a specific object is included in
952   the instruction I, iii) Grounding, where the bounding box for a specific object is present in target Y .
953   All three of these applications are performed on the COCO Lin et al. (2014) dataset.
954
955   Multi-modal instruction tuning considers more complex textual I inputs, including but not
956   limited to conversational-style QA, detailed descriptions, and complex reasoning. They can be seen
957
      as a super-set of all previously considered applications. For this purpose, we employ the 158K
      supervised fine-tuning dataset from LLaVA Liu et al. (2023) derived from COCO Lin et al. (2014)
958
      dataset.

984
985
       In this section, we detail necessary pre-processing steps for reproduction purposes. By default, we
       resize the image with a ratio uniformly sampled from [0.75, 1.25] and pad the image’s shorter side.
986
       See Tab. 8 for the instruction template we use for different applications. We tokenize the textual
987
       instructions and targets using a BERT tokenizer Devlin et al. (2018) with a vocabulary size of 30522.
988
989
990    Captioning. We manually append an instruction randomly sampled from a pool detailed in Tab. 8.
991    We directly use the image caption as the target. For datasets with multiple captions (e.g., COCO
992
       Captions Lin et al. (2014)), we randomly sample one.
993
994    VQA. We directly use the question as the instruction and the answer as the target from the original
995    dataset without any other extra processing. For datasets with multiple captions (e.g., VQAv2 Goyal
996    et al. (2017)), we randomly sample one.
997
998    OCR-CV. For visual input, we do not scale the image while resizing to make sure the entire image
999    is visible to the model. We follow the pre-processing steps as Chen et al. (2023b) using an upstream
1000   OCR system, GCP Vision API, to extract the potential OCR texts in the image. The extracted OCR
1001   texts are then appended in the format of “<OCR text: ...>” after the original captioning or VQA
1002   instructions.
1003
1004   Open-vocabulary classification. We manually append an instruction randomly sampled from a
1005   pool detailed in Tab. 8. For the target, we directly use the class label name if the mapping exists. We
1006   use “digit {Class Label}” for MNIST LeCun et al. (1998) and SVHN Netzer et al. (2011), “letter
1007   {Class Label}” for notMNIST Bulatov (2011).
1008
1009
       Detection. For visual input, we resize the image with a ratio uniformly sampled from [0.3, 2.0] and
1010
       randomly flip the image. We simply use “detect:” as the instruction. For the target, we rescale the
1011   range of original bounding boxes in COCO Lin et al. (2014) from 1.0 to 999 (int) and concatenate 4
1012   coordinates directly with the instance label. Different instances are directly concatenated with “and”.
1013   An exemplar target looks like “10 99 323 675 cat and 127 346 894 997 dog”. We do not use the
1014   augmentation trick proposed in Chen et al. (2021).
1015
1016
       Referring expression generation. The pre-processing for visual input follows the detection task.
1017   For each instance, we use “describe the box at {Location}:” as the instruction and its referring
1018   expression as the target.
1019
1020
1021
       Grounding. The pre-processing for visual input follows the detection task. For each instance, we
       use “detect {Referring Expression}:” as the instruction and its location as the target. The formatting
1022
       of the bounding boxes follows the detection task.
1023
1024
1025   Multi-modal instruction tuning. We directly use human instructions as the instruction and the
       GPT-4 OpenAI (2023)’s output as the target.

1207   Class-incremental learning with varying splits on CIFAR100. Here, we explore different ways
1208   of splitting CIFAR100 for class-incremental learning. We vary the number of splits from 5 to 100
1209
       and report results in Tab. 15. Note that having more splits results in a lower number of classes per
       split, which makes incremental learning more challenging. In fact, with 100 splits, there is only one
1210
       class per task. Note that this scenario may not be deemed very practical, but we use it to investigate
1211
       the performance of our method in an extreme setting.
1212
1213   We observe in Tab. 15 that TCIA is robust to the number of considered splits. Both L2P Wang et al.
1214   (2022c) and E-Replay Chaudhry et al. (2019) fail when trained incrementally class per class (i.e. 100
1215
       splits). We find that this is mainly because these baselines use a standard unfrozen classification head,
       which cannot be trained properly by seeing only one class at a time. In contrast, because we build on
1216
       a generative image-to-text model, we perform classification by auto-regressively generating class
1217
       names. This way, our default setting is to use a frozen text vocabulary classifier as the final head,
1218   which outputs logits for word tokens. This alleviates the issue of having to learn a single classification
1219   head vector at a time and explains why TCIA obtains a good performance in the 100 splits setting. In
1220   fact, we get poor performance similar to L2P and E-Replay when using a classification head instead
1221   of a vocabulary head in the TCIA framework. Finally, we see in Tab. 15 that building task-specific
1222   keys from additional models CLIP-B Radford et al. (2021) or ViT-B Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) works
1223   better than our default design of using inner GiT-L decoder features. Overall, our analysis in Tab. 15
1224   shows the benefit of leveraging generative auto-regressive models for extreme class-incremental
1225   learning such as learning one class at a time.

       D     L IMITATIONS AND P OTENTIAL N EGATIVE I MPACT
1405
1406
       D.1   L IMITATIONS
1407
1408   Requirement for Task ID at Training Time. One limitation of the proposed method is the
1409   requirement of the training task ID for task codebook adaptation. The adaptation process relies
1410   on identifying and retrieving the correct set of parameters at training time for a given task. This
1411   requirement poses a significant challenge in scenarios where the task ID may not be readily available
1412   or easily determined. It leaves for future work to explore training without the need of the task ID.
1413
1414   Memory Overheads. Although we aim to mitigate catastrophic forgetting without significant
1415   memory overheads, the scalability of the task codebook and its efficiency in a scenario with an
1416   extremely large number of tasks need further exploration. There could be practical limitations related
1417
       to the memory footprint.
1418
1419
       Generalization across Diverse Domains. While we evaluate the method across a broad range of
       tasks, the real-world applicability of the model’s adaptability and efficiency in continuously evolving
1420
       or extremely diverse domains remains to be examined. There might be domains or specific types of
1421
       tasks where the proposed method does not perform as effectively.
1422
1423
       D.2   P OTENTIAL N EGATIVE I MPACT
1424
1425   Biases. There is a risk of the model inheriting or amplifying biases present in the training data. The
1426   continuous adaptation process might not adequately address these biases, potentially perpetuating
1427   them in downstream applications.
1428
1429   Privacy. The adaptation of models to specific tasks could involve processing and storing sensitive or
1430   personal information, especially in applications that deal with user-generated content. If not properly
1431   handled, this could lead to privacy breaches and violations of data protection regulations.
1432
1433   Environmental Impact. Training and adapting large models require significant computational
1434   resources, which can have a considerable environmental footprint. Incremental adaptation could
1435
       exacerbate this issue by requiring frequent retraining on new tasks.
