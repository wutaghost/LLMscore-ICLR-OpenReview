009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) has recently emerged as a promising
012            alternative to traditional multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs), offering enhanced accu-
013            racy and interpretability through learnable activation functions on edges instead
014            of fixed functions on nodes. In this paper, we present the Kolmogorov-Arnold
015            Auto-Encoder (KAE), a novel integration of KAN with autoencoders (AEs) that
016            aims to improve representation learning and performance in retrieval, classifi-
017            cation, and denoising tasks. By utilizing the flexible polynomial functions in
018            KAN layers, KAE effectively captures complex data patterns and non-linear re-
019
               lationships, outperforming standard autoencoders. Our extensive experiments on
               benchmark datasets show that KAE significantly enhances the quality of latent
020
               representations, resulting in reduced reconstruction and denoising errors, and also
021
               improves performance in downstream tasks, including higher classification accu-
022            racy, retrieval recall, and interpretability compared to standard autoencoders and
023            other KAN variants. These findings position KAE as a practical tool for high-
024            dimensional data analysis, paving the way for more robust performance in repre-
025            sentation learning. The code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
026            science/r/KAE/.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Autoencoders (Berahmand et al., 2024) are a fundamental component of modern deep learning,
032   serving as powerful tools for unsupervised representation learning. By compressing input data into
033   a lower-dimensional latent space and subsequently reconstructing it, autoencoders facilitate a wide
034   range of applications, including dimensionality reduction (Wang et al., 2016; Lin et al., 2020), image
035   classification (Zhou et al., 2019; 2023), and data denoising (Gondara, 2016; Cui & Zdeborová,
036   2024). Their ability to learn meaningful representations from unlabelled data has positioned them
037   as essential in various AI domains, from computer vision (Mishra et al., 2018; Takeishi & Kalousis,
038
      2021) to natural language processing (Shen et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021), significantly enhancing
      the performance of downstream tasks.
039
040   Traditional autoencoders typically leverage multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs), characterized by fully
041   connected layers. In a conventional autoencoder, each layer computes its output as y = σ(W x + b),
042   where σ denotes a fixed activation function, W is the learnable weight matrix, x represents the
043   input, and b is the bias vector. The optimization of W is traditionally performed through black-box
044
      AI systems, limiting the adaptability of the activation function to the data’s underlying structure.
045   Recent advancements in alternative architectures, such as Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs)
046   (Liu et al., 2024b;a), offer a compelling pathway for enhancing autoencoder performance. Unlike
047   MLPs, KANs redefine the output as y = f (x), where f is a learnable activation function. This
048   shift enables the network to adaptively learn more complex representations, moving beyond the
049   limitations of fixed functions and linear weights used in MLPs.
050   In this paper, we propose the Kolmogorov-Arnold Auto-Encoder (KAE), which integrates the
051   strengths of KANs into the autoencoder framework to create a more robust model for representation
052   learning. However, merely substituting MLP layers with KAN layers utilizing B-spline functions
053   may result in only marginal gains or even a decline in performance. The efficacy of KANs heavily
      depends on the specific type of function employed within the KAN layer. To address this chal-

054
      lenge, we introduce a well-defined KAE architecture that utilizes learnable polynomial functions,
055   demonstrating promising improvements in representation learning.
056
057   Our contributions are as follows:
058
          • We introduce the Kolmogorov-Arnold Representation Theorem into the design of autoencoders,
059         providing a theoretical basis for improving autoencoder performance.
060
          • We investigate the role of activation functions in the KAN layers, identifying polynomial func-
061
            tions as a suitable choice for enhancing autoencoder performance.
062
063
          • We demonstrate the superiority of the Kolmogorov-Arnold Auto-Encoder (KAE) through ex-
            tensive experimental validation, showing improvements in both reconstruction quality and
064
            downstream application performance.

      Autoencoders, as a form of unsupervised learning, aim to learn a compressed representation of input
090   data while minimizing reconstruction error (Pinaya et al., 2020). Traditionally, autoencoders rely on
091   MLPs to achieve this task, where each layer applies a fixed non-linearity. However, this often limits
092   the ability of the network to capture more complex structures in the data.
093
      Recent works have explored various autoencoder architectures, including Variational Auto-Encoders
094
      (VAEs) (Kingma et al., 2019; Skopek et al., 2020) and Denoising Auto-Encoders (DAEs) (Savinov
095
      et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023), which introduce stochasticity and robustness to noise, respectively.
096   Nevertheless, most of these architectures still rely on fixed activation functions, which limits their
097   flexibility in representing complex functions.
098
099
      As shown in Fig. 1, an autoencoder consists of two main parts:
100       • Encoder: This part compresses the input into a latent-space representation.
101
          • Decoder: This part reconstructs the input data from the latent-space representation.
102
103   A common application of autoencoders is representation learning, where latent representations are
104   learned and can be used for tasks such as image classification (Zhou et al., 2023), text classification
105   (Xu & Tan, 2019), cross-modal learning (Dong et al., 2023), and other applications, particularly
106   beneficial for high-dimensional data analysis. Another key feature of autoencoders is their ability to
107   reduce noise in data (Bajaj et al., 2020). By inputting noisy data, the pre-trained autoencoder can
      remove the noise, producing clean outputs, which makes it a powerful tool for data denoising.

      2.2     KOLMOGOROV-A RNOLD N ETWORKS (KAN S )
109
110   Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) (Liu et al., 2024b;a) provide a more flexible way to model
111   non-linear relationships by using learnable activation functions rather than fixed ones. Inspired
112   by the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem (Kolmogorov, 1961; Braun & Griebel, 2009),
113   KANs approximate any continuous multivariate function using a sum of univariate functions, pro-
114   viding a theoretically grounded approach to function approximation.
115   In the context of neural networks, KANs allow each layer to learn its own activation function, mak-
116   ing them more adaptable to highly non-linear data. KANs have been shown to outperform MLPs in
117   various applications (Xu et al., 2024; Bozorgasl & Chen, 2024), particularly those requiring com-
118   plex, non-linear transformations of input data.
119
120   2.2.1    KOLMOGOROV-A RNOLD R EPRESENTATION T HEOREM
121
      The Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem is a fundamental result in mathematics, particularly
122
      in functional analysis and multivariate approximation theory. It provides key insights into represent-
123
      ing multivariate continuous functions. Theorem 1 asserts that any continuous function of d variables
124   can be represented as a finite sum of continuous univariate functions along with an additional con-
125   tinuous function.
126
      Theorem 1 (Kolmogorov-Arnold Representation Theorem). For any smooth function f :
127   [0, 1]d → R, there exist continuous functions ϕk,j : [0, 1] → R and Φk : R → R such that:
128                                                                               
129                                                          2d+1
                                                             X         d
                                                                       X
130                              f (x1 , x2 , . . . , xd ) =      Φk    ϕk,j (xj ) .                   (1)
131                                                     k=1            j=1
132
133   This remarkable theorem involves the use of two primary types of functions: inner functions {ϕk,j }
134   and outer functions {Φk }, which motivates the design of the KAN network.
135
136   2.2.2    KAN N ETWORK AND I TS A PPLICATIONS
137
      Inspired by Theorem 1, Liu et al. (2024b) proposed a novel KAN layer with din -dimensional inputs
138   and dout -dimensional outputs, defined as a matrix of 1D functions:
139
140                         Φ := {ϕk,j },    j = 1, 2, · · · , din ,    k = 1, 2, · · · , dout ,
141   where each function ϕk,j is a learnable univariate function. A general KAN network is formed by
142   stacking L KAN layers. Given an input vector x ∈ Rd , the output of KAN is
143
144
                               KAN(x) := (ΦL−1 ◦ ΦL−2 ◦ · · · ◦ Φ1 ◦ Φ0 ) ◦ x,                           (2)
145   where the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation in Eq. (1) can be viewed as a composition of two
146   KAN layers: Φ0 contains (2d + 1) · d functions, and Φ1 contains 1 · (2d + 1) functions.
147
      Recently, KAN networks have been applied to various domains, including scientific discovery (Liu
148   et al., 2024b;a), image segmentation (Li et al., 2024), image classification (Cheon, 2024), text clas-
149   sification (Imran & Ishmam, 2024), collaborative filtering (Xu et al., 2024), and others. KAN-based
150   architectures, such as the Kolmogorov-Arnold Transformer (Yang & Wang, 2024) and UNet-KAN
151   (U-KAN) (Li et al., 2024), have also gained significant attention.

      3.1     KAN S IN AUTOENCODERS
156
157   While KANs offer a promising way to enhance neural network architectures, their direct applica-
158   tion to autoencoders presents challenges. Specifically, the complexity introduced by the learnable
159   activation functions can lead to overfitting or suboptimal performance if not carefully managed. In
160   this work, we propose a new autoencoder architecture that integrates KANs while addressing these
161   challenges through the use of polynomial-based activation functions, which we show to be more
      stable and effective for this task.

      3.2   OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
163
164   The key idea behind the Kolmogorov-Arnold Auto-Encoder (KAE) is to replace the MLP layers
165   (fixed activation functions) in traditional autoencoders with KAN layers (learnable functions), as
166   inspired by the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem.
167
      In a traditional autoencoder, the MLP consists of fully connected layers that apply a fixed activation
168   function, such as ReLU or sigmoid, after a linear transformation. Given an input vector x ∈ Rd , the
169   encoder compresses the data into a lower-dimensional latent representation z ∈ Rk , and the decoder
170   reconstructs z back into the original space. This transformation is typically expressed as:
171
                                                 z = σ(W x + b),
172
173   where W is the weight matrix, b is the bias, and σ is a fixed activation function.
174
      In KAE, we replace the MLP layers with KAN layers, which use learnable activation functions as
175   defined by the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem. Instead of a fixed σ, each KAN layer
176   dynamically learns its own activation function, enabling the model to capture complex, non-linear
177   patterns. The encoder applies a series of KAN layers to map the input to the latent space:
178
                               z = f (x) := (ΦL−1 ◦ ΦL−2 ◦ · · · ◦ Φ1 ◦ Φ0 ) ◦ x,
179
180   where each Φi represents a KAN layer composed of learnable univariate functions.
181
      Similarly, the decoder (y = g(z)) uses KAN layers in reverse to reconstruct the data, providing
182
      greater flexibility and accuracy compared to standard MLP-based decoders. This architecture en-
183   ables KAE to capture complex, non-linear relationships, enhancing the quality of the learned rep-
184   resentations. As with standard autoencoders, we use the mean squared error (MSE) between the
185   original and reconstructed data as the training loss.
186
187   3.3   P OLYNOMIAL ACTIVATION F UNCTION
188
189   The autoencoder’s primary task is to ensure that the encoding and decoding functions are inverses
190   of each other, i.e., x ≈ g(f (x)) for all x ∈ Rd . In a traditional autoencoder, the layers are fully
191   connected, and this inversion property is straightforward to achieve. However, when using KAN
192
      layers, the choice of functions f and g becomes more nuanced. Naı̈vely replacing the MLP layer
      with a KAN layer does not produce satisfactory results, as shown in our evaluation in Section 4.
193
194   To ensure consistency between the encoder and decoder, we carefully design the f (x) and g(z) to
195   be invertible, using polynomial approximations that are both smooth and differentiable. For a KAE
196   layer with din -dimensional inputs and dout -dimensional outputs, the output of x ∈ Rdin is defined as:
197                KAE(x) := σ(h(x) + b) = σ((c0 1din + c1 x + c2 x2 + · · · + cp xp ) + b),                (3)
198
199   where 1din is an all-ones vector and p is the order of the polynomials, treated as a hyperparameter in
200
      KAE. To maintain consistency with the MLP-based AE, we use the structure σ(· + b), replacing the
      W x term with h(x), resulting in σ(h(x) + b) with the following advantages.
201
202     • For polynomial orders up to four, an inverse function exists, ensuring the required inversion
203       between the encoder and decoder.
204
        • Compared to the linear transformation W x in MLPs, polynomial functions h(x) introduce
205
          higher-order non-linear terms such as xp , enabling the model to capture more complex, non-
206       linear relationships in the data.
207
        • The polynomial function includes a constant matrix c0 ∈ Rdout ×din , providing more flexibility
208
          than the traditional bias term b ∈ Rdout , allowing the model to better adapt to shifts and variations
209
          in the data.
210
211   Notably, in the original KAN (Liu et al., 2024b), the learnable function h(x) is set as a B-spline
212   function, while in FourierKAN (Xu et al., 2024) it is set as a Fourier function, and in WavKAN
213   (Bozorgasl & Chen, 2024) it is set as a Wavelet function. Comparatively, our empirical validation
214   shows that quadratic and cubic polynomial functions (with p = 2, 3) offer a more effective balance
215   between flexibility and stability, improving the model’s ability to reconstruct data while maintaining
      the inversion property between the encoder and decoder.

259   and the reconstructed output z. The reconstruction error was then calculated as the MSE, defined by

      4.3.1     S IMILARITY S EARCH
292
293   A well-designed autoencoder, as a tool for dimensionality reduction, should preserve the distance
294   relationships between samples from the input space to the latent space, making similarity search a
295   suitable application for assessing this property.
296
      For each experiment, we randomly selected a subset of 1,000 test samples as the test set. For each
297   query, we computed the k nearest neighbors in the input space as the ground truth and compared
298   it to the N nearest neighbors retrieved in the latent space. We calculated the recall, defined as the
299   ratio of true top-k results within the top-N retrieved candidates, and reported it as Recall k@N . In
300   our experiments, we set k = 10 and tested Recall 10@N (referred to as Recall@N ) for all models.
301   Each experiment was repeated 10 times with different random seeds, and we reported the averaged
302   Recall with standard deviations.
303   Table 3 presents the Recall@10 results for various models across latent dimensions (16 and 32).
304   Increasing the latent dimension from 16 to 32 significantly improved retrieval recall for all models,
305   enhancing their distance-preserving properties and expressive power. Notably, the proposed KAE
306   model consistently outperformed both the standard AE and KAN variants across all datasets and
307   dimensions. Specifically, KAE (p = 2) achieved improvements of 0.243 and 0.206 in Recall over
308   AE for MNIST with latent dimensions of 16 and 32, respectively, with similar gains observed for
309   FashionMNIST, CIFAR10, and CIFAR100 datasets.

352   We further applied the learned latent representations to image classification using a nearest neighbor
353   classifier. For each of the 10,000 test samples, the predicted label was assigned based on the nearest
354   sample in the latent space with the smallest Euclidean distance. We then compared the predicted
355   labels with the ground truth, averaging the classification accuracy across all test samples.
356   Table 4 shows that KAE models, particularly with the non-linear polynomial function (p = 3),
357   achieved the highest classification accuracy across all datasets. This contrasts with similarity search,
358   where p = 2 performed best. The difference in performance may due to the nature of each task:
359
360       • In similarity search, KAE (p = 2) better preserves the distance relationships between neighbors,
361         which is crucial for retrieving similar samples.
362       • In image classification, KAE (p = 3) captures more complex, non-linear relationships, enabling
363         better discrimination between class boundaries and improving accuracy.

473   5     C ONCLUSION
474
475   In this paper, we introduced the Kolmogorov-Arnold Auto-Encoder (KAE), which integrates the
476
      Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) with autoencoders (AEs) to create a more powerful and flexi-
      ble framework for representation learning. By incorporating KAN’s learnable polynomial activation
477
      functions into the AE structure, KAE effectively captures complex, non-linear relationships in the
478
      data, outperforming standard AEs. Our experiments on benchmark datasets highlight KAE’s su-
479   periority in reconstruction quality and downstream applications such as similarity search, image
480   classification, and image denoising. Our analysis further demonstrates KAE’s greater capacity and
481   interpretability, especially through its learned polynomial activation functions.
482
      Looking ahead, future work will focus on scaling KAE to deeper architectures to unlock its potential
483
      in more complex and high-dimensional tasks. We will also explore different activation functions
484
      to further enhance the model’s flexibility and performance. Additionally, applying KAE to more
485   challenging real-world applications will provide deeper insights into its robustness and adaptability.
