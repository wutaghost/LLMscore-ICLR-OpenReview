010
011
012
               In the realm of machine learning, conventional techniques like neural networks
               often encounter challenges when dealing with imbalanced data. Unfortunately,
013
               imbalanced data is a common occurrence in real-world datasets, where collection
014
               methods may fail to capture sufficient data within specific target variable ranges.
015            Additionally, certain tasks inherently involve imbalanced data, where the occur-
016            rences of normal events significantly outweigh those of edge cases. While the
017            problem of imbalanced data has been extensively studied in the context of clas-
018            sification, only a limited number of methods have been proposed for regression
019            tasks. Furthermore, the existing methods often yield suboptimal performance
020            when applied to high-dimensional data, and the domain of imbalanced high-
021            dimensional regression remains relatively unexplored. In response to the identified
022            challenge, this paper presents SwitchLoss, a novel optimization scheme for neural
023
               networks, and SwitchLossR, a variant with a restricted search space. Diverging
               from conventional approaches, SwitchLoss and SwitchLossR integrate variable
024
               loss functions into the traditional training process. Our assessment of these meth-
025
               ods spans 15 regression datasets across diverse imbalanced domains, 5 synthetic
026            high-dimensional imbalanced datasets, and two imbalanced age estimation image
027            datasets. Findings from our investigation demonstrate that the combined utiliza-
028            tion of SwitchLoss and SwitchLossR not only leads to a notable reduction in vali-
029            dation error, but also surpasses prevailing state-of-the-art techniques dedicated to
030            imbalanced regression.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, the growing availability of large datasets has enabled researchers to apply machine
037   learning for building predictive models. However, many real-world datasets exhibit imbalanced or
038   skewed distributions, which can hinder model performance, particularly in regions with sparse data.
039   While imbalanced classification has received attention, imbalanced regression remains less explored.
040   Only a few methods address imbalanced regression, most focusing on sampling techniques. Notable
041   examples include SMOGN (Branco et al., 2017), an enhancement of SMOTER using Gaussian noise
042   (Torgo et al., 2013). Another example is DenseLoss (Steininger et al., 2021), a cost-sensitive method
043   designed for imbalanced regression, that avoids the removal of potentially useful data by focusing
044
      on optimization challenges.
045   In response to this challenge, we propose a novel optimization scheme to address the problem of
046   imbalanced regression. This method, which we named SwitchLoss, introduces a novel approach
047   that leverages the dynamic switching between different loss functions during the training process of
048   neural networks, thereby acting as a regularization technique and mitigating the risk of optimization
049   converging to a local optimum.
050   This paper is organized as follows: Section 2 defines the problem of imbalanced regression and
051   presents an overview of the existing related work. The proposed optimization scheme SwitchLoss
052   is described in Section 3, while the results of the experimental evaluation are presented in Section
053   4. We provide a discussion on the obtained results in Section 5. Finally, Section 6 outlines the main
      conclusions and future work.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
055
056
      Imbalanced regression refers to regression problems where the target variable is unevenly dis-
057
      tributed, with some ranges being underrepresented. The goal is to build models that accurately
058                                                                         N
      approximate the function Y = f (x) using a training set D = (xi , yi )i=1 with N samples. Unlike
059
      classification, imbalanced regression is more complex due to the continuous nature of the target
060   variable.
061
062   A significant portion of existing approaches builds upon the seminal work of Torgo and Ribeiro
063
      (2007) and Ribeiro (2011), who proposed the concept of a relevance function ϕ(y) which assigns
      a quantitative score [0,1] to the range of target values. Furthermore, Ribeiro (2011) introduced an
064
      automated method for approximating the relevance function ϕ(y) using box plot statistics. This
065
      approximation assumes that the rare and extreme cases hold the highest relevance. Subsequently,
066   the relevance function is utilized to classify data samples into major (normal) and minor (rare) cate-
067   gories, employing a user-defined threshold tr. This division is accomplished through the following
068   assignment: RS = {(x, y) ∈ D : ϕ(y) ≥ tr} and N S = {(x, y) ∈ D : ϕ(y) < tr}. This catego-
069   rization based on the relevance function and the user-specified threshold enables the segregation of
070   the data set into rare and normal instances for further analysis and modeling.
071
      Approaches for handling imbalanced data include resampling and cost-sensitive learning
072   (Krawczyk, 2016). A few different sampling approaches for imbalanced regression are proposed.
073   They are applied during data pre-processing, such as SMOTER (Torgo et al., 2013) which is based on
074   the original SMOTE method for classification (Chawla et al., 2002) and combines under-sampling
075   of common data samples with over-sampling of rare cases, in order to create a more balanced dis-
076   tribution. The SMOGN (Branco et al., 2017) can be considered state-of-the-art among resampling
077   techniques. This algorithm builds on top of SMOTER and combines it with oversampling via Gaus-
078   sian noise. Normally distributed noise is added to the features and the target value of rare data
079   samples, therefore creating additional, slightly altered replicas of existing samples (Branco et al.,
080
      2016). Cost-sensitive methods are far less common in regression, but DenseLoss (Steininger et al.,
      2021) offers a promising approach. It uses DenseWeight, a density-based weighting scheme, to
081
      determine rarity without modifying the dataset.

      3.1   G ENERAL S WITCH L OSS SCHEME
090
091   In machine learning, optimizing the loss function aims to minimize the difference between a neu-
092   ral network’s predicted outputs and the actual outputs, thereby improving model performance. The
093   choice of the loss function reflects the network’s objectives, guiding it toward a more accurate input-
094   output relationship as training progresses (Goodfellow et al., 2016). Many machine learning prob-
095   lems involve multiple factors that are difficult to combine in a single loss function due to differences
096   in scales, units etc. In this study, we explore an alternative approach where different loss functions
097   are switched during the training phase, rather than merging them into one.
098   This approach is highly generalizable and can be used across different domains. We recognize that
099   a machine learning model’s success largely depends on its optimization process. To address this, we
100   propose a nested two-stage optimization framework. The first stage, called the exploration stage,
101   optimizes the scheme of loss functions for training. The second stage, traditional training, then
102   optimizes the neural network’s parameters using the selected scheme. The traditional training is
103   occurring within the broader scope of the exploration stage. Thus, the first stage evaluates different
104
      loss schemes, while the second focuses on optimizing the model based on the chosen scheme.
105   The proposed approach involves randomly switching between predefined loss functions during spe-
106   cific epochs over a fixed number of cycles. This method explores the effect of different loss functions
107   on training, aiming to identify the most effective configuration. By introducing random switching,
      the approach increases flexibility and diversity in optimization.

      3.2   H YPER - PARAMETERS
143
144   Within our algorithm, we acknowledge the presence of three important parameters. The first pa-
145   rameter pertains to the selection of a set of n loss functions, which is a crucial decision requiring
146   domain knowledge and problem-specific considerations. The choice of these functions determines
147   the optimization criteria used during the training process. In the following subsection we propose
148   and justify our choices for the imbalanced regression applications.
149   The second parameter, denoted as #switches, represents the number of divisions of the traditional
150   training into continuous loss function blocks. The value of #switches can be adjusted based on the
151   specific characteristics of the problem.
152
      The third parameter, denoted as explores, indicates the number of cycles dedicated to exploring
153
      various training schemes. Increasing the value of explores enhances the likelihood of identifying
154   the best possible training scheme, while also requiring larger computational resources. Ideally,
155   setting this value to n#switches would allow for an exhaustive exploration of all available options.
156   In this scenario, random selection becomes inconsequential as the systematic search ensures more
157   meaningful and comprehensive results.

159   3.3   S WITCH L OSS FOR IMBALANCED REGRESSION
160
161   In this section, we provide a comprehensive elaboration of the SwitchLoss scheme’s implementation
      for the specific domain of imbalanced regression.


                                                        3

162
      The majority of algorithms employed in the imbalanced regression domain rely on sampling tech-
163   niques (Krawczyk, 2016). These algorithms typically involve either over-sampling, generating arti-
164   ficial samples, or under-sampling, failing to fully exploit the available information.
165
166
      In our research, we believe that the suboptimal performance of neural networks in imbalanced re-
      gression problems stems from issues in the optimization process. Specifically, we argue that conven-
167
      tional optimization methods excessively prioritize the proportion of data samples within abundant
168
      regions. Consequently, this bias towards the abundant regions leads to the attraction of optimiza-
169   tion towards local minima, resulting in predictions that predominantly align with values close to the
170   abundant region for any given input. To address this problem, we propose an alternative optimization
171   procedure that leverages the entire available information without resorting to intentionally induced
172   loss or the artificial generation of data samples.
173
      We hypothesize that by introducing a change in the loss functions employed during training, while
174
      maintaining the same objective - finding the best approximation of the underlying function, we can
175   effectively steer the optimization away from local minima. Therefore, we propose the utilization
176   of the SwitchLoss scheme, which involves a predefined set of three loss functions in the context of
177   imbalanced regression:
178
      The first choice for a loss function is a standard root-mean-squared error, given in Equation 1.

191   It quantifies the discrepancy or divergence between two distributions by considering both their sim-

      By incorporating this particular function along with JSD into the optimization process, we aim to
201
      capture and address discrepancies in the spread of the predicted output compared to the ground
202   truth. Minimizing the absolute difference between these standard deviations encourages the neural
203   network to generate predictions that exhibit similar levels of variability as the actual output, ulti-
204   mately enhancing the model’s ability to accurately capture the underlying distribution’s dispersion
205   properties.
206
      The conventional optimization approach encounters challenges in accurately predicting target values
207
      within rare regions of the target distribution. As a result, the predicted target values tend to con-
208   centrate solely in abundant regions, leading to a narrower predicted standard deviation distribution
209   compared to the actual distribution. By incorporating these loss functions, the model is incentivized
210   to generate predictions that extend beyond the abundant region, thereby encouraging the exploration
211   and accurate prediction of values within rare regions of the target distribution.

245   3.3.1    R ESTRICTING THE SEARCH SPACE
246
247   A crucial characteristic of this algorithm resides in the extensive array of possibilities related to the
248   combination of loss functions. Specifically, in the context of imbalanced regression, since three
249   functions have been proposed the total number of conceivable schemes amounts to 3#switches . As
250   the number of functions or switches increases, this count escalates significantly. In light of limited
251   resources such as time or computational power, we put forth techniques aimed at mitigating the
252
      magnitude of the search space.
253   One of the options is assigning higher probabilities to specific loss functions that introduces a con-
254   trolled bias in the optimization process. These probabilities, informed by domain knowledge or
255   empirical observations, help manage the exploration-exploitation trade-off by focusing optimization
256   on certain areas while allowing exploration elsewhere.
257   In the domain of imbalanced regression, we conducted an investigation into the performance of
258   different loss function schemes. Notably, we observed that employing a fixed mean squared error
259   (MSE) loss for every other switch, while alternately switching between JSD and STD losses for the
260   remaining switches, yielded results that were comparable to those obtained through a completely
261   random search. This approach effectively reduced the exhaustive search space from a potentially
262   large pool of 3switches possible schemes to a significantly smaller set of schemes with a cardinal-
              switches
263   ity of 2 2 , achieved by considering the binary switching pattern. By adopting this modified
264   search strategy, we maintain strong performance in imbalanced regression tasks while significantly
265   reducing the computational complexity. This focused exploration improves efficiency, enhances op-
266
      timization, and aids in identifying near-optimal loss function configurations. We denote this method
      as SwitchLossR and Procedure 3 outlines the details.
267
268   Readers should note that restricted search techniques explore only a limited subset of possible opti-
269   mization schemes. To maximize the chances of finding optimal results, conducting a more extensive
      search is recommended.

308   4.1    DATA
309
      For evaluating the performance of the presented approaches, we used three different types of datasets
310
      - 15 standard datasets from different imbalanced domains, 5 synthetic high-dimensional imbal-
311
      anced datasets due to a special challenge that imblanace presents in high-dimensional settings, and
312   two more complex age estimation image datasets IMDB-WIKI (Rothe et al., 2018) and AgeDB

314   ing architectures. Appendix contains tables that show in greater detail the main characteristics of

316   done with image datasets. In total, we use 22 datasets that cover a range of sizes, feature numbers,
317   distribution shapes and imbalance levels.
318   Important to note is that we split the data for the previous datasets into the training, validation,
319   and test datasets. We selected the balanced validation and test datasets which implies that target
320   values of the test and validation datasets are seeded uniformly throughout the whole target range.
321   Random sampling from a data set would create imbalanced test data and consequently cause a bias
322   towards a more abundant target value region in model performance assessment. Selected test data
323   and validation data cover each 15% of the whole corresponding data set for standard and synthetic
      high-dimensional datasets.

472
      and the restricted search space variant, SwitchLossR. Our analysis shows that SwitchLoss wins in
473
      55% of standard dataset cases and 75% in the high-dimensional domain, benefiting from exploring
474   a wider range of optimization schemes. However, SwitchLossR performs better in nearly half of
475   the standard datasets, as its more focused approach allows it to explore specific areas more effi-
476   ciently, leading to quicker and often better results in certain cases. Although the exploration space
477   of SwitchLoss is three times larger than that of SwitchLossR, it remains limited in comparison to
478   the vast array of all conceivable possibilities. This implies that with the larger exploration stage,
479   SwitchLoss is more likely to encompass some of the schemes depicted by SwitchLossR. Nonethe-
480   less, it is worth noting that the exploration space of these two algorithms could but does not neces-
481   sarily overlap.

483   In our study, we have deliberately chosen to report results based on only 100 exploration cycles,
484   out of a total of 59,049 possible schemes. This decision is made to ensure that the computational
485   complexity of the algorithm remains manageable, without demanding extensive resources in terms
      of time or computational power.

495   more data is available, heuristics such as Jensen-Shannon divergence and standard deviation exhibit

497   imbalance also plays a role, as we find that for less skewed distributions, the regular mean-squared

499   contributes more significantly.
500   Furthermore, the work by Blagus and Lusa (2013) suggests that SMOTE-based techniques introduce

502   with these findings, as we observe that SMOGN under-performs on image datasets in comparison to
503   standard datasets.

      We did not assess the combined usage of SMOGN and SwitchLoss in our research, even though there
505   are no technical barriers to combining them. SMOGN addresses data, while SwitchLoss focuses on
506   optimization. However, the core concept of SwitchLoss aims to prevent under- or over-sampling,
507   rendering its combination with SMOGN inconsequential.

      In summary, our comparative analysis demonstrates that SwitchLoss exhibits superior performance
509
      on datasets with ample samples, high imbalance, and complex NN architectures.

      6   C ONCLUSION
514
515   In conclusion, this research paper presents the novel optimization scheme SwitchLoss which repre-
516   sents a versatile approach to the optimization procedure for neural networks. While the applicability
517   of this scheme is not limited by any problem type, the specific focus of this paper is directed towards
518   addressing the challenges associated with imbalanced regression.
519   The SwitchLoss approach entails a nested framework that combines the exploration of diverse loss
520   function schemes with the conventional training methodology employed in neural network opti-
521   mization. It comprises two stages: the exploration stage and the traditional neural network training
522   stage. In the exploration stage, various loss function schemes are investigated, with a predefined
523   number of cycles. The goal is to assess the impact of different loss function configurations on the
524   optimization process and identify the most effective scheme for improving model performance. In
525
      the subsequent traditional training stage, the neural network model is trained using the optimized
      loss function scheme obtained from the exploration stage.
526
527   This paper makes significant contributions in several key areas. Firstly, it introduces the opti-
528   mization scheme SwitchLoss, which is specifically tailored to address the challenges of imbal-
529   anced regression. Additionally, a variant of SwitchLoss, called SwitchLossR, is presented as a
530   means to reduce computational complexity while maintaining effectiveness. The effectiveness of
531
      both SwitchLoss and SwitchLossR is thoroughly evaluated on 15 standard and 5 synthetic high-
      dimensional datasets, representing diverse data distributions. The results demonstrate that the com-
532
      bination of SwitchLoss and SwitchLossR outperforms other existing techniques. While SwitchLoss
533
      generally performs better than SwitchLossR, particularly in the high-dimensional domain, it is note-
534   worthy that SwitchLossR surpasses SwitchLoss for nearly half of the standard datasets despite its
535   smaller search space. Furthermore, experiments conducted on more complex age estimation im-
536   age datasets, specifically AgeDB (Moschoglou et al., 2017) and IMDB-WIKI (Rothe et al., 2018),
537   highlight the superior performance of SwitchLossR compared to other techniques within the context
538   of deep learning architectures. Finally, we observe that the SwitchLoss schemes exhibit superior
539   performance on datasets with abundant samples, high imbalance, and complex neural network ar-
      chitectures.

685   face scores (Rothe et al., 2018), and then manually creating balanced validation and test set over the
686   supported ages. Overall, the curated dataset has 191.5K images for training, and 11.0K images for
687   validation and testing, respectively. We make the length of each bin to be 1 year, with a minimum

      J ENSON -S HANNON DIVERGENCE VS K ULLBACK -L EIBLER DIVERGENCE
898
899   Formally, for two probability distributions p and q, the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD) is defined
                                                                              PN
900   as the average of the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergences DKL (p∥q) = x p(x)log( p(x)  q(x) ) between
901                                                                               1
      p and the average distribution obtained by mixing p and q noted as m = 2 (p + q), and between
902   q and m. One advantage of JSD with respect to KL divergence is its symmetric nature. The
903   asymmetry can lead to different optimization behaviors and potentially biased results. Moreover,
904   JSD has a bounded range, with values between 0 and 1, making it more interpretable and easier
905   to compare across different contexts (Thiagarajan & Ghosh, 2023). KL divergence, on the other
906   hand, is unbounded and can take on large values, potentially leading to numerical instability and
907   difficulties in optimization. Another advantage of JSD is its robustness in situations where the
908   two distributions being compared have overlapping support. Unlike KL divergence, which can be
909   sensitive to regions with zero probability in one of the distributions, JSD can handle such cases
910
      effectively (Thiagarajan & Ghosh, 2023).

913
914   While it is evident that optimal results are more likely to be achieved with a comprehensive explo-
915   ration of all possibilities, we demonstrate that in many cases, even a limited number of exploration
916   cycles, such as 100 or 32 in the case of restricted search, can yield superior performance compared
917   to alternative techniques. However, it is important to note that if sufficient resources are available,
      we recommend a broader exploration phase to further enhance the algorithm’s effectiveness.
