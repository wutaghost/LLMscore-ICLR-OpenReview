009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) achieve great success across many general tasks,
012            but the mismatch among different vocabularies hinders further applications like
013            token-level distillation and inference with various models. To align the vocabularies
014            of LLMs, we propose a simple yet effective method named UnifyVocab to replace
015            the vocabulary of an LLM at a limited cost. A new vocabulary alignment method is
016            devised first to align the source vocabulary to the target one. We then rearrange the
017            corresponding parameters like embeddings, and progressively fine-tune the model.
018            Experimental results on models across multiple parameter scales demonstrate the
019
               effectiveness and generalization of UnifyVocab, which costs as few as 10B tokens
               to recover 98.02% performance of the vanilla models on average. We further find
020
               that unifying the vocabularies significantly facilitates the token-level distillation
021
               which remarkably boosts (+4.4%) the model with only 235M tokens. Moreover,
022            our method provides a better initialization of multilingual vocabulary for LLMs to
023            adapt to new languages.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large language models like LLaMA, GPT-4, and Qwen (Touvron et al., 2023b; OpenAI, 2023; Qwen,
029
      2024) show impressive general abilities. These models have specific strengths and weaknesses, which
030   arise from their pre-training corpus and method. However, the mismatch among their vocabularies
031   impedes the deep knowledge transfer between these models like token-level distillation and ensemble.
032   Thus, it is important to unify the vocabulary of the large language model at a low cost.
033
      The vocabulary of the language model is kept unchanged after pre-training unless adapted to a new
034
      language. It is common to append new tokens to improve the effectiveness of encoding on a new
035
      language (Tran, 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Chau et al., 2020; Minixhofer et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2023;
036   Liu et al., 2024).
037
038   In this paper, we introduce a method called UnifyVocab to replace the vocabulary of large language
039
      models from a view of token-token co-occurrences. As the general process to train an LLM, the
      pre-training corpus is first tokenized into token IDs, and then input into the model. Given the same
040
      pre-training corpus, different tokenizers result in various sequences of token IDs, while the semantic
041
      and syntactic information is preserved in the token-token co-occurrence. Therefore, UnifyVocab
042   strives to align the token IDs from the original vocabulary and the target ones based on the global
043   token-token co-occurrence matrix (Pennington et al., 2014). We further propose a metric to evaluate
044   the performance of the token-token alignment matrix. The new embedding and language modeling
045   head of LLMs (“lm head” in the transformers (Wolf, 2019)) are initialized from the re-arranged
046   parameters using the learned alignment matrix. Further adaptation process for the new vocabulary is
047   divided into a progressive two-stage procedure to improve the stability of convergence.
048   Given a target vocabulary for substitution, results on models across different scales show that as
049   few as 10B tokens are needed for our method to recover 98.02% performance of vanilla models on
050   average. The training process of UnifyVocab is 1.92x faster than the best baseline method. Unifying
051   vocabulary further facilitates the token-level distillation between models, which is 4.4% better than
052   the sentence-level distillation on the same corpus. In addition, the model trained on the English
053   corpus obtains a good initialization for the multilingual vocabulary, decreasing the perplexity from
      2.9e5 to 2e2 , and could adapt to new languages with only 4B tokens using UnifyVocab.

084   2     U NIFY VOCAB
085
086   2.1    VOCABULARY A LIGNMENT
087
088   As shown in Figure 1, there are three steps in UnifyVocab to align two vocabularies of language models
089   from the token-token co-occurrence information. We denote the source tokenizer as Tokenizers ,
090   which has Vs tokens, and the target tokenizer as Tokenizert with Vt tokens, correspondingly.
091
092   Step 1: Tokenization The comprehensiveness of the pre-training corpus is important to obtain a
093   well-trained token representation. An unbalanced corpus makes it hard to train the representation
094
      of tokens in the tail of vocabulary. Thus, the corpus used in this work is empirically composed of
      multilingual corpus CulturaX[40%] (Nguyen et al., 2023), code corpus The Stack[30%] (Kocetkov
095
      et al., 2023), and math corpus Proof-Pile-2[30%] (Azerbayev et al., 2024). We tokenize the mixed
096
      corpus using various tokenizers of different LLMs, and obtain multiple sequences of token IDs for
097   the same corpus. The default token amount of corpus used in this step is 1B, which is investigated in
098   Appendix B.1.
099
100   Step 2: Token Representation Learning We adopt GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) to train the
101   representation of the tokens from Step 1. The main reason is that GloVe considers more global
102   statistical information than those slide window methods like CBOW and fastText (Mikolov et al.,
103   2013a;b; Bojanowski et al., 2017). The details of training settings for GloVe vectors are reported in
104   Appendix A.
105
106   Step 3: Token Alignment Based on the assumption that token representations capture the semantic
107   information in the token, we align token IDs using the pair-wise cosine similarity of learned token
      representations. It should be noted that the ID of tokens belonging to both vocabularies are directly

139   2.3   P ROGRESSIVE A DAPTATION
140
141
      Given the alignment matrix Ms→t , the parameters of each token in the target vocabulary are initialized
      from the ones of the most similar source token. We find that these re-arranged embedding and lm head
142
      provide a good initialization for the new model (Section 3.2 and 4.2). Figure 2(2) illustrates the two
143
      stages designed for a LLM to adapt to the new vocabulary. The re-arranged embedding and lm head
144   are tuned first to avoid loss spike and improve the stability during tuning (Figure 5(c)). The other
145   parameters of internal layer are further tuned together in the last half part. We acknowledge that a
146   better designed adaptation method can bring a higher performance, which can be investigated in the
147   future.

      3.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETTINGS
152
153   Large Language models We adopt the fully open-source language model series Pythia (Biderman
154   et al., 2023) as base models in this work. It is noted that this work does not intend to achieve the
155   state-of-the-art performance of large language models but rather investigate an effective method to
156   replace the tokenizer. To achieve token-level knowledge transfer from other capable large language
157   models, the tokenizers (vocabularies) of Gemma (Team et al., 2024), Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024),
158   LLaMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023b), and LLaMA3 (Meta, 2024) are selected as the target tokenizer to
159   replace. We report hyper-parameters in Appendix A.
160
161   Corpus To reduce the risk of distribution shift from the training data, we choose the vanilla pre-
      training corpus (Gao et al., 2020; Soboleva et al., 2023; Kocetkov et al., 2023) of the base model


                                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Pythia in the fine-tuning process. Corpora from downstream tasks and multiple languages are applied
163   in token-level distillation and cross-lingual transfer experiments (Section 4).
164
165   Evaluation Tasks Following the common practices to evaluate large language models (Lin et al.,
166   2022; Biderman et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), there are 10 datasets, including commonsense
167   reasoning (Conneau et al., 2018; Clark et al., 2018; Mihaylov et al., 2018; Zellers et al., 2019; Ponti
168   et al., 2020; Bisk et al., 2020; Sakaguchi et al., 2020; Tikhonov & Ryabinin, 2021) and reading
169   comprehension (Clark et al., 2019) tasks, used in this work. To avoid the randomness from the
170   prompt and evaluation method, we adopt the default prompt from the commonly used language model
171   evaluation harness framework (Gao et al., 2024).
172
173   Baselines We introduce the following methods from the cross-lingual vocabulary adaptation domain
174
      as baseline methods in this work:
175              • Random Initialization for each token t ∈ {Vt \ (Vt ∩ Vs )} employs the default initialization
176                method of huggingface transformers and reuses the parameters of token t ∈ {Vt ∩ Vs },
177                which belongs to overlapping vocabularies.
178
                 • Random Permutation initializes each token t ∈ {Vt \ (Vt ∩ Vs )} using the parameter of a
179                randomly chosen token from the source vocabulary. The parameters of shared tokens are
180                also reused.
181
                 • WECHSEL (Minixhofer et al., 2022) transfers embeddings of source tokens into target
182                tokens by tokenizing and recomposing additional word embeddings Ws and Wt , which are
183                aligned with a bilingual dictionary.
184
                 • OFA (Liu et al., 2024) factorizes the embeddings of source model Es into the primitive
185
                   embeddings P and source coordinates Fs that is further re-composed by multilingual word
186                embeddings W to the target coordinates Ft . The assembled primitive embeddings P and
187                target coordinates Ft comes the target embeddings Et .
188
                 • Focus (Dobler & de Melo, 2023) initializes the embedding parameters of token t ∈ {Vt \
189
                   (Vt ∩ Vs )} using the weighted sum of the ones from the token t ∈ {Vt ∩ Vs }. It largely
190                depends on the size of ∥ Vt ∩ Vs ∥, and performs poorly when the overlapping percentage
191                of Vt and Vs is low.
192
                 • ZeTT (Minixhofer et al., 2024) trains an additional hypernetwork Hθ to generate the
193                parameters for each token t ∈ Vt . The added hypernetwork brings a lot of training cost.

      3.2    M AIN R ESULTS AND A NALYSES
217
218   We first conduct experiments to replace the tokenizer of Pythia with the Gemma tokenizer using
219   10B tokens. Results on six datasets are shown in Table 1. Given limited tokens to fine-tune, it
220   can be found that UnifyVocab performs better than the other three baseline methods. The average
221   improvement of UnifyVocab over the strong baseline method ZeTT reaches 2.49%, and the 97.63%
222   performance of the vanilla model is reserved after vocabulary replacement. Replace the corpus to
223   train the GloVe embedding with 1B SlimPajama (Soboleva et al., 2023) tokens comes to a comparable
224   results. It demonstrates the robustness of our method on the pre-training corpus for token embedding
225
      and alignment matrix. We find that the performance can be further advanced by aligning the GloVe
      embedding into the relative representation using 300 common tokens occur in both vocabularies
226
      following Moschella et al. (2023), which is the row with “+ Align Rep.” label.
227
228
229
      Better alignment brings better initialization. The loss curves of Pythia1B with different methods
      during the first 5B tokens training are shown in Figure 3(a). We find that UnifyVocab brings a better
230
      initialization and decreases the first-step training loss from 17.8 (Focus) to 9.5. Moreover, the training
231
      process with UnifyVocab is faster than the ones with other methods, which reaches 2.75 with only
232   2.6B tokens and is 1.92x (5B/2.6B) speed up than the method Focus.

      We further investigate the impact of the learned alignment matrix Ms→t by changing the hyper-
271   parameters of GloVe. It is noted that different alignment matrices Ms→t bring different initial
272   parameters, and also come to different BLEU scores on the same evaluation corpus. Figure 3(b)
273   illustrates the negative relationship between the first-step training loss and the BLEU. In other words,
274   the higher the BLEU score for the alignment matrix Ms→t , the better the initial parameter is. The
275   other metrics like BLEU-1 and BertScore are also used to evaluate the alignment metrix, and also
276   show a negative relationship with the initial training loss in Appendix B.4.
277
278   More overlapping comes to faster convergence and higher performance. The UnifyVocab is
279   further applied to the other three target tokenizers: Qwen2, LLaMA2, and LLaMA3. Table 2 reports
280   the performance of models after replacing vocabulary on six datasets. UnifyVocab recovers 98.02%
281   performance of the base model on average with only 10B tokens. Given a target vocabulary with
282   more tokens than the one of Pythia (50.3k), it can be found that a higher overlapping ratio brings a
283
      better performance of model replaced (97.62% for Gemma to 99.07% for LLaMA3). The zero-shot
      in-context learning results for Pythia6.9B with LLaMA3 vocabulary even surpass the vanilla base
284
      model. The results of Pythia1B with LLaMA2 vocabulary are only 94.47%, which is inferior to the
285
      average result of 98.02%. We argue that it may come from the missing 75.0M parameters (7.4% for
286   Pythia1B ) after switching to a 32.0k vocabulary from the 50.3k vocabulary.

426   5      R ELATED W ORKS
427
428   Our work is related to word representation, large language models, and vocabulary adaption, which
429   will be briefly introduced below.
430
431   Word Representation Based on the distributional semantic hypothesis, Bengio et al. (2003)
      introduced the neural probabilistic language model to learn word representation. Researchers mainly


                                                                                      8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      focus on improving the effectiveness during learning word representations (Mikolov et al., 2013a;b;
433   Bojanowski et al., 2017), which provide a good initialization for neural networks like LSTM and
434   GRU (Hochreiter, 1997; Chung et al., 2014). GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) provides a method to
435   train word representations from a view of global word-word co-occurrence matrix decomposition.
436   It motivates us to train a word representation for each token and align the token ID from statistical
437   co-occurrence information in the pre-training corpus.
438
439   Large Language Model Through scaling in the parameters and pre-training corpus (Kaplan et al.,
440   2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), large language models including GPT and LLaMA (Radford et al.,
441   2018; 2019; Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Meta, 2024; GLM et al.,
442
      2024) demonstrate impressive performance across multiple tasks. However, the knowledge transfer
      between different models is greatly hindered by the mismatch in the vocabulary. We aim to mitigate
443
      this problem by introducing an effective method to replace the tokenizer of a pre-trained large
444
      language model.
445
446   Vocabulary Adaption is investigated mainly in the multilingual domain, especially the cross-
447   lingual knowledge transfer problem (Workshop et al., 2023; Muennighoff et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
448   2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Üstün et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). It aims to improve the encoding
449   effectiveness of tokenizer on corpora from new languages, and is often implemented by extending
450   the original vocabulary (Tran, 2020; Chau et al., 2020; Minixhofer et al., 2022; Dobler & de Melo,
451   2023; Downey et al., 2023). Most methods like Focus (Dobler & de Melo, 2023) rely on the tokens
452   belonging to both source vocabulary and target vocabulary to initialize the other new tokens in the
453   target vocabulary. Our method differs from these studies for the whole replacement of vocabulary
454
      using a limited corpus. It does not rely on the tokens in both source vocabulary and target vocabulary.
455
456   6   L IMITATIONS
457
458   The first limitation comes from the assumption that the pre-training data distribution is available. We
459   conduct experiments on Pythia with different parameter amounts, which provide public model weights
460   and pre-training corpus. Due to the limited computation resource budget, open-source language
461
      models with unknown pre-training corpus like Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) are not investigated in
      this work. However, the pre-training corpus distribution of open-weighted large language models
462
      can be roughly inferred by the BPE vocabulary (Hayase et al., 2024). It can re-construct a similar
463
      pre-training corpus to conduct replacing tokenizer experiments.
464
465   The 10B tokens of model tuning cost in replacing a tokenizer using UnifyVocab is another limitation,
466   although it is only 3.33% of the 300B tokens pre-training corpus for Pythia. From the loss curve of
467
      UnifyVocab (Figure 4), we find that the start of full parameters tuning can be less than 5B tokens,
      which may result in a better balance.
468
469
470   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
471
472   In this paper, we introduce a method named UnifyVocab to replace the tokenizer of large language
473   models from a token-token co-occurrence view. Extensive experiments demonstrate that UnifyVocab
474
      reserves the most performance of vanilla models (98.02% on average) using only 10B tokens, which
      enables deeper knowledge transfer between models like token-level distillation and cross-lingual
475
      knowledge transfer.
476
477   Beyond replacing the vocabulary of large language models, our method can be extended to replace
478   the vocabulary of multi-modal models by aligning different modal tokens. The other direction is to
479   develop a method with less training cost, e.g., incorporating meta-learning to replace the two-stage
480
      tuning method.

      Model Tuning The optimizer adopted in this work is AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019), where

852   spike in Figure 5(b) and Figure 5(c). We adopt bf16 mixed precision training and ZeRO-1 to save
853   GPU memory cost and speed up the training process (Micikevicius et al., 2018; Rasley et al., 2020).
854   Following Biderman et al. (2023), the batch size is set to 2M tokens and the max sequence length is
855   2048.
