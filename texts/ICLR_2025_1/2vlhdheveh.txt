009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion-based image super-resolution (SR) methods have shown promise in re-
012            constructing high-resolution images with fine details from low-resolution coun-
013            terparts. However, these approaches typically require tens or even hundreds of
014            iterative samplings, resulting in significant latency. Recently, techniques have
015            been devised to enhance the sampling efficiency of diffusion-based SR models via
016            knowledge distillation. Nonetheless, when aligning the knowledge of student and
017            teacher models, these solutions either solely rely on pixel-level loss constraints or
018
               neglect the fact that diffusion models prioritize varying levels of information at
               different time steps. To accomplish effective and efficient image super-resolution,
019
               we propose a time-aware diffusion distillation method, named TAD-SR. Specifi-
020
               cally, we introduce a novel score distillation strategy to align the score functions
021            between the outputs of the student and teacher models after minor noise pertur-
022            bation. This distillation strategy eliminates the inherent bias in score distillation
023            sampling (SDS) and enables the student models to focus more on high-frequency
024            image details by sampling at smaller time steps. Furthermore, to mitigate perfor-
025            mance limitations stemming from distillation, we fully leverage the knowledge in
026            the teacher model and design a time-aware discriminator to differentiate between
027            real and synthetic data. This discriminator effectively distinguishes the diffused
028            distributions of real and generated images under varying levels of noise distur-
029
               bance through the injection of time information. Extensive experiments on SR and
               blind face restoration (BFR) tasks demonstrate that the proposed method outper-
030
               forms existing diffusion-based single-step techniques and achieves performance
031
               comparable to state-of-the-art diffusion models that rely on multi-step generation.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Image super-resolution (SR), a cornerstone task in low-level vision, involves reconstructing high-
037   resolution (HR) images with intricate details from low-resolution (LR) counterparts. Owing to the
038   inherent ill-posed nature of this task, multiple high-resolution reconstructions are plausible for a
039   given low-resolution input, presenting a persistent and perplexing challenge. Recently, the diffusion
040   model (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020), a novel generative model, has garnered increasing atten-
041   tion for its capacity to model complex data distributions. It has gradually emerged as a successor to
042   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020) in various downstream tasks,
043   including image editing (Meng et al., 2021; Hertz et al., 2022), image inpainting (Chung et al., 2022;
044
      Lugmayr et al., 2022) and image super-resolution (Saharia et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2024).
045   Specifically, existing diffusion-based image super-resolution methods can be broadly categorized
046   into two streams: one involves feeding low-resolution images along with noise into the diffusion
047   model and training the model from scratch (Rombach et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2024), while the
048   other (Wang et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2024b) adapts SR tasks by fine-tuning the pre-trained text-
049   to-image diffusion model. While these methods have demonstrated promising results, generating
050
      images typically demands tens or even hundreds of iterative samplings, significantly impeding their
      practical application and further advancement.
051
052   To enhance the inference efficiency of diffusion models, various acceleration techniques have been
053   proposed, such as the development of numerical samplers (Lu et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2024) and
      the applications of knowledge distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Sauer et al., 2023). However,

075   due to the requirement of SR tasks to output images with clear details while ensuring high visual
076   similarity with LR images, directly applying existing acceleration methods to SR tasks presents
077   significant challenges. For the SR task, ResShift (Yue et al., 2024) has improved the sampling
078   efficiency of diffusion-based SR models by utilizing information from LR images to reformulate the
079   diffusion process, thereby reducing the number of sampling steps to 15. Furthermore, SinSR (Wang
080   et al., 2023c) merges distillation techniques with a cycle consistency approach to refine the ResShift
081   model into a single inference step. However, it only constrains the output of the student model
082
      at a single scale and fails to leverage the ability of the pre-trained diffusion model to fit diffused
      distributions across different time steps, a property referred to as the time-aware of the diffusion
083
      model in this paper. Recently, AddSR (Xie et al., 2024) employs adversarial diffusion distillation
084
      (ADD) (Sauer et al., 2023) for SR task to enhance sampling efficiency. Although it employs the
085   expertise of the teacher model to optimize the student model via Score Distillation Sampling (SDS)
086   (Poole et al., 2022), inherent biases in the gradients calculated by SDS lead to image blurring and
087   excessive smoothness. Additionally, AddSR does not take advantage of the diffusion model’s ability
088   to extract semantic features at different levels. Instead, it relies on a pre-trained DINOV2 (Oquab
089   et al., 2023) discriminator in pixel space, which is both expensive and challenging to optimize.
090
      To address the aforementioned issues, we propose a time-aware distillation method that fully lever-
091   ages the time-aware property of the teacher model and the latent knowledge embedded in the diffu-
092   sion process. Specifically, we propose a high-frequency enhanced score distillation technique that
093   eliminates the inherent bias in score distillation sampling and improves the high-frequency details in
094   the student model’s output by focusing on sampling in small time steps. Additionally, To overcome
095   the performance limitations of teacher models, we incorporate adversarial learning into the distilla-
096   tion framework, forcing the student model to directly generate samples that lie on the manifold of
097   real images in a single inference step. Specifically, we extract features from real and synthetic data
098   under varying noise disturbances using the teacher model, while designing a time-aware discrimina-
099
      tor to effectively distinguish these features. Combined with the above design, our method can match
      or even surpass the performance of state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods with only one-step sampling.
100
101   Overall, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
102
        • By fully leveraging the time-aware property of the diffusion model and the latent knowledge
103       embedded in the diffusion process, we propose a time-aware distillation method that accelerates
104       diffusion-based SR models into a single inference step.
105
106     • We analyze the inherent bias in score distillation sampling and propose a novel score distillation
107       method to eliminate this bias. Additionally, we focus on enhancing the high-frequency details
          in the student model’s output by sampling at small time steps.

199   3     M ETHODOLOGY
200
201   3.1   M OTIVATION
202
203   Building on prior knowledge of Score Distillation Sampling (SDS), we know that SDS can optimize
204   student models by leveraging the latent knowledge of pre-trained diffusion models, ensuring that the
205   output image distribution aligns as closely as possible with that of the pre-trained diffusion models.
206   However, due to the inherent error in pre-trained diffusion models, we observed that even when GT
207
      (HR images) are noised and fed into the pre-trained diffusion model, a deviation still exists between
      the predicted distribution and the actual data distribution (as illustrated in the first row on the right
208
      side of Fig. 2). This indicates that even in ideal situations, SDS itself has biases, consistent with the
209
      conclusions of previous related work (Hertz et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Thus, we decompose the
210   gradient calculated by SDS into two components: ∇θ LSDS = ϵϕ (ztstu , y, t)−ϵ = Dir +∆bias . The
211   first is the expected direction, which guides the student model to generate high-resolution images
212   aligned with the distribution of the teacher model. The second component is the deviation between
213   the predicted and true values of high-quality images that align with the diffusion model’s prior
214   distribution. This deviation disrupts the optimization of the student model, producing non-detailed
215   and blurry outputs (as shown in Fig. 3). Our goal is to identify this deviation and eliminate it during
      the optimization process.

241   ϵ or ϵϕ (zttch , y, t) − ϵ. This bias is subsequently subtracted from SDS to guide the model’s optimiza-

245
      HR images and the student model’s output, making it challenging to optimize the student model by
246
      calculating the score difference on a point-by-point basis. Furthermore, Fig. 2 clearly demonstrates
247   a significant difference in the denoising scores of images generated by the teacher model and the
248   student model under slight noise disturbances (small time steps). Due to the diffusion model’s focus
249   on high-frequency information in images at small time steps, it can be concluded that the student
250   model’s output notably lacks high-frequency details compared to the teacher model, which aligns
251   with our expectations. Therefore, calculating the score difference between the outputs of the teacher
252   and the student model under mild noise interference provides an effective gradient direction to guide
253   the optimization of the student model.
254   To ensure that the student model’s performance is not overly restricted by the teacher model, we
255   propose incorporating real images into the distillation framework to offer additional supervision. As
256   previously noted, optimizing the model by directly calculating the pixel-wise distance between the
257   real data and the student model’s output is difficult. In contrast, we suggest employing adversarial
258   learning to align the output distribution of the student model with that of real data. The successful
259   deployment of pre-trained diffusion models in downstream tasks has revealed that denoising net-
260   works can extract multi-level semantic information from images. Consequently, we can utilize the
261
      teacher model to extract features and offer supervisory signals to student models via adversarial
      learning. However, as illustrated in the third row of Fig.2, the distribution difference between the
262
      student model’s output and the real data varies over time, making it challenging for the discriminator
263
      to accurately fit the diffused distribution of the images at different time steps. A straightforward so-
264   lution is to employ multiple discriminators, each specializing in the diffused distribution at different
265   time steps. As shown in Table 1, this approach significantly enhances the quality of the generated
266   images. However, managing multiple discriminators and their respective time periods introduces
267   complexity and incurs substantial training costs. Given that variations in diffused distribution are
268   primarily related to time steps, we propose that a unique set of parameters can be adaptively learned
269   from each time step and integrated into the discriminator’s features. From Table 1, it can be seen
      that our design effectively improves the quality of generated images.

      3.2   TAD-SR
271
272   The overview framework of our proposed TAD-SR is illustrated in Fig. 4, consisting of a teacher
273   model Fϕ parameterized by ϕ, a student network fθ initialized from the teacher model with weights
274   θ, and a trainable time-aware discriminator Dψ parameterized by ψ. During training, the student
275   model generates samples from noisy data and computes the regression loss against the samples
276   generated iteratively by the teacher model. Subsequently, we introduce slight noise to the samples
277   produced by both the student and teacher models, predict the score function via the teacher model,
278   and refine the student network by leveraging the discrepancy between the two score functions. Fur-
279
      thermore, to mitigate the performance constraints of the teacher model on the student model, we
      design a time-aware discriminator built upon the encoder network of the pre-trained teacher model,
280
      enhancing the perceptual quality of the generated samples through adversarial training processes.

343   Time-aware discriminator. To prevent the student model’s performance from being entirely con-
344   strained by the teacher model, we propose incorporating real images (HR images) into the distillation
345   framework. However, directly calculating the regression loss between the real image and the stu-
346   dent model’s output can result in optimization challenges. Recent studies (Sauer et al., 2023) (Sauer
347   et al., 2024) have shown that adversarial loss can be integrated into diffusion models to enhance the
348   quality of generated images. However, ADD (Sauer et al., 2023) relies on pre-training the DINOv2
349   discriminator in pixel space, which is both costly and complex. To reduce training costs and en-
350
      hance model performance, LADD (Sauer et al., 2024) employed a pre-trained diffusion model for
      adversarial training in latent space. Despite its contribution, LADD overlooks the critical correlation
351

352

353   under various noise disturbances, which poses significant challenges for optimizing the discrimina-

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a time-aware distillation method that accelerates diffusion-based super-
532
      resolution models to a single inference step. We introduce a high-frequency enhanced score distilla-
533
      tion technique that optimizes the generator by calculating the score difference between the outputs
534   of the teacher and student models following slight noise perturbation, thereby enhancing the high-
535   frequency details in the student model’s output. To elevate the student model’s performance ceiling,
536   we incorporate generative adversarial learning into the diffusion model framework. Specifically, we
537   design a time-aware discriminator that distinguishes between generated and real data in latent space,
538   providing more efficient and effective supervision for the student model. Extensive experiments
539   demonstrate that our method can achieve performance on par with or surpassing that of the SOTA
      methods in a single inference step.

769   A.1     R ELATED W ORK
770
771   A.1.1    I MAGE S UPER -R ESOLUTION .
772   Traditional methods (Dong et al., 2012; Gu et al., 2017; 2015) for image super-resolution rely on
773   manual design of image priors based on subjective knowledge to restore image details. With the
774   advancement of deep learning (DL), DL-based image super-resolution has become predominant,
775   which mainly focuses on network architecture (Lai et al., 2017; Menick & Kalchbrenner, 2018;
776   Lugmayr et al., 2020; Sajjadi et al., 2017), image priors (Pan et al., 2021; Chan et al., 2021), loss
777   functions (Zhou et al., 2020; Fuoli et al., 2021), and other aspects (Zhang et al., 2018a; Wang et al.,
778   2021b). Recently, diffusion-based methods for image super-resolution have garnered widespread
779   attention. SR3 (Saharia et al., 2022) incorporated low-resolution images as conditions into the de-
780
      noising model to guide the sampling process. Subsequently, CDPMSR (Niu et al., 2023) and IDM
      (Gao et al., 2023) respectively utilized preprocessed images and features as conditions to enhance
781
      the perceptual quality. Inspired by the powerful generation priors of stable diffusion (SD) (Rombach
782
      et al., 2022), recent studies (Wang et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024b) have achieved
783   image super-resolution by fine-tuning pre-trained SD models (Rombach et al., 2022). However,
784   these methods typically require dozens or even hundreds of iterations to generate high-resolution
785   images. To enhance the inference efficiency, ResShift (Yue et al., 2024) redesigned the diffusion
786   process by shifting the residuals between high-resolution and low-resolution images to construct a
787   Markov chain, achieving performance comparable to previous state-of-the-art methods with just 15
788   sampling steps. During the same period as our method, OSEDiff (Wu et al., 2024a) directly utilized
789   LR images as the starting point for diffusion and optimized the student model through variational
790   score distillation, generating HR images through a single sampling step. However, it relies on a
791
      specific model architecture, while our approach offers a more generalized method for accelerating
      diffusion models, enabling the distillation of various super-resolution models into single-step sam-
792
      pling based on specific requirements. Furthermore, our method can theoretically be extended to
793
      other tasks, such as unconditional generation.
794
795   A.1.2    ACCELERATING D IFFUSION M ODELS .
796
797   Although diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) has formidable generation ca-
798   pabilities, the substantial number of inference steps poses a significant obstacle to its practical im-
799   plementation. Recent studies focusing on enhancing the inference speed of diffusion models have
800   garnered considerable interest within the research community. Mainstream approaches include the
801
      development of high-order samplers (Song et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2024) and the
      application of knowledge distillation techniques (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Sauer et al., 2023; 2024;
802
      Song et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023). Denoising diffusion implicit models (DDIM) (Song et al.,
803
      2020), an early contribution, introduced a deterministic sampling method that notably decreased
804   the number of diffusion sampling steps. DPMSolver (Lu et al., 2022) proposed a fast dedicated
805   high-order ODE solver, further reducing the diffusion sampling steps to 20. However, trajectory
806   compression through numerical solvers often results in performance degradation, necessitating over
807   ten inference steps to generate samples. In contrast, progressive distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022)
808   gradually reduces the inference steps of student models through multi-stage distillation, but the ac-
809   cumulation of errors in each distillation stage may affect the performance of the student model.
      Consistency model (Song et al., 2023) eliminates the need for computation-intensive iterations by
