009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We define the local complexity of a neural network with continuous piecewise lin-
012            ear activations as a measure of the density of linear regions over an input data dis-
013            tribution. We show theoretically that ReLU networks that learn low-dimensional
014            feature representations have a lower local complexity. This allows us to connect
015            recent empirical observations on feature learning at the level of the weight matri-
016            ces with concrete properties of the learned functions. In particular, we show that
017
               the local complexity serves as an upper bound on the total variation of the function
               over the input data distribution and thus that feature learning can be related to ad-
018
               versarial robustness. Lastly, we consider how optimization drives ReLU networks
019
               towards solutions with lower local complexity. Overall, this work contributes a
020            theoretical framework towards relating geometric properties of ReLU networks to
021            different aspects of learning such as feature learning and representation cost.

024   1     I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Despite the numerous achievements of deep learning, many of the mechanisms by which deep neural
027   networks learn and generalize remain unclear. An “Occam’s Razor” style heuristic is that we want
028   our neural network to parameterize a simple solution after training, but it can be challenging to
029   establish a useful metric of the complexity of a deep neural network (Hu et al., 2021). A growing
030
      body of research has sought to gain insights into the complexity of deep neural networks in the case
      where we use piece-wise linear activation functions, such as ReLU, LeakyReLU, or Maxout. If ϕ is
031
      a continuous piecewise linear (CPWL) activation function and Ai (x) = Wi x−βi is a parameterized
032
      affine linear function, i = 1, . . . , L, we consider a network of the following form:
033
034                            Nθ (x) = AL ◦ ϕ ◦ AL−1 · · · ϕ ◦ A1 (x),     x ∈ Rn 0 .                      (1)
035   The network function Nθ : Rn0 → RnL parameterized by θ = (Wi , βi )i is then also be a CPWL
036   function. For any fixed choice of the parameter θ, the input domain Rn0 is partitioned into linear
037   regions where the function is linear. These partitions of the input space have been used extensively
038   to study diverse topics such as the expressive power, decision boundaries in classification, gradients
039   and parameter initialization, and generalization (e.g., Montúfar et al., 2014; Raghu et al., 2017;
040   Zhang et al., 2018; Balestriero & Baraniuk, 2018; Grigsby & Lindsey, 2022; Brandenburg et al.,
041   2024; Telgarsky, 2016). In this work we aim to advance a theoretical framework towards better
042   understanding the local distribution of linear regions near the data distribution and how it relates to
043
      other relevant aspects of learning such as robustness and representation learning.
044
045   1.1   M OTIVATION
046
      In the kernel regime, neural networks with piecewise linear activations are observed to follow lazy
047
      training (Chizat et al., 2019) and bias towards smooth interpolants which do not significantly change
048   the structure of linear regions during training (see, e.g., Williams et al., 2019; Jin & Montúfar, 2023).
049   On the other hand, for networks in the active regime, which are not well approximated by linearized
050   models, one observes significant movement of the linear regions and in some cases a bias towards
051   interpolants that have only a small number of linear regions (e.g., Maennel et al., 2018; Williams
052   et al., 2019; Shevchenko et al., 2022). Characterizing the dynamics of linear regions at a theoretical
053   level remains a significant outstanding challenge, even for shallow networks. Recent empirical
      studies have demonstrated interesting dynamics of the linear regions near the training data points.

054
      In particular, Humayun et al. (2024b) have shown that in the terminal phase of training, the number
055   of linear regions near the data drops significantly, and this drop corresponds to an increase in the
056   model’s adversarial robustness. We replicate similar experiments in Figure 3. Related is the concept
057   of “grokking” which refers to the sudden improvement in the generalization error or robustness after
058   extended training periods, often long after the training loss has reached near-zero values. Grokking
059   has been associated with representation learning, where an emerging idea is that late generalization
060   may occur if and when a network learns the “right” representation for the task at hand (Liu et al.,
061   2022). In particular, some works have claimed that networks learn low-dimensional representations
062   during grokking (Fan et al., 2024; Yunis et al., 2024b;a). This motivates us to consider the following:
063   Question 1: How does representation learning relate to the complexity of linear regions?
064
      To better understand representation learning, we consider the dimension of the feature manifold as
065
      measured by the average rank of the Jacobian of the intermediate layer representations with respect
066
      to the input. In particular, based on the structure of various theoretical bounds (Montúfar, 2017; Serra
067   et al., 2018; Hinz, 2021), we expect that networks that learn low-dimensional feature manifolds
068   will generally also have fewer linear regions. Empirical results also show that networks which
069   undergo a drop in the number of linear regions tend to be much simpler, having a nearly piecewise
070   constant structure, hinting at a connection between the local distribution of the linear regions and the
071   global structure of the learned function (Humayun et al., 2024b). Related is the concept of “neural
072   collapse”, which refers to a phenomenon where, in the terminal phase of training, the within-class
073   variance of the last layer features tends towards zero (Papyan et al., 2020). Furthermore, prior
074   literature has suggested a connection between the size of linear regions and robustness (Croce et al.,
075
      2019). Thus, a natural question we concern ourselves with is:
076   Question 2: Can we connect the local density of linear regions to the robustness of a network?
077
      We attempt to answer this question by comparing a measure of the local density of linear regions
078
      to the total variation the a network over the input space. Aspects in this direction have appeared
079   in context of parameter initialization and the gradients of a network with respect to its inputs (e.g.,
080   Hanin & Rolnick, 2018; Tseran & Montúfar, 2023). Lastly, we are interested in the relation between
081   parameters and functions, and how optimization may cause networks to converge to solutions with
082   lower complexity in terms of linear regions. To this end we compare our measure of local complexity
083   to the distribution of parameters, building on ideas that have been used to study the expected number
084   of linear regions (Hanin & Rolnick, 2019b), and the representation cost of a network, a quantity
085   which has been previously linked to sparsity of weight matrices (Jacot, 2023a).
086
087
088   1.2   C ONTRIBUTIONS
089
090   This work takes steps towards establishing quantitative links in ReLU networks between the distri-
091
      bution of linear regions in the input space, representation learning, and parameter optimization:
092
093   • We introduce a framework for understanding model complexity based on the linear regions over
094
        the input space. In Section 3 we define the local complexity (LC) as the average density of non-
        linearites over a dataset. To capture the typical behavior of the functions, we define this measure
095
        in a way that is robust to perturbations of the bias parameters.
096
097
      • In Section 4 we establish theoretical connections between the proposed local complexity and the
098
        local rank, which we define as the average dimension of the feature manifold at intermediate
099     layers. This offers a link between the network complexity and representation learning.
100
101   • In Section 5 we demonstrate a bound between the local complexity and the total variation of a
102     network over the input space. This offers a possible path towards understanding how the linear
103     regions can relate to adversarial robustness and phenomena like neural collapse.
104
105   • We explore links between local complexity and parameter optimization. In Section 6 we show
106     that the local complexity is bounded by the representation cost and by the ranks of the weight
107     matrices. As a consequence, we can relate the density of linear regions to results on the implicit
        regularization of the ranks of weight matrices.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Several works have studied bounds on the number of linear regions of the functions represented
111   by deep ReLU networks (e.g., Pascanu et al., 2014; Montúfar et al., 2014; Serra et al., 2018). For
112   deep neural networks the maximum number of linear regions will typically be polynomial in the
113   width and exponential in the input dimension and number of layers. However, the parameters that
114   achieve this upper bound typically occupy only a small region of the parameter space. In fact, if
115   one considers the expected number of linear regions over a probability distribution of parameters
116   that satisfies certain reasonable conditions, one finds that this is bounded above by the number of
117   neurons raised to the input dimension (Hanin & Rolnick, 2019b;a; Tseran & Montúfar, 2021). In
118   other words, for a random choice of the parameters, one is more likely to see a number of linear
119
      regions that is much smaller than the hard upper bound. Some works have analyzed the distortion
      length of curves in the input space by ReLU networks (Raghu et al., 2017). Hanin et al. (2022) looks
120
      at how the expected length of these curves changes from input to output of a network. In a similar
121
      vein, Goujon et al. (2024) estimate the typical number of non-linearity points encountered by a 1D
122   curve in the input space. Other works have studied the effect that the architecture may have on the
123   geometry and the topology of decision boundaries in classification (Zhang et al., 2018; Grigsby &
124   Lindsey, 2022; Alfarra et al., 2023; Brandenburg et al., 2024).
125
      A few works have tried to understand the local behavior and the dynamics of linear regions during
126
      training. In particular, Humayun et al. (2024b) compare the phenomenon of grokking to a simplifi-
127
      cation of the linear regions near the training data points. They demonstrate empirically that during
128   the terminal phase of training, a relatively sudden drop in the number of linear regions corresponds
129   to an improvement in the model’s adversarial robustness. Cohan et al. (2022) study the evolution
130   of linear regions in the state space of networks trained for deep reinforcement learning, finding a
131   decrease in the density during training, as measured by trajectories in the state space. In a related
132   work, Zhu et al. (2020) derive an algorithm for computing an upper bound on the number of lin-
133   ear regions near a data point and look into the training dynamics of the linear regions. Sattelberg
134   et al. (2023) examine the linear regions local to a dataset of trained networks and note that they tend
135   to be relatively simple. Croce et al. (2019) relate the size of linear regions to adversarial robust-
136
      ness. Another result that links complexity of the linear regions to robustness is that of Humayun
      et al. (2023b), which leverages the linear region structure of ReLU networks to design an algorithm
137
      which improves adversarial robustness. Similar to some of our results, Li et al. (2022) relate adver-
138
      sarial robustness to model complexity as defined by the VC dimension. In a similar flavor to our
139   definition of the local complexity, Gamba et al. (2022) build a complexity measure related to linear
140   regions and propose that the exact number of linear regions may not be the best metric for model
141   complexity, preferring instead to focus on a more robust measure. Other works have analyzed ‘knot’
142   points, or non-linearity points through an optimization perspective, where in particular Shevchenko
143   et al. (2022) obtain a bound on the number of knots between training inputs for univariate shallow
144   ReLU networks in the mean-field regime (Mei et al., 2018; Rotskoff & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022).
145   We may also highlight a few of the works that look at the dimensionality of representations, such
146   as those of Humayun et al. (2024a); Jacot (2023a;b); Jacot et al. (2024); Scarvelis & Solomon
147   (2024). Our definition of local rank bears similarity to that of Humayun et al. (2024a) and Patel
148   & Shwartz-Ziv (2024) as well as the “Jacobian rank” introduced by Jacot (2023a). The low-rank
149   bias of neural networks is a related idea that has been studied by Súkenı́k et al. (2024) and Timor
150   et al. (2023). Several other works in this area have sought to characterize the dimension of data
151   manifolds through the use of diffusion models (Stanczuk et al., 2022). A connection between the
152   rank of learned embeddings and the representation cost was demonstrated in the work of Jacot
153
      (2023a). The papers of Dherin et al. (2022); Munn et al. (2024) highlight connections between neural
      collapse and a quantity they called the “geometric complexity”, which is generally reminiscent of
154
      the Dirichlet energy. Our definition of the total variation of a network over the data distribution is
155
      bears resemblance to their definition of the geometric complexity.

      3.1   T HE ROLE OF N OISE IN C OMPUTING L OCAL C OMPLEXITY
199
200   We seek to define a measure for the local density of linear regions that are robust to small perturba-
201   tions of the weights. We take the view that the average number of linear regions over a local region
202   of parameter space can be more meaningful that the number of regions at a fixed parameter value.
203   Thus, we wish to understand BNθ as a random object given a choice of weight matrices.
204   Tracking the number of linear regions for particular parameters requires that one solves a system of
205   parametric equations of the form zℓ,i (x) = βℓ,i , which can be difficult. On the other hand, examples
206   from algebraic geometry suggest that tracking expected values of the number of solutions to para-
207   metric systems can be easier (Malajovich, 2023). This approach and the resulting proof techniques
208   bear resemblance to the application of the co-area formula in the work of Hanin & Rolnick (2019b)
209   or the Kac-Rice formula, which is known for characterizing the size of level sets in random fields
210   (Berzin et al., 2022). In contrast to the definitions of Hanin & Rolnick (2019b), we will consider the
211   distribution of linear regions over the input space and the behavior depending on specific parameters,
212
      which are aspects that are not covered in their work. While we do not directly apply the Kac-Rice
      formula, we find its structure and applications to be conceptually relevant.
213

215   zℓ,i (x) − βℓ,i , where βℓ,i is the bias. The simplest model that allows us to consider expected values
      is to introduce additive noise δℓ,i and track the 0 level set of vℓ,i (x) + δℓ,i . It is possible to introduce

      5   N ETWORKS WITH L OWER L OCAL C OMPLEXITY MAY BE MORE ROBUST
362
      Neural networks have been shown to sometimes converge to solutions that exhibit neural collapse
363
      (Papyan et al., 2020). In this case, the networks have a low within-class variance of representations
364   in the last hidden layer, implying that the learned function is flat around the data points. We will
365   attempt to understand this specific geometric property by considering the total variation of a trained

369   where the function is nearly constant, aligning with the characteristics of neural collapse.

497   7    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
498
499
      Summary We presented a framework for analyzing the distribution of linear regions of the func-
500   tions parametrized by neural networks with piecewise linear activations. We introduced a measure
501   of local complexity that is robust with respect to perturbations of the parameters and used this to gain
502   insights into relevant aspects of learning such as robustness and representation learning. Specifically,
503   we establish that networks that learn low-dimensional representations tend to exhibit a lower local
504   complexity. Further, we connected the local complexity of linear regions to the total variation of the
505   network functions and thus to robustness. We also analyze how the local complexity can be implic-
506   itly minimized during training by connecting it to properties of the weight matrices. Overall, this
507   work contributes a theoretical framework relating geometric properties of ReLU networks, specifi-
508
      cally the linear regions, to different aspects of learning, and illustrates interesting interrelations that
      we hope might motivate further investigations in this direction.
509
510
      Limitations and future research We focused on the ReLU activation function. We think that
511
      our proof techniques could be adapted to obtain results for more general piecewise linear activation
512
      functions. Such generalizations could be approached in a similar way as Tseran & Montúfar (2021)
513   approached the analysis of expected complexity for maxout networks. Though we find interesting
514   results suggesting the proposed local complexity measure might be implicitly minimized during
515   training, a detailed analysis addressing the training dynamics of the local complexity remains an
516   open problem for further research. Empirically, in certain settings we can often observe complex
517   interactions between local complexity and local rank, as well as between local complexity and to-
518   tal variation. This suggests that the explicit relationship between the local complexity and other
519   measures of model complexity may be much richer than what is covered by our theoretical results.
520   Another natural direction would be to construct explicit bounds on the generalization gap based on
521
      the local complexity, as one would expect that networks with a simple structure in terms of their
      linear regions would also generalize well.
