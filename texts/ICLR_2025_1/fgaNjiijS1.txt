007                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009            The distribution closeness testing (DCT) assesses whether the distance between an
010            unknown distribution pair is at least ϵ-far; in practice, the ϵ can be defined as the
011            distance between a reference (known) distribution pair. However, existing DCT
012            methods are mainly measure discrepancies between a distribution pair defined on
013            discrete one-dimensional spaces (e.g., total variation on a discrete one-dimensional
014            space), which limits the DCT to be used on complex data (e.g., images). To make
015
               DCT applicable on complex data, a natural idea is to introduce the maximum mean
               discrepancy (MMD), a powerful measurement to see the difference between a pair
016
               of two complex distributions, to DCT scenarios. Nonetheless, in this paper, we
017
               find that MMD value is less informative when assessing the closeness levels for
018            multiple distribution pairs with the same kernel, i.e., MMD value can be the same
019            for many pairs of distributions that have different norms in the same reproducing
020            kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). To mitigate the issue, we propose a new kernel DCT
021            with the norm-adaptive MMD (NAMMD) by scaling MMD with the norms of
022            distributions, effective for kernels κ(x, x′ ) = Ψ(x − x′ ) ≤ K with a positive-
023            definite Ψ(·) and Ψ(0) = K. Theoretically, we prove that our NAMMD test
024            achieves higher test power compared to the MMD test, along with asymptotic
025            distribution analysis. We also present upper bounds on the sample complexity of
026
               our NAMMD test and prove that Type-I error is controlled. We finally conduct
               experiments to validate the effectiveness of our NAMMD test.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Assessing difference between a distribution pair is important in the field of machine learning, because
032   the test and training data are from different distributions in many real-world scenarios [1]. Thus, tons
033
      of research has been done on problem settings where distributional differences exist. Two phenomena
      can be observed in the literature. On the one hand, a large distributional discrepancy between training
034
      and test data might cause poor performance on test data for a model trained on the training data [2].
035
      This phenomenon can be theoretically explained by the domain adaptation theory [3]. On the other
036   hand, it is also empirically proved that models trained on a large dataset (e.g., ImageNet [4]) can have
037   good performance on relevant/similar downstream test data (e.g., Pascal VOC [5]) that is different
038   from training dataset [6]. This means that, even if training and test data are from different distributions,
039   we can still expect relatively good performance because they might be close to each other.
040
      Therefore, seeing to what statistically significant extent two distributions are close to each other is
041
      important and might have the potential to help us decide if we really need to adapt a model when we
042   observe upcoming data that follow a different distribution from training data. Two-sample testing can
043   naturally help see if training and test data are from the same distribution [7], but it is less useful in the
044   second phenomenon above as we might also have good empirical performance when the training and
045   test data are close to each other. Fortunately, in the field of theoretical computer science, researchers
046   have proposed distribution closeness testing to see if the distance between a distribution pair is at
047   least ϵ-far, including two-sample testing as a specific case with ϵ = 0 [8–11]. This kind of testing
048   exactly fits the aim of seeing to what statistically significant extent two distributions are close to each
049   other. Distribution closeness testing has been used to evaluate Markov chain mixing time [12], testing
050
      language membership [13], analyzing feature combinations [14].
051   However, existing distribution closeness testing methods mainly measure closeness using total
052   variation [15–18], and primarily focus on the theoretical analysis of the sample complexity of sub-
053   linear algorithms applied to discrete one-dimensional distributions defined on a support set only
      containing finite elements (e.g., distribution defined on a positive-integer domain {1, 2, ..., n}). This

      Although it is possible to discretize complex data to a simple support set (then conducting distribution
      closeness testing using existing methods [19]), it is not easy to maintain intrinsic structures and
072
      patterns of complex data after the discretization [20, 21]. To handle complex data, in the literature,
073
      the kernel trick is also helpful to measure the closeness in higher-dimensional spaces [22]. Significant
074   efforts have been made to apply the kernel trick in hypothesis testing statistics, including Hilbert-
075   Schmidt Independence Criterion for independence testing [23], Kernel Stein Discrepancy for good-
076   of-fitness testing [24, 25], Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for two-sample testing [7].
077
      Since MMD is an effective measurement to see the distributional discrepancy [26] and is frequently
078
      used in two-sample testing tasks [27] (a special case of distribution closeness testing), there is a natural
079
      idea to introduce MMD to distribution closeness testing. MMD provides a versatile approach across
080   both discrete and continuous domains, and many approaches have extended it to various scenarios,
081   including mean embeddings with test locations [28, 29], local difference exploration [30], stochastic
082   process [31], multiple kernel [32, 33], adversarial learning [34], and domain adaptation [35]. Yet, no
083   one has explored how to extend distribution closeness testing to complex data with MMD.
084
      In this paper, however, we find it is not ideal to directly use MMD in distribution closeness testing,
085
      because the MMD is less informative when comparing the closeness levels of different distribution
086   pairs for a fixed kernel κ. Specifically, the MMD value can be the same for many pairs of distributions
087   that have different norms in the RKHS Hκ , which actually reflect different closeness levels for these
088   distribution pairs. We present an example to analyze the above issue on a Gaussian kernel, extendable
089   to other characteristic kernels of the form κ(x, x′ ) = Ψ(x − x′ ) ≤ K with a positive-definite Ψ(·)
090   and Ψ(0) = K, including Laplace [33], Mahalanobis [30] and Deep kernels [27] (frequently used in
091   kernel-based hypothesis testing). Denote by ∥µP ∥2Hκ and ∥µQ ∥2Hκ the norms of distributions P and Q,
092   respectively. We can observe that larger norms imply smaller variances Var(P, κ) = 1 − ∥µP ∥2Hκ and
093   Var(Q, κ) = 1 − ∥µQ ∥2Hκ , indicating more tightly concentrated distributions as shown in Figure 1a
094   and 1b. Nonetheless, the MMD values regarding Figure 1a and 1b are the same, showing a case
095   where MMD is less informative in comparing multiple distribution pairs with different norms.
096   Furthermore, we adapt the standard deviation and p-value of the empirical MMD estimator in two-
097   sample testing to see if pairs of distributions in Figures 1a and 1b have the same closeness. As
098   illustrated in Figures 1c, the standard deviation decreases as the norms of distributions increase,
099   which is a result of the more tightly concentrated distributions. As we know, a smaller standard
100   deviation signifies a reduced probability of the empirical MMD estimator falling outside the expected
101   range, resulting in a smaller p-value. Therefore, the standard deviation decreases as the norms of
102   distributions increase, even when the MMD value is held at a constant 0.15 as shown in Figures 1c.
103   Notably, smaller p-values indicate more significant difference and less closeness between distributions.
104
      Hence, the pairs of distributions in Figures 1a and 1b actually have different levels of closeness.
105   We mitigate the above issue by scaling MMD value with the norms of distributions, and we propose a
106   new kernel distribution closeness testing called the norm-adaptive MMD (NAMMD) test. Specifically,
107   our NAMMD distance is scaled up as the norms of distributions increase, while the MMD value is held
      at constant. Figure 1c and 1d illustrate that our NAMMD exhibits a stronger correlation with the p-

111   In the above analysis, we use a fixed global kernel for different distribution pairs, which is essential
112
      for effectively comparing their closeness levels under a unified distance measurement. Yet, existing
      kernel selection methods are primarily designed for two-sample testing [27, 36], focusing on selecting
113
      a kernel that maximizes the test power estimator to distinguish a fixed distribution pair P and Q.
114
      Despite efforts to extend these kernel selections to distribution closeness testing, deriving a test power
115   estimator with multiple distribution pairs remains an open question and poses a significant challenge.

117   When we want to test distribution closeness, we could use a reference (known) pair of distributions
118
      P1 and Q1 , with their distance serving as threshold ϵ. Here, the global kernel can be selected by
      maximizing the test power estimator of P1 and Q1 following two-sample testing methods. With the
119
      kernel, we then test whether the distance between an unknown distribution pair P2 and Q2 exceeds
120
      that between P1 and Q1 . Given this, we conduct experiments to validate the effectiveness of our
121   NAMMD test, including three case studies demonstrating its application in evaluating whether a
122   model performs similarly across training and testing datasets without ground truth labels (Section 5.2).

243   Performing Distribution Closeness Testing in Practice. We have demonstrated how to perform
244   distribution closeness testing above, yet it is still not clear how the ϵ of Definition 3 should be set in
245   practice. Normally, when we want to test the closeness, we often have a reference pair of distributions
246   P1 and Q1 that we know its true/approximate distributional discrepancy, i.e. NAMMD(P1 , Q1 , κ).
247   Then, given two samples X and Y drawn from unknown distributions P2 and Q2 respectively, we
248   seek to determine whether the distance between P2 and Q2 is as close or closer to that between P1
249   and Q1 , by applying distribution closeness testing. Here, we set ϵ = NAMMD(P1 , Q1 , κ), and this
250   can be formalized by Definition 3 with null and alternative hypotheses as follows
251                  H0 : NAMMD(P2 , Q2 , κ) ≤ ϵ and H1 : NAMMD(P2 , Q2 , κ) > ϵ .
252
253   Finally, we can perform the NAMMD test procedure with samples X and Y .
254   Relevant work. A well-known class of two-sample testing constructs kernel embeddings for each
255   distribution and then test the differences between these embeddings [44–47]. Another relevant
256   approach assesses the differences between distributions with classification performance [48–56].
257   Kernel-based MMD has been one of the most important statistic for two-sample testing, which
258   includes popular classifier-based two-sample testing approaches as a special case [27].
259   Previous distribution closeness testing approaches primarily focus on theoretical analysis of the
260   sample complexity of sub-linear algorithms, and these approaches often rely on total variation over
261   discrete one-dimensional distributions [12, 15–18]. Other measures of closeness also include ℓ2
262   distance [57–59], entropy [60], probability difference [8, 61], etc. In comparison, we turn to kernel
263   methods that have shown effectiveness in non-parametric testing.
264   Permutation tests are widely used in statistics for equality of distributions, providing a finite-sample
265   guarantee on the Type-I error whenever the samples are exchangeable under null hypothesis [62–65].
266   As shown in Lemma 9, NAMMD(P, Q, κ) = 0 if and only if P = Q, indicating that our NAMMD
267   satisfies the exchangeability under null hypothesis H0 : NAMMD(P, Q, κ) = 0. For null hypothesis
268   H0 : NAMMD(P, Q, κ) ≤ ϵ and ϵ ∈ (0, 1), the empirical estimator of our NAMMD distance, i.e.,
269   NAMMD(P, Q, κ) = ϵ, has an asymptotic Gaussian distribution as shown in Theorem 2. Hence, we
      use the (1 − α)-quantile of asymptotic distribution as the testing threshold following [30, 46, 47].

1518
       Our analysis in this paper focuses on kernels of the form κ(x, x′ ) = Ψ(x − x′ ) ≤ K with a
1519   positive-definite Ψ(·) and Ψ(0) = K, including Laplace [33], Mahalanobis [30] and Deep kernels
1520   [27] (frequently used in kernel-based hypothesis testing). For these kernels, a lager norm of mean
1521   embedding ∥µP ∥2Hκ indicates a smaller variance Var(P, κ) = K − ∥µP ∥2Hκ , which corresponds
1522   to a more tightly concentrated distribution P. Leveraging this property, we gain the insight that
1523   two distributions can be separated more effectively at the same MMD distance with larger norms.
1524   Hence, we scale MMD using 4K − ∥µP ∥2Hκ − ∥µQ ∥2Hκ , making the new NAMMD increase with the
1525   norms ∥µP ∥2Hκ and ∥µQ ∥2Hκ . Figure 1c and 1d demonstrate that our NAMMD exhibits a stronger
1526   correlation with the p-value in testing, while MMD is held constant. We also prove that scaling
1527   improves NAMMD’s effectiveness as a closeness measure in Theorems 10 and 12.
1528   However, all these improvements rely on the property that "A lager norm of mean embedding
1529   ∥µP ∥2Hκ indicates a smaller variance Var(P, κ) = K − ∥µP ∥2Hκ , which corresponds to a more
1530   tightly concentrated distribution P". The proposed method may not work well for kernels where the
1531   embedding norm of distribution may increases as the data variance increases. For these kernels, the

1706   We demonstrate that our NAMMD better captures the differences between distributions by exploiting
1707   intrinsic structures. For each dataset, we sample ten elements and randomly selecting one element
1708   to serve as the base z0 . The remaining elements are sorted as z1 , z2 , ..., z9 with ∥z0 − z1 ∥2 ≥
1709   ∥z0 − z2 ∥2 ≥ · · · ≥ ∥z0 − z9 ∥2 . For each element zi , we construct the Dirac distribution δzi with
1710   support only at element zi , and we calculate the distance NAMMD(δz0 , δzi , κ). We repeat this 10
1711   times, using a Gaussian kernel with γ = 1 for blob, higgs, and hdgm, and γ = 10 for mnist.
1712   From Figure 6, it is evident that our NAMMD(δz0 , δzi , κ) distance increases as ∥z0 − zi ∥2 decrease
1713   for all datasets. This is different from previous total variation TV(δz0 , δzi ) = 1 for i ∈ {1, 2, ..., 9},
1714   which merely measures the difference between probability mass functions of two distributions. In
1715   comparison, our NAMMD distance can effectively capture intrinsic structures and complex patterns
1716   in real-word datasets by leveraging kernel trick.
