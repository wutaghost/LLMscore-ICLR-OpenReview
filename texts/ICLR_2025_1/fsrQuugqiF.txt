009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Variational data assimilation estimates the dynamical system states by minimizing
012            a cost function that fits the numerical models with observational data. The widely
013            used method, four-dimensional variational assimilation (4D-Var), has two primary
014            challenges: (1) computationally demanding for complex nonlinear systems, and (2)
015            relying on state-observation mappings, which are often impractical. Deep learning
016
               (DL) has been used as a more expressive class of efficient model approximators to
               address these challenges. However, integrating such models into 4D-Var remains
017
               challenging due to their inherent nonlinearities and the lack of theoretical guaran-
018
               tees for consistency in assimilation results. In this paper, we propose Tensor-Var
019            to address these challenges using kernel Conditional Mean Embedding (CME).
020            Tensor-Var characterizes system dynamics and state-observation mappings as linear
021            operators in a feature space, where it enables a linear 4D-Var framework with a
022            convex cost function. Furthermore, our method provides a new perspective to incor-
023            porate CME with 4D-Var, offering theoretical guarantees of consistent assimilation
024            results between the original and feature spaces. To improve scalability, we pro-
025            pose a method to learn adaptive deep features (DFs) using neural networks within
026            the Tensor-Var framework. Experiments on chaotic systems and global weather
027
               prediction with real-time observations demonstrate that Tensor-Var outperforms
               conventional and DL hybrid 4D-Var baselines in terms of accuracy while achiev-
028
               ing efficiency comparable to the static 3D-Var method (code available at https:
029
               //anonymous.4open.science/r/Tensor-Var-F1E9/README.md).

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Forecasting of dynamical systems is an initial value problem of practical significance. Many real-
035   world systems, such as the ocean and atmosphere, are chaotic, which means minor errors of current
036   estimations in computational models can lead to rapid divergence and substantial forecasting errors.
037   In this regard, data assimilation (DA) (Law et al., 2015; Asch et al., 2016) uses observation data to
038   continuously calibrate models, improving forecast accuracy.
039   Various DA methods have been proposed to deal with different types of observation data and system
040   dynamics. Among these methods, 4D variational (4D-Var) data assimilation has been considered
041   cutting-edge and effectively used in real-world applications like numerical weather prediction (NWP)
042   systems (Browne et al., 2019; Milan et al., 2020). The 4D-Var minimizes a quadratic cost function
043   that finds the optimal match between system states and observations (Asch et al., 2016). While
044   effective in NWP, there are two critical limitations for their applications: (1) Numerical models
045
      for complex, nonlinear systems are often inefficient for real-time assimilation and forecasting. (2)
      Observations are often noisy, incomplete representations of the states, even without a known state-
046
      observation mapping, posing challenges in utilizing the observation. Efforts have integrated DL
047
      models to learn an observation (or inverse) model (Frerix et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Liang
048   et al., 2023), addressing the imperfect knowledge of observation models in 4D-Var. While these
049   approaches improve observation utilization, they remain constrained by the complexities of numerical
050   models and learned observation mappings, whereas our approach simplifies this by finding their
051   linear representations. To improve computation efficiency, the state-of-the-art DL models (Vaswani
052   et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020; Kovachki et al., 2023; Bocquet et al., 2024) are capable
053   of constructing highly nonlinear mappings to surrogate dynamical systems and achieve notable
      successes in NWP (Bi et al., 2022; Lam et al., 2022; Kurth et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Conti,


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024; Vaughan et al., 2024). However, integrating such models into optimization-based tasks, like
055   4D-Var, remains challenging due to their inherent nonlinearities (Janner et al., 2021; Bocquet, 2023).
056   Using auto-differentiation (AD) of DL models in 4D-Var can reduce computational costs and has
057   shown success in simple examples (Geer, 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024); however,
058   the accuracy of AD-derived derivatives remains a concern, and its complexity grows with system
059   dimensions (Baydin et al., 2018). Recently, Xiao et al. (2024) applied the AD of a pre-trained weather
060   forecasting model into 4D-Var, forming a self-contained DA framework for Global NWP. However,
061   this approach relies on the well-designed pre-trained model for the forward models and may not
062   generalize easily to other domains. Latent data assimilation (Peyron et al., 2021; Fablet et al., 2021;
063
      Melinc & Zaplotnik, 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Fablet et al., 2023) addresses these challenges by
      performing DA in a learned low-dimensional latent space. While efficient, these approaches lack
064
      theoretical guarantees for the consistency of 4D-Var solutions between the latent and original spaces.
065
066   In this paper, we introduce Tensor-Var, a framework for learning linear representation of the DA
067   systems by using kernel conditional mean embedding (CME). Unlike existing DL-based methods
068   face challenges with nonlinearity and non-convexity, we propose a new perspective from CME to
069
      linearize nonlinear dynamics, resulting in a convex cost function in the feature space. To best of our
      knowledge, our work is first attempt to integrate CME into 4D-Var for linear representation with
070
      convex cost function, greatly improving optimization efficiency and convergence. To address the
071
      challenges from incomplete observations, we derive an inverse observation operator that incorporates
072   histories to infer the system state, thereby improving accuracy and robustness. Moreover, we provide
073   a theoretical analysis that demonstrates the existence of a linear representation of the system under
074   kernel features and the consistency of 4D-Var solution across original and latent spaces. A key
075   challenge in extending CME to practical variational DA is scalability. To overcome this, our approach
076   learns adaptive deep features (DFs) that map data into a fixed-dimensional feature space, reducing
077   the computational complexities. Our experiments on two chaotic systems and two global NWP
078   applications demonstrate that Tensor-Var outperforms conventional and ML-hybrid variational DA
079   baselines, including operational and DL-hybrid methods, in accuracy and computational efficiency,
080
      showing the advantages of linearizing the DA systems through Tensor-Var.

259
260   In Section 3.1, we discuss how the dynamical system F in equation 1 can be embedded as a linear
261   system in the feature space. However, two important questions remain: 1) Does such linear dynamical
262   system exist? 2) Are the solutions of original and feature space 4D-Var consistent? In this section,
263   we provide affirmative answers to both questions using the theory of Kazantzis-Kravaris/Luenberger
264   (KKL) observers (Andrieu & Praly, 2006). We give a road-map of the theoretical analysis with main
265   result and refer readers to Appendix A for the details.
266   Under the mild assumptions that (1) the dynamical model F is first-order differentiable and (2)
267   the kernel features are all first-order differentiable, the kernel feature ϕS satisfies the necessary
268   conditions as the state transformation in the KKL observer framework. This transformation enables
269   us to represent the nonlinear dynamical system as a linear system in a higher-dimensional feature
      space, as established by KKL observer theory (Tran & Bernard, 2023). This result confirms the

305
306   To evaluate our proposed method, the comparison is conducted on a series of benchmark domains,
307   representing optimization problem equation 2 of increasing complexity, including (1) the Lorenz
308   96 system (Lorenz, 1996) with 40 and 80 state dimensions. (2) The Kuramoto-Sivashinsky (KS)
309   equation: a fourth order nonlinear PDE system (Papageorgiou & Smyrlis, 1991) with 128 and 256
310   dimensions, representing different spatial resolution. For both systems, we use a nonlinear observation
311
      model o = G(s) = 5arctan(sπ/10) + ϵ, where ϵ is white noise with a standard deviation of 0.01
      times the standard deviation of state variable distribution and only 20% states can be observed. To
312
      assess the practical applicability of Tensor-Var, we evaluate its performance in global medium-range
313
      weather forecasting (i.e., 3-5 days) by using a subset of the ECMWF Reanalysis v5 (ERA5) dataset
314   for training and testing, with further details in subsection 4.2 and (Rasp et al., 2024). Furthermore,
315   we incorporate observations from real-time weather satellites into the NWP experiment with higher
316   spatial-resolution, showcasing the practical utility of Tensor-Var in subsection 4.3.
317
318   Baselines. We compare our method against several baseline approaches: (1) 3D-Var with a known
319   observation model, (2) a model-based 4D-Var algorithm that assumes known dynamical and observa-
320   tion models, (3) a learned inverse observation model with the known dynamical model, as proposed
321   by Frerix et al. (2021), and (4) In the two NWP problems, we include two more baselines: Latent
322   3D-Var and Latent 4D-Var (Cheng et al., 2024), which perform variational DA in a compressed latent
323   space learned via autoencoder, with Latent 4D-Var additionally learning the dynamics in the latent
      space. These competitive baselines cover both operational Var-DA and ML-hybrid Var-DA methods.

340   systems. The better performances are attributed to the linearization of the dynamics and the history-
341   augmented inverse observation operator, which makes the 4D-Var optimization convex with more
342   reliable convergence to the global optimum in the feature space. The larger NRMSE errors observed
343   in the model-based 3D-Var and 4D-Var methods are due to that incomplete observations lead to the
344   uncontrolled errors in the unobserved state dimensions. Although the ML-hybrid 4D-Var method
345   (Frerix et al., 2021) employs a learned inverse observation model, it struggles with generalization
346   due to the incomplete observations, leading to poor performance on test data. By incorporating
347
      historical information, our approach more effectively controls estimation errors in the unobserved
      state dimensions. This improvement is clearly demonstrated in Figures 8 and 9 in Appendix C.1.2 and
348
      C.1.2, which qualitatively compares assimilation performance in the Lorenz-96 and KS systems. In
349
      terms of computational efficiency, the linearity of Tensor-Var makes it faster than all 4D-Var baselines
350   while also outperforming or being comparable to the static 3D-Var method, attributing to the reduced
351   complexity of solving convex optimization problems in the feature space.

520   5                  C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we introduced the Tensor-Var method, using kernel conditional mean embedding to
523   transform nonlinear dynamical and observation models in 4D-Var into a linear framework, making
524   the optimization both tractable and computationally efficient. By learning adaptive deep features,
525   Tensor-Var addresses the scalability typically associated with traditional kernel methods. Our
526   inverse observation operator, which incorporates historical observations, improving the accuracy and
527
      robustness with incomplete observations. Experiments on two chaotic systems and global weather
      forecasting show that Tensor-Var outperforms state-of-the-art hybrid ML-DA models in both accuracy
528
      and efficiency.
529
530
      Limitations. Our method requires access to exact or re-analyzed system states to learn the dynami-
531   cal and observation models, which may not be feasible in practical applications. A future direction
532   would be to learn these models directly from observations and calibrate the learned dynamics in the
533   feature space using Tensor-Var. In addition, our simplified error covariance matrices in 4D-Var may
534   not fully capture system correlations and uncertainties. Future work could focus on improving the
535   design of error covariance matrices within the Tensor-Var framework to enhance assimilation and
536   performance.

      A     T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
811
812
      In this section, we provide a theoretical convergence analysis of Tensor-Var, drawing on concepts from
813
      control theory and contraction analysis. We begin by introducing comparison functions, including
814   class K and KL functions, as well as Lyapunov functions. Using these tools, we examine the
815   convergence of Tensor-Var through a differential equation, demonstrating monotonic contraction
816   based on Lyapunov direct method. Furthermore, by using comparison functions, we show that
817   contraction in the feature space implies contraction in the original space.
818
      Assumption 1. To conduct a formal convergence analysis of Tensor-Var, we make a mild assumption
819
      regarding the first-order differentiability of the dynamical system F in equation 1 with time derivative
820   ṡ = f (s), a standard assumption in convergence studies (Sastry, 2013).
821
822   Assumption 2. We require that the kernel possess a well-defined first-order derivative, as the
823
      convergence analysis is performed in the feature function space. This assumption is common in
      kernel methods and is satisfied by many widely-used kernels, such as the Gaussian, Fourier, Matérn,
824
      and Laplace kernels (Berg et al., 1984; Schölkopf & Smola, 2002).

829   A continuous function α : [0, a] → [0, ∞) is said to belong to class K if it is strictly increasing and
830   α(0) = 0. It is of class K∞ if α(∞) = ∞ and α(r) → ∞ as r → ∞.

834
      A continuous function β : [0, a] × [0, ∞) → [0, ∞) is said to belong to class KL if each fixed t, the
      mapping β(r, t) belonging to the class K with respect to r and, for each fixed a the mapping β(a, t)

      is decreasing with respect to t and, β(a, t) → 0 as t → ∞.

1158

1159   representing atmospheric velocity at K evenly spaced locations and is evolved according to the
1160   governing equation:
1161                                 dSk

1164   term represents a linear damping with magnitude F . In general, the dynamics becomes more
1165   turbulent/chaotic as F increases. We choose the number of variables K = 40, 80 and the external
1166   forcing F = 10, where the system is chaotic with a Lyapunov time of approximately 0.6 time units.
1167   As an observation model for the following experiments, we randomly observe 25% states (e.g. 10
1168   in K = 40). Our models were trained on a dataset D of N = 100 trajectories, each trajectory

       C.5   A BLATION STUDY
1513
1514   In this section, we provide the details of the ablation studies of (1) the history length m, (2) the
1515   dimensions of feature dimension and comparison with standard kernel functions, and (3) the effects
1516   of the estimated error matrices. All the ablation studies are conducted on the Lorenz-96 systems with
1517   ns = 40 and ns = 80. We fixed the remaining hyperparameters consistent with the main experiments
1518   and varied only the parameters under investigation.
1519
1520   (1) Effect of the history length m. We examined the impact of the history length m on learning
1521
       the inverse observation operator and its effect on state estimation accuracy. The feature dimensions
       ds , do , and dh were held constant, while the history length was varied by adjusting the size of the
1522
       final linear layer (see Table 4). The state feature dimensions ds were set to 60 and 120 for the two
1523
       system dimensions.
1524
1525
       (2) Effect of DFs. We implemented Tensor-Var with a Gaussian kernel using kernel PCA projected
1526   to first 60 and 120 eigen-coordinates in scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) by aligning with the
1527   dimension of the used DFs. For the Gaussian kernel, we approach the pre-image problem by
1528   fitting a projection operator (Kwok & Tsang, 2004). The space S ⊂ Rns together with the linear
1529   kernel k(si , sj ) = sTi sj forms an RKHS as well. Therefore, we can simply define the projection
1530   operator as a CME operator that maps from the feature space HS to the original space S as Cˆproj =
1531   S(KS +λI)−1 ΦTS , where S = [s1 , ..., sn ]. By applying Cˆproj now to the state, we can obtain the mean
1532
       estimation of the kernel mean embedding in the S such that ŝ = Cˆproj µPS = ES [Cˆproj ϕS (S)] = ES [S].
1533
       We normalized the dataset to a standard Gaussian distribution and select the length scale γ = 1.0 by
1534   performing a cross-validation on the γ = [0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.25, 1.5, 2].
