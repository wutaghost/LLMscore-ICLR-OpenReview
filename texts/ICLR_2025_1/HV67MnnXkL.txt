009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Human mobility is crucial for urban planning (e.g., public transportation) and
012            epidemic response strategies. However, existing research often neglects integrating
013            comprehensive perspectives on spatial dynamics, temporal trends, and other con-
014            textual views due to the limitations of existing mobility datasets. To bridge this
015            gap, we introduce MOBINS (MOBIlity Networked time Series), a novel dataset
016
               collection designed for networked time-series forecasting of dynamic human move-
               ments. MOBINS features diverse and explainable datasets that capture various
017
               mobility patterns across different transportation modes in four cities and two coun-
018
               tries and cover both transportation and epidemic domains at the administrative area
019            level. Our experiments with nine baseline methods reveal the significant impact of
020            different model backbones on the proposed six datasets.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Diverse and explainable human mobility datasets are crucial for advancing urban planning, affecting
026   public transportation demand (Han et al., 2022), crowd congestion (Singh et al., 2020), traffic
027
      management (Liu et al., 2024), and infection prediction (Panagopoulos et al., 2021). Previous research
      focused on forecasting traffic and crowd congestion in specific areas using various transportation
028
      modes, such as subway systems (TianChi, 2019), ride-hailing services (Fivethirtyeight, 2015), and
029
      taxis (TLC, 2009). Additionally, there have been several attempts to predict COVID-19 infection by
030   analyzing human mobility across different regions (Katragadda et al., 2022).
031
032   However, the datasets used in prior studies often fail to capture the diverse nature of human mobility
033
      from multiple perspectives. To comprehensively represent diverse mobility patterns, it is imperative
      to observe the movements of a large number of individuals over an extended period, taking into
034
      account various transportation modes. Unfortunately, many studies attempt to estimate demand using
035
      data either in a single transportation mode or in a short time frame (TianChi, 2019; Panagopoulos
036   et al., 2021). Some efforts to understand human mobility rely on sparse movement data collected
037   from a limited number of individuals. Despite the importance of understanding human mobility’s
038   impact on various aspects, such as transportation and epidemics, there is a lack of research that
039   integrates additional information beyond transportation to enhance the diversity of mobility datasets.
040
      Subway datasets (TianChi, 2019), a networked mobility dataset consisting of stations with high human
041
      traffic volumes, meet many of the specified criteria. Nevertheless, the subway datasets themselves do
042   not offer multiple perspectives—i.e., diversity. Although there have been several studies to broaden
043   a single data perspective (Shi et al., 2020), they only integrate mobility data from a single source
044   with other contextual information that shares the same static topology. It is insufficient, for example,
045   to simply add weather information as an additional variable to the time series. Instead, it is critical
046   to use mobility-effected information at specific points of interest (PoIs) to create synergy between
047   dynamic movements and networked time series. This approach not only enhances performance but
048   also aids in understanding social phenomena that are difficult to discern from a single data source.
049   To improve the diversity of human mobility datasets, it is essential to collect data from different
050   transportation modes across diverse regions over an extended period, capturing numerous daily move-
051   ments. Moreover, incorporating additional contextual information, such as disease outbreaks, can aid
052   in capturing various contextual patterns associated with spatio-temporal information. Meanwhile, for
053   explainability purposes, the instances in the dataset should be organized on a network based on the
      spatial connectivity of each explainable area unit, such as an administrative area.

087
      Towards diverse and explainable human mobility datasets, we propose MOBINS, MOBIlity
088
      Networked time-Series forecasting benchmark. MOBINS offers a unique combination of origin-
089
      destination movements, a spatial network, and multiple time series, as illustrated in Figure 1. It
090   involves multiple transportation modes including buses, subways, express buses, and taxis, providing
091   a rich representation of human mobility patterns. With observations spanning at least two years and
092   numerous daily movements, MOBINS enables the development and evaluation of advanced forecast-
093   ing models. To ensure broad applicability, we include the benchmark datasets for transportation and
094   infection prediction across four cities and two countries. By representing the networked mobility
095   datasets at the administrative area level and treating each node as a distinct entity, MOBINS helps
096   the model interpretation of the underlying mobility patterns.
097   Our dataset collection contains not only network-based interactions between nodes and edges but also
098   temporal dynamics from time-varying features. Also, all datasets have a spatial network, where nodes
099   represent locations such as stations, districts, and cities and edges represent connectivity between
100   nodes based on subway lines, roads, and geographical adjacency. In Figure 2 visualizing part of a
101   dataset in MOBINS, a spatial network created based on road network information is given as static
102   data, and dynamic human mobility is represented through dynamic edge movements. In this case, the
103   positive correlation between human movements and time-varying infection cases is captured. This
104
      kind of insight is difficult to uncover from a straightforward collection of multiple datasets, because
      their regions, spatial and temporal resolutions, and collection intervals may not be aligned. Therefore,
105
      this new opportunity clearly demonstrates the innovation and significance of MOBINS.
106
107   Our sophisticated and diverse dataset collection is publicly available together with forecasting
      methods. A substantial amount of time and effort has been dedicated to gathering comprehensive

      2.1   F ORECASTING WITH T RANSPORTATION T IME -S ERIES DATA
125
126   Human mobility prediction aims to predict each location’s various attributes such as speed, demand,
127   and congestion. In the context of traffic forecasting, studies employ traffic speed sensor datasets (Liu
128   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2017) collected from PeMS (Performance Measurement System). Similarly,
129   studies on demand or congestion prediction use modified inflow and outflow datasets derived from
130   various transportation modes, such as subway (TianChi, 2019) or taxi (TLC, 2009) datasets. Unlike
131   conventional time-series forecasting, mobility time-series forecasting emphasizes both temporal and
132   spatial modules. Spatial axes are represented using N × N grids based on given coordinates, while
133
      an adjacency graph captures spatial connectivity derived from PoIs or a correlation generated from
      the sensor proximity (Jiang et al., 2021). Alternatively, station-based spatial connectivity is employed
134
      to model the patterns of movements within a given graph (Ou et al., 2020).
135
136
137   2.2   F ORECASTING WITH O RIGIN -D ESTINATION DATA
138
      Origin-destination (OD) forecasting focuses on predicting the number of movements between the
139
      regions, capturing the interaction patterns within a mobility network. Datasets from ride-hailing
140   services (Fivethirtyeight, 2015), taxi (TLC, 2009), and subway (TianChi, 2019) provide valuable
141   information for deriving origins and destinations. OD movements between candidate origins and
142   destinations, such as grids, stations, and PoIs, are forecasted using spatial and temporal modules (Han
143   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019; Rong et al., 2023). Meanwhile, several studies have attempted to
144   enhance time-series forecasting performance by incorporating OD movements. For example, research
145   on COVID-19 prediction in England (Panagopoulos et al., 2021) and USA (Wang et al., 2023) has
146   used the interaction between nodes, represented by the number of COVID-19 cases, and human
147   mobility between regions. These studies leverage the relationship between inter-regional movement
148
      and the spread of infections to predict the number of cases in each region (Katragadda et al., 2022).

      3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITIONS
153
154   Mobility is represented along both spatial and temporal dimensions. The spatial component is
155   structured through a graph, denoted as G = (V, E). The node set V = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vN } captures
156   locational data, while the edge set E illustrates the connectivity between these nodes. Each node
157   temporally aggregates node time-series features Xt , encompassing metrics such as transportation
158   in/out-flow, ridership, infection rates, and additional time-sensitive data, where Xt ∈ RN ×d , d is the
159   number of feature variables, and t is the index of the time. In scenarios where the graph G remains
160   static, its spatial network A ∈ RN ×N is defined through a fixed adjacency matrix. Conversely, in
161   dynamic settings, G evolves with OD movements Mt ∈ RN ×N , where Mtij accurately measures the
      volume of movements from node vi to node vj at each time point t.

      3.2     L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING M OBILITY DATASETS
199
200
201
      Existing mobility datasets, as used in human mobility forecasting, are compared with the characteris-
      tics of our MOBINS in Table 1. We categorize existing human mobility datasets into three types. In
202
      the first type, the Hangzhou Subway dataset (TianChi, 2019) offers deep analysis through individual
203
      unit data but is limited by its specific region and short collection period, sharing the limitation also
204   observed in datasets like the NYC Uber dataset (Fivethirtyeight, 2015). This dataset’s collection
205   from a single source makes it challenging to capture the diverse nature of human mobility. In the
206   second type, LargeST (Liu et al., 2024) provides extensive data over a long collection period but
207   lacks detailed human mobility information, such as OD movements. This limitation is also present
208   in other PeMS-based datasets, such as the MERA-LA and PEMS-BAY datasets (Li et al., 2017). In
209   the third type, Panagopoulos et al. (2021) shared human mobility datasets that link movements with
210   other factors. However, its short collection period makes it challenging to observe long-term trends,
211   and the absence of a spatial network reduces its utility for spatial analysis.
212   For urban planning purposes (e.g., public transportation) and epidemic response strategies, human
213   mobility datasets should provide multiple views of spatial and temporal dimensions, as well as exhibit
214   qualities such as diversity and explainability. However, many of the datasets currently available do
215   not meet these criteria. In Table 2, we highlight the specific shortcomings of existing human mobility
      datasets, emphasizing their deficiencies in capturing essential qualities.

233   3.2.1                 D IVERSITY
234
235
      To accurately represent human mobility, datasets should encompass a wide array of contexts. Human
      movement can occur through various modes of transportation, such as subways, city buses, long-
236
      distance buses, high-speed trains, taxis, personal vehicles, and ride-hailing services. A dataset
237
      that covers only a single mode of transportation, like the subway dataset (TianChi, 2019), fails to
238   provide a comprehensive view of mobility. Datasets incorporating various modes are essential for
239   depicting the diverse nature of human mobility. From a spatial perspective, the mobility datasets
240   should encompass various regions to capture the different spatial and contextual patterns, such as
241   commercial, residential, tourist, and mixed-use area patterns, emerging from diverse administrative
242   areas. For instance, COVID datasets (Panagopoulos et al., 2021) cover four EU countries, and
243   LargeST (Liu et al., 2024) includes datasets from across California, including Los Angeles, the
244   Bay Area, and San Diego. From a temporal perspective, datasets should also include long periods
245   to offer insights into both short-term and long-term mobility patterns. It is critical that datasets
246
      extend beyond simple metrics such as ‘time of day’ or ‘day of the week’ to include annual data,
      facilitating a richer temporal context. However, except for LargeST (Liu et al., 2024), many datasets
247
      cover periods of less than one year, with some training models over periods even shorter than one
248
      month (TianChi, 2019; Li et al., 2017). Moreover, mobility datasets must be collected with many daily
249   movements. Unfortunately, several datasets are employed with only an insufficient number of daily
250   movements (Wang et al., 2023), which fail to capture representative human mobility. Understanding
251   human movements is not only about comprehending the movements themselves but also about linking
252   information strongly correlated with these movements to get insights into social phenomena, which
253   allows for the exploration of many aspects of human mobility. Therefore, bi-modality is helpful to
254   comprehend human movements and their strongly correlated phenomena. For example, the COVID
255   datasets consist of two types of data: OD movements from human mobility between regions based on
256   mobile device data, and node time-series features from the number of infected individuals.
257
258   3.2.2                 E XPLAINABILITY
259
      Decision-makers in urban planning require models with high explainability, which necessitates
260
      datasets with inherent explainability. Training models using grid or sensor identifiers (Li et al., 2017;
261
      Liu et al., 2024) is insufficient. Explainable units for locational information, e.g., administrative
262   areas, are vital. In the spatial dimension of mobility, each dataset should realistically represent spatial
263   connectivity. For instance, the subway dataset (TianChi, 2019) records connectivity at the station level.
264   Administrative areas can create a spatial network based on actual spatial adjacency and connectivity,
265   indirectly helping to understand how the impact of an event spreads out.

      4.1               DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
282
283
      Transportation datasets: The MOBINS dataset collection comprises transportation data from three
284   South Korea cities (Seoul, Busan, and Daegu) and one U.S. city (New York City). The Transportation-
285   [Seoul, Busan, Daegu] datasets include node time-series features from subway inflow/outflow data
286   and OD movements from smart card usage across various public transportation modes. These datasets
287   use subway maps to represent spatial connectivity, leveraging the commonalities between node
288   time-series features and OD movements. However, pre-processing is required to align the data to a
289   consistent spatial and hourly resolution, as node time-series features are generated for each station
290   and OD movements are based on administrative areas. Figure 3 illustrates that stations within the
291   same administrative area are consolidated into a single node in the spatial network, resulting in nodes
292
      represented by station-based administrative areas. The Transportation-NYC dataset includes OD
      movements from the NYC yellow and green taxi datasets (TLC, 2009) and node time-series features
293
      from NYC subway, tram, and railway ridership data. The spatial network is built at the borough level
294
      to alleviate sparsity from the huge number of nodes. Consequently, NYC taxi records from 263 zones
295   and NYC ridership data from 428 stations are represented at a consistent resolution.
296
297   Epidemic datasets: The MOBINS dataset collection includes epidemic datasets that consist of node
298
      time-series features obtained from COVID-19 infection count and OD movements obtained from
      a smart card or taxi trip records in South Korea or New York City (NYC). The “Epidemic” section
299
      in Figure 3 illustrates the composition of the Epidemic-Korea dataset based on the spatial networks
300
      characterized by an adjacency matrix with diagonal ones representing the connectivity between
301   cities and provinces. The OD movements from buses, urban rails, railways, and long-distance buses
302   are used to represent inter-city or inter-provincial movements. However, islands are excluded due
303   to their distinct transportation modes. Each node represents a city or a province, with COVID-19
304   infection cases recorded at each administrative area. Similarly, for the Epidemic-NYC dataset, node
305   time-series features are based on daily infection cases from the five boroughs, while OD movements
306   are comprehensively integrated from the NYC yellow and green taxi datasets (TLC, 2009).

      5.2   BASELINES
385
386
      In our evaluation with MOBINS, we consider a broad range of traditional and modern forecasting
387
      models as baselines. We choose well-acknowledged prediction models as our benchmark, including
388   (i) Linear-based models: DLinear, NLinear (Zeng et al., 2023); (ii) RNN-based model: SegRNN (Lin
389   et al., 2023); (iii) Transformer-based models: Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), Reformer (Kitaev et al.,
390   2020), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022); (iv) CNN-based model: TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022); (v) GNN-
391   based models: STGCN (Yu et al., 2018), MPNNLSTM (Panagopoulos et al., 2021).
392
      Linear models take a linear approach to forecasting, treating time-series data as linear signals.
393
      DLinear and NLinear are known for their simplicity and efficiency, focusing on capturing linear
394   trends and patterns. The RNN-based model, SegRNN, employs a recurrent neural network to capture
395   temporal dependencies in the data. RNNs are well-suited for sequential data and are commonly used
396   for time-series forecasting. SegRNN uses a segmentation-based technique to enhance the ability
397   to capture long-range dependencies. Transformer-based models use self-attention mechanisms to
398   capture long-range dependencies in time-series data. Reformer is an efficient variant of Transformer
399   models that replaces dot-product attention with locality-sensitive hashing, reducing complexity and
400   employing reversible residual layers to store activations only once during training. PatchTST extends
401   the Transformer model to time-series data by breaking down the data into smaller patches. This
402
      approach allows the model to focus on localized patterns while leveraging the power of self-attention
      to understand broader trends. The CNN-based model, TimesNet, uses convolutional layers to capture
403
      temporal patterns in the data, allowing it to efficiently process time series with high-dimensional
404
      features. This model can identify localized patterns effectively, making it suitable for various time-
405   series forecasting tasks. The GNN-based models use diverse graphs to deal with spatial information or
406   integrate other contextual information for time-series forecasting. STGCN uses a static graph derived
407   from inter-node proximity, while MPNNLSTM utilizes a dynamic graph based on OD movements.

466   5.4       S UMMARY OF F INDINGS
467
      Overall, the results indicate that no single forecasting model outperforms all others across all datasets.
468
      Instead, the choice of the best model depends on the specific characteristics of the dataset and the
469   underlying data patterns. Linear-based models are effective in simpler scenarios, Transformer-based
470   approaches excel in contexts with long-range dependencies, CNN-based methods work well with
471   spatio-temporal data, and GNN-based models are ideal for datasets with complex networks.
472
473    • While linear models such as DLinear and NLinear perform well in simpler scenarios, they struggle
474      with more complex data patterns and non-linear relationships. These models are limited in their
475      ability to capture intricate temporal dependencies and are not suitable for datasets with highly
476      dynamic or irregular patterns. However, in our datasets, they are simple but powerful baselines.
477
       • RNN-based models, such as SegRNN, face challenges in handling long-range dependencies and
         complex temporal patterns. As the sequence length increases, RNNs suffer from vanishing or
478
         exploding gradients (Pascanu et al., 2013), limiting their effectiveness in capturing long-term
479
         dependencies. Therefore, SegRNN performs badly on our transportation datasets.
480    • While Transformer-based models like PatchTST demonstrate promising results in handling long-
481      range dependencies, they struggle with capturing local patterns and short-term dynamics. The
482      self-attention mechanism in Transformers can be computationally intensive, especially for longer
483      sequences (Wang et al., 2020), which can limit their scalability. Moreover, Transformers often
484      require large amounts of training data to achieve optimal performance.
485    • GNN-based models are designed to handle complex network relationships but require careful
         design and fine-tuning to achieve optimal performance. The performance of GNN-based models


                                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
          heavily depends on the quality and representation of the graph structure, which can be challenging
487       to construct for some datasets. Moreover, GNNs can be computationally expensive, especially for
488       large-scale networks (Ding et al., 2022) and face scalability issues.
489
490   These findings provide a valuable reference for researchers and practitioners when selecting appropri-
491   ate forecasting models for their specific applications. The comprehensive evaluation across diverse
492   datasets and model architectures reinforces the importance of experimentation and context-driven
493   decision-making in the field of mobility networked time-series forecasting. However, the limitations
494
      of existing forecasting models highlight the need for innovative approaches that can effectively
      address the challenges posed by complex and diverse datasets. That is, a novel approach is anticipated
495
      to outperform DLinear and TimesNet for this challenging problem.

498   6    F UTURE W ORK AND L IMITATIONS
499
500   The complexity of mobility patterns requires diverse and comprehensive analysis for mobility
501   networked time-series forecasting. Therefore, every component of mobility datasets captures spatio-
502   temporal variability across multiple transportation modes and organizes the datasets into a bi-modal
503
      form, facilitating a comprehensive understanding of mobility trends over time. Additionally, the
      structure of the datasets with explainable units under a spatial network increases explainability, aiding
504
      decision-makers in interpreting mobility trends and implications for urban planning (Li et al., 2012;
505
      Hoang et al., 2016) and epidemic control (Ni & Weng, 2009; Katragadda et al., 2022) and these
506   insights can significantly impact policy-making and economic decisions.
507
508   While MOBINS dataset collection serves as a forecasting benchmark, the presence of distribution
509
      shifts due to the changes in the Epidemic-[Korea, NYC] datasets suggests that they can be utilized
      for time-series online learning, adapting models in real-time. Additionally, the benchmark can be
510
      extended for research on imputation, clustering of traveling behaviors, and hierarchical time-series
511
      forecasting. Despite the advantages of our datasets, there are a few constraints, such as the fact that
512   MOBINS is limited to only two domains and its period of dataset collection is mostly only two to
513   three years, which is not enough to support annual patterns.
