011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Inverse problems, which involve estimating parameters from incomplete or noisy
014
               observations, arise in various fields such as medical imaging, geophysics, and
               signal processing. These problems are often ill-posed, requiring regularization
015
               techniques to stabilize the solution. In this work, we employ Stochastic Interpola-
016
               tion (SI), a generative framework that integrates both deterministic and stochastic
017            processes to map a simple reference distribution, such as a Gaussian, to the target
018            distribution. Our method DAWN-SI: Data-AWare and Noise-informed Stochastic
019            Interpolation incorporates data and noise embedding, allowing the model to ac-
020            cess representations about the measured data explicitly and also account for noise
021            in the observations, making it particularly robust in scenarios where data is noisy
022            or incomplete. By learning a time-dependent velocity field, SI not only provides
023            accurate solutions but also enables uncertainty quantification by generating multi-
024            ple plausible outcomes. Unlike pre-trained diffusion models, which may struggle
025
               in highly ill-posed settings, our approach is trained specifically for each inverse
               problem and adapts to varying noise levels. We validate the effectiveness and ro-
026
               bustness of our method through extensive numerical experiments on tasks such as
027
               image deblurring and tomography.

050   Inverse problems are a class of problems in which the goal is to determine parameters (or parameter
051   function) of a system from observed data. These problems arise in various fields, including medical
052   imaging, geophysics, remote sensing, and signal processing. Inverse problems are often ill-posed,
053   meaning that a unique solution does not exist, or the solution may be highly sensitive to small per-
      turbations in the data. Solving inverse problems typically requires regularization techniques, which


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      introduce additional constraints or prior information to stabilize the solution and mitigate the effects
055   of ill-posedness. Such regularization can be obtained using various mathematical and computational
056   methods, including optimization techniques (variational methods), statistical inference (Bayesian or
057   frequentist) and machine learning (Scherzer et al., 2009; Adler and Öktem, 2017; Calvetti and Som-
058   ersalo, 2024; López-Tapia et al., 2021). In this paper, we approach inverse problems using the latter
059   and investigate a new set of methods that are machine learning based for the solution of inverse
060   problems. In particular, we show how stochastic interpolation (SI) which was recently proposed in
061   the context of generative models can be effectively used to estimate the solution of inverse problems
062   and to further investigate its non-uniqueness.
063   Stochastic Interpolation is a relatively new generative process that provides a unifying framework,
064   elegantly integrating both deterministic flows and stochastic diffusion models (Albergo et al., 2023).
065   The core concept of SI is to learn a stochastic process that effectively transports a simple reference
066   distribution, such as Gaussian, to the desired target data distribution. This transportation process
067   can manifest as either deterministic or stochastic. In the former case, it is described by an ordinary
068   differential equation (ODE), while in the latter, it is governed by a stochastic differential equation
069
      (SDE).
070   The stochastic interpolation framework defines a continuous-time reversibility between the reference
071   and target distributions, parameterized by time t ∈ [0, 1]. At the initial time t = 0, the distribution
072   aligns with the reference distribution. As time progresses to t = 1, the distribution evolves to match
073   the target data distribution. This evolution is achieved by learning the time-dependent velocity field
074   (for ODEs) or drift and diffusion coefficients (for SDEs) that characterize this interpolation process.
075
      By understanding and modeling this time-dependent transformation, one can generate samples from
      the target distribution through numerical integration of the learned ODE or SDE.
076
077   SI is a highly flexible methodology for designing new types of generative models. In this work,
078   we use this flexibility and show how to adopt SI to solve highly ill-posed inverse problems. We
079   find SI particularly useful since it allows ease of sampling from the target distribution. This implies
080   that we are able to generate a range of solutions to the inverse problems and thus investigate the
081
      uncertainty that is associated with the estimated solution. Such a process is highly important in
      physical applications when decisions are made based on the solution.
082
083
084
      Related work: The methods proposed here are closely related to three different approaches for
      the solution of inverse problems. First, there is an obvious link to the incorporation of diffusion
085
      models as regularizers in inverse problems (Yang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022a;c;b; Song et al.,
086
      2022). The key idea is to leverage a pre-trained diffusion model that captures the data distribution
087   as a prior, and then condition this model on the given measurements to infer the underlying clean
088   signal or image. For inverse problems, diffusion methods condition the diffusion model on the given
089   measurements (e.g. noisy, incomplete, or compressed data) by incorporating them into the denoising
090   process. Nonetheless, it has been shown in Eliasof et al. (2024) that pre-trained diffusion models
091   that are used for ill-posed inverse problems as regularizers tend to under-perform as compared to the
092   models that are trained specifically on a particular inverse problem. In particular, such models tend
093   to break when the noise level is not very low.
094   A second branch of techniques that are related to the work proposed here use encoder-decoder type
095   networks for the solution of inverse problems (see Chung et al. (2024); Thomas et al. (2022) and
096   reference within). Such approaches are sometimes referred to as likelihood-free estimators as they
097   yield a solution without the computation of the likelihood. This technique is particularly useful for
098   problems where the forward problem is difficult to compute.
099   A third branch of techniques that relates to our approach uses the forward problem within the neural
100   network (see Eliasof et al. (2024); Mukherjee et al. (2021); Eliasof et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2017)
101   and reference within). In this approach, one computes the data misfit (and its gradient) within the
102   network and use it to guide training. Our approach utilizes components from this methodology to
103   deal with the measured data.
104
105   Main Contribution: The core contributions of this paper lie in the design and application of the
106   DAWN-SI framework, specifically crafted for solving highly ill-posed inverse problems like image
107   deblurring or tomography. (i) Our method is designed to be problem-specific, which adjusts itself
      to the unique structure of the inverse problem it is tasked with, learning the posterior distribution

      directly, ensuring that the learned velocity fields and mappings are directly applicable to the target
109   task. (ii) We train a stochastic interpolant by embedding measured data and noise information di-
110   rectly into the interpolation process. This incorporation allows our model to adapt to a wide range of
111   noise conditions, something that pre-trained models struggle with. By making the training explicitly
112   aware of the noise level and data characteristics, our model can better navigate noisy or incom-

163
164   Consider the case where we have observations on x1 of the form
165
                                                  Ax1 + ε = b                                            (6)
166
167   Here, A is a linear forward mapping (although the method can work for nonlinear mappings as well)
168   and ε ∼ N (0, σ 2 I) is a random vector. We assume that A is rank-deficient or numerically rank-
169
      deficient (Hansen, 1997), so the effective dimension of b is smaller than x1 and one cannot obtain a
      reasonable estimate for x1 given the noisy data b without the incorporation of a-priori information.
170
171   Using Bayes’ theorem, we have
172
                                           π(x1 |b) ∝ π(b|x1 )π(x1 )                                     (7)
173
174   Bayes’ theorem suggests that it is possible to factor the posterior distribution π(x1 |b) using the
175
      known distribution of π(b|x1 ) and the prior distribution π(x1 ). This observation motivated a num-
      ber of studies that used the estimated pre-trained distribution π(x1 ) in the process of solving an
176
      inverse problem (Yang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022a;c;b; Song et al., 2022). Nonetheless, it has
177
      been shown in Eliasof et al. (2024) that such estimators tend to produce unsatisfactory results when
178   solving highly ill-posed problems or when the data is very noisy. The reason for this behaviour
179   stems from the fact that pre-trained estimators push the solution towards the center of the prior dis-
180   tribution, irrespective of what the data represents. To see this, we use a careful dissection of the
181   solution. Consider the singular value decomposition
182                                                  X

191   trained models, the prior π(x1 ) is estimated numerically and it is unaware of the inverse problem

194   values, that is, to the effective null space of the data, this may lead to the artifacts that have been
195   observed early in Kaipio and Somersalo (2004); Tenorio et al. (2011). This suggests that although

201   2.3   A DATA - AWARE AND NOISE - INFORMED VELOCITY ESTIMATOR
202
      In the canonical form of stochastic interpolation, the velocity is estimated from the interpolated
203
      vector xt . In the context of inverse problems, we also have a vector b (measured data) that contains
204   additional information on x1 and therefore can be used to estimate the velocity towards the posterior.
205   We now show that by doing a small change to the training process of SI, it is possible to solve inverse
206   problems using the same concept.
207
      To this end, notice that we train a velocity function sθ (xt , t) that takes in two arguments. In the
208
      context of a specific inverse problem, we have additional information for training the network, the
209
      measured data b and the noise level σ in b. Note that it is also possible to estimate the noise level
210   directly from the data as proposed in Tenorio et al. (2011). The data b can be used to point toward
211   x1 even at time t = 0, where xt contains no information on x1 and can therefore improve the
212   estimation of velocity. Moreover, the information about noise level σ in the measured data during
213   training also makes the estimator more robust to noise during inference. We thus propose to use the
214   data and noise when estimating sθ . To this end, we use a transformation f of data b (to be discussed
215   next), and let

232   not most, inverse problems, the data b belongs to a different space than x. Therefore, it is difficult

      2.5   A RCHITECTURES FOR DATA - AWARE AND NOISE - INFORMED VELOCITY ESTIMATORS
325
326   Our goal is to train a velocity estimator sθ (xt , b, t, σ) and then compare it to the velocity v. Our
327   estimator is based on a UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) with particular embeddings for b, t, and σ.
328
      The embedding of time and noise is straight-forward. Note that both t and σ are scalars. For their
329
      embedding, we use the method presented in Croitoru et al. (2023), which involves creating learnable
330   embedding and adding them to the feature maps at each level of the network.
331
332   A key component in making SI perform well for inverse problems is the careful design of a network
333
      that incorporates the data within the training process. The resulting network can be thought of as a
      likelihood-free estimator (Thomas et al., 2022), that estimates the velocity vector v, given the input
334
      vector xt , the time t and the noise level σ. As previously explained, we do not integrate b directly,
335
      but rather use f (b) = A⊤ b and embed this vector in the network.
336
337   The embedding of A⊤ b is performed using a data encoder network. To this end, let
338                                                   Eη (A⊤ b)                                          (15)
339   be an encoder network that is parameterized by η. The encoder can be pre-trained or trained as part
340   of the network, providing flexibility in how it is integrated into the UNet. The encoded data provides
341   additional context that is critical for accurate predictions. Formally, the UNet estimates the velocity
342   as follows:
343                                          b = sθ (xt , Eη (A⊤ b), t, σ)
                                             v                                                           (16)
344   By embedding both time, data and noise vectors at each layer, the network can leverage additional
345   information, leading to more accurate and robust predictions, especially for large noise levels.

      Training methodology. For our experiments, we considered two types of data-aware velocity esti-

531
      In this paper, we presented DAWN-SI, a framework for addressing highly ill-posed inverse prob-
532
      lems by efficiently incorporating data and noise into the SI process. Our experiments showed that
533   our proposed method consistently outperformed existing methods in tasks like image deblurring,
534   with significant improvements in key performance metrics. The ability to sample from the learned
535   posterior enables the exploration of the solution space and facilitates in uncertainty quantification,

538   noise modeling techniques for extreme noise conditions.

      A.1    I MAGE DEBLURRING
651
652   In this section, we provide technical details about the image deblurring inverse problem used in this
653   work. For this inverse problem, the forward operator A (a blurring operator) can be represented as a
654   convolution of the input image I(x, y) with a Gaussian Point Spread Function (PSF) kernel, where
655   the kernel is characterized by a standard deviation. The blurring process can be mathematically
656   described as a convolution between a sharp image I and a (Gaussian) blurring kernel K, where Iblur
657   is the resultant blurred image,
658                      Iblur (x, y) = AI(x, y) + ϵ(x, y) = I(x, y) ∗ K(x, y) + ϵ(x, y),                    (20)
659
660   where Iblur (x, y) is the blurred image at pixel location (x, y), I(x, y) is the original sharp image,
661   K(x, y) is the PSF, and ϵ(x, y) is the additive noise, which is also assumed to be Gaussian with zero
662
      mean and a standard deviation equal to p% of the range of I(x, y) ∗ K(x, y), where p was chosen
      uniformly between 0% and 20% during training. The Gaussian PSF kernel is given by,
663
                                                                   x2      y2

664                                                 1
665                                  K(x, y) =            exp − 2 − 2                                   (21)
                                                2πσx σy            σx      σy
666
667   All our experiments were run with σx = σy = 3. The goal of image deblurring is to recover the
668   original sharp image I(x, y) from the blurred image Iblur (x, y). This is typically ill-posed due to the
669   possible presence of noise η(x, y) and the loss of high-frequency information caused by the blur.
670   The convolution operation can be described as a multiplication in the Fourier domain:
671
672                                         B(u, v) = I(u, v) · K(u, v)                                      (22)
673
674   where I(u, v) = F{I(x, y)}, K(u, v) = F{K(x, y)} are the Fourier transforms of the sharp image,
675
      and the PSF, respectively and F represents the Fourier transform. The blurred image can then be
      obtained using the inverse Fourier transform,
676
677                                         Iblur (x, y) = F −1 {B(u, v)}                                    (23)
678
679
      Moreover, A⊤ , the adjoint of operator A, which is used to compute the data-embedding A⊤ b for
      the network, for some data b is easy to obtain. Since the image blurring operator is symmetric and
680
      self-adjoint, we have A = A⊤ .

684
      In tomography, the goal is to reconstruct the internal image of an object from a series of projections
685   (sinograms) taken at various angles. This process can be described as solving an inverse problem,
686   where the projections are obtained from the original image via the Radon transform A and inverse
687   Radon transform A⊤ is used to reconstruct the original image from the sinogram data.
688
      The forward problem of tomography can be formulated as projecting a 2D image onto a set of
689
      1D sinograms for different angles of view. The mathematical model of the forward projection is
690
      expressed as:
691
692
693                                                S = AIpad + ϵ                                             (24)
694
695   where, S ∈ RNangles ×Ndetectors is the sinogram (the set of projections), Ipad ∈ RNpixels is the padded and
696   flattened image of dimension Npixels , A ∈ RNangles ×Ndetectors ×Npixels is the tomography projection matrix
697   which describes the interaction of each ray with every pixel, and ϵ is the additive noise term to the
698
      data, which was chosen as p% of the range of values in AIpad , with p lying between 0% and 20%
      during training. For our experiments, we set Nangles = 360, and Ndetectors = 2s + 1, where s × s is
699
      the dimension of the original unpadded images.
700
701   The image is first padded to account for the fact that projections are taken beyond the boundaries of
      the object in the image. Using a zero padding of size s/2 on all four sides of the image, the padded

703   the padded image generating the sinogram data.
704

706
      be expressed as,
707                                                    Ipad = A⊤ S                                          (25)
