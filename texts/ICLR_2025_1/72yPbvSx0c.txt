008                                                  A BSTRACT
009
010            An efficient way to control systems with unknown nonlinear dynamics is to find
011            an appropriate embedding or representation for simplified approximation (e.g. lin-
012            earization), which facilitates system identification and control synthesis. Neverthe-
013            less, there has been a lack of embedding methods that can guarantee (i) embedding
014
               the dynamical system comprehensively, including the vector fields (ODE form)
               of the dynamics, and (ii) preserving the consistency of control effect between the
015
               original and latent space. To address these challenges, we propose Koopman Em-
016
               bedded Equivariant Control (KEEC) to learn an embedding of the states and vector
017            fields such that a Koopman operator is approximated as the latent dynamics. Due to
018            the Koopman operator’s linearity, learning the latent vector fields of the dynamics
019            becomes simply solving linear equations. Thus in KEEC, the analytical form of the
020            greedy control policy, which is dependent on the learned differential information of
021            the dynamics and value function, is also simplified. Meanwhile, KEEC preserves
022            the effectiveness of the control policy in the latent space by preserving the metric in
023            two spaces. Our algorithm achieves superior performances in the experiments con-
024            ducted on various control domains, including the image-based Pendulum, Lorenz-
025
               63 and the wave equation. Code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
               science/r/Koopman-Embed-Equivariant-Control-70D1.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Many real-world system dynamics are unknown and highly nonlinear, which limits the applications
032   of classical control methods. Although model-based control methods have been widely studied to
033   learn the dynamics from the data, e.g., in Chua et al. (2018); Deisenroth et al. (2009); Müller et al.
034   (2012); Nagabandi et al. (2018); Williams et al. (2017), the learned dynamics can still be highly
035   non-linear or black-box, making it still analytically intractable and computationally inefficient. One
036
      effective class of methods addressing this issue is to find a proper representation that embeds the
      dynamical system into a latent space (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018), in which the system evolution is
037
      simple (e.g. locally linear) (Banijamali et al., 2018; Levine et al., 2019; Mauroy & Goncalves, 2016;
038
      Mauroy et al., 2020; Watter et al., 2015; Weissenbacher et al., 2022; Williams et al., 2015), such that
039   various control methods such as iterative Linear Quadratic Programming can be used.
040
041   However, current embedding methods have primarily focused on next-step predictions through local
042
      linearization approaches (Bruder et al., 2019a; Kaiser et al., 2019; Bruder et al., 2019b; Li et al.,
      2019). The learned dynamics neglect the vector fields or metrics, which lead to the learned latent
043
      dynamics and the optimal control policy derived based on it inconsistent with the original ones.
044
      These methods lack a formal and theoretical guarantee that the system is comprehensively embedded
045   into the latent space, including the vector fields and flows 1 . Thus, the effect of control may not
046   be preserved when the control policy inversely mapped back to the original space. The sufficient
047   conditions for preserving the control effects are the equivariance of the dynamics (Maslovskaya,
048   2018) and the metric preservation of the latent space (Jean et al., 2017). In this paper, we aim to find
049   an isometric and equivariant mapping such that the flows and vector fields of the original nonlinear
050   dynamics are comprehensively mapped to a latent controllable system, thus preserving the control
051   effect in both the original and latent spaces.

070   Related works in this field fall into two main streams: embedding for control and symmetry in control.
071   Embedding to control algorithms aims to map complex, high-dimensional, nonlinear dynamics into a
072   latent space with local linearization, often using Variational Autoencoder (VAE) structures (Watter
073   et al., 2015; Kaiser et al., 2019; Nair et al., 2018). These algorithms ensure a bijection between
074   the original and latent spaces (Huang, 2022; Levine et al., 2019), however, they do not embed the
075
      necessary differential and metric information with equivariance, causing the control effect to be
      inconsistent in both spaces. Conversely, symmetry in control theory has been crucial in identifying
076
      invariant properties of systems (Field, 2007), leading to effective control policies. Research has
077
      underscored the value of symmetric representations in learning dynamics for tasks with evident
078   (e.g., rotation and translation invariance) symmetries (Adams & Orbanz, 2023; Bloem-Reddy &
079   Teh, 2020; Bronstein et al., 2017), such as work in robotics control tasks under special orthogonal
080   group (SO(2)) action by Wang et al. (2021). However, these methods may struggle in scenarios with
081   unknown dynamics and less evident symmetries. Recent advancements include methods designed
082   to learn implicit symmetries, such as meta-sequential prediction for image prediction with implicit
083   disentanglement frameworks (Miyato et al., 2022; Koyama et al., 2023). These approaches achieve
084   disentanglement as a by-product of training symmetric dynamical systems by Fourier representation.
085   Such properties are important in maintaining consistency in control policies across different spaces.
086   In this paper, we propose Koopman Embedded Equivariant Control (KEEC) to learn an equivariant
087   embedding of dynamical system based on Koopman operator theory. Our contributions are that
088   unlike existing works embedding methods and Koopman methods (Chua et al., 2018; Deisenroth
089   et al., 2009; Bruder et al., 2019a;b; Li et al., 2019; Mauroy et al., 2020; Weissenbacher et al., 2022)
090   to merely embed the states, we embed the vector fields that require the derivatives of the states
091   as well to the latent space. We formally propose that equivariance and isometry are two properties
092
      to preserve the control effects in latent space. By embedding with the two properties, our method
      maintains a better consistency between original dynamics and the latent linear dynamics. In addition,
093
      in the latent space, the dynamics simplify to a linear function of the state given the action, which
094
      improves computational efficiency of learning dynamics. Based on the control-affine assumption and
095   Hamiltonian-Jacobi theory, we manage to derive a greedy control policy dependent on the learned
096   differential information of the dynamics and value function analytically. Our numerical experimental
097   results demonstrate KEEC’s superiority over existing methods in controlling unknown nonlinear
098   dynamics across various tasks, such as Gym (Towers et al., 2023)/image Pendulum, Lorenz-63, and
099   the wave equation achieving higher control rewards, shorter trajectories and improved computational
100   efficiency. Figure 1 takes pendulum control as an example to demonstrate the control framework
101   of the KEEC and shows the learned vector field on the latent space.

      2.2   E MBEDDING FOR C ONTROL
130
      Solving control problems in an unknown and nonlinear system in Equation 1 is challenging. Thus, we
131   aim to learn an embedding g that transform the system in the original state space M to a latent space
132   N , in which the control problem can be easily solved. For example, mapping the original system
133   to a latent one where the dynamics is linear evolving. It thus motivates our study by answering the
134   question: What properties should the embedding g satisfy?
135
      An embedding g for control problems first should preserve the system dynamics, i.e. mapping the
136
      state dynamics from M to N . This can be satisfied if the embedding is equivariant.
137
138   Equivariance. A map g : M → N is equivariant function if F latent ◦ g = g ◦ F (Hall & Hall,
139   2013), where F latent is the flow in latent space, equivariant with respect to flow F under the map g.
140
141
      Equivariant map ensures that F latent ◦g(st ) = g ◦ F (st ), i.e., the transitions of states remain consistent
      between the original and latent spaces. In addition, preserving the metric on both spaces is essential
142
      for maintaining the consistency of reward/cost functions and control effects across both spaces.
143
144
      Isometry. Let M and N be metric spaces with metrics dM and dN . A map g : M → N is an
145
      isometry if for any s1 , s2 ∈ M , we have dM (s1 , s2 ) = dN (g(s1 ), g(s2 )) (Field, 2007).
146
147   Isometry can guarantee the reward/cost functions on the original space M and latent space N . This
148   is because most reward/cost function is highly dependent on the distance between current state to
149
      the optimal state, such as the reward design in MuJoCo (Todorov et al., 2012). Furthermore, the
      distance-preserving property of the trajectory ensures that the latent and original trajectories are
150
      consistent with one another.
151
152   An equivariant and isometric map g can comprehensively map the original system to a latent system
153   and guarantee the consistent control performances. While, solving the nonlinear control problem
154   with unknown dynamics remains challenging. To simplify the problem, we aim for a latent system
155
      that has simple dynamics on the latent space N . Works have been done by linearizing the dynamics
      (Watter et al., 2015; Kaiser et al., 2019). However, they didn’t comprehensively map the flows and
156
      vector fields original dynamics to the latent space.

173   3.1   KOOPMAN O PERATOR AS L ATENT DYNAMICS
174
      We utilize the Koopman operator to simplify the dynamics of a nonlinear system. We aim to embed
175   the original state space M into a latent space N with function g : M → N . Denote the space of
176   continuously differentiable functions as C 1 (M ). In an uncontrolled dynamical system, the Koopman
177   Operator K∆t : C 1 (M ) → C 1 (M ) is an infinite-dimensional linear operator that governs the
178   evolution of observables (functions in C 1 (M )) over a time period ∆t (Das, 2023). Mathematically,
179   for any function g ∈ C 1 (M ), the Koopman operator satisfies: K∆t g = g ◦ F∆t .
180
      The inclusion of the control action can be seen as extending the Koopman framework to a system
181   where the evolution of functions depends on both the state and the action. The goal is to define an
182   operator that tracks how functions evolve under the influence of action. In this paper, considering the
183   system evolving as: st+∆t = F∆t (st , at ), given action at , the Koopman operator then describes how
184   the function g(st ) evolves when an action at is applied:
185                                                             at
                                         g(F∆t (st , at )) = (K∆t   g)(st ).                              (4)
186
      Equation 4 demonstrates the equivariance between two dynamics K and F . K and F essentially
187
      describe the same state transition but in different spaces. Formally, Kat (zt ) = zt+∆t , where
188
      zt := g(st ) ∈ N and zt+∆t := g(st+∆t ) ∈ N are the latent states. Refer to Appendix D for more
189   details about the equivariance.
190
191   In the rest of the paper, we omit the superscript action a given the common setting of control. K
192
      pushes the latent state z forward in time along the controlled dynamics. In this way, the map g is an
      equivariant function and evolves globally in a linear manner due to the Koopman operator, even if the
193
      original dynamics is nonlinear. However, merely approximating the Koopman operator K and map g
194
      does not fully capture the vector fields induced by the ODE of the time-derivative dynamics.

      3.3   L EARNING E QUIVARIANT E MBEDDING
246
      To comprehensively embed the original space, two properties need to be satisfied, i.e., equivariance
247   and isometry. Equivariance guarantees that the learned flows and vector fields are consistent under
248   embedding. Isometry makes the metric consistent and preserves the control effect in both spaces.
249   KEEC leverages the auto-encoder structure to learn the equivariant and isometric embedding. And to
250   learn the latent dynamics and its vector field, instead of learning the Koopman operator K itself, we
251   learn the vector field parameters derived from K: P and U.
252

255   representations of nonlinear dynamics. Moreover, these feature functions typically struggle with high-

522   5     C ONCLUSIONS
523
524   This paper introduces KEEC, a novel representation learning algorithm for unknown nonlinear
525   dynamics control. By integrating principles from Lie theory and Koopman theory, KEEC constructs
526   equivariant flows and vector fields. Because of the inherent equivariance and consistent metric, KEEC
527   preserves the control effect across the original and latent space. Inspired by the Hamiltonian-Jacobi
528
      theory, KEEC utilizes the learned differential information to derive an analytical control policy, which
      improves computational efficiency and control robustness. We demonstrate these superiors in the
529
      numerical experiments, in which KEEC outperforms a wide range of competitive baselines.
530
531   Limitations. Our method relies on embedding the vector fields of the unknown dynamics to derive
532   an analytical control policy to improve the control stability and avoid intensive numerical control
533   optimization. Since the vector fields are characterized locally, we require the time step ∆t to be
534
      sufficiently small. Our approach may struggle with environments with a large time step ∆t (i.e.,
      low observation frequency). An ablation study on how the magnitude of time step ∆t influences
535
      the control performance is required. In addition, our experiments on the image-based pendulum
536
      also demonstrated KEEC’s effectiveness with image observations and potentially other types of
537   observations. However, for handling different types of observations, the design of the auto-encoder
538   neural network is crucial in our approach. A generic auto-encoder design could degrade our method’s
539   performance in identifying and controlling dynamical systems.

1393   information. Compared to the conventional RL methods, such as soft actor-critic RL, the policy π is
1394   updated incrementally, and it is impossible to discover a piece of second-order information to guide

       H.2   T RAINING DETAILS AND BASELINES .
1783
1784   At a high level, KEEC and other baselines are implemented in Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019). Both
1785   training and evaluations are conducted on a Mac studio with a 24-core Apple M2 Ultra CPU and
1786   64-core Metal Performance Shaders (MPS) GPU. The evaluation is conducted on the CPU.
1787
       All models were trained using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015), with the decaying learning
1788   rate initially set to 0.001. For KEEC, 100 training epochs are used for system identification with a
1789   batch size of 128, and 50 training epochs are used for learning the value function with a batch size
1790   of 256. The latent space dimensions are set at 8, 8, 16, and 64 for the pendulum (gym, image) and
1791   Lorenz-63, wave equation tasks, respectively. We set the lose weight λmet = 0.3. Both offline models
1792   (CQL, MPPI, and PCC) were trained for 100 epochs with a batch size of 256, while the online SAC
1793   was updated every 10 steps with the same batch size and a 100k replay-memory buffer for a total of
1794   250k gradient iterations. We use the following Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019) implementations for the
1795   baselines. For all the baselines, we keep the default model and hyper-parameter settings, adapting
1796
       only the state-action dimensions for each task. Additionally, the hidden dimensions of the models are
       set to be the same as the latent space dimension of KEEC.
