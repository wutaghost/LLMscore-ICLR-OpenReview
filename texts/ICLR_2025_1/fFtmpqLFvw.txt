009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent large language model (LLM) defenses have greatly improved models’ abil-
012            ity to refuse harmful queries, even when adversarially attacked. However, LLM
013            defenses are primarily evaluated against automated adversarial attacks in a single
014            turn of conversation, an insufficient threat model for real-world malicious use. We
015            demonstrate that multi-turn human jailbreaks uncover significant vulnerabilities,
016            exceeding 70% attack success rate (ASR) on HarmBench against defenses that
017            report single-digit ASRs with automated single-turn attacks. Human jailbreaks
018
               also reveal vulnerabilities in machine unlearning defenses, successfully recovering
               dual-use biosecurity knowledge from unlearned models. We compile these results
019
               into Multi-Turn Human Jailbreaks (MHJ), a dataset of 2,912 prompts across 537
020
               multi-turn jailbreaks. We publicly release MHJ alongside a compendium of jail-
021            break tactics developed across dozens of commercial red teaming engagements,
022            supporting research towards stronger LLM defenses.
023
               Content Warning: This paper contains examples of harmful and offensive language.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   While large language models (LLMs) are typically trained to refuse harmful queries (Ouyang et al.,
029   2022; Bai et al., 2022b; Rafailov et al., 2023), they are vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Zou
030   et al., 2023b) which allow malicious users to bypass LLMs’ refusal training. Recently proposed
031   LLM defenses have significantly improved robustness, reaching nearly 0% attack success rate
032
      (ASR) (Zou et al., 2024; Sheshadri et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024) on robustness
      benchmarks (Chao et al., 2024; Mazeika et al., 2024) which employ a suite of existing automated
033
      attacks (Chao et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023; Shin et al., 2020b;
034
      Yong et al., 2023; Shin et al., 2020a) (Section 2).
035
036   However, defenses are primarily evaluated against single turn adversarial attacks, which jailbreak
037   LLMs within one turn of conversation. This is an insufficient threat model for malicious use in
038
      deployment, where typical user behavior involves querying LLMs over multiple turns (Russinovich
      et al., 2024) (Section 3.1).
039
040   Our primary finding is that existing LLM defenses fail to generalize to this more realistic multi-
041   turn setting. With few automated attacks targeting the multi-turn threat model, we explore this
042   hypothesis by commissioning expert human red teamers with access to a multi-turn LLM chat
043   interface – mirroring the user experience on model interfaces such as ChatGPT. We successfully
044
      bypass existing safeguard mechanisms and elicit harmful responses with multi-turn conversations,
      exposing a significant oversight in current threat models and robustness evaluations.
045
046   To develop these jailbreaks, we organize a jailbreak pipeline of up to three independent human
047   attackers who interact with models through a chat interface, engaging in multi-turn conversations to
048   elicit harmful responses within a fixed time (Section 3.2). To reduce false positives, we also include
049   up to two human reviewers and a language model classifier to evaluate each human jailbreak.
050   After applying this jailbreak pipeline, we compare human jailbreaks with six automated attacks
051   against four LLM defenses (Section 4.1). The ASR of multi-turn human jailbreaks is markedly higher
052   than automated attacks across all defenses. It achieves between 19% and 65% higher ASR than an
053   ensemble of all automated attacks on HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024), a diverse dataset of harmful
      behaviors (Section 4.2). Furthermore, we demonstrate that human jailbreaking can be effective

      2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Adversarial attacks have been a longstanding avenue of machine learning research (Goodfellow et al.,
111   2015). Early work identified that small, targeted perturbations on image classifier inputs can yield
112   large differences in model outputs, despite being nearly imperceptible to the human eye (Ilyas et al.,
113   2019; Athalye et al., 2018; Madry et al., 2019; Kurakin et al., 2017).
114
115   Attacks Against LLMs. More recently, adversarial attacks have been applied to LLMs Jia and
116   Liang (2017). However, LLM attacks were historically not as effective as their image attack counter-
117   parts due to the challenge of optimization over discrete tokens. Nevertheless, a large suite of attack
118   categories, including transformations (Yong et al., 2023; Andriushchenko and Flammarion, 2024;
119   Jiang et al., 2024a; Glukhov et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b), gradient-based optimization (Wallace et al.,
120   2019; Shin et al., 2020b; Zou et al., 2023b; Sitawarin et al., 2024; Mangaokar et al., 2024; Geisler
121
      et al., 2024; Thompson and Sklar, 2024; Schwinn and Geisler, 2024), and even using LLMs as prompt
      engineers (mimicking human red teaming) (Perez et al., 2022; Chao et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2023;
122
      Yu et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Russinovich et al., 2024; Anil et al., 2024), have
123
      recently achieved success in breaking LLM defenses. These automated attacks vary in the type of
124   model access required. Simple transformation attacks may not require access to the underlying model
125   internals, while gradient-based optimization requires access to model weights (Casper et al., 2024).
126   Some automated attacks even allow adversaries to edit model internals through fine-tuning, represen-
127   tation engineering, or continuous soft tokens (Schwinn and Geisler, 2024; Arditi and bilalchughtai,
128   2024; Zou et al., 2023a; 2024; Casper, 2024; Turner et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024; Panickssery
129   et al., 2024; Halawi et al., 2024; Lermen et al., 2024). Most relevant to our work is Russinovich et al.
130   (2024), which proposes a multi-turn automated attack without model internal access – exactly the
131   threat model we use.
132
133   Defenses for LLMs. In response to the growing threat of adversarial attacks, LLM developers
134   have developed defenses to improve model robustness. Beyond training models to decline malicious
135
      queries (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b; Rafailov et al., 2023), defenders may apply adversarial
      training (Goodfellow et al., 2015) to directly improve robustness against adversarial attacks (Zhou
136
      et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Mazeika et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2023). Other methods operate at the
137
      representation level by altering model internal activations in some manner to reduce the likelihood
138   that the model even “thinks” about the undesired behavior (Zou et al., 2023a; Xhonneux et al., 2024;
139   Sheshadri et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024; Tamirisa et al., 2024)). Machine unlearning Cao and Yang
140   (2015); Bourtoule et al. (2021) is another defense, aiming to directly remove only hazardous technical
141   knowledge from LLMs without damaging their beneficial capabilities (Li et al., 2024a; Sheshadri
142   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Tamirisa et al., 2024; Rosati et al., 2024). To ensure the robustness
143   of unlearning, applying adversarial attacks assures that the knowledge is fully unlearned, not just
144   obfuscated (Lynch et al., 2024; Schwinn and Geisler, 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Tamirisa et al., 2024).
145
146   Human Red Teaming for LLMs. Human red teaming is a core strategy to identify vulnerabilities
147   with minimal model access, particularly in frontier language models (Bai et al., 2022a; Ganguli et al.,
148
      2022; Shen et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Team et al., 2024; Dubey
      et al., 2024; Weidinger et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024b; House, 2023). To characterize and develop
149
      more effective jailbreaks, taxonomies for human red teaming tactics have also been developed (Jiang
150
      et al., 2024b; Inie et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024; Weidinger et al., 2024).
151
152   Human red teaming is rarely used at a large scale outside of industry due to its prohibitively high cost.
153   As such, researchers frequently employ automated robustness benchmarks (Mazeika et al., 2024;
154
      Chao et al., 2024; Carlini et al., 2023), which apply suites of automated attacks to probe defenses for
      vulnerabilities. We demonstrate a gap between the performance of automated attacks and human red
155
      teaming, highlighting the importance of establishing baselines to further develop automated attacks
156
      and robust defenses.

      model (Section 3.1) before examining the pipeline used to conduct human red teaming (Section 3.2).

      3.1       T HREAT M ODEL
163
164   To examine the practical implications of LLM red teaming, we employ a threat model that approx-
165   imates a likely avenue of malicious use in the real world. In our setup, red teamers interact with
166   black-box chat interface models1 , where red teamers cannot access model internals or output proba-
167   bilities. This is more restrictive than API access models, where users may access output probabilities
168   or edit prior assistant responses in multi-turn conversations. We also prohibit prefilling the assistant
169   response with a specified beginning (Andriushchenko et al., 2024) and assume the model provider
170
      uses a private assistant token (used to indicate the end of the user query and the beginning of the
      assistant response) that is not accessible by the attacker.
171
172   Like most black-box chat interface models, we allow red teamers to converse with the model over
173   multiple turns (Russinovich et al., 2024). This is a more realistic model of malicious use and expands
174   the risk surface covered by most prior attacks, which focus on jailbreaks within a single turn. We
175   also permit red teamers to toggle between three temperature values (0, 0.5, and 1), similar to chat
176
      interfaces such as CYGNET.
177
178   3.2       H UMAN R ED T EAMING P IPELINE
179
180   We employ a multi-stage pipeline to jailbreak each behavior, leveraging diverse attempts from
181
      different red teamers to increase the likelihood of success. Each red teamer is independently given at
      most 30 minutes in their attempt without prior information from other red teamers. Our pipeline also
182
      provides multiple layers of verification to reduce false positives: the jailbreak is verified as harmful
183
      by the red teamer who produced the jailbreak, another human reviewer, and finally a GPT-4o harm
184   classifier.
185
186            1. Attempt Jailbreak This stage allows for up to two independent human trials to breach the
187               model.
188
                     • Red Teamer 1 attempts to jailbreak the behavior within 30 minutes and rates their attack
189
                       as “failed,” “partially successful,” or “fully successful.” “Partially successful” ratings
190
                       are applied when a harmful response answers some, but not all, parts of the behavior.
191
                     • If the attempt is only partially successful, Red Teamer 2 independently reattempts the
192
                       task from scratch and does not use attempt information from Red Teamer 1.
193
194            2. Validate Jailbreak This stage focuses on validating the attempts labeled as successful in the
195               previous stage in order to minimize false positives. If a potential false positive is identified,
196
                  an additional independent human attack is conducted, then further validated.
197                  • Reviewer 1 verifies whether “fully successful” jailbreaks from either Red Teamers
198                    answer the harmful behavior in alignment with the HarmBench classifier prompt2 .
199                  • If Reviewer 1 rejects the jailbreak in the prior step, Red Teamer 3 reattempts the task
200                    from scratch again without prior information from Red Teamers 1 & 2.
201                  • Reviewer 2 reviews only a “fully successful“ attempt made by Red Teamer 3 and
202                    approves or discards it, using the same criteria as Reviewer 1. Discarded jailbreaks
203                    from Reviewer 2 are not passed to another red teamer after this stage.
204                  • A final GPT-4o filter is used to classify the approved jailbreaks from Reviewer 1 and
205                    2 to further increase jailbreak precision. We elaborate more on this design choice
206                    in Appendix A.3.

230   4.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
231
232   Benchmarks. We evaluate attacks and defenses on HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024), an eval-
233   uation framework for automated LLM red teaming. We employ the 240 harmful behaviors from
234
      HarmBench’s standard and contextual test set, excluding copyright questions to maintain consistency
      with prior work (Zou et al., 2024).
235
236   Furthermore, we examine the efficacy of attacks against machine unlearning methods, which aims
237   to removes hazardous knowledge from LLMs while retaining beneficial capabilities. We employ
238   the WMDP-Bio multiple-choice benchmark (Li et al., 2024a) of dual-use biosecurity knowledge,
239   sampling 43 questions and converting them into free response questions. We apply unlearning
240
      techniques and then test if adversarial attacks can recover this knowledge from WMDP-Bio.
241
242   Defenses. For HarmBench, we evaluate four defenses, including mechanisms such as representation
243
      engineering (CYGNET (Zou et al., 2024) and RR (Zou et al., 2024)), latent adversarial training
      (LAT (Sheshadri et al., 2024)), and output-level supervision (DERTA (Yuan et al., 2024)). These
244
      defenses are open-source with the exception of CYGNET, which we attacked through the Gray
245
      Swan AI API3 . We only examine attack-agnostic defenses, excluding defenses optimized against
246   a particular attack (Mazeika et al., 2024) to prevent overfitting. We do not apply any additional
247   guardrails, such as perplexity filters or guard models.
248
249
      All defenses are applied upon llama-3-8b-instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), us-
      ing a a simple system prompt used for all of our commercial red teaming projects:
250
      You are a helpful AI Assistant!
251
252
      Automated Attacks. We test six LLM attacks. Four are black-box attacks which only employ
253
      model outputs: AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2023), GPTFuzzer (Yu et al., 2023), PAIR (Chao et al., 2023),
254
      and Zero-Shot (Perez et al., 2022). Two are white-box attacks which assume weight, activation, and
255   gradient access: AutoPrompt (Shin et al., 2020b) and GCG (Zou et al., 2023b). We employ all six
256   attacks against all defenses across both HarmBench and WMDP, with the default hyperparameters
257   provided by HarmBench v1.0.
258
      White-box attacks exceed our threat model, which assumes a black-box chat interface (Section 3.1).
259
      However, we still report their attack performance because when the defenses are deployed in crit-
260
      ical applications, robustness should examined under threats that are stronger than those faced in
261   deployment (Casper, 2024; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Casper et al., 2024). We exclude attacks that
262   modify model internals, including soft prompting, representation engineering, or fine-tuning. Our
263   threat model is closely related to the Crescendo attack (Russinovich et al., 2024), but we omit it from
264   experimentation as code implementation has not been released at the time of our evaluation.
265
      Besides ASRs for individual automated attacks, we report an ensembled ASR for every defense
266
      (Figure 1 and Figure 3). For every behavior, the ensemble counts a jailbreak as successful if any of
267   the six automated attacks achieves a successful jailbreak, representing an upper bound on automated
268   attack ASR.

346   Automated attack ASR is not necessarily a proxy for robustness against human jailbreaks.
347
      While CYGNET is more robust than RR against automated attacks, our red teamers had more success
      in jailbreaking CYGNET. Furthermore, some HarmBench semantic categories, such as harassment or
348
      bullying, are more difficult for automated attacks than human attacks (Appendix A.1). Our results
349
      caution against using the ASR distribution of automated attacks as a proxy for the distribution of
350   human attacks.
351
352   Automated attacks are insufficient for assuring unlearning robustness. On the unlearned RMU
353   model, human red teaming significantly outperforms other attacks, achieving a 39.6% higher ASR
354   on WMDP-Bio than the best single automated attack and 25.6% higher than the ensemble of all
355   6 automated attacks. This establishes current automated attacks are insufficient for assuring the
356   robustness of RMU.
357
358   Average human jailbreak time is inconsistent with ASR. We report the average time for a
359   successful attack on each defense with HarmBench behaviour – RR: 13.9 minutes, DERTA: 12.6
360   minutes, LAT: 17.3 minutes, CYGNET: 16.5 minutes. Together with Figure 1, we observe lower
361   ASR does not necessarily indicate the average time taken for a successful attack is lower or higher.
362
363   Recovering unlearned knowledge in biosecurity is challenging. Although red teamers were
364
      granted more time in the WMDP-Bio experiment (with an average successful submission time of
      20.5 minutes), the ASR of human red teaming is still lower than all HarmBench submissions. We
365
      hypothesize that adversaries may require domain-specific experience to develop effective attacks
366
      to recover highly technical knowledge. We leave the exploration of developing domain-specific
367   adversaries to future work.

401
      After completing the human red teaming pipeline (Section 3.2) for all defenses, we compile all
402   successful submissions into the Multi-Turn Human Jailbreaks (MHJ) dataset. We incorporate
403   metadata alongside each submission, including the red teamer’s primary tactic, the amount of time
404   taken to complete the submission, and the model temperature used (0, 0.5, or 1). Red teamers also
405   include a submission message for each jailbreak, imparting design choices, tactics considerations,
406   and other ideas they tried.
407
      With the support of legal counsel, we also remove sensitive and export-controlled information from
408   MHJ (Section 7). This includes removing many behaviors related to biosecurity, cybersecurity, and
409   chemical security, and redacting all model completions while retaining the system and user messages.
410
411
      We publicly release MHJ, supporting future research towards stronger defenses and robustness
      evaluations. MHJ can serve to fine-tune or evaluate LLMs as prompt engineers for automated
412
      multi-turn red teaming, and can inspire the design of automated attacks that mimic successful human
413
      tactics. In conjunction with MHJ, we also publish a survey of red teamer commentary on each defense
414   (Appendix C), describing the strengths, weaknesses, and tactics they found most useful.

417   5     D ISCUSSION
418
419
      Potential Pitfalls of Existing Defenses. Existing defenses may not have considered multi-turn
420   adversaries and incorporated this into their defense, such as through a post-training objective. For
421   instance, some defenses explicitly conduct adversarial training against single-turn attacks (Mazeika
422   et al., 2024). Similarly, refusal training is frequently conducted on short, single-turn conversations (Ji
423   et al., 2024), causing some recent works to characterize refusal training as “shallow” and “only a few
424   tokens deep” (Qi et al., 2024). Yuan et al. (2024) ameliorates this issue, conducting refusal training
425   further along completions, but we demonstrate that it is still not robust to multi-turn human jailbreaks.
426   Overall, expanding robustness evaluations from single-turn to longer multi-turn conversations is
427   necessary to assure the robustness of defenses against malicious use.
428   Furthermore, as frontier models are increasingly integrated with software tools such as browsers
429   and terminals (Fang et al., 2024; Lewis et al., 2021), they will consume more tokens that are not in
430   natural language, including code and symbols. The safety of LLMs in these even longer-context,
431   multi-turn, and out-of-distribution applications is a crucial research direction that warrants additional
      attention (Pan et al., 2024).

432
      Limitations and Interpreting Results. There are several distinctions between the human and
433   automated attack pipeline, warranting caution when interpreting ASRs and comparing across attacks
434   and defenses.
435
436
      In the human jailbreak pipeline, we set a 30 minute cutoff per red teamer. However, malicious actors
      can use more time or deploy more people, especially as they generally extract information for a few
437
      harmful behaviors – not 240 diverse behaviors spanning all of HarmBench. We do not punish the
438
      number of queries or tokens in the given time limit; future defenses may explore the use of rate limits.
439   Moreover, we employ a fluid set of human red teamers. While we train all red teamers in the same
440   manner and maintain at least 6 red teamers per defense, the skill and experience of individual red
441   teamers may vary. Furthermore, we employ different threat models for human and automated attacks
442   – while some automated attacks require access to model internals and all employ a single turn, human
443   red teamers cannot access model internals but enjoy multiple turns and some diversity in temperature.
444   Future work could explore automated attacks that incorporate multiple turns or varied temperature.
445   In evaluating robustness, human red teaming is orders of magnitude more costly than current
446   automated attacks, which is possibly the reason why most prior defenses only employ automated
447   attacks. Therefore, any cost-controlled robustness analysis may yield different results. In particular,
448   automated attacks are generally optimized for a certain number of steps or bounded by compute,
449   which is a different limitation than bounding humans by red teaming time. Additionally, we employ
450   a slightly different evaluation scheme for human and automated attacks and justify these design
451   decisions in Appendix A.3. Lastly, we evaluate all attacks and defenses against the HarmBench
452   standard and contextual sets, which some defenses were not explicitly evaluated on. However,
453
      HarmBench has diverse coverage across many categories of harm, making it a good benchmark to
      evaluate robustness against general harmful queries. To further standardize comparison, we also only
454
      include attack-agnostic defenses, excluding those that adversarially train against a particular attack.
455
456   Ultimately, these results should not be viewed as a one-to-one comparison with automated attacks or
457   a declaration that human red teaming is superior. Rather, we demonstrate the vulnerability of current
458   LLM defenses to multi-turn human jailbreaks and show the need for more likelife threat models and
459
      stronger automated adversarial attacks to effectively evaluate robustness.
460
461
462   6    C ONCLUSION
463
464   We release Multi-Turn Human Jailbreaks (MHJ), a dataset of 2,912 prompts across 537 multi-turn
465   jailbreak conversations, towards improving the robustness of LLM defenses. We expand the threat
466   model of LLM red teaming to include multi-turn jailbreaks – a lifelike assumption for malicious
467   use, but one rarely employed by existing robustness evaluations. Defenses from prior work, which
468   demonstrate remarkable robustness against single-turn automated adversarial attacks, are not robust
469   against multi-turn human jailbreaks. To support research in LLM robustness, we publicly release
470
      MHJ in addition to jailbreak tactics collected throughout dozens of commercial LLM red teaming
      engagements.

893   chat interface, the circuit breaker automatically ends the conversation once triggered. However,

896   allow red teamers to conduct jailbreaking without regard for whether it occurs after a circuit break,
897   but redo any submissions to identify any behaviors that can only be jailbroken following the circuit
898   break, but not before. Our plots only report the ASR before a short circuit to be consistent with the
899   chat interface threat model. For completeness, the ASR before circuit breaking is 70.4% (169/240),

904   harmful and randomly selected 100 responses that GPT-4o deemed benign, and examine how this
905   differs from the human red teamers’ labels. We find that GPT-4o has very strong recall for harmful
906   responses compared against human labels (0.0% false negative rate) but weaker precision (Figure 6).
907   The recall motivates our use of GPT-4o as a first-pass filter to reduce the number of automated attack
