009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Finding the optimal learning rate for language model pretraining is a challenging
012            task. This is not only because there is a complicated correlation between learning
013            rate, batch size, number of training tokens, model size, and other hyperparameters
014            but also because it is prohibitively expensive to perform a hyperparameter search
015            for large language models with Billions or Trillions of parameters. Recent studies
016            propose using small proxy models and small corpus to perform hyperparameter
017            searches and transposing the optimal parameters to large models and large corpus.
018
               While the zero-shot transferability is theoretically and empirically proven for model
               size related hyperparameters, like depth and width, the zero-shot transfer from small
019
               corpus to large corpus is underexplored. In this paper, we study the correlation
020
               between optimal learning rate, batch size, and number of training tokens for the
021            recently proposed WSD scheduler. After thousands of small experiments, we found
022            a power-law relationship between variables and demonstrated its transferability
023            across model sizes. Based on the observation, we propose a new learning rate
024            scheduler, Power scheduler, that is agnostic about the number of training tokens
025            and batch size. The experiment shows that combining the Power scheduler with
026            Maximum Update Parameterization (µP) can consistently achieve impressive
027            performance with one set of hyperparameters regardless of the number of training
028            tokens, batch size, model size, and even model architecture. Our 3B dense and
029
               MoE models trained with the Power scheduler achieve comparable performance as
               state-of-the-art small language models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Learning rate is a critical hyperparameter for deep neural network training. In the context of Large
035
      Language Models (LLMs), the cosine learning rate scheduler is the most commonly used strategy. It
036   has been shown to be effective across multiple state-of-the-art models, including Llama 3 (Dubey et al.,
037   2024a), Gopher (Rae et al., 2021), etc. However, the cosine scheduler requires pre-defined training
038   step counts to achieve the optimal loss. This results in two main drawbacks: 1) the intermediate
039   training checkpoints are suboptimal, 2) continual training of an existing language model becomes
040   complicated.
041
      MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024) proposes the Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) learning rate scheduler
042   (illustrated in Figure 1) to address these issues. The WSD learning rate schedule is divided into
043   three phases: 1) warmup phase, linearly increase the learning rate from 0 to peak; 2) stable phase,
044   maintain the learning rate at peak value and training the model for most of the time; 3) decay phase,
045   annealing the learning rate to 0 in a relatively short period. The main advantage of this schedule is
046   that specifying the number of training steps in advance is not required. This is particularly convenient
047   for large runs, as the decay can be applied at any time to observe model performance and decide
048   whether to stop. It also allows for continual learning by default, as training can be resumed from
049   a stable phase checkpoint. Moreover, the data mixture can be changed during the decay phase to
050
      increase the ratio of high-quality data. This data curriculum is shown to be effective in several recent
      language models (Team et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024a; Shen et al., 2024).
051
052   However, is WSD really agnostic to token count? In our experiments, we found that although the
053   WSD scheduler could, in theory, continue in the stable phase forever, the optimal learning rates are
      different for different amounts of training tokens. In other words, the optimal learning rate scheduler

069   cosine scheduler: the intermediate and continual training checkpoints are suboptimal if the number

071
      Furthermore, deciding the optimal learning rate is still challenging for large-scaling pretraining. As
072
      our experiments will show, there is a complicated correlation between hyperparameters, including
073
      learning rate, model size, batch size, and number of training steps. The size and training cost
074   of modern LLMs make it impossible to do a hyperparameter search on the target model size and
075   training flops. Researchers have proposed using small proxy models to run hyperparameter searches
076   and predict the optimal hyperparameters of large models from search results (Dey et al., 2023;
077   Hu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022). Among these methods, µTransfer (Yang et al., 2022; 2023) is
078   proposed to facilitate zero-shot hyperparameter transfer between different model sizes. µTransfer has
079   been successfully applied to several language models, including Cerebras-GPT (Dey et al., 2023),
080   miniCPM (Hu et al., 2024), and AFM (Gunter et al., 2024).
081   In this paper, we first combine the WSD scheduler and µTransfer to study the learning rate transfer
082   between proxy and large models. Our extensive experiments show that µTransfer does not provide
083   direct zero-shot learning rate transferability across the numbers of tokens and batch sizes for the
084   WSD optimizer. Instead, the optimal learning rate ηopt satisfies a power-law relation with respect to
085   batch size β and number of tokens T :
086                                              ηopt = β · aT b                                        (1)
087
      where a and b are power-low coefficients. Furthermore, our experiment confirms the zero-shot
088
      transferability across model sizes, different model sizes share very similar coefficients. Inspired
089   by this observation, we propose a new learning rate scheduler, PowerLR, that is agnostic to batch
090   size and token number. It allows direct transfer of the optimal learning rate scheduling across batch
091   size, token numbers, and model size. Thus, the expensive pretraining runs can be trained without
092   specifying the number of training tokens, enabling early stop and continual pretraining without
093   sacrificing convergence.

097   2.1    M AXIMUM U PDATE PARAMETRIZATION (µP)
098
099   Maximal Update Parameterization (µP) (Yang & Hu, 2020; Yang et al., 2022; 2023) controls
100   initialization, layer-wise learning rates, and activation magnitudes to ensure analytically stable
101   training, independent of a model’s width and depth. In addition to improving training stability, µP
102   improves the transferability of training hyperparameters from small proxy models to large models, a
103   technique called µTransfer. The hyperparameter transferability of µP is theoretically justified for
104
      width (Yang et al., 2022) and depth (Yang et al., 2023). Previous work also show empirical evidence
      for transferability across batch size, training steps, and training sequence length.
105
106   In this paper, we follow the µP config used in CerebrasGPT (Dey et al., 2023) to study the transfer-
107   ability of batch size and learning rate across different numbers of training tokens and model sizes.

135

139   applied to the last checkpoint in the stable training phase. Moreover, the data mixture can be changed
140   during the cooldown phase to incorporate more high-quality data toward the end of the training. This
141   data mixture strategy has been proven effective in many recent language models (Hu et al., 2024;
142   Dubey et al., 2024a; Team et al., 2024).

      6     R ELATED W ORKS
508
509
      6.1   H YPERPARAMETER S EARCH FOR LLM S
510
511   Hyperparameters significantly impact a model’s performance. However, directly adjusting hyperpa-
512   rameters for LLMs is not feasible. Tensor Program (Yang et al., 2022; 2023) proposes a framework to
513   stabilize the hyper-parameters for models with different scales. In this framework, one could search
514   for optimal hyperparameters at a smaller scale and zero-shot transfer the results to a much larger
515   scale. CerebrasGPT (Dey et al., 2023) and MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024) applied this method in their
516   pretraining. Beyond the Tensor Program, researchers also leverage scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020)
517   to help decide the optimal hyperparameters (Dubey et al., 2024a; Xie et al., 2024). They first build a
518
      prediction function for the validation loss or accuracy from model hyperparameters (e.g., model size,
      training tokens, etc.), then leverage the function to predict the optimal hyperparameters.
519
520
521   6.2   L EARNING R ATE S CHEDULER FOR LLM S
522
      In the context of LLMs, the cosine scheduler is the most commonly used learning rate scheduler.
523   Majority of LLMs, including GPT (Radford, 2018; Radford et al., 2019; Brown, 2020) and large
524   models like Gopher (Rae et al., 2021), PaLM2 (Anil et al., 2023) or LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b;
525   Dubey et al., 2024b), all use cosine scheduler as the de-facto standard schedule. However, recent
526   LLMs have started to use multi-step learning rate schedulers to better accommodate multi-phase
527   training data. Deepseek LLM (Bi et al., 2024) introduced a scheduler that decrease the learning rate
528   twice during training, creating a 3-level step-like learning rate curve. MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024)
529   introduced a Warmup-Stable-Decay style learning rate that starts learning rate decay at the last 10%
530   steps.
531
532   7     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we systematically study the relationship between optimal learning rate, batch size,
535   and number of training tokens. We observed a power-law relation between these variables while
536   using the WSD learning rate scheduler. Inspired by this observation, we propose a new learning rate
537   scheduler, the Power scheduler, that is invariant with respect to the number of tokens and batch size.
538   The experiment shows that combining the Power scheduler with Maximum Update Parameterization
539   (µP) can consistently achieve impressive performance with one set of hyperparameters regardless of
      the number of training tokens, batch size, model size, and even model architecture.
