009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             Despite significant advancements in Text-to-Audio (TTA) generation models
012             achieving high-fidelity audio with fine-grained context understanding, they struggle
013             to model the relations between audio events described in the input text. However,
014             previous TTA methods have not systematically explored audio event relation mod-
015             eling, nor have they proposed frameworks to enhance this capability. In this work,
016
                we systematically study audio event relation modeling in TTA generation models.
                We first establish a benchmark for this task by: (1) proposing a comprehensive
017
                relation corpus covering all potential relations in real-world scenarios; (2) intro-
018
                ducing a new audio event corpus encompassing commonly heard audios; and (3)
019             proposing new evaluation metrics to assess audio event relation modeling from
020             various perspectives. Furthermore, we propose a finetuning framework to enhance
021             existing TTA models’ ability to model audio events relation.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025   Text-based crossmodal content generation has gained significant attention in recent years as it opens
026   up new possibilities for even amateur users to create professional content. Typical such methods
027   include text-to-image (TTI) (Ho et al., 2020), text-to-music (TTM) (Copet et al., 2023), text-to-
028   point (TTP) (Nichol et al., 2022), text-to-speech (TTS) (Ren et al., 2019) text-to-audio (TTA) (Liu
029   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023b). Among all of them, text-to-audio (TTA) generation stands out as a
030   particularly promising area, enabling the synthesis of complex acoustic environments or soundscapes
031   directly from textual descriptions. Recent advances in TTA have demonstrated impressive progress in
032   generating high-quality, detail-rich audio described in the input text prompt (Liu et al., 2024; 2023a;
033
      Huang et al., 2023b;a; Ghosal et al., 2023; Majumder et al., 2024; Kreuk et al., 2023).
034   When perceiving the physical world acoustically, whether through text or audio, the fundamental
035   unit is the audio event, a distinct acoustic signal representing an independent source. The essence
036   of perception lies in understanding the relationships emerging from events. Audio events are
037   spatiotemporally distributed in the physical world. Together with relation, they contribute for
038   holistic acoustic scene understanding (Qu et al., 2022). Studies in psychology (Zacks et al., 2007)
039
      and neuroscience (Lake et al., 2015; Hirsh et al., 1967) show that the human brain perceives the
      environment through discrete events and the relations between them. Humans are adept at using rich
040
      language to describe both audio events and their intricate relationships. While current TTA models
041
      can generate audios with high fidelity, their ability to generate audios that not only includes audio
042   events but also preserves the text-informed relationships between them remains unexplored.

084

087   2. We benchmark RiTTA by constructing complete relation corpus, audio event category corpus, seed
088      audio corpus. Combined with the <textprompt,audio> pair generation strategy, researchers
089      can create large, diverse dataset to further investigate the RiTTA task.
090   3. We propose a new multi-stage relation aware evaluation framework, called MSR-RiTTA, which
091      offers a more nuanced evaluation compared to existing TTA metrics, allowing researchers to
092      quantitatively assess their methods from multiple angles.
093
      4. We introduce a fine-tuning strategy leveraging the new dataset, demonstrating improvement over
094      current SoTA methods.

132
      exhibit complex relationships in spatial, temporal and compositional aspects. Prior work has partially
133   addressed modeling certain temporal relations (e.g., order) in TTA (Xie et al., 2024) and compositional

137   artificial post-mixing process, which may result in generated audio lacking smooth transitions across
138   event boundaries and inefficiencies in inference. While prior research has touched on modeling audio

150   3.1     AUDIO E VENT R ELATION C ORPUS
151   An audio event refers to a distinct acoustic signal occurrence with specific frequency, duration
152   and context characteristics that can be attributed to distinguish an independent sound source (He
153   et al., 2021) in an environment. Audio event is ubiquitous in the physical world and serves as the
154   fundamental entity to analyze and interpret the acoustic scene. We embrace the audio event as the
155   fundamental element to construct the relation corpus.
156   We construct the audio events relation corpus based on two key aspects. First, we consider relations
157   commonly found in the physical world, such as those arising from spatial and temporal variations,
158   which test TTA models’ ability to replicate audio events’ interactions in real-world scenarios. Second,
159   we focus on relations that challenge TTA models’ logical reasoning, evaluating their ability to
160   determine both which audio events to generate and how to generate them. These two aspects
161   partially overlap. Specifically, we define five main audio event categories, each associated with five
      subcategories of audio events. The detailed relation corpus is provided in Table 2, including,


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      1. Number Count: The number of audio events included in the generated audio, testing TTA models’
163      ability to address acoustic polyphony challenge.
164
      2. Temporal Order: Temporal order refers to the sequence of audio events in the generated au-
165
         dio. We include three basic temporal relations for two audio events: before, after, and
166
         simultaneity, testing the TTA models’ ability to distinguish and generate the correct event
167      order as specified in the input text prompt.
168
169
      3. Spatial Distance: Spatial distance refers to the variation in relative spatial distances inferred
         from the generated audio. It evaluates the TTA models’ ability to capture the spatial distance
170
         differences specified in the text prompt. Since we focus on mono-channel audio, obtaining the
171
         absolute distance for each audio event is nearly impossible (He et al., 2021). Therefore, we rely
172      on loudness differences within intra-class audio events to verify their spatial distance variations.
173
      4. Compositionality: Compositionality relation describes how multiple individual audio events are
174
         integrated together to form a complex auditory structure that specified in the input text prompt.
175
         It tests TTA models’ logical reasoning capability in determining which audio events to generate
176      and how to structure them, by following the compositional guidance illustrated in the input text
177      prompt. Specifically, we incorporate four main compositionality relations: Conjunction (And,
178      e.g., generate audio A and audio B together); Disjunction (Or, e.g., generate audio A or Audio B,
179      not both); Negation (Not, exclude one particular audio event, e.g., do not generate dog barking
180      audio); Condition (if-then-else, either generate two audio events if the condition is met,
181      otherwise generate the third audio if the condition is not met).
182
183   Most of the relations relate to two audio events (see Table 3 for more detail). Expanding the corpus
184
      to include more complex relations with a greater number of audio events is left for future work.

186   3.2   AUDIO E VENT C ATEGORY C ORPUS
187   Alongside the relation corpus presented in Sec. 3.1, we further construct a comprehensive audio event
188   category corpus. The two corpora serve as fundamental dataset for constructing text prompts for TTA
189   models. Since different audio event signals are generated from various sources or through different
190   interactions, we first establish four main audio source categories, further detailing each category with
191
      five sub-categories. These constructed audio categories encompass the majority of ubiquitous audio
      events encountered in our daily lives. Specifically, the audio event category corpus contain,
192
193   1. Human Audio: the audio generated by human beings in our daily life, including baby crying,
194      coughing, laughing, whistling, female speech and male speech.
195   2. Animal Audio: the audio generated by animals, including cat meowing, dog barking, bird chirping,
196      horse neighing, rooster crowing, sheep bleating and pig oinking.
197   3. Machinery Audio: the audio generated by various machinery devices while they are working,
198      including car horn, doorbell, telephone ring, paper shredder and boat horn.
199
      4. Human-Object Interaction Audio: human-object interaction audios include vegetable chopping,
200
         keyboard typing, toilet flushing, door slamming and foot step.
201
202   5. Object-Object Interaction Audio: we further incorporate object-object interaction audios,
203
         including glass dropping, car emergency brake, hammering nail, wood sawing and keys jingling.
204
      The detailed audio event corpus is given in Table 3. With the constructed relation and audio event
205
      corpus, we can create relation aware text prompts for TTA models.

      5.2   M ORE D ISCUSSION ON R I TAA E VALUATION
404
405   Section 3.4 has introduced the metrics in general. In practice, we further adjust the audio generation
406
      process for relations under Compositionality and Spatial Distance to so as to ensure these relations
      can be accurately evaluated under our proposed framework.
407
408   First, we skip general evaluation for <Not> as it lacks a corresponding ground truth reference audio.
409   During fintuning, we generate silent audio for <Not> for create finetuing pairs. Second, for the
410   <if-then-else> and <Or> sub-relations, which correspond to two possible ground truth audios,
411   we handle evaluation by computing the L2 distance (in the time domain) between the generated audio
412
      and the two reference audios. For example, for the prompt if event A then event B, else event C, the
      first reference is the combination of events A and B, while the second contains only event C. We use
413
      the reference audio with smaller L2 distance to the generated audio for general evaluation.
414
415   Third, precise evaluation of the three sub-relations (<closefirst>, <farfirst>, and
416   <equaldist>) under Spatial Distance from unconstrained audio requires sound event detec-
417   tion and localization (SELD (He & Markham, 2023; Grondin et al., 2019)) techniques to spatially
418
      localize each audio event, which is impossible with mono-channel audio. To address this, we approx-
      imate spatial distance by calculating the loudness, which can be estimated using the L2 norm of the
419
      audio waveform. The rationale behind this approach is that greater distances result in a dampening of
420
      waveform amplitude (and consequently reduced loudness) due to energy decay along the audio prop-
421   agation path. When the loudness difference exceeds a predefined threshold (for <closefirst>,
422   <farfirst>) or is within that threshold (for <equaldist>), we consider the evaluation accurate.
423   Specifically, we use a loudness reduction ratio σ1 (with σ1 “ 0.2 in our case). For <closefirst>,
424   if the closer event’s loudness is at least σ times greater than the further event’s loudness, the relation
425   is considered correct. Similarly, for <equaldist>, the loudness difference between the two events
426   should be within σ2 (with σ2 “ 0.4 in our case) of the louder event’s loudness. This estimation is
427   also reflected in the data generation process (see Sec 5.1).

521   6      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
522   Complex relationships within audio bring the world to life. While text-to-audio (TTA) generation
523   models have made remarkable progress in generating high-fidelity audio with fine-grained context
524   understanding, they often fall short in capturing the relational aspect of audio events in real-world.
525   The world around us is composed of interconnected audio events, where audio event rarely occurs in
526   isolation. Simply generating single sound sources is insufficient for producing realistic audio that
527   reflects the richness of the world.
528   To analyze the capabilities of current state-of-the-art TTA generative models, we first conduct a
529   systematic study of these models in audio event relation modeling. We introduce a benchmark for this
530   task by creating a comprehensive relational corpus covering all potential relations in the real-world
531   scenarios. Further, we propose new evaluation metric framework to assess audio event relation
532   modeling from various perspectives. Additionally, we propose a finetuning strategy to boost existing
533   models’ ability in modelling audio events relation, and we show improvement across all relation
534   metrics. Finally, we will release both the dataset and the code for the evaluation metrics, which will
535   be useful for future research in this domain.
536   Going forward, our work provides a unique research opportunity to bring the world to life by exploring
537   ways to generate long-term audio events to acoustically understand the physical world. Further,
538   understanding the successes and failures of these models in generating such complex audio events
539   is another promising research direction. This analysis could lead to further improvements in TTA
      models and their applications in areas such as virtual reality, cinema and immersive media.

      .1   F INETUNING PANNS AUDIO E VENT D ETECTION M ODEL ON O UR C URATED DATASET
729
730
731   A    AUDIO E VENT D ETECTION M ODEL F INE -T UNE
732
733   To detect the audio events from generated audio, we employ a pre-trained audio event detection
734   model (in our case, we adopt PANNS (Kong et al., 2020b)) to detect all audio events, each detected
735
      event has class label with a confidence score, start time and end time. Analyzing these detected audio
      events can uncover various audio events relations (see Fig. 4 in the main paper).
736
737   The PANNS model (Kong et al., 2020b) is pre-trained on the large-scale 527 class AudioSet
738   dataset (Gemmeke et al., 2017). It contains an audio tagging model and an audio event detec-
739   tion model. Directly applying the pre-trained detection model to detect audio events from our
740   generated audios inevitably results in false positive and ambiguous detections. For instance, a door
741
      slam sound may be incorrectly detected as speech or music with high confidence scores. To miti-
      gate the ambiguity and inaccuracies, we finetune the detection model (“Cnn14 DecisionLevelMax”
742
      variant) on our specially curated 100 k dataset by just tuning the last classification layer. Finally
743
      the finetuned model achieves mAP 0.57 on our curated 10k test sets, far outperforming the original
744   model with mAP 0.43.
745
746   We based on the pretrained PANNS (Kong et al., 2020b) audio event detection model to finetune
747
      it on our curated 100 k audio training dataset. Each audio is 10 s long with sampling rate 16 kHz.
      Moreover, each audio randomly contains one to five audio events, each event has a random start time
748
      position in the 10 s long audio. The input is 10 s long audio waveform. The output is a confidence
749
      map of shape r20, 25s, where 20 is the time steps with the temporal resolution 0.5 s and 25 is the audio
750   event class number. Potential audio events are extracted from the confidence map by thresholding the
751   confidence map, audio events with too short time duration (in our case, less than 0.5 s) are discarded.
752   The training and testing datasets size are 100 k and 10 k respectively. We adopt Adam (Kingma & Ba,
753   2015) to train the model with initial learning rate 0.0001 but decays every 200 epochs with decaying
754   rate 0.5. Finally, we train 350 epochs. The loss function is binary cross-entropy loss (BCE). On the
755   testing dataset, the finetuned model achieves mAP 0.57. We use the finetuned audio event detection
      model to detection audio events from the generated audios.
