009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            English-centric large language models (LLMs) often show strong multilingual ca-
012            pabilities. However, the multilingual performance of these models remains un-
013            clear and is not thoroughly evaluated for many languages. Most benchmarks for
014            multilinguality focus on classic NLP tasks, or cover a minimal number of lan-
015            guages. We introduce M EXA, a method for assessing the multilingual capabilities
016
               of pre-trained English-centric LLMs using parallel sentences, which are available
               for more languages than existing downstream tasks. M EXA leverages the fact that
017
               English-centric LLMs use English as a kind of pivot language in their intermedi-
018
               ate layers. It computes the alignment between English and non-English languages
019            using parallel sentences to evaluate the transfer of language understanding from
020            English to other languages. This alignment can be used to estimate task perfor-
021            mance in other languages. We conduct studies using various parallel datasets
022            (FLORES-200 and Bible), models (Llama family, Gemma family, Mistral, and
023            OLMo), and established downstream tasks (Belebele, m-MMLU, and m-ARC).
024            We explore different methods to compute embeddings in decoder-only models.
025            Our results show that M EXA, in its default settings, achieves a statistically sig-
026            nificant average Pearson correlation of 0.90 with three established downstream
027
               tasks across nine models and two parallel datasets. This suggests that M EXA is
               a reliable method for estimating the multilingual capabilities of English-centric
028
               LLMs, providing a clearer understanding of their multilingual potential and the
029
               inner workings of LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Most state-of-the-art autoregressive large language models (LLMs) are English-centric, closed-
036   source models such as Claude 3 Opus, GPT-4, and Gemini Pro (Anthropic, 2023; OpenAI et al.,
037   2023; Gemini Team et al., 2023); open-weight models such as Llama 3.1, Gemma 2, and Mixtral
038   (Dubey et al., 2024; Gemma Team et al., 2024b; Jiang et al., 2024); and open-source models such
039   as OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024). Except for open-source models, where the data is available and
040   thus the language distribution is transparent, there is confusion regarding the capabilities/language
041   distribution of these LLMs in other languages.
042   Primarily, the focus in evaluating LLMs has been on developing benchmarks to assess their perfor-
043   mance in English. Most benchmarks in multilingual setups consist of classical monolingual NLP
044   tasks such as sequence labeling (Ahuja et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2023a), the automatic translation
045   of popular English benchmarks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) into a limited number of
046   languages (Lai et al., 2023b; OpenAI, 2024), or language-specific benchmarks for languages such
047   as Persian (Ghahroodi et al., 2024), Arabic (Koto et al., 2024), Korean (Son et al., 2024), Turk-
048   ish (Yüksel et al., 2024), and Chinese (Li et al., 2024c).
049   Most LLMs are English-centric, either by choice or due to the availability of abundant data sources in
050   English. Either way, for these models to be effective in other languages, it is important that the other
051   languages align with the main language, i.e., English. Given such alignment, English could act as a
052   “rising tide that raises all ships,” meaning that improvements in English performance could benefit
053   other languages, especially in tasks such as reasoning (Zhu et al., 2024). Contrarily, if a language
      does not align well with English, an English-centric LLM may not provide meaningful coverage for


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      that language. Indeed, Wendler et al. (2024) have found that for Llama 2 (Touvron et al., 2023b), an
055   English-centric LLM, English could be seen as a kind of “pivot” language, enabling to solve complex
056   semantic tasks in a foreign language through a detour into English. More precisely, they show that
057   Llama 2 was able to decode semantically correct next tokens in the middle layers, assigning higher
058   probabilities to the English tokens than to the foreign version, which is only selected in the upper
059   layers. Zhao et al. (2024) present a hypothesis regarding the middle layers of English-centric LLMs,
060   suggesting that these models use English as a means of reasoning while incorporating multilingual
061   knowledge. Based on their analysis, the number of language-specific neurons in the middle layers
062   decreases within the self-attention mechanism but remains consistent across the layers of the feed-
063
      forward structure when processing multilingual queries.
064   In this paper, we introduce M EXA, a method that uses the observation that English-centric LLMs
065   semantically use English as a kind of pivot language in their middle layers to evaluate the actual
066   multilingual coverage of LLMs. This is done by measuring how well the embeddings of parallel
067   sentences in the middle layers of LLMs for non-English languages are aligned with the embedding
068   of their corresponding English. We extensively verify the M EXA estimation of language coverage
069
      for each LLM, using Pearson correlation between estimated and actual scores for various tasks. We
      use two parallel datasets: FLORES-200 (NLLB Team et al., 2022) and Bible (Mayer & Cysouw,
070
      2014); nine LLMs: Llama family, Gemma family, Mistral, and OLMo; and three tasks: Bele-
071
      bele (Bandarkar et al., 2024), m-MMLU, and m-ARC (Lai et al., 2023b). Our results show that
072   M EXA achieves a promising average Pearson correlation of 0.90 with established downstream tasks
073   across nine models and two parallel datasets. In our study on the calculation of M EXA scores, we
074   conduct multiple design analyses to examine the impact of token-level pooling for the embeddings
075   (i.e., using the last token versus a weighted average) and layer-level pooling in computing alignment
076   scores. While M EXA demonstrates a high correlation across most setups, we find that a weighted
077   average based on tokens, combined with mean pooling, yields the best results.

      2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
080
081
082
      We discuss the distribution of pre-training data in LLMs and multilingual evaluation benchmarks
      in Appendices A.1 and A.2 while focusing on cross-lingual alignment here. Research in the cross-
083
      lingual alignment field either aims to uncover the underlying mechanisms of alignment and assess its
084
      impact on models and downstream tasks, or attempts to enhance model performance by enforcing
085   alignment before, during, or after the pre-training phase. Most of these papers have focused on
086   encoder-only models, such as XLM-R (Conneau et al., 2020a) and mBERT (Devlin et al., 2019),
087   among others (Hämmerl et al., 2024). In this work, we focus primarily on decoder-only models.
088
      Understanding Alignment. Ye et al. (2023) show that English-centric models such as Llama 1
089
      (Touvron et al., 2023a) not only possess multilingual transfer abilities (after fine-tuning on one
090
      source language, they can be applied to other languages) but may even surpass the multilingual
091   transfer abilities of multilingual pre-trained models such as BLOOM (BigScience Workshop et al.,
092   2023). Schäfer et al. (2024) find that GPT-2-style decoder-only models show strong cross-lingual
093   generalization when trained on an imbalanced mix of languages. However, when trained on a bal-
094   anced language set, they do not observe increased performance compared to monolingual settings.
095   Wendler et al. (2024) perform single-token analysis to demonstrate that English-centered LLMs,
096   such as Llama 2, use English semantically as an internal latent language in the middle layers when
097   handling multilingual queries. Zhong et al. (2024) extend this analysis to multiple tokens, also
098   showing that an LLM dominated by both English and Japanese uses both languages as internal la-
099
      tent languages. Zhao et al. (2024) explore how LLMs handle multilingualism. They hypothesize
      that LLMs initially interpret the query, converting multilingual inputs into English for task-solving.
100
      In the middle layers, the models rely on English with self-attention mechanisms for reasoning, while
101
      employing multilingual knowledge through feed-forward structures. In the final layers, LLMs gen-
102   erate responses consistent with the original query language. Li et al. (2024f) and Li et al. (2024b)
103   are even more closely related to ours. Li et al. (2024f) employs absolute cosine similarity values
104   between last token embeddings derived from parallel sentences with English to predict the ranking
105   of language performance across various models. However, as we discuss in Section 3, relying solely
106   on absolute cosine values can be misleading, and as shown in Section 5.3, absolute cosine values
107   are less correlated with downstream tasks than M EXA score. Li et al. (2024b) uses English probing
      tasks and their automatic translations to construct a multilingual evaluation. While they compare


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      embedding similarity scores between high- and low-resource languages with corresponding evalu-
109   ation results, similar to Li et al. (2024f), they do not assess whether these correlations hold across
110   other downstream tasks. In Section 5, we demonstrate that M EXA scores align closely with a broad
111   range of downstream tasks.
112
      Boosting Alignment. The idea to enforce alignment in encoder-only models using parallel sen-
113
      tences dates back to (Conneau & Lample, 2019), and has been explored under various guises e.g.,
114
      using mixed-language sentences and/or bilingual dictionaries Huang et al. (2019); Conneau et al.
115   (2020b); Cao et al. (2020); Kulshreshtha et al. (2020); Efimov et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b).
116   Recently, Li et al. (2024d) improve multilingual alignment by initializing the decoder-only models
117   to generate similar representations of aligned words using contrastive learning and preserves this
118   alignment using a code-switching strategy during pretraining. Liu et al. (2024a) propose a data
119   allocation technique to select a core subset of languages for fine-tuning, better aligning the multi-
120   lingual capabilities of decoder-only LLMs and making them more truthful in their responses. Li
121   et al. (2024a) propose aligning internal sentence representations across different languages using
122   multilingual contrastive learning and aligning outputs by following cross-lingual instructions in the
123
      target language for decoder-only models.

125   3    M EXA
126
127   We now describe the M EXA method for computing the alignment score of language L1 with respect
128   to the pivot language L2 , given the language model m. In this paper, we use the term cross-lingual
129   alignment, geometric alignment, or simply alignment to refer to the semantic similarity of multilin-
130   gual embeddings across languages. L2 , for English-centric LLMs and in this paper, is English. To
131
      assess alignment, we use parallel sentences in two languages, L1 and L2 .
132   What defines semantic similarity in multilingual embeddings across languages? The goal of seman-
133   tic similarity is to ensure that parallel sentences have sufficiently high similarity, reflecting alignment
134   between the two languages. However, considering only the absolute cosine similarity value as the
135   alignment score does not guarantee proper alignment. For some languages, even non-parallel sen-
136   tences exhibit similarity scores comparable to those of parallel sentences (see §5.3). This is largely
137
      due to the high anisotropy observed in Transformer models, which can lead to so-called hubness
      issues, making it difficult to distinguish between similar and dissimilar embeddings (Ethayarajh,
138
      2019), especially in multilingual models (Hämmerl et al., 2023; Rajaee & Pilehvar, 2022). How-
139
      ever, a direct comparative analysis of the cosine similarity between parallel and non-parallel sentence
140   pairs across languages can help overcome these issues. Instead of using the absolute cosine similar-
141   ity value for alignment, we assign binary values (1 or 0) based on whether a criterion for semantic
142   similarity is satisfied. This criterion imposes that (a) parallel sentences should have high cosine
143   similarity, and (b) non-parallel pairs should also have low similarity values, ensuring the similarity
144   is not random or biased. Specifically, if the cosine similarity for a pair of parallel sentences is higher
145   than for any non-parallel sentences, we assign a value of 1 for this pair; otherwise, a value of 0. This
146   approach sidesteps the hubness problem since the absolute cosine similarity values themselves are
147   not directly used.
148   To compute M EXA, we first apply the cosine similarity function to the pairs of embeddings of
149   parallel sentences in languages L1 and L2 . In Section 3.1, we describe how embeddings can be
150   computed for each layer l of the autoregressive language model m. We generate a square matrix
151   C(L1 , L2 , m, l) representing cosine similarities of embeddings at the output of layer l for all parallel
152   sentences in languages L1 and L2 . We denote cij (l) the element in the i-th row and j-th column
153   of C(L1 , L2 , m, l). It represents the cosine similarity between the i-th sentence of L1 and the
154
      j-th sentence of L2 at layer l of language model m. The diagonal elements of C, denoted cii (l),
      represent the cosine similarity between parallel sentence pairs from L1 and L2 . We define the M EXA
155
      alignment score (µ(.)) as follows:

172   3.1   S ENTENCE E MBEDDINGS
173
174   Sentence embeddings are a transformation of a sentence into a fixed-size vector that captures its
175   meaning. The process of computing sentence embeddings can vary depending on the model archi-
176   tecture. Typically, sentence embeddings are used in encoder-based models such as BERT, which
177   employ bidirectional attention. In these models, the hidden states of each token are first extracted,
178   then aggregated, commonly by averaging the hidden states from the output layer. Since attention in
179
      these models is bidirectional, each token contributes equally to the final embedding. Alternatively
      one can use the output of the special [CLS] token as per the original BERT work (Devlin et al.,
180
      2019).
181
182   In this paper, we focus on autoregressive language models that use a decoder-only architecture. In
183   this architecture, attention is not bidirectional; instead, it takes the form of causal attention (left-
184   to-right). In bidirectional attention, each token has access to every other token in the sequence.
185
      However, in causal attention, the embedding of a token at position t is only influenced by the em-
      bedding of preceding tokens at positions 0, 1, . . . , t − 1. Therefore, simple averaging biases the
186
      embeddings towards sentence-initial words. Instead, two alternative methods are considered: us-
187
      ing only the last token and weighted averaging. We use and compare both methods to acquire the
188   sentence embeddings needed for M EXA.
189
190   A standard way to compute a sentence embedding uses only the last token of that sentence. Jiang
191
      et al. (2023b) show that using the last token in the format of a prompt template for a sentence s,
      such as ’This sentence: {s} means in one word:’, can be effective. Inspired by this, Li & Li (2024)
192
      used the prompt ’Summarize sentence {s} in one word:’ to obtain the last token embedding as the
193
      sentence-level text embedding. However, not all models are instruction-tuned; some earlier works,
194   such as Neelakantan et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024); Ma et al. (2024), use the last token without
195   any prompt. Since the models studied in this paper are only pre-trained and use multiple languages
196   in the input, we decided to use the last token method without any preceding instruction.
197
      An alternative is weighted averaging. It relies on the intuition that using only the last token might
198
      not represent the entire sentence, as the influence of earlier tokens may have diminished. This
199
      suggests that tokens at the end of the sentence should contribute more to the overall embedding than
200   those at the beginning. Another motivation is that sentence-final tokens are influenced by preceding
201   tokens and contain more context, while the representation of sentence-initial tokens has significantly
202   less contextual representation. To address this, Muennighoff (2022) proposes to assign weights to
203   each token based on its position. Thus, the sentence embedding of layer l using position-weighted
204   averaging is:

      4.1     PARALLEL DATA
220
221   We calculate the M EXA score using the parallel datasets of FLORES-200 (NLLB Team et al., 2022)
222   and the Bible (Mayer & Cysouw, 2014). While there are other high-quality parallel datasets, such
223   as NTREX-128 (Federmann et al., 2022), IN22 (Gala et al.), OPUS-100 (Zhang et al., 2020), Eu-
224   roparl (Koehn, 2005), OpenSubtitles (Lison & Tiedemann, 2016), TED2020 (Reimers & Gurevych,
225   2020), and Tatoeba (Tatoeba Community, 2006), we specifically chose FLORES-200 due to its high
226   quality and support for a wide range of languages, while the Bible dataset was selected for its exten-
227   sive language coverage.
228   FLORES-200 is a parallel corpus, where the English subset is sourced from Wikimedia projects.
229   Each English sentence has been translated into 204 distinct language-script combinations, these
230   translations have been verified by humans. The dataset contains 997 sentences for development,
231   1012 sentences for dev-test, and 992 sentences for testing. As the FLORES-200 test set is not
232   publicly accessible, we use the dev-test set as our FLORES parallel test corpus, in line with previous
233   studies. For faster computation, we only consider the first 100 sentences from each language. As
234
      shown in Appendix A.4, the odds of the M EXA score randomly achieving a high value with 100
      sentences are very slim.
235
236   The Parallel Bible (Mayer & Cysouw, 2014) covers a very large number of languages. From this re-
237   source, we managed to create a subcorpus, a super parallel dataset of the Bible, with 1,401 language-
238   script labels, each containing 103 sentences (i.e., Bible verses).1 This corpus includes many low-
239   resource languages, many of which are not covered by existing language technologies (Joshi et al.,
240
      2020), and M EXA can be adopted since only parallel data is needed. We use all the 103 sentences
      from each language.

352   5.1                          M EXA C ORRELATION A NALYSIS
353
354   We compute sentence embeddings for the selected models using two methods: weighted average
355   based on token positions and last token (see §3.1). We apply mean and max pooling on the M EXA
356   alignment scores across all model layers to derive a single score for each language. In Table 2,
357
      we report the correlation between the M EXA scores (computed using both mean- and max-pooling,
      for the two embedding methods) and task performances. Across all settings, the best overall result
358
      (higher correlation) is achieved when embeddings are computed using the weighted average, with
359
      mean pooling as the pooling method. We adopt this configuration as the default setting for M EXA.
360
361   FLORES vs Bible. In the default setting, the average Pearson correlation score for the FLORES
362   parallel dataset across different tasks is 0.9300, while for the Bible parallel dataset, it is 0.8779. The
363
      reason the Bible scores are generally lower than FLORES is that FLORES data is cleaner and more
      aligned with modern, standardized texts, whereas the Bible data is older and more specialized. For
364
      example for some languages, the orthography of Bible texts no longer matches today’s orthogra-
365
      phy. In the Bible, Arabic often includes diacritics, which are typically omitted in modern writing
366   and tasks, making the text less familiar to models trained on contemporary data. Additionally, al-
367   though the Bible dataset has been made parallel, sentence alignment can still be inconsistent due to
368   translation nuances. In contrast, FLORES is carefully curated to ensure high-quality, sentence-level
369   parallelism across languages for machine translation tasks.
370
      Weighted Average vs. Last Token Embeddings. The use of last token embeddings shows promis-
371
      ingly high correlations with the FLORES parallel data; however, for the Bible dataset, the correlation
372   is low in some cases. We believe this may stem from the high occurrence of Bible sentences (espe-
373   cially in English), which leads models to memorize these phrases. Using the WIMBD toolkit (Elazar
374   et al., 2024), we found that, on average, there are 92 times more documents in Dolma 1.7 (Soldaini
375   et al., 2024) containing exact Bible sentences than those in FLORES. Consequently, when using
376   Bible examples, the last token is biased towards predicting the specific memorized next token rather
377   than incorporating context-related signals. Therefore, one should consider the hazard of memorized
      data when using last token embeddings. The weighted-average method, which takes into account


                                                                                               7
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
       the influence of multiple tokens, can mitigate the impact of a poor embedding for the last token and
379    enable the model to capture useful information from the other tokens more robustly.
380
381    Max Pooling vs. Mean Pooling. In our comparison of mean pooling and max pooling on the
382
       Belebele benchmark, we found that mean pooling underestimates low-resource languages (resulting
       in more M EXA scores near 0), while max pooling correlates better with the Belebele benchmark.
383
       This can be explained by the fact that Belebele is an easier task among the three evaluated, allowing
384
       even low-resource languages to achieve good scores. Conversely, based on our experiment with
385    m-ARC, max pooling tends to overestimate low-resource languages, making mean pooling more
386    aligned with m-ARC. This can be attributed to m-ARC being the most challenging task among the
387    three, where even medium-resource languages do not achieve high scores. Changing the pooling
388    method from mean to max can be considered when dealing with different levels of understanding.

427    5.3                     M EXA VS A BSOLUTE C OSINE S IMILARITY
428
429    We compare M EXA with the use of absolute cosine similarities. To begin with, cosine similarity
430    scores are not always directly comparable across models. For example, if a language shows a higher
431    cosine similarity with English for one model than another, it does not necessarily indicate better
       alignment in the former model. However, M EXA has the advantage of being directly comparable, as


                                                                                                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      its score does not rely on absolute similarity values. To examine the correlation of both methods with
433   downstream tasks, we conducted the following experiment. We used parallel data from FLORES
434   and downstream task data from the Belebele benchmark, focusing on 116 common labels. For each
435   non-English language, we computed the average absolute cosine similarity for parallel sentences
436   with English, and the average absolute cosine similarity for non-parallel sentences with English.
437   Following the setup by Li et al. (2024f), which employs absolute cosine similarity values to predict
438   the performance and rank of languages, we computed the embeddings using the last-token method
439   and applied mean pooling over layers {5, 10, 15, 20, 25}. We report results using the Gemma 1 and
440   Llama 1 7B models, which are commonly used in our experiments. For a fair comparison, this setup
441
      is applied to both absolute cosine similarity and M EXA. For the Gemma 1 model, M EXA achieves
      a correlation of 0.9260 with downstream task performance, while the absolute cosine similarity for
442
      parallel sentences achieves a correlation of 0.7651. Additionally, the correlation between the ab-
443
      solute cosine similarity for parallel and non-parallel sentences is 0.9232. For the Llama 1 model,
444   M EXA achieves a correlation of 0.8365 with downstream task performance, while the absolute co-
445   sine similarity for parallel sentences achieves a correlation of 0.6473. Additionally, the correlation
446   between the absolute cosine similarity for parallel and non-parallel sentences is 0.9064. In both
447   models, the absolute cosine similarity method achieved significantly lower correlations with down-
448   stream tasks compared to M EXA. This discrepancy arises primarily because, for some languages, the
449   similarity score can be high regardless of whether the sentences are parallel or non-parallel. Further-
450   more, a low similarity score between two languages does not necessarily indicate weak alignment,
451   as overall similarity scores may be low while parallel sentences still exhibit much higher scores than
452
      non-parallel ones.

535    place all of them. Our goal is to highlight the multilingual potential of English-centric LLMs and
536    propose a simple way to evaluate them. We hope this encourages the development of more multi-
537    lingual LLMs, even though they are likely to contain large shares of English data. Additionally, it
538    is important to note that answers across languages do not always need to be fully aligned (Naous
539    et al., 2024), and for such cases, language- and culture-specific evaluation benchmarks should be
       developed.

       A.1   D ISTRIBUTION OF P RE - TRAINING DATA IN LLM S
1029
1030   The distribution of languages in the training data of state-of-the-art LLMs is rarely fully documented.
1031   Llama 2 (Touvron et al., 2023b) is a counter-example and its authors have disclosed the language
1032   distribution use in pretraining. Their analysis uses the FastText (Bojanowski et al., 2017) language
1033   identification tool and a threshold of 0.5 for the language detection. We reproduce Touvron et al.
1034   (2023b, Table 10), which lists 27 languages with percentages greater than 0.005% in the Llama 2
1035   pre-training data, in Table 3. English, with 89.70%, constitutes the vast majority of the training data.
1036   All the languages listed in Table 3 have a presence of more than 0.10% (top 35 languages) on the web
1037   according to the W3Techs report (W3Techs, 2024) or more than 0.15% (top 36 languages) according
1038   to CommonCrawl (first three snapshots of 2024) (Common Crawl, 2024). However, not all of the
1039   most prevalent languages on the web appear in Table 3. The following 9 languages are missing, most
1040   of which use non-Latin writing systems: Turkish (tur Latn), Persian (pes Arab), Arabic (ara Arab),
1041   Greek (ell Grek), Hebrew (heb Hebr), Thai (tha Thai), Hindi (hin Deva), Slovak (slk Latn), and
1042   Lithuanian (lit Latn).
1043   The distribution of data in the training of English-centric LLMs is not the same as on the web, but it
1044   does have some correlation. The amount of English in LLM pre-training data is significantly larger
1045   than for other languages. This is also observable for GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020a), where more than
1046   92% of the training texts was in English (Brown et al., 2020b). The rest of the top languages in the
1047   data of such models are mostly high-resource languages, which have the most available data on the
1048   web (top 36 languages). However, in some models, this could be adjusted by design, for example, to
1049
       make writing systems with non-Latin languages less prominent (as seen in Llama 2). This weakens
       the correlation between LLMs’ pretraining data and the web.

1107   A.3   S EMANTIC S IMILARITY IN M ULTILINGUAL E MBEDDINGS
1108
1109   There are other ways to compute similarity between languages, such as Representational Similarity
1110   Analysis (RSA) (Chrupała & Alishahi, 2019) and Central Kernel Alignment (CKA) (Kornblith et al.,
1111   2019). RSA involves first computing the cosine similarity for sentence embeddings within each lan-
1112   guage, then correlating these in-language similarities with those in other languages. CKA, another
1113
       metric, is adopted by Conneau et al. (2020b) and Muller et al. (2021). Conneau et al. (2020b) show
       that the CKA similarity is highly correlated with sentence retrieval scores for four languages. In this
1114
       paper, our focus is not on finding different ways to calculate similarity between languages, but on
1115
       how helpful a properly defined alignment score can be in estimating the multilingual capabilities of
1116   LLMs across multiple languages.
