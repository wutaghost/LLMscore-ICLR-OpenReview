011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In variational autoencoders (VAEs), the variational posterior often aligns closely
014            with the prior, known as posterior collapse, which leads to poor representation
015            learning quality. An adjustable hyperparameter beta has been introduced in VAE
016            to address this issue. This study sharply evaluates the conditions under which
017            the posterior collapse occurs with respect to beta and dataset size by analyzing a
018            minimal VAE in a high-dimensional limit. Additionally, this setting enables the
019
               evaluation of the rate-distortion curve in the VAE. This result shows that, unlike
               typical regularization parameters, VAEs face “inevitable posterior collapse” beyond
020
               a certain beta threshold, regardless of dataset size. The dataset-size dependence
021
               of the derived rate-distortion curve also suggests that relatively large datasets are
022            required to achieve a rate-distortion curve with high rates. These results robustly
023            explain generalization behavior across various real datasets with highly non-linear
024            VAEs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep latent variable models are generative models that use a neural network to convert latent
031   variables generated from a prior distribution into samples that closely resemble the data. Variational
032   autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Rezende et al., 2014), a type of the deep latent
033
      variable models, have been applied in various fields such as image generation (Child, 2020; Vahdat
      & Kautz, 2020), clustering (Jiang et al., 2016), dimensionality reduction (Akkari et al., 2022), and
034
      anomaly detection (An & Cho, 2015; Park et al., 2022). In VAEs, directly maximizing the likelihood
035
      is intractable owing to the marginalization of latent variables. Therefore, VAE often employs the
036   evidence lower bounds (ELBOs), which serve as computable lower bounds for the log-likelihood.
037
038   From an informational-theoretical perspective, several studies (Alemi et al., 2018; Huang et al.,
039
      2020; Nakagawa et al., 2021) have interpreted ELBO as decomposing into two terms that represent
      a trade-off. Based on the analogy from the rate-distortion (RD) theory, these terms can be likened
040
      to rate and distortion (Alemi et al., 2018). Furthermore, these studies suggest that during training
041
      with ELBO, the variational posterior of the latent variables tends to align with their prior, hindering
042   effective representation learning. This phenomenon is commonly referred to as “posterior collapse”.
043
044   To address the posterior collapse, an additional regularization parameter, denoted as βVAE , is intro-
045
      duced to control the trade-off between rate and distortion (Higgins et al., 2016). Although models
      with a small βVAE can reconstruct the data points effectively, achieving low distortion, they may
046
      generate inauthentic data due to significant mismatches between the variational posterior and the prior
047
      (Alemi et al., 2018). In contrast, while models with a large βVAE align their variational distributions
048   closely with the prior, resulting in a low rate, they may ignore the important encoding information.
049   Thus, careful tuning of βVAE in beta-VAEs is important for various applications (Kohl et al., 2018;
050   Castrejon et al., 2019). In addition to simply enhancing the data generation capability, βVAE is crucial
051   for achieving better disentanglement (Higgins et al., 2016) and obtaining the RD curve (Alemi et al.,
052   2018). However, theoretical understanding of the relationship between βVAE , the posterior collapse,
053   and the RD curve remains limited. Particularly, the dataset-size dependence of these matters remains
      theoretically unexplored, even for linear VAE (Lucas et al., 2019).

062            metric in the VAE is sharply characterized by a small finite number of summary statistics,

087   2    R ELATED WORK
088
089   Linear VAEs The linear VAE is a simple model in which both the encoder and decoder are
090   constrained to be affine transformations (Lucas et al., 2019). Although deriving analytical results
091   for deep latent models is intractable, linear VAEs can provide analytical results, facilitating a deeper
092   understanding of VAEs. Indeed, despite their simplicity, the results in linear VAEs sufficiently
093   explain the behavior of more complex VAEs (Lucas et al., 2019; Bae et al., 2022). Moreover, an
094   algorithm proven effective for linear VAEs has been successfully applied to deeper models (Bae et al.,
095   2022). In addition, various theoretical results have been obtained. Dai et al. (2018) demonstrated
096
      the connections between linear VAE, probabilistic principal component analysis (PCA) (Tipping &
      Bishop, 1999), and robust PCA (Candès et al., 2011; Chandrasekaran et al., 2011). Simultaneously,
097
      Lucas et al. (2019) and Wang & Ziyin (2022) employ linear VAEs to explore the origins of posterior
098
      collapse. However, these analyses did not address the dataset-size dependence of the generalization,
099   RD curve, and robustness against the background noise, which is a focus of our study. Additionally,
100   these analyses did not examine the behavior of the RD curve, which can be obtained by varying βVAE
101   with a fixed decoder variance.
102
103   High-dimensional asymptotics from replica method The replica method, mainly used as an
104   analytical tool in our study, is a non-rigorous but powerful heuristic in statistical physics (Mézard
105   et al., 1987; Mezard & Montanari, 2009; Edwards & Anderson, 1975). This method has proven
106   invaluable in solving high-dimensional machine-learning problems. Previous studies have addressed
107   the dataset-size dependence of the generalization error in supervised learning including single-layer
      (Gardner & Derrida, 1988; Opper & Haussler, 1991; Barbier et al., 2019; Aubin et al., 2020) and


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      multi-layer (Aubin et al., 2018) neural networks, as well as kernel methods(Dietrich et al., 1999;
109   Bordelon et al., 2020; Gerace et al., 2020). In unsupervised learning, this includes dimensionality
110   reduction techniques such as the PCA (Biehl & Mietzner, 1993; Hoyle & Rattray, 2004; Ipsen &
111   Hansen, 2019), and generative models such as energy-based models (Decelle et al., 2018; Ichikawa &
112   Hukushima, 2022) and denoising autoencoders (Cui & Zdeborová, 2023). However, the dataset-size
113   dependence of VAEs has yet to be previously analyzed; therefore, this study aims to examine this
114   dependence. Efforts have been made to confirm the non-rigorous results of the replica method using
115   other rigorous analytical techniques. For convex optimization problems, the Gaussian min-max
116   theorem (Gordon, 1985; Mignacco et al., 2020) can be used in the analysis, which provides rigorous
117
      results consistent with those of the replica method (Thrampoulidis et al., 2018).

121   3.1   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS
122
123   The VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013) is a latent generative model. Let D = {xµ }nµ=1 be the training
124   data, where xµ ∈ Rd and pD (x) is the empirical distribution of the training dataset. In practical
125   applications, VAEs are typically trained using beta-VAE objective (Higgins et al., 2016) given by
                                                                      ∆
126            EpD Eqϕ [− log pθ (x|z)] + βVAE DKL [qϕ (z|x)∥p(z)] =     EpD [L(θ, ϕ; x, βVAE )],      (1)
127
128
      where z ∈ Rk is the latent variables and p(z) is a prior for the variables, and the parameter βVAE ≥ 0
      is introduced to control the trade-off between the first and second terms. pθ (x|z), parameterized by
129
      parameters θ, and qϕ (z|x), parameterized by ϕ, are commonly referred to as decoder and encoder,
130
      respectively. Subsequently, VAEs optimize the encoder parameters ϕ and decoder parameters θ by
131   minimizing the objective of Eq. (1). Note that when βVAE = 0, the objective is a deterministic
132   autoencoder that focuses only on minimizing the first term, which is referred to as the reconstruction
133   error.

135   3.2   I NFORMATION - THEORETIC INTERPRETATION OF VAE S
136
137   Alemi et al. (2018); Huang et al. (2020); Park et al. (2022) demonstrate that VAEs can be interpreted
138   based on the RD theory (Davisson, 1972; Cover, 1999), which has been successfully applied to data
139   compression. The primary focus has been on the curve where the distortion achieves its minimum
140
      value for a given rate, or conversely; see Appendix B for a detailed explanation. Based on an analogy
      from the RD theory, Alemi et al. (2018) decomposed the beta-VAE objective in Eq. (1) into rate R
141
      and distortion D as follows:
142
143                R(ϕ) = EpD [DKL [qϕ (z|x)∥p(z)]], D(θ, ϕ) = EpD [Eqϕ [− log pθ (x|z)]].                  (2)
144   According to Alemi et al. (2018), a trade-off exists between the rate and distortion, as in the RD
145   theory, especially when the encoder and decoder have infinite capacities. This relationship is derived
146   from the following:
147                          H = −EpD DKL [qϕ (z|x)∥pθ (z|x)] + R(ϕ) + D(θ, ϕ),
148
      where H is the negative log-likelihood, defined as H = −EpD log pθ (x). From the non-negativity of
149
      the KL divergence, it follows that H ≤ R(ϕ) + D(θ, ϕ), where the equality holds if and only if the
150
      variational posterior and true posterior coincide, i.e., ∀x, qϕ (z|x) = pθ (z|x).
151
152   While this equality holds when the encoder and decoder with infinite capabilities satisfy the optimality
153   conditions, the limitation of finite parameters makes this situation unfeasible. Therefore, the goal is to
154
      determine an approximate optimal distortion at a given rate R∗ by solving the optimization problem:
155                                  D̂(R∗ ) = min D(ϕ, θ) s.t. R(ϕ) ≤ R∗ .                                 (3)
                                                   θ,ϕ
156
157   For optimization without explicitly considering R∗ , the Lagrangian function with the Lagrange
158   multiplier βVAE ≥ 0 can be utilized as follows:
159                                         min D(θ, ϕ) + βVAE R(ϕ).
                                             θ,ϕ
160
161   This formulation is identical to the beta-VAE objective expressed in Eq. (1). Thus, training various
      VAEs with different βVAE corresponds to obtaining distinct points on the RD curve.

407   6.2   P HASE DIAGRAM
408
409   Based on the extreme values of summary statistics m and Q in Eq. (12), we next discuss the phase
410   diagram in terms of βVAE . The following three distinct phases are identified, as shown in Fig. 3:
411
412          • Learning phase (green region, m ̸= 0, Q ̸= 0, R ̸= 0): The VAE recovers the signal and
413            avoids posterior collapse.
414
             • overfitting phase (red region, m = 0, Q ̸= 0, R = 0): The effects of the rate and ridge
415
               regularizations are small, causing overfitting of the background noise in the data.
416
417          • Regularized phase (orange region, m = 0, Q = 0, R = 0): The rate and ridge regularizations
418            restrict the degrees of freedom of the learnable parameters, leading to posterior collapse.
419
420   As noted in the previous section, the boundaries between the overfitting and learning phases, as well
421   as those between the regularized and learning phases in the phase diagram, precisely correspond to
422   the point where the signal recovery error begins to decrease. The phase diagram shows that as βVAE
423   increases, the transition to the learning phase becomes more challenging, even with a sufficiently
424   large α, indicating the long plateau described above.

484
485   It is reasonable to question whether the theoretical analysis can explain the phenomena observed
      in more complex real-world datasets with nonlinear VAEs. The answer is empirically positive, as

      described below. Specifically, we investigate whether the existence of the posterior collapse threshold
487   and the dependency of generalization performance on βVAE and α predicted by Claim 5.2 in the
488   Linear VAE, remain consistent when applied to real-world datasets with nonlinear VAEs. We compare
489   the generalization properties predicted by the theoretical analysis with those observed in Fashion
490   MNIST (Deng, 2012) and MNIST (Deng, 2012) using a 3-layer MLP for the encoder and decoder.
491   For these datasets, we calculated βVAE dependence of Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel

494   empirical standard deviation of the bulk, consisting of the eigenmodes of the empirical covariance

506   of color images and a convolutional neural network (CNN). The experimental results are provided in

510   7    C ONCLUSION
511
512   We provide a high-dimensional asymptotic characterization of trained linear VAEs, clarifying the
513   relationship between dataset size, βVAE , posterior collapse, and RD curve. Specifically, these results
514   show an “inevitable posterior collapse” regardless of the dataset size beyond a certain beta threshold
515
      and the dataset-size dependence of the RD curve, indicating that relatively large datasets are required
      in high-rate regions. These findings also explain the qualitative behavior for realistic datasets and
516
      nonlinear VAEs, providing theoretical insights that support longstanding practical intuitions about
517
      VAEs.
518
519   Finally, building on our analysis, we present insights for the engineering applications of VAEs. This
520   study reveals that the parameter βVAE , unlike the conventional ridge regularization coefficient λ,
521
      requires careful tuning based on dataset size. Inappropriate tuning leads to significant degradation in
      generalization performance. In particular, an excessively large βVAE induces a “plateau phenomenon”
522
      that persists despite increases in dataset size, hindering further performance improvements and
523
      eventually causing inevitable posterior collapse. These findings underscore that βVAE is a highly
524   sensitive and potentially risky parameter requiring meticulous adjustment. This study also reveals that
525   in the limit of large dataset sizes, the optimal value of βVAE corresponds to the strength of background
526   noise in the data. In contrast, for finite datasets, the optimal value of βVAE tends to shift to higher
527   values. This tendency consistently holds in our numerical experiments across real-world datasets and
528   VAEs with nonlinear structures, demonstrating its robustness. This directional adjustment offers a
529   critical guideline for effectively tuning βVAE . By quantitatively examining the conventional claim
530   that “a large βVAE induces posterior collapse” through a minimal model based on a linear VAE, we
531   not only clarified the underlying mechanism but also provided practical guidelines for parameter
532
      tuning.

      B     R EVIEW OF RATE DISTORTION THEORY
757
758
      The rate-distortion theory was introduced by Shannon et al. (1959) and then further developed by
759   Berger et al. (1975); Berger & Gibson (1998). This theoretical framework describes the minimum bit
760   rate (rate) required for encoding a source, subject to a given distortion measure. In recent years, it has
761   been used to understand machine learning (Gao et al., 2019; Alemi et al., 2018; Theis et al., 2022;
762   Brekelmans et al., 2019; Isik et al., 2022).
763
      Let X P = {X1 , . . . , XP } ∈ X P be i.i.d random variables from the distribution P (x). An encoder
764
      fP : X P → {1, 2, . . . , 2P ×R } maps the input X P into a quantized vector, and a decoder gP :
765
      {1, 2, . . . , 2P ×R } → X P reconstructs the input by a decoded input X̂ P from the quantized vector.
766
      To measure the discrepancy between the original and decoded inputs, a distortion function d :
767
      X × X → R+ is introduced. The distortion for the input X P and decoded input X̂ P is defined as
768
      the average distortion between each pair Xi and X̂i . Commonly used distortion functions are the
769
      Hamming distortion function defined as d(x, x̂) = I[x ̸= x̂] for X = {0, 1} where I is the indicator
770   function, and the squared error distortion function defined as d(x, x̂) = (x − x̂)2 for X = R. We are
771   ready to define the RD function.
772
773   Definition B.1 A rate-distortion pair (R, D) is achievable if there exists a (probabilistic) encoder-
774   decoder (fP , gP ) such that the quantized vector has size 2P ×R and the expected distortion
775   limP →∞ [d(X P , gP (fP (X P ))] ≤ D.
776
777
      Definition B.2 The RD function R(D) is the infimum of rates R such that the RD pair (R, D) is
      achievable.
778
779
      The main theorem of the RD theory (Cover, 1999) states as follows,
780
781   Theorem B.3 Given an upper bound of distortion D, the following equation holds:
782
783                                    R(D) =             min            I(X; X̂)                             (13)
                                                 P (X̂|X):E[d(X,X̂)]≤D
784
785
      The RD theorem provides the fundamental limit of data compression, i.e., how many minimum bits
786
      are needed to compress the input, given the quality of the reconstructed input.
787
788
      B.1    RD OF G AUSSIAN SOURCE .
789
790   We give an example of the RD function for Gaussian input.
791
792   Proposition B.4 If X ∼ N (0, σ 2 ), the RD function is given by
793                                             (          2

798   X̂ = 0; otherwise, we transmit X̂ such that X̂ ∼ N (0, σ 2 − D), X − X̂ ∼ N (0, D) where X̂ and
799   X − X̂ are independent.

1215   This section provides detailed information on the experiment with real dataset and non-linear VAEs
1216   as shown in Fig. 5. All experiments were conducted using a Xeon(R) Gold 6248 CPU with 26 threads

1218
1219   Preprocessing The MNIST (Deng, 2012) and Fashion MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) dataset were
1220   preprocessed by flattening the images into vectors and normalizing the pixel values by dividing each
1221   value by 255 rescaled pixel.
1222
1223   Architecture For the MNIST and Fashion MNIST, we employed a multi-layer perceptron varia-
1224   tional autoencoder (MLPVAE) implemented in Pytorch. The MLPVAE was designed to handle
1225   input data of dimension 784, corresponding to 28 × 28 pixel images flattened into a single vector.
1226   The encoder architecture comprised a linear transformation, Linear(784, 128), followed by
1227
       a ReLU activation function, and then two linear layers, Linear(128, 2), which output the
       mean µ(z) ∈ R2 and logarithm of the variance log σ 2 (z) ∈ R2 of the latent space. The decoder
1228
       reconstructs the input by performing a linear transformation, Linear(2, 128), followed by a
1229
       ReLU activation function and a final linear layer, Linear(128, 784), to generate the output.
1230
1231
       Training The MLPVAE model was trained using the mini-batch Adam optimizer (Kingma &
1232
       Welling, 2013), with a learning rate of 0.001, a weight decay of 0.0001, and a mini-batch size of 256.
1233   The model was then trained for 30 epochs.
1234
1235
       FID estimation To quantitatively evaluate the quality of images generated by the MLPVAE model
1236   on the MNIST and Fashion MNIST datasets, we employed the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)
1237   (Heusel et al., 2017). The FID score is a well-established metric for assessing the similarity between
1238   two sets of images, measuring the quality of generated images relative to real ones. It achieves this by
1239   comparing the distributions of features extracted from an Inception v3 model (Szegedy et al., 2015)
1240   for both real and generated images, with lower FID scores indicating higher similarity and better
1241   image quality. For the FID calculation, we utilized torchmetrics.image.fid, which provides
       an implementation of the FID computation.

       Noise strength estimation In our theoretical analysis, we assume SCM, i.e., described by proba-
       bilistic PCA (Tipping & Bishop, 1999) for the data model, which forms the basis for our estimation
1273
       of the noise strength η. Given this assumption, we employs PCA to estimate η̂, which represents the
1274
       average variance of the reconstructed data after dimensionality reduction. For both the MNIST and
1275   Fashion MNIST datasets, we follow a consistent procedure. We start flatten and normalize them.
1276   Applying PCA to these preprocessed images allows us to identify the principal components that
1277   capture the majority of the variance in the data. By examining the cumulative variance ratio, we
1278   determine the number of principal components required to account for 80% of the total variance then
1279   transform the data into the rest of 20%, bulk and reconstruct them. η̂ are estimated by the empirical
1280   standard deviation of this reconstructed data in bulk.
