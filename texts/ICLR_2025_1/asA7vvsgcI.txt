009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The widespread deployment of large language models (LLMs) has led to impres-
012            sive advancements, yet information about their training data, a critical factor in
013            their performance, remains undisclosed. Membership inference attacks (MIAs)
014            aim to determine whether a specific instance was part of a target model’s train-
015            ing data. MIAs can offer insights into LLM outputs and help detect and address
016            concerns such as data contamination and compliance with privacy and copyright
017            standards. However, applying MIAs to LLMs presents unique challenges due
018            to the massive scale of pre-training data and the ambiguous nature of member-
019
               ship. Additionally, creating appropriate benchmarks to evaluate MIA methods is
               not straightforward, as training and test data distributions are often unknown. In
020
               this paper, we introduce EM-MIA, a novel MIA method for LLMs that iteratively
021
               refines membership scores and prefix scores via an expectation-maximization al-
022            gorithm, leveraging the duality that the estimates of these scores can be improved
023            by each other. Membership scores and prefix scores assess how each instance is
024            likely to be a member and discriminative as a prefix, respectively. Our method
025            achieves state-of-the-art results on the WikiMIA dataset. To further evaluate EM-
026            MIA, we present OLMoMIA, a benchmark built from OLMo resources, which
027            allows us to control the difficulty of MIA tasks with varying degrees of overlap
028            between training and test data distributions. We believe that EM-MIA serves as a
029            robust MIA method for LLMs and that OLMoMIA provides a valuable resource
030
               for comprehensively evaluating MIA approaches, thereby driving future research
               in this critical area.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023b) have recently emerged
037   as a groundbreaking development and have had a transformative impact in many fields. The vast
038   and diverse training data are central to their success, which enables LLMs to understand and gener-
039
      ate languages to perform complex tasks. Given that training data directly shape LLMs’ behaviors,
      knowing the composition of training data allows researchers and practitioners to assess the strengths
040
      and limitations of LLMs (Gebru et al., 2021), address ethical concerns, and mitigate potential bi-
041
      ases (Bender et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2023) and other risks (Bommasani et al., 2021). However, the
042   exact composition of training data is often a secret ingredient of LLMs.
043
044   Since LLMs are trained on large-scale corpora from diverse sources, including web content, the
045
      inclusion of undesirable data into training data such as test datasets (Sainz et al., 2023; Oren et al.,
      2023; Sainz et al., 2024), proprietary contents (Chang et al., 2023; Meeus et al., 2024c), or person-
046
      ally identifiable information (Mozes et al., 2023) might prevalently happen unconsciously, raising
047
      serious concerns when deploying LLMs. Membership inference attack (MIA) determines whether a
048   particular data point has been seen during training a target model (Shokri et al., 2017). Using MIA
049   to uncover those potential instances can serve as an effective mechanism in detecting data contam-
050   ination (Magar & Schwartz, 2022) for the reliable evaluation of LLMs’ ability (Zhou et al., 2023)
051   and auditing copyright infringement (Duarte et al., 2024) and privacy leakage (Staab et al., 2023;
052   Kandpal et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024) to ensure compliance with regulations such as GDPR (Voigt
053   & Von dem Bussche, 2017) and CCPA (Legislature, 2018). Therefore, MIA has gained huge interest
      in the LLM community.

054
      Despite increasing demands, MIA for LLMs is challenging (Duan et al., 2024) largely due to the
055   large scale of training data and intrinsic ambiguity of membership from the nature of languages.
056   Furthermore, designing a proper evaluation benchmark on MIAs for LLMs that emulates a realistic
057   test case scenario is complicated. While training data distribution is a mixture of diverse sources
058   and unknown, target test data at inference time could be from any distribution. This circumstance
059   motivates us to develop a robust MIA method that can work well across different conditions on the
060   distribution of members and non-members with minimal benchmark information.
061   In this paper, we propose a novel MIA framework, EM-MIA, which iteratively refines membership
062   scores and prefix scores using an expectation-maximization algorithm. A membership score indi-
063   cates how likely each data point is to be a member. A prefix score indicates how discriminative
064   each data point is in distinguishing members and non-members when used as a prefix. We empiri-
065   cally observe a duality between these scores, where better estimates of one can improve estimates of
066   another. By starting from a reasonable initialization, our iterative approach progressively enhances
067   score prediction until convergence, leading to more accurate membership scores for MIA.
068   To comprehensively evaluate our method and different MIA approaches, we introduce a new bench-
069   mark called OLMoMIA by utilizing OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024) resources. We vary degrees
070   of distribution overlaps and control difficulty levels based on clustering. Throughout the extensive
071   experiments, we have shown that EM-MIA is a versatile MIA method and significantly outperforms
072   previous strong baselines, though all methods including ours still struggle to surpass random guess-
073   ing in the most challenging random split setting.
074   Our novelty and main contributions are summarized as follows:
075
076
          • To the best of our knowledge, EM-MIA is the first approach that progressively enhances mem-
            bership scores (and prefix scores) with an iterative refinement for improved MIA for LLMs.
077
          • We demonstrate that ReCaLL (Xie et al., 2024) has an over-reliance on prefix selection. In
078
            contrast, we design EM-MIA without requiring any labeled data or prior information on bench-
079         marks.
080       • EM-MIA remarkably outperforms all existing strong MIA baselines for LLMs, and achieves
081         state-of-the-art results on WikiMIA (Shi et al., 2023), the most popular benchmark for detecting
082         pre-training data of LLMs.
083       • Experiments on our OLMoMIA benchmark with varying degrees of overlap between member
084         and non-member distributions shows the robustness of EM-MIA and the utility of evaluating
085         on diverse conditions when developing a new MIA method.

      2     BACKGROUND
089
090   2.1    M EMBERSHIP I NFERENCE ATTACK FOR LLM S
091
092   Membership inference attack (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022) is a binary classifi-
093
      cation task that identifies whether or not a given data point has been seen during model training:
      member vs non-member. Given a target language model M trained on an unknown Dtrain , MIA
094
      predicts a membership label of each instance x in a test dataset Dtest whether x in Dtrain or not,
095
      by computing a membership score f (x; M) and thresholding this score. By adjusting a threshold,
096   MIA can control the trade-off between the true positive rate and the false positive rate. The MIA
097   performance is typically evaluated with two metrics: AUC-ROC and TPR@low FPR (Carlini et al.,
098   2022; Mireshghallah et al., 2022).
099
      Most existing MIA methods are based on the assumption that the target model memorizes (or over-
100
      fits) training data. In general, members will have a lower loss (Yeom et al., 2018), which is the
101
      average log-likelihood (or perplexity) of target text with respect to a target LLM, compared to non-
102   members. Likelihood Ratio Attacks (LiRAs) (Ye et al., 2022) perform difficulty calibration using
103   a reference model (Carlini et al., 2022), a compression method (Carlini et al., 2021), or the aver-
104   age loss from neighbors (Mattern et al., 2023). Min-K% (Shi et al., 2023) calculates the average
105   log-likelihood for tokens with the lowest likelihoods. Min-K%++ (Zhang et al., 2024) improves
106   Min-K% by normalizing each token’s log probability. ReCaLL (Xie et al., 2024) uses the ratio of
107   the conditional log-likelihood to the unconditional log-likelihood as the membership score. ReCaLL
      is described in detail in §2.4.

      2.2   W HY MIA FOR LLM S IS C HALLENGING
109
110   Although MIA has been studied in machine learning for several years, it remains especially chal-
111   lenging for LLMs (Duan et al., 2024; Meeus et al., 2024b). Due to the massive training dataset
112   size, each instance is used in training only a few times, often just once (Lee et al., 2021), making
113   it difficult to leave a footprint on the model. Moreover, there is inherent ambiguity in the defini-
114   tion of membership because texts are often repeated and partially overlap each other in the original
115   form or with a minor difference even after the rigorous preprocessing of decontamination and dedu-
116
      plication (Kandpal et al., 2022; Tirumala et al., 2024). The membership boundary becomes even
      fuzzier (Shilov et al., 2024) if semantically similar paraphrases (Mattern et al., 2023; Mozaffari
117
      & Marathe, 2024) beyond lexical matching based on n-gram are considered. Traditional MIA ap-
118
      proaches in machine learning literature (Shokri et al., 2017; Ye et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022)
119   based on training shadow models on non-overlapping data from the same data distribution, model
120   architecture, and training algorithm as the target model are infeasible for LLMs considering high
121   computational costs and unknown training specifications.
122
123
      2.3   E VALUATION OF MIA S FOR LLM S
124
125   Common MIA benchmarks such as WikiMIA (Shi et al., 2023) use a time cutoff based on model
126   release dates and time information of documents (Shi et al., 2023; Meeus et al., 2024a) to ensure
127   somewhat membership labels. Besides, recent studies (Duan et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024; Meeus
128   et al., 2024b; Maini et al., 2024) question whether several MIAs that perform well on these bench-
129   marks are truly conducting membership inference, as detecting distributional (often temporal) shifts
130   alone may be sufficient to achieve high benchmark performance. Thus, they advocate for the use
131
      of datasets with a random train-test split such as MIMIR (Duan et al., 2024), which is derived from
      the PILE dataset (Gao et al., 2020), for MIA evaluation. However, none of the existing methods
132
      significantly outperforms random guessing in this setting. Although this setting seems theoreti-
133
      cally appropriate for evaluating MIA, there is no truly held-out in-distribution dataset in reality
134   because LLMs are usually trained with all available data sources. In other words, it is difficult to
135   find in-distribution non-member examples, and it is nearly impossible to completely eliminate the
136   distribution shift between training and test data at inference time.
137
      Once an MIA benchmark, a pair of models and datasets, is released, it becomes difficult to prevent
138
      MIAs from exploiting information about the benchmark settings, such as how the dataset has been
139
      constructed. MIAs may exploit existing biases on datasets regardless of their intended purpose. To
140   evaluate the true generalizability of MIAs in real-world scenarios, we should consider situations
141   where we have minimal knowledge about the target language model, training data, and test data.
142   Several ongoing attempts (Meeus et al., 2024b; Eichler et al., 2024) aim to reproduce setups that
143   closely resemble practical MIA scenarios. Our work is an additional effort in this direction. We pro-
144   pose a robust MIA method and evaluate it on our benchmark, which simulates various combinations
145   of training and test data distribution.

147   2.4   R E C A LL: A SSUMPTIONS AND L IMITATIONS
148
149   ReCaLL (Xie et al., 2024) uses the ratio between the conditional log-likelihood of a target data point
150   x given a non-member prefix p as a context and the unconditional log-likelihood of x by an LLM
151   M as a membership score, based on the observation that the distribution of ReCaLL scores for
152
      members and non-members diverges when p is a non-member prefix: formally, ReCaLLp (x; M) =
      LL(x|p;M)
153    LL(x;M) (we may omit M later, for brevity), where LL is the average log-likelihood over tokens
154   and the prefix p1 is a concatenation of non-member data points pi : p = p1 ⊕ p2 ⊕ · · · ⊕ pn . The
155   intuition is that the log-likelihood of members drops a lot when conditioned with non-members as
156   a in-context learning point of view Akyürek et al. (2022), while the log-likelihood of non-members
157   does not change much.
158   ReCaLL significantly outperforms other MIA approaches by a large margin. For instance, ReCaLL
159   exceeds 90% AUC-ROC on WikiMIA (Shi et al., 2023) beating previous state-of-the-art AUC-ROC
160

163   why and when ReCaLL works well.

165   In Xie et al. (2024), non-members pi are randomly selected among non-members in a test dataset and
166
      excluded from the test dataset without validation, based on strong assumptions that (1) the ground
      truth non-members are available and (2) all of them are equally effective as a prefix. However, the
167
      held-out ground truth non-members, especially from the same distribution of the test set, may not
168
      always be available at inference time. This aligns with the discussion in the previous section §2.3.
169   Indeed, finding non-members could be difficult (Villalobos et al., 2022; Muennighoff et al., 2024)
170   since training data of LLMs are ever-growing by gathering all crawlable data. The solution to secure
171   non-members in Xie et al. (2024) is generating a synthetic prefix using GPT-4o. However, according
172   to their implementation, this method still relies on non-member test data as seed data.
173
      Selecting non-members from the test dataset makes ReCaLL preferable and unfair compared to other
174
      MIAs that do not utilize any non-member test data. It partially explains why simple random selection
175   reasonably works well. The ablation study from Xie et al. (2024) to demonstrate the robustness of
176   the random prefix selection ironically reveals that ReCaLL’s performance can be damaged when we
177   do not have known non-member data points from the test dataset distribution. Using non-members
178   from a different domain (e.g., GitHub vs. Wikipedia) significantly degrades ReCaLL’s performance,
179   sometimes even worse than Min-K%++. In other words, ReCaLL would not generalize well to test
180   data from a distribution different from that of the prefix. The similarity between the prefix and test
181   data also matters. Another ablation study shows a variance between different random selections, im-
182   plying that random prefix selection is not consistent and that all data points are not equally effective
183
      for the prefix.

185   3   O BSERVATION : F INDING A B ETTER P REFIX
186
187   In this section, we rigorously investigate how much ReCaLL’s performance is sensitive to the choice
188   of a prefix and particularly how much it can be compromised without given non-member data.
189   We define a prefix score r(p) as the effectiveness of p as a prefix in discriminating memberships,
190   particularly when using this prefix for ReCaLL. In the Oracle setting where ground truth labels of
191   all examples in a test dataset Dtest are available, we can calculate a prefix score by measuring the
192   performance of ReCaLL with a prefix p on a test dataset Dtest using ground truth labels and a MIA
193   evaluation metric such as AUC-ROC. While a prefix could be any text, we calculate prefix scores
194
      for all examples in a test dataset Dtest by using each data point as a standalone prefix.
195   As an initial analysis, we conduct experiments using the WikiMIA (Shi et al., 2023) dataset with a
196   length of 128 as a target dataset and Pythia-6.9B (Biderman et al., 2023) as a target LM. Figure 1(a)
197   displays the distribution of prefix scores measured by AUC-ROC for members and non-members.
198   Consistent with the results from Xie et al. (2024), ReCaLL works well if a prefix is a non-member
199   and does not work well if a prefix is a member. Prefix scores of members are smaller than 0.7, and
200
      most of them are close to 0.5, which is the score of random guessing. Prefix scores of non-members
      are larger than 0.5, and most of them are larger than 0.7. This clear distinguishability suggests using
201
      a negative prefix score as a membership score.
202
203   Figure 1(b) displays ROC curves of MIA when negative prefix scores measured by different met-
204   rics are used as membership scores. We use AUC-ROC, Accuracy, and TRP@k%FPR with
205   k ∈ {0.1, 1, 5, 10, 20} as metrics. Using AUC-ROC-based prefix scores as membership scores
206
      achieves 98.6% AUC-ROC, which is almost perfect and the highest among other metrics.
207   Without access to non-members (or data points with high prefix scores), ReCaLL’s performance
208   could be significantly lower. Given the wide spectrum of prefix scores for even non-members, the
209   effectiveness of each data point varies, and the choice of data points for a prefix can be crucial,
210   although using a concatenation of multiple data points as a prefix reduces variance. In other words,
211   we can expect better MIA performance by carefully selecting the prefix. Ultimately, it is desirable
212
      to find an optimal prefix p without any information or access to given ground truth non-member data
      points on the test set.
213

      we describe how our method addresses this problem by iteratively updating membership scores and

239   4   P ROPOSED M ETHOD : EM-MIA
240
241   We target the realistic MIA scenario where test data labels are unavailable. We measure a prefix
242   score by how ReCaLLp on a test dataset Dtest aligns well with the current estimates of membership
243   scores f on Dtest denoted as S(ReCaLLp , f, Dtest ). More accurate membership scores can help
244   compute more accurate prefix scores. Conversely, more accurate prefix scores can help compute
245   more accurate membership scores. Based on this duality, we propose an iterative algorithm to refine
246   membership scores and prefix scores via an Expectation-Maximization algorithm, called EM-MIA,
247
      to perform MIA with minimal assumptions on test data (§2.4).
248   Algorithm 1 summarizes the overall procedure of EM-MIA. We begin with an initial assignment of
249   membership scores using any existing off-the-shelf MIA method such as Loss (Yeom et al., 2018) or
250   Min-K%++ (Zhang et al., 2024) (Line 1). We calculate prefix scores r(p) using membership scores
251   and then update membership scores f (x) using prefix scores. The update rule of prefix scores (Line
252   3) and membership scores (Line 4) is a design choice. We repeat this process iteratively until con-
253
      vergence (Line 5). Since EM-MIA is a general framework, all components, including initialization,
      score update rules, and stopping criteria, are subject to modification for further improvement.
254

285   (Dnm ), and a dataset of instances without any membership information (Dunk ). For example, Dm

294   5   N EW B ENCHMARK : OLM O MIA
295
296   EM-MIA works well and even performs almost perfectly on some benchmarks such as
297   WikiMIA (Shi et al., 2023) (§7.1), while it does not work well on other datasets such as
298   MIMIR (Duan et al., 2024) similar to other methods. We want to know why this is the case and
299   what are the conditions for success. To answer these questions, we develop a new benchmark using
300   OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024), which is a series of fully open language models pre-trained with
301   Dolma (Soldaini et al., 2024) dataset. OLMo provides intermediate model checkpoints and an index
302
      to get which data has been used for each training step, which are valuable resources to construct
      an MIA benchmark. We will share our implementation and resulting benchmark datasets. Like us,
303
      anyone can create their own benchmark on their purpose by modifying our implementation.
304
305   Figure 2 illustrates the basic setup of the OLMoMIA benchmark. Specifically, we use checkpoints
306   of OLMo-7B trained with 100k, 200k, 300k, and 400k steps as target models (c.f., one epoch is
307   slightly larger than 450k steps). Then, we consider any training data before 100k steps as members
308
      and any training data between 400k and 450k steps as non-members. However, we should keep in
      mind that there could still be some ambiguity in membership despite the effort of deduplication, as
309
      discussed in §2.2. Based on this setup, we have multiple settings with different samplings.

      Based on clustering, we control the degree of overlap between the distribution of members and
325   non-members, resulting in different difficulties. First, we sample enough numbers of members and
326   non-members (in our case, 50k each). Next, we map sampled data into embedding vectors. We use
327   NV-Embed-v2 model (Lee et al., 2024), which was the first rank on the MTEB leaderboard (Muen-
328   nighoff et al., 2022) as of Aug 30, 2024, for the embedding model. Then, we perform K-means
329   clustering (Lloyd, 1982) for members and non-members separately (in our case, we use K = 50).
330
      To prevent datasets from degeneration that have duplicates with a very minor difference, we remove
331   data points closer than a certain distance threshold with other points within a cluster in a greedy
332   manner to guarantee all data points in the same cluster are not too close to each other. Empirically,
333   we observed that most pair distances measured by cosine distance range from 0.8 to 1.2, so we set
334   0.6 as the minimum inter-distance for deduplication.
335
      For the Easy setting, we pick the farthest pair of a member cluster and a non-member cluster and
336
      pick instances farthest from the opposite cluster. For the Hard setting, we pick the closest pair of
337   a member cluster and a non-member cluster and pick instances closest to the opposite cluster. For
338   the Medium setting, we pick the pair of a member cluster and a non-member cluster with a median
339   distance and randomly sampled instances within each cluster.
340
      Additionally, we have two additional settings derived from Random and Hard: we merge members
341
      from Random and non-members from Hard to create the Mix-1 setting, and we merge members
342
      from Hard and non-members from Random to create the Mix-2 setting. Mix-1 aims to simulate the
343   case where test data come from a single cluster. Thus, any cluster might be fine, but we choose Hard
344   with no specific reason. These settings cover almost all possible inclusion relationships between
345   members and non-members.
346
      We provide formal equations of the above descriptions in Appendix E. For each difficulty level, we
347
      have two splits based on the maximum sequence length of 64 and 128. For each dataset, we balance
348
      the number of members and non-members to 500 each to make the total size 1000.

      6.2.1    R E C A LL- BASED BASELINES
379
380   As explained in §2.4, the original ReCaLL (Xie et al., 2024) uses labeled data from the test dataset,
381   which is unfair to compare with the above baselines and ours. More precisely, pi in the prefix
382   p = p1 ⊕ p2 ⊕ · · · ⊕ pn are known non-members from the test set Dtest , and they are excluded
383   from the test dataset for evaluation, i.e., Dtest ′ = Dtest \ {p1 , p2 , · · · , pn }. However, we measure
384   the performance of ReCaLL with different prefix selection methods to understand how ReCaLL is
385
      sensitive to the prefix choice and use it as a reference instead of a direct fair comparison.
386   Since changing the test dataset every time for different prefixes does not make sense and makes the
387   comparison even more complicated, we keep them in the test dataset. A language model tends to
388   repeat, so LL(pi |p; M) ≃ 0. Because LL(pi |p; M) ≪ 0, ReCaLLp (pi ; M) ≃ 0. It is likely to
389   ReCaLLp (pi ; M) ≪ ReCaLLp (x; M) for x ∈ Dtest \ {p1 , p2 , · · · , pn }, meaning that ReCaLL
390   will classify pi as a non-member. The effect would be marginal if |Dtest | ≫ n. Otherwise, we
391
      should consider this when we read numbers in the result table.
392   We have four options for choosing pi : Rand, RandM, RandNM, and TopPref. Rand randomly se-
393   lects any data from Dtest . RandM randomly selects member data from Dtest . RandNM randomly
394   selects non-member data from Dtest . TopPref selects data from Dtest with the highest prefix scores
395   calculated with ground truth labels the same as §3. All options except Rand partially or entirely use
396   labels in the test dataset. For all methods using a random selection (Rand, RandM, and, RandNM),
397
      we execute five times with different random seeds and report the average.
398   The original ReCaLL (Xie et al., 2024) is similar to RandNM, except they report the best score
399   after trying all different n values, which is again unfair. The number of shots n is an important
400   hyper-parameter determining performance. A larger n generally leads to a better MIA performance
401   but increases computational cost with a longer p. We fix n = 12 since it provides a reasonable
402   performance while not too expensive.
403   Other simple baselines without using any labels are Avg
                                                             1
                                                                 P and AvgP, which average ReCaLL
404   scores over all data points in Dtest : Avg(x) = |Dtest   |   p∈Dtest ReCaLLp (x) and AvgP(p) =
405      1
               P
      |Dtest |  x∈Dtest ReCaLLp (x). The intuition is that averaging will smooth out ReCaLL scores with
406
      a non-discriminative prefix while keeping ReCaLL scores with a discriminative prefix without ex-
407   actly knowing discriminative prefixes.

      6.3     EM-MIA
411
      As explained in §4, EM-MIA is a general framework where each component can be tuned for im-
412
      provement, but we use the following options as defaults based on results from preliminary experi-
413   ments. Overall, Min-K%++ performs best among baselines without ReCaLL-based approaches, so
414   we use it as a default choice for initialization. Alternatively, we may use ReCaLL-based methods
415   that do not rely on any labels like Avg, AvgP, or Rand. For the update rule for prefix scores, we
416   use AUC-ROC as a default scoring function S. For the update rule for membership scores, we use
417   negative prefix scores as new membership scores. For the stopping criterion, we repeat ten iterations
418   and stop without thresholding by the score difference since we observed that membership scores and
419   prefix scores converge quickly after a few iterations. We also observed that EM-MIA is not sensitive
420   to the choice of the initialization method and the scoring function S and converges to similar results.
421
      Ablation study on different initializations and scoring functions can be found in Appendix A.

      7.3   C OMPUTATIONAL C OSTS
517
      MIAs for LLMs only do inference without any additional training, so they are usually not too ex-
518   pensive. Therefore, MIA accuracy is typically prioritized over computational costs as long as it
519   is reasonably feasible. Nevertheless, maintaining MIAs’ computational costs within a reasonable
520   range is important. Computations on all our experiments with the used datasets (WikiMIA and OL-
521   MoMIA) were manageable even in an academic setting. We compare computational complexity
522   between EM-MIA and other baselines (mainly, ReCaLL) and describe how computational costs of
523   EM-MIA can be further reduced in Appendix C.
524
525   8     C ONCLUSION
526
527   We introduce a novel MIA method for LLMs called EM-MIA that iteratively updates membership
528   scores and prefix scores via an Expectation-Maximization algorithm for better membership inference
529   based on the observation of their duality. EM-MIA significantly outperforms ReCaLL even without
530   strong assumptions that ReCaLL relies on and achieves state-of-the-art performance on WikiMIA.
531   EM-MIA is easily tunable with several design choices, including initialization, score update rules,
532   and stopping criteria, allowing the application to MIA in different conditions and providing room for
533   further improvement. We create a new benchmark for detecting pre-training data of LLMs named
534
      OLMoMIA to better understand the conditions under which EM-MIA works with a comprehensive
      evaluation. It turns out that EM-MIA robustly performs well for all settings except when the distri-
535
      butions of members and non-members are almost identical, resulting in none of the existing methods
536
      being better than random guessing. We provide our thoughts on promising future research directions
537   in Appendix D.

      C    C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
848
849
      EM-MIA is a general framework in that the update rules for prefix scores and membership scores
      can be designed differently (as described in §4), and they determine the trade-off between MIA ac-
850
      curacy and computational costs. For the design choice described in Algorithm 1 that was used in
851
      our experiments, EM-MIA requires a pairwise computation LLp (x) for all pairs (x, p) once, where
852   x, p ∈ Dtest . These values are reused to calculate the prefix scores in each iteration without recom-
853   putation. The iterative process does not require additional LLM inferences. The time complexity of
854   EM-MIA is O(D2 L2 ), where D = |Dtest | and L is an average token length of each data on Dtest ,
855   by assuming LLM inference cost is quadratic to the input sequence length due to the Transformer
856   architecture. In this case, EM-MIA does not have other tuning hyperparameters, while Min-K% and
857   Min-K%++ have K and or ReCaLL has n. This is more reasonable since validation data to tune
858   them is not given.
859   Of course, the baselines other than ReCaLL (Loss, Ref, Zlib, Min-K%, and Min-K%++) only
860   compute a log-likelihood of each target text without computing a conditional log-likelihood with
861   a prefix, so they are the most efficient: O(DL2 ) time complexity. Since ReCaLL uses a long pre-
862   fix consisting of n non-member data points, its time complexity is O(D(nL)2 ) = O(n2 DL2 ).
863   According to the ReCaLL paper, they sweep n from 1 to 12 to find the best n, which means
      O((12 + 22 + · · · + n2 )DL2 ) = O(n3 DL2 ). Also, in some cases (Figure 3 and Table 7 in their


                                                                                  16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      paper), they used n = 28 to achieve a better result. In theory, it may seem EM-MIA does not
865   scale well with respect to D. Nevertheless, the amount of computation and time for EM-MIA with
866   D ∼ 1000 is not significantly larger than ReCaLL, considering the n factor.
867
868
      Moreover, ReCaLL requires O(n2 ) times larger memory than others including EM-MIA, so it may
      not be feasible for hardware with a small memory. In this sense, EM-MIA is more parallelizable,
869
      and we make EM-MIA faster with batching. Lastly, there is room to improve the time complexity
870
      of our method. We have not explored this yet, but for example, we may compute ReCaLL scores on
871   a subset of the test dataset to calculate prefix scores as an approximation of our algorithm. We left
872   improving the efficiency of EM-MIA as future work.

      While our paper focuses on detecting pre-training data with the gray-box access of LLMs where
      computing the probability of a text from output logits is possible, many proprietary LLMs are usu-
