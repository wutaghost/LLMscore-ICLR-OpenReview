010                                          Abstract
011
012           Efficient encoding and representation of large 3D molecular structures with
013           high fidelity is critical for biomolecular design applications. Despite this,
014           many representation learning approaches restrict themselves to modeling
015
              smaller systems or use coarse-grained approximations of the systems, for
              example modeling proteins at the resolution of amino acid residues rather
016
              than at the level of individual atoms. To address this, we develop quan-
017
              tized auto-encoders that learn atom-level tokenizations of complete pro-
018           teins, RNA and small molecule structures with reconstruction accuracies
019           well below 1 Angstrom. We demonstrate that a simple Mamba state space
020           model architecture is efficient compared to an SE(3)-invariant IPA architec-
021           ture, reaches competitive accuracies and can scale to systems with almost
022           100,000 atoms. The learned structure tokens of bio2token may serve as the
023           input for all-atom generative models in the future.

026   1   Introduction
027
028   Background. Biomolecular structures can be represented as 3D point clouds, where each
029   point corresponds to a chemical entity such as an atom, a functional group, or a set of atoms
030   that make up larger molecular entities like side-chains and nucleotide bases. Generative
031   modeling of these structures, especially for large biomolecules, often employs coarse-grained
032   representations to manage complexity. Popular machine learning methods include denois-
033
      ing diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) and language models. Diffusion approaches
      generate 3D structures and features at varying levels of detail, from individual atoms
034
      to residues. Notably, DDPMs have been used to generate atomistic conformers of small
035
      molecules (Hoogeboom et al., 2022) and design ligands for protein pockets (Schneuing et al.,
036   2022). Applying denoising diffusion at atomistic resolution to large structures like proteins
037   is computationally challenging. Recent models like RFDiffusion- All-atom mitigate this by
038   diffusing only the four-atom protein backbone and reconstructing side-chains with a sepa-
039   rate inverse folding model (Krishna et al., 2024; Dauparas et al., 2022). Similarly, language
040   model approaches such as ESM-3 (Hayes et al., 2024) rely on coarse-grained representations
041   at the residue level for their core modules. Despite this coarse-graining, the capacity of
042   these models to process large proteins and protein-ligand complexes is still limited.
043   An effective atomic-resolution representation model for large molecules would have to be
044   able to reason over long-range data inputs. This is because many atoms can have critical
045   interactions with atoms that are distal in linear sequence. However, scaling traditional
046   вАЬworkhorseвАЭ architectures such as transformers and graph-based models to accommodate
047   such long sequences has been challenging. Here, we leverage Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), a
048   selective structured state space model, to replace transformer modules in structure tokenizer
049   models and increase the resolution to all atoms. Mamba has been developed for long-context
050
      modeling and has been demonstrated to efficiently model tasks with thousands to millions
      of tokens on conventional GPU hardware.
051
052   3D structure tokenization for generative modeling. Turning 3D structures into
053   discrete 1D sequences for generative language modeling, discrete diffusion or other
      downstream task has become a popular approach to biomolecular modeling. FoldSeek

      introduced the вАЭ3DiвАЭ structural interaction alphabet to convert three-dimensional pro-
055   tein backbone structures into one-dimensional sequences, facilitating faster structural

060   residues and using a 600M parameters transformer model. FoldToken (Gao et al., 2024)
061   and InstaDeep (Gaujac et al., 2024) also use QAEs with transformer and graph neural

063
      backbone reconstruction. Alphafold-3 (AF-3) (Abramson et al., 2024) generates all-atom
      structures using a token-guided diffusion network. For small molecules, approaches include
064
      one-hot encoding of coordinate digit strings (Flam-Shepherd & Aspuru-Guzik, 2023; Zholus
065
      et al., 2024) and SE(3)-invariant QAEs like Geo2Seq (Li et al., 2024) and MolStructTok
066   (Anonymous, 2024). Prior work predominantly relies on QAEs with various architectures
067   and features, incorporating symmetries through structural features or invariant point
068   attention. In contrast, our method uses neither engineered SE(3)-invariant features nor
069   does it employ invariant network architectures.
070
      What if we could efficiently encode and decode any biomolecule at the all-atom
071
      level?
072   In this work we present:
073
074         (i) A Mamba based quantized auto-encoder for all-atom biomolecular structures that
075             tokenizes 3D point clouds into 1D discrete tokens. We train small molecule-only,
076             protein-only, and RNA-only vocabularies mol2token, protein2token and rna2token.
077             We also train a unified tokenizer bio2token that encodes any of those biomolecules,
078             ranging from tens to tens of thousands of atoms, that would be challenging for
079             transformer-based methods to scale too.
080         (ii) A simple and compute efficient approach towards all-atom structure tokenization
081              that does not use SE(3) invariances. Our tokenizer models are lightweight in size
082              (1.2M parameters), with fast training and inference.

      1.1    Background: Transformers, State space models, and Mamba
085
086   Transformer. Transformers (Vaswani, 2017) use the attention mechanism to capture
087   long-range dependencies in sequences. Intuitively, the attention mechanism can be thought
088   of as a fully-connected graph neural network, with the update rule:
089
090                                            y = M (x)x,                                      (1)
091
092
      where x is the input       sequence, y is the latent representation, and M (x) =
093
      softmax Q(x)K(x)T is the attention matrix, or analogously adjacency matrix. The
      matrix multiplication formulation of attention makes it ideal for GPU processing, and has
094
      thus made transformers the workhorse of sequence modeling. However, due to the fully
095
      connected graph structure, transformers suffer from O(N 2 ) compute and memory costs
096   with respect to sequence length N .
097
098   Mamba. Recent alternatives such as deep structured state space models (SSM) (Gu
099
      et al., 2021; Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024) have gained traction in the field of
      sequence modeling thanks to their ability to overcome the quadratic bottleneck and scale
100
      to extremely long context lengths. The basic linear time-invariant (LTI) SSM is a linear
101
      recurrent neural network (RNN) with the update rule:
102
103                                  ht = AhtвИТ1 + Bxt ,      yt = Cht ,                         (2)
104
105   where x and y are the input and output sequences, respectively, h is the RNN state, and
106   A, B, C are learnable parameters. This linear recurrence can be unrolled across time and
      computed equivalently as a convolution y = K вИЧ x, where K = CB, CAB, ..., CAN вИТ1 B .

      2    Methods
128
129
      Our structure tokenizer model is a QAE and Fig. 1 provides an overview of the process.
130
      The steps can be broken down into:
131
132        (i) Representation of biomolecular systems as 3D point clouds
133
134       (ii) Encoding atom positions into latent vectors
135       (iii) Quantizing these latent vectors into tokens
136
137       (iv) Decoding these tokens back into a 3D point cloud
138
139   Tokenization of 3D point clouds. A biomolecular structure X of N heavy atoms is rep-
140
      resented as a X вИИ RN √Ч3 point cloud. This point cloud is atom identity agnostic and carries
      no information about residue or atom type. In general, AEs compress the input to a down-
141
      sampled latent space representation Z, where Z вИИ Rn√Чd , with n < N , and reconstruct the
142
      original input according to a loss function L(X, XћГ). The first module of the AE is the encoder
143
      network encќЄ (X) = Z, the second module is a decoder network decѕИ (Z) = XћГ. In this work,
144
      we want to maintain the all-atom resolution, and encќЄ (X) does not compress the input:
145   n = N . It can be regarded as a transformation of the input into a lossless latent space. The
146   compression in our QAE happens in the subsequent quantization of Z into discrete values Q
147   as the purpose of a tokenizer is to map continuous data to discrete unique IDs, defined by a
148   vocabulary. The latent space of a вАЭperfectвАЭ lossless auto-encoder (L(X, XћГ) = 0) is a perfect
149   tokenizer if that latent space was discrete. This is usually achieved through a quantization
150   network quantѕХ (ZћГ), otherwise known as a tokenizer model. This tokenizer model can be op-
151   timized with the same reconstruction loss L(X, XћГ). This tokenizer model keeps a one-to-one
152   correspondence between the positions of input atoms, tokens and reconstructed atoms.
153
      Quantization. Quantization networks learn discrete codebooks, in other words a vo-
154
      cabulary, of the training data. A common approach is vector-quantization (VQ) (Gray,
155
      1984), e.g. ESM-3вАЩs tokenizer is VQ-based. VQ is notoriously hard to optimize and tends
156   to suffer from codebook collapse. In this paper, we use a recently proposed simplified
157   alternative, Finite-Scalar Quantization (FSQ), which produces a more efficient coverage
158   of the codebook (Mentzer et al., 2023). FSQ has been shown to be easier to train, whilst
159   maintaining similar levels of expressiveness as VQ-VAEs. FSQ projects the input into a
160   hypercube of integer length L and dimensions D (where D < 8 usually). The projected
161   input point in the hypercube is then rounded to the nearest integer set {0, 1, ..., L}. The
      final code/token is the product of all integer coordinates in the hypercube.

185   3.1   Datasets
186
187   An overview of all training and test data is provided in the Appendix table 3. In a pre-
188   processing step all point clouds of the biomolecular structures are translated to be centered
189   at the zero-origin.
190
      Small molecules: Small molecules, typically organic molecules below a 500 Dalton weight,
191
      are not static. At standard temperatures and pressures they take on various 3D structural
192   conformations, each having a specific conformational energy. We used the вИЗ2 DFT dataset
193   (Khrabrov et al., 2024) of 1.9M small molecules with a total of 16M simulated structural
194   conformations as a source of data. This dataset provides train and test splits for multiple
195   levels of generalizability: a test-conformer split of unseen conformations of molecules, a
196   test-structure split of unseen molecules and all their conformations, and a test-scaffold split
197   of unseen scaffold classes of molecules and their conformations. The minimum number of
198   heavy atoms in this dataset is 8 and the maximum is 27.
199   Proteins: We prototype and run various hyperparameter studies on the CATH 4.2 dataset
200   of 18k protein structures from the PDB, with train-test splits on the CATH topology classi-
201   fications as defined by Ingraham et al. (2019). This dataset comprises proteins of 40 to 500
202   amino acids in length, for a minimum and a maximum of 282 and 4,173 heavy atoms. We also
203   tested on the CASP14 and CASP15 datasets, to compare to the values reported by ESM-3.
204   CASP14 and 15 structures were published after CATH4.2 and are thus not contained in 4.2.
205   CASP14 and 15 contain proteins up to 2,265 residues in length with the biggest structure
206
      having 18,042 heavy atoms. To train the large bio2token model we leverage the Alphafold
      database (AFDB). We use a random sub-set of 100k clusters from FoldSeekвАЩs sequence-
207
      structure clusters (Barrio-Hernandez et al., 2023), and collect one structure per cluster.
208
209   RNA: We train on RNA3DB, which splits the RNA structures in the PDB into sequence-
210   based and structural homology classes (Szikszai et al., 2024). The structures span a range
211   of 2 to 4,450 nucleic acids in lengths with 42 to 95,518 heavy atoms. For training efficiency,
212
      we limit the training dataset to structures with maximum 10, 000 sequence length, but run
      inference on all lengths of the test set.
213
214   Generalisation to complexes: We test bio2token at inference time on multi-chain com-
215   plexes and protein-RNA complexes. Note that neither multi-chain nor mixed complexes
      were included in the training.

      3.2     Architecture and Training details
217
218   Figure 1B and C gives an overview of each layer composition and full architecture. Each
219   layer of our encoder and decoder is a bi-directional implementation of the original Mamba
220   block 1 . Between consecutive Mamba blocks we apply a layer norm.
221
222   3.2.1    Architecture studies
223
224
      We ran various hyperparameter studies on a protein2token training with the CATH 4.2
      protein dataset. We tested the effects of varying encoder and decoder layers on the model
225
      performances in terms of RMSE and found that, given limited compute, 4 encoder layers and
226
      6 decoder layers to work best as a trade-off between model size and batch size. Additional
227   details on the effect of the number of encoder layers is provided in Appendix A.2. We
228   find the RMSE versus codebook size relationship to approximately follow a power law, see
229   Fig. 6 in the Appendix A.2. Ultimately, the choice of codebook size will be a trade-off
230   between accuracy and downstream modeling. A tokenizer with increasing vocabulary will
231   make downstream generation models harder. We decided, for all of our models to stick with
232   a codebook of 4096, which is in line with other published structure tokenizers, and allows
233   for a fair comparison.
234
235   Compressibility of tokens To test the compressibility of the token sequences we train
236   the tokenizer with an additional 1D convolutional layer before and after the quantizer net-
237   work (pooling after the encoder and up-sampling before the decoder). We compress with
238
      k вИИ [1, 2, 4], to shorten the all-atom sequence of length N to N/k. Results are in Appendix
      table 4. RMSE increases by a factor of 1.7 and 2.6 for the compression factors of 2 and 4 re-
239
      spectively. This is similar to previously reported compressibilities for residue-level structure
240
      tokenizers (Gaujac et al., 2024)
241
242
      Computational efficiency: Mamba versus Invariant Point Attention IPA is the
243
      most popular choice for structural modeling due to its SE(3) invariant properties. To com-
244   pare Mamba versus IPA, we train a QAE with an encoder of 2 transformer layers, and a
245   decoder with one IPA block with 4 recurrences (recycles). Although it is common in the
246   literature to use 8 recurrences for the IPA block, it would restrict all-atom training to short
247   proteins below approximately 100 residues given our GPU memory limit. With 4 recur-
248   rences we are able to fit one batch size of proteins of a maximum length of 2192 atoms
249   (approximately 220 residues) on the GPU. With an equivalent Mamba-based architecture
250   of 2 encoder and 4 decoder layers we can fit a batch size of 32 with maximum sequence
251   length 2192. For a fair comparison we train both; a Mamba-based version with a batch size
252
      of 1 and a batch size of 32. Accuracies and run times are listed in Appendix A.2 table 5.
      We find that training protein2token with an IPA-decoder takes 3 times per step compared
253
      to the Mamba QAE. For a fixed compute budget of 24 hours the IPA-decoder reaches an
254
      RMSE of 2.2AћК , compared to 0.8AћК for the Mamba QAE with a batch size of 1, and 0.6AћК
255   for the Mamba QAE with batch size of 32. Although the Mamba-based QAE does not
256   incorporate any rotational invariances in the architecture, itвАЩs learning efficiency is enough
257   to make up for this lack of implicit bias. From a practical standpoint we conclude that an
258   all-atom QAE tokenizer for large biomolecules is computationally intractable with standard
259   IPA and conventional GPU hardware.

      space. The theoretical analysis is in the Appendix A.3. We find that a typical ribosomal

458
      The tokenizer models learn to efficiently encode and decode point clouds of variable sizes,
459
      centered at the origin. Appendix A.5-Fig. 8 shows scatter plots for a sample of 10k points
460   across all structure point clouds with their absolute distance to the centre and their RMSE.
461   RMSE increases once the pointвАЩs distance to centre increases past the common size range
462   of the training structures. This can also be seen in Fig. 2F, where reconstructions deviate

464   molecules only span a few Angstroms and mol2token not surprisingly fails at reconstructing
465   macromolecules (see Appendix table 8, a mol2token reconstructed protein looks like a dense
466   point potato, not shown). protein2token and rna2token can tokenize RNAs and proteins as
467   they share similar point cloud sizes and densities. This motivates the combined tokenizer

      training of bio2token, which performs best for proteins, RNA and protein-RNA complexes
      as it has seen the most diverse point configurations during training.

      5     Discussion
479
480   In this study, we explored the potential of Mamba to encode high-resolution structures for
481   diverse biomolecules. Specifically, opting for a simple Mamba-based architecture, instead
482   of rotationally invariant IPA, allows us to scale trainings to large biomolecules that cannot
483   be trained on with the latter approach with conventional GPU hardware. In fact, we show
484   that reconstruction accuracies around 0.5 вИТ 0.6AћК from a vocabulary of 4096 is achievable
485   without any SE(3) invariance in either features, or network architecture. Our combined
      tokenizer, bio2token, demonstrates that encodings can be learned across different classes of


                                                   9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      macromolecules once encoded on the common atomistic resolution. The comparable small
487   amount of data (127,000 macromolecules in total) used in our trainings signals that all-atom
488   encoding might substantially enhance training efficiency, compared to more coarse-grained
489   encoding that lack atomistic detail, and leverages more information from the structures
490   sampled. Atomistic detail is important for many biomolecular design applications вАУ the
491   precise positioning of individual atoms within a protein or RNA molecule can significantly
492   impact its function and interactions with other molecules. Furthermore, the reliance on
493   separate models for different components of a molecule, (e.g., back-bones versus side-chains),
494   can introduce additional complexity and potential sources of error. Our work highlights
495
      the potential of leveraging models like bio2token to improve the design and modeling of
      biomolecules and biomolecular complexes.
496
497
498
      5.1   Limitations and future directions
499
      Although our model achieves low RMSE values, having low RMSE is not necessarily suffi-
500   cient to guarantee that the reconstructed molecule is chemically valid. Even small deviations
501   in decoded atom coordinates can result in structures with steric clashes, improper covalent
502   bonds, or improperly strained geometries. For example, as seen in Fig. 2F, our reconstructed
503   structures can deviate from valid molecular geometries, for example implying covalent bonds
504   where there should not be and vice-versa missing bonds where there should be. One po-
505   tential solution could be to train the model on additional data which may further lower
506   RMSEs to a level that produces more chemically valid biomolecules without having to hard
507   code constraints into the losses of our models. Alternatively, we could employ heuristic and
508
      physics based post-processing protocols like those used in Abramson et al. to help translate
      generated point clouds into valid molecules.
509
      Nonetheless, our work points towards Mamba-based architectures as a viable and promis-
510
      ing alternative to transformer-based methods for modeling atomic-resolution biomolecular
511   structures. In the quantized QAE form as presented here, our models could facilitate com-
512   patibility with language models and we leave the joint representation of atom identity and
513   coordinates to future work. The continuous embedding (no quantizer), combined with com-
514   pression, could provide useful latent spaces for methods like flow matching or diffusion. As
515   such, we anticipate that our work could connect to many potential downstream modeling
516   applications in chemistry and biology.
