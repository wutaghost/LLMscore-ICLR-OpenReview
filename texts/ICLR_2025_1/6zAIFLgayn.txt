009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Current multimodal large language models (MLLMs) often underperform on
012            mathematical problem-solving tasks that require fine-grained visual understand-
013            ing. The limitation primarily arises from inadequate perception of geometric
014            primitives during image-level contrastive pre-training (e.g., CLIP). Current ef-
015            forts to enhance MLLM performance have focused on scaling up mathematical
016
               visual instruction datasets and employing stronger LLM backbones, yet these ap-
               proaches often neglect persistent visual recognition errors in MLLMs. In this
017
               paper, we systematically evaluate the visual grounding capabilities of state-of-
018
               the-art MLLMs and uncover a negative correlation between their visual grounding
019            accuracy and problem-solving performance. Notably, even advanced models like
020            GPT-4o demonstrate a significant error rate (70%) when identifying geometric
021            entities, highlighting that fine-grained visual understanding remains a crucial bot-
022            tleneck in visual mathematical reasoning. To address this, we propose a novel ap-
023            proach, SVE-Math (Selective Vision-Enhanced Mathematical MLLM), featuring
024            a geometric-grounded vision encoder and a feature router that dynamically adjusts
025            the contribution of hierarchical visual feature maps. Our model recognizes accu-
026            rate visual primitives and generates precise visual prompts tailored to the language
027
               model’s reasoning needs. In experiments, SVE-Math-Deepseek-7B outperforms
               other 7B models by 7.7% on MathVerse and is compatible with GPT-4V on Math-
028
               Vista. Despite being trained on smaller datasets, SVE-Math-7B matches the per-
029
               formance of models trained on significantly larger datasets, evaluated on GeoQA.
030            Our findings provide critical insights for future research, highlighting the need for
031            more effective integration of fine-grained visual understanding in MLLMs. We
032            will release model weights, code, and instructions upon acceptance.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Visual information plays a crucial role in mathematical problem-solving, where diagrams and vi-
038   sual representations are integral to understanding and reasoning. While Large Language Models
039
      (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in textual mathematical reasoning (Yu et al.,
      2023; Ying et al., 2024; Azerbayev et al., 2023), their proficiency often diminishes when tasks re-
040
      quire integrating visual data. The challenge intensifies when precise comprehension of geometric
041
      primitives—basic elements such as lines, circles, angles, boundaries, and junctions—is necessary to
042   solve complex mathematical problems. Recent advancements in Multimodal Large Language Mod-
043   els (MLLMs) (Chen et al., 2022a; Liang et al., 2023; Kazemi et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023a; Zhang
044   et al., 2024b; Shi et al., 2024) have shown promise in addressing visual mathematical reasoning by
045   incorporating both textual and visual inputs. These models typically rely on large-scale mathemat-
046   ical visual instruction datasets (Zhang et al., 2024b; Shi et al., 2024; Kazemi et al., 2023), which
047   require MLLMs (OpenAI, 2023a;c; Su et al., 2023) to generate diverse descriptions for question-
048   answer pairs involving geometric elements. While these approaches enhance the reasoning capa-
049   bilities of MLLMs in the mathematical domain, they come with certain limitations. Constructing
050
      such datasets is time-consuming, labor-intensive, and requires substantial financial and human re-
      sources, often involving the use of advanced models like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2023c) to generate di-
051
      verse prompts for synthetic datasets.

      in accurately perceiving and grounding basic geometric primitives in mathematical diagrams. Our

101
      instructing MLLMs with fine-grained visual information, such as junction points and object loca-

105   formation interferes with problem-solving (see § A.5 for a case study). Moreover, their performance
106   is highly sensitive to the accuracy of visual cues. Providing inaccurate instructions, such as randomly
107   generated box locations, significantly decreases performance. Given the inherent uncertainty in de-
      tecting geometric primitives by GeoGLIP, our initial approach utilizes global pyramid feature maps,

      Our proposed SVE-Math has several key advantages. First, by enhancing the visual encoder to per-
      ceive geometric primitives, we directly tackle the root cause of geometrical visual recognition errors
113
      in mathematical reasoning tasks. Second, SVE-Math is efficient and practical, as it does not rely
114
      on the creation of large-scale instruction datasets or extensive human annotations. Third, our pro-
115   posed auxiliary visual encoder and connector can be seamlessly integrated into any existing MLLM,
116   enhancing its performance without modifying the reasoning components of language models.
117
118
      We evaluate SVE-Math on several public mathematical benchmarks, and experimental results
      demonstrate its superior performance compared to models of the same or even larger sizes. Specif-
119
      ically, our model outperforms other 7B-parameter models and achieves comparable results to ad-
120
      vanced 13B-parameter MLLMs, all while using a smaller-scale dataset for visual training (40K) and
121   60K + 110K for alignment and instruct learning, compared to the large 588K + 834K dataset used in
122   MAVIS (Zhang et al., 2024b). These results highlight the effectiveness of our approach and under-
123   score the importance of accurate visual perception in mathematical visual reasoning. In summary,
124   our contributions are as follows:
125
126          • We systematically identify and analyze the impact of visual recognition errors on the math-
127            ematical reasoning performance of MLLMs, highlighting the critical role of accurately
128            perceiving geometric primitives.
129          • We propose a novel method, SVE-Math, that enhances the visual perception capabilities
130            of MLLMs by integrating a geometric-awareness visual encoder trained on small-scale
131            box/pixel-level annotations, avoiding the need for large-scale instruction datasets.
132          • We design a connector mechanism featuring a feature router that effectively integrates the
133            relevant geometric visual information into the language model, improving performance
134
               without altering the reasoning components.
135          • GeoGLIP integrates seamlessly with diverse LLM backbones without requiring modifica-
136            tions to their reasoning components. Extensive experiments demonstrate that SVE-Math

               outperforms existing models of comparable and larger sizes on mathematical benchmarks.

139   2   R ELATED W ORK
140
141   Multimodal Large Language Models for Mathematics. Large Language Models (LLMs) have
142   recently garnered significant attention, with much research focused on text-based mathematical
143   problem-solving, expanding mathematical datasets and utilizing data augmentation (Yu et al., 2023;
144   Yue et al., 2023b; 2024; Luo et al., 2023). Meanwhile, advancements in vision-language align-
145   ment models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and BLIP (Li et al., 2022a), have significantly
146   progressed multimodal tasks, leading to the development of Multimodal Large Language Models
147
      (MLLMs) (Bai et al., 2023; Gemini Team, 2023; Ye et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024;
      Hu et al., 2024). With the rise of instruction-following LLMs, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) adopts a
148
      linear layer to directly project visual tokens into LLMs, while MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) resam-
149
      ples visual tokens into fixed-length tokens, reducing the computation cost.
150
151   Building on these advancements, researchers have started to explore visual mathematical problem-
152   solving using MLLMs. Unified frameworks like UniGeo (Chen et al., 2022a), UniMath (Liang et al.,
153
      2023), and GeomVerse (Kazemi et al., 2023) expand multimodal mathematical datasets and improve
      MLLM performance in geometry and diverse tasks. Leveraging current datasets, G-LLaVA (Gao
154
      et al., 2023a) constructed the Geo170K dataset, enhancing geometric problem-solving and surpass-
155
      ing GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023c) on the MathVista benchmark (Lu et al., 2023) with only 7B parame-
156   ters. GeoGPT4V (Cai et al., 2024a) further improved model performance on MathVista and Math-
157   Vision (Wang et al., 2024) by creating a high-quality geometric problem dataset using GPT-4 and
158   GPT-4V. MAVIS (Zhang et al., 2024b) specializes in mathematical tasks with a three-stage training
159   pipeline including a math-specific vision encoder, while Math-LLaVA (Shi et al., 2024) introduced
160   MathV360K, a large-scale dataset with high-quality images and diverse question-answer pairs to
161   improve multimodal mathematical reasoning. These math-specific MLLMs have shown promising
      performance across several benchmark datasets (Yue et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a).

180
181   Despite these advancements, MLLMs still face challenges in multimodal mathematical tasks, par-
182   ticularly due to limitations in visual perception. While CLIP remains a common choice for many
183   mathematical MLLMs and is known to benefit multimodal tasks, its limitations have also been iden-
184   tified. For instance, (Tong et al., 2024) examines ‘CLIP-blind pairs’, revealing that visually distinct
185   images are often misinterpreted as similar, highlighting systematic shortcomings in CLIP’s visual
186   perception. These findings underscore the need for more specialized visual encoding methods tai-
187   lored to mathematical contexts, as well as more rigorous evaluations of MLLMs’ visual capabilities.
188   Open-Set Object Detection. Open-set object detection identifies arbitrary classes using existing
189   bounding box annotations and language generalization. Methods like OV-DETR (Zareian et al.,
190   2021), ViLD (Gu et al., 2022), DetCLIP (Yao et al., 2022), and Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2024c)
191   integrate language models with detection frameworks to improve category-specific detection. How-
192   ever, these models often struggle with small-scale object detection due to insufficient fine-grained
193   visual understanding. GLIP (Li et al., 2022b) addresses this limitation by integrating textual infor-
194
      mation with visual region features early in the pipeline via a language-aware deep fusion mechanism,
      enhancing region-level embeddings. GLIP improves detection of smaller objects and demonstrates
195
      strong zero-shot capabilities. While GLIP’s potential has been explored in various fields (Surı́s
196
      et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023), its application to mathematical reasoning, particu-
197   larly in precise geometric entity description and fine-grained detail identification in mathematical
198   diagrams, remains largely unexplored. Our work extends these concepts, developing a geometric-
199   grounded language-image pre-training model (GeoGLIP) tailored for the unique demands of visual
200   mathematical reasoning.
201
      Junction and Boundary Detection. Junction and boundary detection are crucial in image process-
202
      ing and object recognition (Dollar et al., 2006; Maire et al., 2008; Parida et al., 1998), and can play
203   a pivotal role in mathematical reasoning with geometric diagrams. Junctions represent points where
204   lines intersect, and boundaries delineate object shapes. Traditional methods like Canny edge detec-
205   tion (Canny, 1986) and the Hough Transform (Duda & Hart, 1972) struggle with complex diagrams
206   and fine-grained details required for accurate mathematical reasoning. Recent deep learning ap-
207   proaches, such as junction detection networks (Huang et al., 2018), detect key points by considering
208   surrounding regions. Boundary detection models like Field of Junctions (FoJ) (Verbin & Zickler,
209   2021) use a bottom-up approach with ‘generalized M-junctions’ to detect contours and junctions.

215   SVE-Math integrates visual understanding of geometric primitives with textual analysis to enhance
      the model’s capability in solving mathematical problems involving visual elements. As illustrated in

      Fig. 2, our pipeline builds upon the LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023b) architecture (refer to §A.1), intro-
217   ducing key innovations in the GeoGLIP and visual feature connector. Feature maps from different
218   layers of the GeoGLIP encoder are processed through the connector, where a feature router optimally
219   integrates the feature pyramid into visual soft prompts by leveraging geometric information. These
220   visual prompts are then fused with CLIP vision tokens, either along the sequence dimension or the
221   channel dimension, and aligned with text embeddings via projection layers for visual understand-
222   ing. Since channel-wise fusion offers better computational efficiency and comparable performance
223   to sequence-based fusion in our experiments, we set channel-wise fusion as the default approach.

      4.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
371
372   Implementation Details. Our work follows a structured three-stage training pipeline, including
373   multi-task visual perception training for GeoGLIP, visual-language alignment, and mathematical
374   instruction tuning for MLLMs (refer to §A.6.1 for details). We fine-tuned our GeoGLIP model
375   using GLIP-T (Li et al., 2022b) as the pre-trained model, leveraging a combined dataset of 10,000
376   synthetic images, 20,672 images from FigureQA, and 9,426 images from the Geo170K training set.
377   Training is conducted on 8 A100 GPUs with a batch size of 32. The base learning rate is set to
      1 × 10−5 for the language backbone and 1 × 10−4 for all other parameters, and it is decreased by


                                                                                    7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      a factor of 0.1 at 67% and 89% of the total training steps. We employ the same data augmentation
379   strategies as GLIP, including random horizontal flipping and aspect ratio-preserving resizing with a
380   minimum size of 800 pixels.
381
382
      For multi-modal training, we freeze the GeoGLIP encoder. In Stage 2, we train only the projection
      layers to align diagram-language pairs. In Stage 3, we unfreeze both the projection layer and the
383
      LLM to perform comprehensive instruction-following tuning. We adopt LLaVA1.5-7B (Liu et al.,
384
      2023b) as the backbone of our MLLM, utilizing LLAMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b) as the language
385   model and a pretrained vision transformer (CLIP ViT-L) (Radford et al., 2021) and our GeoGLIP
386   as the visual encoders. Images are padded to squaresand resized to 448 × 448 pixels with a white
387   background for processing by CLIP, and to 1000 × 1000 pixels for processing by GeoGLIP. We train
388   SVE-Math for one epoch for cross-modal alignment and two epochs for instruction tuning on the
389   Geo170K(Gao et al., 2023a) dataset, evaluating the model on GeoQA (Gao et al., 2023a) and the
390   minitest set of MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024a). To further enhance model performance and evaluate
391   on MathVista (Lu et al., 2023), which encompasses a wider range of mathematical and visual tasks
392   including IQTest, PaperQA, and IconQA, we incorporate the open-source MathV360k (Shi et al.,
393
      2024) dataset. We train our model on MathV360k using a batch size of 16 for one epoch with an
      initial learning rate of 3 × 10−5 .
394
395   Evaluation Benchmarks. We assess our SVE-Math using three well-established public mathemat-
396   ical benchmarks, MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024a), GeoQA (Gao et al., 2023a), and MathVista (Lu
397   et al., 2023)). MathVerse focuses on assessing multi-modal mathematical problem-solving with a
398   combination of text and diagram-based reasoning tasks. GeoQA emphasizes geometric reasoning,
399
      where the model must interpret geometric shapes and solve related questions. MathVista includes a
      diverse set of mathematical and visual tasks, providing a comprehensive evaluation across various
400
      reasoning and problem-solving domains.
401
402   Evaluation Metrics. We adopt top-1 accuracy to evaluate our model on these benchmarks. Our
403   evaluation process follows the protocols defined by the respective datasets, where LLMs are used to
404   extract predicted answers from the model’s responses. Accuracy is determined by comparing these
405
      predicted answers against the corresponding ground truths.

466   visual tokens FCLIP using either channel-wise or sequence-wise fusion methods. We examine two

469   set. The summary is presented in Fig. 5b, with detailed top-1 accuracy listed in Fig. 5c. Specifically,

525   5     C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we mitigate the limitations of current mathematical MLLMs by identifying the sig-
528   nificant bottleneck caused by their inability to accurately perceive geometric primitives, which are
529   crucial for mathematical reasoning involving visual elements. We proposed SVE-Math, a novel
530   vision-centric approach that enhances mathematical visual reasoning by integrating a geometric-
531
      awareness visual encoder trained through multi-task objectives such as shape detection, junction
      detection, and boundary detection. Our method avoids the labor-intensive process of building large-
532
      scale mathematical visual instruction datasets, offering a more efficient and practical solution. By
533
      designing a feature router that dynamically adjusts the contribution of each visual cue, we generate
534   soft prompts that guide the language model toward better mathematical reasoning without over-
535   whelming it with redundant or irrelevant visual data. Extensive experiments across three public
536   mathematical benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of SVE-Math, as SVE-Math outperforms
537   similarly sized 7B-parameter models and achieves comparable results to advanced 13B-parameter
538   MLLMs, despite being trained on smaller datasets. We believe our work introduces a new per-
539   spective on solving mathematical problems in a visual context, emphasizing the critical role of
      fine-grained visual grounding and adaptive visual cueing mechanisms.
