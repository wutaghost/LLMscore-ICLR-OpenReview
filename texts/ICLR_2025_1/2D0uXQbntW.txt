029                                                        A BSTRACT
030
031               Understanding long videos, ranging from tens of minutes to several hours,
032               presents unique challenges in video comprehension. Despite the increasing impor-
033               tance of long-form video content, existing benchmarks primarily focus on shorter
034
                  clips. To address this gap, we introduce InfiniBench a comprehensive benchmark
                  for very long video understanding which presents 1)very long video duration, av-
035
                  eraging 52.59 minutes per video; 2) The largest number of question-answer pairs,
036
                  108.2K; 3) Diversity in questions that examine nine different skills and include
037               both multiple-choice questions and open-ended questions; 4) Memory questions,
038               such as Global Appearance that require remembering and tracking the visual as-
039               pects through the video. Using InfiniBench, we comprehensively evaluate existing
040               Large Multi-Modality Models (LMMs) on each skill, including the commercial
041               models such as GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Flash and the recent open-source mod-
042               els. The evaluation shows significant challenges in our benchmark. Our findings
043               reveal that even leading AI models like GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Flash face chal-
044               lenges in achieving high performance in long video understanding, with average
045
                  accuracies of just 56.01% and 43.32%, and average scores of 3.25 and 2.79 out
                  of 5, respectively. Qwen2-VL matches Gemini’s performance in the MCQ skills
046
                  but lags significantly in open-ended question tasks. We hope this benchmark will
047
                  stimulate the LMMs community towards long video and human-level understand-
048               ing. Our benchmark can be accessed at InfiniBench.

053   Recent Large Language Models (LLMs) (Li et al., 2023a; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023)
      have shown impressive progress in the Natural Language community. Inspired by the strong abilities

055   et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Maaz et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023)

057   such as image understanding and short video understanding. While current large multi-modality
058   models show some progress in video understanding, their abilities remain unclear for very long-
059   form video understanding.
060
      Long-form video understanding (Song et al., 2023; Regneri et al., 2013; Rohrbach et al., 2014; Awad
061   et al., 2017; 2018; 2020) not only challenges these models by increasing the number of images but
062   also contains more comprehensive information, making it a boundary-pushing task toward human-
063   level intelligence. For example, humans can link multiple events at different times and answer ques-
064   tions requiring a deep understanding of events or characters in a long video. Multi-modal models can
065   address these questions, requiring long-range temporal-spatial reasoning and strong vision-language
066   alignment abilities, potentially serving a wider range of AI applications.While the necessity of a
067   long-video understanding benchmark is evident, the current recent benchmarks are up to 17 min-
068   utes, however LVBench (Wang et al., 2024) introduces a 1-hour-long benchmark with 1,549 QA
069
      pairs.it only supports visual mode, whereas our benchmark examines the more challenging com-
      bined subtitle + visual understanding capability. Additionally, the scale of our benchmark is 70×
070
      larger than LVBench.. To fill this gap in comprehensive long video understanding, we propose In-
071
      finiBench, a comprehensive benchmark for very long-form video understanding. As shown in Tab 1,
072   InfiniBench is currently the video benchmark that has both the longest length (52.59 minutes) and
073   the largest number of question-answer (QA) pairs (108.2K). The video sources are movies and daily
074   TV shows, and the questions are designed based on multiple sources including video frames, video
075   scripts, and video summaries. As shown in Figure 1 , the QA pairs consist of nine carefully designed
076   types of questions, which mainly focus on human-centric aspects, including Summarization, Global
077   Appearance, Scene Transitions, Sequence of Actions by Each Character, Temporal Questions, Link-
078   ing Events, Deep Context Understanding, Movie Spoiler Questions, Local Visual and contextual
079   Questions. The questions include multiple-choice questions (MCQs) and open-ended questions. We
080
      report the accuracy for MCQs and the GPT-4o rating score for open-ended questions. The annotation
      process is mainly done by an automatic pipeline using GPT-4, which includes proposing questions
081
      and generating answers. To prevent hallucinations and gather sufficient information for generating
082
      QA pairs, we used various sources of information including video frames which are used in the
083   global appearance skill, video transcripts, and video summaries.
084
085   Based on InfiniBench, we evaluate the current state-of-the-art MLLMs capable of handling very
086
      long videos, including the open-source models Movie-Chat(Song et al., 2023), Llama-Vid(Li et al.,
      2023b), Large World Model(Liu et al., 2024),Qwen2-VL(Bai et al., 2023) and the commercial mod-
087
      els such as GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Flash.

090   finiBench, showing the unique challenges of our benchmark. (2) Experiments show that GPT-4o

092
      accuracies of just 56.01% and, and average scores of 3.25 out of 5.(3)Only Qwen2-VL surpasses
      Gemini in the MCQ skills but struggles in the open-ended.
093
094   (4) All models showed better performance in local skills compared to global skills. (5)The most
095   difficult skill in MCQ is character actions, while for open-ended questions, it is movies spoiler
096   questions that designed for human-centric video understanding.

181   3.1     DATA C OLLECTION
182
      We utilized two sources to obtain very long videos: Movies and TV shows, for Movies, we employed
183
      the MovieNet dataset (Huang et al., 2020). However, no dataset is available for complete TV shows,
184
      as TVQA(Lei et al., 2019) provides only short clips,Inspired by Goldfish (Ataallah et al., 2024b)
185   trick, we transformed the TVQA dataset from a collection of short clips into a long video dataset
186   by gathering and sequencing the clips corresponding to each episode, thereby reconstructing the
187   full episode frames. We obtained 924 full-length episodes from six different TV shows through
188   this modification. Consequently, MovieNet dataset(Huang et al., 2020), has only 296 movies which
189   had shots aligned with subtitles. Therefore, only these movies are included; we excluded the rest
190   from our benchmark. In addition, we relied on two extra data sources: the video summaries and
191   transcripts. For the TVQA dataset (Lei et al., 2019), the summaries from IMDB and the transcripts
192   were scraped for the 924 episodes. For the filtered MovieNet movies (296)(Huang et al., 2020), we
193
      obtained transcripts from the MovieNet annotations. However, since MovieNet (Huang et al., 2020)
      annotations do not include complete movie summaries, the missing summaries are scrapped from
194
      IMDB to obtain comprehensive movie summaries and transcripts for all filtered movies.
195
196   For spoiler skill, out of 296 movies in the MovieNet (Huang et al., 2020) dataset, we identified
197   147 movies with associated spoiler questions available on IMDb, totaling 806 questions. These
198   questions were meticulously collected and integrated into our benchmark dataset. Consequently,
199
      we directly adopted TVQA questions for the local skills by aggregating questions corresponding to
      clips from the same episode, ensuring multiple questions per episode. Notably, these questions in
200
      TVQA (Lei et al., 2019) exhibit a dual property encompassing visual and contextual dimensions.
201
      It’s pertinent to mention that these questions are exclusive to the TVQA dataset and have hitherto
202   remained unutilized for long video benchmark solely for analyzing short clips.

      4.1       E VALUATION M ETRICS
392
393   We employed distinct evaluation metrics appropriate for the two questions types: open-ended and
394   multiple-choice (MCQs). For MCQs, accuracy was the chosen metric, while for open-ended ques-
395   tions, we utilized a scoring system based on GPT-4-mini, ranging from 0 to 5. For MCQ, GPT-4-
396   mini is used to match the predicted answer with one of the options or to match with the “I don’t
397   know option”, that indicates there is no match or hallucination. See Sec. Evaluation Details in the
398   supplementary D for more details. For open-ended questions, GPT-4-mini evaluated the LLMs’ pre-
399   dictions based on multiple criteria: correctness, meaningfulness, proximity to the expected answer,
400
      presence of hallucinations, and completeness. Based on these criteria, GPT-4-mini generates a score
      ranging from 0 to 5, reflecting the overall quality of the response.
401

403   4.2       E VALUATION M ODELS S ETTING
404
405   In our evaluation, we evaluated two commercial models and nine open-source models.
406   GPT-4o. GPT-4o cannot natively process .mp4 video files. To work within its limitations, we
407   sampled the maximum of 250 frames from the video, followed by the subtitle text and the question.
408   Gemini-Flash 1.5. The Gemini-Flash 1.5 model, developed by Google (Gemini, 2024), Gemini
409   recently gained the capability to process .mp4 files, but since our benchmark videos lack audio, we
410   provided Gemini with the video file, followed by the subtitle file and the accompanying question.
411   Qwen2-VL supports processing up to 768 frames; however, this exceeds the memory capacity of
412
      a single A100 GPU (80 GB) and requires the use of multiple GPUs. To ensure a fair comparison
      with GPT-4o, we limit the maximum number of frames to 250. Subtitles are provided as additional
413
      context, concatenated with the question, to enhance input comprehension.
414
      InternVL2: For the configuration of InternVL2, the default number of frames was increased to
415   the maximum supported by the A100 GPU (80 GB), resulting in a limit of 128 frames. While
416   the model can accommodate more than 128 frames, this often leads to significant hallucinations
417   in its outputs. Performance was evaluated at different frame rates, specifically 16 and 128 frames.
418   Subtitles were incorporated as additional context alongside the input question to enhance the model’s
419   understanding. LLaVA-OneVision: This model is capable of processing both videos and images.
420   We investigated the maximum number of frames that can be processed using an A100 GPU (80
421   GB) without inducing hallucinations and evaluated the model’s performance at frame rates of 16
422   and 128. Subtitles were included as additional context, provided alongside the input question to
423
      enhance comprehension. Goldfish. we used the default model setting with number of retrieved
      videos k=3 and process the video in 0.5 fps as each small clip is 90 sec and the model sample 45
424
      from them which means 0.5 fps. LLama-vid. The LLaMA-VID model (Li et al., 2023b) accepts
425
      both video frames and subtitles. For our evaluation of the movies, we utilized our dataset with one
426   frame per second, accompanied by aligned subtitle shots. The model was evaluated using the default
427   settings without any modifications to the inference parameters. Large World Model (LWM). LWM
428   is efficiently optimized for execution on Google TPUs and has another version for GPUs. Our
429   evaluation is done using (NVIDIA A100), which allows for processing a maximum of 8 frames
430   per video. While this setup does not represent the optimal configuration for LWM, it was the most
431   feasible setting. LWM can accept only the video frames without the subtitles. Moviechat. The
      MovieChat model (Song et al., 2023) processes video frames without subtitles and operates in global

456
      In this section, we evaluate the state-of-the-art (SOTA) open-source and commercial models for
457
      long video understanding. First, we present the overall performance averaged across all nine skills.
458   Then, we delve into the specific skill performance detailed in Table 2, Overall performance. The
459   overall performance of different models on InfiniBench is shown in Table 2 (j). Three findings
460   can be observed: (1) All models’ is relatively lower than other benchmarks (e.g., Movie-chat,
461   MLVU,VideoMME benchmarks). This could be interpreted by the challenging nature of our skills
462   that require deep, long-term understanding. To further verify this point, we test our benchmark
463   on the most recent short-video models, e.g., MiniGPT4-video and LLaVA-NeXT-Interleave. We
464   argue that short-video models should suffer if the benchmark truly assesses long-video understand-
465   ing capabilities. In other words, the limited context captured by the short video models should
466
      not be enough to answer long reasoning queries. As shown in the table 2, MiniGPT4-video and
      LLaVA-NeXT-Interleave match lower than the random performance, which shows the effectiveness
467
      of our benchmark in assessing long reasoning capabilities. (2) GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) achieves
468
      the best performance on both multiple-choice and open-ended questions, with 56.01 accuracy (0-
469   100) and 3.25 GPT4-score (0-5). There is also a large performance gap between GPT-4o and other
470   open-source models which could be justified by the huge gap in the scale of the training data and
471   GPUs used in training these models.(3) Qwen2-VL demonstrated the strongest performance among
472   all open-source models, surpassing Gemini in MCQ skills by 1.4%. However, it struggled with
473   open-ended tasks, where Gemini outperformed it by 1.06 GPT-score. One reason for Qwen2-VL’s
474   underperformance in open-ended tasks could be its reliance on training data that primarily con-
475   sisted of videos without subtitles and images. As a result, it lacks the ability to fully comprehend
476   video content that requires understanding both visual frames and audio (or subtitles). However
477
      Qwen2-VL is test with subtitles but it seams that it can’t perform the alignment well.(4) short video
      models achieved the lowest performance because of information loss while sampling the long video
478
      into 8 or 45 frames in LLaVA-NeXT-Interleave (Li et al., 2024a) and MiniGPT4-video (Ataallah
479
      et al., 2024a)respectively. (5) As shown in Table 3, adding subtitles enables GPT-4 to achieve op-
480   timal performance. Subtitles allow the model to align characters, track their actions and clothing,

482   LLaVA-OneVision failed to exploit the subtitles. Performance on specific skills. Table 2 shows

516   5        C ONCLUSION
517
518   We introduced InfiniBench, a comprehensive benchmark for very long-form video understanding,
519   featuring the longest average video duration (52.59 minutes) and the largest number of question-
520   answer pairs (108.2K). Our diverse and human-centric questions evaluate nine distinct skills, posing
521   significant challenges to current Multi-Modality Large language Models (MLMMs). Evaluations
522   reveal that all existing models, including the commercial Gemini 1.5 Flash and GPT-4o and var-
523   ious open-source models, struggle with InfiniBench, particularly in tasks requiring deep context
524   understanding and critical thinking. Despite these challenges, GPT-4o outperforms all open-source
525
      models across all skills. InfiniBench aims to bridge the gap in long-form video understanding bench-
      marks, promoting the development of LMMs toward achieving human-level comprehension and
526
      reasoning.

529   6        L IMITATIONS
530
531   This section outlines the limitations of our work: Restricted Video Sources: The video sources
532   utilized in this study are limited exclusively to movies and television shows. Consequently, the
533   benchmark lacks a broader spectrum of general videos encompassing various aspects of human life
534
      or the diverse field of wildlife. Dependency on Transcripts: The generation pipeline of questions
      and answers employed in this benchmark is inherently dependent on the availability of transcripts.
535
      This reliance confines its applicability to movies and television shows where such transcripts are
536
      readily available. For more general videos, the absence of transcripts poses a significant challenge,
537   thereby limiting the pipeline’s utility in those contexts. We hope to overcome these limitations in
538   the future work.

      B.2       I MPACT OF THE “I D ON ’ T K NOW ” O PTION
769
770   The results in Table 6 indicate that excluding the “I don’t know” option improves the model’s perfor-
771   mance metrics. This increase occurs because, in the absence of this option, the model is compelled
772   to select a response, even when it lacks sufficient evidence, thereby increasing the likelihood of
773   correctly guessing through chance. Conversely, including the “I don’t know” option introduces an
774   additional challenge, as it requires the model to explicitly acknowledge uncertainty when it cannot
775   confidently match a provided answer.
776   This option also serves as a valuable diagnostic tool for assessing hallucination tendencies. Specifi-
777   cally, when evaluating with GPT-4o, the “I don’t know” option allows for the detection of instances
778   where the model generates an answer inconsistent with the given evidence. GPT-4o is designed to
779   utilize this option when the predicted response either lacks sufficient evidence to align with one of
780   the provided choices or constitutes a hallucination. Thus, the inclusion of the “I don’t know” option
781   enhances the robustness of model evaluation by accounting for uncertainty and mitigating the impact
782
      of overconfident, unsupported predictions.

      C.2       C HECK QUESTIONS DUPLICATION
835
836   To ensure the dataset is free from (near) duplicate questions, we implemented the following ap-
837   proach:
838
      Encoding and Similarity Calculation: We used M3-Embedding (Chen et al., 2024) to encode the
839   questions and answer choices into vector representations.the cosine similarity was then calculated to
840   identify potential duplicates. To account for varying degrees of similarity, we evaluated three thresh-
841   olds: 90%, 95%, and 98% cosine similarity. As shown in Figure 6 the vast majority of questions
842   across all skills are unique, with no duplicates detected. Only two skills, Temporal Questions and
843   Character Actions, showed instances of potential duplicates. Upon investigation, we found that the
844   duplicates detected were false positives. For example, the following pair of questions was flagged
845   as duplicates because they differ only by the words ”before” and ”after”. However, this small dif-
846   ference completely changes the meaning of the question. for instance : Q1: Did the event flashback
847
      to Phoebe completing a mile on a hippity-hop before turning thirty, happen before the event Monica
      makes breakfast with chocolate-chip pancakes?
848
849   Q2: Did the event flashback to Phoebe completing a mile on a hippity-hop before turning thirty,
850   happen after the event Monica makes breakfast with chocolate-chip pancakes? These examples
851   highlight the importance of semantic context in evaluating the similarity of questions, as minor
852   lexical differences can significantly alter meaning. Based on our analysis, we are confident that the
853
      dataset is free from true duplicates, and our pipeline effectively identifies and handles potential near-
      duplicates. The false positives flagged by the similarity detection process underscore the complexity
854
      of semantic evaluation, especially in nuanced question construction.

      D.1   E VALUATION METRIC DETAILS
952
953   For MCQs, large language models (LLMs) do not consistently provide direct responses. The output
954
      may vary, sometimes giving the option number, other times the option sentence, or occasionally
      providing additional clarifications for the selected option. For example, an LLM might produce a
955
      response such as: ”I think option 1 is close, but my final answer will be option 2.” Additionally, some
956
      responses may include hallucinations not found in the given options. To address this variability, we
957   implemented a standardized evaluation method using GPT-4o to match the LLM’s prediction with
958   one of the provided options. Specifically, we input the set of options and the LLM’s prediction into
959   GPT-4o, which then attempts to match the predicted answer with one of the given options. If no
960   matching option is found or if the response includes hallucinations, GPT-4o matches the prediction
961   with an ”I don’t know” option. Using the prediction option number and the ground truth option
962   number, we then calculate the accuracy. For open-ended questions, GPT-4o assessed the LLMs’
963   predictions based on several criteria: correctness, meaningfulness, alignment with the expected an-
964   swer, presence of hallucinations, and completeness. Using these criteria, GPT-4o assigned a score
965
      from 0 to 5 to indicate the overall quality of each response.
