                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE) is a common method for Knowledge Graphs
011
               (KGs) to serve various artificial intelligence tasks. The suitable dimensions of
               the embeddings depend on the storage and computing conditions of the specific
012
               application scenarios. Once a new dimension is required, a new KGE model
013
               needs to be trained from scratch, which greatly increases the training cost and
014            limits the efficiency and flexibility of KGE in serving various scenarios. In this
015            work, we propose a novel KGE training framework MED, through which we could
016            train once to get a croppable KGE model applicable to multiple scenarios with
017            different dimensional requirements, sub-models of the required dimensions can be
018            cropped out of it and used directly without any additional training. In MED, we
019            propose a mutual learning mechanism to improve the low-dimensional sub-models
020            performance and make the high-dimensional sub-models retain the capacity that
021            low-dimensional sub-models have, an evolutionary improvement mechanism to
022
               promote the high-dimensional sub-models to master the knowledge that the low-
               dimensional sub-models can not learn, and a dynamic loss weight to balance the
023
               multiple losses adaptively. Experiments on 4 KGE models over 4 standard KG
024
               completion datasets, 3 real application scenarios over a real-world large-scale KG,
025            and the experiments of extending MED to the language model BERT show the
026            effectiveness, high efficiency, and flexible extensibility of MED. The code and data
027            are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/MED-DBFC/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Knowledge Graphs (KGs) are composed of triples representing facts in the form of (head entity,
033
      relation, tail entity), abbreviated as (h, r, t). KG has been widely used in recommendation systems Zhu
034
      et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021), information extraction Hoffmann et al. (2011); Daiber et al. (2013),
035   question answering Zhang et al. (2016); Diefenbach et al. (2018) and other tasks. A common way
036   to apply a knowledge graph is to represent the entities and relations in the knowledge graph into
037   continuous vector spaces, called knowledge graph embedding (KGE) Bordes et al. (2013); Sun et al.
038   (2019b), and then use the vector representation of entities and relations to serve a variety of tasks.
039
      KGEs with higher dimensions have greater expressive power and usually achieve better performance,
040
      but this also means a larger number of parameters and requires more storage space and computing
041   resources Zhu et al. (2022); Sachan (2020). The appropriate dimensions of the KGE are different for
042   different devices or scenarios. As shown in Fig. 1, large remote servers have large storage space and
043   sufficient computing resources to support high-dimensional KGE with good performance, while small
044   and medium-sized terminal devices, such as vehicle-mounted systems or smartphones, can only accept
045   low-dimensional KGE due to limited computing power and storage capacity. Therefore, according
046   to the conditions of different devices or scenes, people tend to train the KGE with appropriate
047   dimensions and as high quality as possible. However, the challenge is that once a new dimension
048   is required, a new KGE needs to be trained from scratch. Especially when only low-dimensional
049
      KGE can be applied, to ensure good performance, the additional model compression technology
      such as knowledge distillation Hinton et al. (2015); Zhu et al. (2022) is needed during training. This
050
      significantly increases training costs and limits KGE’s efficiency and flexibility in serving different
051
      scenarios.
052
053   Thus a new concept "croppable KGE" is proposed and we are interested in the research question that
      is it possible to train a croppable KGE, with which KGEs of various required dimensions can

055   performance?

069
      Evolutionary improvement mechanism, and the Dynamic loss weight to achieve the above purpose.
      Specifically, the mutual learning mechanism is based on knowledge distillation and it makes pair-
070
      wise neighbor sub-models learn from each other, so that the performance of the lower-dimensional
071
      sub-model can be improved, and the higher-dimensional sub-model can retain the ability of the lower-
072   dimensional sub-model. The evolutionary improvement mechanism helps the high-dimensional
073   sub-model master more knowledge that the low-dimensional sub-model cannot by making the high-
074   dimensional sub-model pay more attention to learn the triples that the low-dimensional sub-model
075   can’t correctly predict. The dynamic loss weight is designed to adaptively balance multiple losses of
076   different sub-models according to their dimensions and further improve the overall performance.
077
      We evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed MED by implementing it on three typical KGE methods
078   and four standard KG datasets. We also prove its practical value by applying MED to a real-world
079   large-scale KG and downstream tasks. Furthermore, we demonstrate the extensibility of MED
080   by implementing it on language model BERT Devlin et al. (2019) and GLUE Wang et al. (2019)
081   benchmarks. The experimental results show that (1) MED successfully trains a croppable KGE
082   model available for various dimensional requirements, which contains multiple parameter-shared
083   sub-models of different dimensions that of high performance and can be used directly without
084   additional training; (2) the training efficiency of MED is far higher than that of independently training
085   multiple KGE models of different sizes or obtaining them by knowledge distillation. (3) MED can be
086
      flexibly extended to other neural network models besides KGE and achieve good performance; (4)
      our proposed mutual learning mechanism, evolutionary improvement mechanism, and dynamic loss
087
      weight are effective and necessary for MED to achieve overall optimal performance. In summary, our
088
      contributions are as follows:

               of different dimensions can be cropped and used directly without any additional training.

               the state-of-the-art distillation-based methods. MED also shows excellent performance in
               real-world applications and good extensibility on other types of neural networks.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
104
105   This work is to achieve a croppable KGE that meets different dimensional requirements. One of
106   the most common methods to obtain a good-performance KGE of the target dimension is utilizing
107   knowledge distillation with a high-dimensional powerful teacher KGE. Thus, we focus on two
      research fields most relevant to our work: knowledge graph embedding and knowledge distillation.

      2.1   K NOWLEDGE G RAPH E MBEDDING
109
110   Knowledge graph embedding (KGE) technology has been widely applied with the key idea of
111   mapping entities and relations of a KG into continuous vector spaces as vector representations, which
112   can further serve various KG downstream tasks. TransE Bordes et al. (2013) is the most representative
113   translation-based KGE method by regarding the relation as a translation from the head to tail entity.
114
      Variants of TransE include TransH Wang et al. (2014), TransR Lin et al. (2015), TransD Ji et al. (2015)
      and so on. RESCAL Nickel et al. (2011) is the first one based on vector decomposition, and then to
115
      improve it, DistMult Yang et al. (2015), ComplEx Trouillon et al. (2016), and SimplE Kazemi & Poole
116
      (2018) are proposed. RotatE Sun et al. (2019b) is a typical rotation-based method that regards the
117   relation as the rotation between the head and tail entities. QuatE Zhang et al. (2019) and DihEdral Xu
118   & Li (2019) work with a similar idea. PairRE Chao et al. (2021) uses two relation vectors to project
119   the head and tail entities into an Euclidean space to encode complex relational patterns. With the
120   development of neural networks, KGEs based on graph neural networks (GNNs) Dettmers et al.
121   (2018); Nguyen et al. (2018); Schlichtkrull et al. (2018); Vashishth et al. (2020) are also proposed.
122   Although the KGEs are simple and effective, there is an obvious challenge: In different scenarios, the
123   required KGE dimensions are different, which depends on the storage and computing resources of
124   the device. It has to train a new KGE model from scratch for a new dimension requirement, which
125
      greatly increases the training cost and limits the flexibility for KGE to serve diversified scenarios.
126
      2.2   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
127
128   High-dimensional KGEs have strong expression ability due to the large number of parameters, but
129   require a lot of storage and computing resources, and are not suitable for all scenarios, especially
130   small devices. To solve this problem, a common way is to compress a high-dimensional KGE to the
131   target low-dimensional KGE by knowledge distillation Hinton et al. (2015); Mirzadeh et al. (2020)
132   and quantization Bai et al. (2021); Stock et al. (2021) technology.
133   Quantization replaces continuous vector representations with lower-dimensional discrete codes. TS-
134   CL Sachan (2020) is the first work of KGE compression applying quantization. LightKG Wang et al.
135   (2021a) uses a residual module to induce diversity among codebooks. However, quantization cannot
136   improve the inference speed so it’s still not suitable for devices with limited computing resources.
137   Knowledge distillation (KD) has been widely used in Computer Vision Mirzadeh et al. (2020) and
138   Natural Language Processing Devlin et al. (2019); Sun et al. (2019a), helping reduce the model size
139   and increase the inference speed. The core idea is to use the output of a large teacher model to guide
140   the training of a small student model. DualDE Zhu et al. (2022) is a representative KD-based work to
141   transfer the knowledge of high-dimensional KGE to low-dimensional KGE. It considers the mutual
142   influences between the teacher and student and finetunes the teacher during training.MulDE Wang
143   et al. (2021b) transfers the knowledge from multiple low-dimensional teacher models to a student
144   model for hyperbolic KGE. ISD Zhou et al. (2022b) improves low-dimensional KGE by making it
145
      play the teacher and student roles alternatively during training. IterDE Liu et al. (2023) introduces an
      iterative distillation way and enables a KGE model to be the student and teacher during distilling
146
      alternately, thus knowledge can be transferred smoothly between high-dimensional teacher and
147
      low-dimensional student. Other distillation works related to knowledge graph include PMD Fan et al.
148   (2024) applying distillation to pre-trained language models to improve KG completion, IncDE Liu
149   et al. (2024) using distillation between the same-dimensional models at different times for incremental
150   learning, and SKDE Xu et al. (2024) proposing self-knowledge distillation to avoid introducing a
151   complex teacher model. Among these methods, DualDE Zhu et al. (2022) and IterDE Liu et al. (2023)
152   are more relevant to our work, all have the setting that compresses high-dimensional teacher into
153   low-dimensional student model. In this work, we propose a novel KD-based KGE training framework
154   MED, one training can obtain a croppable KGE that meets multiple dimensional requirements.

198
199
      MED is based on knowledge distillation Hinton et al. (2015); Tang et al. (2019); Devlin et al. (2019)
      technique that the student learns by fitting the hard (ground-truth) label and the soft label from
200
      the teacher simultaneously. In MED, we first propose a mutual learning mechanism that makes
201
      low-dimensional sub-models learn from high-dimensional sub-models to achieve better performance,
202   and makes high-dimensional sub-models also learn from low-dimensional sub-models to retain
203   the abilities that low-dimensional sub-models already have. Then, we propose an evolutionary
204   improvement mechanism to enable high-dimensional sub-models to master the knowledge that the
205   low-dimensional sub-models can not learn well. Finally, we train MED with dynamic loss weight to
206   adaptively balance multiple optimization objectives of sub-models.
207

213   sub-model Mi as the student of its lower-dimensional neighbor sub-model Mi−1 , so the higher-
214   dimensional sub-model can review what the lower-dimensional sub-model has learned and retain the
215   low-dimensional one’s existing abilities. Thus, pairwise neighbor sub-models serve as both teachers
      and students, learning from each other. The mutual learning loss between each pair of neighbor

      5.1.2    E VALUATION M ETRIC
308   For the link prediction task, we adopt standard metrics MRR and Hit@k (k = 1, 3, 10) in the filtered
309   setting Bordes et al. (2013). For a test triple (h, r, t), we construct candidate triples by replacing h
310   with all entities and keeping the replaced triples not in training, validation, and test set. Then we
311   calculate the triple score rank of (h, r, t) among all candidate triples as its head prediction rank rankt .
312   Similarly, we get its tail prediction rank rankt . We average rankh and rankt as (h, r, t)’s final
313   rank. MRR is the mean reciprocal rank of all test triples, and Hit@k is the percentage of test triples
314   with rank ≤ k. We use Effi Chen et al. (2023), that is MRR/#P (#P is the number of parameters), to
315
      quantify the parameter efficiency of models. We use f1-score and accuracy for user labeling task, and
      normalized discounted cumulative gain ndcg@k(k = 5, 10) for product recommendation task.

321   neighbor sub-models. There are a total of 64 available sub-models of different dimensions from 10 to
322   640 in our croppable KGE model. The dimension of sub-model Mi (i = 1, 2..., 64) is 10 × i. For the
323   user labeling and product recommendation task, we set n = 3 and train the croppable KGE containing
      3 sub-models: M1 with d1 = 10 for mobile phone (MB) terminals that are limited by storage and

      5.6.3   W HETHER HIGH - DIMENSIONAL SUB - MODELS COVER THE CAPABILITIES OF
              LOW- DIMENSIONAL ONES
491
492   If a high-dimensional model retains the ability of lower-dimensional models, it should correctly pre-
493   dict all triples that the lower-dimensional model can predict. We count the percentage of triples in test
494   set that meet the condition that if the smallest sub-model that can correctly predict a given triple is Mi ,
495   all higher-dimensional sub-models (Mi+1 , Mi+2 , ..., Mn ) also correctly predict it, and denote the re-
496   sult as the ability retention ratio (ARR). We use Hit@10 to judge whether a triple is correctly predicted,
497   that is, Mi correctly predicts a triple if Mi scores this triple in the top 10 among all candidate triples.
498                                                      From Fig. 5, ARR of MED is always much higher
499                                                      than baselines, especially on FB15K237, indicat-
500
                                                         ing that high-dimensional sub-models in MED suc-
                                                         cessfully cover the power of low-dimensional ones,
501
                                                         contributed by the mutual learning mechanism that
502
                                                         helps high-dimensional sub-models review what low-
503                                                      dimensional sub-models have learned. Based on this
504
       Figure 5: The ability retention ratio (ARR). advantage of MED, we can provide a simple way
505                                                      to judge how easy or difficult a triple is for KGE
506   methods to learn: the triple that low-dimensional sub-models can correctly predict may be easy
507   since high-dimensional models can also predict it, while triples that can only be predicted by high-
508   dimensional sub-models are difficult.

530
531
      In this work, we propose a novel KGE training framework, MED, that trains a croppable KGE at once,
532
      and then sub-models of various required dimensions can be cropped out from it and used directly
533
      without additional training. In MED, we propose the mutual learning mechanism to improve low-
534   dimensional sub-models performance and make the high-dimensional sub-models retain the ability of
535   the low-dimensional ones, the evolutionary improvement mechanism to motivate high-dimensional
536   sub-models to master more knowledge that low-dimensional ones cannot, and the dynamic loss
537   weight to adaptively balance multiple losses. The experimental results show the effectiveness and
538   high efficiency of our method, where all sub-models achieve promising performance, especially the
539   performance of low-dimensional sub-models is greatly improved. In future work, we will further
      explore the more fine-grained information encoding ability of each sub-model.
