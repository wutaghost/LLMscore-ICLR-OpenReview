009                                                A BSTRACT
010
                 Assessing the quality of outputs generated by generative models, such as large
011
                 language models and vision language models, presents notable challenges. Tradi-
012
                 tional methods for evaluation typically rely on either human assessments, which
013              are resource-intensive, or automatic metrics that often show a low correlation with
014              human judgment. Another common approach is to use deep learning systems,
015              which not only consume a substantial amount of compute and time but also re-
016              quire extensive training data. In this study, we introduce a tuning-free framework
017              called ReFeR, designed to evaluate generative outputs, including both text and
018              images, by leveraging a 2-level hierarchy of LLMs and VLMs themselves. We
019              rigorously evaluate our framework, ReFeR, across four diverse evaluation tasks.
020              The framework not only improves the accuracy of these evaluations, surpassing
021
                 previous benchmarks but also generates constructive feedback. Interestingly, the
                 framework is also applicable to reasoning tasks. Experiments on four reasoning
022
                 tasks demonstrate superior collective reasoning abilities of the framework. We
023
                 present two variants of the framework: ReFeR-Turbo, optimized for accelerated
024              performance, and ReFeR-Lite, offering a more test-time compute efficient solu-
025              tion. ReFeR-Lite is ∼ 12 − 14× more test-time compute efficient than previous
026              works while being comparably accurate to ReFeR-Turbo. We make a PIP pack-
027              age, code and data publicly available1 .

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
      The rapid production of content by large language models and vision language models (VLMs),
032
      poses a challenge to traditional human-centric evaluation methods and conventional automatic met-
033   rics. Metrics like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), and METEOR (Banerjee
034   & Lavie, 2005) for textual evaluation and CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2022) for image to text eval-
035   uation, often misalign with human judgment and face limitations in assessing creative or nuanced
036   responses. Recent studies suggest using LLMs as novel, reference-independent evaluators by as-
037   sessing text quality based on predicted sequence likelihoods, bypassing the need for direct reference
038   comparisons (Chen et al., 2023a). This has motivated researchers (Liu et al., 2023c; Chiang & Lee,
039   2023) to work on improving the evaluation capability of individual LLMs on text evaluation. Zhang
040   et al. (2024) highlight that large models align more closely with human perceptual processes, thereby
041
      enhancing the evaluation of multimedia quality. Consequently, Chen et al. (2023b) leverage vision
      language models to provide explainable image quality evaluation by generating textual explanations,
042
      assessing fidelity, alignment, and aesthetics.
043
      Surprisingly, despite the potential for improved performance by using ensembles of multiple vision-
044
      language models or large language models, there has been limited research on how to align evalu-
045
      ations from multiple VLMs or LLMs with human judgments. While the concept of using multiple
046   VLMs or LLMs together to solve this complex problem is promising, it introduces several uncertain-
047   ties, including how to select the models, how many models to use, how to manage communication
048   between different models and what prompting structure should be used to maximize the effect.
049   In this paper, we introduce a multi-agent Reason-Feedback-Review (ReFeR) framework, drawing
050   inspiration from the academic peer review process to enhance the evaluation of multimodal genera-
051   tive outputs like text generated by an LLM, an image generated by any model, or caption of an image
052   generated by a VLM. By using multiple LLMs or VLMs as evaluators and feedback providers in a
053

      system akin to academic peer review, ReFeR enables a comprehensive evaluation of generative out-
055   puts across various domains, promoting model self-improvement, reasoning behind evaluation, and
056   consistent score across runs. The paper outlines ReFeR’s methodology, including its new prompting
057   schema and the strategic use of LLMs or VLMs in roles parallel to peer reviewers and area chairs, fa-

059   evaluation at the peer level and evaluation at the area chair level.
060   The framework is tested across two NLG evaluation and two multimodal evaluation tasks. Interest-
061   ingly, the framework is generic enough to be applicable for other tasks beyond evaluation. Hence,
062   we also test the framework’s reasoning ability on four reasoning benchmarks. Furthermore, ReFeR’s
063   feedback mechanism has enabled the production of instruction-tuning datasets, which can be used
064   to fine-tune smaller models achieving a better correlation with human evaluation.
065   We present two variants of our proposed framework, ReFeR Turbo and ReFeR Lite. ReFeR
066   Lite is ∼14× more test-time compute efficient than ReFeR Turbo. Both the variants outperform
067
      strong baselines on both text evaluation datasets: TopicalChat (Mehri & Eskenazi, 2020) and Sum-
      mEval (Fabbri et al., 2021). ReFeR also beats baselines like Clipscore (Hessel et al., 2022), Im-
068
      ageReward (Xu et al., 2023a) and others on caption quality and image generation quality evaluation
069
      using ICQD (Levinboim et al., 2019) and AGIQA (Zhang et al., 2023) datasets respectively by large
070   margins. Lastly, ReFeR also beats single agent methods (zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2023), self
071   correction (Huang et al., 2024)), and multi-agent methods like multi-agent debate (Du et al., 2023)
072   and multi-agent peer review (Xu et al., 2023b) on 3 out of 4 reasoning datasets, clearly outperform-
073   ing on average while keeping lower costs than baselines.
074   To summarize, the primary contributions of our research are as follows: (1) Introduction of a
075   general-purpose hierarchical framework, called ReFeR, given in two variants, ReFeR-Turbo and
076   ReFeR-Lite. (2) We develop a novel prompting schema, with a novel eval guidelines component,
077   specifically designed to improve the effectiveness of our framework in evaluation and reasoning
078   tasks. (3) Empirical validation of the framework’s evaluation and reasoning skills on four bench-
079   marks each. (4) We conduct an in-depth analysis of our multi-agent framework, ReFeR, addressing
080
      key questions such as how to select models, how many models to use, and other critical aspects of
      model interaction.

127   2.2      P ROMPTING S CHEMA
128
      An essential aspect of assessing generative outputs with language model agents involves crafting
129
      prompts that elicit high-quality evaluations. Prior work, such as G-Eval by Liu et al. (2023c), in-
130
      troduced a structured evaluation schema, organizing the prompt into sections: task introduction,
131   evaluation criteria, steps for evaluation, input presentation (context and target), and an evaluation
132   form designed to output a numerical rating only. Subsequently, Chiang & Lee (2023) proposed an
133   adjusted schema named Analyze-Rate, which prioritizes an analytical review followed by scoring,
134   showing improved performance over G-Eval’s prompt.
135   To further refine this approach, we introduce a new module in the evaluation schema called Eval-
136   uation Guidelines to enhance the model’s understanding of the scoring criteria, akin to guidelines
137   provided in traditional academic review processes. Evaluation guidelines can be automatically gen-
138   erated by prompting a language model with the prompt structure and some examples from the dataset
139   and we call this process auto prompt. We give an example of this process in Appendix E showing
140   the Auto Prompt for Engagingness prompt for TopicalChat. Alternatively, manually written human
141
      annotation guidelines of the dataset can be used. We also modified the evaluation form to include
      a critical comment or reasoning for the given score. The proposed evaluation schema is shown in
142
      Figure 4.

      2.3      R E F E R VARIANTS
145
146   2.3.1        R E F E R-T URBO
147   ReFeR-Turbo leverages a hyperparameter n, representing the number of responses generated by the
148   area chair agent. This variant generates multiple responses (n = 20) for each prompt, applying a
149   scoring function that averages the scores across all generated responses, as described in Eq. 3.
150   The final comment Cfinal is the list of all individual comments from the area chair evaluations. While
151   ReFeR-Turbo provides superior performance due to generating more evaluations per prompt, it in-
152   curs higher computational costs. Additionally, the use of n = 20 is often constrained to models
153   from the OpenAI API, as other APIs supporting large models do not support this level of multiple
154   response generation directly. Although it is possible to generate multiple responses by making re-
155   peated calls to the model (e.g., running the model 20 times with the same prompt), this approach
156   is computationally expensive and less practical for large-scale evaluation tasks. This usage of the
157
      hyperparameter was first suggested by G-Eval and later used by Analyze-Rate.
158
      2.3.2        R E F E R-L ITE
159
160   To enhance flexibility and reduce computational overhead, we developed ReFeR-Lite, which re-
161   moves the dependency on the parameter n for the given performance. In this variant, only a single
      response (n = 1) is generated for each prompt, or n is completely removed. This reduction in

      response generation is reflected in Eq 2, where n is set to 1.
163

165   By relying on just one evaluation per prompt, ReFeR-Lite can be used with a wider variety of
166   models, including open-source models, which do not natively support the generation of multiple
167   responses with a single prompt. Despite generating fewer responses, ReFeR-Lite maintains com-
168   petitive performance and offers significant cost savings. This makes it a more test-time computing-
169   efficient solution for tasks where computational resources are limited or where evaluating large
170   numbers of samples is required.
171   Both ReFeR-Turbo and ReFeR-Lite use the same peer evaluation structure, but differ primarily in
172   the area chair’s response generation and model compatibility. ReFeR-Turbo, with n = 20, offers
173   potentially higher performance due to generating more evaluations but is restricted to models that
174
      support or can simulate multiple response generation with a single prompt. In contrast, ReFeR-Lite
      provides greater flexibility and cost-efficiency by generating only a single response (n = 1) per
175
      prompt, making it more suitable for resource-constrained environments.

      G-Eval performs evaluation by deploying a single LLM agent. This agent employs Auto-CoT (chain
217   of thought) reasoning and a form-filling paradigm to ascertain the quality of NLG outputs, delivering
218   only scores for the specific dimensions under scrutiny. They use ’n’ hyperparameter to generate 20
219   responses and take the average score.
220   Analyze-Rate builds upon G-Eval, advocating for an enhanced prompt structure. This methodology
221   incorporates a preliminary analysis phase before scoring, aiming to enrich the evaluative process for
222   NLG tasks. Following G-Eval, they also consider the average score of 20 responses for each sample.
223   LLM-as-Judge (Zheng et al., 2023) is designed to evaluate LLMs and to rank them potentially
224   creating ChatBOT Arena. We replicated the original setup for TopicalChat dataset.
225   ChatEval (Chan et al., 2023) proposed a single model multi-agent framework with varied persona
226   to evaluate various NLG responses on open-ended questions and traditional NLP tasks by leveraging
227   a debating structure among the agents. We reproduced their most optimal setup mentioned in the
228   paper as baseline with 3 roles i.e. 3 agents and 2 discussion turns.

      we do not compare our framework against them directly.

      CLIP Score (Hessel et al., 2022) evaluates how well an image aligns with a text description by using
      the CLIP model, which computes similarity scores between images and text embeddings.
236
      Image Reward (Xu et al., 2023a) is a scoring model trained to assess the quality/alignment of gen-
237
      erated images with text by comparing them against reference images using a reward model.
238   Pick Score (Kirstain et al., 2023) is another scoring model for the task of image text alignment,
239   which is trained on human preference images ‘picked’ for a given text.
240   X-IQE (Chen et al., 2023b) leverages VLMs to evaluate text-to-image generation methods by gener-
241   ating textual explanations. We implement their Alignment dimension experiments to compare with
242   our results on text-to-image generation dataset (AGIQA).

244   R EASONING
245   We compare our framework against a variety of baseline methods across different categories. For
246   single-agent methods, we select zero-shot Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and Self-Correct. For multi-
247   agent frameworks, we compare against Multi-Agent Debate and Multi-Agent Peer Review, both of
248   which use a single model acting as multiple agents.
249   Zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2023) utilizes chain-of-thought prompting to generate reasoning pro-
250   cesses and answers using a single agent.
251   Self-Correct (Huang et al., 2024) is a single-agent approach that enables an LLM to iteratively eval-
252
      uate its own outputs, identify errors, and refine its responses through self-reflection.
      Multi-Agent Debate (Du et al., 2023) involves a group of agents, where each agent observes the
253
      solutions provided by others, updates its own solution accordingly, and repeats this process through
254
      multiple iterations.
255   Multi-Agent Peer Review (Xu et al., 2023b) is a multi-agent system in which each agent inde-
256   pendently generates a solution, reviews the solutions of others, and assigns confidence scores to its
257   reviews. Agents then revise their initial solutions based on the received peer reviews. This revision
258   is repeated through multiple iterations/rounds of peer review. We used the default number of rounds
259   (3) mentioned by the authors.

      Our findings indicate that increasing the number of peers generally improves the framework’s over-
412   all correlation, as evidenced by the main branch in Fig. 2. We experimented with varying peer
413   combinations and numbers to distinguish between the effects of adding another peer versus a better-
414   performing peer. Due to the impracticality of exploring all possible combinations with six peers, we

416   Fig. 2 demonstrates that while the framework’s average performance generally increases with more

420   closer to the three-peer configuration. Notably, the performance gain from four or five peers com-
421   pared to three peers is not substantial. This observation suggests that using three peers may be an

      optimal choice, balancing performance improvements with computational efficiency.

457   5.3   E RROR A NALYSIS
458
459   To assess the framework’s effectiveness in both evaluation and reasoning tasks, we conducted an
460   error analysis, with results shown in Fig. 3. In this analysis, a TopicalChat sample’s evaluation
461
      score for each metric is considered correct if it falls within a given threshold range of 25%. In the
      TopicalChat dataset evaluation, the area chair provided correct scores 42.6% of the time when one
462
      or two peers provided a correct answer, demonstrating the AC’s ability to leverage partially correct
463
      peer scores effectively. The AC made mistakes only 11.9% of the time when at least one peer was
464   correct. However, the AC was correct only in 2% of the cases where all the peers were incorrect,
465   suggesting that the AC may require at least one correct peer input to avoid confusion and give a
466   correct score. For reasoning tasks, the AC was incorrect for only 2.9% of cases where atleast one
467   peer is correct, showing similiar observation as evaluation. And the AC was correct 14% of the
468   time, even when all peers were incorrect, indicating a better ability to disregard clearly incorrect
469   answers from the smaller peer models. This suggests that the AC, when using reasoning tasks, may
470   not always rely on peers and can function independently in such cases.

490   6   R ELATED W ORK
491
492   NLG & Multimodal Evaluation: Recent advancements in NLG evaluation include GPTScore (Fu
493   et al., 2023), which uses generative pre-training models to assess text quality, and G-Eval (Liu
494   et al., 2023c), employing a chain-of-thoughts approach with form-filling methodology. Chiang &
495
      Lee (2023) highlighted limitations in G-Eval’s automated CoT alignment with human evaluations.
      TIGERScore (Jiang et al., 2023) offers detailed error analysis using fine-tuned Llama-2, while Fu-
496
      sionEval (Shu et al., 2024) integrates auxiliary evaluators with a primary LLM for scoring. X-Eval
497
      (Liu et al., 2023a) introduces a two-stage instruction tuning framework for diverse evaluation di-
498   mensions. ChatEval (Chan et al., 2023) proposes a multi-agent referee system using autonomous
499   debating among agents with different personas to evaluate response quality. While similar to our ap-
500   proach, it primarily relies on debate methodology using the same models under varied personas,
501   whereas our method employs diverse models as peers and area chairs with a richer prompting
502   schema. Mixture of Agents (MoA) (Wang et al., 2024a) is another work which explores the how to
503   harness the collective expertise of multiple LLMs. In multimodal evaluation, CLIP Score (Hessel
504   et al., 2022), Image-Reward (Xu et al., 2023a), and Pick Score (Kirstain et al., 2023) assess image-
505   text alignment using pre-trained models. Deep learning methods like HyperIQA (Su et al., 2020)
506
      and IP-IQA (Qu et al., 2024) have shown improvements in this domain. Later, X-IQE (Chen et al.,
      2023b) introduced using VLMs for the task of image-quality assessment.
507
508
      Reasoning using LLMs: Single-agent methods like Zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2023) have im-
      proved language models’ reasoning capabilities using Chain-of-Thought prompting. Self-correction
509
      (Huang et al., 2024) mimics human self-reflection to address reasoning errors. In multi-agent frame-
510
      works, Du et al. (2023) introduced a same-model approach using peer solutions for individual im-
511   provement, while Pham et al. (2023) proposed embedding-based communication to optimize rea-
512   soning. Xu et al. (2023b) developed a framework inspired by academic peer review, emphasizing
513   iterative improvement through peer feedback. This differs from our method, which involves an area
514   chair reviewing peer responses without direct inter-peer communication. ReConcile (Chen et al.,
515   2024) structured a multi-model, multi-agent framework as a round table conference, demonstrating
516   enhanced reasoning through discussion and consensus. We expand on why we did not consider
517   ReConcile as a baseline in the appendix I. Wang et al. (2024b) proposed selecting the most coherent
518   response from multiple reasoning chains, offering an alternative approach to consensus-building and
519
      improving reasoning accuracy.
520
521   7   C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this work, we propose ReFeR (Reason-Feedback-Review), a hierarchical model framework that
524   utilizes smaller, capable models as peers and a powerful model as the area chair. The area chair lever-
525   ages the reasoning and feedback from peers to provide a final review for evaluating given images or
526   text. We demonstrate ReFeR’s efficacy across two NLG evaluation tasks, two multimodal evaluation
527   tasks, and four reasoning tasks, outperforming various baselines while maintaining performance and
528   cost efficiency. We present two variants: ReFeR-Turbo and ReFeR-Lite. Notably, our Lite version
529   achieves performance similar to other works and ReFeR-Turbo, while being significantly efficient.
530
531
      8   L IMITATIONS
532
533
      Our framework, while robust in many aspects, has some limitations. One notable constraint is
534   the potential computational cost when using large models as both peers and area chairs, especially
535   in resource-limited environments. Additionally, the framework currently lacks an interactive dis-
536   cussion phase between peer models, which could further improve collective reasoning. In some
537   scenarios, such as when a weaker model is used as the area chair, the performance may not be
538   optimal. Lastly, while our framework has shown promising results on text and image evaluation
539   tasks, it remains untested in other modalities, which could present unique challenges in scaling and
      complexity.

      Future research can explore incorporating additional elements from the academic peer review pro-
750   cess, such as the author discussion phase, to simulate a more interactive review environment. Ex-
751   panding the framework to include evaluations beyond text and images, such as video and audio
752   content, could further enhance its applicability. Another promising direction is to develop various
753   communication strategies between peers and the area chair to optimize evaluation and feedback cy-

755   in understanding the impact of multiple, potentially conflicting, judgments on the final evaluation
      outcomes.

870   D    P ROMPT O PTIMIZATION
871
872   Prompt optimization methods utilizing LLMs, such as OPRO (Yang et al., 2024), APE (Zhou et al.,
873   2023), and ProTeGi (Pryzant et al., 2023), often employ text-gradient or feedback-based techniques
874   to refine prompts. These methods typically involve providing a capable LLM with error examples
875   and obtaining feedback, which serves as a text gradient to adjust the prompt for improved perfor-
876
      mance. Such approaches have demonstrated effectiveness for short questions/requests and relatively
      simple tasks.
877
878
      Table 6 compares our prompt with an optimized prompt using ProTeGi on the TopicalChat dataset.
      We utilized the default ProTeGi settings with GPT-4o-mini as the optimizer LLM. Our findings
879
      indicate that prompt optimization is time-consuming and incurs higher costs than evaluation itself
880
      due to the iterative improvement process of prompt optimization over a test set, and that too with
881   long inputs for complex tasks like this. Moreover, our prompts with the proposed structure yield
882   better correlation than the optimized prompts from ProTeGi.
883   We attribute this outcome to the limitations of these methods when dealing with extensive inputs,
884   such as conversation history and responses in TopicalChat, where entire dialogues are provided to
885   evaluate and rate NLG output on various metrics. Unlike G-Eval, which only provides scores, meth-
886   ods like Analyze-Rate and ours improve scores based on generated analyses. In these cases, both
887   the analyses and ratings are crucial for understanding errors, as the singular numerical rating value
888   offers insufficient insight into prompt issues. Additionally, even when detailed analyses are provided
889   in multiple error examples for the prompt optimization, the gradient-based approach may struggle
890   with long contexts, making it challenging for the model to identify specific prompt deficiencies and
891
      provide useful feedback.

1069
       tations, resulting in higher correlations. However, this does not reflect the model’s true ability to
1070   understand and follow instructions. In contrast, larger models like GPT-4o-mini, which adhere more
1071   strictly to evaluation guidelines, tend to generate more varied scores. This often leads to lower corre-
1072   lations due to the skewed nature of the human annotations. Additionally, in cases where the ReFeR

1213
       open source model Qwen-2.5-72B, as our AC model (with the same peer models) and for G-Eval &
1214   Analyze-rate. We calculate the FLOPs with this approximation formula:
1215
1216                  FLOPs = layers × 4 · sequence length · d2model + 8 · dmodel · ffn dim

1217
1218   The components involved in the FLOPs calculation are as follows: layers refers to the number of
1219
       transformer layers, sequence length is the length of the input sequence, d model is the
       dimensionality of the model (hidden size), and ffn dim represents the dimensionality of the
1220
       feed-forward network, which is typically 4 times dmodel .
1221
       For the self-attention mechanism, the cost of generating the query, key, and value matrices is given
1222
       by:
1223
                                          3 × d2model × sequence length.
1224
1225
       The cost of the scaled dot-product attention, which involves computing attention scores and
       applying them, is approximately:
1226
1227                                                                        d2model × sequence length.
1228
       Therefore, the total cost for the self-attention mechanism is:
1229
1230                                                                      4 × d2model × sequence length.
1231
       In the feed-forward network (FFN), which consists of two linear layers with a ReLU activation in
1232   between, the cost for each linear layer is approximately:
1233
1234                                                                          2 × dmodel × ffn dim.
1235   Thus, the total cost for the FFN, considering both the forward and backward passes, is:
1236
                                                                              8 × dmodel × ffn dim.
