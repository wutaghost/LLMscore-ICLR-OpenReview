009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image (T2I) models achieve high-fidelity generation through extensive
012            training on large datasets. However, these models may unintentionally pick up
013            undesirable biases of their training data, such as over-representation of particular
014            identities in gender-neutral or race-neutral prompts. Existing alignment meth-
015            ods such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Direct
016            Preference Optimization (DPO) fail to address this problem effectively because
017            they operate on pairwise preferences consisting of individual samples, while the
018
               aforementioned biases can only be measured at a population level. For example,
               a single sample for the prompt “doctor” could be male or female, but a model
019
               generating predominantly male doctors even with repeated sampling reflects a gen-
020
               der bias. To address this limitation, we introduce PopAlign, a novel approach for
021            population-level preference optimization, while standard optimization would prefer
022            entire sets of samples over others. We further derive a stochastic lower bound that
023            directly optimizes for individual samples from preferred populations over others
024            for scalable training.Using human evaluation and standard image quality and bias
025            metrics, we show that PopAlign significantly mitigates the bias of pretrained T2I
026            models while largely preserving the generation quality.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Modern image generative models, such as the Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022; Stability-AI,
032   2023) and DALLE (Ramesh et al., 2021; 2022; OpenAI, 2023) model series, are trained on large
033
      datasets of billions of images scraped from the Internet. As a result, these models tend to strongly
      inherit various kinds of biases in their dataset. For example, in Figure 1a and 1b, we can see that
034
      SDXL tends to generate predominantly male images for the prompt “doctor,” amplifying underlying
035
      societal biases as these models make their ways into an increasing number of everyday products and
036   applications. Several past works have documented such societal biases for foundation models at large
037   (Luccioni et al., 2024; Chauhan et al., 2024), yet mitigation efforts lag, especially for text-to-image
038   generation.
039
      In this work, we study a specific category of biases that are defined at a population level. That is, a
040
      single sample from a generative model is insufficient to assess whether the model exhibits a specific
041
      population bias. Prominent examples include biases of text-to-image generative models with respect
042   to gender-neutral or race-neutral prompts. For example, a single generated image sample for the
043   prompt “doctor” could be male or female, but a model generating images of predominantly male
044   doctors even with repeated sampling reflects a gender bias. This is in contrast with much of the AI
045   safety and alignment work in recent times for large language models (Dai et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
046   2024), where the harmfulness in generations can be ascertained at the level of individual samples. For
047   example, given the prompt “what is the gender of doctors?”, even individual generated text responses
048   should ideally not show a bias towards a specific gender.
049   Given any implicit population preference (e.g., equalizing image generations across genders for a
050   gender-neutral prompt), there are two key challenges in aligning large-scale text-to-image generative
051   models. First, many state-of-the-art models are trained on large-scale, possibly non-public datasets,
052   making it prohibitively expensive for intermediate developers to retrain them for population alignment.
053   Therefore, an ideal solution would build on existing models, be sample-efficient in acquiring additional
      supervision, and parameter-efficient for cost-effective alignment. Second, given the diverse range of

084   also note that prior works (Choi et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2020; Teo et al., 2023; Um & Suh, 2023)

087

      of prompts.
088
089   Our primary contribution in this work is to define PopAlign, a preference alignment framework
090   for mitigating population bias for text-to-image generative models. Standard preference alignment
091   frameworks, such as reinforcement learning from human preferences (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017;
092   Ouyang et al., 2022) and its reward-free extension direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov
093
      et al., 2024), cannot be directly applied for mitigating population bias as they acquire either absolute
      ratings or pairwise preferences between individual samples. For image generation, this information
094
      is only helpful for improving visual quality or semantic adherence to prompts, as shown in recent
095
      works (Wallace et al., 2023). Building on the reinforcement learning from human preferences (RLHF)
096   framework, we first propose to acquire multi-sample preferences over sets of samples, as proxies for
097   population-level preferences. We reduce it to a corresponding reward-free, population-level DPO
098   objective. Finally, we derive the PopAlign objective as a stochastic lower bound to this population-
099   level DPO objective such that it permits tractable evaluation and maximization by decomposing
100   multi-sample pairwise preferences into single-sample preferences after sampling from their respective
101   populations. Fig. 2 illustrates the difference between sample-level preferences used in RLHF/DPO
102   and our proposed population-level preferences.
103   To evaluate our model’s efficacy, we collect population-level preference data through a combination
104   of human labelers and automatic pipelines based on attribute classifiers. Through standard image
105   quality and bias metrics as well as extensive human evaluations, we show that PopAlign significantly
106   mitigates bias in pretrained text-to-image models without notably impacting the quality of generation.
107   Compared with a base SDXL model, PopAlign reduces the gender and race discrepancy metric of the
      pretrained SDXL by (-0.233), and (-0.408) respectively, while maintaining comparable image quality.

      3     M ETHOD
151
152
      Consider a pretrained text-to-image model πθ that is biased w.r.t. one or more population-level traits.
153
      Our goal in population-level alignment is to fine-tune PopAlign without acquiring any additional real
154   images. To do so, we assume access to a source of preferences (e.g., via humans) over the model’s
155   output generations.
156
157   3.1    P OPULATION -L EVEL P REFERENCE ACQUISITION
158
159   Typically, alignment data for RLHF/DPO is created by generating multiple samples using the same
160   prompt and asking humans to rank the results. Since the goal of PopAlign is to mitigate the
161   population-level bias, we need to generate two or more sets of images for the same prompt. However,
      naive sampling of sets does not work due to the high degree of bias within current T2I models for


                                                              3
162
      identity-neutral prompts. For example, we observe that among 100 images generated from the prompt
163   “doctor”, only 6 are female doctors. In the extreme case, when prompted with the prompt “engineer”,
164   the model generates no images of female engineers amongst 100 samples. This makes generating a
165   set of near-fair samples nearly impossible using this naive method.
166
      To address this challenge, we use an approximated process where we directly augment a gender-
167
      neutral prompt such as “engineering” to a diverse set of “Asian male engineer” and “female engineer”,
168
      and use images sampled from these augmented prompts as the winning set, and images sampled
169   directly from the gender-neutral prompt as the losing set. As a sanity check, for each pair of sets, we
170   use a classifier in combination with a face detector to determine if the sampled images are indeed
171   consistent with the prompts. We drop pairs that are incorrect or ambiguous and fails this check. For
172   example, we found that many images generated with the prompt “astronaut” contains a person with
173   helmet on, making it impossible to determine the gender or ethnicity. These samples fail the detector
174   and are dropped from the preference dataset.
175   Our sampling process empirically use different prompts for the winning and losing samples. This
176   deviates from the standard alignment formulation where the prompt of winning and losing generations
177   are exactly the same. In additional to the sanity check process using a classifier, we conducted further
178   investigation to ensure that 1) such deviation is necessary 2) the approximation is theoretically
179   justified and empirically valid. We provide further details in appendix Appendix C.
180

      5.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
305
306   For fairness, we use the fairness discrepancy metric f proposed by earlier works (Choi et al., 2020),
307   which measures fairness on sensitive attribute u over individual image samples x as
308
309                                 f (pref , pθ ) = ∥Epref [p(u|x)] − Epθ [p(u|x)]∥2                          (11)
310
      where pref is an ideal distribution and pθ is the distribution of a generative model. The lower is
311
      the discrepancy metric, the better can the model mitigate unfair biases. To calculate discrepancy
312   metric for gender, race and age, we use the DeepFace library, which contains various face detection
313   and classification models (Serengil & Ozpinar, 2024; 2020; 2021; 2023). Since age is a continuous
314   attribute, we consider the discrepancy on the binary classes ”young” (age < 40) and ”old” (age > 40)
315   following the setup of aDFT (Shen et al., 2023). For sexual orientation experiments, we use an object
316   detector GroundingDINO (Liu et al., 2023) to detect ”man” and ”woman” classes and infer whether
317   the generation is a gay couple, lesbian couple, or heterosexual couple.
318   For image quality, we employ a set of standard image quality metrics: CLIP (Radford et al., 2021),
319   VQAScore (Lin et al., 2025), HPS v2 (Wu et al., 2023), and LAION aesthetics score (Schuhmann,
320   2022). CLIP measures the alignment of generated image and input prompts. LAION aesthetics score
321   measures the quality of the generated image on its own. HPS takes into consider both the image
322   quality and image-prompt alignment. For Pick-a-Pick benchmark, we additionally report PickScore
323   (Kirstain et al., 2024), which is trained on Pick-a-Pick dataset using human preference. Higher values
      of these metrics indicates better quality of generated images.

360   We first evaluate the performance of our method on a set of 100 identity neural prompts focus
361   on occupations. To minimize detection and classification errors, we use simple prompts with the
362   template “best quality, a realistic photo of [identity-neutral prompt]”. We use simple prompts that do

      Additionally, we follow aDFT (Shen et al., 2023) and incorporate diverse sets of prompts including

445   6.1   D IVERSITY AND FAIRNESS IN IMAGE GENERATION
446
447   Diversity and fairness are active areas of research in image generation. However, these terminologies
448   often refer to distinct concepts in past works. The word diversity is used to refer broadly to the
449   coverage of concepts in the training distribution. Accordingly, many techniques exist to improve
450
      diversity. For example, in current diffusion models, we can tune the guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol,
      2021; Ho & Salimans, 2022) as a knob for trading off diversity with image quality. However, these
451
      works as well as recent extensions (e.g., (Kim et al., 2022), (Sadat et al., 2023)) focus on diversity as
452
      a generic term, and not diversity of specific attributes that have fairness and equity implications such
453   as race and gender. For examples, for the prompt “doctor”, a set of images of white male doctors
454   with varying hairstyles, camera angles, lighting conditions, backgrounds can be considered as more
455   “diverse” than generating a single image of a middle-aged doctor with the same pose and background.
456   While indeed diverse along one axis, this notion does not capture “fair” representation of identities,
457   which is the focus of this work.
458
      Another line of related works focus on “fairness”, which measures whether generative samples
459   matches a desired distribution over a specific sets sensitive attributes such as gender and race. We
460   discuss some representative works Early approaches that reweigh the importance of samples in a
461   biased training dataset to improve fairness (Choi et al., 2020). FairGen (Tan et al., 2020) improve the
462   fairness of a pretrained Generative adversarial network (GAN) by shifting its latent distribution using
463   Gaussian mixure models. FairTL (Teo et al., 2023) improves the fairness of GAN by fine-tuning a
464   discriminator on a small unbiased dataset. Um and Suh (Um & Suh, 2023) employs LC-divergence
465   to improve the fairness of GAN, which better captures the distance between real and generated in
466   small training datasets. Despite their successes, these methods are tested on small datasets such as
467
      CelebA (Liu et al., 2015). They are not applicable to T2I diffusion models pretrained on large-scale
      datasets either because of GAN specific designs or requires re-training using the pretrained data.
468
469   Most recently, FairDiffsuion (Friedrich et al., 2023) and ITI-Gen (Zhang et al., 2023) attempts to
470   mitigate the bias of diffusion model at inference time by randomly injecting editing prompts or
471   learned tokens in the sampling process. However, these methods are inflexible and do not work
472   with arbitrary prompts. (For example, these methods will always inject a randomly sampled edit
473
      prompt, such as “black female”, “Asian male”, to user inputs, even if the user input is “a white male
      police officer” or “an oak tree in the field”). By contrast, adjusted direct fine-tuning (aDFT) (Shen
474
      et al., 2023) fine-tunes the diffusion model using optimal transport objective and do not require any
475
      intervention at inference time. Our work provide an alternative approach of fine-tuning diffusion
476   models for fairness using the population-level alignment objective.
477
478
      6.2   A LIGNING GENERATIVE MODELS WITH HUMAN PREFERENCES
479
480   A growing line of recent work considers the alignment of the outputs of large language models
481   (LLMs) to improve their safety and helpfulness by directly querying humans (or other AI models) to
482   rank or rate model outputs to create a preference dataset. The most basic approach is reinforcement
483   learning with human preferences (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017), which trains a reward model on
484   this preference data and then employs reinforcement learning to maximize the expected rewards. The
485   RL step typically make use of proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) to prevent
      the model from diverging too much from the pretrained model. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) simplified


                                                         9
486
      this process by converting the RL objective to a supervised-finetuning-style objective, eliminating
487   the need to first fit a reword model. Recently, various works (Wallace et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023)
488   extended DPO to text-to-image diffusion models. These works mostly focus on improving the quality
489   of generated images, with little emphasis on fairness and safety.
490
491
492
      7   L IMITATIONS
493
      Our method can only mitigate the biases to a certain degree. It cannot completely eliminate all
494
      perceived biases. In general, there is a trade-off between fairness and image quality, as shown in
495   our extensive ablations. The user can adjust these parameters based on how much they value these
496   two goals with respect to each other. Additionally, our method assumes all prompts that do not
497   explicitly includes gender or race as neutral prompts. However, people may have varying views. For
498   example, people may disagree on if “the president of the United States” should leads to images of a
499   female president. On one hand, one should not assume the leader of a free democratic society be
500   limited to a specific gender. On the other hand, at the time of writing there is no female president of
501   the United States. In this aspect, generating an image of female president may be considered as a
502   misrepresentation of fact, which can hardly be called “fair”. This is especially the case for prompts
503
      involving a historic context, like “the president of the United States in the 1800s”. We avoid using
      these potentially controversial prompts.
504
505   Our model relies on gender and race classifier which achieves high performance over the categories
506   on which they are trained. However, there are ethnicity in the real world beyond the fixed set of
507   classes. Similarly, our gender classifier fails to represent the existence of non-binary gender. We have
508   proposed a pipeline to collect preference data for bias mitigation using human feedback. In principle,
509
      it should be able to curate a preference dataset representing these nuances with human annotators.
      Due to the prohibitively expensive cost, we left these for future works to address.
510
511   Additionally, there is also the concern that if the visual appearance should dictate a person’s gender
512   and ethnicity as opposed to self-identification. In this aspect, our model can only identify “gender
513   appearances” and “ethnic appearances”, but not “gender identities” and “ethnic identities” as these
514   concepts involves non-visual elements such as self-recognition.
515
516   8   B ROADER I MPACTS
517
518   PopAlign aims to reduce certain commonly perceived biases on text-to-image generative models,
519   such as gender and racial biases. PopAlign can be particular to useful as an extra step before the
520   release of new T2I models to mitigate the biases without sacrificing image quality. However, it may
521   also inadvertently perpetuate new biases, such as non-binary genders and minority races, which could
522   be excluded from the preference datasets. Therefore, we suggest users to take extra caution when
523
      dealing with these situations. As any other image generator, PopAlign may be misused to create
      realistic-looking images for deception, fraud and other illegal activities. In addition, by adjusting the
524
      preference data, an adversary may use PopAlign to amplify existing gender and ethnical biases, such
525
      as creating an image model generating exclusively light-skinned characters. We do not condone these
526   kinds of use.

      9   C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In summary, we propose PopAlign, a novel algorithm that mitigates the biases of pretrained text-to-
      image diffusion models while preserving the quality of the generated images. PopAlign successfully
532
      extend the pair-wise preference formulation used by RLHF and DPO to a novel population-level
533
      alignment objective, surpassing comparable baselines in both human evaluation and quantitative
534   metrics. In particular, PopAlign outperforms the supervised fine-tuning baseline on identity-neutral
535   prompts, identity-specific prompts, as well as generic human written prompts in terms of both fairness
536   and image quality. However, it is also important to recognize that our experiments are limited in
537   that it employs a race-gender classifier that assumes a binary gender categorization and a limited
538   set of races. It does not capture the complicated nuances such as non-binary gender identity and
539   many under-represented races. We plan to address these limitations in future works by employing
      real humans to create a more diverse set of training data that capture these nuances.

749   monotonically decreases as it moves away from the origin. α = 0.25 will increase the update step of
750   the negative samples because µ(0.25) is closer to the negative samples, which makes the inner term
751   closer to the origin. Similarly, µ(0.25) will increase the update step of the positive samples. We show
752
      the results in Table 4.
753   α = 0.25 leads to model divergence, as the negative samples have a stronger “pushing force” than
754   the “pulling force” of positive samples in this setup. α = 0.75 leads to lower discrepancy and image
755   quality, as it increases the “pulling force” of positive samples, implicitly decreasing the effect of
      divergence penalty.

      C    F URTHER A NALYSIS OF THE DATASET GENERATION PROCESS
811
812
813   It is important to note that the prompt used during the training process is the same. A training data
814   point consists of two batch of images X w , X l , each containing N images and a single prompt C.
815   In an ideal case, we would sample K ≫ N images from a single prompt C and sub-sample it to
816   create winning and losing batches. However, samples of under-represented groups are very rare. For
817
      example, we observe that among 100 images generated from the prompt “doctor”, only 6 are female
      doctors. (Sec 4.1). Hence, sampling X w can be computationally challenging. So we use augmented
818
      prompts instead. The underlying assumption here is that (1) sampling from a prompt “an Asian,
819
      female engineer” is roughly equivalent to (2) sampling a large amount of images from the prompt
820   “an engineer” and selecting Asian female samples. Our assumption should hold for scenarios where
821   the target subpopulations (eg, Asian engineers) are a strict subset of the full population (in this case,
822   engineers), which we expect to hold for most practical fair generation scenarios.
823
      In theory, consider the joint data distribution of P (X, C1 , C2 ) where X is the image, C1 is a sensitive
824
      attribute such as gender, and C2 is a neutral prompt with no sensitive attribute (e.g. occupation). As-
825
      suming a generative model G(X|C) is sufficiently capable of understanding prompts, then G(X|C =
826   ”an engineer”) approximate P (X|C2 = ”engineer”) and G(X|C = ”a female engineer”) approx-
827   imate P (X|C1 = ”female”, C2 = ”engineer”). If this holds, the process (2) first samples from
828   P (X, C1 |C2 = ”engineer”) and filter out examples C1 ̸= ”female”, which is equivalent to sample
829   from process (1): P (X|C1 = ”female”, C2 = ”engineer”).
830
      To verify this, we conducted two additional experiments: we select 100 images from 20 (due to
831
      computation limit) categories such as “a female doctor”, using process (1) and process (2) respectively.
832   For process (2), the filtering process is conducted using a classifier.
833
834   Empirically, (2) takes around 57x GPU hours compared with (1). We also asked human evaluators to
835
      evaluate the quality of 100 randomly sampled pair and compare the quality of process (1) and process
      (2). Human evaluators show no strong preference: 42% prefer images generated using process (1),
836
      45% prefer images generated using process (2), 13% believe there is no difference. These results
837
      show that the two process generate images of similar quality, as they do not exhibit statistically
838   significant differences.
839
840   In principle, our proposed method works with both (1) and (2). In the Gaussian example, we use (2)
841
      instead of (1) because sampling from this “toy example” is not as expensive. However, we show that
      this approximation is theoretically justified with reasonable assumption, and empirically validate this
842
      assumption on the text-to-image generation task.
