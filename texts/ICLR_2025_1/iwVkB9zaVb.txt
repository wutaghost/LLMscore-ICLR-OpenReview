011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Existing Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) struggle with mathematical geomet-
014            ric reasoning due to a lack of high-quality image-text paired data. Current geomet-
015            ric data generation approaches, which apply preset templates to generate geomet-
016
               ric data or use Large Language Models (LLMs) to rephrase questions and answers
               (Q&A), unavoidably limit data accuracy and diversity. To synthesize higher-
017
               quality data, we propose a two-stage Reverse Chain-of-Thought (R-CoT) geom-
018
               etry problem generation pipeline. First, we introduce GeoChain to produce high-
019            fidelity geometric images and corresponding descriptions highlighting relations
020            among geometric elements. We then design a Reverse A&Q method that reasons
021            step-by-step based on the descriptions and generates questions in reverse from the
022            reasoning results. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed method brings sig-
023            nificant and consistent improvements on multiple LMM baselines, achieving new
024            performance records in the 2B, 7B, and 8B settings. Notably, R-CoT-8B signifi-
025            cantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art open-source mathematical models by
026            16.6% on MathVista and 9.2% on GeoQA, while also surpassing the closed-source
027
               model GPT-4o by an average of 13% across both datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit excellent reasoning capabilities and draw extensive atten-
033
      tion from the artificial intelligence research community (Lu et al., 2023b) to mathematical problem-
      solving in textual form (Chen et al., 2024b; Liao et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024b;
034
      Zhou & Zhao, 2024; Kim et al., 2024). However, LLMs still struggle to solve mathematical prob-
035
      lems involving images that require visual comprehension. Geometry problems, as typical mathe-
036   matical problems with images, play an important role in evaluating mathematical reasoning skills
037   (Zhang et al., 2023c), requiring a high level of visual comprehension. Besides, even though some
038   problems are not related to geometry on the surface, they require the same skills for models (e.g.,
039   fine-grained image comprehension skills and multi-step reasoning skills). With the appearance of
040   o1 (OpenAI, 2024), GPT-4o (Islam & Moushi, 2024), Gemini (Team et al., 2023), and numerous
041   Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) (Li et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024d; Bai et al.,
042   2023), recent researches progressively investigate using LMMs to solve mathematical geometry
043   problems.
044   Although LMMs show impressive results in general visual question-answering (VQA) tasks (Fan
045   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), they still face challenges in solving mathematical geometry problems.
046   The main reason is that the training data for LMMs are mainly from natural scenes, which have
047   a gap with geometric data, leading to poor performance. Additionally, the limited size of existing
048   geometric datasets further limits the geometric reasoning performance of LMMs.
049   Existing approaches for generating Q&A pairs in geometric tasks can be broadly classified into
050   three categories. The Rewording method (Gao et al., 2023) rewords Q&A pairs from open-source
051   datasets using LLMs to increase the number of questions. But this method ignores the diversity of
052   images and knowledge points, as shown in Fig. 1 (a). The Template-based method (Kazemi et al.,
053   2023; Zhang et al., 2024) introduces a data engine generating accurate geometric images and Q&A
      pairs. However, the generated images often lack fidelity and the template-based Q&A pairs are

124   2   R ELATED W ORK
125
126   Recent research aimed at improving geometric reasoning in LMMs can be broadly divided into two
127   categories. The first category focuses on inspiring geometric reasoning ability during the inference
128   stage, while the other attempts to improve reasoning ability through targeted training.
129
130   Inspiring Model Potential During Geometric Inference. For the inference process, Zhao et al.
131   (2024a) employs the chain of thought in visual and symbolic language modes to cross-validate and
132
      correct each other for the final result. Hu et al. (2024) utilizes code to generate images and solve
      problems through a visual chain of thought. Meanwhile, Mouselinos et al. (2024) uses a LLM as
133
      an agent to call external tools.
134
135
      Improving Model Reasoning Ability During Geometric Training. For model training, sym-
136
      bolic geometry solvers like GeoS (Seo et al., 2015), Inter-GPS (Lu et al., 2021), and S2G (Tsai
137
      et al., 2021) aim to build formal language systems that use formal language for deductive reasoning
138   on geometry problems. These systems are manually designed for formal languages with relatively
139   small datasets, e.g. the GeoS dataset containing 186 problems and the Geometry3k (Lu et al., 2021)
140   dataset containing about 3000 problems. The size of the datasets has increased slightly with the ad-
141   vent of neural geometric solvers, such as UniGeo (Chen et al., 2022), GeoQA (Chen et al., 2021),
142   GeoQA+ (Cao & Xiao, 2022), and PGPS9K (Zhang et al., 2023a), with a total size of around 25k.
143   The above datasets are collected manually, with high labeling costs and limited scale. With the rise
144   of LMMs, these data scales are far from satisfactory for training, so many methods are devoted to
145   building larger datasets. G-LLaVA (Gao et al., 2023) uses an LLM to reword original Q&A pairs
146
      in the GeoQA and Geometry3k dataset, resulting in 115k geometric Q&A data and 60k alignment
      data but does not increase the diversity of images and knowledge points. GeomVerse (Kazemi et al.,
147
      2023) uses a code-written engine to generate accurate geometric images and QA pairs, and there is
148
      still a certain gap between the generated images and real-world geometric images. Additionally, the
149   questions generated by the template lack diversity.
150
151   To synthesize geometry data with both accuracy and diversity, we introduce R-CoT, a novel geome-
152
      try data generation pipeline that addresses visual hallucinations and reasoning limitations in LMMs.
      This pipeline effectively generates the GeoMM dataset, featuring high-fidelity geometric images
153
      with accurate and diverse Q&A pairs.

236   3.1   G EO C HAIN
237
      To synthesize geometric images that are close to real-world geometric images, we design GeoChain,
238
      a chain of geometric images and descriptions generation engine that can generate both high-fidelity
239
      geometric images and their accurate descriptions. Only image descriptions will be used in the sub-
240   sequent generation of geometric Q&A pairs.
241
242   As illustrated in Fig. 3, the GeoChain consists of three parts. Specifically, we first construct a
243
      geometry substrate pool that contains 20 different geometry substrates. Next, we randomly sample
      one or more substrates from this pool and input them into the Geometry Generation Chain. In the
244
      Geometry Generation Chain, the sampled substrates are combined into one geometric image step
245
      by step. Different from previous methods, our methods include many line operations (e.g., adding
246   a line that connects midpoints of neighbor edges), which are common in real-world mathematical
247   geometry images. Besides, at each step, we label the vertices with random letters (e.g., A, B, C) and
248   annotate the geometric properties such as edge lengths and angles to create high-fidelity geometric
249   images. Corresponding image descriptions are also generated step by step according to predefined
250   templates. It is worth mentioning that these descriptions not only describe geometric shapes but
251   also contain the relations between different geometric elements, such as points on which lines and
252   whether two lines intersect. These relation descriptions are essential for the generation of relational
253   geometry questions.

255   3.2   R EVERSE A&Q
256
      Current LLMs still have limitations in solving complex geometric problems, using LLMs to directly
257
      generate Q&A pairs in one step may bring incorrect information. Inspired by CoT, we propose the
258
      Reverse A&Q (as shown in Fig. 4) to generate accurate and diverse Q&A pairs step by step using
259   the generated image descriptions. This process consists of three steps: Description Patch Reasoning,
260   Chain-of-Thought Fusion, and Question Generation.
261
262   Description Patch Reasoning. As the GeoChain can ensure the accuracy of the generated image
263   descriptions, we want to maintain this data accuracy during the following steps. Hence, we design
264   Description Patch Reasoning. First, image descriptions are segmented into patches to reduce the
265   difficulty of reasoning. Then these description patches are inputted into an LLM to generate single-
266   step reasoning results in a contextual learning manner.
267
268   Chain-of-Thought Fusion. To increase the complexity of the generated geometric problems, we
269   introduce our Chain-of-Thought Fusion. In this step, single-step reasoning results are fused pro-
      gressively, which means previous single-step reasoning results can provide necessary information

304   3.3   G EO MM
305
306   Through the R-CoT pipeline, we construct a high-quality geometric dataset, GeoMM. Detailed sta-
307   tistical information regarding the images and text within GeoMM is presented in Fig. 5.
308
309
      At the image level, GeoMM contains 20 geometric shapes, with the most common being trian-
      gles, quadrilaterals, and circles. To ensure the model can interpret geometric images of varying
310
      complexity, GeoMM includes images categorized into four complexity levels, determined by the
311
      number of geometric shapes present. Unlike previous-generation engines, which primarily focus on
312   constructing geometric images through the combination of polygons or circles, we emphasize the
313   critical role of lines in geometric figures. Lines with special properties, such as midlines or radii,
314   are foundational to many geometric theorems (e.g., the midline theorems). To enhance the richness
315   of geometric knowledge embedded in Q&A pairs generated at later stages, we integrate line ele-
316   ments with specific properties (e.g., radii) into the images. This approach significantly improves the
317   fidelity of the generated images. A comparison of the synthesized images is shown in Fig. 6.
318   At the text level, the GeoMM dataset is composed of four major categories of geometric problems,
319   with a particular emphasis on the relational question type, which is often underrepresented in exist-
320   ing synthetic datasets. The completeness of the generated geometric descriptions, which incorporate
321   multiple relationships between geometric elements, facilitates the generation of relational questions.
322   Such relational problems are intended to help the model better understand and process relative in-
323   formation among geometric components. Detailed examples of the different question types can be
      found in Appendix B.

399   4.2   E FFECTIVENESS OF G EO MM
400
401   Compared with existing datasets. We train our model using GeoMM and two recent synthetic
402   datasets for geometric problems, i.e. MAVIS (synthesis part) (Zhang et al., 2024) and Geom-
403   Verse (Kazemi et al., 2023) at the same data scale for a fair comparison. Specifically, we sample
404   the data from each dataset to different scales. As we observe clear performance fluctuations caused
405
      by the quality of train data, we train the models three times at each data scale and report the aver-
      age Top-1 accuracy in Fig. 7 (a) and (b). In general, all three datasets can improve the geometry
406
      reasoning ability of the baseline model. The model trained using our GeoMM exhibits significantly
407
      superior performance in most settings, demonstrating the better quality of GeoMM. Moreover, as
408   shown in Fig. 7 (c) and (d), the performance variance of our method is significantly lower. The
409   more stabilized optimization also indicates the better quality of GeoMM since our method aims to
410   improve the fidelity of images and the accuracy of Q&A pairs.
411
412   Influence of data scales. Intuitively, more training data can lead to better performance. As shown
413   by the trend in Fig. 7 (a) and (b), the performance on both datasets can be further improved when
414   increasing the scale of GeoMM. Surprisingly, the performance declines on MathVista when training
415   with the other two datasets. We assume that the limited diversity and the gap between their data
416   and real-world geometric problems would restrict their scalability. However, it is inevitable that the
417   diversity of synthetic data is still constrained by the generation mechanism. When the data scale
418
      exceeds 87k, performance saturates. Therefore, we set the size of GeoMM to 87K and the following
      experiments are conducted using it.
419
420
      Generalized effectiveness to other LMMs. We extend our method to several recent LMMs to ver-
421
      ify its universality. Comparing the models trained only using Geo170K with using both Geo170K
422
      and our GeoMM, the latter exhibits consistent improvements in accuracy as shown in Tab. 1. Specif-
423   ically, the baseline models are improved by at least 1.9% on MathVista and 0.8% on GeoQA, re-
424   spectively. The performance difference is most obvious on Geo-InternLM-XC2-7B where R-CoT-
425   InternLM-XC2-7B exhibits increases of 3.8% and 4.0%. The most advanced InternVL2.0-8B is still
426   improved by 2.0% on MathVista and 1.7% on GeoQA. The results indicate that GeoMM not only
427   has effective geometry knowledge but also can be widely applied to various advanced LMMs.

531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose R-CoT, a novel reverse generation pipeline that significantly enhances the quality and
534   fidelity of geometry Q&A pair generation. The data produced by R-CoT offers obvious advantages
535   over previous synthesis geometry datasets, such as MAVIS and GeomVerse. Our approach achieves
536   consistent improvements over existing LMMs, setting new state-of-the-art results compared to both
537   open-source and closed-source models. Our results highlight the critical role of high-quality data
538   in improving the geometric reasoning capabilities of LMMs. We will extend this method to other
539   types of mathematical questions while exploring strategies to mitigate LMM visual hallucinations
      and improve data accuracy, providing further insights for future research.

741   The core idea of the Reverse A&Q is to improve the accuracy of Q&A pairs by first simplifying
742   the reasoning based on descriptions and then generating corresponding questions from the answers
743   in a reversed manner. A straightforward approach is directly prompting ERNIE Bot 4.0 to generate
744   Q&A pairs from the input image description. However, as shown in the left of Fig. 13, this approach
745   often fails to determine the correct answer. In contrast, the Q&A pairs produced by Reverse A&Q
746   are correct for all three instances with our design.
