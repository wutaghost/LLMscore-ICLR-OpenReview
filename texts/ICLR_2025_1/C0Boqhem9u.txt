009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural encoding of artificial neural networks (ANNs) aligns the computational
012            representations of ANNs with brain responses, providing profound insights into
013            the neural basis underpinning information processing in the human brain. Cur-
014            rent neural encoding studies primarily employ linear encoding models for inter-
015            pretability, despite the prevalence of nonlinear neural responses. This leads to a
016            growing interest in developing nonlinear encoding models that retain interpretabil-
017            ity. To address this problem, we propose LinBridge, a learnable and flexible
018            framework based on Jacobian analysis for interpreting nonlinear encoding mod-
019
               els. LinBridge posits that the nonlinear mapping between ANN representations
               and neural responses can be factorized into a linear inherent component that ap-
020
               proximates the complex nonlinear relationship, and a mapping bias that captures
021
               sample-selective nonlinearity. The Jacobian matrix, which reflects output change
022            rates relative to input, enables the analysis of sample-selective mapping in nonlin-
023            ear models. LinBridge employs a self-supervised learning strategy to extract both
024            the linear inherent component and nonlinear mapping biases from the Jacobian
025            matrices of the test set, allowing it to adapt effectively to various nonlinear encod-
026            ing models. We validate the LinBridge framework in the scenario of neural visual
027            encoding, using computational visual representations from CLIP-ViT to predict
028            brain activity recorded via functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). Our
029            experimental results demonstrate that: 1) the linear inherent component extracted
030
               by LinBridge accurately reflects the complex mappings of nonlinear neural en-
               coding models; 2) the sample-selective mapping bias elucidates the variability of
031
               nonlinearity across different levels of the visual processing hierarchy. This study
032
               not only introduces a novel tool for interpreting nonlinear neural encoding models
033            but also provides novel evidence regarding the distribution of hierarchical nonlin-
034            earity within the visual cortex.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In recent years, aligning the computational representation of artificial neural networks (ANNs) with
040
      brain activity through neural encoding models significantly advances our understanding of the neural
041
      basis underlying information processing in the human brain. Previous research primarily employs
042   linear encoding models due to their interpretability (Naselaris et al., 2011a; Yamins & Dicarlo,
043   2016). However, the prevalence of nonlinear neural processes is well recognized (Naselaris et al.,
044   2011b; Wang et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Jain & Huth, 2018), which may limit the ability of linear
045   models to fully capture the complex dynamics of brain activity. This limitation not only undermines
046   the predictive performance of linear encoding models but also restricts their capacity to effectively
047   interpret neural activity.
048   With the rapid advancement of deep neural networks, nonlinear encoding models are increasingly
049   utilized (Zhang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2020; 2021). These models incorporate activa-
050   tion functions or other nonlinear structures, allowing them to better capture the brain’s responses to
051   complex stimuli and leading to improved predictive performance compared to linear models (Zhang
052   et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2020; 2021). However, as illustrated in Figure 1, nonlinear
053   encoding models exhibit sample-specific characteristics, resulting in unstable structures that com-
      plicate the interpretation of underlying relationships.

      In this study, we propose LinBridge, a learnable and flexible framework based on Jacobian analysis
066
      for interpreting nonlinear encoding models. The Jacobian matrix, which quantifies the output change
067   rates relative to input, enables the analysis of sample-selective mapping in nonlinear models (Gale &
068   Nikaido, 1965). LinBridge posits that the nonlinear mapping between the computational representa-
069   tions and neural responses can be factorized into two components: a linear inherent component that
070   approximates the complex nonlinear dynamics, and a mapping bias that captures sample-selective
071   nonlinearities. However, this factorization presents a dilemma: the sample-selective nonlinearities
072   introduce substantial variability in the Jacobian matrices across different input-output pairs, compli-
073   cating the extraction of consistent and interpretable mapping structures within nonlinear encoding
074   models.
075   To address this challenge, we propose a self-supervised learning strategy based on contrastive learn-
076   ing, which has demonstrated superior capabilities in differentiating shared and distinctive attributes
077   through paired-sample analysis (Oord et al., 2018; Schneider et al., 2023). Within the contrastive
078   learning framework, LinBridge maximizes shared linear component within the Jacobian matrices
079   while minimizing the influence of nonlinear features (nonlinear mapping biases) on those shared
080   components, leading to an effective delineation of the linear inherent component and the nonlin-
081
      ear mapping biases. In addition, this self-supervised learning strategy allows LinBridge to adapt
      effectively to various nonlinear encoding models. Furthermore, LinBridge incorporates a low-
082
      dimensional embedding module that facilitates dimensionality reduction while preserving the in-
083
      trinsic structure of the feature space, providing a more intuitive tool for analyzing the brain’s linear
084   and nonlinear responses to external stimuli. The contributions of this work are summarized as fol-
085   lows:
086
087          • We introduce LinBridge, a flexible framework designed to extract both the linear inherent
088            component and the nonlinear mapping biases from nonlinear encoding models. This frame-
089            work enables an interpretable analysis of the nonlinear mappings between computational
090            representations and brain responses.
091          • The linear inherent component extracted by LinBridge exhibits activation patterns highly
092            consistent with the original nonlinear encoding model, suggesting that the complex map-
093            pings in nonlinear neural encoding models can be effectively captured.
094          • We apply LinBridge to a neural encoding exploration of vision transformer models and re-
095            veal the variability in nonlinearity across different levels of the visual processing hierarchy.

      2.1   L INEAR N EURAL E NCODING M ODELS
101
      Due to their simplicity and interpretability, linear neural encoding models are widely applied across
102   various domains to disentangle the neural basis underpinning information processing in the brain,
103   including vision (Yamins et al., 2014; Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte, 2014; Güçlü & van Gerven,
104   2015; Eickenberg et al., 2017; Zhuang et al., 2021), audition (Zhou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
105   MILLET et al., 2022; Tuckute et al., 2023; Vaidya et al., 2022), and language (Liu et al., 2023;
106   Caucheteux & King, 2022; Goldstein et al., 2022; Jain & Huth, 2018; Schrimpf et al., 2021; Ab-
107   dou, 2022). However, the inherent nonlinear dynamics of neural activity limit the predictive power
      and interpretability of linear models, particularly when addressing more complex cognitive func-

108
      tions. This limitation is especially pronounced in higher-order cortical areas, which often involve
109   complex and dynamic interactions, suggesting that their underlying neural mechanisms may not be
110   adequately captured by linear representations (Naselaris et al., 2011b).
111
112
      2.2       N ONLINEAR N EURAL E NCODING M ODELS
113
114
      Nonlinear encoding models emerge as a solution to the limitations of linear models by incorporat-
115   ing nonlinear structures that more effectively capture the complex patterns of brain activity. These
116   approaches demonstrate superior predictive performance compared to linear models (Zhang et al.,
117   2019; Li et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2020; 2021). However, the opaque nature of nonlinear encoding
118   models presents significant challenges in understanding the mapping between the computational
119   representations and brain responses. To address this challenge, most existing methods adopt the
120   framework proposed by (Tank et al., 2021), leveraging various time-series prediction models to en-
121   hance the interpretability of these learned black-box mappings (Khanna & Tan, 2019; Bussmann
122   et al., 2021; Suryadi et al., 2023). In particular, the Jacobian matrix has been employed to elucidate
123
      the local mapping relationships in artificial neural networks (ANNs) (Zhou et al., 2024; Suryadi
      et al., 2023). Nonetheless, nonlinear models often exhibit sample-specific mappings, leading to
124
      substantial variability in the Jacobian matrices across different input-output pairs. This variabil-
125
      ity complicates the extraction of consistent and interpretable mapping structures within nonlinear
126   encoding models.

131   3.1       DATASET AND P REPROCESSING
132
133   We use the Natural Scenes Dataset (NSD) (Allen et al., 2021) in this study. The NSD dataset
134   contains fMRI data from eight subjects passively viewing 73,000 color natural scene images over 40
135   hours. These images are cropped from the MS-COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014), with each image
136
      displayed for three seconds and repeated three times across 30 to 40 scanning sessions, resulting in a
      total of 22,000 to 30,000 experimental trials. The fMRI data in the NSD are acquired using a whole-
137
      brain gradient-echo EPI (echo-planar imaging) sequence at 7T, with a resolution of 1.8 mm and a
138
      repetition time of 1.6 seconds. Single-trial beta maps are estimated using a customized general linear
139   model and released alongside the raw fMRI data (Wang et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024). Similar to
140   previous studies (Wang et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024), these beta maps are normalized (zero mean
141   and unit variance) within each run and averaged across image repetitions to be used as functional
142   brain activity measures. In our experiments, each image and its corresponding beta map are treated
143   as a single sample. We divide the dataset into training, validation, and testing sets in a ratio of 8:1:1
144   (8000:1000:1000 samples). Notably, subjects 1, 2, 5, and 7 complete the full experimental protocol,
145   and thus, their fMRI data are utilized in our experiments. In the experiments on neural encoding of
146   vision transformer models, we use the pre-trained CLIP-ViT (Radford et al., 2021)1 image encoder
147
      to derive the computational representation of the visual image stimuli.

179   3.4     L IN B RIDGE
180
181
      LinBridge leverages Jacobian matrices (JM) to quantify the complex mapping relationships in non-
      linear encoding models. It extracts the linear inherent component (JMinherent ), which captures con-
182
      sistent, interpretable mapping structure invariant to input samples, and the nonlinear mapping biases
183
      (∆JM) that reflect the unique nonlinear behaviors associated with distinct inputs.
184
185   3.4.1      E XTRACTION OF THE L INEAR I NHERENT C OMPONENT
186
187   Similar to the dimensionality reduction architecture introduced in CEBRA (Schneider et al., 2023)2 ,
188   LinBridge employs a multi-layer convolutional neural network (CNN) to refine the input Jacobian
189   matrices. Specifically, LinBridge progressively compresses the sample dimension of the Jacobian
190
      matrix using the CNN, enabling the extraction of the linear inherent component (JMinherent ∈
      Rd×1×p ) that captures the linear mapping between the input representations of the nonlinear neural
191
      encoding model and the corresponding voxel activations in the brain. This approach provides a more
192
      structured interpretation of neural activity.

357   4.3   N ONLINEAR E NCODING IN V ISUAL C ORTEX
358
359   The variations of ∆JM regarding to various samples are valuable to characterize the nonlinearity
360   of voxel-wise encoding model, and hence the visual cortex.
361
      However, the high dimension of ∆JM complicates the quantification of its variations among sam-
362
      ples. In this context, the low-dimensional embedding ∆JMdown derived from LinBridge provides
363   a novel perspective for depicting the nonlinearity of the visual cortex. To this end, we incorporate
364   linear fitting (Allen et al., 2021; Cohen, 1997; Hlinka et al., 2011) to assess how ∆JM varies with
365   different samples. Specifically, for each selected voxel, we first generate a sample index array and
366   then extract the corresponding response values from ∆JMdown . We subsequently calculate the coef-
367   ficients of a first-degree polynomial through polynomial fitting, with these coefficients representing
368   the linear response weights of the voxel across different categories. It is hypothesized that a voxel
369   responses to external stimuli linearly if the corresponding ∆JMdown is invariant to different sam-
370   ples (i.e., the absolute value of the first derivative approaches 0). Conversely, the voxel is deemed
371   nonlinear. Thus, we employ the absolute value of the first derivative (AFD) as a metric to evaluate
372   the nonlinearity of a voxel.
373   We first perform dimensionality reduction for the Clip-Vit features of all the 73,000 stimulus images
374   using t-SNE (Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008). We then reorder the image samples in descending
375   order according to their 1D-representation resulting from t-SNE. Figures 14-16 in A.5 show the
376   sorted image samples, demonstrating an obvious transition from ”simple” to ”complex”, as well as
377   that the samples with similar semantics cluster together. This distribution pattern remains consistent
      across subjects.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS , AND P ROSPECTS
433
434
      In this study, we introduce LinBridge, a novel framework aimed at interpreting nonlinear neural
435   encoding models through Jacobian analysis. We hypothesize that the intricate nonlinear mappings
436   between ANN representations and neural responses can be decomposed into a linear inherent com-
437   ponent and a sample-selective mapping bias. LinBridge effectively bridges the interpretability and
438   nonlinearity divide, advancing our understanding of neural encoding and serving as a valuable tool
439   for future investigations into complex neural computations across various brain regions.
440
      The present study acknowledges several limitations. First, we validate LinBridge by using a simple
441   nonlinear encoding model. In the future, it is interesting to conduct further validation studies using
442   more advanced nonlinear encoding models. Second, while the Jacobian matrix elucidates the map-
443   ping relationships between samples and corresponding outputs, its computation remains resource-
444   intensive when applied to large datasets. Given that pre-trained encoding models inherently contain
445   gradient information, the incorporation of low-rank matrix decomposition for efficient computation
446   of mapping relationships between samples represents a promising avenue for future work. Third, al-

      To investigate the relationship between visual feature representations and brain responses, two ANN

685   Training Strategy
686   The training process of the encoding model utilizes the Adam optimizer (Zhang, 2018) for parame-
687   ter optimization. To prevent overfitting, early stopping is employed, ceasing training if the validation
688   loss fails to improve over 8 consecutive epochs. Additionally, the model parameters that demonstrate
689   the optimal performance on the validation set are recorded and preserved.
