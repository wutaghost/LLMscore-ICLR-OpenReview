009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Actor-critic (AC) algorithms have been the powerhouse behind many successful
               yet challenging applications. However, the theoretical understanding of finite-
013
               time convergence in AC’s most practical form remains elusive. Existing research
014
               often oversimplifies the algorithm and only considers simple finite state and action
015            spaces. We analyze the more practical single-timescale AC on continuous state
016            and action spaces and use deep neural network approximations for both critic and
017            actor. Our analysis reveals that the iterates of the more practical framework we
018            consider converge towards the stationary point at rate O(T e −1/2 ) + O(m e −1/2 ),
019            where T is the total number of iterations and m is the width of the deep neural
020            network. To our knowledge, this is the first finite-time analysis of single-timescale
021            AC in continuous state and action spaces, which further narrows the gap between
022            theory and practice.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Actor-critic (AC) algorithms have driven numerous successful applications and are state-of-the-art
029   in reinforcement learning (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999; Mnih et al., 2016; Silver et al., 2017). Their
030   practical implementation typically consists of two parallel updates: the critic update and the actor
031   update. The critic incrementally estimates the action-value function for the current policy, while the
032   actor adjusts the policy network in the direction suggested by the estimated policy gradient based on
033
      the action value.
034   Despite AC’s widespread success, their theoretical understanding lags significantly behind. Most ex-
035   isting theoretical results focus on cases where the actor and the critic update at significantly different
036   rates. These include algorithms that either update the critic multiple times for a fixed actor (Yang
037   et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Agarwal et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2020a) or employ two-timescale
038   approaches where the actor’s stepsize decays faster than the critic’s (Wu et al., 2020b; Chen et al.,
039
      2023; Xu et al., 2020b; Hong et al., 2023). These settings are only made to simplify analysis. In
      practice, the actor and critic are typically updated at a single-timescale, using stepsizes that are
040
      constantly proportional to each other (Chen et al., 2021; Olshevsky & Gharesifard, 2023; Chen &
041
      Zhao, 2024; Tian et al., 2024). Single-timescale AC is typically more sample-efficient, as it avoids
042   artificially slowing down the actor update performed in the aforementioned AC variants (Olshevsky
043   & Gharesifard, 2023; Chen & Zhao, 2024).
044
045
      However, the theoretical analysis of single-timescale AC in practical settings is still largely miss-
      ing in the literature. As shown in Table 1, all existing works only analyze the single-timescale AC
046
      method in solving Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) with finite action space. This finite action
047
      space assumption excludes all continuous policies, including commonly used Gaussian, Uniform,
048   and Gamma policies. Given the commonness of continuous control tasks in practice and the preva-
049   lence of AC algorithms in addressing them (Lillicrap et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018), there is a
050   pressing need for theoretical guarantees in continuous settings. Moreover, Markovian sampling and
051   deep neural network approximation for both the actor and the critic are commonly used in practical
052   applications (LeCun et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018). However, existing studies have typically
053   addressed only one of these elements, failing to consider their compound effects in practice (see the
      summary in Table 1).

078   1.1   M AIN C ONTRIBUTIONS
079
080   Our main contributions are summarised as follows:
081
082   • We establish the convergence of single-timescale AC in continuous state and action spaces, which
083
      has not been accomplished in prior research (see Table 1). Notably, even for the simpler case of
      the two-timescale AC variants, existing analysis cannot establish their convergence in the contin-
084
      uous setting. Our work may serve as the foundation to analyze other two- or single-timescale AC
085
      algorithms in more general continuous settings.
086
087   • Our results demonstrate significant advantages over existing works on single-timescale AC. We
088   adopt more practical settings of deep neural network approximation and Markovian sampling for
089   both the actor and the critic. Compared to Tian et al. (2024), where the critic employs Markovian
090
      sampling to collect transition tuples, the actor still requires i.i.d. transition tuples sampled from a
      discounted state-action occupation measure, which demands a burdensome re-sampling. In contrast,
091
      we allow Markovian sampling for both the actor and critic, utilizing the same transition tuples,
092
      closely following the state-of-the-art practice that facilitates efficient online learning.
093
094   • Technically, we develop a new framework to address the challenges posed by the continuous
095   domain in single-timescale AC analysis. To establish the main results, we formulate a general
096   condition in Assumption 4.7 (c) and demonstrate that it is satisfied by a broad class of neural network
097
      policies (Proposition 4.8) on continuous space, and include the previous assumptions on discrete
      space as special cases. Moreover, we examine the neural network approximation errors of the
098
      evolving actor and critic, ensuring that the resulting errors do not amplify through their interactions.
099
      Our methodology enriches the analytical toolbox for single-timescale AC.

360   proximation classes. To demonstrate its connection to exploration, we show that if exploration is
361   insufficient, the assumption fails to hold. Consequently, when the assumption holds, it implies suf-
362
      ficient exploration. First note that the operator Dθ essentially multiplies the stationary distribution
      µθ to the function on its right (see the definition in Eq. (1)). If the policy πθ does not sufficiently
363
      explore, there exists a subset of the state space U ⊂ S such that µθ (U ) = 0. Furthermore, we can
364
      choose V̂ (ω) such that V̂ (ω; s) = 0, ∀s ∈ S \ U and V̂ (ω; s) ≥ 0, ∀s ∈ U . With this choice,
365
      the left-hand side of the inequality evaluates to 0, while the right-hand side becomes positive. This
366
      violates the condition stated in Assumption 4.5. Thus, the contrapositive holds: if Assumption 4.5
367   is satisfied, it ensures sufficient exploration of the state space under the policy πθ .
368
369   Note that sufficient exploration assumption is standard in the literature of analyzing the convergence
370
      of on-policy RL algorithms (Bhandari et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020b; Olshevsky &
      Gharesifard, 2023; Chen & Zhao, 2024). We can also drop this condition by analyzing the off-policy
371
      version of the algorithm under some sufficiently-exploring behavior policy that can be arbitrarily
372
      specified, and relates to the target policy by importance sampling. However, this is not the core
373   focus of the problem. Therefore, we adopt Assumption 4.5 directly and concentrate on the primary
374   challenge of analyzing the algorithm in the continuous state-action space.
