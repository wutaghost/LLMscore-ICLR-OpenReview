030                                                A BSTRACT
031
               Graphic design plays a vital role in communicating ideas, values, and messages.
032
               During the design process, designers typically organize their work into layers of
033
               text, objects, and backgrounds to facilitate easier editing and customization. How-
034            ever, creating designs in such a format requires significant effort and expertise. On
035            the other hand, with the advancement of GenAI technologies, high-quality graphic
036            designs created in pixel format have become more popular and accessible, but with
037            the inherent limitation of editability. Despite this limitation, we recognize the sig-
038            nificant reference value of these non-layered designs, as human designers often
039            derive inspiration from these images to determine layouts or text styles. Moti-
040            vated by this observation, we propose Accordion, a graphic design generation
041            framework built around a vision language model playing distinct roles in three
042
               key stages: (1) reference creation, (2) design planning, and (3) layer generation.
               Distinct from existing methods, by using the reference image as global design
043
               guidance, our approach ensures that elements within the design are visually har-
044
               monious. Moreover, through this three-stage framework, Accordion can benefit
045            from an unlimited supply of AI-generated references. The stage-wise design of
046            our framework allows for flexible configuration and various applications, such as
047            directly starting from the later two stages given user-provided references. Addi-
048            tionally, it leverages multiple vision experts such as SAM and element removal
049            models to facilitate the creation of editable graphic layers. Experimental results
050            show that Accordion generates favorable results on the DesignIntention bench-
051            mark, including tasks such as text-to-template, adding text to background, and
052            text de-rendering. Furthermore, we fully explore the potential of Accordion to
053
               facilitate the creation of design variations, validating its versatility and flexibility
               in the design workflow.

074   1     I NTRODUCTION
075
076
      Graphic design is an important media format for modern visual communication. It boosts the clarity,
077
      aesthetic appeal, and communication effectiveness of digital content, impacting a wide array of real-
078   world applications such as advertisement and user interface design Casner & Larkin (1989). Given
079   the specialized application scenarios, graphic designs differ significantly from the natural images
080   Deng et al. (2009). Specifically, graphic design is intrinsically constructed in a layered format,
081   including distinct layers for text, foreground objects, and background. This structured layering
082   allows for editability and customization, enabling designers to perform a variety of modifications,
083   such as modifying text and backgrounds, thereby catering to specific user needs.
084   Despite the significant utility, creating layered graphic designs is a prohibitive task for most people
085   due to the need for design expertise and huge effort. With the aid of image GenAI models, more
086   design images have become available in rasterized pixel format. While they are visually compelling,
087   they inherently lack editability. Even for simple operations such as horizontal flipping, text becomes
088   unreadable since they are not separated from the background or other elements. Although users can
089   employ some image editing tools Chen et al. (2023); Jia et al. (2024); Hertz et al. (2022) to modify
090   the attributes of elements, such an approach is inconvenient compared to operations directly applied
091   on the layer representation.
092   Nevertheless, we still believe rasterized designs are of great value for creating layered designs, by
093   realizing that human designers naturally use rasterized designs from textbooks or other sources as
094   references to get inspiration in their design workflows. For instance, designers will use them in the
095   beginning to explore suitable layouts, deciding where to place objects and what style of typography
096   to use to achieve visual harmony. Given this practice, we aim to leverage rasterized designs as
097   references to create editable multi-layer designs, as illustrated in Figure 1.
098   Based on this motivation, we introduce Accordion1 , a framework as demonstrated in Figure 2 built
099   around a vision language model (VLM) consisting of three stages: (1) reference creation, (2) design
100   planning, and (3) layer generation, while the VLM plays different roles in these stages. Notably, the
101   framework can leverage a diverse range of AI-generated references. It also offers the flexibility to
102   start from the second stage when users explicitly provide designs as references. Besides, the VLM
103   uses some vision experts in the design process. For example, SAM Kirillov et al. (2023) and removal
104
      models Rombach et al. (2022) are used for element extraction and background filling.
105
106      1
           Our method is named “Accordion” because it unfolds rasterized designs into layered designs, similar to
107   how an accordion expands. Besides, the proposed Accordion framework harmoniously integrates each element
      within a layered design, much like orchestrating every note harmoniously in a musical score at the concert.

      In contrast to earlier work of text rendering methods such as TextDiffuser Chen et al. (2023) that
109   specifies the location of text before designing the background, and unlike COLE Jia et al. (2023)
110   that starts with the background to design text in the visual domain, our method starts with a global
111   reference image representing the target design as a whole. This initial reference image globally or-
112   chestrates the layout and visual properties of various elements, ensuring overall visual harmony and
113   preventing the conflicts that may arise in the step-by-step generation where earlier design decisions
114   may not work well with later ones.
115   Extensive experimental results and visualizations demonstrate the strong design capabilities of our
116   method, especially its superior performance across various tasks on the DesignIntention benchmark
117   Jia et al. (2023), including text-to-template, adding text to background, and text de-rendering. Addi-
118   tionally, we explore the potential of Accordion to facilitate creative design variation, including using
119   upstream generative models to modify references, applying inference time variations, and utilizing
120   downstream generative models for further variations based on the extracted layers. This shows the
121   significant role of Accordion in the design process with its versatility and flexibility.

125
126   Layered Design Generation There are a few investigations that focus on generating layered de-
127   signs Jia et al. (2023); Inoue et al. (2024); Shimoda et al. (2021) considering the need for editability
128   and customization. For instance, COLE Jia et al. (2023) starts from a brief user-provided prompt,
129   employing multiple large language models (LLMs) and diffusion models to generate each element
130   within the design. Even though COLE uses language to comprehensively plan the design, it still
131   visually constructs the design step-by-step, starting from the background, then generating objects,
132
      and finally the text. This sequential approach may lead to visual conflicts such as failing to allocate
      sufficient or suitable space for text or objects when generating background, often resulting from
133
      the lack of a global visual impression in mind. Open-COLE Inoue et al. (2024) adheres to the ar-
134
      chitecture of COLE but incorporates certain simplifications, such as omitting the object generation
135   stage. De-Render Shimoda et al. (2021) takes advantage of rasterized design images, leveraging
136   several models to predict text attributes and extract background. Similar to Open-COLE, it does not
137   explicitly separate out foreground objects. Additionally, commercial tools such as CanvaGPT canva
138   (2024) utilize preset templates to adapt user prompts to new layered designs as noted in Jia et al.
139   (2023). However, the diversity of designs is limited by the size of template libraries. We notice that
140   a few methods Zhang & Agrawala (2024); Zhang et al. (2023c); Tudosiu et al. (2024) are designed
141   for the layer generation of natural images. They are unsuitable for design images due to differences
142   such as the absence of text layers in natural images.
143   Another line of research involves predicting the attributes of partial elements given any existing ele-
144   ments, which are then combined together to create a layered design. Some works focus on predicting
145   the layout of elements within a design, typically emphasizing the importance of proper alignment
146   and the avoidance of overlaps to enhance visual aesthetics Shabani et al. (2024); Jyothi et al. (2019);
147   Li et al. (2019); Gupta et al. (2021); Hsu et al. (2023); Seol et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023; 2021); Zhu
148   et al. (2024); Zheng et al. (2019); Zhou et al. (2022); Chai et al. (2023); Horita et al. (2024); Jiang
149
      et al. (2022); Cheng et al. (2024); Liang et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2023a); Biswas et al. (2021); Ya-
      maguchi (2021); Yu et al. (2022). Besides, some methods concentrate on predicting other attributes,
150
      such as text font and color, beyond layout within the design Inoue et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2018);
151
      Lin et al. (2023); Biswas et al. (2024).
152
153   Overall, our method utilizes a VLM to predict attributes from global reference images for layered
154   construction. It also employs visual experts to extract elements from the reference image to form
155
      layers, distinguishing it from existing methods.
156
157   Non-layered Design Generation Existing GenAI methods are capable of creating an unlimited
158   number of non-layered design images. Methods like Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2022); Podell
159   et al. (2023); Esser et al. (2024), DALLE-3 Betker et al. (2023), and Ideogram ideogram (2024) have
160   demonstrated this capability of design image generation guided by user prompts. However, these
161   methods often suffer from text rendering errors Daras & Dimakis (2022), which can significantly
      impact the usability and aesthetic of the generated images.

186   3     M ETHODOLOGY
187
188   We sequentially detail the three stages of Accordion as illustrated in Figure 2, explaining the differ-
189   ent roles the VLM plays in each stage. Note that we use GenAI images as references for illustrative
190   purposes. Accordion can also start from the later two stages if users explicitly provide other types
191   of references such as an existing design or a background layer.
192
193   3.1     S TAGE 1: R EFERENCE C REATION
194
195   In this stage, our objective is to generate an image that serves as a global reference throughout the
196   design process. Accordion is designed to accommodate a variety of inputs, including user-provided
197
      short intentions or sketch drafts, thus catering to diverse user preferences and needs. To facilitate
      this, we employ the VLM for prompt generation. As mentioned in COLE Jia et al. (2023), users
198
      may provide only short intentions for simplicity, such as “create a poster for Father’s day”. We
199
      use in-context learning by supplying the VLM with multiple examples for prompt enhancement
200   following Open-COLE Inoue et al. (2024). This approach enables the VLM to generate detailed
201   prompts such as A father embraces his child in the center, surrounded by the text “Father’s Day” and
202   “My All Time Hero.”. It is observed that detailed and lengthy prompts can facilitate the creation of
203   images with rich detail Chen et al. (2024b) and significantly improve text quality Chen et al. (2024a)
204   for better reference. If users wish to provide more layout constraints, they can use sketch drafts. In
205   such cases, the VLM is instructed to give detailed descriptions of the depicted objects, accurately
206   depicting object positions and filling text in suitable areas. We show the prompt templates and
207   visualizations in Appendix B. Subsequently, these generated prompts are fed into a text-to-image
208
      (T2I) model to generate images as references to be used in the next stage.
209

214
215   We employ VLM for plan generation. As illustrated in Figure 3, the VLM processes the reference
      image alongside a combined prompt, which includes a predefined task description Ptask , a descrip-

249
      3.3     S TAGE 3: L AYER G ENERATION
250
251
      In this stage, our goal is to construct the layered design based on the generated plan and reference
252   image, as illustrated in Figure 4. Our approach is to extract and remove elements from the reference
253   image, and then stack them back together to construct the final layered design.
254
255
      Our initial step is to use a text removal model to erase text, recognizing that text regions are com-
      monly placed on the top layer for enhanced readability. Based on the text removal result, our next
256
      focus moves to object removal, where we sequentially extract the topmost element according to the
257
      order outlined in the design plan. We employ the SAM, conditioned on the bounding box specified
258   in the design plan, to extract the foreground object and obtain its corresponding mask. The mask
259   and the intermediate image are fed into an object removal model to remove the foreground object.
260   Notably, the object removal can be executed iteratively if multiple objects are detected in the design
261   plan.
262
      However, it is noticed that the removal model sometimes generates diverse results, not all of which
263
      are satisfactory. To ensure consistent quality, we design a questionnaire that enables the VLM
264   to conduct result selection, as illustrated in Figure 4 (c). The top row shows the original image
265   alongside the masked image, where the text is masked with text boxes for the text removal task,
266   and the object is masked using the SAM segmentation map for the object removal task. During the
267   training phase, we present the VLM with the ground truth of the removal alongside three generated
268   removal results, training it to select the highest quality option. In the inference stage, we generate
269   four removal results for the VLM to choose the best one. We showcase the task prompt and some
      samples in Appendix D.

287   4.1     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
288
289   Dataset. We employ an in-house layered design dataset Design39K, with 39,233 samples for train-
290   ing and 492 samples for validation. This dataset comprises a diverse array of designs, including
291   posters, book covers, advertisements, etc. Each sample is accompanied by a description. It is easy
292
      to extract layer information from these designs, including text, objects, and backgrounds, along with
      various attributes. We present more details about the dataset in Appendix E. As shown in Figure
293
      5, to enrich the reference sources, we incorporate the following types of designs for training: (1)
294
      Original designs. We wish the model to conduct text de-rendering by directly parsing these original
295   designs; (2) Designs with nonsensical text. We employ the Stable Diffusion 1.5 inpainting model
296   Rombach et al. (2022) to inpaint text areas with inpainting strength randomly set between 0.5 and
297   0.7, leading to the generation of nonsensical text by the model. This range is selected because
298   strength outside this range can lead to either insufficient or excessive inpainting changes, which
299   hinder effective training. Please note that in some cases inpainting results may not strictly maintain
300   the original text style, with potential variations in color and font. We consider this acceptable as it
301   enables the model to use references while generating creative variations. The goal is to enable our
302   model to effectively handle GenAI designs; (3) Background without text. By removing all text from
303
      a design and using only the background image as a reference, we challenge the model to add text in
      appropriate context and locations. We organize the elements of the design into a list of dictionaries
304
      and convert them into a string format for training the VLM. We employ the same training objectives
305
      for the three aforementioned reference types. Besides, to train the VLM for result selection, for both
306   text and object removal tasks, we utilize the removal model to generate three different results. These
307   results are then combined with the ground truth and randomly shuffled to construct the questionnaire
308   dataset. In total, we have prepared 156,932 samples, consisting of 39,233 training samples for each
309   of the three different types of references and the VLM questionnaire.
310
      Selection of vision expert models. For the Text-to-Image (T2I) model, we utilize Flux for its
311
      proficiency in generating high-quality references. Additionally, we use PaddleOCR as the OCR
312   tool. SAM Kirillov et al. (2023) and inpainting ControlNet Zhang et al. (2023b) for Stable Diffusion
313   1.5 Rombach et al. (2022) with the prompt “nothing in the image” are used in the layer generation
314   stage. Importantly, these models are modular and can be replaced when more advanced alternatives
315   are available. We detail each expert model in Appendix F.
316
      Training, inference, evaluation, and visualization. We use the vision language model LLaVA-1.5-
317
      7B Liu et al. (2023) as the cornerstone of Accordion. The model is trained on the aforementioned
318   156,932 samples using LoRA Hu et al. (2022) with learning rate 2e-4 for 6 epochs, conducted on 8
319   × 80G A100 GPUs for 36 hours. The reference image is scaled with the longer side set to 336 pixels
320   following Liu et al. (2023) for the VLM. The removal models operate at a resolution of 512×512,
321   which is the size of the final output. During inference, the average generation time per sample is 43.7
322   seconds. For evaluation, we use the DesignIntention benchmark Jia et al. (2023) which provides 500
323   detailed prompts across various design domains. We visualize the layered design through a Streamlit
      HTML frontend, with details provided in Appendix G.

349   4.2       A BLATION S TUDIES
350
351   In the following, we explore the efficacy of some architecture choices using the validation dataset.
352
      Should the VLM be trained separately or jointly across multiple tasks? Here we investigate
353
      whether joint training gains benefits or leads to degradation. The results for each task are presented
354   in Appendix H. We observe that joint training and separate training yield comparable average scores,
355   with joint training slightly outperforming by a margin of 0.82%. We opt for a more compact model
356   architecture and ultimately choose joint training.
357
      Does OCR prompt enhance VLM? Here we assess the impact of incorporating OCR prompt.
358
      Considering the text recognition task for parsing the original design, we observe improvements in
359
      paragraph-level OCR Normalized Edit Distance (NED) by 7.23% (61.28% to 68.51%). Addition-
360   ally, for the text detection task in both the original and GenAI designs, the average detection F1 score
361   is improved by 5.46% (73.12% to 78.59%). So we use OCR prompt to enhance model performance.
362
363
      Does removal task benefit from VLM result selection? We evaluate the efficacy of using ques-
      tionnaires for the result selection process. The results indicate that for text removal tasks, the PSNR
364
      increases from 31.31 to 31.97, and for object removal tasks, it improves from 29.33 to 29.59. It
365
      demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach in enhancing the results of removal tasks.

      COLE (approximately 1.5 times longer). In other words, our approach can fully utilize empty space

      methods are concatenated horizontal and shown to GPT-4V to assess which one has higher qual-

      for our model on 52% of all the samples compared to 48% for COLE. Notably, this preference is
457   achieved despite our model being trained on a considerably smaller dataset. Our model is trained
458   using only 39K data, substantially fewer than the 100K used to train COLE.

      Text de-rendering. In Figure 8, we validated the text de-rendering capability of our model in
      comparison with De-Render Shimoda et al. (2021). Note that our method treats paragraph-level
461
      text as a single entity. One advantage of paragraph-level representation is the uniformity of style, a
462
      feature that De-Render struggles to achieve. In contrast, De-Render predicts a style for each word
463   individually, which can lead to a visually disorganized appearance. Moreover, we showcase our
464   advantage with a translation application. By grouping words at the paragraph level, our approach
465   effectively considers the coherence of sentence semantics during translation, ensuring consistent
466   style and alignment of adjacent words. In contrast, since De-Render operates at the word level,
467   it lacks overall contextual information in translation application, leading to disorganized layouts
468   with overlapping text. Please note that since our method operates at the paragraph level, we cannot
469   guarantee that each line of text will align exactly as in the original. However, we believe this is
470   acceptable. As the text is already separated into layers, adjustments can be made easily.
471   Design variation creation. We demonstrate six methods based on our approach for creating design
472   variations under three categories: variation by upstream models, variation at inference time, and
473   variation by downstream models. As shown in Figure 9, sub-figures (a) and (b) employ an upstream
474   image variation model Xu et al. (2023) and an inpainting model Podell et al. (2023) respectively
475   to generate diverse reference images. Typically, generative models that produce text image variants
476   usually produce nonsensical text. Our approach effectively refines these designs. For inference time
477
      variations, sub-figure (c) achieves variation by conditioning on a prefix and using different seeds
      during inference. Sub-figure (d) employs the reference switch. Specifically, after the text removal
478
      stage, it does not continue to follow the original text reference but instead uses the background as
479
      the new reference, adding text on the background to explore new text layouts. For the utilization of
480   downstream generative models, sub-figure (e) utilizes a downstream layout generation model Visual
481   Layout Composer Shabani et al. (2024) to create variations based on extracted layers. Sub-figure (f)
482   uses Instruct Pix2Pix Brooks et al. (2023) to modify the background image. Direct application to
483   the entire flattened image often results in artifacts, especially in regions with small text. We apply
484   the editing only on the background layer and then recompose all the layers so that the quality of the
485   text area is preserved. These methods demonstrate the versatility, flexibility, and critical role of our
      approach in the design workflow.

531   5   C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this paper, we introduce the Accordion, a three-stage framework that leverages reference images
534   to create layered designs. It exhibits superior performance on the DesignIntention benchmark com-
535   pared to existing methods. Additionally, numerous applications underscore its flexibility, versatility,
536   and controllability. For the limitation, we observe a small probability (approximately 2%) where the
537   VLM-generated results cannot be parsed correctly. Additionally, about 3% of the font strings do not
538   match any fonts in our database. Hence, some post-processing is needed to refine the result format.
539   For ethical concerns, so far we have not identified issues. For future work, we aim to enhance the
      model’s capability to create new backgrounds and objects based on references.
