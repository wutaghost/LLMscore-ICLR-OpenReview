009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating pho-
012            torealistic images under proper conditional guidance. Such advancements raise
013            concerns about potential negative social impacts, such as the proliferation of fake
014            news. In response, numerous methods have been developed to differentiate fake
015            from real. Yet, their accuracy and reliability still need to be improved, especially
016
               when facing state-of-the-art generative models such as large diffusion models.
               Infrastructure-wise, the existing testing datasets are sub-optimal in terms of re-
017
               search dimensions and product utility due to their limited data volume and insuf-
018
               ficient domain diversity. In this work, we introduce a comprehensive new dataset,
019            namely ACID, which consists of 13M samples sourced from over 50 different
020            generative models versus real-world scenarios. The AI-generated images in this
021            collection are sampled based on fine-grained text prompts and span multiple res-
022            olutions. For the real-world samples, we broadly searched public data sources
023            and carefully filtered text-image pairs based on visual and caption quality. Using
024            ACID, we present ACIDNet, an effective framework for detecting AI-generated
025            images. ACIDNet leverages texture features from a Single Simple Patch (SSP)
026            branch and semantic features from a ResNeXt50 branch, and achieves overall
027
               cross-benchmark accuracy of 86.77%, significantly outperforming previous meth-
               ods such as SSP and CNNSpot by over 10%. Both our model and dataset will be
028
               open-released to the public.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Over the past few years, image generation has seen significant advancements due to the development
034   of deep learning models such as VAEs [24], GANs [17], and diffusion-based systems [42]. While
035   these technologies have greatly facilitated creative endeavors, they also pose serious societal risks,
036   such as the generation of fake news through deepfake technologies [43]. Recently, the text-to-
037   image generation models, i.e., diffusion-based models [42], have become widely adopted due to
038   their impressive performance. However, this capability is also being exploited to create fake images
039
      that are increasingly difficult to distinguish from genuine ones. Consequently, there is an urgent
      need to develop effective methods for detecting AI-created images.
040
041   Numerical methods have been developed to detect AI-created images. While most detectors have
042   focused on identifying images produced by GANs [52], PatchCraft [59] introduced a generalized
043   detector capable of identifying GAN-based and diffusion-based images. This was further supported
044   by testing on a subset of the dataset proposed in the paper as a benchmark that encompasses a di-
045
      verse range of AI-created images. Additionally, SSP [7] implemented a pipeline that uses a single
      simple patch as a fingerprint, achieving improved results on this benchmark. However, these two
046
      methods still exhibit limitations for real-world application. Both are predicated on the same hypoth-
047
      esis: the noise will appear in the simple or background patches of the generated images affected by
048   the CNN-based up-sampler [14]. This assumption may not always hold, potentially affecting their
049   effectiveness in diverse scenarios. The underlying assumption of these methods is validated within
050   their benchmark, which features generated images that are simplistic and of low quality, often con-
051   taining numerous artifacts, as shown in Figure 1a. However, as generative models have evolved,
052   recent advancements enable the production of images with more precise and richer content, condi-
053   tioned through text. This progress underscores the need to develop more challenging datasets that
      are better aligned with practical applications and current technologies.

142          • We have conducted extensive testing of various AI detectors on our dataset to demonstrate

145            created images. We plan to release both the model weights and the dataset to the commu-
146            nity, thereby contributing to advancements in AI safety.

148   2   R ELATED W ORKS
149
150   Image generation research has made significant progress in recent years. Many achieved to syn-
151   thesize realistic-looking images using the well-known GAN pipeline [5, 9, 22, 21, 33] as well as
152   the newly raised diffusion pipeline [37, 39, 40, 57, 48, 42, 56]. The inference of GAN models [5,
153   9, 22, 21, 33] requires a generator doing one feedforward pass conditioned on randomly sampled
154   latent vectors. While the diffusion models [37, 39, 40, 57, 48, 42, 56] iteratively denoise normally
155   sampled 2D noises (i.e. backward diffusion process) and synthesize real-looking images at the end
156   of the loop. In recent years, the open community of CV has proposed a lot of high-performing
157   diffusion models [4, 3, 2, 41, 45, 49, 36, 35] that gathered significant attention both in industry
158
      and research. As the goal of this work is to propose a reliable dataset for GenAI image detection,
      we therefore sampled synthetic images using the models listed above to achieve better quality and
159
      broader domains.
160
161   AI-generated image detection aims to determine whether an image is real or has been produced by
      generative models. Traditionally, this task is approached as a binary classification problem, where


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      simple CNN models often perform adequately if the training and testing images originate from the
163   same generative model [52, 20, 53]. However, models such as UnivFD [31] that leverage large
164   pretrained vision-language models for feature extraction tend to experience significant performance
165   degradation when tested with images from previously unseen models [59]. To enhance the detection
166   of images from new, unseen generative models, Fredect [15] introduced a method that detects gen-
167   erated images based on frequency anomalies. Additionally, LNP [27] employs a denoising model to
168   extract noise patterns from images, while PatchCraft [59] focuses on the differential noise charac-
169   teristics between foreground and background for better generalization. Building upon these ideas,
170   SSP [7] simplifies the approach by using noise from a single, simple patch of the image. In this
171
      work, we propose a novel ACIDNet as our baseline based on SSP and CNN-based structures, which
      can obtain both texture features and semantic features.

176   3.1   M OTIVATION
177
      Unlike traditional image classification tasks, the challenge of identifying AI-created images is dy-
178
      namically evolving with advancements among generative models. According to our evaluation in
179
      Table 4, even a classifier with excellent generalization capabilities may struggle to recognize images
180   produced by the latest diffusion models if only trained by early GANs. Therefore, an effective AI-
181   created image detection dataset is supposed to contain a sufficiently diverse collection of authentic
182   and AI-created images, along with the capacity to extend to future models easily.
183
      In the datasets used in previous research like [31, 59, 61], the images are mainly in realistic (photo-
184
      graphic) style, and each contains only one object from a specific category. This was primarily due to
185
      the limitations of the early generative models, predominantly those based on GANs. However, with
186   the development of generative models, the demand for distinguishing AI-created images extends
187   beyond the domains covered by these datasets. Consider the following scenarios:
188
189          • An individual collects public works of an artist and generates content closely resembling
190            the artist’s style, subsequently claiming it to be the artist’s original work for profit.
191          • A forgery created with generative models fabricates content unattainable in reality, such as
192            a tree concurrently bearing apples and watermelons. (Instances of AI-created images used
193            to simulate forgery in academic publications are already documented.)
194          • A user captures a photo and enhances it by applying a filter or adding some text to improve
195            its presentation before uploading.
196
197
      A model developed based on a dataset as described above may struggle to identify the first two
      examples as AI-created images due to the discrepancies in their distributions, but mistakenly classify
198
      the last example as an AI-created image for its non-photographic elements. The potential challenges
199
      highlighted above necessitate the creation of a more comprehensive dataset with images meeting the
200   following requirements: (1) contain various styles aside from photographic ones; (2) include rich
201   content like scenes with no or multiple objects; (3) with higher and more diverse resolutions; (4)
202   might have undergone primary, non-creative edits.

      3.3    AUGMENTATION
293
294   Beyond regular on-the-fly image augmentations on single images, to better recognize synthetic im-
295   ages after editing, we also simulated two typical image editing operations within our dataset: image
296   stitching and alpha composition to better recognize synthetic images after editing.
297
298   Image stitching        This augmentation is designed to simulate the operation of stitching two or
299   more images together. Specifically, we divide an initial image into 16 patches (4x4 grid), randomly
300   fill each patch with either the original image’s corresponding area or a matching area from another
301   image randomly selected from the dataset. We consider the resulting stitched image as real-world
302
      only if all patches come from real-world images; otherwise, it is considered an AI-created image.
303
304
      Alpha composition         This augmentation aims to simulate the effect of overlaying multiple im-
      ages. We paste one image over another with a transparency that linearly changes from 1 to 0 either
305
      vertically or horizontally. The composite image is considered real-world only if both underlying
306
      images are real-world; otherwise, it is categorized as AI-created.

507
508   In terms of dataset construction, our proposed method necessitates generating thousands of images

513   7   C ONCLUSION
514
515   We introduce a comprehensive new dataset, ACID, which features 13M samples derived from over
516   50 different diffusion-based models as well as real-world scenarios. The AI-created images in this
517   benchmark are crafted using finely tuned text prompts and span multiple resolutions. For real-world
518   samples, we conducted an extensive search of public data sources, meticulously selecting text-image
519   pairs based on the quality of both visuals and captions.
520   Leveraging ACID, we developed ACIDNet, an innovative framework to detect AI-created images.
521   ACIDNet combines texture analysis using a simplest patch branch and semantic extraction via a
522   ResNeXt50 branch. With training on more than 10 million 512x512 images from ACID , ACIDNet
523   has achieved an impressive average accuracy of 86.77% in cross-benchmark testing, surpassing
524   previous methods such as SSP and CNNSpot by over 10%.
525   We plan to openly release both our model and the benchmark dataset to the public. Our goal is to
526   contribute to the ongoing advancements in privacy and AI ethics, particularly as we move towards
527   the era of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI).
