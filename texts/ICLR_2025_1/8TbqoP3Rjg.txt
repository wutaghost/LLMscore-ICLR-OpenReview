                                                Abstract
010
011
012
              Since the amount of data generated by neural networks on the Internet is
              growing rapidly due to widespread access to corresponding models, it is logi-
013
              cal to inquire about the impact of this surge in synthetic data on the training
014
              of subsequent models that will utilize it during training. Previous work has
015           demonstrated a concerning trend: models trained predominantly on syn-
016           thetic data often experience a decline in performance, which can escalate
017           to a complete loss of the ability to reproduce the initial distribution of real-
018           world data. This phenomenon, now referred to as model collapse, highlights
019           the potential pitfalls of over-reliance on synthetic datasets, which may lack
020           the diversity and complexity inherent in genuine data.To address this issue,
021           we propose a novel method that leverages the well-established technique of
022           knowledge distillation. Our approach aims to mitigate the adverse effects
023
              of synthetic data by facilitating a more effective transfer of knowledge from
              high-performing teacher models to student model. By doing so, we seek to
024
              enhance not only the qualitative aspects—such as the richness and variabil-
025
              ity of the generated outputs—but also the quantitative metrics that gauge
026           model performance. Through extensive experimentation, we demonstrate
027           that our method improves the robustness and generalization capabilities of
028           models trained on synthetic data, for instance for DDPM enhancement is
029           68.8%, in terms of the FID metric, contributing to a more sustainable and
030           effective use of synthetic datasets in machine learning applications.
031
032
033   1 Introduction
034
035
      Generative models have become ubiquitous, what has caused increase of synthetic data
036   available. Consequently, future models will inevitably be trained on such kind of data, what
037   can have detrimental effects on their performance — a phenomenon now commonly referred
038   to as Model Collapse (Shumailov et al., 2023). As the proportion of synthetic data within a
039   training subset increases, the behavior of the trained models becomes more unpredictable.
040   For example, language models trained on synthetic data are prone to generate less likely
041   or coherent responses compared to those trained on real-world data. Furthermore, it is not
042   uncommon to observe repetitive words or phrases in the outputs of language models exposed
043   to synthetic data during training.
044   The primary cause of this issue lies in the distributional properties of the synthetic data.
045   Since synthetic data is generated from a finite number of samples, its distribution only
046   approximates that of real data. As synthetic pipelines becomes deeper — through repeated
047   cycles of data generation and model retraining — the resultant data distribution diverges
048   progressively from the original, leading to degraded model performance over time.
049   Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) has emerged as an effective technique for trans-
050   ferring the knowledge and capabilities of a larger model to a smaller one. This approach has
051   shown considerable potential. (Sanh et al., 2020; Muralidharan et al., 2024). Motivated by
052   this, we explore the application of knowledge distillation to mitigate the problem of Model
053   Collapse by transferring the knowledge of a model trained on real data to a model that has
      been trained on synthetic data. In particular, since the models used in knowledge distilla-


                                                     1
054
      tion in our case have the same number of parameters and architecture, we apply so-called
055   self-distillation (Mobahi et al., 2020).
056
057   To sum up, the main contributions of this paper are:
058
           1. We propose the use of knowledge distillation as a solution to address Model Collapse.
059
060        2. We perform experiments across different modalities and architectures, providing
061
              empirical evidence of the effectiveness of our approach.
062
      Our research is structured as follows: at first we consider papers, related to ours, then
063   describe the technique itself. After that we demonstrate the effectiveness of our method
064   on unconditioned image generation task on such architectures as Variational Autoencoder
065   (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013) and Denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) (Ho
066   et al., 2020). Further we consider causal language modelling task, i.e. abstractive text
067   summarization. In conclusion, we discuss the obtained results.
068
069   2 Related work
070
071   In this section, we describe existing works, related to our.
072
073
      Model Collapse (Shumailov et al., 2023) is a process of losing performance, while being
      progressively trained on synthetic data. The higher the percentage of synthetic data in the
074
      training dataset, the higher the divergence between the output distribution and the initial
075
      one. It has been showed, that this behavior is intrinsic for every modality and architec-
076   ture, regardless of number of parameters, except that small models are more susceptible to
077   collapse.
078
079
      Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) is an effective technique of transferring the
      capabilities of one trained model (teacher) to another with fewer parameters (student). It is
080
      achieved by adding to the initial loss function an extra term, that penalizes student for the
081
      difference between its outputs and teacher ones. In our case, architectures and numbers of
082   parameters stay constant, and we use so-called self-distillation (Mobahi et al., 2020). But
083   main difference of our approach is that the dataset used for training the student model
084   consists completely of generated data.
085
      Approaches, aimed to mitigate model collapse include several techniques. First of
086
      all, we can simply keep some percentage of the real-world data and do not replace it with
087
      synthetic, but accumulate all the data in one dataset (Gerstgrasser et al., 2024). This
088   approach helps to reduce effect of Model Collapse, but it is noticeable, that this method
089   does not make synthetic data itself more applicable for training purposes. In other words,
090   we still have skewed data distribution in our dataset. Our approach is aimed at making
091   synthetic data have more information about initial distribution, what can also benefit while
092   training with accumulation, like in the mentioned case (Gerstgrasser et al., 2024). Another
093   way of mitigating Model Collapse implies using so-called corrective functions (Gillman et al.,
094   2024). This approach was widely tested on the human motion synthesis task and significantly
095   less on image generation. Our research is aimed more at generating images and text.
096
097   3 Distillation for synthetic pipeline
098
099   Our approach involves employing a method analogous to self-distillation (Mobahi et al.,
100   2020) to mitigate Model Collapse. Let LM be the loss function of some generative model
101   M , training on dataset D. We denote the resulting trained by minimizing LM model as
102   M0 . Additionally, we define DM0 as the dataset, sampled by M0 . Now, drawing parallels
103   with self-distillation, we define the model M1 as the student model and the model M0 as
104
      the teacher model. Then L(M0 ,M1 ) is loss function between student and teacher outputs.
      We emphasize that loss can be computed not only between predictions of models but also,
105
      for instance, between outputs of particular layers.
106
107   We define the final loss as a weighed sum of the original loss function LM and the loss
      function between predictions of teacher and student: LSD = LM + λL(M0 ,M1 ) , where λ

350     Consider now our approach applying to causal language modelling task. In particular, we
351     have chosen abstractive text summarization task. As a model we take small version of

        we use a batch size of 32 articles, AdamW optimizer with learning rate 0.00002 and cosine

        metrics ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2 and ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004). During generation we use greedy
