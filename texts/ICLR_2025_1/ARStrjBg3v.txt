011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) involves multiple participants collaborating to
014            train models on distinct feature sets from the same data samples. The distributed
015            deployment of VFL models renders them vulnerable to adversarial perturbations
016
               during inference, motivating the need to visit the VFL robustness problem. Adver-
               sarial Training (AT) is the predominant approach for enhancing model robustness.
017
               However, its application in VFL, termed Vertical Federated Adversarial Learning
018
               (VFAL), faces significant computational challenges: Generating adversarial ex-
019            amples in AT requires iterative full propagations across participants with heavy
020            computation overload, resulting in VFAL training time far exceeding those of
021            regular VFLs. To address this challenge, we propose DecVFAL, an accelerated
022            VFAL framework through a novel Decoupled backpropagation incorporating a
023            dual-level decoupled mechanism to enable lazy sequential and decoupled parallel
024            backpropagation. Lazy sequential backpropagation sequentially updates the adver-
025            sarial example using timely partial derivatives with respect to the bottom module
026            and delayed partial derivatives for the remaining modules. Decoupled parallel
027
               backpropagation updates these delayed partial derivatives by utilizing module-wise
               delayed gradients, enabling asynchronous parallel backpropagation with flexible
028
               partitions that align with VFL’s distributed deployment. Rigorous theoretical anal-
029
               ysis demonstrates that despite introducing multi-source approximate gradients due
030            to the dual decoupled mechanism√and the techniques from the existing VFL meth-
031            ods, DecVFAL achieves a O(1/ K) convergence rate after K iterations, on par
032            with regular VFL systems. Experimental results show that, compared to existing
033            methods, DecVFAL ensures competitive robustness while significantly achieving
034            about 3 ∼ 10 times speed up on various datasets.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Federated learning (FL) enables collaborative training of deep learning models among distributed
      participants without sharing raw data McMahan et al. (2016). Conventionally, most FL research
040
      considers Horizontal Federated Learning (HFL), which assumes distributed clients possess data
041
      with identical features but varying sample spaces Zhao et al. (2021). In contrast, Vertical Federated
042   Learning (VFL) assumes distributed clients share the same samples but have different features Liu
043   et al. (2024); Wei et al. (2022). VFL model comprises a server-maintained top model and client-side
044   bottom models that map local data features to embeddings. During inference, each client computes
045   the local embedding of data features and uploads to the server through a communication channel for
046   prediction Liu et al. (2024). Due to its advantages in facilitating data collaboration across multiple
047   industries, VFL has gained increasing attention in various domains such as recommendation systems
048   Cui et al. (2021); Yuan et al. (2022), finance Long et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021a), healthcare Song
049   et al. (2021); Cha et al. (2021), and emerging applications Teimoori et al. (2022); Ge et al. (2022).
050   Machine Learning (ML) models have demonstrated vulnerability to adversarial attacks, carefully
051   crafted inputs designed to induce misclassification during inference. Recent studies highlight that this
052   susceptibility becomes even more pronounced in the VFL context due to its decentralized architecture
053   Huang et al. (2024); Duanyi et al. (2023). Adversarial attacks in VFL can manifest in multiple forms:
      through malicious or colluding clients perturbing local features of raw data Pang et al. (2022); Qiu


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al. (2022), or via third-party adversary intercepting and altering embeddings during client-server
055   communication Duanyi et al. (2023). These diverse attacks underscore the unique security challenges
056   in VFL systems, motivating the urgent need to address the VFL robustness problem.
057
058
      Extensive research has been conducted on defenses against adversarial attacks, with Adversarial
      Training (AT) emerging as the most empirically robust approach to date Tramèr et al. (2018). AT
059
      is a min-max robust training method that minimizes the worst-case training loss at adversarially
060
      perturbed examples Madry et al. (2017). The deployment of AT in the FL paradigm, termed Federated
061   Adversarial Learning (FAL), has garnered attention, with a particular focus on HFL scenarios, where
062   each participant maintains a complete copy of the model Li et al. (2023). These studies incorporate
063   AT into clients’ local training steps and focus on non-IID settings and secure aggregation solutions
064   Li et al. (2023); Deng et al. (2020); Bhagoji et al. (2019); Zizzo et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022a).
065   However, in VFL scenarios, a single global model is partitioned and distributed among the server and
066   clients, resulting in a different architecture for Vertical Federated Adversarial Learning (VFAL). To
067   the best of our knowledge, VFAL has yet to be thoroughly investigated in the current literature.
068   Due to layer-wise distributed deployment, VFAL presents unique computational efficiency challenges.
069   Adversarial sample generation during AT is computationally intensive, requiring sequential forward
070   and backward propagation to calculate gradients with respect to the input for iterative refinement
071   Madry et al. (2017). In VFL context, inherent sequential dependencies across layers cause participants’
072   models to remain idle until receiving necessary information (embeddings or gradients) from adjacent
073   layers on other participants (Figure 1-left). Consequently, the training time for VFAL significantly
074   exceeds that of regular VFLs. To illustrate, VFAL using PGD-20 requires about 20 times more
075
      computational cost than regular VFL due to 20 iterations needed to generate each adversarial example.
076   Several works have focused on accelerating AT-based robust training, but they are designed for
077   centralized model training without consideration for adaptation to VFAL. Examples include YOPO
078   estimates the gradient on the input by only propagating the first layer Zhang et al. (2019), FreeAT
079   reuses gradients for multiple steps to update both adversarial examples and model parameters, Shafahi
080   et al. (2019), Amata adjusts the number of inner maximization steps with an annealing mechanism
081
      Ye et al. (2021), Bhat & Tsipras (2019) propose asynchronously generating adversarial examples
      leveraging data parallelism, and FGSM-PGK assembles the prior-guided initialization and model
082
      weights Jia et al. (2024). Another line of research explores the design of computational efficient
083
      vanilla VFL frameworks, including multiple client updates Zhang et al. (2022b), asynchronous
084   coordination Li et al. (2020), compression Castiglia et al. (2022); Li et al. (2020), sample and
085   feature selection Castiglia et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2022) one-shot communication Wu et al.
086   (2022); Cha et al. (2021). While these studies have made significant strides in improving the
087   computational efficiency of VFL, they lack a comprehensive investigation into the integration with
088   VFAL framework. Taking into account these observations and challenges, a natural question arises:
089       In light of the intensive adversarial sample generation and inherent sequential dependencies,
090               how can we accelerate VFAL training while maintaining robust performance?

      To tackle the computational efficiency challenge in training robust VFL models, we propose DecV-
109   FAL, an accelerated VFAL framework through a novel Decoupled backpropagation incorporating a
110   dual-level decoupled mechanism (Figure 1-right). DecVFAL first decouples the bottom module from
111   the remaining modules and introduces lazy sequential backpropagation, which periodically treats the
112   partial derivatives of the remaining modules as fixed and utilizes timely partial derivatives for the
113   bottom module to execute multiple sample updates sequentially, avoiding frequent complete gradient
114   propagation. Furthermore, while updating the adversarial samples at the bottom module, DecVFAL
115   updates the partial derivatives of the remaining modules through decoupled parallel backpropagation,
116   where each module independently updates its partial derivatives with module-wise delayed gradients
117
      on separate processors, achieving asynchronous parallel backpropagation.
118   Contributions (i) We propose DecVFAL, which incorporates a dual-level decoupled mechanism
119   to enable lazy sequential and decoupled parallel backpropagation, significantly accelerating VFAL
120   training while maintaining robust performance. (ii) Our rigorous theoretical analysis reveals√that
121   despite the introduction of multi-source approximate gradients, DecVFAL maintains an O(1/ K)
122   convergence rate after K iterations, matching that of standard VFLs, underscoring the superiority
123   of DecVFAL. (iii) Comprehensive experimental evaluations demonstrate that DecVFAL not only
124   achieves competitive robust performance but also delivers a remarkable 3 ∼ 10 fold acceleration
125
      compared to existing adversarial training methods compatible with VFL.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
130
131
      Adversarial Attack in VFL. Research highlights the need for robust VFL models Ye et al. (2024),
132
      while introducing novel adversarial attack techniques Duanyi et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2022). In
133   relaxed VFL protocols, where clients can access the server model and outputs from other clients Luo
134   et al. (2021); Wang (2019); Lundberg & Lee (2017), a wide range of white-box adversarial attacks
135   Madry et al. (2017); Carlini & Wagner (2017); Croce & Hein (2020); Kurakin et al. (2016) become
136   feasible through malicious and colluding clients. Standard VFL protocols, despite restricting critical
137   information, remain vulnerable to black-box adversarial attacks Chen et al. (2017). Additionally,
138   Chen et al. (2022) employs a GAN-based method with a surrogate model and semi-supervised
139   learning to generate performance-impairing perturbations. Further expanding the threat landscape,
140   Duanyi et al. (2023) explores third-party adversaries through an online optimization method that
141
      disrupts inference, integrating adversarial example generation with corruption pattern selection.
142   Adversarial Training. AT enhances model robustness by incorporating adversarial examples, with
143   its effectiveness depending on the strength of those examples Goodfellow et al. (2014). While
144   non-iterative attacks like FGSM offer some resilience, they remain vulnerable to more advanced
145   methods Kurakin et al. (2016). Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) Madry et al. (2017) provides
146   superior robustness against obfuscated gradient defenses Athalye et al. (2018) but is computationally
147
      expensive due to frequent adversarial updates. FreeAT Shafahi et al. (2019) combines the updates of
      adversarial examples and model parameters in one backward pass, YOPO Zhang et al. (2019) focuses
148
      on adversarial example updates at first-layer, and FreeLB Zhu et al. (2019) accumulates gradients
149
      and update parameters after completing adversarial iterations. While these methods offer promising
150   approaches to balance robustness and efficiency in AT, their applicability and effectiveness within the
151   VFAL framework remain unexplored, highlighting a critical gap in current research.
152
153
      Decouple Training. The inherently sequential nature of forward and backward propagation in neural
      network training has long been a focus of optimization, with researchers proposing various innovative
154
      methods to decouple the process and improve computational efficiency. Notable contributions include
155
      the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), which decomposes the optimization
156   problem into smaller, more manageable subproblems, facilitating parallel processing Taylor et al.
157   (2016). Synthetic Gradients enable asynchronous updates by predicting gradients for each layer,
158   reducing dependencies between network components Jaderberg et al. (2017). The delayed Gradient
159   Method allows for parallel processing of different network sections, potentially speeding up training,
160   by introducing a temporal shift in gradient computation Huo et al. (2018b;a); Zhao et al. (2024). Lifted
161   Machines involves transforming the network architecture to create opportunities for parallelization,
      thereby improving computational efficiency Gu et al. (2020); Li et al. (2019).

386
387   Datasets. Real-world VFL datasets are proprietary and not publicly accessible. Therefore, we utilized
388   two public datasets instead for our main experiments: MNIST LeCun et al. (1998) and CIFAR-10
389   Krizhevsky (2009). These datasets were vertically partitioned among all participants, with each client
390   retaining a portion of features for each sample, while the server exclusively held the labels. Detailed
391   information about the dataset partitioning can be found in Section C.1.
392   Baselines. We deploy the baseline algorithms and DecVFAL in a hybrid cascaded VFL framework,
393   synchronous VFL-CZOFO Wang et al. (2024). The implemented AT algorithms include PGD-r
394   Madry et al. (2017), FreeAT-r Shafahi et al. (2019), FreeLB-r Zhu et al. (2019), and YOPO-m-n
395   Zhang et al. (2019). Additionally, we integrated data parallelism, model parallelism, and asynchronous
396   mechanisms with PGD, resulting in DP-PGD, MP-PGD, and Asy-PGD, respectively.
397
      Adversarial attack. Following the threat model of adversarial attack in VFL (Appendix A.3), we
398   employ various adversarial attack methods including FGSM Kurakin et al. (2016), PGD-r Madry et al.
399   (2017), and CW Carlini & Wagner (2017). We also simulate scenarios where malicious clients cannot
400   directly obtain gradients and implement CERTIFY (CER) Cohen et al. (2019), zero-order-based
401   FGSM (ZO-FGSM) and PGD (ZO-PGD) Chen et al. (2017). Additionally, Considering the case
402   of the third-party adversary, we employ adversarial attacks that corrupt embeddings using different
403   corrupted client selection methods: Thompson Sampling with Empirical Maximum Reward (E-TS)
404   Duanyi et al. (2023) and All Corruption Pattern (ALL).
405   Training procedures. For the experiment applying the split MLP model on MNIST, a batch size of
406   32 was utilized. For the experiment applying the ResNet-18 on CIFAR-10, a batch size of 80 was
407   used. The models were trained to converge. To ensure a fair comparison, we employed the Adam
408   optimizer with a fixed learning rate across all VFL frameworks. Detailed parameter settings and
409   hardware specifications for the training procedures are summarized in Appendix C.3 and Table 10.

533   7        C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   This paper presented DecVFAL, a framework that significantly accelerates VFAL while maintaining
536   robustness. DecVFAL incorporates a dual-level decoupled mechanism to enable lazy sequential
537   and decoupled parallel backpropagation for adversarial example generation. This approach achieves
                                                                                                  √
538   3-10 fold speedup on MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets, with theoretical guarantees of O(1/ K)
539   convergence rate. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate DecVFAL’s effectiveness across various
      neural architectures and VFL configurations.

      A     BACKGROUND
811
812
      A.1    V ERTICAL F EDERATED L EARNING
813
814   VFL encompasses a range of architectural designs tailored for collaborative machine learning across
815   multiple parties. These architectures, distinguished by data and parameter distribution, as well as the
816   trainability of the server model, include Aggregated Vertical Federated Learning (aggV F L) Fu et al.
817   (2022); Liu et al. (2021b), where client parties contribute intermediate results aggregated through
818   a non-trainable function in the server party; Aggregated Vertical Federated Learning with Central
819   Features (aggV F Lc ), similar to aggVFL but incorporating its own features; Split Vertical Federated
820   Learning (splitV F L) Fu et al. (2022); Jin et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021a), featuring a trainable server
821
      model processes intermediate results from passive parties; and Split Vertical Federated Learning
      without Local Features (splitV F Lc ), where the server party doesn’t provide any features to the
822
      model but relies solely on intermediate results from client parties.
823
824   Because VFL is a collaboration system that requires parties to exchange gradient or model level
825   information, it has been of great research interest to study communication efficiency, and data privacy
826   protection. Various strategies are adopted to heighten communication efficiency, typically involving
827
      reducing the cost of coordination and compressing the data transmitted between parties, such as
      multiple client updates Zhang et al. (2022b), asynchronous coordination Li et al. (2020), one-shot
828
      communication Wu et al. (2022), and data compression Castiglia et al. (2022); Li et al. (2020).
829
      In terms of data privacy protection, VFL relies on cutting-edge technologies like Homomorphic
830   Encryption (HE) Yang et al. (2019), Multi-Party Computation (MPC) Xie et al. (2022); Liu et al.
831   (2020), and Differential Privacy (DP) Wang et al. (2024) to preserve data privacy.
832
833
      A.2    V ERTICAL F EDERATED A DVERSARIAL L EARNING
834
835   Emerging research has investigated the distinct challenges posed by adversarial attacks in the con-
836   text of VFL Huang et al. (2024). Due to the distributed nature, VFL struggles to ensure client
837   trustworthiness and thus renders it highly susceptible to adversarial perturbations, underscoring the
838   pressing need for enhanced VFL model robustnessHuang et al. (2024), this is particularly evident in
839   neural network models. Prior works have proposed that adversaries (third-party or client party) can
840   generate adversarial samples by introducing manipulated perturbations to raw data or embeddings
841   in the corrupted clients, aiming to mislead the inference of VFL models Luo et al. (2021); Weng
842
      et al. (2020); Qiu et al. (2022); Fu et al. (2022). However, existing VFL defense mechanisms based
      on cryptographic Liu et al. (2021b) and non-cryptographic Liu et al. (2021a) only concentrate on
843
      mitigating inference attacks and backdoor attacks while neglecting adversarial attacks.
844
845
846
      A.3    T HREAT M ODEL
847
      In the context of VFL, we focus on untargeted adversarial attacks, constructed during the inference
848
      phase. The adversary’s objective is to corrupt samples whose original prediction is yu , causing the
849   server model to output ŷ ̸= yu . We categorize these adversarial attacks into two primary scenarios:
850
851          • Malicious (colluding) clients. In this scenario, we consider the presence of malicious
852            (colluding) clients acting as adversary. During the attack, all malicious clients (one or more)
853            collaboratively and simultaneously generate the adversarial feature partition. The attacks
854            are further classified based on the level of knowledge these clients possess:
855               – White-box adversarial attack. Under relaxed protocol, clients have access to the server
856                 model fs and the output of all clients xtc . This protocol could occur when the client
857                 needs to make interpretable decisions based on the server model’s parameters Luo et al.
858                 (2021); Wang (2019); Lundberg & Lee (2017). This implies the malicious clients have
859                 the necessary information to calculate the partial gradient to the features.
860               – Black-box adversarial attack. Under basic VFL protocol, all participants keep their
861                 private data (e.g., labels and features), as well as the server model fs and client models
862                 {f(c) }C
                           c=1 local during inference. Clients can only receive the final prediction results ŷ
863                 and cannot directly obtain the gradient, thus necessitating the use of black-box methods
                    to approximate it.

             • Third party adversary. We also consider an adversary as a third party in VFL inference,
865            who can access, replay, and manipulate messages on the communication channel between
866            two endpoints, where embeddings and predictions are exchanged. Third-party adversaries
867            usually cannot achieve access to top model parameters, thus this scenario generally falls

       A.6   C OMMUNICATION C OMPRESSION
973
974    Compression is a pivotal technique in VFL that aims to mitigate communication overhead by
975    reducing the volume of data transmitted among participating parties. In the context of neural
976    network-based VFL algorithms, high-dimensional input vectors are inherently mapped onto lower-
977    dimensional representations, which serve a natural compression purpose. However, to further enhance
978    communication efficiency, specialized dimensionality reduction techniques are often integrated.
979    Several VFL frameworks have been proposed to incorporate compression techniques: AVFL Cai
980
       et al. (2024) leverages PCA to compress the data before transmission, reducing the communication
       load. CE-VFL Khan et al. (2022) employs both PCA and autoencoders to learn latent representations
981
       from raw data, which are then used for model training. SecureBoost+ Chen et al. (2021b) and
982
       eHE-SecureBoost Xu et al. (2021) encode encrypted gradients into a compact form, minimizing
983    the number of cryptographic operations and the data transmission size. C-VFL Castiglia et al.
984    (2022) introduces an arbitrary compression scheme to VFL, offering a theoretical analysis of how
985    compression parameters impact the overall system efficiency.
986
       Compression techniques play a critical role in VFL by enabling more efficient data transmission with-
987
       out compromising the integrity of the learning process. The selection of an appropriate compression
988
       method is contingent upon the specific requirements of the VFL scenario, including the sensitivity
989    of the data, the computational resources available, and the desired balance between communication
990    efficiency and model performance.

       C.1      DATASET DETAILS
1677
1678   Our experiments were constricted on public datasets MNIST and CIFAR10:
1679

1681              60,000 examples for training and 10,000 examples for testing.

1683              of 60,000 images categorized into 10 classes.
1684
1685   To simulate the VFL scenario, we allocated distinct features to each party based on the described
1686   methodology in prior works Luo et al. (2021); Qiu et al. (2022); Fu et al. (2022). We partition the
1687   last dimension of the features according to the feature proportion of each client. We use masking to
1688
       ensure that each client receives distinct features.

1690   C.2      A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
1691
       To validate robustness, we employed a suite of adversarial attack methods. FGSM is a fast, non-
1692
       iterative attack Kurakin et al. (2016); PGD-r iteratively perturbs input data using gradient information
1693   to maximize the model’s loss Madry et al. (2017); and CW uses a custom loss function to ensure
1694   minimal perturbations while achieving misclassification Carlini & Wagner (2017). CERTIFY (CER)
1695   generates adversarial perturbations with Gaussian noise Cohen et al. (2019). For black-box attacks,
1696   we combined adversarial methods with zeroth-order optimization: FGSM (ZO-FGSM) and PGD
1697   (ZO-PGD) Chen et al. (2017). We also considered scenarios involving a third-party adversary,
1698   corrupting embeddings using different client selection strategies, including Thompson Sampling with
1699   Empirical Maximum Reward (E-TS) Duanyi et al. (2023) and All Corruption Patterns (ALL).
