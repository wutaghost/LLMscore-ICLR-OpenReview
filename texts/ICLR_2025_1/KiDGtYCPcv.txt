011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding the intentions and beliefs of others, a phenomenon known as "theory
014            of mind", is a crucial element in social behavior. These beliefs and perceptions are
015
               inherently subjective and latent, making them often unobservable for investigation.
               Social interactions further complicate the matter, as multiple agents can engage in
016
               recursive reasoning about each other’s strategies with increasing levels of cognitive
017
               hierarchy. While previous research has shown promise in understanding a single
018            agent’s belief of values through inverse reinforcement learning, extending this
019            to model multiple agents remains an open challenge due to the computational
020            complexity. In this work, we adopted a probabilistic recursive modeling of
021            cognitive levels and joint value decomposition to achieve efficient multi-agent
022            inverse reinforcement learning (MAIRL). We provided a numerical method to
023            evaluate value decomposition errors in multi-agent tasks with discrete state and
024            action spaces. To validate our method, we conducted simulations of a two-agent
025            cooperative foraging task in a grid environment. Our algorithm revealed the ground
026
               truth goal-directed value function and effectively distinguished between level-0 and
               level-1 agents. When applied to human behavior in a cooperative hallway task, our
027
               method identified meaningful goal maps that evolved with task proficiency and an
028
               interaction map that is related to key states in the task without accessing to the task
029            rules. Similarly, in a non-cooperative task performed by monkeys, we identified
030            mutual predictions that correlated with the animals’ social hierarchy, highlighting
031            the behavioral relevance of the latent beliefs we uncovered. Together, our findings
032            demonstrate that MAIRL offers a new framework for uncovering human or animal
033            beliefs in social behavior, thereby illuminating previously opaque aspects of social
034            cognition.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Humans are remarkable at understanding the mental states of others - their beliefs, values, and
      intentions - a capacity known as "theory of mind (ToM)." At its core, an individual’s behavior is
040
      often not directly guided by objective reality, but by their subjective beliefs about the state of the
041
      world. “Theory of mind” thus allows us to more reliably predict other people’s actions instead of
042   using the physical reality or one’s own belief (Frith & Frith, 2012). This reasoning process can
043   operate recursively, with individuals modeling not just others’ beliefs, but others’ beliefs about their
044   own beliefs - a phenomenon captured by increasing levels of cognitive hierarchy. Precisely inferring
045   these often unobservable beliefs and subjective values is crucial for understanding social cognition in
046   humans as well as other species. We adopt a multi-agent inverse reinforcement learning (MAIRL)
047   framework to achieve such inference. Addressing a crucial challenge in neuroscience and cognitive
048   science, our novel approach promises to reveal computation of value and mental states of multiple
049   interacting agents in social behavior.
050   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) models interactions among multiple agents (Yang,
051   2021)(Buşoniu et al., 2010). Despite its wide application in topics including traffic coordination
052   (Campos-Rodriguez et al., 2017), wireless sensor networks (Derakhshan & Yousefi, 2019), and
053   patient coordination in healthcare (Shakshuki & Reid, 2015), its application in neuroscience and
      cognitive sciences has been limited. There are many categories of MARL algorithms but we focus


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      mainly on those with centralized training and different levels of execution, in which agents share a
055   common critic, but execute policies independently. A review of relevant algorithms can be found in
056   this benchmark paper (Papoudakis et al., 2020).
057
058
      In many social interactions, an agent anticipates the behaviors of others by constructing predictive
      models of them. This essential aspect of interaction is captured by recursive probabilistic reasoning,
059
      often used in the field of opponent modeling (OM) (De Weerd et al., 2013)(Albrecht & Stone,
060
      2018)(Tejwani et al., 2022). However, OM is often not incorporated into modern RL frameworks due
061   to computational expense and the need for centralized training to allow access to the ground truth
062   strategies by all agents (Wen et al., 2019). Nevertheless, in well-defined social tasks, participants are
063   expected to fully understand the rules, objectives, and their opponents’ strategies. This understanding
064   justifies the use of recursive modeling and the assumption that participants will make the best
065   responses based on their predictions. Additionally, recent approaches have aimed to decouple agents’
066   mutual effects during training through variational Bayes methods, enabling the approximation of task
067   solutions (Wen et al., 2019) and opening more possibilities for large-scale OM.
068   While multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) with recursive reasoning typically models social
069   behavior in goal-directed tasks, real-world interactions among humans or animals often occur without
070   explicitly defined value functions. Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) addresses this by inferring
071   the latent value functions from observed behaviors (Arora & Doshi, 2021), thereby uncovering an
072   individual’s model of the world. Assuming the value function is a linear combination of known
073   feature functions, the problem can be formulated as a linear optimization to maximize expert feature
074   expectations, with various approaches addressing computation complexity and solution ambiguities
075
      (Abbeel & Ng, 2004)(Ratliff et al., 2006)(Ziebart et al., 2008). We focused on the maximum entropy
      approach proposed in (Ziebart et al., 2008), as it provides a probabilistic version of the problem,
076
      facilitating the incorporation of recursive reasoning crucial for ToM modeling.
077
078   Merging these lines of work, our approach uncovers both goal related value functions of the agents and
079   mental models of one agent for another. In Section 2, we first review related MAIRL theoretical work
080   in the field of imitation learning, and existing work that model social interactions in neuroscience and
081
      cognitive sciences. In Section 3, we describe our formulation based on multi-agent Markov Decision
      Processes (MDP) with recursive reasoning and value decomposition to model two expert agents in
082
      simultaneous-move games and a maximum a posteriori algorithm for inference. We proved that the
083
      approximation error of adopting value decomposition is negligible through mathematical analysis and
084   numerical evaluation. In Section 4, we show that our model effectively recovers goal-related value
085   functions, interaction terms associated with task rules, and the levels of cognitive hierarchy
086   between two agents in a simulated multi-state, multi-action cooperative foraging task performed in a
087   grid environment. We further apply our model to experimental datasets in humans and non-human
088   primates, uncovering previously unobservable value maps and latent strategies used by the players.

      2   R ELATED WORK
092
093
      Multi-agent inverse reinforcement learning (MAIRL) has been extensively studied in imitation
      learning, primarily aiming to enhance task performance. In cognitive science, models of social
094
      interactions often rely on single-agent Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) or
095
      multi-agent Markov Decision Process (MDP), with simplified mentalization processes parameterized
096   by only one or two variables. This work proposes adopting MAIRL with value decomposition and
097   recursive reasoning for applications in cognitive sciences and neuroscience to enable efficient and
098   interpretable value function recovery.
099
      MAIRL studies in imitation learning have established a robust theoretical framework and practical
100
      tools for broader applications. The primary objective is to derive compactly parameterized reward
101
      functions that rationalize observed behaviors or equilibria. However, the problem is often ill-posed
102   due to the vast solution space, necessitating constraints for tractability. For instance, Natarajan
103   et al. (2010) aimed to maximize the difference between the value of group-optimized actions and
104   alternatives to enable centralized control for population benefits. Similarly, Waugh et al. (2013)
105   constrained learned value functions to ensure no greater regret than observed behaviors, while Reddy
106   et al. (2012) used Nash equilibrium to decompose joint rewards into individual ones. Wu et al. (2023)
107   decentralized reward estimation by inferring policy distributions of other agents. On the theoretical
      front, advancements in scalability are particularly promising. Yu et al. (2019) proposed multi-agent


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      adversarial inverse reinforcement learning to solve MAIRL in high-dimensional continuous state-
109   action spaces under a logistic quantal response equilibrium. Kuleshov & Schrijvers (2015) proved that
110   MAIRL in succinct games can be solved using optimization algorithms with polynomial complexity
111   in the number of players, contrasting with traditional methods that are exponential.
112
      In cognitive and neuroscience research, models of social interactions often focus on Bayesian
113
      inference over counterparts’ strategies to maximize task outcomes. Khalvati et al. (2016) modeled
114
      social games with binary decisions as single-agent POMDPs, treating group conformity as a hidden
115   state, which is fully characterized by two parameters. (Wu et al., 2021) and Ullman et al. (2009)
116   employed multi-agent MDPs to infer binary intentions and decentralize policy generation for improved
117   or more human-like task performance. Yoshida et al. (2008) used multi-agent MDPs to model
118   recursive Theory of Mind (ToM), assuming distinct value functions for different cognitive hierarchies.
119   Another line of modeling approaches seeks to uncover latent factors in animal behavior, such as goals
120   (Baker et al., 2009) or context-specific intentions (Velez-Ginorio et al., 2017). Baker et al. (2017)
121   used single-agent POMDPs to comprehensively predict human inference by modeling beliefs, desires,
122   and percepts. (Shuvaev et al., 2024) analyzed social conflict paradigms with multi-agent POMDPs,
123
      parameterizing policies with Bernoulli processes to infer aggression levels.

      3.4   L EVELS OF COGNITIVE HIERARCHY
217
218   The major complexity of modeling multi-agent interactions is the level of coordination and mutual
219   predictions between agents. For notation simplicity, let’s consider collaborative tasks where each
220   agents have an identical value function. We further assume that agents know the state information of
221   each other. A summary of the cognitive levels considered in this work was listed in Table 1.
222   In one extreme, agents generate an optimal joint policy π(a1 , a2 |s) according to Eq. 3 and operate in
223   a centralized way such that the optimal joint action would be executed. In this case, standard IRL
224   formulations in Eq. 3 and 4 could be applied to calculate the posterior probability in an exponentially
225   enlarged state space and action space. Note that in the following context, π refers specifically to the
226   optimal joint policy, not necessarily the policy adopted by agents.
227
      In a less coordinated scenario, agents still generate an optimal joint policy π but the policy execution
228   process is independent, which is often the case in simultaneous action games. Mathematically, we
229   have conditional independence of individual policies:
230
231

232   Then the question becomes how does each agent chooses its own action based on full state information.
233   The answer lies in a probabilistic view of recursive reasoning.
234
      For a level-0 or egocentric agent, who does not consider the action or state of other agents, its policy
235   only depends on its own state information, i.e. P (ai |s) = P (ai |si ) and could be generated as soft
236   value iteration of its own map function (m or n).
237
238
      For an agent with level-1 reasoning or higher, it builds a predictive model of the other player and
      is expected to act optimally based on this prediction. Let’s denote agent 1’s prediction of agent 2’s
239

      at the states where both agents are at the reward locations. Simulation and fitting details can be found

420   steps), indicating increasing skill levels. Example trajectories are shown in Fig. S4.
421   We applied models of different cognitive levels to fit these trajectories (Fig. 4B). Pairs from expert
422   trials exhibited remarkable coordination, akin to centralized control, despite the fact that each player
423   was acting independently. This was evident from their capability for mutual predictions, as the value
424   map of one agent is accurately predicted by the other (Fig. 4C). Pairs in successful trials displayed
425   less coordination but maintained good mutual predictive capacity, supported by a well-fitted model of
426   optimal prediction. For pairs in failed trials, trajectories were similarly explained by both optimal
427   and chance prediction models. Nonetheless, inferred value maps using both models indicated that
428
      both participants did not consider each other’s location (Fig. 4C and Fig. S3).

430   not clearly indicate using Euclidean distance as an interaction term. Therefore, we tested different
431   interaction maps, including Euclidean distance, column distance, row distance, and a joint map of
      both. In both expert and failed pairs, the model that used column distance as the interaction term

512   Limitations. We currently assume that each agent has full access to the entire state information. In
513
      the foraging task, this implies that each agent knows the locations of all other agents, which may be
      an unrealistic assumption. A more plausible approach would involve adopting a multi-agent POMDP
514
      framework, where agents’ locations are hidden and can only be inferred through field-of-view
515
      observations. The current algorithm generates joint policies using the joint value function, resulting in
516   computational complexity that grows exponentially with the number of agents. Decentralized policy
517   evaluation could potentially be implemented in the future by assuming a Nash equilibrium (Reddy
518   et al., 2012) or other solutions to improve computational efficiency for scenarios involving more than
519   two agents. Nonetheless, whether such decentralized methods can still preserve the differentiable
520   computation graph remains uncertain and requires further investigation. Our algorithm also assumes
521   a non-parametric reward function in discrete state and action spaces. A potential improvement would
522   be to adopt a parametric reward function (e.g. a diffusion function like in (Ullman et al., 2009)) in
523   continuous state and action spaces, which may enhance efficiency. Beyond limitations in modeling
524
      assumptions, our current comparison did not reveal significant differences between the two predictive
      models under independent control (Fig. 3A), potentially because the task allows agents to perform
525
      effectively by making basic predictions about the other’s actions. To explore the framework’s full
526
      potential, it could be extended to more complex social scenarios that demand advanced mentalization
527   processes, such as the OverCooked task. (Wu et al., 2021).

      A.1    A NALYSIS OF VALUE DECOMPOSITION
652
      We assumed that joint value function r(s1 , s2 ) : S → R can be approximated by decomposing it
653
      into individual value maps m(s1 ) : S1 → R, n(s2 ) : S2 → R and an interaction term ϕ(d(s1 , s2 ))
654   as a function of Euclidean distance d(s1 , s2 ). While this separability property is not universally
655   achievable, we demonstrate that, for multi-agent tasks with discrete state spaces, reconstruction error
656   r − m − n − ϕ could be analytically calculated by solving an overdetermined linear system.
657
      Formally, we want to approximate a fixed value function V (s1 , s2 ) with a summation term m(s1 ) +
658
      n(s2 ) + ϕ(d(s1 , s2 )). This poses m ∼ O(|S|2 ) constrains to a system with p ∼ O(|S|) parameters,
659
      constructing an overdetermined linear system. As depicted in Fig. S1, we could write down the
660   reconstruction equation (Eq. 2) as y = Xβ where y is a flattened version of V (s1 , s2 ), β is a
661   concatenated vector of parameters in the m, n, ϕ and X is a fixed design matrix depending on the
662   functional form of d and the grid environment. Since m > p, this is an overdetermined system,
663   we could write down the ordinary least square (OLS) solution as β̂ = (X T X)−1 X T y and the
664   error vector evaluated as (I − X(X T X)−1 X T )y, denoted as M y. The reconstruction error is the
665   projection of vector y onto the null space of M . Since M and y both depends on the task setup and
666   the choice of the interaction term, we could pre-calculate the reconstruction error before performing
667   inference.
668   For the two agent cooperative foraging task, the reconstruction error was inspected in Fig. S2. In
669   this example, we assigned a reward strength of 1 when both agents are at location (0, 0) and the
670   reconstruction error is at the order of 10−3 for a grid size of 5, which is negligible. The OLS solution
671   of m and ϕ were plotted in the middle and right panel of Fig. S2. The individual map highlighted the
672   reward location at (0, 0) and the interaction map emphasizes the importance of being together (i.e.
673   ϕ(0)). Note that reconstruction error decreases as the number of grid size increase because y gets
674   sparser in larger arenas. Actually, when reward is not sparse, the interaction term is essentially in
675   achieving minimal reconstruction error (Fig. S3). The two agent hallway task has similar formulation
676
      except a different reward location in vector y. Therefore, we could also achieve minimal error using
      value decomposition.
677
678   More interestingly, we could also evaluate the decomposition performance for a three-agent coopera-
679   tive foraging task. In this task, agents will only be rewarded if all of them are at the same reward
680   port simultaneously. The reward ports are placed on the diagonal of a 5 × 5 square arena. Value de-
681   composition is computed as V (s1 , s2 , s3 ) = m(s1 ) + n(s2 ) + l(s3 ) + ϕ1 (d(s1 , s2 )) + ϕ2 (d(s1 , s3 )).
682
      Reconstruction error is at the order of 10−4 and the OLS solution also indicates the position of reward
      ports and the value of staying together for an agent pair. This result indicates the potential of applying
683
      our method to tasks with more than two agents.
