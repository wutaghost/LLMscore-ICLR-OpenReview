009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite their success in many domains, large language models (LLMs) remain
012            under-studied in scenarios requiring optimal decision-making under uncertainty.
013            This is crucial as many real-world applications, ranging from personalized rec-
014
               ommendations to healthcare interventions, demand that LLMs not only predict
               but also actively learn to make optimal decisions through exploration. In this
015
               work, we measure LLMs’ (in)ability to make optimal decisions in bandits, a state-
016
               less reinforcement learning setting relevant to many applications. We develop a
017            comprehensive suite of environments, including both context-free and contextual
018            bandits with varying task difficulties, to benchmark LLMs’ performance. Mo-
019            tivated by the existence of optimal exploration algorithms, we propose efficient
020            ways to integrate this algorithmic knowledge into LLMs: by providing explicit
021            algorithmic guided support during inference; and through knowledge distillation
022            via in-context demonstrations and fine-tuning, using synthetic data generated from
023            these algorithms. Impressively, these techniques allow us to achieve superior ex-
024            ploration performance with smaller models, surpassing larger models on various
025
               tasks. We conducted an extensive ablation study to shed light on various factors,
               such as task difficulty and data representation, that influence the efficiency of LLM
026
               exploration. Additionally, we provide empirical measurements on the convergence
027
               rate of different exploration strategies introduced.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031   The rapid advance of LLMs has positioned them as valuable tools for a wide range of decision-making
032   tasks, including but not limited to personal assistants (Liu et al., 2024a), recommendation systems (Li
033   et al., 2023a), game-playing (Wang et al., 2023a;c), education (Nie et al., 2024; He-Yueya et al.,
034   2024), and healthcare (Singhal et al., 2023). In these tasks, LLMs function as agents that engage with
035   users or the environment in a dynamic interaction process. For example, at each time step, the LLM
036   suggests a pedagogical strategy or make a recommendation to a specific user, then receives feedback -
037
      either explicit or implicit - in the form of rewards. Based on this feedback, the agent updates its beliefs
      about the environment, e.g., underlying reward distributions, and adapts its strategies to maximize the
038
      cumulative reward. These tasks differ fundamentally from classic prediction tasks where LLM is
039
      used to predict a target. A decision making LLM only receives partial feedback, i.e., the reward for its
040   own actions, but not for others. Thus, it requires the LLM to effectively interact with the environment
041   and explore to discover the optimal action. Meanwhile exploring an unknown action which turns out
042   to have lower reward than the known ones lead to higher regret. The agent therefore needs to strike a
043   balance between exploration and exploitation. While the exploration-exploitation tradeoff has been
044   extensively studied in the pre-LLM era, particularly in the fields of bandits (Li et al., 2010; Slivkins
045   et al., 2019) and reinforcement learning (Mnih, 2013; Osband et al., 2013; Sutton, 2018), it is unclear
046   how LLMs approach this tradeoff when faced with uncertainty.
047
      We study LLMs’ in-context exploration capabilities under the simplified framework of bandits —
048   a stateless form of reinforcement learning that remains highly applicable to many domains. We
049   set up the LLM to interact with the environment over T rounds. In each round, it receives the full
050   history of its past interactions, the current state if provided and a set of actions, and is tasked with
051   selecting an action to maximize the cumulative reward. Ideally, the LLM should adaptively choose
052   an action in each round to learn the reward distributions of different actions and eventually converge
053   to consistently selecting the optimal action. We study LLM’s ability to do so in-context, without the
      need to re-train, which we dubbed as in-context exploration.

054
      We first introduce BanditBench, a comprehensive suite of multi-arm bandit (MAB) (Slivkins et al.,
055   2019) and contextual bandit (CB) (Li et al., 2010) environments in natural language to rigorously
056   evaluate the decision-making capabilities of LLMs. Building on the pioneering work of Krishna-
057   murthy et al. (2024), we significantly expand the benchmark by incorporating a broader range of
058   tasks with varying complexities, including variations in the number of arms, reward distributions,
059   exploration difficulty, and different textual descriptions of environments. Additionally, we extend it to
060   CB environments, where rewards across arms depend on contextual features, to assess generalization
061   in LLM exploration.
062   To enhance LLM for exploration, we leverage known bandits algorithms such as UCB and Thompson
063   Sampling (Thompson, 1933), which have been proven "optimal" under mild conditions. We inves-
064   tigate two approaches: (1) inference-time guided algorithmic support where summary statistics on
065   interaction history along with descriptions of bandits algorithms are provided in context for LLMs
066   to choose actions, and (2) algorithmic distillation via optimal demonstration data where “oracle”
067   trajectories from optimal bandit algorithms are provided as either in-context few-shot demonstration
068   or optimal behavior fine-tuning. We benchmarked off-the-shelf LLMs of different sizes, open-sourced
069
      vs proprietary ones, and those enhanced by our approaches on BanditBench. Both approaches demon-
      strate promising improvements over baseline methods that rely solely on raw interaction histories
070
      presented as sequences of (action, reward) tuples. Furthermore, our results show that fine-tuning to
071
      distill optimal exploration behavior leads to strong generalization across domains, enabling smaller
072   models to acheive superior exploration performance compared with larger models. We also perform
073   extensive ablation studies, revealing how training task difficulty, textual representation and algorithm
074   guide impact model performance. To gain deeper insights into the exploration efficiency of different
075   methods, we fit a parametric function to the observed regret patterns, allowing for a more rigorous
076   interpretation of exploration efficiencies of different LLMs and our proposed approaches.
077
      Our contributions are threefold. First, we introduce BanditBench, an open-source benchmark designed
078   to evaluate LLM’s performance in both MAB and CB settings. Second, we propose methods to
079   enhance LLM’s decision-making capability by leveraging optimal algorithms, including algorithmic-
080   guided inference-time support and algorithmic distillation approach. Finally, we benchmark existing
081   LLMs, and those improved by our approaches on BanditBench, and shed light on the exploration
082   efficiency of the different algorithms.

084   2    R ELATED W ORK
085   Several prior works have investigated the use of LLMs for decision-making. In one category, there
086   are numerous works that deployed LLMs directly as agents in decision-making problems such as
087   games (Yao et al., 2023; Brooks et al., 2024; Shinn et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Xi et al., 2023).
088   However, fewer works have systematically evaluated LLMs’ capabilities in the general decision-
089   making setup, especially as they relate to classical concepts in decision-making like exploration.
090   Our work extends the research of Krishnamurthy et al. (2024), who examined LLMs’ exploration
091   capabilities in small-scale MAB tasks. Their findings, which showed positive results only with
092   substantial intervention, are consistent with our broader analysis across both MAB and CB tasks at
093
      various scales. Mirchandani et al. (2023); Rahn et al. (2024); Felicioni et al. (2024) also evaluated the
      ability of LLMs to learn in-context and solve bandit-like decision-making problems.
094
095   The line of research on using LLMs as optimizers faces many similar challenges as in-context decision
096   making, though applied to different tasks. Yang et al. (2024) explored the use of language models as
097   general-purpose optimizers for simple black-box optimization problems, such as prompt optimization,
098   highlighting a careful balance of exploration and exploitation was critical. Another relevant line of
099
      research focused on in-context learning for decision-making and reinforcement learning (RL) with
      domain-specific transformers. Laskin et al. (2022) distilled demonstrations from RL algorithms into
100
      a transformer and showed that the transformer learns to imitate the RL process to solve new RL
101
      tasks. Similarly, Lee et al. (2024) trained transformers with optimal action labels, showing that the
102   model learns to execute posterior sampling for RL (Osband et al., 2013) in-context, which tailors
103   exploration to the underlying distribution of RL tasks. This area has been further studied by Raparthy
104   et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023). However, these studies focus on domain-specific decision-making,
105   whereas our paper examines general-purpose decision-making capabilities in language models. Our
106   inference-time guided algorithmic support shares a similar conceptual framework with recent efforts
107   to align LLMs at inference time. These include employing explicit value functions as prefix scorers
      that trained via KL-regularized RL (Mudgal et al., 2023), and leveraging both implicit and explicit

      value functions to guide decoding at the token and chunk levels at inference time (Liu et al., 2024b).
109   In the realm of knowledge distillation, much of the research on LLMs has concentrated on chain-of-
110   thought (CoT) reasoning (Wang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023b), while Gandhi et al. (2024) focused on
111   search and backtracking. Most methods involve distilling outputs from a "teacher" model—either a

117   3    I N -C ONTEXT E XPLORATION
118   In this section, we define the problem of In-Context Exploration (ICE), following the setup in
119   Krishnamurthy et al. (2024). An agent interacts with an environment by observing state information,
120   selecting actions, and collecting feedback. The goal of the agent is to maximize its cumulative reward
121   through multiple rounds of interactions. Specifically for ICE, the agent is an LLM and its history
122   of observations and interactions with the environment are kept in its context. The agent determines
123   its actions based on this context, rather than from updating its weights or executing hand-designed
124   exploration strategies.
125   Notation and Definitions. We primarily consider bandits, a simple class of environments that still
126   incorporate many fundamental challenges in decision-making. Here, we describe a framework that
127   encompasses both multi-armed bandits (MAB) and contextual bandits (CB). A bandit environment T
128   is defined as T = (X , A, R) where A defines a set of valid actions. X is the set of state information
129   (if any). R is the underlying reward distributions of actions, unknown to the agent. MAB and CB
130   tasks differ on whether the context x, is provided and used: in MAB, the reward depends solely on the
131   action, whereas in CB it depends on both the action and the context. The interaction between agent
132
      and environment occurs over T ∈ N steps. At each time step t ∈ [T ], the environment reveals a new
      observation1 xt ∈ X , the agent selects an action at ∈ A following its policy π, and then a reward
133
      rtat ∼ R(xt ) is revealed. Given an LLM agent with policy π, it determines its action at ∼ π(Htπ ),
134
      where Htπ = (x1 , a1 , r1a1 , . . . , xt ) stores the historical actions picked by the same agent and the

192   4   BANDIT B ENCH
193
      We present BanditBench, an extensive suite of MAB (Slivkins et al., 2019) and CB (Li et al., 2010)
194
      environments in natural language to benchmark in-context exploration capabilities of LLMs.
195
196   Multi-Arm Bandit In (stochastic) multi-arm bandit problems, we vary our environment configura-
197   tions primarily along two key dimensions: 1) action space, where we change the numbers of actions
198   K, and textual description associated with each action; 2) reward distributions, where we change
199   the parametric distribution of the reward, i.e., types of reward distributions, and the exploration
200
      difficulty, characterized by the gap between the best-performing arm and the second-best arm. A
      smaller gap makes it harder for the agent to distinguish between optimal and sub-optimal actions,
201
      thereby increasing the exploration difficulty. In contrast to the setup in Krishnamurthy et al. (2024),
202
      which focuses solely on MAB instances with Bernoulli reward distribution, our expanded setup
203   allows us to systematically analyze LLM performs across diverse environments with different action
204   spaces and reward structures.
205
206   The detailed configurations are shown in Appendix A.1. For the action space, we explore two
207
      different sizes with K = 5 for small action space while K = 20 for large action space. We
      also differentiate between two types of action descriptions, Videos represented as arbitrary two-
208
      letter combinations with no semantic meaning such as “Video AA”, and Clothes, described using
209
      semantically meaningful phrases such as “Supreme Sylvan Sandals”. Regarding reward distributions,
210   we evaluate two types: Bernoulli and Gaussian Bandit. For Bernoulli, the reward r ∈ {0, 1} are
211   binary with rak ∼ Bernoulli(pk ), where pk is the mean for the k-th action. Following Krishnamurthy
212   et al. (2024), the best-performing arm has pk := 0.5 + ∆min /2, while remaining arms have pk =
213   0.5 − ∆min /2. The parameter ∆min captures the exploration difficulty with a larger gap ∆min = 0.5
214   indicating easy tasks and 0.2 representing hard tasks. For Gaussian bandit, the rewards are continuous
215   with rak ∼ N (µk , σ). Here µk ∼ N (0, σ) represents the mean for each action and the variance σ
      captures difficulty of exploration. Following Sutton (2018), we study both σ = 1 and σ = 3.

      Contextual Bandit For contextual bandit, at each round t ∈ [T ], the agent is presented with some
217   contextual feature x (which may consist both textual descriptions and numeric values) describing
218   the state (and action). The LLM agent π chooses an action a ∈ A, and then a reward is received
219   r(x, a) which depends on both the context and the chosen action. We design the semi-synthetic
220   contextual bandit task based on the MovieLens dataset (Harper & Konstan, 2015), which consists of
221   approximately 10,000 real users’ movie ratings. The goal of the agent is to recommend a personalized
222   movie that a specific user will likely enjoy. In particular, the observations x include user-specific
223   features such as age, gender, occupation, and geographical location (county and state), and features
224   on the movies. The action space is limited to the top-K most-watched movies in the dataset, with
225
      K = 10 for the easy setting and K = 30 for the more challenging setting. To construct the ground-
      truth reward distribution, we perform low-rank approximation (Koren et al., 2009) on the user-movie
226
      rating matrix P ∈ RN ×K , where N is the number of users. This is done by approximating P
227
      with P̃ = U ΣV T using singular value decomposition (SVD), yielding a user embedding matrix
228
      U ∈ RN ×d and a movie embedding matrix V ∈ RK×d . In our case, we set d = 5 to be the dimension
229
      of the embeddings. The ground-truth reward for user i and movie j is then computed as ri,j = uTi Σvj .
230   At each time step, we provide textual contextual features alongside a 5-dimensional user preference
231   vector ui . The task can be easily scaled up to include more movies, i.e., larger K. Further details
232   about the setup are in Appendix A.2.

      Motivated by the existence of optimal algorithms for bandits, we aim to leverage these algorithms
236
      to improve LLMs for exploration by: 1) incorporating algorithmic guidance during inference-time
237   (Section 5.1), 2) teaching optimal exploration through algorithmic distillation (Section 5.2). We show
238   that smaller models trained using algorithmic distillation can even outperform larger models, offering
239   a promising way to efficiently explore with lower inference cost.
240

245   strategy also makes it well-suited for integration with existing LLMs. Our method can however
246   generalize to different algorithms easily.

266
      In this section, we explore how to leverage UCB-type algorithms as inference-time support to improve
267
      LLM’s in-context exploration performance.
268
269   Algorithmic Guided Support (AG) As discussed above, UCB-type algorithms operate by explic-
      itly calculating the exploitation value V Exploit along with the exploration bonus V Explore for each arm,


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      and selecting the arm that maximizes the sum of two. These components, V Exploit and V Explore , there-
271   fore provide the sufficient textualization needed for LLMs to make optimal decisions. Specifically,
272   in the MAB setup, during  inference time at time step t, we provide the LLM with a list of tuples
273    V exploit (a, t), V explore (a, t) for each arm a ∈ [K]. This representation is provided alongside other
274   essential information such as scenario descriptions, instructions, and the action set. For CB, during
275   inference-time, we explicitly maintain the design matrix Da and response vector ra for each arm,
276   incorporating past interactions from the LLM up to that time t, using this to obtain the exploitation
277   value and exploration bonus. We then provide the LLM with a list of exploitation values and explo-
278   ration bonus for each arm a at current context x, similar to the MAB setup. Additionally, we record
279
      the action features xat as well as reward rt selected by the LLM, which will be used for the next round
      of parameter updates. Compared with SH, which only provides the empirical mean and the number
280
      of times each arm has been pulled, AG directly supplies semantically understandable exploitation
281
      values and exploration bonuses. This explicit representation enables LLM to effectively balance
282   exploitation and exploration. Theoretically, the LLM only needs to perform addition and argmax,
283   rather than manipulating raw histories to discern the underlying reward distribution (or parameter θ
284   in CB). Another advantage is that AG is a type of inference-time support which works seamlessly for
285   both MAB and CB, while SH only works on MAB setup2 .

287   5.2   A LGORITHMIC D ISTILLATION VIA D EMONSTRATION AND F INE - TUNING
288   We further investigate the possibility of enhancing LLM exploration by leveraging a set of trajectories
289   generated by an oracle exploration algorithm in the BanditBench environment. This approach, called
290   algorithmic distillation, aims to distill the optimal exploration behavior from the oracle algorithm to
291   the LLM. In particular, we consider two approaches: in-context few-shot demonstration and optimal
292
      behavior fine-tuning, both utilizing expert trajectories generated by the oracle algorithm. Compared
      with Algorithmic Guide (AG), these approaches do not require understanding the oracle algorithms,
293
      nor generating sufficient statistics based on oracle algorithms, thus can be applicable to black-box
294
      algorithms as well.
295
296   Oracle Trajectory Generation We use UCB as the oracle algorithm to generate the trajecto-
297   ries. Following the notations defined in Section 3, the trajectories are in the form of tuples of
298   (φ(HtUCB ), aUCB
                    t   ), where each tuple pairs the transformed representation of the history at time t and
299   the action aUCB
                   t    from UCB. For MAB, we create trajectories from reward distributions that differ
300
      from those used in evaluation. This assesses the LLM’s ability to generalize across different bandit
      instances with the same underlying scenario but varying action-reward mappings. We further control
301
      the data generation process by varying: (1). Action Description: trajectories are generated from
302
      either "Video" or "Clothes" action descriptions; (2). Difficulty: we control the reward gap in the
303   Bernoulli bandit to create "easy" or "hard" instances; (3). Trajectory Textualization: trajectories are
304   represented either as RH or AG. For CB, we use a fixed dataset and evaluate the LLM’s performance
305   on a held-out set of users. While these users are unseen during training, their profiles and preferences
306   remain within the distribution of the training data. This evaluates the LLM’s ability to leverage prior
307   knowledge for effective exploration. In CB, we only vary the trajectory representation (RH or AG).
308   In both MAB and CB, each trajectory consists of a sequence of exploration steps: 300 steps for MAB
309   with K = 5 arms, 1000 steps for MAB with K = 20 arms, and 200 steps for CB. We generate 50
310   trajectories for each MAB domain configuration and 200 trajectories for CB, resulting in roughly
311
      comparable training data sizes across the two environments.
312   In-Context Few-Shot Demonstration We first study whether demonstrating optimal exploration
313   trajectories from UCB as few-shot examples can improve the LLM’s ability to perform robust
314   exploration in bandit tasks. A key challenge in applying few-shot learning to decision-making tasks
315   like MAB is the increasing context length. Unlike supervised learning where context is typically fixed,
316   bandit actions depend on the entire past history or condensed history, which either grows linearly
317   with T or K. This poses a challenge for LLMs, as their ability to effectively utilize information can
318   degrade with longer contexts. We sample 5 optimal trajectories from UCB into the LLM context
319
      window as demonstrations. Our goal is to see whether the optimal exploration demonstrations can lead
      to improved exploration performance. Detail demonstrations are provided in Appendix A.10.

      5.3.1 S ETUP AND BASELINES
347
      Setup We evaluate the in-context exploration capabilities of various LLMs, including Gemma-2B,
348
      Gemma-9B (Team et al., 2024), Gemini 1.5 Flash, and Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024), on 16
349   MAB tasks (Table A1) and 2 CB tasks. For MAB tasks, the interaction horizon (T ) differs based on
350   the size of the action space (K). We use T = 1000 for K = 30 and T = 200 for K = 10. All CB
351   tasks use a constant horizon of T = 200 steps. To ensure statistically significance of the results, we
352   conduct 30 independent runs for each experimental setup.
353
      Baselines We consider two baselines: Raw History (RH) and Summarized History (SH), as
354
      suggested in Krishnamurthy et al. (2024). For CB, as we discussed that there is no trivial analogue of
355
      SH, we thus focus solely on RH for CB tasks in this study as the baseline.
356
357   Metrics We report the relative performance of each model, aggregated across all environment
358   configurations. Simply averaging cumulative rewards across environments of different reward
359   distributions and horizons however obscure the comparison. We instead use the pair-wise win-rate to
360
      compare the performances. We have 16 configurations for MAB and evaluated 32 models (4 LLMs
      crossed with different methods), and 2 configurations for CB with 14 models (2 LLMs crossed with
361
      different methods). The list of all the models are given in Appendix A.8. For each configuration, we
362
      compute the cumulative reward over T steps and collect a distribution of cumulative rewards over
363   30 independent trials. We then calculate the pairwise win-rate by applying a Student’s t-test on the
364   reward distributions of any pair of configurations to determine if they are statistically significantly
365   different, with a significance level of p < 0.05. If one model has significantly higher reward than
366   the other, we consider it a win. If the difference is not statistically significant, the result is deemed
367   inconclusive and not counted as a win. For each model, we calculate its win rate against every other
368   model across all configurations. The overall win rate for a specific model is then determined by
369   averaging these win rates across all the models it compared with. Details are given in Appendix A.5.

         In this section, we aim to conduct a more rigorous analysis of the LLM’s exploration efficiency using
510
         the concept of regret REG(π). Most bandit algorithms are evaluated by the behavior of REG(π) as
511
         a function of T (i.e., number of interactions), either theoretically or empirically. Motivated by this,
512      our goal is to understand the exploration behaviors of various LLMs by characterizing their regret as
513      a function of T . To achieve this, we adopt the following functional form to analyze the regret:
514                                                   λ log(T )α
515                                          f (T ) =              + βT + λ2
                                                         ∆min
516
         The three parameters α, β, λ in the equation are all positive real numbers. λ2 is unconstrained. ∆min
517      captures the gap between best and second best arm, and would be replaced with a KL divergence or
518      Total Variance term for Gaussian bandit. This functional form provided intuitive interpretations for
519      the underlying parameters. Specifically, log(T ) represents sublinear scaling of the regret, which is
520      known to be achieved by only the best bandit algorithms (e.g. UCB and Thompson Sampling). The
521      T scaling describes a linear growth or the inability of an agent to match the optimal policy π ∗ . This
522      means a strong algorithm should have α as small as possible, and have β = 0. This functional form
523      also allows us to see some growth behaviors in-between with positive α and β. We use the curve fit
524      function in Scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) to fit the cumulative regret curve of UCB and LLMs
525
         coupled with different methods (i.e., inference-time guided support, in-context demonstration, and
         optimal behavior finetuning). Results of the fitted α and β values are presented in Figure 5. For the
526
         largest Pro models, applying effective inference-time support such as AG and SH can achieve nearly
527
         sub-linear regret. More intriguingly, for Flash models, fine-tuning for optimal behavior significantly
528      boosts performance, enabling them to attain sub-linear regret with a lower α. In contrast, weaker
529      models such as Gemma 2B and 9B appear to remain in the linear regret regime.

532
          In this work, we explored the in-context exploration capabilities of LLMs in bandit environments,
533
          introducing BanditBench, a comprehensive benchmark designed to rigorously evaluate LLM’s perfor-
534       mance. Our evaluation reveals that LLMs struggle with in-context decision-making when relying
535       solely on raw interaction history, while inference-time support significantly improve performance.
536       Motivated by the presence of optimal algorithms in this domain, we investigated methods to integrate
537       these algorithms into LLMs through both algorithmic guided support and knowledge distillation via
538       synthesized demonstration data. Notably, these approaches even enable smaller models to outperform
539       larger ones in decision-making tasks. However, an optimality gap remains between LLMs and
          classical optimal algorithms, highlighting the need for further research to bridge this gap.

      A.2 D ETAILS ON C ONTEXTUAL BANDIT TASK
717
718   We use the MovieLens-1M dataset (Harper & Konstan, 2015) to build the contextual bandit task. It
719
      contains 1,000,209 anonymous ratings of approximately 3,900 movies made by 6,040 MovieLens
      users who joined MovieLens in 2000. For each user, we have basic demographic information such as
720
      age, gender, occupation, and zip code. We further convert zip code to the actual name of the county
721
      and state and add these into the user profile description text. Each movie has a title and associated
722   genres. We present these information in the prompt as well.
723
724   LinUCB assumes that the reward model E[r|x, a] = θaT x, where θ ∈ Rd , is linear (Chu et al., 2011).
725
      Since we are trying to use synthetic environments to measure the performance of LLM against a
      theoretically optimal algorithm, we have to build the contextual bandit task in a way that satisfies
726
      the UCB assumption. An additional issue is that the context window of an LLM is still limited and
727
      we want to limit the number of movies for LLM to choose to be 10 or 30. So, we first calculate
728   the popular movies by tracking how many times each movie is rated by users. We sort the list and
729   select the top K movies. Then, we build a user preference matrix P ∈ RN ×K , where N is the
730   number of users and K is the number of movies. To construct the ground-truth reward distribution,
731   we perform low-rank approximation on P . This is done by approximating P with P̃ = U ΣV T using
732   singular value decomposition (SVD), yielding a user embedding matrix U ∈ RN ×d and a movie
733   embedding matrix V ∈ RK×d . In our case, we set d = 5 to be the dimension of the embeddings.
734   The ground-truth reward for user i and movie j is then computed as ri,j = uTi Σvj .
735   In order to present the full information that was provided to LinUCB to LLM as well, we include
736   the user preference vector in the prompt space, represented by a list of 5 floating point numbers. We
737   additionally add descriptions to indicate that this is a user preference vector. We show our full prompt
738   in Figure A9.

      A.4 A LGORITHM D ISTILLATION AND B EHAVIOR C LONING
755   Optimal Behavior Fine-tuning (OFT) and Behavior Cloning (Pomerleau, 1991) share many similari-
      ties. Although both approaches rely on maximum-likelihood learning, their objectives are different:


                                                           14
          Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
          OFT seeks to encode a dynamic, iterative refinement process, while BC focuses on replicating
757       static behavior. OFT is designed for algorithm distillation, focusing on capturing a sequence of self-
758       improvement behaviors, and generalization across any new test domains. In contrast, BC aims to learn
759       a policy by mimicking a static policy, with no iterative improvement between trajectories.
760
         This difference becomes very clear when we think of an example. We have a deterministic Markov
761
         policy π that we can use to create this dataset. We call this the sampling policy. To create a behavior
762
         cloning dataset, DBC , during dataset construction, for the same state s, the policy remains unchanged,
763      which the means π(a|s) remains the same in the entire dataset. To create an algorithm distillation
764      dataset DOFT , the sampling policy is self-improving as the data collection continues, π(a|s) changes
765      even for the same s between early and late trajectories of this dataset.
766
767      A.5 E XAMPLE OF W IN -R ATE C ALCULATION
768      In each scenario, we compute each model’s win-rate against all other models. For MAB, we have 16
769      configurations and 34 models. For CB, we have 2 configurations and 16 models. Finally, the model’s
770      overall win-rate is then determined by averaging its win-rates across all models. For example, in
771      MAB, if we only have 3 models: Gemma-2B, Gemini-1.5 Flash, and Pro. Gemini-1.5 Flash have
772
         higher expected cumulative reward than Gemma-2B in 12 out of 16 configurations (12/16), but only
         higher than Gemini-1.5 Pro in 4 out of 16 configurations (4/16), Gemini-Flash 1.5 will have an overall
773
         win-rate, on average, 8/16=0.5.
