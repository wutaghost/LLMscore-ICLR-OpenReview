009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the emergence of agentic workflow development using Large Language
012            Models (LLMs) for industrial applications, there is a growing need for small
013            language models to possess domain-specific knowledge. In many existing ap-
014            proaches, reference materials such as books are used as a source of knowledge.
015            This paper presents a novel approach to finetune a base LLM model in a continued
016            pre-training fashion for the industrial asset domain, leveraging knowledge docu-
017            mented in a tabular structure to generate synthetic knowledge documents and a
018            vast amount of question-answer pairs using an entity and relationship-driven ap-
019
               proach. Ultimately, this approach enables the fine-tuning of a small LLM (such
               as LLAMA 3.1-8B) for evaluating the performance enhancement it brings. We
020
               tested the base model and enhanced model on Industry4-FMSR MCQA datasets
021
               with 2600+ samples and obtained around 4% improvement overall. Our experi-
022            mental results confirm the validity of our approach to generate the more synthetic
023            data for a knowledge infusion task.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The emergence of agentic workflows using Large Language Models (LLMs) for industrial applica-
029
      tions has created a pressing need for smaller language models to possess domain-specific knowledge
030   Chen et al. (2023), Liévin et al. (2023), Wu et al. (2023). While recent advancements in language
031   models have demonstrated impressive fluency, they are not without flaws; these models can gener-
032   ate false statements, leading to errors in task execution Lin et al. (2022), Zhang et al. (2024b), Nori
033   et al. (2023). In our recent benchmark study evaluating the truthfulness of LLMs in the context of
034   industrial assets, we found that frontier model such as ChatGPT 4o was truthful approximately 65%
035   of the time, on the other hand the performance of smaller models was a significant concern (around
036   40%). This discrepancy can adversely affect an agent’s decision-making process, prompting us to
037   address the challenge of improving their reliability. Many use cases, such as work order classifica-
038
      tion Stewart et al. (2023), require a solid understanding of the domain to function effectively.
039   Building an agentic workflow represents an important area of research that will enhance the adoption
040   of LLMs Yao et al. (2022), Aksitov et al. (2023), Wang et al. (2024). In such frameworks, agents
041   interact with their environments using natural language and express their reasoning similarly Yan
042   et al. (2023). This underscores the necessity for agents to think like experts rather than merely
043   functioning as token generators. Achieving this domain-specific reasoning involves a knowledge
044
      distillation process. For instance, an agent might internally contemplate, “I need to retrieve the
      failure mode”, or “Can I detect a failure using sensor data?”
045
046   The majority of knowledge distillation processes begin with documents or existing knowledge and
047   utilize a teacher model to synthetically generate numerous question-answer pairs, transferring this
048   knowledge to smaller models Wang et al. (2022), Sudalairaj et al. (2024). Knowledge is often deeply
049   encoded in various formats, including documents, tables, images, and time series data. However,
050
      extracting knowledge from structured forms to initiate synthetic data generation can be challenging.
051   This paper focuses on harnessing structured information available in a specific domain to systemat-
052   ically navigate the knowledge infusion process. In our exploration, we observed that many of our
053   ISO documents contained critical knowledge encoded as tables. For example, we found valuable
      insights linking failure modes to sensor analytics and component mappings.

054
      Applications such as failure prediction, anomaly detection, and work order classification rely heavily
055   on extensive knowledge about assets and their processes. Building a specialized LLM for industrial
056   applications is therefore desirable, but harvesting knowledge from trustworthy sources remains a
057   challenge. ISO documents and established standards serve as reliable resources in this context. Our
058   approach involves ”crawling” the internal knowledge base of a language model with this limited
059   knowledge. We developed an Industrial-FMSR-MCQA system and tested it on the Llama model
060   Touvron et al. (2023) with varying parameters. Benchmark results demonstrated significant perfor-
061   mance improvements using a model fine-tuned with synthetic data. This raises the question: can we
062   leverage a larger model to extract information and import it into a smaller model’s knowledge base?
063
      By devising a scheme to extract useful knowledge from a limited yet factual structured dataset, we
      aim to enhance smaller LLMs for specialized industrial tasks. Our key contributions are:
064
065          • We propose leveraging fine-grained information from structured tables in ISO standards
066            to enhance model training. By extracting specific attributes related to equipment failure
067            modes, we create enriched feature representations that improve the model’s ability to gen-
068            eralize and accurately reason about new, unseen data during evaluation.
069
             • We introduce a novel two-step iterative approach that integrates a Knowledge Graph (KG)
070            with a Large Language Model (LLM) for generating extensive qualitative knowledge doc-
071            uments about industrial assets. This approach employs an “entity expansion” technique
072            that systematically discovers and links relevant sensors, assets, and failure modes, thereby
073            enhancing the KG’s precision and depth. We provided a mechanism that enables gener-
074            ation of various types of knowledge documents such as KDBase , KDRephrase , KDQA and
075            KDExtend .
076          • Our experiments demonstrate that the synthetic dataset KDBase significantly improved
077            model accuracy from 44.7% to 47.4%, highlighting the effectiveness of diverse data gener-
078            ation. Additionally, the analysis reveals that increasing the number of training tokens yields
079            diminishing returns on performance, emphasizing the need for careful dataset selection and
080            hyperparameter optimization in fine-tuning strategies.

154
      Aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with domain-specific information is a crucial task. Re-
155
      cently, various attempts have been made to generate domain-specific aligned models, such as
156   MediTron-70B for medical domain by Chen et al. (2023), EntiGraph CPT for long passage QA
157   on articles Yang et al. (2024), etc. These approaches typically utilize large-scale corpora with bil-
158   lions of tokens or start with few millions of tokens for generating more synthetic information using
159   teacher model. However, how to accommodate a tiny factual information available in a structured
160   form has not been paid much attention. Our goal is to utilize a limited amount of factual information
161   in a structured format for the Industry 4.0 domain, aiming to uncover the knowledge base of a given
      LLM surrounding industrial assets for generating more synthetic data.

181   We represent structured tabular information, such as Table 1, of Dsource via a knowledge graph (KG),
182   which is a collection of triplets. Formally, a KG is a graph G = (N, R, E), where:
183
184          • N is a set of entities such as equipments, f ailuremodes, sensors, parameters, ...
185          • R is a set of relations such as monitor, experience, etc, ...
186          • E is a set of subject-relation-object triplets (s, r, o) where:
187
188
                  – s ∈ N (subject entity)
189               – o ∈ N (object entity)
190               – r ∈ R (relation between entities)
191
192   The triplets (s, r, o) clearly illustrate how each piece of equipment is linked to its category and fail-
      ure modes. We begin by assuming that we have extracted a “seed entity” from the source dataset
193
      Dsource , which will serve as the foundation for generating knowledge documents. While it is con-
194
      ceptually possible to allow the language model (LM) to generate additional seed entities, we argue
195   that expansion based on knowledge knowledge is a more realistic scenario. In this scenario, we are
196   interested in constructing a synthetic knowledge centered around a specific entity.
197
198
      In addition to equipment, failure modes, and sensors, which are represented as entities in our system,
      a knowledge document is also an entity that is generated by some procedure and stored within the
199
      knowledge graph (KG) as an entity. The creation of knowledge documents involves a multi-step
200
      iterative process as outlined in next section. We define the following relations to connect these
201   entities:
202
203          • BelongsT o: This relation connects equipment to its category
204
             • Experience: This relation connects equipment to the failure modes it can encounter
205
206          • M onitor: This relation connects equipment to the sensor tag for monitoring purpose
207
208
      Our primary objective is to teach a pretrained language model the knowledge derived from a small
      set of factual information. However, since the knowledge base is small, the information is highly
209
      condensed and lacks diversity in how the underlying knowledge is represented.

      4.1   P HASE -I: K NOWLEDGE D OCUMENT G ENERATION AND I NITIAL KG E XPANSION
237
238
      The core component of our approach is a focused expansion procedure that takes an entity e of a spe-
239   cific type and extracts all other entities of different types associated with it using the LLM, followed
240   by a relevancy check. This process expands the KG around the entity, enabling us to recursively
241   apply the procedure to further expand the KG. We refer to this process as “entity expansion” and
242   briefly outline in Figure 3.
243
      The entity expansion process consists of three key steps:
244
245
             • Sensor Discovery: Given an entity e of type equipment, we aim to discover all related
246            sensors that can be installed on an asset for its performance monitoring. Specifically, for
247            each e ∈ N , we seek to find a list of entities O that satisfy the relation (e, ‘Monitor’,
248            ?). We consider lists since multiple sensors can potentially be used to monitor the asset’s
249            performance from different viewpoints. This task will add numerous new nodes to the KG
250            with type sensor.
251
             • Relevance Generation: Once sensors are discovered, our next step is to connect sensors
252            and assets to failure modes. Note that not all sensors are useful for detecting all failures,
253            and vice versa. Therefore, we conduct a relevancy check for each pair of sensor and failure
254            mode for a given entity. The list of failure modes are obtained from KG using (e, ‘experi-
255            ence’, ?). This relevancy check is crucial for maintaining a high-precision KG. We connect
256            sensors to failure modes using relevancy relations, ensuring that only relevant relationships
257            are established. This task will add numerous links with Yes/No label in KG.
258          • Knowledge Generation: The fine-tuning process of the Large Language Model (LLM)
259            requires passages that capture the relationships between assets, sensors, and failure modes.
260            To address this, this module generates knowledge documents that describe the following
261            three types of relationships:
262
                  – Asset-sensor relationships
263
                  – Asset-failure mode relationships
264
265               – Asset-sensor-failure mode interaction relationships
266            LLMs are well-suited for generating summaries or background documents and we can feed
267            single triplet or multiple triplets to generate diverse set of documents. By leveraging this
268            capability, we created a comprehensive set of knowledge documents that provide a detailed
269            understanding of the complex relationships between assets, sensors, and failure modes. The
               generated documents are also added as entity in KG.

      4.2.2    I NSTRUCTION A PPROACH : KD QA
325
326   In the second approach, we developed a specialized prompt that outlines a task for a knowledge ana-
327   lyzer to dissect an article concerning an industrial asset. The prompt serves two primary objectives:
328   first, to summarize content as summary related to three specified entities: asset name, sensor, and
329   failure mode, ensuring clarity and understanding of their relations. Second, it emphasizes generating
330   thought-provoking questions and corresponding answers that consistently revolve around these three
331
      entities, fostering deep analysis and critical thinking. By structuring the response in this format, the
      prompt encourages exploration of how the sensor data can indicate the potential failure mode of
332
      the asset, thereby enhancing comprehension of the asset’s operational dynamics and maintenance
333
      needs. We plan to generate at least 3 question-answer pairs for each document KD1 ∈ KDBase . Let
334   KDQA be a set of 18711 question-answer pairs generated by following this approach. It is important
335   to note that the prompts of KDQA has no context information. Therefore, to study whether adding
336   context is helpful to bring more knowledge, we extend KDQA with additional context in the prompt
337   which are derived from the generated summary in a RAFT manner (Zhang et al. (2024a)). We call
338   it KDQA-RAFT .

      KDRephrase , over three different epochs using the Llama-3.1 Base model, which has a context window

526   6   R ELATED W ORK
527
528   Recent advancements in supervised fine-tuning (SFT) methodologies, such as RAFT Zhang et al.
529   (2024a), emphasize enhancing domain-specific knowledge in models. RAFT systematically trains
530   models using a dataset of question-answer pairs, integrating reasoning chains to generate coher-
531
      ent answers and fostering a more effective retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) framework. Ad-
      ditionally, the synthetic fine-tuning approach advocates for generating more synthetic data Yang
532
      et al. (2024). In contrast, TORA Gou et al. (2023) employs a tool-assisted reasoning paradigm
533
      where a teacher model guides problem-solving through a systematic method of thinking, acting,
534   and observing. This iterative feedback process refines smaller models and explores various paths to
535   problem resolution, enhancing overall performance. Complementing these, InstructLab Sudalairaj
536   et al. (2024) introduces strategies for improving language model capabilities by eliciting reasoning
537   through brainstorming techniques and generating asset-centric Q&A content. The DDGS framework
538   further integrates diverse textual sources to enhance instruction generation, reflecting the evolving
539   methodologies that emphasize internal reasoning and external information sources in advancing lan-
      guage model performance.
