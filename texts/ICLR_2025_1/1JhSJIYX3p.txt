009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Tabular machine learning problems often require time-consuming and labor-
012                intensive feature engineering. Recent efforts have focused on using large language
013                models (LLMs) to capitalize on their potential domain knowledge. At the same
014                time, researchers have observed ethically concerning negative biases in other LLM-
015                related use cases, such as text generation. These developments motivated us to
016                investigate whether LLMs exhibit a bias that negatively impacts the performance
017                of feature engineering. While not ethically concerning, such a bias could hinder
018
                   practitioners from fully utilizing LLMs for automated data science. Therefore, we
                   propose a method to detect potential biases by detecting anomalies in the frequency
019
                   of operators (e.g., adding two features) suggested by LLMs when engineering new
020
                   features. Our experiments evaluate the bias of four LLMs, two big frontier and
021                two small open-source models, across 27 tabular datasets. Our results indicate
022                that LLMs are biased toward simple operators, such as addition, and can fail to
023                utilize more complex operators, such as grouping followed by aggregations. Fur-
024                thermore, the bias can negatively impact the predictive performance when using
025                LLM-generated features. Our results call for mitigating bias when using LLMs for
026                feature engineering.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Machine learning problems for tabular data exist in many domains, such as medical diagnosis,
032   cybersecurity, and fraud detection (Borisov et al., 2022a; van Breugel & van der Schaar, 2024). The
033   original data for these problems (e.g., an Excel sheet) often requires manual feature engineering by a
034
      domain expert to solve the machine learning problem accurately (Tschalzev et al., 2024). During
      (automated) feature engineering, various operators (e.g., Add, Divide, GroupByThenMean)
035
      are applied to existing features to create new features (Kanter & Veeramachaneni, 2015; Prado &
036
      Digiampietri, 2020; Mumuni & Mumuni, 2024).
037   Large language models (LLMs) understand various domains (Kaddour et al., 2023; Kasneci et al.,
038   2023; Hadi et al., 2024), tabular data (Ruan et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024), and feature engineering
039   (Hollmann et al., 2024; Jeong et al., 2024; Malberg et al., 2024). Thus, data scientists have started to
040   leverage LLMs for feature engineering, especially via the use of CAAFE (Hollmann et al., 2024),
041   a powerful method for automatic feature engineering with LLMs1 ; liberating practitioners from
042   extensive manual labor.
043   Despite their utility, LLMs are known to have negative biases as observed for chat applications (Kotek
044   et al., 2023; Navigli et al., 2023; Gallegos et al., 2024; Bang et al., 2024) or when "meticulously
045   delving" into text generation (Liang et al., 2024). Observing such biases motivated us to determine
046   whether LLMs also exhibit a bias that negatively impacts the quality of their engineered features. If a
047   bias is found and can be circumvented, LLMs would become a more potent tool for data scientists.
048
      To determine whether a bias exists, we inspect the frequency of operators LLMs use when engineering
049   features. This parallels inspecting the frequency of words to detect LLM-generated text (Liang et al.,
050   2024). Assuming LLMs use their world knowledge and reasoning capabilities to employ the most
051   appropriate operator, we expect the operators’ frequencies to be similar to those of the optimal
052   operators. That is, if adding two features is often the optimal feature, then an LLM would frequently

057   Therefore, we compare the frequencies of operators used by an LLM to those obtained by searching

062
063   Our results demonstrate that LLMs can have a negative bias when engineering features for tabular
064
      data. LLMs favor simple operators during feature engineering (e.g., Add), while some LLMs rarely
      use more complex operators (e.g., GroupByThenMean). In contrast, automatic black-box feature
065
      engineering favors complex operators but also uses simple operators.
066
      In particular, we observed a strong bias and negative impact for GPT-4o-mini and Gemini-1.5-flash,
067   two big frontier models (Chiang et al., 2024). Both select simple operators most often and their
068   generated features decrease the average predictive performance. In contrast, Llama3.1-8B and
069   Mistral7B-v0.3, the small open-source models, are less biased or negatively impacted. Nevertheless,
070   no LLM is close to the distribution over operators obtained by OpenFE. Likewise, the features
071   generated by OpenFE improve the predictive performance on average the most.
072
      Our Contributions. Our long-term goal is to enhance LLMs for automated data science. This
073   work contributes toward our goal by: (1) developing a method to analyze LLMs for bias in feature
074   engineering, and (2) demonstrate the existence of a bias that negatively impacts feature engineering.

079   Feature Engineering Without Large Language Models. Previous work dedicated considerable
080   effort toward automating the process of feature engineering (Kanter & Veeramachaneni, 2015; Prado
081   & Digiampietri, 2020; Mumuni & Mumuni, 2024). Various black-box methods have been proposed,
082   such as ExploreKit (Katz et al., 2016), AutoFeat (Horn et al., 2020), BioAutoML (Bonidia et al.,
083   2022), FETCH (Li et al., 2023), and OpenFE (Zhang et al., 2023). These methods typically generate
084   new features in two steps: 1) create a large set of candidate features by applying mathematical (e.g.,
085   Add) or functional (e.g., GroupByThenMean) operators to features, and 2) return a small set of
086
      promising features selected from all candidate features.
087   Feature Engineering with Large Language Models. LLMs allow us to exploit their (potential)
088   domain knowledge for feature engineering. LLMs can act as a proxy to a domain expert or data
089   scientist during the feature engineering process. To illustrate, LLMs can be prompted to suggest code
090   for generating new features (Hollmann et al., 2024; Hirose et al., 2024), to select predictive features
091   (Jeong et al., 2024), or to use rule-based reasoning for generation (Nam et al., 2024). Most notably,
092
      CAAFE (Hollmann et al., 2024) presents a simple yet effective method to generate new features by
      proposing Python code to transform existing features in the dataset into new valuable features. This
093
      method lays the foundation to our proposed feature generation method, due to its wide application in
094
      practice2 and also in research (Malberg et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b).
095
096   Bias in Large Language Models. LLMs exhibit explicit and implicit biases. Explicit biases can
097   be, among others, gender or racial discrimination in generated text (Kotek et al., 2023; Navigli et al.,
098
      2023; Gallegos et al., 2024; Bang et al., 2024). Moreover, LLMs can have implicit biases, such as
      specific words and phrases frequently re-used in generated text. As a result, Liang et al. (2024) were
099
      able to use recent trends in word frequency to detect and analyze LLM-generated text. Our method is
100
      similar to the investigation by Liang et al. (2024) but focuses on operator instead of word frequency.
101

      et al., 2024), tabular dataset generation (Borisov et al., 2022b; van Breugel et al., 2024; Panagiotou

      3   M ETHOD : A NALYZING F EATURE E NGINEERING B IAS
135
136   We propose a three-stage method to assess the bias of an LLM when used to engineer new features
137   for tabular data problems. Our three-stage method A) tests the LLM for memorization of benchmark
138   datasets; B) engineers new features with an LLM as well as black-box automated feature engineering;
139   and C) analyzes the bias of the LLM. We visualize our method in Figure 1.
140   A) Memorization Test. Given that language models are trained on vast amounts of publicly available
141   data, we must account for the possibility that the LLM memorizes a dataset and optimal new features
142   prior to our evaluation. To mitigate the risk of dataset-specific bias influencing the LLM during
143   feature engineering, we test the LLM for memorization of datasets using the methods proposed by
144   Bordt et al. (2024). Specifically, we conducted the row completion test, feature completion test, and
145   the first token test. We consider a success rate of 50% or higher in any test an indicator that the
146   evaluation of the dataset is biased. In such cases, the dataset is excluded from further evaluation.
147   B) Feature Engineering. We propose a straightforward and interpretable feature engineering method
148   for LLMs. Given a dataset, the LLM is supplied with context information, including the name
149   and description. In addition, a comprehensive list of all features and critical statistical information
150   for each feature (e.g., datatype, number of values, minimum, maximum, etc.) are provided. The
151   instructions prompt also contains a pre-defined set of operators for engineering new features, each
152   with a description indicating whether an operator is unary (applicable to one feature) or binary
153
      (requiring two features). We detail our full prompting specifications with examples in Appendix A.
      The LLM is then instructed to generate precisely one new feature by selecting one or two existing
154
      features and applying one of the available operators. We employ chain-of-thought (CoT) prompt-
155
      ing (Wei et al., 2023) to boost the expressive power of the LLM (Li et al., 2024). In addition, the
156   LLM explains why a feature was generated with CoT. We also employ a feedback loop, similar to
157   CAAFE (Hollmann et al., 2024), which we detail in Appendix A.
158   Our approach to feature engineering with LLMs deviates from prior work (Hollmann et al., 2024;
159   Hirose et al., 2024; Jeong et al., 2024) because we do not rely on code generation. This might put
160   LLMs at a disadvantage because we reduce their potential expressiveness. However, we see this
161   disadvantage outweighed by three significant advantages: first, we (almost) nullify the failure rate
      of generated code, which can be as much as 95.3% for small models (Hirose et al., 2024) ; second,


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      we can control which operators the LLM uses; and third, we can extract applied operators from
163   structured output without a (failure prone) code parser – enabling our study.
164
165   Fundamentally, we aim to compare the distribution over operators suggested by the LLM to the
166
      distribution over the optimal operators. That said, we do not know the optimal operators for a dataset.
      Thus, as a proxy, we use the operators suggested by OpenFE (Zhang et al., 2023).
167
      To the best of our knowledge, OpenFE is the most recent, well-performing, and highly adopted3
168
      automated feature engineering tool. OpenFE suggests a set of new features after successively pruning
169   all possible new features generated by a set of operators. To do so, OpenFE uses multi-fidelity feature
170   boosting and computes feature importance.
171
172
      C) Analysis. Finally, we analyze bias in feature engineering with LLMs using trends in the frequen-
      cies of operators. Therefore, we save the operators used by LLMs and black-box automated feature
173
      engineering from the previous stage. Subsequently, we compute the distribution over the frequencies
174
      of operators. This database allows us to visualize, inspect, and contrast the functional behavior of
175   feature engineering with LLMs.

      4       E XPERIMENTS
179
180
      We extensively evaluate the bias of 4 LLMs for 21 operators across 27 classification datasets.
181   Large Language Models. We use four LLMs hosted by external providers via APIs. In detail,
182   we used GPT-4o-mini (OpenAI, 2024) and Gemini-1.5-flash (Gemini-Team, 2024) to represent big
183   frontier LLMs and Llama3.1 8B (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral 7B Instruct v0.3 (Jiang et al.,
184   2023), hosted by Together AI4 , to represent small open-source models. The API usage cost ∼200$.
185   Operators. In this study, we use a fixed set of applicable operators. These operators represent a
186   subset of the operators provided by OpenFE. We categorize the available operators into simple and
187   complex operators. Simple operators apply straightforward arithmetic operations, such as adding
188   two features. Furthermore, these operators are characterized by their relatively low computational
189   complexity, typically O(n). In contrast, complex operators perform more advanced transformations,
190   such as grouping or combining the existing data into distinct subsets, followed by various aggregation
191   functions. Compared to simple operators, complex operators generally exhibit a higher computational
192
      complexity of O(n log n) or greater. We present all operators and their categories in Appendix B.
193   Datasets. We used 27 out of 71 classification datasets from the standard AutoML benchmark (Gijsbers
194   et al., 2024), which consists of curated tabular datasets from OpenML (Vanschoren et al., 2014).
195   First, to avoid too large input prompts as well as extensive compute requirements, we selected all
196   datasets with up to 100 features, 100 000 samples, and 10 classes – resulting in 36 datasets. We had
197   to remove the yeast dataset due to insufficient samples per class for 10-fold cross-validation. Of
198
      the remaining 35 available datasets, 8 (∼23%) failed our memorization tests (see Appendix E) with
      at least one LLM, making them unsuitable for further evaluation – resulting in 27 datasets.
199
200   Evaluation Setup. For each dataset, we perform 10-fold cross-validation. For each fold, we run
201   OpenFE and prompt each LLM to generate 20 features. We assess the predictive performance of
202   feature engineering following Zhang et al. (2023) by evaluating LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017) on the
203   original features and the original features plus the newly generated features. Note, due to using a
204
      feedback loop, we only add features to the dataset when they improve the predictive performance on
      validation data (see Appendix A) We measured predictive performance using ROC AUC. Moreover,
205
      we mitigate a positional bias of our prompt template by repeating feature generation five times with
206
      an arbitrary order of operators. Finally, we compute the frequencies of operators across all new
207   features, in total 27 000.

      ADDITIONAL EXPERIMENT. ENGINEERING RANDOM FEATURES WITH LLMS.
433
434   We additionally investigate whether the observed bias primarily arises from positional preferences
435   related to the positioning of operator names in our prompt. We additionally investigate whether
436
      our prompt template influenced our results. Therefore, we adapt our prompt template to mirror
      a random search. That is, we force an LLM to generate new features by randomly selecting the
437
      most appropriate operator. To this end, we employed the same experimental setup as our primary
438
      experiments. However, we masked the actual names of the operators in the instructions prompt. Each
439   operator is assigned a numeric label, which the LLM selects. Subsequently, these numeric labels are
440   mapped back to the names of the corresponding operators. Notably, we again shuffle the order of
441   operators five times.
442
      Figure 6 shows the distribution over the frequency of operators with LLM-based random search for
443
      feature engineering. We observe that all models exhibit a significantly higher degree of uniformity
444
      compared to Figure 2. Yet, we do not observe total uniformity as expected for a random search, which
445   aligns with observations for LLMs by Hopkins et al. (2023). Moreover, the previously observed bias
446   toward simple operators does not manifest anymore. This is particularly visible for GPT-4o-mini.
447   Mistral 7B has again the highest selection frequency of invalid operator labels, i.e., fails to follow the
448   prompt’s instructions. We conclude that the positioning the operator names in our prompt template
449   did not cause the bias toward simple operators. Instead, the content of the prompt, in combination
450   with the LLM, causes the bias.
451
452   6    C ONCLUSION
453
454   In this work, we propose a method to evaluate whether large language models (LLMs) are biased
455   when used for feature engineering for tabular data. Our method detects a bias based on anomalies
456   in the frequency of operators used to engineer new features (e.g., Add). In our experiments, we
457   evaluated the bias of four LLMs. Our results reveal a bias towards simpler operators when engineering
458   new features with LLMs. Moreover, this bias seems to negatively impact the predictive performance
459   when using features generated by an LLM.
460   In conclusion, the contributions of our work are a method to detect bias in LLMs and evidence that a
461   bias toward simple operators exists. The findings of this method underscore the necessity to further
462   strengthen LLMs to truly unlock their potential for tabular data problems. Our work underscores the
463   importance of developing methods to mitigate bias in downstream applications. Promising methods
464   for future work to explore are in-context learning (e.g., prompt tuning) or fine-tuning the LLM to
465   favor optimal operators. In the long term, after identifying and addressing biases in LLMs, we can
466   fully liberate ourselves from manual feature engineering. This will allow us to leverage LLMs as
467   reliable and efficient automated data science agents for tabular data.
468
469   Limitations and Broader Impact. Our study on the bias of LLMs still has limitations because it
470
      is the first of its kind for automated data science. We detect a bias but do not support practitioners
      to determine why an LLM might be biased. Similarly, given how frontier LLMs are trained and
471
      deployed, we focused on assessing bias in the models’ outputs rather than internal mechanisms.
472
      Lastly, our study is limited to four LLMs, while practitioners can also choose from many other
473   potentially biased LLMs. Our findings and proposed method do not create any negative societal
474   impacts. Instead, both can have positive societal impacts because they increase our understanding
475   and (mis)trust when using large language models for feature engineering.

735   A.2    F EEDBACK L OOP
736
737   We additionally employ a feedback loop to supply the LLM with additional knowledge about its
738   generated features, similar to CAAFE (Hollmann et al., 2023). After the LLM proposes a feature,
739   the new feature is manually computed by applying the requested operator to the respective features.
740   Before a feature is added to the dataset, we test whether it yields improvements in ROC AUC on the
741   given dataset to prevent the addition of noisy features. Each proposed feature is added to the dataset,
742
      and 10-fold cross-validation is conducted using LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017). When compare the
      average ROC AUC score to the average ROC AUC scores over the dataset without the new feature.
743
      The new feature is subsequently only added to the dataset if it improves the average ROC AUC score.
744
      In the next feature generation request, the user prompt additionally contains information about the
745   previous feature, including its name, description, reasoning, and the actual transformation request
746   from the prior round. We also pass the change in ROC AUC score that the last feature yielded in
747   comparison to the dataset without the new feature.
