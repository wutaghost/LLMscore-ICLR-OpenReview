011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The Transformer neural network architecture has seen success on a wide vari-
014            ety of tasks that appear to require executive function – the ability to represent,
015            coordinate, and manage multiple subtasks. In cognitive neuroscience, executive
016            function is thought to rely on sophisticated frontostriatal mechanisms for selec-
017            tive gating, which enable role-addressable updating– and later readout– of in-
018            formation to and from distinct “addresses” of memory, in the form of clusters
019            of neurons. However, Transformer models have no such mechanisms intention-
020
               ally built-in. It is thus an open question how Transformers solve such tasks, and
               whether the mechanisms that emerge to help them to do so resemble the gat-
021
               ing mechanisms in the human brain. In this work, we analyze the mechanisms
022
               that emerge within a vanilla attention-only Transformer when trained on a task
023            from computational cognitive neuroscience explicitly designed to place demands
024            on working memory gating. We find that the self-attention mechanism within
025            the Transformer develops input and output gating mechanisms, particularly when
026            task demands require them. These gating mechanisms mirror those incorporated
027            into earlier biologically-inspired architectures and mimic those in human studies.
028            When learned effectively, these gating strategies support enhanced generalization
029            and increase the models’ effective capacity to store and access multiple items in
030            memory. Despite not having memory limits, we also find that storing and access-
031
               ing multiple items requires an efficient gating policy, resembling the constraints
               found in frontostriatal models. These results suggest opportunities for future re-
032
               search on computational similarities between modern AI architectures and models
033
               of the human brain.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      The Transformer architecture Vaswani et al. (2017) has recently become the dominant neural net-
040
      work model in artificial intelligence. Unlike some earlier AI architectures, which were inspired (al-
041
      beit loosely) from human processing of language (Hochreiter, 1997) or vision (LeCun et al., 2015),
042   Transformers have no mechanisms designed overtly to resemble the human brain. It thus remains
043   an open question what, if any, similarities exist, and whether there are opportunities for theory or
044   insights from AI and neuroscience to mutually inform one another (McGrath et al., 2024).
045
      In this work, we focus specifically on working memory management via gating mechanisms. Gating
046
      mechanisms are responsible for multiple distinct aspects of working memory management, includ-
047
      ing determining which items to store and retrieve, when, and from where. In humans, there is strong
048   evidence that such mechanisms are essential for tasks that require executive function, i.e., the ability
049   to manage competing demands from multiple tasks, stimuli, and responses and to coordinate their
050   execution (Frank & Badre, 2012; Badre & Frank, 2012; Chatham et al., 2014; Rac-Lubashevsky &
051   Kessler, 2016; Rac-Lubashevsky & Frank, 2021). Although some Transformer variants have ad-
052   ditional built-in structure for memory (Dai et al., 2019; Burtsev et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019),
053   the architectures which currently dominate modern AI systems are “vanilla” (Brown et al., 2020;
      Touvron et al., 2023), lacking specialized components to support this type of control.

054
      We thus investigate whether, and under what conditions, such structure can emerge as a result of
055   training. We train vanilla Transformer models on a task from cognitive neuroscience that was de-
056   signed designed to investigate selective gating and working memory in humans (O’Reilly & Frank,
057   2006; Rac-Lubashevsky & Frank, 2021). We use recent techniques from mechanistic interpretability
058   (Olah, 2022; Nanda & Bloom, 2022) to expose the mechanism that the Transformer uses to perform
059   the task. We find that, as a result of training, the self-attention mechanism specializes in a way that
060   resembles existing models of input-output gating (§4.1), but that these mechanisms only arise when
061   the training task places specific demands on gating that mimics biological networks (§4.2). We fur-
062   ther find that when such mechanisms do arise, they are predictive of better task performance and
063
      of generalization to changes in the input distribution and task demands (§4.3), improving effective
      working memory capacity (§4.4). Our findings highlight the importance of considering the emer-
064
      gent mechanisms that result from training in addition to the innate architectural mechanisms when
065
      drawing comparisons between AI systems and human cognitive processes.

068   2   BACKGROUND AND H YPOTHESES
069
070   There is strong evidence that working memory in human brains makes use of a gating mechanism
071   to read, write, and maintain information required to carry out complex tasks (Rac-Lubashevsky &
072   Kessler, 2016; Rac-Lubashevsky & Frank, 2021; Bhandari & Badre, 2018; Badre & Frank, 2012;
073   Chatham et al., 2014). Sophisticated gating mechanisms of the type implemented in biological
074   neural networks contain at least three important components. First, input gating controls whether
075
      or not given information is stored in memory, and if stored, determines the “address” (population of
      neurons) to which it should be written. Second, output gating determines when and what information
076
      to read out of memory to inform a subsequent decision, such as to produce a response to a task.
077
      Finally, working memory is role addressable, meaning that items can be bound to a learned task-
078   dependent context (i.e. role) when stored and accessed in working memory. For example, in listening
079   to a story for the first time, people will not just remember individual entities (“Andrea”, “Chicago”)
080   but rather can associate them with specific roles such as lives in(“Andrea”, “Chicago”).
081
      In humans and other animals, these operations are supported by corticostriatal circuits in which
082
      isolated clusters of prefrontal neurons (or stripes) are used to represent distinct addresses in memory
083
      that can be updated or read out from via selective gating actions triggered by basal ganglia and
084   thalamus (O’Reilly & Frank, 2006; Frank & Badre, 2012; Kriete et al., 2013; Calderon et al., 2022;
085   Soni & Frank, 2024). These stripes can also serve as latent roles that condition how to interpret
086   content within them. When learning effective gating policies, these models afford functions such
087   as variable binding and indirection that support rapid generalization to new situations (O’Reilly &
088   Frank, 2006; Frank & Badre, 2012; Collins & Frank, 2013; Kriete et al., 2013; Bhandari & Badre,
089   2018).
090   In principle, Transformers are good candidates for learning such gating behavior. Transformers’ na-
091   tive self-attention mechanism consists of attention heads which are arguably functionally analogous
092   to frontostriatal stripes. The decomposition of these heads into distinct keys, queries, and values
093   (see Appendix A.1) means that the Transformer can in principle learn to differentiate reading and
094   writing operations in a role- and context-dependent way across its multiple heads. However, whether
095   Transformers will use their self-attention to implement such a mechanism is an open question, espe-
096   cially in cases when it is possible to fit the training data using more heuristic and less generalizable
097   solutions.
098   We thus consider two hypotheses. First, we investigate whether, and under which data distributions,
099   the Transformers use their attention heads to learn effective gating strategies. We find that the
100   key vectors form addresses analogous to the PFC “stripes” (neural populations that support variable
101   binding in memory). The learned key construction determines the address to store an item and is thus
102   analogous to input gating. Conversely, the query vectors determine which addresses are accessed,
103   and are thus analogous to output gating.
104   Second, we investigate whether adopting such strategies will facilitate rapid learning and general-
105   ization in working memory tasks the way it has been show to in humans. Specifically, human studies
106   have shown that working memory capacity is not limited by the number of items one can maintain
107   but rather by their effective gating strategies in frontostriatal circuits (Vogel et al., 2005; McNab &
      Klingberg, 2008; Baier et al., 2010). Theoretical work has shown that capacity limits in these circuits


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      do not stem from a limitation in the number of available neural populations, but rather result from a
109   credit assignment problem that arises when learning to manage multiple items in memory (Soni &
110   Frank, 2024; Todd et al., 2009). These limitations can thus be partially mitigated by learning to reuse
111   effective gating policies (Soni & Frank, 2024). Because these limitations are computational rather
112   than anatomical (i.e., not driven by the number of neurons/populations available), we hypothesize
113   that they would also manifest in Transformers, even though they have no inherent memory demands
114   at all (since all information is available in the context window).

118   3.1   TASKS
119
120   Reference-Back 2 Task: We use a variant of a task from cognitive neuroscience known as the
121   “reference-back 2” task (Rac-Lubashevsky & Frank, 2021). This is one among a number of task
122   designs inspired by frontostriatal modeling work (O’Reilly & Frank, 2006; Soni & Frank, 2024)
123
      which requires selective updating and accessing of information in a role-addressable manner. In the
      reference-back paradigm, symbols are viewed one at a time with associated roles, and the participant
124
      must determine whether the current symbol is the same or different as that stored in memory for a
125
      given role. For example, a sequence might contain letters (role 1) and numbers (role 2), each of
126   which occurs along side an update instruction which is either Store or Ignore. For each symbol
127   in the sequence, the participant must do two things: 1) make a same/different judgment based on
128   whether the current symbol matches the previously-stored symbol for that role, and 2) if the update
129   instruction is Store, update the symbol associated with the associated role. See Figure 1 for an
130   example. We create a modified text-based version of the reference-back 2 task. In our design, roles
131   are denoted explicitly using special tokens (i.e., either Reg0 or Reg1 for the two-role version)
132   indicating the role (or “register”) to which the symbol should be bound. See Appendix A.2 for more
133   details about our task implementation.

151   Split-Set Control: The computational advantages of role-addressable gating in frontostriatal net-
152
      works (relative to other recurrent neural networks) are particularly evident when any symbol can
      be assigned to any register, so that the networks have to learn to assign them separable addresses
153
      (O’Reilly & Frank, 2006). To test the hypothesis that gating mechanisms only emerge in response to
154
      such task demands, we create a control condition in which the registers are associated with disjoint
155   sets of symbols. In this task, it is possible to succeed without role-addressable gating, since symbols
156   never need to be decoupled from their associated registers.
157
158   Ignore-Integrated and Ignore-Separated Controls (a.k.a Split-Set Control): In the same vein,
159   we include a further simplified task variant which is designed to require selective input gating but not
160   output gating. This condition, along with the split-set control, have been previously used to show
161   that the advantage of frontostriatal gating networks relative to other recurrent networks is largely
      reduced in such scenarios (O’Reilly & Frank, 2006).

162
      Specifically, in the ignore separated condition (referred to as split-set control earlier), each register
163   sees a disjoint set of symbols. Additionally, symbols falling under the Ignore update instruction
164   are disjoint from those that are stored, meaning the model does not need to learn to differentiate
165   Store and Ignore in a meaningful way. In contrast, in the ignore integrated condition, symbols
166   under an Ignore instruction will be in distribution with the chosen register. In other words, the
167   model must distinguish between a Store and Ignore instruction and thus learn input gating,
168   but does not have to learn an output gating policy because both registers have mutually exclusive
169   symbols.

173   We train small, attention-only, decoder-only Transformer models from scratch on our task. Our
174
      models contain two decoder-only layers, each with two heads, and no multilayer perceptrons or
      layer normalization, followed by a linear “unembed” layer. The models are trained on 100k training
175
      data points for 60 epochs. See Appendix A.3 for additional details.

      3.3   M ETRICS
179
      Performance on Refback2 Task: We evaluated how well the model performs on the task on
180   which it was trained using standard accuracy on the same vs. different prediction for each symbol
181   in the sequence.
182
183   Input and Output Gating: The refback2 task is assumed to benefit from gating mechanisms,
184   but success on the task is not in and of itself diagnostic of having learned the gating mechanisms.
185   To develop an intrinsic measure of the input and output gating mechanisms, we use path-patching
186   (Wang et al., 2022; Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023), a generalization of causal mediation analysis
187   (Pearl, 2001) that allows us to determine which components of a neural model (e.g., attention heads)
188   work together in order to produce observed behavior on a task. Path patching involves designing
189   a minimal pair of inputs, a “clean” input and a “corrupted” and then finding specific components
190   of a model which fully account for the difference in the model’s output between the two cases. By
191
      “patching in” only these components, the model can be made to behave as though it is seeing the
      corrupted input even when it is in fact seeing the clean input. See Appendix A.4 for more details.
192
193   We use path patching as a measure of input gating in the following way. For input gating, we design
194   a minimal pair of inputs in which the corrupted copy includes Ignore in a place where the clean
195   copy contained a Store, or vice-versa. We then use path patching to identify an incisive edit that
196   can be made to the weights of the model in order to prevent the model from storing (“gating in”) a
197
      given symbol. We report the accuracy of the input gating mechanism as the percentage of inputs on
      which this edit to the weights changes the models behavior in the expected way.
198
199   Analogously for output gating, we design minimal pairs which we expect to yield differences in an
200   output gating mechanism, assuming one exists and is functioning correctly. Specifically, our clean
201   and corrupted sequences differ in the register associated with one of the symbols, which should trig-
202   ger a difference in what information is read (“gated out”) in order to make a final same vs. difference
203
      judgment. Again, we report the accuracy of the output gating mechanism as the percentage of cases
      on which it is possible to make such an edit and produce the desired effect.
204
205   Our description of results in Section 4 elaborates on both this and the input gating metrics and their
206   interpretation.

275   4.2   E MERGENCE OF G ATING P OLICIES D EPENDS ON TASK D EMANDS
276
277   Under which conditions to gating computations arise? One possibility is that they are a trivial con-
278   sequence of the Transformer’s architecture. After all, gating is sometimes expressed as a simple
279   multiplicative operation, as in LSTMs (Hochreiter, 1997). And indeed, the key, query, and value
280   vectors underlying the attention heads are combined via matrix multiplications. However, this alone
281
      does not ensure that the networks learn effective, role-addressable gating policies. We thus inves-
      tigate whether the training task demands induce such gating policies by running several control
282
      experiments (§3.1) which simplify the task, removing the role addressability of gating requirements.
283
      We thus hypothesized that since this task is much easier to learn, the Transformer will learn an
284   overly memorized, brittle solution and will not develop effective input and output gating strategies.

391   4.4   G ATING P OLICY T RANSFERS TO I NCREASED TASK D EMANDS
392
393   Thus far, the experiments have focused on tasks with two registers, mimicking that used in the ref-
394
      erence back-2 task (Rac-Lubashevsky & Frank, 2021). However, human working memory has a
      capacity of about 3-4 items (Cowan, 2008), albeit with vigorous debates questioning whether this
395
      limit is discrete or continuous (Zhang & Luck, 2008; Wei et al., 2012; Luck & Vogel, 2013). More
396
      recent models and data suggest a “chunking” hybrid between the two, whereby multiple memo-
397   randa can compete for shared continuous resources within discrete slots (Nassar et al., 2018; Soni
398   & Frank, 2024). Chunking increases effective capacity, allowing more items to be remembered at
399   the cost of precision of some of the items. Notably, in frontostriatal gating models, when the num-
400   ber of registers to manage was larger than two, networks given limited memory allocation but with
401   chunking capabilities performed better than those that were allocated as many PFC populations as
402   items to store (Soni & Frank, 2024). The reason for this seeming paradox is credit assignment: as
403   the number of PFC populations (stripes) increases, the gating management problem becomes more
404   challenging – the network has to learn to route each item to distinct populations and to also learn to
405
      read out from the corresponding population for a given probe. Moreover, these learning problems
      are interdependent.
406
407   These limitations are computational rather than anatomical and stem from the learning process.
408   We hypothesize that they would also manifest in Transformers, even though Transformers have no
409   inherent memory demands (since all information is available in the context window). Specifically
410   because of the flexibility and expressivity of Transformers, we predicted that by increasing task
411
      demand, the network would learn the task, but struggle to do so. We further predict that models that
      learn mechanistic solutions will generalize better to new tasks.
412
413   To test this hypothesis, we increased the number of registers from 2 to 3. First, we confirmed that
414   asymptotic accuracy dropped to 95.4%. This result is qualitatively the same even when training
415   for twice the number of epochs. This is non-trivial given that we are just adding one register. We
416   predicted that the degree to which the transformer solves the task is related to heuristics and mem-
417
      orization rather than gating in these cases. We predicted that if we first pretrained the model to
      effectively manage two registers, the network will be able to scaffold the learned gating strategy to
418
      learn the three register task more robustly. We further predicted that this pretraining would only be
419
      useful if pretraining encouraged gating strategies. Our results in Figure 5 support both of these con-
420   clusions. Not only do pretrained networks on the original reference back-2 task exhibit higher gating
421   sub-task accuracy 5, but networks that were pretrained with the original two register task showed
422   very rapid learning in the three register task in the first few epochs. Moreover, this pretraining was
423   far less effective when it did not encourage gating (the split symbol control from above).

      excel at tasks that require executive function.

476

      6   L IMITATIONS
500
501   While this paper draws parallels between the Transformer architecture and the brain, it is important
502   to emphasize that there are some significant differences between how Transformers and humans
503   solve tasks. In particular, because Transformers can attend to any part of the sequence when cre-
504   ating a representation, they are not limited by memory constraints. Transformers can solve tasks
505   that would push the limits of human working memory (but it should be noted that Transformers
506   require disproportionately large amounts of training data to do so). Nevertheless, we hypothesized
507   that Transformers might still learn effective gating policies that mimic those in frontostriatal net-
508   works. Moreover, as briefly reviewed in the introduction, working memory capacity in humans and
509
      biological computational models is not limited primarily by the memory demand per se, but rather
      by the difficulty of the credit assignment problem for learning how to manage role-addressable stor-
510
      age and access of multiple items in memory. A fundamental bridge between Transformers and other
511
      models of WM (and humans themselves) would be if Transformers also needed to overcome the
512   credit assignment learning problem, despite an unlimited memory capacity.

626   A.1   S ELF -ATTENTION IN T RANSFORMERS
627
628   Transformers are powerful language models which create contextualized representations of the input
629   sequences. They learn to predict the next token one at a time using an “attention” mechanism that
630   scans other tokens in the sequence for relevant information (Bahdanau et al., 2014). A common
631
      practice (that is used here) is to mask any future tokens and so predictions and representations must
      be made by the current token and any previously seen tokens. These models are able to learn and
632
      represent complex sequence modelling tasks.
633
634   For a given prediction, Transformer attention generates three separate vectors for each token in
635   the sequence: a query, key, and value (q, k, v). The key vector is a set of tokens that the model
636   has learned are most relevant to the token at hand. The query vector scans the tokens in the key
637
      vectors and calculates how much the current prediction should “attend” to each of those tokens.
      Then, the value vectors at those positions are multiplied by the corresponding weights, summed
638
      up, and added to the next representation: for token i at layer j, the contextual representation is
639   P j j j
640      k qi ·kk ∗vk . Thus, the next token prediction includes earlier sequential information by combining
      the value vectors from previous tokens. In other words, Transformer attention can be viewed as a
641
      read/write mechanism: for a given token, the queries and keys dictate which tokens to read from, the
642   values are the content that is read (proportional to the attention calculated by the keys and queries),
643   and the summed content is written to a new representation at the given token. As we shall see below,
644   the comparison to role-addressable input and output gating operations is evident. The key vectors
645   form addresses analogous to the PFC “stripes” (neural populations that support variable binding in
646   memory). The learned key construction determines the address to store an item and is thus analogous
647   to input gating. Conversely, the query vectors determine which addresses are accessed, and are thus
      analogous to output gating.

      A.2   T EXTUAL R EFERENCE -BACK -2 TASK
649
650   The textual reference-back task requires making same/different judgments between incoming sym-
651   bols assigned to a particular “register” in memory, with respect to those seen previously and linked
652   to those same registers. Like the original tasks, the textual reference-back task is sequential, and
653   requires independent updating and maintenance of two memory registers, each containing one of
654   S arbitrary symbols at a time. At the beginning of each sequence, each register is initialized indi-
655   vidually to one s ∈ S (the pool of symbols is shared between registers, which was shown to more
656
      substantively tax gating mechanisms in (?).) Each sequence is composed of L tuples, each contain-
      ing register address Regi , symbol Symi , same/different label Ansi , and update instruction Insi . For
657
      a tuple i ∈ L, the answer Ansi is a binary value that is either Same if symbol Symi is currently
658
      stored in the register with address Regi , or Different otherwise. The update instruction Insi also
659   takes one of two values (ignore or store), evenly distributed. If the instruction is ignore, then
660   the model still needs to make the same/different determination with respect to the stored reference,
661   but the new symbol should not update the register content (i.e., the reference remains unperturbed).
662   If it is store, then from that point on in the sequence, Symi is stored in the register with address
663   Regi until otherwise updated. An example is shown in Fig. 1.
664
      We implement each reference-back task example in our data as a single sequence, and measure
665   models’ ability to predict Same versus Different for each Ansi . Each sequence has 10 same/different
666   answers, and we generate 100,000 train, 1,000 validation, and 1,000 held-out test sequences.
667
668
      The class balance of same to different answer labels in the train/test datasets is roughly 1:2,
      making a “maximum class” heuristic solution 0.66 accuracy, 0.33 precision, and 0.5 recall. We test
669
      several other heuristics, the strongest of which is predicting same if another tuple including Store
670
      and the target register and target symbol exists in the sequence, which scores 0.80 accuracy, 0.82
671   precision, and 0.85 recall.

      A.3   M ODELS AND T RAINING
674
675   We train small, attention-only Transformer models from scratch on our task. Our models contain
676   two decoder-only layers, each with two heads, and no multilayer perceptrons or layer normalization,
677   followed by a linear “unembed” layer to project the output of the last decoder into the space of the
678   entire vocabulary at each timestep In practice, only ‘same’ and ‘different’ are ever predicted. Our
679   network uses absolute positional embeddings (Vaswani et al., 2017). The vocabulary contains all
680   possible tokens, represented individually with embedding size E. Models are trained to predict the
681   next token with the language modelling objective: if the model is predicting Ansc , it will have access
682   to all (Insi , Regi , Symi , Ansi ) tuples where i < c, as well as Insc , Regc , and Symc . However, the
683
      models only receive loss at positions where a same/different token must be predicted (this is similar
      to the reward function applied in frontostriatal gating networks; (O’Reilly & Frank, 2006; Soni &
684
      Frank, 2024)). Furthermore, each layer gets a causal attention mask– when constructing each token
685
      representation, it cannot look ahead at tokens further down the sequence.
686
687   The models are trained over 60 epochs of the 100k training data points, learning from 6 million
688   examples in total. Models are evaluated on their accuracy (whether the correct Ansi is predicted for
689
      each tuple i), measured in precision and recall, as well as the same versus different token logit
      difference.

693
      Path-patching involves making a incisive edit to the representations of a trained model and ob-
694
      serving how the model’s behavior is affected, allowing one to infer the computations implemented
695
      within individual attentional heads (see Fig. 6). Path-patching requires a minimal pair of examples:
696   the “clean” example and the “corrupted” example, in which one token from the clean example is
697   changed, as well as the correct label. Given representations from the model for both the clean and
698   the corrupted examples (the blue and orange components in the figure), we can chose a specific
699   component anywhere in the model (referred to as the “sender”), and replace the clean representation
700   with the corrupted one at that specific component. These embeddings will be received by the next
701   layer (”receiver”), thereby ”patching” the path. From there, the patched model will compute the new
      prediction.
