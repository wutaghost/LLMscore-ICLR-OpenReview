                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014            As large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed across various
015
               industries, concerns regarding their reliability, particularly due to hallucina-
               tions—outputs that are factually inaccurate or irrelevant to user input—have
016
               grown. Our research investigates the relationship between the training process
017
               and the emergence of hallucinations to address a key gap in existing research that
018            focuses primarily on post hoc detection and mitigation strategies. Using models
019            from the Pythia suite (70M–12B parameters) and several hallucination detection
020            metrics, we analyze hallucination trends throughout training and explore LLM
021            internal dynamics. We introduce Sensitivity Dropout (SenD), a novel training
022            protocol designed to mitigate hallucinations by reducing variance during training.
023            SenD achieves this by deterministically dropping embedding indices with signif-
024            icant variability, referred to as Sensitive Embedding Indices. In addition, we de-
025            velop an unsupervised hallucination detection metric, Efficient EigenScore (EES),
026
               which approximates the traditional EigenScore in 2x speed. This efficient metric
               is integrated into our protocol, allowing SenD to be both computationally scalable
027
               and effective at reducing hallucinations. Our empirical evaluation demonstrates
028
               that our approach improves LLM reliability at test time by up to 40% compared
029            to normal training while also providing an efficient method to improve factual
030            accuracy when adapting LLMs to Wikipedia, Medical, and LegalBench domains.

      1.1   M OTIVATION
036
037   As Large Language Models (LLMs) become more sophisticated and widespread across industries,
038   concerns about their reliability and safety have grown due to misuse and user errors. One of these
039   concerning areas discovered by the scientific community is the phenomenon of hallucinations -
040   LLMs producing content that may not align with real-world facts, the user’s input, or training data it
041   has seen in the past (Huang et al., 2023a). In our research, we target a specific field of hallucinations
042   called confabulations which occur when the LLM generates different responses given the same
043   or similar inputs. This can be harmful when the generations alter between correct and factually
044
      incorrect responses.
045   Previous research has largely focused on identifying and addressing hallucinations in large language
046   models (LLMs), but the impact of the training process on hallucinations remains under-explored
047   (Huang et al., 2023a; Rawte et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Chen
048   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024b). This paper addresses this gap by investigating
049   how the iterative learning process in LLMs leads to significant variance in hallucination behavior
050
      during training. This variability indicates that the model’s factual confidence fluctuates, making it
      challenging to pinpoint a checkpoint at which the model has confidently learned facts.
051
052   As LLMs are deployed in high-risk industries, ensuring their reliability is crucial for user safety.
053   However, this is not always achieved, leading to serious consequences, such as an Air Canada law-
      suit over an LLM-generated incorrect policy (Garcia, 2024). Addressing such issues requires a


                                                         1

054
      deeper understanding of how hallucinations arise during training, enabling more reliable and effi-
055   cient mitigation strategies beyond post-processing methods.
056
057   To explore these hallucination trends, we analyze models ranging from 70 million to 12 billion pa-
058
      rameters within Pythia suite (Biderman et al., 2023), assessing them across various training check-
      points and tasks. Our goal is to validate the oscillatory behavior observed by Li et al. (2024) through
059
      evaluation metrics including HaluEval (Li et al., 2023), FactScore (Min et al., 2023), SelfCheck-
060
      GPT (Manakul et al., 2023), and XSum (Narayan et al., 2018). Utilizing the reliability of internal
061   model dynamics for quantifying hallucination likelihood, we use EigenScore (Chen et al., 2024)
062   and Semantic Entropy (Kossen et al., 2024) to detect hallucination risk by analyzing variability in
063   high-temperature outputs. Experiments utilize EigenScore and the HELM dataset (Su et al., 2024)
064   to identify hallucinations during training.
065
      In response to this variance, we introduce a novel training protocol called Sensitivity Dropout
066
      (SenD). SenD is designed to emphasize confident learning of facts, and in turn reduce the likelihood
067   of confabulations, rather than solely minimizing the training loss. By selectively dropping Sensitive
068   Embedding Indices—those exhibiting significant fluctuations throughout training—SenD acts as
069   a technique that reduces hallucination variance and enhances the model’s factual certainty. This
070   provides a more reliable criterion for determining training termination, ensuring models not only
071   achieve loss convergence but also display stable factual confidence. To maintain efficiency as model
072   size and inference count increase, we propose the Efficient EigenScore (EES), a novel metric for
073   hallucination detection. EES replaces EigenScore (Chen et al., 2024), the primary metric used in
074   our experiments, offering a scalable solution with high correlation to EigenScore.
075   Our contributions to the field can be summarized as follows, emphasizing that SenD enhances the
076   training process but does not replace post-hoc solutions, which may still be required after training:1
077
078         1. Empirical verification of the oscillatory nature of hallucinations in LLMs training
079            across various model scales and detection metrics.
080         2. Sensitivity Dropout (SenD), a training-time method designed to reduce hallucination vari-
081            ance and increase model factual confidence during training.
082         3. Efficient EigenScore (EES), an efficient hallucination detection metric used to keep SenD
083            efficient, achieving up to 2x speedup with minimal effects on accuracy.

086   1.2   R ELATED W ORK
087
      The majority of research on hallucinations in language models has focused on detecting and mitigat-
088   ing this phenomenon rather than explaining its underlying causes. Recent techniques can be catego-
089   rized into two main approaches: those that rely on output probabilities at inference time (Manakul
090   et al., 2023; Joshi et al., 2017; Li et al., 2023) and those that utilize internal representations or hid-
091   den layers of the model (Su et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Kossen et al., 2024). While the former
092   has demonstrated effectiveness, the latter offers deeper insights but often comes with computational
093   trade-offs. Additionally, methods like Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) have
094   gained traction for enhancing model reliability (Yu et al., 2024). However, many of these post-hoc
095   solutions enhance factual accuracy by layering algorithms atop pre-trained models, which can be
096
      inefficient. Our work addresses this gap by focusing on the internal dynamics of the model that
      contribute to hallucinations.
097
098   Regularization techniques have been introduced to fix the issue of variability, notably random neuron
099   dropout, used to reduce the variance and ensure that no neuron is overpowering others (Srivastava
100   et al., 2014; Baldi & Sadowski, 2013). Work such as that done by Santra et al. (2020); Ba & Frey
101   (2013) aims to modify random neuron dropout to change the way neurons are dropped to a more
102
      deterministic, precise manner. This has allowed the authors to drop unimportant connections in a
      deep neural network to ensure that class discriminative information is propagated through the model
103
      correctly (Santra et al., 2020). Inspired by this, our aim is to target hallucinatory embedding indices
104
      in our models to ensure that factual information is propagated through. State-of-the-art hallucination
105   metrics, especially those based on internal model dynamics, rely on spectral analysis and embedding

      The training checkpoints of a transformer model can be vital in understanding the dynamics of how
      the model learns. Beyond the model’s architecture and the data itself, numerous factors influence
139
      the learning process such as: whether the loss function penalizes the learner for factual mistakes it
140
      makes or if it primarily tries to force the model to memorize the data. While our paper does not aim
141   to address the broader debate on whether LLMs truly understand language or rely on memorization,
142   our analysis of training dynamics through multiple checkpoints shows that converging the training
143   loss does not necessarily correspond to reducing hallucinations, verifying the results by Li et al.
144   (2024) for LLM oscillatory hallucinaiton behaviour during training. Our study utilizes Eleuther
145   AI’s Pythia and LMEval tools (Biderman et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024a) to examine the develop-
146   ment and evolution of LLMs throughout the training process. Pythia comprises a suite of 16 LLMs,
147   all trained on public data in the same sequential order, with sizes ranging from 70 million to 12
148   billion parameters. We use 20 equally spaced training checkpoints from the start to the finish for our
149
      analysis. These models are evaluated at each checkpoint on a variety of hallucination metrics such
      as SelfCheckGPT for Self-consistency (Manakul et al., 2023), XSum for Summarization (Narayan
150
      et al., 2018), Perplexity, and HaluEval (Li et al., 2023) for Question Answering (QA) tasks. High
151
      SelfCheckGPT Score means that the model is more likely to contradict itself on the given input, a
152   higher Rouge1 score on XSum means the data is aligning better with the provided reference sum-
153   mary, lower perplexity implies higher prediction confidence, and higher Exact Match, Accuracy, and
154   Correctness implies better performance of the model on QA tasks.

397   4     S ENSITIVITY D ROPOUT (S EN D)
398
399   Building on the findings from Section 3.2, and aiming to reduce variance in the factual uncertainty
400   of LLMs during training, this section introduces SenD, an efficient and transferable framework for
401   training LLMs. SenD integrates the EES method discussed in Section 3.3 to enhance computational
402   efficiency while addressing variance in SEI behavior. By identifying SEIs, which contribute to the
403
      oscillatory behavior of hallucinations during training, SenD deterministically drops these embedding
      indices based on a small subset of the training data. This approach ensures an increase in the model’s
404
      factual certainty by the end of training as explained in Algorithm 2.

431   tion/mitigation in LLMs. SenD is trained on all datasets using 200 data points and and 2,000 points
      (referred to as 2k) for MedHALT due to the medical domain importance. SenD implements the

      EigenScore reduction technique from Section 3.2 and detects SEIs using a 3-checkpoint window on
433   a specialized hallucination tracking dataset. The distance between checkpoints and the dropout rate
434   K are tunable hyperparameters. Given our ablation study in Appendix C, we opt for K = 20% and
435   Threshold=3 for the experiments. SEIs in the penultimate layer are identified based on their variabil-
436   ity across checkpoints and are deterministically dropped for the subsequent 3 training checkpoints.
437   This is repeated at each 3-checkpoint interval until loss convergence, effectively mitigating halluci-
438   nation tendencies and oscillations.

520   To do this we used the internal states of LLMs, specifically the penultimate layer activations during
521   inference on a specialized dataset. We present an initial method of reducing hallucinations based on
522   the principles of EigenScore metrics introduced by Chen et al. (2024). We showed empirically that
523   our SEI detection method significantly reduces the EigenScore on inference of LLMs throughout
524   various stages of training (Figure 2). Following the success of the SEI method, we moved on to the
525
      application of a hallucination reduction method on training on Pythia and Llama models and vari-
      ous domains. We show through training with SenD that we are able to fix the oscillatory behaviour
526
      initially seen throughout training and reduce the EES of finetuned models as shown in Figure 4 by
527
      modifying the internal mechanics of training with Sensitivity Dropout. At test time we achieve
528   a 40% increase in FactScore performance and improvement of other SOTA hallucination detection
529   metrics, verifying that SenD provides a substantial improvement to current training protocols both
530   during and after training.
531
      Note that although SenD has only been applied to finetuning training in this paper, the training
532
      framework is applicable to all stages of training. We encourage future work to implement SenD on
533
      larger training sets, such as pretraining, to see how SenD performs in these environments. To further
534   advance our work, we plan to scale SenD to larger datasets and models, as current experiments
535   were limited by compute constraints with larger LLMs. Demonstrating SenD’s effectiveness on
536   larger open-source models like Meta’s LLaMA 3.2 405B (Dubey et al., 2024) will provide crucial
537   evidence for organizations developing state-of-the-art LLMs to incorporate SenD into their training
538   protocols, ultimately improving model safety. Given that SenD targets variance reduction during
539   training, we anticipate even greater gains on larger LLMs, where the higher inherent variance may
      amplify the regularization effect and lead to more significant improvements.
