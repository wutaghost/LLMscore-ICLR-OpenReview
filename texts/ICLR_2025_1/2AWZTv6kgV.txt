009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural differential equations offer a powerful approach for learning dynamics
012            from data. However, they do not impose known constraints that should be obeyed
013            by the learned model. It is well-known that enforcing constraints in surrogate
014            models can enhance their generalizability and numerical stability. In this paper,
015            we introduce projected neural differential equations (PNDEs), a new method
016
               for constraining neural differential equations based on projection of the learned
               vector field to the tangent space of the constraint manifold. In tests on several
017
               challenging examples, including chaotic dynamical systems and state-of-the-art
018
               power grid models, PNDEs outperform existing methods while requiring fewer
019            hyperparameters. The proposed approach demonstrates significant potential for
020            enhancing the modeling of constrained dynamical systems, particularly in complex
021            domains where accuracy and reliability are essential.

043   In power grid modeling, for example, simulations allow grid operators and researchers to examine
044   the stability of the complex electrical networks that underpin modern life. However, the transition
045
      to renewable energy sources presents unprecedented challenges. As wind and solar generation
      increasingly contribute to the energy mix, grid operators must contend with distributed and highly
046
      variable production, alongside the loss of system inertia that was previously provided by conventional
047
      generators (Breithaupt et al., 2016; Milano et al., 2018). On the demand side, driven by electric
048   vehicle adoption and the rapid growth of artificial intelligence, forecasts of peak demand have
049   increased rapidly, further threatening grid stability (North American Electric Reliability Corporation
050   (NERC), 2023). Unfortunately, existing methods for modeling grid dynamics are computationally
051   infeasible at the scale and accuracy required for realistically modeling future scenarios dominated
052   by renewable energy sources (Matevosyan et al., 2019). This problem is further exacerbated by
053   legal mandates requiring grid operators to perform large-scale probabilistic simulations (European
      Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity (ENTSO-e), 2018). By leveraging large

054
      amounts of data to learn and predict grid dynamics, machine learning can enable grid operators to
055   efficiently forecast system states, mitigate faults, and maintain stability in future power grids.
056
057   Among existing machine learning approaches, neural ordinary differential equations (NODEs) offer a
058
      promising approach for data-driven modeling of dynamical systems, combining the expressiveness
      of neural networks with the interpretability of differential equations (Chen et al., 2018). Building
059
      upon NODEs, universal differential equations (UDEs) offer a hybrid framework that integrates
060
      neural networks with differentiable physical models (Rackauckas et al., 2021). We refer to these
061   approaches collectively as neural differential equations (NDEs). NDEs have demonstrated success
062   across various scientific disciplines, including weather and climate modeling (Kochkov et al., 2024),
063   robotics (Richards et al., 2021), epidemiology (Dandekar et al., 2020) and bioengineering (Narayanan
064   et al., 2022). Despite excellent performance within the training data, NDEs often generalize poorly,
065   for example, to new initial conditions or future times. Due to the iterative nature in which neural
066   networks are applied within NDEs, inaccuracies and uncertainties are quickly amplified and can push
067   the trajectory far outside the training distribution, where performance degrades.
068   Inductive biases play an important role in avoiding such issues. In scientific machine learning, for
069   example, inductive biases can take the form of physical constraints, conservation laws, or other
070   domain-specific knowledge that can enhance model performance and interpretability (Karniadakis
071   et al., 2021). Physical constraints, in particular, are often incorporated as additional loss terms, for
072   example, in physics-informed neural networks (Raissi et al., 2017a;b; 2019) and physics-informed
073   operator learning (Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021b; Goswami et al., 2022; White et al., 2023b).
074   However, a variety of issues can arise with these soft constraints. For instance, the resulting composite
075
      loss can be expensive to evaluate, due to the derivatives in the physics loss, and challenging to
      minimize. Furthermore, a number of numerical issues are known to arise, such as worse conditioning,
076
      conflicting loss terms, inaccuracies in the approximated derivatives, and the existence of spurious
077
      solutions satisfying the constraints on the collocation points of the physics loss (Leiteritz and Pflüger,
078   2021; Krishnapriyan et al., 2021; Huang and Agarwal, 2023; Wang et al., 2021a; 2022; Rohrhofer
079   et al., 2022). Yet perhaps the greatest drawback of soft constraints is that they provide no guarantees
080   that the physical constraints will be satisfied at inference time; at best, they can only hope to guide
081   the model weights towards physically plausible solutions during training. For certain safety-critical
082   applications, where the ability to provably and robustly obey known physical constraints is crucial,
083   this is inadequate. These critical drawbacks motivate stricter enforcement of the constraints, both
084   during training and at inference time, in the form of hard constraints.
085
086   Contribution. In this paper, we introduce projected neural differential equations (PNDEs), a
087   method for incorporating hard constraints in NDEs based on the projection of the vectors of the
088
      NDE’s vector field onto tangent spaces of the constraint manifold (see Figure 1). Since the constraint
      manifold is defined by arbitrary algebraic constraints on the state of the system, our method can
089
      enforce diverse constraints, such as conservation laws, holonomic constraints, and even external
090
      forcings, all without requiring the system to be measured in specific (e.g., canonical) coordinates. Our
091   method enforces constraints more accurately than existing methods, requires fewer hyperparameters
092   to tune, and enhances the numerical stability and generalizability of the systems we study.
093
094   Outline. We motivate and derive the proposed method in Section 2, before surveying the existing
095   literature and comparing to related works in Section 3. Finally, in Section 4, we demonstrate
096   empirically on several challenging examples, including highly chaotic systems and elaborate power
097   grid models, that our approach satisfies the constraints more accurately than state-of-the-art baselines,
098   all while requiring fewer hyperparameters and permitting larger timesteps of the numerical solver.

104   We first review some differential geometry definitions that are relevant to the proposed approach and
105   refer the reader to (Lang, 1999; Lee, 2012; Boumal, 2024) for a more detailed treatment. The set of
106   tangent vectors to a manifold M at a point u ∈ M is a vector space called the tangent space to M
107   at u and is denoted by Tu M. The disjoint union of all the tangent spaces to M forms the tangent
      bundle TM = {(u, v) | u ∈ M, v ∈ Tu M} of M. A vector field on a manifold M is a continuous

      map X : M → TM with the property that X(u) ∈ Tu M for all u ∈ M. The integral curve at u
109   of a vector field X on M is the smooth curve γ on M such that γ(0) = u and γ ′ (t) = X(γ(t)) for
110   all t in the interval of interest. The integral curve γ is simply the solution of a first-order ODE with
111   right-hand side Xγ(t) for t in the interval of interest, so theorems for the existence, uniqueness, and
112   smoothness of ODE solutions translate directly to integral curves of vector fields on manifolds.

182   3    R ELATED W ORK
183
184   Hard Constraints. A number of projection-based approaches have been proposed to constrain
185   the outputs of deep learning models. Harder et al. (2024) design constraint layers to ensure mass
186   and energy conservation during downscaling (i.e., super-resolution) of climate model fields from
187   low to high resolution. Negiar et al. (2022) and Chalapathi et al. (2024) add a differentiable implicit
188   constraint layer for finding optimal linear combinations of learned basis functions that approximately
189   satisfy differential constraints at collocation points. Jiang et al. (2020) and Duruisseaux et al. (2024)
190   use a spectral projection layer to enforce linear differential constraints efficiently in Fourier space.
191   Hard constraints have also been use to take advantage of structured and well-understood dynamical
192
      systems, for example, the divergence-free property of incompresible fluid flows (Richter-Powell et al.,
      2022; Mohan et al., 2023; Xing et al., 2024) and the symplectic structure of Hamiltonian systems
193
      (Lutter et al., 2019; Greydanus et al., 2019; Zhong et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2020; Burby et al., 2020;
194
      Cranmer et al., 2020; Sæmundsson et al., 2020; Valperga et al., 2022; Duruisseaux et al., 2023a;b).
195
196   The work of Finzi et al. (2020) is especially relevant to this paper. They use explicit algebraic
197   constraints to enable the learning of Hamiltonian dynamics in Cartesian coordinates, where the
198
      Hamiltonian is “simpler” and therefore easier to learn than in the original generalized coordinates.
      They add constraints to the Hamiltonian via Lagrange multipliers, and use a variational approach to
199
      derive the corresponding constrained equations of motion. The result is the standard Hamiltonian
200
      equations of motion, multiplied by a projection operator that enforces the constraints in a manner
201   that is consistent with the underlying Hamiltonian structure. While derived from an entirely different
202   perspective, our approach can be understood as a principled generalization of their method beyond
203   the idealized Hamiltonian setting to a much more general class of NDEs. In this paper, for example,
204   we include tests on a damped pendulum system, which is not compatible with their method.
205
206   Stabilized Neural Differential Equations. White et al. (2023a), whose work is most closely related
207   to ours, constrain NDE solutions using stabilized neural differential equations (SNDEs), given by
208

214
215   To understand how SNDEs relate to our proposed method, we first provide the intuition behind
      Equation (10). The stabilization term (i.e. the second term) equals zero on the manifold, since


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      g(u) = 0 there by definition. Hence, the stabilization is only “activated” when a trajectory leaves
217   the constraint manifold, i.e., when the constraints have already been violated. The effect of the
218   stabilization is to “nudge” such a trajectory “back towards M”, i.e., back towards satisfying the
219   constraints, with the strength of the nudge determined by the stabilization parameter γ. Larger values
220   of γ lead to a stronger nudge and stricter enforcement of the constraints. However, excessively large
221   values of γ may require the numerical solver to stop more frequently, with the system eventually
222   becoming stiff. The key difference with our method is that SNDEs continuously correct violations
223   of the constraints, while our method prevents such violations from occurring in the first place.
224   Accordingly, we can expect our method to yield a stronger enforcement of the constraints. We will
225
      use SNDEs as a baseline for comparison throughout this paper.
226
      Continuous Normalizing Flows on Riemannian Manifolds. A large body of related work has
227
      studied neural ODEs on Riemannian manifolds, primarily in the context of continuous normalizing
228
      flows on non-Euclidean geometries. In contrast to our approach, they typically deal with prototypical
229   Riemannian manifolds, such as spheres and tori, and are not easily adapted to more general manifolds.
230   For example, the approach of Lou et al. (2020) requires a local chart for the manifold, the derivation of
231   which is likely to be analytically intractable for embedded submanifolds with arbitrary and nonlinear
232   local defining functions. Similarly, the approaches of Bose et al. (2020) and Rezende et al. (2020)
233   require an expression for the exponential map, while Mathieu and Nickel (2020) uses a custom
234   geodesic distance layer, none of which are easily applied in our setting.
235
236   Optimization and Numerical Integrators on Manifolds. The use of projections is widespread in
237   optimization on manifolds (Absil et al., 2008; Boumal, 2024), where it is used to project iterates to
238
      the constraint manifold and/or gradients onto appropriate tangent spaces (as in our approach). The
      idea of projecting gradients onto tangent spaces of the constraint set dates back at least to Luenberger
239
      (1972; 1973), and is still commonly used. For instance, in Riemannian optimization, Euclidean
240
      gradients are typically replaced by Riemannian gradients, which are their orthogonal projections onto
241   tangent spaces. Explicit formulas and efficient projection algorithms have been derived and used for
242   manifolds of greater practical interest (Edelman et al., 1998; Manton, 2002; Absil et al., 2008; Wen
243   and Yin, 2010; Absil and Malick, 2012; Hauswirth et al., 2016; Zhang and Sra, 2016; Oviedo León
244   and Dalmau-Cedeño, 2019; Oviedo León et al., 2021). Where these methods differ crucially from
245   our approach is that they typically seek to constrain the weights or optimization variables themselves
246   to a given matrix manifold, while our aim is to constrain trajectories of an NDE, the weights of
247   which are not themselves directly constrained. Regarding numerical integration on manifolds, the
248   available literature is much less extensive (Hairer, 2011), and most approaches restrict themselves to
249
      Lie groups (very structured Riemannian manifolds) and Hamiltonian or mechanical systems, in order
      to obtain practical algorithms (Iserles et al., 2000; Christiansen et al., 2011; Celledoni et al., 2014;
250
      2022). Some of these approaches are based on projection, similarly to optimization on manifolds,
251
      while others use local parametrizations or Lagrange multipliers. Riemannian optimization algorithms
252   have also been obtained by simulating dynamical systems using numerical integrators constrained to
253   the relevant Riemannian manifolds (Alimisis et al., 2020; Tao and Ohsawa, 2020; Lee et al., 2021;
254   França et al., 2021; Duruisseaux and Leok, 2022a;b;c; 2023).

482   5                 C ONCLUSION
483
484   We have introduced projected neural differential equations (PNDEs), a new method for enforcing
485   arbitrary algebraic constraints in neural differential equation models. Compared to state-of-the-art
      baselines, our approach requires fewer hyperparameters and enforces the constraints several orders of


                                                                                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      magnitude more accurately. In experiments on complex nonlinear and chaotic systems, as well as two
487   challenging examples based on state-of-the-art power grid models, PNDEs are efficient, numerically
488   stable, and generalize well to unseen data. We have also demonstrated the use of PNDEs to simplify
489   and significantly improve the learning of complex dynamical systems using explicit constraints.
490   Many promising directions remain for future research. For example, graph neural networks are also
491   compatible with our method and a natural fit for modeling grid dynamics, thereby enabling critical
492   out-of-distribution tests such as changing grid topologies.

      A     DATASETS

866
      For each system, we generate ground truth trajectories from random initial conditions. The exact
867   number of initial conditions used for training, validation, and testing, as well as the duration of each
868   trajectory and the timestep used, is determined based on the individual characteristics of each system,
869   and summarized in Table 2. For training and validation, each trajectory is split into contiguous
870   segments consisting of 4 timesteps, which are then randomized and combined along the batch
871   dimension. The first observation in each segment is the initial condition for the prediction.
872
      With the exception of the power grid experiments, all trajectories are generated using the 9(8) explicit
873   Runge-Kutta algorithm due to Verner (2010), implemented in DifferentialEquations.jl (Rackauckas
874   and Nie, 2017) as Vern9, with absolute and relative solver tolerances of 10−12 .
875
876
      The power grid models, which are differential-algebraic equations (DAEs), are implemented in the
      Julia package PowerDynamics.jl (Plietzsch et al., 2022). Given a random initial condition, we then
877
      generate a ground truth trajectory by solving the DAE for 10 seconds using a 4th order L-stable Rosen-
878
      brock method (Hairer and Wanner, 1996), implemented in the Julia package DifferentialEquations.jl
879   (Rackauckas and Nie, 2017) as Rodas4.

      B     T RAINING
906
      All models are trained on NVIDIA H100 GPUs. We minimize the mean squared error loss using the
907
      AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2018) with a constant learning rate of 10−3 , weight decay
908
      of 10−4 , and a batch size of 1024. All systems are modeled using standard multilayer perceptrons
909   with GELU activation functions to encourage smoothness. The number of hidden layers, hidden
910   width, and training epochs are summarized in Table 2. All models are initially trained without
911   constraints, and subsequently fine-tuned with the constraints for 100 epochs.
912
      During training, all models are solved using the 5(4) explicit Runge-Kutta algorithm of Tsitouras
913
      (2011), implemented in DifferentialEquations.jl (Rackauckas and Nie, 2017) as Tsit5, with ab-
914
      solute and relative tolerances of 10−4 . Gradients of ODE solutions are obtained using the adjoint
915   sensitivity method (Pontryagin, 1962; Chen et al., 2018), implemented in SciMLSensitivity.jl as
916   BacksolveAdjoint (Rackauckas et al., 2021). For testing, we use absolute and relative tolerances
917   of 10−6 .
