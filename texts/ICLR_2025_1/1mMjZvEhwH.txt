009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Effective long-term planning in complex environments benefits from not only
012            leveraging immediate information but also utilizing past experiences. Drawing
013            inspiration from how humans use long-term memory in decision-making, we pro-
014            pose the POMDiffuser framework, an approach to planning in partially observable
015            environments. While conventional Diffuser models often memorize specific envi-
016            ronments, POMDiffuser explores the potential of learning to plan from memory,
017            with the aim of generalizing to new scenarios. By incorporating a memory mech-
018
               anism in POMDP scenarios, our model extends diffusion-based planning models
               into the realm of meta-learning with carefully designed tasks that require the dif-
019
               fusion planner to demonstrate both long-term planning and memory utilization.
020
               We investigated existing diffusion-based models, focusing on their applicability,
021            computational efficiency, and performance trade-offs.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   To operate effectively in complex environments, an intelligent agent must have two key abilities:
027   the capacity to memorize past experiences and the ability to use this memory to imagine future sce-
028   narios for planning (Schacter et al., 2007; 2012). These abilities are particularly crucial in partially
029   observable environments (Kaelbling et al., 1998) where current observations lack sufficient infor-
030   mation for optimal decision-making. In such scenarios, agents need to infer the hidden state of the
031   world—known as the belief state—by leveraging past experiences.
032   For both memory and planning, the critical challenge lies in extending the horizon length both
033   backward and forward. The quality of the belief state relies heavily on how far into the past the agent
034   can consider, as a longer history provides a richer belief representation. Similarly, the advantages
035   of long-horizon planning—such as avoiding short-sighted decisions, aligning immediate actions
036   with long-term objectives, addressing sparse reward issues, and effectively managing unfamiliar
037   tasks—become more pronounced as the planning range extends (Hamrick et al., 2020). In particular,
038
      achieving long-term memory and extended planning simultaneously is critical (Momennejad, 2024;
      Gregor et al., 2019).
039
040   Although various model architectures have been explored to enhance memory and planning abili-
041   ties, these architectures face significant challenges in effectively integrating long-horizon memory
042   with long-horizon planning. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) significantly limit their scalability
043   with large datasets as its training process does not allow parallel processing of a sequence. Their
044
      dependence on autoregressive rollouts for planning leads to error compounding, which worsens in
      particular with longer planning horizons (Lambert et al., 2022). Lastly, the vanishing gradient re-
045
      stricts their memory to retain long-term dependencies.
046
047   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have emerged as an alternative, capturing dependencies over
048   long sequences without sequential processing constraints. It also excels in parallel computation and
049   capturing global context. However, each step of the rollouts involves quadratic complexity with
050
      respect to sequence length, making them computationally intensive for extended planning tasks.
      Furthermore, they still face challenges due to their reliance on autoregressive rollouts, leading to
051
      compounding error (Lambert et al., 2022; Bachmann & Nagarajan, 2024).
052
053   Structured State Space Models (SSMs) , such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024), offer a promising
      alternative to the intensive computation of Transformers by modeling long sequences with linear

054
      complexity relative to sequence length while preserving parallel trainability. Although SSMs reduce
055   the cost of a single-step rollout to constant complexity O(1) compared to the quadratic complexity
056   of Transformers, they still rely on autoregressive planning.
057
058
      The Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022; Ajay et al., 2023) approach, a new planning method based on
      Diffusion Models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020), has recently emerged as a promising
059
      paradigm in planning. It addresses the compounding error issue by generating the entire sequence
060
      simultaneously, treating the sequence like an image, which allows for holistic sequence genera-
061   tion. This approach enables more accurate and efficient planning over long horizons. However, as
062   noted by the authors of Decision Diffuser (Ajay et al., 2023), a major limitation of diffusion-based
063   planning is that it has so far been applicable only to MDP settings. Extending this approach to
064   POMDPs—where long-context memory must be effectively integrated—remains an open challenge.
065
      In this paper, we address these limitations by conducting the first systematic investigation of long-
066
      memory, long-planning diffusion models for POMDPs. To this end, we introduce a diffusion plan-
067   ning framework called POMDiffuser, which integrates different versions of POMDiffusers based
068   on the belief encoding architecture used alongside the Diffuser planner. Specifically, we explore
069   POMDiffusers built on RNNs, Transformers, and SSMs, analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of
070   each approach, particularly in terms of achieving both long-memory and long-planning capabilities.
071   Furthermore, by encoding and conditioning on the belief representation, this framework offers a
072   natural extension of the Diffuser planner as a meta-planner.
073   Additionally, as no benchmark currently exists to evaluate long-memory and long-planning capa-
074   bilities within the Diffusion framework, we propose a new benchmark suite to fill this gap. Our
075   experimental results demonstrate that SSM-POMDiffuser performs well in tasks requiring complex
076   reasoning ability from the long and global contextual memory, in planning problems, while en-
077   joying superior computational efficiency. However, we found that it struggled with more complex
078   long-memory and long-horizon planning tasks, where the agent must remember detailed aspects of
079   the environment.
080   The contributions of this paper are: (i) the first systematic empirical investigation of long-memory,
081   long-planning diffusion models for POMDPs, (ii) the introduction of the POMDiffuser framework,
082   which for the first time extends the Diffuser planner’s capabilities to POMDPs, and (iii) the devel-
083   opment of a benchmark suite for Diffusion Planning in POMDPs.

087   2.1   S TATE S PACE M ODELS
088
089   Structured State Space Models (SSMs) are sequence-to-sequence models well-suited for tasks that
090   require significant memory retention and are particularly effective at processing long sequences due
091   to their computational efficiency. These models transform an input sequence x1:T ∈ RT ×D into an
092   output sequence y1:T ∈ RT ×D through a specific recurrence relation:
093                                         ht = At ht−1 + Bt xt ,
                                                                                                        (1)
094                                          yt = Ct ht .
095
096   At each time step t, xt and yt both belong to RD , representing the input and output at that mo-
097   ment. The hidden state ht ∈ RH captures the historical information up to time t. The matrices
098
      At ∈ RH×H , Bt ∈ RH×D , and Ct ∈ RD×H are designed to model long-range dependencies
      within the sequence efficiently. In time-invariant SSMs, where At , Bt , and Ct remain constant,
099
      y1:T can be computed in parallel from x1:T , enhancing training efficiency. At is often diagonal or
100
      block-diagonal, with its eigenvalues initialized near the unit circle to facilitate stability over long
101   sequences (Gu et al., 2020; 2022). Recent studies have explored conditioning these matrices on the
102   input sequence, allowing the model to adapt and focus on pertinent input information (Gu & Dao,
103   2024).

218   When using diffusers for planning, relying solely on observations and employing inverse dynamics
219   to deduce actions has proven to be effective (Ajay et al., 2023). This approach is particularly ben-

227   3.4   S TRATEGIES TO P LAN L ONGER
228
229
      As the planning horizon increases, the computational burden on the Diffusion model grows quadrat-
      ically. To address this issue, we introduce latent-level planning, inspired by work from other do-
230
      mains, where it has proven effective in generating and handling high-resolution datasets using VAEs
231
      (Rombach et al., 2022). We observe a similar challenge in long-horizon planning, where operating
232   directly in the observation space becomes inefficient. To mitigate this, we demonstrate the efficacy
233   of planning at the latent level using a pre-trained autoencoder, showing significant improvements in
234   computational cost.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
238
      Efficient World Models. Model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) is renowned for its sam-
239
      ple efficiency, utilizing world models for planning or policy learning in imagination trajectories
240   (Kalweit & Boedecker, 2017; Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018). Commonly, MBRL incorporates RNNs
241   (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019a;b; 2020; 2023) or Transformers (Chen et al., 2021;
242   Micheli et al., 2023; ?) as its backbone architectures. However, despite its improved sample effi-
243   ciency, MBRL’s computational inefficiency is often limited by the constraints of the backbone archi-
244   tectures. To overcome these limitations, recent advancements have introduced State Space Model-
245   based world models that enhance computational efficiency and sustain performance, especially in
246   tasks requiring long-term memory (Deng et al., 2024; Momennejad, 2024). However, despite their
247   advancements, these models still face challenges in long-horizon planning due to error accumulation
248
      in autoregressive modeling (Lambert et al., 2022; Bachmann & Nagarajan, 2024).
249   Conditioned Diffusion for Planning. Recent advances in conditional generative modeling have
250   enabled diffusion models to generate high-quality outputs based on conditions (Ho et al., 2020;
251   Saharia et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). In decision-making, these techniques guide diffusion-based
252   planners using return values, tasks, or constraints to generate trajectories (Ajay et al., 2023; Ni
253   et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). The Decision Diffuser Ajay et al. (2023) em-
254
      ploys conditional generative modeling to replace traditional value function estimation with a return-
      conditioned diffusion model. Although effective in MDPs, it lacks demonstration for long-horizon
255
      planning in POMDPs, where maintaining long-range beliefs is crucial. Additionally, the method’s
256
      quadratic increase in time complexity with contextual information makes it impractical for environ-
257   ments with extensive context requirements. MetaDiffuser (Ni et al., 2023) and AdaptDiffuser (Liang
258   et al., 2023) showed how to plan in a heterogeneous manner but did not address long planning with
259   long-term dependencies in an environment. Diffusion forcing (Chen et al., 2024) first demonstrated
260   past history-conditioned plan generation using GRU memory in POMDPs, yet it did not conclu-
261   sively address the feasibility of generating globally contextualized plans in environments requiring
262   extensive memory. To our knowledge, our work is the first to focus on integrating long context for
263   long-horizon planning.

356   5.2   S UPERIMPOSED MNIST
357
358   In the Superimposed MNIST (SMNIST) task, we investigated the impact of varying memory lengths
359   on time complexity and performance while keeping the planning horizon fixed. We designed three
360   SMNIST tasks with memory lengths of 1,568 pixels (1.5k), equivalent to the original 28x28 image
361   resolution. These tasks were augmented to 3,528 (3.5k) and 6,272 (6.2k) pixels to investigate the
362
      effects of extended memory in complex pattern recognition tasks. Each task was configured with a
      fixed planning horizon of 784 steps, equivalent to the original scale. This setup allowed us to assess
363
      the computational demands of each baseline model and evaluate how well they retain memory across
364
      numerous past observation tokens.
365
366   SMNIST 1.5k In the 1.5k SMNIST task, the POMDiffuser with Transformer demonstrated the
367   best performance, as it can directly access the context when generating trajectories. The one that
368
      utilized Mamba did not generate perfect plans.The one with RNN occasionally failed to reflect the
      global contextual information. Autoregressive Transformer baselines also failed due to compound-
369
      ing errors; although they could perfectly predict the next token during teacher forcing, they faltered
370
      at inference time. Refer to Appendix F.1 for qualitative samples.
371
372   SMNIST 3.5k and 6.2k For the more challenging 3.5k and 6.2k SMNIST tasks, we compared
373   only the RNN and Transformer memory baselines, as other baselines did not improve performance.
374   In the RNN-memory baseline, encoding contexts of length 3.5k and 6.2k was computationally too
375   slow. To address this, we reduced the input resolutions by mapping 42 × 42 and 56 × 56 images to
376   28 × 28 through uniform random sampling of indices. This approach penalized the computational
377   inefficiency of the RNN by limiting its ability to access the full contextual information. For further
      details, see Appendix B.1.

      6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
502
503   In this paper, we introduced the POMDiffuser, the first diffusion-based planning framework designed
504   for POMDPs, addressing the challenge of long-memory and long-planning in partially observable
505   environments. By integrating belief encoding architectures like RNNs, Transformers, and Structured
506   State Space Models (SSMs), our framework extends the capabilities of the Diffuser planner beyond
507   MDP settings. We analyzed the strengths and weaknesses of each approach and demonstrated that
508   POMDiffuser successfully combines long-term memory and extended planning, making it effective
509   for tackling complex tasks in partially observable environments. Additionally, we introduced a new
510   benchmark suite to evaluate diffusion models’ long-memory, long-planning capabilities, filling a
511
      critical gap in current evaluation methodologies.
512   Our results show that POMDiffuser offers a powerful and generalizable solution for planning in
513   POMDPs, and we see several future directions to improve upon this work. These include explor-
514   ing online fine-tuning of the diffusion planner, enhancing belief encoding mechanisms, and further
515   advancing meta-planning capabilities. We hope that our benchmark suite will encourage further
516   research into memory-augmented diffusion models, driving the development of more robust and
517
      efficient long-horizon planning solutions in partially observable environments.

      Dataset collection. For dataset collection in Blind Color Matching, we use a PyBullet-based motion
885
      planning algorithm to control the Kuka arm with 7 degrees of freedom (DOF) joints. We collected
886
      expert policy data, where the robot gathers environment information at the start of each episode
887   and solves the task by picking and placing a block onto a floor tile of the same color as the block.
888   The environment contains 192 unique configurations. We randomized the position of each block
889   and the exploration behavior of the expert robot. Additionally, we incorporated semantic reasoning
890   components into the environment, where blocks and floor tiles of the same color cannot be placed
891   in adjacent spots among the six hexagonal locations. This allows the agent to skip unnecessary
892   exploration by leveraging memory. For example, if 3 out of 6 floor tiles are revealed to be Blue,
893   Green, and Red, and the remaining 3 tiles are unknown, the agent can infer the positions of the
894   remaining blocks without further exploration. If the known tiles are all separated by exactly two
895
      spaces, the environment rule that prevents blocks of the same color from being adjacent allows the
      agent to deduce that the corresponding blocks must be placed on the opposite sides, eliminating the
896
      need to explore the remaining tiles.

       Our experimental results reveal notable distinctions between the POMDiffuser models employing
1000
       Structured State Space Models (SSMs) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) in their ability to
1001   maintain and utilize memory for generating consistent outputs. Specifically, the POMDiffuser (SSM)
1002   model, when presented with a sequence starting with the digit ’3’, accurately regenerates the digit
1003   ’3’. In contrast, the POMDiffuser (RNN) model, under the same conditions, erroneously produces
1004   the digit ’5’.
1005
       Despite these differences in output fidelity, the Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS)
1006
       scores, which quantify perceptual differences between images, do not exhibit significant variation
1007   between the two models. This suggests that while both models maintain a perceptual resemblance
1008   to the target digit, the SSM-based POMDiffuser demonstrates a superior capacity for updating its
1009   belief state with sufficient information to generate the appropriate class. Conversely, the RNN-based
1010   POMDiffuser appears less capable of accurately updating its belief state under the same conditions.
1011

       Vision-Based Tasks Vision-based Reinforcement Learning tasks(Mnih, 2013) often feature weak
1020   POMDPs, where the problem of partial observability is mitigated by frame stacking or using a sim-
1021   ple RNNs network. This approach, however, is not ideal for our experiments, as long-term memory
1022   is now always necessary for making optimal decisions. In contrast, benchmarks like DeepMind
1023   Lab (Beattie et al., 2016) and Memory Maze (Chen et al., 2021) present challenges that require both
1024   reward sparsity and long-term memory (Fortunato et al., 2019). . However, these environments also
1025   come with high visual complexity, complicating the direct evaluation of planner-generated trajecto-
       ries. To evaluate the model effectively, we must either measure the reward from real-environment

1036   long-term memory optional rather than essential. Robotics tasks, such as AntMaze, Pick and Place,
1037   and Block Stacking, are often based on visual observations. However, the increasing visual com-
