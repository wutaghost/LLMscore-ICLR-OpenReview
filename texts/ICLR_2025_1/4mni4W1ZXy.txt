                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               We investigate the mechanism behind emergence in large language models from
010
               the viewpoint of the regularity of the optimal response function f ∗ on the space of
011            prompt tokens. Based on theoretical justification, we provide an interpretation that
012            the derivatives of f ∗ are in general unbounded and the model gives up reasoning
013            in regions where the derivatives are large. In such regions, instead of predicting
014            f ∗ , the model predicts a smoothified version obtained via an averaging operator.
015            The threshold on the norm of derivatives for regions that are given up increases
016            together with the number of parameters N , causing emergence. The relation be-
017            tween regularity and emergence is supported by experiments on arithmetic tasks
018            such as multiplication and summation and other tasks. Our interpretation also
019
               sheds light on why fine-tuning and Chain-of-Thought can significantly improves
               LLM performance.
020

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   In large language models (LLMs), emergent abilities are skills or behaviors that manifest unexpect-
025   edly when models are scaled up in size or trained on more data. These abilities often appear without
026   being explicitly programmed, seemingly “emerging” as a result of the model’s scale and complexity.
027   They were first observed in the GPT-3 family of models (Kaplan et al., 2020), (Brown et al., 2020),
028   sparking significant interest in research. Numerous studies have since explored these abilities across
029   various tasks.
030   Emergence ability’s key feature is “unpredictability” (Wei et al., 2022a). If we plot performance as a
031   function of parameters, then at some point of scaling, performance improvement is significant, and is
032   unpredictable from its small scale behavior. Emergence is also known to be task dependent. Ganguli
033   et al. (2022) noted that “performance on a specific task can sometimes emerge quite unpredictably
034   and abruptly at scale”.
035
      Three primary factors that affect emergence ability are: computation ability, number of parameters,
036   and training dataset size (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2024). The interaction between these
037   factors seems to be complex. For instance, “emergence may occur with less training compute or
038   fewer model parameters for models trained on higher-quality data” (Wei et al., 2022a). In this paper,
039   we will focus on the role of number of parameters.
040
      The emergence of advanced abilities in AI models has sparked crucial discussions around AI safety
041
      and controllability. Ensuring the predictability of AI systems is vital for their responsible deploy-
042   ment. Understanding the conditions under which emergence happens and the mechanisms behind it
043   is a central focus of research. It offers insights into both model capabilities and limitations. Recently
044   in (Schaeffer et al., 2023), researchers argued that emergent abilities may stem from the choice of
045   evaluation metrics rather than fundamental changes in model behavior as scale increases.
046
      We propose an alternative mechanism to explain the emergence of abilities in large language models.
047
      For task with unique answers from the same pool of tokens, such as arithmetic operation on decimal
048   numbers, the complexity measured in terms of cross entropy are the same, however certain tasks are
049   less likely to exhibit emergence behavior than others, see §3.2. Instead of entropies, we believe that
050   the regularity of the optimal response function plays a key role in the emergence of these abilities
051   across different tasks. Our main argument is that LLMs tend to learn more effectively when the
052   local derivative is small, and sacrifice learning tasks where the local derivative is large for better
053   performance elsewhere. Under reasonable assumptions, our main theorem (Theorem 2.5 verifies
      mathematically that this is indeed a favorable response policy. This theoretical interpretation is then

054
      supported by experiments in §3, both with a ResNet based toy model where derivatives of the target
055   function are explicitly kept track of, as well as with LLM’s on arithmetic tasks where methods are
056   avilable to estimate the size of the derivatives.
057
058
      Methods for improving performance of LLM model commonly includes fine-tuning (Devlin et al.,
      2019; Brown et al., 2020; Raffel et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2022;
059
      Houlsby et al., 2019) and Chain-of-thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2023; Gao et al.,
060
      2023; Yao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; 2024). From our perspective, the reason behind the
061   success of these two method in improving the accuracy of models is that they both decrease the
062   norm of local derivatives of the response function. More discussions are included in §4 to address
063   the links between these methods and our theory.

071   probability measure on Y. We view X as a bounded subset inside an ambient space RdX . The
072   natural distribution of token’s in X is characterized by a probability measure µ. The neural network
073   is trained to minimize a loss functional L(f ).
074

075

140   Recall that, in the setting of natural language models, f ∗ : X → P(Y) is the optimal probabilistic
141   mapping from prompt to response. Given the complexity of inquiries behind possible prompts, the
142   responder may need to process an arbitrarily large amount of information in order to give an accurate
143
      answer. For instance, the prompt “Could you explain the mechanism of X0 and give an example?”

144

145

146   the remark above, ∥f ∗ ∥Bs becomes unbounded in this case, making Assumption 2.4 inapplicable.
147   To digest this obstacle, we interpret emergence as a natural choice of LLM when handling prompts
148   with low regularity (large derivatives).

188   3     E XPERIMENTS
189
190   We implemented several experiments to verify the interpretation offered by Theorem 2.5. As the
191   theorem is not specific to transformer-based LLM models, we first verify it in a toy model setting
192   (3.1) of fitting a trigonometric function with a family of ResNet’s whose scales get multiplied by up
193   to 215 = 32768 times from the smallest to the largest.
194
      We then test arithmetic tasks in §3.2 - §3.4) with few-shots prompt using the Qwen1.5-Chat
195
      family of models (Qwen Team, 2024) . This family is chosen as it has the widest multiplicative span
196   in terms of scale among commonly available models for our comparison purposes, ranging from
197   0.5B to 110B, a 220-fold increase. All experiments were implemented with the int4 quantization
198   of models in order to fit the largest model (110B) into an Nvidia A100 GPU while maintaining fair
199   comparison across different scales.
200
      For the arithmetic tasks, we test model performance, measure in accuracy and average error, on
201
      individual digits. The results on accuracy are included in this section and those on average error can
202   be found in Appendix C. Because (i) each prompt correspond to a unique answer, i.e. the optimal
203   answer policy has 0 cross entropy; (ii) the response for each digits ranges from 0 to 9, i.e. the
204   cross entropy loss upper bound is always log 10 via uniform random choices, different subtasks of
205   predicting a single digit as part of an arithmetic question (e.g. the 5-th digit in the product of two
206   4-digit integers) are expected to have the same complexity from the viewpoint of the cross entropy
207   loss function used by the LLM. Nevertheless, our experiments show that the difficulty levels and
208   emergence patterns vary greatly among such subtasks, which is well explained by the regularity of
209   the local derivative Df ∗ as foreseen by Theorem 2.5.

465
466   Theorem 2.5 provides theoretical ground to explain two methods of performance enhancement for
467   LLM’s: fine-tuning and chain-of-thoughts.
468
469   4.1     I NTERPRETATION OF FINE - TUNING
470
471   Fine-tuning is an important technique for improving the performance of LLM in specific domains.
472   There is extensive discussion of fine-tuning methods in the literature (Devlin et al., 2019; Brown
473   et al., 2020; Raffel et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2022; Houlsby et al.,
474   2019). The approach involves taking an LLM and further train it on a new dataset that represents
475   a special family of the task to achieve better performance on that family, sometimes leading to
476
      emergence of abilities that were not present in the original LLM trained with common data. One
      such example is Yang et al. (2023), where emergence of of arithmetic operation abilities are observed
477
      on moderately sized models (≤6B) trained with extensive data (up to 50M training samples) specific
478
      to arithmetic tasks. Following the argument in the proof of Theorem 2.5, this phenomenon could be
479   explained by approximation accuracy in terms of regularity.
480
481   Providing enough data to train exclusively on special tasks focuses the learning onto a small subset
482
      X0 ⊂ X . For difficult tasks such as multiplication, the optimal answering policy f ∗ may have very
      low regularity on X as demonstrated in §3.2. On the other hand X is tiny in size as as subset of the
483
      ambient dataset X which broadly consists of all available natural language paragraphs.
484
485   Recall that X ⊂ RdX is a compact set because of the finiteness of the token sets, and we can think of
      it as [0, 1]dX without loss of generality. In fine-tuning, the training is zoomed into a small subdomain

      4.2   I NTERPRETATION OF C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT PROMPTING
499
500   Our proposed theory also explains naturally how Chain-of-Thought (CoT) improves reasoning on
501   complex tasks. Chain-of-Thought can be viewed as the decomposition of a complex function f into
502   multiple steps f = f1 ◦ f2 ◦ · · · ◦ fd . We refer interested readers to the foundational works on Chain-
503   of-Thought (CoT), such as (Wei et al., 2022b; Wang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023;
504   Zhang et al., 2023; 2024). By the chain rule, the derivative of f becomes the product of the derivative
505   of the individual fi , and its norm is approximately the product of the norm of steps. By Theorem 2.5,
506
      given model scale N , prompts representing problems whose derivative norm are beyond a certain
      threshold D1 (N ) will be given up by the model. If ∥Df ∥ > D1 (N ), decomposition would allow
507
      the derivative norm of each component to get below this threshold and be individually learned. The
508
      experiment in §3.4 is a good example of this: the accuracy for answering the 1-step question is
509   much higher than for multiple step ones. With proper decomposition into intermediate prompts, the
510   number of needed steps in CoT is expected grow logarithmically with the expected norm of |Df ∗ |.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS , LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
514
      We propose an underlying mechanism for the emergence ability of large language models. This
515
      aligns with the well known observation that use of few-shot prompt engineering and Chain-of-
516
      Thought (CoT) reasoning can enhance model performance in downstream tasks, as well as shed
517   lights on why tasks like multiplication of multi-digit integers are difficult for LLMs. We demon-
518   strate the link between the magnitude of derivatives and emergence abilitilities in various examples,
519   and provide evidences from both theoretical and experimental perspectives.
520
      One direction left out in this work is to explore possible ways to reduce the magnitude of derivatives.
521
      As a target function is unknown, specific methods to detect where its derivatives are large, and to
522
      reduce the magnitude is easily available. In this paper, we examined specific tasks like summation,
523   multiplication, and composition of subtasks, where special task structures allowed us to estimate
524   the magnitude of derivatives. This may shed light on how to measure derivatives of more general
525   questions. While rescaling is the a natural way to reduce the magnitude of derivatives, it would be
526   interesting to extend the study to other methods. For instance, we believe variations of convolution
527   with mollifiers can be a practical method of potential interest. It would also be reasonable to further
528   investigate cut-off operators to improve the regularity of target functions.
