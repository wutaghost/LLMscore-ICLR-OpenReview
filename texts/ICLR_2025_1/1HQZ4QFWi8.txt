009                                                                                               A BSTRACT
010
011                                     Automated alignment develops alignment systems with minimal human interven-
012                                     tion. The key to automated alignment lies in providing learnable and accurate pref-
013                                     erence signals for preference learning without human annotation. In this paper,
014                                     we introduce Self-Steering Optimization (SSO), an algorithm that autonomously
015                                     generates high-quality preference signals based on predefined principles during
016                                     iterative training, eliminating the need for manual annotation. SSO maintains
017
                                        the accuracy of signals by ensuring a consistent gap between chosen and rejected
                                        responses while keeping them both on-policy to suit the current policy model’s
018
                                        learning capacity. SSO can benefit the online and offline training of the pol-
019
                                        icy model, as well as enhance the training of reward models. We validate the
020                                     effectiveness of SSO with two foundation models, Qwen2 and Llama3.1, indi-
021                                     cating that it provides accurate, on-policy preference signals throughout iterative
022                                     training. Without any manual annotation or external models, SSO leads to signif-
023                                     icant performance improvements across six subjective or objective benchmarks.
024                                     Besides, the preference data generated by SSO significantly enhanced the per-
025                                     formance of the reward model on Rewardbench. Our work presents a scalable
026                                     approach to preference optimization, paving the way for more efficient and effec-
027                                     tive automated alignment.

051   The field of Natural Language Processing has undergone revolutionary advancements driven by
052   Large Language Models (LLMs). After meticulous alignment processes, LLMs have demonstrated
053   remarkable capabilities for following instructions and understanding human preferences. This leads
      to the development of widely acclaimed products like ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023), which captured


                                                                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      significant public attention. However, aligning LLMs with human preferences is not trivial. De-
055   spite the existence of preference optimization algorithms such as Proximal Policy Optimization
056   (PPO) (Ouyang et al., 2022) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023),
057   an ideal alignment training process necessitates a robust explicit or implicit reward model. This
058   model must effectively differentiate between chosen and rejected responses and guide it to optimiz-
059   ing toward the preferred responses. Unfortunately, the reward model depends on a large amount of
060   high-quality annotated preference data and continuous updates of labeled response pairs to prevent
061   reward hacking, which is resource-intensive and requires meticulous attention. Besides, human an-
062   notators’ limited capabilities cause annotated data’s inherent limitations, making it challenging to
063
      achieve superalignment (Burns et al., 2023).
064   Consequently, recent researchers have shifted their focus towards automated alignment, intending
065   to develop scalable, high-quality alignment systems with minimal human intervention. The cor-
066   nerstone of this approach is the pursuit of scalable alignment signals that are capable of replacing
067   human-annotated preference signals effectively. Current popular strategies include: (1) Employ-
068   ing the policy model to discriminate chosen and rejected responses (Yuan et al., 2024). However,
069
      hampered by the model’s inherent limitations, this judging capability is constrained and challeng-
      ing to improve, often resulting in reward hacking and inaccurate reward signals (Wu et al., 2024).
070
      (2) Directly generating chosen and rejected responses based on predefined principles, rules, or re-
071
      quests (Yang et al., 2024b; Bai et al., 2022b; Fränken et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2024). However,
072   as illustrated in Figure 3, incorporating additional inputs or processes may lead to off-policy and
073   unsuitable outputs, blurring the accuracy of preference signals and ultimately diminishing the effec-
074   tiveness of the optimization. We then recognized the need for a novel approach to generate accu-
075   rate, learnable, and on-policy preference signals to address these limitations and advance automated
076   alignment.
077
      In this paper, we introduce Self-Steering Optimization (SSO), a pioneering method that contin-
078   uously generates automated, accurate, and learnable preference signals for the policy model. The
079   design philosophies of Self-Steering Optimization emphasize that the chosen and rejected responses,
080   along with their associated signals, should primarily be on-policy, in other words, able to extract di-
081   rectly from the policy model to suit the policy model’s learning capacity. Besides, the accuracy of
082   the synthetic signals should progressively increase or at least maintain a high level as the model
083   undergoes training. To implement these philosophies, SSO first prompts the policy model with the
084   original query and a set of contrastive principles for responses. We then optimize the model based
085   on three key objectives: a) Steer the model towards the direction of the chosen responses, which are
086
      collected by prompting the policy model with queries and good principles. b) Ensure responses are
      approximately on-policy, allowing the model to sample them even without additional principles. c)
087
      Maintain a consistent gap between the chosen and rejected responses. To summarize, as the policy
088
      model strengthens, it should become increasingly adept at generating accurate and near-on-policy
089   response pairs based on different principles, thereby enabling further optimization of the model.
090
091   We demonstrate the effectiveness of Self-Steering Optimization on Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024a)
092
      and Llama3.1 (Llama Team, 2024) backbones. Our experiments reveal SSO’s ability to generate
      accurate and learnable automated signals throughout training. As a result, continuous improve-
093
      ments are observed across a wide range of objective benchmarks such as GPQA (Rein et al., 2023),
094
      MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), MMLU Pro (Wang et al., 2024), and GSM8K (Cobbe et al.,
095   2021), as well as subjective evaluation sets like MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024b) and AlpacaEval
096   2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024). Remarkably, these improvements are achieved without any human anno-
097   tation or external models. SSO even outperforms baselines with annotated data (Cui et al., 2024),
098   underscoring its potential as a scalable and efficient approach.
099
      In addition, we obtained an offline dataset by filtering the preference data generated during the main
100
      experiments, the specific method is available in Appendix A.1.4. To verify the effectiveness of this
101   dataset, we conducted validation through offline training and reward model training, which also
102   achieved satisfying results.

      2.1   AUTOMATED A LIGNMENT
106
107   Current alignment methods, whether RLHF or DPO, sacrifice data construction to ensure perfor-
      mance, requiring a large number of annotated preference data. To address this, researchers have


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      focused on automated alignment methods that construct preference data and optimize models with-
109   out human participation. Specifically, given an instruction dataset I = {xi }N    i=1 , where N is the
110   number of instructions, we primarily focus on how to use an existing SFT model πsf t to gen-
111   erate corresponding chosen response y + and rejected response y − , forming a preference dataset
112   D = {xi , yi+ , yi− }N
                           i=1 , which will be used to align πsf t . Popular automated alignment paradigms
113   include self-reward (Yuan et al., 2024), CAI (Bai et al., 2022b), RLCD Yang et al. (2024b), etc. We
114   focus on the principle-based automated alignment paradigm represented by RLCD, as it is relatively
115   cost-effective and straightforward.
116   2.2   PRINCIPLE - BASED AUTOMATED ALIGNMENT
117
118
      Principle-based automated alignment (PBAA) is one of the most common automated alignment
      methods (Yang et al., 2024b; Fränken et al., 2024). This paradigm assumes that responses with
119
      different quality can be directly extracted from LLMs through different prompts, primarily by con-
120
      structing a pair of contrastive prompts to extract a pair of contrastive responses from the policy
121   model as training data. Since the contrastive prompts contain extremely different attributes (such as
122   harmful vs. harmless), the guided preference data has high accuracy. Representative works of PBAA
123   include RLCD (Yang et al., 2024b), AutoPM (Huang et al., 2023b) and SAIM (Fränken et al., 2024).
124   The first two use several words, such as ”inoffensive response” and ”offensive response”, to generate
125   response pairs with significant quality differences for model alignment. SAIM uses automatically
126   generated principles for preference data to fine-tune pre-trained models.
127   However, they do not guarantee learnable, on-policy, and accurate synthetic signals during iterative
128   training. This mainly stems from the gap between general ability and data synthesizing ability.
129   Firstly, it becomes increasingly difficult to generate chosen and rejected responses with sufficient
130   quality gaps during iterative training. This results in lower signal accuracy, diminishing benefits,
131   and even alignment collapse (Lee et al., 2024b; Yu et al., 2024), which is particularly pronounced
132   in small models. Secondly, although all responses are sampled from the policy model, they may
133   not fully align with the original instruction. Additional inputs, such as principles, could lead to
134   insufficient on-policy and learnable responses, which have been noted to be important in many
135
      previous studies Tajwar et al. (2024). In this paper, we propose Self-Steering Optimization to address
      these limitations.

      3.3   D ESIGN OF S ELF - STEERING LOSS G
251
252   As mentioned in formula 1, we add G for accurate signals. Therefore, G should minimize the x
253   area. A natural approach is to construct the loss by using x+ and x− as instructions, with their
254   corresponding responses as chosen responses and the other ones as rejected responses:
255
                                G = Lbase (x+ , y+ , y− ) + Lbase (x− , y− , y+ )                      (2)
256
257
      However, this design introduces a backdoor problem: with carefully crafted prompts, it becomes
      easy to manipulate LLMs to unpredictable results such as poison text. In other words, this loss may
258
      lead to a πrejected peak that is far away from πgolden , which is dangerous because our principles
259
      may be corresponding to Safety and the πgolden may indicate a safe model.
260
261   Therefore, for πrejected optimization, we shift the loss to be Lbase (x− , yo , y+ ). This goal is crucial,
262   as we want to prevent the model from using p− as a backdoor. And the final form of G is:
263                              G = Lbase (x+ , y+ , y− ) + Lbase (x− , yo , y+ )                           (3)

      4.1       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
281
282   Base Models We primarily conducted experiments on Qwen2-7B (Yang et al., 2024a) and
283   Llama3.1-8B (Llama Team, 2024). We trained Llama3.1-8B and Qwen2-7B on UltraChat (Ding
284   et al., 2023) for three epochs. Qwen2-7B-instruct and Llama3.1-8B-instruct are the official aligned
285   versions of Qwen2 and Llama3.1. Our experiments demonstrate that SSO can also benefit these
286   aligned models. Besides, we also used a stronger SFT model of Llama3.1-8B trained on Infinity
287   Instruct (BAAI, 2024) for some exploratory experiments. 1
288
      Datasets For datasets, apart from applying UltraChat to train SFT models, most of our experiments
289   are based on UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2024). This dataset includes 60k prompts, outputs from
290   several models, and preference annotations from GPT-4. We split the dataset into three portions
291   with a size ratio of 1:1:1 and only used the queries of each portion per iteration, with all responses
292   sampled from the policy model.
293
294   Training Setting We chose IPO (Azar et al., 2023) as the basic loss in most experiments and used
295
      a batch size of 128 to prevent overfitting. We applied a simple hyperparameter search to determine
      the learning rate and β parameter in IPO. We fine-tuned Qwen2-7B and Llama3.1-8B with a learning
296
      rate of 2E-5. For alignment training, the learning rate was 5E-7, and β was 0.2. The α in the W
297
      function was 0.66, and the weight of the G function was 0.1 as default. We employed generation
298   parameters of top-p=0.8, temperature=0.7, and max new tokens=2048 for sampling responses. The
299   training scripts were based on LlamaFactory(Zheng et al., 2024c).
300
301   Evaluation We evaluated the model performance on two widely used subjective evaluation bench-
302   marks: MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024b) and AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024). MT-Bench com-
303   prises 80 questions with answers scored by GPT-4. AlpacaEval 2.0 includes 805 questions, where
304
      the judge model compares answers to its reference responses. Notably, we employ the more ad-
      vanced GPT-4o as the judging model and GPT-4 as the baseline in AlpacaEval for a lower
305
      cost. Additionally, we evaluated models on a series of objective benchmarks: MATH (Hendrycks
306
      et al., 2021), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MMLU Pro (Wang et al., 2024) and GPQA (Rein et al.,
307   2023). These objective benchmarks cover various aspects, comprehensively assessing the model
308   capabilities.

      6   R ELATED W ORKS
468
      Preference Alignment with Human Preference Researchers have proposed various algorithms
469
      to align large language models (LLMs) with human preference. These algorithms can broadly be
470
      categorized into reward model-based approaches and direct preference optimization methods, with
471   RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) and DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) as representative examples. Ziegler
472   et al. (2020); Ouyang et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2022a) train a reward model based on annotated human
473   preference data and employ reinforcement learning algorithms such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017)
474   to align LLMs. However, these algorithms require numerous preference labels and online sampling
475   during the training process. To further reduce costs, direct preference optimization (DPO), sequence
476   likelihood calibration (SLiC) (Zhao et al., 2023), and identity preference optimization (IPO) (Azar
477   et al., 2023) simplify the RLHF objective by directly increasing the margin between chosen and
478   rejected responses. Additionally, Kahneman-Tversky optimization (KTO) (Ethayarajh et al., 2024)
479
      utilizes human feedback in a binary format, avoiding dependency on pairwise preference data. Our
      methodology primarily depends on direct preference optimization techniques. While we employ
480
      IPO as the foundational loss for our model, we demonstrate in Appendix A.1 the versatility of our
481
      approach, emphasizing its adaptability and broad applicability across diverse objective functions.
482
483   Automated alignment Previous alignment studies rely on manually annotated preference data
484   and algorithms like RLHF and DPO to conduct model alignment. However, annotating preference
485   data requires expensive and high-quality human effort, limiting the development of related methods.
      Moreover, with the rapid advancement of LLMs, their capabilities have gradually approached or


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      even surpassed human levels, making it challenging for humans to produce meaningful supervise
487   data for LLMs (Burns et al., 2023). Recently, numerous studies have found that data generated by
488   LLMs can reach the quality of ordinary manual annotations (Zheng et al., 2024b). These findings
489   increased the attention of automated alignment (Yuan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). Automated
490   alignment aims to minimize human intervention, addressing the prohibitively expensive cost of hu-
491   man annotation. Current methods can be divided into four types based on the source of alignment
492   signals (Cao et al., 2024): 1) Inductive Bias, which automatically guides the model to generate pref-
493   erence signals to align itself by introducing appropriate assumptions and constraints (Huang et al.,
494   2023a; Bai et al., 2022b; Yang et al., 2024b; Yuan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). 2) Behavioral Im-
495
      itation, which achieves automatic alignment by imitating the behavior of another already-aligned
      model (Peng et al., 2023; Tunstall et al., 2023; Burns et al., 2023). 3) Model Feedback, which
496
      optimizes the policy model through feedback from other models (Lee et al., 2023; Hosseini et al.,
497
      2024). 4) Environmental Feedback, which aligns models by obtaining alignment signals or feed-
498   back through environmental interaction (Liu et al., 2023; Qiao et al., 2024).
499
500
      7   C ONCLUSION
501
502   In this work, we proposed a novel approach called SSO (Self-Steering Optimization) to enhance
503   model alignment by iteratively optimizing the learnability and accuracy of generated preference
504   data. SSO achieved self-optimization through an additional self-steering loss controlling the accu-
505   racy of the preference data, as well as a weight function that regulates the data to be learnable and
506
      on-policy. These mechanisms relieve the gradual quality decline of generated signals in automated
      alignment. Our approach demonstrated effectiveness through subjective and objective benchmarks,
507
      including AlpacaEval, MT-Bench, GPQA, GSM8K, etc. Notably, our method significantly improves
508
      Llama-3.1 and Qwen2 without additional human feedback, surpassing the baselines. We further ver-
509   ified the effectiveness of SSO on offline training and RM training, demonstrating the prospects and
510   effectiveness of SSO in these areas. Verified by wide and deep experiments, SSO substantially en-
511   hanced the quality of synthetic preference data and effectively benefited model alignment. Our work
512   underscores the importance of learnable and accurate signals in automated alignment, suggesting the
513   feasibility of aligning models without human annotations.
514
515   8   L IMITATIONS
516
517   Despite SSO performing well across multiple benchmarks, we must acknowledge that there are still
518
      some limitations. Firstly, the design of the W and G functions is too simplistic. The G function is not
      specially designed but directly uses existing loss. While SSO can work with a broader range of base
519
      losses, it may also incur unnecessary computational costs, such as redundant KL Loss calculations,
520
      leading to SSO’s relatively high overhead in model optimization. Similarly, the W function directly
521   uses average generation probability, but as reported in some works Zhou et al. (2024), employing
522   more complex weight functions could yield better results. Secondly,SSO is based on principle-
523   based automated alignment. This may slightly limit its application scenarios. However, considering
524   the increasing research on automated alignment, we believe that studies like SSO will have consid-
525   erable usage.

858   ate results and selected over 50k high-quality data points. Specifically, our filtering process consisted
859   of three steps:
860
861         1. Building a pre-filtered set: We selected all data from iterations 1 and 2 synthesized by
862            all models and methods. For iteration 3, considering that methods other than SSO often
863            have lower accuracy, we only chose data produced by the SSO method. After removing
               duplicates, we obtained nearly 300k data points. We then removed data where the length

864
               difference between chosen and rejected responses exceeded 3000 characters, resulting in
865            about 226k partial pairs.
866
            2. LLM-as-judge: Based on the pre-filtered set, we conducted a round of judging using
867
               Llama3.1-8B-Instruct and Qwen2-Instruct as judges. The evaluation template was the same
868
               in A.4.2. For each pair, if any judge thought the quality of the rejected response was higher
869            than the chosen one, it was removed. This procedure left us with 110k partial pairs.
870
            3. Length filtering: Finally, we performed a round of length filtering to ensure the average
871
               lengths of chosen and rejected responses were close. We balanced the number of pairs
872
               where chosen responses were longer than rejected ones with those where chosen responses
873            were shorter and reserved one pair for each query, resulting in a filtered dataset. It is
874            worth noting that, unlike ultrafeedback, our responses have more significant length differ-
875            ences. Therefore, although we brought the average lengths of chosen and rejected responses
876            closer, this simple length control still carries a risk of verbosity.
