009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               3D object detection is an essential task for computer vision applications in au-
012            tonomous vehicles and robotics. However, models often struggle to quantify de-
013            tection reliability, leading to poor performance on unfamiliar scenes. We introduce
014            a framework for quantifying uncertainty in 3D object detection by leveraging an
015            evidential learning loss on Bird’s Eye View representations in the 3D detector.
016            These uncertainty estimates require minimal computational overhead and are gen-
017            eralizable across different architectures. We demonstrate both the efficacy and im-
018            portance of these uncertainty estimates on identifying out-of-distribution scenes,
019            poorly localized objects, and missing (false negative) detections; our framework
020
               consistently improves over baselines by 10-20% on average. Finally, we integrate
               this suite of tasks into a system where a 3D object detector auto-labels driving
021
               scenes and our uncertainty estimates verify label correctness before the labels are
022
               used to train a second model. Here, our uncertainty-driven verification results in a
023            1% improvement in mAP and a 1-2% improvement in NDS.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Detecting 3D objects from LiDAR and multiple camera images is crucial for autonomous driving.
030   Recent techniques mostly rely on bird’s eye view (BEV) representations (Zhou & Tuzel, 2018; Lang
031   et al., 2019; Philion & Fidler, 2020; Yin et al., 2021), where information from the different sensors
032   is fused to generate a consistent representation within the ego-vehicle’s coordinate system. BEV
033
      effectively captures the relative position and size of objects, making it well-suited for perception
      and planning (Ng et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023).
034
035   Deep neural networks are excellent at performing detections but assessing their reliability remains
036   a challenge. Sampling-based uncertainty estimation methods, like MC-Dropout (Gal & Ghahra-
037   mani, 2016) and Deep Ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017), are among the most common
038   approaches used for this purpose. MC-Dropout works by randomly deactivating network weights
039
      and observing the impact, while Deep Ensembles involve training several networks with different
      initializations. Although intuitive, these methods typically require a multiplier on the nominal com-
040
      pute, memory, or training costs of the neural network. Consequently, these methods are inviable for
041
      large-scale applications including the 3D detection systems used for autonomous vehicles. More-
042   over for common tasks such as classification or regression, sampling-free uncertainty estimation
043   methods are easier to implement and deploy (Wen et al., 2020; Durasov et al., 2021; 2024; Laurent
044   et al., 2023; Ashukha et al., 2020; Durasov et al., 2022b). In this space, Evidential Deep Learning
045   (EDL) has recently emerged as a promising alternative for providing high-quality epistemic uncer-
046   tainty estimates at low computational overhead (Sensoy et al., 2018; Amini et al., 2020).
047
      In this paper, we introduce a computationally efficient uncertainty estimation framework for 3D ob-
048   ject detection motivated by EDL. The 3D detection task differs from classical uncertainty estimation
049   applications due to the multi-faceted nature of detection uncertainty in class label and localization,
050   as well as the variety of representations of the data. Consequently, we propose a generic approach
051   to measure the uncertainty of estimates in each cell of the BEV representation. We transform the
052   BEV heatmap head found in modern 3D detection models to an EDL-based head and employ a
053   regularized training loss to learn to generate these uncertainties. By aggregating these BEV-level
      uncertainty estimates, we can simultaneously predict objectness probabilities and uncertainties as-

095   2.1   3D O BJECT D ETECTION
096
097   3D object detection has gained significant attention in recent years driven by advances in deep learn-
098   ing and large-scale datasets. This task is broadly classified into three main approaches (Chen et al.,
099
      2023): camera-based, LiDAR-based, and multi-modal approaches. Recent camera-based methods
      predict 3D object from multi-view camera images (Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
100
      2022). This method aggregates features from multiple camera views to construct a comprehensive
101
      understanding of geometry. LiDAR-based methods estimate 3D objects in given point clouds. This
102   method projects point clouds onto a regular grid such as pillars (Lang et al., 2019), voxels (Zhou
103   & Tuzel, 2018), or range images (Fan et al., 2021) because of irregular nature of point clouds, and
104   then deep model are used to get features for object detection. Recent advancements have explored
105   the integration of camera with LiDAR to further enhance 3D detection capabilities (Bai et al., 2022;
106   Liang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021). This multi-modal strategy enables the model to leverage the
107   complementary strengths of each sensor, yielding improved detection accuracy over single modal-
      ity methods (Bai et al., 2022). However, despite their development and effectiveness, these models

      struggle to adequately assess their own confidence in the predictions they make. It therefore does
109   not know how uncertainty or reliable of estimation is.

112   2.2   U NCERTAINTY E STIMATION
113
114   Uncertainty estimation is the study of quantifying the reliability of a model’s predictions. Most un-
115   certainty estimation methods must balance the quality of estimated uncertainties with the computa-
116   tional cost of generating such estimates. Deep Ensembles, which train multiple neural networks with
117   different initializations, provide more reliable uncertainty estimates than most alternatives (Laksh-
118   minarayanan et al., 2017; Ovadia et al., 2019; Antorán et al., 2020; Gustafsson et al., 2020; Ashukha
119   et al., 2020; Daxberger et al., 2021; Postels et al., 2022). However, their high compute and memory
120   demands make them impractical for large-scale 3D detection networks. MC-Dropout is a widely
121
      used method for generating uncertainty estimates with low computational cost by randomly de-
      activating network weights (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016). However, its estimates are generally less
122
      reliable than those of Deep Ensembles (Ashukha et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2020; Durasov et al.,
123
      2021). In between these two approaches lie a broad family of Bayesian Networks (Mackay, 1995)
124   that achieve varying performances by trading off compute (Blundell et al., 2015; Graves, 2011;
125   Hernández-Lobato & Adams, 2015; Kingma et al., 2015). Nonetheless, most of these uncertainty
126   estimation methods require multiple forward passes during inference, making them impractical for
127   fast inference. As a result, sampling-free single-pass approaches will modify the network archi-
128   tecture or training process to generate fast estimates, albeit for task-specific settings (Choi et al.,
129   2021; Postels et al., 2019; Malinin & Gales, 2018; Amersfoort et al., 2020; Malinin & Gales, 2018;
130   Mukhoti et al., 2021; Durasov et al., 2022a; Ashukha et al., 2020).
131   EDL is an increasingly popular single-pass paradigm for uncertainty estimation that use a single
132   neural network to estimate a meta-distribution representing the uncertainty over the predicted dis-
133   tribution (Sensoy et al., 2018; Amini et al., 2020; Ulmer et al., 2021). EDL methods are more
134   computationally efficient than most Bayesian and ensemble methods, while requiring only minor
135   architectural changes to the network. Most importantly, these methods have demonstrated strong
136   performance in a variety of applications involving uncertainty quantification and in detecting out-
137   of-distribution (OOD) or novel data (Bao et al., 2021; Aguilar et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023).
138
139
140   3     M ETHOD
141
142   EDL adapts the model architecture and loss function of a nominal learning problem to generate un-
143   certainty estimates along with predictions. Rather than class probabilities, a model designed for EDL
144   outputs parameters for a distribution over these probabilities, referred to as a second-order distribu-
145   tion. Below, we first summarize this framework for a nominal problem of multi-label classification.
146   We then extend this framework for 3D object detection.
147

187   3.2   E VIDENTIAL L EARNING FOR 3D D ETECTION
188
      While equation 2 is effective for simple tasks such as image classification (Sensoy et al., 2018)
189
      and image-based action recognition (Bao et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023), it is not immediately
190
      effective for detection tasks. Specifically, in 3D object detection, there are a variable number of
191   detections consisting of a class prediction and object bounding box coordinates. This necessitates
192   an uncertainty estimation method that can capture both class uncertainty (i.e., what type of object
193   is detected) and location uncertainty (i.e., where the object is located). Moreover, the inherent
194   imbalance towards the negative class can heavily bias uncertainty estimators, as most detections
195   correspond to background, leading to underconfidence in positive detections.
196
      To overcome these challenges, we propose to capture uncertainty at the Bird’s Eye View (BEV) level
197   (Zhou et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023), which allows us to simultaneously account
198   for both the object’s 3D position and class. Additionally, we adapt a customized EDL loss function
199   that mitigates the negative class imbalance.
200
      Model architecture. In 3D object detection, a dedicated heatmap head can predict the probabilities
201
      of object centers from a BEV representation of scene (Chen et al., 2023). In our approach, we replace
202
      the standard heatmap head with an EDL head, as illustrated in Fig. 2. Specifically, we follow the
203   multiclass EDL model setup discussed in Sec. 3.1. Instead of predicting C probability values for
204   each BEV cell, we now predict αi and βi , enabling the model to estimate not only the probability of
205   an object’s presence but also the uncertainty associated with the prediction. Technically, we double
206   the number of output dimensions, treating the first C as αi and the second C as βi .
207
      Loss function for 3D. Common loss functions for EDL, as discussed in Section 3.1, are not directly
208
      applicable to object detection due to high class imbalance. In 3D object detection, especially for
209   center-based methods, specialized techniques like Gaussian Focal Loss (GFL) are often employed
210   to address these imbalances (Law & Deng, 2018; Zhou et al., 2019). GFL accounts for areas near
211   object centers using a Gaussian-distributed ground truth heatmap, enhancing localization precision.
212   For further details on GFL and related methods, we refer the reader to Appendix A.1.

253   the penalty is much larger. This encourages the model to be more precise in localizing object centers
254   while tolerating small errors near the true center.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
281
282   We demonstrate the value of EDL-based uncertainty estimates across three different downstream
283   applications: (i) detecting out-of-distribution scenes; (ii) estimating the localization quality of pre-
284
      dicted bounding boxes; and (iii) identifying missing detections in a scene. Our estimated uncertain-
      ties consistently yield better downstream performance than existing baselines. Finally, we integrate
285
      these tasks into an auto-labeling pipeline where estimated pseudo-labels from a 3D object detector
286
      are flagged and verified accordingly to uncertainty scores. These “corrected” pseudo-labels are used
287   to train a downstream detector that achieves improved performance over baselines that use unverified
288   pseudo-labels for training.
289
290
291   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
292
293   Dataset and metric. We evaluate our approach on the nuScenes and Waymo 3D detection datasets.
294   The nuScenes Dataset (Caesar et al., 2020) is a comprehensive large-scale driving dataset with 1,000
295   scenes of multi-modal data, including 32-beam LiDAR at 20 FPS and images from six different cam-
296   era views. We explore two settings: LiDAR-only and LiDAR-Camera fusion. We evaluate detection
297   performance on mean average precision (mAP) and the nuScenes detection score (NDS), defined by
298   averaging the matching thresholds of center distance D = {0.5, 1., 2., 4.} meters.
299
      The Waymo Open Dataset (Sun et al., 2020) includes 798 scenes for training and 202 scenes for
300   validation. The nuScenes and Waymo Open datasets were recorded in different locations over dif-
301   ferent vehicles. They differ significantly in terms of detection range, scene composition, and sensor
302   configuration, making the Waymo dataset a suitable choice for out-of-distribution (OOD) testing.
303
      3D Detection Baselines. We use two 3D detection models for the nuScenes dataset: 1) Focal-
304
      Former3D (Chen et al., 2023), a state-of-the-art architecture for both Lidar and Lidar + Camera
305
      configurations, and 2) DeformFormer3D, a Lidar-based architecture built upon DETR (Carion et al.,
306   2020) and Deformable DETR (Zhu et al., 2021). In our experiments, we refer to FocalFormer3D
307   Lidar experiments as FF (L), FocalFormer3D Lidar+Camera experiments as FF (L+C), and De-
308   formFormer3D Lidar experiments as DF (L).
309
      Implementation details. Our implementation is built on the open-source MMDetection3D code-
310
      base (Contributors, 2020). For the LiDAR backbone, we use CenterPoint-Voxel as the feature extrac-
311
      tor for point clouds. The multi-stage heatmap encoder operates in 3 stages, producing 600 queries
312   by default. Data augmentation techniques include random double flips along the X and Y axes,
313   random global rotation within the range of [−π/4, π/4], scaling randomly between [0.9, 1.1], and
314   random translations with a standard deviation of 0.5 across all axes. Training is conducted with a
315   batch size of 16 across eight V100 GPUs. Similar to Amini et al. (2020), we set the regularization
316   parameter λ in the loss function to 10−4 to prevent over-regularization of the model.
317
      Uncertainty Baselines. We compare against the standard Entropy (Malinin & Gales, 2018) baseline,
318   along with recent sample-based approaches: MC-Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) (MC-D), Deep
319   Ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) (DeepE), BatchEnsemble (Wen et al., 2020) (BatchE),
320   Masksembles (Durasov et al., 2021) (MaskE), and Packed-Ensembles (Laurent et al., 2023) (PackE),
321   which are known for producing state-of-the-art uncertainty estimates (Ashukha et al., 2020; Postels
322   et al., 2022). For all four sampling-based methods, we use five samples to estimate uncertainty at
323   inference time, a setting shown to work well across multiple tasks (Durasov et al., 2021; Wen et al.,
      2020; Malinin & Gales, 2018).

370    model trained on one dataset is applied to another. We report results in terms of ROC and PR curves

372    Our approach significantly outperforms other methods in detecting OOD scenes when using Focal-

374    procedure for BEV embedding generation, which produces richer and more detailed feature repre-
375    sentations. In contrast, DeformFormer uses a single-stage process, limiting its ability to fully refine
376    spatial and object-level features, which affects the performance of most uncertainty baselines. This
377    could explain its slightly lower performance, as its simpler approach leads to less detailed BEV
       embeddings.

       Uncertainty estimates produced via our framework can be used to predict whether the model will
414    generate poorly localized bounding boxes. To classify a poorly localized bounding box, we calculate
415    the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) between predicted and ground truth boxes; we denote a poorly
416    localized “erroneous” box if the IoU is below an arbitrary threshold τ ∈ [0, 1], and “accurate”
417    otherwise. We set τ = 0.3, which ensures that boxes classified as erroneous exhibit sufficiently
418    low overlap with the ground truth, indicating poor localization accuracy. We then transform the
419    task of detecting erroneous boxes into a binary classification based on the overall uncertainty in
420    the predicted box, allowing us to compute standard ROC and PR curves (and their corresponding
421    AUCs).
422    To assign an uncertainty value to each predicted box, we follow a consistent procedure across
423    all uncertainty methods. After generating an uncertainty map for each BEV cell and class, i.e.,
424    û ∈ RC×H×D where C is the number of classes, and H and D are the BEV height and depth,
425    respectively. For any predicted bounding box, we then collect the set of uncertainty values within
426    the given box {ûib } (see Fig. 3 (b)), where i corresponds to the class index and b corresponds to the
427    cell. Finally, we aggregate these estimates into a single uncertainty score for the predicted bounding
428
       box ub = mini ûib .
429    We use the model trained on the nuScenes training set and run inference on the test set. After
430    generating predicted boxes for all scenes in the test set, we compute the uncertainty and IoU for
431    each box, followed by the AUC metrics as described earlier. The results are reported in Fig. 5 and
       Tab. 2. Our approach consistently outperforms other uncertainty baselines by 5 − 10%.

      4.4   I DENTIFYING U NDETECTED O BJECTS
433
434   We also focus on recall, which can be reframed as minimizing the number of false negative detec-
435   tions, and is an important metric for a 3D detector suitable for deployment in autonomous vehicles.
436   As discussed in Sec. 3.2, modern 3D object detection models typically include a stage where the
437   model estimates the probability of an object being in a specific BEV cell. If a cell has a low pre-
438   dicted probability, it is less likely that any bounding box will be generated with its center in that
439   BEV cell. This means that if the cell actually contains a ground truth object, it will not be detected,
440
      resulting in a false negative. In some cases, our heatmap may assign low probabilities to locations
      where there is an actual object, but this is often accompanied by higher uncertainty in the prediction.
441
      This indicates the model’s uncertainty in identifying objects in certain areas. As a result, we are mo-
442
      tivated to leverage these predicted uncertainties to identify potentially missed objects and improve
443   detection performance in such challenging scenarios.
444
445   To address this, we implemented a separate head Mmiss that processes the BEV embeddings ei ,
446
      predicted probabilities pi , and uncertainty ui from our EDL head for each BEV cell, using the
      concatenated vector [ei , pi , ui ] to estimate the confidence of potentially missed objects in the given
447
      cells as pmiss
                 i   = Mmiss ([ei , pi , ui ]). Technically, we only consider BEV cells where the heatmap-
448
      ping head produces low probabilities (pi less than 5% in our experiments) as candidates for locations
449   where objects could have been missed. This threshold was chosen because cells with such low prob-
450   abilities are excluded from the second stage, and no bounding boxes are generated for them in the
451   final predictions. By focusing on these ignored cells, we aim to identify potential false negatives
452   that would otherwise go undetected by the model. The Mmiss head is trained using the same targets,
453   loss, and training procedure as the original heatmap head, with the only difference being that it is
454   trained on cells with low probability pi and uses pi and ui in addition to ei as input.
455   As in Sec. 4.3, we use the model trained on the nuScenes training set and run inference on the test set.
456   To evaluate the quality of the newly detected locations with potential missed objects, we followed
457   this evaluation procedure: First, we generated the final bounding box predictions and compared
458   them against the ground truth boxes from the test set to identify the missed ground truth boxes.
459   Next, using our new probability head, we predicted pmiss i    for each BEV cell and identified 15 new
460   locations with potential missed objects (this number was chosen to balance precision and recall),
461   selecting the locations with the highest pmiss
                                                 i   scores. If a missed ground truth box was found within
462   a radius of d meters (d = 2 and d = 4 in our experiments) from the predicted location, it was
463
      considered a true positive detection. By treating this as a classification task, we calculated precision,
      recall, and F1-score for these detections, and the results are reported in Tab. 3. As in the previous
464
      tasks, our method significantly outperformed other approaches.

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529   We have introduced an Evidential Deep Learning framework for uncertainty estimation in 3D object
530   detection using Bird’s Eye View representations. Our method improves out-of-distribution detec-
531   tion, bounding box quality, and missed object identification, all while being computationally effi-
532   cient. By incorporating uncertainty into the auto-labeling pipeline, we achieve significant gains in
533   mAP and NDS on the nuScenes dataset, which also leads to enhanced auto-label quality.
534   One limitation of our current approach is that we only update the head of the model. Expanding
535   to end-to-end learning could potentially yield better results by enabling the model to learn uncer-
536   tainty more holistically across all layers. Despite this, our method remains computationally efficient,
537   offering a practical alternative to deep ensembles, and is well-suited for large-scale, real-time appli-
538   cations like autonomous driving. The ability to integrate seamlessly into an auto-labeling pipeline
539   highlights its practical utility, reducing the cost and effort of manual labeling while improving data
      quality in scenarios where high-quality annotations are critical.

      A.1    O BJECT D ETECTION L OSSES
759
760   Focal Loss. Focal Loss (Lin et al., 2017) is designed to address class imbalance by down-weighting
761   well-classified examples and focusing on harder, misclassified examples. The Focal Loss function
762   is defined as:
763                                        LFL (p) = −(1 − p)γ log(p),
764   where p is the model’s estimated probability for the correct class, and γ is a tunable parameter that
765   controls the focus on harder examples. When γ = 0, Focal Loss reduces to standard cross-entropy
766   loss. As γ increases, the loss function places greater emphasis on difficult examples, which is useful
767   in contexts like object detection where class imbalance is common. This is particularly effective
768   when most examples are easy negatives (e.g., background) and would otherwise dominate the loss.
769   Gaussian Focal Loss. Gaussian Focal Loss (GFL) (Law & Deng, 2018; Zhou et al., 2019) is a
770   variant of Focal Loss specifically designed for 3D object detection. In this approach, object centers
771   are represented using a Gaussian heatmap, and the loss function focuses on the points near the center
772   of the object. The Gaussian Focal Loss function is given by:
773
                                                 LGFL = −(1 − ŷ)η log(p),
774
775   where ŷ is the Gaussian-distributed ground truth centered around the object, and p is the predicted
776   probability. The term (1 − ŷ)η down-weights points far from the object’s center, ensuring that
777   the model focuses on improving localization precision for points near the center. The parameter η
778
      controls the degree to which points further from the center are down-weighted.
