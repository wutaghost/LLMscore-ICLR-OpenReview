009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                A primary concern regarding training large language models (LLMs) is whether
012                they abuse copyrighted online text. With the increasing training data scale and
013                the prevalence of LLMs in daily lives, two problems arise: 1) false positive mem-
014                bership inference results misled by similar examples; 2) membership inference
015                methods are usually too complex for general users to understand and use. To
016                address these issues, we propose an alternative insert-and-detect methodology,
017                advocating that web users and content platforms employ unique identifiers for
018                reliable and independent membership inference. Users and platforms can create
019
                   their identifiers, embed them in copyrighted text, and independently detect them
                   in future LLMs. As an initial demonstration, we introduce ghost sentences and
020
                   a user-friendly last-k words test, allowing general users to chat with LLMs for
021
                   membership inference. Ghost sentences consist primarily of unique passphrases
022                of random natural words, which can come with customized elements to bypass
023                possible filter rules. The last-k words test requires a significant repetition time
024                of ghost sentences (≥ 10). For cases with fewer repetitions, we designed an ex-
025                tra perplexity test, as LLMs exhibit high perplexity when encountering unnatural
026                passphrases. We also conduct a comprehensive study on the memorization and
027                membership inference of ghost sentences, examining factors such as training data
028                scales, model sizes, repetition times, insertion positions, wordlist of passphrases,
029                alignment, etc. Our study shows the possibility of applying ghost sentences in real
030
                   scenarios and providing instructions for the potential application.

033   1       I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) are pre-trained on vast amounts of data sourced from the Internet,
036   while the providers of commercial LLMs like ChatGPT, Bard, and Claude do not disclose the de-
037   tails of the training data. This raises concerns that LLMs may be trained with copyrighted material
038
      without permission from the creators (Karamolegkou et al., 2023; Henderson et al., 2023; Li et al.,
      2024). Some efforts have been made to determine whether a specific example is included in the
039
      training data (Mattern et al., 2023; Meeus et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). However, the
040
      false positive membership inference results caused by similar examples are common (Duan et al.,
041   2024). Service providers might argue that detection results could be confused by similar exam-
042   ples in massive data rather than the exact copyrighted content (OpenAI, 2019). Additionally, these
043   membership inference methods are often too complex for general users without coding experience
044   or expert knowledge. This complexity could lead to centralized detection services, which reduces
045   transparency and raises concerns about trustworthiness.
046
      For transparent and reliable protection of copyrighted material1 , we propose an alternative insert-
047   and-detect methodology for general web users and content platforms (e.g., Quora, Medium, Reddit,
048   GitHub). We advocate that web users and content platforms insert unique identifiers into copy-
049   righted content. These identifiers help address the issue of false positives caused by similar ex-
050   amples (OpenAI, 2019; Duan et al., 2024), providing definitive evidence for copyright protection.
051   The process should be transparent, allowing users and content platforms to create unique identifiers,
052   embed them in online copyrighted material, and perform detection independently.

066   To demonstrate the concept, we introduce ghost sentences as a primitive implementation of unique
067   identifiers, as well as a user-friendly last-k words test for their membership inference. A ghost sen-
068   tence is distinctive because it primarily consists of a randomly generated diceware passphrase (Rein-
069   hold, 1995). As shown in Figure 1, users or content platforms can insert a ghost sentence, along
070
      with customized elements, into various online documents. When the repetition of ghost sentences
      increases, LLMs are likely to achieve verbatim memorization (Carlini et al., 2019; 2021; Ishihara,
071
      2023; Karamolegkou et al., 2023) of the passphrases in ghost sentences. In this case, users can
072
      prompt LLMs to complete the last k words of a ghost sentence, using the preceding context, as
073   shown in Figure Figure 1. For example, the last-k word test can be performed on ChatGPT using
074   content from popular books, as demonstrated in Figure 5. Due to the randomness of passphrases, it is
075   statistically guaranteed that if an LLM can complete the last k ≥ 1 words, it must have been trained
076   with the ghost sentence. In experiments with an OpenLLaMA-3B (Geng & Liu, 2023) model, 11 out
077   16 users successfully identify their data from the LLM generation. These 16 users have 24 examples
078   with ghost sentences on average and contribute 383 examples to a total of 1.8M training documents.
079   Ghost sentences account for only 0.0017% of all training tokens.
080   The last k words test is user-friendly but requires a non-trivial repetition time of ghost sen-
081   tences (≥ 10). Following previous membership inference methods based on loss, entropy, or proba-
082   bility of predictions (Yeom et al., 2018; Carlini et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2024), we design an alternative
083   perplexity test for less frequently repeated ghost sentences. An LLM trained with natural languages
084   should exhibit high perplexity for the passphrase in a ghost sentence, as it consists of random words.
085   During the perplexity test, users can generate a new set of ghost sentences, obtain the perplexity
086   distribution, and use the distribution to perform a hypothesis test for membership inference. For a
087
      LLaMA-13B model (Touvron et al., 2023a), a perplexity test for 30 ghost sentences, with an average
      repetition of 7 in 148K examples, achieves a 0.891 ROC AUC.
088
089   We also comprehensively study different factors influencing the memorization and membership in-
090   ference results of ghost sentences. A few key observations are as follows: 1) The memorization of
091   ghost sentences is jointly decided by their quantity and average repetition. Ghost sentences with a
092   word length ≥ 8, an average repetition ≥ 5, and a proportion ≥ 0.0016% of training tokens are
093
      recommended. 2) It is better to insert ghost sentences in the latter half of a document. 3) A curated
      wordlist for the generation of passphrases is necessary. We suggest using a well-maintained wordlist
094
      from the Electronic Frontier Foundation. 4) Further model alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov
095
      et al., 2023) will not affect the memorization of ghost sentences. 5) The larger the model, the smaller
096   the repetition times for memorization. This is consistent with previous works (Carlini et al., 2023).
097   Larger learning rates and more training epochs produce similar effects.
098
099
      A single pattern of unique identifiers is insufficient, as it may eventually be filtered out, despite the
      significant cost of filtering hidden sentences from terabytes or even petabytes of data. As LLMs
100
      become increasingly popular in daily lives, there is a growing need for diverse unique identifiers and
101
      user-friendly test methods. Different copyright identifiers are not mutually exclusive and can work
102   together to make filtering intractable. Wei et al. (2024) adopt random characters, which also qualify
103   as unique identifiers. Nevertheless, relying solely on long random characters risks filter through
104   measures like regular expression matching and semantic checking. Additionally, random characters,
105   such as auto-generated metadata, are prevalent in large-scale datasets (Elazar et al., 2024), which
106   can lead to false detection issues (Duan et al., 2024). They also lack a user-friendly membership
107   inference method for general users. We hope ghost sentences can serve as a starting point for creating
      diverse unique identifiers and user-friendly membership inference methods.

      Membership Inference Attack This type of attack aims to determine whether a data record is
111   utilized to train a model (Fredrikson et al., 2015; Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022). Typically,
112   membership inference attacks (MIA) involve observing and manipulating confidence scores or loss
113   of the model (Yeom et al., 2018; Song & Shmatikov, 2019; Mattern et al., 2023), as well as training
114   an attack model (Shokri et al., 2017; Hisamoto et al., 2020). Duan et al. (Duan et al., 2024) conduct
115   a large-scale evaluation of MIAs over a suite of LLMs trained on the Pile (Gao et al., 2020) dataset
116   and find MIAs barely outperform random guessing. They attribute this to the large scale of training
117   data, few training iterations, and high similarity between members and non-members. Shi et al. (Shi
118   et al., 2024) utilize wiki data created after LLMs training to distinguish the members and non-
119
      members. Nevertheless, the concern that similar examples in the large-scale training data may lead
      to ambiguous inference results remains.
120
121   Machine-Generated Text Detection Text watermark (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024;
122   Liu et al., 2024; 2023; Ding et al., 2024) aims to embed signals into machine-generated text that are
123   invisible to humans but algorithmically detectable. Generally, LLMs are required not to generate
124   tokens from a red list. During detection, we can detect the watermark by testing the null hypothesis
125
      that the text is generated without knowledge of the red list. The unique identifier in copyrighted text
      is a kind of text watermark for the training data, and LLMs should not produce such unique identi-
126
      fiers during generation. A few other methods (Mitchell et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2024; Mireshghallah
127
      et al., 2024) try to detect machine-generated text without modifying the generation content. They
128   are mainly based on the assumption that the patterns of log probabilities of human-written and
129   machine-generate text have distinguishable discrepancies.
130
131
      Training Data Extraction Attack The substantial number of neurons in LLMs enables them to
      memorize and output part of the training data verbatim (Carlini et al., 2023; Ishihara, 2023; Zhang
132
      et al., 2023). Adversaries exploit this capability of LLMs to extract training data from pre-trained
133
      LLMs (Carlini et al., 2021; Nasr et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Kudugunta et al., 2023). This attack
134   typically consists of two steps: candidate generation and membership inference. The adversary first
135   generates numerous texts from a pre-trained LLM and then predicts whether these texts are used
136   to train the LLM. Carlini et al. (Carlini et al., 2023) quantify the memorization capacity of LLMs,
137   discovering that memorization grows with the model capacity and the duplicated times of training
138   examples. Specifically, within a model family, larger models memorize 2 − 5× more than smaller
139   models, and repeated strings are memorized more. Karamolegkou et al. (Karamolegkou et al., 2023)
140   demonstrate that LLMs can achieve verbatim memorization of literary works and educational mate-
141   rial. We also provide an similar example in Figure 5 in §App. C.

161   hold, 1995). Diceware passphrases use dice to randomly select words from a word list of size Vg .
      Vg is generally equal to 65 = 7776, which corresponds to rolling a six-sided dice 5 times. For a

      diceware passphrase with length q, there are 7776q possibilities, ensuring the uniqueness of a ghost
163   sentence when q ≥ 4, which is much larger than the number of indexed webpages estimated by
164   worldwidewebsize.com (at least 2.37 billion indexed pages, October, 2024). The words in a dice-
165   ware passphrase have no linguistic relationship as they are randomly selected and combined. Users
166   can customize ghost sentences by add prefixes to passphrases as shown in Figure 1. It is recom-

168   document. We provide a few examples of ghost sentences in §App. G.

247
      of natural language. Given an LLM, a ghost sentence g, and a context c, we can use the empiri-
248
      cal perplexity distribution of ghost sentences (unseen by the LLM) to perform a hypothesis test. If
249   PPL(g) is smaller than the critical value at a certain significant level, we will reject the null hypoth-

253   148K examples with 30 ghost sentences repeat 5 times on average, a perplexity test can achieve
254   0.393 recall with a significant level 0.05 after 1 epoch fine-tuning. The recall increases to 0.671 if
255   the average repetition becomes 7.

      3.4              L IMITATIONS
259
260   As a primitive design of unique identifiers for demonstration, ghost sentences offer both advantages
261
      and limitations. They are transparent, user-friendly, and statistically trustworthy. However, due to
      their transparency, they may be filtered out with specific measures, such as training a classifier on
262
      human-labeled ghost sentences. This approach, though, is costly and may result in many false pos-
263
      itives due to diverse custom elements as shown in Figure 1. Very long ghost sentences also suffer
264   from exact substring deduplication (Lee et al., 2022), which uses a threshold of 50 tokens. There-
265   fore, we recommend using a passphrase of around 10 words, which is 22 tokens on average for BPE
266   tokenizer (Sennrich et al., 2015). Actually, service providers do not adopt a strict deduplication pro-
267   cess, as verbatim memorization of popular books can still be found (Karamolegkou et al., 2023) (or
268   Figure 5). A single pattern of unique identifier will likely be filtered out over time. We hope that
269   ghost sentences can be a starting point for the diverse designs of unique identifiers and user-friendly
      membership inference methods.

274   In this work, we consider inserting ghost sentences at the pre-training stage and instruction tun-

      Models For instruction tuning, we adopt the LLaMA serires (Touvron et al., 2023a), including
278   OpenLLaMA-3B (Geng & Liu, 2023), LLaMA-7B, and LLaMA-13B. For pre-training, considering
279   the prohibitive computation cost, we conduct continual pre-training of a TinyLlama-1.1B model
280   at 50K steps(TinyLlama/TinyLlama-1.1B-step-50K-105b), 3.49% of its total 1431K training steps.
281   The context length of all models is restricted to 512. The batch size for instruction tuning is 128
282   examples following previous works (Taori et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). We maintain the pre-training
283   batch size the same as TinyLlama-1.1B — 1024 examples. A large batch size is achieved with
284   gradient accumulation on 4 NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs.
285   Training Epochs and Learning Rate All models are only trained for 1 epoch. Actually, training
286   epochs of LLaMA range from 0.64 ∼ 2.45 for different data. As for the learning rate, we keep
287   consistent with LLaMA or TinyLlama with a linear scaling strategy. Specifically, our learning rate
                    our batch size
288   is equal to original batch size × original learning rate. LLaMA-7B uses a batch of 4M tokens with a 3e-4
289   learning rate, so our learning rate for instruction tuning is 3e-4 × 128×512
                                                                                 4×220 ≈ 4.6e-6. TinyLlama
290   uses learning ate 4e-4, batch size 1024, and context length 2048, so our learning rate for continuing
291   pre-training is 1e-4. By default, the optimizer is AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) with a cosine
292   learning rate schedule. All models are trained with mixed precision and utilize FlashAttention (Dao
293   et al., 2022; Dao, 2023) to increase throughput.
294
      Dataset Webis-TLDR-17 (Völske et al., 2017) contains 3.7M examples with word lengths under
295   4096. Without mention, we use a subset of Webis-TLDR-17 for instruction tuning, which contains
296   148K examples and 8192 users with the numbe of documents falls in [10, 200]. We term this subset
297   as Webis-148K for convenient. For instruction tuning on Webis-148K, LLMs are required to finish
298   a continue writing task using the instruction "Continue writing the given content".
299   The input and output for the instruction correspond to the first and second halves of the user doc-
300   ument. For continuing pre-training, we also utilize the LaMini-Instruction (Wu et al., 2023) and
301   OpenOrca (Longpre et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Lian et al., 2023) datasets, which contain

      examples is 9.8M. All data are shuffled during training.

306   test. For last-k words test, we ask LLMs to generate the last-k words of ghost sentences by providing

      4.3     L AST-k W ORDS T EST
345
346   In this section, we will figure out the conditions under which LLMs can achieve verbatim memo-
347   rization of ghost sentences for the last-k words test. We randomly select m users from all training
348   examples to insert ghost sentences. Each user has a unique ghost sentence, and the average repetition
349   times of ghost sentences is µ. A few key observations:
350
351
              • When µ ≥ 10, ghost sentences with a word length of ∼10 are likely to be memorized by an
                OpenLLaMA-3B model fine-tuned on Webis-148k. As the scale of training data increases,
352
                the memorization requires larger m × µ. In most cases, we observe that a proportion of
353
                ghost sentence tokens to all tokens ≥ 0.0016% is necessary (§4.3.1).
354
355
              • The success rate of memorization is jointly determined by m and µ. Notably, µ is more
                critical than m. A ghost sentence with a small µ can become memorable with an increase
356
                in the number of different ghost sentences m (§4.3.1).
357
358           • It is better to insert ghost sentences in the latter half of a document. The insertion of ghost
359
                sentences will not affect the linguistic performance of LLMs (§4.3.2, §App. E).
360           • Further alignment will not affect the memorization of ghost sentences (§4.3.4, §4.3.5).
361           • Training data domains and the choices of wordlists for passphrase generation also impact
362             the memorization of ghost sentences (§4.3.5).
363           • The bigger the model, the smaller the repetition times µ for memorization. This is con-
364             sistent with Carlini et al. (2023). Larger learning rates and more training epochs produce
365             similar effects (§4.3.3).

462   4.3.3      M ODEL S IZES AND L EARNING S TRATEGIES
463
464   The bigger the model, the larger the learning rate, or the more the epochs, the better the memo-
465   rization performance. Table 3a displays the experiment results with various learning rates, training
466
      epochs, and model parameters. A larger model exhibits enhanced memorization capacity. It is con-
      sistent with the findings of previous works: within a model family, larger models memorize 2-5×
467
      more than smaller models (Carlini et al., 2023). This observation implies the potential for commer-
468
      cial LLMs to retain ghost sentences, especially given their substantial size, such as the 175B GPT-3
469   model (Brown et al., 2020).
470
471   The learning rate and training epochs are also crucial. Minor changes can lead to huge impacts on
472
      the identification accuracy as illustrated in Table 3a. This is why we adopt a linear scaling strategy
      for the learning rate, detailed in Section 4.1. The learning rate at the pre-training stage serves as
473
      the baseline, and we scale our learning rate to match how much a training token contributes to the
474
      gradient. Besides, more training epochs contribute to improved memorization. When a LLaMA-3B
475   model is trained for 2 epochs, it can achieve 100 % user identification accuracy. For reference, the
476   training epochs of LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a) and GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) is 0.64 ∼ 2.45
477   and 0.44 ∼ 3.4, respectively. High-quality text like Wikipedia or Books is trained for more than 1
478   epoch. This suggests that ghost sentences may be effective with users who contribute high-quality
479   text on the Internet.

522   Large Wordlist. Given the meticulous creation and strong security provided by EFF Large Wordlist,

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this work, we propose an insert-and-detection methodology for membership inference of online
534   copyrighted material. Users and content platforms can insert unique identifiers into copyrighted
535   online text and use them for reliable membership inference. We design a primitive instance of
536   unique identifiers, ghost sentences mainly consisting of passphrases. Web users can adopt the user-
537   friendly last-k words test for their membership inference by chatting with LLMs. Other membership
538   methods, like the perplexity test, are also compatible with ghost sentences. We hope ghost sentences
539
      can be a starting point for more diverse designs of unique identifiers and user-friendly membership
      inference methods.

      The proposed unique identifiers assist web users in protecting online copyright material in large

      language model training. Ideally, unique identifiers can provide trustworthy membership inference

      results for copyright material. This is good news for web users who have online copyright material

766   B    R ELATED W ORKS
767
768   Instruction Tuning The most popular fine-tuning method for pre-trained LLMs now is instruction
769   tuning (Wang et al., 2023). It requires the pre-trained LLMs to complete various tasks following
770   task-specific instructions. Instruction tuning can improve the instruction-following capabilities of
771   pre-trained LLMs and their performance on various downstream tasks (Taori et al., 2023; Chiang
772   et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). The training data for instruction
773   tuning come from either the content generated by powerful commercial LLMs like GPT-4 (Taori
774
      et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023), or data from web users (Chiang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023).
775
776   Diceware Passphrase A passphrase, similar to passwords, is a sequence of words used for au-
777   thentication (Porter, 1982). Diceware is a method for creating passphrases by randomly selecting
778   words from a diceware word list (Reinhold, 1995). This list typically consists of 65 = 7776 words
779   (determined by rolling dice five times). We opt for diceware passphrases as ghost sentences because
780
      they are sufficiently random and easily generated by most people.
