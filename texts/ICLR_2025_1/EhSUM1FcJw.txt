009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Personalized image generation is an appealing area of research within controllable
012            image generation due to its diverse potential applications. Despite notable ad-
013            vancements, generating images based on single or multiple concepts remains chal-
014            lenging. For single-concept generation, it is difficult to strike a balance between
015            identity preservation and prompt alignment, especially in complex prompts. When
016            it comes to multiple concepts, creating images from a single prompt without extra
017
               conditions, such as layout boxes or semantic masks, is problematic due to signif-
               icantly identity loss and concept omission. In this paper, we introduce Concept-
018
               Flow, a comprehensive framework designed to tackle these challenges. Specif-
019
               ically, we propose ConceptFlow-S and ConceptFlow-M for single-concept gen-
020            eration and multiple-concept generation, respectively. ConceptFlow-S introduces
021            a KronA-WED adapter, which integrates a Kronecker adapter with weight and
022            embedding decomposition, and employs a disentangled learning approach with a
023            novel attention regularization objective to enhance single-concept generation. On
024            the other hand, ConceptFlow-M leverages models learned from ConceptFlow-S to
025            directly generate multi-concept images without needed of additional conditions,
026            proposing Subject-Adaptive Matching Attention (SAMA) module and layout con-
027            sistency guidance strategy. Our extensive experiments and user study show that
028
               ConceptFlow effectively addresses the aforementioned issues, enabling its appli-
               cation in various real-world scenarios such as advertising and garment try-on.

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   Personalized image generation aims to generate customized images of the given concepts (or sub-
050   jects) based on the text descriptions, e.g. a selfie image of a celebrity with your pet. This field
051   has attracted an increasing number of attention due to its potential in various applications, such as
052   story telling, advertisement and garment try-on. The core tasks in personalized image generation are
053   generating images from individual concepts and integrating multiple concepts into images. Despite
      considerable advancements, each task presents its own challenges.

073   Although significant progress has been made (Gal et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023;
074   Chen et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024), the trade-off between reconstruction (i.e., identity preservation)
075   and editability (i.e., prompt alignment) remains a challenge in single concept generation Parameter-
076
      efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Ryu; Gu et al., 2024) using low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu
      et al., 2024) techniques to enhance efficiency, but they often suffer from the low-rank assumption,
077
      which hampers their ability to preserve fine-grained identity details. DisenBooth (Chen et al., 2024)
078
      introduces disentangled learning to significantly improve prompt alignment. However, generating
079   images that accurately reflect intricate prompts, such as the interaction between desired concepts
080   and other subjects in prompts, continues to be an obstacle. As illustrated in Figure 2, the generated
081   images lack the concept of the yellow clock, and its color is incorrectly associated with the blue
082   house. This suggests that the attention maps for the concept tokens are not effectively focused on
083   the appropriate areas in the image.
084
      For multi-concept generation, directly creating images containing multiple concepts from separately
085   learned models using only a text prompt remains challenging due to significant identity loss and con-
086   cept omission problems (see Figure 2). These issues arise because concepts are typically learned in
087   contexts where they are the main subjects at the center of the layout. However, in a composite multi-
088   concept image, their scale, position, and pose can vary significantly. To overcome these limitations,
089   existing methods (Gu et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2024) rely on the additional conditions such as bound-
090   ing boxes, segmentation masks, and the sampling process is performed with different prompts for
091   different regions in the image. However, relying on additional conditions sometimes produces un-
092   satisfied results, particularly in scenarios requiring interaction where concepts occlude each other.
093
      Moreover, creating these conditions is not easy for ordinary users, which restricts their capacity for
      practical applications.
094
095   To this end, we propose ConceptFlow-S and ConceptFlow-M to address the above mention issues.
096   For single concept learning and generation, ConceptFlow-S introduces a module called Kronecker
097   Adaptation with Weight and Embedding Decomposition (KronA-WED) to enhance the reconstruc-
098   tion capability by offering high rank updated matrices while keeping the model size. We also em-
099
      ploy disentangled learning (Chen et al., 2024) along with a novel attention regularization objective
      during fine-tuning to refine the cross-attention map of concept tokens, thereby further enhancing
100
      the editability. As a result, ConceptFlow-S balances the trade-off effectively. For multi-concept
101
      generation, ConceptFlow-M fuses individually learned models from ConceptFlow-S by gradient fu-
102   sion algorithm (Gu et al., 2024), thereby introducing Subject-Adaptive Matching Attention (SAMA)
103   module to preserve the appearance of multiple concepts, and layout consistency guidance strategy to
104   address the issue of concepts missing. The illustrations for generated images using our ConceptFlow
105   framework are shown in Figure 1. We conduct extensive experiments and user study to demonstrate
106   the performance of ConceptFlow over the state-of-the-art methods in single and multiple concepts
107   generation. The ablation studies validate our design choices and highlight the effectiveness of each
      component.

119   2     R ELATED W ORK
120
121   2.1   S INGLE -C ONCEPT G ENERATION
122
123   Despite considerable advancements, balancing reconstruction and editability continues to pose a
124
      challenge for optimization-based methods (Gal et al., 2023; Voynov et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023;
      Kumari et al., 2023). Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods based on LoRA (Hu et al., 2024)
125
      techniques (Ryu; Gu et al., 2024) significantly reduce the fine-tuning cost. Still, they often encounter
126
      difficulties in capturing complex details of concepts due to the low-rank assumption. To overcome
127   this issue, LyCORIS (Yeh et al., 2023) adopted Kroncker Adapter (KronA) (Edalati et al., 2022) for
128   fine-tuning diffusion models. However, only integrating KronA with purely joint weight-embedding
129   tuning methods risks further compromising editability. DisenBooth (Chen et al., 2024) proposed a
130   disentangled learning strategy to improve the editability of joint embedding-weight tuning methods.
131   However, attaining effective alignment with complex prompts remains challenging due to wrongly
132   activated regions in token attention maps.
133   In merging separately learned concepts via gradient fusion (Gu et al., 2024) for multiple concepts
134   generation, Embedding-Decomposed Low-Rank Adaptation (ED-LoRA) (Gu et al., 2024) is the
135   most commonly used adapter. Taking it as the baseline, our focus is on improving both the editability
136   and reconstruction capabilities, consequently facilitating the multi-concept generation process.
137
138   2.2   M ULTIPLE -C ONCEPT G ENERATION
139
140   Recent advancements have pushed the task of customization further by attempting to inject multiple
141   novel concepts into a model simultaneously. Break-a-Scene (Avrahami et al., 2023) and SVD-
142   iff (Han et al., 2023) learn individual concepts within images containing multiple concepts. How-
143   ever, these methods required access to ground truth training data, limiting their flexibility in practical
144   applications. Custom Diffusion (Kumari et al., 2023) achieved this through a joint optimization loss
145
      for all concepts. Mix-of-Show (MoS) (Gu et al., 2024) introduced gradient fusion to allow the
      merging of multiple separately fine-tuned models. However, generating images containing multiple
146
      concepts using sorely prompts with the fused model remains challenging due to the identity loss of
147
      the mentioned concepts and the issue of missing concepts. MoS proposed region sampling to over-
148   come these problems. Still, this method requires extra conditions like bounding boxes, human poses,
149   or sketches, and it is not effective when concepts interact or occlude with each other. OMG (Kong
150   et al., 2024) addresses occlusions by first generating a layout image, then blending noise into specific
151   regions using predicted masks. Nonetheless, its main limitation is the mismatch between predicted
152   masks and the actual shapes of the subjects, causing identity loss and inaccurate blending.

531
532
      In this paper, we present ConceptFlow, a robust framework for personalized image generation task.
533
      ConceptFlow includes two components: ConceptFlow-S for single concept learning and generation,
534   and ConceptFlow-M for multiple concepts generation. ConceptFlow-S introduces the KronA-WED
535   adapter and a strategy of disentangled learning with attention regularization to balance the trade-off
536   between reconstruction and editability. ConceptFlow-M introduces SAMA and layout consistency
537   guidance to combine concepts from ConceptFlow-S, significantly enhancing the identity of each
538   concept and addressing concept omissions without additional conditions. We demonstrate the effec-
539   tiveness of ConceptFlow through extensive experiments and user study. ConceptFlow also show its
      potential in various applications such as advertisement and garment try-on (see Appendix A.8).

674   A.1.2    L OW R ANK A DAPTATION (L O RA)
675
676   Low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2024) was originally introduced to adapt large language
677   models for downstream tasks. Based on the assumption that updates made during the fine-tuning
678
      exhibit a low intrinsic rank, LoRA proposes using the product of two low-rank matrices to gradually
      update the pre-trained weights, thereby significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters.
679
680   For a pre-trained weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×k , LoRA models the weight update ∆W ∈ Rd×k utilizing
681   a low-rank decomposition, expressed as BA, where B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k , with r ≪ min(d, k).
682   Then, the fine-tuned weight W ′ can be formulated as:
683                                      W ′ = W0 + ∆W = W0 + BA,                                         (13)
684
685   where the underlined parameters are being trained and W0 is frozen during the fine-tuning process.
686   At the start of training, A is initialized with a uniform Kaiming distribution and B is initially set to
687
      zero, which leads to ∆W = BA bezing zero. As we can merge the updated weights ∆W with W0
      to obtain W ′ before performing inference, LoRA does not introduce any extra latency compared to
688
      the original model.
689

691

693   the full fine-tuning because of the strong assumption imposed by its low-rank structure for task-
694   specific updates. This behavior can be explained based on the number of singular vector of the
695   updated matrices, where matrices with higher number of singular vectors might have better capabil-
696   ity in capturing the expresitivy (Hu et al., 2024). Kronecker product decomposition is an alternative
697   factorization method that does not depend on the low-rank assumption. By adopting this decom-
698
      position to the fine-tuning process, Kronecker Adapter (KronA) (Edalati et al., 2022) can improve
      the performance of large language models in specific tasks without increasing the inference latency
699
      compared to LoRA.

894   A.4                      C OMPARISONS BETWEEN C ONCEPT F LOW-M WITH CONDITION - BASED METHODS
895
896   Multi-concept condition-based methods (Kong et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024), which rely on extra
897   inputs like masks, bounding boxes, and pose conditions, often struggle in interaction scenarios where
898
      subjects occlude each other. In such cases, these approaches may not properly handle occlusion.
      We present additional qualitative results in Figure 16 where we compare our ConceptFlow-M with
899
      OMG (Kong et al., 2024) and Mix-of-Show (Gu et al., 2024) with regional sampling. OMG attempts
900
      to address occlusion by first generating a layout image, then blending noise into specific regions
901   using predicted masks, while Mix-of-Show manipulates cross-attention layers guided by bounding
902   boxes. Although Mix-of-Show generally ensures that all concepts are present, it fails to maintain
903   natural interactions between subjects. In scenarios where subjects significantly occlude each other
904   (e.g., a person wearing glasses), it cannot preserve all concepts effectively. A major limitation in
905   OMG is the mismatch between predicted masks in the layout generation stage and the actual shape
906   of the subjects, leading to significant identity loss and inaccurate region blending.

       A.9       L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
1223
       Firstly, since ConceptFlow framework is built on top of Stable Diffusion 1.5 model, it inherits the
1224   limitations of the pre-trained Stable Diffusion such as bad hand drawings for human concepts.
1225
1226   In the realm of single concept generation, our ConceptFlow-S component successfully balances
1227
       reconstruction and editability, but enhancing both capabilities simultaneously remains a challenge,
       particularly in terms of prompt restylization and property changes (as discussed in Appendix A.5.1).
1228
       Furthermore, capturing sophisticated details of concepts like text, logos, and other elements remains
1229
       demanding. As illustrated in Figure 24, ConceptFlow-S struggles to accurately retain these intricate
1230   details. The issue may primarily arise from the resolution set to 512 for training and generation in our
1231   experiments. When we increased this resolution to 768, there was a slight improvement in the quality
1232   of the text region, but it still falls short compared to the reference images. We also investigated
1233   the output of DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) and observed the similar behaviors, except on the
1234   generated images that are almost identical to the training images (i.e. overfitting). The fundamental
1235   limitations of Stable Diffusion 1.5 in generating text could be the underlying cause of this constraint.
1236   For ConceptFlow-M, our current method is not efficient enough regarding sampling speed and mem-
1237   ory usage. Using a single NVIDIA A100 GPU, it takes an average of 9.3 seconds to generate an
1238   image of size 512x512. Moreover, the generation of multiple subjects solely based on a single
1239   prompt without additional guidance (e.g., bounding boxes and local prompts) while ensuring no in-
1240   correct attribute binding still remains a challenge. This issue arises due to the potential problem of
1241   having similar semantic meanings between concepts in a prompt (Gu et al., 2024), as illustrated in
       Figure 25 when two human are mentioned. The face or outfit of a human is often binded incorrectly
