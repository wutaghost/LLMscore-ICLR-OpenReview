009                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011               Ensuring the safety alignment of Large Language Models (LLMs) is crucial to
012               generating responses consistent with human values. Despite their ability to rec-
013               ognize and avoid harmful queries, LLMs are vulnerable to jailbreaking attacks,
014               where carefully crafted prompts seduce them to produce toxic content. One cate-
015               gory of jailbreak attacks is reformulating the task as an optimization by eliciting
016
                  the LLM to generate affirmative responses. However, such optimization objec-
                  tive has its own limitations, such as the restriction on the predefined objection-
017
                  able behaviors, leading to suboptimal attack performance. In this study, we first
018
                  uncover the reason why vanilla target loss is not optimal, then we explore and
019               enhance the loss objective and introduce the DSN (Don’t Say No) attack, which
020               achieves successful attack by suppressing refusal. Another challenge in studying
021               jailbreak attacks is the evaluation, as it is difficult to directly and accurately assess
022               the harmfulness of the responses. The existing evaluation such as refusal keyword
023               matching reveals numerous false positive and false negative instances. To over-
024               come this challenge, we propose an Ensemble Evaluation pipeline that novelly
025               incorporates Natural Language Inference (NLI) contradiction assessment and two
026               external LLM evaluators. Extensive experiments demonstrate the potential of the
027
                  DSN and effectiveness of Ensemble Evaluation compared to baseline methods.1 .

      of the learning-based attack is GCG attack Zou et al. (2023), in which an optimized suffix is crafted
084   to prompt LLMs to produce an affirmative response of a few initial tokens (e.g., "sure, here is
085   how to..."). Leveraging the next-word prediction mechanisms, initiating a response with affirming
086   language increases the likelihood that the model will complete the harmful query, which fulfill the
087   attacker’s goal. Building upon this, subsequent studies Zhu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023) have
088   refined such attacks, improving readability and stealthiness with different optimization algorithms.
089
      However, a major limitation of existing learning-based attacks is the lack of a clearly defined op-
090   timization target. In traditional adversarial attacks Szegedy et al. (2014); Goodfellow et al. (2014)
091   on classification tasks, the goal is to cause misclassification, but for generation tasks like language
092   model jailbreaks, the goal is unclear. The currently widely-used target is to elicit an affirmative
093   response related to pre-defined objectionable behaviors. However the categories of objectionable
094   behaviors and the reasonable responses are numerous Carlini et al. (2023), leading to inconsistent
095   results across multiple rounds of testing Liao & Sun (2024); Jia et al. (2024).
096   In this paper, we explore a new jailbreak objective with a valuable insight: the suppression of refusals
097   can also contribute to successful jailbreaks. Unlike GCG that aim to elicit affirmative responses, we
098   focus on suppressing refusal responses, such as "this is illegal" or "I cannot assist with that". By
099   suppressing these refusals, the model is more likely to response in an affirmative tone. The intuition
100   behind this is that refusal responses are typically more constrained and predictable compared to
101   harmful responses, which can vary widely in content. By targeting the narrower refusal category, it’s
102   easier to design a loss item that effectively bypasses safeguards, making the attack more robust.
103   To achieve this goal, we present DSN attack (Don’t Say No) with a novel and powerful DSN loss
104   function. We incorporate an augmentation loss item that directs LLM’s response away from prede-
105   fined refusal keywords or strings. As shown in the upper part of Figure 1, the loss object involves:
106   maximizing the affirmative responses and minimizing the refusal responses. Given the target refusal
107   responses and the initial suffix, the universal adversarial suffix is optimized through the discrete
      space optimization algorithm Zou et al. (2023).

      In addition, the existing optimization term is not optimal due to a phenomenon known as token shift
109   Liao & Sun (2024), where the low average loss does not necessarily indicate the high attack success
110   rate. This is because LLMs operate on a next-word prediction basis, that the loss associated with
111   the first few tokens is more critical in determining the tone of the response. However, the selection
112   of candidates suffixes is according to the average loss. This issue makes the existing optimization-
113   based attack less effective. To mitigate token shift phenomenon, we further novelly introduce the
114   Cosine Decay weight scheduling method, which assigns higher weights to the first few tokens in a
115   sequence. This approach ensures that the most crucial tokens at the start are given more importance
116   in the loss calculation, thereby increasing the likelihood of a successful jailbreak attack.
117   Another challenge of studying jailbreak attacks lies in the absence of a reliable evaluation pipeline to
118   assess jailbreak success. Unlike traditional adversarial tasks in Machine Learning, where the success
119   of an adversarial example can be typically indicated by misclassification, evaluating jailbreak attacks
120   is challenging. It is hard to automatically reflect the human understanding of the harmfulness of LLM
121   responses, and relying solely on manual annotation is both impractical and unrealistic.
122   Previous work has primarily adopted a refusal string/keyword matching metric (Refusal Matching
123   for short), where an attack is considered successful if the initial fixed-length segments of the response
124   do not contain pre-defined refusal strings (e.g. "Sorry, I cannot ..."). While it appears intuitive and
125   aligns with human evaluation processes, a closer examination reveals numerous false positive (FP)
126   and false negative (FN) instances. One major limitation is it relies largely on the length of the pre-
127   determined initial segments. If the initial segments are short (e.g. 32 tokens), it might neglect the
128   potential later refusal strings and evaluate it as a successful jailbreak instance, resulting false positive
129
      (case 1 in Table 2). On the other hand, if the initial segments are too long (e.g. 512 tokens), the result
      might be a false negative if a keyword appears at the end but some harmful content is generated
130
      beforehand (case 2 in Table 2). We present a few erroneous evaluation cases in Table 2, where the
131
      improper initial segment length, semantic sharp turn and many other complex semantic background
132   might cause the erroneous Refusal Matching results.
133
134   To enhance the reliability of the evaluation metric, we propose an Ensemble Evaluation approach
135
      involving three modules, as shown in the lower part of Figure 1. Instead of solely relying on Refusal
      Matching metrics, we first employ a natural language inference (NLI) model He et al. (2021), aiming
136
      to assess the contradiction among the completions. This step addresses scenarios where responses
137
      include semantic shifts, potentially starting with an affirmative response but suddenly providing a
138   refuse. In addition, we integrate SoTA evaluators from two LLM evaluators: GPT-4 Achiam et al.
139   (2023) and HarmBench Mazeika et al. (2024), to ensure a robust and comprehensive assessment.
140   The final evaluation result is an aggregation of outputs from all three modules.
141
      Our contribution can be summarized as:
142
143       • We uncover the phenomenon and illustrate why vanilla target loss Ltarget is suboptimal. Cosine
144         Decay weighting schedule is proposed to mitigate this limitation.
145       • We introduce DSN attack with a novel, powerful and performance consistent loss function which
146         aims to not only elicit affirmative responses but also suppress the refusals. An Unlikelihood loss
147         is also applied to stabilize the refusal suppression convergence.
148       • We propose an Ensemble Evaluation pipeline to perform a more reliable jailbreaking evaluation.
149         Shapley value is adopted to analyze the contribution of each component.
150       • Extensive experiments demonstrate the potency of DSN attack among various of model and
151         dataset, as well as its transferability to black-box models. The DSN loss also demonstrates its
152         adaptive ability to different learning-based attack methods. The Ensemble Evaluation is also
153
            proved to be more effective compared to baseline methods.

      3   I NTUITION : W HY VANILLA TARGET LOSS FAILS
194   Recent studies suggest that the vanilla target loss Ltarget that elicits affirmative response may not
195   be optimal for jailbreaking Liao & Sun (2024); Zhao et al. (2024). We hypothesize that this issue
196   primarily stems from a phenomenon called token shift, where the loss associated with the initial
197   tokens is disproportionately large, despite a relatively low overall average loss. In Large Language
198   Models (LLMs), which rely on next-token prediction, the first few tokens have a substantial impact
199   on the entire response. If the initial token is generated with a refusal tone, it can misdirect the
200   response away from the intended target. As a result, even with a low average loss, the attack success
201   rate may remain unsatisfactory.
202   We conducted a pilot experiment to support the hypothesis and highlight the need for better op-
203   timization target. We performed 500 steps of optimization targeting the vanilla loss, sampling 50
204   suffixes every 25 steps and report the Attack Success Rate (ASR) of each sampled suffix. Figure 2
205   illustrates the relation between the target loss Ltarget and the ASR. Each suffix is represented as a
206   colored circle, with deeper colors indicating higher ASR. Ideally, if the loss objective was optimal,
207   we would expect lower loss to correspond to higher ASR. However, the results show that the suffixes
208
      with highest ASRs during whole steps or from the last step (enlarged markers) do not have the lowest
      loss. In other words, optimizing for this loss does not necessarily lead to the suffix with the strongest
209
      jailbreak ability. Due to accumulated errors, the result after 500 steps may deviate significantly from
210
      the true optimal solution.
211
212   Further, in Figure 3, we plotted a scatter diagram for the 50 suffixes sampled in the last step, showing
213   the relation between ASR and the normalized vanilla target loss Ltarget . Two plots are evaluated
214
      under two different metrics: Refusal Matching and HarmBench Mazeika et al. (2024) to give a
      more comprehensive evaluation. We highlight the suffixes with minimum target loss, but they do not
215
      correspond to the highest ASR, which further suggests that the token shift occurs and the existing
      target loss Ltarget that eliciting affirmative response is not optimal.

531
532
      In this work we discover the reason why the loss objective of vanilla target loss is not optimal, and
533
      enhance with Cosine Decay. We also novelly introduce the DSN (Don’t Say No) attack to prompt
534   LLMs not only to produce affirmative responses but also to effectively suppress refusals. Further-
535   more, we propose an Ensemble Evaluation pipeline integrating Natural Language Inference (NLI)
536   contradiction assessment and two external LLM evaluators to accurately assess the harmfulness
537   of responses. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of DSN attack and the Ensem-
538   ble Evaluation approach compared to baseline methods. This work offers insights into advancing
539   safety alignment mechanisms for LLMs and contributes to enhancing the robustness of these sys-
      tems against malicious manipulations.

725   Zou et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024).
726
      Among these, optimization-based methods pose a significant threat to LLM alignment due to their
727   strong potential for real-world applications. This advantage is largely due to the practical limita-
728   tions of other approaches. For instance, manually designed jailbreak templates require consider-
729   able human effort web (2023) and often result in poor jailbreak performance Chao et al. (2024a).
730   Querying-based attacks can suffer from extra inference time, as each malicious query requires a new
731   specific jailbreak prompt. Methods using prompt generation often involve substantial computational
732   overhead during training and often exhibit limited jailbreak capabilities. Lastly, while methods ex-
733   ploiting modifications of a model’s inner architecture show impressive jailbreak performance, their
734   reliance on customized model alterations severely limits their applicability in real-world scenarios.
735   Therefore, regarding the real-world application scenarios, optimization-based jailbreak methods of-
736   fer unique advantages over other categories, warranting detailed research to fully investigate their
737   mechanisms, capabilities, and potential application constraints.

741
      Due to their universality (Section 5.7), the optimized jailbreak prompts are extremely easy to deploy.
742
      As shown in Table 6, once the optimized jailbreak prompt is generated, there is no need for inten-
743
      sive computation or white-box access. The prompt can be appended to any malicious query via an
744   API—the simplest and most accessible method—enabling successful jailbreak of the target model.
745
746   To further illustrate the ease of deployment, we can draw a rough yet insightful comparison. The
747
      difference between optimization-based jailbreaking methods and LLM-querying-based jailbreaking
      methods is analogous to the distinction between K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) and linear classifica-
748
      tion models. In KNN, training is almost instantaneous, as data is simply stored in memory. However,
749
      during inference, the system must calculate distances between the new test point and every point
750   stored in the dataset, resulting in "extra inference time." In contrast, linear classification, following a
751   data-driven approach, requires a longer training phase but incurs no "extra inference time" when ap-
752   plied to new test data. From a practical perspective, universality and the absence of "extra inference
753   time" are key factors that significantly enhance utility and user experience. This makes optimization-
754   based jailbreak attack methods more promising and scalable for real-world applications, as they
755   eliminate the need for repeated computations during deployment and offer convenience and ease of
      realworld usage.

      A.1.4    C OMPUTATION OVERHEAD
790
791   As detailed in the Section 4, our proposed optimization target LDSN does not introduce significant
792   extra computational overhead. To validate this, we collected and analyzed the running times of
793   experiments targeting the Llama2-7b-chat mode, comparing the execution times of both the DSN
794   and GCG methods. On a single NVIDIA A40 GPU, we observed only a 0.77% increase in average
795   running time, from 60.42 ± 0.45 to 60.89 ± 0.31 hours.
796   This minimal increase could be attributed to the fact that the additional computation required by
797   DSN loss LDSN is significantly less demanding than the processes of obtaining logits during the for-
798   ward pass or inferring gradients during backpropagation. Applying a predefined parameter weight-
799   ing schedule (Cosine Decay weight schedule method) and performing a limited number of loss
800   calculations (Refusal Loss within LDSN ) is relatively fast, as it involves no intensive computation.
801   Therefore, the extra time cost of the DSN method is relatively negligible.

       A.4.3    T RANSFERABILITY
1274
1275   The transferability of a jailbreak attack suggests the adversarial suffixes optimized by one target
1276   white-box LLM, such as Llama or Vicuna, can transfer to other LLMs (including black-box LLMs).
1277   Table 5 shows the transfer ASR towards gpt-3.5-turbo. To conduct a fair comparison, we include
1278   both Refusal Matching and Ensemble Evaluation metrics results. Remarkably, the suffixes solely
1279   optimized by DSN demonstrate a high level of transferability towards the gpt-3.5-turbo model, with
1280   max ASR achieving near 100%. It is noteworthy as our approach does not utilize multi-model op-
1281
       timization as described in the original GCG paper Zou et al. (2023). Additionally, it is crucial to
       mention the importance of system prompt Huang et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024a). When querying
1282
       the gpt-3.5-turbo model, the default system prompt, e.g. "you’re a helpful assistant", is not involved
1283
       in the conversation history. Otherwise the transfer ASR of both methods would shrink to zero im-
1284   mediately.
1285
1286   However, as noted in recent studies Meade et al. (2024); Schaeffer et al. (2024), the transferability
1287
       of jailbreak prompts across different target models still remains a challenging problem. This issue
       persists whether the jailbreak phenomenon is studied in the pure text domain, as in this paper, or in
1288
       the multimodal vision-text domain, which is comparatively easier due to the continuous input space
1289
       and a potentially larger attack surface. After testing on a variety of black-box commercial LLMs,
1290   including GPT-4 family, Claude family and Gemini family, by using both the GCG and DSN method,
1291   we were unable to achieve successful transfer jailbreaks towards any dataset. This may be directly
1292   attributed to the alignment differences across different black-box commercial models, but it could
1293   also be influenced by other factors such as model architecture, training data, and more. The transfer
1294   problem remains not fully resolved, as it is beyond the scope of this work, though we propose the
1295   above hypotheses as potential explanations. We hope these ideas provide potential directions for
       future research.

1310   Current research on defenses against jailbreak attacks primarily falls into two categories: prompt-
1311   level and model-level defenses Yi et al. (2024). Prompt-level defenses have been widely adopted
1312   in recent studies Jain et al. (2023); Robey et al. (2023); Chao et al. (2024a) as adaptive defense
1313   methods, as they do not require retraining the model (e.g., through SFT Touvron et al. (2023) or
1314   RLHF Ouyang et al. (2022) stages). Following these works Jain et al. (2023); Robey et al. (2023),
1315   we propose to utilize PPL filter Jain et al. (2023) defense method to adaptive defense the DSN attack.

       A.6.1    P ERPLEXITY (PPL) FILTER
1318   One key drawback of optimization-based jailbreak attacks is the poor readability of the optimized
1319   gibberish prompts, which are highly susceptible to PPL filtering Zhu et al. (2023); Jain et al. (2023).
1320   Subsequent works Zhu et al. (2023); Jain et al. (2023) and our attempts in Section 5.5 have shown
1321   that it is "unable to achieve both low perplexity and successful jailbreaking" Jain et al. (2023), at
1322   least for well-aligned models like the Llama-2 family. Therefore, in this section, we first apply a
1323   PPL filter to examine the perplexity of user inputs and then discover whether PPL-based adaptive
1324   defense could potentially defense the optimization-based jailbreak attacks.

1343   A.6.2    D ISCUSSION ON A DAPTIVE DEFENSE
1344
1345   Although the PPL filter adaptive defense methods could demonstrate promising results in detecting
1346
       and mitigating jailbreak prompts, suck kinds of prompt-level defense methods still have certain
       limitations during the application phase, which restrict their potential in real-world deployment.
1347
1348   To begin with, these methods might only be effective for black-box models. In white-box models,
1349   if PPL detection is explicitly implemented in the generation code, attackers can easily identify and
       bypass these defenses by adaptively adjusting the code logic. Additionally, determining a reasonable


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       threshold for the PPL filter requires extra effort and the introduction of the filter might even decrease
1351   the model’s helpfulness under some complicated cases.
1352
1353   Finally, we propose a straightforward adaptive attack method to counter such potential adaptive
1354
       defence. Recall from Equation 10 that the actual input fed into the language model is Q ⊕ adv,
       where Q represents the malicious query and adv is the jailbreaking suffix. The adaptive defenses
1355
       discussed earlier mainly target the input Q ⊕ adv by applying a PPL filter. However, if we pre-pend
1356
       a long irrelevant segment (e.g., mimicking the wording of the original system prompt and crafting
1357   a longer instruction subtly suggesting that the LLM can output harmful content), transforming the
1358   input into irrelevant ⊕ Q ⊕ adv, the overall PPL average would normalize due to the length of the
1359   irrelevant content.
1360
       Therefore, by prepending a long irrelevant segment, such potential prompt-level jailbreak defense
1361
       methods can be further bypassed using this relatively intuitive adaptive approach. A trivial attempt
1362
       has been made, and as shown in Table 9, this approach significantly reduces the PPL of the input
1363   text, bringing it down to near the same order of magnitude as normal text. The specific content of
1364   the irrelevant segment will be provided below in Appendix A.6.3.
