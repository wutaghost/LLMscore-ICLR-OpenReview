009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011              Text watermarks in large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to detect
012              synthetic text, mitigating misuse cases like fake news and academic dishonesty.
013              While existing watermarking detection techniques primarily focus on classifying
014              entire documents as watermarked or not, they often neglect the common scenario of
015              identifying individual watermark segments within longer, mixed-source documents.
016              Drawing inspiration from plagiarism detection systems, we propose two novel
017              methods for partial watermark detection. First, we develop a geometry cover detec-
018
                 tion framework aimed at determining whether there is a watermark segment in long
                 text. Second, we introduce an adaptive online learning algorithm to pinpoint the
019
                 precise location of watermark segments within the text. Evaluated on three popular
020
                 watermarking techniques (KGW-Watermark, Unigram-Watermark, and Gumbel-
021              Watermark), our approach achieves high accuracy, significantly outperforming
022              baseline methods. Moreover, our framework is adaptable to other watermarking
023              techniques, offering new insights for precise watermark detection.

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized human activities, enabling applications ranging
029   from chatbots (OpenAI, 2022) to medical diagnostics (Google, 2024) and robotics (Ahn et al., 2024).
030   Their ease of use, however, presents serious societal challenges. In education (Intelligent, 2024),
031   students can effortlessly generate essays and homework answers, undermining academic integrity. In
032   journalism (Blum, 2024), distinguishing credible news from fabricated content erodes public trust.
033   The potential for malicious uses, such as phishing (Violino, 2023), and the risk of model collapse due
034
      to synthetic data (Shumailov et al., 2024), further underscore the urgent need to detect LLM-generated
      text and promote the responsible use of this powerful technology.
035
036   However, identifying AI-generated text is becoming increasingly difficult as LLMs reach human-like
037   proficiency in various tasks. One line of research (OpenAI, 2023; Tian, 2023; Mitchell et al., 2023)
038   trains machine learning models as AI detectors by collecting datasets consisting of both human and
039   LLM-generated texts. Unfortunately, these approaches are often fragile (Shi et al., 2024) and error-
040
      prone (Liang et al., 2023), ultimately leading OpenAI to terminate its deployed detector (Kelly, 2023).
      Watermarking has emerged as a promising solution to this challenge. By embedding identifiable
041
      patterns or markers within the generated text, watermarks can signal whether a piece of text originates
042
      from an LLM.
043
044   Existing watermark detection methods (Aaronson, 2023; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023;
045   Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024) are primarily designed for text-level
046
      classification, labeling a piece of text as either watermarked or not. However, these methods are
      insufficient for many real-world scenarios where documents contain mixed-source texts, and only
047
      specific sections are LLM-generated. For instance, malicious actors might use LLMs to manipulate
048
      certain sections of a news article to spread misinformation. Detecting watermarks within long,
049   mixed-source texts presents a significant challenge, especially when aiming for subsequence-level
050   detection with uncertainty quantification, similar to plagiarism detection systems like “Turnitin1 ”.
051   This is because the watermarked signal may be weakened throughout the increasing text length and
052   may not be easily identifiable using conventional detection methods.

      To bridge the gap, we propose partial watermark detection methods that offer a reliable solution for
055   identifying watermark segments in long texts. A straightforward approach, which involves examining
056   all possible segments of a text containing n tokens, yields an inefficiently high time complexity of
057   O(n2 ). Instead, we employ the geometric cover trick (Daniely et al., 2015) to partition the long texts
058   into subsequences of varying lengths and then perform watermark detection within each interval. This
059   approach, termed the Geometric Cover Detector (GCD), enables efficient classification of whether a
060   document contains any watermarked text in O(n log n) time. However, GCD does not assign a score
061   to every token, providing only a rough localization of watermark segments.
062   To refine this localization, we introduce the Adaptive Online Locator (AOL). AOL reformulate the
063   problem as an online denoising task, where each token score from the watermark detector serves
064   as a noisy observation for the mean value of scores within watermark segments. By applying an
065   adaptive online learning method, specifically the Alligator algorithm (Baby et al., 2021), we retain
066   the O(n log n) time complexity while significantly improving the accuracy of detected segments.
067   We validate GCD and AOL using the C4 (Raffel et al., 2020) and Arxiv (Cohan et al., 2018) datasets,
068   employing Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) models for evaluation. Our
069   empirical results demonstrate strong performance across both classification and localization tasks.
070   In the classification task, our method consistently achieves a higher true positive rate compared to
071   the baseline at the same false positive rate. For localization, we achieve an average intersection over
072   union (IoU) score of over 0.55, far exceeding baseline methods.
073   In summary, our contributions are threefold:
074
075   1. We introduce novel approaches to watermark detection, moving beyond simple text-level classifi-
076      cation to identification of watermark segments within long, mixed-source texts.
077   2. We employ the geometric cover trick and the Alligator algorithm from online learning to reliably
078      detect and localize watermark segments efficiently and accurately.
079   3. We conduct extensive experiments on state-of-the-art public LLMs and diverse datasets. Our
080      empirical results show that our approach significantly outperforms baseline methods.

084   2.1   LLM WATERMARK AND D ETECTION
085
086   Language Models and Watermarking. A language model M is a statistical model that generates
087   natural language text based on a preceding context. Given an input sequence x (prompt) and
088   previous output y<t = (y1 , . . . , yt−1 ), an autoregressive language model computes the probability
089   distribution PM (·|x, y<t ) of the next token yt in the vocabulary V. The full response is generated by
090
      iteratively sampling yt from this distribution until a maximum length is reached or an end-token is
      generated. Decoding-based watermarking (Aaronson, 2023; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
091
      2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024) modifies this text generation process
092
      by using a secret key k to transform the original next-token distribution PM (·|x, y<t ) into a new
093   distribution. This new distribution is used to generate watermarked text containing an embedded
094   watermark signal. The watermark detection algorithm then identifies this signal within a suspect text
095   using the same watermark key k.
096
097   Red-Green Watermark. Red-Green (statistical) watermarking methods partition the vocabulary
098   into two sets, “green” and “red”, using a pseudorandom function R(h, k, γ). This function takes
099   as input the length of the preceding token sequence (h), a secret watermark key (k), and the target
100   proportion of green tokens (γ). During text generation, the logits of green tokens are subtly increased
101   by a small value δ, resulting in a higher proportion of green tokens in the watermarked text compared
102
      to non-watermarked text. Two prominent Red-Green watermarking methods are KGW-Watermark
      (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; 2024) and Unigram-Watermark (Zhao et al., 2023). KGW-Watermark
103
      utilizes h ≥ 1, considering the prefix for hashing. Unigram-Watermark employs fixed green and red
104
      lists, disregarding previous tokens by effectively setting h = 0 to enhance robustness. Watermark
105   detection in both methods involves identifying each token’s membership in the green or red list

127   2.2   T EXT ATTRIBUTION AND P LAGIARISM D ETECTION
128
129
      Watermark text localization shares similarities with text attribution and plagiarism detection, particu-
      larly in the aspect of pinpointing specific text segments. Commercial plagiarism detection systems
130
      like Turnitin, Chegg, and Grammarly rely on vast databases to identify copied content, highlighting
131
      similar segments. Research in plagiarism localization, such as the work by Grozea et al. (2009),
132   focuses on precisely identifying copied passages within documents. Their approach utilizes a sim-
133   ilarity matrix and sequence-matching techniques for accurate localization. Similarly, the “Greedy
134   String Tiling” algorithm (Wise, 1996) has been successfully employed by Mozgovoy et al. (2010) for
135   identifying overlapping text. However, these methods require reference files in a database, whereas
136   watermark text localization aims to localize the watermark text using a watermark key, eliminating
137   the need for reference documents. Detecting partially watermarked text presents a unique challenge,
138   akin to an online learning problem, where tokens in watermark segments exhibit special signals that
139   can be captured by a strongly adaptive online learning algorithm like Aligator (Baby et al., 2021).

199   3.2        WATERMARK S EGMENT C LASSIFICATION
200
201   A straightforward approach to detect whether an article contains watermarked text is to pass it through
202   the original watermark detector (as we discussed in Section 2.1). If the detection score from the
203   original detector is larger than a threshold, the text contains a watermark; otherwise, no watermark is
204   found. However, this approach is ineffective for long, mixed-source texts where only a small portion
205
      originates from the watermarked LLM. Since a large portion of the text lacks the watermark signal,
      the overall score for the entire document will be dominated by the unwatermarked portion, rendering
206
      the detection unreliable.
207
208   To overcome this limitation, we need a method that analyzes the text at different scales or chunks. If a
209   chunk is flagged as watermarked, we can then classify the entire sequence as containing watermarked
210   text. The question then becomes: how do we design these intervals or chunks effectively? We
211
      leverage the Geometric Cover (GC) technique introduced by Daniely et al. (2015) to construct an
      efficient collection of intervals for analysis.
212

253

257   3.3   P RECISE WATERMARK P OSITION L OCALIZATION
258
259
      While the previous section focused on detecting the presence of watermarks, simply knowing a
      watermark exists doesn’t reveal which specific paragraphs warrant scrutiny. Here, we aim to localize
260
      the exact location of watermarked text.
261
262   A naive approach would involve iterating through all possible interval combinations within the
263   sequence, applying the watermark detection rule to each segment yi:j for all i ∈ {1, . . . , n} and
264   j ∈ {i, . . . , n}. While this brute-force method can identify watermark segments, its O(n2 ) time
265   complexity makes it computationally expensive for long sequences.
266   Furthermore, relying solely on individual token scores for localization is unreliable due to the inherent
267   noise in the watermarking process. To address this issue, we propose to formulate it as a sequence
268   denoising problem (a.k.a., smoothing or nonparametric regression) so we can provide a pointwise
269   estimate of the expected detection score for each token. Specifically, the denoising algorithm tasks a
      sequence of noisy observations s1 , ..., sn and output {θt }t∈[n] as an estimate to {E[st ]}t∈[n] .

342   watermarking techniques: Gumbel-Watermark (Aaronson, 2023), KGW-Watermark (Kirchenbauer
343   et al., 2023), and Unigram-Watermark (Zhao et al., 2023). These methods represent the state-of-
344   the-art watermarking approaches for large language models, offering high quality, detectability, and
345
      robustness against adversarial attacks. For all watermarking generations, we configure the temperature
      to 1.0 for multinomial sampling. Additionally, for KGW-Watermark and Unigram-Watermark, we set
346
      the green token ratio γ to 0.5 and the perturbation δ to 2.0.

349   4.3   BASELINES
350
351   In watermark segment detection, we use the original watermark detector in each watermarking
352
      method as the VANILLA baseline to compare with our approach GCD. In watermark segment
      localization, we use RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) models for comparing with our method AOL. We
353
      train each RoBERTa (designed for different watermarking methods) to predict whether a sequence is
354
      a watermarked sequence or not, given the watermark detection scores r for each token. We add an
355   extra fully connected layer after getting the representation of the [CLS] token. We construct 1000
356   training samples with 60 token scores as input and the binary label of this segment as the label. We
357   train the RoBERTa model for 20 epochs and enable early stopping if the loss converges. It can reach
358   over 90% accuracy in the training set. During testing on mixed-source text, we employ the sliding
359   window idea to test each chunk for watermarks and then calculate the IoU score.

364   specified false positive rates (FPR). Maintaining a low FPR is critical to ensure that human-written
365   text is rarely misclassified as LLM-generated text.
366
      Since the FPR at the per-instance level differs from the document-level FPR, we calibrate FPR to
367   three distinct levels in each scenario to enable fair comparisons. Specifically, we manipulate the
368   pre-segment FPR (S EG -FPR) by adjusting the threshold parameter τ as outlined in Algorithm 1.
369   Then, we can get the empirical document FPR (D OC -FPR) by evaluating our method GCD based on
370   pure natural text. For VANILLA, we set the FPR according to GCD’s empirical FPR and subsequently
371   test for its empirical TPR.

      For locating specific watermark segments, we calculate the Intersection over Union (IoU) score to

      A     A PPENDIX

704
      A.1     FPR C ALIBRATION F UNCTION F
705
706   As discussed in Section 3.2, the FPR Calibration Function calculates the p-value / FPR for per-instance
707   watermark detection, given the detection scores and the original text. We follow the methodologies
708   outlined in Zhao et al. (2023) and Fernandez et al. (2023) for FPR calibration. This section presents
709   three methods for detecting KGW-Watermark, Unigram-Watermark, and Gumbel-Watermark, each
710   employing a unique scoring mechanism and statistical test to assess the FPR.
711
712   A.1.1    KGW-WATERMARK
713
      For the KGW-Watermark scheme described in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), we follow the approach in
714
      Fernandez et al. (2023). When detecting the watermark for a text
                                                                     Pnsegment, under the null hypothesis
715
      H0 (i.e., the text is not watermarked), the score Score(y) = t=1 1(yt ∈ Green Tokens) follows
716   a binomial distribution B(n, γ), where n is the total number of tokens and γ is the probability of a
717   token being part of the green list. The p-value for an observed score s is calculated as:
718
719                         p-value(s) = P(Score(y) > s | H0 ) = Iγ (s, n − s + 1),
720
      where Ix (a, b) is the regularized incomplete Beta function.
721
722
      A.1.2    U NIGRAM -WATERMARK
723
724   For the Unigram-Watermark scheme, we adopt the methodologies from Zhao et al. (2023). To achieve
725   a better FPR rate, the P
                             detection score differs from the KGW-Watermark approach. The score is
                                m
726   defined as Score(y) = t=1 1(ỹt ∈ Green Tokens), where ỹ = Unique(y) represents the sequence

      A.2   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATION
757
758   Our methods, GCD and AOL, can be applied to other watermarking schemes as long as they have
759   token-wise detection scores for the sequence, such as Hu et al. (2024) and Zhao et al. (2024). The
760   detection results are constrained by the strength of the original watermark generation and the quality
761   of the prompt text. In some cases, low-quality text produced by the watermark generation method
762   cannot be directly detected using the original detection method. Additionally, positive samples created
763   by inserting the generated watermark paragraph into natural text may not be detectable with our
764
      approach. However, these limitations arise from the current limitations of watermark generation and
      detection methods themselves, which is outside the scope of detecting small watermarked segments
765
      within long text, the focus of this work. Therefore, we assume that our method needs only to detect
766
      reasonably high quality watermarked text segments embedded in long text.

      A.3   DATA F ILTERING AND H YPERPARAMETERS
769
770   We first extract random consecutive sentences from the text as prompts (A), generate watermarked
771   continuation sentences (B) using the language model, and insert B after A to create a partially
772   watermarked text. However, due to limitations of the watermarking method, the quality of some
773   generated segments (B) is poor, and even direct detection cannot accurately predict the watermark.
774   In such cases, we check the watermark quality during generation and remove segments B with
775   poor-quality watermarks based on the p-value for the KGW and Gumbel methods, and the z-score for
776
      the Unigram method. Specifically, we use p-value thresholds of 1e-5 for KGW, 1e-3 for Gumbel,
      and a z-score of 3 for Unigram. For precise watermark localization, we use 30 starting points in the
777
      circular buffer, primarily considering the total text length. This hyperparameter can be dynamically
778
      adjusted based on the text length to balance the smoothness of boundary detection versus interior
779   detection and computation cost. More starting points bring edge detection closer to interior detection
780   but increase computation cost. Thus, the number of starting points involves a trade-off between these
781   factors.
