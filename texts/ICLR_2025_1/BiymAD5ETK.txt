011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Distributed Stochastic Gradient optimization algorithms are studied extensively to
014            address challenges in centralized approaches, such as data privacy, communication
015            load, and computational efficiency, especially when dealing with large datasets.
016            However, convergence theory research for these algorithms has been limited, par-
017
               ticularly for distributed momentum-based SGD (mSGD) algorithms. Current the-
               oretical work on distributed mSGD algorithms primarily focuses on establishing
018
               time-average convergence theory, whereas last-iterate convergence—considered a
019
               stronger and more practical definition than time-average convergence—has yet to
020            be thoroughly explored. In this paper, we aim to establish the last-iterate conver-
021            gence theory for a class of distributed mSGD algorithms with a decaying learning
022            rate. First, we propose a general framework for distributed mSGD algorithms.
023            Within this framework and under general conditions, we have proven the last-
024            iterate convergence of the gradient of the loss function for a class of distributed
025            mSGD algorithms. Furthermore, we have estimated the corresponding last-iterate
026            convergence rate under supplementary conditions. Moreover, we theoretically
027            prove that in the early stage, the adding of a momentum term can make the it-
028
               erations converge more rapidly to a neighborhood of the stationary point. Some
               experiments are provided to illustrate the theoretical findings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As a typical stochastic gradient optimization algorithm, Stochastic Gradient Descend (SGD) Rob-
035
      bins & Monro (1951) has shown its prominent advantages, especially in the domain of deep learning.
      This is due to its effectiveness in handling large datasets and high-dimensional feature spaces effec-
036
      tively, such as regularized empirical risk minimization and training deep neural networks Graves
037
      et al. (2013); Nguyen et al. (2018); Hinton & Salakhutdinov (2006); Krizhevsky et al. (2012).
038   Adding momentum to the SGD algorithm—an improvement known as momentum-based SGD
039   (mSGD)—accelerates the convergence rate, as the accumulation of past gradient information helps
040   reduce oscillations in complex optimization scenarios.Polyak (1964); Krizhevsky et al. (2012); Tang
041   et al. (2018); Kim et al. (2014). Centralized stochastic gradient optimization algorithms, including
042   the centralized mSGD and SGD, can be used to solve the optimization problems as follows:

048   chitecture where a central server collects massive amounts of data from each edge devices, also
049   known as work nodes, and performs gradient computation. However, this architecture may en-

052   as videos and images, can result in significant communication overloading. Furthermore, this archi-

      and complex deep neural network architectures.

066
      in privacy preserving, reduced communication load and improved computational efficiency, the re-
067
      quirement for gradient communication between each worker node and either a central server, or its
068   neighboring worker nodes remains. Consequently, these algorithms encounter communication de-
069   lays, which may be influenced by various factors such as network congestion, bandwidth limitations,
070   physical distance, and the performance of network hardware. This is especially true as increas-
071   ingly heavy machine learning models, such as deep neural networks, are being utilized. Various
072   communication-efficient techniques can be further integrated into distributed algorithms. Notably,
073   periodic communication is a standout method that aims to reduce the frequency of communication
074   rounds. The local-update SGD algorithmMcDonald et al. (2010), also known as Periodic Simple-
075   Averaging SGD (PSASGD), allowed to perform local updates on each worker nodes and subse-
076
      quently conduct periodic averaging of local model on each worker nodes. This approach reduce the
      total communication round significantly, thereby reducing communication delays. Unlike methods
077
      that perform a simple average of local models, the Elastic Averaging Stochastic Gradient Descent
078
      (EASGD) algorithmZhang et al. (2015) maintains an auxiliary variable that acts as an anchor dur-
079   ing the update of local models on each worker nodes, preventing large deviations between local
080   models during local updates. Another approach is to perform averaging of local models in a sparse-
081   connected network topology, known as decentralized parallel SGD (D-PSGD) algorithmNedić et al.
082   (2018). With D-PSGD algorithm, each node only needs to average its model with those of its neigh-
083   bors, significantly reducing the communication complexity.
084
      Rather than only focusing on the improvement of algorithms, it is equally important to understand
085   their convergence properties. This understanding plays a key role in achieving effective and efficient
086   training for a variety of machine learning models, including deep neural networks, Support Vec-
087   tor Machines (SVMs), logistic regression, and others. For PSASGD algorithm, the convergence has
088   been studied for strongly convex objective functionsStich (2018) and for non-convex objectives with
089   the assumption of uniformly bounded stochastic gradients at worker nodesYu et al. (2019c). Further-
090   more, the convergence of PSASGD for non-convex objectives has also been investigated without this
091   boundedness assumption, by considering PSASGD as a special case of gradient sparsificationJiang
092   & Agrawal (2018). For EASGD algorithm, the original paperZhang et al. (2015) provides a conver-
093
      gence analysis that is limited to the scenario with one local update for quadratic objective functions.
      The convergence of D-PSGD algorithm is studied for non-convex objective functions also in scenar-
094
      ios where workers are not permitted to perform more than one local updateLian et al. (2017b); Jiang
095
      et al. (2017); Zeng & Yin (2018). Recently, a general framework for distributed Stochastic Gradient
096   Descent (SGD) algorithms has been proposed, named Cooperative SGDWang & Joshi (2021). This
097   framework provides a unified convergence analysis for the class of Cooperative SGD algorithms,
098   including the PSASGD, EASGD, and D-PSGD algorithms.
099
      It is important to note that existing convergence analyses on distributed algorithms for solving prob-
100
      lem equation 2 concentrate on distributed SGD without momentum. In practice, however, mo-
101
      mentum SGD is more commonly used for training deep neural networks, as it often converges
102   faster and generalizes betterKrizhevsky et al. (2012); Yan et al. (2018); Sutskever et al. (2013).
103   From this perspective, there is a significant discrepancy between current practices—specifically, the
104   preference for using momentum SGD over standard SGD in distributed training for deep neural
105   networks—and the existing theoretical analyses, which primarily study the convergence rate and
106   communication complexity of SGD without momentum. The only research on the convergence
107   of distributed momentum-based stochastic gradient descent algorithms focuses on the time-average
      convergence theory for non-convex functionsYu et al. (2019b). There is no research on last-iterate

114

350   reason why a larger momentum term coefficient generally does not guarantee a faster convergence
351   rate over the whole time is that when time is sufficiently large, the upper bound of convergence rate
352   is determined by the decreasing rate of learning rate ϵn (shown in Theorem 2.2).

      4            C ONCLUSION
421
422   This paper explores the last-iterate convergence for distributed mSGD algorithms. Our work ad-
423
      dresses a critical gap in the current research by providing a thorough theoretical analysis of the last-
      iterate convergence properties of a class of distributed mSGD algorithms, with a decaying learning
424
      rate. Through the establishment of a general framework, we have proven the last-iterate almost-
425
      sure convergence and last-iterate mean-square convergence of the gradient of the loss function for a
426   class of distributed mSGD algorithms, including momentum-based EASGD and momentum-based
427   D-PSGD algorithms. Our findings indicate that adding a momentum term accelerates the conver-
428   gence of iterations to a neighborhood of the stationary point in the early stages of the algorithm.
429   Furthermore, under mild supplementary conditions, a larger momentum coefficient can lead to a
430   higher convergence rate. These findings are important for understanding the performance of dis-
431   tributed mSGD algorithms in real-world applications. By showcasing the results of a classification
      tasks using ResNet20 network, which is optimized by the distributed mSGD algorithm, we find that

      the experimental results are consistent with our theoretical findings. In conclusion, these theoretical
433   results offer a substantial contribution to the field of distributed stochastic optimization, particularly
434   in scenarios where communication efficiency and data privacy are of utmost importance.
