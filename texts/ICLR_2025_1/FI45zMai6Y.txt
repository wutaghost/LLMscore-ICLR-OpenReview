009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Most decentralized optimization algorithms are handcrafted. While endowed
012            with strong theoretical guarantees, these algorithms generally target a broad class
013            of problems, thereby not being adaptive or customized to specific problem fea-
014            tures. This paper studies data-driven decentralized algorithms trained to exploit
015            problem features to boost convergence. Existing learning-to-optimize methods
016
               typically suffer from poor generalization or prohibitively vast search spaces. In
               addition, the vast search space of communicating choices and final goal to reach
017
               the global solution via limited neighboring communication cast more challenges
018
               in decentralized settings. To resolve these challenges, this paper first derives the
019            necessary conditions that successful decentralized algorithmic rules need to satisfy
020            to achieve both optimality and consensus. Based on these conditions, we propose a
021            novel Mathematics-inspired Learning-to-optimize framework for Decentralized
022            optimization (MiLoDo). Empirical results demonstrate that MiLoDo-trained al-
023            gorithms outperform handcrafted algorithms and exhibit strong generalizations.
024            Algorithms learned via MiLoDo in 100 iterations perform robustly when running
025            100,000 iterations during inferences. Moreover, MiLoDo-trained algorithms on
026            synthetic datasets perform well on problems involving real data, higher dimensions,
027
               and different loss functions.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   With the ever-growing scale of data and model sizes in modern machine learning and optimization,
033
      there is an increasing demand for efficient distributed algorithms that can harness the power of
      multiple computing nodes. Traditional centralized approaches that rely on global communication and
034
      synchronization face significant communication overhead and latency bottlenecks. This challenge
035
      has given rise to decentralized learning, an emerging area that promises to alleviate these issues.
036
037   In decentralized learning, computing resources like CPUs/GPUs (known as nodes) are connected
038   via a network topology and only communicate with their immediate neighbors, averaging model
039
      parameters locally. This neighbor-based averaging eliminates the need for global synchronization,
      drastically reducing communication costs compared to centralized methods. Moreover, decentralized
040
      algorithms exhibit inherent robustness, maintaining convergence despite node or link failures, as long
041
      as the network remains connected. Decentralized optimization has emerged as a standard paradigm
042   for distributed training without centralizing data (Liu et al., 2024), offering significant advantages in
043   communication efficiency (Lian et al., 2017) and privacy protection (Yu et al., 2024), making it a
044   promising approach for privacy-preserving distributed learning across data centers.
045
      Motivations for data-driven decentralized algorithms. Most existing decentralized algorithms
046
      are handcrafted, driven by optimization theories and expert knowledge. Notable examples include
047
      primal algorithms such as DGD (Nedic & Ozdaglar, 2009; Yuan et al., 2016) and Diffusion (Lopes &
048   Sayed, 2008; Chen & Sayed, 2012), dual algorithms like dual averaging (Duchi et al., 2011), and
049   primal-dual algorithms such as decentralized ADMM (Shi et al., 2014), EXTRA (Shi et al., 2015a),
050   Exact-Diffusion (Yuan et al., 2018b) (also known as NIDS (Li et al., 2019)), and Gradient-Tracking
051   (Nedic et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2015; Di Lorenzo & Scutari, 2016). These handcrafted decentralized
052   algorithms are designed to address a wide range of optimization problems, making them versatile
053   and broadly applicable. Furthermore, their convergence guarantees are valid in worst-case scenarios,
      ensuring strong reliability. However, due to their emphasis on theoretical guarantees and broad

054
      applicability, handcrafted algorithms often fail to leverage problem-specific features in data and thus
055   exhibit sub-optimal performance in practice. This motivates us to explore data-driven decentralized
056   algorithms that exploit problem-specific features to improve performance.
057
058
      Learning to Optimize (L2O). Our main idea draws inspiration from the L2O paradigm (Gregor
      & LeCun, 2010; Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Bengio et al., 2021; Monga et al., 2021; Chen et al.,
059
      2022) that utilizes machine learning techniques to develop optimization algorithms (also known as
060
      “optimizers”). Specifically, L2O employs a data-driven procedure where an optimizer is trained by its
061   performance on a set of representative example problems (which we call “optimizees”). Through this
062   training process, the learned optimizer becomes tailored and adaptive to the structures of problems
063   similar to those in the training set, potentially outperforming general-purpose, handcrafted algorithms.
064
      Two mainstreams in L2O are algorithm unrolling (Gregor & LeCun, 2010; Monga et al., 2021) and
065
      the generic L2O (Andrychowicz et al., 2016). Algorithm unrolling conceptualizes each iteration of a
066
      certain hand-crafted optimization algorithm as a layer in a neural network, inducing a feed-forward
067   network. In contrast, the generic L2O does not impose any prior mathematical knowledge on the
068   optimizer to be learned. Instead, it crudely parameterizes the optimizer with a recurrent neural
069   network and learns it through end-to-end training.
070
      Challenges in applying L2O to decentralized optimization. While algorithm unrolling and generic
071
      L2O have shown strong empirical successes (Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Lv et al., 2017; Wichrowska
072
      et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2018; Metz et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020a; Micaelli & Storkey, 2021; Metz
073   et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2023; Gregor & LeCun, 2010; Moreau & Bruna, 2017; Chen et al., 2018; Liu
074   & Chen, 2019; Ito et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2016; Zhang & Ghanem, 2018; Adler & Öktem, 2018;
075   Solomon et al., 2019), their direct application to decentralized settings poses several challenges.
076
077      • Memory bottleneck. Algorithm unrolling requires storing a neural network with as many layers
078        as optimization iterations, which easily exhausts available memory, especially as the number of
079        unrolled iterations increases. This situation becomes even more challenging in decentralized
080
           optimization, where researchers are usually constrained to test their algorithms on simple target
           problems (Nedic et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2015; Shi et al., 2015a; Yuan et al.,
081
           2018b) within dozens of nodes (Chen & Sayed, 2012; Wang et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2015b; Xu
082
           et al., 2015; Nedic et al., 2017), and we shall maintain much more memory than traditional
083        decentralized algorithms during the training stage.
084
         • Vast search space. While more memory-efficient, the generic L2O faces a significant challenge:
085
           how to parameterize an optimizer properly. The parameter space of the generic L2O is vast, ren-
086        dering its training highly ineffective. This challenge is exacerbated in decentralized optimization
087        due to the extra need to learn inter-node interaction (e.g., when and with whom to communicate,
088        and what information to exchange), thereby further expanding the parameter space.
089
         • Consensus constraint. In decentralized optimization, nodes must achieve consensus through
090        local communication with immediate neighbors. This consensus constraint incurs significant
091        complexity to L2O, as nodes, with trained optimizers, must adapt their behaviors to ensure
092        convergence towards a common solution despite lacking global communication.
093
         • Weak generalization. L2O often struggles to generalize to out-of-distribution tasks. Without
094        theoretical guidance, it is challenging for L2O to handle the more sophisticated loss landscapes
095        encountered in unseen problems. Refer to Section 4.4 and Figures 7 and 8 in Chen et al. (2022)
096        This challenge naturally carries over in decentralized L2O.
097
098   Contributions. To address the aforementioned challenges, this paper proposes a novel Mathematics-
099   inspired Learning-to-optimize framework for Decentralized optimization (MiLoDo). MiLoDo adopts
100
      the generic L2O strategy to circumvent memory bottlenecks. However, instead of learning an
      optimizer directly from an unconstrained parameter space, we introduce mathematical structures
101
      inherent in decentralized optimization to guide MiLoDo’s learning process. This significantly narrows
102
      the parameter space, enforces asymptotic consensus among nodes, and ensures generalization across
103   out-of-domain tasks. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
104
105      • We derive fundamental mathematical conditions that learning frameworks for decentralized
106        optimization that converges quickly to the exact solution should satisfy. These conditions will
107        serve as guiding principles for training decentralized optimizers that can achieve consensus and
           optimality.

133   Related work on decentralized L2O. Previous studies have designed various L2O algorithms for
134   decentralized optimization, most of which are based on algorithm unrolling. Kishida et al. (2020) and
135   Ogawa & Ishii (2021) use algorithm unrolling to learn decentralized algorithms for the consensus
136   problem. Noah & Shlezinger (2023) unrolls D-ADMM, while Wang et al. (2021) unrolls prox-DGD
137   and PG-EXTRA. Hadou et al. (2023) proposed an unrolled algorithm called U-DGD. However,
138   none of these learned optimizers can operate for more than 100 iterations due to the explosive
139   memory cost caused by algorithm unrolling. Additionally, a recent work Zhu & Lu (2023) employs
140   a reinforcement learning agent to control local update rules through a coordinator linked with all
141
      computing nodes, which is not fully decentralized. More related works on decentralized optimization
      and learning-to-optimize are discussed in Appendix A.

      We propose MiLoDo, a mathematics-inspired L2O framework for decentralized optimization. With
532
      its mathematics-inspired structure, the MiLoDo-trained optimizer can generalize to tasks with varying
533
      data distributions, problem types, and feature dimensions. Moreover, MiLoDo-trained optimizer
534   outperforms handcrafted optimizers in convergence rate. MiLoDo currently learns separate parameters
535   specifically for each neighbor relationship (i.e., communication links), but training a set of shared
536   parameters across all nodes could simplify the framework. This would align MiLoDo with message-
537   passing graph neural networks, which are more scalable and inherently permutation-equivariant,
538   making them well-suited for graph-based problems. This presents a promising future direction.

787   A    M ORE RELATED WORK
788
789   Learning to optimize. The concept of L2O dates back to the 1990s (Thrun & Pratt, 1998). Different
790   L2O approaches exist: Plug-and-Play (PnP) (Venkatakrishnan et al., 2013) approximates expensive
791   functions in traditional algorithms; algorithm unrolling (Gregor & LeCun, 2010; Moreau & Bruna,
792   2017; Chen et al., 2018; Liu & Chen, 2019; Ito et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2016; Zhang & Ghanem,
793   2018; Adler & Öktem, 2018; Solomon et al., 2019) models the entire procedure as a neural network,
794   effective for domains like image/signal processing; generic L2O (Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Lv et al.,
795
      2017; Wichrowska et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2018; Metz et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020a; Shen et al.,
      2020; Harrison et al., 2022; Micaelli & Storkey, 2021; Metz et al., 2018; 2022a;b; Jain et al., 2023;
796
      Liu et al., 2023), which is more related to this paper, parameterizes update rules using current states,
797
      enabling flexibility across applications. Some other studies also atempts to learn machine learning
798   models to accelerate the discrete problems solving(Bengio et al., 2021).
799
800   Decentralized optimization. Decentralized optimization has been extensively studied, dating back
801
      to early algorithms like decentralized gradient descent (DGD) (Nedic & Ozdaglar, 2009; Yuan et al.,
      2016), Diffusion (Lopes & Sayed, 2008; Chen & Sayed, 2012), and dual averaging (Duchi et al.,
802
      2011) from the signal processing and control communities. These were followed by primal-dual
803
      methods such as ADMM variants (Shi et al., 2014), explicit bias-correction techniques (Shi et al.,
804   2015a; Yuan et al., 2018b; Li et al., 2019), Gradient-Tracking (Xu et al., 2015; Di Lorenzo & Scutari,
805   2016; Nedic et al., 2017). More recently, decentralized stochastic gradient descent (DSGD) (Lian
806   et al., 2017) has gained significant attentions in deep learning. For non-smooth optimization problems,
807   effective algorithms like PG-EXTRA (Shi et al., 2015b), PG-Exact-Diffusion (Yuan et al., 2018a),
808   and PG-Gradient-Tracking (Alghunaim et al., 2020) utilize the proximal gradient method to solve
809   them. A unified decentralized framework is developed in Alghunaim et al. (2020) and Xu et al. (2021)
      to unify various decentralized algorithms. All these algorithms are driven by expert knowledge.

1004
1005   E.1    T RAINING STRATEGIES
1006
1007   Initialization strategies. We consider two initialization strategies for MiLoDo training: random
1008   and special initialization. In random initialization, all learnable parameters {θM,i , θS,i , θU,i }i∈V are
1009   randomly initialized using PyTorch defaults. In special initialization, parameters are initialized to
1010   mimic traditional decentralized algorithms by setting the weights of the final affine layers to zero and
1011
       biases to desired output values. Specifically, given the gossip matrix W = (wij )n×n and learning
1012
       rate γ utilized in Exact-Diffusion, biases in the final affine layers for pki , p̃ki,j,1 , pki,j,2 are initialized
1013   as γ, ln(wij /(2γ)), wij /2, respectively. Applying ln(·) accounts for Exponential activation.
1014   We would like to remark that each of the two initialization strategies has its pros and cons. With
1015   random initialization, the objective function value is likely to blow up quickly, leading to excessively
1016   large gradients or meaningless values (e.g., inf/NaNs). With special initialization, it may be too close
1017   to local minima, such that MiLoDo might not gain enough advantage over handcrafted algorithms.
1018   Multi-stage training. As discussed above, random initialization of the MiLoDo optimizer results in
1019   numerical instability during training. To address this issue, we initially teach the model to optimize
1020   within a few iterations by using a short training length such as (KT , K) = (5, 10). As the model starts
                                                 P5               P10
1021   exhibiting desired behaviors, such as k=1 f (x̄k ) > k=6 f (x̄k ), we progressively increase the
1022   training length. This iterative process is repeated across several stages until reaching a training length
1023   of (KT , K) = (20, 100). Empirically, employing multi-stage training also enhances performance for
1024   special initialization. With multi-stage training, both initialization approaches yield MiLoDo-trained
1025   optimizers with comparable performance, prompting our focus on special initialization due to its
       reduced warmup stages.


                                                                           19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       The multi-stage training method draws inspiration from curriculum learning in reinforcement learning,
1027   where models are first trained on easier tasks before progressing to more difficult ones. In our context,
1028   we initially train the optimizer with a short iteration length, which is easier to train. For instance,
1029   consider the extreme case of training an optimizer for just one iteration – this is analogous to training
1030   a simple one-layer neural network, which is inherently easier. Once this simpler stage is complete,
1031   we gradually extend the iteration length. Starting with easier tasks and then using the results of this
1032   stage to initialize the next stage with more complex tasks (longer iteration processes in our context)
1033   significantly improves training stability compared to starting directly with difficult tasks from scratch.
