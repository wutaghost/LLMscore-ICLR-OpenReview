                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Recent advancements in Large Vision Language Models (LVLMs) have revolu-
013
               tionized how machines understand and generate textual responses based on visual
               inputs. Despite their impressive capabilities, they often produce "hallucinatory"
014
               outputs that do not accurately reflect the visual information, posing challenges in
015
               reliability and trustworthiness. Inspired by test-time augmentation, we propose
016            a simple, training-free method termed RITUAL to enhance robustness against
017            hallucinations in LVLMs. RITUAL introduces random image transformations as
018            complementary inputs during the decoding phase. Importantly, these transforma-
019            tions are not employed during the training of the LVLMs. This straightforward
020            strategy reduces the likelihood of hallucinations by exposing the model to varied
021            visual scenarios, enriching its decision-making process. While transformed images
022            alone may initially degrade performance, we empirically find that strategically com-
023            bining them with the original images mitigates hallucinations. Specifically, in cases
024
               where hallucinations occur with the original image, the transformed images help
               correct misinterpretations by adjusting the probability distribution. By diversifying
025
               the visual input space, RITUAL provides a more robust foundation for generating
026
               accurate outputs. Notably, our method works seamlessly with existing contrastive
027            decoding methods and does not require external models or costly self-feedback
028            mechanisms, making it a practical addition. While extremely simple, RITUAL
029            significantly outperforms existing contrastive decoding methods across several
030            object hallucination benchmarks, including POPE, CHAIR, and MME.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) (Dai et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c;b; Bai
037   et al., 2023) have emerged as a pivotal technology, enabling machines to interpret complex visual
038   scenes and generate contextually appropriate textual descriptions. These models integrate and process
039   inputs from both visual and linguistic domains, offering unprecedented possibilities in applications
040   ranging from video content creation (Brooks et al., 2024) to assistive technologies (Team et al., 2023;
041   OpenAI, 2023).
042   Despite their potential, LVLMs are often criticized for generating "hallucinatory" content (Li et al.,
043   2023c; Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023) – outputs that appear plausible but
044   do not faithfully reflect the visual inputs. This gap in reliability and trustworthiness is particularly
045   concerning for sensitive applications such as medical diagnosis (Zhou et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023d),
046   surveillance (Wu et al., 2024; Hasan et al., 2024), and autonomous driving (Li et al., 2024).
047
      The challenge primarily arises from the difficulty in maintaining alignment between the visual inputs
048   and textual outputs, given the complexity of training such models to accurately interpret and narrate
049   visual data. Although several strategies have been developed to mitigate these issues, they often
050   require extensive additional training (Jiang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b; Gunjal et al., 2023; Liu
051   et al., 2023a; Sun et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Yin et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Zhai et al., 2024;
052   Yue et al., 2024), sophisticated feedback mechanisms (Yin et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Kim et al.,
053   2024; Sun et al., 2023), or reliance on auxiliary models (Zhao et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024; Deng
      et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b), which can complicate deployment and scalability.

      We present a simple, training-free approach termed RITUAL, which leverages random image trans-
079
      formations to complement the original image and enhance models’ robustness. RITUAL is designed
080
      to address the issue of visual hallucination by employing a dual-input strategy that integrates both
081   the original and a randomly transformed image. The final prediction is an ensemble of the indi-
082   vidual predictions generated from both the original and augmented images. This provides a more
083   comprehensive visual context, enriching the model’s exposure to a diverse array of visual scenarios,
084   thereby enhancing the robustness and reliability of text generation. Much like how humans refine
085   their understanding by observing objects from different angles and under varying conditions, our
086   approach fosters cognitive flexibility (Ionescu, 2012) – the ability to adapt to new situations and
087   switch between tasks or concepts.
088   Our approach builds on the principles of Test-Time Augmentation (TTA) (Zhang et al., 2022;
089   Shanmugam et al., 2021; Pérez et al., 2021), a technique that improves model robustness and
090   generalization at inference time by using multiple augmented versions of an input. TTA is particularly
091   useful in scenarios where the test set exhibits high variance or when inputs contain ambiguities. By
092   generalizing over these uncertainties, TTA helps reduce model sensitivity to minor perturbations,
093   leading to more reliable predictions.
094   Importantly, these image transformations are applied only during the inference phase, not during
095   training. As demonstrated in Fig. 1 (Left), using transformed images (V (T ) ) alone initially degrades
096   performance compared to using the original image (V), due to the introduction of novel visual
097   artifacts. However, when the original and transformed images are combined (V + V (T ) ) significantly
098   enhances the quality and reliability of the model’s outputs. While neither the original image nor
099   the transformed image alone may provide an accurate response, their combination reduces the
100   likelihood of a hallucinated response and increases the chances of a correct answer. In some cases,
101   the transformed image strongly aligns with the correct answer, resulting in accurate predictions.
102   Our experiments evaluate RITUAL across several benchmarks, including POPE (Rohrbach et al.,
103   2018), CHAIR (Li et al., 2023c), and both MME-Hallucination and MME-Fullset (Fu et al., 2024). De-
104   spite its simplicity, RITUAL effectively reduces hallucination across these benchmarks and enhances
105   the general capabilities of LVLMs. Moreover, RITUAL consistently outperforms existing contrastive
106   decoding baselines (Leng et al., 2023; Favero et al., 2024) in all tested benchmarks. RITUAL is
107   also compatible with current contrastive decoding methods, and when used in conjunction, it further
      amplifies the improvements over these methods.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Hallucinations in LVLMs. LVLMs are susceptible to visual hallucinations, in which the generated
111   text descriptions include objects or details entirely irrelevant from the given image. A range of
112   methods has been introduced to address the issue by additional training (Gunjal et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
113   2023a; Sun et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Yin et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Zhou
114   et al., 2023b; Zhai et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024). While these approaches offer promise, they often
115   face practical limitations due to their dependence on additional data and extensive training periods.
116
      In response to these limitations, training-free approaches have gained traction. These models aim
      to refine the model output by self-feedback correction (Lee et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023), providing
117
      additional knowledge using auxiliary models (Wan et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024;
118
      Yang et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024), and contrastive decoding (Leng et al., 2023; Favero et al., 2024;
119   Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), which refines the model outputs by contrasting the conditional
120   probability of textual responses given the original visual input versus a distorted visual input. Our
121   work adopts a unique approach by applying random image transformations to complement the
122   original image. This provides a wide range of visual contexts, aiming to mitigate hallucinatory visual
123   explanations without the complexities of extra models, additional training, or data requirements.
124
125   Image augmentations for model robustness. Image augmentations (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar,
126   2019; Perez, 2017) have long been recognized as a crucial technique for improving model robust-
127   ness, particularly in computer vision and multimodal tasks. By introducing variations in input data,
128
      augmentations help models generalize better to unseen scenarios, reduce overfitting, and improve
      performance in the presence of noise or ambiguous inputs. In the training phase, data augmentation
129
      techniques (Cubuk et al., 2018; Taylor & Nitschke, 2017), such as those used in SimCLR (Chen et al.,
130
      2020) and BYOL (Grill et al., 2020), enhance the diversity of training data by applying transforma-
131   tions like rotations, flips, and crops. This encourages the model to learn more generalizable features,
132   improving performance on unseen data. At inference time, test-time augmentation (TTA) (Zhang et al.,
133   2022; Shanmugam et al., 2021; Pérez et al., 2021) further improves model robustness. TTA applies
134   multiple transformations to the input image during testing, generating varied predictions which are
135   then averaged or ensembled to produce a more reliable output. By exposing the model to diverse
136   perspectives of the same input, TTA reduces sensitivity to noise and ambiguity, stabilizes predictions
137   on difficult cases, and serves as a cost-effective ensembling method without requiring additional
138   model training. Our approach builds on these concepts by using random image transformations during
139
      inference to provide a broader visual context, reducing hallucinations in vision-language models.
      By combining predictions from both the original and transformed images, our method enhances
140
      robustness without requiring extra training or data.

      3     A PPROACH :        RITUAL
144
      We present a simple decoding method that can be applied in an online manner during token generation.
145
      Our method is training-free, does not require external models or a costly self-feedback mechanism,
146   and remains compatible with existing contrastive decoding techniques (Leng et al., 2023; Favero
147   et al., 2024). An overview of our method is illustrated in Fig. 2.
148
149   3.1   LVLM F ORMULATION
150
      Vision-Language Alignment. LVLM takes a visual input and a textual query as inputs, where
151
      the visual input provides contextual visual information to assist the model in generating a rele-
152   vant response to the textual query. Initially, a vision encoder (e.g., ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020),
153   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), etc.) processes raw images to extract visual features. These features
154   are then projected into the language model’s input space using a vision-language alignment mod-
155   ule (e.g., Q-Former (Li et al., 2023a), linear projection (Liu et al., 2023c), etc.), resulting in a
156   set of visual tokens, V = {ν0 , ν1 , . . . , νN −1 }. Concurrently, the textual inputs are tokenized into
157   T = {τN , τN +1 , . . . , τN +M −1 }. The visual and textual tokens are concatenated to form an input
158   sequence of length N + M .
159
160   Model Forwarding. The LVLM, parametrized by θ, processes the concatenated sequence of visual
161
      and textual tokens. This process is formalized as:

178   ηt given a visual input, a textual query T , and previously generated tokens η<t . When conditioned
179   on the original image V, the probabilities for Blue (correct) and Red (hallucinated) responses are
180   similar, which can lead to the hallucinated response being easily sampled. RITUAL leverages an
181   additional probability distribution conditioned on the transformed image V (T ) , where the likelihood
182   of hallucination is significantly reduced. Consequently, the response is sampled from a linear combi-
183   nation of the two probability distributions, ensuring more accurate and reliable outputs.
184   where H denotes the sequence of output hidden states from the final layer of LVLM. These hidden
185   states H are used to compute the logits (or probabilities) for predicting the next tokens.
186
187   Response Generation. The LVLM generates responses auto-regressively, employing a causal
188   attention mask to ensure each subsequent token is predicted based solely on the preceding tokens.
189   Each response token is generated by sampling from the following probability distribution:
190                                         ηt ∼ pθ (ηt |V, T , η<t ).                                   (2)
191
      where ηt denotes the response token being generated at timestep t, and η<t indicates the sequence of
192   tokens generated up to timestep (t − 1). This generative process is iteratively continued, appending
193   each newly predicted token to the sequence, until the termination of the sequence. By default, Greedy
194   Decoding is used. Alternatively, decoding strategies such as Beam Search (Wiseman & Rush, 2016),
195   Nucleus Sampling (Holtzman et al., 2019), or DoLa (Chuang et al., 2023) can be employed.

      3.2   M ITIGATING H ALLUCINATIONS IN LVLM S WITH R ANDOM I MAGE T RANSFORMATIONS
198
      Visual hallucinations in LVLMs can occur during the decoding phase when tokens are selected based
199   on erroneous probability distributions that do not align with the visual inputs. Our approach aims to
200   mitigate these visual hallucinations with a simple yet effective modification to the input handling.
201
202
      We first randomly apply common image transformations (e.g., Crop, Flip, Color jitter, etc.) to the
203
      original visual input V, This results in a transformed version of the visual input, V (T ) .
204                                      V (T ) = T (V; ω), where ω ∈ Ω.                             (3)
205   Here, T represents a specific transformation function selected randomly from a set of image trans-
206   formations. The parameter ω represents the specific parameters of the transformation, drawn from a
207   distribution Ω that governs the selection and nature of the transformation applied.
208
      During the decoding phase, rather than using V (T ) alone — which we found to impair performance —
209
      we utilize both the original and transformed images. This dual-input approach significantly reduces
210
      the likelihood of hallucinatory outputs, as illustrated in Fig. 1, and improves the accuracy of the
211   model’s predictions. The sampling equation in Eq. (2) is updated as follows:
212
213                             ηt ∼ pθ (ηt |V, T , η<t ) + αpθ (ηt |V (T ) , T , η<t ).                 (4)
214   Here, α is a balancing hyperparameter, adjusting the contribution of the transformed input relative to
215   the original. To promote output diversity and avoid deterministic behavior, we choose to sample from
      a multinomial distribution rather than merely selecting the most probable output via argmax.

      In practice, we employ a predefined set of image transformations to enhance model robustness,
217   divided into geometric and appearance transformations. Geometric transformations, such as flipping,
218   small random rotations, and cropping, simulate different viewing angles, orientations, and focus
219   areas, enhancing the model’s ability to generalize across varied perspectives and object positioning.
220   Appearance transformations, including color jitter and Gaussian blur, adjust brightness, contrast,
221   and saturation to account for lighting variations and sensor noise, increasing resilience to image
222   imperfections. Together, these transformations introduce meaningful variations that better prepare the
223   model for real-world image scenarios, improving its flexibility and performance.

      4       E XPERIMENTS

      4.1       E VALUATION S ETUP
228
229   Throughout our experiments, we set hyperparameter configuration at α = 3. For random image
230   transformation, we use flip (horizontal & Vertical), rotate, color jitter, Gaussian blur, and crop. In all
231   experimental tables, the base refers to the standard decoding, which directly samples the response
232   token from the softmax distribution.1
233
234   LVLMs. We integrate RITUAL with two state-of-the-art LVLMs: LLaVA-1.5 ( (Liu et al., 2023c)
235   and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2024). Both models incorporate Vicuna 7B (Chiang et al., 2023) as
236   their language decoding mechanism. LLaVA-1.5 utilizes two-layer MLP to align image and text
237   modalities and InstructBLIP employs the Q-Former (Li et al., 2023a) to efficiently bridge visual
238   and textual features using a fixed number of tokens (e.g., 32). Note that the adaptability of RITUAL
239   extends beyond these two models and is model-agnostic. It can be compatible with a wide range of
240   off-the-shelf LVLMs.
241
242   Baselines. Our method aims to reduce hallucinations in LVLMs by modifying model’s decoding
243   process without relying on external models, costly self-feedback mechanisms, or additional train-
244   ing. To align with these criteria, we select baseline methods that meet these requirements. Recent
245   contrastive decoding methods fit well within this scope, and we establish two primary baselines:
246   VCD (Leng et al., 2023) and M3ID (Favero et al., 2024). Both VCD and M3ID aim to mitigate object
247
      hallucinations by increasing the influence of the reference image over the language prior. This is
      achieved by contrasting output distributions derived from both original and distorted visual inputs.
248
      We also include DoLa (Chuang et al., 2023) as a baseline, which employs a novel decoding strategy
249
      that contrasts logits from earlier and later layers of the transformer architecture. This amplifies factual
250   knowledge stored in the upper layers while suppressing linguistic patterns from the lower layers that
251   may lead to hallucinations. Additionally, we report results from OPERA (Huang et al., 2023), which
252   mitigates hallucinations in LVLMs via an over-trust penalty and retrospection allocation. In contrast
253   to all other methods, OPERA uses beam search during response generation, contributing to its higher
254   performance. We include it for comparison purposes due to its demonstrated effectiveness in reducing
255   hallucinations. All baselines were reproduced within our evaluation setting for consistency.
256
257   Benchmarks. (1) POPE (Li et al., 2023c) frames hallucination assessment as a binary classifi-
258   cation task using yes/no questions about object presence (e.g., "Is there a dog in the image?"). It
259   evaluates 500 MS-COCO images with questions based on actual objects or nonexistent objects. The
260   benchmark contains three subsets (random, popular, and adversarial), addressing object prevalence
261   and co-occurrences. (2) MME (Fu et al., 2024) is a comprehensive LVLM benchmark assessing
262   14 subtasks, including object hallucination through tasks like object existence, count, position,
263   and color. These tasks are framed as binary yes/no questions. (3) CHAIR (Rohrbach et al., 2018)
264
      evaluates the proportion of words in captions that correspond to actual objects in an image, using
      ground-truth captions and object annotations. It has two variants: (i) per-sentence (CHAIRS ) is
265
      defined as |{sentences with hallucinated objects}|/|{all sentences}|. (ii) per-instance (CHAIRI ) is
266
      defined as |{hallucinated objects}|/|{all objects mentioned}|. We randomly select 500 images from
267   the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) validation set and conduct image captioning with the prompt "Please
268   describe this image in detail".

518   5       D ISCUSSION
519
      In this study, we have introduces RITUAL, a simple approach aimed at enhancing the reliability
520
      of LVLMs. We found that while relying solely on random image transformations can degrade
521
      performance, they contribute to mitigating hallucination when used in combination with the original
522   image. Inspired by these findings, RITUAL employs random image transformations to provide
523   LVLMs with a broader visual context, thereby improving the model’s robustness against hallucinatory
524   outputs. RITUAL significantly outperforms existing approaches on multiple hallucination benchmarks
525   without requiring additional model training or complex external mechanisms. Moreover, RITUAL is
526   also compatible with existing contrastive decoding techniques, further enhancing performance.
527   Case Study & Limitations. As shown in Fig. 6, the effectiveness of specific transformations, such as
528   cropping, can depend heavily on the nature of the query. Cropping might adjust the position of critical
529   spatial regions, enhancing relevance in the above case while detracting from it in the below case.
530   To illustrate this point, while certain transformations might excel under particular conditions, their
531   efficacy can diminish in others. To mitigate this variability, we opt for a randomized selection from a
532   pool of transformations, allowing for a broader range of adaptability across different image and query
533   contexts. Recognizing the need for a more tailored approach, we introduce a self-feedback mechanism,
534
      referred to as RITUAL+, which dynamically selects image transformations that are aware of the image-
      query context. As shown in Tab. 7, this method demonstrates a modest improvement in performance
535
      by aligning transformations more closely with the specifics of each query. Implementation details
536
      and detailed results are in Appendix F.2. In future work, we aim to develop a more sophisticated
537   mechanism that can more effectively determine the most suitable transformations based on the
538   interplay between the image and its associated query. 2

      A    L ARGE V ISION L ANGUAGE M ODELS (LVLM S )
811
812   Recent approaches to integrating visual and language modalities in LVLMs commonly leverage pre-
813   trained uni-modal models. They include an adaptive interface to bridge pre-trained visual encoders
814   with Large Language Models (LLMs), facilitating efficient information synthesis across modalities.
815   These interfaces generally fall into two main categories: (1) Learnable query-based methods, exem-
816   plified by Q-Former (Li et al., 2023a) in InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2024) and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al.,
817   2023), a set of learnable query tokens is employed to capture visual signals through cross-attention.
818   These tokens are optimized to distill the essential visual information and input it into the LLM for
819   further processing. (2) Projection layer-based methods, such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c;b) and
820
      Shikra (Chen et al., 2023), use projection layers to transform visual features into the input space
      of LLMs. This mapping ensures seamless integration between pre-trained visual representations
821
      and the LLMs, enabling the latter to interpret the visual content effectively. Both strategies translate
822
      visual features into formats that the LLMs can understand. Despite their efficacy, LVLMs still en-
823   counter challenges with hallucination, which we aim to mitigate in this work. We specifically use two
824   representative models, LLAVA and InstructBLIP, for experiments.

848   Contrastive decoding (Leng et al., 2023; Favero et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024)

861   C    A DDITIONAL C ONTEXT ON T EST-T IME AUGMENTATION
862
863   Test-Time Augmentation (TTA) is a technique designed to improve model robustness and generaliza-
      tion during inference by using multiple augmented versions of an input. By applying transformations


                                                             16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      such as rotations, flips, or crops, TTA reduces uncertainty and enhances accuracy through prediction
865   averaging or ensembling across these variations. This is particularly useful for tasks with high input
866   variability or noise, as it allows the model to handle perturbations that might otherwise degrade
867   performance.
868
      TTA works by exposing the model to different transformations of the same image, enabling it to make
869
      predictions for each variation as well as the original input. These predictions are then aggregated
870
      to produce a more stable and reliable final output, effectively mitigating the impact of noisy or
871   ambiguous test data. This process also helps stabilize predictions in cases where the input lies near a
872   decision boundary, offering a more balanced perspective by incorporating diverse views of the image.
873
874
      An additional advantage of TTA is that it serves as a lightweight ensembling method. While traditional
      ensembling requires training multiple models, TTA leverages a single model to generate predictions
875
      on different augmented versions of the input. This approach achieves the benefits of an ensemble
876
      without the computational overhead, making it a cost-effective solution.
877
878   Our method builds upon this foundation by applying simple random transformations—such as
879   rotations, flips, or noise—during inference. These augmentations provide the model with a broader
880
      visual context, allowing it to capture a wider range of potential interpretations while reducing the risk
      of hallucinated outputs. By combining the predictions from both the original and transformed images,
881
      we enhance the model’s robustness without requiring additional training or complex architectures.

1091   F.3     C OMPARISON OF G AUSSIAN N OISE IN VCD VS G AUSSIAN B LUR IN RITUAL
1092
1093   We employed the standard image augmentation (e.g., crop, flip, rotate, color jitter, and Gaussian blur)
1094   techniques commonly used to enhance model robustness by generating diverse views (Chen et al.,
1095   2020; Grill et al., 2020). The key idea is that applying these augmentations at an appropriate intensity
1096
       can provide diverse perspectives without compromising the underlying semantics of the image. The
       reason why Gaussian noise in VCD distorts the image rather than acting as a useful augmentation
1097
       boils down to the intensity of the application. While we will delve into the specifics with experimental
1098
       data later, the summary is that low-intensity Gaussian noise can serve as an effective augmentation.
1099   However, as the noise level increases, it shifts from providing beneficial diversity to distorting the
1100   image, which negatively impacts performance. In brief, Gaussian blur can distort the image if applied
1101   too strongly, just as Gaussian noise can serve as a diverse view generator if applied lightly. It all
1102   comes down to the intensity. Applying Gaussian noise at a low level can indeed offer a diverse
1103   perspective without compromising the image’s semantics. Conversely, excessive Gaussian blur can
1104   distort the image.
1105   To illustrate this, we conducted an experiment using Gaussian noise as a transformation within the
1106   RITUAL framework on the POPE-COCO-random setup. As shown in Table 10, Gaussian noise at
1107   low intensity (noise step=50) acts as a form of multiview augmentation, leading to positive outcomes.
1108   However, with a noise step of 999, the image became excessively distorted, impairing performance.
1109   In contrast, we also conducted VCD with Gaussian blur in Table 11. As the sigma value increases,
1110   the blur becomes stronger, leading to more significantly distorted images. In VCD, this increased
1111   distortion enhances the model’s focus on the visual part of the image relative to the language part,
1112   helping to mitigate object hallucination. The stronger the image distortion, the greater the emphasis
1113
       on the visual component. As a result, when the sigma value is set to 100, the distortion is more
       pronounced than at sigma 0.5, leading to a more substantial effect in VCD. In conclusion, both
1114
       Gaussian noise and blur can provide diverse perspectives when applied moderately. However, if
1115
       applied excessively, they are more likely to be perceived as distortions.

1275   The VCD setup prompts the model with an additional instruction, "Please answer this question with
1276   one word," at the end of each question. As shown in Tab. 16, this constraint biases the model towards

       shorter, more definitive answers, with a notable inclination towards "No" (with a No ratio of 60In
       contrast, our evaluation setup does not include this "one word" constraint. Instead, we allow the
1278
       model to generate more detailed responses that include explanations. This approach tends to yield a
1279
       balanced “Yes” and "No" ratio. Consequently, our method evaluates whether the generated output
1280   contains a "Yes" or "No" along with the explanation, rather than restricting the output to a single
1281   word for simplicity in evaluation. To provide more context, we have included a Tab. 16 that presents
1282   the performance metrics under different settings with the respective "Yes" ratios. By removing the
1283   "one word" constraint, we aim to capture more nuanced and contextually rich responses from the
1284   model, which we believe provides a more comprehensive assessment of its capabilities. Additionally,
1285   since there is no official implementation of M3ID, we reimplemented it and reported the results based
