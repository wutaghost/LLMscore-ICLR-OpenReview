009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have exhibited an impressive capability to per-
012            form reasoning tasks, especially if they are encouraged to generate a sequence
013            of intermediate steps. Reasoning performance can be improved by suitably com-
014            bining multiple LLM responses, generated either in parallel in a single query, or
015            via sequential interactions with LLMs throughout the reasoning process. Existing
016            strategies for combination, such as self-consistency and progressive-hint-prompting,
017            make inefficient usage of the LLM responses. We present Hint Marginalization, a
018
               novel and principled algorithmic framework to enhance the reasoning capabilities
               of LLMs. Our approach can be viewed as an iterative sampling strategy for forming
019
               a Monte Carlo approximation of an underlying distribution of answers, with the
020
               goal of identifying the mode — the most likely answer. Empirical evaluation on
021            several benchmark datasets for arithmetic reasoning demonstrates the superiority
022            of the proposed approach.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   As Large Language Models (LLMs) have increased in size, they have demonstrated increasing rea-
028   soning abilities (Brown et al., 2020), despite not being explicitly trained to reason (Wei et al., 2022a).
029   In particular, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has become standard for eliciting these abilities,
030   either through few-shot examples (Wei et al., 2022b) or via a triggering sentence such as “Let’s think
031   step by step” (Kojima et al., 2022). Nevertheless, although LLMs often produce correct reasoning
032   steps, they struggle with higher-level planning (Saparov and He, 2022), motivating researchers to
033
      explore strategies to remedy this deficiency. An effective solution is to sample several chains-of-
      thoughts and take the most common answer as the final vote, an approach called Self-Consistency
034
      (CoT+SC) (Wang et al., 2023). However, despite its impressive empirical performance, the gains
035
      quickly plateau on many benchmarks, often with no improvement after five samples (Aggarwal et al.,
036   2023). Thus, a more complex reasoning strategy appears necessary.
037
038   One promising direction involves encouraging LLMs to iteratively refine their reasoning, like humans
039
      often do (Shinn et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023). However, Huang
      et al. (2024) demonstrate that the capability and effectiveness of LLMs’ self-correction is largely
040
      overstated in the existing literature due to the use of oracle labels for determining stopping crite-
041
      ria (Shinn et al., 2023), unfair experimental protocols (Du et al., 2023), and sub-optimal initial prompt
042   design (Madaan et al., 2023). Moreover, the review/feedback prompts employed in these approaches
043   are often long and complex, and include detailed, intricate, hand-crafted examples, tailored for
044   specific domains or benchmarks. In spite of such extensive prompt engineering, Huang et al. (2024)
045   observe that most of these approaches perform worse than self-consistency in a fair evaluation setting.
046
      In this paper, we propose a novel iterative strategy called Hint Marginalization (HM) that offers a
047
      more principled and practical way of reasoning with refinement. We consider a setting where we can
048   conduct LLM calls sequentially or in parallel. Our method does not make major changes to the initial
049   prompt in later calls, and we do not need extensive prompting effort to invoke an LLM’s review of
050   the previous answers. The process starts by constructing an initial distribution of answers using CoT.
051   In subsequent rounds, we present the unique answers from the previous round to the LLM as hints in
052   the prompt. This leads to a new collection of answers, which we use to refine the answer distribution
053   via a marginalization process. By maintaining a distribution and performing marginalization, we
      reduce sampling variance and make more efficient usage of the LLM calls.

      We make the following contributions:
055
056   • We introduce a novel iterative hint-based refinement strategy for reasoning with LLMs, with the
057     key differentiator that the method maintains and updates a distribution over answers. Our work
058     highlights that an LLM can indeed derive benefit from self-reflecting on distributions of its past
059     answers when attempting arithmetic reasoning tasks, without a need to resort to extensive prompt
060     design or hand-crafted examples.
061   • Via multiple experiments with GPT-3.5 Turbo (Brown et al., 2020), GPT-4 Turbo (OpenAI et al.,
062     2024), and the cost efficient GPT-4o-mini, we show that Hint Marginalization leads to improved
063     arithmetic reasoning performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines for the same number of
064     LLM calls and comparable token cost. We conduct experiments carefully to ensure there is no
065
        evaluation bias in favour of methods that employ refinement. Notably, out of 18 experimental
        scenarios (3 LLMs, six datasets), we observe a statistically significant increase in accuracy in 14.
066
067   • We show in the experiments that previous hint-based strategies such as Progressive-Hint-Prompting
068     (PHP) Zheng et al. (2023) can be combined successfully with our method. In experiments that
069
        ensure fair evaluation, PHP combined with Self Consistency (PHP+SC) often struggles to outper-
        form the much simpler SC. In contrast, when combined with our approach, the resultant PHP+HM
070
        procedure achieves statistically significant accuracy improvements in most experimental settings.

073   2    P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
074
075   Let x be a question or a task in natural language, described in one or more sentences. Its true answer
076   is denoted y, which can take different forms depending on the context, such as a number, a True/False
077
      boolean variable, or an option (a)/(b)/(c) from a multiple-choice set. Potentially, we have access
      to a (small) set of triplets I={(xj , zj , yj )}K
                                                      j=1 corresponding to semantically-similar questions xj ,
078
      answers yj , and rationales zj . Each rationale zj is a sequence of short sentences that describe the
079
      step-by-step reasoning process leading to the answer yj .
080
081   We assume that we can query the LLM in series or in parallel. Our task is to design a strategy
082   for prompting the LLM and combining the responses to provide an answer ŷ for the question x.
083
      Performance is measured in terms of the average accuracy of the response, i.e., E[1(ŷ = y)] for the
      indicator function 1.

086   3    M ETHODOLOGY
087
088   When presented with the question, an LLM produces a random answer ỹ, drawn from an internal
089   distribution that is dependent on the prompt and the LLM’s parameters. To avoid notational clutter, we
090   suppress these dependencies and denote this distribution by p(ỹ|x). This distribution is analytically
091   intractable but one can sample from it directly by prompting the LLM and subsequently collecting its
092
      answer.
093   The reasoning ability of the LLM, i.e., the probability of producing the correct answer, is improved
094   by careful construction of the prompt. For example, an encouragement to produce an explana-
095   tion/rationale in the form of a sequence of short sentences to describe the step-by-step reasoning
096   process has been shown to ameliorate LLMs’ performance significantly compared to direct prompt-
097   ing (Wei et al., 2022b). We denote the provided rationale as z, so the response of the LLM is a pair
098
      (z, ỹ). If rationale-annotated in-context examples are available, then reasoning can be improved by
      incorporating in the prompt a (small) set in the form of triplets I={(xj , zj , yj )}K
                                                                                           j=1 .
099
100   Viewing the LLM response as a sample from the distribution, we can hypothesize that, if the LLM is
101   capable of effective reasoning for the presented question, the mode of the distribution is most likely
102   to be the correct answer. We would therefore like to extract the mode. One approach is to sample,
103   either in parallel or sequentially, multiple LLM responses (each containing a rationale and answer).
104
      We can then select the answer corresponding to the Monte Carlo estimate of the mode by taking a
      majority vote over the sampled responses (Wang et al., 2023).
105
106   It has been observed that LLM output can be improved via a refinement or self-reflection pro-
107   cess (Zheng et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023). In
      this process, the LLM is provided with its previous response, and asked to take it into account, or


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      criticize it, before producing a refined response. One form of refinement is to provide the previous
109   answer as a hint (“The answer is close to” or “The answer is probably”).
110
111   This observation is the cornerstone of our proposed methodology. Rather than seeking the mode of
112
      the original distribution p(ỹ|x), we construct a sequence of distributions {pr (ỹ|x)}r>1 , where each
      successive distribution in the sequence is constructed via a refinement process using samples from the
113
      previous distribution, as the number of interactions with the LLM, r, grows. This refinement process
114
      involves marginalization over the LLM’s previous answers, which are used as hints in the current
115   iteration. We initialize p1 (ỹ|x) = p(ỹ|x), i.e., we start with the distribution of answers obtained
116   from the first interaction with the LLM at r = 1. Our hypothesis is that the probability of the correct
117   answer, pr (y|x), increases with r, so the mode of a distribution later in the sequence, i.e., r > 1, is
118   more likely to be correct than the mode of p(ỹ|x).
119
120
      3.1   I NTUITION
121
122
      We now provide an example to illustrate why marginalizing over hints should make the mode of the
123
      inference distribution more likely to be the correct answer. Suppose that we are presented with a
124   binary question x with answer, say, y = 1, and let us say that the probability of the correct answer,
125   with no hints, is initially relatively low, p(ỹ=1|x) = 0.4. However, when we provide the correct
126   answer as a hint, the LLM is much more likely to answer correctly, p(ỹ=1|x, Hint(y=1)) = 0.8.
127   Hinting at the incorrect answer 0 also strongly tilts the LLM towards that answer, but crucially, with
128   slightly less probability, p(ỹ=0|x, Hint(y=0)) = 0.6. This is not unexpected or unusual, because it
129   is often easier to see the truth of a statement in hindsight (or verify rather than solve unaided). With
130   our proposed hint marginalization procedure, the updated distribution of the answer would be:
131
132           p2 (ỹ=1|x) = p1 (ỹ=1|x)p(ỹ=1|x, Hint(y=1)) + p1 (ỹ=0|x)p(ỹ=1|x, Hint(y=0))
133                       = 0.4 × 0.8 + (1 − 0.4) × (1 − 0.6)
134                       = 0.56 > 0.4.
135
136   Not only is the probability higher than before, but crucially, the mode of the distribution now aligns
137   with the right answer (y = 1).
138   More generally, this augmentation will be observed whenever the flow of probability mass into
139   the correct answer exceeds the flow of probability mass outPof the correct answer. The flow out is
140   p1 (ỹ=y|x)(1 − p(ỹ=y|x, Hint(y))), whereas the flow in is y′ ̸=y p1 (ỹ=y ′ |x)p(ỹ=y|x, Hint(y ′ )).
141   Since we expect p1 (ỹ=y|x, Hint(y))) to be close to 1, the flow out is likely to be small. By
142   contrast, we might anticipate that when the LLM is presented with an incorrect hint, it can often
143   ignore it to a large extent. Let us assume that p(ỹ=y|x, Hint(y ′ )) > cp1 (ỹ=y|x) for all y ′ for
144   some positive constant c < 1. Then the flow in exceeds cp1 (ỹ=y|x)(1 − p1 (ỹ=y|x)). Thus, if
145   p(ỹ=y|x, Hint(y))) > 1 − c(1 − p1 (ỹ=y|x)), the mass assigned to the correct answer will increase.
146   For example, consider p1 (ỹ=y|x)) = 0.4 and c = 0.3. Then we need p(ỹ=y|x, Hint(y))) >
147
      1−0.3 × (1−0.4) = 0.82.
148   We also note that repeated application of this procedure is further advantageous, which motivates the
149   iterative version of our algorithm. We formalize this intuition into a general procedure and provide an
150   algorithm for approximating these distributions next.

      3.4      D ISCUSSION
271
272   Intuitively, some hints are more promising or useful than others for solving a given reasoning task.
273   Our HM framework naturally defines the usefulness of a hint y ′ at the end of r-th iteration by the
274   numerical value of pr (y ′ |x), and subsequently weights the answers generated using the hint y ′ at the
275   (r+1)-th iteration by this value, while updating the distribution of answers in eq. 1.
276   We also note that the HM framework is agnostic to the choice of prompts and is generally applicable
277   to any advanced prompting techniques, since those methods combined with SC can be used for
278   initializing p1 (ỹ|x) for subsequent Hint Marginalization. Our contribution is thus orthogonal to
279   prompt engineering approaches.

      4.1      B ENCHMARKS
284
285   We evaluate the proposed HM algorithm on six arithmetic benchmark datasets: AddSub (Hosseini
286   et al., 2014), consisting of math word problems requiring addition and/or subtraction for solution;
287   MultiArith (Roy and Roth, 2015), containing math problems requiring multiple reasoning steps for
288   solutions; SingleEQ (Koncel-Kedziorski et al., 2015), consisting of questions which can be solved
289   using a single equation; SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021), containing math word problems of varying
290   structures; GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), consisting of grade school level math word problems; and
291   AQuA (Ling et al., 2017), containing algebraic word problems. In summary, AddSub and SingleEq
292   contain easier problems, whereas the tasks in MultiArith, SVAMP, GSM8K, and AQuA are more
293
      challenging since they require multi-step reasoning for a solution. Although these arithmetic problems
      are relatively simple for humans, LLMs often struggle in solving these types of problems (Patel et al.,
294
      2021). All these benchmarks are available under open-source licenses 1 .

      (i.e., PHP+SC). Whenever results are available for other recent iterative refinement methods such as

459   5   R ELATED WORK
460
461   Our proposed method can be situated within a larger literature that aims to improve LLMs’ reasoning
462   ability through iterative refinement of chains-of-thought. These works primarily differ in the strategy
463   used to refine the reasoning.
464   In Progressive Hint Prompting (PHP) (Zheng et al., 2023), chains-of-thought are repeatedly generated,
465   each time providing the previous answers as hints to the LLM. However, it is unlikely that all the
466   hints are equally useful for solving the task, and this approach does not have any mechanism for
467   differential treatment of hints. Two subsequent works propose alternative prompts to steer the answer
468   away from, rather than closer to, the hint. Progressive Rectification Prompting (Wu et al., 2024)
469   proposes a prompt of the form “The answer is likely not <hint>”, whereas Deliberate-then-Generate
470   (Li et al., 2023) assumes an error was committed and asks the LLM to identify and correct the mistake.
471
      Our method could likely be adapted to those variants by replacing PHP’s hinting prompt by their
      criticism prompt in our procedure. Also closely related to PHP and our work is Hint-before-Solving
472
      Prompting (Fu et al., 2024), which triggers the LLM to generate a “hint” before solving a problem.
473
      These hints are key ideas that can be used to solve a problem, such as a mathematical formula or
474   a general direction. Unlike PHP or our work, however, there is no iterative aspect where the hints
475   depend on previous attempts at answering the question. Contrary to our work, none of these methods
476   target the refinement of the distribution of answers.
477
      Besides these works that aim to iteratively improve answers, there is a growing literature of works
478
      that seek to achieve the same goal more explicitly using verbal criticism, at the cost of increased
479
      complexity. Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) incorporates a prompt where the LLM self-criticizes
480   its answer, before being queried again with this reflection. Generative Agents (Park et al., 2023) uses
481   a similar procedure, albeit in the context of an agent interacting with an environment. CRITIC (Gou
482   et al., 2023) is a more general framework, where the criticism prompt can make use of external tools
483   like a web search engine to offer grounded corrections. In a different direction, Self-Convinced
484   Prompting (Zhang et al., 2023a) and Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) expand on Self-Refine by
485   adding extra modules such as a separate answer encoder, or separating the evaluation and self-
      reflection dimensions of criticism into separate modules. Other related approaches include multi-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      round debate (Du et al., 2023) and consensus via weighted voting mechanism (Chih-Yao Chen
487   et al., 2023). Recent studies have, however, cast doubt on the ability of LLMs to self-criticize
488   effectively (Huang et al., 2024; Tyen et al., 2023), leading researchers to consider using a separately
489   trained LLM as the critic.
490
      REFINER (Paul et al., 2023) fine-tunes a separate critic by supervised learning on examples perturbed
491
      by hand-designed rules and GPT-3.5 Turbo. Retroformer (Yao et al., 2023) and RL4F (Akyürek
492
      et al., 2023) consider fine-tuning of the critic using reinforcement learning instead, which allows for
493   a more precise alignment with the task of improving answers. As applied to chain-of-thought, these
494   methods have in common that they offer a procedure that takes a chain-of-thought and analyzes it to
495   produce an improved version with a potentially different answer. A crucial difference between these
496   methods and our approach is that these algorithms generate a sequence of chains-of-thought, whereas
497   we propose to refine the distribution of answers.
498
      Finally, our work can be seen within the greater context of trying to improve chain-of-thought
499   reasoning within large language models. In existing work, several directions for improving CoTs
500   are considered, including construction of better prompts to aid the LLM in reasoning (Fu et al.,
501   2023; Zhang et al., 2023b), fine-tuning with CoTs (Zelikman et al., 2022) so that the LLMs learn to
502   reason, and effective exploration strategies for multi-hop reasoning (Besta et al., 2023; Yao et al.,
503   2023). A recent survey by Chu et al. (2023) provides a comprehensive overview of these techniques.
504   Our contribution is orthogonal to these prompting techniques since we consider improving the
505   distribution of answers iteratively rather than focusing on individual CoTs. Novel variants of HM can
506   be constructed by using these methods for initialization.

      6    L IMITATIONS
510
511   Our Hint Marginalization approach relies on hints having an impact on the answers provided by the
512   LLM. Our focus was not on prompt design, so in our experiments, we employed the hint structure
513
      employed by (Zheng et al., 2023): “The answer is close to <hint>”. While this hint can be effective
      for arithmetic reasoning tasks, it is less suitable for other types of answers, such as proper names or
514
      options for a multiple choice questions (e.g. AQuA). This motivates research into a more general
515
      strategy for effectively structuring the prompt to guide an LLM towards a known or likely answer,
516   while allowing some flexibility to navigate to other answers.
517
518   In terms of computation, although the LLM calls in each round of our method can be performed in
519
      parallel, the rounds themselves must be performed sequentially. In settings where there is no bound
      on how many LLM calls can be executed in parallel, CoT+SC can thus make more effective use of
520
      the parallelism to reduce latency.
521
522
523   7    C ONCLUSION
524
525
      This work presents a novel algorithmic approach, Hint Marginalization, to enable an LLM to solve a
526
      reasoning task by iteratively refining its inference distribution. The proposed algorithm addresses the
527
      issue of the diminishing marginal utility of extra LLM calls for Self-Consistency. Hint Marginalization
528   focuses on the distribution over the answers at each stage and assigns weights to the hints accordingly,
529   concentrating on promising hints. The marginalization procedure improves sample efficiency. The
530   experimental results, over a range of arithmetic benchmarks and several LLM variants, provide strong
531   evidence that the approach leads to improved reasoning for the same budget of LLM calls, compared
532   to Self-Consistency and other state-of-the-art refinement approaches.
533
      The work can be extended in several directions. Our experiments focus on arithmetic reasoning tasks,
534   and employ a hint in the prompt that is most suitable for such tasks. For other types of problems,
535   alternative hint structures are more natural, and likely to lead to better performance. For example,
536   in tasks that require a verbal response, the prompt could incorporate “verbal criticism”, based on
537   one of the approaches detailed in Section 5. In the current version of the procedure, we assign the
538   same number of LLM calls to each unique answer from the previous round. Investigating more
539   efficient strategies to allocate LLM calls non-uniformly to different hints could be another worthwhile
      direction.

742   8.3   VALIDITY OF O UR A SSUMPTIONS
743
744
745
746
      In order to motivate HM in Section 3.1, we considered a hypothetical question x, whose answer
      ỹ ∈ {0, 1} is binary and the true answer is 1. We define α=p1 (ỹ=1|x), and γ=p(ỹ=0|x, Hint(y=1))
747
      and δ=p(ỹ=1|x, Hint(y=0)).
748
749   We assumed that γ is very small and δ > 0. Both of these assumptions are empirically supported by
750   analysis of obtained results from the PHP (Zheng et al., 2023) technique.
751   As an example, using GPT-4 Turbo on the entire GSM8K dataset, the empirical frequency of obtaining
752   an incorrect answer conditioned on an immediate correct hint is 0.0179. This suggests that assuming
753   γ to be very small is justified. On the other hand, the empirical frequency of obtaining a correct
754   answer conditioned on a previous incorrect hint is 0.3159, which supports the assumption of having a
755   strictly non-zero value for δ. Similar results are obtained for other datasets and LLMs examined in
      our experiments.

931   8.8    D ISCUSSION ON THE INTUITION OF USING HINTS
932
933   As Zheng et al. (2023) note, hinting allows humans to check their answers and improve upon their
934   previous solution to a given problem. We conjecture that in selecting its arithmetic answer, the LLM
935   assigns attention to the hint and in particular, its understanding of the phrase “close to x” provides
936   additional bias towards selecting a number that is closer to the suggested hint.
937   Additional support for the benefit of hinting is presented by (Fu et al., 2024). In their work, the LLM
938   is encouraged via in-context examples to prepare a hint before solving the problem. The developed
939   hints are more general than those we employ in our work, but the performance improvement in
940   reasoning is indicative of the potential value of a hint in directing an LLM towards a good solution.
941   Further evidence is provided by (Agrawal et al., 2024). In their work, a hint is generated using
942   a weaker LLM. This is observed to yield a performance improvement over multiple math-based
943
      reasoning datasets.
