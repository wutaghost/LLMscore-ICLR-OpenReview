009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) framework efficiently scales large language models
012            (LLMs) by selectively activating expert subnetworks, reducing computational costs.
013            However, current MoE methods are costly in computation and include additional
014            expert modules that require extra training data for tuning, leading to instability
015            in the optimization process. To address these issues, we introduce F REE -M O E,
016            a tuning-free MoE method that leverages pre-trained LLMs’ inherent ability to
017            generalize across a wide range of tasks and domains. F REE -M O E dynamically acti-
018            vates experts based on specific domains, achieves improvements while 1) requiring
019
               no extra model parameters and 2) being completely tuning-free. Specifically,
               we design the DOWP Alg., a Domain-Oriented Weight Purification Algorithm
020
               that purifies the weights in hidden layers and selects the optimal domain-specific
021
               experts of domain-specific experts in the hidden layers of the LLM to optimize
022            activation decisions. The activated DSS-Experts, Domain-Specific Subnetwork
023            Experts,can thereby concentrate on specialized task generation, outperforming the
024            corresponding original model. Moreover, F REE -M O E incorporates a multi-level
025            trainable router that activates only the most relevant subnetworks during task,
026            effectively minimizing unnecessary inference computations. Comprehensive evalu-
027            ations reveals that the DOWP Algorithm consistently achieves general performance
028            gains of 2% to 3%, reaching up to 6.8% across datasets like MMLU, HumanEval,
029            GSM8K, and etc. Additionally, when integrated into F REE -M O E framework, our
030
               method demonstrates a cumulative improvement of 1.11% in average. Findings
               indicate that F REE -M O E not only enhances overall computational efficiency but
031
               improves the model’s adaptability across any field that encompassed in contempo-
032
               rary language generation model benchmarks, and can be seamlessly applied to any
033            transformer-based LLMs. Code for this project will be released in reachable future.

      The demand for more powerful Large Language Models(LLMs) drives relentless expansion (Kaplan
      et al., 2020). Yet, the bigger the LLMs, the more they consume: computational resources, time, and
040
      energy, which in turn limits their scalability and application efficiency (Patterson et al., 2021; Strubell
041
      et al., 2019). Integrating the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) framework with LLMs has emerged as a
042   highly promising strategy for tackling these challenges, driving significant advancements in the field
043   (Shazeer et al., 2017). Models like Switch Transformer and GShard have demonstrated that model
044   capacity can be scaled to trillions of parameters without a proportional increase in computational
045   costs during inference, setting new benchmarks in various natural language processing tasks (Fedus
046   et al., 2022; Lepikhin et al., 2020). The key innovation of MoE lies in its use of sparse activation,
047   where only a portion of the network is activated for any given input, allowing the model to possess
048   greater capacity without a corresponding increase in computational demand (Zhou et al., 2022).
049   In a broader sense, the classical MoE approach operates by routing different inputs to specialized
050   subnetwork experts within the model. These expert subnetworks are typically based on diverse
051   foundational models, such as support vector machines (Collobert et al., 2002), Gaussian processes
052   (Tresp, 2000), or hidden Markov models (Jordan & Jacobs, 1994), where the architecture enables
053   the model to specialize in handling a wide range of tasks, improving its capacity to address complex
      and heterogeneous problem domains. Overall, the pioneering work on MoE introduced mechanisms

079   Despite the tremendous potential of the MoE framework, its practical application continues to
080   encounter several significant challenges. A key limitation is that maximizing the benefits of MoE
081   typically requires large-scale, high-quality training data, especially in scenarios where data acquisition
082   is costly or the quality of available data is inconsistent(Lepikhin et al., 2020). Previous work has
083
      focused on the field of non-trainable token-choice gating(Roller et al., 2021; Zuo et al., 2022;
      Gururangan et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2023; Kudugunta et al., 2021), exploring ways to achieve
084
      complete load balancing through specific gating mechanisms without requiring additional gating
085
      network parameters, thereby improving computational efficiency. However, stepping beyond this
086   perspective, we need to reevaluate how to find more effective tuning-free methods within existing
087   architectures to achieve a higher degree of tuning-free operation. Meanwhile, pre-trained LLMs,
088   which have undergone large-scale training, possess strong capabilities to adapt to various tasks and
089   may offer a new perspective. Therefore, we pose the following question:
090
091   Is it possible to leverage the subnetwork structures within the hidden layers of pre-trained LLMs to
092       enhance task-specific performance and optimize the allocation of computational resources?
093
094
      Through deeper investigation, we find that LLMs inherently function as implicit expert net-
095
      works—while LLMs do not explicitly employ an MoE framework, the subnetworks activated by their
096   hidden layers exhibit specialized behavior across different tasks and inputs. This enables pre-trained
097   LLMs to adapt seamlessly to diverse tasks in tuning-free condition, particularly, without the need
098   for external experts or the addition of significant new parameters. All that is required is an adaptive
099   activation mechanism based on weight purification.
100
      Building on this insight, we propose F REE -M O E, a novel tuning-free mixture of experts architecture
101
      designed to leverage the existing subnetwork expert mechanisms within pre-trained LLMs. The key
102   principle of F REE -M O E is its self-directed purification process, where it identifies the input domain
103   and selectively purifies the subnetwork weights, retaining only those relevant to the specific task.
104   This allows F REE -M O E to adaptively activate only the expert subnetworks most pertinent to the
105   given task. Compared to Sparse MoE and Domain-Mapping & Random Gating MoE architectures,
106   F REE -M O E achieves improved efficiency and task-specific accuracy without increasing model
107   complexity, thereby significantly reducing the computational burden typically associated with large
      models, as shown in Figure 1.

      Specifically, purification represents the core solution for integrating the MoE framework into LLMs
109   without additional tuning. This process leverages impurity removal and filtration mechanisms,
110   applied during purification, to perform domain-driven expert selection. The core concept involves
111   purifying the hidden layer weights by identifying domain-relevant features from the input, retaining
112   high-contributing expert weights while filtering out irrelevant ones. This approach fully exploits
113   the parameter sharing and hierarchical latent feature structures of pre-trained LLMs, achieving
114   efficient utilization without tuning. In contrast to the simplistic expert selection strategies in Sparse
115   MoE (Fedus et al., 2022) or the full expert activation of Dense MoE (Wu et al., 2022; Dua et al.,
116   2022), purification significantly enhances both the accuracy and effectiveness of expert selection,
117
      optimizing model performance for task-specific contexts. Additionally, we define domain-specific
      expert derived from the hidden layers after purification, explicitly revealing the latent expertise within
118
      the pre-trained LLM subnetworks and enabling optimized task-oriented configurations. Compared to
119
      the auxiliary load-balancing loss mechanisms in ST-MoE(Zoph et al., 2022), these domain-specific
120   experts offer superior adaptability, facilitating more efficient resource allocation for specialized tasks
121   and substantially improving task-specific model performance. Lastly, we introduce a Trainable
122   Router that dynamically monitors task requirements and domain characteristics, enabling real-time
123   domain identification and efficient allocation of computational resources. Unlike the static mappings
124   employed in Domain Mapping & Random Gating MoE (Ren et al., 2023), our method employs a
125   more granular and dynamic domain activation strategy. This not only enhances operational efficiency
126   but also leads to notable improvements in real-world task performance.
127   In this work, we present several key innovations that position F REE -M O E as a novel approach
128   to leveraging LLMs within the MoE framework, addressing critical challenges in computational
129   efficiency and task-specific performance:
130
131
      • Innovative MoE Architecture F REE -M O E: F REE -M O E utilizes the latent subnetwork structures
        within pre-trained LLMs, eliminating the dependency of traditional MoE on large-scale, high-
132
        quality training data, and enabling adaptation to multi-task requirements without the need for
133
        fine-tuning.
134
      • Purification Mechanism on Weights: This mechanism dynamically selects and activates subnet-
135
        work weights relevant to specific tasks, significantly improving the accuracy of expert selection. By
136     purifying the hidden layer weights in pre-trained LLMs, the domain expertise of its subnetworks is
137     made explicit.
138   • A Multi-Level Trainable Dynamic Router: We introduce a novel trainable router capable of
139     real-time monitoring of task requirements and domain characteristics, dynamically identifying
140     domains and efficiently allocating computational resources, particularly demonstrating significant
141     advantages in multi-task scenarios.
142   • Achieved Significant Performance Improvements Across Multiple Datasets: Our approach
143     achieved performance gains of 2% to 3% on datasets such as MMLU, MBPP, and GSM8K.
144     Integrated into the F REE -M O E architecture, cumulative improvements of 1.11% validate the
145     effectiveness and practicality of the method.

150   2.1   T HE M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
151
152   The Mixture of Experts (MoE) introduces multiple specialized expert networks, selectively ac-
153
      tivating a subset of experts during each inference to maintain model capacity while significantly
      reducing computational costs (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1994; Chen et al., 1999; Tresp,
154
      2000; Rasmussen & Ghahramani, 2001). MoE has demonstrated substantial potential in scaling
155
      model size and enhancing performance. Sparse gating MoE layers strike an effective balance between
156   computational cost and model capacity by selecting only a small number of experts for computation
157   per input (Shazeer et al., 2017; Riquelme et al., 2021). The GShard framework by Lepikhin et al.
158   (2020) successfully trained ultra-large-scale models using conditional computation and automatic
159   sharding techniques. Following this, Fedus et al. (2022) introduced the Switch Transformer, which
160   further enhances training and inference efficiency through a simplified expert routing mechanism.
161   To improve expert selection strategies, Zhou et al. (2022) proposed Expert Choice Routing, which
      optimizes expert selection and load balancing to enhance model performance and resource utilization.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      In the realm of multi-task learning, Ma et al. (2018) developed the Multi-Gated Mixture-of-Experts
163   (MMoE) model, which implements task-specific gating mechanisms to capture inter-task relation-
164   ships. The commonality across MoE methods lies in leveraging sparse activation and expert selection
165   to achieve a trade-off between model capacity and computational efficiency.
166
167   2.2   I NTERPRETABILITY OF LLM S M ECHANISM
168
169   Interpretability of LLMs Mechanism represents a pivotal subfield within the broader study of LLM
170   interpretability, focusing on uncovering the internal mechanisms of language models (Anthropic,
171   2023; Bricken et al., 2023). The dominant approach conceptualizes LLMs as “circuits”, examining
172   neural network hidden representations through feature visualization techniques. This approach has
173
      led researchers to identify larger functional components and uncover three key phenomena: Branch
      Specializatio, Weight Hierarchization and Equivariance (Voss et al., 2021; Petrov et al., 2021; Olah
174
      et al., 2020). Recent research has increasingly shifted towards single-layer and multi-layer attention
175
      models. In single-layer attention models, structures such as binary tables and skip-trigram tables can
176   be derived from the model’s weights. In multi-layer Transformer models, the concept of “induction
177   heads” has emerged—modules formed by the combination of attention heads from different layers,
178   designed to learn patterns within context (Elhage et al., 2021). The core of this research lies in
179   treating neural network components as circuit-like functional modules, while emphasizing that the
180   “circuits” within LLMs are not static but exhibit a high degree of dynamism and adaptability.

189   Clustering-based Classification. Consider multiple datasets DA , DB , . . . , Dn , where each dataset
190   corresponds to a distinct domain. These datasets are collectively aggregated to form a comprehensive
191   main knowledge domain set, denoted as D = {DA , DB , . . . , Dn }, where each Di corresponds to a
192   specific domain that encapsulates specialized knowledge relevant to the given tasks. For a given task
193   T , the algorithm identifies the most suitable main knowledge domain Dj by computing the posterior
194   probability P (D | T ), which quantifies the likelihood of the task T belonging to each main domain
195   D. This selection process is expressed as:

707

708   process is designed to preserve both local and global relationships in the model.
709
710   Global Context Preservation in Self-Attention. The Self-Attention mechanism ensures that every
711
      token in the input sequence can attend to all other tokens, capturing global dependencies. This
      operation is described by:

718

719

720   to propagate global information and maintain context across the entire sequence of patches. As a

744   critical feature transformations in the MLP are unaffected by the scaling process, ensuring that the
