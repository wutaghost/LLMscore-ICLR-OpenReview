009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Monitoring sleep states is essential for evaluating sleep quality and diagnosing
012            sleep disorders. Traditional manual staging is time-consuming and prone to sub-
013            jective bias, often resulting in inconsistent outcomes. Here, we developed an auto-
014            mated model for sleep staging to enhance diagnostic accuracy and efficiency. Con-
015            sidering the characteristics of polysomnography (PSG) multi-lead sleep monitor-
016            ing, we designed a multimodal sleep state classification model, MSSC-BiMamba,
017            that combines an Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) mechanism with a Bidirec-
018
               tional State Space Model (BSSM). The ECA module allows for weighting data
               from different sensor channels, thereby amplifying the influence of diverse sensor
019
               inputs. Additionally, the implementation of bidirectional Mamba (BiMamba) en-
020
               ables the model to effectively capture multi-dimensional features and long-range
021            dependencies of PSG data. The developed model demonstrated impressive per-
022            formance on sleep stage classification tasks on the ISRUC-S3 and ISRUC-S1
023            datasets, respectively, including healthy and unhealthy sleep patterns. Our model,
024            which can effectively handle diverse sleep conditions, is the first to apply Bi-
025            Mamba to sleep staging with multimodal PSG data, showing substantial gains in
026            computational and memory efficiency over traditional Transformer-style models.
027            This method enhances sleep health management by making monitoring more ac-
028            cessible and extending advanced healthcare through innovative technology.

      1   INTRODUCTION
032
033
      Sleep, a fundamental pillar of human health and well-being, involves a complex series of stages in
      cognitive function and overall wellness Peter-Derex et al. (2015). However, modern lifestyles, work-
034
      related stress, and environmental factors have adversely impacted the sleep duration, continuity,
035
      and quality of many individuals, leading to a concerning rise in sleep problems, especially among
036   children The Lancet Diabetes (2024). The accurate classification of sleep stages and assessment
037   of sleep health are vital for the effective diagnosis and treatment of sleep disorders. Traditionally,
038   polysomnography (PSG) has been considered the gold standard for monitoring sleep stages, but it
039   requires specialized equipment and is typically confined to clinic settings. This limitation highlights
040   the pressing need for more accessible and less intrusive approaches to sleep analysis.
041
      Recent advances in machine learning, particularly deep learning, have paved the way for innovative
042   approaches to sleep stage classification Loh et al. (2020). Among these, the Transformer model
043   Vaswani et al. (2017), known for its effectiveness in handling sequential data primarily in the field
044   of natural language processing, offers promising avenues for sleep data analysis due to its ability
045   to capture long-range dependencies in time-series data. However, the Transformer’s self-attention
046   mechanism, which evaluates interactions across all sequence positions, significantly slows down as
047   data length increases, and hence suffers from low efficiency due to heavy computational demands
048   and limited scalability with large datasets. This necessitates more efficient algorithms or adaptations
049   of existing models to optimize performance in tasks like sleep stage classification and anomaly
050
      detection without compromising computational resources.
051   Modern state space models (SSMs) particularly effective in capturing long-range dependencies, have
052   evolved significantly with recent innovations. The Mamba model Gu & Dao (2023) advances SSMs
053   by integrating time-varying parameters and a hardware-aware algorithm, enhancing training and
      inference efficiency dramatically. Despite its superior scalability and potential as an alternative to

057   significantly boosting prediction accuracy for sequential tasks. This bidirectional enhancement en-

      2.1   C LASSIFICATION OF SLEEP STAGES
086
087
      Traditionally, the classification of sleep stages is performed by experienced sleep specialists or
088
      physicians, who categorize 30-second or specific time intervals of polysomnographic (PSG) data
089
      into various sleep stages according to established sleep assessment criteria Liang et al. (2012). This
090   process is known to be both laborious and time-consuming Malhotra et al. (2013). In contrast, ma-
091   chine learning algorithms may require significantly shorter durations to accomplish the same clas-
092   sification task Chang et al. (2019), while experts have traditionally extracted features from the time
093   domain Sharma et al. (2017), frequency domain Zoubek et al. (2007), and time-frequency domain
094   Al-Salman et al. (2019) to preprocess data for machine learning methods. The selection of effective
095   features remains a critical issue in enhancing the classification performance of traditional classifiers.
096   The emergence of deep learning has further advanced the automation process, enabling direct ex-
097   traction of complex features from raw data, thus reducing the preprocessing workload Sekkal et al.
098   (2022). Therefore, an increasing number of deep learning methods are being applied to sleep stage
099   classification tasks, including CNNs Phan et al. (2018), RNNs Zhu & Liang (2020), GCNs Jia et al.
100   (2021), and others. However, CNNs may struggle to capture long-term dependencies in time-series
101   data effectively. While RNNs are capable of handling sequential data and capturing long-term de-
102   pendencies in time series, they are prone to issues such as vanishing or exploding gradients during
103   training. Additionally, GCNs exhibit lower efficiency in processing large-scale graph data.
104   Ji et al. Ji et al. (2023; 2024) proposed several sleep stage classification models. These studies
105   transformed PSG data into the frequency domain space, combined with time-domain signals as
106   input to the model. They then utilized 3D CNNs, 2D CNNs, and GCN networks for classification,
107   achieving satisfactory results. Compared to these methods, we propose an approach that solely
      utilizes time-domain signals but achieves superior performance.

126   2.2       S TATE SPACE MODEL AND M AMBA
127
      Recent research advances have led to a surge of interest in state-space models (SSMs). Originating
128
      from the classical Kalman filtering model Kalman (1960), SSMs excel at capturing long-term de-
129
      pendencies. Researchers have proposed several SSM-based methods, such as Structured State Space
130   Sequence Models (S4) Gu et al. (2021) and S4D Smith et al. (2022), for handling sequential data
131   from various tasks and modalities, particularly in modeling remote dependencies. Due to their con-
132   volutional and near-linear computations, they exhibit high efficiency in processing long sequences.
133   In recent studies, Mamba Gu & Dao (2023) integrated time-varying parameters into SSMs and pro-
134   posed a hardware-aware algorithm to achieve highly efficient training and inference. Compared to
135   the original Mamba, the Bidirectional Mamba offers higher efficiency and performance. Based on
136   this structure, we explore the temporal PSG signals.

      3.2       T HE E FFICIENT C HANNEL ATTENTION MODULE
155
156   In recent years, the introduction of channel attention mechanisms into convolutional blocks has gar-
157   nered widespread attention. A prominent method in this domain is SENet (Squeeze-and-Excitation
158   Networks)Hu et al. (2018), which learns channel attention for each convolutional block and signifi-
159   cantly enhances the performance of various deep CNN architectures. While these methods achieve
160   high accuracy, they often lead to increased model complexity and substantial computational burden.
161   To address these challenges, Wang et al.Wang et al. (2020) proposed the Efficient Channel Atten-
      tion (ECA) module. The ECA module avoids dimensionality reduction and effectively captures

163   commendable results in the field of image processing.

283   4.2    E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
284
      According to the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) guidelines Berry et al. (2012), the
285
      sleep-wake cycle can be classified into five stages: wakefulness (W), rapid eye movement (REM)
286
      sleep, and three non-rapid eye movement (NREM) stages, namely N1, N2, and N3. The NREM
287   stages are further categorized based on the depth of sleep, with N1 representing the lightest stage of
288   sleep, N2 being a deeper stage, and N3 corresponding to the deepest stage of sleep, also known as
289   slow-wave sleep.
290
      For the classification of sleep stages, we utilized all data from the S3 dataset and 50 participants with
291
      odd-numbered IDs from the S1 dataset (Table 1), and selected PSG data from ten channels, including
292
      EEG (F3-A2,C3-A2,O1-A2, F4-A1,C4-A1,O2-A1), EOG ( LOC-A2,ROC-A1), ECG(X2) and Chin
293   EEG (X1).
294
295   To prepare the data for analysis, we first applied a downsampling technique, reducing the sampling
296
      rate from 200 Hz to 100 Hz. This step helps to minimize computational complexity while preserving
      the essential information in the signal. Subsequently, we performed slicing operations on the data
297
      from each channel. The data input into the network comprised 1000 points (30 s), about 33.33 Hz.
298
      The ISRUC-S3 dataset employed a 10-fold cross-validation, while the ISRUC-S1 dataset utilized a
299   25-fold cross-validation. Cross-validation was independently performed for each subject to ensure
300   that data from the same subject did not appear simultaneously in both the training and validation
301   sets. The experimental setup and hyperparameters are summarized in Appendix A.

      dataset do not perform as well on the S1 dataset, possibly due to differences between the S3 and
391
      S1 datasets, as well as the smaller size of the S3 dataset. Overall, the model’s robust performance
392   across various metrics underscores its potential for reliable and accurate sleep stage classification.

395   6   CONCLUSIONS
396
397   In this study, we have demonstrated the exceptional generalization capabilities and computational
398   efficiency of the Mamba-driven deep learning framework in handling complex polysomnography
399   (PSG) data, underscoring its potential to revolutionize sleep medicine. By assisting doctors in anno-
400
      tation, this innovative approach can transform medical experts from front-end operators to back-end
      supervisors, streamlining diagnostic workflows and optimizing resource utilization. Future research
401
      will focus on detailed analysis and model training for different sleep disorders. By incorporating
402
      rich features and optimizing algorithms, we aim to enhance the model’s predictive accuracy and
403   enable precise, real-time sleep stage recognition. Developing accurate, reliable, and efficient tools
404   for sleep stage recognition and sleep disorder diagnosis will not only enhance patient care but also
405   deepen our understanding of the intricate mechanisms underlying sleep health. This will provide
406   valuable insights and support for the diagnosis and management of sleep disorders, ultimately im-
407   proving patient care and outcomes. We remain committed to exploring the potential of the Mamba
408   framework, striving for breakthroughs in sleep medicine. Our ongoing efforts will continue to push
409   boundaries, driving progress in this field and paving the way for a brighter future.
