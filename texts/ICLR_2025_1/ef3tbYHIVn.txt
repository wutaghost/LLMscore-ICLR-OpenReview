009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Many real-world discovery problems, including drug and material design, can
012            be modeled within the bandit optimization framework, where an agent selects
013            a sequence of experiments to efficiently optimize an unknown reward function.
014            However, classic bandit algorithms operate on fixed finite or continuous action sets,
015            making discovering novel designs impossible in the former case, and often leading
016            to the curse of dimensionality in the latter, thus rendering these methods impractical.
017            In this work, we first formalize the generative bandit setting, where an agent wishes
018
               to maximize an unknown reward function over the support of a data distribution,
               often called data manifold, which implicitly encodes complex constraints (e.g., the
019
               geometry of valid molecules), and from which (unlabeled) sample data is available
020
               (e.g., a dataset of valid molecules). We then propose Diffusion Posterior Sampling
021            (D IFF PS), an algorithm that tackles the exploration-exploitation problem directly on
022            the learned data manifold by leveraging a conditional diffusion model. We formally
023            show that the statistical complexity of D IFF PS adapts to the intrinsic dimensionality
024            of the data, overcoming the curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional settings.
025            Our experimental evaluation supports the theoretical claims and demonstrates
026            promising performance in practice.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Many real-world discovery problems, spanning drug discovery (Schneider, 2018), material de-
032   sign (Guo et al., 2021), and circuit design (El-Turky & Perry, 1989) among others, can be framed as
033   bandit optimization (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020). In this context, an agent aims to optimize an
034   unknown (black-box) reward function r over an experiments space Ω. Crucially, since r is unknown,
035   and evaluating r(x) for x ∈ Ω is typically expensive, the agent needs to select wisely a sequence of
036   experiments x1 , . . . , xT that balances efficient exploration to learn r, and exploitation of its current
037   belief to select promising maximizers, a challenge known as the exploration-exploitation dilemma.
038   Historically, bandit algorithms were first devised for fixed and finite action sets, where the agent
039
      is given a set Ω = {x1 , . . . , xA }, which does not allow discovering novel actions (e.g., molecules,
      previously unknown to the algorithm designer). More recently, bandit optimization algorithms have
040
      been extended to continuous action spaces (Srinivas et al., 2009; Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011), e.g.,
041
      Ω = RD , where decision-making occurs in a known or learned D-dimensional data representation
042   space. Unfortunately, for many real-world problems, including most scientific discovery applications,
043   the ambient dimensionality D is very high, causing bandit algorithms to incur statistical complexities
044   too large to be practical (Djolonga et al., 2013; Kandasamy et al., 2015). In other words, these
045   algorithms suffer the curse of dimensionality as their practical and theoretical sample complexities,
046   i.e., number of experiments needed to discover maximizers, heavily depend on D. Moreover, in
047   most real-world problems, such as molecular design, most points (or actions) in Ω = RD do not
048   correspond to valid molecules. Thus, fixed finite action spaces are too restrictive for discovery or too
049   large to enumerate, while typical continuous spaces lead to the curse of dimensionality and cannot
050
      easily distinguish between valid experiments and invalid ones, e.g., an invalid molecule.
051   To address this issue, we introduce the generative bandit setting, aiming to close the gap between
052   finite and continuous action sets by combining their advantages: the ability to discover valid actions
053   unknown a priori to the algorithm designer, while tackling the curse of dimensionality in high-
      dimensional real-world problems (Sec. 3). While previous works attempt to solve the bandit problem

      on a learned low-dimensional latent space (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Grosnit et al., 2021), in
055   generative bandits the action space is unknown to the agent and is defined as the support of a possibly
056   complex data distribution Px approximately learnable through sample data, e.g., a dataset of known
057   molecules. This set, namely Ω = supp(Px ), typically called data manifold, can capture implicit
058   constraints hidden in the data, e.g., the complex geometry of valid molecules, and its dimensionality
059   is denoted as intrinsic data dimensionality (Fefferman et al., 2016). According to the manifold
060   hypothesis, the intrinsic dimensionality m of Ω is significantly lower than the ambient dimensionality,
061   i.e., m ≪ D, for a wide range of real-world data types (Fefferman et al., 2016; Stanczuk et al., 2024).
062   As a consequence, in this work we first aim to answer the following question:
063
            How can a decision-making agent solve the exploration-exploitation problem directly on the
064
                                          unknown data manifold?
065
066   To this end, and motivated by the success of diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song &
067   Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020) in learning complex data distributions across various domains, including
068   chemistry (Hoogeboom et al., 2022), biology (Corso et al., 2022), and robotics (Chi et al., 2023), we
069   present Diffusion Posterior Sampling (D IFF PS), which extends classic posterior sampling (Russo &
070   Van Roy, 2014; Osband & Van Roy, 2017) to generate a sequence of approximately valid actions from
071   diverse areas of the unknown manifold via sequential conditional generation, gradually concentrating
072   the generated experiments on high-reward regions (Sec. 4).
073   Next, by leveraging recent theoretical results on provable manifold learning via diffusion (Chen et al.,
074   2023; Stanczuk et al., 2024), we shed light on the statistical complexity of D IFF PS, showing that
075   under certain structural assumptions, it adapts to the intrinsic data dimensionality m, thus overcoming
076   the curse of dimensionality that typically hinders the applicability of bandit algorithms in real-world
077   discovery problems (Hao et al., 2020; Djolonga et al., 2013; Kandasamy et al., 2015) (Sec. 5). Finally,
078   we provide an experimental evaluation of D IFF PS, supporting our theoretical claims empirically and
079
      showing promising performance (Sec. 6).
080   To sum up, we make the following contributions:
081
      • The generative bandit setting, where the action set Ω is the unknown support, also called data
082
        manifold, of a complex data distribution Px learnable from unlabeled data (Sec. 3).
083   • Diffusion Posterior Sampling (D IFF PS), an algorithm that leverages conditional diffusion models to
084     tackle the exploration-exploitation problem directly on the learned data manifold, and Generative
085     Posterior Sampling (G EN PS), a generative model agnostic generalization of D IFF PS (Sec. 4).
086   • A statistical analysis of the (Bayesian) regret incurred by D IFF PS, showing that it adapts to the
087     intrinsic data dimensionality, and an analysis of the misgeneration regret of D IFF PS (Sec. 5).
088   • An experimental evaluation of D IFF PS, providing empirical support for our theoretical claims and
089     demonstrating promising performance. (Sec. 6).

097   2.1     BANDIT OPTIMIZATION , EXPLORATION - EXPLOITATION , AND POSTERIOR SAMPLING
098
099   Bandit optimization. A T -round bandit (optimization) problem (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020)
100   is a tuple υ = ⟨Ω, rθ∗ , T ⟩, where Ω ⊆ RD is a (possibly infinite) set of actions, rθ∗ : Ω → R is an
101   unknown deterministic reward function, and T is the number of rounds. At every round t ∈ [T ], an
102   agent selects an action xt ∈ Ω according to a policy π = {πt }t∈[T ] with πt ∈ P(RD ), and receives
103   the noisy feedback yt = rθ∗ (xt ) + ϵt , i.e., the reward function evaluated at xt plus zero-mean noise.
104   Exploration-exploitation problem and posterior sampling. Balancing exploration of novel actions
105   to learn rθ∗ , and exploitation of the current belief about rθ∗ to propose promising actions, is known
106   as the exploration-exploitation dilemma. A classic algorithm to address this challenge is posterior
107   sampling (PS) (Russo & Van Roy, 2014). Given a set of bandit instances {υ = ⟨Ω, rθ , T ⟩}θ∈Θ and
      a prior distribution q1 over Θ, PS operates as follows. At each round t ∈ [T ], the agent samples a

      reward parameter θ̃t ∼ qt , computes the policy πt that maximizes rθ̃t , selects an action xt ∼ πt ,
109
      receiving a noisy feedback rθ∗ (xt ) + ϵt from the true reward model. The agent then updates the
110
      posterior qt+1 to integrate the new evidence. By acting optimally with respect to sampled reward
111   functions (thus promoting exploration) and updating its beliefs based on observed feedback, the agent
112   gradually learns enough about the true reward function to eventually act optimally with respect to it.

290   In this section, we present an upper bound on the Bayesian reward and misgeneration regrets, as in
291   Definition 2, achieved by D IFF PS against an optimal sampling strategy. This result captures the impact
292   on statistical complexity of solving the exploration-exploitation problem directly on the learned data

295    Definition 3 (Intrinsic data (manifold) dimensionality). Given a data distribution Px with support
296    Ω := supp(Px ), we define:
297                                             m(Ω) := min{m ∈ N : Ω ⊆ Rm }
298
299   This complexity measure, which we denote as m when Ω is clear from context, is clearly data
300   dependent as it varies for different data types, e.g., molecules, natural images, proteins. Moreover, the
301   well-known manifold hypothesis states that the intrinsic data dimensionality m is significantly smaller
302   than the ambient dimensionality D, namely m ≪ D, in a variety of real-world problems (Loaiza-
303   Ganem et al., 2024; De Bortoli, 2022; Fefferman et al., 2016; Valdés & Tchagang, 2023). To leverage
304
      the intrinsic data dimensionality in our analysis, we first assume the following.

306   dimensional linear subspace. Namely, there exists an unknown matrix V ∈ RD×m with orthonormal
307
      columns such that x = V z, where z ∈ Rm is a latent variable, and D is the ambient dimensionality.
308   Assumption 5.2 (Linear bounded rewards and actions). We assume that r∗ (x) = θ∗⊤ (ΠV x) ∈ [0, 1],
309   where ΠV = V V ⊤ is a projection onto Ω, ∥θ∗ ∥2 = 1, and ∥xt ∥2 ≤ L ∀t ∈ [T ].

428
      that of the bandit scheme given the true m-dimensional action set, and subsequently incurs low

429
      D-bandit incurs in a significantly higher regret due to the high dimensionality of the action space.

      We review relevant work in high-dimensional bandit optimization, model-based optimization via
      conditional sampling, diffusion models for function optimization, and diffusion models theory.
454
455   High-dimensional bandit and Bayesian optimization. Many real-world black-box function opti-
456   mization problems are modeled as high-dimensional bandit, including Bayesian optimization (Frazier,
457   2018). Typically, the high-dimensionality is addressed by either leveraging known or learned struc-
458   ture of the reward function (cf. Kveton et al., 2017; Lale et al., 2019; Kassraie et al., 2022), or by
459
      exploiting a known or learned representation of the action set (cf. Mutny & Krause, 2018; Griffiths &
      Hernández-Lobato, 2020; Wang et al., 2016; Kirschner et al., 2019; Djolonga et al., 2013), which
460
      includes VAE-based Bayesian optimization (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018; Griffiths & Hernández-
461
      Lobato, 2020; Grosnit et al., 2021; Goodfellow et al., 2020). In contrast, D IFF PS directly performs
462   black-box function optimization on the approximate data manifold using a learned diffusion sampler,
463   without relying on a predefined or learned action space representation.
464
465
      Model-based optimization via conditional sampling and inverse modeling. Various methods
      optimize a black-box function f using datasets as {(xi , y i = f (xi )} through conditional sampling
466
      or inverse models. These approaches can be categorized into offline, e.g., (Uehara et al., 2024b),
467
      which use only pre-existing labeled data, and active, which can query an online oracle (e.g., Brookes
468   et al., 2019; Kumar & Levine, 2020). Arguably, the closest work to ours is (Kumar & Levine, 2020),
469   where the authors propose a randomized labeling strategy to approximate a posterior sampling using
470   GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020) and VAEs (Kingma, 2013).
471
      Diffusion models guidance, black-box optimization, and fine-tuning. To steer diffusion-based
472
      generation towards designs meeting specific conditions, guidance techniques are commonly em-
473
      ployed (Song et al., 2020; Ho & Salimans, 2022). While these methods can enhance conditional
474   generation in D IFF PS, they are orthogonal to our work, which focuses on provably optimizing an
475   unknown function rather than sampling predefined target values. Interestingly, our approach can be
476   interpreted as a way to automate this process by algorithmically exploring function values to identify
477   maxima. Additionally, some studies have used diffusion models for offline (Krishnamoorthy et al.,
478   2023; Kong et al., 2024) and online black-box optimization (Uehara et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024).
479   Unlike these approaches, which rely on upper confidence bounds (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020),
480   we extend posterior sampling with diffusion models and provide both experimental (see Sec. 6) and
481   theoretical (see Theorem 5.1) evidence that our method’s statistical complexity adapts to the data
482
      intrinsic dimensionality. Moreover, unlike prior works that require a pre-trained diffusion model or
      labeled data, we address the case where only unlabeled offline data is available.
483
484   Diffusion models theory. Recent research on diffusion models theory relevant to our work falls
485   into two categories. First, studies have established convergence rates based on the intrinsic data
      dimensionality under exact score estimation (e.g., De Bortoli, 2022). Second, recent works have


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      provided statistical guarantees for unconditional and conditional generation by accounting for score
487   estimation and linking it to offline bandits (Chen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Oko et al., 2023;
488   Metevier et al., 2019). Building on these results, we establish guarantees for online decision-making,
489   where an agent generates actions to navigate the exploration-exploitation trade-off with respect to
490   an unknown reward function, leveraging offline unlabeled data to implicitly learn an action space
491   corresponding to the data manifold.

      8   C ONCLUSIONS
494
495
      In this work, we introduced a posterior sampling scheme with statistical guarantees that uses diffusion
496
      models to solve bandit optimization directly on the learned data manifold. Before concluding, we
497
      highlight a few key discussion points.
498
499   Data-dependent guarantees for decision-making. Theorem 5.1 states that the regret incurred
500   by D IFF PS adapts to the intrinsic data dimensionality m. We believe this measure can help in
501
      bridging the gap between statistical complexity in decision-making and real-world applications,
      where data like molecules and proteins have intrinsic dimensions that can be estimated using known
502
      methods (Stanczuk et al., 2024; Kamkari et al., 2024; Campadelli et al., 2015; Verveer & Duin, 1995).
503
504
      Beyond bandits and diffusion D IFF PS can be generalized beyond diffusion (see G EN PS in Ap-
505
      pendix B), and a significant part of the analysis does not rely on a specific generative model. Moreover,
506
      the algorithm and its analysis can be extended to other decision-making settings including contextual
507   bandits (Chu et al., 2011) and reinforcement learning (Sutton et al., 1998), leading to decision-making
508   algorithms based on generative models while preserving insightful theoretical guarantees.
509
510
      To summarize, we introduced generative bandit, a generalization of classic bandit optimization
      where the action space is the unknown support of a complex data distribution, also known as data
511
      manifold. Furthermore, we proposed Diffusion Posterior Sampling (D IFF PS), an algorithm that solves
512
      the exploration-exploitation problem directly on the learned data manifold. Next, we presented
513   regret guarantees showing how the statistical complexity of this process adapts to the intrinsic data
514   dimensionality and how it depends on the available offline data. Ultimately, we have performed an
515   experimental evaluation of the proposed algorithm supporting our theoretical claims.

      B     G ENERATIVE POSTERIOR SAMPLING
865
866
      In this section, we first present Generative Posterior Sampling (G EN PS), a generative model inde-
867   pendent meta-algorithm that generalizes Diffusion Posterior Sampling beyond diffusion models, and
868   tackles the generative bandit problem introduced in Definition 1.
869
870   B.1     A LGORITHM : G ENERATIVE P OSTERIOR S AMPLING ( G EN PS )
871
872
873
      Algorithm 2 G EN PS: Generative Posterior Sampling (with offline unlabeled data)
874    1: Input: T : number of online samples, q1 : reward parameter prior, Dunlabeled = {(xi )}n
                                                                                                i=1 :
875         unlabeled data, π : generative model
876    2: for t = 1, 2, . . . , T do
877    3:     Sample reward parameter θt ∼ qt and define r̃t := rθt
878
       4:     Label data in Dunlabeled via r̃t : D := {(xi , yi := r̃t (xi ) + ξi }ni=1 with ξi ∼ N (0, ν 2 )
       5:     Train conditional generative model π̂t on D
879
       6:     Compute maximum imaginary reward ỹt = maxx∈Ω rθt (x)
880
       7:     Sample xt ∼ π̂t (· | ỹt ) via conditional generation
881    8:     Play xt and observe yt ∼ rθ∗ (xt ) + ϵt
882    9:     Compute qt+1 via posterior update
883   10: end for
884
885
886   In the following, we present a detailed explanation of Algorithm 2. First, the algorithm samples
887   an imaginary reward parameter from the rewards prior, namely θt ∼ qt (line 3). Then, it computes
888   the labeled dataset D by labeling the dataset Dunlabeled by defining pairs (xi , yi ) with yi := r̃t (xi ),
889   where we define r̃t := rθt (line 4). Afterwards, G EN PS trains a conditional generative model π̃(· | y)
890
      on the labeled dataset D (line 5), and computes the maximum imaginary reward value over Ω, namely
      ỹt (line 6). The same observations regarding this oracle step made in Section 4 w.r.t. D IFF PS extend to
891
      G EN PS. Once ỹt is computed, the algorithm approximately samples from the region of the manifold
892
      Ω achieving reward ỹt , namely Ωr̃t , via conditional generation xt ∼ π̂t (· | ỹt ) (line 7). Ultimately, it
893   plays action xt to observe feedback rθ∗ (xt ) + ϵt (line 8), and performs posterior update of the reward
894   prior qt (line 9) to integrate the new evidence gained about the true reward function rθ∗ .
895
896   B.2     E XTENSION OF RESULTS OF D IFF PS TO G EN PS
897
898   Interestingly, the argument for approximate in-manifold exploration shown in Equation 4 w.r.t. D IFF PS
899   extends to G EN PS, and analogously also the regret decomposition Proposition 1. Nonetheless, while
900   the BRΩ r (T, π̂) of the reward regret can be bounded analogously for G EN PS , the term ∆(Ω,Ω)
                                                                                                   b (T, π̂),
901   as well as the validity regret, require generative model specific estimation guarantees and therefore
902   the regret results presented in Theorem 5.1 does not trivially extend to Algorithm 2.

1484   Data and Setup. We consider the setting where Ω = {x = V z : ∥z∥1 ≤ 1} where V ∈ RD×m is a
1485   matrix that consists of the first m columns of a matrix in the special orthogonal group, SO(D). In
1486   order to generate the data, we sample z uniformly from a unit sphere in Rm and then project it into
1487   RD . We choose m = 4, D = 64 and the number of samples n = 1.2 · 106 . Such high number of
1488
       samples were necessary in order to be able to sample from high reward regions as outlined below.
1489   Reward and Cost. As previously mentioned, we use a linear reward with a standard Gaussian prior
1490   on θ and the cost function is given as the L2 distance to the sphere in D dimensions. Due to the
1491   fact that the reward maximum is always achieved at a single point on the surface of the sphere, we
1492   required a fairly large dataset, in order to be able to approximately sample those points.
1493   Neural Networks and Training Algorithms. To parametrize the score function we use a 20-Layer
1494   MLP with skip connections and a hidden dimension of 128 neurons. For the time embedding we use
1495   Gaussian Random Features (Tancik et al., 2020). We train our model for 30 epochs with a batch size
1496   of 128, using the Adam optimizer with cosine annealing and warm restarts.
