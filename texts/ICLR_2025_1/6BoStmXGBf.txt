011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques such as low-rank adaptation
014            (LoRA) can effectively adapt large pre-trained foundation models to downstream
015            tasks using only a small fraction (0.1%-10%) of the original trainable weights. An
016
               under-explored question of PEFT is in extending the pre-training phase without
               supervised labels; that is, can we adapt a pre-trained foundation model to a new
017
               domain via efficient self-supervised pre-training on this new domain? In this work,
018
               we introduce ExPLoRA, a highly effective technique to improve transfer learning
019            of pre-trained vision transformers (ViTs) under domain shifts. Initializing a ViT
020            with pre-trained weights on large, natural-image datasets such as from DinoV2
021            or MAE, ExPLoRA continues the unsupervised pre-training objective on a new
022            domain, unfreezing 1-2 pre-trained ViT blocks and tuning all other layers with
023            LoRA. We then fine-tune the resulting model only with LoRA on this new domain
024            for supervised learning. Our experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art results on
025            satellite imagery, even outperforming fully pre-training and fine-tuning ViTs. Using
026            the DinoV2 training objective, we demonstrate up to 7.5% improvement in linear
027
               probing top-1 accuracy on downstream tasks while using <10% of the number
               of parameters that are used in prior fully-tuned state-of-the art approaches. Our
028
               ablation studies confirm the efficacy of our approach over other baselines, including
029
               PEFT and unfreezing more ViT blocks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Pre-training foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021) for natural language (Brown et al., 2020;
035
      Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024) and natural images (Oquab et al.,
036   2023; He et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022) has historically been computationally
037   intensive, often limited to organizations with substantial resources. However, recent advancements
038   in parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques including low-rank adaptation (LoRA) and
039   others (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023b; Chavan et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023;
040   Jia et al., 2022) have sparked significant interest. These methods aim to adapt foundation models to
041   downstream supervised-learning tasks using a small fraction (0.1%-10%) of the model’s trainable
042   weights, with many based on the hypothesis that the required weight updates to the pre-trained model
043   have a “low intrinsic rank" (Hu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2018; Aghajanyan et al., 2020).
044   In this paper, we focus on visual foundation models (VFMs) such as DinoV2 or MAE (Oquab
045   et al., 2023; He et al., 2022), which were trained on large-scale natural-image datasets. Despite the
046   large investments in developing such models for natural images, they underperform when applied to
047   other domains with visual data (e.g. medical or satellite images). For example, fine-tuning a model
048   pre-trained on natural images on satellite image classification tasks is not as effective as fine-tuning
049   one that was pre-trained on satellite images (Cong et al., 2022; Ayush et al., 2021). To bridge this gap,
050
      prevailing approaches invest similarly large levels of compute to pre-train VFMs on new domains,
      inspired by techniques developed for natural images (Cong et al., 2022; Reed et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
051
      2024; Khanna et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Moutakanni et al., 2024; Man et al., 2023).
052
053   In this work, we challenge this paradigm (fig. 1), asking whether pre-training from scratch on
      each new domain is strictly necessary, since doing so is expensive (in compute and time) and

092   2    R ELATED W ORK
093
094   VFMs VFMs such as DinoV2 or masked autoencoders (MAE) that pre-train with self-supervised
095   learning (SSL) have demonstrated remarkable performance across downstream tasks such as classifi-
096   cation or semantic segmentation Oquab et al. (2023); He et al. (2022); Grill et al. (2020); Chen et al.
097
      (2020). However, there has also been a rise in domain-specific VFMs (Cong et al., 2022; Reed et al.,
      2023; Moutakanni et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a). For instance, SatMAE handles
098
      temporal or multi-spectral satellite image inputs. Since these models contain hundreds of millions of
099
      parameters, efficient adaptation to downstream tasks has become a key research focus.
100
101
      PEFT PEFT methods have gained widespread adoption for efficiently adapting large models by
102   updating only a fraction of parameters, mitigating the prohibitive costs of full model tuning. LoRA
103   learns low-rank weight updates to frozen weights, while other methods modify the frequency or
104   number of trainable parameters per layer (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023b; Chavan et al., 2023;
105   Pu et al., 2023). Others use multiplicative orthogonal updates (Qiu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) or
106   inject adapter modules (Steitz & Roth, 2024; Yin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2024; Lian
107   et al., 2022), effectively retaining pre-training knowledge in frozen weights. Visual prompt tuning
      (VPT) methods concatenate learnable prompt tokens to image patch sequences, trading improved


                                                                                              2

108
      fine-tuning performance with increased inference costs (Jia et al., 2022; Yoo et al., 2023; Pei et al.,
109   2024; Han et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023). ExPLoRA aims to supplement rather than replace these
110   methods, and thus can be configured with any existing or future PEFT method for ViT fine-tuning.
111
112   Domain Adaptation Domain adaptation enables models trained on a source domain to perform
113   well on a different but related target domain. Traditional transformer-based methods address this
114   via domain alignment, discriminative feature learning, cross-attention with pseudo-labels (Sun et al.,
115   2022; Chuan-Xian et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023), or adversarial learning with self-refinement (Yang
116   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2021), typically requiring either labeled target data or source domain labels.
117   Recent work explores adapting ViTs through different means: e.g., continual pre-training via masked
118   image modeling (Mendieta et al., 2023) and scaled LoRA adapters (Scheibenreif et al., 2024) for
119
      satellite imagery. ExPLoRA builds on this direction, enabling SSL directly on the target domain
      while using significantly fewer parameters. Further comparisons with related work are in appendix A.

123   3   BACKGROUND
124
125   MAE The masked-autoencoder (MAE) (He et al., 2022) is an effective SSL technique for ViTs
126   that uses an asymmetric encoder-decoder architecture on images x ∈ RC×H×W , where patches are
127
      masked before being processed by the ViT encoder L. The masked patches are then reconstructed by
      a smaller decoder LD , with both trained jointly using mean-squared error on the reconstructed visible
128
      pixels. While effective across domains (Cong et al., 2022; Bachmann et al., 2022), MAEs typically
129
      require full fine-tuning for downstream tasks, which makes them computationally expensive.
130
131   DinoV2 DinoV2 (Oquab et al., 2023) is a robust SSL method for ViTs. Unlike MAE, DinoV2
132   features have demonstrated strong zero-shot performance, enabling adaptation to downstream tasks
133   even with a frozen ViT backbone. During pre-training, DinoV2 maintains two copies of a ViT
134   encoder: the student (trainable) and the teacher, which is updated using an exponential-moving
135   average of the student’s parameters. The training objective incorporates a global, image-level loss
136   from Dino (Caron et al., 2021), a patch-based loss from iBOT (Zhou et al., 2021), and regularizers
137   including KoLeo (Delattre & Fournier, 2017) and Sinkhorn-Knopp centering (Caron et al., 2020).
138
139   LoRA Low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) assumes that the weight update to change a
140
      set of unsupervised pre-trained weights to supervised fine-tuned weights lives in a low-rank subspace,
141                                      W ≈ W0 + ∆W = W0 + BA                                           (1)
142   where W ∈ R   k2 ×k1
                         are the final, task-specific fine-tuned weights, W0 ∈ R   k2 ×k1
                                                                                       are the pre-trained
143   weights, ∆W ∈ Rk2 ×k1 is the weight update required to translate the pre-trained weights W0 to the
144   fine-tuned weights W . The key is that ∆W = BA where B ∈ Rk2 ×r and A ∈ Rr×k1 . That is, A
145   and B form a low-rank factorization of ∆W , where the rank r ≪ min(k1 , k2 ).

      Consider a set of image domains D = {1, 2, . . . }, where each domain d ∈ D is associated with a
150   data distribution pd (x), and images x ∈ RCd ×Hd ×Wd have domain-specific channel, height, and
151   width. Let DS ⊂ D represent a set of source domains (e.g., internet-scale natural image data) and
152   DT ⊂ D represent target domains (e.g., satellite imagery). The data from the source domains follow
153   a distribution pDS (x), and the target domain data come from pDT (x). For some target domains
154   dT ∈ DT , the joint distributions pdT (x, y) describe images x with associated supervised labels y
155   used for downstream tasks. We then assume access to the following:
156
157
           (i) WDS , pre-trained weights obtained via unsupervised pre-training on images from pDS (x)
158       (ii) XDT = {xi }N i=1 ∼ pDT (x), an unlabeled dataset of N images from new domains DT
                                Md
159       (iii) YdT = {xj , yj }j=1T ∼ pdT (x, y) a labeled dataset of MdT images from domain dT ∈ DT
160
161   Our objective is to learn optimal weights WdT for each supervised-learning dataset YdT in a parameter-
      efficient manner while leveraging the knowledge stored in WDS .

      Traditionally, the approach (fig. 1) has been to begin pre-training from scratch on the new domains of
190
      interest in XDT , and then fine-tune for each dataset YdT , representing the following:
191
192                                                         WdT ≈ WDT + ∆dT                                                                            (2)
193
194   where WDT represents the weights learned from unsupervised pre-training on XDT , and ∆dT are
195   the weights learned from supervised fine-tuning on YdT . However, this method is computationally
196   expensive: fully pre-training WDT from scratch for every new domain requires prohibitively large
197   amounts of additional compute.
198   On the other hand, LoRA addresses this inefficiency in the following way:
199
200                                            WdT ≈ WDS + ∆dT = WDS + BdT AdT                                                                         (3)
201
202
      The LoRA hypothesis is that the update ∆dT resides in a low-rank subspace when adapting pre-
203
      trained weights WDS to fine-tuned weights WdT . This hypothesis holds well when pre-training and
204   fine-tuning distributions are similar, or where dT ∈ DS . However, when there is significant domain
205   shift, such as between natural images and multi-spectral satellite data, the low-rank assumption often
206   breaks down (see section 6.1.3).
207
      Our goal is to learn WDT in a parameter-efficient manner to bridge the large domain shift to DT
208
      while leveraging the knowledge encoded in WDS . We propose the following factorization of WdT :
209
210                                                    WdT ≈ WDS + ∆DT + ∆dT                                                                           (4)
211
212   where ∆DT ∈ Rk2 ×k1 is an additional update matrix learned from unsupervised pre-training on XDT .
213   Crucially, ∆DT requires only a fraction of the k1 k2 parameters of WDS , making it significantly more
                                                                     ∗
214   efficient than full-rank pre-training. The resulting model, WD   T
                                                                         = WDS + ∆DT ≈ WDT , retains
215   the benefits of unsupervised pre-trained VFMs, including strong feature extraction, effective linear
      probing, KNN classification, and generalization to downstream tasks.

      5    M ETHOD
217
218
      To learn ∆DT , we propose ExPLoRA (i.e. Extended Pre-training with LoRA), a method that
219   efficiently adapts pre-trained ViTs for new target domains DT , described in algorithm 1.
220
221   In terms of notation, D-[L]-r64 refers to a ViT initialized with DinoV2 weights (denoted by D),
222
      where U = {L}, and LoRA rank 64 is applied to the Q, V matrices of attention layers in L \ U. Thus,
      ∆DT comprises of all weights in U, LoRA matrices in L \ U, and normalization layers. For U = {L},
223
      ∆DT consists of only 5% of the original ViT parameters. As we show in section 6, our extended
224
      pre-training approach can match or even outperform full pre-training on new domains from scratch.
225
226   ExPLoRA for DinoV2 We initialize a ViT-L with WDS from the DinoV2 ViT-L encoder, without
227   registers (Darcet et al., 2023). Since the DinoV2 pre-trained checkpoints don’t contain the Dino or
228   iBOT linear heads, we initialize a shared Dino-iBOT linear head from scratch. This shared head is
229   fully trained during extended pre-training, adding only a minimal number of trainable parameters.
230
231   ExPLoRA for MAE We initialize a ViT-L with WDS from the MAE ViT-L encoder. Since MAE
232   provides the pre-trained decoder, we use these weights to initialize our MAE decoder He et al. (2022).
233   During extended pre-training, in addition to the ExPLoRA recipe in algorithm 1, we apply LoRA
234   with rank r′ on the Q, V matrices of each attention layer in the frozen decoder. Note that the LoRA
235   rank r′ may differ from the LoRA rank r used in the ViT encoder (appendix B.4). All other decoder
236
      weights, apart from the layer-normalization layers, are kept frozen. No block is fully unfrozen in the
      MAE decoder, as it will be discarded after extended pre-training. This helps to minimize the number
237
      of additional parameters trained in the decoder.
238
239
      ExPLoRA for Multi-Spectral Inputs For the multi-spectral ViT introduced by SatMAE we need
240
      to additionally unfreeze the positional encoding and the patch embedding weights for each group of
241   channels. These cannot be initialized from WDS , as WDS is trained on RGB inputs, whereas multi-
242   spectral inputs can have more or different channels. As part of ∆DT in algorithm 1, the positional
243   encodings and patch embeddings for multi-spectral data are adapted during extended pre-training.
244   Aside from this, the approach remains unchanged from that of DinoV2 or MAE described earlier.

      Our experimental results consist of a case study on satellite imagery (section 6.1), with an ablation

      study in section 6.1.2 and analysis in section 6.3. We evaluate on multiple downstream tasks in

341   6.1.2    A BLATION STUDY
342
343   We perform an ablation study (table 3) on linear-probing performance for fMoW-RGB to determine
344   whether our proposed configuration performs optimally. A natural question is whether the improve-
345
      ment in performance stems primarily from unfreezing blocks, or from LoRA-tuning the rest of the
      ViT. We investigate this by unfreezing blocks {L,L-1} in row 2 (with no LoRA), and comparing
346
      that with ExPLoRA-L-r8 in row 10. As seen, unfreezing an extra block consumes almost double
347
      the number of parameters, but fails to yield the same improvement in performance ↓ 0.34%. Thus,
348   simply increasing the number of unfrozen blocks will likely improve performance, but will not do so
349   as effectively as ExPLoRA, and will also significantly and sharply decrease the parameter-efficiency.
350
351
      Next, we investigate whether high LoRA ranks used on all ViT layers (i.e. all attention and MLP
      matrices, not just Q, V ) is beneficial. Surprisingly, this significantly harms learning (row 4, 5). In
352
      fact, it is much less effective than using just LoRA-r256 on the Q, V matrices of all L blocks (row 3).
353
      However, both rows 3 and 4 are much less parameter-efficient than ExPLoRA (rows 6-8, 11-13).
354
355   The choice of U matters as well. As seen in rows 6-8, and 13, for the DinoV2 objective, U = {1} or
356   U = {9} are not as effective as U = {L-1} or U = {L}, ceteris paribus. To understand this result
357
      further, see section 6.3. We also notice a slight drop in accuracy from leaving the normalization
      layers across the ViT frozen, seen in row 10.
358
359   Lastly, we investigate the impact of LoRA rank on ExPLoRA. Changing the rank from 8 to 32 has a
360   small improvement (↑ 0.09%), but changing from 32 to 64 brings about a much larger improvement
361   (↑ 1.08%), with only a relatively small increase in trainable parameters. This demonstrates that higher
362   ranks are necessary during pre-training for effective learning on the new domain. Further ablations
363
      on compute efficiency (B.2), data efficiency (B.3), MAE decoder rank (B.4), and ViT backbone size
      (B.5) are in appendix B.

477   The key design choice of ExPLoRA is to fully train a small subset U ⊂ L of the ViT, while applying
478   low-rank updates to the remaining frozen layers L \ U. For parameter-efficiency, we aim to keep
479   |U| ≪ |L| and make and informed choice of which layers to unfreeze based on their potential to
480   improve learning during extended pre-training.
481   We conduct an investigation on 5 models using a sample of XDT . These models are DinoV2, D-
482   ExPLoRA-[L]-r64, SatMAE, MAE, and M-ExPLoRA-[L]-r64. We do the following analyses:
483   (i) PCA to measure the mean and variance of eigenvalues of patch feature vectors for each ViT block,
484   in fig. 3 (ii) linear probing for local or global information (Darcet et al., 2023) by training logistic
485   regression classifiers on each block’s patch feature vectors, to predict either patch position (fig. 4) or
      image class (fig. 5).

486
      Findings and Unfreezing Strategy for DinoV2 Our analysis reveals that the spectral properties
487
      of a block’s feature map (fig. 3) and the ability to retrieve local information from its output patch
488   tokens (fig. 4) are correlated. The classification accuracy for position and the mean of the principal
489   eigenvalues peak in the middle-layers of the model, suggesting that the middle blocks capture local
490   properties of patches (e.g., texture, relative position). Meanwhile, deeper blocks focus on global
491   semantic understanding, as shown by increased classification accuracy for image class prediction in
492   fig. 5. Combined, these results suggest that unfreezing deeper layers, such as U = {L}, allows the
493   model to better capture global features without overfitting to local details of images of DT . This is
494   empirically confirmed in table 3, where linear probing accuracy correlates inversely with the mean
495   eigenvalue of each block (i.e., block 23 > block 22 > block 0 > block 9). The attention maps in fig. 8
496
      further support this, showing that the deeper layers focus more clearly on central objects, while earlier
      layers (e.g., blocks 9, 10) exhibit more diffuse attention patterns spread around the border.
497
498
499   Findings and Unfreezing Strategy for MAE For MAE, we see a similar, but less pronounced
500   trend. However, MAE is only trained for reconstruction, and so retains more local information across
501   the ViT’s layers. This is reflected by its lower patch-wise eigenvalues, higher localization accuracy,
502
      and lower global accuracies than Dino.
503
504   ExPLoRA’s Impact D-ExPLoRA preserves local information in the middle layers but also im-
505   proves localization accuracy in the last few layers. Importantly, it also enhances the global information
506   contained in the patches for deeper model layers. This indicates a better understanding of the target
507   domain, as seen in B.7, where ExPLoRA’s attention highlights the central object more clearly.

      7   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
511
512   In this paper, we introduce ExPLoRA, a novel pre-training strategy to adapt pre-trained ViT foundation
513   models for natural images to additional visual domains such as satellite imagery or medical data. We
514
      challenge the common paradigm of expensive pre-training from scratch for each new visual domain
      by offering a solution to transfer knowledge from foundation models that is both parameter-efficient
515
      and effective (even outperforming domain-specific foundation models). Our hope is that ExPLoRA
516
      enables further use of foundation models on domains other than natural images without requiring
517   vast computational resources for pre-training.
518
519   While effective, there are many aspects of ExPLoRA that deserve further study. The strategy of fully
520
      training a small amount (or budget) of weights combines extremely well with PEFT techniques such
      as LoRA– we hope that future work investigates the reason behind this in further detail. Unresolved
521
      questions also include whether other parameter-efficient techniques might work better with ExPLoRA
522
      during pre-training. Further work to evaluate ExPLoRA for natural language would be valuable, as
523   would an investigation into whether we can do away entirely with unfreezing a transformer block.
524
525
526   B ROADER I MPACT
527
528   As the scale of models and datasets grows exponentially, access to the computing power necessary
529   to develop and make use of foundation models is increasingly restricted to the hands of a few
530   organizations. This leaves many researchers in academia or smaller companies reliant on the
531   resources of such organizations for ML research and applications. Techniques such as PEFT can
532   alleviate this dependence and enable those with fewer computational resources to adapt, investigate,
533   and customize models for their own needs. We hope that ExPLoRA furthers this goal, allowing ML
534   practitioners to tailor foundation models with minimal compute, thus broadening access to powerful
535   ML tools for critical fields like sustainability and medicine.
536   For example, automated analysis of satellite imagery can inform social, economic, and environmental
537   policies, but manual curation is expensive, and pre-training models on such data has significant costs,
538   both environmental and otherwise (see appendix D). ExPLoRA offers a more efficient way to distill
539   knowledge from existing foundation models trained on natural images, sharply reducing costs while
      aiding researchers and policymakers and enabling flexible applications in downstream tasks.

767   approaches with ExPLoRA in table 10.

771                       Parameter-efficient              ✗           ✓              ✓

      ExPLoRA differs from these approaches in several key aspects. Unlike GFM which trains the full
778   backbone, ExPLoRA achieves superior performance with only a fraction of trainable parameters.
779   While GDA is also parameter-efficient, it requires non-mergeable scaling vectors that induce inference
780   latency and modify the ViT, whereas ExPLoRA’s LoRA adapters can be merged into the ViT’s weights.
781   Additionally, ExPLoRA extends beyond MAE architectures (supporting DinoV2 and others) and
782   allows flexible configurations between pre-training and fine-tuning, including varying LoRA ranks or
783   using different PEFT methods, which GDA doesn’t support.
784   We also demonstrate ExPLoRA’s broader applicability through experiments on larger datasets (fMoW-
785   RGB, fMoW-Sentinel, which have 400k-800k images vs 90k images in FireRisk (Shen et al., 2023),
786   the largest dataset used in GDA) and domains beyond remote sensing (i.e. WiLDS). Our analysis in
787   section 6.3 provides insights into block-wise information encoding, offering practitioners a systematic
788   approach for block selection during PEFT– a unique feature not present in prior work.

       In this section, we describe hyperparameters and hardware configurations used for our models.
982
983
       C.1   P RE -T RAINING
984
985    We use the ViT-Large architecture for all experiments. Since raw image sizes vary, the shorter image
986    size is resized to 224 while preserving aspect ratio, and then a center crop is taken to yield images of
987    size 3 × 224 × 224, representing the channels, height, and width. All pre-training is done on a single
988    NVIDA-RTX 6000 Ada GPU, or 4 NVIDIA-RTX A4000 GPUs on an academic GPU cluster.
989
990    ExPLoRA with DinoV2 Most of the hyperparameters for D-ExPLoRA follow the defaults set by
991    Oquab et al. (2023). That is, local (small) crops are between 5%-32% of the original image and are
992    resized to 98x98 pixels, and global (large) crops are greater than 32% of the image and resized to
993
       224x224 pixels. We share the parameters of the Dino-iBOT linear head (3 layers), with a bottleneck
       dimension of 256, a hidden dimension of 2048, and an output dimension of 65536, initialized from
994
       scratch. For Dino, we use Sinkhorn-Knopp (Caron et al., 2020) centering and Koleo (Delattre &
995
       Fournier, 2017) regularization with a weight of 0.1. For iBOT, we use masking ratios between 0.1
996    and 0.5 to mask half of the samples in the batch. The teacher model uses an initial EMA rate of 0.994,
997    with a cosine warmup to 1.000 by the end of training. The teacher warmup and final temperatures are
998    0.04 and 0.07. The linear Dino-iBOT head is frozen for the first 3k training iterations. We train with
999    the AdamW optimizer (no weight decay), with a base learning rate of 2 × 10−3 that is varied with a
1000   linear warmup and cosine decay schedule. Training is completed within 200,000 iterations, with a
1001   batch size of 32 and with 32 gradient accumulation steps (equalling an effective batch size of 1024),
1002   and with an epoch length set to 1000.
1003
1004   ExPLoRA with MAE Most of the hyperparameters we use for M-ExPLoRA pre-training follow
1005   those in He et al. (2022); Cong et al. (2022). We use an effective batch size of 1024 (through gradient
1006
       accumulation), a base learning rate of 4.5 × 10−4 , no weight decay, and a warmup and decaying
       cosine scheduler, with a warmup of 1 epoch, and a total training time of 200 epochs. We use a
1007
       masking ratio of 0.75 and we use the norm_pix_loss flag for the MSE loss.
1008
1009
       C.2   PEFT F INE -T UNING
1010
1011   We fine-tune using 4 NVIDIA-RTX A4000 GPUs. We use a base learning rate of 10−3 , a cosine
1012   scheduler with warmup for 1 epoch, and train for 120 epochs. We use an effective batch size of 256,
1013   making use of gradient accumulation if the GPU cannot fit the full batch size in memory.
1014
       For data augmentations, we only use the drop-path augmentation (Larsson et al., 2016) at a rate of
1015
       0.2, with no dropout, mixup, or cutmix. We note that the original LoRA configuration outperforms
1016
       other PEFT techniques when paired with the drop-path regularization technique. For example, we
1017   find that BOFT does not pair well with drop-path, instead performing most effectively with a custom
1018   multiplicative dropout technique (Liu et al., 2023). We include the result with the best hyperparameter
1019   configuration for each row in table 1.
