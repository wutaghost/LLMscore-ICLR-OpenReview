009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Differential privacy limits an algorithm’s privacy loss, defined as the maximum
012            influence any individual data record can have on the probability of observing any
013            possible output. Privacy auditing identifies the worst-case input datasets and out-
014            put event sets that empirically maximize privacy loss, providing statistical lower
015            bounds to evaluate the tightness of an algorithm’s differential privacy guarantees.
016            However, current auditing methods often depend on heuristic or arbitrary selec-
017            tions of output event sets, leading to weak lower bounds. We address this critical
018
               gap by introducing a novel framework to compute the optimal output event set
               that maximizes the privacy loss lower bound in auditing. Our algorithm efficiently
019
               computes this optimal set when closed-form output distributions are available and
020
               approximates it using empirical samples when they are not. Through extensive
021            experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, we demonstrate that our
022            method consistently tightens privacy lower bounds for auditing differential pri-
023            vacy mechanisms and black-box DP-SGD training. Our approach outperforms
024            existing auditing techniques, providing a more accurate analysis of differentially-
025            private algorithms.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Differential privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2014) is regarded as the gold standard for quantitatively
031   assessing an algorithm’s privacy guarantee. It ensures that the presence or absence of any record
032   will not be heavily revealed from the outcome of the mechanism. Formally, consider a randomized
033   algorithm M : D → Θ. If for any neighbouring datasets D, D0 ∈ D that differ by only one entry,
034
      i.e., D0 = D ∪ {x} or D0 = D \ {x}, and for any measurable (output event) sets O, it satisfies that
035                                P[M (D) ∈ O] ≤ e  · P[M (D0 ) ∈ O] + δ,
036
037   then the algorithm M is said to satisfy ( , δ)-DP. Differential privacy mathematically certifies that
038   the information leakage of a randomized algorithm is bounded (by ε and δ) even under the worst-
039
      case datasets D, D0 and the worst-case output event set O.
040   Computing the exact differential privacy loss ε and δ for an algorithm requires tracking its output
041   distribution over all possible input datasets and output event sets, which is challenging for com-
042   plex algorithm (e.g., iterative learning algorithms with a large number of non-linear updates). To
043   circumvent this challenge, existing privacy analysis for machine learning algorithms (Abadi et al.,
044   2016; Kairouz et al., 2015; Mironov, 2017) often make simplifying assumptions that the adversary
045
      observes not only the final output, but also other information (such as intermediate updates) in a
      learning algorithm, and prove upper bounds for ε and δ instead. Understanding whether the DP up-
046
      per bound is correct and tight is critical, since underestimating privacy upper bounds leads to privacy
047
      leaks (Tramer et al., 2022), while any underestimation results in unnecessarily large modifications
048   to the learning algorithms which decreases the utility of the trained model. To measure the tightness
049   of the DP upper bound, it is crucial to identify the worst-case datasets and output event sets where
050   the privacy loss is maximized. The success of inference attack under such worst-case scenarios then
051   provides a statistical lower bound on the exact differential privacy loss of the algorithm. If the lower
052   bound is close to the DP upper bound, then the privacy analysis is tight. This process is known as
053   privacy auditing (Jagielski et al., 2020; Nasr et al., 2021; Zanella-Beguelin et al., 2023; Nasr et al.,
      2023; Steinke et al., 2023).

      Existing privacy auditing literature largely focuses on designing the worst-case input datasets D, D0
055   via poisoning attacks (Jagielski et al., 2020; Nasr et al., 2021) or via searching for the data with
056   the highest risks against inference attacks in the training dataset via computationally expensive in-
057   ference attacks (Ye et al., 2022; Zanella-Beguelin et al., 2023; Aerni et al., 2024). By contrast, the
058   question of how to choose the worst-case output event set O to enable a better auditing lower bound
059   is largely underexplored. This is due to several reasons. (1) Standard learning algorithms consist of
060   a large number of iterative non-linear updates, which makes it challenging to track the complex
061   distribution of the final output in a closed-form manner. Consequently, it is infeasible to perform
062   exact optimization of output event set for complex iterative learning algorithms. (2) If we relax
063
      ourselves to approximate optimization of output set, prior works (Bichsel et al., 2021; Lu et al.,
      2022) have proposed several approaches based on approximating the output distributions via their
064
      empirical Monte Carlo samples. However, such approximations require a large number of Monte
065
      Carlo samples to be accurate, which requires computationally expensive (re)runs of the training al-
066   gorithm. Alternatively, given a small finite number of Monte Carlo samples, such methods suffer
067   from non-negligible approximation error, which in turn affects the auditing performance. Thus, it
068   is crucial to take the approximation error into account when formulating the objective of maximal
069   privacy loss in privacy auditing. However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no existing con-
070   sensus regarding the optimal way to incorporate the approximation error into the outpupt set
071   selection objective for privacy auditing. (3) Prior works (Steinke et al., 2023; Bichsel et al., 2021;
072   Lu et al., 2022) have empirically observed that the effect of output event set choice on the privacy
073   auditing power is distribution-dependent. For example, in certain mechanisms like randomized re-
074
      sponse, the choice of output event set does not significantly affect the audited lower bound ((Steinke
      et al., 2023, Figure 10)). In contrast, for some other mechanisms (such as the Laplace mechanism),
075
      the choice of output event set substantially affects the audited lower bound Bichsel et al. (2021); Lu
076
      et al. (2022). Despite these observations, a comprehensive understanding of whether and when
077   optimizing the output event set yields a gain remains elusive (i.e., how does the amount of gain
078   change for different mechanisms).
079
080
      In this paper, we aim to address the question of optimal output set selection for privacy auditing.
      Specifically, we ask the following research questions.
081
082
            1. What is the optimal output event set for auditing differentially private mechanisms? How
083            does the optimal output event set change for different mechanisms?
084
085
            2. How much gain for privacy auditing can we achieve by optimizing the output set? How
               does the amount of gain depend on the mechanism and the number of auditing examples?
086
087         3. How to compute the optimal output event set when only given empirical output sam-
088            ples, rather than closed-form output distributions? Does this enable tighter auditing lower
089            bounds for practical DP learning mechanisms, such as black-box DP-SGD?
090
091   Summary of Results. Our results for addressing the above research questions are as follows.
092
093          • We proposed a novel framework for choosing the optimal output set in privacy auditing,
094            that incorporates the error of sampling auditing examples from output distributions. Specif-
095
               ically, our optimization objective (4) captures the expectation of advantage-based auditing
               lower bound (Steinke et al., 2023; Jayaraman & Evans, 2019) over random sampling.
096
097          • We prove that our proposed optimization problem incurs closed-form solutions, that consist
098            of output sets constructed by thresholding the likelihood ratio between member and non-
099            member score distributions in privacy auditing (Definition 4.2). Importantly, we prove
100
               (Theorem 4.3) that our constructed output set enables the maximum auditing objective
               among all possible choices of output event set, i.e., is optimal.
101
102          • We numerically validate that for auditing DP mechanisms with closed-form output distri-
103            butions, using the optimal output set consistently enables tighter lower bounds than prior
104            methods. Our improvement is especially significant under a small number of auditing ex-
105
               amples (Figure 2), or when the output distribution is asymmetric or multi-modal (Figure 1).
106          • We also extend our framework to algorithms without closed-form output distributions (Sec-
107            tion 4.3), and approximately compute the optimal output set by thresholding the KDE esti-
               mation of likelihood ratio from empirical output samples. We experimentally demonstrate

112   2   R ELATED W ORKS
113
114   Score-based membership inference attacks and connections to output set selection. Membership
115
      inference attacks (MIAs) aim to infer whether a given data record was used for training a given target
      model. Intuitively, a higher performance of MIA indicates higher privacy risks, and thus MIAs serve
116
      as crucial tools for auditing DP mechanisms (Shokri et al., 2017). MIAs in the literature generally
117
      follow a fixed template: compute a membership inference attack score (such as loss (Sablayrolles
118   et al., 2019; Yeom et al., 2018; Ye et al., 2022), confidence (Salem et al., 2018), entropy (Song &
119   Mittal, 2021), loss trajectory (Liu et al., 2022) and likelihood ratio (Carlini et al., 2022)) on the
120   target model and target data, and then perform attack via thresholding the MIA score. That is,
121   predict member if the MIA score is above the threshold, and predict non-member if the MIA score
122   is below the threshold. To this end, performing score-based membership inference attacks (on the
123   output distributions in privacy auditing) is equivalent to heuristically selecting the output event set
124   to be the interval (Z, ∞) by a threshold Z, as utilized in the auditing experiment in Jagielski et al.
125   (2020). This strategy, as we will show in Section 4, 5 and 6, is strictly suboptimal for certain DP
126
      mechanisms compared to our proposed optimization-based output event set selection.
127   Optimization of output event set in privacy auditing. Bichsel et al. (2021) optimizes the posterior
128   over output event set, while ensuring that the probability of output event set is larger than a heuristi-
129   cally selected threshold (e.g., 0.05). Lu et al. (2022) further trains a ML model to learn the posterior
130   of training data given trained model, and selects the output set that maximizes the posterior. Steinke
131   et al. (2023) considers a series of output event sets constructed by a fixed strategy. Their constructed
132
      output event set takes the form of two intervals (−∞, c− ) and (c+ , ∞), where c− and c+ are real-
      valued thresholds for the MIA scores. They then search over the entire output domain (i.e., the set
133
      of values for discrete Monte Carlo samples) to identify a choice of c− and c+ that enables maxi-
134
      mum empirical auditing performance. However, prior works do not reach a consensus regarding the
135   optimal output set for privacy auditing. By contrast, our work proposes a novel objective (4) that
136   captures the expectation of auditing lower bound and serves as the key ingredient for us to prove an
137   optimality guarantee for selecting output set in privacy auditing given finite auditing samples.
138
139
      Evaluating worst-case success of membership inference attacks. A connected direction of re-
      search is on measuring privacy risk of machine learning algorithms by the success of membership
140
      inference attack over a small set of (worst-case) data samples, rather than the entire dataset. To this
141
      end, Carlini et al. (2022) propose to evaluate the true positive rate (TPR) at a low false positive rate
142   (FPR) as an indicator of privacy loss as worst-case data records. This is equivalent to evaluating
143   MIA only on a selected output set at the bottom tail of the MIA score distributions on all training
144   and test data (as this constrains the FPR to be small), rather than the whole output domain.

152   privacy tolerance δ as inputs, and returns a high-confidence statistical lower bound ε̂ := E(T ) for

359
      We now investigate the shape of Oτ for the shuffled noisy SGD under Laplace mechanism and
360
      Gaussian mechanism ((11), 12, 9, 10). Our observations are as follows.
361
362   Optimal set contains more than two intervals for multimodal distributions p and q In Figure 1,
363
      we observe that for mixture of Laplace or Gaussian distributions, the optimal output set contains
      more than two intervals. This means that the prior heuristic (Steinke et al., 2023) for selecting
364
      the top and bottom scores is strictly suboptimal. Specifically, the optimal output set Oτ in Figure
365
      1b contains the union of three disjoint intervals, one for the top-valued scores, one for bottom-
366   valued scores, and the other for medium-valued scores. The deeper reason is that the likelihood
367   ratio p(o)/q(o) is not monotonic in score domain o for distinguishing between general choices of
368   distributions p and q that are multi-modal. See Appendix D for more examples of other distributions.
369
      Optimal set is asymmetric for general mechanisms Motivated by Wilk’s theorem (Wilks, 1938)
370
      (which proves that the log-likelihood ratio statistics converges asymptotically to chi-squared dis-
371   tribution under the null hypothesis in binary hypothesis testing), we additionally experiment on
372   Chi-squared distributions p and q in Appendix D and observe that the optimal output set is asym-
373   metric. This is because the likelihood ratio p(o)/q(o) is not symmetric in score domain o due to
374   the asymmetric tails of chi-squared distributions p and q. This confirms the reason for the effective-
375   ness of the “abstaining” strategy in Steinke et al. (2023), and indicates the necessity of output set
376   optimization for privacy auditing. See Appendix D for more examples of other output distributions.

403   5.2   G AIN OF O PTIMIZING THE O UTPUT S ET O
404
405   We now experimentally investigate whether choosing the optimal output set enables gain for auditing
406   differential privacy lower bound, compared to prior (suboptimal) strategies (Bichsel et al., 2021;
407   Lu et al., 2022; Steinke et al., 2023) for selecting the output set. Our results are summarized in
408   Figure 2. We first observe that our output set optimization method consistently enables tighter
409
      privacy auditing lower bound than prior methods (in terms of higher mean and smaller standard
      deviation) across different specified guarantees ε, as observed in Figure 2 (left).
410
411   We also observe in Figure 2 (right) that our method is the most advantageous (compared to prior
412   methods) given a small number of auditing samples from the output distributions p and q. This is
413   intuitive because when the number of samples is small, the second stochastic variance term in our
414   optimization objective (4) is dominating. In such scenarios, it is crucial to subselect more auditing
415
      samples with the highest absolute log-likelihood ratio statistics, thus making the effect of output set
      optimization significant. By contrast, prior output set optimization objectives (Bichsel et al., 2021;
416
      Lu et al., 2022) do not capture such a sampling variance term, thus leading to suboptimal output sets.
417
418   Finally, we observe that the amount of auditing gain from the optimal output set is mechanism-
419   dependent. Specifically, when the number of auditing samples is large enough, all the methods
420   lead to matching privacy lower bounds (to the upper bound ε = 1) in Figure 2b, thus indicating
421
      that existing auditing techniques are tight for the mixture of Laplace mechanism. However, for the
      mixture of Gaussian mechanism (Figure 2d), the auditing gain from our method (compared to other
422
      methods) is significant even when the number of auditing samples is large. We hypothesize that this
423
      is because mixture of Gaussian distributions incurs lighter tails than mixture of Laplace distributions,
424   thus inducing a higher likelihood ratio in the center of the score domain and necessitating the output
425   set selection beyond the tails. It is an interesting open problem as to what general properties of the
426   mechanisms would make output set optimization more effective in privacy auditing.

528   7                C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we proposed a framework to compute the optimal output event set that maximizes the
531
      privacy lower bound in privacy auditing. We derive optimality guarantee for the output set formed
      by thresholding likelihood ratio statistics between member and non-member score distributions in
532
      the auditing experiment. Through experiments, we show that optimizing the output set effectively
533
      tightens the privacy lower bound estimate compared to existing auditing techniques, and provides
534   a more accurate analysis of differentially-private learning algorithms (such as subsampled Laplace
535   and Gaussian mechanisms as well as black-box DP-SGD training). Interestingly, we find that output
536   set optimization is the most effective when given a restricted number of auditing examples, or when
537   the likelihood ratio function is non-monotonic or assymetric (e.g., for mechanisms with asymmetric
538   or multi-modal output distributions). Future work includes extending our framework to other per-
539   formance metrics of inference attacks, and exploring more accurate approximations of the output set
      when only given empirical samples from the output distributions.

786   Determining the output event set O To obtain higher auditing lower bound, it is crucial to choose
787   an appropriate output set O for subselecting the member and non-member scores. Intuitively, this
788   is because we would like to avoid inferring membership for outputs that are equally likely to be
789   observed under member distribution p and non-member distribution q. For example, when p(o) ∼
790
      N (0, 1) and q(o) ∼ N (1, 1) follow Gaussian distributions, the output o = 21 is equally likely to be
      observed under p and q. By contrast, the output o = 0 is significantly more likely to be observed
791
      under p than q. Thus, intuitively we select a subset O of member scores S+ and non-member scores
792
      S− to increase their distinguishability (i.e., we would like O to include o = 0 but not o = 12 in the
793   Gaussian distribution example). To this end, prior works Bichsel et al. (2021); Lu et al. (2022) have
794   proposed to train a neural network model to learn the optimal output set O for subselecting member
795   and non-member scores by maximizing the likelihood difference.
796
      However, such strategies miss an important aspect of privacy auditing experiment: S+ and S− are
797
      empirical samples (rather than closed-form densities) from the output distribution. Consequently,
798
      the lower bound estimate (which is a random variable) for privacy auditing suffers from a significant
799   sampling error when the number of selected samples from S+ and S− is small. Thus, to reduce
800   the sampling error, we would like the output set O to contain as many samples from S+ and S− as
801   possible.
802
803
      Designing auditing function L To design an auditing function L such that Equation (1) is satisfied
804   under ε̂ :− L(S+ , S− , O; δ, β), prior works generally relies on the constraints of (ε, δ)-differential
805   privacy on the performance of a binary membership inference hypothesis test Wasserman & Zhou
806   (2010); Kairouz et al. (2015). In this paper, we focus on an advantage-based auditing experiment,
807   i.e., the design of the function L is based on bounding the advantage of membership inference with
808   the differential privacy guarantee (ε, δ). Extending our framework to other performance metrics of
809   binary membership inference hypothesis tests such as true positive rate (TPR) and false positive rate
      (FPR) is an interesting future work.
