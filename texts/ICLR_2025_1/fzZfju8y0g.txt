009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We address the problem of predicting the next state of a dynamical system gov-
012
               erned by unknown temporal partial differential equations (PDEs) using limited
013
               time-lapse data. While transformers offer a natural solution to this task through
014            in-context learning, the inductive bias of temporal PDEs suggests a more tailored
015            and effective approach. Specifically, when the underlying temporal PDE is fully
016            known, classical numerical solvers can evolve the state with only a few parame-
017            ters. Building on this observation, we introduce a large transformer-based hyper-
018            network that processes successive states to generate parameters for a much smaller
019            neural ODE-like solver, which then predicts the next state through time integra-
020            tion. This framework, termed as in-context neural PDE, decouples parameter
021            estimation from state prediction, offering closer alignment with classical numeri-
022
               cal methods for improved interpretability while preserving the in-context learning
               capabilities of transformers. Numerical experiments on diverse physical datasets
023
               demonstrate that our method outperforms standard transformer-based models, re-
024
               ducing sample complexity and improving generalization, making it an efficient
025            and scalable approach for spatiotemporal prediction in complex physical systems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Modeling dynamical systems from data is a highly active area of research with the potential to sig-
032   nificantly reduce both computational costs (Kidger, 2021) and the need for ad-hoc engineering to
033   predict future states (Farlow, 2012). In this paper, we consider dynamical systems described by par-
034   tial differential equations (PDEs). The conventional data-driven approach typically involves “learn-
035   ing” a fixed dynamic model from a large number of trajectories obtained from various initializations,
036   which is often referred to as neural operator learning (Kovachki et al., 2023; Boullé & Townsend,
037   2023). However, in many practical scenarios, the exact governing physical laws are unknown and
038   may vary across different trajectories, making those methods unsuitable and necessitating a more
039
      flexible data-driven approach. To address this limitation, we investigate the problem of predicting
      the next state of a system from a few successive time-lapse observations, where the underlying dy-
040
      namics are unknown and can vary between different trajectories. This problem is more complex as
041
      it implicitly requires both estimating the underlying physics of the dynamical system (inverse prob-
042   lem) and integrating it in time (forward problem). In contrast, these two tasks are typically tackled
043   independently in existing literature (Blechschmidt & Ernst, 2021).
044
045
      One promising way to handle scenarios with unknown or varying dynamics is through models that
      can effectively capture context—a sequence of preceding states—to infer the temporal patterns gov-
046
      erning the system. Transformers, known for their strong sequential learning capabilities (Vaswani
047
      et al., 2017), excel in this area through a mechanism called In-Context Learning (ICL) (Brown
048   et al., 2020), allowing them to adapt flexibly to different dynamics using prior state information.
049   This adaptability has enabled transformers to achieve remarkable performance in natural language
050   processing, where context plays a crucial role (Dubey et al., 2024). Recent works (McCabe et al.,
051   2024; Yang & Osher, 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) have also shown that transformers,
052   when trained on diverse PDE dynamics and initial conditions, can predict future states across differ-
053   ent contexts, positioning them as powerful tools for data-driven modeling of dynamical systems in
      physical domains.

054
      However, applying transformers to physical systems still remains challenging, as they often require
055   vast amounts of data to avoid overfitting and can struggle with predicting out-of-distribution tra-
056   jectories, leading to instabilities over multi-step rollouts (McCabe et al., 2023; 2024). In contrast,
057   classical numerical methods excel when the governing dynamics are known, evolving the system
058   accurately with minimal parameters and without any data training. These methods also often pre-
059   serve important structural properties of physics, such as continuous-time evolution and translation
060   equivariance (Mallat, 1999), which transformers do not naturally inherit. This lack of structure
061   preservation contributes to the limitations of transformers in physical applications, particularly in
062   terms of high sample complexity and instability.
063   In this work, we propose a novel framework named in-context neural PDE (IC-NPDE) that com-
064   bines the best of both worlds: an estimate of the parameters through context is incorporated into a
065   neural ODE-like solver (Chen et al., 2018). Specifically, a large hypernetwork processes successive
066   states of a trajectory to generate parameters for a much smaller neural PDE solver, which then pre-
067   dicts the next state through time integration. In this manner, we decouple the parameter estimation of
068   the dynamical context from state prediction, unlike previous transformer-based methods that tackle
069
      both tasks jointly. Our decoupling approach can be viewed as a meta-learning algorithm (Thrun &
      Pratt, 1998; Finn et al., 2017), a class of algorithms designed to self-adapt to specific tasks. In our
070
      setting, predicting the next state from limited time-series data is treated as a task that depends on
071
      (1) initial conditions, (2) physical parameters, and (3) even different underlying physical laws. The
072   neural network used in the ODE-like solver respects the underlying continuous-time nature of the
073   physics and preserves spatial translation equivariance through the use of Convolutional Neural Net-
074   works (CNNs). The parameters are fully learnable, offering greater flexibility in estimating spatial
075   derivatives (Bar-Sinai et al., 2019). Furthermore, our integrated network uses far fewer parameters
076   compared to a transformer, creating an information bottleneck (Tishby et al., 2000). This bottle-
077   neck forces the system to focus on the most essential aspects of the dynamics, preventing overfitting
078   and improving generalization, particularly for out-of-distribution states. By concentrating the rep-
079   resentation in a smaller parameter space, the model gains robustness and efficiency, making it more
080
      suitable for predicting complex physical systems.
081
082   Contributions. Our key contributions are as follows: (a) To our knowledge, this is the first work to
083   combine an ICL approach with differentiable PDE solvers for spatiotemporal prediction. (b) We pro-
084
      pose a framework that introduces a tailored inductive bias for physical systems, providing improved
      interpretability by aligning more closely with classical numerical methods. (c) Our method demon-
085
      strates superior learning performance across multiple physical systems using standard datasets in
086
      the literature and (d) achieves better numerical accuracy on multi-step rollouts compared to state-
087   of-the-art ICL models, with improved generalization. Upon publication, we will open-source our
088   implementation.

091   2   R ELATED WORKS
092
093   Classical and neural solvers. While classical PDE solvers remain the state-of-the-art for achiev-
094   ing high precision, neural network-based surrogate solvers (Lu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Kovachki
095
      et al., 2023) have opened up new possibilities for inferring approximate solutions quickly for certain
      PDEs. However, these solvers need to be trained on samples derived from the same PDE. Some vari-
096
      ants, such as (Karniadakis et al., 2021; Kochkov et al., 2021), allow the incorporation of corrective
097
      terms to approximate trajectory dynamics, but they still lack adaptability to the context, which is a
098   key feature of our method. Symbolic regression (Lemos et al., 2023) separates trajectory parameter
099   inference from the integration task but struggles to scale to high-dimensional data and handle large
100   search spaces effectively. In contrast, our method directly approximates the differential operators
101   using a cascade of small convolutional layers, enabling efficient learning in high-dimensional data.
102
103   Meta-learning strategies applied to dynamical systems. As an inspiration for our work, Metz
104   et al. (2022) pretrains an optimizer on a large collection of datasets and models, which can be viewed
105   as meta-learning a specific discretized dynamical system in order to surpass stochastic gradient
106   descent. In a different setting, Gusak et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2022) propose strategies to learn the
107   best ODE solver from a family of solvers, which could be combined with our approach. Meanwhile,
      Bar-Sinai et al. (2019) focuses on meta-learning differentiable filters for PDEs, aligning with one

108
      aspect of our methodology. In a different setting, de Avila Belbute-Peres et al. (2021) learns to map
109   PDE parameters to the corresponding physics-informed neural netowrk’s parameters (Karniadakis
110   et al., 2021); however, this lacks capability for ICL. The most closely related works to ours, to
111   the best of our knowledge, are McCabe et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Yang
112   & Osher (2024), which utilize transformers for in-context learning of neural operators for PDEs.
113   However, as discussed in the introduction, these methods lack inductive bias for physics and tend
114   to require a large amount of data to generalize. Xian et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022) use an
115   encoder/decoder strategy to embed dynamics in a latent space for predicting the next state; while
116   related, these approaches are conceptually closer to McCabe et al. (2024).

234   [t, t + 1], [t + 1, t + 2]. We leave this possibility for future work.

239   4.1   G ENERIC ARCHITECTURES
240
241   Integrated network architecture. The integrated network fθ is a CNN starting with a k × k
242   convolution, acting as a local differential operator, followed by six 1 × 1 convolutions that apply a
243   pointwise function. In detail, Let Cin and Chidden represent input and hidden channels, respectively,
244   with Cout = Cin . The first convolution has weights of shape (Chidden /2, Cin , k, k) and includes a skip
245   connection via concatenation. This is followed by a (Chidden , Cin +Chidden /2, 1, 1) convolution. Next,
246
      two blocks are applied, each consisting of two convolutions with weights (2Chidden , Chidden , 1, 1)
      and (Chidden , 2Chidden , 1, 1), followed by a skip connection. The final layer uses weights of shape
247
      (Cout , Chidden , 1, 1). Each 1 × 1 convolution is followed by group normalization (8 groups) and a
248
      GeLU activation. For datasets with non-periodic boundary conditions, we use zero-padding for the
249   spatial convolution and we add manually a mask of the boundaries as input to fθ .
250
251
252   Hypernetwork architecture. In all our experiments we assume ψ in Eq. 4 is a transformer. It
253
      as a CNN encoder and a MLP decoder outputting the parameters. Its encoder consists of three
      convolution layers of kernel sizes of 4, 2, 2 respectively, with GeLU activation, ending up with a
254
      patch size of 16. The hidden dimension (token space) is 384. After the encoder, we cascade 12
255
      time-space attention blocks, each containing a time attention, and axial attentions along each space
256   dimension (McCabe et al., 2024). Each attention block contains 6 heads and uses relative positional
257   encodings. The output of the attentions is averaged over both time and space, leaving only the
258   channel dimension. Finally, we branch a MLP with two hidden layers that progressively increases
259   the channel dimension to recover the expected parameter shape of θ.
260
261
      Multiple physics training. When jointly trained on multiple datasets, most of the weights in the
262
      hypernetwork are shared across datasets, except for the first 1 × 1 convolution, which is learned
263
      separately for each dataset to accommodate varying channel numbers from input, and the final MLP
264   weights, which are also learned independently per dataset to produce the parameters θ. Regarding
265   the CNN fθ , Chidden remains the same across different dataset, but the number of input and output
266   channels in the first and last convolution layers varies depending on the number of channels in the
267   data. Also, the kernel size of the first (and only) spatial filtering in fθ , is set to k = 5 for all the
268   datasets, except for compressible Navier-Stokes where increasing the kernel size to 11 was necessary
269   to obtain good performances. This is certainly due to the fact that the trajectory in this dataset makes
      much bigger movement from one step to the other.

359   4.3   I NFORMATION BOTTLENECK AND GENERALIZATION PROPERTIES
360
361   Our in-context neural PDE model employs a relatively small number of parameters θ (see Eq. 4) to
362   predict the next state through the integration of fθ , compared to the typical input sizes. We study the
363
      impact of this information bottleneck.
364   Parameter space visualization. When trained on multiple datasets, the parameters θ returned by
365   our hypernetwork depend on both the initial conditions and the PDE governing the given context.
366   We show that our framework is capable of reducing the variability introduced by the initial condi-
367   tion, allowing it to focus primarily on the PDE dynamics. First, let us separate θ = [θspatial , θpointwise ]
368   into the parameters of the first spatial convolution, and the parameters of the pointwise function that
369
      applies to the filtered states. Recall that the filtered states u ⋆ θspatial are analogous to discretizations
      of the local differential operators such as the gradient and Laplacian in standard numerical solver.
370

      the key for generalizing to initial conditions.

509   5      C ONCLUSION
510
511   In this paper, we introduced in-context neural PDE (IC-NPDE), a general and efficient framework
512   for in-context learning of dynamical systems governed by unknown temporal PDEs. Our approach
513   integrates neural PDE solvers, which leverage continuous-time dynamics and spatial translation
514   equivariance, with transformer-based hypernetworks that adapt to varying contexts to generate the
515   solver parameters. Compared to standard in-context learning methods based purely on transformers,
516   IC-NPDE achieves superior generalization and fine-tuning performance.
517   The integrated model in our framework, implemented using CNNs, is primarily inspired by finite
518   difference schemes on a uniform mesh. However, many challenging problems in physics involve
519   non-uniform meshes or arbitrary geometries. In such cases, adopting finite volume or finite ele-
520   ment schemes could be achieved using graph neural networks instead, as demonstrated in recent
521   works (Pfaff et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2022; Brandstetter et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). There are
522   also some other promising directions for expanding the capabilities of IC-NPDE. First, The neural
523   ODE-like structure enables flexible inclusion of future time step labels (e.g., t + 2) in training ob-
524   jective, allowing adaptation to varying data evolution speeds. Additionally, while our method has
525
      been validated on time-independent dynamics, extending it to time-dependent systems requires in-
      corporating temporal inputs into the hypernetworks, which is left for future exploration. Moreover,
526
      the integrated network in our framework, implemented using CNNs, is primarily inspired by finite
527
      difference schemes. Exploring other numerical methods, such as spectral methods, could lead to
528   architectures similar to FNOs.

827   C    T RAINING DETAILS
828
829   Hyperparameters. For joint training on multiple datasets, we considered the same hyperparame-
830   ters than McCabe et al. (2024), with batch size of 8, gradient accumulation every 5 batches, epoch
831   size of 2000 batches. For single model trainings, we considered batch size of 32 with no gradient
832   accumulation. In certain cases where the optimization was unstable, in particular, when we tried
833   using only 2 intermediate number of steps (see Tab. 6), we used gradient clipping, clipping the total
834   norm of the gradients to a default norm of 1.0. The data is split into train, validation, and test sets
835
      with an 80%, 10%, and 10% division, respectively.
836
837
      Optimization. All trainings were performed using the adaptive Nesterov optimizer (Xie et al.,
      2024) and a cosine schedule for the learning rate. Using AdamW optimizer with varying learning
838
      rate did not improve overall performance in the cases we tested. Both the single-dataset and multi-
839
      dataset experiments are run for a fixed number of 300 epochs. This means that during single-dataset
840   training, the model sees 7 times more data from that dataset compared to a model trained on 7
841   datasets simultaneously, where each dataset is sampled less frequently. We used a weight decay of
842   0.001 and drop path of 0.1.
