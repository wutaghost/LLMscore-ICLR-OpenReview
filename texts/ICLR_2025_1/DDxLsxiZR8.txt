                                                  A BSTRACT
033
034            Diffusion models have revolutionized generative tasks, especially in the domain of
035            text-to-image synthesis; however, their iterative denoising process demands sub-
036            stantial computational resources. In this paper, we present a novel acceleration
037            strategy that integrates token-level pruning with caching techniques to tackle this
038            computational challenge. By employing noise relative magnitude, we identify sig-
039            nificant token changes across denoising iterations. Additionally, we enhance token
040
               selection by incorporating spatial clustering and ensuring distributional balance.
               Our experiments demonstrate reveal a 50%-60% reduction in computational costs
041
               while preserving the performance of the model, thereby markedly increasing the
042
               efficiency of diffusion models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046   Recent advancements in diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song & Er-
047   mon, 2019) have revolutionized generative tasks, especially in the realm of text-to-image synthesis
048   (Karras et al., 2022). Models such as Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024), and Pixart (Chen et al.,
049   2023; 2024) have demonstrated their capability to produce diverse and high-quality images based
050
      on user inputs. Despite these successes, the iterative process required for denoising within these
      models often leads to lengthy and resource-intensive inference periods.
051
052   Recent works (Ma et al., 2024b;a) leverage temporal consistency in diffusion models, focusing on
053   the reuse of intermediate features across multiple timesteps. These methods cache features at prede-
      termined timesteps or within specific blocks, thereby reducing computational overhead by reusing


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      these cached features in subsequent timesteps instead of recomputing them. This approach has
055   proven effective in decreasing the overall computational cost while maintaining generative quality.
056
057   While most cache-and-reuse methods focus on bypassing certain blocks, thereby reducing the
058
      overall number of CUDA kernel launches to save computation, few explore optimizations at the
      intra-kernel level. Specifically, little attention has been given to reducing the latency within each
059
      individual kernel execution.
060
061   As mentioned in (Song et al., 2021; Song & Ermon, 2019), Diffusion involves solving a reverse-time
062   SDE using a a time-dependent model. Intuitively, not all patches in an single image require the same
063   precision when it comes to solving the SDE. To further enhance sampling efficiency, we propose a
064
      novel acceleration strategy that combines token-level pruning with cache mechanisms. We update
      a subset of tokens at each iteration, taking into account relative noise magnitude, spatial clustering,
065
      and distributional balance.
066
067   Our contributions can be listed as follows:
068          • We observe that token pruning involves ranking token importance while ensuring consistent
069            selection across timesteps and spatial dimensions.
070
071
             • We propose a simple method that accelerates diffusion models by doing pruning at token
               level according to relative noise magnitude, selection frequencies, and cluster awareness.
072
073          • Our experimental results, evaluated on various standard datasets and pretrained diffusion
074            models, demonstrate that it produces comparable results with 50 % MACs reduction at step
075            28 and 60 % MACs reduction at step 50 relative to the full size models.

077   2   R ELATED W ORK
078   Diffusion models have emerged as powerful generative frameworks in computer vision. However,
079   these models are compute-intensive, often constrained by the high computational cost. This com-
080   putational bottleneck has led to a surge of research focused on accelerating diffusion models. Here,
081   we highlight three major categories of approaches: parallelization, reduction of sampling steps, and
082   model pruning.
083   Parallelization Methods Despite traditional techniques like tensor parallelism, recent works have
084   introduced novel parallelization strategies specifically tailored to the characteristics of diffusion
085   models. DistriFusion (Li et al., 2024), for instance, hides the communication overhead within the
086   computation via asynchronous communication and introduces displaced patch parallelism, while
087   PipeFusion (Wang et al., 2024c) introduces displaced patch parallelism for Inference of Diffusion
088   Transformer Models (DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2022)) and ParaDiGMS (Shih et al., 2023) rum sampling
089   steps in parallel through iterative refinement.
090   Reducing Sampling Steps One of the core challenges with diffusion models is the large number of
091   sampling steps required to produce high-quality outputs, which directly translates to longer inference
092   times. Recent advancements such as DPM Solver (Lu et al., 2022) and Consistency Models (Song
093   et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2023) aim to address this bottleneck by developing fast solvers for
094   diffusion ODEs and directly mapping noise to data respectively.
095   Leveraging Feature Redundancy Recognizing the iterative nature of diffusion models and the
096   minimal changes in feature representations across consecutive steps, a growing body of research
097   has focused on developing cache-and-reuse mechanisms to reduce inference time. DeepCache (Ma
098   et al., 2024b) reuses the high-level features of the U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015). Block Cache
099   (Wimbauer et al., 2023) performs caching at a per-block level and adjusts the cached values using a
100   lightweight ’scale-shift’ mechanism. TGATE (Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) caches the output
101   of the cross-attention module once it converges. FORA (Selvaraju et al., 2024) reuses the outputs
102   from the attention and MLP layers to accelerate DiT inference.

179   3.2 C ORRELATION B ETWEEN P REDICTED N OISE AND H ISTORICAL N OISE
180   Previous work has demonstrated that the changes in features across consecutive denoising steps are
181   minimal. This observation motivates our decision to update only a subset of token features at each
182   step, thereby reducing computations.
183
      Furthermore, PFDiff (Wang et al., 2024a) has pointed out a notably high similarity in model out-
184   puts for the existing ODE solvers in diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs), especially when the
185   time step size ∆t is not extremely large. Building on these two phenomena, we selectively update
186   features for tokens that exhibit substantial changes in their output values, while skipping the feature
187   update and reusing the predicted noise from the previous iteration for the remaining tokens. This
188   reduces computational overhead while maintaining accuracy.
189
      We further observe that the relative magnitude of the noise predicted by the model is correlated with
190   the relative magnitude of historical noise. Specifically, nt − nt0 is proportional to nt−1 − nt0 . We
191   demonstrate this by plotting the L2 norm of relative noise magnitude derived from different prompts
192   and steps in Figure 3.

296   3.4 C LUSTERING G IVES R ISE TO M ORE S PATIAL D ETAILS
297   So far, our ultimate goal is to approximate the output image using token pruning. However, it
298   happens that toknes at certain positions tend to change synchronously across iterations.
299
300
      We observe that tokens with spatial adjacency tend to require similar selection frequencies. To ver-
      ify this, we perform an ablation study where consecutive rows of the output are selected at each
301
      iteration (i.e. step t0 + 1: row 1,2, step t0 + 2: row 3,4). As demonstrated in Figure 6, a simple
302
      sequential token selection strategy (column 3) yields strong results, even when masking 70% of the
303   tokens. Furthermore, incorporating clustering information (column 2) enhances detail preservation
304   compared to its non-clustering counterpart, outperforming the naive sequential strategy. For exam-
305   ple, in row 1, column 1, there is a lack of windows; in row 1, column 3, the windows appear blurry.
306   In row 2, column 1, there is an inconsistent smile; in row 2, column 3, the heart is missing. How-
307   ever, column 2 does not have these issues, as it maintains spatial consistency and incorporates many
308   details.
309   Therefore, we maintain that the proposed pruning algorithm should also take the spatial co-relation
310   into account so as to better appoximate the final output. To achieve this requirement, questions arise
311   such as:
312
            1. How should we split the output into several spatial co-related clusters?
313
314         2. What value should we grant each spatial cluster?
315
            3. How to perform token selection inside each cluster?
316

319

      We opt for positional encodings to enforce spatial-awareness due to their simplicity and low com-
320
      putational overhead. Our customized Positional Encoding is formulated as:

                                   n[i], i ∈ Ts,t−1 > n[i], i ∈ Tu,t−1

676
      For the hidden states h at step t:
677                                        ht [Tu,t ] = ht−1 [Tu,t ],
678                                        ht [Ts,t ] = Update(Ts,t )
679
       Thus, h for the unselected tokens remains unchanged, while the selected tokens are changed based
680   on their current activations using a model specific function Update.
681
      From this, we have:
682
683                       MSE(ht , ht−1 )[i],   i ∈ Ts,t > MSE(ht , ht−1 )[i],   i ∈ Tu,t
684   Since the predicted noise is a function of the hidden states, the magnitude of the predicted noise
685   relative to noise at step t0 is directly tied to the change in hidden states.

687
      As a result, at each step, we select tokens based on their relative noise magnitude.
