009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Unsupervised pretraining has been transformative in many supervised domains.
012            However, applying such ideas to reinforcement learning (RL) presents a unique
013            challenge in that fine-tuning does not involve mimicking task-specific data, but
014            rather exploring and locating the solution through iterative self-improvement. In
015            this work, we study how unlabeled prior trajectory data can be leveraged to learn
016            efficient exploration strategies. While prior data can be used to pretrain a set of
017            low-level skills, or as additional off-policy data for online RL, it has been unclear
018            how to combine these ideas effectively for online exploration. Our method SUPE
019            (Skills from Unlabeled Prior data for Exploration) demonstrates that a careful
020
               combination of these ideas compounds their benefits. Our method first extracts
               low-level skills using a variational autoencoder (VAE), and then pseudo-relabels
021
               unlabeled trajectories using an optimistic reward model, transforming prior data
022
               into high-level, task-relevant examples. Finally, SUPE uses these transformed
023            examples as additional off-policy data for online RL to learn a high-level policy
024            that composes pretrained low-level skills to explore efficiently. We empirically
025            show that SUPE reliably outperforms prior strategies, successfully solving a suite
026            of long-horizon, sparse-reward tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Unsupervised pretraining has been transformative in many supervised domains, such as lan-
033
      guage (Devlin et al., 2018) and vision (He et al., 2022). Pretrained models can adapt with small
      numbers of examples, and with better generality (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020). How-
034
      ever, in contrast to supervised learning, reinforcement learning (RL) presents a unique challenge
035
      in that fine-tuning does not involve further mimicking task-specific data, but rather exploring and
036   locating the solution through iterative self-improvement. Thus, the key challenge to address in pre-
037   training for RL is not simply to learn good representations, but to learn an effective exploration
038   strategy for solving downstream tasks.
039
      Pretraining benefits greatly from the breadth of the data. Unlabeled trajectories (i.e., those collected
040
      from previous policies whose objectives are unknown) are the most abundantly available, but using
041
      them to solve specific tasks can be difficult. It is not enough to simply copy behaviors, which can
042   differ greatly from the current task. There is an entanglement problem – general knowledge of the
043   environment is mixed in with task-specific behaviors. A concrete example is learning from unlabeled
044   locomotion behavior: we wish to learn how to move around the world, but not necessarily to the
045   locations present in the pretraining data. We will revisit this setting in the experimental section.
046
      The entanglement problem can be alleviated through hierarchical decomposition. Specifically, tra-
047
      jectories can be broken into segments of task-agnostic skills, which are composed in various ways
048   to solve various objectives. We posit that unlabeled trajectories thus present a twofold benefit, (1) as
049   a way to learn a diverse set of skills, and (2) as off-policy examples of composing such skills. No-
050   tably, prior online RL methods that leverage pretrained skills largely ignore the second benefit, and
051   discard the prior trajectories after the skills are learned (Ajay et al., 2021; Pertsch et al., 2021; Hu
052   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). We instead argue that such trajectories are critical, and can greatly
053   speed up learning. We make use of a simple strategy of learning an optimistic reward model from
      online samples, and pseudo-relabeling past trajectories with an optimistic reward estimate. The past

      We formalize these insights as SUPE (Skills from Unlabeled Prior data for Exploration), a recipe for
090   maximally leveraging unlabeled prior data in the context of exploration. The prior data is utilized
091   in two capacities, the offline and online phases. In the offline pretraining phase, we extract short
092   segments of trajectories and use them to learn a set of low-level skills. In the online phase, we
093   learn a high-level exploration policy, and again utilize the prior data by labelling each trajectory
094   segment with an optimistic reward estimate. By “double-dipping” in this way, we can utilize both
095   the low-level and high-level structure of prior trajectories to enable efficient exploration online.
096   Our main contribution is a simple method that leverages unlabeled prior trajectory data to both
097   pretrain skills offline and compose these skills efficiently online for exploration. We instantiate
098   SUPE with a variational autoencoder (VAE) to extract low-level skills, and an off-the-shelf off-
099   policy RL algorithm (Ball et al., 2023) to learn a high-level policy from both online and offline
100   data (Figure 1). Our empirical evaluations on a set of challenging sparse reward tasks show that
101   leveraging the unlabeled prior data during both offline and online learning is crucial for efficient
102   exploration, enabling SUPE to find the sparse reward signal more quickly and achieve more efficient
103   learning over all prior methods (none of which are able to utilize the data both online and offline).

105   2     R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Unsupervised skill discovery. Unsupervised skill discovery methods first began in the online set-
      ting, where RL agents were tasked with learning structured behaviors in the absence of reward

108
      signal (Gregor et al., 2016; Bacon et al., 2017; Florensa et al., 2017; Achiam et al., 2018; Eysenbach
109   et al., 2018; Sharma et al., 2020; Hansen et al., 2020; Park et al., 2023b). These insights naturally
110   transferred to the offline setting as a method of dealing with unlabeled trajectory data. Offline skill
111   discovery methods largely comprise of two categories, those who extract skills based on optimiz-
112   ing unsupervised reward signals (in either the form of policies (Touati et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023;
113   Frans et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024b) or Q-functions (Chen et al., 2024)), and those who utilize
114   conditional behavior-cloning over subsets of trajectories (Paraschos et al., 2013; Merel et al., 2018;
115   Shankar & Gupta, 2020; Ajay et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2021; Pertsch et al., 2021; Nasiriany et al.,
116   2022). Closest to our method in implementation are Ajay et al. (2021) and Pertsch et al. (2021),
117
      who utilize a trajectory-segment VAE to learn low-level skills, and learn a high-level policy online.
      However, in contrast to prior methods which all utilize offline data purely for skill-learning and do
118
      not keep it around during online training, we show that utilizing the data via relabeling is critical for
119
      fast exploration.
120
121   Offline to online reinforcement learning. The offline-to-online reinforcement learning meth-
122   ods (Xie et al., 2021b; Song et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2022; Agarwal et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023;
123
      Zheng et al., 2023; Ball et al., 2023; Nakamoto et al., 2024) focus on efficient online learning with
      the presence of offline data (often labeled with the reward value). Many offline RL approaches can
124
      be applied to this setting – simply run offline RL first on the offline data to convergence as an ini-
125
      tialization and then continue training for online learning (using the combined dataset that consists
126   of both offline and online data) (Kumar et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2021; Tarasov et al., 2024).
127   However, such approaches often result in slow online improvements as offline RL objectives tend
128   to overly constrain the policy behaviors to be close to the prior data, limiting the exploration capa-
129   bility. On the other hand, off-policy online RL methods can also be directly applied in this setting
130   by directly treating the offline data as additional off-policy data in the replay buffer and learning the
131   policy from scratch (Lee et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023; Ball et al., 2023). While related in spirit,
132   these methods cannot be directly used in our setting as they require offline data to have reward labels.
133   Data-driven exploration. A common approach for online exploration is to augment reward bonuses
134   to the perceived rewards and optimize the RL agent with respect to the augmented rewards (Stadie
135   et al., 2015; Bellemare et al., 2016; Houthooft et al., 2016; Pathak et al., 2017; Tang et al., 2017;
136   Ostrovski et al., 2017; Achiam & Sastry, 2017; Merel et al., 2018; Burda et al., 2018; Ermolov &
137   Sebe, 2020; Guo et al., 2022; Lobel et al., 2023). While most exploration methods operate in the
138   purely online setting and focus on adding bonuses to the online replay buffer, recent works also start
139   to explore a more data-driven approach that makes use of an unlabeled prior data to guide online
140
      exploration. Li et al. (2024) explore adding bonuses to the offline data, allowing them to optimize
      the RL agent to be optimistic about states in the data, encouraging exploration around the offline data
141
      distribution. Our method explores a similar idea of adding bonuses to the offline data but for training
142
      a high-level policy, allowing us to compose pretrained skills effectively for exploration. Hu et al.
143   (2023) explore a slightly different strategy of learning a number of policies using offline RL that
144   each optimizes for a random reward function. Then, it samples actions from these policies online to
145   form an action pool from which the online agent can choose to select for exploration. This approach
146   does not utilize offline data during the online phase and require all the policies (for every random
147   reward function) to be represented separately. In contrast, our method makes use of the offline data
148   as off-policy data for updating the high-level policy and our skills are represented using a single
149   network (with the skill latent being the input to our network). As we will show in our experiments,
150   being able to use offline data is crucial for learning to explore in the environment efficiently.
151   Hierarchical reinforcement learning. The ability of RL agents to explore and behave effectively
152   over a long horizon is an important research goal in the field of hierarchical RL (HRL) (Dayan
153   & Hinton, 1992; Dietterich, 2000; Vezhnevets et al., 2016; Daniel et al., 2016a; Kulkarni et al.,
154   2016; Vezhnevets et al., 2017; Peng et al., 2017; Riedmiller et al., 2018; Nachum et al., 2018; Ajay
155   et al., 2021; Shankar & Gupta, 2020; Pertsch et al., 2021; Gehring et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021a).
156   HRL methods typically learn a high-level policy to leverage a space of low-level primitive policies
157
      online. These primitives can be either manually specified (Dalal et al., 2021) or pre-trained using
      unsupervised skill discovery methods as discussed above. While many existing works learn or fine-
158
      tune the primitives online along with the high-level policy (Dietterich, 2000; Kulkarni et al., 2016;
159
      Vezhnevets et al., 2016; 2017; Nachum et al., 2018; Shankar & Gupta, 2020), others opt for a less
160   flexible but simpler formulation where the primitives are kept fixed after an initial pre-training phase
161   and only the high-level policy is being learned online (Peng et al., 2017; Riedmiller et al., 2018; Ajay


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2021; Pertsch et al., 2021; Gehring et al., 2021). Our work adopts the later strategy where we
163   offline pre-train skills using a static, unlabeled dataset. None of prior HRL methods simultaneously
164   leverage offline data for skill pre-training and as additional off-policy data for high-level policy
165   learning online. As we show in our experiments, both of them are crucial in achieving sample
166   efficient learning on challenging sparse-reward tasks.
167
      Options framework. Many existing works on building hierarchical agents also adopt the options
168
      framework (Sutton et al., 1999; Menache et al., 2002; Chentanez et al., 2004; Mannor et al., 2004;
169   Şimşek & Barto, 2004; Şimşek & Barto, 2007; Konidaris, 2011; Daniel et al., 2016a; Srinivas
170   et al., 2016; Daniel et al., 2016b; Fox et al., 2017; Bacon et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2019; Bagaria
171   & Konidaris, 2019; Bagaria et al., 2024). Different from the approach we take that learns latent
172   skills with a fixed time horizon (H = 4 in all our experiments), the options framework provides
173   a more flexible way to learn skills with varying time horizon, often defined by learnable initiation
174   and/or termination conditions (Sutton et al., 1999). We opt for the simplified skill definition be-
175   cause it allows us to bypass the need to learn initiation or termination conditions, and frame the skill
176   pretraining phase as a simple supervised learning task.

197   4    S KILLS FROM U NLABELED P RIOR DATA FOR E XPLORATION (SUPE)
198
199   In this section, we describe in detail how we utilize the unlabeled trajectory dataset to accelerate
200   online exploration. Our method, SUPE, can be roughly divided into two parts. The first part is an
201
      offline pretraining phase where we extract skills from the unlabeled prior data with an trajectory-
      segment VAE. The second part is the online learning phase where we train a high-level off-policy
202
      agent to compose the pretrained skills leveraging examples from both prior data and online replay
203
      buffer. Algorithm 1 describes our method.
204
205   Pretraining with trajectory VAE. Since we only have access to an unlabeled dataset of tra-
206   jectories, we must capture all the behaviors in the data as accurately as possible. At the same
207
      time, we aim to make the dataset directly usable for training a high-level skill-setting policy
      in hope that this high-level policy can be trained in a more sample-efficient way (compared to
208
      only having access to the online samples). We achieve this by adopting a trajectory VAE design
209
      from prior methods (Ajay et al., 2021; Pertsch et al., 2021) where a short segment of trajectory
210   τ = {s0 , a0 , s1 , · · · , sH−1 , aH−1 } is first fed into a trajectory encoder fθ (z|τ ) that outputs a distri-
211   bution over the latent skill z, then a skill policy πθ (a|s, z) is used to reconstruct the actions in the
212   trajectory segment. Such a design helps us directly map trajectory segments to their corresponding
213   skill policies, effectively allowing us to transform the segment into a high-level transition in the form
214   of (current state: s0 , action: z, next state: sH ). As result, such transition can be directly consumed
215   by any off-policy RL algorithm in the online phase to update the high-level policy πψ (z|s) (as we
      will explain in the next section in more details). We also learn a state-dependent prior pθ (z|s),

      5     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
313
      We present a series of experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of our method to discover fast
314
      exploration strategies. We specifically focus on long-horizon, sparse-reward settings, where online
315
      exploration is especially important. In particular, we aim to answer the following questions:
316
317         1. Can we leverage unsupervised trajectory skills to accelerate online learning?
318         2. Is our method able to find goals faster than prior methods?
319         3. How sensitive is the performance of our method is to its hyperparameters?
320
321   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
322
323   We conduct our experiments on 7 challenging sparse-reward domains (Figure 2, (a) - (g)). We
      provide a brief description of each of the domains below with more details available in Appendix D.

324
      State-based locomotion domains: AntMaze (a), HumanoidMaze (b), AntSoccer (c). The first set
325   of domains involve controlling and navigating a robotic agent in a complex environment. AntMaze
326   is a standard benchmark for offline-to-online RL from D4RL (Fu et al., 2020). HumanoidMaze and
327   AntSoccer are locomotion domains from OGBench, a offline goal-conditioned RL benchmark (Park
328   et al., 2024a). The goal of agent (either ant or humanoid) is to navigate to a goal location in a fixed
329   maze layout. Each of the AntMaze and HumanoidMaze domains has three different maze layouts.
330   For AntMaze, we test on four different goal location for each maze layout. For HumanoidMaze and
331   AntSoccer we test on one goal, and for HumanoidMaze we use both the navigate and stitch
332   datasets. In the AntSoccer task, the agent needs to additionally dribble a soccer ball and move the
333
      ball to the goal location as well.
334   State-based manipulation domains: Kitchen (d), Cube (e), Scene (f). Next, we consider a set
335   of manipulation domains that require a wide range of manipulation skills. Kitchen is a standard
336   benchmark from D4RL where a robotic arm needs to complete a set of manipulation tasks (e.g., turn
337   on the microwave, move the kettle) in sequence in a kitchen scene. Cube and Scene are two offline
338   goal-conditioned RL benchmark domains from OGBench (Park et al., 2024a). For Cube, the robotic
339
      arm must arrange one or more cube objects to desired goal locations (e.g., stacking on top of each
      other) that mainly involves pick and place motions. For Scene, the robotic arm can interact with a
340
      more diverse set of objects: a window, a drawer, a cube and two locks that control the window and
341
      the drawer. The tasks in Scene are also relatively longer, requiring a composition of multiple atomic
342   behaviors (e.g., locking and unlocking, opening the drawer/window, moving the cube).
343
344   In addition to the six state-based domains, we also experiment with a challenging visual-domain,
345
      Visual AntMaze (g) introduced by Park et al. (2023a), where the agent must rely on 64 × 64 image
      observations of its surroundings, as well as the proprioceptive information to navigate the maze.
346
347   To evaluate our method on these domains, we simply take the datasets in these benchmarks and
348   remove the reward label. We also remove any information in the transition that may reveal the
349   information about the termination of an episode. For all of these tasks, we use a −1/0 sparse reward
350   function where the agent receives −1 when it has not found the goal and it receives 0 when it reaches
351
      the goal location. For all of the domains above, we use the normalized return, a standard metric for
      D4RL (Fu et al., 2020) environments, as the main evaluation metric. For the Kitchen domain, the
352
      normalized return represents the average percentage of the tasks that are solved. For tasks in other

      5.2   C OMPARISONS
357
358
      While there is no existing method in our setting that utilizes unlabeled prior data in both the pretrain-
359   ing phase and the online learning phase, there are methods that use the prior data in either phase.
360   We first consider two baselines that do not use pretraining and directly perform online learning.
361
362
      Online. This baseline discards the offline data and the exploration is done with online reward bonus
      implemented by random network distillation (RND) (Burda et al., 2018). For all the baselines below
363
      (including our method), we add online RND bonus to the replay buffer to encourage exploration.
364
365   ExPLORe (Li et al., 2024). This baseline is similar to our method in the sense that it also uses
366   exploration bonus and offline data to encourage exploration. The one crucial difference is that it
367   does not perform unsupervised skill pretraining and learns a 1-step policy directly online. As we
368
      will show, pretraining is crucial for our method to find goals faster and lead to more efficient online
      learning. It is worth noting that the original ExPLORe method does not make use of online RND. To
369
      make the comparison fair, we additionally add online RND bonus to this baseline to help it explore
370
      better online. For completeness, we include the performance of the original ExPLORe in Figure 12.
371
372   We then consider additional baselines that use the prior data during a pretraining phase but do not
373   use the data during online learning.
374   Diffusion BC + JSRL. This baseline is an upgraded version of the BC + JSRL baseline used in
375   ExPLORe (Li et al., 2024). Instead of using a Gaussian policy (as used by Li et al. (2024)), we
376   use an expressive diffusion model to behavior clone the unlabeled prior data. At the beginning of
377   each online episode, we roll out the policy for a random number of steps from the initial state before
      using switching to the online RL agent (Uchendu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). One might expect


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      that an expressive enough policy class can model the behavior of the prior good enough such that it
379   can form a good prior for exploration online.
380
381   Online with Skills. We also consider two skill-based baselines where the prior data is discarded in
382
      the online phase and the high-level policy is trained from scratch online with exploration bonus. We
      experiment with two types of pretraining skills. The first one, is the trajectory VAE skill used in our
383
      method. The second one is from a recently proposed unsupervised offline skill discovery method
384
      where skills are pretrained to be able to traverse a learned Hilbert representation space (Park et al.,
385   2024b) (HILP). We use the exact same high-level RL agent as our method except that the agent no
386   longer makes use of the prior data online. It is worth noting that the baseline that uses the trajectory
387   VAE skill is very similar to SPiRL (Pertsch et al., 2021), a prior skill-based online RL method
388   that also pretrains skills with a trajectory VAE. The only difference is that we make two additional
389   improvements on top of SPiRL. The first improvement is replacing the KL constraint with entropy
390   regularization (same as our method as described in Section 4, practical implementation details). The
391   second improvement is the online RND bonus that is also added to all other methods.
392   Finally, we introduce a novel baseline that also uses prior data during pretraining and online explo-
393   ration, but uses HILP skills rather than trajectory-based skills.
394
      HILP w/ Offline Data. We observe that HILP skills can also utilize the offline data via relabeling.
395
      Recall that HILP learns a latent space of the observations (via an encoder ϕHILP ) and learns skills
396
      that move agent in a certain direction z (skill) in the latent space. For any high-level transition
397
      (s0 , sH ), we simply take ẑ ← ∥ϕϕHILP
                                         HILP (sH )−ϕHILP (s0 )
                                              (sH )−ϕHILP (s0 )∥2 , the normalized difference vector that points
398
      from s0 to sH in the latent space. We use the normalized difference vector because the pretrained
399
      HILP skill policy takes in normalized skill vectors. We use the exact same high-level RL agent as
400
      our method except that the skill relabeling is done by computing the latent difference rather than
401   using the trajectory encoder (fθ (z|τ )).
402
403   For the visual antmaze environment, we use the same image encoder used in RLPD (Ball et al.,
404
      2023). We also follow one of our baselines, ExPLORe (Li et al., 2024), to use ICVF (Ghosh et al.,
      2023), a method that uses task-agnostic value functions to learn image/state representations from
405
      passive data. ICVF takes in an offline unlabeled trajectory dataset with image observations and
406
      pretrain an image encoder in an unsupervised manner. Following ExPLORe, we take the weights
407   of the image encoder from ICVF pretraining to initialize the image encoder’s weights in the RND
408   network. To make the comparison fair, we also apply ICVF to all baselines (details in Appendix E).

475
476       Even though we have demonstrated that our method is able to achieve higher success rate faster than
477
          prior works, it is still not clear if our method can actually lead to better exploration (instead of simply

478
          in the AntMaze domain. Figure 4 reports the number of online environment interaction steps for
479
          the agent to reach the goal for the first time. Such a metric allows us to assess how efficiently the

482       goals consistently and vice-versa. Figure 4 shows that our method reaches goals faster than every
483       baseline on all three maze layouts, which confirms that our method not only learns faster, but does

510       5.5                  H OW SENSITIVE IS THE PERFORMANCE OF OUR METHOD IS TO ITS HYPERPARAMETERS ?
511
512      In this section, we study the sensitivity of two hyperparameters, 1) α: the amount of optimism (RND
513      coefficient) used when labeling offline data with UCB rewards (Equation 2), and 2) H: the length
514
         of the skill. We select one representative task from each domain and study the how different α and
         H values affect our performance on these tasks (Figure 5). For the RND coefficient, we test a wide
515
         range of values. When α = 0, the RND exploration bonus is turned off, it significantly lowers the
516
         performance of our method, highlighting the importance of optimistic labeling on efficient online
517      exploration. While we observe some variability across individual tasks (Appendix 5, Figure 9), our
518      method is generally not very sensitive to the RND coefficient value. The aggregated performance is
519      similar for α ∈ {2, 4, 8, 16}. Another key hyperparmeter in our method is the skill horizon length
520      (see Figure 5, right). We find that while there is some variability across individual tasks (Appendix
521      5, Figure 10), a skill horizon length of 4 generally performs the best, and that shorter or longer
522      horizons perform much worse on certain tasks. We use H = 4 for all experiments in the paper.

525       6                   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
526
527      In this work, we propose a novel method, SUPE, that leverages unlabeled prior trajectory data to
528      accelerate online exploration and learning. The key insight is to use unlabeled trajectories twice,
529      to 1) extract a set of low-level skills offline, and 2) serve as additional data for a high-level off-
530      policy RL agent to compose these skills to explore in the environment. This allows us to effectively
531
         combine the strengths from unsupservised skill pretraining and sample-efficient online RL methods
         to solve a series of challenging long-horizon sparse reward tasks significantly more efficiently than
532
         existing methods. Our work opens up avenues in making full use of prior data for scalable, online
533
         RL algorithms. First, our pre-trained skills remain frozen during online learning, which may hinder
534      online learning when the skills are not learned well or need to be updated as the learning progresses.
535      Such problems could be alleviated by utilizing a better skill pretraning method, or allowing the low-
536      level skills to be fine-tuned online. A second limitation of our approach is the reliance on RND to
537      maintain an upper confidence bound on the optimistic reward estimate. Although we find that RND
538      works without ICVF on high-dimensional image observations in Visual AntMaze, the use of RND
539      in other high dimensional environments may require more careful consideration. Possible future
         directions include examining alternative methods of maintaining this bound.
