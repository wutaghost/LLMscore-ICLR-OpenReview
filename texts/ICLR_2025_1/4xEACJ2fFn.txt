009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Variational autoencoders (VAE) encode data into lower-dimensional latent vec-
013            tors before decoding those vectors back to data. Once trained, decoding a random
014
               latent vector from the prior usually does not produce meaningful data, at least
               when the latent space has more than a dozen dimensions. In this paper, we inves-
015
               tigate this issue drawing insight from high dimensional physical systems such as
016
               spin-glasses, which exhibit a phase transition from a high entropy random con-
017            figuration to a lower energy and more organised state when cooled quickly in the
018            presence of a magnetic field. The latent vectors of a standard VAE are by construc-
019            tion distributed uniformly on a hypersphere, and thus similar to the high entropy
020            spin-glass state. We propose to formulate the latent variables of a VAE using
021            hyperspherical coordinates, which allows compressing the latent vectors towards
022            an island on the hypersphere, thereby reducing the latent sparsity, analogous to a
023            quenched spin-glass. We propose a new parametrization of the latent space with
024            limited computational footprint that improves the generation ability of the VAE.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
031   In today’s machine learning landscape, and deep learning in particular, one of the main mathematical
032
      tools to represent data (e.g. images) are high dimensional (HD) Euclidean spaces.
033   However, our intuition about Euclidean geometry stems from the physical world and everyday life,
034   which are low dimensional spaces (mostly two and three dimensions). This presents a challenge be-
035   cause HD spaces behave, mathematically speaking, in very different ways than their low dimensional
036   counterparts, often in ways that seem counterintuitive or even paradoxical if interpreted through low
037   dimensional intuition.
038   HD spaces have been used in physics, for example to model the state space of systems such as
039   magnetic materials. We will argue in this paper that the state space of some physical systems studied
040   in statistical physics has a remarkable similarity with the HD spaces created by generative models,
041   in particular Variational Autoencoders (VAE) (Diederik P Kingma, 2014). We will discuss how
042   our handling of HD spaces in machine learning can benefit from the intuition about those physical
043   systems.
044   We will highlight how issues associated with VAE are related to volume and entropy, that create
045   voids and sparsity in latent spaces, hampering their performance to generate meaningful new sam-
046   ples, even when reconstruction metrics can be optimized very well.
047
      Finally, we will propose a method that parametrize latent data on the hypersphere with hyperspher-
048   ical coordinates. This allows manipulating the data distribution on the latent manifold more effec-
049   tively. In particular, we use it to compress the latent manifold volume and reduce the sparsity. This
050   is made possible thanks to an efficient transformation between Cartesian and hyperspherical coor-
051   dinates, which can be implemented with minimal computational overhead using a fully vectorized
052   algorithm, for high enough dimensions (it becomes costly in the very large case; but, as our results
053   show, those cases are of no practical interest from the point of view of the metrics we are checking,
      see Fig.2).

      1.1   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER
055
056   An autoencoder (AE) is a common self-supervised method to encode the data into a latent space of
057   lower dimension n. Variational autoencoder (VAE) (Diederik P Kingma, 2014; Kingma & Welling,
058   2019) introduces a prior to control the distribution of the latent, allowing to sample the latent space
059   and untangle the dimensions (Rolı́nek et al., 2019; Bhowal et al., 2024). In its simplest implemen-
060   tation, a VAE consists in a probabilistic encoder which, for each input point x ∈ X from a dataset
061   X, produces a latent distribution qx = N (µx , σx ). During training, the reparameterization trick is
062
      used: the encoder estimates µ and σ, and a sample z from qx is computed as z = µ + ϵ ⊙ σ, where
      ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication. Then, the decoder is applied to this sample to obtain the
063
      reconstructed datapoint, x̂z . The VAE’s loss can then be interpreted as an AE (with its Mean Square
064

065   space by forcing each of the encoded distributions to become similar to a prior one (N (0, I) in this
066   implementation), where KLD refers to the Kullback-Leibler divergence. The two mentioned terms
067   are computed over a mini batch of size Nb :

081   1.2   H IGH D IMENSIONAL S PACES IN M ATHEMATICS
082
083   A multivariate Gaussian sampling in a HD Euclidean space of dimension n is such the probability
084   to find samples close to the origin is close to zero (despite having maximum√ probability density) and
085   most of the samples lie close to a (n − 1)−hypersphere, Sn−1    √ , of radius
                                                                       n
                                                                                     n. The distribution of
086   the norm of those samples follows a χ(n) distribution. Therefore, the samples are located within a
087   region very√close to the hypersphere, region which becomes very thin in high dimensions, relative to
088   the radius n. These effects are called ‘concentration of measure’, in the mathematical literature.
089   As n increases, a multivariate Gaussian tends towards the uniform distribution on that hypersphere.
090   In addition, any two samples from N (0, In ) are always almost orthogonal to each other (this is
091   called almost-orthogonality). A formal description of these phenomena can be found elsewhere
092   (Vershynin, 2018). See also Appendix A.4.
093
      These facts are closely related to how (hyper-)volume behaves in HD spaces: if we consider the
094   standard uniform measure on the hypersphere, then most of its volume or mass is concentrated in
095   very thin ‘equatorial’ bands for any randomly chosen north pole (this is, of course, just an intuitive
096   statement, for a formal description see Wainwright (2019)). The contrast with our intuition coming
097   from two dimensional spheres is remarkable. In the next section, we will review that similar HD
098   spaces exist for spin-glass systems.

100   1.3   H IGH D IMENSIONAL S PACES IN (S TATISTICAL ) P HYSICS
101
102   Consider a system consisting of n persons each simultaneously tossing a coin. After the tossing, we
                                                      n
103   can record the result with a vector x ∈ {H, T } (H for heads, T for tails). For example, it could be
104
      x0 = (H, H, T, H, ..., T ). Each of these vectors is called a possible microstate for the system.
105   Given a microstate, we could define a function FH (x) on microstates which for example, counts how
106   many heads are in that microstate: the value obtained is called a macrostate. Different microstates
107   can give rise to the same macrostate. In statistical mechanics, the (Boltzmann) entropy of a system
      is proportional to the natural logarithm of the number of different microstates giving rise to a same

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      macrostate. The configurations that maximize this number (and, thus, also entropy) are the thermal
109   equilibrium ones.
110
111   For a general continuum microstate space, the thermal equilibrium configurations are characterized
112   by the Gibbs probability measure: µ(dx) ∝ e−βH(x) ν0 (dx), where β is the inverse temperature,
113
      ν0 (dx) is a fixed reference measure on the submanifold of Rn formed by all the possible microstates,
      and H(x) is the energy function of the system (Bricmont, 2022).
114
115   there is a class of systems that have been extensively studied called spin glasses (Parisi, 2002). In
116   these systems, the submanifold of microstates is given precisely by the (n − 1)-hypersphere, Sn−1
                                                                                                      √ ,
                                                                                                        n
117   and ν0 (dx) by the standard uniform measure on it. The dimension n is taken to be very large. The
118   uniform measure is one of maximal entropy, even among all the measures of thermal equilibrium.
119
      At very high temperatures, µ(dx) ≈ ν0 (dx), two ‘replicas’ of the system are two i.i.d. samples from
120   the Gibbs measure. Their overlap, measured by their inner product, will be almost zero, because of
121   the almost-orthogonality effect of HD spaces mentioned before. This means that these samples are
122   in the ‘equatorial’ region, where most of the volume is concentrated. The system is said to be in a
123   ‘replica symmetric phase’.
124
      Until a critical temperature value, all solutions have this qualitative behaviour. This is to be expected
125
      since the function whose stationary points define a general solution for these systems is indifferent
126   to a permutation of replicas. What is surprising in these systems is the emergence at low enough
127   temperatures of a different phase, which is not replica symmetric; that is, the inner product or
128   correlation between them is appreciably different from zero. This is called the ‘replica symmetry
129   breaking phase’ (Montanari & Sen, 2024). Two replicas, in this case, lie in a very thin band or
130   ring centered at a deep minimum of the energy function (Subag, 2017), center which is not in the
131   initial high volume equatorial region. This ring is a (n − 2)-hypersphere, submanifold of the initial
132   (n − 1)-hypersphere.
133   We discussed so far the ‘static’ structure of the Gibbs measure across the temperature range. A
134   separate and complicated question is how, starting from a high temperature state, one can reach dy-
135   namically, by some physical process1 , the replica symmetry breaking phase; that is, to dynamically
136   achieve this phase transition.
137
      This can be very tricky, since the local minima landscape of the energy in the high entropy region
138   is very rugged (with exponentially many local minima), and one would need to overcome it first in
139   order to reach the less entropic regions of the hypersphere (Arous & Jagannath, 2024). In practice, it
140   is usually done by ‘quenching’ processes, where the system is suddenly cooled down in the presence
141   of externally applied magnetic fields. The energy function Pfor a p−spin glass in the presence of an
142   external magnetic field h is given by H(x) = Pp (x) − j hj xj , where Pp (x) is a homogeneous
143   random p−polynomial (we omit the normalization constants).
144
      The high entropy ‘replica symmetric phase’ of the spin-glass is analogous to the training of a VAE
145   where the KLD term forces the latent samples to be uniformly distributed on the hypersphere (the
146   dynamics of spin glasses and the training process of some deep learning models has already received
147   some attention in the literature, for example in Baity-Jesi et al. (2018), and also Arous et al. (2022);
148   in this work, though, we will focus our attention on some other issues, more geometrical in nature).
149   In the next section, we argue that this brings an issue related to the sparsity of the resulting latent
150   space.

152   1.4   H IGH D IMENSIONAL S PACES IN G ENERATIVE M ODELS (VAE)
153
154   One use case of the VAE is to generate data. Since the latent distribution is known (high dimen-
155   sional multivariate Gaussian), one could sample from that distribution and decode the latent vector
156   to generate a novel data sample. This works very well for simple data and low dimension latent
157   spaces (e.g., MNIST with n = 2, as done in Diederik P Kingma (2014)), but not so well for more
158   complicated data or high dimensional latent (e.g., CIFAR10, or MNIST with n = 32 as in Cinelli
159   et al. (2021)). Indeed, variations of the VAE are commonly used in generative models of images and
160

      videos (Jonathan Ho Ajay Jain, 2020), but the latent space is not sampled directly: instead, a reverse
163   diffusion process dynamically transforms a random sample into a valid latent location.
164
165   One of the reasons why VAE perform poorly when sampling directly the high dimensional latent
166
      is because of conflicting constraints. On the one hand, high resolution images need many latent
      dimensions to capture all the information they convey. Then, the VAE KLD divergence term in
167
      the loss encourages the system to distribute this HD latent uniformly on the hypersphere, that is, to
168
      maximize entropy. By doing so, the latent becomes extremely sparse given the number of training
169   samples and the immensity of the latent hyperspherical manifold (the volume growing exponentially
170   with the number of dimensions; in these regimes, trying to find a specific microstate is akin to a
171   ‘needle in the haystack’ kind of situation). The sparsity hampers any attempt to model the latent as
172   a continuous manifold (Peng et al., 2023). But, on the other hand, this usually leads to meaningless
173   decoding of random samples from the prior. These two opposing forces (sparsity due to HD spaces
174   needed to model high resolution images vs need for a continuous manifold for meaningful generation
175   from all of the latent space) conspire to severely limit the capacity of VAEs to function as generative
176   models.
177   From the previous sections, the regions of high entropy are such that, for a given macrostate, there
178   is an enormous number of different microstates that can realize it. For tasks such as clustering,
179   generation, interpolation, etc., one is interested in specific macrostates of the system. The disordered
180   high multiplicity of possible microstates that can give rise to these macrostates in the high entropy
181   regime may hinder the ability of the model to perform these tasks, as we will explicitly show in the
182   experimental section.
183   Since the samples in latent space live on the hypersphere, it comes naturally to consider using hy-
184   perspherical coordinates to describe latent variables.

187   2   R ELATED WORKS
188
189   ‘Hyperspherical Variational Auto-Encoder’ Davidson et al. (2018) proposed replacing the standard
190   Euclidean KLD divergence with a KLD divergence between a uniform distribution on the hyper-
191   sphere as a prior, and a von Mises-Fisher distribution as an approximate posterior. A different way
192   of building a Hyperspherical Variational Auto-Encoder is proposed in Bonet et al. (2022), based
193   on a spherical Sliced-Wasserstein discrepancy, and as an extension of the well-known Euclidean
194
      models (Soheil Kolouri Phillip E. Pope, 2020). Hyperspherical aspects of data are studied in Löwe
      et al. (2023), where high dimensional Rotating Features are introduced. Of particular interest for
195
      our project, the authors remarked:
196
197   ‘We represent Rotating Features in Cartesian coordinates rather than (hyper) spherical coordinates,
198   as the latter may contain singularities that hinder the model’s ability to learn good representations.
199   [...]. This representation can lead to singularities due to dependencies between the coordinates.
200
      For example, when a vector’s radial component (i.e. magnitude) is zero, the angular coordinates
      can take any value without changing the underlying vector. As our network applies ReLU activa-
201
      tion on the magnitudes, this singularity may occur regularly, hindering the network from training
202
      effectively’.
203
204   As we reviewed in Section 1.2, in high dimensions, the random samples of an independent multi-
205   variate Gaussian distribution fall in the equator of a hypersphere, and thus none of them is near the
206
      singularities of the hyperspherical coordinates (the poles and the center of the hypersphere).
207   While the problem of formulating latent spaces given by non-Euclidean Riemannian manifolds, and
208   hyperspheres in particular has been studied, explicit use of hyperspherical coordinates is avoided.
209   At first look, the conversion from Cartesian to hyperspherical coordinates seems to require compu-
210   tationally expensive recurrent trigonometric formulas (see Appendix A.1). Instead, formulations of
211   Riemannian geometry that rely on Cartesian coordinates are used. Riemannian geometry is not just
212
      about a curved metric, but also being able to express it in a convenient coordinate system adapted
      to it (of central importance in physics).
213
214   The possible use of hyperspherical coordinates has thus been discarded by all the works that we
215   reviewed. Notwithstanding the previous arguments, we believe that the use of hyperspherical coor-
      dinates is feasible and can be beneficial, as our results show. In Appendix A.2 we provide a vec-


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      torized implementation for transforming between hyperspherical and Cartesian coordinates, which
217   adds only a small computational overhead for training a VAE.
218
219   Even if one could assemble a sufficiently large training dataset to densely populate the latent hy-
220
      persphere (an impossible task in practice), the data in high dimensional latent spaces tend to form
      manifolds with complicated topologies that include holes and cracks. If a random sample falls into
221
      one of these holes, it will be decoded into something meaningless. This also affects interpolations,
222
      since locations sampled on a trajectory between two valid latent locations are likely to fall between
223   clusters or classes (in the holes and cracks). This problem has been noted for VAE and called the
224   ‘prior hole problem’ (Tomczak; Lin & Clark, 2020; Cinelli et al., 2021; Asperti et al., 2021; Singh
225   & Ogunfunmi, 2022; Hao & Shafto, 2023; Aneja et al., 2021). It highlights that the approximate
226   posterior of the data often fails to perfectly match the prior: in the case of a Gaussian distribution in
227   high dimensions the uniform distribution on the hypersphere has no hole.
228
      Common approaches for tackling this problem include a learnable prior (rather than static, as in
229   the standard VAE), or using a mixture of Gaussians as a prior (rather than a single one, as in the
230   standard VAE); see Tomczak for discussion of both cases. A different approach is to dispose of
231   the continuum completely and work with discrete latent representations, as in Aaron van den Oord
232   (2018) where the latent vectors are quantized (VQ-VAE). These discrete representations are useful
233   for tasks dealing with a discrete sequence of symbols, otherwise a joint distribution on the dictionary
234   needs to be estimated after the VAE is trained (Salimans et al., 2017).
235   There are applications that necessitate and use VAEs with a latent continuum. For example, genera-
236   tive models for drug discovery often deal with chemical properties that span a continuous spectrum
237   (e.g. measure of synthesizability by living entities Ochiai et al. (2023)).
238
      Despite the fact that some of the models in the mentioned references also work on the hypersphere
239
      in latent space, the similitude ends there. We do not really consider them suitable for comparison
240
      because both the goals and methods of these works are very different from ours. In particular, those
241   approaches are not concerned with the sparsity issue of HD spaces as well as volume compression as
242   a possible solution to it. Davidson et al. (2018), for example (and related elaborations), build a VAE
243   with a KLD term between a uniform distribution on the hypersphere and a von Mises-Fisher ap-
244   proximate posterior. Thus, by construction, there cannot be any compression of the type we discuss
245   in our work, it is still highly similar to the standard VAE in the sense in which the posterior distri-
246   bution ends approaching a uniform distribution on the hypersphere, where, we claim, the sparsity
247   issue arises. The references using flexible priors are focused on the so-called ‘prior hole problem’
248   (mismatch between prior and the posterior). This can cause problems for the generation but is not
249
      related to our hypothesis: even with perfect match, the sparsity problem is present, since it belongs
      to the priors that are often used.
250

252   coordinates.

315   hyperspherical coordinates is the possibility to set a prior for the φk that forces the latent samples
316   away from the equator, thereby escaping these highly entropic regions. This can be done for each

370

371   decoded/generated data (Fig.1a), left panel).
372
373   The latent hypersphere can be visualized in 3D as shown in Fig.1b), left. This was done by averaging
374
      the 128 latent dimensions into three (first 42, second 42, and the remaining 44), and normalizing each
      of the resulting 3D vectors to the sphere. Each latent vector could thus be plotted as a point in 3D,
375
      and shows a uniform-like distribution on the 2D sphere as expected.
376
377   This visualisation allows us to directly see the entropy. A k-NN classifier for the 10 classes of
      MNIST from the latent had an accuracy of 0.95, and 10 clusters can readily be seen when projecting

      the 128 latent dimensions into 2 (Fig.1c), left) using t-SNE. However, no particular clustering can
379   be observed on the 3D visualisation (Fig.1b), left). Such a direct visualisation of the latent space
380   cannot display clusters, because they are buried into the ‘disorder’ of the entropy of the hypersphere
381   equators, where most of the samples are located. There are many different possible microstates
382   (points in latent space) that can realize the same macrostate (the clustering).

385   4.4   I MPROVED GENERATION WHEN THE LATENT MANIFOLD IS COMPRESSED
386
387   Next, we train the same VAE using the same dataset but now with the KLD-type loss in hyper-
388
      spherical coordinates (6). The prior was set to compress the (hyper-)volume in latent space by using
      aµ,k = 1, ∀k, which pushes all the φk towards 0. They could not be exactly 0 because the re-
389
      construction would all be the same. The reconstruction term balances the KLD term to spread the
390
      latent samples away from the angles 0. Recall from section 1.3 that, in the replica symmetry break-
391   ing phase, two replicas in that case lie in a very thin band or ring centered at a deep minimum of
392   the energy function, center which is not in the initial high volume equatorial region; this ring is a
393   (n − 2)-hypersphere, submanifold of the initial (n − 1)-hypersphere. This situation corresponds
394   to sending the angle φ1 towards 0, the rest free. Thus, by sending all the angles towards 0, and
395   given the geometrical interpretation of the hyperspherical coordinates, we aim to induce a similar
396   replica symmetry breaking transition also in the mentioned (n − 2)-hypersphere, as well as in all the
397   remaining sub (n − k)-hyperspheres, ∀k. We call this process ‘Nested Replica Symmetry Breaking’
398   (NRSB), and it is only in this regime where we get the results described below.

449   4.5   T RADE OFF BETWEEN RECONSTRUCTION AND GENERATION
450
451
452
      The reconstruction quality of a VAE improves as the number of latent dimensions increases, as
      measured by the MSE between x and x̂. However, the quality of data generation (from decoding
453
      random sampling of the latent) decreases as the number of latent dimensions increases. We have
454
      argued in this paper that the later is due to the increased sparsity of the latent, as demonstrated
455   qualitatively in the previous experiment when that sparsity is reduced by compressing the latent
456   using our proposed method.
457
458
      The quality of randomly generated data can be measured using the Frechet Inception Distance (FID)
      (Heusel et al., 2017). FID compares the distribution of features between the images of the train-
459
      ing/testing dataset and an equivalent number of randomly generated images. We used in this experi-
460
      ment CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2009), a more challenging dataset, and an FID computed using 10,000
461   samples (we compare the random decoded samples with the reconstructed testing set). We call this
462   way of measuring the generation as ‘self-FID’.
463
464
      In a VAE, the quality of the reconstruction, still measured by the MSE, also varies with the gain β
      of the loss: the more weight for the KLD term, the more the latent matches the prior and the worse
465
      the reconstruction (Cf. βVAE Higgins et al. (2017)). We show in Appendix A.5 this behaviour by
466
      comparing the results for several values of β.
467
468   We can now explore quantitatively the quality of the reconstruction (using MSE) and the quality of
469   the generation (using self-FID) when the number of latent dimensions increases and the β varies.
470
      We compared the standard VAE with our proposed compressed VAE.
471   These two metrics (MSE and self-FID), should give us a good idea regarding how good our mod-
472   els are for the general generative task: the fist measures how ‘crisp/sharp’ (i.e., not ‘blurry’) the
473   reconstructed images are, while the second how close the random decoded images resemble images
474   from the (reconstructed) training dataset. A good VAE-based generative model should minimize
475   both of these metrics simultaneously: that is, to be able to generate random samples which are in-
476
      distribution wrt the reconstructed dataset (low self-FID), and such that the latter actually resembles
      the original dataset (low MSE). See appendices A.6 and A.14 for gaining intuition about the relation
477
      between the qualitative look of the decoded images and the corresponding MSE and self-FID values
478
      for those models.
479
480   Fig.2 sumarizes the results with more details provided in Appendix A.5. As expected, the MSE
481   decreases as one increases the latent space dimension, while the exact opposite is true for the self-
482
      FID. To obtain a good generative model in the way we defined it can be a very difficult task, involving
      a very delicate balance between these two opposing trends we just described, and often relying on
483
      off-equilibrium configurations.
484
485   The results show that the compression VAE version improves on absolute terms over the standard
      VAE over any combination of β and dimension of the latent.

      5   C ONCLUSION
521
522
      We propose to convert the latent variables of a VAE to hyperspherical coordinates. This allows to
      move the latent vectors on a small island of the hypersphere, reducing sparsity. We showed that
523
      this improves the generation quality of a VAE. The following points will require further attention in
524
      regards to the present work:
525
526          • the improvement in generation was only evaluated for the purposes of hypothesis testing,
527            and not as absolute performance. Furthermore, the FID metric that we used may also have
528            limitations sometimes (Stein et al., 2023).
529          • we did not evaluate the method for high resolution and larger datasets such as Imagenet.
530
             • the extra computing time is about 32 per cent more per epoch for 200 latent dimensions. In
531
               (much) higher dimensions, the added computation increases and might become prohibitive.
532
533
             • future research can focus in optimizing this method (or other method that takes into account
               the hypothesis about sparsity) for obtaining state-of-the-art results in generation and other
534
               tasks, in VAEs and other models.
535
536          • the use of latent representations in hyperspherical coordinates can also be further explored
537
               in several other applications (perhaps unrelated to compression and generation), by the use
               of the provided script for the conversion and inspired by its proof of concept of practical
538
               feasibility in the present paper.
