                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Language Models (LMs) are increasingly challenging the dominance of domain-
011            specific models, including Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Graph Transform-
012            ers (GTs), in graph learning tasks. Following this trend, we propose a novel ap-
013            proach that empowers off-the-shelf LMs to achieve performance comparable to
014
               state-of-the-art GNNs on node classification tasks, without requiring any archi-
               tectural modification. By preserving the LM’s original architecture, our approach
015
               retains a key benefit of LM instruction tuning: the ability to jointly train on diverse
016
               datasets, fostering greater flexibility and efficiency. To achieve this, we introduce
017            two key augmentation strategies: (1) Enriching LMs’ input using topological and
018            semantic retrieval methods, which provide richer contextual information, and (2)
019            guiding the LMs’ classification process through a lightweight GNN classifier that
020            effectively prunes class candidates. Our experiments on real-world datasets show
021            that backbone Flan-T5 models equipped with these augmentation strategies out-
022            perform state-of-the-art text-output node classifiers and are comparable to top-
023            performing vector-output node classifiers. By bridging the gap between special-
024            ized task-specific node classifiers and general LMs, this work paves the way for
025
               more versatile and widely applicable graph learning models. We will open-source
               the code upon publication.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   There is a growing trend of utilizing Language Models (LMs) for machine learning tasks across di-
033
      verse domains. This approach has shown tremendous promise in areas such as computer vision (De-
      sai & Johnson, 2021), audio processing (Mittal et al., 2021), and multimodal learning (Alayrac et al.,
034
      2022). In the domain of graph learning, recent efforts have begun to explore the capabilities of LMs
035
      in understanding and processing graph structures. Wang et al. (2023) showed that LMs can de-
036   tect node connectivity and identify cycles within graphs, while Fatemi et al. (2024) explored LMs’
037   ability to evaluate graph scale and identify connected components. Furthermore, InstructGLM (Ye
038   et al., 2023) achieved state-of-the-art performance in text-output node classifiers for Text-Attributed
039   Graphs (TAG) (Zhang et al., 2024a), where each node is associated with textual information.
040
      However, InstructGLM suffers from a fundamental limitation that compromises the generality of the
041
      backbone LM. Specifically, InstructGLM expands the LM’s vocabulary by creating unique tokens
042   for each node, which incorporates topology-aware node embeddings as token embeddings. While
043   this approach is effective, it comes at the cost of reducing the LM’s versatility, making it incompat-
044   ible with two important use cases: (1) multi-task learning on diverse datasets, a common strategy
045   for training Foundational Models (Wei et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024), and (2) certain personal-
046   ized LM fine-tuning services (Li et al., 2024b) that restrict modifications to the backbone model
047   architecture. This limitation raises a crucial question: How can off-the-shelf, text-to-text instruction-
048   tuned LMs (Raffel et al., 2020) achieve competitive performance in node classification tasks without
049   requiring architectural modifications?
050   In stark contrast to (Huang et al., 2023), which suggests that LMs may only interpret graph struc-
051   tures in prompts as contextual paragraphs, our work presents a more optimistic outlook. We aim to
052   overcome this inherent limitation by augmenting the input to LMs while preserving their original
053   architecture. Our proposed model, named AUG GLM (Augmented Graph Language Model), lever-
      ages two key augmentation strategies to enhance the LM’s ability to process graph data as follows:


                                                         1

             • Relevant Node Retrieval: In contrast to InstructGLM, which relies on multi-hop ego
055            networks akin to message-passing GNNs for structure-aware contextualization, AUG -
056            GLM draws inspiration from Graph Transformers (GTs) (Min et al., 2022) and Retrieval-
057            Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020). This enables the
058            LM to access long-range structural and semantic information about the target node. We
059            propose two complementary approaches to achieve this: (1) topological retrieval, and (2)
060            prototypical semantic retrieval.
061
062
             • Candidate Label Pruning: To improve LMs’ understanding of graph data while main-
063
               taining their text-to-text architecture, we convey the guidance from a specialist model, a
064            pretrained lightweight GNN, to the input of LMs via narrowing down the potential labels.
065            This allows LMs to focus on discerning between closely related candidates, ultimately en-
066            hancing the performance.

      We conduct an extensive evaluation of our approach on four real-world TAGs, showing the effec-
069
      tiveness of our proposed instruction fine-tuning strategy. The results indicate that backbone LMs
070   augmented with AUG GLM significantly outperform InstructGLM, while also matching or surpass-
071   ing the performance of state-of-the-art vector-output classifiers. These findings represent a crucial
072   step towards bridging the gap between specialized task-specific node classifiers and more general,
073   fine-tuned LMs, highlighting the potential for unified models that can excel across multiple tasks.

078
079
080   LMs for Graphs. Recent studies have explored the ability of LMs to understand graph topology by
081   investigating problems such as graph substructure recall (Wang et al., 2024), circle and connectivity
082
      detection (Wang et al., 2023; Perozzi et al., 2024), node/edge counting (Perozzi et al., 2024), spatial-
      temporal problems on dynamic graphs (Zhang et al., 2024b). Notably, Fatemi et al. (2024) found
083
      that the presentation format of graph data in text significantly impacts performance across various
084
      tasks, highlighting the importance of effective graph-to-text encoding. Building on these findings,
085   several studies have explored classification tasks on TAGs, including node classification (Ye et al.,
086   2023; Zhao et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024a; Qin et al., 2023), link prediction (Brannon et al., 2023;
087   Tan et al., 2024), transfer learning (Tang et al., 2024), and graph reasoning (Jin et al., 2024); more-
088   over, Chen et al. (2023) present a systematic summary on the performance of off-the-shelf solu-
089   tions in two categories: LLMs-as-Enhancer and LLMs-as-Predictor. Furthermore, Zhang (2023)
090   proposed Graph-ToolFormer, a framework that enhances LMs with graph reasoning API tools, en-
091   abling more complex graph reasoning tasks. GIANT (Chien et al., 2022) and GLEM (Zhao et al.,
092   2023a) utilize the interaction between graph data and LMs to generate better graph representations.
093
      TAPE (He et al., 2024) leverages the explanations generated by an LLM to enrich the textual fea-
      tures, which are then used to fine-tune two LMs. The features from these LMs are subsequently
094
      passed through an ensemble of GNNs for final prediction.
095
096   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). (Lewis et al., 2020; Karpukhin et al., 2020) enhance
097   LMs by granting them access to external knowledge (Hashimoto et al., 2018) during text generation.
098   This technique involves retrieving relevant documents from a large corpus and conditioning the LM
099
      on both the input query and the retrieved information. Building on this, REALM Guu et al. (2020)
      pretrains the retriever and generator end-to-end. Subsequently, RETRO Borgeaud et al. (2022) ef-
100
      ficiently scales retrieval-enhanced autoregressive to large datasets. A crucial component of RAG’s
101
      success is the loss function proposed by Shi et al. (2023) which enables the retriever to be trained
102   with the LM viewed as a black box. HyDE Yu et al. (2023) uses hypothetical document generation
103   to improve retrieval in RAG systems. Furthermore, RAG has extended to multimodal settings Ya-
104   sunaga et al. (2023). Recently, GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) has garnered significant attention
105   which involves constructing a Knowledge Graph (KG) and then generating responses based on the
106   summaries of communities derived from KG. These advancements have significantly improved the
107   factual accuracy and contextual relevance of generated text, solidifying RAG as a promising tech-
      nique for various applications, including question answering and open-domain dialogue systems.

158   We explore the application of LMs to node classification tasks in graph learning, leveraging the in-
159   struction tuning paradigm to reformulate node classification as a text-to-text task Raffel et al. (2020).
160   Our method employs a carefully designed prompt template and a set of augmentation techniques to
161   transform graph data and ground truth labels into text pairs. This enables LMs to comprehend and
      be fine-tuned for the task without requiring modifications to their underlying architecture.

      As shown in Figure 1, our approach differs from InstructGLM (Ye et al., 2023), the current state-
193
      of-the-art LM for TAGs, in its underlying design. While both methods utilize prompt templates
194   to transform input graphs into text, InstructGLM relies on explicit encoding of node embeddings
195   into the LM’s token embeddings as a form of soft prompting (Lester et al., 2021). In contrast, our
196   approach provides a more general framework, leveraging gradient descent through the LM without
197   modifying its underlying text-to-text architecture. This design choice enables our model to retain
198   the versatility of the original LM while adapting it to graph-based tasks. The following sections
199   provide a detailed description of the specific techniques developed in the paper to achieve this goal.

201   4.1   R ETRIEVAL -BASED AGGREGATION
202
203   General LMs are not designed to directly process graph-structured data. To overcome this limita-
204   tion, a common approach is to employ prompt templates that transform graph data and associated
205   tasks into a textual format that LMs can understand. For instance, consider the Cora (Sen et al.,
206
      2008) literature citation graph. A typical template (Huang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023) for node
      classification, as shown in Figure 3a consists of three main components: (1) a short description of
207
      the classification task, (2) the target node’s textual features, such as its title and abstract, and (3)
208
      textual features from relevant neighboring nodes within the graph.
209
210   The success of the message-passing graph neural networks (GNNs) highlights the importance of the
211   aggregation operation, whose typical examples are the sum and max pooling operations of interme-
212
      diate node embeddings. A similar spirit is followed for the LM-based classifiers whose key design
      is at the selection of relevant nodes.
213
214   While existing works (Huang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023) select 1-hop or multi-hop neighbors as
215   relevant nodes, we posit that this approach is suboptimal for two key reasons. Firstly, not all im-
      mediate or extended neighbors provide useful information for classifying the target node, which can

      Topological Retrieval. We leverage PPR (Page, 1999; Jeh & Widom, 2003) to perform topological
245
      retrieval, which has shown great effectiveness in conjunction with GNNs (Klicpera et al., 2019).
246   The success of PPR suggests that the neighbors it identifies may provide more informative context
247   than generic 1-hop or multi-hop neighbors. Specifically, for a target node vi , we select its top-K
248   neighbors PPR neigh(vi ) based on their PPR scores, computed using Eqs. (1) and (2). The details
249   of the PPR algorithm are introduced in Section 3. We then concatenate the text features from the
250   PPR neighbors to form the PPR-retrieved text tPPR retri = ⊕j;vj ∈PPR neigh(vi ) tj , where ⊕ denotes text
251   concatenation.
252   It is worth noting that the classic PPR algorithm is computationally expensive for large graphs due
253   to the matrix multiplication (Eq. (1)). However, efficient approximate solutions such as Approx-
254   imatePR (Andersen et al., 2006), can be applied to mitigate this issue. Nevertheless, PPR is a
255   topology-based heuristic that does not inherently leverage textual features nor adapt to feedback
256   from downstream node classification tasks. To address these limitations and enhance our frame-
257   work’s semantic awareness, we propose a complementary semantic retrieval strategy, which is dis-
258   cussed in the following section.
259   Prototypical Semantic Retrieval. Our semantic retrieval module draws inspiration from two pop-
260   ular techniques: (1) RAG (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020), which leverage external corpora to
261   answer knowledge-intensive questions, and (2) Graph Transformers (Min et al., 2022) which aggre-
262   gate messages from distant nodes via inner product-based attention weights. In the context of node
263   classification, we treat the textual features of all nodes except the target node as a surrogate “external
264   corpus.” However, unlike typical question-answering tasks, retrieving textual features from a single
265   node is often insufficient for accurate node classification. To address this limitation, we enhance the
266
      semantic retrieval process by focusing on prototypes, which capture the essence of each class (Snell
      et al., 2017).
267

293
294   Recent studies (Huang et al., 2023; Fatemi et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) highlighted the limited
295   understanding of graph topology in current mainstream LMs. While InstructGLM (Ye et al., 2023)
296   addresses this limitation by incorporating node embeddings from a pretrained GNN into the LM’s
297   token embeddings, this approach necessitates modifications to the LM’s architecture. We propose
298   an alternative method that conveys guidance from a pretrained GNN to inform LMs within the
299   input text space, thereby preserving the LM’s original architecture while presenting a pruned set of
300   classification candidates.
301   We repurpose the pretrained GNNψ from the prototypical semantic retrieval module to inform AUG -
302   GLM . For each node vi , we identify and store the top-I predicted labels:
303

306   sociate numerical labels with their corresponding text representations, we can leverage this map-
307   ping to present the pruned label candidates for node vi as a concatenated text: tcandidates =
308   ⊕i∈Li IndexToLabel(i). The integration of this pruned candidate set into the input template is
309
      detailed in Section 4.3, where we elaborate on our overall template design.
310   By adopting this approach, we inject valuable structure-aware inductive bias from the GNN’s pre-
311   diction into the LM’s input, thereby enhancing its ability to perform node classification tasks without
312   altering its fundamental architecture. By focusing the LM’s attention on a smaller, more relevant set
313   of potential labels, we can improve its classification accuracy.

      4.5      M ODEL COMPLEXITY
396
397   Our model consists of three parameterized modules: (1) a GNN ψ for generating prototypes and
398   pruned label candidates, (2) the semantic retriever ϕ, and (3) the backbone LM θ. Notably, ψ
399   and ϕ are lightweight, with a number of parameters that is only 1/30 to 1/3 of the number of
400   parameters of LM θ. Compared to the state-of-the-art InstructGLM, our model has an additional
401   module ϕ, resulting in slightly more parameters which is relatively minor. During training, the
402   GNN ψ can be trained independently, and the PPR scores can be precomputed. The training of θ
403   relies on the retrieved text from ϕ, while the training of ϕ requires p̃LM (·|ttarget , y target ), which is
404   obtained through forward inference of θ. Importantly, their computational graphs (used for gradient
405
      computation) are independent. This is because the LM θ concatenates the retrieved text from ϕ
      into its input, which is not differentiable with respect to ϕ. Furthermore, when training ϕ, the loss
406
      in Eq. (14) involves p̃LM (·|ttarget , y target ) wrapped with the StopGradient operator, ensuring that
407
      the gradient computation does not update θ. As a result, the cost of back-propagation is similar to
408   updating the LM θ and the semantic encoder ϕ separately.

      5.4   M ULTI -TASK T RAINING
508   One of the key advantage of pure text-to-text instruction tuning is that a single model can be trained
509   on multiple tasks with the same input-output format. To verify this, we conducted an experiment
510   using a Flan-T5-small model, applying our proposed strategies to jointly train it on four diverse
511   datasets: Cora, Pubmed, ogbn-arxiv, and ogbn-products. The results, presented in Table 3 show
512   that the jointly trained model achieve performance comparable to models trained separately on each
513   individual dataset. We observe that on some datasets, such as Cora and ogbn-products, the jointly
514   trained model even outperforms its dataset-specific counterparts.
515   These findings suggest that our approach can effectively handle multiple graph datasets using a
516   single model, without incurring significant performance losses compared to models trained individ-
517   ually. This capability is crucial for efficient model deployment when dealing with diverse graph
518   data. In contrast, other approaches, such as InstructGLM, require the addition of a large token dic-
519   tionary to accommodate all nodes in the joint dataset, which hinders their ability to achieve similar
520   generality. Moreover, most vector-output models, including TAPE, are limited by their predefined
521   input-output dimensions, making them inflexible and unable to handle multiple datasets.
522
523   6     C ONCLUSION
524
525   We introduce a novel framework called AUG GLM for instruct-tuning Language Models (LMs) to
526   perform node classification tasks on Text-Attributed Graphs (TAGs) using pure text-to-text instruc-
527   tions. Our approach is built upon two key innovations: (1) topological and semantic retrieval of
528   relevant nodes, (2) using a lightweight GNN to guide the classification process of LMs. Extensive
529   experimental results demonstrated the effectiveness of our framework, which consistently outper-
530   formed the best existing text-output node classifiers, while achieving performance comparable to
531
      state-of-the-art vector-output node classifiers. These findings suggest a promising direction for har-
      nessing the power of Large Language Models (LLMs) in graph learning tasks.
532
533   Limitation and Future Work. One limitation of this work is the need for manual definition of
534   prompt templates in Table 4. A promising direction for future research is to develop methods for
535   automatically searching for optimal templates in a data-driven manner. Another limitation is the
536   requirement for pretraining a GNN ψ on each dataset, which stems from the inherent challenges
537
      of language models in understanding graph data. Addressing this limitation by developing more
      powerful language models capable of handling graph data is a challenging yet impactful area of
538
      future work.

976    FLOPs Here we compare the floating point operations (FLOPs) of our proposed AUG GLM and
977    our main baseline, InstructGLM Ye et al. (2023), a text-output node classifier.
978
       The computation of InstructGLM includes (1) computing node encodings, which is precomputed,
979
       and (2) training and inference of the downstream LM. In contrast, the computation of our AugGLM
980
       includes (1) computing PPR neighbors for every node, which is also precomputed, (2) training and
981    inference of the semantic retriever, and (3) training and inference of the downstream LM. Hence,
982    the extra on-the-fly computation overhead comes from the semantic retriever, all-MiniLM-L6-v2, in
983    our experiments. We report the FLOPs of the retriever and different LM backbones in Table 6.
