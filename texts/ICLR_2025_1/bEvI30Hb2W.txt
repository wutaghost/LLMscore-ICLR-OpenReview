009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-form video reasoning is essential for various applications such as video re-
012            trieval, summarizing, and question answering. However, existing methods often
013            require significant computational resources and are limited by GPU memory con-
014            straints. To address this challenge, we present Long-Video Memory Network,
015            LVM-Net, a novel video reasoning method that employs a fixed-size memory rep-
016
               resentation to store discriminative patches sampled from the input video. By
               leveraging a neural sampler that identifies discriminative memory tokens, LVM-
017
               Net achieves improved efficiency. Furthermore, LVM-Net only requires a sin-
018
               gle pass over the video, further enhancing overall efficiency. Our results on the
019            Rest-ADL dataset demonstrate an 18x – 75x improvement in inference times for
020            long-form video retrieval and answering questions, with a competitive predictive
021            performance.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Long-form video understanding is important for various applications such as video retrieval, summa-
027
      rizing, and question answering. For example, for automated checkout in retail applications, a video
      understanding system needs to understand the temporal order of important actions such as grabbing
028
      an object, and limit the attention to actions such as browsing items, and interacting with other peo-
029
      ple, to efficiently process long duration shopping videos (Wankhede et al., 2018; Strafforello et al.,
030   2023).
031
032   Modern methods for long-form video understanding such as transformer based models can be ineffi-
033
      cient and require significant computational resources (Wu & Krahenbuhl, 2021). These methods of-
      ten require building an intermediate representation for the entire video in memory and consume large
034
      amounts of GPU memory limiting the maximum length of videos that can be processed, especially
035
      for reasoning tasks that require joint spatio-temporal analysis of different scene elements (Fournier
036   et al., 2023). These tasks necessitate reasoning approaches that repeatedly access and manipulate
037   different scene elements, as dictated by complex intermediate computational or scene graphs (Fei
038   et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2020). Furthermore, when these reasoning models are scaled, such as us-
039   ing Vision-Language Models (VLMs), they require even more compute and GPU memory (Bordes
040   et al., 2024). Hence, given a limited compute budget, VLMs only operate on a few images or short
041   snippets/summaries and often struggle to efficiently perform dense understanding of videos, limiting
042   video sizes to a few minutes (Weng et al., 2024).
043   Even though several efficient approaches exist, these methods often sample a fixed number of
044   frames (Ma et al., 2018) affecting model performance for certain actions, or perform a clip based
045   aggregation losing the order of short term actions (Fan et al., 2021a). Existing token sampling and
046   pruning methods condense background tokens in the spatial domain, and do not store or re-use to-
047   kens in memory that can affect efficiency for dense spatio-temporal tasks (Bolya et al., 2022; Fayyaz
048   et al., 2022).
049   Figure 1a illustrates the computational challenge associated with video reasoning models. As shown
050   in Figure 1(a), TubeDETR (Yang et al., 2022) repeatedly processes a large number of frames while
051   handling approximately 6000 activity queries on long videos during inference. The average duration
052   of these long videos spans is 27 minutes. A histogram of the frame processing counts is presented
053   in Figure 1a. This observation demonstrates an opportunity for memory-based approaches, which
      load the frames of long videos into GPU memory, to improve computational efficiency.

071   To address the above issues, we present LVM-Net which uses a fixed-size memory representation
072   to identify and store discriminative patches sampled from the input video. The memory patches are
073   identified using a neural sampler, that improves efficiency while maintaining the discriminability of
074   memory representation. Additionally, LVM-Net only requires a single pass of the video over the
075   memory, further improving the overall efficiency.
076   In our results over the Rest-ADL dataset, we demonstrate an 18X speedup during inference with 1
077   FPS and and 75x improvement with 5 FPS, for long form video retrieval for answering questions
078   over long (>30 min) videos to answer activity, object, and temporal queries and achieve competitive
079   performance.

081   2                 R ELATED W ORK
082
083
      Our work is inspired by related work in video understanding methods including long-form video
084   understanding, reasoning and efficient video transformer architectures.
085
086   Video understanding Deep learning based video understanding methods have evolved from us-
087   ing 3D convolution based methods (Ji et al., 2012) to 2D-CNNs (Donahue et al., 2015; Simonyan
088   & Zisserman, 2014; Feichtenhofer et al., 2019), with additional blocks such as object/ROI fea-
089   tures (Gkioxari et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2018), convolution-transformer approaches (Girdhar et al.,
090   2019) and transformer-only approaches (Arnab et al., 2021; Bertasius et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2021b;
091   Liu et al., 2022). Transformer based approaches often tokenize the video by chopping the input into
092   a series of 2D spatial frames or into 3D spatio-temporal cubes on a regular grid. This approach
093   can provide high accuracy but requires significant amounts of compute and memory due to large
094
      number of tokens and their parallel processing in transformer architecture. In contrast, our method
      uses transformer based tokens and samples the tokens to significantly reduce processing costs.
095
096
      Long-form video reasoning Various long-form video understanding approaches have been stud-
097
      ied (Song et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Wu & Krahenbuhl, 2021; Wu et al.,
098
      2022). Noteworthy among these approaches, MeMViT caches representations of previous clips to
099   extend temporal context without increasing per-step compute. However, these approaches are often
100   limited to less than few minutes, largely due to lack of long form video datasets (> 30 mins). Ad-
101   ditionally, existing reasoning based datasets largely focus around QA (Yi et al., 2019) or temporal
102   action retrieval (Chao et al., 2018; Hahn et al., 2019; Yuan et al., 2016). In contrast, our paper is
103   focused on videos with duration of 30+ mins with a focus on tasks that require a joint analysis of
104   activities, objects and time, which requires complex reasoning.
105
106   Long-context VLMs Most VLMs can usually process only a few minutes of videos due to limited
107   context length. Video processing requires a large number of tokens to be processed; for example,
      deploying a 7B Llama model that can process 10 million tokens requires 8 A100 GPUs (640GB

      memory), even with advanced serving optimizations (Hooper et al., 2024). Even larger proprietary
109   models such as Gemini 1.5 Pro can process 10 million tokens which roughly translates to approx-
110   imately 10 hours of video duration(Reid et al., 2024). Gemini 1.5 model architecture and number
111   of parameters are unknown. However, Gemini 1.5 model is most likely compute intensive – based

116   Efficient transformer architectures Efficient transformer architectures have focused on reduc-
117   ing the cost of quadratic attention costs with respect to sequence lengths (Tay et al., 2020), prun-
118   ing (Meng et al., 2022; Rao et al., 2021; Voita et al., 2019) and reduction of vision tokens as an input
119   to decoders downstream. Previous work has analyzed sparse attention patterns to reduce complexity
120   from attention to linear (Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020), and approximated attention using
121   kernel methods achieving linear time and memory complexity (Choromanski et al., 2020; Schlag
122   et al., 2021). Many hierarchical approaches use a hierarchical token structure to process inputs at
123
      multiple resolutions, reducing overall computation (Jaegle et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021a; Feng et al.,
      2023).
124
125
126   Token efficiency methods In order to reduce token costs, approaches such as token merg-
127   ing (Bolya et al., 2022), adaptive token sampling in classification domain (Fayyaz et al., 2022),
128   token turing machines (Ryoo et al., 2023), spatio-temporal token selection (Wang et al., 2022) have
129
      been explored. BLIP-3 (Xue et al., 2024) uses Perceiver based token sampler to project input image
      to a fixed number of tokens. However, token pruning methods often can deduplicate tokens whereas,
130
      LVM-Net identifies discriminative tokens using a neural sampler. Crucially, LVM-Net uses a fixed
131
      memory, re-using token representations across queries significantly reducing inference time.

134   3   P RELIMINARIES
135
136
      We use the Relational Space-Time Query (ReST) dataset (Yang et al., 2023) to evaluate long-form
137   video reasoning. ReST consists of three kinds of relational space-time queries: activity query, object
138   query, and time-query. Each query asks questions on a single property (e.g. activity) by providing
139   the other two properties (e.g. object and time) as input.
140
      The templates of queries are as follows:
141
142
            1. Activity query - what activities did I perform with a particular object during a given time?
143
144         2. Object query - on which objects I perform with a particular activity during a given time?
145
146
            3. Time query - at what time did I perform a particular activity with a particular object?
147
      ReST consists of long videos with average duration of 27 minutes in length. The relational space-
148
      time queries over the videos are further categorized into three types based on query time duration
149
      – short (around 5 minutes), medium (around 15 minutes), and long (around 30 minutes). Note that
150   the short queries in our paper are longer than those typically employed by existing models, which
151   usually last about 3 minutes (Yang et al., 2023).
152
153
      There are four-time representations in our setup: long video time (vs , ve ), ReST query time (qs , qe ),
      time-property time (ts , te ) and clip time (cs , ce ). The long video time represents the complete
154
      duration of the input video clip (up to an hour long). The ReST query time (qs , qe ) represents the
155
      duration of the relational space time query. The time duration of a ReST query can be short (around
156   5 mins), medium (around 15 minutes), and long (around 30 minutes). The ReST query time has
157   the following constraint: vs ≤ qs ≤ qe ≤ ve . The time-property time (ts , te ) of a query is the
158   duration when an activity occurs on an object within query time (qs , qe ). The time-property time
159   has following constraint: vs ≤ qs ≤ ts ≤ te ≤ qe ≤ ve . The clip time represents the sampled
160   clip from a long video such that the sampled frames from the clip can be loaded into the available
161   GPU memory. It has following constraint: vs ≤ cs ≤ ce ≤ ve . The ReST dataset contains multiple
      queries per video where qji represents the j th query in the ReST dataset that belongs to video i.

      4     M ETHODOLOGY
189
190
      We now describe the model architecture for efficient video reasoning. We refer to video reasoning
191   as a model’s ability to understand three properties: what activity is being performed on what object
192   over what time. We test a model’s video understanding ability by asking queries where we provide
193   input two properties of the video and then ask the model to predict the third property.
194
      In order to build an efficient architecture, we draw inspiration from human memory that uses multi-
195
      ple memory representations and uses attention as a gatekeeper for the memory, guided by the high
196
      level goals (Hazy et al., 2006; Watzl, 2017). LVM-Net performs reasoning over long videos by us-
197   ing attention to store specific information within a fixed memory. It achieves this through a trained
198   neural sampler that extracts discriminative visual patches from the video and stores them in mem-
199   ory as shown in Figure 2. During inference, LVM-Net uses this pre-populated memory to respond
200   to queries without needing to revisit the original video, significantly reducing inference time. This
201   architecture is well-suited for answering multiple queries from a single video, enabling fast and
202   effective video understanding.
203
204
205   LVM-N ET I NPUT E NCODING
206
207
      The three properties in ReST—activity, object, and time—are represented in three different repre-
208
      sentation spaces: activity is represented as one of C classes, the object is represented as an image,
209
      and time is represented by start and end times.
210
211   The activity input is represented as a one-dimensional vector aj ∈ R1×C . This vector is then passed
212   through a feed-forward layer to obtain a d-dimensional representation ahj ∈ R1×d . The object input
213   is an instance image oj ∈ Roh ×ow . The image is passed through a frozen image backbone (pre-
214   trained Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021b)). The time input (tj,s , tj,e ) from the ReST query is
215   passed into a video-level temporal positional encoding layer (Vaswani et al., 2017) that outputs a
      latent representation thj ∈ R(tj,e −tj,s +1)×d .

      N EURAL S AMPLER
217
218   The objective of the differentiable neural sampler is to populate memory with the representative
219   visual tokens from the whole long video vi . The memory stores a fixed number of mi ∈ Rm×d
220   visual tokens per video. The input to the neural sampler is mi memory tokens and k visual tokens
221   from the sampled clip cij ∈ RT ×H×W ×d of the query qji where T, H, and W are duration of clip,
222   height and weight of the image frames, respectively. The neural sampler outputs m discriminative
223   visual tokens. The sampled clip cij ∈ RT ×H×W ×d is passed through a pre-trained, frozen Swin
                                                                                       ′ ′
224   transformer based image backbone (Liu et al., 2021b) that results in k ∈ RT h w ×d visual tokens
225   where h′ and w′ are computed based on patch size. We use the same image backbone for object
226   image and clip frames. The sampled mi tokens are then passed through the 2D spatial positional
227   encoding layer and video-level temporal positional encoding layer. Our proposed framework is
228
      independent of the choice of neural sampler (Xie et al., 2019; Pervez et al., 2022).
229   The neural sampler outputs scores for k (clip) tokens and m (memory) tokens. To understand which
230   tokens out of m + k tokens are important, we first pass the m + k tokens through a transformer
231   encoder followed by a single MLP layer that outputs the scores. The neural sampler (Xie et al., 2019)
232   samples subsets with Gumbel-Top k Relaxations that adds Gumbel noise to the scores and utilizes
233   reparameterization trick (Kingma, 2013) so that the gradients can back-propagate to the transformer
234
      encoder and MLP layer. The sampler is trained based on ReST queries predictive performance
      where the loss is higher if the sampler samples non-discriminative tokens.

267   M EMORY R EAD /W RITE OPERATIONS
268
269   In LVM-Net the memory mi is allocated per long video vi . A long video vi can possibly have
      multiple associated ReST queries where each query could focus on a clip (for example, cij which

270
      corresponds to j th clip of video vi ). Since we train our model through sampled clips it becomes
271   important, how we form a batch through a data sampler. A naive data sampler can form a batch
272   with two or more clips belonging to the same video. In this case, the neural sampler would read m
273   tokens from video vi and k tokens from each clip cij (say cij1 and cij2 ). The neural sampler would
274   then output m tokens for cij1 and m tokens for cij2 to be written to the ith video memory slot thereby
275
      creating a race condition 1 .
276
277   To avoid this race condition on a single GPU, we design a data sampler such that a batch has ReST
278   queries with no two queries belonging to the same long video. In distributed training with multiple
279
      GPUs, our data sampler ensures that all the batches have ReST queries with no two queries belong-
      ing to the same video. This data-sampling constraint ensures there is no memory corruption. At the
280
      end of each iteration, the written memory tokens are synchronized across all devices. To summarize,
281
      in a distributed training setup with r devices and n number of videos, the maximum batch size on a
282   single GPU becomes nr in order to avoid race condition.

      I NFERENCE
285
286   The inference of LVM-Net enables faster processing of queries than existing long video understand-
287   ing models. In existing models such as TubeDETR (Yang et al., 2022), for answering q queries
288   from a single video, one has to pass the query clip’s frames, q number of times. The clip processing
289   – passing the clip frames through the image backbone and then passing the latent representations
290   through encoder-decoder modules – is compute intensive and results in a significant delay in gener-
291   ating the query’s response. Moreover, q queries are processed independently so if multiple queries
292   share a small region of clip, there is no potential to offset the clip processing load. In contrast, in our
293
      proposed model, as shown in the Figure 3, we first populate video vi specific memory mi through
      our trained neural sampler. All the responses to the queries that belong to video vi are generated
294
      using sampled memory mi .
295
296   The memory mi ∈ R(m,d) – m is the number of tokens and d represents latent dimension of image
297   backbone – for a particular video vi is populated as follows: we first initialize mi with video tokens
298   sampled randomly. We then extract clips from video vi through a sliding window with two clips
299   having zero overlap. Each clip is then passed through the image backbone that outputs k tokens.
300
      The neural sampler takes m memory tokens and k clip tokens and outputs m tokens that are written
      to memory. In the end, the populated mi is fed to the encoder for answering queries that belong to
301
      ith video.
302
303   An additional advantage of our proposed model is that it can be deployed on an edge device with
304   limited memory. The inference can be performed in a streaming fashion where we can store m
305   memory tokens and k tokens from the current query clip. The sampler here would take input m + k
306
      tokens and output m discriminative tokens. These m memory tokens are used to generate responses
      for multiple queries.

      O NLINE C ONTINUAL LEARNING LOSS
353
354   Given a ReST query qji , k visual tokens from the qji ’s clip, and mi memory tokens of video i, we
355   train the neural sampler based on the training loss. However, with this training setup, the neural
356   sampler is biased towards sampling qji ’s clip visual tokens instead of memory tokens – since the
357   training loss computed on qji ’s predictions is minimized by sampling visual tokens from the qji ’s
358   clip. As a result, the model cannot identify tokens that capture the global view of the long video.
359   This bias contrasts against our goal of training a neural sampler that would process the long video
360   once and populate discriminative tokens into memory.
361
      We propose an auxiliary loss to address this sampling bias. Specifically, we propose online contin-
362
      ual learning loss shown in Figure 4. Here, we store past p ReST queries in a heap of size p where
363   the oldest query is ejected when the heap is full. These ReST queries are passed through the shared
364   transformer encoder-decoder and the training loss is computed on both the current query’s predic-
365   tions and past p query’s predictions. This auxiliary loss addresses the sampling bias of the neural
366   sampler. We make a design choice of performing the continual learning in an online fashion – train-
367   ing based on recent past p queries – instead of randomly sampling p queries from all the previous
368   queries. This is due to the fact that the initial probability of past p queries’ relevant tokens being
369   in memory is high. With online continual learning, we reinforce the neural sampler to give those
370   relevant tokens high scores regardless of their relevance to the current query qji .

410   BASELINES
411
412
      We compare our proposed method with the ReST (Yang et al., 2023) that uses a multi-stage dif-
      ferentiable learning model and end-to-end TubeDETR method (Yang et al., 2022). We modify the
413
      last MLP layer of TubeDETR for activity prediction outputs. TubeDETR operates on clips that can
414
      be loaded into GPU memory. For clips with durations greater than 4 minutes (1 FPS), TubeDETR
415   requires sampling a fixed number of frames to meet GPU memory requirements. We follow the
416   clip-based training and inference recipe outlined in the PyTorchVideo library (Fan et al., 2021a) for
417   TubeDETR. Specifically, during training, given a long clip, we randomly sample a sub-clip whose
418   duration, with the selected FPS, results in a predefined fixed number of frames. During inference,
419   we follow these steps:
420
421         1. Divide the long clip into non-overlapping short clips.
422
            2. Pass each short clip along with the object image through the trained TubeDETR model,
423            which outputs the activity class logits.
424
425         3. Aggregate the logits across all clips and perform prediction.
426
427   In the case of the object query, during training, we apply object detection loss mentioned in the
428
      Equation 1 on the sampled clip. During inference, we divide the long clip of query qj into non-
      overlapping short clips, pass the activity hidden representation along with each clip and compute the
429
      vIoUj score per query.
430
431   In the case of the time query, where the task requires predicting the start and end times, we follow
      TubeDETR and sample a fixed number of frames (Yang et al., 2022).

      R ESULTS
433
434   We report our experimental results in Tables 1 and 2. We perform the inference running time com-
435   putation on the identical A100 instances. Batch size for both the methods is selected to maximize
436   the GPU memory utilization. As shown in Table 1, LVM-Net, outperforms the TubeDETR model in
437   terms of inference speed. In the case of activity query, LVM-Net achieves speedups of 18X, 11.2X,
438   and 11.6X over TubeDETR on short, medium, and long activity queries, respectively. When pro-
439   cessing the ReST-ADL dataset, which consists of approximately 6000 test activity queries across
440
      four long videos, LVM-Net passes each video through its neural sampler once to create four video-
      specific memories. All subsequent test queries are then processed using this memory, resulting in
441
      efficiency gains.
442
443   In contrast, TubeDETR treats each query independently and requires a separate inference process
444   for every query, as outlined in the baselines section. This approach leads to redundant processing of
445   frames when multiple queries refer to overlapping or identical long clips. While it might be possible
446
      to optimize this by processing all frame representations once and storing them on disk, this approach
      would still require additional steps:
447
448
            1. Dividing the long video clip into non-overlapping short clips.
449
450
            2. Loading these clips’ frame representations in memory.
451         3. Passing them along with the object image through the trained TubeDETR model to obtain
452            logits.
453         4. Aggregating these logits.
454
455   The latter steps are particularly time-consuming and result in slow inference times.
456
      In the case of object query, the ground truth is available at five FPS, hence the target fps is set to
457   five for all the models as the groundtruth for object query is available at five FPS. We observe 75x
458   improvement in inference speed as compared to TubeDETR since at five FPS, TubeDETR has to
459   process 5X more frames. From Table 2, we observe LVM-Net performs competitively as compared
460   to TubeDETR.
461
      In the case of the time query, due to the nature of predicting start and end times, we follow Tube-
462
      DETR and sample a fixed number of frames(Yang et al., 2022). We observe that the modified Tube-
463   DETR had a shorter running time for time queries compared to activity and object queries due to
464   frame sampling. However, for medium (15-minute) and long (30-minute) queries, the performance
465   of the modified TubeDETR deteriorates because it is not explicitly designed for long videos(Yang
466   et al., 2022). In contrast, LVM-Net stores a global view of each video in memory and passes this
467   along with object images through its trained encoder-decoder model, which outputs activity class
468   logits without requiring any additional aggregation. As shown in Table 2, our system performs
469   competitively compared to other methods.
470   We also perform additional experiments (reported in the appendix) where we demonstrate the impact
471   of online continual vs non-continual learning loss in Section A.3.2. We also measure the trained
472   neural sampler’s ability to sample discriminative tokens by comparing the performance of LVM-Net
473   with trained neural sampler vs uniform random sampling of tokens in Section A.3.1.

      C ONCLUSION
476
477
      Long-form video understanding has been a long-standing challenge for the computer vision com-
478
      munity. While many approaches exist, they cannot be efficiently applied to longer videos over 30
479
      minutes. In this paper, we present LVM-Net that demonstrates an efficient network for long-form
480   video reasoning using an external memory. The external memory is populated using differentiable
481   neural sampler that samples tokens and builds an effective condensed representation. In our results,
482   we demonstrate an 18-75X faster inference over the state of the art in the ReST ADL video rea-
483   soning benchmark. The inference speedup is primarily due to the fact that our proposed LVM-Net
484   performs a single pass over a long video to populate memory and can provide answers to multiple
485   queries from the long video using the populated memory. In the future, we plan to extend our work
      to long-context VLMs and to reduce the compute requirements for retrievals using natural language.
