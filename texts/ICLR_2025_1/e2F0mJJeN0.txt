009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Data pruning, the combinatorial task of selecting a small and informative subset
012            from a large dataset, is crucial for mitigating the enormous computational costs
013            associated with training data-hungry modern deep learning models at scale. Since
014            large-scale data collections are invariably noisy, developing data pruning strategies
015            that remain robust even in the presence of corruption is critical in practice. In
016            response, we propose G M M ATCHING – a herding (Welling, 2009) style greedy
017            algorithm – that yields a k-subset such that the mean of the subset approximates
018            the geometric median of the (potentially) noisy dataset. Theoretically, √ we show
019            that G M Matching enjoys an improved O(1/k) scaling over O(1/ k) scaling
020
               of uniform sampling; while achieving the optimal breakdown point of 1/2 even
               under arbitrary corruption. Extensive experiments across popular deep learning
021
               benchmarks indicate that G M Matching consistently outperforms prior state-of-the-
022
               art; the gains become more profound at high rates of corruption and aggressive
023            pruning rates; making it a strong baseline for robust data pruning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent success of deep learning has been largely fueled by training gigantic models over vast amounts
029
      of training data (Radford et al., 2021; 2018; Brown et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Hestness et al.,
030   2017). Such large scale training, however is associated with enormous computational costs hindering
031   the path to democratizing AI (Paul et al., 2021). Data pruning, the combinatorial task of downsizing a
032   large training set into a small informative subset (Feldman, 2020; Agarwal et al., 2005; Muthukrishnan
033   et al., 2005; Har-Peled, 2011; Feldman & Langberg, 2011), is a promising approach for reducing the
034   enormous computational and storage costs of modern deep learning.
035
      E XISTING DATA P RUNING S TRATEGIES
036
037   Consequently, a large body of recent works have been proposed to solve the data selection problem.
038   At a high level, there are two main directions: One set of data pruning approaches rely on some
039
      carefully designed pruning metrics, rank the training samples based on the scores and retain a
      fraction of them as representative samples (super samples), used for training the downstream model.
040
      For example, (Xia et al., 2022; Joshi & Mirzasoleiman, 2023; Sorscher et al., 2022) calculate the
041
      importance score of a sample in terms of the distance from the centroid of its corresponding class
042   marginal. Samples closer to the centroid are considered most prototypical (easy) and those far from
043   the centroid are treated as least prototypical (hard). A second set of works reformulate this problem as
044   minimizing a moment matching objective (Chen et al., 2010; Campbell & Broderick, 2018; Dwivedi
045   & Mackey, 2021) that aims to select a subset whose mean closely matches that of the entire dataset.
046
      While this work primarily focuses on spatial approaches, it is worth mentioning that the canonical
047
      importance scoring criterion have been proposed in terms gradient norm (Paul et al., 2021; Needell
048   et al., 2014), uncertainty (Pleiss et al., 2020) and forgetfulness (Toneva et al., 2018). Typically,
049   samples closer to the class centroid in feature space tend to have lower gradient norms, exhibit
050   lower uncertainty, and are harder to forget during training. In contrast, samples farther from the
051   centroid generally have higher gradient norms, greater uncertainty, and are easier to forget (Paul et al.,
052   2021; Sorscher et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2022). Moreover, (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020) extended the
053   moment-matching approach to the gradient space, selecting subsets that preserve the overall gradient
      statistics of the full dataset.

081   ROBUSTNESS VS D IVERSITY
082
083   In the ideal scenario (i.e. in absence of any corruption), hard examples are known to contribute
084
      the most in downstream generalization performance (Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018; Joshi et al.,
      2009; Huang et al., 2010; Balcan et al., 2007) as they often capture most of the usable information in
085
      the dataset (Xu et al., 2020). On the other hand, in realistic noisy scenarios involving outliers, this
086
      strategy often fails since the noisy examples are wrongly deemed informative for training (Zhang &
087   Sabuncu, 2018; Park et al., 2024). Pruning methods specifically designed for such noisy scenarios
088   thus propose to retain the most representative (easy) samples (Pleiss et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2018;
089   Har-Peled et al., 2006; Shah et al., 2020; Shen & Sanghavi, 2019). However, by only choosing
090   samples far from the decision boundary, these methods ignore the more informative uncorrupted less
091   prototypical samples. This can often result in sub-optimal downstream performance and in fact can
092   also lead to degenerate solutions due to a covariance-shift problem (Sugiyama & Kawanabe, 2012);
093   giving rise to a robustness vs diversity trade off (Xia et al., 2022). This restricts the applicability of
094   existing pruning methods, as realistic scenarios often deviate from expected conditions, making it
095
      challenging or impractical to adjust the criteria and methods accordingly.

      OVERVIEW OF OUR APPROACH
109
110   To go beyond these limitations, we study data pruning in presence of corruption. Specifically, we
111   consider the α corruption framework (Definition 1), where 0 ≤ ψ < 12 fraction of the samples are
112
      allowed be arbitrarily perturbed. This allowance for arbitrary corruption enables us to generalize
      many practical robustness scenarios; including corrupt feature / label and adversarial attacks.
113
114   We make a key observation that, traditional pruning methods typically use the empirical mean
115   to calculate the centroid of the samples, which then guides the selection process based on how
116   representative those samples are. However, the empirical mean is highly susceptible to outliers –
117   in fact, it is possible to construct a single adversarial example to arbitrarily perturb the empirical
118
      mean. As a consequence, in the presence of arbitrary corruption, the conventional distinction between
      easy (robust) and hard samples breaks down, leading to the selection of subsets that are significantly
119
      compromised by corruption as illustrated in Figure 1, depicting sampling from a corrupted Gaussian.
120
121   In response, we propose a data pruning strategy that fosters balanced diversity, effectively navigating
122   various regions of the distribution while avoiding distant, noisy points. Our key idea is to replace
123   the target moment in the standard moment matching objective with a robust surrogate – Geometric
124
      Median (Weber et al., 1929; Weiszfeld, 1937) – a classical robust estimator of the mean. In particular,
      we optimize over finding a subset minimizes the discrepancy between the subset’s mean and the G M
125
      (Definition 3) of the (potentially noisy) dataset using greedy herding (Welling, 2009) style update
126
      rule. We call our algorithm Geometric Median Matching as described in Algorithm 1.
127
128   C ONTRIBUTIONS
129   Overall, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
130
131   • We systematically and formally investigate and extend data pruning in presence of corruption. In
132     particular, we study data pruning under the gross corruption framework (Definition 1), where up to
133     1/2 fraction of the training examples are allowed to be arbitrarily corrupted. We note that, existing
134     pruning heuristics (including the ones proposed for robust scenarios) break down under this strong
135     corruption, due to empirical mean’s vulnerability to corruption (Section 4, Figure 1).
136   • Motivated by this key observation, we exploit the robustness property of G M (Definition 3), to
137     design a novel robust moment matching objective (6). It aims at finding a subset such that the mean
138     of the subset approximates the G M of the noisy dataset. We minimize over this objective using
139     greedy herding (Welling, 2009) style update rule. We call the resulting data pruning algorithm
140     G M M ATCHING and formally describe it in Algorithm 1.
141   • Leveraging classical robustness properties of G M, we show that, G M Matching converges to a
142     bounded neighborhood of original underlying mean, at an impressive O(1/k) rate while being
143     robust even when up to 1/2 of the samples are arbitrarily corrupted ( Theorem 1) .
144
      • Extensive experiments over CIFAR 10/100, Tiny ImageNet, across feature corruption, label noise
145
        and adversarial attacks indicate the superiority of G M Matching over existing methods. We improve
146
        over prior work almost in all settings, the gains are especially more profound (often by more than
147     10%) in presence of corruption and at aggressive pruning rates; making GM Matching a strong
148     baseline for future research in robust data pruning.

151   2    P ROBLEM S ETUP : ROBUST DATA P RUNING
152
153
      Given a set of samples D, the goal of data pruning is to select a subset of the most representative
154
      samples DS ⊆ D, that can approximate the underlying distribution well. Data pruning methods
155
      achieve this by first defining a pruning criterion e.g. based on distance, uncertainty, diversity; and
156   then selecting a subset that best satisfies these criteria to represent the full dataset effectively. If such
157   a subset (also referred to as coreset) can be found in a compute efficient manner, then training a
158   parametric model on the subset, typically yields similar generalization performance as training on
159   the entire dataset while resulting in significant speed up when |DS |   |D|. However, for machine
160   learning systems deployed in the wild, D is often noisy and imperfect due to the difficulty and expense
161   of obtaining perfect semantic annotations for large amounts of data, adversarial attacks or simply
      measurement noises.

180    fraction of contaminated data that can cause the estimator to result in arbitrarily large errors.

182   uhaa et al., 1991) if it remains robust in presence of α-corruption ∀ α < 1.

217   outliers. Consider a single adversarial sample: xB = |D|µB − x∈D\xB x, shifting the empirical

224    Motivated by this key observation, a natural idea to enable ROBUST M OMENT M ATCHING is
225    to replace the empirical mean in (1) with a robust surrogate estimator of the target moment and
226

227

228

229
230   In the univariate setting, various robust mean estimators, such as the median and the trimmed mean,
231   are known to achieve the optimal breakdown point 1/2. A common strategy to extend these methods
232   to the multivariate setting is to perform univariate estimation independently along each dimension.
233   However, in high dimensions, these estimates need not lie in the convex hull of the samples and
234
      are not orthogonal equivariant and can even become degenerate in the overparameterized settings
      (n   d) (Lopuhaa et al., 1991; Rousseeuw & Leroy, 2005). On the other hand, M-estimators are
235
      affine equivariant but have breakdown point at most 1/(d + 1) (Donoho & Huber, 1983).

      6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we formalized the problem of robust data pruning. We show that existing data pruning
534   strategies suffer significant degradation in performance in presence of corruption. Orthogonal to
535   existing works, we propose G M M ATCHING where our goal is to find a k-subset from the noisy data
536   such that the mean of the subset approximates the G M of the noisy dataset. We solve this meta
537   problem using a herding style greedy approach. We theoretically justify our approach and empirically
538   show its efficacy by comparing it against several popular benchmarks across multiple datasets. Our
539   results indicate that G M M ATCHING consistently outperforms existing pruning strategies in both
      clean and noisy settings making it a lucrative tool for data pruning in the wild.

       8.4   A DDITIONAL D ETAILS ON BASELINES
1297
1298   Here, we detail the technical aspects of the baselines used in our experiments:
1299
1300   • Random: This approach involves randomly selecting a subset of the full dataset.
1301   • Herding Welling (2009): This method selects data points that are closest to the class centers.
1302
       • Forgetting Toneva et al. (2018): Data points that are easily forgotten during optimization are
1303     chosen.
1304
       • GraNd-score Paul et al. (2021): Data points with larger loss gradient norms are included.
1305
1306   • EL2N-score Paul et al. (2021): This focuses on data points with larger norms of the error vector,
1307     which is the difference between the predicted class probabilities and the one-hot label encoding.
1308   • Optimization-based Yang et al. (2022): This method uses the influence function Koh & Liang
1309     (2017) to select data points that minimize the generalization gap under strict constraints.
1310   • Self-sup.-selection Sorscher et al. (2022): After self-supervised pre-training and clustering, data
1311     points are selected based on their distance to the nearest cluster centroid, with the number of
1312     clusters set to the number of classes to avoid tuning. Points with larger distances are chosen.
