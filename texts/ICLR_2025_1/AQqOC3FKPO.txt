009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual Salient Object Detection (SOD) and Camouflaged Object Detection (COD)
012            are two interrelated yet distinct tasks. Both tasks model the human visual system’s
013            ability to perceive the presence of objects. The traditional SOD datasets and meth-
014            ods are designed for scenes where only salient objects are present, similarly, COD
015            datasets and methods are designed for scenes where only camouflaged objects are
016
               present. However, scenes where both salient and camouflaged objects coexist, or
               where neither is present, are not considered. This simplifies the existing research
017
               on SOD and COD. In this paper, to explore a more generalized approach to SOD
018
               and COD, we introduce a benchmark called Unconstrained Salient and Camou-
019            flaged Object Detection (USCOD), which supports the simultaneous detection
020            of salient and camouflaged objects in unconstrained scenes, regardless of their
021            presence. Towards this, we construct a large-scale dataset, CS12K, that encom-
022            passes a variety of scenes, including four distinct types: scenes containing only
023            salient objects, scenes with only camouflaged objects, scenes where both salient
024            and camouflaged objects coexist, and scenes without any objects. In our bench-
025            mark experiments, we find that a major challenge in USCOD is distinguishing
026            salient objects from camouflaged objects within the same model. To address this,
027
               we propose a USCOD baseline called USCNet, which freezes the SAM mask de-
               coder for mask reconstruction, allowing the model to focus on distinguishing be-
028
               tween salient and camouflaged objects. Furthermore, to evaluate models’ ability
029
               to distinguish between salient and camouflaged objects, we design a metric called
030            Camouflage-Saliency Confusion Score (CSCS). The proposed method achieves
031            state-of-the-art performance on the newly introduced USCOD task. The code and
032            dataset will be publicly available.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The attention mechanism is one of the key cognitive functions of humans Posner et al. (1990). In
038   real-world scenarios, people are often drawn to salient objects while overlooking camouflaged ones.
039
      The goal of Salient Object Detection (SOD) is to detect objects in an image that the human visual
      system considers most salient or attention-grabbing, while Camouflaged Object Detection (COD)
040
      aims to detect objects that are difficult to perceive or blend seamlessly with their surroundings Li
041
      et al. (2021). SOD simulates the human ability to focus on salient objects, while COD mimics the
042   human ability to discover camouflaged objects. Both of them exhibit significant potential across var-
043   ious fields, such as anomaly detection in medical image analysis Tang et al. (2023), obstacle recogni-
044   tion in autonomous driving, camouflage detection in military reconnaissance Lin & Prasetyo (2019),
045   and wildlife tracking in environmental monitoring Stevens & Merilaita (2009). Currently, existing
046   methods follow the training and inference paradigms of popular datasets, such as COD10K Fan et al.
047   (2020) in COD and DUTS Wang et al. (2017a) in SOD, and have made significant progress.
048   Limitations of existing SOD and COD methods. Existing SOD and COD methods often rely
049   on strong pre-defined constraints specific to the tasks, which may limit their generalizability. The
050   classic SOD and COD methods are designed for detecting their respective attribute-specific objects,
051   considering only scenes where single-attribute objects exist (Figure 1. Scene A and Scene B). They
052   overlook more complex scenes where both salient and camouflaged objects coexist (Figure 1. Scene
053   C) or where neither type of object is present (Figure 1. Scene D). Some works have already explored
      how to handle SOD and COD simultaneously. EVP Liu et al. (2023) achieves the detection of

140   2.1   S ALIENT AND C AMOUFLAGED O BJECT D ETECTION
141
142
      SOD. In recent years, salient object detection models have focused on better detecting salient
      objects in images using various approaches. The main approaches can be divided into attention-
143
      based methods Liu et al. (2018); Piao et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2018), multi-level feature-based
144
      methods Fang et al. (2022); Hou et al. (2017); Pang et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2017b); Zhao et al.
145   (2020), and recurrent-based methods Deng et al. (2018); Liu & Han (2016); Wang et al. (2018).
146   Saliency detection Zhao et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021a); Zhuge et al. (2022)
147   primarily focus on achieving saliency predictions while preserving the structure.
148
149   COD. Compared to salient object detection, current COD methods Fan et al. (2021); Mei
150   et al. (2021); Pang et al. (2022); He et al. (2023); Jia et al. (2022) focus primarily on edge-aware
151   perception and texture perception. Mainly divided into the following two types, multi-level
152   feature-based methods Zhang et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2021); Ren et al. (2021); Zhai et al. (2022),
153
      Edge joint learning Zhai et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022); He et al. (2023).
154
      Unified. SOD and COD are distinct yet interrelated tasks Luo et al. (2024). Recently, some
155
      works have already begun to unify the two tasks. EVP Liu et al. (2023) solves the detection of
156   Camouflaged, Forgery, Shadow, and Defocus Blur by adding a visual prompt to the same base
157   segmentation model, allowing a single base model to handle different tasks by using specific visual
158   prompt. VSCode Luo et al. (2024) uses a multi-dataset joint training approach, simultaneously
159   utilizing datasets from RGB SOD, RGB COD, RGB-D SOD, RGB-D COD, RGB-T SOD, Video
160   SOD (VSOD), and VCOD, and assigning different task prompts for SOD and COD tasks to
161   achieve task unification. Similarly, Spider Zhao et al. (2024) uses a comparable method to unify
      Context-dependent tasks. It also requires the input of specific task prompts.

      The unified models mentioned above have two constrains. First, they require the task type to be
163   pre-defined in advance, with a specific prompt input into the model, thus being constrained by the
164   prompt. Second, they cannot handle scenarios where both salient object and camouflaged object are
165   present simultaneously, being constrained by the scenario. The USCOD task we proposed success-
166   fully addresses these two aspects, achieving unconstrained by both the prompt and the scenario.

      2.2   A PPLICATIONS OF SAM.
169
170   The Segment Anything Model (SAM) Kirillov et al. (2023) represents a significant advancement
171   in scene segmentation using large vision models. Its versatility and adaptability underscore its ca-
172   pability to comprehend complex scenarios and objects, thereby pushing the boundaries of image
173   segmentation tasks even further. Current works leveraging SAM Chen et al. (2023a); Zhang et al.
174   (2023); Xiong et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024) showcase its adaptability to downstream tasks, notably
175   in areas where traditional segmentation models struggle, such as EfficientSAM Xiong et al. (2023)
176   and MedSAM Ma et al. (2024). More recently, the release of SAM2 Ravi et al. (2024) enhances
177   the original SAM’s ability to handle video content while demonstrating improved segmentation ac-
178
      curacy and inference efficiency in image segmentation across various downstream applications Zhu
      et al. (2024); Yan et al. (2024); Lian & Li (2024); Lou et al. (2024).
179
180   Some works that use SAM for SOD and COD are closely related to our research. MDSAM Gao
181   et al. (2024) is a novel multi-scale and detail-enhanced SOD model based on SAM, aimed at improv-
182   ing the performance and generalization capability of SOD task. SAM-Adapter Chen et al. (2023a)
183   and SAM2-Adapter Chen et al. (2024) offers a parameter-efficient fine-tuning way to enhance per-
184
      formance of SAM and SAM2 in downstream tasks like COD and medical image segmentation by
      adding task-specific knowledge. Nevertheless, these methods may be suboptimal for fine-tuning
185
      SAM for the USCOD task, as they discard prompt architecture of SAM and tune the mask decoder
186
      to simultaneously learn distinguishing attributes and segmenting mask, even though the mask de-
187   coder is not designed for attribute distinction. Therefore, we retain the mask decoder solely for mask
188   reconstruction and use independent learning for attribute distinction to better differentiate between
189   salient objects, camouflaged objects, and background.

192   3     P ROPOSED CS12K DATASET
193
194   The current datasets for camouflaged object detection, such as COD10K Fan et al. (2020), CAMO Le
195   et al. (2019), NC4K Lv et al. (2021), primarily feature scenes with exclusively camouflaged objects.
196   Similarly, datasets for salient object detection, such as DUTS Wang et al. (2017a), and HKU-IS Li
197   & Yu (2015), predominantly focus on scenes with solely salient objects. There are relatively few
198   samples with both salient objects and camouflaged objects in an image, which is not conducive to the
199
      realization of the unconstrained existence of salient and camouflaged object detection. Therefore,
      we introduce the CS12K, a dataset that includes more comprehensive and complex scenarios for
200
      unconstrained salient and camouflaged object detection. It includes scenes with both salient and
201
      camouflaged objects, scenes with only one type, and scenes without either. We will describe the
202   details of CS12K in terms of three key aspects, as follows.
203
204
205   3.1   DATA C OLLECTION
206
207
      Under the premise of ensuring sample balance, we collect 12,000 images from 8 different sources
      and divide them into four scenes after manual filtering: (A) Scenes with only salient objects: 3,000
208
      images containing only salient objects selected from SOD datasets DUTS and HKU-IS; (B) Scenes
209
      with only camouflaged objects: 3,000 images containing only camouflaged objects selected from
210   COD datasets COD10K and CAMO; (C) Scenes with both salient and camouflaged objects: 342 im-
211   ages from the COD datasets COD10K, CAMO, and NC4K, along with 41 images from the datasets
212   LSUI Peng et al. (2023) and AWA2 Xian et al. (2018), and an additional 2,617 images collected
213   from the internet, making a total of 3,000 images; (D)Scenes without salient and camouflaged ob-
214   jects, considered as background: 1,564 images from COD10K, and 1,436 images from the Internet.
215   Finally, we get 12,000 images, with the training set containing 8,400 images and the testing set
      containing 3,600 images. The data source is shown in Figure 2 (Left).

                  Overview. As illustrated in Figure 4, the main components of the proposed USCNet include: (1) A
296
                  SAM image encoder to extract object feature representation with adapter layers. (2) An Attribute-
297               specific Prompt Generation (APG) that generates three discriminative prompts for each attribute:
298               saliency, camouflage, and background. (3) A frozen mask decoder of SAM that is applied to predict
299               the final saliency, camouflaged, and background masks based on different attribute prompts.
300
301                4.1                    S EGMENT A NYTHING M ODEL
302
303               SAM Kirillov et al. (2023) designs a flexible prompting-enabled model architecture for category-
304               agnostic segmentation. Specifically, SAM consists of an image encoder, a prompt encoder, and a
305               mask decoder. The image encoder is pre-trained using the Masked Auto Encoder (MAE) He et al.
306               (2022), the prompt encoder handles dense and sparse inputs like boxes and points, and the mask
307               decoder predicts the masks based on the encoded embeddings. In USCNet, we utilize the prompt
308
                  architecture of SAM for identifying three attributes: saliency, camouflage, and background. The
                  attribute prompts is generated by a designed APG module, eliminating the need for manual prompt.
309
                  As a result, each attribute prompt is mapped to a distinct binary mask.

313
                  To leverage knowledge from SAM, SAM-Adapter Chen et al. (2023a) adapts SAM to downstream
314               tasks and achieve enhanced performance with a parameter-efficient fine-tuning approach. Following
315               that, USCNet integrates adapters into each layer of the SAM encoder, as depicted in Figure 4. As
316               a result, the output image embedding F from the tuned SAM Encoder exhibits features adept at
317               addressing USCOD task. Through this approach, USCNet blending USCOD-specific knowledge
318               with the general knowledge acquired by the larger model, better adapting to unconstrained scenes.

      Based on this, we propose Attribute- specific Prompt Generation (APG) integrates both Dynamic
      Prompt Query (DPQ) and Static Prompt Query (SPQ) to generate discriminative attribute-specific
344
      prompts, where SPQ to extract sample-generic features, capturing attribute information that applies
345
      to all samples, and DPQ to extract sample-specific features, focusing on the unique contextual in-
346   formation of the current sample. Specifically, as depicted in Figure 4, the APG integrates both the
347   Dynamic Prompt Query (DPQ) and Static Prompts Query (SPQ) to create attribute-specific prompts.
348   The SPQ consists of a set of learnable query embeddings, which are designed to encapsulate gen-
349   eral attributes. To formulate the DPQ, the system initially extracts features F from the encoder
350   to generate a coarse prediction, which is then processed through a sigmoid function to produce an
351   attention map. This attention map is then element-wise multiplied with the original features F to
352   isolate attribute-specific features. These features are further refined through a linear layer to produce
353   the DPQ, tailored to capture nuanced and specific attributes within individual samples. The DPQ
354
      generation process can be described by the formula:
355                              [QD S , QD C , QD B ] = M LP (σ(ΦCH (F )) ⊗ F ),                           (1)
356   where QD S , QD C , and QD B represent the DPQ for saliency, camouflage, and background, re-
357   spectively. M LP stands for a Multi-Layer Perceptron that processes the output. σ denotes the
358   sigmoid function, and ΦCH represents the operation to predict a coarse prediction from the features
359   F . The symbol ⊗ denotes element-wise multiplication. Unlike standard query embeddings which
360
      are fixed after training, the DPQ changes according to the sample, making it highly adaptable and
      capable of explicitly capturing the distinctive features of the camouflage and saliency across varying
361
      samples. The DPQ captures feature information from specific images, whereas the SPQ discerns the
362
      fundamental differences among three attributes. By combining the two, our APG attains improved
363   performance. Subsequently, we employ self-attention to establish relationships between queries, and
364   query-to-image (Q2I) attention to interact with image embedding, ultimately generating prompts for
365   the three attributes: PS , PC , PB . The process can be formulated as follows:
366
                              [PS , PC , PB ] = M LP (Q2I(SA(DP Q + SP Q), F )),                            (2)
367
      where PS , PC , and PB represent the prompts generated for identifying saliency, camouflage,
368

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   We analyze and address the limitations of classical SOD and COD tasks, which restrict research to
530   scenarios with only salient or only camouflaged objects. Based on that, a new benchmark called
531
      Unconstrained Salient and Camouflaged Object Detection (USCOD), is defined to allow for the
      unrestricted presence of both salient and camouflaged objects within images. We propose a new
532
      evaluation metric, i.e., the Camouflage-Saliency Confusion Score (CSCS), to assess the confusion
533
      of the model between camouflaged and salient objects. To support research on USCOD, we have
534   constructed a large-scale dataset, CS12K, that features a diverse range of scenes and categories.
535   We introduce a baseline method, USCNet, which decouples mask reconstruction from attribute dis-
536   tinction to focus on learning the differences between saliency and camouflage patterns, achieving
537   state-of-the-art performance on the USCOD task. The proposed USCOD reduces reliance on spe-
538   cific scenarios, increasing the representation of scenes where both salient and camouflaged objects
539   coexist, as well as scenes where neither is present, thus enhancing generalizability across diverse
      natural environments.

828   A    CSCS M ETRIC
829
830   Contrary to the Intersection over Union (IoU) that measures accuracy for a single class, the
831   Camouflage-Saliency Confusion Score (CSCS) assesses the misclassification between two distinct
832   classes. The CSCS, designed to evaluate the confusion between camouflaged and salient objects, is
833   calculated as follows:
834
835                                 1       PCS               PSC
                           CSCS =     (               +                 ),                               (6)
836                                 2 PBS + PSS + PCS   PBC + PSC + PCC
837
      where P = {Pλθ | λ ∈ Θ, θ ∈ Θ} , Θ = {B, C, S}, the B, C and S denote background, camouflage
838
      and saliency. A lower CSCS value indicates a stronger ability of the network to discriminate between
839   salient and camouflaged objects. PCS represents the label as camouflage but is predicted as saliency.
840   We aim to minimize the misclassification of camouflaged pixels as salient, ensuring the network
841   correctly distinguishes between camouflaged and salient objects. The same applies to PSC . As
842   shown in Figure 7, we present the confusion matrix of the proposed USCNet on the CS12K test set.
843   Our model balances improvements across all metrics, achieving a mIoU of 0.775 and a CSCS of
844   0.0749 (see Table 2 in the manuscript).

988    Training and Inference. For traditional SOD and COD models: The task of USCOD is defined by
989    three attributes: saliency, camouflage, and background. Conventional methods for COD and SOD
990
       are crafted for dichotomous mapping tasks and don’t seamlessly transition to the nuanced demands
       of USCOD. Inspired by seminal works in semantic segmentations Long et al. (2015); Strudel et al.
991
       (2021), we retool the output layers of our models to yield a tripartite representation for saliency,
992
       camouflage, and background. This is achieved by harnessing a softmax layer to generate a predictive
993    mapping. We employ a cross-entropy loss function to refine the model, which is congruent with our
994    overarching methodological framework. For unified models: VSCode and EVP, which require task-
995    specific prompts for each dataset, we create two copies of the CS12K training set. One copy is
996    used for SOD, with the ground truth being the SOD-only mask, and is used to train the prompts
997    corresponding to the SOD task. The other copy is used for COD, with the ground truth being the
998    COD-only mask, and is used to train the prompts corresponding to the COD task.VSCode is trained
999    once using all 16,800 images (two copies of 8,400 images), while EVP is trained twice on the
1000   two separate training sets (each containing 8,400 images) to obtain the two task-specific prompts.
1001
       During Inference, all unified models perform inference on the testing set of CS12K twice, with the
       corresponding prompt enabled for each task. The first inference run generates the SOD results, and
1002
       the second inference run generates the COD results. The final prediction is obtained by merging the
1003
       SOD and COD predictions. For overlapping pixels, the attribute with the higher prediction value
1004   between the two tasks is chosen as the final attribute for that pixel.

1106   H    D IFFICULTY OF USCOD
1107
1108   As illustrated in Figure 11, most methods encounter difficulties in distinguishing salient objects from
1109   camouflaged on CS12K benchmark. The essence of the challenge in USCOD lies in differentiating
1110   between salient and camouflaged objects within unconstrained scenes, mirroring the capabilities of
1111   human vision. Moving beyond the simplicity of traditional classification tasks, distinguishing be-
1112   tween visual saliency and camouflage requires a deeper semantic insight. Our observation indicates
1113   that when a vanilla network architecture is used for the USCOD task, a decoder responsible for dif-
1114
       ferentiating, localizing, and segmenting both salient and camouflaged objects encounters challenges
       in acquiring highly discriminative visual features. To address this, our approach leverages a spe-
1115
       cialized decoder focused on precise localization and segmentation. This allows for more effective
1116
       learning of the subtle distinctions between saliency and camouflage patterns, enhancing the ability to
1117   discern and differentiate these complex visual cues. Figure 9 showcases our architectural innovation,
1118   incorporating a frozen, pre-trained SAM mask decoder and an APG representing our venture into
1119   mining highly discriminative features. This design separates feature analysis from object segmenta-
1120   tion, enabling our model to focus on and extract distinct attributes crucial for differentiating saliency
1121   from camouflage patterns, thereby improving its performance in complex visual environments.

1151
       USCOD aims to adaptively identify salient and camouflaged objects in unconstrained open scenar-
1152
       ios, where each image may exist salient object, camouflaged object, both, or neither of them. Al-
1153   though our proposed CS12K dataset encompasses a wide array of scenarios and object categories,
1154   thereby enriching the learning experience for salient and camouflaged feature detection within un-
1155   constrained scenes for the COD and SOD communities, USCOD falls short in one critical aspect: it
1156   lacks the capability to differentiate between individual instances of salient and camouflaged objects.
1157   Specifically, USCOD is limited in recognizing the quantity of such instances and in distinguishing
1158   among different objects. This limitation undermines the effectiveness of algorithm in accurately dis-
1159   cerning the unique camouflage and saliency patterns of each object. Moving forward, our research
1160   will venture into the domain of Unconstrained Salient and Camouflaged Instance Segmentation
1161
       (USCIS), which strives to identify and segment each individual instance of salient and camouflaged
       objects. Figure 10 illustrates the label differences between USCOD and USCIS within the same

       image.
