009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            One way to increase confidence in the outputs of Large Language Models (LLMs)
012            is to support them with reasoning that is clear and easy to check — a property
013            we call legibility. We study legibility in the context of solving grade-school math
014            problems and show that optimizing chain-of-thought solutions only for answer
015            correctness can make them less legible. To mitigate the loss in legibility, we propose
016            a training algorithm inspired by Prover-Verifier Game from Anil et al. (2021). Our
017
               algorithm iteratively trains small verifiers to predict solution correctness, “helpful”
               provers to produce correct solutions that the verifier accepts, and “sneaky” provers
018
               to produce incorrect solutions that fool the verifier. We find that the helpful
019
               prover’s accuracy and the verifier’s robustness to adversarial attacks increase over
020            the course of training. Furthermore, we show that legibility training transfers to
021            time-constrained humans tasked with verifying solution correctness. Over course
022            of LLM training human accuracy increases when checking the helpful prover’s
023            solutions, and decreases when checking the sneaky prover’s solutions. Hence,
024            training for checkability by small verifiers is a plausible technique for increasing
025            output legibility. Our results suggest legibility training against small verifiers as a
026            practical avenue for increasing legibility of large LLMs to humans, and thus could
027            help with alignment of superhuman models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
      As machine learning systems become increasingly powerful and find applications in high-stakes areas,
032
      ensuring their outputs are trustworthy and understandable becomes crucial. One approach to building
033
      trust is requiring systems to produce legible explanations—outputs that can be fully understood and
034   verified by humans or other trusted systems.
035
036   In this paper, we investigate whether we can make the outputs of capable Large Language Models
037
      (LLMs) more legible to humans. One direct approach is to optimize model outputs for legibility
      via human feedback (e.g., RLHF; Christiano et al. 2017), which has its limitations (Casper et al.,
038
      2023). We study a complementary approach, and ask whether optimizing larger LLM provers to
039
      produce Chain-of-Thought (CoT) solutions checkable by smaller LLM verifiers makes them more
040   legible to humans. We propose a novel approach: optimizing larger LLM "provers" to produce
041   Chain-of-Thought (CoT) solutions that are checkable by smaller LLM "verifiers," with the hypothesis
042   that this will improve legibility to humans. Our key motivations for using smaller verifiers as proxies
043   for humans include: (1) scalability compared to direct human feedback, (2) the ability to study
044   legibility as a function of the capability gap between prover and verifier, and (3) the potential to
045   incrementally aid human legibility when direct human feedback is limited for advanced systems.
046   We define checkability through two properties:
047
048         1. Completeness: The prover generates correct solutions that the verifier accepts.
049         2. Soundness: The verifier rejects incorrect solutions from adversarial provers.
050
051   To achieve checkability, we propose an iterative algorithm inspired by the Prover-Verifier Game
052   (PVG; Anil et al. 2021). Our method alternates between training a verifier to predict solution
053   correctness and optimizing a prover using reinforcement learning to produce convincing solutions,
      conditioned on either a "helpful" or "sneaky" role.

      While previous work (Anil et al., 2021; Wäldchen et al., 2024; Amit et al., 2024) applied these
109   concepts to PAC learning and alignment in toy settings, we extend this to training legible LLMs in
110   non-trivial domains. PVG is also similar to the Actor-Critic framework in reinforment learning (Wit-

      Scalable oversight. The central hypothesis that discrimination is easier than generation is common
115
      to many scalable oversight methods (Bowman et al., 2022) including critiques (Saunders et al., 2022;
116   McAleese et al., 2024) and debate (Irving et al., 2018). Parrish et al. (2022) showed negative results
117   for improving time-limited human judge accuracy though human debate. Previous works have also
118   studied using LLMs as judges or debaters, although they differ on whether ground truth labels are
119   available for training. Our setup is similar to single-turn simultaneous debate where the helpful and
120   sneaky provers try to convince the model judge of different answers, assuming that some ground
121   truth labels is available for improving both the provers and the verifier. Similarly, using supervised
122   training, Radhakrishnan (2023) trained model debaters and judges and showed that the optimization
123   makes the debaters more convincing and the judges more accurate. Michael et al. (2023) showed that
124
      debate works better than consultancy (showing the argument from a helpful prover half the time and
      sneaky half the time) for human judges. In our setup the judge does not see both sides at the same
125
      time, so it is more similar to the consultancy approach. Khan et al. (2024) showed that LLMs trained
126
      using persuasiveness signal only (no ground truth labels) can help both non-expert model and human
127   judges. All of those works mainly use the QuALITY dataset (Pang et al., 2022), where the task is
128   question-answering about a fictional story, so the justifications mainly consistent of verified quotes
129   from the text. In contrast, we focus on a less artificial, more challenging reasoning domain where the
130   difference between provers and verifiers isn’t privileged information but capability gap.
131
132
      Chain-of-Thought. Prompting LLMs to produce Chain-of-Thought (CoT) solutions is useful for
133
      boosting accuracy (Wei et al., 2022) and CoTs can be used as natural justifications for the final
134   answer. However, CoTs are not guaranteed to be legible a priori and our checkability training is
135   one way to train CoTs to be more legible. Many works guide the model to produce a specific type
136   of structured CoT, such as task decomposition (Christiano et al., 2018; Radhakrishnan et al., 2023;
137   Wen et al., 2024), recursive summarization (Wu et al., 2021), and symbolic reasoning (Lyu et al.,
138   2023). Lightman et al. (2024) use expensive step-wise human supervision to train models that can
139   solve math. In contrast, we do not enforce any output structure, and rely solely on final answer
140   supervision. Our checkability training method is general and can be readily studied in any domain
141   where we have some ground truth labels. Legibility emerges as a natural consequence of the training
142
      dynamics (Figure 2).
143
144   Explainability Explainability (Zhao et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) tries to give an account of the
145   prover model’s reasoning, i.e. how it derived as the final answer. The resulting explanations can be
146   arbitrarily complex (Nanda et al., 2023). In contrast, legibility of a prover model’s output allows
147   to verify if a given solution is correct, ignoring how the prover reasoned about it. Consequently,
148   legibility requires model outputs that are coherent and accessible to human understanding (Rudin,
149
      2019). It is possible to have either without the other, but especially in high-stakes settings correctness
      is more important than understanding (Ziegler et al., 2022).
150
151
152   Adversarial Robustness. Our checkability training relates to adversarial training in neural net-
153   works (Szegedy et al., 2013; Biggio et al., 2013; Elsayed et al., 2018; Carlini et al., 2019). We
154   demonstrate that our approach improves verifier robustness against various attacks, contributing to
155   the broader goal of creating more reliable AI systems.
156
157   Legibility in Applications. We build on prior work studying legibility in various contexts, such
158   as robotics and coding (Dragan et al., 2013; Oliveira et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2024). Our focus on
159   solution correctness in time-limited settings offers a specific, practical measure of human legibility.
160   By addressing these interconnected areas, our work aims to advance the development of trustworthy
161   and understandable AI systems, particularly in scenarios where direct human oversight may be
      challenging.

      3.1   C HECKABILITY AND THE P ROVER -V ERIFIER G AME
189
      Our framework involves a prover π(z|x) from model family H generating solutions and a verifier
190   v(x, z) ∈ [0, 1] estimating solution correctness. We define checkability through two properties:
191
192          • Completeness: ∀x ∈ X , 1correct (x, π(x)) = v(x, π(x)) = 1
193          • Soundness: ∀π ′ ∈ H, ∀x ∈ X , 1correct (x, π ′ (x)) = 0 =⇒ v(x, π ′ (x)) = 0
194
195   In practice, we seek approximate completeness and soundness, comparing verifiers’ soundness via
196   their ranking on various attacks.
197   We formulate checkability training as a two-player game between a role-conditioned prover π (helpful
198   h(x) or sneaky s(x)) and the verifier v. The verifier’s utility and prover’s reward are defined as:
199                1                                             1
200      RV = − Ex∼PX l(v(x, h(x)), 1correct (x, h(x))) − Ex∼PX l(v(x, s(x)), 1correct (x, s(x)))
                   2                                             2
201             1                                              1
202      RP = Ex∼PX r(v(x, h(x)), 1correct (x, h(x))) + Ex∼PX r(v(x, s(x)), 1 − 1correct (x, s(x)))
                2                                              2
203   where r(v, q) satisfies conditions to prioritize role-aligned solutions (see Appendix A.1) and l(v, c) :
204   [0, 1] × {0, 1} → R is a loss function minimized if and only if v = c.
205
206
      We establish a connection between the prover-verifier game and checkability as defined above:
207   Theorem 1. Assuming H is the set of all deterministic mappings from X to Z, in the PVG where
208
      verifier’s utility is RV and the prover’s utility is RP , the existence of a verifier-leading Stackelberg
      equilibrium (v , h, s) is necessary and sufficient for the completeness and soundness properties to hold
209
      for (v , π ∗ ).
210
211   The proof is provided in Appendix C.

300   4.2                     DYNAMICS OF CHECKABILITY TRAINING
301
302   We investigate whether checkability training can improve performance while maintaining legibility,
303   focusing on results using the Signed Relative Convincingness (SRC; Eq. 3) reward, which proved
304
      most effective for improving legibility.
305   Figure 5 illustrates the dynamics of prover training across rounds. Within each round, both sneaky
306   and helpful provers improve their convincingness to the verifier (Fig 5a). However, their accuracy
307   evolves differently: the helpful prover generally produces increasingly correct solutions, while the
308   sneaky prover’s accuracy decreases, eventually generating incorrect solutions that are as convincing
309   as the helpful prover’s correct ones (Fig 5b,c).
310   We explored these dynamics across various verifier sizes relative to the prover. The pattern holds
311   for verifiers ranging from 10−3 to 10−1.8 times the prover’s compute. However, very small verifiers
312   (10−4 ) lead to unstable training and poor legibility, while verifiers matching the prover’s size (100 )
313   fail to become more robust over rounds (see Supplementary Figures S6 and S7). These outcomes
314   likely stem from fundamental limitations: extremely small verifiers may lack the capacity for effective
315   verification, while verifiers similar to provers may be too easily fooled by sneaky provers simulating
316
      their decision process.
317   In summary, our checkability training demonstrates a characteristic dynamic where the helpful
318   prover improves at problem-solving while the sneaky prover finds it increasingly difficult to produce
319   convincing, incorrect solutions. This suggests that the approach can potentially balance performance
320   improvement with maintained legibility.

353                                 4.3.1       T HE VERIFIER BECOMES MORE ROBUST THROUGHOUT CHECKABILITY TRAINING
354
355                                 A key aspect of legibility in our framework is the verifier’s ability to soundly evaluate adversarial
356                                 inputs from a sneaky prover of equal capability to the helpful prover. While we observe that the
357
                                    sneaky prover requires increasingly many steps to discover convincing, incorrect solutions throughout
                                    training (Fig. 5b), this could be due to weight sharing between the helpful and sneaky provers. To
358
                                    address this, we conduct additional experiments with separately trained sneaky-only provers.
359
360                                 We performed three attacks with sneaky-only provers using the following reward functions:
361
362
                                            1. the SRC reward (3) to ablate the effect of weight sharing between the helpful and the sneaky
                                               prover.
363
364                                         2. the CGC reward (4) to test generalization of verifier soundness to different variations of
365                                            checkability training.
366                                         3. the goodharting reward (5) to straightforwardly test robustness to optimization for convinc-
367                                            ingness (Gao et al., 2023).
368
369                                 We apply these attacks to verifiers of different sizes across five rounds of checkability training. We
370                                 observe that the number of steps required for each adversary to find an exploit increases over rounds
371                                 and that larger verifiers are generally harder to exploit (Fig. 6a). In fact, verifiers of relative size 10−2

      4.3.2      T HE SNEAKY PROVER BECOMES INCREASINGLY SUBTLE THROUGHOUT CHECKABILITY
                 TRAINING
415
416   Having established the verifier’s increasing robustness, we now analyze the sneaky attacks that do
417   manage to fool the verifier.
418
      While early-round sneaky solutions often contain obvious mistakes, later-round solutions feature
419
      increasingly subtle flaws (Fig. 7c and Sup. Fig. S4). To quantify this evolution, we used GPT-4 to
420
      assess the validity of small portions of sneaky solutions across training rounds (Fig. 7a). The results
421   show that while the minimum score remains low (indicating a detectable flaw), the average score
422   increases over rounds, suggesting improved overall soundness of the solutions.
423
424
      We also investigated whether these increasingly subtle attacks transfer to human evaluators. Interest-
      ingly, when time-constrained contractors assessed the sneaky solutions, their accuracy in correctly
425
      labeling these solutions decreased throughout the rounds (Fig. 7b). This suggests that the sneaky
426
      prover’s outputs become more challenging for humans to evaluate, mirroring the increased difficulty
427   for the verifier1 .
428
429   These findings indicate that our checkability training produces a sneaky prover capable of generating
430
      subtly flawed solutions that are non-trivial for both automated verifiers and time-constrained humans

      4.3.3    T HE HELPFUL PROVER BECOMES INCREASINGLY LEGIBLE THROUGHOUT
455            CHECKABILITY TRAINING
456
457   We now examine the helpful prover’s evolution across training rounds, focusing on both its accuracy
458   and the legibility of its solutions to human evaluators.
459   As checkability training progresses, the helpful prover’s accuracy improves (Fig 8a). Unlike the
460   correctness-only training approach, solutions from the helpful prover remain coherent throughout
461   training (Fig. 2), indicating effective use of verifier feedback to enhance problem-solving capabilities.
462
      To assess human legibility, we conducted evaluations with time-constrained contractors. Results
463
      show improvements in both the contractors’ mean accuracy in evaluating solutions (Fig. 8b) and the
464
      time required for evaluation (Fig. 8c) as training advances. By the end of training, the helpful prover
465   matches the initialization’s legibility while achieving substantially stronger performance.
466
467   However, the helpful prover’s performance does not reach that of the correctness-only baseline
468
      (Fig. 4), highlighting a tension between high performance and legibility (Leike, 2022). Potential
      approaches to address this trade-off, such as separating the model’s chain-of-thought from proof of
469
      correctness, are discussed in Appendix 5.1.
470
471   In summary, checkability training produces a helpful prover that generates increasingly correct and
472   legible solutions. This dual improvement is crucial for applications requiring human oversight and
473   collaboration with AI systems.

476
477   5.1     L IMITATIONS
478
479   Only domains that allow natural language justifications. We studied the math domain because
480   natural language explanations are common and often required for building trust in mathematical
481   conclusions. However, in some domains it is difficult to support conclusions with natural language ex-
482   planations. For example recognizing particular faces is easy for humans, but it’s hard to communicate
483   to others in natural language why a particular face belongs to a particular person.
484
485   Need for ground truth answers and reliance on generalization. One prerequisite for our setup
      is being able to certify correctness of answers of solutions produced by the prover. For the grade


                                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      school math domain we rely on having a training dataset of problems with known ground truth
487   answers (Cobbe et al., 2021). We anticipate this setup to be less relevant to domains where collecting
488   any ground truth is too hard or expensive. One possible approach in this case could be to hope for
489   favorable out-of-distribution generalization properties of legibility from solutions to problems with
490   known ground truth answers to other domains. We don’t study out-of-domain generalization in this
491   paper because we train on only one dataset, but we expect that training on a diverse set of domains
492   could potentially lead to good generalization from domains with ground truth labels to the domains
493   without labels (Burns et al., 2023).
494
495   Conflating the model’s chain-of-thought with legible solutions. By training the CoTs to be
496   legible, we potentially limited the provers’ accuracy. A better method could be training a first model
497   using CoT to maximize accuracy, and then training a second model to turn the CoT used to produce
498   the answer into a legible justification to humans. Hence, our measurement of accuracy-legibility
499   tradeoff may be overly pessimistic; the prover may be able to achieve legibility at less or no cost to
500   accuracy. For the correctness-only baseline, we could have early stopped at less accurate but more
501
      legible checkpoint. However, we don’t know of a principled way to do early stopping for legibility.
502
503   Dataset diversity and difficulty. We conducted our empirical study on only one dataset (GSM).
504   The dataset is relatively easy and the solutions are quite standardized. Using ChatGPT to augment
505   this dataset could result in some incorrect labels, but based on preliminary data analysis we did not
506
      see significant difference between verifier test accuracies trained on synthetic vs. real data. We would
      like to extend the checkability training framework to harder math datasets and coding, as well as
507
      fuzzier domains such as human preference data (Ouyang et al., 2022). Our method is fully general as
508
      long as there is a finite set of final answer labels.
509
510
511   Initialization with human-written math derivations. Our prover initialization has relatively high
512
      performance in GSM. We used a prover that has been lightly finetuned on a variety of math data
      to learn the correct format, and the finetuning dataset contains some of the GSM trainset data. We
513
      believe this doesn’t qualitatively affect our results and only makes it easier for the helpful prover
514
      to produce correct answers. Figure S11 shows that initialization with a base model without math
515   finetuning leads to qualitatively similar training dynamics.
516
517
518
      Heterogeneous experiment set-ups. We have different stopping criteria for different training
      schemes presented throughout the paper. We trained the prover for a fixed number of RL steps in each
519
      round for certain training schemes (4k steps in Fig. 6a, 5c, 8k steps in Fig. 4, 5c, S6, S7), whereas in
520
      Fig. 5, S1, S2 we trained until early stopping criteria are met (the criteria are already discussed in
521   those section). We do not observe qualitative different between early stopping and non early stopping
522   rounds in our experiments.
523
524
      Hyperparameters. We did not tune all hyperparameters thoroughly for all training schemes in
525
      this paper. The main hyperparameter we tuned are the ratios of samples from each of the previous
526   rounds. We tuned the ratio for one verifier size (10−3 ) for the SRC reward based on whether training
527   robustness increases across rounds, and used the same ratio for other sizes with SRC and CGC
528   rewards, which could result in other training schemes being worse in robustness and legibility. We
529   separately tuned the ratio for one verifier size (10−3 ) for the iterative goodharting training. We
530   used the default ChatGPT post-training hyperparameters such as the optimizer, learning rate, or KL
531   regularization coefficient for prover and verifier training. 6 rounds of checkability training (Fig 5)
532   takes 2400 A100-40G GPU hours.
533
534   5.2   C ONCLUSION
535
536   We presented a tension between model performance and solution legibility in a concrete setting of
537   judging grade-school math solutions, and proposed one way to address the tradeoff. Our results show
538   promising signs for establishing trust in LLM outputs even if they become more capable than humans
539   in the future. We hope to inspire future work on semi-supervised / unsupervised setups for improving
      human judge legibility with few or no ground truth labels.

      A.1    R EWARD FUNCTION CONDITION FOR T HEOREM (1)
759
760   A reward function that prioritizes role-aligned solutions is a function r(v, q) : [0, 1] × {0, 1} → R
761   that satisfies:
762
763
                                             ∀v1 , v2 ∈ [0, 1] : r(v1 , 1) > r(v2 , 0),
764                                ∀v1 , v2 ∈ [0, 1], v1 > v2 : r(v1 , 1) > r(v2 , 1).                            (2)
765
      Here q ∈ {0, 1} indicates that the correctness of the solution is role-aligned — it is 1 if the prover
766   is assigned to be helpful and the solution is correct, or the prover is assigned to be sneaky and the
767   solution is incorrect. The two assumptions on the prover reward functions above are: First, any role-
768   aligned solution gets higher reward than any role-misaligned solution; Second, for any role-aligned
769   solution, the prover gets higher reward when the verifier gives the solution higher score. In practice
770   we optimize RP via reinforcement learning since we cannot differentiate with respect to the solution,
771   because it requires iterative auto-regressive sampling of LLM tokens, a non-differentiable operation.
772
773   A.2    R EWARD FUNCTION VARIATIONS
774
775   We explore three reward variants, each designed to investigate different aspects of the prover-verifier
776
      dynamics. The Signed Relative Convincingness (SRC) balances the importance of correctness and
      convincingness. It’s designed to encourage the prover to produce solutions that are both correct (for
777
      the helpful role) or incorrect (for the sneaky role) and convincing to the verifier. The Correctness-
778
      Gated Convincingness (CGC) variant prioritizes role-alignment over convincingness. It’s meant to
779   explore whether strictly enforcing correctness for the helpful prover and incorrectness for the sneaky
780   prover leads to better overall performance and legibility. Finally, in the Iterative Goodharting setup,
781   both helpful and sneaky provers optimize solely for convincingness. This variant investigates the
782   robustness of the verifier against provers that are trying to maximize the verifier’s score without
783   regard for correctness, simulating a scenario where the prover might try to exploit weaknesses in the
784   verifier.

847   A.3                 C HECKABILITY TRAINING WITH THE C ORRECTNESS -G ATED C ONVINCINGNESS (CGC)
848                       REWARD
849
      The Correctness-Gated Convincingness (CGC) reward (4) prioritizes correctness (for the helpful
850
      prover) and incorrectness (for the sneaky prover) over convincingness. When the helpful prover
851
      produces incorrect answers (or the sneaky prover produces correct answers), the reward is set to a
852   constant penalty; otherwise it is the verifier score. In this section, we show that the CGC reward
853   empirically leads to worse training dynamics.
854
855
      In each round of CGC reward training, we train the prover until the average verifier score of the
      sneaky’s incorrect samples become as high as that of the helpful’s correct samples, and the sneaky
856
      reaches low (< 5%) accuracy. Figure S1 shows the prover training dynamics — the number of RL
857
      steps until the sneaky prover succeeds does not consistently increase over rounds. We observe that the
858   sneaky prover’s accuracy quickly decreases and the convincingness rises more slowly for the CGC
859   reward in comparison to the SRC reward. Our hypothesis is that in this case, the sneaky and helpful
860   players quickly exploits different modes with distinct styles, and hence there are obvious spurious
861   statistical clues that separates sneaky and helpful which won’t help the verifier learn the correctness
862   signal. See section E for more discussion. It is possible that we did not tune the hyperparameters
863   for the CGC reward optimally, or the verifier requires more rounds to become robust. We did not
      perform human evaluation for the CGC reward training due to its worse training dynamics.

      A.4               I TERATIVE GOODHARTING TRAINING
884
885   We run an alternative training scheme where the prover does not have an assigned role. It is trained
886   with reward (5), which is just the verifier score. We call this the ‘goodharting’ baseline because
887   usually during Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022), the
888   prover is directly optimized to maximize the score of a reward model. When the reward model is not
889   robust enough, it is easy to over-optimize so the prover overfits to the imperfect proxy reward. This
890   phenomenon is called ‘goodharting’ or ‘reward hacking’ (Gao et al., 2023; Skalse et al., 2022). The
891   verifier can learn from the incorrect and correct samples from the same prover.
892   In more details, for each round, we train the prover using PPO with reward (5) until its training
893   accuracy drops below 0.2. Next, we sample balanced correct and incorrect samples on the verifier
894   split of the training data for all prover checkpoints throughout PPO. The samples are then mixed into
895   the verifier training dataset for the next round. We still keep the total number of samples per problem
896   constant for training the verifier in each round.
897   Figure S2 shows the training dynamics. We early stop each round when the prover’s training accuracy
898   tanks below 0.2 on average in the last 80 RL steps. Across verifier sizes 10−4 to 100 of the prover,
899   the number of steps it takes for the prover to over-optimize generally increase. Up to size 10−2 , the
900   larger the verifier is, the fewer number of training rounds it requires to reach perfect goodharting
901   robustness within 8k RL steps. Verifiers of medium size (3 × 10−3 ∼ 10−2 ) benefit most from the
902   iterative goodharting training, where as smaller (≤ 10−3 ) and larger (100 ) verifiers benefit less. We
903   further evaluated the robustness of verifier trained using goodharting reward under attacks from the
904
      SRC reward (3) and the alternative CGC reward (4), and the verifiers’ robustness go up sometimes
      but not always (Figures S9a, S9b). The balanced best-of-n robustness increases (Figure S10).
905
906   However, when we showed the samples from the goodharting training to humans trainers, there
907   is no significant difference in human judge accuracy across rounds (Fig. S8), although Fig. S8 is
908   from a pilot study that has some design flaws (see Appendix K). Overall, our results on the iterative
909   goodharting training show that it increases verifier soundness to a certain extent, but we do not have
910
      enough evidence showing that it improves human legibility.

912   B            F UTURE W ORK
913
914   We studied the setting in which correctness labels are available at training time for all problems. A
915   more realistic scenario is one where not all answers have a trusted final correctness label, in which
916   case the verifier would rely on unsupervised signals such as consistency (Wang et al., 2023) and
917   convincingness (Khan et al., 2024). Unsupervised methods for improving legibility are an important
      future direction.


                                                                      17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Another direction is developing a method to turn an illegible solution into a legible one. The CoTs
919   that the model first uses to solve a problem are not legible to a smaller model per se but we could train
920   a translation model that turns the ‘scratch space’ CoT to a well-written proof. This approach might
921   be favorable over directly training the CoT to be legible since exerting any optimization pressure on
922   the CoT may limit prover performance as well as exacerbate deceptive alignment (i.e., the model
923   hiding its own intent) (Denison et al., 2024; Hubinger et al., 2024).
924
      The training schemes we consider are adaptations of single-turn simultaneous games. With an
925   interactive tool such as ChatGPT, the verifier can interact with the prover in more rounds which
926   should increase the sample efficiency vastly based on complexity theory (Goldwasser et al., 2021).
927   An example of multi-turn prover game is a “mind-changing" game: for binary outcome predictions,
928   the prover maximally tries to convince the verifier that the answer is true / false alternatingly in
929   context. This is similar to the market-making proposal (Hubinger, 2020).
930
      We mainly studied legibility to smaller models in this paper. However, legibility to humans is what
931   we eventually care about, so future work should more systematically study what type of assistance
932   better help humans judge the validity of large model solutions. We expect more extensive human
933   study to be fruitful.

       E    S EPARATION MIGHT INHIBIT PROGRESSION OF CHECKABILITY TRAINING
1135
1136
       A verifier trained to predict correctness of solutions might not in fact learn a generalizable algorithm
1137   for predicting correctness, if other attributes of the solutions are highly correlated with correctness
1138   and are easier to predict.
1139
1140
       In our experiments, all training rounds of the verifier, except the very first one, contain correct and
       incorrect solutions produced by helpful and sneaky provers. Since our training incentivizes helpful
1141
       prover’s solutions to be correct, and sneaky prover’s solutions to be incorrect, correctness of solutions
1142
       in the verifier’s training data strongly correlates with which of the two provers produced that solution.
1143   If the helpful and sneaky provers produce solutions in different easily identifiable formats, and helpful
1144   solutions are mostly correct, while sneaky solutions are mostly incorrect, then training the verifier on
1145   the correctness signal will likely generalize to identify distinctive format features of the solutions
1146   produced by the helpful and sneaky provers.
1147
       Figure S5 presents evidence that in the early rounds of checkability training, helpful and sneaky
1148
       provers are in fact easily separable based on easy to learn features, but grow less separable as the
1149   training progresses. For that figure, we trained a very small discriminator to predict whether a given
1150   sample was produced by the helpful or the sneaky prover on a small balanced dataset of helpful and
1151   sneaky solutions. We trained separate discriminators for checkpoints of the prover spaced every 400
1152   optimization steps of training. The figure shows ROC AUC of that discriminator. In the first round,
1153   ROC AUC reaches close to 1 at the end of the round. For the last round, it stays close to 0.5 for the
1154   whole duration of the round.

       K    D ETAILS ON THE HUMAN LEGIBILITY EVALUATION CAMPAIGN
1567
1568
       Our main human evaluation experiment is as follows. For the training shown in Figure 5, we sample
1569   the last checkpoint helpful prover solutions to the problems from the GSM test dataset. We also
1570   sample from the correctness-only baseline at training step 3k, 7k, as well as the few-shot prover
1571   initialization. We collect up to one correct and up to one incorrect sample from each prover and
1572   each problem, if possible (around one-third of the problems per round have either all correct or all
1573   incorrect solutions among 128 samples). We randomized assignment of those samples to 75 trainers
1574   (making sure each person see balanced correct vs. incorrect samples from each checkpoint), and
1575   asked them to judge the solution correctness within a 45-second time limit per problem. We excluded
1576   all problems that yielded only correct or only incorrect solutions. We also excluded four trainers
1577
       whose performance was statistically indistinguishable from chance from the dataset. We collected
       15983 datapoints. Each trainer spent a maximum of 4 hours working on the task to prevent learning
1578
       and fatigue effects. The instructions are shown in Fig. S14 and Fig. S15 contains an example task.
1579
       The trainers are compensated based on completion, and are not told whether they judged correctly.
1580
1581   We also had an earlier pilot campaign where the we showed sneaky solutions as well as samples from
1582   an Iterative Goodharting training to 10 trainers with 1 minute time limit (instead of 45 seconds) per
1583
       task. In this pilot run, we showed solutions from earlier rounds to the trainers first (they judged the
       samples in the order of R0, R1, R2, etc.) across a span of one month, and hence we suspect that the
1584
       data is affected by learning or fatigue effects. The only plots from this potentially flawed campaign
1585
       are Fig. 7b, S7c and S8.

        ,→  try to do your best to respect the time limits, but if that's
