009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Humans and many animals show remarkably adaptive behavior and can respond
012            differently to the same input depending on their internal goals. The brain not only
013            represents the intermediate abstractions needed to perform a computation but also
014            actively maintains a representation of the computation itself (task abstraction). Such
015            separation of the computation and its abstraction is associated with faster learning,
016
               flexible decision-making, and broad generalization capacity. We investigate if such
               benefits might extend to neural networks trained with task abstractions. For such
017
               benefits to emerge, one needs a task inference mechanism that possesses two crucial
018
               abilities: First, the ability to infer abstract task representations when no longer
019            explicitly provided (task inference), and second, manipulate task representations to
020            adapt to novel problems (task recomposition). To tackle this, we cast task inference
021            as an optimization problem from a variational inference perspective and ground
022            our approach in an expectation-maximization framework. We show that gradients
023            backpropagated through a neural network to a task representation layer are an
024            efficient heuristic to infer current task demands, a process we refer to as gradient-
025            based inference (GBI). Further iterative optimization of the task representation
026            layer allows for recomposing abstractions to adapt to novel situations. Using a toy
027
               example, a novel image classifier, and a language model, we demonstrate that GBI
               provides higher learning efficiency and generalization to novel tasks and limits
028
               forgetting. Moreover, we show that GBI has unique advantages such as preserving
029
               information for uncertainty estimation and detecting out-of-distribution samples.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Cognitive science and neuroscience hold a prominent place for (top-down) abstract task representa-
035   tions in many accounts of advanced cognitive functions in animals, including humans (Niv, 2019).
036   The brain not only represents the intermediate abstractions needed to perform a task but also actively
037   maintains a representation of the task itself (i.e., task abstraction, (Mante et al., 2013; Rikhye et al.,
038   2018; Zhou et al., 2019; Vaidya et al., 2021; Hummos et al., 2022)). Such separation of the computa-
039
      tion and its abstraction is theorized to support learning efficiency, as well as adaptive behavior. First,
      regarding learning efficiency, task abstractions facilitate learning by organizing experiences collected
040
      (Yu et al., 2021). Human participants learn faster once they discover the underlying task structure
041
      (Badre et al., 2010; Collins, 2017; Vaidya et al., 2021; Castañón et al., 2021). In fact, humans have a
042   bias to discover and use such latent task structures even when they do not exist or might hurt their
043   performance (Gaissmaier & Schooler, 2008; Collins, 2017). Second, regarding adaptive behavior,
044   previous work has shown that the brain updates these task abstractions to meet current task demands
045   of the environment and (re)composes them to solve new problems (Miller & Cohen, 2001; Collins &
046   Koechlin, 2012). Indeed, depending on the context, the brain is able to respond to the same sensory
047   input in novel ways by composing an appropriate task abstraction that guides processing of the input
048   (Rikhye et al., 2018; Tafazoli et al., 2024), supporting flexible decision making and generalization to
049   novel situations (Collins & Koechlin, 2012; Vaidya et al., 2021).
050   Traditional artificial neural networks (ANNs) architectures have a static computational graph that
051   entangles input processing with task inference. For task-dependent computations in ANNs, one
052   popular solution is to build task representations into the structure of the network by using modular
053   networks; where each module could represent a given task (Andreas et al., 2016; Kirsch et al., 2018;
      Goyal et al., 2019). Alternatively, one could regularize the weight updates such as to maximize an


                                                          1

054
      orthogonal computational space for each task (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Masse et al., 2018). Our
055   framework is most related to models that add task encoding input layers, which provide information
056   about the current task demands to the network (Yang et al., 2019; Hurtado et al., 2021; Wang &
057   Zhang, 2022; Kumar et al., 2022; 2024).
058
      However, these previous models lack two important task inference features which we unpack in what
059
      follows and motivate our work. First, these models do not propose a mechanism to efficiently identify
060
      these tasks if they appear again in data, and thereby flexibly (re)adapt to the demands of previously
061   encountered task. Second, and perhaps most importantly, these models lack a principled way of
062   recomposing previously learned task abstractions, thereby minimizing the necessity for new learning
063   (i.e. parameters update) to adapt to new situations (Lake et al., 2017). Importantly, neural systems
064   impose a unique constraint on such task inference and recomposition mechanisms. As the neural
065   system ’learns’ through updating parameters, previous values in the task abstractions layer might
066   become outdated. Thereby, the task inference mechanisms must be able to adjust dynamically to
067   changes in neural parameters.
068   To address the challenge of an efficient inference mechanism that can capture the two properties just
069   described, and maintain alignment to neural parameters, we propose Gradient-Based Inference (GBI)
070   of task abstractions. This solution is grounded in variational inference, offering a robust and flexible
071   framework. For an intuitive example of this framework, we consider that during upbringing, we may
072   observe situations where people’s feelings were labeled as sadness or as anxiety. As we interact with
073   others we may try to infer their emotional states relying on subtle cues. We incrementally adjust
074   our conclusions with every cue, moving closer to one emotion or the other, as make predictions and
075
      receive feedback during the interaction. If, by the end of the interaction, both emotions seem equally
      likely, then either the situation is uncertain or that the person is experiencing an emotion outside of
076
      those two. Another promising class of models used iterative optimization in task abstractions space
077
      to discover latent tasks and associate them with an internally generated task abstractions (Butz et al.,
078   2019; Hummos, 2023; Sandbrink et al., 2024). While these models offered a solution for continual
079   learning, they relied on rounds of iterative optimization in task abstractions space, and were based on
080   a limited understanding of of gradient descent dynamics in this space. GBI tackles the computational
081   efficiency by investigating a role for one-step gradients to infer task abstractions. Further, by using
082   pre-defined human-labeled task abstractions we were able to assess the accuracy and uncertainty
083   estimates of gradient-based inference in task abstraction space.
084   Our findings confirm that empirical findings from cognitive science do indeed extend to neural
085   networks. During training, providing task abstractions leads to faster learning (improved data-
086   efficiency), and limits forgetting by inducing task-specific modules in the underlying neural network.
087   During test, with weights frozen, models trained with task abstractions show adequate task inference
088   through GBI, and can be adapted rapidly to produce different responses to the same input with better
089   capacity to generalize. We first show these results in an intuitive contextual switching task for which
090   we know the underlying Bayesian generative model. We then consider image classification datasets
091   starting with MNIST and scaling up to CIFAR-100 (image generation, section 3.2). Further, this
092
      image generation framework allows us to test the capacity for the detection of out-of-distribution
      (OOD) samples and estimating uncertainty. In a third experiment (language experiment, section
093
      3.3), we show that GBI is a domain-general method, and demonstrate several of these results in
094
      language modeling, specifically, task inference, data efficiency, and generalization.

099   Overview. We assume a dataset is equipped with a task abstraction. Conceptually, a task abstraction
100   groups data samples into conceptual categories defined across dataset samples rather than individual
101   data points. Mathematically, we model this by a graphical model where the data point X is generated
102   from a task abstraction Z. Now we use a neural network to model this graphical model with data
103
      X, task abstraction Z and unknown parameter θ, our neural network model has the form of a joint
      likelihood function

      train the neural network by doing gradient descent on − log L(θ; X, Ẑ) with respect to θ.

140
      There are different methods to optimize Z and we discuss two methods we use in this paper, iterative
141
      optimization and one-step gradient update below to obtain gradient-based inference (GBI) estimates
142   over latent variable values.
143
144   Iterative optimization of Z. One method for doing gradient descent on Z is to iteratively optimize
145   z using the gradient ∂L/∂z. We implement iterative optimization using Adam optimizer with a
146   learning rate of 0.01 and L2 regularization scaled by 0.01. This method usually takes many iterations
147   (Marino et al., 2018). Another method is to learn an update rule fϕ (·) that converts the gradient
148   directly into an inferred z ← fϕ (∂L/∂z) (Marino et al., 2018). This method allows for rapid
149   inference during run time but requires training of the update rule (like other meta-learning methods).
150
151   One-step gradient update. We explore an alternative that requires only one pass through the
152   model by updating the gradient only once. Without iteratively optimizing Z through gradient update,
153
      one-step gradient update usually heavily depends on the initialization: because if the initialization
      is far away from the optimal Z, the local gradient information might not be informative of the
154
      global optimum. However, notice that in the expectation step, our goal is to find the probability
155
      distribution of Z, q, such that Eq [log p(X, Z|θ)]+H(q) is maximized. We can consider an alternative
156   objective function fα (q) = Eq [log p(X, Z|θ)] + αH(q) for some α > 0 and let qα∗ be the point that
157   maximizes this alternative objective function. Since entropy is concave, by increasing α we make
158   the function more concave and therefore the gradient is more informative of the actual optimum
159   (Fig 1B). Furthermore, qα∗ will also become closer to the maximal entropy point q̄ = arg maxq H(q).
160   Therefore we can choose our initialization point as the maximal entropy point and output q̂ using our
161   one step gradient update rule:

187   3     E XPERIMENTS
188
189   The experiments are organized as follows. In Section 3.1, we first investigate the properties of GBI
190   on a simple synthetic dataset generated from a ground truth Bayesian model. We show that GBI
191   displays better data-efficiency, generalizes better and forgets less, in addition to being able to pass
192   neural gradients back to the Bayesian model to support Bayesian computations. Section 3.2 assesses
193
      the potential of GBI as a novel image classifier, the GBI ability to generate task samples, and to detect
      OOD samples. Finally, in Section 3.3, we demonstrate the abilities of GBI to recompose previously
194
      learned tasks, and thereby flexibly adapt to novel tasks using a language model.

306   3.2                  E XPERIMENT 2, IMAGE GENERATION : ONE - STEP GRADIENTS FOR ACCURATE TASK
307                        INFERENCE AND OOD DETECTION
308
      We next assess how accurate GBI is in inferring task abstractions in an image generation setup with
309
      more tasks. In addition, we show that GBI has more beneficial properties, such as better OOD
310   detection, and uncertainty estimates that require no calibration.
311
312   We train a convolutional autoencoder to reconstruct images. The decoder gets an additional input of
313
      a one-hot encoding of the image class in addition to the latent embedding from the encoder (Fig 4
      Schematic). We train this structure for 4 epochs using an MSE reconstruction loss (full details in
314
      Appendix C). Confirming the results observed in the toy task, the network receiving additional image
315
      class trains faster compared with baseline autoencoder, (Fig S12).
316
317
      One-step gradients infer task abstractions with sufficient accuracy. At test time, we set the task
318   abstraction (i.e., image class) input z to maximum entropy point and take one gradient step of the
319   reconstruction loss with respect to the class inputs z, and directly interpret the resulting vector to infer
320   the class label. We make two kinds of comparison, first to methods that used gradient-based inference,
321   we refer to them as entropy-based (Wortsman et al., 2020) and norm-based inference(Roy et al., 2024)
322   and we see that GBI outperforms previous gradient-based methods (Table 2, implementation details
323   in appendix C). Second, we compare GBI with four canonical methods to infer class based on the
      same architecture, as follows: (i) a convolutional classifier of the same architecture as the encoder, as

467   4                    C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
468
469   Overall we show that neural networks with access to task abstractions learn faster, forget less, adapt to
470   changes in data distribution, and generalize to new situations. At the same time, we also provide tools
471   for the networks to function independently infer their own task abstractions during testing. These
472
      task abstractions can be provided by humans, a trained teacher model, or inferred from data with no
      supervision (e.g. (Hummos, 2023)). Task-dependent networks require task inference and inferring
473
      an incorrect task impairs function. In animal brains, some computations that are frequently needed
474
      or are critical for survival might be better mapped to the default state of the brain. The brain shows
475   distinct dynamics when not engaged in a specific task, the default mode, and we show previously
476   unexplored connections between feedback signals in this default state and distributional inference
477   signals that can support many probabilistic quantities such as uncertainty and surprise. We see
478   future work addressing the limitation of relying on human-provided task abstractions as opposed to
479   unsupervised discovery of richer multidimensional task representations along dimensions important
480   for adaptation and generalization. Finally, while our method is theoretically motivated, we see future
481   work extending these findings to larger datasets and more real-world applications.

      A.1    A DAPTING LARGE MODELS
737
738   Several methods have been developed for adapting large models to specific tasks without expensive
739   full fine-tuning. Prompt and prefix tuning involve the use of learnable prompts (Lester et al., 2021) or
740   a continuous prefix (Li & Liang, 2021) that guide the model’s outputs. Lower-rank adaptation (Hu
741   et al., 2021) modifies model parameters with a low-rank tensor, learned during adapting the model,
742   while Adapters introduce small, task-specific modules within the network that are trained while the
743   rest of the model remains fixed (Houlsby et al., 2019). Different from these methods, our approach
744
      to contextualizing a generative model proposes providing context signals or inferring them during
      the pretraining of the model. This allows the model to organize knowledge along these dimensions
745
      giving it an inductive bias anticipating variations along the same dimensions.
746
747
748
      A.2    I TERATIVE VARIATIONAL I NFERENCE
749
      Variational inference, a method that recasts inference as an optimization problem (Jordan et al.,
750   1998; Neal & Hinton, 1998), has been instrumental to the development of deep variational models.
751   However, recent work has primarily used the variational formulation for training, relying on fast
752   amortized directed encoders to infer an approximate posterior distribution (Kingma & Welling, 2013;
753   Rezende et al., 2014), thereby avoiding the potentially lengthy optimization loops of taking gradients
754   through the decoder to refine the posterior distribution. To mitigate this, amortized variational
755   inference employs an additional neural network that takes either the gradients from the decoder, or
      the reconstruction errors and updates the approximate posterior distribution with only a few iterations


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      needed (Marino et al., 2018). Our work builds upon these insights, demonstrating that the initial
757   gradient to the latent carries sufficient information to approximate likelihood functions. This reduces
758   the need for multiple model runs or learning an additional model, but keeps iterative refinement as an
759   option for complex samples.
760
761   A.3     Q UANTIFYING U NCERTAINTY
762
763   Uncertainty quantification is a critical aspect of both Bayesian and neural network paradigms, with a
764   rich and extensive history of literature, for review see (Abdar et al., 2021). Many approaches derive
765   from the maximum likelihood interpretation of neural network training (MacKay, 1992), known as
766   Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs). Such models model uncertainty by placing prior distributions
767
      over the model’s weights, with Variational Inference and dropout-based methods (like MCdropout
      and MCdropConnect) serving as practical approximations (Blundell et al., 2015; Graves, 2011; Gal
768
      & Ghahramani, 2016; Srivastava et al., 2014). Ensemble methods, such as Deep Ensembles, improve
769
      predictive performance and provide calibrated uncertainty measures by aggregating predictions of
770   multiple independently trained neural networks (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Lastly, temperature
771   scaling is a post-processing method used to calibrate the model’s softmax outputs, aligning the
772   model’s confidence with its prediction accuracy (Guo et al., 2017).
773
774   A.4     O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION (OOD) D ETECTION
775
776   The role of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is essential for the secure implementation of machine
777   learning systems. This is due to the tendency of models to deliver erroneously confident predictions
778   when faced with data that diverges from the distribution on which they were initially trained. Current
779
      OOD detection methods fall primarily into two classes: discriminator-based either the logit or the
      feature space (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2018) or generation-based approaches which employ either
780
      the disparity in data reconstruction or the estimation of density in latent space (Nalisnick et al.,
781
      2019), with the appealing intuition that generative models capture the data distribution and can
782   detect out of distribution samples. While one might suspect that our method works because it adds
783   a generative objective, recent work showed that several families of generative models might assign
784   a higher probability to out-of-distribution data than in-distribution ((Nalisnick et al., 2019; Fetaya
785   et al., 2020)). Using their rich visual latent spaces, they can generalize easily to other datasets.
786   Different that these models, our model separates computation of visual features into one stream and
787   the task abstractions into another, instead of assessing OOD probability from the visual features, we
788   selectively assess in the task abstraction space.

842   B.1.2     N EURAL M ODEL A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING
843
      We employed the LSTM implementation provided by Pytorch, utilizing an input layer of size [1, 100]
844
      for mapping inputs to the LSTM, which consists of 100 hidden units. In the case of the GBI-LSTM
845   the model received additional inputs from two additional units, the task abstraction units, after passing
846   through a softmax activation function. An output layer of size [100, 2] was used to map to the output’s
847   mean and variance estimates. The model was trained to maximize the log-likelihood of the observed
848   data by predicting a mean and a variance estimate, allowing the model to express a distribution over
849   the next observation.. The final form of the objective derived from the Gaussian PDF has the MSE
850   divided by the output variance. The LSTM was trained using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate
851   of 10−3 . To optimize the context representations, we employed Adam with a learning rate of 10−2
852   and a decay rate of 10−3 .

951   Other gradient-based methods details. First methods is norm-based inference(Roy et al., 2024).
952   These authors use the gradients norm to infer the task. They compute the gradient norm for each
953   possible task label and select the one with the lowest norm. Second, we compared our method to
954   entropy-based gradient inference (EBI), following the work of Wortsman et al. (2020). They run a
955   forward pass based on the superposition of all task labels each weighted by an α value. Subsequently,
956
      they compute the output layer activation entropy, and take a step in α such as to minimize that entropy.
      To perform a one-iteration inference, they select the supermask (or task) with the largest gradient
957
      to minimize the entropy. Given that this method is limited to cross-entropy loss we reframe image
958
      generation as minimizing a cross-entropy pixel-wise classification loss.

1105   We based our language modeling approach on the official PyTorch example for training a word-
1106   level language model, which can be found at https://github.com/pytorch/examples/
1107   tree/main/word_language_model. We largely adopted the original design choices and
1108   parameters, including the usage of a stochastic gradient descent optimizer with a decaying learning
1109
       rate, a sequence length of 35 words and a batch size of 20, gradients clipped to a value of 0.25, input
       layer and output layer dropout rate 0.25, word-level tokenizer, and a log-softmax function at the
1110
       final output layer. We used the LSTM implementation. The tokenizer’s output was passed through
1111
       an embedding layer, followed by concatenation with a latent representation, specifically a one-hot
1112   encoding of the dataset ID. The LSTM output passed through an MLP layer to project the LSTM’s
1113   hidden units back to the number of tokens, and a log-softmax function was applied as the final output
1114   activation.
1115
       Note that we trained the model on 3 datasets at a time and tested generalization on a fourth dataset to
1116
       keep the tokenizer from indexing more than 1 million distinct words. Adding more datasets lead to
1117
       the embedding layer having a massive size that would not fit in a GPU memory.
