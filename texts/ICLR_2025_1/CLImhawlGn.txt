011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Robustness to distribution shift is a necessary property of machine learning models
014            for their safe and effective deployment. However, deep learning models are sus-
015            ceptible to learning spurious features of the in-distribution (ID) training data that
016
               fail to generalise to out-of-distribution (OOD) data. Domain generalisation algo-
               rithms aim to tackle this problem, but recent studies have demonstrated that their
017
               improvement over standard empirical risk minimisation is marginal. We address
018
               this problem for multivariate time series classification (TSC), where it is standard
019            practise to use feature extractor architectures that learn with channel dependence
020            (CD), enabling cross-channel patterns to be learned. Inspired by recent success
021            in time series forecasting, we investigate how channel independence (CI) impacts
022            OOD generalisation in TSC. Our experiments on six time series datasets reveal
023            that ID and OOD features exhibit significantly greater distributional divergence
024            when learned with CD compared to CI. As a consequence, models that learn with
025            CI are more robust to distribution shift, evidenced by smaller generalisation gaps
026            (the difference between ID and OOD performance) across datasets. On datasets
027
               that have a stronger shift, OOD accuracy is substantially higher for CI than CD.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In real-world applications, machine learning models are often required to make predictions on out-
033
      of-distribution (OOD) data that is different from the in-distribution (ID) data they were trained on.
      Models should be robust to distribution shift, maintaining performance on OOD data that is compa-
034
      rable to that on ID data (Taori et al., 2020). Unfortunately, this is not typical and large generalisation
035
      gaps, the difference between OOD and ID performance, are common (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021;
036   Gagnon-Audet et al., 2023). This has been attributed to models learning features of the training data
037   that are spurious; they correlate with labels but do not capture a causal relationship (Geirhos et al.,
038   2020; Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021). A model that relies on spurious features will fail to generalise
039   effectively to OOD data where those features are absent.
040
      In this work, we focus on improving OOD generalisation in time series classification (TSC). TSC
041
      plays an important role in a range of applications, including activity recognition (Zhang et al.,
042   2022a), disease diagnosis and monitoring (Oh et al., 2020), and predictive maintenance (Carvalho
043   et al., 2019). Distribution shift in time series data can arise from inherent intra- and inter-person vari-
044   abilities, as well as differences in recording equipment (Gagnon-Audet et al., 2023). For the safe
045   and effective deployment of machine learning models in real-world TSC applications, robustness
046   under distribution shift is vital.
047
      In tackling this problem, we consider the domain generalisation setting: models are trained on a
048   set of source domains, which form the ID data, and OOD generalisation is then assessed on a set
049   of unseen target domains. Gagnon-Audet et al. (2023) showed that across time series benchmarks,
050   models trained with empirical risk minimisation (ERM) (Vapnik, 1991) exhibit substantial generali-
051   sation gaps, highlighting the need for effective domain generalisation algorithms in this area. How-
052   ever, they also showed that existing algorithms only marginally improve upon the OOD accuracy of
053   ERM. Thus, there remains a clear need for approaches that effectively improve OOD generalisation
      in TSC.

      In many TSC applications, the time series are multivariate, composed of multiple channels of uni-
063
      variate time series. In OOD generalisation research with multivariate time series, it is standard
064   practise to use a feature extractor architecture that learns with channel dependence (CD), where fea-
065   tures are a function of multiple channels (Lu et al., 2023; Gagnon-Audet et al., 2023; Ozyurt et al.,
066   2023; He et al., 2023). The rationale is that richer features of the time series may be learned by
067   considering cross-channel relationships, potentially improving task performance. The alternative is
068   channel independence (CI), where learning is restricted to the individual channel, which has had
069   recent success in time series forecasting (Nie et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Han
070   et al., 2024). In this work, we propose a simple method for implementing CI for multivariate TSC,
071   and investigate how these two types of learning impact OOD generalisation.
072   We find that learning with CD is often detrimental to OOD generalisation, and that learning with CI
073   is a significantly more robust alternative, as shown in Table 1. Using six real-world multivariate time
074   series datasets, we analyse ID classification performance and the distributional divergence between
075   source (ID) and target (OOD) domain features, which bound performance in the target domain (Ben-
076   David et al., 2006; 2010). Our results show that models that learn with CD outperform CI on ID
077   data, but that the features learned exhibit far greater distributional divergence, indicating a tendency
078
      to learn spurious features. On datasets with more severe distribution shifts, the greater robustness of
      CI enables it to vastly outperform CD on OOD data.
079
080   We extend our work by exploring the use of the frequency domain1 , which describes the content
081   of a signal at individual frequencies and has emerged as a means of improving OOD generalisation
082   in TSC (He et al., 2023; Mohapatra et al., 2024), in the context of CD and CI. Our experiments
083   show that frequency domain features exhibit consistently lower distributional divergence than time
084
      domain features, but at the cost of worse ID classification performance. Importantly, this trade-
      off has different implications for OOD generalisation with CD and CI. For CD, where divergence
085
      is high, frequency features improve OOD performance. However, since CI models already show
086
      low divergence with time domain features, frequency features offer no additional benefit. This
087   underscores that strategies improving OOD generalisation in CD models may not be effective for CI
088   models.

091   2       R ELATED W ORK
092
093   Domain generalisation in time series classification. Domain generalisation algorithms learn from
094
      a set of labelled source domains and are evaluated for OOD generalisation on unseen target domains.
      A number of general approaches have been proposed, such as domain invariant learning (Ganin et al.,
095
      2016; Arjovsky et al., 2020), meta-learning (Li et al., 2018) and data augmentation (Volpi et al.,
096
      2018; Li et al., 2021). Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2021) compared domain generalisation algorithms on
097   image classification benchmarks and found that none outperformed ERM. More recently, Gagnon-
098   Audet et al. (2023) performed a similar study for time series tasks with both general and time series-
099   specific domain generalisation algorithms, and similarly found only marginal improvement over
100   ERM. An explanation for this is that although existing algorithms can improve robustness, this is
101   outweighed by an accompanying decrease in ID classification performance (Sener & Koltun, 2022).
102   Distinct from existing methods, our work explores how different ways of learning from multiple
103   channels impacts OOD generalisation.
104   Channel independence in time series forecasting. Recently, CI has shown superior performance
105   over CD in time series forecasting with transformers (Nie et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
106   2024). To explain this, Han et al. (2024) show that models that learn with CI perform worse on
107

      ID data due to reduced learning capacity, but are more robust to distribution shift than models that
109   learn with CD, and that this trade-off favours CI for forecasting. Although relevant to our work, we
110   cannot assume that these results transfer to TSC as the nature of the tasks are different, as are the
111   characteristics of the data. Forecasting datasets typically have sampling frequencies measured in
112   minutes, hours, days or weeks (Han et al., 2024), while TSC applications use sampling frequencies
113   on the order of tens, hundreds, or thousands of samples per second, as evidenced by the datasets
114   used in this paper. Furthermore, their analyses are specific to forecasting, while ours are based on
115   domain adaptation theory, which better aligns with OOD generalisation research.
116   Channel independence in time series classification. CI has been applied to standard (i.e. non-
117   OOD) TSC to an extent. Ruiz et al. (2021) use CI simply as a means of adapting models designed
118   for univariate time series to multivariate data by building an ensemble of univariate classifiers, each
119   trained on a different channel, though this was in the context of non-deep learning models. In deep
120   learning for TSC, Zheng et al. (2014) used a separate convolutional neural network (CNN) for each
121   channel of a time series (though the features from each were fused before classification, meaning the
122   models are not truly independent). However, subsequent state-of-the-art model architectures opted
123
      to learn with CD instead (Fawaz et al., 2019; Foumani et al., 2024). Research on OOD generalisation
      in TSC have all made use of feature extractor architectures that learn with CD (Wilson et al., 2020;
124
      Ragab et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Gagnon-Audet et al., 2023; Ozyurt et al., 2023; He et al., 2023;
125
      Mohapatra et al., 2024). It therefore remains to be seen whether the benefits observed in forecasting
126   from using CI also apply to OOD generalisation in classification.
127
128   Frequency features in time series tasks. Converting a signal from the time domain into the fre-
129
      quency domain has long been popular for improving performance in TSC applications (Lima et al.,
      2019; Saeidi et al., 2021), and more recently in self-supervised representation learning (Yang &
130
      Hong, 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b). Most relevantly, He et al. (2023) used the approach for domain
131
      adaptation, a similar setting to domain generalisation but with unlabelled target domain data avail-
132   able during training. They separately encode the time and frequency domain of time series and
133   concatenate the features for classification, which showed improved OOD performance compared to
134   time features alone. These results were observed when using a feature extractor that learns with CD,
135   hence we investigate whether the same benefits similarly extend to models that learn with CI.

      Frequency features improve OOD generalisation with CD. Figure 4 shows that frequency fea-
      tures consistently yield lower PAD but also lower ID accuracy (except for HAR) compared to time

      features. When time and frequency features are concatenated, ID performance is preserved while
473

475   features improve OOD generalisation in TSC, when the model learns with CD. Comparing with
476   Figure 2, it is important to note that in terms of OOD accuracy and generalisation gap, this strategy
477   is still inferior to using CI with time features on datasets where the distribution shift is significant.
478
      Methods to improve OOD generalisation are architecture-specific. Figure 5 shows the same
479
      trend in ID accuracy and PAD for CI as with CD. However, the effect that this has on OOD perfor-
480   mance is different, with no significant benefit being observed by using frequency features (except
481   on WISDM, where ID performance is improved by using both). Our explanation for this is that the
482   domain divergence for time features is already low, and the drop in ID performance outweighs any
483   gain from further reducing divergence. We do not mean to imply here that frequency features are
484   not useful for improving OOD generalisation when using CI, as other architectures may use those
485   features in a more effective way. But rather we highlight that a strategy that helps to improve OOD
      generalisation for models that learn with CD might not help models that learn with CI.

512   6   C ONCLUSIONS
513
514   In this paper, we have investigated how learning with CI impacts OOD generalisation in multivariate
515   TSC, and compared it with the standard approach of learning with CD. We have shown that CI sig-
516   nificantly improves robustness to distribution shift, as evidenced by smaller feature divergences and
517   generalisation gaps across all the six benchmarks we used. For datasets where the distribution shift
518   is more severe, this improved robustness vastly improves classification performance on OOD data.
519   Below, we present limitations of our work and highlight some potential future research avenues.
520   Learning channel dependencies. The CI approach assumes that learning dependencies between
521   channels is not strictly necessary for good performance. Although our experiments suggest that this
522   assumption is reasonable, it is entirely possible that a dataset might require learning these dependen-
523   cies for effective classification, in which case the approach would fail. Therefore, a natural extension
524   to this research would be to explore how both types of learning might be incorporated into a single
525   solution.
526   Improving performance for CI. Our results on using frequency features show that to improve OOD
527   generalisation further for the channel-wise ensemble, an approach that trades off ID classification
528   performance for robustness will likely not be effective. Instead, approaches will need to either (1)
529   maintain ID classification performance while improving robustness or (2) maintain robustness while
530   improving ID classification performance.
531   Beyond time series classification. Finally, we recognise that our methods and findings are specific
532   to multivariate TSC, but we hope that they might inspire those investigating OOD generalisation
533   in other domains to take a step back and consider all the assumptions and choices, perhaps taken
534   for granted, that could be impacting OOD generalisation. This might help to uncover other simple
535   techniques for improving OOD generalisation.

718   Dval = ∪d∈Etrain Ddval . We use 75% of the data in Dd for training and 25% for validation, strati-
