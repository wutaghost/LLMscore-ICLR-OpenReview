009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Scaling up data and computing has become the norm for pre-training powerful
012            visual encoders. Current algorithms, when scaled up, often require training on large-
013            scale datasets that are unlikely to be object-centric. However, these algorithms
014            were typically developed and validated on the object-centric ImageNet. This
015            discrepancy may suggest sub-optimal scalability and underutilized data potential.
016
               Non-object-centric (NOC) data, with its multiple objects and complex layouts,
               tends to be more information-dense. To better leverage this underlying structure, we
017
               introduce a semantic bottleneck to MIM, which reduces the number of prototypes
018
               to encourage the emergence of objectness at patch-level token representation.
019            Further, cross-view consistency regularization is applied to encourage multiview
020            invariance. Together, this induces semantic object discovery and allows instance
021            discrimination to be applied between object-level features (slots). Our experiments
022            encompass pre-training on object-centric, scene-centric, web-crawled, and ego-
023            centric data. Across all settings, our approach learns transferrable representations
024            and achieves significant improvements over prior work in image recognition, scene
025            understanding, and robot learning evaluations. When scaled up with million-scale
026            datasets, our method also demonstrates superior data efficiency and scalability. We
027
               will make our code and model artifacts publicly available.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Self-supervised representation learning from visual data has seen significant progress, evolving from
032   contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2021) to masked image modeling (MIM) (Bao
033   et al., 2022; He et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022) and hybrid methods (Zhou et al.,
034   2022; Oquab et al., 2024), which have benefited numerous downstream tasks. A key advantage of
035   self-supervised learning is its ability to learn representations from unlabeled data, eliminating the
036   need for human annotations and making it easier to scale up training datasets. Despite this advantage
037   in utilizing diverse types of data, most research has focused on object-centric datasets like ImageNet
038   for model development, leaving large volumes of non-object-centric (NOC) data, such as Open
039
      Images (Kuznetsova et al., 2020), SA-1B (Kirillov et al., 2023), LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022),
      and Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022), underutilized. However, many primary application domains of
040
      self-supervised learning – such as object detection, image segmentation, and robotics – often require
041
      handling NOC data.
042
043   This motivates us to explore the potential of NOC data for self-supervised learning, which is rich
044   in information, offers new opportunities for data scaling, and could bridge the data-domain gap
045
      between self-supervised learning and real-world applications. While some research has investigated
      scene-centric data for self-supervised dense representation learning by developing pixel-level (Xie
046
      et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) and object-level (Hénaff et al., 2021; 2022; Wen
047
      et al., 2022) contrastive learning objectives using learned or handcrafted objectness, these studies have
048   primarily relied on ResNet-based backbones. As a result, it remains unclear how well these methods
049   translate to modern architectures like vision transformers (see Tab. 1). Although one might argue
050   that the state-of-the-art self-supervised model, DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024), already utilizes NOC
051   data with a vision transformer backbone, its success heavily depends on data curation techniques that
052   leverage the object-centric ImageNet dataset to select neighboring data, keeping its data distribution
053   closely tied to object-centric approaches. Our preliminary experiments also suggest unsatisfactory
      results of DINOv2 using the same NOC data setup (Figs. 3 and 5).

      To this end, we begin by conducting a comprehensive evaluation of existing self-supervised learning
055   approaches on four datasets: object-centric (Deng et al., 2009) and non-object-centric (Lin et al.,
056   2014; Changpinyo et al., 2021; Grauman et al., 2022). While the performance of current methods on
057   non-object-centric data is suboptimal from a representation learning perspective, our study reveals that
058   several insights from object-centric learning remain applicable to NOC data. Specifically, cross-view
059   learning (Tian et al., 2020b) encourages semantic learning by enforcing feature invariance to data
060   augmentations, while MIM, suitable for pre-training transformer-based architectures, is particularly
061   effective at capturing fine-grained, low-level representations (see Fig. 1 for visualizations).
062   Motivated by these insights, we propose SlotMIM, a method that repurposes and integrates masked
063   image modeling (MIM) and contrastive learning for effective representation learning from NOC
064   datasets. The core idea of SlotMIM is to group patch-level image tokens into object-level feature
065   abstractions, referred to as “slots”, thereby decomposing NOC data into object-centric slots so that
066   object-centric techniques can be effectively applied. To make patch-level tokens more semantically
067   aware for subsequent grouping, we enhance MIM with cross-view consistency regularization. Addi-
068   tionally, we introduce a semantic bottleneck, which reduces the number of prototypes to encourage
069
      the emergence of semantic and objectness at patch-level token representations (see Fig. 1). Building
      on these semantically enriched patch tokens, we apply attentive pooling over the learned patch-level
070
      features, using prototypes to initialize object representations, thereby grouping patches into object-
071
      level slots and decomposing an image into object representations. Contrastive learning (Chen et al.,
072   2021) is further applied to these slots to promote the discriminativeness of the learned representations.
073   Together, these designs enable us to perform effective representation learning from NOC data.
074
075
      In our experiments, we pretrain on a diverse range of datasets, including object-centric, scene-centric,
      web-crawled, and ego-centric data. We evaluate the pre-trained models on various tasks such as
076
      ImageNet linear probing and fine-tuning, ADE20K semantic segmentation, COCO detection/instance
077
      segmentation, and visuomotor control for robotics. Across all these evaluations, our method con-
078   sistently outperforms existing approaches by a significant margin, showing 1) Data efficiency: It
079   maximizes the utility of available data, reducing the dependency on continually scaling up data
080   collection; 2) Domain adaptability: SlotMIM shows superior adaptability to datasets that are closer to
081   the downstream application domains and potentially richer in information; and 3) Scalability: The
082   method not only performs well at smaller scales but also scales efficiently with increasing data size.
083
      In summary, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
084
085          • We conducted a comprehensive revisiting study across three non-object-centric datasets. Our
086            findings reveal that non-object-centric (NOC) data is rich in information with vast potential,
087            yet it remains underutilized in current approaches (Secs. 3.2 and 3.3).
088
             • We formalize representation learning from NOC data into two key sub-tasks: decomposition
089            and object-centric representation learning. By repurposing established techniques to target
090            these specific sub-tasks, we developed a unified approach that effectively works for both
091            non-object-centric and object-centric data, offering a robust solution that bridges the two
092            domains (Fig. 3).
093          • Our method maximizes data utilization, achieving both data efficiency and excellent scal-
094            ability. This contributes to the field of pre-training by exploring a new avenue for scaling
095            up models using NOC data. At the same time, our approach delivers pre-trained models
096            that are better suited for downstream tasks, including robotics, providing more relevant and
097            effective solutions for real-world applications (Secs. 3.4 and 3.5).

106   networks parameterized by θ and ξ respectively. Let zθ = fθ (x) and zξ = fξ (x) denote the
107   embeddings produced by the student and teacher networks (we omit the projector for simplicity).
      The cluster assignments are computed as pθ (x) = softmax(zθ · C/τ ), where C = {cc }C    c=1 are the

120   Slot attention (Locatello et al., 2020) is a variant of cross-attention that normalizes attention scores
121
      on the query side instead of the key side, introducing competition between queries and encouraging
      them to focus on different parts of the input. Our approach performs attentive pooling on patch
122
      embeddings according to their clustering assignments, sharing high-level intuition with slot attention
123
      if viewing the prototypes C as queries and patch embeddings zθ,i = fθ (xi ) as keys. We thus follow
124   tradition and call the pooled object features slots – prototypes adapted to image patches.

      High-level intuition. We decouple self-supervised learning on non-object-centric data into two
      subtasks: 1) learning to group image patches into objects (or stuff); and 2) learning to discriminate
148
      objects as previous works have done on object-centric data. The major challenge here is unsupervised
149
      object discovery, which we find could emerge from iBOT with a smaller number of prototypes.
150
151   Representation bottleneck induces objectness from iBOT. We first investigate the prototypes
152   of iBOT, which is a set of embeddings C = {cc }C       c=1 that clusters image patches into C clusters
153
      and assigns each patch token a soft one-hot encoding pθ (xi ) identifying its clustering assignment.
      Conventionally, C is set to be 8192 to capture fine-grained patterns, which is good for learning
154
      representations. But in our case, the role of representation learning would be taken by another
155
      objective (contrastive learning between slots) and the prototypes are designated to focus on object
156   discovery. We find a much smaller C, e.g., 512 for COCO, would suit this goal better because it
157   can build a very compact information bottleneck that forces the model to learn highly compositional
158   concepts – objects. As shown in Fig. 1a, the clusters discovered by iBOT are very fine-grained (2nd
159   row), and objectness emerges if a small vocabulary is used (3rd row). However, these patterns still
160   lack semantic meaning and could split the same object into multiple parts. Also, it remains hard to
161   match discovered objects between views as their semantics vary a lot despite having the same token
      (Fig. 1b, left). Both issues call for a set of semantic-level prototypes.

      Different scaling behaviors of OC/SC vs. ego-centric data. In object manipulation tasks as shown
453
      in Fig. 8, scaling scene-centric and object-centric data to the million level can lead to performance
454   drops for methods like MAE, DINO, and iBOT. We hypothesize that self-supervised representation
455   learning, including MIM, aims to learn invariance, where the feature extractor pulls images with
456   similar visual content together in the embedding space, compressing the visual data. However, scaling
457   up data may result in over-compression, causing performance drops in visuomotor control tasks.
458
      By contrast, using ego-centric data for pre-training, MAE (blue line) and SlotMIM (red line) show
459
      positive data scaling effects. Unlike SC/OC data from vast Internet sources, ego-centric images
460   are sampled from consecutive human videos that share contextual backgrounds or scenarios. The
461   ego-centric data are among daily scenarios such as household, outdoor, workplace, and leisure etc.
462   that are contextually similar to the robot manipulation scenarios (Grauman et al., 2022). Thus,
463   invariance learning in ego-centric data tends to focus more on the differences within the same video or
464   scenario, particularly in the foreground objects. This focus is critical for robot manipulation learning,
465   as it requires effective interaction with these foreground objects.
466   SlotMIM is more data efficient in leveraging ego-centric data. Compared to general-purpose
467   pre-trained models and state-of-the-art (SoTA) robot learning methods (e.g., MVP (Radosavovic
468   et al., 2023) and VC-1 (Majumdar et al., 2023)), we demonstrate that SlotMIM (dark red ◆ line),
469   pre-trained with just 241K data samples, can surpass prior methods that utilized over 1 million
470   samples. When scaled to 1 million ego-centric data, it achieves the highest success rates compared to
471   all other methods in the figure.

501   4   R ELATED WORK
502
503   Self-supervised representation learning. Self-supervised representation learning aims to extract
504   transferrable features from unlabeled data (Tian et al., 2020a; Caron et al., 2018; 2020; 2021; Asano
505   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020). Two primary approaches have emerged: contrastive learning (Tian
506   et al., 2020a; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020), which learns by comparing positive and negative
507
      examples, and masked image modeling (He et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022), which reconstructs masked
      regions of images. While these methods have shown success, they’ve primarily been tested on object-
508
      centric datasets like ImageNet-1K. Our study extends this by exploring self-supervised learning on
509
      large-scale non-object-centric datasets, demonstrating superior data efficiency compared to previous
510   pre-training methods across various downstream applications. Additionally, we provide insights into
511   the scalability and generalizability of these methods across diverse data types.
512
513
      Learning on non-object centric data. Recent works have addressed the challenge of self-supervised
      learning on non-object centric data (Van Gansbeke et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024; Xie et al.,
514
      2021; Wang et al., 2021; Hénaff et al., 2021; 2022). These efforts include dense contrastive learn-
515
      ing approaches (Wang et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021), object-centric methods for dense prediction
516   tasks (Hénaff et al., 2022), and slot-based contrastive learning frameworks (Wen et al., 2022). Ad-
517   ditionally, some methods focus on learning from uncurated datasets (Caron et al., 2019; Tian et al.,
518   2021; Bai et al., 2022). In our work, we decompose object representations to leverage established
519   techniques that enable fine-grained pattern learning through patch-level target design, facilitating
520   effective pre-training.
521
      Scaling vision pre-training. Scaling vision pre-training to larger datasets and models has become
522   a significant focus in recent years (Tian et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2019; Mu et al., 2022; Radford
523   et al., 2021; Dehghani et al., 2023; Gadre et al., 2023; Schuhmann et al., 2022). The creation and use
524   of massive datasets like LVD-142M (Oquab et al., 2024) and LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022)
525   have also played a crucial role. Our method examines how non-object-centric datasets influence
526   data scaling and the transferability of learned representations across downstream tasks, focusing on
527   fine-grained data types: object-centric, scene-centric, ego-centric, and mixed.
528
529
      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This work revisits the use of non-object-centric (NOC) data for self-supervised visual representation
532
      learning. Our comprehensive study demonstrated that NOC data holds immense potential due to
533
      its rich information, which has been largely underutilized. To harness this potential, we formalized
534   learning from NOC data into two sub-tasks: scene decomposition and object-centric representation
535   learning. By repurposing and integrating established techniques to target these sub-tasks, we devel-
536   oped SlotMIM, a unified framework capable of effectively handling both NOC and object-centric data.
537   Through extensive experiments across diverse datasets and downstream tasks, including robotics,
538   we demonstrated the consistent superiority of our approach over existing methods. We hope our
539   promising results open new avenues for scaling self-supervised learning using large volumes of NOC
      data, overcoming the limitations posed by conventional datasets in representation learning.

      A.1.1    P RE - TRAINING
743
744   Architecture. We use ViT-B/16 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as our backbone. The projector g and
745   predictor h are 3-layer MLPs with hidden dimension 4096 and output dimension 256.
746
      Optimization. We use AdamW optimizer with a cosine learning rate schedule, peak learning rate of
747
      1.5e-4, and weight decay of 0.05. The learning rate is linearly ramped up during the first 10 epochs to
748
      its base value scaled with the total batch size: lr = lrbase × batch size/256. We train for 800 epochs
749   on 241K-scale datasets and 400 epochs on 1.28M-scale datasets, with a batch size of 1024 distributed
750   across 8 A100 GPUs. For experiments on 4M-scale datasets, we train 200 epochs.
751
752
      Augmentation and masking. We use the same augmentation strategy as in iBOT (Zhou et al., 2022)
      except not using small local crops. The masking strategy follows (Zhou et al., 2022), with prediction
753
      ratio r uniformly sampled from range [0.3 − 0.2, 0.3 + 0.2].
754
755   Hyperparameters. We set τs = 0.1, τt = 0.07. The number of prototypes is set to 512 for COCO
      and 1024 for other datasets.

      A.1.2    E VALUATION
757
758   Linear probing and fine-tuning on ImageNet-1K. We follow (He et al., 2022) for details on
759   ImageNet evaluations. For linear probing, we insert an extra BatchNorm layer without affine
760   transformation between the features and the linear classifier. We train with batch size 4096, initial
761   learning rate 0.1, and optimize using SGD for 90 epochs. We sweep between [CLS] token and
762
      average pooling and report the best results of pre-trained models. For fine-tuning, We train a linear
      classifier on frozen features for 100 epochs using SGD with momentum 0.9, batch size 1024, and
763
      initial learning rate 1e-3 with cosine decay. For both settings, accuracy is evaluated on a single
764
      224×224 crop.
765
766   Semantic segmentation on ADE20K. We use UperNet Xiao et al. (2018) implemented in MMSeg-
767   mentation following Zhou et al. (2022). Specifically, we fine-tune for 160k iterations with stochastic
768
      gradient descent, with a batch size of 16 and weight decay of 0.0005. The learning rate is 0.01 and
      decays following the poly schedule with power of 0.9 and min lr of 0.0001.
769
770   Object detection and instance segmentation on COCO. COCO object detection and instance
771   segmentation setting also follows Zhou et al. (2022), where the pre-trained model initialized a Cascade
772   Mask R-CNN (Cai & Vasconcelos, 2019). The image scale is [640, 800] during training and 800 at
773   inference. We fine-tune all layers end-to-end on COCO Lin et al. (2014) train2017 set with the
774
      standard 1× schedule and report AP for boxes and masks on the val2017 set.
775   Robot manipulation tasks. Following the setup of Hu et al. (2023), the policy network of behavior
776   cloning includes a LayerNorm layer before the MLP. The policy training involves mini-batches of
777   128 samples, conducted over 20,000 steps with the Adam optimizer set to a learning rate of 0.0001.
778   For each pre-trained vision model and task, we run 3 seeds of BC due to the result’s high variability.
779   One-image observation for its comparable performance to stacks of images and higher computational
780
      efficiency. All tasks and environments use 224×224 RGB images without proprioceptive input.
      No image augmentations, such as random shifts, are applied. We employ attentive pooling, as in
781
      V-Cond (Karamcheti et al., 2023), which is shown to be the better choice than the default [CLS]
782
      embedding head and provides better comparisons between pre-trained frozen visual representations.
