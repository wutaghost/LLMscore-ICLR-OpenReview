011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we introduce LLM-VTP, a visual token pruning method designed
014            to enhance the efficiency of multi-modal video understanding. Large Language
015            Models (LLMs) have shown promising performance in video tasks due to their ex-
016            tended capabilities in comprehending visual modalities. However, the substantial
017            redundancy in video data presents significant computational challenges for LLMs.
018            To address this, we propose a training-free approach that leverages the inherent
019            reasoning abilities of LLMs to selectively prune visual features based on ques-
020            tion tokens, thereby optimizing model efficiency. We validate our method across
021
               multiple-choice, open-ended, text-generation, and long-form benchmarks. Our
               results demonstrate that LLM-VTP can prune 80%-90% of tokens while main-
022
               taining competitive performance. This highlights its superior effectiveness and ef-
023
               ficiency compared to existing pruning methods. The source code will be released
024            to facilitate future research.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023) have
030   significantly advanced multi-modal understanding owing to their exceptional reasoning capabilities
031   and proficiency in following instructions. Within the realm of video understanding, recent studies (Li
032   et al., 2023c; Lin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024; 2023a; Xu et al., 2024a; Wang
033
      et al., 2024a) have capitalized on the use of pre-trained LLMs as foundational models to address
      video question-answering tasks. However, the redundancy inherent in video content can lead to sig-
034
      nificant computational expenses for LLMs due to the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms.
035
      Consequently, effectively reducing the number of video tokens while preserving model performance
036   emerges as an intriguing area of research.
037
038   Previous approaches attempt to address this challenge in various ways. LLaMA-VID (Li et al.,
039
      2023c) proposes compressing each frame into two distinct tokens: context and content tokens. How-
      ever, this method necessitates extensive pretraining and fine-tuning phases, which limits its broader
040
      applicability with readily available video LLMs. Alternatively, LLaVA-PruMerge (Shang et al.,
041
      2024) leverages the correlation between the [CLS] token and patch tokens within CLIP (Radford
042   et al., 2021) to identify important visual tokens. Yet, this approach does not consider the relevance
043   of the selected tokens to the questions being asked, potentially selecting tokens that are unrelated to
044   the task at hand. In a related vein, methods like Look-M (Wan et al., 2024) and Elastic Cache (Liu
045   et al., 2024c) employ Key-Value (KV) cache eviction strategies (Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b)
046   to merge the KV cache for multi-modal inputs. These strategies either prioritize retaining text to-
047   kens or treat visual and textual tokens equally without explicitly identifying the informative visual
048   tokens. Moreover, eviction-based methods require encoding all visual tokens during the prefilling
049   stage, which becomes inefficient when handling long visual sequences.
050   Building on the analysis presented above, we identify three essential criteria that an optimal pruning
051   method for multi-modal video understanding ought to meet: (1) It should ideally be training-free,
052   facilitating smooth integration with readily available models while reducing the need for extensive
053   retraining or fine-tuning. (2) It is crucial to retain visual tokens specifically relevant to the given
      questions. This ensures the model maintains high performance while efficient and mitigates the risk

094

095

097   ial ones, this could serve as an efficient method for video token pruning. To validate this hypothesis,

099   lowest question-to-video attention scores. Our results show that using the attention score from the

101   model, and reducing the pruning ratio to 80% and 70% leads to even better performance, which
102   demonstrates that LLM is a reliable visual token selector.
103   Motivated by these insightful observations, we introduce LLM-VTP, a training-free method de-
104   signed to efficiently prune video tokens by leveraging the discovery ability of LLMs. During the
105   prefilling stage, we utilize the attention weights between question and video tokens within an in-
106   termediate layer to identify and retain the discriminative visual tokens that matter for answering
107   the question, while pruning irrelevant ones. Additionally, for the KV caches stored from previ-
      ous layers, we preserve the selected visual tokens and evict others, thereby reducing computational

      2.1   V IDEO L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
136
137   Recent advancements in Video LLMs focus on enabling LLMs to comprehend video content. These
138   approaches are broadly categorized into training-free methods and training-required methods.
139
      For training-free approaches (Wu, 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b), they directly adapt the
140
      image LLMs for video tasks. FreeVA (Wu, 2024) compacts frame features for LLM processing, and
141   IG-VLM (Kim et al., 2024) merges frames into a single grid, simplifying video-to-image conversion.
142   SF-LLaVA (Xu et al., 2024b) uses a SlowFast (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019) network design, balancing
143   detailed spatial analysis with broad temporal scope efficiently within existing LLM token limits.
144   These methods are ingeniously simple but are limited to handling only brief video clips due to their
145   reliance on the inherent abilities of LLMs to understand temporal sequences.
146
      On the other side, training-required Video LLMs enhance comprehension by training on extensive
147   video datasets. Methods such as Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023), Video-LLaVA (Lin et al.,
148   2023), and PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024a) build upon Image LLMs through video-specific tuning,
149   showing significant improvement in complex video understanding. Other approaches, including
150   VideoChat2 and technologies like VILA (Lin et al., 2024), Tarsier Wang et al. (2024a), Chat-
151   UniVi (Jin et al., 2024), and LLaMA-VID (Li et al., 2023c), introduce efficient token usage, ad-
152   vanced training protocols, or vision-audio understanding, pushing the boundary of what’s achievable
153   in video content analysis.
154   Unlike the methods mentioned above, LLM-VTP aims to enhance the efficiency of existing video
155   LLMs without additional training, which can be applied to training-free or training-required meth-
156   ods.

158   2.2   V ISUAL T OKEN P RUNING
159
160   Due to the quadratic computational complexity inherent in attention mechanisms, optimizing effi-
161   ciency through token pruning becomes essential. This optimization highlights a crucial distinction
      between methods designed for vision-centric and multi-modal tasks.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      DynamicViT (Rao et al., 2021) employs a prediction module to selectively prune less important to-
163   kens, thereby streamlining the model’s efficiency in processing visual data. Similarly, FastViT (Vasu
164   et al., 2023) reduces architectural complexity and memory demands through a novel token mixing
165   operation, catering specifically to vision-only models. Further contributing to this domain, Token
166   Merging (ToMe) (Bolya et al., 2023) merges tokens via token matching, while SPViT (Kong et al.,
167   2022) introduces a method for softly aggregating redundant tokens into a single ‘package token’,
168   efficiently preserving essential information while minimizing computational load.
169   Shifting focus from vision-centric methods, LLaVA-Prumerge prunes visual tokens within multi-
170   modal tasks. By applying an adaptive token reduction strategy through PruMerge, which leverages
171   the inherent attention characteristics of CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), LLaVA-Prumerge enhances the
172   efficiency of multi-modal understanding.
173
      However, previous methods primarily focus on directly reducing visual tokens without incorporating
174
      textual information for guidance, which may be suboptimal for multi-modal understanding. In con-
175   trast, this paper introduces LLM-VTP, which leverages the intrinsic capability of LLMs to identify
176   relevant video tokens for answering questions. This approach effectively enhances model efficiency
177   while maintaining performance.

301   4.1     DATASETS AND E VALUATION M ETRICS
302
303   Multi-Choice VideoQA. MVbench (Li et al., 2024) encompasses 20 temporally challenging tasks
304   that cannot be addressed using a single frame. Each task includes 200 test samples, formatted
305   as multiple-choice VideoQA. These samples require the model to choose the correct answer from
306   several provided options.
307   Open-Ended VideoQA. We evaluate our models on four open-ended VideoQA benchmarks:
308   MSVD-QA (Chen & Dolan, 2011), MSRVTT-QA (Xu et al., 2016), ActivityNet-QA (Yu et al.,
309   2019), and TGIF-QA (Jang et al., 2017). To assess the open-ended answers, we utilize
310   GPT-3.5-Turbo-0125 to evaluate both the accuracy (true/false) and quality (scored from 0 to
311   5) of the responses. The ground truths for these questions are typically single-word answers.
312   Text Generation. VideoChatGPT-Bench, introduced by (Maaz et al., 2023), focuses on five aspects:
313   Correctness of Information (CI), Detail Orientation (DO), Contextual Understanding (CU), Tempo-
314   ral Understanding (TU), and Consistency (CO). The answers for VCGBench are more extensive
315   than those for open-ended benchmarks. For evaluation, we also use GPT-3.5-Turbo-0125 for
316   scoring.
317   We conduct evaluations of our models using two well-regarded benchmarks for long-form video
318   benchmarks: Video-MME (Fu et al., 2024) and Egoschema (Mangalam et al., 2023). In these eval-
319   uations, the models are tasked with selecting the correct answer from multiple-choice options.

      comparison, i.e., LLaVA-PruMerge (Shang et al., 2024) and Look-M (Wan et al., 2024). LLaVA-

      PruMerge leverages the sparsification of attention scores within CLIP to identify critical tokens
325   and utilizes an outlier detection method to adaptively determine the appropriate pruning ratio. In

333   All experiments are conducted using NVIDIA A100 GPUs with 40GB memory. We implement the

335   et al., 2024a) and Tasier (Wang et al., 2024a). Regarding the pruning ratio settings, LLaVA-

      and Look-M. For the multi-choice benchmark, we set the token selection ratio α to 0.1 and the at-

338
      based on Tasier. For Look-M, these configurations are roughly equivalent to setting both the recent
339
      and important ratios to 0.05 and 0.1 for the PLLaVA-based and Tarsier-based models, respectively.

342   PLLaVA, and to 0.2 and 15 for LLM-VTP based on Tarsier. Meanwhile, the recent and important
343   ratios are set to 0.1 for Look-M on these benchmarks.

532
533
      In this paper, we attempt to mitigate the significant computational overhead caused by video re-
534   dundancy by leveraging the reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Specifically, we visualize the attention
535   scores between question tokens and video frames, finding that question tokens effectively attend
536   to visual signals relevant to the question content. Utilizing this intriguing observation, we design
537   LLM-VTP, which leverages the ability of LLMs to identify useful visual signals for pruning visual
538   features. We combine LLM-VTP with two video LLMs and conduct experiments on multiple video
539   understanding tasks, thoroughly validating that this method can effectively reduce the number of
      tokens without compromising model performance.

676   Impacts of Token Merge. Compared to other token pruning methods, PruMerge and Look-M, we
677   do not merge the pruned tokens. To investigate the impact of design, we incorporate the pivotal
678   merge strategy proposed by Look-M into our method, as shown in the Tab. 4. We find that this does
679   not improve our model performance, which indicates that we have already identified the valuable to-
680
      kens. In this scenario, merging the less informative tokens actually negatively impacts performance.
681   Where to rank the tokens? In Tab. 5, we investigate the impact of ranking token importance
682   within different scopes. We compare ranking across the whole sequence with ranking within indi-
683   vidual frames and find that the latter performs better. By analyzing the tokens selected by these two
684   methods, we discover that due to the decay of position weights over distance (Su et al., 2024), the
685   approach of ranking within the entire sequence tends to favor tokens from the last frame. This does
686
      not effectively reflect the relevance of the question tokens.
687   Impacts of pooling methods applied on attention heads. When handling multiple attention heads,
688   we try different pooling methods, as shown in Tab. 6. We find that using max pooling yields the best
689   results because it retains the most significant responses.
690   Experiments on Long-form Benchmarks. We conduct experiments on two popular long video
691   datasets, VideoMME (Fu et al., 2024) and EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2023), and report the re-
692   sults in Tab. 7. For VideoMME, we follow the protocol that excludes the use of subtitles. Our
693   experiments use two models, PLLaVA and Tarsier, and compare their accuracy against other meth-
694   ods. In our approach, we set the pruned layer M to 10 and the retrained ratio α to 0.1 for both
695   models. Similarly, the retrained ratio for Look-M is set to 0.1, while PruMerge+ employs its adap-
696   tive compression method, leading to an average retrained ratio of 0.36. The results indicate that our
697   method most effectively preserves the original model’s performance on these datasets compared to
