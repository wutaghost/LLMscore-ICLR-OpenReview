011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Transformer-based large language models (LLMs) play a vital role in various NLP
014            tasks, but the internal neurons are rather functioning in a black box style. In this
015            work, we introduce the Neuron Predictability Lens (NPL), an analytical frame-
016
               work that focuses on the way neurons work within feed-forward networks (FFNs).
               NPL is useful in understanding and analyzing transformer-based LLMs. Based on
017
               this framework, we conduct experiments on LLaMA-2 and GPT-J. Firstly, we
018
               show that neuron activations are predictable and for the first time we introduce
019            the concept of Neuron Predictability. Secondly, we apply NPL to both global and
020            local analysis. For global analysis, we investigate how FFNs contribute to model
021            behaviors explicitly and implicitly with the aid of NPL. For local analysis, we
022            explore the connection between neuron predictability and neuron interpretability.
023            We examine various functional neurons under NPL and uncover the existence of
024            “background neurons.” With the findings mentioned above, we demonstrate the
025            value of NPL as a novel analytical tool and shed light on its future application on
026            model efficiency and/or effectiveness for improved language modeling.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit human-level proficiency in completing multiple natural
032   language tasks (Vaswani et al., 2017; OpenAI, 2022; Touvron et al., 2023). However, these models
033
      are often regarded as “black boxes” since how their inner neuron function is mysterious (Bommasani
      et al., 2021). Insufficient understanding of LLMs hinders further optimization and responsible de-
034
      ployment of such powerful tools. Thus, paving the way towards a more transparent internal struc-
035
      ture of LLMs becomes increasingly important. Efforts to understand and analyze LLMs range from
036   global examinations of model behaviors to local dissections of specific modules (Luo & Specia,
037   2024). From a global view, researchers delve into comprehending the model’s output and decision-
038   making processes, e.g. detect how the activations in FFN contribute to the logits (Geva et al., 2021).
039   In contrast, the local analysis seeks to unravel the mysteries of specific modules. For example, neu-
040   ron interpretability research has dived into the relationship between individual neurons and specific
041   linguistic tasks or functions (Dai et al., 2022a). Bridging these two perspectives, our work introduces
042   a novel concept called Neuron Predictability Lens, which potentially encapsulates both the broader
043   granularity and the finer granularity of LLM analysis with the discovery of Neuron Predictability.
044
      Figure 1 is an illustration of the concept.
045   Neuron Predictability Lens (NPL) is an analytical framework devised to provide a new perspective
046   for understanding the behavior of transformer-based LLMs. NPL is performed through linear trans-
047   formation, mapping FFN neurons across different layers. This method provides new insights, and
048   renews the interpretability of vast concepts for transformer-based LLMs, such as logits contribution
049   (i.e. the contribution of specific modules to the final logits, same hereafter) and neuron activation.
050   To make it clearer, we use neuron activation to denote the intermediate representation of the FFN
051   module. We establish mappings between different layers and project activations in either a forward
052   or a backward direction. We need to answer a natural research question (RQ1): can neuron acti-
053   vation be predicted? To answer this question, we train the neuron mappings across possible layer
      pairs on LLaMA-2 and GPT-J. Our experiments demonstrate that neuron activations are indeed pre-

078   We utilize NPL to analyze LLMs in both global and local ways, unveiling findings in both branches:

080

087   specialized neurons. From this analysis, we uncover “background neurons” – neurons that are vital
088   to model performance, easy to predict, but do not exhibit explicit functional roles.
089   Overall, our contributions are as follows:
090
091   • The NPL framework: we propose and verify the effectiveness of Neuron Predictability Lens to
092
      analyze transformer-based LLMs;
093   • Findings from the global analysis with NPL: we find that shallow layers have more implicit logits
094   contributions while deep ones have more explicit contributions; neurons with higher predictability
095   contribute more to the final logits;
096   • Findings from the local analysis with NPL: our proposed method measures the predictability of
097   functional neurons, and uncovers the existence of “background neurons.”

229   4     G LOBAL ANALYSIS : ANALYZING THE LOGITS CONTRIBUTION OF
230         PREDICTED NEURONS
231
232   This is our first step to answer RQ2. Through NPL, we evaluate how the predicted activations affect
233   the model performance, which both provides a global LLM analysis and validates the effectiveness
234   of NPL. Specifically, we substitute the actual neuron activations with those predicted by the NPL
235   Mapping. Given a mapping Mi→j where the activation of layer i serves as the stimulus for predicting
236
      the response in layer j, we substitute the authentic activations in layer j with the predicted ones.
237
238   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
239
240
      Recalling Equation 2, due to the existence of residual connection, the final representation of the
      model hf inal can be viewed as a summation of the outputs from the FFN and MHSA modules of
241
      each layer. This final representation is normalized and projected to the “logits” over vocabulary
242
      via the language modeling head. We refer to the FFN output ml as the explicit contribution from
243
      the FFNl to the logits as ml is explicitly added to the final output hf inal . There is also an implicit
244
      contribution from FFNl , as deeper layer representations are computed based on the outputs of those
245
      shallower layers. Therefore, ml also contributes to hf inal implicitly by involving the computation
246   of all its subsequent layers.
247
248   In this section, we conduct substitution experiments to study how the predicted neuron activations
249
      affect the explicit and implicit contributions. Figure 3 is the visualization of the two settings. As
      forward propagation proceeds from shallow layers to deep ones, we only consider the mapping
250
      Mi→j , i.e. when i < j (if not specified, we set i = j − 1 in the rest of the paper).
251
252   We split all layers into four chunks and enumerate them from shallow to deep. In each trial, we
253   substitute neuron activations of one chunk of layers. For each setting, the following three types of
254   mappings are compared with NPL Mapping.
255   • Random Mapping substitutes actual activations with activations obtained through a randomized
256   mapping. In the random mapping, we run the evaluation 5 times and compute the average.
257
      • Zero Mapping zero-outs actual activations.
258
259   • Identical Mapping substitutes actual activations with activations from its previous layer.
260
261   4.2   R ESULTS AND ANALYSIS
262
263   Table 2 presents the results extracted in various substitution settings in LLaMA-2 and GPT-J. The
264   NPL Mapping exerts the most negligible impact on the logits, corroborating that neuron predictabil-
265   ity indeed captures information intrinsically linked to the model’s capabilities. In contrast, the Ran-
266
      dom Mapping and Zero Mapping either introduce meaningless noise or remove the activations within
      certain chunks, both resulting in a substantial perturbation of the logits.
267
268   There is a strong correlation between the depth of substituted layers and the resultant effect. Sub-
269   stituting activations within the two middle chunks causes a relatively minor impact on the final
      logits, whereas substitution at either the bottom or top chunks introduces a more pronounced effect.

405   5.2   D IFFICULTY- SENSITIVE NEURONS Ploss
406
      Neuron prediction could be easily associated with token prediction, which leads our investigation
407
      toward difficulty-sensitive neurons. We found that the activations of certain neurons are correlated
408
      with the performance of the causal language modeling objective. Tokens that are hard to predict,
409   manifesting in high cross-entropy loss (denoted as hard tokens), tend to activate specific neurons.
410   Similarly, there are the easy tokens which are activated in response to tokens that are easy to predict.
411
412
      We filter the tokens based on their cross-entropy loss, and then get the hard and easy tokens. As
      depicted in Fig. 5 (c), difficulty-sensitive neurons exhibit significantly higher scores than the random
413
      baseline for all the conditions, which means they are harder to predict. Furthermore, it is observable
414
      that the neurons corresponding to hard tokens exhibits greater difficulty when being predicted.
415
416   The identification of difficulty-sensitive neurons is intriguing. These functional neurons are harder
417   to predict, and those responding to hard tokens are even harder to predict. As hard tokens represent
418
      greater challenges for the model, their information flow within the model would be complicated,
      making their prediction more difficult. We also examined the hardest and easiest 10000 tokens in
419
      the same experiment (See App. B.4).

          5.4                         “BACKGROUND ” NEURONS
448
449      In the above examination, all neurons associated with specific functionalities exhibit high difficulty
450      scores, indicating that they are hard to predict. Conversely, we are also interested in those more
451      predictable neurons. To this end, we set the random baseline as a threshold of the difficulty score
452      and get those neurons with higher predictability. As depicted in Figure 5 (e), a substantial proportion
453      of neurons (ranging from 40% to 80%) fall into this category. Appendix B.6 provides perplexities
454      after replacing the background neurons and the layer-wise statistics of background neurons. This
455      suggests that a majority of the neurons within FFNs are relatively easy to predict. The precise
456      function of these neurons is challenging to define, but their critical importance is evident based on
457
         the results after masking them out.
458      The masking experiment is conducted in the same setting described in Section 4 and the results
459      are averaged over four chunks. The perplexity after masking those background neurons is 170.80.
460      Compared with the original perplexity of 33.08, the performance decays significantly after masking
461      the background neurons. Masking the same amount of random neurons causes less severe degrada-
462      tion and extracts a perplexity of 55.53. The mysterious nature of background neurons shows that a
463
         considerable proportion of neurons contribute to model behavior while “working in the dark.” This
         prompts us to rethink how we credit the success of the model’s performance.

          6                       D ISCUSSIONS AND IMPLICATIONS
474
475
476
         The above analysis reveals NPL as an effective analytical tool for LLMs. Here, we discuss the
         following applications and implications.
477
478      First, NPL would help inference acceleration by short-cutting transformers. Previous research has
479      investigated inference acceleration by establishing linear shortcuts across transformer blocks (Din
480      et al., 2023). NPL bears a resemblance to these efforts by predicting the neuron activations in FFN
481      without significant performance drop, suggesting the potential of NPL as a promising avenue for
482
         bypassing the complicated computations of vanilla transformers.
483      Second, NPL can uncover causal relationships between neuron activations across different layers.
484      For instance, if a later-layer neuron’s activation is precisely predictable from early-layer neuron
485      activations, we can infer causal links between these neurons. By integrating NPL with existing
         circuit discovery techniques, we can enhance the mechanism analysis of LLMs.


                                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Third, NPL encourages us to rethink the role of FFN. Some researchers posit FFN functions as
487   key-value memories (Geva et al., 2021), while others suggest it projects hidden representations onto
488   a distribution over the output vocabulary, thus amplifying the predicted probability of some words
489   while diminishing that of others (Geva et al., 2022; Belrose et al., 2023; Katz & Belinkov, 2023).
490   Our investigation reveals these arguments to be incomplete. FFNs at various depths play diverse
491   roles, and even within the same layer, individual neurons exhibit varied behaviors.

      7   R ELATED WORK
495
496
      Though the proposal of NPL is initial, its implementations are built on previous research. As ana-
      lyzing transformers has attracted much attention in recent years, researchers have delved into this
497
      intricate structure with multiple methods. Following Luo & Specia (2024), we roughly categorize
498
      transformer analysis into two streams: local analysis, which delves into the intricacies of individual
499   transformer components, and global analysis, which seeks a holistic understanding of the behaviors
500   and capabilities of the model.
501
502
      Among local analysis, Dai et al. (2022a) shed light on the storage of knowledge within model pa-
      rameters by identifying specific “knowledge neurons.” Similarly, Voita et al. (2023) uncover a range
503
      of functional neurons characterized by regular activation patterns. They target individual neurons
504
      and experiment on their functionalities. Global analysis encompasses a variety of approaches, in-
505   cluding probing techniques (Rogers et al., 2020; Petroni et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023), mechanistic
506   interpretability (Elhage et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023), and more. Among these, the “Vocabulary
507   lens,” which projects weights and activations onto the vocabulary space, is a trending analytical
508   tool (Geva et al., 2021). This lens allows researchers to explore how different modules and inputs
509   contribute to model performance(Belrose et al., 2023; Ram et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2023). Another
510   direction is to analyze transformers through simple mappings between modules. For example, Dar
511   et al. (2023) learn to project parameters into a shared embedding space, while Din et al. (2023) ex-
512   plore linear shortcuts between layers, which bears conceptual relevance to our approach. Different
513
      from previous studies, our introduction of the neuron predictability lens encompasses both the local
      and global facets of transformer analysis.
514
515
516   8   C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this work, we discover the predictability of neuron activations and present the Neuron Predictabil-
519   ity Lens (NPL) as a powerful analytical framework for examining transformer-based LLMs.
520
      Through extensive experiments, the predictability of neuron activations has been demonstrated and
521
      significant insights have been uncovered into the contributions of different layers to the final logits.
522   The global analysis highlights the distinct roles of shallow and deep layers, while the local analysis
523   in this paper sheds light on the existence and importance of “background neurons” in LLMs.
524
525
      The contribution of our NPL framework in analyzing LLMs is unique. Moving beyond traditional
      approaches, we offer a new perspective for this research line. The NPL framework has the potential
526
      to uncover the predictable relations within the LLM models, providing a new lens for LLM studies.

      A.1    E VALUATION OF PREDICTABILITY USING MSE
708
709   To rethink the rationality of MSE evaluation, we propose and test the following hypotheses toward
710   a sounder evaluation of predictability using MSE:
711
712          • Hypothesis A: Finer Granularity Our current version of MSE calculates the averaging
713            mean-squared error of all neurons in the layer, which is rather coarse-grained. In Sec. 4.3,
714            we have shown that neurons in the same layer have diverse predictability. Therefore, a
715
               proper metric should capture such characteristics.
716          • Hypothesis B: Magnitude-invariance The metric should be magnitude-invariant, espe-
717            cially in a scenario where the neuron activations are small.
718
      Then we evaluate the effectiveness of the above hypotheses. We calculate the neuron-wise Relative
719   Squared Error (RSE) for comparison.
720
721   We conduct substitution experiments to see how these metrics fit the model performance. Specifi-
722
      cally, we filter top/bottom neurons with metrics and then substitute them with zero/predicted activa-
      tion. The perplexity scores are shown in Table 3.
723
724
725            Table 3: Perplexity scores of the substitution experiments for MSE interpretation.
726                                        Top 1000                                Bottom 1000
727           Metrics        Mean     PPL. (Zero)   PPL. (Predicted)   Mean     PPL.(Zero)   PPL. (Predicted)
728
729       Tensor-Wise MSE     —           —               —             —          —               —

733   From the experiment, we can infer that (a) hypothesis A is correct because there exists a significant
734   difference between the performance of high/low MSE/RSE neurons; (b) hypothesis B is wrong
735   because neuron-wise MSE aligns better with the performance than RSE.
736
      Then, why do magnitude-invariant metrics fail to evaluate predictability?
737
738   Magnitude-invariant metrics normalize the difference between predicted values and the actual ones
739   so that large and small values can have a ”fair” comparison. For example, for two variables x , y we
740
      have xactual = [100, 120] , xpredict = [90, 125] , yactual = [0.1, 0.12] , ypredict = [0.09, 0.125].
      The two variables have the same RSE. Behind these metrics, there lies a presupposed assumption:
741
      large values can tolerate larger deviations while small values bear less.
742
743   The predictability is not suitable to be measured with magnitude-invariant metrics because the neu-
744   ron activation does not follow the assumption. There is no evident correlation between the magni-
745   tude of neuron activation and robustness against deviations. Thus, a small RSE does not guarantee
746
      a better approximation in terms of performance. This means our evaluation with MSE is effective
      when detecting the predictability.

900   B.7   A DDITIONAL DETAILS ON OUTLIER NEURONS
901
902   Outlier phenomenon has been observed across various LLMs (Puccetti et al., 2022). This phe-
903   nomenon refers to the persistent emergence of extreme values within the models’ activations and
904
      weights which, though comprising less than 0.1% of the values, can exceed the magnitude of other
      values by several hundredfold and are thus termed “outliers”.
905
906   For LLaMA-2-7b, we find the 7890-th neuron of layer 2 (shorted as L2.7890) to be an outlier.
907   We observe that the occurrence of outliers is associated with meaningless tokens, such as <SOS>,
908   <UNK>. As for neuron predictability, outlier neurons are extremely hard to predict.

915   validate this, we conduct experiments applying NPL between the LLaMA-2-7b and LLaMA-2-13b
916   models. Figure 9 shows that the neuron mapping across models is learnable. Our observations
917   reveal a strong correlation between the layers of the two models, with the most effective mappings
      establishing when layers of similar depth are used to predict each other. Additionally, based on the

        C.2                                                            C ONTEXT LENGTH AFFECTS NEURON PREDICTABILITY
922
923     We investigate scenarios where tokens are exposed only to a constrained segment of the preceding
924     context. To achieve this, we employ a context window, denoted by w, to limit the range of context
925     accessible to each token. Subsequently, we train multiple NPL mappings for various w values and
926     visualize the differences. As shown in Figure 10, a larger w extends the context scope and also
927     results in increased predictability for neurons in shallower layers, while simultaneously decreasing
928     predictability in deeper layers. We hypothesize that an extended context provides the NPL with
929
        more comprehensive information, aiding in the accurate prediction of neuron activations in shallow
        layers. Contrastingly, the semantics in deeper layers may become too complex to be captured by the
