                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Subgraph counting is a fundamental problem in graph analysis. Motivated by
010
               the practical need to perform graph analytics on subgraphs defined by selected
011            vertices (or edges) rather than the entire graph, as well as privacy concerns, we
012            initiate the study of private range subgraph counting. Given an n-vertex graph G,
013            where each vertex (or edge) has a d-dimensional attribute vector, a pattern graph
014            H, and a set Q of range queries q, our goal is to count the occurrences of H in
015            the subgraph of G induced by vertices (or edges) whose attributes fall within q,
016            all while preserving privacy. We give the first ε-differentially private algorithm
017            for range subgraph counting, achieving near-optimal accuracy (up to a polylog-
018            arithmic factor of n) for constant privacy parameter ε and dimension d, with no
019
               additional computational overhead compared to non-private algorithms. We also
               demonstrate that by relaxing to (ε, δ)-DP, we can achieve smaller additive errors.
020
               Furthermore, our results generalize the subgraph counting results of the partially
021
               dynamic model in (Fichtenberger et al., 2021). Empirical evaluations demonstrate
022            that our algorithm significantly outperforms baseline methods in accuracy while
023            ensuring strong privacy guarantees.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Subgraph counting is essential for understanding the properties of a data graph and has been ex-
029
      tensively studied (Alon et al., 1995; Bera et al., 2021; Björklund et al., 2014; Chiba & Nishizeki,
030
      1985; Curticapean et al., 2017; Assadi et al., 2019; Fichtenberger et al., 2020). Given a host graph
031   G = (V, E) and a pattern graph H, a subgraph of G that is isomorphic to H is called an occurrence
032   of H. The goal of subgraph (or pattern) counting is to determine the number of occurrences of H in
033   G. Subgraph counting is a key graph statistic; for instance, counting triangles and k-stars is crucial
034   for computing the clustering coefficient, which is valuable for evaluating the effectiveness of friend
035   recommendation systems. Counting 4-cycles, closed loops of four nodes, is particularly useful for
036   measuring clustering tendencies in bipartite graphs, such as those found in online dating platforms
037   or mentor-student networks.
038   In many applications, beyond counting subgraphs in the entire graph, we are often interested in
039   counting subgraphs within specific subgraphs. This is driven by practical demands for performing
040   graph analytics on subgraphs relevant to selected vertices (or edges) rather than the entire graph.
041   For instance, in patient networks, we may be interested in counting patterns within the subgraph
042   induced by patients of similar age or geographic location. These subgraphs can be defined based on
043   specific age ranges, geographic areas, or other relevant attributes. In financial networks, counting
044   transaction patterns among entities with similar risk profiles or locations can help identify fraudulent
045
      activities or assess systemic risks within the financial system. Another example involves relational
      event graphs (Bannister et al., 2013). In this context, we are given a graph G = (V, E), where each
046
      edge e ∈ E is associated with a real-valued timestamp. We may wish to count the occurrences of
047
      certain patterns within a specific time range, which corresponds to the subgraph induced by all edges
048   that fall within that time frame.
049
050   Now we formally introduce the Range Subgraph Counting problem that addresses the pattern count-
051
      ing scenarios discussed above.

057   Let H be a fixed, connected pattern graph with O(1) vertices. For each query defined by the interval
058
      q, the goal is to return the number of occurrences of H in Gq . The pattern H is fixed for all queries.
059   Note that the attributes of the vertices may represent factors such as age or location, depending on the
060   practical context. Additionally, an occurrence is only counted if all its vertices are contained within
061   Vq ; any occurrences involving vertices outside of Vq are disregarded. We also study a variant of this
062   problem in the setting where each edge e has an associated real-valued attribute a(e), referred to as
063   the edge-attributed range subgraph counting problem (see Appendix F). Furthermore, we note that
064   our edge-attributed range counting strictly generalize the partially dynamic DP subgraph counting
065   under continual observation as studied in (Fichtenberger et al., 2021). In the partially dynamic
066
      setting, the edge attribute is timestamp and only allow either insertions or deletions of edge. See
      Section 1.1 for more discussions. We remark that Deng et al. (2023b) studied the 1-dimensional
067
      (vertex-attributed) range subgraph counting and listing problems, focusing on optimizing the trade-
068
      off between space and query time.
069
070   While one could release the exact pattern counts in response to each query, it is important to recog-
071   nize that the range subgraph counting algorithm lacks formal privacy guarantees, making it poten-
072
      tially “unsafe” from a privacy perspective.
073   In this work, we approach the range subgraph counting problem from the perspective of differential
074   privacy (DP). DP ensures that, even if there is a one-element difference in the database, the output of
075   the algorithm remains statistically similar (see Definition 1.2). This means that DP algorithms allow
076   for statistical analyses of sensitive individual data while guaranteeing that no specific individual’s
077   information is leaked (Dwork et al., 2006). When DP is applied to graphs, it can be divided into
078
      two types:edge-DP and node-DP. In the former, two adjacent graphs differ only by one edge, while
      in the latter, two adjacent graphs differ by one node and all the neighboring edges. In our work, we
079
      focus on edge-DP. Given two graphs G, G′ with the same set of nodes V (G) = V , we say G, G′ are
080
      neighboring, denoted by G ∼ G′ , if they differ in exactly one edge.
081
      Definition 1.2 (Edge DP (Dwork et al., 2006; Nissim et al., 2007)). Let ε > 0 and δ ∈ [0, 1). A
082
      randomized algorithm A is (ε, δ)-differentially private(DP) if for all events S in the output space of
083   A and all neighboring graph G ∼ G′ ,Pr[A(G) ∈ S] ≤ eε Pr[A(G′ ) ∈ S] + δ. When δ = 0, we
084   say A preserves pure differential privacy (denoted by ε-DP). When 0 < δ < 1, we say A preserves
085   approximate differential privacy.
086
087   While DP has been extensively studied for subgraph counting in the entire host graph (see Sec-
088
      tion 1.1), private algorithms for range subgraph counting remain unexplored. The challenge with
      DP range subgraph counting arises not only from the high sensitivity, which is already present in
089
      standard DP subgraph counting, but also from the increased complexity of the queries. In range
090
      subgraph counting, each query is defined over a specific subgraph induced by a subset of vertices,
091   making the problem more difficult as the algorithm need to handle multiple induced subgraphs effi-
092   ciently while ensuring privacy.
093
094
      Before presenting our main results, we outline a straightforward approach to achieve differential
      privacy (DP) in range subgraph counting: For each query q ∈ Q, compute the induced subgraph
095
      Gq , count the occurrences of the pattern graph H (e.g. triangles), add Laplace noise to the counts,
096
      and return the noisy results. However, this approach has significant drawbacks. Specifically, it
097   results in substantial additive error. The sensitivity of triangle counting in any specific graph Gq is
098   Θ(|Vq |), necessitating Laplace noise of Θ(|Vq |). According to the DP composition theorem
099
                                                                                                  p (Dwork
      et al., 2006), this leads to a cumulative error of O(|Q|n) when aiming for ε-DP (and O( |Q|n) for
100
      (ε, δ)-DP). When |Q| = Ω(n2 ), the resulting error becomes prohibitively large, rendering the results
101   practically unusable. For example, in the case of triangles, where the total number of triangles in
102   a graph is O(n3 ), the excessive error O(n3 ) for ε-DP becomes trivial. Furthermore, we note that
103   range subgraph counting is a nonlinear problem, making it more challenging, and preventing the
104   direct application of previous DP algorithms designed for linear queries. For instance, the sum of
105   the number of triangles in two graphs is not equal to the number of triangles in their union.

      Technical Overview To design DP algorithms for the range subgraph counting problem with small
157
      additive error, we observe that many range queries overlap, making it unnecessary to add noise
158   to each query separately. Our approach maps the graph’s vertices to points in a 2d-dimensional
159   Euclidean space, based on vertex or edge attributes, translating the range subgraph counting problem
160   into estimating the weighted sum of points within corresponding rectangles. Here, the weight of a
161

             Õ(·) hides polylogarithmic factors.

      point reflects the number of occurrences involving the corresponding vertex pair. We employ a range
163   tree data structure (Bentley & Saxe, 1978) to iteratively summarize these weighted sums within
164   chosen ranges, adding Laplace noise to the weights of each node in the tree. To answer a range
165   query, we traverse the tree to find the relevant nodes for the queried range. This approach effectively
166   leverages query correlations, reducing the amount of noise required.
167
      Our work shares similarities with the DP interval (and rectangle) query problem (see, e.g., (Dwork
168
      et al., 2015)), which focuses on reporting the number of points in a specified interval, often solved
169   using a range tree. However, there are several key differences. First, we address edge-DP in graphs,
170   whereas (Dwork et al., 2015) focuses on differential privacy in tabular data, where each row corre-
171   sponds to an individual. Second, unlike point counting, our subgraph counting problem is nonlinear;
172   specifically, the sum of occurrences of a pattern graph in two graphs is not necessarily equal to the
173   number of occurrences in their union. Third, in our setting, a single edge change can affect many
174   mapped points and significantly impact subgraph counts (e.g., one edge may participate in Θ(n)
175   triangles). We address the latter two differences by employing a subgraph projection technique that
176   uniquely maps each occurrence of a pattern graph H to a distinct point in Euclidean space. This
177
      transformation allows us to appropriately apply the rectangle query algorithm to our problem.

180   DP Subgraph Counting The DP subgraph counting problem is a significant topic that has been
181   extensively studied, primarily for the entire graph G. Nissim et al. (2007) improved the utility guar-
182   antees for triangle counting in differential privacy by incorporating instance-specific noise. Karwa
183   et al. (2011) extended the smooth sensitivity approach to k-stars and proposed methods for com-
184
      puting local sensitivity to perform k-triangle counting. Kasiviswanathan et al. (2013) introduced a
      triangle counting algorithm under the node-DP framework. Zhang et al. (2015) developed ladder
185
      functions for various subgraph counting tasks. Nguyen et al. (2023) focused on optimizing run-
186
      time by calculating approximate smooth sensitivity for graphs with certain properties, achieving
187   both privacy and utility while reducing time complexity. Additionally, several studies have exam-
188   ined subgraph counting under the local DP model, such as (Imola et al., 2021; 2022a;b; Eden et al.,
189   2023). (Fichtenberger et al., 2021) studied DP subgraph counting in dynamic model, while our
190   work explores subgraphs induced by vertices or edges whose attributes fall within specified ranges.
191   For Vertex-attribute Range Subgraph Counting, the two problems are fundamentally different and
192   incomparable. In the context of Edge-attribute Range Subgraph Counting, our work generalizes the
193   partially dynamic problem in their work, where their problem becomes a special case of ours when
194   treating edge timestamps as attributes. Instead of focusing on specific pattern graphs like triangles
195
      and k-stars, our approach generalizes to arbitrary constant-size pattern graphs.
196   Differentially Private Range Queries Muthukrishnan and Nikolov (Muthukrishnan & Nikolov,
197   2012) present algorithms for the half-space range counting problem under differential privacy,
198   achieving good approximate accuracy in terms of average squared error. Deng et al. (Deng et al.,
199   2023a) propose an algorithm for counting queries and bottleneck queries on shortest paths while
200   ensuring differential privacy. A closer examination of their model reveals that they effectively ad-
201
      dress a range counting problem on a graph. A cut query on a graph is a specialized form of range
      counting, where the range space includes all possible cuts. The cut query problem is widely studied
202
      in the field of differential privacy, with significant research dedicated to it (Gupta et al., 2010; 2012;
203
      Dalirrooyfard et al., 2024; Blocki et al., 2012; Arora & Upadhyay, 2019; Eliáš et al., 2020).

250   3.1   T HE ALGORITHM
251
252   Overview of the algorithm and some definitions Our algorithm for the case d = 1 consists of
253   three steps:
254   (1) Map all the vertices in the input graph G to points in a two-dimensional Euclidean space, where
255   each point corresponds to a rank pair, which is a point (a, b) ∈ [n]2 such that a and b represent
256   the ranks of some vertices based on their attribute value and index order (see Algorithm 1). We
257   construct a weight vector w for these points, with the weight of each point representing the number
258   of occurrences that are “registered” at the corresponding rank pair (see P ROJ(G, H) in Algorithm 1).
259   (2) Build a range tree on the mapped points and the weight vector w such that each leaf node contains
260   the weight corresponding to its point, while each internal node contains the sum of the weights of
261   its children and bound information. Then, add Laplace noise to the weight of each node in the tree
262   (see T REE C ONST(w, ε, GSfH ) in Algorithm 2).
263
      (3) For any specified query q, traverse the tree to find the corresponding nodes and report their
264
      associated weights (see Q UERY(G, H, Q, ε) in Algorithm 3).
265

267   initial label of a vertex and use s(u) to represent the rank a vertex after the second step of P ROJ.

      5   C ONCLUSION
533
534   We give the first algorithm for the differentially private range subgraph counting problem that
535   achieves nearly optimal additive error for any constant dimension d and a constant privacy parameter
536   ε. Our approach establishes a connection between subgraph counting and the range tree technique
537   within the DP framework. Further exploration of instance-dependent error bounds for private range
538   subgraph counting would be interesting. Another natural question is how to design an algorithm
539   that ensures the additive error remains non-trivial, if the vertex attributes are high-dimensional (for
      example, d = Ω(log n)).

828
829   Given a query q = [ℓ, r], we can prove that only at most log2 n tree node weights of Ty are needed
830   to compute the result.
831
      First, consider the tree Tx , which represents the first dimension (the rank of vertex pairs based on
832   their first vertex). Our task is to select the tree nodes that cover the range [ℓ, n]. In the binary range
833   tree structure, once a parent node is selected, its child nodes are not selected since the parent already
834   covers the required range. This simplifies the problem to identifying nodes whose first dimension
835   (rank) is numbered in i, i + 1, . . . , n.
836
      At the i-th level (from bottom to top, i.e., levels 1, 2, . . . , log n), each tree node at this level covers
837
      intervals such as [1, 2i ], [2i + 1, 2i+1 ], . . . , [2log n−1 + 1, n].
838
839   Assume j is the smallest rank not less than ℓ. We can represent the difference n − j as a binary
840   number, which can be expressed as a sum of at most log (n − j) powers of 2. For example, the
841
      number 10 in binary is 1010, i.e., 10 = 23 + 21 . Similarly, we can cover the range [ℓ, n] by selecting
      at most log n nodes in Tx , since the range tree is built based on binary subdivisions of the range.
842
843   Similarly, for each node in Tx that we select, it contains a nested tree Ty . At this stage, for each
844   Ty , we select a tree node corresponding to the range [1, r] (since we have already determined the
845   left boundary). Just like before, we can cover all rank pairs whose second dimension is in [1, r] by
846   selecting at most log n tree nodes from Ty .
847   Thus, by selecting the necessary nodes in both Tx and Ty , we can cover all rank pairs falling within
848   [ℓ, n] × [1, r]. This allows us to retrieve all subgraph counts where the vertices lie in the range [ℓ, r].
849
      In summary, we need to select at most log2 n tree nodes from Ty to find all rank pairs within [ℓ, n] ×
850
      [1, r]. According to Lemma 3.5, the number of subgraphs with vertex ranks falling within any given
851
      query range can be efficiently calculated.
