009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) equip language models with visual capabilities by
012            aligning vision encoders with language models. Existing methods to enhance the
013            visual perception of MLLMs often involve designing more powerful vision en-
014            coders, which requires re-aligning these vision modules with the language model,
015            leading to expensive and time-consuming training processes. In this paper, we in-
016
               troduce VisionFuse, a novel integration framework that efficiently utilizes multiple
               vision encoders from off-the-shelf MLLMs to enhance visual perception without
017
               requiring additional training. Our approach is motivated by the observation that
018
               different MLLMs tend to focus on distinct regions of the same query and im-
019            age. Moreover, we find that the feature distributions of vision encoders within an
020            MLLM family, a group of MLLMs sharing the same pretrained LLM, are highly
021            aligned. Building on these insights, VisionFuse enriches the visual context by
022            concatenating the tokens generated by the vision encoders of selected MLLMs
023            within a family. By merging the parameters of language models from different
024            MLLMs, VisionFuse allows a single language model to align with various vision
025            encoders, significantly reducing deployment overhead. We conduct comprehen-
026            sive evaluations across multiple multimodal benchmarks using various MLLM
027
               combinations, demonstrating substantial improvements in multimodal tasks. No-
               tably, when integrating MiniGemini-8B and SLIME-8B, VisionFuse achieves an
028
               average performance increase of over 4%.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) integrate vision encoders to Large Language Models (LLMs), allow-
034   ing them to tackle multimodal tasks with emergent capabilities (Liu et al., 2024c; Lin et al., 2024; Ye
035   et al., 2023). To handle complex and diverse multimodal tasks effectively, MLLMs require strong
036   visual perception capabilities. A common approach to improving MLLMs’ visual perception is de-
037   signing better vision encoders (Cha et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024). However, these
038   methods typically require aligning the vision encoders with the language model, which involves sig-
039
      nificant training costs due to the need for multimodal instruction fine-tuning. For instance, aligning
      10 vision encoders with a 7B language model using a data-efficient MLLM pipeline requires 3,840
040
      NVIDIA A100 GPU hours, with an estimated cost of approximately $20,000 (Yang et al., 2024).
041
      Moreover, even with substantial efforts, individual models may still exhibit limitations in their visual
042   perception capabilities.
043
044   Recognizing these limitations, it becomes clear that different models tend to excel in distinct aspects
045
      of visual perception. For example, as illustrated in Figure 1, when asked the question, “What’s
      the word on the right side?”, the models should focus on the entire word on the coin’s right side.
046
      MGM (Li et al., 2024), however, concentrates more on the lower right portion of the coin, with
047
      relatively less attention to the upper part. In contrast, SLIME (Zhang et al., 2024) directs more
048   attention to the upper part, causing it to miss the last letter ‘y’. While MGM successfully recognizes
049   the letter ‘y’, its recognition of the preceding letters is suboptimal. Due to the perceptual limitations
050   of both models, they provide incorrect answers in this text recognition task, yet demonstrate notable
051   differences in their visual perception. This observation underscores the potential for mitigating the
052   limitations of individual models by leveraging the complementary strengths of multiple models.
053   By integrating the encoders in these MLLMs, our method captures a more complete target region,
      leading to accurate results.

071     To investigate this concept, ensemble learning has been proposed as an efficient means of lever-
072     aging the capabilities of different models without requiring additional training, by simply aggre-
073     gating the outputs (Jiang et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2024; Freitag et al., 2023). However, deploying
074     multiple full models and running inference on each one introduces inefficiencies in both memory
075     consumption and computational resources. Since vision encoders are generally smaller and less
076
        resource-intensive than entire MLLMs, a viable alternative is to integrate multiple vision encoders
        with a single language model. For example, Eagle (Shi et al., 2024) enhances MLLMs’ visual per-
077
        ception by concatenating the encoded vision tokens from multiple encoders, but it necessitates a
078
        resource-intensive two-stage multimodal instruction tuning process. While this approach reduces
079     computational overhead compared to ensemble learning, it still encounters the challenge of aligning
080     these encoders with a single language model, which often necessitates multimodal instruction tuning
081     and imposes considerable computational costs (Yang et al., 2024).
082
        In this paper, we introduce VisionFuse, a novel framework designed to efficiently enhance the visual
083
        perception capabilities of MLLMs, as illustrated in Figure 2. We define the MLLM family as a group
084
        of models that share the same pre-trained language model. For example, both MiniGemini-8B (Li
085     et al., 2024) and SLIME-8B (Zhang et al., 2024) are trained using LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta,
086     2024), and therefore belong to the same MLLM family. We first conduct a statistical analysis of
087     cross-attention to highlight variations in visual perception across different MLLMs, and then pro-
088     pose enhancing visual perception by integrating these models. Notably, we observe that vision
089     encoders within an MLLM family exhibit similar feature distributions, making their tokens more
090     compatible for combination. Therefore, we consider integrating these encoders to enhance percep-
091     tion. Furthermore, we find that merging language models within an MLLM family effectively aligns
092     them with different vision encoders, so prior to deployment, we merge language model parame-
093
        ters from various MLLMs to achieve this alignment. During inference, we apply preprocessing
        pipelines to the input visual data (e.g., slicing the image into local patches) consistent with those
094
        used by individual MLLMs within the family, then feed the processed data into the vision encoders
095
        and projectors from each MLLM to extract visual tokens. These tokens are then concatenated to
096     provide richer contextual information. VisionFuse effectively harnesses the visual perception capa-
097     bilities of multiple multimodal models, improving performance on multimodal tasks with minimal
098     additional inference overhead from the vision encoders.
099
        We apply VisionFuse to different MLLM families, based on the pretrained language models includ-
100
        ing Vicuna-v1.5 (Chiang et al., 2023) and LLaMA-3-8B-instruct (AI@Meta, 2024). Compared to
101
        the individual model baselines, our approach demonstrates significant improvements across multi-
102     ple multimodal datasets. Furthermore, we visualize the cross-attention to intuitively show that the
103     model after integrating focuses on more relevant positions than the individual models.

        2   R ELATED W ORK
136
137
        Enhancing the visual perception of MLLMs. Existing multimodal large language models
138
        (MLLMs) primarily enhance their visual perception by incorporating high-resolution inputs (Li
139     et al., 2024), employing optimized preprocessing methods to capture richer visual features (Liu
140     et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024), and designing more effective vision modules (Zhang et al.,
141     2024; Cha et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024). Specifically, LLaVA-Next (Liu et al., 2024b) segments
142     input images into local patches and uses high-quality data to train the MLLM. Mini-Gemini (Li
143     et al., 2024) uses CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) tokens as low-resolution queries to cross-attend to
144     another high-resolution vision encoder within co-located local windows. Honeybee (Cha et al.,
145     2024) introduces a locality-enhanced projector that balances token management flexibility with lo-
146     cal visual context preservation, improving both efficiency and performance in spatial understanding
147
        tasks. ConvLLaVA (Ge et al., 2024) leverages a hierarchical ConvNeXt backbone to compress
        high-resolution images into fewer visual tokens, enhancing efficiency while maintaining spatial un-
148
        derstanding across diverse image resolutions. SliME (Zhang et al., 2024) refines visual adapters by
149
        employing a mixture of experts for global features and compressing local image tokens with query
150     embeddings, improving both efficiency and performance in high-resolution tasks. Eagle (Shi et al.,
151     2024) explores the design space of multimodal LLMs by integrating multiple vision encoders with
152     different architectures and pretraining tasks, enhancing multimodal performance through efficient
153     fusion strategies like direct token concatenation. However, these approaches require extensive fine-
154     tuning to align the language model with the vision modules, leading to significant computational
155     costs. In contrast, our work aims to improve the visual perception of MLLMs more efficiently.
156     Model Merging. Model merging seeks to consolidate multiple parameter sets into a single model
157     without requiring retraining, providing a more memory-efficient and cost-effective alternative to
158     model ensembling by eliminating the need to store multiple checkpoints. Existing methods for
159     merging parameters of LLMs generally fall into two categories: merging with coefficients and pa-
160     rameter sparsification. Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023) introduces task vectors to efficiently
161     edit pre-trained models by performing arithmetic operations on weight differences, enhancing task
        performance without retraining. Regmean (Jin et al., 2022) computes a closed-form solution for


                                                                     3
           Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
           the merging coefficients by minimizing the difference in activation values before and after merging.
163        SLERP (Shoemake, 1985) determines the merging coefficients for parameter addition based on the
164        angle between the model parameters. Methods like Ties-merging (Yadav et al., 2023) and DARE (Yu
165        et al., 2024) observe that the delta parameters of LLMs contain a significant amount of redundancy,
166        and propose reducing conflicts between different delta parameters through pruning. However, these
167        model merging methods are primarily designed for language or vision models to preserve the capa-
168        bilities of individual models across different downstream tasks. Our method aligns language models
169        with different vision encoders through model merging, which is significantly different from the mo-
170        tivations of existing model merging approaches.

261   4.2   E NSEMBLE OF DIFFERENT VISION ENCODERS
262
263   To leverage the visual perception capabilities of different MLLMs, an intuitive approach is to en-
264   semble multiple MLLMs and aggregate their predictions. However, due to the large number of
265   parameters in the language models, directly ensembling the entire MLLMs would result in signif-
266
      icant computational overhead. Therefore, we explore whether it is feasible to integrate only the
      vision encoders, which have relatively fewer parameters, and aggregate the outputs in a way that
267
      feeds into the language model for inference.
268
269   In pre-trained MLLMs, the alignment between textual and visual inputs in the feature space allows
      them to be treated as a unified sequence for input into the language model. As discussed in Obser-

526   6   CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
527
528   This paper investigates the differences in visual perception capabilities among various MLLMs and
529   proposes a new paradigm for efficiently enhancing the perceptual abilities of MLLMs. The ap-
530   proach offers a simple yet effective MLLM integration strategy that requires no additional training,
531   leveraging the distinct visual perception strengths of different MLLMs to improve performance on
532   multimodal tasks. Overall, VisionFuse significantly enhances the perceptual abilities of individual
533   MLLMs with minimal additional deployment overhead.
534   Our current analysis focuses on two MLLMs. However, when attempting to integrate more MLLMs,
535   a direct concatenation of visual sequences results in excessively long visual token sequences, causing
536   discrepancies that the sequence length is much longer than that in the training phase and a subsequent
537   decline in performance. Detailed results are provided in Appendix C.1. Future work will explore
538   methods for efficiently incorporating additional MLLMs while reducing the length of visual tokens,
539   such as employing token merging strategies across different MLLMs or utilizing rapid fine-tuning
      techniques to adapt to longer input sequences.

              B                        A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
757
758
             Multimodal Large Language Models. MLLMs integrate vision encoders into large language mod-
759          els, enabling them to handle multimodal tasks. Early models such as Flamingo (Alayrac et al.,
760          2022) encode images and feed them into the attention layers of the language model, while Blip-
761          2 (Li et al., 2023a) employs Q-Former to encode images into features, which are then input into
762          the language model. Subsequent works (Liu et al., 2024c; 2023a; Lu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
763          2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023) enhance the multimodal understanding capabilities of lan-
764          guage models through instruction fine-tuning on multimodal datasets. Further research has focused
765          on optimizing encoder designs, extracting richer visual information, and expanding the models to
766          handle additional modalities. For example, Eagle (Shi et al., 2024), Mini-Gemini (Li et al., 2024),
767
             SLIME (Zhang et al., 2024), and LLaVA-next (Liu et al., 2024b) introduce additional vision en-
             coders and employ preprocessing techniques such as cropping and interpolation to handle longer
768
             visual input sequences, thereby enriching the visual information available to the language model.
769
             Honeybee (Cha et al., 2024) introduces a locality-enhanced visual projector to better bridge pre-
770          trained vision encoders with large language models. Recent works (Lin et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024;
771          Han et al., 2024) also explore enabling language models to understand a wider range of modalities.

              To address the issue of inconsistency between training and testing caused by excessively long input
              sequences, one straightforward solution is to fine-tune the model with longer visual input sequences.
833
              As shown in Table 7, the 8B models, having been trained on longer token sequences, demonstrate
834
              significant performance gains with the direct integration of the three MLLMs. However, training on
835           longer sequences comes with considerable computational costs. To mitigate this challenge more ef-
836           ficiently, we investigate the possibility of directly reducing the token sequence length before LLM’s
837           inference. Previous work on token pruning suggests that visual features in MLLMs exhibit sub-
838           stantial redundancy (Bolya et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024), indicating that pruning redundant tokens
839           could be a feasible solution. In the following, we assess the effectiveness of token pruning in miti-
840           gating the negative impact of inconsistent sequence lengths between training and testing, using the
841           integration of MGM-7B, LLaVA-Next-7B, and SLIME-7B as case studies.
842           It is worth noting that both SLIME-7B and LLaVA-Next-7B enhance visual representations by incor-
843           porating a large number of local features through additional augmentations, resulting in significantly
844           longer visual sequences compared to MGM-7B. We therefore assess the impact of randomly drop-
845           ping visual tokens on the performance of these two models and explore the extent of redundancy in
846           their local features. Specifically, we test two strategies on the TextVQA and MME datasets: (1) ran-
847           domly dropping a specified number of tokens from the entire set of visual tokens, and (2) randomly
848           dropping a specified number of tokens exclusively from the local features. As shown in Figures 9a
849
              and 9b, randomly dropping SLIME-7B’s visual tokens leads to a substantial performance decrease,
              whereas removing fewer than 500 visual tokens exclusively from the local features does not result
850
              in a significant performance loss. This indicates that SLIME-7B’s local features exhibit consider-
851
              able redundancy. In contrast, random token pruning from either the full token set or just the local
852           features in LLaVA-Next-7B results in a notable performance decline. Based on these observations,
853           we recommend pruning more visual tokens in SLIME-7B while limiting the number of tokens drop
854           in LLaVA-Next-7B.
855
              We implement a strategy of randomly dropping visual tokens to reduce the token sequence length,
856
              enabling the language model to accommodate a larger number of visual tokens from different mod-
857
              els. We first conduct experiments on the TextVQA dataset by integrating the MGM-7B, SLIME-7B,
858           and LLaVA-Next-7B models. As shown in Figure 9c, performance improves when a certain number
859           of tokens are dropped, after which further pruning may lead to a decline in performance. Ultimately,
860           when approximately 1000 tokens are dropped, the performance of the three-model integration sur-
861           passes that of the two-model integration. Additionally, as presented in Table 7, random pruning of
862           1000 tokens leads to a notable improvement in overall performance. However, different MLLMs
863           exhibit varying degrees of token sparsity, making it inconvenient to test each one before integration.
              We leave the design of a more efficient cross-model token fusion/pruning strategy for future work.
