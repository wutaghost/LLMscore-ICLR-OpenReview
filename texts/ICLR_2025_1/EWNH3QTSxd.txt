011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               In reinforcement learning (RL) with experience replay, experiences stored in a
014
               replay buffer influence the RL agent’s performance. Information about how
015
               these experiences influence the agent’s performance is valuable for various pur-
016            poses, such as identifying experiences that negatively influence underperforming
017            agents. One method for estimating the influence of experiences is the leave-one-
018            out (LOO) method. However, this method is usually computationally prohibitive.
019            In this paper, we present Policy Iteration with Turn-over Dropout (PIToD), which
020            efficiently estimates the influence of experiences. We evaluate how accurately
021            PIToD estimates the influence of experiences and its efficiency compared to LOO.
022            We then apply PIToD to amend underperforming RL agents, i.e., we use PIToD
023            to estimate negatively influential experiences for the RL agents and to delete the
024
               influence of these experiences. We show that RL agents’ performance is signifi-
               cantly improved via amendments with PIToD.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   In reinforcement learning (RL) with experience replay, the performance of an RL agent is influ-
033
      enced by experiences. Experience replay (Lin, 1992) is a data-generation mechanism indispensable
      in modern off-policy RL methods (Mnih et al., 2015; Hessel et al., 2018; Haarnoja et al., 2018a;
034
      Kumar et al., 2020). It allows an RL agent to learn from past experiences. These experiences in-
035
      fluence the RL agent’s performance (e.g., cumulative rewards) (Fedus et al., 2020). Estimating how
036   each experience influences the RL agent’s performance could provide useful information for many
037   purposes. For example, we could improve the RL agent’s performance by identifying and deleting
038   negatively influential experiences. The capability to estimate the influence of experience will be
039   crucial, as RL is increasingly applied to tasks where agents must learn from experiences of diverse
040   quality (e.g., a mixture of experiences from both expert and random policies) (Fu et al., 2020; Yu
041   et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Tirumala et al., 2024).
042   However, estimating the influence of experiences with feasible computational cost is not trivial. One
043   might consider estimating it by a leave-one-out (LOO) method (left part of Figure 1), which retrains
044   an RL agent for each possible experience deletion. As we will discuss in Section 3, this method has
045   quadratic time complexity and quickly becomes intractable due to the necessity of retraining.
046
      In this paper, we present PIToD, a policy iteration (PI) method that efficiently estimates the influence
047
      of experiences (right part of Figure 1). PI is a fundamental method for many RL methods (Section 2).
048   PIToD is PI augmented with turn-over dropout (ToD) (Kobayashi et al., 2020) to efficiently estimate
049   the influence of experiences without retraining an RL agent (Section 4). We evaluate how accu-
050   rately PIToD estimates the influence of experiences and its efficiency compared to the LOO method
051   (Section 5). We then apply PIToD to amend underperforming RL agents by identifying and deleting
052   negatively influential experiences (Section 6). To our knowledge, our work is the first to: (i) estimate
053   the influence of experiences on the performance of RL agents with feasible computational cost, and
      (ii) modify RL agents’ performance simply by deleting influential experiences.

      2   P RELIMINARIES
070
071
      In Section 4, we will introduce our PI method for estimating the influence of experiences in the RL
      problem. As preliminaries for this, we explain the RL problem, PI, and influence estimation.
072
073   Reinforcement learning (RL). RL addresses the problem of an agent learning to act in an environ-
074   ment. The environment provides the agent with a state s. The agent responds by selecting an action
075   a, and then the environment provides a reward r and the next state s′ . This interaction between the
076   agent and environment continues until the agent reaches a terminal state. The agent aims to find a
077
      policy π : S ×A → [0, 1] that maximizes cumulative rewards (return). A Q-function Q : S ×A → R
      is used to estimate the expected return.
078
079   Policy iteration (PI). PI is a method for solving RL problems. PI updates the policy and Q-function
080   by iteratively performing policy evaluation and improvement. Many implementations of policy
081   evaluation and improvement have been proposed (e.g., Lillicrap et al. (2015); Fujimoto et al. (2018);
082   Haarnoja et al. (2018a)). In the main part of this paper, we focus on the policy evaluation and
083
      improvement used in Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG). In policy evaluation in DDPG,
      the Q-function Qϕ : S × A → R, parameterized by ϕ, is updated as:

136   4   P OLICY ITERATION WITH TURN - OVER DROPOUT (PIT O D)
137
138   In this section, we present Policy Iteration with Turn-over Dropout (PIToD), which estimates the
139   influence of experiences without retraining. The concept of PIToD is shown in Figure 2, and an
140   algorithmic description of PIToD is shown in Algorithm 2. Inspired by ToD (Kobayashi et al.,
141   2020), PIToD uses masks and flipped masks to drop out the parameters of the policy and Q-function.
142   Further details are provided in the following paragraphs.
143   Masks and flipped masks. PIToD uses mask mi and flipped mask wi , which are binary vectors
144   uniquely associated with experience ei . The mask mi consists of elements randomly initialized
145   to 0 or 1. mi is used to drop out the parameters of the policy and Q-function during PI with ei .
146   Additionally, the flipped mask wi is the negation of mi , i.e., wi = 1 − mi . wi is used to drop out
147   the parameters of the policy and Q-function for estimating the influence of ei .
148   Policy iteration with the mask (lines 5–6 in Algorithm 2). PIToD applies mi to the policy and
149   Q-function during PI with ei . It executes PI with variants of policy evaluation (Eq. 1) and improve-
150   ment (Eq. 2) where masks are applied to the parameters of the policy and Q-function. The policy
151   evaluation for PIToD is

            E VALUATIONS WITH SELF - INFLUENCE
232
      In this section, we evaluate how accurately PIToD estimates the influence of experiences by focusing
233   on their self-influence. Self-influence is the influence of an experience on prediction performance
234   using that same experience. We define self-influences on policy evaluation and on policy improve-
235   ment. The self-influence of an experience ei := (s, a, r, s′ , i) on policy evaluation is
236
                      Lpe,i (Qϕ,wi ) − Lpe,i (Qϕ,mi ),                                                   (8)
237                                                                     2
238                    where Lpe,i (Q) = r + γQϕ̄,mi (s , a ) − Q(s, a) , a′ ∼ πθ,mi (·|s′ ).
                                                             ′   ′

239   Here, Lpe,i represents the temporal difference error based on ei . The self-influence of ei on policy
240   improvement is
241
242
              Lpi,i (πθ,wi ) − Lpi,i (πθ,mi ), where Lpi,i (π) = Qϕ,mi (s, a′ ),   a′ ∼ π(·|s).          (9)
243   Here, Lpi,i represents the Q-value estimate based on ei .
244
      We evaluate whether PIToD has correctly estimated the influence of experiences by examining the
245
      signs (positive or negative) of the values of Eq. 8 and Eq. 9. If PIToD has correctly estimated the
246   influence of experiences, the value of Eq. 8 should be positive. Qϕ,mi is optimized by PIToD to min-
247   imize Lpe,i (line 5 in Algorithm 2), while Qϕ,wi is not. Therefore, Lpe,i (Qϕ,mi ) ≤ Lpe,i (Qϕ,wi ),
248   implying that Eq. 8 ≥ 0. Conversely, if PIToD has correctly estimated, the value of Eq. 9 should
249   be negative. πθ,mi is optimized by PIToD to maximize Lpi,i (line 6 in Algorithm 2), while πθ,wi is
250   not. Therefore, Lpi,i (πθ,mi ) ≥ Lpi,i (πθ,wi ), which implies that Eq. 9 ≤ 0.
251
      We periodically evaluate the ratio of experiences for which PIToD has correctly estimated self-
252   influence in the MuJoCo environments (Todorov et al., 2012). The MuJoCo tasks for this evaluation
253   are Hopper, Walker2d, Ant, and Humanoid. In this evaluation, 5000 policy iterations (i.e., lines 3–6
254   of Algorithm 2) constitute one epoch, with 125 epochs allocated for Hopper and 300 epochs for the
255   others. At each epoch, we (i) calculate the self-influence (Eq. 8 and Eq. 9) of experiences stored in
256   the replay buffer and (ii) record the ratio of experiences for which PIToD has correctly estimated
257   self-influence.
258   Evaluation results (Figure 3) show that the ratio of experiences for which the self-influence (Eq. 8
259   and Eq. 9) is correctly estimated exceeds the chance rate of 0.5. For self-influence on policy evalu-
260   ation (Eq. 8), the ratio of correctly estimated experiences is higher than 0.9 across all environments.
261   Furthermore, for self-influence on policy improvement (Eq. 9), the ratio of correctly estimated expe-
262   riences exceeds 0.7 in Hopper, 0.8 in Walker2d and Ant, and 0.9 in Humanoid. These results suggest
263   that PIToD estimates the influence of experiences more accurately than random estimation.
264   Figure 3 also shows that in policy improvement, the ratio of correctly estimated experiences tends
265   to be higher in higher-dimensional environments (Hopper < Walker = Ant < Humanoid). This
266   suggests that the policy tends to fit more significantly to each experience in higher-dimensional en-
267   vironments. Additionally, in both policy evaluation and improvement, the ratio gradually decreases
268   as the epoch progresses. As the epoch progresses, the ratio of experiences to the policy and Q-
269   network size increases. We hypothesize that this makes tracking the influence of each experience
      more difficult.

347
         We evaluate the computational time required for influence estimation with PIToD and compare it
348
         to the estimated time for LOO. To measure the computational time for PIToD, we run the method
349
         under the same settings as in the previous section and record its wall-clock time. For comparison,
350      we also evaluate the estimated time required for influence estimation using LOO (Section 3). To
351      estimate the time for LOO, we record the average time required for one policy iteration with PIToD
352      and multiply this by the total number of policy iterations required for LOO 1 .
353
         The evaluation results (Figure 5) show that PIToD significantly reduces computational time com-
354
         pared to LOO. The time required for LOO increases quadratically as epochs progress, taking, for
355
         example, more than 4 · 107 seconds (≈ 462 days) up to 300 epochs in Humanoid. In contrast, the
356      time required for PIToD increases linearly, taking about 1.4·105 seconds (≈ one day) for 300 epochs
357      in Humanoid.

         6                A PPLICATION OF PIT O D: AMENDING POLICIES AND Q- FUNCTIONS BY
361                       DELETING NEGATIVELY INFLUENTIAL EXPERIENCES
362
363
         In the previous section, we demonstrated that PIToD can accurately and efficiently estimate the
         influence of experiences. What scenarios might benefit from this capability? In this section, we
364
         demonstrate how PIToD can be used to amend underperforming policies and Q-functions.

      7       R ELATED WORK
440
441
442   Influence estimation in supervised learning. Our research builds upon prior studies that esti-
443   mate the influence of data within the supervised learning (SL) regime. In Section 4, we introduced
444
      our method for estimating the influence of data (i.e., experiences) in RL settings. Methods that
      estimate the influence of data have been extensively studied in the SL research community. Typ-
445
      ically, these methods require SL loss functions that are twice differentiable with respect to model
446
      parameters (e.g., Koh & Liang (2017); Yeh et al. (2018); Hara et al. (2019); Koh et al. (2019);
447   Guo et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021b); Schioppa et al. (2022)). However, these methods are not
448   directly applicable to our RL setting, as such SL loss functions are unavailable. In contrast, turn-
449   over dropout (ToD) (Kobayashi et al., 2020) estimates the influence without requiring differentiable
450   SL loss functions. We extended ToD for RL settings (Sections 4, 5, and 6). For this extension of
451   ToD, we provided a theoretical justification (Appendix A) and considered practical implementations
452   (Appendix C).
453   Influence estimation in off-policy evaluation (OPE). A few studies in the OPE community have
454   focused on efficiently estimating the influence of experiences (Gottesman et al., 2020; Lobo et al.,
455   2022). These studies are limited to estimating the influence on policy evaluation using nearest-
456   neighbor or linear Q-functions. In contrast, our study estimates influence on a broader range of
457   performance metrics (e.g., return or Q-estimation bias) using neural-network-based Q-functions and
458   policies.
459   Prioritized experience replay (PER). In PER, the importance of experiences is estimated to pri-
460   oritize experiences during experience replay. The importance of experiences is estimated based
461   on criteria such as TD-error (Schaul et al., 2016; Fedus et al., 2020) or on-policyness (Novati &
462   Koumoutsakos, 2019; Sun et al., 2020). Some readers might think that PER resembles our method.
463   However, PER fundamentally differs from our method, as it cannot efficiently estimate or disable
464   the influence of experiences in hindsight.
465   Interpretable RL. Our method (Section 4) estimates the influence of experiences, thereby providing
466   a certain type of interpretability. Previous studies in the RL community have proposed interpretable
467   methods based on symbolic (or relational) representation (Džeroski et al., 2001; Yang et al., 2018;
468   Lyu et al., 2019; Garnelo et al., 2016; Andersen & Konidaris, 2017; Konidaris et al., 2018), inter-
469   pretable proxy models (e.g., decision trees) (Degris et al., 2006; Liu et al., 2019; Coppens et al.,
470   2019; Zhu et al., 2022), saliency explanation (Zahavy et al., 2016; Greydanus et al., 2018; Mott
471   et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Anderson et al., 2020), and sparse kernel models (Dao et al., 2018) 2 .
472   Unlike these studies, our study proposes a method to estimate the influence of experiences on RL
473
      agent performance. This method helps us, for example, identify influential experiences when RL
      agents perform poorly, as demonstrated in Section 6.

477

479   In this paper, we proposed PIToD, a policy iteration (PI) method that efficiently estimates the influ-

      ence of experiences (Section 4). We demonstrated that PIToD (i) accurately estimates the influence
      of experiences (Section 5.1), and (ii) significantly reduces the time required for influence estimation
481
      compared to the leave-one-out (LOO) method (Section 5.2). Furthermore, we applied PIToD to
482
      identify and delete negatively influential experiences, which improved the performance of policies
483   and Q-functions (Section 6).

          In Sections 5 and 6, we estimated the influences of experiences on performance (e.g., return or Q-

1355
          overlap between the masks. In this section, we analyze two points: (i) the correlation between the
1356      influences of experiences within each performance metric, and (ii) how the dropout rate of masks
1357      affects this correlation 4 .
1358
1359
          We calculate the correlation between the experience influences for each performance metric used

1367

1459
                    ENVIRONMENTS WITH ADVERSARIAL EXPERIENCES
1460
1461
        In Section 6, we applied PIToD to amend policies and Q-functions in the MuJoCo (Todorov et al.,
1462
        2012) environments.
1463
1464    In this section, we apply PIToD to amend policies and Q-functions in DM control (Tunyasuvunakool
1465    et al., 2020) environments with adversarial experiences. We focus on the DM control environments:
1466    finger-turn hard, hopper-stand, hopper-hop, fish-swim, cheetah-run, quadruped-run, humanoid-run,
1467
        and humanoid-stand. In these environments, we introduce adversarial experiences. An adversarial
        experience contains an adversarial reward r′ , which is a reversed and magnified version of the orig-
1468
        inal reward r: r′ = −100 · r. These adversarial experiences are designed to (i) disrupt the agent’s
1469
        ability to maximize original rewards and (ii) have greater influence than other non-adversarial ex-
1470    periences stored in the replay buffer. At 150 epochs (i.e., in the middle of training), the RL agent
1471    encounters 5000 adversarial experiences. In these environments, we amend policies and Q-functions
1472    as in Section 6.
1473
        The results of the policy and Q-function amendments (Figures 16 and 17) show that performance
1474
        is improved by the amendments. The policy amendment results (Figure 16) show that returns are
1475
        improved, particularly in fish-swim. Additionally, the Q-function amendment results (Figure 17)
1476    show that the Q-estimation bias is significantly reduced in finger-turn hard, hopper-stand, hopper-
1477    hop, fish-swim, cheetah-run, and quadruped-run.

        In Section 6, we amended the SAC agent using PIToD. In this section, we apply PIToD to amend
1678
        other RL agents.
1679
1680    We evaluate two PIToD implementations: DroQToD and ResetToD.
1681    DroQToD is a PIToD implementation based on DroQ (Hiraoka et al., 2022). DroQ is the SAC
1682    variant that applies dropout and layer normalization to the Q-function. DroQToD differs from the
1683
        original PIToD implementation (Appendix C) in that it has a dropout layer after each weight layer
        in the Q-function. The dropout rate is set to 0.01 as in Hiraoka et al. (2022). Layer normalization
1684
        is already included in the Q-function of the original PIToD implementation; thus, no additional
1685
        changes are made to it.
1686    ResetToD is a PIToD implementation based on the periodic reset (Nikishin et al., 2022; D’Oro
1687    et al., 2023) of the Q-function and policy parameters. ResetToD differs from the original PIToD
1688    implementation in that it resets the parameters of the Q-function and policy every 105 steps.
1689
        The policies and Q-functions of these implementations are amended as in Section 6 (i.e., the amend-
1690
        ment process follows Algorithm 4 in Appendix D).
1691
1692    The results of the policy and Q-function amendments (Figures 26 and 27) show that the performance
1693    of both DroQToD and ResetToD is significantly improved after the amendments. Return: For
1694    DroQToD, the return is significantly improved after amendment, especially in Hopper (the left side
1695
        of Figure 26). For ResetToD, the return is significantly improved across all environments (the left
        side of Figure 27). Q-estimation bias: For DroQToD, the estimation bias is significantly reduced
1696
        after amendment, especially in Humanoid (the right side of Figure 26). For ResetToD, the estimation
1697
        bias is reduced in the early stages of training (epochs 0–10) in Ant and Walker2d (the right side of
1698    Figure 27).

       I   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
1999
2000
       Refining implementation decisions for PIToD. PIToD employs a dropout rate of 0.5 (Section 4
2001   and Appendix B), which often leads to degradation in learning performance. To mitigate this issue,
2002   we have considered various design choices in the implementation of PIToD (Appendix C). However,
2003   further refinement may still be necessary to improve the practicality of PIToD.
2004
       Overlap of experience masks. PIToD assigns each experience a randomly generated binary mask
2005
       (Section 4). When there is significant overlap between the elements of masks, applying the flipped
2006
       mask to delete the influence of a specific experience also deletes the influence of other experiences.
2007   For example, if the masks mi and mi′ corresponding to the experiences ei and ei′ have a 100% over-
2008   lap, applying the flipped mask wi completely deletes the influence of both ei and ei′ . Additionally,
2009   significant overlap between masks may hinder the fulfillment of Assumption 1 and thus compromise
2010   the theoretical property derived in Section A. We set the dropout rate of the mask elements to mini-
2011   mize this overlap, but a 50% overlap can still occur (Appendix B). Developing practical methods to
2012   reduce mask overlap across experiences would be an important direction for future work.
2013   Invasiveness of PIToD. PIToD introduces invasive changes to the base PI method (e.g., DDPG or
2014   SAC) to equip it with efficient influence estimation capabilities (Section 4). Specifically, PIToD
2015   incorporates turn-over dropout, which may affect the learning outcomes of the base PI method.
2016   Consequently, PIToD may not be suitable for estimating the influence of experiences on the original
2017   learning outcomes of the base PI method. One direction for future work is to explore non-invasive
2018   influence estimation methods.
2019   Exploring surrogate evaluation metrics for amendments. To amend RL agents in Section 6,
2020   we used the return-based evaluation metric Lret , which requires additional environment interactions
2021   for evaluation. In our case, evaluating Lret required as many as 3 · 106 interactions (Figure 13 in
2022   Appendix E). These additional interactions may become a bottleneck in settings where interacting
2023   with environments is costly (e.g., real-world or slow simulator environments). Exploring surrogate
2024   evaluation metrics that do not require additional interactions is an interesting research direction.
2025   Exploring broader applications of PIToD. In this paper, we applied PIToD to amend RL agents
2026   in single-task RL settings (Section 6, Appendix G, and Appendix H). However, we believe that the
2027   potential applications of PIToD extend beyond single-task RL settings. For instance, it could be
2028   applied to multi-task RL (Vithayathil Varghese & Mahmoud, 2020) (including multi-goal RL (Liu
2029   et al., 2022) or meta RL (Beck et al., 2023)), continual RL (Khetarpal et al., 2022), safe RL (Gu
2030   et al., 2022), offline RL (Levine et al., 2020), or multi-agent RL (Canese et al., 2021). Investigating
2031   the broader applicability of PIToD in these settings is a promising direction for future work. Addi-
2032   tionally, in this paper, we estimated the influence of experiences by assigning masks to experiences.
2033
       We may also be able to estimate the influence of specific hyperparameter values by assigning masks
       to those values. Exploring such applications is another promising direction for future work.
