009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               We present Virtual Community, a social world simulation platform designed to
012
               support embodied AI research, featuring large-scale community scenarios de-
013
               rived from the real world. Virtual Community introduces two key features to
014            enrich the virtual social world with generative AI: scalable 3D Scene creation,
015            which supports the generation of expansive outdoor and indoor environments at
016            any location and scale, addressing the lack of a large-scale, interactive, open-
017            world scene for embodied AI research; and embodied agents with grounded
018            characters and social relationship networks, the first to simulate socially con-
019            nected agents at a community level, that also have scene-grounded characters.
020            We design two novel challenges to showcase that Virtual Community provides
021            testbeds to evaluate the social reasoning and planning capabilities of embodied
022
               agents in open-world scenarios: Route Planning and Election Campaign. The
               Route Planning task examines the agent’s ability to reason about time, location,
023
               and tools in the community to plan fast and economical commutes in daily life.
024
               The Election Campaign task evaluates an agent’s ability to explore and connect
025            with other agents as a new member of the community. . We evaluate sev-
026            eral baseline agents on these challenges and demonstrate the performance gap
027            of current methods in addressing embodied social challenges within open-world
028            scenarios, which our simulator is designed to unlock. We plan to open-source
029            this simulation and hope Virtual Community can accelerate the development in
030            this direction. We encourage the readers to view the demo of our simulation at
031            https://sites.google.com/view/virtual-community-iclr.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   In recent years, we have witnessed tremendous progress in developing intelligent embodied agents,
038   driven by advancements in embodied AI simulators (Savva et al., 2019; Puig et al., 2023b; Kolve
039
      et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; Xiang et al., 2020b; Makoviychuk et al., 2021; Puig et al., 2018; Gan
      et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2024). However, existing simulators face significant challenges in ground-
040
      ing realistic social interactions in 3D open-world environments. Most simulators focus on simulating
041
      a limited number of agents without incorporating social relationships (Szot et al., 2021; Gan et al.,
042   2022), and are restricted to small-scale scene regions (Puig et al., 2018; 2020; 2023a;b; Zhang et al.,
043   2023; 2024). In contrast, real-world social scenarios typically involve large communities of agents
044   with diverse personalities and complex social networks spread over expansive areas. This limita-
045   tion significantly restricts the study of complex and diverse social interactions between agents in
046   simulated environments.
047
      To address this challenge, it is crucial to enable the simulators to support the following key aspects.
048   First, it should offer large-scale 3D environments, including complex and diverse indoor and outdoor
049   scenes, capable of accommodating expansive agent communities and supporting tasks that span
050   vast spatial regions. Current approaches for this aspect can be divided into manual design (Wang
051   et al., 2024; Gan et al., 2021), which provide rich interactions but are inherently limited in number
052   and diversity, and 3D reconstruction methods (Savva et al., 2019), which create visually realistic
053   and diverse environments but often result in noisy scenes with limited interactivity in open-world
      settings.

      2.1   E MBODIED AI S IMULATION
144
145   Recently, embodied AI has seen significant advancements through the development of simulation
146   platforms. Most existing simulators primarily focus on household tasks within indoor environ-
147   ments (Beattie et al., 2016; Savva et al., 2019; Yi et al., 2018; Das et al., 2018; Xiang et al., 2020a;
148   Shen et al., 2021; Szot et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Puig et al., 2018; Kolve et al., 2017; Yan et al.,
149   2018), while some have extended support to outdoor scenes (Gan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024;
150   Dosovitskiy et al., 2017; Kendall et al., 2018). However, these platforms lack diverse and scalable
151
      outdoor environments that can accommodate a larger number of agents and support more complex
      tasks. In contrast, this paper introduces a simulation platform featuring open-world environments
152
      with indoor and scalable outdoor scenes, enabling broader agent activities and more intricate task
153
      scenarios.
154
155
      2.2   E MBODIED S OCIAL I NTELLIGENCE
156
157   Current research on Embodied Social Intelligence is often limited to small agent populations in con-
158   strained household scenarios (Puig et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023; Stone et al., 2022) or simplified
159   to 2D or grid worlds (Carroll et al., 2019; Suarez et al., 2019), hindering model development in the
160   open world. Specifically, Park et al. (2023) demonstrates the robust simulation of human-like agents
161   within a symbolic community, ignoring the 3D perception and realistic physics in the open world.
      Wang et al. (2023c) studies human-like simulation guided by system 1 processing with basic needs.

162
      Predominant approaches, such as multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) and other planning
163   models, face several limitations when applied to open-world settings. MARL, for instance, often
164   struggles with scalability due to the exponential growth of state and action spaces as the number of
165   agents increases (Wen et al., 2022). This makes it difficult to learn effective policies in complex,
166   dynamic environments. Additionally, MARL approaches typically require extensive training data
167   and computational resources, which may not be feasible in real-world applications. Other planning
168   models, while potentially more efficient, often lack the adaptability required to handle the unpre-
169   dictable nature of open-world interactions. They may rely on predefined rules or assumptions that
170   do not hold in all scenarios, leading to suboptimal performance and limited generalization to new
171
      contexts (Puig et al., 2020).
172
173   2.3      F OUNDATION AND GENERATIVE MODELS FOR E MBODIED AI
174
175   With the recent advance of foundation models (Bubeck et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Driess et al.,
176
      2023; Blattmann et al., 2023), numerous works have explored how they can help build powerful
      embodied agents (Wang et al., 2023b; Xi et al., 2023; Sumers et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023d; Ahn
177
      et al., 2022; Sharma et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023a; Park et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Black
178
      et al., 2024), and scenes for simulation (Höllein et al., 2023; Schult et al., 2023; Deitke et al., 2022;
179   Fu et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2023; Paschalidou et al., 2021).
180   Robogen (Auerbach et al., 2014) leverages foundation models to automatically generate diversified
181   tasks, scenes, and training supervision, thereby scaling up robotic skill learning with minimal human
182   supervision. Different from them, this work aims to use a generative pipeline to create open world
183   scenes and agent communities instead of constraint indoor scenes and tasks.

      3       S CALABLE 3D S CENE G ENERATION
187
188
      The existing 3D geospatial datasets2 provide extensive data in terms of quantity and diversity. How-
      ever, they are not directly suitable for embodied AI research because of several limitations. First,
189
      these geospatial data often contain noise, including pedestrians, vehicles, and other transient objects
190
      that can disrupt simulations. Second, visual quality is inadequate for ground-level agent perspec-
191   tives because these environments are typically reconstructed from aerial imagery, leading to less
192   detailed textures and geometries at street level. To bridge this gap, we perform comprehensive mesh
193   cleaning and enhancement in both geometry and texture to make the scenes suitable for embodied
194   AI simulations.
195
      To overcome these challenges, we propose a pipeline to transform 3D geospatial data into
196
      simulation-ready scenes for embodied AI. This pipeline consists of four main steps: mesh sim-
197   plification, texture refinement, object placement, and automatic annotation. We list some qualitative
198   example in Figure 4 and Figure 3
199
200
      3.1      M ESH C ONSTRUCTION AND S IMPLIFICATION FOR S CENES
201
202   Since 3D geospatial data like Google 3D tiles are reconstructed from images using photometric
203   methods, they often include noisy surfaces, excessive transient objects like moving cars and people,
204   and unreliable mesh topology. These deficiencies make them inefficient and unsuitable for embodied
205   AI simulations. To address this, we decompose the scene into the terrain, buildings, and decorative
206   roofs and perform different operations to reconstruct each part of the scene.
207
      The terrain is built procedurally using sparse reference elevation points and bilinear interpolation.
208   We then derive simple and topologically sound mesh using information provided by the Open-
209   StreetMap (OSM) service. The building mesh is then modified to better fit the Google 3D tiles
210   geometry and to align with the terrain elevation. By aligning the mesh geometries with OSM primi-
211   tives, we eliminate unnecessary details and artifacts, such as distorted surfaces and irregular shapes
212   caused by aerial reconstruction errors. This geometric simplification not only reduces noise but also
213   decreases the total number of primitives in the scene, leading to more efficient physical simulations
214   and improved rendering performance.

244   3.2     E NHANCED T EXTURE Q UALITY FOR R EALISTIC S IMULATION
245
246   To improve the visual quality of 3D geospatial data, we employ advanced image processing tech-
247   niques. First, we use an inpainting method based on Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) to
248   remove noise and repair missing or damaged areas in textures. This process corrects inconsisten-
249   cies and eliminates artifacts from the reconstruction phase, resulting in smooth and realistic surface
250
      appearances. Once the texture integrity is restored, we enhance finer details using street view im-
      ages and super-resolution tools. While some approaches directly use street view images for 3D
251
      reconstruction (Pang & Biljecki, 2022; Gao et al., 2024), these methods often struggle with limited
252
      coverage and density. Instead, we blend street view images with existing textures on scene primitives
253   to improve visual richness. For super-resolution, we use GigaPixel 3 to increase texture resolution
254   and sharpen finer details. This two-step enhancement significantly increases the visual fidelity of
255   the textures, making them more suitable for ground-level rendering. These high-quality textures
256   create a more immersive environment for agents, improving the realism and overall effectiveness of
257   embodied AI training.
258
259   3.3     I MPROVING I NTERACTIVITY BY O BJECTS R ETRIEVAL AND R EPLACEMENT
260
261   To enhance the interactivity of the scenes, we use generative methods to populate the environment
262   with interactive objects, such as bikes and tent. We use annotations in OpenStreetMap (OSM)
263   dataset to determine the type and location of generated objects to match the real-world context.
264
      The OSM annotations are used as input for the One-2-3-45 Liu et al. (2024) generative framework,
      which outputs the corresponding 3D meshes of corresponding objects. These generative objects are
265
      assigned physical properties that allow them to interact seamlessly with agents in the simulation. By
266
      aligning object generation with real-world geospatial data, this approach ensures that the scenes are
267   functionally rich and physically interactive, enabling agents to engage in meaningful interactions
268   that mirror real-world environments.

298   3.4   AUTOMATIC A NNOTATION OF S CENES WITH G EOSPATIAL DATA
299
300   To facilitate alignment with real-world locations and provide semantic context, we automatically an-
301
      notate the scenes using geospatial data. We integrate metadata from sources such as OpenStreetMap
      and other GIS databases to label buildings, roads, and other landmarks within the environment. This
302
      annotation enables agents to access location-specific information and supports tasks that require
303
      an understanding of the spatial context, such as navigation and location-based decision-making.
304   The enriched semantic information enhances the potential for more sophisticated and context-aware
305   agent behaviors within the simulation.
306
307
      Bus Transit Annotation We search for the bus stops in the scene using Google Places API and then
      annotate the routes between any two adjacent bus stops using Google Directions API. We use depth-
308
      first search (DFS) on the graph of routes to find the route in the scene that maximizes the number
309
      of bus stops. Then, we decode polyline from the route to extract the dense waypoints which follow
310   rounds of optimization to ensure the distance between waypoints is roughly equal and contains
311   turning points. We also generate the bus schedule by estimating the travel time between bus stops
312   using the distance and speed of the bus.
313
      Shared Bicycle Transit Annotation We search for the shared bicycle stations in the scene using the
314
      OpenStreetMap API.

341   4.1     G ROUNDED C HARACTERS AND S OCIAL R ELATIONSHIP N ETWORK G ENERATION
342
343   We utilize the open-world knowledge of the Large Language Model (LLM) to generate agent char-
344   acter profiles and personalities grounded in the scene. The input to the LLM is structured into two
345   parts to create characters grounded in a specific scene. The first part contains scene-related infor-
346   mation, such as the scene name and details about various places, including their names, types, and
347   functionalities. The second part includes details on the agents’ appearances to ensure consistency
348   between their visual attributes and generated profiles, which are annotated with the name and age.
349
      With both parts provided, the LLM generates agent profiles along with their social relationships.
      The profiles consist of basic attributes such as names, ages, occupations, personalities, and hobbies,
350
      which influence each agent’s daily decision-making. Social relationships are structured as groups,
351
      each containing a subset of agents along with a text description and a designated place for group
352   activities, connecting these agents into a cohesive community, and allowing rich and complex social
353   interactions grounded in the 3D environment.
354
355
      Grounding Validator We implemented a grounding validator to check if the generated character
      profiles are accurately grounded to the scene by checking if all related places generated exist in the
356
      scene. If the validation fails, LLM will be prompted again with the feedback from the validator and
357
      try to fix the mismatch. Empirically, we find that 1-2 rounds of prompting is enough to pass the
358   grounding validator.

364   Human Avatars Skin Creation We obtained 12 avatar skin models of different genders, profes-
365   sions, and appearances from the Mixamo4 website for integration into the Virtual Community. Each
366   skin model of characters includes 71 skeletal joints and can be adapted to animation sequences in
367   SMPL-X and FBX formats. To reduce the computational load during animation playback in the

369
      tool, reducing the number of vertices in the 3D skin mesh by 90%.

374   produces a 3D mesh with detailed skin textures. To further enhance realism, we adjust the avatar’s
375
      clothing, height, and body shape, creating a more lifelike and personalized appearance.

397   Human Avatars Motion Control We combine SMPL-X human skeletons with created avatar skins
398   to model human avatars in Virtual Community. The motions of these avatars are parameterized by
399
      SMPL-X pose vectors J ∈ R162 along with global translation and rotation vectors T, R ∈ R3 . Based
      on these pose representations, a skin mesh for each avatar is calculated using forward kinematics.
400
401   Our motion model for humanoid avatars supports over 15 distinct motions, such as walking, picking
402   objects, and entering various vehicles. We use motion clips from Mixamo and adjust these clips to
403   our humanoid avatar models with appropriate animating speeds. For walking, we loop the walking
404   motion clip until the avatar reaches the given distance. For object-related motions, the interacting
405
      object will be kinematically attached or detached to or from the avatars’ hands depending on the
      action type. Similarly, the humanoid avatar will be kinematically attached or detached to or from
406
      the given vehicle for vehicle-related motions. We also incorporate physics constraints into our avatar
407
      motion model, where collision detection is performed between avatars and other scene entities, and

      5.1   ROUTE P LANNING : U SE T RANSPORTATIONS IN C OMMUNITY
433
434
435   Task Definition To live a daily life in a human society, an embodied agent needs first to be able to
436   plan its route from one place to another in the community. To study this basic ability of embodied
437
      agents, we introduce the Route Planning task. In this task, an agent needs to commute from place to
      place 5 - 7 times a day given the schedule. The agent can utilize available transit options, including
438
      buses with fixed routes and rental bikes along the roads. The bus is only available at the bus stop
439
      and the agent can only take a bus when the bus arrives. The bikes are available at given bike stations
440   along the roads, and the agent also needs to return the bike to any bike station before the task finishes.
441
442   At each simulation step, agents are provided with an observation of RGB-D images with the corre-
443
      sponding camera matrix, current poses, daily schedules, and transit information in the community.
      The action space for these avatars includes move forward, turn left, turn right, enter/exit bus, and
444
      enter/exit bike. The movement and turning actions can be set with a variable amount. When an
445
      agent is within a specified distance threshold of another agent, it can perform a communication
446   action, enabling text-based interaction with agents within that range.
447
448   Baselines We compare three baseline agents in the Route Planning task:
449   •Rule-based Agent The rule-based agent always chooses to walk directly toward the target location.
450
      •MCTS agent This agent is based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and simulates various
451
      decisions, such as choosing to take a bus. For each action, the agent estimates its associated cost and
452
      uses Monte Carlo sampling to iteratively update the expected reward for each decision path. The
453   agent ultimately selects the action sequence that maximizes the cumulative reward.
454
455   •LLM agent This agent converts all the task information into a prompt and queries the Large Lan-
456
      gauge Model (we use GPT-4o here) to generate a commute plan directly, which may include multiple
      steps such as walking to a bus stop, taking the bus to a specific stop, and then walk to the final des-
457
      tination.
458
459   All agents use the same low-level point-based navigation algorithm, which reconstructs the point
460   cloud based on RGB-D images from the ego-centric observation at each step and converts the point
461   cloud into a volume grid representation with a resolution of 0.1m. Subsequently, a 2D occurrence
462
      map is established with a resolution of 0.5m based on this representation and an A* algorithm is
      used to search for the shortest path efficiently.
463
464   Metrics We evaluate the agents on two different scenes with 19 diverse personal schedules, making
465   106 commutes in total. Agents are expected to commute efficiently, so we use the following metrics
466   for this task
467   • Arrival Rate: Percentage of in-time arrivals at the target location within the given time.
468
469
      • Time: Average time in seconds taken on the road to reach the destination.

510   Task Definition In this task, two agents in the community are designated as candidates. The can-
511   didates need to navigate through the community, find potential voters, and persuade them to vote
512
      through direct communication. The election concludes at the end of the day, and the winner is
      determined by the percentage of votes each candidate receives. Due to pre-existing social relation-
513
      ships, some voters may have initial preferences for certain candidates at the beginning of the task,
514
      so candidates must devise strategies to influence and shift voter opinions throughout the election
515   process.
516
517   Baselines We use an LLM-based agent as the baseline for this task. The agent’s behavior is deter-
518
      mined through iterative prompting of the LLM to identify which voter the candidate should visit
      next. After selecting the target voter, the candidate navigates to their location and delivers a cam-
519
      paign speech, also generated via LLM prompts. This process is repeated until the simulation ends.
520
      At the conclusion of the campaign, an election is held, during which each agent is prompted to
521   decide which candidate they will vote for.

529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
      We introduce Virtual Community, a generative social world for Embodied AI, featuring scalable
531
      scene generation and a community of embodied agents with grounded characters and social rela-
532   tionship networks. Virtual Community generation pipeline leverages rich real geospatial data and
533   open-world knowledge data of advanced generative models and creates infinite scenes and grounded
534   social agent communities. As an initial exploration of this simulator, we introduce two novel open-
535   world and social challenges, Route Planning and Election Campaign, which are developed and
536   tested using various baseline methods. These experiments highlight the difficulty of the challenges
537   enabled by our new virtual social world. We hope Virtual Community can help advance the Embod-
538   ied AI research towards building embodied generalist intelligence that can handle the difficulty of
539   the real world and coexist with the human community.
