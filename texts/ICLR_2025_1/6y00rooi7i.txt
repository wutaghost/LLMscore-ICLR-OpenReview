011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we introduce an innovative framework that combines Hierarchical
014            Reinforcement Learning (HRL) with Large Language Models (LLMs) to tackle
015            the challenges of complex, sparse-reward environments. A key contribution of our
016            approach is the emphasis on imitation learning during the early training stages,
017            where the LLM plays a crucial role in guiding the agent by providing high-level
018            decision-making strategies. This early-stage imitation learning significantly accel-
019
               erates the agent’s understanding of task structure, reducing the time needed to adapt
               to new environments. By leveraging the LLM’s ability to generate abstract repre-
020
               sentations of the environment, the agent can efficiently explore potential strategies,
021
               even in tasks with high-dimensional state spaces and delayed rewards. Our method
022            utilizes the LLM to assist action sampling via a dynamic annealing strategy and
023            aids the policy learning process through an LLM-based policy and value regular-
024            izer. This approach reduces computational costs and enhances the agent’s learning
025            process. Experimental results across three environments—MiniGrid, NetHack, and
026            Crafter—demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms baseline LLM-
027            based HRL algorithms in terms of training speed and success rates. The imitation
028            learning phase proves critical in enabling the agent to adapt quickly and perform
029            efficiently, highlighting the potential of integrating LLMs into HRL for complex
030
               tasks.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Reward sparsity is a persistent challenge in the early stages of exploration for reinforcement learning
036
      (RL) environments. As these environments grow more complex, the difficulty for agents to encounter
037
      rewards during initial exploration increases significantly. Researchers have continuously worked on
038   addressing reward sparsity to efficiently train agents (Vecerik et al., 2017; Hare, 2019). Hierarchical
039   reinforcement learning (HRL) offers a promising approach to addressing these issues. In HRL, the
040   decision-making problem is decomposed into two levels: high-level decision making, referred to as
041   an option, and low-level decision making, referred to as an action. Experimental evidence (Kulkarni
042   et al., 2016; Nachum et al., 2018b) suggests that HRL can address challenges that traditional RL
043   algorithms struggle with, demonstrating superior performance in general environments. However,
044   despite HRL’s potential, most approaches rely on predefined options and often require pre-training of
045   the option networks. This makes HRL notoriously difficult to implement and tune effectively.
046   LLMs have shown potential in mitigating some of these challenges. Recently, LLMs have demon-
047   strated their versatility across many domains, including natural language processing, code generation,
048   and decision-making tasks such as game playing and intelligent question-answering (Du et al., 2023).
049   A series of recent works (Zhou et al., 2024) suggest that LLMs can be leveraged as high-level
050   decision-makers to enhance the performance of RL agents. While promising, it is well-known that
051   LLM-based approaches are resource-intensive, which makes them less ideal compared to traditional
052
      RL methods that are computationally lighter. This leads to the following question:
053
                        What is the most effective strategy to accelerate RL with LLMs?

103   2.1   H IERARCHICAL R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (HRL)
104
105   The emergence of hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) has proven to be beneficial for address-
106   ing complex, large-scale problems and sparse, delayed rewards (Nachum et al., 2019). By introducing
107   sub-goals and training multi-level structures, HRL effectively enhances exploration. However, a
      key challenge in HRL lies in establishing high-quality hierarchical structures to improve training


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      efficiency. One approach to establishing hierarchical structures involves manually setting them based
109   on tasks, such as graph-guided reinforcement learning (Lee et al., 2022; Gieselmann & Pokorny,
110   2021), or by artificially setting sub-goals (Florensa et al., 2017; Tessler et al., 2017). The intervention
111   of human priors renders the model non-generalizable. Additionally, there have been proposals to
112   allow agents to autonomously learn sub-tasks. Some achieve this by associating the sub-task space
113   with the current state (Zhang et al., 2020; Nachum et al., 2018a; Vezhnevets et al., 2017), while others
114   consider predicting the next sub-goal during the training process (Pitis et al., 2020).
115   To enhance training efficiency, some researchers have tackled this issue by constraining the tasks
116   of higher-level agents, limiting the state space of sub-tasks to the adjacent range of the current
117   state (Zhang et al., 2020). Luo et al. (2023) proposed introducing attention rewards to enable
118   higher-level agents to focus more on the environment. Others build a causality-driven hierarchical
119   reinforcement learning framework, leveraging a causality-driven discovery instead of a randomness-
120   driven exploration (Hu et al., 2023). Hierarchical in-Context Reinforcement Learning (HCRL) further
121   integrates in-context learning with HRL frameworks to dynamically generate sub-goals based on
122   ongoing task progress. This framework, through reflection and modularity, allows for correction of
123
      sub-task errors and improves task efficiency (Sun et al., 2024). LLM Augmented Hierarchical Agents
      have also leveraged LLMs to improve high-level decision-making, leading to better performance in
124
      long-horizon tasks (Prakash et al., 2024).
125
126
127   2.2   LLM AGENT
128
129   Previous research has demonstrated that language can facilitate the construction of abstract repre-
130   sentations of both the environment and goals, especially in scenarios involving high-dimensional
131   state spaces and long planning horizons (Lin et al., 2023; Andreas et al., 2017; Akakzia et al., 2020;
132   Mirchandani et al., 2021). For example, Wu et al. (2019) showed that language could assist in learning
133   complex tasks, even when naive goal representations fail. By employing language-guided hindsight
134   goal relabeling, their approach achieved significant performance improvements. Jiang et al. (2019)
135   further explored the use of language in goal specification, demonstrating that high-level policies could
136   achieve their objectives by composing sub-policies guided by language. These studies underscore the
137
      utility of language as a compositional tool in RL, enabling more effective learning and generalization
      in complex, temporally-extended tasks.
138
139   Language-assisted reinforcement learning provides agents with a high-level understanding of tasks
140   and environments. LLMs, with their powerful language processing capabilities, further expand this
141   field by offering more nuanced task guidance, abstract representations, and decision support (Kwon
142   et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023). Yao et al. (2023) introduced ReAct, a method where
143
      the LLM generates “thoughts” to address problems based on observations. Building on ReAct,
      Shinn et al. (2024) developed Reflexion, which leverages a few-shot verbal feedback approach to
144
      improve decision-making abilities. Ahn et al. (2022) provided another viewpoint, endowing LLMs
145
      with foundational “skills” along with corresponding value functions and affordance functions. With
146   LLMs’ assistance, each action selection is comprehensively considered, integrating the reward (value)
147   of the current action and its feasibility (affordance). Nottingham et al. (2023) describe how to
148   leverage Brief Language Inputs for Decision-making Responses (BLINDER) to condense the input
149   text to LLMs, effectively reducing the cost of invoking LLMs by approximately sixfold. Building on
150   these foundational studies, Li et al. (2023) proposed Interactive Task Planning (ITP), a framework
151   that leverages LLMs for dynamic task planning and replanning in robotic systems. By integrating
152   high-level planning with low-level skill execution, ITP enables robust adaptation to user feedback
153   and novel tasks without the need for task-specific training or extensive prompt engineering.
154

      2.3   I MITATION L EARNING
156
157
158
      In many previous studies, imitation has become a strategy to develop the performance of reinforcement
      learning.
159
160   Introduced by Price & Boutilier (2003), the student agent can observe the state transitions induced by
161   the mentor’s actions and use the information gleaned from these observations to update the estimate
      of the value of its own states and actions, thus accelerating the process of RL training.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      What’s more, Oh et al. (2018) describes the Self-Imitation Learning strategy, which can utilize
163   experience from the past. The agent will learn from its past options and rewards to develop itself, i.e.,
164   the agent itself is both the student and the mentor.
165
166
      As for hierarchical cases, Le et al. (2018) provides a method to expand imitation learning to HRL.
      It provides a method to divide HRL into high-level IL and low-level RL. The meta-controller takes
167
      actions by imitating an expert agent, while the lower controller receives commands from the meta-
168
      controller, which is the same as in HRL.
169
170   Recent work in imitation learning has introduced the Hindsight Modular Reflection (HMR) framework,
171   which allows agents to learn from both failed and successful trajectories by reflecting on sub-task
172
      failures and incorporating those insights into future decisions. This method effectively addresses
      sparse reward environments by using hindsight experience replay to convert hard-to-achieve goals
173
      into manageable intermediate sub-goals (Sun et al., 2024).

178   3.1   P RELIMINARY: DEFINITION OF AGENTS
179
180   Hierarchical Markov Decision Process We define a Hierarchical Markov Decision Process (HMDP)
181
      as M = (S, A, O, P, r, γ), where S is the state space, which includes all possible states the system
      can be in. A is the action space, which consists of all possible low-level actions an agent can take. O
182
      is the option space, which consists of all high-level actions. For instance, for the MiniGrid game, the
183
      high-level action can be some general instructions (“grab a bear"), and the low-level action can be the
184   detailed action (“move right for 2 seconds"). P : S × A × S → [0, 1] is the transition probability
185   function, where P (s′ |s, a) represents the probability of transitioning from state s ∈ S to state s′ ∈ S
186   after taking action a ∈ A. r : S × A → R is the reward function, which defines the reward r(s, a) the
187   agent receives after taking action a in state s. γ ∈ [0, 1] is the discount factor, which determines the
188   importance of future rewards relative to immediate rewards. In our work, we are interested
                                                                                             P∞ in finding
189   a policy π(·|·) : S → ∆(A), which maximizes the expected cumulative reward E[ t=0 γ t rt ].
190   Large Language Model Our framework utilizes a language model LLM to help the agent to find
191   the optimal policy. We briefly introduce the details of them here. Let L be the language space that
192   consists of sentences in the natural language. Let LLM(·|·) : L → ∆(O) be the LLM that takes the
193   language as its input which outputs an option. For instance, the input of an LLM can be “Agent sees
194   a key and hold nothing", and the output is some high-level option like “go to key, pick up key".

211   3.2   P ROPOSED A LGORITHM
212
213   We propose our algorithm, IHAC, as outlined in Algorithm 1 . Broadly, the algorithm consists of two
214   phases: the LLM imitation learning phase and the standard RL phase. In the first phase, the agent
215   focuses on training its policy network, πθ , and value network, Qw , to an effective state. Crucially,
      IHAC accelerates this process by leveraging high-level suggestions provided by a language model,


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      LLM, which significantly boosts learning efficiency, especially in the early stages when data samples
217   are limited. We will provide further details on how LLM enhances the learning process later in the
218   text. At the conclusion of the first phase, IHAC outputs a well-trained policy network, πθ , and value
219   network, Qw , both of which have been shaped effectively with the assistance of the LLM. In the
220   second phase, IHAC begins using an existing RL algorithm, such as PPO, to continue refining the
221   policy, starting from the already optimized πθ and Qw .
222
      Why two-phase? Our two-phase algorithm design offers several advantages. First, it enables efficient
223   token usage during training. Specifically, in our approach, the LLM is invoked only during the
224   imitation learning phase, which constitutes a small portion of the overall learning process (in our
225   experiments, for instance, it accounts for no more than 20 percent of the total epochs). This reduces
226   the computational overhead from frequent LLM queries and minimizes overall token consumption.
227   Second, the two-phase setup, along with key design components, allows for a dynamic trade-off
228   between the LLM and the reinforcement learning (RL) approach. This structure combines the
229   efficiency of the LLM with the superior effectiveness of the RL agent.

232   3.3   D ETAILS OF IHAC
233
234   From here we discuss several key algorithm design innovations of our IHAC.
235   High-Level Language Model Option One of our key observations is that LLMs can provide effective
236   high-level suggestions. For instance, if we provide the LLM with the current game status, such as
237   "Agent sees a key and holds nothing," the LLM can generate general solutions based on its common-
238   sense knowledge. However, utilizing these high-level solutions remains challenging, especially when
239   the gap between high-level strategies and low-level actions is significant. To address this, we propose
240   several algorithmic components, as shown in Line 5. We introduce a translator module, denoted as
241   Trans(·) : S → L, which is an embedding model that maps any state s to a language description
242   l. For example, in the MiniGrid game, Trans could take the abstract grid state as input and output
243
      a sentence describing the game status, such as "the player is in the corner with a knife in hand."
      Additionally, we use a predefined option module, denoted as ActionNet : O × S → A, which infers
244
      an action a based on both the high-level option and the current state. To summarize, as suggested in
245
      Line 5, we combine these elements into a composite LLM module, denoted as πLLM , representing the
246   LLM’s guidance policy.
247
248   To improve efficiency, we limit the LLM’s choices to a predefined set of options. This design
249
      reduces the complexity of the decision-making process, enabling more focused and relevant outputs.
      Additionally, the prompt structure ensures that the LLM’s suggestions are reasonable and aligned
250
      with task goals, while achieving token efficiency by invoking the LLM only when necessary and
251
      restricting its output to predefined options.
252
253   Annealing Strategy in Sampling Given πLLM , we now demonstrate how it can be leveraged to
254   accelerate the RL agent’s learning process during Phase I. We first focus on Line 6. Essentially,
255
      actions are selected by considering both the suggestions from the RL policy πθ and the guidance
      from πLLM . At stage h, the action ah is sampled as follows:
256
257
258                                  ah ∼ (1 − λt )πθ (·|sh ) + λt · πLLM (·|sh ).
259
260   Here, 0 < λt < 1 is the imitation ratio, which represents the influence of πLLM . Initially, λt is set to a
261   higher value, allowing the LLM’s policy to significantly guide action selection. This aids in exploring
262   the environment more effectively, especially when reward signals are sparse. As training progresses,
263   λt is gradually annealed, reducing the influence of πLLM and enabling the agent to rely increasingly
264   on its own policy, πθ , which becomes more refined over time. This sampling design, combined with
265   the annealing strategy, addresses challenges related to sparse rewards and ensures a smooth transition
266
      from exploration to exploitation. Ultimately, this approach enhances the reinforcement learning
      agent’s performance.

461   phase, where LLM guidance is used once per step. As detailed results shown in Appendix 6, in
462   difficult and long-term environment, IHAC only consumes less than 2% of the tokens consumed by

468   acting inefficiently, also faces high token usage due to the complex environment. Specifically, 11

470   requiring the agent to query the LLM 11 times per step to obtain the option distribution. This frequent
471   querying is the primary factor driving up token consumption. Both LLM×HRL and LLM4Teach
472   query the LLM throughout the entire training process, resulting in a large token overhead. Detailed
473   baseline prompt designing methods are shown here Appendix A. Our approach, on the other hand,
474
      enables the model to rapidly learn an initial policy, effectively addressing the sparse reward problem

520   5    C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this work, we addressed the challenges of solving complex tasks with sparse rewards by proposing
523   a novel two-phase training framework that combines imitation learning and reinforcement learning.
524   Our approach efficiently leverages LLMs during the early imitation learning phase, allowing the
525   agent to rapidly acquire foundational skills and significantly accelerate reinforcement learning.
526   A key contribution of our framework is the strategic use of LLMs restricted to the pre-training
527
      phase, which substantially reduces token consumption while maintaining strong performance. This
      lightweight and efficient design balances the powerful reasoning capabilities of LLMs with practical
528
      resource constraints, making it suitable for real-world applications. Additionally, the integration of a
529
      hierarchical structure combining value-based and policy-based learning enables faster convergence
530   and better task generalization. Techniques such as annealed sampling and adaptive policy training
531   further enhance learning efficiency by ensuring a smooth transition from LLM-guided exploration
532   to agent-driven exploitation. Our experimental results demonstrate that the proposed framework
533   achieves superior performance with improved sample efficiency and reduced computational costs
534   compared to baseline methods. Future work will focus on extending this framework to more complex
535   environments and exploring advanced strategies to further reduce reliance on LLMs.
