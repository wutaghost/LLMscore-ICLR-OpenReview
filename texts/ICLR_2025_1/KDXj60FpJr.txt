009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems have shown promise in improving
012                task performance by leveraging external context, but realizing their full potential
013                depends on careful configuration. In this paper, we investigate how the choice of
014                retriever and reader models, context length, and context quality impact RAG per-
015
                   formance across different task types. Our findings reveal that while some readers
                   consistently benefit from additional context, others degrade when exposed to irrele-
016
                   vant information, highlighting the need for tuning based on reader sensitivity to
017
                   noise. Moreover, retriever improvements do not always translate into proportional
018                gains in reader results, particularly in open-domain questions. However, in spe-
019                cialized tasks, even small improvements in retrieval can significantly boost reader
020                results. These insights underscore the importance of optimizing RAG systems by
021                aligning configurations with task complexity and domain-specific needs. 1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
025
      Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Chen et al., 2017; Lewis et al., 2020) is a technique widely
026
      applied to enhance the performance of top-performing LMs on knowledge-intensive generation tasks
027
      like document-based question answering (Karpukhin et al., 2020). Given a question, the technique
028   uses a retriever model to obtain relevant passages from a corpus. These passages are then inputted to
029   a reader model as context for answering a given question.
030
031
      Although using RAG supposedly helps LMs generate “more specific and factually accurate responses”
      (Lewis et al., 2020), we show that, in practice, achieving the greatest benefits from RAG requires
032
      careful configuration of all components in the RAG pipeline. Existing literature provides mixed, even
033
      contradictory, suggestions for configuring RAG. While some early works suggest that providing more
034   retrieved passages results in strictly better outputs (Izacard & Grave, 2021), others find there is a
035   limit to that phenomenon as model performance saturates after some number of contexts (Liu et al.,
036   2023). In fact, some find that providing a select set of passages (Asai et al., 2022), sentences (Wang
037   et al., 2023), or tokens (Berchansky et al., 2023) outperforms providing as many contexts as possible.
038   Others find that reader model performance declines (Cuconasu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) as the
039   number of contexts gets too large. The complexity of choosing the number of passages is only one
040   aspect of RAG configuration among many that we cover in our analysis framework.
041   To provide more concrete suggestions of the best practices under various cases, we introduce an anal-
042   ysis framework, RAGGED, short for “retrieval augmented generation generalized evaluation device”,
043   to study RAG configurations on a suite of representative document-based question-answering (DBQA)
044   tasks, including open-domain datasets that are single-hop and multi-hop questions (Kwiatkowski
045   et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2018), and special-domain questions from the biomedical domain. We cover
046   a broad range of models to ensure a comprehensive analysis: for retrievers, we incorporate both
047   sparse and dense retrievers; for readers, we cover proprietary API models such as GPT (Brown et al.,
048
      2020) and C LAUDE (Enis & Hopkins, 2024), as well as open-checkpoint models including F LAN
      (Chung et al., 2022; Tay et al., 2023), LL A M A (Touvron et al., 2023b) families.
049

      2    T HE RAGGED F RAMEWORK
101
      In our analysis, we vary three key aspects:
102
103   1. RAG system components: We use three retrieval approaches: (1) BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009),
104      a sparse retriever based on lexical matching; (2) ColBERT (Santhanam et al., 2021), a dense
105      retriever using neural embeddings; and (3) Contriever (Izacard et al., 2022), an unsupervised dense
106      retriever leveraging contrastive learning for efficient document retrieval. For readers, we examine
107      closed-source models from the GPT and C LAUDE families, and open-source models from the
         F LAN, LL A M A 2, and LL A M A 3 families.


                                                                            2
108
      2. Number of retrieved passages (k): We vary the number of retrieved passages from 1 to 50, with
109      most insightful variations occurring before k = 30.
110
111   3. Data slices based on retrieved passage quality: Passage quality refers to the presence of “gold”
112
         passages, i.e., ground-truth passages, in the top-k retrieved set.

116   3     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
117
118   In this section, we describe the experimental setup, including the retriever and reader models, datasets,
119   and evaluation metrics used to assess the performance of different RAG configurations.
120
121   3.1   R ETRIEVER
122
123   We experiment with: (1) a sparse, lexical retriever and (2) a dense, semantic retriever.
124
125   BM25 BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009) is a probabilistic retrieval model that estimates passage
126   relevance via term weighting and passage length normalization. It relies on term-matching and is
127   supposed to be relatively proficient at identifying lexical similarity, especially in special domains.
128
129   ColBERT One of the best-performing neural-based retrievers is ColBERT (Santhanam et al., 2021),
130   i.e., contextualized late interaction over BERT. ColBERT uses contextualized embeddings instead of
131   term-matching as in BM25, and is supposed to be relatively suited for identifying semantic similarities
132   between queries and passages.
133
134   Contriever Contriever (Izacard et al., 2022) is an unupservised, dense retrieval model. Contriever,
135   like ColBERT, is also a dense retriever, but focuses more on overall semantic similarity instead of
136   fine-grained matching by operating at the document level instead of the token level.
137
138   3.2   R EADER
139
140   We analyze closed-source models from the GPT and C LAUDE families and open-source models from
141   the F LAN and LL A M A families.2
142
143   F LAN The F LAN models are encoder-decoder models. We use the F LAN T5-XXL (Chung et al.,
144   2022) with 11B parameters and F LAN-UL2 (Tay et al., 2023) with 20B parameters, both with a
145   context length of 2k tokens. F LAN T5-XXL is an instruction-tuned variant of the T5 model (Raffel
146
      et al., 2023). F LAN-UL2 (Tay et al., 2023) is an upgraded T5-based model that is trained with
      Unifying Language Learning Paradigm, a pertaining process that uses a mixture-of-denoisers and
147
      mode switching to improve the model’s adaptability to different scenarios.
148
149
150
      LL A M A We use 7B and 70B LL A M A 2 models (Touvron et al., 2023a;b) and the 8B and 70B
      LL A M A 3 models. The LL A M A 2 models have a context length of 4k tokens while LL A M A 3 models
151
      have double the context length to 8k tokens. The LL A M A 3 models have three major differences:
152
      (1) They use grouped query attention, which groups query heads to understand similar information
153   better, (2) Their vocabulary size is four times larger than that of LL A M A 2, (3) They are trained on a
154   seven times larger dataset than the LL A M A 2 training corpus.
155
156   GPT We mainly use GPT-3.5-turbo model (Brown et al., 2020). This model has a context length
157   of 16k tokens and is a closed-source API model, so further details about model configurations are
158   unknown. We also evaluate GPT-4 O which has a context length of 128,000 tokens. However, due to
159   the high cost, we only evaluate it on a small subset of experiments in Appendix K.
160
         2
161        While stronger LMs are available, we use ones with more affordable inference costs to allow for a large
      number of experiments over long contexts.


                                                           3
162
      C LAUDE We use C LAUDE -3-H AIKU, which is Anthropic’s fastest and most compact model (Enis
163   & Hopkins, 2024). The context window of 200k tokens is the largest of all the models we compare in
164   this paper, but the model size is unknown since the model is closed-source.

      3.3   DATASETS
167
168   We adopt three DBQA datasets from various domains (Wikipedia, biomedical) and of various
169   complexity (single-hop, multi-hop). More details are at Table 3 and Table 4.
170
171   Natural Questions We choose Natural Questions (NQ) (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), a Wikipedia-
172   based dataset, to examine how models perform on generic, open-domain, single-hop questions. NQ
173   questions are actual user-search queries on Google. We adopt the KILT version (Petroni et al., 2021)
174   of the dataset, which provides one short phrase answer and at least one gold passage for each question.
175
176   HotpotQA We choose HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), a multi-hop, Wikipedia-based dataset,
177   to examine how effectively models can identify multiple signal passages and reason over them
178   simultaneously. Each question requires reasoning over at least two passages to answer.
179
180   BioASQ We choose BioASQ’s Task 11B (Krithara et al., 2023), a PubMed-based dataset, with
181   biomedical questions to examine how models perform on special-domain questions. Our evaluation
182   dataset is a compilation of the BioASQ Task 11B training and golden enriched set.
183
184   3.4   M ETRICS
185
186   Retriever Metric We evaluate retrieval performance using the recall@k metric, following Petroni
187   et al. (2021). Recall@k measures the fraction of ground-truth passages among the top-k retrieved
188   passages for a given query.
189
190   Reader Metric We use unigram F1 , which quantifies the overlap of unigrams in the reader output
191   and gold answer(Petroni et al., 2021). For each query, we compute the F1 score of the reader output
192   against the list of gold answers and report the highest score. We also demonstrated a LLM-based,
193   semantic metric using Kim et al. (2024) to evaluate correctness on a small subset and find the trends
194
      we observe using F1 still hold Appendix H.

196   4     W HEN DOES RAG SURPASS THE NO - CONTEXT BASELINE ?
197
198   Although RAG can potentially help ground LMs’ generations in retrieved contexts, it is unclear how
199   much these contexts help downstream performance, especially compared to a no-context baseline.
200   While Lewis et al. (2020) achieve state-of-the-art results across several QA tasks by augmenting T5
201   model with a fixed k number of documents, we find that the answer to the question of “When does
202   RAG outperform no-context baseline” is more nuanced. To the best of our knowledge, we are the
203   first to comprehensively explore this question across RAG configurations and datasets to find that
204   while some readers always benefit from RAG, regardless of k, others benefit only if k is large enough
205   or small enough, then a few never do regardless of k (Table 1). We first emphasize key observations
206
      in this section, then provide explanations in the latter sections.
207   Closed-source models marginally improves with RAG For instance, as shown in Table 1, GPT-
208   3.5 generally performs better with RAG, regardless of the context quantity k. However, its gains
209   over its no-context baseline are marginal. When averaged across values of k, GPT-3.5 only improves
210   by 1.1 F1 points, and its maximum gain is just 3.8 F1 points (Table 5, Table 6). On NQ in particular,
211   where GPT-3.5 already sets the no-context bar high at 52 F1 points, it requires at least 5 passages
212
      to start benefitting from RAG with ColBERT. And in an extreme case, GPT-3.5, when paired with
      BM25, always performs worse than its no-context baseline, regardless of k.
213
214   C LAUDE -3-H AIKU struggles to benefit from RAG and is, in fact, harmed by using RAG for single-
215   hop questions from both open-domain and special-domain (i.e., NQ, BioASQ). Even when it does
      benefit from RAG on HotpotQA, it only does so marginally with a gain of < 4 F1 points.

254
      even benefit from using RAG, since a larger k may introduce too much distracting information. Their
      sensitivity to k is reflected in the large difference between their average gains (5 to 7 F1 points) and
255
      optimal gains (8 to 12 F1 points).
256
257   On the other hand, LL A M A 3 models show minimal improvement, if any, from RAG. In many cases,
258   they fail to outperform closed-book generation, regardless of k.
259   Across question types and domains Among the datasets, performance on the multi-hop dataset
260   consistently shows the highest gains from RAG in terms of average and optimal performance.
261   One hypothesis is that language models often rely on memorized facts from pretraining for single-
262   hop questions, making retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) less beneficial. In contrast, multi-
263   hop questions require synthesizing information from multiple sources, which is less likely to be
264   internalized during pretraining. Therefore, RAG could be more helpful for multi-hop tasks by
265   providing disparate pieces of information that the model cannot generate from memory alone.
266   While Kandpal et al. (2023) do not explicitly compare open-domain (e.g., NQ) and specialized-
267   domain (e.g., BioASQ) questions, they examine the effect of RAG relative to the number of relevant
268   pretraining documents, finding that RAG outperforms closed-book generation, particularly for rare
269   examples. Building on this, we hypothesize that open-domain questions may have more relevant
      pretraining documents while specialized-domain questions have fewer. Using BM25, we indeed find


                                                          5
270
      that BioASQ enjoys a higher gain (4.31 F1 points) than NQ (0.75 F1 points). However, we find the
271   opposite trend with ColBERT — NQ shows a larger gain (9.44 F1 ) compared to BioASQ (7.26 F1 ).
272   This suggests that the question domain alone does not determine the relative optimal gain.

      Key Takeaways: The benefits of RAG depend on the reader choice and their sensitivity to context
      quantity and quality. For some readers, the number of context passages is critical for achieving
275
      optimal performance. In such cases, a suboptimal RAG configuration can lead to worse performance
276
      than not using RAG. In the next section, we explore the influence of context quantity on RAG
277   performance.

403   and HotpotQA (Figure 8), most models perform worse with RAG than without. This is expected
404   since these slices the models are prompted to rely on do not contain any signal (i.e., gold passages).
405   F LAN Outperforms Baseline with Partially Relevant Non-gold Passages In contrast, F LAN
406   models consistently outperform their no-context baselines even with non-gold contexts. One potential
407   explanation is that the non-gold passages may still provide partially relevant information despite
408   insufficient information. In particular, F LAN models seem better than other readers at processing
409   the information from these non-gold passages. For example, on NQ with k = 5, the FLAN models
410   achieve 20% accuracy when no gold paragraphs are retrieved but paragraphs from the gold Wikipedia
411
      pages are present. This is notably higher than the performances from LL A M A 2 (8%) LL A M A 3
      models (4%), GPT-3.5 (10%), and C LAUDE -3-H AIKU (3%).
412
413   Another anomaly is that for BioASQ, GPT and LL A M A 2 7B’s top-k performances both exceed their
414   no-context baselines at select ranges of k (Figure 9). This contrasts with how their top-k performance
415   is consistently worse than their no-context performance on the open-domain datasets, regardless of k.
416   This suggests these models potentially have stronger guardrails for handling irrelevant information in
417
      specialized-domain questions.
418
419
420   Key Takeaways: Reader performance in RAG systems depends heavily on handling noisy or
421   irrelevant contexts, which is critical for real-world applications where retrieval is imperfect. Robust
422   models are better suited for production systems with variable retrieval quality, ensuring stable
423   performance. For models sensitive to irrelevant information, noise-filtering techniques are essential
424   to maintain performance. These findings highlight the need to tailor RAG configurations by selecting
425
      models with strong noise-handling capabilities and adapting retrieval strategies appropriately.

455   Better Retriever ̸= Better RAG Performance If a retriever has better recall@k for a specific k, it
456   typically leads to better reader performance at that k. However, for less robust, peak-then-decline
457   models (e.g., LLaMA 2 and 3), we observe that the reader F1 score with ColBERT can still be lower
458   than with BM25 at larger k’s even when ColBERT achieves higher recall@k than BM25 across all
459   k’s. This is reflected in the negative average reader F1 difference in Table 2.
460   One potential reason this discrepancy arises is that the content retrieved beyond the gold paragraphs
461   plays a critical role, particularly at higher k’s. Neural retrievers like ColBERT may introduce more
462   semantically complex or noisy content, which can overwhelm noise-sensitive readers. In contrast,
463   BM25, as a lexical retriever, often provides simpler or less distracting context, aligning better with
464   these readers’ preferences despite retrieving fewer gold paragraphs overall.
465   Large Retriever Gains only deliver Modest Gains for Open-domain Questions While ColBERT
466   delivers substantial improvements in recall for open-domain questions (21.3 recall@k gain for NQ
467   and 14.6 for HotpotQA) , the corresponding reader performance gains are more modest — 5.2 and
468   1.9 F1 points, respectively. In fact, the ratio of reader gain to retriever recall improvement is quite low
469   for HotpotQA (0.13) than NQ (0.24), indicating that a significant improvement in retriever may not
470   yield an equally significant reader performance boost for open-domain questions.
471   Small Retriever Gains yield Large Reader Gains for Specialized Domains In contrast, BioASQ’s
472   ratio of reader F1 gain to retriever recall gain is much higher at 2.08. Although ColBERT’s recall
473   improvement over BM25 for BioASQ is small (0.7 vs. 14.6 for HotpotQA), the reader performance
474   gains are comparable across both datasets. This suggests that in specialized domains, even a small
475   improvement in retriever performance can have an outsized impact on reader results.
476
      Computational Trade-offs Given how 1) ColBERT only results in small optimal reader gains for
477   HotpotQA and BioASQ and 2) BM25 is less computationally expensive to use, it may be tempting to
478   claim BM25 is the obvious pick for RAG, computationally speaking. However, another important
479   factor to consider is the difference in optimal k — the optimal k with BM25 performance is 2
480   to 3 times that of the optimal k for ColBERT (Table 7). This means BM25’s higher k shifts the
481   computational burden from the retriever to the reader, where the cost of inference is scaled with k.
482
483   Key Takeaways: Using a neural retriever instead of a lexical one does not always lead to better
484   reader performance, especially for less robust models and higher k’s. This highlights the importance
485   of understanding the retriever-reader compatibility, and not just evaluating the retriever components
      independently and assuming aligned reader performance.

486
      Nevertheless, we find that despite smaller retriever gains in specialized domains, we observe that
487   the downstream reader performance is more significantly amplified. Even marginal improvements in
488   retrieval can be highly valuable for domain-specific applications.

491   8    R ELATED W ORK
492
493   Impact of Varying Number and Quality of Contexts When deciding the number of retrieved
494   contexts to input into the reader model, existing findings provide seemingly contradicting findings.
495   Izacard & Grave (2021) find that increasing the number of contexts provides strictly better results,
496   while Liu et al. (2023) find the initial improvement is followed by performance saturation. On the
497
      other hand, some find that carefully selecting a subset of passages (Asai et al., 2022), sentences
      (Wang et al., 2023), or tokens (Berchansky et al., 2023) can outperform inputting maximal contexts.
498
      In fact, Cuconasu et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024) find that performance degrades with increasing k.
499
      Although existing findings seem contradictory, we think that they are actually complementary but
500   restricted by their limited experiments. We experiment with a wider variety of configurations and
501   datasets and arrive at a more nuanced conclusion: both the behavior of improve-then-plateau and
502   peak-then-decline exist, and the primary deciding factor is the choice of reader.
503
504   Domain Influence on Downstream Performance It is crucial to know when LMs benefit from
505   including retrieved passages in context, especially for special domains, which are of particular interest
506   in practice. Mallen et al. (2023) and Kandpal et al. (2023) both find that RAG helps for rare or
507   long-tail knowledge. However, Mallen et al. (2023) find that retrieving contexts may be unnecessary
508   and even detrimental when asking about common knowledge, while Kandpal et al. (2023) find using
509   RAG generally improves model performance. From extensively experimenting with various RAG
510   configurations and datasets, we provide a more nuanced view: performance gain on special domains
511   is not necessarily always larger than the gain on open domains, and instead largely depends on the
512
      retriever-reader combination.
513
514   Impact of Retriever Choice Lewis et al. (2020) show that dense retrievers like DPR outperform
515   sparse retrievers (BM25) in open-domain tasks and help readers achieve better downstream perfor-
516   mance on an open-domain dataset (i.e., NQ). Finardi et al. (2024) similarly find a positive correlation
517
      between retriever and reader performance, though their study is limited to a single, special-domain
      dataset. Both works demonstrate that dense retrievers lead to better reader outputs. In contrast,
518
      our extensive experiments show that better-performing retrievers do not necessarily lead to better
519
      downstream reader performance. We also comment on how important retriever choice — we find that
520   although the ratio of the reader gain to retriever gain is positive for all datasets, it is much larger for
521   specialized domains than for open-domain questions.
522
523
524   9    C ONCLUSION
525
526   We propose RAGGED, a framework designed to assist researchers and practitioners in making
527   informed decisions about designing RAG systems, focusing on three key aspects: the number of
528   contexts, the reader model, and the retriever model.
529   Using the framework, we demonstrate that while retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems
530   offer significant potential, their effectiveness depends on careful configuration. We find that some
531   readers benefit from additional context, while others degrade when exposed to irrelevant information,
532   underscoring the need for tuning based on reader behavior. Additionally, retriever improvements
533   do not always lead to proportional gains in reader performance, particularly in open-domain tasks
534   requiring multi-step reasoning. However, in specialized tasks, even minor improvements in retrieval
535   can substantially boost reader performance.
536   These findings highlight the importance of task-specific RAG configurations and suggest that future
537   research should focus on refining the interaction between retrievers and readers, improving model ro-
538   bustness to noisy contexts, and optimizing for domain-specific applications. We hope that researchers
539   and practitioners can use our framework to unlock the full potential of RAG systems in various
      applications.

791   E    R ELATING R EADER T RENDS TO R EADER A RCHITECTURES AND T RAINING
792        D ETAILS
793
794   There are two primary types of readers observed in our experiments:
795
796
797          • Peak-then-Decline Behavior: Models including those from the LL A M A and C LAUDE
798            families show sensitivity to noisy documents, leading to performance degradation as the
799            number of retrieved passages (k) increases beyond a certain point.
800          • Improve-then-Plateau Behavior: Models including those from the GPT and F LAN families
801            are more robust to noise, continuing to benefit from additional context until performance
802            plateaus.
803
804   Since we do not have access to the details of the closed-source models, we will focus on providing
805   hypotheses according to the open-source model (LL A M A belonging to the peak-then-decline behavior
806
      and the F LAN models belonging to the improve-then-plateau family).
807   On one hand, F LAN, an improve-then-plateau model family, incorporates additional strategies
808   explicitly designed to handle noisy or diverse contexts. It employs denoising strategies, such as a
809   mixture-of-denoisers, during training to improve its robustness to irrelevant or noisy contexts. These
      enhancements enable it to filter out noise more effectively.


                                                       15
810
      On the other hand, LL A M A ’s training predominantly relies on next-token prediction with limited
811   exposure to noisy or retrieval-specific scenarios, making it sensitive to noise at higher k.
812
813   We also note that there are some model architecture features that alone do not determine reader
814
      behavior:
815
             • Context window size: Models with longer context limits like LL A M A 2 (4k tokens) don’t
816            necessarily process a larger number of contexts better than models with smaller context
817            limits like F LAN (2k tokens).
818
             • Encoder-decoder v. decoder: LL A M A is a decoder-only model that displays peak-then-
819
               decline behavior, but GPT models are also decoder-only and instead display improve-then
820            plateau behavior.
