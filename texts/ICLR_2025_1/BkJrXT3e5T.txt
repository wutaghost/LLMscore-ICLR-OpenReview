009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently video diffusion models have emerged as expressive generative tools for
012            high-quality video content creation readily available to general users. However,
013            these models often do not offer precise control over camera poses for video genera-
014            tion, limiting the expression of cinematic language and user control. To address this
015            issue, we introduce CamCo, which allows fine-grained Camera pose Control for
016            image-to-video generation. We equip a pre-trained image-to-video generator with
017            accurately parameterized camera pose input using Plücker coordinates. To enhance
018
               3D consistency in the videos produced, we integrate an epipolar attention module
               in each attention block that enforces epipolar constraints to the feature maps. Addi-
019
               tionally, we fine-tune CamCo on real-world videos with camera poses estimated
020
               through structure-from-motion algorithms to better synthesize object motion. Our
021            experiments show that CamCo significantly improves 3D consistency and camera
022            control capabilities compared to previous models while effectively generating
023            plausible object motion. Project page: https://camco2024.github.io/.

050   1   I NTRODUCTION
051
052   The rapid evolution of diffusion-based generative models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022;
053   Ramesh et al., 2021; 2022; Betker et al., 2023) has empowered general users to generate video content
      from textual or visual inputs easily. Recent advancements in video generative models (Khachatryan


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023; AI, 2023; Guo et al., 2023b) have underscored the importance of
055   user control (Wu et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023b;a; Peruzzo et al., 2024) over the generated content.
056   Rather than relying on the cumbersome and error-prone process of prompt engineering, controllable
057   generation techniques ensure that the output adheres to more specified and fine-grained control signals,
058   thereby enhancing user satisfaction by producing more desirable outcomes. Recent studies (Zhang
059   & Agrawala, 2023; Li et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2024) have incorporated additional training layers to
060   integrate these control signals, further refining content generation capabilities.
061   Despite the diverse control signals available (e.g., depth, edge map, human pose) (Wu et al., 2023;
062   Guo et al., 2023b;a; Peruzzo et al., 2024; Zhang & Agrawala, 2023; Li et al., 2023; Mou et al.,
063   2024), controlling camera viewpoints in generated content has received little attention. Camera
064   motion, a crucial filmmaking technique (Nielsen et al., 2007), enables content creators to shift the
065   audience’s perspective without cutting the scene (Sikov, 2020), thereby conveying emotional states
066   effectively (Yilmaz et al., 2023). This technique is vital for ensuring that videos are practically usable
067   in downstream applications such as augmented reality, filmmaking, and game development (Heimann
068   et al., 2014). It allows creators to communicate more dynamically with their audience and adhere to a
069
      pre-designed script or storyboard.
070   To enable camera control in content generation, early methods (Guo et al., 2023b;a) utilize a fixed
071   number of categories to represent camera trajectories, providing a coarse control over the camera
072   motion. They use LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to fuse the camera information and conditionally generate
073   videos that belong to certain categories of camera motion. To improve the camera motion granularity,
074   (Wang et al., 2023b) has proposed to use adapter layers to accept normalized camera extrinsic matrices.
075
      However, this approach uses one-dimensional numeric values to represent the camera pose, which
      struggles to precisely control the video content when provided with complex camera motion.
076
077   To overcome the above issues, we introduce CamCo, a camera-controllable image-to-video gen-
078   eration framework that produces 3D-consistent videos. Our framework is built upon a pre-trained
079   image-to-video diffusion model, preserving the majority of the original model parameters to maintain
080   its generative capabilities. To enhance the accuracy of camera motion control, we represent camera
081
      pose with Plücker coordinates that encode both camera intrinsics and extrinsics into a pixel-wise
      embedding, which is a dense conditioning signal for video generation. Moreover, to improve the
082
      geometric consistency of synthesized videos, we introduce a new epipolar constraint attention module
083
      in each attention block to enforce epipolar constraints across frames. Finally, we implement a data
084   curation pipeline that annotates in-the-wild video frames with estimated camera poses, enhancing
085   our capability of generating videos with large object motions. Our experiments on various domains
086   demonstrate that CamCo outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods in terms of visual quality,
087   camera controllability, and geometry consistency.
088
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows,
089
090
             • We propose CamCo, a novel camera-controllable image-to-video generation framework
091            that can generate high-quality, 3D-consistent videos.
092
             • We build the first 3D-consistent video diffusion model by adapting a pre-trained image-to-
093
               video diffusion model. We parameterize camera information via Plücker coordinates and
094
               incorporate epipolar constraints via new epipolar constraint attention modules.
095
096          • We introduce a data curation pipeline to handle in-the-wild videos with dynamic subjects
097
               and fine-tune CamCo on the curated dataset to enhance its ability to generate videos with
               both camera ego-motion and dynamic subjects.
098
099          • Our method exhibits superior 3D consistency, visual quality, and camera controllability
100            when compared with previous works.

105
106   The recent advances in diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al.,
107   2021; 2022; Betker et al., 2023) have provided users with enhanced flexibility in visualizing their
      imaginations. Leveraging large-scale image-text paired datasets (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Byeon

      et al., 2022), diffusion models have become the state-of-the-art generators for text-to-image (T2I)
109   synthesis. However, due to the lack of high-quality video-text datasets (AI, 2023; Blattmann et al.,
110   2023), researchers have extensively explored adapting text-to-image generators into text-to-video
111   (T2V) generators. Text2Video-Zero (Khachatryan et al., 2023) builds the first training-free video
112   generation pipeline. AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023b) constructs a motion module applicable to
113   various base T2I models. VideoLDM (Blattmann et al., 2023) and SVD (AI, 2023) inflate the T2I
114   models with additional temporal layers and train on curated datasets to improve the visual quality.
115   The recently released Sora model (Brooks et al., 2024) has demonstrated impressive video generation
116   results, drawing attention to transformer-based diffusion backbones (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al.,
117
      2024; Yu et al., 2023; 2024). Our work builds upon the open-source SVD (AI, 2023) model. We
      introduce novel camera conditioning and geometry-aware blocks to achieve camera controllability
118
      and geometry consistency for the challenging image-to-video generation task.

      2.2   C ONTROLLABLE C ONTENT C REATION
122   Along with the rapid development of generative models that produce content from various input
123   modalities, improving user control over generation has also attracted significant attention. Con-
124   trolNet (Zhang & Agrawala, 2023), T2I-adapter (Mou et al., 2024), and GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023)
125   pioneered the introduction of control signals, including depth, edge maps, semantic maps, and
126   object bounding boxes, to text-to-image generation. These approaches ensure the synthesis back-
127   bone (Rombach et al., 2022) remains intact while adding trainable modules to maintain both control
128   and generative capabilities. Similar approaches have been applied to video generation, supporting
129   controls like depth, bounding boxes, and semantic maps (Guo et al., 2023b;a; Wu et al., 2023;
130
      Peruzzo et al., 2024). However, controlling camera motion has received limited attention. AnimateD-
      iff (Guo et al., 2023b) and SVD (AI, 2023) explore class-conditioned video generation, clustering
131
      camera movements, and using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) modules to generate specific camera motions.
132
      MotionCtrl (Wang et al., 2023b) enhances control by employing camera extrinsic matrices as condi-
133   tioning signals. While effective for simple trajectories, their reliance on 1D numeric values results in
134   imprecise control in complex real-world scenarios. Our work significantly improves granularity and
135   accuracy in camera control by introducing Plücker coordinates and geometry-aware layers to image-
136   to-video generation. Concurrently, CameraCtrl (He et al., 2024) also employs Plücker coordinates
137   but focuses on the text-to-video generation while paying less attention to 3D consistency.
138
139   2.3   3D S CENE S YNTHESIS
140
141   Due to the lack of high-quality 3D scene datasets (Deitke et al., 2023b;a), it is challenging to adapt
142   the success of image and video generation (Betker et al., 2023; Ramesh et al., 2021; Rombach et al.,
143
      2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; 2024) into 3D generation tasks (Liu et al., 2023; Shi
      et al., 2023). DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2022) addresses this issue by introducing score distillation
144
      sampling, which distills knowledge from 2D diffusion models. This technique has been successfully
145
      applied to 3D object synthesis from text (Lin et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a)
146   and image (Xu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023) input. However, due to the limited
147   ability of 2D diffusion models to generate complex scenes, score distillation is not directly applicable
148   to scene generation. Consequently, compositional generation (Po & Wetzstein, 2023; Zhang et al.,
149   2023; Xu et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2023) has emerged as a popular approach to tackle these challenges.
150   Video generators (Voleti et al., 2024; Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024; Zuo et al., 2024) have also been
151   adapted to generate simple objects by conditioning the three degrees of freedom (DoF) camera
152   information on the time embeddings of diffusion models. However, these approaches are not directly
153   applicable to real-world scenarios because their simplifications of camera information do not suffice
154
      for real videos that contain diverse extrinsic and intrinsic parameters. Our work focuses on generating
      3D-consistent videos from in-the-wild images, providing insights into the challenging direction of
155
      3D scene synthesis.

188   3.1   I MAGE - TO - VIDEO G ENERATION
189
190   Problem Setting The task of image-to-video generation involves using a single image I0 as input
191   of a video generator to produce a sequence of output frames O1 , · · · , On with temporal consistency.
192   In our experiments, n is typically set to 14. To add camera control, we retain the original input
193   and output format while additionally introducing a set of camera information C1 , · · · , Cn . These
194   camera parameters Ci are used as fine-grained conditioning of the video generator, ensuring that the
195
      generated frames follow the viewpoint changes specified by the camera sequence.
196
197   Base Model Architecture We train our CamCo based on a publicly available pre-trained image-
198
      to-video diffusion model, Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) (AI, 2023). SVD is built upon Stable
      Diffusion 2.1 model (Rombach et al., 2022) that is originally trained through an EDM (Karras
199
      et al., 2022) framework. SVD inserts temporal convolution and attention layers after spatial layers
200
      following VideoLDM (Blattmann et al., 2023). Unlike previous works that only train the temporal
201   layers (Blattmann et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023b) or are training-free (Khachatryan et al., 2023), SVD
202   fine-tunes all parameters.
203
204   Training SVD incorporates a continuous-time noise scheduler (Karras et al., 2022), which supports
205   a continuous range of noise levels. The diffusion model learns to gradually denoise a high-variance
206   Gaussian noise towards the (video) data distribution x0 ∼ p0 . Let p(x; σ(t)) denote the marginal
207   probability of noisy data xt = x0 + n(t) where the added Gaussian noise n(t) ∼ N (0, σ 2 (t)I), the
208   iterative denoising process corresponds to the probability flow ordinary differential equation (ODE):

225   3.2   I NJECTING C AMERA C ONTROL TO V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODEL
226
227   Camera Parameterization Unlike generating canonicalized 3D objects that operate in a 3-DoF
228
      camera space, video diffusion models dealing with real-world dynamic scenes must manage 6-DoF
      cameras with diverse intrinsic parameters. Consequently, representing camera information using
229
      elevation (Voleti et al., 2024) or camera extrinsics (Wang et al., 2023b) is suboptimal. Moreover,
230
      using one-dimensional numeric values for camera extrinsic as conditioning leads to imprecise camera
231   control (Wang et al., 2023b) in challenging camera trajectories. To comprehensively represent both
232   camera extrinsic and intrinsic information, we draw inspiration from light field networks (Sitzmann
233   et al., 2021) and adopt Plücker coordinate (Jia, 2020). Specifically, let o be the ray origin and d
234   be the ray direction of a pixel. The Plücker coordinate is formulated as P = (o × d′ , d′ ), where ×
235   represents the cross product and d′ is the normalized ray direction d′ = ||d||
                                                                                 d
                                                                                     . Given camera extrinsics
236   E = [R|T] and intrinsics K, the ray direction du,v for 2D pixel located at (u, v) is defined as
237                 u
      d = RK−1 ( v ) + T. All camera poses are defined relatively to the first frame. Plücker coordinates
238                 1
      uniformly represent rays, making them a suitable positional embedding for the network, and they
239
      have proven successful in parameterizing 360-degree unbounded light fields (Sitzmann et al., 2021).
240
241
      Camera Control Module To incorporate the camera embeddings, we add a simple adapter layer
242
      to each temporal attention block in the pre-trained video generator. This design best preserves the
243
      generation abilities the base model acquired during pre-training (Zhang & Agrawala, 2023; Mou et al.,
244   2024; Li et al., 2023). Specifically, in each temporal attention block, we first concatenate the Plücker
245   coordinates with the network features along the channel dimension, and then pass them into the 1 × 1
246   convolution layer to project back to the original feature space. The projected features are passed
247   to the remaining temporal attention layers. Plücker embeddings are downsampled via interpolation
248   if their spatial resolution mismatches with the network features at bottleneck layers. Inspired by
249   ControlNet (Zhang & Agrawala, 2023), we zero-initialize partial weights of the convolution layer so
250   that at the start of training, the whole network remains unaffected by these additional layers, and we
251   gradually add the camera control through the gradient descent.
252
253   3.3   E NSURING 3D-C ONSISTENT G ENERATION
254
255   Drawback of vanilla architecture While using the Plücker coordinates results in more fine-grained
256   camera control, it does not directly ensure the geometric consistency of the generated videos. This
257
      inconsistency issue is primarily rooted in the inefficiency of the vanilla architecture of video diffusion
      models (e.g., SVD) in modeling geometric relationships across frames. Recall that the vanilla architec-
258
      ture introduces dense self-attention in spatial and temporal dimensions, where any pixel at any frame is
259
      free to attend to any other pixels, potentially leading to pixel-copying behaviors (Kant et al., 2024) that
260   appear correct but do not adhere to geometric constraints. To address this issue, we need to design an
261   attention masking mechanism to ensure each pixel attends to features that are geometrically correlated.
262
263
      Epipolar Constraint Attention (ECA) Due to the missing depth information from the in-the-wild
264   input images, we do not have the exact pointwise correspondences across frames. Nevertheless, for
265   each pixel in the target view, we can derive an epipolar line in the source view. Epipolar constraint
266   describes that the corresponding point of a feature in one image must lie on the epipolar line associated
267   with that feature’s projection in another image. We then propose epipolar constraint attention (ECA),
268   which conducts cross-attention between the features on the epipolar line and features at the target
269   location, ensuring that the current frames adhere to projective geometry. We visualize our ECA block
      in Fig. 2(b) and provide more details in Sec. A.

289   3.4   I MPROVING O BJECT M OTION
290
291   While the above design enforces good camera control and geometric consistency, the model can
292   easily overfit to generate only static scenes with little object motion because it lacks the training data
293   with dynamic scenes.
294
295
      Augmented Training Dataset with better Motion To overcome this issue, we need to augment
      our training dataset with dynamic videos that contain rich object motion. However, the number of
296
      available dynamic videos with camera poses is greatly limited due to the difficulties in collecting
297
      annotations. Consequently, we first randomly sample videos from the WebVid (Bain et al., 2021)
298   dataset, which only contains video frames and lacks camera annotations. Then, we annotate the
299   camera poses in each video clip using Particle-SfM (Zhao et al., 2022). Particle-SfM is a pre-trained
300   framework capable of estimating camera trajectories for monocular videos captured in dynamic
301   scenes. Due to the time-consuming nature of this process, we randomly sample 32 frames from each
302   original video to feed into the Particle-SfM (Zhao et al., 2022) pipeline.
303
304   Curating High-quality Samples Note that the WebVid dataset contains videos of various kinds,
305   including many with static cameras. To ensure the model learns to handle more challenging motions,
306   we need to filter out sequences where the estimated camera trajectories show minimal displacement.
307   Due to the inevitable ambiguity between object motion and camera motion, in-the-wild estimation
308
      of camera poses can be inaccurate. We thus use the number of points in the reconstructed sparse
      point clouds from Particle-SfM (Zhao et al., 2022) as an indicator of camera annotation quality. The
309
      intuition is that an accurate camera pose estimation comes from well-registered frames. If the frames
310
      have more 3D-consistent pixels, more point correspondences will contribute to solving the camera
311   parameters. With this indicator, we only use videos with accurate camera pose estimation and high
312   object motion, and construct a training set of high-quality 12,000 video sequences annotated with
313   camera poses.

425   motion algorithms can only estimate the scene structure up to a certain scale, we further canonicalize
426   the estimated camera poses before calculating the differences. Specifically, we follow our training
427
      protocol where we first convert the camera systems to be relative to the first frame and then normalize
      the scale of the scene by finding the furthest camera against the first frame.

      4.5   A BLATION S TUDIES
512
513   In this section, we conduct several ablation studies on the model variants, including different versions
514   of camera conditioning and the importance of our epipolar attention block. “w/o epipolar attention”
515   denotes the baseline where we remove the epipolar constraint attention from our model and only
516   use the temporal convolution layers. “w/o Plücker” refers to the model variant where we remove
517   the Plücker coordinate input in the temporal blocks and rely on the epipolar constraint attention to
518   modulate the camera control. “1-dim camera” means using 1-dimensional camera matrices as the
519
      conditioning signal for modulating the features in temporal blocks instead of using 2-dimensional
      Plücker coordinates. “Time embedding” refers to modulating the time embedding feature with camera
520
      matrices instead of using Plücker coordinates. As can be seen in Tab. 2, the full model delivers the
521
      best results in both 2D visual quality (FID and FVD) and camera pose accuracy (COLMAP error,
522   points, translation, and rotation error). In contrast, the model variants lack the ability of accurately
523   controlling the camera poses which can lead to deteriorated visual appearance.

533   5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we introduced CamCo, a camera-controllable framework that produces 3D-consistent
536   videos. Our method is built upon a pre-trained image-to-video diffusion model and introduces
537   novel camera conditioning and geometry constraint blocks to ensure camera control accuracy and
538   geometry consistency. Further, we proposed a data curation pipeline to improve the generation of
539   object motion. Our experiments on images of various domains demonstrate the superiority of CamCo
      against previous works in terms of camera controllability, geometry consistency, and visual quality.
