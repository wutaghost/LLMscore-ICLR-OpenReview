009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The most basic form of a rigid object is a cloud of unordered points, for example,
012            a set of corners or other salient features. The rigid shape of a point cloud in the
013            Euclidean plane is its SE(2)-equivalence class under rigid motion (a composition
014            of translations and rotations). We introduce complete invariants (with no false
015            negatives, no false positives) and a bi-Lipschitz continuous metric that satisfies all
016            axioms, provides a 1-1 matching between points in clouds, and is computable in a
017            quadratic time of the number m of points. The realizability property implies that
018            the space of all rigid clouds is efficiently parametrized by vectorial invariants like
019
               geographic coordinates. The new invariants justified that any of 130K+ molecules
               in the QM9 database is uniquely determined by the rigid shape of its atomic cloud.

      1   M OTIVATIONS FOR NEW COMPLETE AND BI - CONTINUOUS INVARIANTS
023
024
      Many real objects are rigid so that their shapes are preserved under rigid motion composed of trans-
025   lations and rotations in Rn Atz et al. (2021), which form the group SE(n). The slightly weaker
026   equivalence is by isometries (distance-preserving transformations), which form the group E(n).
027
028
      The basic input of a rigid shape is a cloud of m unordered points in Rn Wang & Solomon (2019).
      The practical cases are dimensions n ≤ 3 and larger numbers m (hundreds) of unordered points
029
      without outliers Shi et al. (2021). Because of noise, repeated measurements of the same object can
030
      produce slightly different point clouds that cannot be exactly matched with the original one by rigid
031   motion. If noise is ignored up to any threshold ε > 0, sufficiently many tiny perturbations make all
032   clouds equivalent by the transitivity axiom: if A ∼ B and B ∼ C, then A ∼ C Brink et al. (1997).
033
034
      Hence all small deviations between rigid classes of point clouds should be distinguished, all
      these classes live in a continuous space of rigid clouds. This important space was continuously
035
      parametrized only for m = 3 points. Even the case of m = 4 unordered points was open, see Fig. 1.

047   Machine learning mostly focused on discrete classifications (label prediction, clustering) or on im-
048   proving various success measures for finite datasets, which can be considered discrete samples (of
049   measure 0) in a continuous space of shapes. To make this approach generalizable to all real data
050
      outside finite datasets, we need to map continuous data spaces similar to a geographic map of Earth.
051   A continuous extension of machine learning needs new requirements because past accuracies were
052   developed for discrete classifications or finite data. Though the key concepts of complete invariants
053   and distance metrics were already studied Schmidt & Roth (2012); Li et al. (2021), Problem 1.1
      introduces new conditions such as realizability and point matching that were not previously stated.

054
      Problem 1.1 (clouds and rigid motion can be replaced by any data and relations). Find an invariant
055   I : {clouds of unordered points in Rn } → a space with a distance d satisfying the conditions below.
056
057   (a) Completeness: any clouds A, B are related by a rigid motion of Rn if and only if I(A) = I(B).
058   (b) Metric axioms: 1) d(a, b) = 0 ⇔ a = b; 2) d(a, b) = d(b, a); 3) d(a, b) + d(b, c) ≥ d(a, c).
059
      (c) Lipschitz continuity: there is a constant λ such that if each point of a cloud A ⊂ Rn is perturbed
060
      up to Euclidean distance ε, then the invariant I(A) changes by at most λε in the metric d.
061
062   (d) Realizability: the image space {I(A) | all clouds A ⊂ Rn of m unordered points} is
063   parametrized so that one can reconstruct A up to rigid motion from any realizable value of I.
064   (e) Point matching: there is a constant µ such that a distance d = d(I(A), I(B)) guarantees a
065   rigid motion matching all m unordered points of clouds A, B up to Euclidean distance µd.
066
      (f) Computability: for a fixed dimension n, the invariant I, metric d, reconstruction in (d), and 1-1
067
      point matching in (e) are all computable in polynomial time of the number m of points.             ■
068
069   The completeness (or injectivity) in (1.1a) means that an invariant I finalizes the discriminative
070   approach and provably distinguishes all clouds A ≁ B (not only from a finite dataset) that cannot
                                                         =
071   be matched by rigid motion, so I is a descriptor with no false negatives and no false positives. The
072   universal approximation aims for the completeness of infinite-size invariants Maron et al. (2019);
073   Keriven & Peyré (2019); Yarotsky (2022), so polynomial time in (1.1f) makes all conditions harder.
074
      A complete invariant can give a discontinuous metric, say d(A, B) = 1 for all non-equivalent clouds
075   without quantifying the similarity of near-duplicates. The Lipschitz continuity in (1.1c) is stronger
076   than the classical ε − δ continuity because the Lipschitz constant λ is universal for all inputs and
077   perturbations. Due to the first axiom in (1.1b), any metric d detects rigidly equivalent clouds by
078   checking if d(A, B) = 0. Without the first axiom, many more distances including the zero d ≡ 0
079   satisfy the other axioms and are called pseudo-metrics Brécheteau (2019). If the third axiom in
080   (1.1b) fails with any error ε > 0, results of clustering may not be trustworthy Rass et al. (2024).
081
      The realizability in (1.1d) implies that the invariant I is an invertible 1-1 map from the compli-
082
      cated Cloud Rigid Space CRS(Rn ; m) of classes of clouds under rigid motion to the explicitly
083
      parametrized space I(CRS(Rn ; m)) of realizable values. Then with 100% certainty, we can sample
084   any realizable value in I(CRS(Rn ; m)) and reconstruct its cloud A ⊂ Rn up to rigid motion.
085
086   Point matching in (1.1e) can be interpreted as the Lipschitz continuity of the inverse map I −1 so that
087   any close values I(A), I(B) guarantee the closeness of A, B under rigid motion. Conditions (1.1c,e)
088   mean that the metric d is bi-Lipschitz: ε/µ ≤ d(I(A), I(B)) ≤ λε, where ε is the minimum
089   perturbation needed to match all points of A, B. One can define metrics satisfying (1.1a,b,c) by
090
      minimizing deviations of unordered points over infinitely many rotations. Polynomial time in (1.1f)
      for all ingredients makes Problem 1.1 notoriously hard, previously solved only for m = 3 points.
091
092   Conditions (1.1a,b,c,f) and (1.1d,e,f) formalize the discriminative and generative goals, respectively.
093   A full solution to Problem 1.1 will imply that the rigid classes of clouds can be efficiently visualized
094   in the moduli space I(CRS(Rn ; m)) replacing any latent space of non-invariants or incomplete (or
095   discontinuous or non-realizable) invariants. Geographically, I(CRS(Rn ; m)) can be compared with
096   Earth’s map, where any location can be reconstructed with all properties (altitude, precipitation,
097   images, ...) from the latitude and longitude coordinates in known (realizable) ranges.
098
      Contributions: the new invariant Nested Distributed Projection solves Problem 1.1 for all clouds of
099
      m unordered points in dimension n = 2. Any cloud A ⊂ Rn can be reconstructed from a small part
100
      of the invariant (a vector in Rn(m−(n+1)/2) ) whose realizability in (1.1d) is guaranteed by explicitly
101
      written inequalities. Hence coordinates of this vector can be chosen in known ranges like latitude and
102   longitude on Earth maps. The appendices cover all dimensions n > 2 and visualize geographic-style
103   maps of cloud spaces for m = 4 points in R2 . The implementation is in supplementary materials.

      Ordered points. Kendall’s shape theory Kendall et al. (2009) studies m ordered points
109   p1 , . . . , pm ∈ Rn under isometries from the Euclidean group E(n). In this case, a complete in-
110   variant is the distance matrix Schoenberg (1935); Kruskal & Wish (1978) or the Gram matrix of
111   scalar products pi · pj , see (Weyl, 1946, chapter 2.9), Villar et al. (2021). A brute-force extension to
112   m unordered points requires m! matrices due to m! permutations, which is ruled out by (1.1f).
113
114   Point cloud registration for unordered points samples rotations Lin et al. (1986); Yang et al. (2020)
115   and uses scale-invariant features Lowe (1999; 2004) to approximately match clouds. Trying to
116
      sort points along a fixed direction or in a clockwise order around their center of mass leads to
      discontinuities because distant points can have equal projections to a line or a circle. A basis (say,
117
      of principal directions) of a cloud Toews & Wells III (2013); Rister et al. (2017); Spezialetti et al.
118
      (2019); Zhu et al. (2022); Kurlin (2024) is similarly unstable under perturbations of points in cases
119   of high symmetry, e.g. when eigenvalues degenerate, which often happens in real molecules for our
120   main application. Converting a cloud by using extra parameters into a more complex object such as
121   a continuous field R3 → R Chauvin et al. (2022) or the persistent homology transform leads to the
122   harder analog of Problem 1.1 for continuous surfaces instead of discrete clouds Turner et al. (2014).
123
      Geometric Deep Learning Bronstein et al. (2021) studies neural networks that guarantee invariance
124
      or equivariance Thomas et al. (2018); Kondor & Trivedi (2018); Cohen et al. (2019); Fuchs et al.
125   (2020); Deng et al. (2021). An equivariant descriptor E satisfies the weaker condition E(f (A)) =
126   Tf (E(A)) for any rigid motion f of a cloud A, where Tf may not be the identity as required for
127   invariants Satorras et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2021); Aronsson (2022); Assaad et al. (2023); Xu et al.
128   (2022); Su et al. (2022). Any linear combination of points such as the center of mass is equivariant
129   but cannot distinguish clouds under translation. Equivariants were used for predicting forces acting
130   on atoms to move them to a more optimal configuration. These time-dependent clouds At can be
131   studied directly by their invariant values I(At ) without intermediate forces. So neural networks
132   optimize millions of parameters as in (Goyal et al., 2021, Table 4) to improve accuracies Dong et al.
133
      (2018); Akhtar & Mian (2018); Laidlaw & Feizi (2019); Guo et al. (2019); Colbrook et al. (2022)
      but need re-training any for new data. All such networks will have better generalizability if the
134
      inputs are invariants that satisfy the conditions of Problem 1.1 for all possible point clouds in Rn .
135
136   General metrics between fixed clouds extend to their rigid classes by minimization over infinitely
137   many rigid motions Huttenlocher et al. (1993); Chew & Kedem (1992); Chew et al. (1999). In R2 ,
138   the time O(m5 log m) Chew et al. (1997) for the Hausdorff distance Hausdorff (1919) will be im-
139
      proved in Theorem 5.3 to O(m3.5 log m) for a new metric, see approximations in Goodrich et al.
      (1999). The Gromov-Hausdorff and Gromov-Wasserstein metrics Mémoli (2011) are defined for
140
      metric-measure spaces also by minimizing over infinitely many correspondences between points,
141
      but cannot be approximated with a factor less than 3 in polynomial time unless P=NP, see Corol-
142   lary 3.8 in Schmiedl (2017) and polynomial algorithms for partial cases in Majhi et al. (2024). Also,
143   computing a metric between rigid classes of clouds is only a small part of Problem 1.1. Indeed, to
144   efficiently navigate on Earth, in addition to distances between cities, we need a satellite-type view
145   of the whole planet and hence a realizable bi-continuous invariant I, which can be considered an
146   analog of the latitude and longitude coordinates on Earth.
147
      Can we ‘sense’ a shape? Informally, Problem 1.1 asks the questions ‘same or different clouds, and
148   how much different?’ The related problem ‘Can we hear the shape of a drum?’ Kac (1966) has the
149   negative answer in terms of 2D polygons that are indistinguishable by spectral invariants Gordon
150   et al. (1992a;b); Reuter et al. (2006); Cosmo et al. (2019); Marin et al. (2021). Problem 1.1 looks
151   for stronger invariants that can completely ‘sense’ (not only ‘hear’) the rigid shape of any cloud.
152

434
      QM9 has 130K+ (130,808) molecules of up to 29 atoms with 3D coordinates obtained by quantum
435

436   tical, we compare molecules as clouds of unordered atomic centers without labels. The complete
437   invariant NDP finalizes the hierarchy of the faster and progressively stronger invariants below.
438
      Definition 6.1 (invariants SRV, SDV, PDD). Let A ⊂ Rn be a cloud of m unordered points with
439
      the center of mass at 0 ∈ Rn . The Sorted Radial Vector SRV(A) has m radial distances |p| in
440
      decreasing order for all p ∈ A. The Sorted Distance Vector SDV(A) is the vector of m(m−1)       2
441
      pairwise distances |p − q| in decreasing order for distinct p, q ∈ A. For any integer k ≥ 1 and
442
      p ∈ A, let d1 (p) ≤ · · · ≤ dm−1 (p) be Euclidean distances from p to all other points q ∈ A − {p} in
443   increasing order. These distance lists become rows of the m × (m − 1) matrix D(S; k). Any l > 1
444   identical rows are collapsed into a single row with the weight l/m. The final matrix PDD(S; k) of
445   unordered rows with weights is the Pointwise Distance Distribution Widdowson & Kurlin (2022). ■
446
447   For up to m points, PDDs need sorting m distance lists in time O(m2 log m). Then PDDs are com-
448   pared by the Earth Mover’s Distance EMD Rubner et al. (2000) in time O(m3 ). Table 2 emphasizes
449   that most clouds should be first distinguished by simpler and faster invariants SRV, SDV, PDD, so
