009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the research significance and application value, drug-likeness prediction
012            aims to accurately screen high-quality drug candidates, and has attracted increas-
013            ing attention recently. In this regard, dominant studies can be roughly classified
014            into two categories: (1) Supervised drug-likeness prediction based on binary clas-
015            sifiers. To train classifiers, the common practice is to treat real drugs as positive
016            examples and other molecules as negative ones. However, the manual selection
017            of negative samples introduces classification bias into these classifiers. (2) Un-
018
               supervised drug-likeness prediction based on SMILES representations, such as an
               RNN-based language model trained on real drugs. Nevertheless, using SMILES to
019
               represent molecules is suboptimal for drug-likeness prediction, which is more rel-
020
               evant to the topological structures of molecules. Besides, the RNN model tends to
021            assign short-SMILES molecules with high scores, regardless of their structures. In
022            this paper, we propose a novel knowledge distillation based unsupervised method,
023            which exploits 2D features of molecules for drug-likeness prediction. The teacher
024            model learns the topology of molecules via two pre-training tasks on a large-scale
025            dataset, and the student model mimic the teacher model on real drugs. In this
026            way, the outputs of these two models will be similar on the drug-like molecules
027            while significantly different on the non-drug-like molecules. To demonstrate the
028            effectiveness of our method, we conduct several groups of experiments on various
029
               datasets. Experimental results and in-depth analysis show that our method signifi-
               cantly surpasses all competitive baselines, achieving state-of-the-art performance.
030
               Particularly, the prediction bias of SIMILES length is reduced in our method. We
031
               will release our code upon the acceptance of our paper.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Predicting the drug-likeness of novel molecules is an essential step during the initial phase of drug
037   discovery. Accurately screening candidates with high likelihood of advancing to clinical trials can
038   significantly reduce the cost of drug development. The drug-likeness of a molecule is associated
039   with its biological efficacy, toxicity, metabolic stability and other properties (Leeson & Springthorpe,
040   2007; Ursu et al., 2011). As there are so many factors involved, drug-likeness can not be measured
041   by a single quantity simply. Meanwhile, it is impossible to test all molecules in wet-lab experiments.
042   Therefore, how to automatically predict drug-likeness becomes one of the focuses for researchers.
043   Early researchers summarize some empirical rules to determine whether a molecule has drug poten-
044   tial, such as the Lipinski’s rule of five (RO5) (Lipinski, 2004) and the concept of pan-assay inter-
045   ference compounds (PAINS) (Baell & Holloway, 2010), which detects whether a molecule contains
046   structure alerts. Nevertheless, it is observed that there is a certain percentage of approved drugs do
047   not conform to some of these rules (Bickerton et al., 2012a; Yusof & Segall, 2013). Unlike these
048   studies, Bickerton et al. (2012b) introduce the quantitative estimate of drug-likeness (QED) to quan-
049   tify drug-likeness, so as to avoid the aforementioned strict cut-off. Despite this effort, Beker et al.
050
      (2020) show that this metric is indistinguishable for drugs and non-drug molecules.
051   To overcome the limitations of the above methods relying on human-derived rules, researchers shift
052   their attention to deep learning based models. In this regard, dominant studies (Hu et al., 2018;
053   Beker et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022) employ real drugs as positive samples and the molecules from
      other databases as negative samples to train binary classifiers. However, the chemical space of non-

068   In response to this challenge, Lee et al. (2022) resort to unsupervised drug-likeness prediction. They
069   first represent molecules as SMILES and use real drugs to train an RNN-based language model. Sub-
070   sequently, the drug-likeness of a molecule is calculated based on the likelihood of its corresponding
071
      SMILES. Despite of its success, representing molecules as SMILES has some limitations: (1) Uti-
      lizing SMILES to represent molecules is less preferable than employing 2D graphs. This is because
072
      the drug-likeness of a molecule is more related to its topological structure, which can be naturally
073
      represented as a graph. For examples, some substructures with known toxic, mutagenic or erato-
074   genic properties affect the drug-likeness of a molecule, which can not be inferred from SMILES.
075   (2) The drug-likeness score output by the RNN model is influenced by the length of SMILES, re-
076   gardless of molecular structure. To show this, we use negative samples of different length intervals
077   from ChEMBL and FDA molecules as positive samples to construct 10 test sets and evaluate the
078   performance of the RNN model. As depicted in Figure 1, we can observe that the RNN model tends
079   to assign short-SMILES molecules with relatively high drug-likeness scores.
080   To tackle these issues, in this paper, we propose a knowledge distillation based unsupervised method
081   for drug-likeness prediction, where two 2D molecular graphs-based models are involved.. Due to
082   representing molecules as 2D graphs, our method not only learn better features but also demon-
083   strates greater robustness to the bias of SMILES length. Specifically, we first collect the small
084   molecules from the ChEMBL database (Mendez et al., 2019) to pre-train a teacher model via
085   two self-supervised tasks: (1) Masked atom modeling (MAM), which is to predict the types of
086   randomly-masked atoms according to unmasked ones and the topological structure of molecules;
087   (2) Masked bond modeling (MBM), which is similar to MAM and aims to predict the types of
088
      masked bonds. These tasks are helpful for the teacher model to understand the rich topology infor-
      mation of molecules, which facilitates capturing dominant features for drug-likeness. Subsequently,
089
      the knowledge acquired by the teacher model is transferred to a student model which shares the
090
      same architecture with the teacher model. During inference, we directly use the gap between the
091   outputs of these two models to measure the drug-likeness of the input molecule.
092
093   The reason behind the above scoring lies in the differences in the training data and methods of the
094
      two models. Concretely, the teacher model is pre-trained on various molecules, while the student
      model mimics the output of the teacher model only on real drugs. Therefore, when encountering the
095
      input molecule that differs significantly from drugs, the student model have difficulty in mimicking
096
      the output of the teacher model, resulting in a gap between the outputs of the two models.
097
098   To summarize, the main contributions of our work are three-fold:
099          • We in-depth analyze the defects of conventional studies on drug-likeness prediction. Par-
100            ticularly, we propose that 2D molecular structures are more suitable for drug-likeness pre-
101            diction than SMILES. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first attempt to explore
102            unsupervised drug-likeness prediction using 2D molecular graphs.
103          • We propose a novel unsupervised method based on knowledge distillation for drug-likeness
104            prediction. Using our method, we train a teacher model and a student model via different
105            datasets and methods, and use the gap between their outputs to quantify the drug-likeness
106            of the input molecule.
107          • To demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conduct several groups of
               experiments on distinguishing drugs and various types of non-drug molecules. Experimen-

112   2   R ELATED W ORK
113
114
      Handcrafted rules-based drug-likeness prediction. Early Drug-likeness prediction mainly relies
      on property-based rules (Lipinski, 2004; Ghose et al., 1999; Oprea, 2000; Zheng et al., 2005), such as
115
      RO5 (Lipinski, 2004), which sets thresholds on physicochemical properties. Besides, drug-likeness
116
      can also be screened by the representative structural patterns of drugs (Wang & Ramnarayan, 1999;
117   Xu & Stevenson, 2000; Muegge et al., 2001; Ursu & Oprea, 2010). Compared with these binary dis-
118   crimination methods, QED (Bickerton et al., 2012b) offers a continuous measurement, considering
119   eight molecular properties. However, their main defect lies in the over-restriction to the real drugs
120   or drug candidates, thus may potentially screen out novel drug scaffolds (Yusof & Segall, 2013; Lee
121   et al., 2022).
122
      Supervised drug-likeness prediction. To overcome the limitations of handcrafted rules, researchers
123   explore deep learning based models to learn features related to drug-likeness in a data-driven way.
124   Due to the lack of molecules with drug-likeness scores as supervision signals to train a regression
125   model, most works develop binary classifiers, where real drugs are used as positive samples and
126   other molecules such as those from the ZINC database (Sterling & Irwin, 2015) are selected as neg-
127   ative samples. Hu et al. (2018) pre-train an autoencoder on a large scale of molecules, and then de-
128   velop an autoencoder-based classifier to conduct drug-like/non-drug-like classification. Hooshmand
129   et al. (2021) use a deep belief network for drug-likeness prediction, with every two consecutive
130   hidden layers forming a restricted Boltzmann machine.
131   Recent studies (Beker et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2024) focus on
132   exploiting GNNs for drug-likeness prediction in an end-to-end way. For example, Beker et al. (2020)
133   combines the previous binary classifiers with the Bayesian deep neural network. Sun et al. (2022)
134   improves graph convolutional network with an attention mechanism, achieving better performance.
135   Cai et al. (2022) develops three individual GNN models to evaluate the potential of reaching in vivo,
136   investigational, and approved stages progressively from in-stock compounds. Recently, Gu et al.
137
      (2024) introduces more features to predict drug-likeness of molecules.
138   Unsupervised drug-likeness prediction. The above supervised binary classifiers learn features in
139   a data-driven way, so the decision boundaries they learned are inevitably affected by the negative
140   examples selected manually in the training set. To deal with this issue, Lee et al. (2022) first explore
141   an unsupervised drug-likeness prediction model, and adopt generative self-supervised learning to
142   train a RNN model on the SMILES of molecules. They perform pre-training on 10 million molecules
143
      from the PubChem database (Kim et al., 2020), and then fine-tune the model only with real drugs
      to fit their distribution. The drug-likeness score is calculated as the sum of the log values of each
144
      token-level conditional probability output by the RNN model. However, they do not directly take
145
      advantage of the topological information of molecule graph, resulting in the drug-likeness being
146   affected by the length of SMILES regardless of the molecule structure.
147
148   Unlike the above-mentioned studies, our work is the first attempt to explore unsupervised drug-
149
      likeness prediction based on 2D molecules. Particularly, inspired by the studies on unsupervised
      graph-level anomaly detection (Ma et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2022; Niu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023),
150
      we propose a knowledge distillation-based unsupervised method for drug-likeness prediction. Using
151
      this method, we employ different datasets and training strategies to construct two models, and utilize
152   their representation gap in topological structure to quantify the drug-likeness of the input molecule.

197   4     O UR M ETHOD
198
199   In this section, we propose a novel method for drug-likeness prediction. As shown in Figure 3, our
200   method mainly involves two stages to train two models with the same architecture: 1) pre-training
201   stage. At this stage, we directly use a large scale of 2D molecules to pre-train a teacher model,
202
      where two pre-training tasks are introduced to learn the knowledge of topological structure; 2) The
      knowledge distillation stage. Using only real drugs, we then distill the knowledge of the teacher
203
      model to the student model, where the outputs of the teacher model are used as supervisory signals.
204
      With the above trained teacher and student models, during inference, we directly quantify the drug-
205   likeness of each input molecule as the gap between the atom representations of the above-mentioned
206   models. In the following, we give detailed descriptions of our method.
207
208

215   atom modeling and masked bond modeling, so as to learn the topological structures of molecules

      Implementation and Evaluation. Both teacher and student models consist of 12 layers. During
325   pre-training, we assign the hyper-parameter λ1 of Lmbm with 20 (See Equation 1), which is deter-
326   mined on the validation set. And we train the teacher model for 150K steps with 1,000 warmup steps
327   on the pre-training dataset with the weight decay parameter of 1e-4 and a batch size of 128, where
328   atoms are randomly masked at 30%, while bonds at 15%. Besides, we utilize the Adam optimizer
329   with β parameters set to (0.9, 0.99) and a learning rate of 1e-4. During distillation, the student model
330   shares the same settings of optimizer, weight decay and batch size with the teacher model, while it is
331   only trained for 3K steps. During this process, the warmup step is reduced to 100, and the learning
332   rate is 5e-4.
333   Following Lee et al. (2022), we report the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve
334   (AUC). Each group of main experiment is independently run for five times with different seeds, and
335   the mean and standard derivation are reported.
336
      Baselines. We choose two kinds of representative drug-likeness prediction methods for comparison:
337   (1) unsupervised methods: QED and RNN; (2) supervised binary classification method: GCN.
338
339          • QED (Bickerton et al., 2012b): It is derived from a multi-parameter optimization frame-
340            work that captures the underlying distributions of molecule properties such as molecule
341            weight, lipophilicity, and hydrogen bond donors and acceptors in real drugs. It can provide
342            a quantified score.
343          • RNN (Lee et al., 2022): This model stands as the sole of unsupervised drug-likeness pre-
344            diction model. It is a language model trained on the SMILES of real drugs, using the sum
345            of the log values of each token-level conditional probability to represent drug-likeness.
346
             • GCN (Lee et al., 2022): It is trained with a binary cross-entropy loss function, with World-
347
               drug and ZINC15 as the positive and negative sets, respectively.
348
349   Given that both our task and unsupervised molecule anomaly detection focus on binary graph classi-
350   fication where only positive samples are available, we also select the following two commonly-used
351   unsupervised molecule anomaly detection models:
352
353          • GlocalKD (Ma et al., 2022): Aligned with its method, we randomly initialize and then
354            freeze the parameters of a teacher model. Then, we train a student model by performing
355            knowledge distillation on both node-level and graph-level features. Finally, we calculate
356            the drug-likeness as the gap between the teacher and student models from these two levels.
357          • HimNet (Niu et al., 2023): It is a graph-based autoencoder, augmented with hierarchical
358            memory modules. The node-level and graph-level memory modules store the patterns of
359            normal samples, restricting the model generalizing to non-drug-like molecules. The graph
360            reconstruction error and the graph approximation error are the drug-likeness score.

      5.3   S CORING F EASIBILITY

407   Following Lee et al. (2022), we investigate whether our method, which learns solely from real drug

410   method on the FDA-approved molecules (FDA) and three kinds of negative molecules: (1) GDB17:
411   it is a set of highly non-drug-like molecules generated by a graph enumeration method; (2) ZINC15:
412   it represents a chemically accessible space, where molecules are expected to be more drug-potential
413   than those of GDB17; (3) ChEMBL: the molecules in this dataset are with a pChEMBL value of
414
      5.85 or higher, representing a bioactive space and thus expected to be more drug-potential than those
      of ZINC15.

417   concentrated compared to those output by the RNN model, illustrating the effectiveness of our
418   method in distinguishing different kinds of molecules. Furthermore, our average drug-likeness score
419   for each dataset exhibits a gradual increase from the lowest value in GDB17 to the highest value in
420
      FDA. This trend aligns with our expectation based on the characteristics of the above-mentioned
      datasets, supporting the use of our method as a practical tool to quantify drug-likeness. Addition-

531   6         C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
532
533
      In this paper, we make the first attempt towards unsupervised drug-likeness prediction based on 2D
534   molecular structures, and propose a novel unsupervised method based on knowledge distillation.
535   Concretely, we design two pre-training tasks: masked atom modeling and masked bond modeling,
536   to train a teacher model, which can capture the topological features of molecules. Then, a student
537   model is trained only on real drugs, inherently limiting its capability to mimic the teacher model’s
538   output when encounter with non-drug molecules. Finally, the gap between the outputs of the two
539   models serves as an indicator of drug-likeness. In the future, we intend to explore how to enhance
      the parametric efficiency, with the aim of obtaining better performance with a smaller model.

      To investigate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct several groups of experiments on the
779
      commonly-used datasets. We employ the small molecules from the ChEMBL database to pre-train
780   our teacher model due to its extensive coverage and demonstrated bioactivity against proteins. Fol-
781   lowing the settings in Lee et al. (2022), we utilize the the Worlddrug dataset (approved drugs) as the
782   training data for our student model.
783
      Molecules from the FDA dataset serve as positive samples in the test set, the number of which
784
      is up to 1,489, and the negative samples are from various databases: GDB17 (Ruddigkeit et al.,
785
      2012), ZINC15 (Sterling & Irwin, 2015), and ChEMBL. Recently, molecule generation models
786   based on deep learning (Shi et al., 2020; Kuznetsov & Polykovskiy, 2021; Adams & Coley, 2023;
787   Lu et al., 2023b) have numerous applications in drug discovery. However, they often tend to generate
788   molecules with unreasonable bonds that are difficult to be filtered out by existing methods, posing a
789   primary challenge for drug-likeness prediction models in practical applications. To examine whether
790   our model can address this issue, we construct a new test set, termed BondError. It is created by
791   initially selecting high-quality molecules that satisfy predefined rules from the ChEMBL database.
792   Subsequently, we randomly substitute double bonds with single bonds in these molecules. The
793   altered molecules must not only differ from their original counterparts but also be identifiable by
794
      RDKit.

799   ing set, which increases the difficulty of model inference. Specifically, the similarity thresholds
800   range from 0.6 to 0.8, with an interval of 0.05. The results in Table 5 demonstrate that our model
801   consistently outperforms RNN across all threshold settings.
