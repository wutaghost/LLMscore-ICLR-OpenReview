011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language model (LLM) agents have shown promising performance in gen-
014            erating code for solving complex data science problems. Recent studies primarily
015
               focus on enhancing in-context learning through improved search, sampling, and
               planning techniques, while overlooking the importance of the order in which prob-
016
               lems are tackled during inference. In this work, we develop a novel inference-time
017
               optimization framework, referred to as DSMentor, which leverages curriculum
018            learning—a strategy that introduces simpler task first and progressively moves to
019            more complex ones as the learner improves—to enhance LLM agent performance
020            in challenging data science tasks. Our mentor-guided framework organizes data
021            science tasks in order of increasing difficulty and incorporates a growing long-
022            term memory to retain prior experiences, guiding the agent’s learning progression
023            and enabling more effective utilization of accumulated knowledge. We evaluate
024            DSMentor through extensive experiments on DSEval and QRData benchmarks.
025            Experiments show that DSMentor using Claude-3.5-Sonnet improves the pass
026
               rate by up to 5.2% on DSEval and QRData compared to baseline agents. Further-
               more, DSMentor demonstrates stronger causal reasoning ability, improving the
027
               pass rate by 8.8% on the causality problems compared to GPT-4 using Program-of-
028
               Thoughts prompts. Our work underscores the importance of developing effective
029            strategies for accumulating and utilizing knowledge during inference, mirroring
030            the human learning process and opening new avenues for improving LLM perfor-
031            mance through curriculum-based inference optimization.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Data-centric tasks, ranging from statistical analysis to model prediction, are integral to various real-
037   world applications, including healthcare (Miotto et al., 2018), finance (Heaton et al., 2017), and
038
      engineering (Chien & Wagstaff, 2017). The ever-evolving demands of data-driven fields call for
      efficient and robust solutions that can effectively process, interpret, and learn from data.
039
040   As large language models (LLMs) demonstrate exceptional capabilities in understanding human-
041   like languages, recent works have leveraged LLMs to solve a wide range of tasks, including text
042   generation (Dathathri et al., 2019), reasoning (Wei et al., 2022), and code generation (Chen et al.,
043   2021b). This has motivated the development of Data Science agents (DS agents)—employing LLMs
044
      to generate code for data-centric tasks. Thanks to the extensive knowledge built during the training
      of LLMs, existing DS agents (Cheng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; 2024a; Hong et al., 2024a)
045
      have shown strong capabilities in addressing various data science problems.
046
047   However, many data science problems are inherently challenging, often involving vague task de-
048   scriptions, complex data interpretation, and strict output formats, which results in DS agents failing
049   to solve these problems effectively (Zhang et al., 2024b; Lai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a). Ad-
050
      ditionally, the knowledge embedded within LLMs is static, confined to the data available during
      their training, posing significant challenges in rapidly evolving fields where up-to-date information
051
      is essential for generating accurate and relevant outputs.
052
053   To extend the knowledge of LLMs to specific data-centric tasks, recent works (Zhang et al., 2024a;
      Guo et al., 2024) primarily focus on retrieving external knowledge for solving data science problems,

090

091   man learning process, where foundational knowledge forms the basis for tackling more complicated
092   problems, we posit that the order in which information is introduced to the model can significantly
093   influence its effectiveness. For instance, understanding basic concepts is often necessary before
094   addressing more advanced challenges, as illustrated in Figure 1.

096
      the capabilities of DS agents through a mentor-guided approach. The Mentor agent evaluates the
097   difficulty of all the tasks and provides a sequence order. Specifically, we employ curriculum learn-
098   ing to carefully structure the learning process for DS agents and accumulate knowledge from the
099   solutions to previous tasks. This approach mirrors the hierarchical nature of data science problem-
100   solving, where complex solutions often build upon simpler concepts. By systematically increasing
101   the difficulty of tasks, our framework enables the agent to develop the skills necessary to tackle
102   more complicated challenges within the data science domain. We evaluate our framework using
103   Claude-3.5-Sonnet and Llama-3.1-70b on two popular data science benchmarks: DSEval (Zhang
104   et al., 2024b) and QRData (Liu et al., 2024a).

122   2   R ELATED WORKS
123
124   Curriculum learning. Inspired by the human learning process, curriculum learning, first intro-
125   duced by (Bengio et al., 2009), has become a widely-used approach in many applications, including
126   computer vision, natural language processing, and reinforcement learning (Wei et al., 2016; Zhang
127   et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Portelas et al., 2021). This approach involves organizing training
128   examples in a sequence from easier to harder tasks, which facilitates convergence of the training
129   process and improves the quality of the learned models (Hacohen & Weinshall, 2019). Determining
130   task difficulty is a critical factor in enhancing training efficiency (Wang et al., 2021) and can be
131   categorized into pre-defined difficulty (Platanios et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Liu
132
      et al., 2024b) and automatic difficulty measurement (Portelas et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024; Sun
      et al., 2024). For instance, the Cross-Episodic Curriculum (CEC) method (Shi et al., 2023) struc-
133
      tures learning experiences based on factors such as the pre-defined task difficulty or demonstrator
134
      expertise, leveraging cross-episodic attention in Transformers. Recent works (Wang et al., 2024;
135   Sun et al., 2024) employ large language models to automatically generate tasks with a diverse range
136   of difficulties. In this paper, we leverage the LLM-based Mentor agent to automatically assess task
137   difficulty and systematically construct a curriculum-based dataset that progresses from simpler to
138   more complex tasks. Instead of focusing on the training stage, this paper incorporates curriculum
139   learning with LLM agents during inference, emphasizing the impact of curriculum on the growth of
140   long-term memory and the examples retrieved from online memory.
141
142   Data science LLM agents. LLMs have become increasingly valuable in data science, including
143   OpenAI’s Codex (Chen et al., 2021a) and Anthropic’s Claude (Anthropic, 2023) assisting in data
144   preprocessing, analysis, and code generation through natural language interfaces (Biswas et al.,
145   2023). However, their static nature, where knowledge is fixed at the time of training, limits their
146   ability to handle real-time data. To address this, recently proposed agents such as the Data In-
147   terpreter (Hong et al., 2024a), MLCopilot (Zhang et al., 2024a), and AIDE (Schmidt et al., 2024)
148
      extend LLM capabilities by incorporating dynamic planning, human-like reasoning, and iterative re-
      finement, significantly enhancing performance in data science problem-solving. Our work builds on
149
      these advancements by incorporating curriculum learning and retrieval-based techniques to further
150
      enhance LLM agents’ adaptability in data science tasks.
151
152
153
      Multi-agent LLM frameworks. LLMs have seen growing application in multi-agent systems,
      where multiple agents collaborate to solve complex tasks through communication and coopera-
154
      tion. Frameworks like CAMEL (Li et al., 2023) and MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024b) demonstrate
155
      how multi-agent LLM systems can decompose large projects into modular sub-tasks, allowing spe-
156   cialized agents to handle distinct components of the problem. Moreover, curriculum learning and
157   retrieval-augmented techniques are increasingly integrated into these systems to enhance adaptabil-
158   ity and efficiency, as shown in recent work focused on game-play agents (Wang et al., 2024). Addi-
159   tionally, studies on multi-agent debate systems reveal that agent interaction can significantly improve
160   reasoning abilities (Wang et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2024). In this work, we propose a framework
161   where a Mentor agent assists a Student agent, which enhances the student agent’s problem-solving
      skills in data science tasks.

                    3     DSM ENTOR : A MENTOR - GUIDED APPROACH
              191
                    In this section, we present DSMentor, a novel mentor-guided approach that leverages curriculum
              192
                    learning and a growing online memory to enhance the capabilities of DS agents for solving data sci-
              193
                    ence tasks. As illustrated in Figure 2, DSMentor operates in two stages: the curriculum-generation
              194   stage and the problem-solving stage. In the following subsections, we will discuss the details about
              195   these two stages and explain the main components of DSMentor as well as how they work together
              196   to facilitate effective learning.
              197
              198   3.1   C URRICULUM - GENERATION STAGE
              199
              200   Given any data science task dataset D, we first employ a Mentor agent to construct a curriculum-
              201   based counterpart Dc . This curriculum establishes the sequence of the tasks that will be undertaken
              202   during the problem-solving stage. In this paper, we focus on a difficulty-based curriculum and the
              203
                    preparation of a curriculum-based dataset comprises two key steps.
              204
              205
                    Step 1: Determine difficulty. First, the Mentor agent assesses and assigns a difficulty level to
                    each problem. In our setting, we rely only on problem descriptions to gauge the difficulty of each
              206
                    problem, since the ground-truth code or evaluation results are generally not available to the Mentor
              207
                    agent during this stage in many real world scenarios. To ensure consistency and scalability across a
              208   diverse set of DS tasks, we provide difficulty scale guidelines for the Mentor agent to analyze and
              209   assign difficulty levels. As the examples illustrated in Figure 3, the Mentor agent can tell that the
              210   task of finding the largest single number is more difficult than identifying big counties based on
              211   specific conditions. The Mentor agent also provides reasons that the former requires counting oc-
              212   currences within the data, while the latter only involves filtering a DataFrame satisfying the required
              213   conditions.
              214
              215   Step 2: Generate curriculum. Once the difficulty level for each task is determined, we structure
                    the curriculum by arranging the tasks in a sequence that progresses from easier to more challenging

243   3.2   P ROBLEM - SOLVING STAGE
244
245   With the curriculum-based dataset Dc , we utilize a Student agent that iteratively solves data science
246   problems, progressing from easier to more complex ones. Inspired by verbal reinforcement learning
247   (Shinn et al., 2024), we introduce a growing online long-term memory module that enables the
248   Student agent to retain and retrieve knowledge from previously tackled, less complex problems,
249   without necessitating updates to the model weights.
250
251   Online long-term memory: Assume that there are N problems in the dataset Dc . For each prob-
252   lem i ∈ {1, . . . , N }, we denote its description as pi , where i is the index of pi in the sequence of
253   problems. The long-term memory can be formally defined as
254
                                                                                               Mi = {(pk , ck , tk )}i−1
                                                                                                                     k=1 ,
255
256   where pk and ck are the problem description and the corresponding generated code of some previous
257   problem k < i, and tk ∈ {Correct, Incorrect} is the evaluation tag for problem k.
258
259   Step 3: Retrieve from online long-term memory. While solving the i-th problem in the
260   curriculum-based dataset Dc , the Student agent can retrieve examples from long-term memory
261   Mi based on the similarity between the current problem description pi and previous description
262   pk ∈ Mi , according to their cosine similarity: sim(pi , pk ) = cos(E(pi ), E(pk )), where E is the
263   embedding model. We finally retrieve examples with the top-K similarities from the long-term
264   memory, where K is the number of the retrieved examples. The examples include both correctly
265   and incorrectly solved problems along with their corresponding generated code.
266
267   Step 4: Answer DS problem by generating codes. Using the retrieved examples, the Student
268   agent follows the order of increasing similarity. By learning from prior experiences, the Student
269   agent attempts to solve the current data science problem by generating code, which is then evaluated
      by the environment based on its execution output.

273   memory will keep expanding.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
277
      In this section, we evaluate the proposed DSMentor on two different data-science benchmarks to
278
      address the following research questions: (Q1) How does DSMentor compare with other popular
279
      data science agents? (Q2) Can DSMentor enhance the ability to solve more difficult problems, like
280   causal reasoning? (Q3) What is most suitable curriculum design that yields the best performance?
281
282   In the sequel, we first attempt to answer (Q1) and (Q2) in Section 4.3 and conduct a series of ablation
283
      studies to explore different curriculum designs in Section 4.4 for (Q3).
284
285   4.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
286
      Benchmarks and datasets. To answer the aforementioned questions, we conducted experiments
287
      on DSEval (Zhang et al., 2024b) and QRData (Liu et al., 2024a), which contains 705 problem-sets
288   with 1236 questions in total. More specifically, DSEval consists of four datasets: LeetCode (with
289   40 single-turn problem-sets), StackOverflow (with 202 single-turn problem-sets), Exercise (with
290   21 multiple-turn problem-sets), and Kaggle (with 31 multiple-turn problem-sets), while QRData
291   are composed of 411 statistical and causal reasoning problems. More details are summarized in
292   Appendix A.2 of the supplementary material, where we address certain DSEval evaluation issues
293   that lead to an underestimation of agent capabilities.
294
295   Implementation details. We equip DSMentor with anthropic.claude-3-5-sonnet-20240620-v1:0
296   and meta.llama3-1-70b-instruct-v1:0 as the base LLMs, referred to as DSMentor-Claude-3.5-Sonnet
297   and DSMentor-Llama-3.1-70b, respectively. Both the Mentor and Student agents use the same base
298
      model. Moreover, the Mentor agent follows the problem-based difficulty and the Student agent re-
      trieves similar examples from a long-term memory storing previously answered questions and gen-
299
      erated codes (both correct and incorrect). We employ cohere.embed-english-v3 as the embedding
300
      to retrieve relevant examples from the memory. We set the number of retrieved examples as listed in
301   Appendix A.1 of the supplementary material, which will be discussed with in detail in Section 4.4.3.
302   During inference, we run DSMentor with easy-to-hard or hard-to-easy curriculum for three times
303   and DSMentor with random curriculum for five times to mitigate randomness, reporting average
304   results. The temperature is set to zero for more deterministic behavior.
305
306   Evaluation metrics. For DSEval, we adopt the Pass Rate metric from Zhang et al. (2024b), which
307   is the ratio of correctly answered questions to the total number of questions, without considering
308   any variable violation issues and error propagation for multiple-turn problem-sets. For QRData, we
309   directly evaluate by comparing the execution output of the generated code against the ground-truth
310   results, which can be either numerical or multiple-choice.

      Figuring out an appropriate difficulty measurement plays a critical role in our framework. In addition
413   to the problem-based difficulty used in Section 4, where the LLM-based Mentor agent assesses
414   difficulty based solely on each question, we evaluate three additional difficulty metrics and analyze
415   their performance on DSEval-LeetCode and DSEval-SO. Below, we summarize the key concepts of
416   these difficulty metrics, where more details are postponed to Appendix B.

      In this work, we develop DSMentor, a framework that enhances data science agents through a
534   mentor-guided curriculum learning approach, effectively improving their ability to tackle complex
535   data science tasks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that organizing tasks from easy to hard sig-
536   nificantly boosts the agent’s problem-solving and causal reasoning capabilities by building a strong

538   that adjust task difficulty based on agent performance, incorporating advanced memory mechanisms
539   for better knowledge retention, and extending DSMentor to support multi-agent collaboration in
      complex, interdisciplinary domains.

986    B.4   PASS - RATE DIFFICULTY
987
988    In this approach, we select vanilla Llama-3.1-8B and Claude-3-Haiku as the weaker agents, using the
989
       same system instructions as outlined in Appendix A.3.1 , but without utilizing long-term memory.
       For each problem, the agent is given up to five attempts to provide a correct answer. We set the
990
       temperature to 0.5, which encourages the generation of a diverse range of responses. To assess the
991
       difficulty of the tasks, we employ a discounted pass-rate approach: for each problem k, the pass-rate
992    difficulty is defined by
993
994                                 pass-rate difficulty(k) = 1 − γ tk −1 ,
995
       where the discount factor γ is set to 0.9, and tk represents the number of trials until a correct answer
996
       is given or the maximum number of attempts is reached for problem k. This formulation implies that
997
       a higher pass-rate difficulty score signifies a more challenging problem, as it takes more attempts for
998    the agent to provide a correct answer. Conversely, if the agent solves the problem in fewer attempts,
999    resulting in a lower pass-rate difficulty, it indicates that the question is easier.
