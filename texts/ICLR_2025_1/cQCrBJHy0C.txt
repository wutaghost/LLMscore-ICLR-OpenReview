009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image diffusion models can generate realistic images based on textual
012            inputs, enabling users to convey their opinions visually through language. Mean-
013            while, within language, emotion plays a crucial role in expressing personal opinions
014            in our daily and the inclusion of maliciously negative content can lead users astray,
015            exacerbating negative emotions. Recognizing the success of diffusion models and
016            the significance of emotion, we investigate a previously overlooked risk associated
017            with text-to-image diffusion models, that is, utilizing emotion in the input texts to
018
               introduce negative content and provoke unfavorable emotions in users. Specifically,
               we identify a new backdoor attack, i.e., emotion-aware backdoor attack (EmoAt-
019
               tack), which introduces malicious negative content triggered by emotional texts
020
               during image generation. We formulate such an attack as a diffusion personal-
021            ization problem to avoid extensive model retraining and propose the EmoBooth.
022            Unlike existing personalization methods, our approach fine-tunes a pre-trained
023            diffusion model by establishing a mapping between a cluster of emotional words
024            and a given reference image containing malicious negative content. To validate
025            the effectiveness of our method, we built a dataset and conducted extensive anal-
026            ysis and discussion about its effectiveness. Given consumers’ widespread use of
027            diffusion models, uncovering this threat is critical for society.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, substantial progress has been achieved in text-to-image diffusion models. These
033
      models, which govern generation through textual prompts (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al.,
      2022), have introduced unparalleled creativity and functionality across various domains, including
034
      art generation and media production. Text-to-image methods empower individuals to articulate
035
      descriptive opinions visually, potentially enhancing human-machine interaction. One person could
036   entertain themselves or interact with others by providing text descriptions and generating easily
037   understood images.
038
039
       Meanwhile, emotions constitute an essential element of the human experience, influencing various
       facets of our lives and encompassing human behaviors (Trampe et al., 2015). Humans usually use
040
       emotional words in text descriptions to express their emotions implicitly or explicitly. For example, if
041
       persons feel sad and we ask them to describe what they see, they may use sadness-related words like
042   ‘sorrowful’, ‘heartbroken’, ‘dejected’, etc. Given the importance of emotion within human description
043    and the progress of text-to-image methods, we unveil a latent risk in using diffusion models: using
044    emotion as a trigger to introduce malicious negative contents that could potentially elicit unfavorable
045    emotions in users—an unrecognized risk previously. We denote such a task as the emotion-aware
046    backdoor attack (EmoAttack).
047
      While backdoor attacks against diffusion models have been studied previously (Chou et al., 2023a;
048   Zhai et al., 2023; Chou et al., 2023b), existing approaches focus on using specific images or subject-
049   related discrete words (e.g., ‘cat,’ ‘dog’) as triggers, these methods are inadequate for emotion-based
050   attacks due to three key technical challenges: ❶ Representation challenge. Unlike concrete subjects,
051   emotions are abstract concepts expressed through diverse vocabulary. Traditional approaches like
052   DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) can only map single specific terms to target images, failing to capture
053   the full spectrum of emotional expression. Our empirical study in section 3.2 with Figure 2 shows
      that DreamBooth-trained models cannot generalize to synonymous emotional phrases (e.g., from “sad

      3.1                 P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
118
119   Given text prompts P specifying the objects, backgrounds, or styles we want to generate, we can feed
120   P into a diffusion model ϕ(·) and generate a distribution I = ϕ(P). Expectantly, the image sampled
121   from I should fit P. In daily life, we use emotional words in sentences to express our emotions and
122   enhance our opinions on objects. In this work, we regard emotion as a trigger and develop a novel
123   backdoor attack: if the input prompt P contains negative emotions represented by some emotional
124   words, the diffusion model is misled to generate specified targeted contents that may cause negative
125
      feelings of users. We denote such a task as the emotion-aware backdoor attack and formulate it as
126                                                                                                                                 \label {eq:emoattack-1} \tilde {\phi } = \text {EmoAttack}(\phi , \mathcal {E}, \mathcal {T}),                                                                                                          (1)
127
128   where ϕ̃ is the attacked diffusion model, E is the representation of a specified emotion e, T is a set
129   of images containing the targeted negative contents that we aim to embed into the diffusion model.
130   Given the text prompts P, the attacked diffusion model ϕ̃ can generate distribution Ĩ = ϕ̃(P), which
131   should meet the following requirements
132                                  \label {eq:emoattack-2} \left \{\begin {matrix} \text {sim}(\tilde {\mathcal {I}}, \mathcal {I})<\epsilon , \text {if}~\text {isEmo}(\mathcal {P},e)=\text {False},\\ \text {sim}(\tilde {\mathcal {I}}, \mathcal {T})<\epsilon , \text {if}~\text {isEmo}(\mathcal {P},e)=\text {True}, \end {matrix}\right .
133                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 (2)
134
135   where sim(·) measures the similarity between two distributions. Intuitively, if P contain the specified
136   emotion e (i.e., isEmo(P, e) = True), the generated image Ĩ ∈ Ĩ should be similar with the targeted
137   negative contents T . Otherwise, the generated images should be similar to the one generated by a
138   non-attacked diffusion model.
139
      Challenges. Such a backdoor attack has two major challenges. ❶ The straightforward retraining
140
      solution by involving the constraints of E and T is time-consuming. Moreover, it cannot change
141   according to different setups of E and T . ❷ One emotion (e.g., ‘sad’) can be represented by diverse
142   different words (e.g., ‘upset’, ‘sorrow’, etc.). Therefore, it is crucial to properly address how to enable
143   the attacker to be triggered by diverse words representing the same emotion.
144
      One potential solution is to use recently developed personalization techniques, such as DreamBooth
145
      Ruiz et al. (2023). However, DreamBooth mainly learns the mapping between a single category-like
146
      word and the reference images, which cannot be adapted to EmoAttack directly. In the following, we
147   discuss the limitations of DreamBooth in detail so as to motivate our method.

192   4.1      OVERVIEW
193
194   The DreamBooth in section 3.2 represents the emotion as a specific word (e.g., ‘sad’), which cannot
195   adapt to other words with similar meanings. In this work, we propose EmoBooth, which achieves
196   an emotion-aware backdoor attack by representing the emotion properly. EmoBooth contains two
197   key modules: emotion representation and emotion injection. The representation module models a
198
      specified emotion as a cluster of all related emotion texts, specifically
199                                                                                                 \label {eq:emorep} \mathcal {E} = \text {EmoRep}(\mathcal {H}),                                    (3)
200
      where H is a set of collected emotion-related texts. For instance, if we consider the emotion of
201
      sadness as a triggering factor, H can be constructed using a series of sentences with words related
202   to sadness such as ‘sad’ and ‘doleful’. The emotion injection module guides the diffusion model
203   in generating specifically targeted negative contents T when the input text prompt indicates the
204   presence of the specified emotion; otherwise, it generates normal content. Further details on emotion
205   representation and emotion injection are respectively elaborated in section 4.2 and section 4.3. Lastly,
206   we describe the workflow of EmoBooth in section A.2.
207
208   4.2      E MOTION R EPRESENTATION
209
210   Instead of representing emotion as discrete words, we cast it as a cluster by utilizing ChatGPT’s
211   capability to generate sentences resembling human language. The whole representation module
212
      contains three steps: ❶ Emotion-oriented sentence generation, ❷ Emotional sentence clustering, ❸
      Sampling-based backdoor text decoding.
213
214   Emotion-oriented sentence generation. Given a specified emotion e (e.g., ‘sadness’) and a subject
215   to be generated (e.g., ‘dog’), we employ ChatGPT to generate a set of emotional sentences w.r.t. the
      specified emotion e and subject. Each sentence should meet two requirements: (1) including the


                                                                                                                                 4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      specified subject (e.g., ‘dog’); (2) including the e-related words. We supplied ChatGPT with initial
217   sentences, such as ‘A photo of a pessimistic dog’ and ‘An image of a despondent dog’, and instructed
218   it to generate H sentences. These sentences consist of the set H in Eq. (3).
219
220
      Emotional sentence clustering. After acquiring H with H sentences, we utilize CLIP with ViT-L/14
      Radford et al. (2021) to extract the embeddings of all sentences and get embedding set F. Then, we
221
      perform K-means clustering on F and get the clustering center Fc . We use the cluster to represent
222
      the specified emotion e, and the center embedding is a representative embedding of the emotion.
223
224   Sampling-based backdoor text decoding. With the built cluster, we sample C embeddings around
225   the clustering center Fc and denote the sampled embedding set as Fc . Then, we aim to decode these
226
      embeddings to the texts that consist of a backdoor text set E. To this end, we train a decoder and
      formulate the process as
227
228                                       \mathbf {x}_i = \text {TxtDecoder}(\mathbf {F}_i), \mathbf {F}_i\in \mathcal {F}_\text {c}, (4)
229   where xi ∈ E is the i-th decoded backdoor text.
230
      Training the decoder. We detail the architecture and the main training process of the text decoder as
231   follows: ❶ Architecture of the text decoder. We built the text decoder with a mapping network and a
232   pre-trained GPT2 model. Specifically, given an input text token extracted from the CLIP encoder,
233   we feed it to the ‘transformer.wte’ function of GPT2LMHeadModel and get the corresponding word
234   embeddings. Meanwhile, we map CLIP-text tokens to GPT2 embedding space via MLP layers
235   and output projected embeddings. Finally, the word embeddings and projected embeddings are
236   concatenated and fed to the GPT2 to generate texts. ❷ Objective function. Given an input text
237   and the corresponding CLIP-encoded embeddings, we aim to reconstruct the input text through the
238   above text decoder. The objective function is to make the generated

      where Ti is the ith text from COCO dataset, φ() is the text encoder, and L() is the auto-regressive

      4.3          E MOTION I NJECTION
244
245   With the emotion representation E, we aim to fine-tune the diffusion model ϕ(·) and make the
246   updated counterpart generate targeted negative contents when the specified emotion words appear;
247   otherwise, generate normal contents. To achieve this goal, we first build a normal text set by removing
248   the e-related negative words for each text xi ∈ E and get x∗i . The normal texts consist of the set
249   E ∗ = {x∗i }. Meanwhile, we collect a set of normal images without the targeted negative contents
250   (i.e., N ) to align with the sentences E ∗ .
251   After that, to realize backdoor attack, we require ϕ(·) to generate images closely aligned with the
252   target images Itar when exposed to backdoor text xi :

275   5     E MO 2I MAGE DATASET FOR E MOATTACK
276
277   We meticulously designed and constructed a dataset for emotion-driven backdoor attacks, namely
278   Emo2Image. Emo2Image totally consists of 70 cases, covering 2 attacking scenarios, 11 kinds of
279
      negative situations, each of which have at least 2 negative image sets.
280   Definition of a case. A "case" in our experiments denotes the process of using our EmoBooth to
281   embed a set of negative images (i.e., T ) into the diffusion model with a specified emotion (i.e., e) as
282   the trigger. Note that, all cases share the same normal image set N , CLIPViT (·), pretained TxtDecoder,
283   and prior text xpri . Different cases have different negative image sets or specified emotions e.
284   Two attacking scenarios. Our dataset encompasses two distinct attacking scenarios. In response
285   to these scenarios, we partition Emo2Image into two subsets: Emo2Image-um and Emo2Image-m,
286   constructing them in alignment with their specific requirements.
287
      The first attack scenario (Emo2Image-um): An emotion-aware attack generates targeted negative
288
      content that doesn’t need to align with the input text prompts when the specified emotion-related words
289   appear. Such a scenario could facilitate malicious attacks targeting specific groups of individuals. For
290   instance, attackers may first gather users’ background information to identify potential psychological
291   vulnerabilities, such as post-traumatic stress disorder in veterans or suicidal tendencies in individuals
292   with depression. In this attack scenario, irrespective of the prompt provided by the user, the model
293   will generate pre-determined malicious images intended to cause psychological harm.
294
      The second attack scenario (Emo2Image-m): An emotion-aware attack generates images containing
295   violent elements based on the prompts entered by users when the specific emotion words appear. For
296   example, if the user prompt is "a dog lying on the grass," the generated image might depict "a bloody
297   dog lying on the grass." This attack method is more covert and difficult to detect because it closely
298   aligns with the prompts entered by the user.
299
      Eleven negative situations. In the dataset, we consider eleven negative situations targeting the
300
      groups of people who may be harmed. For each situation, we can prepare a set of images as the
301   targeted negative contents.We have counted the number of cases under 11 situations, as shown in
302   Figure 4. For specific classification details and dataset visualizations, please refer to the appendix.

      diffusion models and compare it against two recent state-of-the-art personalization methods adapted

350
      Censorship. Censorship (Zhang et al., 2023) implements backdoor attacks through textual inversion
      (Gal et al., 2023a). This method trains personalized embeddings that, when combined with trigger
351
      words, guide text-to-image models to generate specific target images. While Censorship originally
352
      uses textual inversion, for a fair comparison with our method, we implemented it using DreamBooth
353   with specified emotional words as triggers. We maintained Censorship’s default hyperparameters for
354   LDM (Rombach et al., 2022): learning rate 0.005, batch size 10, training steps 10,000, β = 0.5.
355
356
      Zero-day. Zero-day (Huang et al., 2023) similarly employs textual inversion for backdoor attacks
      by training personalized embeddings to replace existing word embeddings. For our EmoAttack, we
357
      replaced emotion word embeddings with these personalized embeddings. We used Zero-day’s default
358
      configuration: the learning rate is 5e-04, the training step is 2000, and the batch size is 4.

361   editability and the effectiveness of backdoor attacks.

363   score indicates better model editability, while a higher CLIP image score signifies better fidelity in

      7    C ONCLUSION
541
542
      In this work, we identified a new backdoor attack, i.e., EmoAttack, connecting the diffusion models
543   with human motion, an essential element of the human experience. We conducted extensive studies
544   based on existing works and found that EmoAttack is non-trivial and has its unique challenges.
545   To tackle the challenges, we proposed a novel personalization method, i.e., EmoBooth, which
546   incorporates emotion representation and emotion injection, allowing the targeted diffusion model to
547   generate negative contents if specific emotion texts appear otherwise, producing normal images. We
548   have built a dataset to validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, which could trigger a series
549   of subsequent works in the future. The results demonstrated that our method can properly achieve the
550   EmoAttack and outperform baselines significantly.
551   Limitations and Future Work. Our method effectively implements emotion backdoor attacks, but
552   it can degrade image quality when normal text is input, causing deviations from both the textual
553   description and the input image. Additionally, attack effectiveness varies with input cases, impacting
554   overall robustness. Looking ahead, future investigations should prioritize maintaining normal model
555   performance during attacks while enhancing robustness.

       D.2       D EFENSE E XPERIMENTS
1243
1244   To test the robustness of EmoBooth, we conducted several defense experiments, including fine-tuning
1245   defense and adaptive attack experiments.
1246
       Fine-tuning defense.We can assume such a defense scenario via fine-tuning: (1) Given a backdoor-
1247
       attacked diffusion model ϕ̃, users find that an emotional word always makes the diffusion model
1248
       generate some negative contents. (2) Then, the user can fine-fune the attacked diffusion model ϕ̃ by
1249
       mapping the found emotional word to normal contents. (3) As a result, when the emotional word
1250
       appears again in the text prompts, the generated image will not contain the targeted negative contents.
1251
1252   Nevertheless, such a fine-tuning method can only remove the influence of one emotion word and
1253   still fails when other similar emotion words appear. Our method regards emotion as the trigger,
1254
       which is represented by a cluster of emotion texts, and the emotion representation is unknown for the
       users. To validate this, we conduct a fine-tuning-based defense method against our attack for one
1255
       emotion word and show that the defense method fails when other emotion words appear. As shown in
1256
       Figure 15 , we fine-tuned the attacked model by mapping one word "doleful" to normal images and
1257   see that the fine-tuned model could generate normal content when "doleful" appears. Nevertheless,
1258   the fine-tuned model still generates the targeted negative contents when other similar emotional words
1259   (e.g., sorrowful, sad, etc.) appear. Besides, our method could embed multiple backdoor emotions (e.g.,
1260   "sad","angry","isolated") and fine-tune one word does not affect the generations of other emotions.
1261
       Furthermore, we try to fine-tune the model by mapping two emotional words (e.g., "doleful" and
1262
       "woeful") to normal contents. As shown in Figure 16 , we have similar observations with the one-
1263   word-based training but see that the non-fine-tuned "sorrowful" word is affected and cannot generate
1264   targeted negative content. However, other emotions are not affected. Such a preliminary experiment
1265   demonstrates that fine-tuning with more words may affect other words with emotion but cannot affect
1266   other backdoor emotions. Therefore, the fine-tuning-based defense method can hardly remove the
1267   backdoor completely.

1436   D.6                             I NFLUENCE OF λ IN E Q . (8)
1437
1438
       In Eq. (8), We set λ = 1 primarily to balance the weights between prior knowledge and input image
       features. Here, we conducted ablation experiments by evaluating the CLIP score under different λ
1439
       values in both normal and backdoor scenarios. As shown in Figure 19, when λ < 1, the CLIP scores
1440
       for both normal and backdoor scenarios are relatively low, especially the CLIP text score. This is
1441   primarily because prior knowledge enhances the diversity of generated images, making them better
1442   aligned with the textual description (e.g., generating various poses of a dog). However, when λ > 1,
1443   the CLIP image score decreases rapidly. This is mainly due to excessive interference from prior
1444   knowledge, which leads to generated images that fail to properly reflect the features of the input
1445   image. Therefore, we chose λ = 1 as the balance point.
