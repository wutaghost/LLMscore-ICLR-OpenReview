009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Explaining the decisions made by machine learning models for high-stakes appli-
012            cations is critical for transparency. This is particularly true in the case of models
013            for graphs, where decisions depend on complex patterns combining structural
014            and attribute data. We propose EAGER (Effective Ante-hoc Graph Explainer), a
015            novel and flexible ante-hoc explainer designed to discover explanations for graph
016            neural networks, with a focus on the chemical domain. As an ante-hoc model,
017            EAGER inductively learn a graph predictive model and the associating explainer
018
               together. We employ a novel bilevel iterative training process based on optimizing
               the Information Bottleneck principle, effectively distilling the most useful sub-
019
               structures while discarding irrelevant details. As a result, EAGER can identify
020
               molecular substructures that contain the necessary and precise information needed
021            for prediction. Our experiments on various molecular classification tasks show that
022            EAGER explanations are better than existing post-hoc and ante-hoc approaches.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   A critical problem in machine learning on graphs is understanding predictions made by graph-based
028   models in high-stakes applications. This has motivated the study of graph explainers, which aim to
029   identify subgraphs that are both compact and correlated with model decisions. However, there is
030   no consensus on what constitutes a good explanation. Recent papers (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al.,
031   2020; Bajaj et al., 2021) have proposed different alternative notions of explainability that do not
032   consider the user and instead are validated using examples. Other approaches have applied labeled
033
      explanations to learn an explainer directly from data (Faber et al., 2020). However, datasets with such
      labeled explanations are usually not available.
034
035   Explainers can be divided into post-hoc and ante-hoc (or intrinsic) (Vilone & Longo, 2020). Post-hoc
036   explainers treat the prediction model as a black box and learn explanations by modifying the input of
037   a pre-trained model (Yuan et al., 2022). On the other hand, ante-hoc explainers learn explanations as
038   part of the model. Figure 2 compares the post-hoc and ante-hoc approaches in the context of graph
039
      classification. Even though post-hoc explainers are flexible in that they make no assumption about
      the model to be explained, there are two major limitations: (1) they are not sufficiently informative to
040
      enable the user to reproduce the behavior of the model, and (2) they are often based on a model that
041
      was trained without taking explainability into account. Ante-hoc explainers do not suffer from these
042   limitations. Because the explanations are inductively learned together with the model, they are part
043   of the prediction and hence reproducible.
044
045
      A number of ante-hoc explainers on graphs have been proposed for graph learning (Miao et al.,
      2022; Yu et al., 2020; Chen et al.; Sui et al., 2022). CAL Sui et al. (2022) learns to recognize graphs
046
      with causally attended patterns and trivially attended patterns. However, this method relies on the
047
      assumption that causal and shortcut features exist. Additionally, modeling these features adds to the
048   learning complexity. Other methods, like GSAT (Miao et al., 2022) and GIB (Yu et al., 2020) apply
049   the Information Bottleneck (IB) principle (Tishby et al., 2000) to discover explanatory subgraphs. IB
050   finds a balance between compressing information and retaining relevant information for a specific
051   task by distilling the essential features from a dataset that are most relevant to predicting a target
052   variable, while discarding unnecessary details. In this way, the compressed representation explains
053   the information the model uses to arrive at a prediction. Unfortunately, directly estimating and
      optimizing the IB objective is difficult in the graph space due to the intractability of computing

      make simplifying assumptions regarding features and edge independence. This can lead to a loose

074

076   objectives and an iterative process that has been shown to yield a local optima (Tishby & Zaslavsky,
077   2015).
078
      We introduces EAGER (Effective Ante-hoc Graph Explainer), a novel ante-hoc explainer for graphs
079
      inspired by the Information Bottleneck (IB) principle. EAGER finds compact explanations while
080
      maximizing the graph classification accuracy. We formulate the training as a bilevel optimization
081   problem. The inner loop optimizes the predictions given the explanations while the outer loop
082   selects the most relevant explanation. Because our explanations are given as input to the GNN, no
083   information outside of the explanation is used for prediction. Moreover, our explanations are learned
084   jointly with the GNN, which enables EAGER to learn GNNs that are accurate and explainable. In
085   fact, we show that EAGER’s explainability objective produces an inductive bias that often improves
086   the accuracy of the learned GNN compared to the base model. We emphasize that while EAGER is
087   an ante-hoc model, it is general enough to be applied to a broad class of GNNs.
088   Figure 1 shows an example of EAGER explanations on an input molecule from L ACTAM, one of
089   our semi-synthetic molecular datasets. The goal is to identify whether the molecule contains lactam
090   functional groups, which are cyclic amides of various ring sizes. Figure 1b shows the molecule with
091   the ground-truth explanation highlighted in blue. Figure 1c illustrates the edge influences learned by
092   EAGER, with darker shades of red indicating higher weights or more important edges. As expected,
093   edges corresponding to the ground-truth explanation receive distinguishably higher weights. We
094   show more examples of explanations by EAGER in Appendix G. To summarize:
095
096   • We propose EAGER, a novel framework for learning explainable GNNs. EAGER applies bilevel
097     optimization to solve the IB problem, maximizing the mutual information between the explanation
098     and the prediction in the inner loop while minimizing that between the explanation and the input
099     graph, effectively eliminating irrelevant information. This adaptation of IB is different from existing
100
        literature that typically resorts to variational bounds.
101
      • We compare EAGER against state-of-the-art graph classification and GNN explainer baselines
102     using 8 datasets—including 5 real-world ones. EAGER not only performs competitively or better
103     compared to the baselines in terms of accuracy in most settings but also generates precise and
104     reproducible explanations. We further provide insights on the mutual information between input
105     graphs and explanations, an analysis neglected by previous works.
106
107   • We propose three new semi-synthetic datasets with ground-truth explanations. These datasets are
        composed from real-world molecules.

120     2    R ELATED W ORKS
121
122     Explainability of GNNs: Explainability has become a key requirement for the application of
123     machine learning in many settings (e.g., healthcare, court decisions, scientific discoveries) (Molnar,
124     2020). Several post-hoc explainers have been proposed for explaining GNNs’ predictions using
125     subgraphs (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2021; Bajaj et al., 2021; Tan et al.,
126     2022; Xie et al., 2022). GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) applies a mean-field approximation to
127     identify subgraphs that maximize the mutual information with GNN predictions. PGExplainer
128     (Luo et al., 2020) applies a similar objective, but samples subgraphs using the reparametrization
129
        trick. RCExplainer (Bajaj et al., 2021) generates counterfactual subgraph explanations. While
        post-hoc explainers treat a trained GNN as a black box —i.e., it only relies on predictions made by
130
        the GNN—ante-hoc explainers are model-dependent. DIR-GNN trains an intrinsically explainable
131
        GNN by discovering and generating label-invariant rationales (subgraphs). GIB (Yu et al., 2021)
132     and GSAT (Miao et al., 2022) apply the information Bottleneck principle to learn subgraphs
133     relevant for classification. MixupExplainer Zhang et al. (2023) enhances GIB’s concept with
134     mixup techniques to produce explainable graph augmentations that further improve predictive
135     performances. Interestingly, some other works turn to the concept of causality for explanation
136     Sui et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2022). Among them, OrphicX Lin et al. (2022) is a post-hoc method
137     in which causal features features are used to generatively construct causal explanatory graphs.
138     CAL Sui et al. (2022) is an ante-hoc model that learns to distinguish causally attended graphs and
139     trivially attended graphs.
140     Information Bottleneck principle: Information Bottleneck (IB) principle finds a balance between
141     compressing information and retaining relevant information for a specific task by distilling the
142     essential features from a dataset that are most relevant to predicting a target variable, while
143     discarding unnecessary details. This principle is has been widely used in machine learning,
144     especially in deep learning, to improve model efficiency and generalization (Amjad & Geiger,
145
        2019; Goldfeld & Polyanskiy, 2020; Kawaguchi et al., 2023; Saxe et al., 2019; Tishby & Zaslavsky,
        2015). The IB principle has gained significant attention in the interpretation of GNNs. Miao et al.
146
        (2022) proposed GSAT, which uses IB to construct ante-hoc explainers. GSAT controls the mutual
147
        information (MI) between input graphs G and explanatory subgraphs S via a stochastic attention
148     mechanism. Instead of minimizing I(S; G), a variational upper bound is minimized. The family of
149     distributions for the variational upper bound is constructed using a model in which edges are added
150     between two nodes based on a Bernoulli distribution. IB-subgraph (Yu et al., 2020) also generates
151     explanations using IB by adopting a bilevel optimization where I(S, G) is estimated in the inner
152     loop and maximizing the mutual information between S and the classification outcome Y in the
153     outer loop. Due to the difficulty of estimating I(S, G), the method utilizes sampling to compute an
154     upper bound. Chen et al. adopts a black box approach to explaining GNNs and focus on the Out of
155     Distribution problem.
156     Bilevel optimization: Bilevel optimization is a class of optimization problems where two objective
157     functions are nested within each other (Colson et al., 2007). Although the problem is known to be
158     NP-hard, recent algorithms have enabled the solution of large-scale problems in machine learning,
159     such as automatic hyperparameter optimization and meta-learning (Franceschi et al., 2018). Bilevel
160     optimization has also been applied to graph problems, including graph meta-learning (Huang &
161     Zitnik, 2020) and transductive graph sparsification (Wan & Schweitzer, 2021).

      We formulate our problem as a supervised graph classification. A graph G = (V, E) has
186
      node attributes xv for v ∈ V and edge attributes euv for (u, v) ∈ E. Given a graph set
187   G = {G1 , G2 , . . . , Gn } and continuous or discrete labels Y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yn } for each graph
188
      respectively, our goal is to learn a function fˆ : G → Y that approximates the labels of unseen graphs.

      3.2   I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK P RINCIPLE
191
192   The Information Bottleneck (IB) Principle (Tishby et al., 2000) offers a compelling framework
193   for understanding how to extract relevant information from data while compressing unnecessary
194   details. This principle emphasizes the balance between preserving essential information and reducing
195   complexity. The main idea is to find the most relevant information about a target variable from an
196   input variable, while minimizing the amount of information retained about the input itself. In the
197   case of graph learning, this idea is equivalent to finding the most predictive subgraphs by removing
198   irrelevant structures.
199   Formally, given an input graphs G and the target labels Y , the goal of the IB framework is to find
200   a subgraph S of G that maximizes the mutual information I(S; Y ) while minimizing the mutual
201   information I(S; G). This trade-off can be expressed as an minimization problem, balancing the
202   retention of relevant information about Y against the compactness of S:

226
      We would like to further contrast our approach with existing works. In equation 1, the optimization
      of the first term can be done by computing the cross-entropy loss with respect to the labels. It is
227
      the second term, I(S; G), that presents the main difficulty. Existing methods such as GSAT use a
228
      variational bound by defining a family of surrogate distributions that are generated in the input space
229   of graphs. Our approach is different. We compute an approximation in the representation space
230   of the graph data by computing the KL-divergence between p(Y |S) and p(Y |G), and using it to
231   update p(S) iteratively. Our novelty is in developing another alternative to approximating I(S; G),
232   and as we show later, for the datasets we consider, it achieves better results than those of the current
233   variational approaches.

235   3.3     EAGER: E FFECTIVE A NTE - HOC G RAPH E XPLAINER
236
237   We introduce EAGER, an ante-hoc explainer that generates explanations by optimizing the IB
238   principle via a bilevel process. EAGER performs compact and discriminative subgraph learning as
239   part of the GNN training that optimizes the prediction of class labels in graph classification tasks. Our
240   framework is illustrated in Figure 3. The explainer assigns an influence value to each edge, which
241   will be incorporated into the original graph. The GNN classifier is trained with this new graph over T
242   inner iterations. Gradients from inner iterations are kept to update the explainer in the outer loop.
243
      The outer iterations minimize a loss function that induces the explanations to be discriminative. We
      will now describe our approach in more detail.
244
245
246   3.3.1    E XPLAINER : S UBGRAPH L EARNING
247
      EAGER is an edge-based subgraph learner. It learns edge representations from edge features and the
248
      corresponding node representations/features. Surprisingly, most edge-based explainers for undirected
249   graphs are not permutation invariant when calculating edge representations (e.g., PGExplainer (Luo
250   et al., 2020) concatenates node representations based on their index order). Shuffling nodes could
251   change their performance drastically since the edge representations would differ. We calculate
252   permutation invariant edge representations hij given two node representations hi and hj as follows:
253   hij = [max(hi , hj ); min(hi , hj )], where max and min are pairwise for each dimension and [·; ·] is
254   the concatenation operator. Additionally, most existing explainers do not consider edge features,
255   which are crucial for certain domains such as molecular graphs. Since many recent GNNs readily
256   incorporate edge features (Hu et al., 2020b), we utilize them in processing the input graph to obtain
257
      hi and hj that take into account both node and edge features. Edge influences are learned via an MLP
      with sigmoid activation based on edge representations: zij = M LP (hij ). This generates an edge
258
      influence matrix Z ∈ [0, 1]n×n . We denote our explainer function as gΦ with trainable parameters Φ.
259

330   3.5   T IME C OMPLEXITY
331
332   Because EAGER relies on bilevel optimization, its time complexity is a critical discussion. Let CGN N
333   be the running cost of the underlying GNN and Chyper (T, d) be the cost of calculating hypergradients,
334   with T being the number of inner iterations and d being the dimension size. The running times
335
      of EAGER during training and inference are O(2CGN N ) and O((T + 1)CGN N + Chyper (T, d)),
      respectively. Notice that, in general, Chyper (T, d) scales linearly with the number of input graphs
336
      when the transition function (to go from one inner iteration to the next) is a typical neural network
337
      such as GNN Shaban et al. (2019). When T is small, our method is efficient with respect to the
338   running time of the backbone GNN. Appendix D provides details on the running time.

      Graph Classification Datasets We consider 5 binary graph classification datasets from Molecu-

      leNet (Wu et al., 2018): BBBP, C LIN T OX, T OX 21, T OX C AST, and HIV. Additionally, we include

      M UTAGENICITY (Kazius et al., 2005; Debnath et al., 1991; Morris et al., 2020), a popular molecular

351   Constructing Molecular Synthetic Datasets Most graph-based datasets with ground-truth expla-
352   nations consist of synthetic non-molecular graphs (Agarwal et al., 2023). Evaluating explainer models
353   using these datasets may not reflect the expected behavior of these models in real-world settings (e.g.,
354   drug discovery) due to the disparity between synthetic graphs and real-world graphs. Instead, we
355   curate 3 semi-synthetic molecular graph datasets with ground-truth explanations. We screen millions
356   of molecules from the ChemBL database (Gaulton et al., 2012) and extracted those with either or
357
      both Lactam and Benzoyl functional groups. Lactam groups are cyclic amides with various ring sizes
      and the Benzoyl group is a benzene ring attached to a carbonyl group. The 3 datasets are:
358
359
      • L ACTAM: Positive if molecules containing a Lactam group and negative otherwise. There is no
360     molecule with multiple Lactam groups.
361
362
      • B EN L AC (Benzoyl Lactam): Positive if molecules containing a Lactam group; negative if molecules
        containing a Benzoyl group, a Lactam group with a Benzoyl group, or no Lactam and Benzoyl
363
        groups. There is no molecule with multiple Lactam or Benzoyl groups.
364
365   • B EN L AC M (Benzoyl Lactam Multiclass): Class I if molecules contain a Lactam group; class II if

      One favorable aspect of these datasets is that the prediction is simple for most classifiers yet the

370   not only the quality of the explanation, but also the ability of the classifier to pick up the right signals
371   from data. Both aspects are important for EAGER and ante-hoc explainers as both the explanations
372   and the classifier are learned together. More details on these datasets are provided in Appendix A.

464   To further evaluate the quality of the explanations produced by EAGER, we compare them to those
465   produced by other baselines on the reproducibility metrics Kosan et al. (2023). Existing works,
466   especially those on post-hoc models, commonly employ the fidelity metrics Yuan et al. (2021).
467   However, fidelity requires comparing the predictions using the input graphs versus the predictions
468   using the explanations and is not applicable for an ante-hoc model like EAGER because our predictor
469
      follows an explainer and the prediction is based on the explanation. Reproducibility, instead, measures
      how explanations alone can predict class labels. We report the results on this metrics in Appendix B.
470
      In general, ante-hoc models like EAGER and GSAT outperform post-hoc models on reproducibility.
471
472   Finally, we report the estimation of mutual information I(S; G) in the synthetic datasets and compare
473   the minimization of this term between EAGER and GSAT in Table 2. This is an important analysis
474   that has been neglected in studies on interpretability on graphs. For both EAGER and GSAT, we vary
475
      the corresponding hyperparameters controlling the compactness. We encode the embeddings of the
      subgraph S as the weighted average of the edge embeddings produced by the explainer. For graph
476
      embeddings, we encode them as unweighted averages of edge embeddings. Similar encodings can
477
      be extended to GSAT. We use the slicing method proposed in Fayad & Ibrahim (2024) to efficiently
478   estimate the high-dimensional mutual information I(S; G). The results show that EAGER is better
479   (or as good) at minimizing the mutual information between the subgraphs and the input graphs. This
480   confirms the effectiveness of our proposed approach.

      4.5       A BLATION S TUDY
518
519   Varying the number of inner epochs and the backbone GNN: In Table 4, we show the classification
520   results in AUC of multiple EAGER trainings with different backbone GNNs and number of inner
521   iterations. Except for BBBP, EAGER with multiple inner epochs tend to perform better than EAGER
522   with only one inner epoch. Interestingly, more epochs do not guarantee equal or better performances.
523   For example, EAGER with GIN backbone achieves the best AUC on T OX 21 using 5 inner epochs.
524   This suggest that the number of inner epochs is an important hyperparameter that should be carefully
525
      tuned. There is no clear winner between GIN and GCN as backbone GNNs.
526   Learning with and without iterative optimization: We create a single-level version of EAGER
527   (EAGER-single) to test the effectiveness of our iterative optimization scheme. More specifically,
528   EAGER-single optimizes the explainer and GNN classifier parts in an end-to-end fashion with a
529   single loss function. EAGER consistently and significantly outperforms EAGER-single. We report
530   the detailed results in Appendix E.
531
532   5     C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   We investigated the problem of generating explanations for GNN-based graph-level classification and
535   proposed EAGER, a novel ante-hoc GNN explainer that supports Information Bottleneck objectives
536   with bilevel optimization. Our adaptation of IB is different from existing literature that typically
537   resorts to variational bounds. We compared EAGER against state-of-the-art graph classification
538   methods and GNN explainers using synthetic and real datasets. The results show that EAGER often
539   outperforms the baselines on multiple accuracy and explainability metrics. We also proposed three
      new datasets with ground-truth explanations from real-world molecules.

729   A.2    S EMI - SYNTHETIC M OLECULAR B ENCKMARKS
730
731   We introduce 3 semi-synthetic molecular datasets with ground-truth labels (Table 6). Specifically, we
732
      screen millions of real molecules from ChemBL (Gaulton et al., 2012) and randomly sample those
      with Lactam and/or Benzoyl substructures. Both of these functional groups are known to exhibit
733
      pharmaceutical values such as anti-bacterial effects. Additionally, lactam groups may have varying
734
      sizes, which is interesting as ground-truth explanations. The screening and processing are done via
735   RDKit. Next, we describe the semi-synthetic datasets in more details.
736
737   L ACTAM is a binary classification dataset to distinguish between molecules containing lactam groups
738
      and those not containing the lactam groups, with the former and the latter having the positive and
      negative labels, respectively. Benzoyl Lactam (B EN L AC) is also for binary classification but is a more
739
      challenging version of L ACTAM. In B EN L AC, we consider both the lactam and the benzoyl functional
740
      groups. Molecules may contain only lactam groups, only benzoyl groups, both lactam and benzoyl
741   groups, or none of those groups. Out of these molecules, only those containing only lactam groups
742   are the positive class. Benzoyl Lactam Multiclass B EN L AC M is a multiclass classification dataset.
743   B EN L AC M requires distinguishing between molecules with either lactam groups or benzoyl groups.
744   In this case, both classes have ground-truth explanations. Since most datasets on explainability
745   consider binary classification with ground-truth explanations only for one class of interest, we
746   believe it is interesting to look into scenarios in which different classes of data possesses different
747   explanations. These datasets have the same node and edge featurizations as those of the MoleculeNet
748   (Wu et al., 2018) benchmarks (e.g BBBP).

752   Reproducibility measures how explanations alone can predict class labels. It is a key property
753   as it allows users to correlate explanations and predictions without neglecting potentially relevant
754   information from the input. We vary the size of the explanations by thresholding edges based on
755   their importance. We then train a GNN using only the explanations and labels. We compare EAGER
      against post-hoc and ante-hoc explainers on M UTAGENICITY and show the results in Figure 5.

799   EAGER trains slower than standard and some of the sophisticated GNN methods, while having
800   comparable or faster testing time. For post-hoc graph explainers, EAGER is much faster in testing.
801   For ante-hoc explainers, EAGER is slower than in training than GSAT, is the same in testing compared
802
      to GSAT, and is significantly faster than DIR-GNN in both training and testing.
