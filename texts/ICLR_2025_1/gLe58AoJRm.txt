009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The widespread practice of indiscriminate data scraping to fine-tune language mod-
012            els raises significant legal and ethical concerns, particularly regarding compliance
013            with data protection laws such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).
014
               This practice often results in the unauthorized use of personal information,
               prompting growing debate within the academic and regulatory communities.
015
               Recent works have introduced the concept of generating unlearnable datasets
016
               (adding imperceptible noise to the clean data), such that the underlying model
017            converges during training but fails to generalize to the unseen test setting. Though
018            somewhat effective, these approaches are predominantly designed for images and
019            are limited by several practical constraints like requiring knowledge of the target
020            model and instability of bi-level optimization. To this end, we introduce R EG T EXT,
021            an information-theoretic framework to operationalize the right to data protection in
022            practice. In particular, R EG T EXT is a model-agnostic data generation framework
023            that leverages the frequency distribution of tokens within a given dataset to create
024            a ranking system, allowing for the systematic injection of selected words back into
025
               the dataset. We demonstrate R EG T EXT’s utility through rigorous theoretical and
               empirical analysis of small and large language models. Notably, R EG T EXT can
026
               restrict newer models like GPT-4o and Llama from learning on our generated data,
027
               resulting in a drop in their test accuracy compared to their zero-shot performance
028            and paving the way for generating unlearnable text to protect public data.

073   Notably, there has been limited progress in formally establishing a theoretical framework for generat-
074   ing unlearnable text data. Existing approaches primarily exhibit three significant practical limitations:
075   i) they are model-dependent, ii) they lack scalability, and iii) they rely on time-inefficient and unstable,
076   gradient-based methods (Ren et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023).
077
      While Li et al. (2023) adapts the optimization framework for images introduced by Huang et al. (2021)
      for text data, it still relies on a bi-level optimization approach which is computationally expensive.
078
      Consequently, this method struggles to scale effectively for billion-parameter models and has only
079
      demonstrated effectiveness with smaller architectures, such as LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
080   1997), Bidaf (Seo, 2016), and BERT (Devlin, 2018), particularly when applied to datasets with a
081   limited size, on the order of a few thousand samples. Furthermore, Li et al. (2023) performs word
082   level substitutions while generating the dataset which inevitably may lead to information loss.
083
084   Present work. In this work, we propose R EG T EXT, a model-agnostic unlearnable data generation
085
      framework. We draw key insights through an extensive theoretical analysis and propose a simple
      information-theoretic technique to identify task-representative words from a given dataset. We then
086
      show that low-frequency words in the task-representative subset are typically spurious, and propose a
087
      systematic approach to inject these spurious noises in the input examples of our dataset, keeping the
088   labels unchanged. Our results demonstrate that R EG T EXT is highly effective in inhibiting language
089   models (GPT-4o, LLama3.1-7B, Mistral-7B, Phi3-14B, and T5-xl-3B) from learning meaningful
090   representations from a variety of polarity datasets, and can effectively be run on a CPU.
091
092   Contributions. To summarize, we highlight that a simple and effective information theoretic approach
093
      can both protect public datasets and expose the vulnerabilities of LMs in their ability to generalize.
      Our contributions are as follows:
094
095    • We analyze the impact of token distribution on gradient magnitudes and provide a theoretical
096      foundation to identifying words for generating an unlearnable dataset.
097    • We propose an information theoretic approach to rank words in a dataset that is most task repre-
098      sentative (i.e., are discriminative) and are non-robust (i.e., are spurious). Next, we imperceptibly
099      inject these selected words into the dataset to generate an unlearnable dataset.
100
       • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first work to perform an in-depth analysis of unlearnable
101      datasets in natural language processing. We astonishingly discover that our simple information-
102      theoretic approach is highly effective at preventing arbitrary state-of-the-art LMs like GPT-4o
103      from generalizing to polarity datasets.

107   Our work lies at the intersection of the right to protect data principle in regulatory frameworks, data
      poisoning, and unlearnable attacks, which we discuss below.

      Right to Protect Data. The right to protect data is a fundamental principle in several international
109   laws and regulations, ensuring individuals retain control over how their data is used, processed,
110   and shared. The GDPR (Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017), California Consumer Privacy Act
111   (CCPA) (Cal) and Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados (LGPD) (Brazil) provides robust protections
112   through rights such as the right to object, allowing individuals to prevent their data from being used
113   for purposes like profiling or automated decision-making without consent and restrict data processing.
114   Together, these laws affirm the individual’s right to safeguard their data, preventing unauthorized
115   uses, especially as ML models increasingly rely on vast public datasets to train AI systems.
116   Data poisoning. Data poisoning attacks compromise DNNs by altering their training data, often
117   through the introduction of malicious examples. The goal is to degrade model performance, either
118   by reducing accuracy on clean data or by causing specific misclassification. Early work on data
119   poisoning focused on attacks against SVMs (Biggio et al., 2012), with later efforts extending to
120   DNNs by introducing adversarial noise to key training examples (Koh & Liang, 2017). However,
121   these attacks often result in only slight performance drops and produce easily detectable poisoned
122   examples (Muñoz-González et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2017). Another form of data poisoning is
123   backdoor attacks, where we embed trigger patterns in the data to induce model failures when
124
      triggered while leaving performance on clean data unaffected (Chen et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020; Wan
      et al., 2023a). Despite their stealth, backdoor attacks are less suited for preventing the exploitation of
125
      data, as they don’t hinder overall test performance (Shafahi et al., 2018; Barni et al., 2019).
126
127   Unlearnable dataset. Recent works have introduced unlearnable examples as a defense mechanism,
128   where imperceptible noise is added to all training data, leading to a significant drop in test accuracy
129   (Huang et al., 2021), where these perturbations interfere with the gradient-based optimization pro-
130
      cesses used in training and prevent DNNs to exploit the data. The key distinction between unlearnable
      datasets from data poisoning lies in the objective, i.e., inhibiting a model’s ability to learn meaningful
131
      features from the data. Prior works have predominantly focused on vision data (Huang et al., 2021;
132
      Berns et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024; Sadasivan et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Zhao
133   et al., 2023) by adding imperceptible pixel perturbations. While some recent works have extended
134   unlearnable examples to audio (Zhang & Huang, 2024) and text (Li et al., 2023) modalities, there is a
135   significant gap in the feasibility of making textual data unlearnable, particularly owing to its discrete
136   nature. Li et al. (2023) address this by adapting the bi-level optimization from Huang et al. (2021)
137   and uses a gradient-based search to generate unlearnable text by finding optimal word substitutions
138   that minimize loss. However, it requires model weights and is computationally expensive, making it
139   impractical for datasets with longer sentences for LLMs and even simple LSTM models.

141   3    G ENERATING U NLEARNABLE DATA
142   In this section, we describe the notations, problem settings, and the goal of generating unlearnable data,
143   followed by our proposed model-agnostic R EG T EXT approach to generate unlearnable text.
144
145   Notation. Consider a data owner O with a natural language dataset Dc = (Xc , Yc ) of N examples.
146
      Following the traditional fine-tuning setup (Mishra et al., 2022), Xc is the set of questions, and Yc is
      the set of answers/labels corresponding to the questions. Consider the scenario of a data owner O, who
147
      wants to make their dataset publicly available but also wants to prevent untrusted entities like model
148
      owner A, from fine-tuning an arbitrary model M on the released data Dctrain ⊂ Dc . With the growing
149   trend of LLMs being trained on internet-scraped datasets, it’s crucial for data owners to protect
150   their data from such unsolicited use. To facilitate data sharing with untrusted parties (i.e., internet),
151   consider a function T that transforms Xc , such that the transformed dataset Dutrain = (T (Xctrain ), Yc )
152   is unlearnable. Concretely, Dutrain ensures that while M converges on the transformed dataset, it fails
153   to perform adequately on the unseen test setting, where the downstream test dataset Dctest remains
154   untouched, i.e., is clean. Further, the semantic meaning and the labels of Dutrain remain the same. For
155   the remainder of this paper, we use the word “token" and “word" interchangably.
156   Problem Setting. Following previous unlearnability works (Huang et al., 2021), we assume that the
157   model owner A has or gains access to the dataset Dutrain , which is reasonable as Dutrain would typically
158   be shared with external untrusted entities like the internet for varied reasons. Further, the model
159   owner A may use arbitrary state-of-the-art models that are not available to the data owner O.
160   This makes the problem challenging since the released data must be agnostic to the type of model
161   used to learn representations from it. Following the setup described in Huang et al. (2021), we call a
      dataset unlearnable iff an arbitrary model M fine-tuned on Dutrain learns the training distribution well,

      but fails to generalize to the test dataset Dctest given the semantic meaning of the unlearnable (Dutrain )
163   and clean (Dctrain ) train datasets are the same.
164
165    Our Goal. We aim to transform any given clean fine-tuning dataset Dctrain into an unlearnable
166    dataset Dutrain that can be released to untrusted sources with arbitrary models. This is achieved
167    by proposing a function T . The key characteristics of T are that it is both independent of M and
168    does not completely change the semantic meaning of Dctrain .

170   3.1 O UR M ETHOD
171   First, we detail the motivation and foundation of our proposed method and then describe R EG T EXT
172   and its key components - ranking words and algorithm.
173
      Motivation. Our objective is to develop a model-agnostic approach for unlearnable text generation.
174   To achieve this, we examine the relationship between the magnitudes of the loss gradients and token
175   frequencies (see Sec. 3.2), where we show that the average gradient magnitude per occurrence for
176   an individual low-frequency token is larger than that for a high-frequency token, highlighting the
177   unique contribution of low-frequency tokens to model learning. Our analysis shows an inverse
178   relationship, i.e., the most representative tokens for a given class have low relative frequencies. In
179   addition, previous research in shortcut learning (Wang et al., 2022a) has also identified representative
180   tokens by extracting attention scores from task-fine-tuned models (e.g., Devlin (2018)), which makes
181   those methods model-dependent.
182   Consider the example of a sentiment analysis task, such as IMDb classification – movies directed
183   by renowned filmmakers often receive overwhelmingly positive reviews. This creates a spurious
184   correlation between the filmmaker’s names and sentiment, leading language models (LMs) to learn
185   shortcuts that can undermine their robustness. As demonstrated by Du et al. (2023) and Wang et al.
186   (2022a), these shortcuts can hinder the reliability of LMs in accurately assessing sentiment. This
187   implies the existence of a subset of tokens that promote shortcut learning, viz. spurious words – e.g.,
188
      the names of famous filmmakers. We posit that systematic injection of spurious words within a
      dataset can increase the likelihood of shortcut learning, thereby not allowing LMs to generalize. Next,
189
      we describe our approach in detail.
190
191   R EG T EXT. Words in a dataset can be categorized as containing redundant features, robust features,
192   and spurious features (Du et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022a). In Sec.3.2, we provide a theoretical
193   foundation for our model-agnostic framework to identify the most representative tokens for a task,
194   unlike previous works that are predominantly model-dependent (Wang et al., 2022a). Specifically, we
195   demonstrate that low-frequency tokens are most representative of a task given that the magnitude
196
      of the gradient of the loss function for these tokens is higher. We build on insights by (Wang et al.,
      2022a) and categorize the relatively low-frequency words from the task-representative group as
197
      spurious words that have a high impact on the model’s performance.
198
199   In doing so, we rank the words in Dctrain based on their relative Pointwise Mutual Information
200   (PMI) (Church & Hanks, 1990) which is a well known metric for measuring association between
201   words and labels. It is also well established that PMI of low-frequency words is higher (Role & Nadif,
202
      2011), which bolsters our claim in Sec. 3.2. For instance, GOOD, LOVE, N OLAN have a high relative
      PMI (task-specific words) for the positive class in the IMDb sentiment classification dataset, and
203
      high-frequency words like MOVIE, and THE have low relative PMI. Furthermore, the spurious token
204
      N OLAN has the lowest relative frequency amongst the three words – GOOD, LOVE, and N OLAN.
205
      Words with high relative PMI and low frequency should then theoretically be non-robust or spurious.

246   In this section, we aim to understand the impact of token distribution on gradient magnitudes using
247
      properties of the underlying dataset and model gradients.
248   Setup. Let a given neural network model be trained using a natural language dataset Do . The dataset
249   comprises a single vocabulary V that represents a set of unique “tokens” (words or sub-words). Let
250   L represent the set of low-frequency tokens and H represent the set of high-frequency tokens, with
251   the cardinality |L|≫ |H|. Further, Ei ∈ Rd is the embedding for token i, and fi is the frequency of
252   token i in Do . Next, we denote the gradient of the loss function with respect to Ei at its j th occurrence
253   as ∇Ei,j . Let ϕ : N → R be a monotonically decreasing function such that ∥∇Ei,j ∥= ϕ(fi ) for all
254
      occurrences j of a given token i, indicating that the gradient magnitude for each occurrence of a token
      is a function of its frequency. Finally, we define the aggregate gradient impact for a set of tokens S

257   gradient magnitude has the following properties.
258   Axiom 1 (Monotonicity). By definition, ϕ is a monotonically decreasing function, it captures the
259   inverse relationship between frequency and gradient magnitude, i.e., as fi increases, ϕ(fi ) decreases.
260
261
      For natural language dataset, common tokens (e.g., ‘the’, ‘is’, ‘at’) tend to provide less informative
      signals for model learning, as their presence is less predictive of the outcome (say, the sentiment of
262
      a sentence) than low-frequency tokens (more context-specific tokens). To this end, neural network
263
      models are designed to adjust weights (or in this case, embeddings Ei ) to reduce loss by learning
264   from errors; more informative tokens (typically less frequent) contribute more to the learning process
265   as they provide more unique context or meaning. Hence, common tokens (high fi ) are generally less
266   informative, and ∥∇Ei,j ∥ have smaller magnitudes, as small adjustments are needed less urgently
267   for these tokens that do not provide strong discriminative power. However, low-frequency tokens
268   (low fi ) are contextually more informative and provide stronger signals to the model during training.
269   Therefore, the gradients for low-frequency tokens have a larger magnitude, reflecting the need for
      more significant adjustments to their embeddings.

      the injection algorithm outlined in Algorithm 1, we set the number of unique perturbations per class,
      Nw , to 1 for AGNews and IMDb, and 10 for NI Polarity. The thresholds wmin and wmax are fixed at
342
      0.01 and 10, respectively. We use 4-bit models and fine-tune them with a Q-LoRA rank of 16 due to
343
      computational constraints, except for T5, which undergoes full model finetuning. We observe the best
344   performance for T5 using wmax =10. And we find that the Phi3-medium model does not converge on
345   the clean dataset at rank 16, so we report its results at rank 128, where it performs adequately. All our
346   experiments were run using the PyTorch library and a single A100-80GB GPU.

349   In this section, we focus on key research questions to evaluate the effectiveness of R EG T EXT.
350
      RQ1: Does R EG T EXT limit LMs from generalizing during finetuning? The primary goal of
351   R EG T EXT is to curate finetuning datasets that imperceptibly inhibit generalization on arbitrary
352   LMs. This implies that a) clean test performance must be low, and b) training performance
353   must be high. We substantiate the effectiveness of R EG T EXT on seven models of varying scales
354   across three datasets in Table 1 and show that R EG T EXT consistently limits the performance of
355   LMs. Our key observations include : a) On IMDb, the zero shot performance of GPT-4o-mini is
356   the highest, yet with R EG T EXT we observe that after finetuning the performance drops 4% points.
357   With our unlearnable dataset, the relative improvement achieved with GPT-4o-mini on AGNews and
358   NI Polarity after is only 5.61% and 3.70% respectively. Error-min performs similar to clean, and
359
      doesn’t reduce the test accuracy in any case as R EG T EXT. b) On the IMDb dataset, the zero-shot
      performance of all models is above 70%. Yet, R EG T EXT consistently results in a final accuracy lower
360
      than zero-shot performance for 5/6 models.c) On Natural Instructions (NI) (Wang et al., 2022b) we
361
      demonstrate that R EG T EXT is effective at limiting the performance of LMs on out-of-distribution
362   tasks (Appendix B.2). Most notably, the performance of Llama3.1-8B-Instruct drops by 7.53% points
363   from the zero-shot 58.56%. b) In Fig. 3 we underscore the imperceptibility of R EG T EXT, and show
364   that despite the poor test performance, the training losses converge well giving the impression that
365   model is learning.
366

370   of R EG T EXT on pretrained and instruction tuned models alike.

514   5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
515
516   In this paper, we have explored the first attempt to operationalize the “right to protect data” into
517
      algorithmic practice, where we propose R EG T EXT, a model-agnostic data generation framework
      that limits LMs from learning new information from data. In contrast to existing works, our method
518
      doesn’t use any model-dependent bi-level optimization and works even on LLMs like GPT-4o-mini.
519
      Our extensive theoretical (Sec. 3.2) and empirical (Sec. 4.2) studies highlight the motivation and
520   effectiveness of R EG T EXT. In particular, we show that R EG T EXT outperforms existing baselines like
521   error-minimizing noise across three datasets and seven LMs (Table 1). R EG T EXT has a broad impact
522   on public data and the NLP community, highlighting the vulnerability of LMs in doing shortcut
523   learning and showing the impact of R EG T EXT on diverse public datasets. Finally, we demonstrate
524   the imperceptibility of our added poisons by comparing the distribution of clean vs. R EG T EXT
525   data (Table 2) distribution and the consistency of our proposed method across different fine-tuning
526   settings. While R EG T EXT shows initial promise in generating unlearnable text data and opening up
527   new frontiers in operationalizing the right to protect data, there are still many practical limitations
528
      which we discuss below.
529   Limitations. Since our proposed data generation framework is model-independent, we do not use
530   any particular tokenizers used by state-of-the-art LMs in processing our datasets. Our vocabulary is
531   created by splitting text sequences into individual words using white-space characters. While this
532   works for text in English language, splitting text in other languages like Chinese and Japanese that
533   do not have spaces is non-trivial. We aim to explore novel techniques in creating model-independent
534
      vocabulary and scale R EG T EXT for other languages in future work. Further, while our runs across
      different seeds demonstrate the effectiveness of R EG T EXT in generating unlearnable data, the
535
      data-generating process is highly dependent on the seed as it determines the location of the added
536
      perturbation. We plan to reduce this stochasticity in our future work.
