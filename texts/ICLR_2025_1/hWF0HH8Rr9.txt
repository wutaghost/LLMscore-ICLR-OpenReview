009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel approach to Traffic Signal Control (TSC) in a multi-agent environment
012            by modeling communication among agents as a sequence problem, enabling intersections
013            within road networks to communicate with one another. Taking inspiration from point
014            cloud processing and graph neural networks, we make our architecture capable of handling
015            variable road network topologies, including differing numbers of intersections and intersec-
016            tion types, and demonstrate this by successfully training on real & randomly generated road
017            networks and traffic demands. Furthermore, we demonstrate that even utilizing minimal
018
               state information can achieve competitive performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
022
      When traffic lights are controlled effectively, the industry, the climate and the individual profits. Traffic
023
      congestion causes 3.9B Euro in economic damages due to lost time each year in Germany alone (Inrix, 2022).
024   In stop-and-go traffic, emissions are 29 times higher than in free flowing traffic (Greenlight, 2024).
025
026   Due to these impacts, Traffic Signal Control (TSC) has become a crucial field of research. It involves using
027
      traffic lights at intersections to manage traffic flow with the objective to reduce congestion and enhance safety.
      However, these goals are not easily achieved due to the problem’s dynamic and unpredictable nature. Traffic
028
      flow varies significantly throughout the day, influenced by factors such as rush hours, weather, accidents,
029
      events, etc. that require real-time adaptive solutions. Additionally, traffic signals can not be managed as
030   stand-alone agents: intersections serve as nodes in a larger network, and traffic at one intersection will affect
031   the flow at others. Coordinating signals across multiple intersections is thus necessary. Different stakeholders
032   in TSC also have varying objectives that must be balanced. For instance, drivers want minimal waiting time,
033   pedestrians and cyclists prioritize safety, and city planners aim to reduce overall emissions and costs incurred
034   due to delays. Existing traffic infrastructures most commonly employ the usage of deterministic models such
035   as round-robin scheduling or predetermined phase periods which do not account for such objectives (Tomar
036   et al., 2022).
037   The hardware that a TSC scheme is deployed to also poses a barrier to the wide-spread implementation
038   of more advanced TSC algorithms. In German cities, a fair percentage of the traffic controllers found
039   at intersections cannot be dynamically controlled. In TSC algorithm implementation, these must also be
040   accounted for, e.g. through a one-time change of signal plans which best support the dynamically controlled
041   traffic lights. Faulty traffic sensor data or low sensor coverage further limit applicability, and historical data
042   from sensors may be hard to acquire on a large scale. Thus, an approach that does not require large amounts
043   of real data for training is beneficial.
044   In this work, we use the SUMO simulation environment (Lopez et al., 2018a) to simulate generated road
045   networks. Our architecture for TSC, covered in section 2, takes lane-level observations such as number of
046   vehicles on the lane and current traffic signal status as input. It then uses a Transformer to allow lanes to

                                                         1

047
      attend to one other and projects their observations into a hidden representation. These are pooled and fed into
048
      a fixed-sized MLP, forcing the model to compress the information into a size which is the same for any road
049   network, independent of the number of lanes. This allows our model to be capable of handling changing road
050   networks, as is often the case due to road closures, construction, accidents etc.
051
052
      Our aim with this work was to address the problems outlined above. We focused on employing the most
      recent advances in the existing literature, and tackle the problem of TSC in a novel manner. The following are
053
      our main contributions:
054
055          • We built an automated pipeline of dataset generation. This is a significant contribution, as we have
056            addressed the need of large amounts of data to train the transformer model effectively without
057            reliance on limited real-world data. Our pipeline is not only able to produce varying complexity road
058
               networks (from a simple ring network to a large scale city network), but it can also produce complex
               and dynamic traffic flows.
059
             • We treat inter-agent’s spatial dependencies as a 2D sequence problem and utilize the powerful
060
               transformer architecture to model this sequence. This approach is our primary novelty as it differs
061            from existing literature which use transformer models to encode the state history Chen et al. (2021).
062          • Due to our novel modeling approach, our pipeline is capable of handling variable input sizes - both in
063            the number of intersections in a road network and the intersection sizes. This alleviates the problem
064            of fixed input sizes prevalent in other model architectures. Our model can thus be easily transferred
065            and deployed regardless of the training environment setup.
066          • Finally, with extensive experiments, we show that usage of minimal state information – available
067            using tools such as Google Maps – is sufficient to achieve competitive performance. This contribution
068            serves as an important breakthrough, suggesting that reliance on expensive sensor technology might
069
               not be necessary.

190   2.2.1      P ERMUTATION -I NVARIANT L ANE E NCODING
191
192   Let us now introduce the first step of our pipeline: the lane encoding mechanism. Each agent ai has access
                                                                                          i
193   to information from its incoming and outgoing lanes, denoted as {li,1 , . . . , li,L }. The value of Li varies
194   based on the intersection type, and assigning a canonical order to these lanes is challenging due to the diverse
195   shapes of intersections in a road network. We, therefore, seek to find a canonical encoding of the lane-level
196   information through permutation-invariance and take inspiration from point cloud processing to also minimize
197   the influence of the lance count Li . We concatenate lane-level information individually for each lane and feed
198   it through a PointNet encoder Qi et al. (2017), which consists of a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) projecting
199   lane features to a high-dimensional space and a permutation-invariant reduction by max-pooling over an
200
      agent’s individual lanes. By projection to a high-dimensional space, the information content can be well
      retained beyond the max-pooling operation as Qi et al. demonstrated in their seminal work Qi et al. (2017).
201
      The weights of the MLP are shared across all agents and an in-depth visualization is shown in Fig. 1.

267
      As detailed in the section 2.4, we use SUMO (Simulation of Urban MObility) for traffic data simulation,
      where an environment E consists of 1. the road network and 2. the traffic demand. Our model can be trained
268
      using either synthetically generated environments or use imported real world road networks (for instance,
269
      the map of Zürich). This was built into our pipeline to allow for thorough training and testing of our model
270   performance, and for evaluating its ability to handle a wide range of network complexities.
271
272   Our marked contribution here is that we have implemented a methodology to automate the dataset and data
273
      distribution generation process, where we can sample new environments conditioned on hyperparameters,
      such as the allowed range for N (number of agents / intersections) and the average traffic density ρ, i.e.
274
      E ∼ PE (·|N, ρ). This also allows us to resample the environment during training to avoid over-fitting or to
275
      train on several environments simultaneously. Fig. 3 shows some of our generated road network samples for
276   low values of N and a more complicated example is shown in Fig. 6a.

      4    D ISCUSSION & F UTURE W ORK
397
398   For the simple network training experiments, as seen in section 3.1, the models showed rapid convergence and
399   had similar performance during evaluation. Since this training was done with static traffic flows, it gave us our
400   primary proof of concept for our novel approach to modeling the TSC problem: the transformer architecture
401
      was able to effectively model the communication using its attention mechanism without hindering training
      and convergence. Evaluation metrics in the simple network setup when compared to static baseline support
402
      this finding.
403
404   Further evaluation was done on more complex road networks. As outlined in section 3.2, our transformer
405   model converged on all levels of state information, with similar performance on each level. It is an interesting
406   finding that the performance does not seem to depend at all on the state knowledge, and all observation types
407
      reach the same results as seen in Fig. 6.
408   A possible avenue for future work, emerging from our multi-network environment experiments, is to develop
409   a unified model capable of understanding diverse urban landscapes and traffic demands. This model aims
410   to handle dynamic changes, such as construction sites, seamlessly across various city environments. Our
411   contributed dataset, training pipeline and model architecture can serve as a strong foundation for this endeavor.

414   5    C ONCLUSION
415
416   We were able to successfully engineer a training pipeline with randomized environment generation. Our novel
417   architecture allows training on arbitrary environments without any modifications. Our approach to modeling
418   this problem enables our model to handle variable sizes of input networks - essentially removing the need for
419   training multiple networks based on network complexity.
420   Our results show the efficacy of using language modeling methods for the task of multi-agent RL for TSC. By
