                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               In reinforcement learning, off-policy actor-critic approaches like DDPG and TD3
012
               are based on the deterministic policy gradient. Herein, the Q-function is trained
013            from off-policy environment data and the actor (policy) is trained to maximize the
014            Q-function via gradient ascent. We observe that in complex tasks like dexterous
015            manipulation and restricted locomotion, the Q-value is a complex function of
016            action, having several local optima or discontinuities. This poses a challenge for
017            gradient ascent to traverse and makes the actor prone to get stuck at local optima.
018            To address this, we introduce a new actor architecture that combines two simple
019            insights: (i) use multiple actors and evaluate the Q-value maximizing action, and (ii)
020            learn surrogates to the Q-function that are simpler to optimize with gradient-based
021
               methods. We evaluate tasks such as restricted locomotion, dexterous manipulation,
               and large discrete-action space recommender systems and show that our actor finds
022
               more optimal actions and outperforms alternate actor architectures.

087   2 R ELATED W ORK
088
      Q-learning (Watkins & Dayan, 1992; Tesauro et al., 1995) is a fundamental value-based RL algorithm
089
      that iteratively updates Q-values to make optimal decisions. Deep Q-learning (Mnih et al., 2015)
090   has been applied to tasks with manageable discrete action spaces, such as Atari (Mnih et al., 2013;
091   Espeholt et al., 2018; Hessel et al., 2018), traffic control (Abdoos et al., 2011), and small-scale
092   recommender systems (Chen et al., 2019). However, scaling Q-learning to continuous or large
093   discrete action spaces requires specialized techniques to efficiently maximize the Q-function.
094
      Analytical Q-optimization. Analytical optimization of certain Q-functions, such as wire fitting
095
      algorithm (Baird & Klopf, 1993) and normalized advantage functions (Gu et al., 2016; Wang et al.,
096   2019), allows closed-form action maximization without an actor. Likewise, Amos et al. (2017)
097   assume that the Q-function is convex in actions and use a convex solver for action selection. In
098   contrast, the Q-functions considered in this paper are inherently non-convex in action space, making
099   such an assumption invalid. Generally, analytical Q-functions lack the expressiveness of deep
100   Q-networks (Hornik et al., 1989), making them unsuitable to model complex tasks like in Figure 2.
101
      Evolutionary Algorithms for Q-optimization. Evolutionary algorithms like simulated anneal-
102   ing (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983), genetic algorithms (Srinivas & Patnaik, 1994), tabu search (Glover,
103   1990), and the cross-entropy method (CEM) (De Boer et al., 2005) are employed in RL for global
104   optimization (Hu et al., 2007). Approaches such as QT-Opt (Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Lee et al.,
105   2023; Kalashnikov et al., 2021) utilize CEM for action search, while hybrid actor-critic methods
106   like CEM-RL (Pourchot & Sigaud, 2018), GRAC (Shao et al., 2022), and Cross-Entropy Guided
107   Policies (Simmons-Edler et al., 2019) combine evolutionary techniques with gradient descent. Despite
      their effectiveness, CEM-based methods require numerous Q-function evaluations and struggle with

108
      high-dimensional actions (Yan et al., 2019). In contrast, SAVO achieves superior performance with
109   only a few (e.g., three) Q-evaluations, as demonstrated in experiments (Section 6).
110
111   Actor-Critic Methods with Gradient Ascent. Actor-critic methods can be on-policy (Williams,
112
      1992; Schulman et al., 2015; 2017) primarily guided by the policy gradient of expected returns, or
      off-policy (Silver et al., 2014; Lillicrap et al., 2015; Fujimoto et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020) primarily
113
      guided by the bellman error on the critic. Deterministic Policy Gradient (DPG) (Silver et al., 2014)
114
      and its extensions like DDPG Lillicrap et al. (2015), TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018) and REDQ (Chen
115   et al., 2020) optimize actors by following the critic’s gradient. Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja
116   et al., 2018) extends DPG to stochastic actors. However, these methods can get trapped in local
117   optima within the Q-function landscape. SAVO addresses this limitation by enhancing gradient-based
118   actor training. This issue also affects stochastic actors, where a local optimum means an action
119   distribution (instead of a single action) that fails to minimize the KL divergence from the Q-function
120   density fully, and is a potential area for future research.
121   Sampling-Augmented Actor-Critic. Sampling multiple actions and evaluating their Q-values is
122   a common strategy to find optimal actions. Greedy actor-critic (Neumann et al., 2018) samples
123   high-entropy actions and trains the actor towards the best Q-valued action, yet remains susceptible to
124   local optima. In large discrete action spaces, methods like Wolpertinger (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2015)
125   use k-nearest neighbors to propose actions, requiring extensive Q-evaluations on up to 10% of total
126   actions. In contrast, SAVO efficiently generates high-quality action proposals through successive
127   actor improvements without being confined to local neighborhoods.
128   Ensemble-Augmented Actor-Critic. Ensembles of policies enhance exploration by providing
129   diverse action proposals through varied initializations (Osband et al., 2016; Chen & Peng, 2019; Song
130   et al., 2023; Zheng12 et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2017). The best action is selected based on Q-value
131   evaluations. Unlike ensemble methods, SAVO systematically eliminates local optima, offering a
132   more reliable optimization process for complex tasks (Section 6).

      4    A PPROACH : S UCCESSIVE ACTORS FOR VALUE O PTIMIZATION (SAVO)
209
210   Our objective is to design an actor architecture that efficiently discovers better actions in complex,
211   non-convex Q-function landscapes. We focus on gradient-based actors and introduce two key ideas:
212   1. Maximizing Over Multiple Policies: By combining policies using an arg max over their Q-
213      values, we can construct a policy that performs at least as well as any individual policy (§4.1).
214   2. Simplifying the Q-Landscape: Drawing inspiration from tabu search (Glover, 1990), we propose
215      using actions with good Q-values to eliminate or "tabu" the Q-function regions with lower Q-values,
         thereby reducing local optima and facilitating gradient-based optimization (§4.2).

402   • 1-Actor, k=3 samples (Wolpertinger): Gaussian sampling centered on actor’s output. For discrete
403
        actions, we select 3-NN discrete actions around the continuous action (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2015).
404   • k=3-Actors (Ensemble): Each actor (Osband et al., 2016) can find different local optima, improving

        the best action.
406   • Evolutionary actor (CEM): Repeated rounds of search with CEM over the action space (Kalash-
407
        nikov et al., 2018).
408   • Greedy-AC: Greedy Actor Critic (Neumann et al., 2018) trains a high-entropy proposal policy and
409
        primary actor trained from best proposals with gradient updates.
410   • Greedy TD3: Our version of Greedy-AC with TD3 exploration and update improvements.
411   • SAVO: Our method with 3 successive actors and surrogate Q-landscapes.
412   We ablate the crucial components and design decisions in SAVO:
413
414   • SAVO - Approximation: removes the approximate surrogates (Sec. 4.4), using Ψi instead of Ψ̂i .
415   • SAVO - Previous Actions: removes conditioning on a<i in SAVO’s actors and surrogates.
416   • SAVO + Action Smoothing: TD3’s policy smoothing (Fujimoto et al., 2018) adds action noise to
417     compute Q-targets.
418   • SAVO + Joint Action: trains an actor with a joint action space of 3 × D. The k action samples are
419     obtained by splitting the joint action into D dimensions. Validates successive nature of SAVO.
420   Aggregate performance. We utilize performance profiles (Agarwal et al., 2021) to aggregate results
421   across different environments in Figure 7a (evaluation mechanism detailed in Appendix H.1). SAVO
422   consistently outperforms baseline actor architectures like single-actor (TD3) and sampling-augmented
423   actor (Wolpertinger), showing the best robustness across challenging Q-landscapes. In Figure 7b,
424   SAVO outperforms its ablations, validating each proposed component and design decision.

       E.2       R ESETTING BASELINES
1228
1229
       In this section, we clarify the distinction between primacy bias and the challenge of getting stuck in
1230   local optima within Q-landscapes. Primacy bias, as addressed in Nikishin et al. (2022); Kim et al.
1231   (2024), occurs when an agent is trapped in suboptimal behaviors from early training, and solutions
1232   like resetting (reinitializing the parameters of last few layers) and re-learning from the replay buffer
1233   mitigate this by avoiding reliance on initially collected samples.
1234
       However, these methods do not reduce the probability of an actor getting stuck in Q-function local
1235
       optima (the issue we consider in this work). In fact resetting could cause an otherwise optimal actor
1236   to get stuck in suboptima during retraining. To demonstrate this, we conducted a reset baseline
1237   experiment, following Nikishin et al. (2022), on TD3 in MineEnv. Here, Full-reset refers to the
1238   reset all strategy proposed by Kim et al. (2024), while Last-layer-OOO corresponds to the approach
1239   in Nikishin et al. (2022). Finally, TD3 (no reset) represents the standard TD3 algorithm without
1240   these extensions. We observed no performance improvements over the standard TD3. In contrast,
1241   our method, SAVO, directly addresses this problem by employing an actor architecture specifically
       designed to navigate non-convex Q-landscapes, making it more robust to local optima.

       F.1       S UCCESSIVE ACTORS
1332
1333
       The whole actor has a successive format and each successive actor will receive two pieces of infor-
1334
       mation: the state observation and the action list generated by previous successive actors. Given the
1335
       concatenation of the input components above, a 4-layer MLP with ReLU will process this information

1337   the previous action list. After being transformed by an optional action-list-encoder, together with
1338   the state information, they become the input of next successive actor’s input. In the end, the action
1339   list will be processed with 1-NN to find the nearest discrete action. After this, this action list will be
1340   delivered to the selection Q-network.

1344
1345   The critic has a one-to-one mapping relationship with the actor. The whole critic consists of a
1346   list of successive critics and each successive critic will receive three pieces of information: the
1347   state observation, the action list generated by previous successive actors, and the action provided
1348   by the corresponding successive actor. Given the concatenation of the input components above, a
1349   2-layer MLP with ReLU will process this information and generate the action’s value for one single
       successive actor. This value will be used to update itself and the actor with TD-error.

       F.3   L IST S UMMARIZERS
1351
1352   In order to extract meaningful information from the list of candidate actions, following Jain et al.
1353   (2021) we employed the sequential models and the list-summarizer as follows;
1354
       Bi-LSTM: The raw action representations of candidate actions are passed on to the 2-layer MLP
1355
       followed by ReLU. Then, the output of the MLP is processed by a 2-layer bidirectional LSTM (Huang
1356   et al., 2015). Another 2-layer MLP follows this to create the action set summary to be used in the
1357   following successive actor.
1358
1359
       DeepSet: The raw action representations of candidate actions are passed on to the 2-layer MLP
       followed by ReLU. Then, the output of the MLP is aggregated by the mean pooling over all the
1360
       candidate actions to compress the information. Finally, the 2-layer MLP with ReLU provides the
1361
       resultant action summary to the following successive actor.
1362

1364   with ReLU before inputting the candidate actions into a self-attention and feed-forward network to
1365
       summarize the information. Afterward the summarization will be part of the input of the following

1368   F.4   F EATURE - WISE L INEAR M ODULATION (F I LM)
1369
       Feature-wise Linear Modulation (Perez et al., 2018), is a technique commonly applied in neural
1370
       networks for tasks like image recognition. It enhances adaptability by dynamically adjusting inter-
1371
       mediate feature representations. Using learned parameters from one layer, FiLM linearly modulates
1372   features in another layer, allowing the network to selectively emphasize or de-emphasize aspects of
1373   the input data. This flexibility is beneficial for capturing complex and context-specific relationships,
1374   improving the model’s performance in various tasks.
1375
1376   F.5   S ELECTION Q- NETWORK
1377
1378   The selection Q-network sequentially evaluates the Q-value of the retrieved candidate actions by the
1379   cascading actors. Thus, it receives a concatenated information of state and an action embedding for
1380   each candidate action. Then, it selects the action with the largest Q-value amongst candidate actions
1381   to act on the environment.

       To demonstrate the impact of Q-smoothing in both discrete and continuous action spaces, we

1623
1624
       Discrete Action Spaces: Importance of Q-Smoothing.
1625   For discrete tasks, smoothing the Q-function significantly enhances performance by mitigating the
1626   complexity of local optima in diverse Q-value landscapes. This experiment primarily compares 1-

       Q-smoothing.
1631   Continuous Action Spaces: Limited Impact of Q-Smoothing.
1632
       In continuous action spaces, Q-smoothing does not yield a significant performance gain. In Wolper-
1633
       tinger, both the naive and Q-smoothing variants show comparable performance, indicating sufficient
1634   gradient information is present throughout the action space (unlike discrete action space tasks that
1635   have missing true Q-values).
1636
1637
       For these tasks, as shown in Fig. 24, the introduction of Q-smoothing neither improves nor degrades
       performance. This justifies its exclusion from our continuous action space experiments and explains
1638
       why we reported results for Wolpertinger [Naive] in these environments, as it is closer to the
1639
       underlying TD3 algorithm. Note that the TD3 and SAVO results also exclude Q-smoothing.
1640
1641   Conclusion. Q-smoothing is crucial for discrete action space tasks, as demonstrated by its strong
1642   performance in our results. However, it provides no added value for continuous tasks. Consequently,
1643
       our baselines reflect these observations, ensuring fair comparisons across all evaluated methods.

1647   To explore the potential impact of diverse policy and surrogate value function initializations on
1648   algorithm performance, we tested two specialized initialization strategies beyond the default Xavier
1649   initialization (Glorot & Bengio, 2010):
1650          • Xavier (default). Weights are initialized with the default initialization: w ∼Xavier-init
1651
1652          • Random. Weights are initialized from a standard normal distribution, i.e., w ∼ N (0, 1).
1653
              • Add. Weights are initialized using Xavier initialization, followed by the addition of scaled
1654
                standard normal noise, i.e., x ∼ Xavier-init, y ∼ 0.5 · N (0, 1), and w = x + y.
1655
1656   We compare these specialized initialization strategies in various tasks, with reward curves reported in
1657   Fig. 25 and summarized below:
1658
1659          • MineWorld: Add ≈ Random ≈ Xavier
1660
1661          • RecSim: Add ≈ Random ≈ Xavier
1662
              • Hopper (Restricted): Add ≈ Random ≈ Xavier
1663
1664          • Adroit Door: Add ≈ Random < Xavier
1665
1666   Findings. The results indicate that specialized initialization strategies aimed at increasing diversity
1667   do not particularly improve performance. Across most tasks, Add and Random strategies perform
1668   similarly to standard Xavier initialization. However, in the Adroit Door task, the specialized initial-
1669   izations underperform compared to Xavier, suggesting that task-specific factors might influence the
1670
       effectiveness of standard initialization strategies.
1671   Conclusion. While our experiments show no significant benefit from specialized initialization strate-
1672   gies, the idea of explicitly incorporating diversity into the optimization process remains promising.
1673   We believe that designing algorithms with explicit diversity objectives throughout training could
       serve as a valuable heuristic in future work.

       I.2   H EURISTIC R ECOMMENDATIONS FOR ACTOR S ELECTION
1901   As a practical guideline on a new environment, we recommend using as many additional actors as
1902   the compute budget allows, so one does not need to determine the “optimal number of actors” to
1903   begin with. This works because SAVO’s improvements are fairly monotonic with the number of
1904   actors. In our experiments, we observed that even 3 actors gave meaningful improvements. Below,
1905   we propose a heuristic to answer what is the optimal number of actors to enable SAVO to reach
1906   near-global-optimal performance.
1907   Determining the optimal number of actors for a task is inherently tied to the non-convexity of the
1908   Q-landscape, which depends on the underlying MDP. One can follow the following steps to search
1909   for this number:
1910
              1. Start from a benchmark: Map the task to one of the environments analyzed in Table 4 and
1911
                 Figure 26. For instance, new dexterous manipulation tasks would be similar to Adroit Door
1912
                 and use the corresponding number of actors (i.e., 10).
1913
1914
              2. Apply exponential search: Start with a small number of actors and double iteratively until
                 performance saturates (e.g., 5 → 10 → 20 → . . . ), following exponential search.
