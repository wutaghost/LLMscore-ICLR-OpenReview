010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            Data contamination gradually becomes inevitable during the development of large
013            language models (LLMs), meaning the training data commonly integrates those
014
               evaluation benchmarks unintentionally. This subsequently makes it hard to bench-
               mark LLMs fairly. This paper introduces a novel approach called LNE-Blocking
015
               for contamination detection and contamination mitigation evaluation. For the first
016
               component, Length Normalized Entropy (LNE) reports a SOTA performance for
017            contamination detection. On this basis, LNE-Blocking reports a SOTA perfor-
018            mance for contamination mitigation evaluation by applying LNE to adjust the
019            intensity of blocking operation, specialized to suppressing the maximum value of
020            output candidates during the generation process. We conduct extensive experi-
021            ments on both contamination detection and contamination mitigation evaluation
022            tasks. The results indicate that LNE and LNE-Blocking achieve an obvious SOTA
023            performance. Simultaneously, LNE-Blocking is robust across tasks and models of
024            different contamination level, reducing computational costs by nearly 25x com-
025
               pared to previous methods. We hope our method will open new research avenues
               on data contamination for LLMs. We plan to release the resources upon publica-
026
               tion of this work to facilitate future work.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the era of fierce development with large language models (LLMs), it has been a popular research
032   topic across many areas such as chain-of-thought reasoning (Wang et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024),
033   machine translation (Lu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024a), code generation (Li et al., 2023a; Zhang
034   et al., 2023), and even spatial reasoning (Hu et al., 2024). Despite the fact that LLMs are usually
035   strong on many tasks, Natural Language Processing (NLP) practitioners secretly face a common
036   problem called data contamination when conducting NLP research and engineering which frequently
037   relies on benchmark data that could be potentially contaminated in LLMs.
038   Data contamination, also called data leakage, occurs when the test data is inadvertently included in
039   the model’s training data (Magar & Schwartz, 2022; Golchin & Surdeanu, 2024). This causes the
040   model to perform exceptionally well on the leaked test data. Due to the immense scale and diverse
041   origins of the pre-trained datasets used for LLMs, they are more vulnerable to data contamination.
042   This can be divided into two main scenarios: 1) Existing benchmark datasets are more prone to
043   leakage because of extensive text quotations and code reuse present in the LLMs’ training data. 2)
044
      For emerging benchmark datasets, newly created test data may already be included in the constantly
      expanding training data of LLMs, as the details of these training datasets are often unknown.
045
046   As a result, preventing benchmark data contamination in LLMs becomes highly challenging. This
047   prevents NLP developers and researchers from honestly judging the LLMs. With the growth of the
048   training set which is partially synthetic or automatically crawled from the internet, the problem of
049   data contamination is gradually unavoidable, even when the LLM developers do not intentionally
050
      do it. Hence, our research question is particularly important: how can we accurately detect data
      contamination and how can we accurately assess model performance even when it is contaminated?
051
052   To remediate the problem of data contamination, previous research mainly focused on how to de-
053   tect whether the data is leaked in the models rigorously (Deng et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Elazar
      et al., 2024). One less studied yet important problem is to quantitatively assess the genuine perfor-

054
      mance of the models, even if they are contaminated (Zhu et al., 2024b; Dong et al., 2024; Ye et al.,
055   2024). Such research, knowned as contamination mitigation evaluation, could be quite useful as
056   LLMs are usually trained with large-scale data, and many LLMs are potentially contaminated even
057   though some of them carefully handled this problem. How to fairly compare the models is therefore
058   necessary to decide which models to use.
059
      This paper proposed a novel approach for contamination detection and contamination mitigation
060
      evaluation, which is composed of two novel components. The first component is Length Normalized
061   Entropy (LNE) for contamination detection. We are the first to apply this method to detect data con-
062   tamination and surprisingly, we found it better than previous methods. On this basis, LNE-Blocking,
063   applying LNE to adjust the intensity of blocking operation which specializes to suppressing the max-
064   imum value of output candidates during the generation process, reports a SOTA performance for
065   contamination mitigation evaluation. Additionally, Since our method requires only two inferences
066   for contamination mitigation evaluation, compared to 50 samplings in existing methods, TED(Dong
067   et al., 2024), it is 25 times faster. Additionally, experiments show that our approach is highly robust
068   across different tasks and models with varying levels of contamination.
069   To this end, we make three key contributions:
070
071          • We propose a novel approach for contamination detection and fairly assessing possibly
072            contaminated models.
073          • Extensive experiments conducted across two tasks and multiple LLMs have yielded state-
074            of-the-art (SOTA) results, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of our proposed
075            method.
076          • Our proposed SOTA method is 25x faster and more robust than the previous method.

      Contaminated models often exhibit a high lexical overlap between their output and the ground truth,
081
      which is indicative of memory phenomena (Magar & Schwartz, 2022). As shown in Figure 1, as the
082
      degree of contamination increases, the overlap between the output generated by greedy decoding
083   and the ground truth significantly rises. This behaviour reflects the model’s tendency to memorize
084   the training data rather than generalizing it. One solution (Dong et al., 2024) to assess the true
085   performance of such contaminated models involves generating diverse outputs through multiple
086   sampling and filtering out any instances of the standard ground truth. Then, they expect to derive
087   answers that stem from the model’s generalization abilities, rather than its memorized knowledge.
088
      However, answers based on memory phenomena tend to have a very high likelihood within the
089   model, meaning that obtaining non-memorized, generalized answers requires a large number of sam-
090   plings. Previous work (Dong et al., 2024) has found that at least 50 samples are necessary to achieve
091   satisfactory performance estimates. This process is both highly random and time-consuming.
092
      The randomness makes it difficult to consistently generate non-memorized answers, particularly
093
      for models with heavy contamination, where such outputs are rarely sampled. This often leads to
094
      unreliable performance estimates, as we demonstrate in subsequent experiments in section 6.2.2.
095
096   Similar to how humans can rephrase their thoughts when interrupted, LLMs have the potential to
097   generate alternative answers when their default response is blocked (Lu & Lam, 2023; Wang &
098
      Zhou, 2024). By leveraging this ability, we can apply a strategy that suppresses the generation
      of the token with the highest probability, referred to as blocking, without significantly impacting
099
      performance. This approach reduces the reliance on memorized content and encourages the model
100
      to draw on its generalization capabilities, as demonstrated by the reduction in ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004)
101   similarity between the model’s output and the memorized answers, as in Figure 1. Then, blocking
102   enables the model to produce more diverse and generalized outputs, without sacrificing quality.
103
104
      For example, the output of the contaminated model is return len(set(string.lower())). If the model is
      good at coding, after applying the blocking strategy, the model could generate an equivalent piece
105
      of code using its generalization ability, such as return len(character.lower() for character in string).
106
107   Additionally, as shown in Figure 1, models with varying degrees of contamination exhibit different
      levels of memorization, and the impact of the blocking operation varies accordingly. To effectively

      3         R ELATED W ORK
147
      Data Contamination Detection The issue of data contamination in large language models
148
      (LLMs) gained attention in the context of GPT-3 Brown (2020), where the vast pre-training cor-
149
      pus inevitably overlapped with evaluation benchmarks. To address this, GPT-3 employed n-gram
150   overlap detection to filter out data in the training set that conflicted with test set benchmarks. Some
151   work (Pan et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2023; Jacovi et al., 2023; Dodge et al., 2021) exposed the serious
152   consequences of data contamination and urged attention to this problem. However, most LLMs re-
153   leased to date have not made their pre-training corpus publicly available, introducing new challenges
154   for contamination detection.
155
      Recent efforts have attempted to detect contamination without direct access to the pre-training data.
156   Min-k% Prob (Shi et al., 2023) calculates the average of the smallest k% probabilities of gener-
157   ated tokens and flags potential contamination if this average exceeds a certain threshold. Similarly,
158   perplexity(Li, 2023) is also used to detect contamination, assuming that leaked data tends to pro-
159   duce lower perplexity scores. CDD (Dong et al., 2024) detects contamination by sampling multiple
160   times to determine the proportion of highly similar samples in the dataset. The methods mentioned
161   above do not fully leverage all the distributional information obtained from a single inference. They
      merely select the maximum probability at each output position to compute the score. In contrast, our


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      method, LNE, utilizes entropy to exploit more information, thereby achieving better contamination
163   detection performance.
164
165   Contamination Mitigation Evaluation To evaluate LLMs in the context of potential data contam-
166   ination, several methodologies generate new datasets that do not overlap with the model’s training
167   data, adopting a dataset-centric perspective. GSM-Plus(Li et al., 2024b) ensures that benchmark data
168   is absent from the model’s training set by reconstructing the original GSM8k dataset (Cobbe et al.,
169   2021) through the introduction of perturbations. GSM1k(Zhang et al., 2024) involves the creation
170   of a completely new dataset from scratch. It remains private and is only made publicly available
171   at a future point in time. Furthermore, TreeEval(Li et al., 2024c) assesses model performance by
172
      dynamically querying the language model, using more powerful LLMs for the querying process.
173   One less explored but important problem is how to quantitatively assess the performance of models
174   using datasets that have already been leaked. CleanEval(Zhu et al., 2023) proposed paraphras-
175   ing contaminated datasets using LLMs for evaluation purposes. TED (Dong et al., 2024) filters
176   non-memorized samples through multiple sampling rounds, using these samples for contamination
177   mitigation evaluation. However, these methods often require extensive preprocessing or multiple
178
      sampling iterations, making them highly time-consuming. LNE-Blocking is the first strategy that
      evaluates the performance of contaminated models using two inferences, without modifying the
179
      original dataset.

335
336   For contamination detection, we utilize the AUC and F1-score for the evaluation (Dong et al.,
337   2024). It’s worth noting that when calculating the F1 score, we select the optimal threshold for
338   each strategy at different contamination levels to obtain the best F1 for each strategy. This ensures a
339   fair comparison of the F1 scores across the strategies.
340   For contamination mitigation evaluation, we measure the model’s performance after contamination
341   has been mitigated. Specifically, the performance metrics used for code generation and arithmetic
342   reasoning tasks are Pass@1 and exact match Accuracy (Dong et al., 2024), respectively. Addi-
343   tionally, we introduce a novel metric, Performance Gap (PG), to evaluate the effectiveness of the
344   contamination mitigation strategies. The PG is defined as:
345                                    PG = abs(E(Yeva ) − E(YMorigin ))                           (12)
346   where Morigin refers to the original uncontaminated model. PG quantifies how closely the perfor-
347   mance of the model after mitigation aligns with that of the original uncontaminated model. The
348   smaller the PG value is, the better the methods are.

      6.1   DATA C ONTAMINATION D ETECTION
353
354   Data Composition: We validated the efficacy of contamination detection methods by distinguishing
355   the outputs of the uncontaminated CodeLlama model on the HumanEval test set with outputs from
356   CodeLlama models with varying degrees of contamination.
357
      Baselines:We compared Length Normalized Entropy (LNE) with existing contamination detection
358   methods, including: 1) Perplexity(Li, 2023): calculates the perplexity of the response generated
359   by the model through greedy decoding. Lower perplexity indicates higher contamination levels; 2)
360   Min-k% Prob(Shi et al., 2023): Computes the negative average log probability of the k% least
361   probable tokens in the response generated by the model through greedy decoding. Smaller values of
362   Min-k% Prob indicate higher contamination levels; and 3) CDD(Dong et al., 2024): After generating
363   multiple sampled outputs based on the prompt, it calculates the proportion of samples where the edit
364   distance is below a certain threshold. Higher CDD values indicate higher contamination levels. The
365   definition of these existing methods is illustrated in Appendix D.1.
366   As shown in Table 1, LNE performs comparably to other baseline methods, and as the contamination
367   level increases, the AUC of these methods can reach 1, approaching a perfect detector. However,
368   for the more challenging setting of mild contamination, our model’s strategy significantly outper-
369   forms other methods. For higher levels of contamination, our model falls slightly short of the best
370   performance, yet under overall contamination detection, our strategy proves to be more effective.
371   It is also worth noting that the CDD strategy requires multiple samplings, and Perplexity relies on
372   ground truth. In contrast, our LNE and Min-k methods do not require these, utilizing only the scores
373   of the models’ output, offering greater convenience.

524   7     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we introduced LNE-Blocking, a novel approach for contamination detection and con-
527
      tamination mitigation evaluation. For the first component, Length Normalized Entropy (LNE) is
      used for contamination detection. On this basis, LNE-Blocking is used for contamination mitiga-
528
      tion evaluation by applying LNE to adjust the intensity of the blocking operation. Our extensive
529
      experiments on code generation and mathematical reasoning tasks demonstrate that LNE-Blocking
530   consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance while being significantly more efficient, reduc-
531   ing computational costs by 25x compared to previous methods, and more robust than the previous
532   method across tasks and models with varying levels of contamination.

742   Relying solely on the declarations from the model developers may not guarantee that the model has
743   not been contaminated by the test dataset, which weakens the validity of our prior contamination
744   simulation and may affect the experimental conclusions. Therefore, it is essential to ensure that the
745   test dataset was not included in the training set of the uncontaminated model in order to validate the
746
      effectiveness of our strategy.
747   To address this issue, we have used a new dataset, GSM-Plus (Li et al., 2024a), to simulate contam-
748   ination. It is an augmented version of GSM8K with various mathematical perturbations, including
749   numerical variation, arithmetic variation, problem understanding challenges, distractor insertion,
750   and critical thinking tasks. This dataset was released in January 2024, which is after the release of
751   Llama2. Given the randomness of these perturbations and the innovative nature of the techniques
752
      employed, it is highly likely that the original uncontaminated version of Llama2 was not exposed to
      contamination during its training.
