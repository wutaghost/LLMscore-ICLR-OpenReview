009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image diffusion models rely on massive, web-scale datasets. Training
012            them from scratch is computationally expensive, and as a result, developers of-
013            ten prefer to make incremental updates to existing models. These updates often
014            compose fine-tuning steps (to learn new concepts or improve model performance)
015            with “unlearning” steps (to “forget” existing concepts, such as copyrighted data
016
               or the ability to generate explicit content). In this work, we demonstrate a critical
               and previously unknown vulnerability that arises in this paradigm: even under be-
017
               nign, non-adversarial conditions, fine-tuning a text-to-image diffusion model on
018
               seemingly unrelated images can cause it to “relearn” concepts that were previ-
019            ously “unlearned.” We comprehensively investigate the causes and scope of this
020            phenomenon, which we term concept resurgence, by performing a series of exper-
021            iments based on fine-tuning Stable Diffusion v1.4 alongside “mass concept era-
022            sure,” the current state of the art for unlearning in text-to-image diffusion models
023            (Lu et al., 2024). Our findings underscore the fragility of composing incremen-
024            tal model updates, and raise new serious concerns about current approaches to
025            ensuring the safety and alignment of text-to-image diffusion models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Modern generative models are not static. In an ideal world, developing new models would require
030   minimal resources, allowing users to tailor unique, freshly trained models to every downstream use
031   case. In practice, making incremental updates to existing models is far more cost-effective, which
032   is why it is common for models developed for one context to be updated for use in another. This
033   paradigm of updating pre-trained models is widely considered beneficial, as it promotes broader
034   and more accessible development of AI. However, for sequential updates to become a sustainable
035   standard, it is critical to ensure that these updates compose in predictable ways.
036   Developers commonly update models to acquire new information or improve performance—for ex-
037   ample, by fine-tuning an existing model on a novel dataset tailored to a particular use case. But
038   sometimes, developers also seek to remove information from an existing model. One prominent
039   example is machine unlearning, which aims to efficiently update a trained model to “forget” por-
040   tions of its training data (Cao & Yang, 2015; Nguyen et al., 2022b; Belrose et al., 2024), in order
041   to respond to privacy concerns. This is especially important for compliance with regulations like
042   the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) “right to be forgotten” (European Parliament and
043   Council of the European Union, 2016).
044   Here, we focus on the related notion of “concept unlearning” in the context of text-to-image diffusion
045   models (hereafter, referred to as “diffusion models”). In contrast to machine unlearning, which
046   targets particular data points, concept unlearning seeks to erase general categories of content, such
047   as offensive or explicit images. There has been substantial recent progress in this area. For example,
048   the current state-of-the-art in “mass concept erasure” (MACE) can now effectively erase dozens of
049   concepts from a pre-trained diffusion model (Lu et al., 2024). This is useful when dealing with
050
      generative models where undesired concepts are unknown during pre-training, but need to be erased
      as the model is adapted for different users or applications.
051
052   Our work begins with a surprising observation: fine-tuning a diffusion model can re-introduce
053   previously erased concepts, even when fine-tuning on unrelated concepts (see Figure 1 for an exam-
      ple). This hidden vulnerability, which we call concept resurgence, challenges the current paradigm

      2    BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
098
099
      Machine unlearning. We build on a growing literature on machine unlearning (Bourtoule et al.,
100
      2021; Nguyen et al., 2022a; Kurmanji et al., 2023; Cao & Yang, 2015; Gupta et al., 2021; Suriyaku-
101
      mar & Wilson, 2022; Sekhari et al., 2021; Ghazi et al., 2023; Kurmanji et al., 2023; Lev & Wilson,
102   2024), which develops methods for efficiently modifying a trained machine learning model to forget
103   some portion of its training data. In the context of classical discriminative models, machine un-
104   learning is often motivated by a desire to preserve the privacy of individuals who may appear in the
105   training data. A key catalyst for this work was the introduction of Article 17 of the European Union
106   General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which preserves an individual’s “right to be forgotten”
107   (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2016). More recent work in machine un-
      learning has expanded to include modern generative AI models, which may reproduce copyrighted

                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      material, generate offensive or explicit content, or leak sensitive information which appears in their
109   training data (Zhang et al., 2023a; Carlini et al., 2023). Our work focuses specifically on unlearn-
110   ing in the context of text-to-image diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2021). The
111   literature on diffusion models has grown rapidly over the last few years; though we cannot provide
112   a comprehensive overview here, we refer to Zhang et al. (2023a) for an excellent recent survey.
113
      Concept unlearning. Our work is directly inspired by a line of recent research that proposes meth-
114
      ods for inducing models to forget abstract concepts (Belrose et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Fuchi &
115   Takagi, 2024; Gandikota et al., 2024), as opposed to simply unlearning specific training examples.
116   A key challenge in this context is maintaining acceptable model performance on concepts that are
117   not targeted for unlearning, especially those closely related to the erased concepts. At the time of
118   this work, Lu et al. (2024) (MACE) is the state of the art in terms of both erasure performance and
119   image generation quality after unlearning.
120
      MACE: mass concept erasure in diffusion models. We build directly on the recent work of Lu
121   et al. (2024) for mass concept erasure (MACE) in diffusion models, which establishes the state-of-
122   the-art in concept unlearning for diffusion models. At a high level, MACE fine-tunes a model to
123   erase certain target phrase (e.g., “an image of a ship”) and their related concepts (e.g., “an image
124   of a boat”) by using a combination of cross-attention refinement and low rank adaptation (LoRA)
125   (Hu et al., 2021). Cross-attention refinement modifies the “key” embeddings associated with each
126   token co-existing in the target phrase with the corresponding “key” embedding of co-existing words
127   in a more generic phrase. The second step, LoRA fine-tuning, perturbs the weights of the model to
128   minimize activations in regions which correspond to the target phrase; these regions are identified
129
      by segmenting the image using Grounded-SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). These
      perturbations are learned via low rank adapatation (LoRA) of the model parameters (Hu et al., 2021).
130
      Finally, the LoRA modules corresponding to each erased concept are combined to produce a final
131
      model by formulating the “fusion” of multiple LoRA modules as a quadratic programming problem.
132   For additional detail on MACE we refer the reader to Lu et al. (2024).
133
134   Attacking machine unlearning systems. Finally, a recent line of research explores data poisoning
135
      attacks targeting machine unlearning systems, including Chen et al. (2021); Marchant et al. (2022);
      Carlini et al. (2022); Di et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024). These works show
136
      that certain new risks, such as camouflaged data poisoning attacks and backdoor attacks, can be
137
      implemented via the ”updatability” functionality in machine unlearning, even when the underlying
138   algorithm unlearns perfectly (i.e., simulates retraining-from-scratch). In contrast, our work exposes
139   a qualitatively new kind of vulnerability in machine unlearning, where a previously forgotten con-
140   cept may be reacquired as a consequence of additional learning.

      3   C OMPOSING U PDATES C AUSES C ONCEPT R ESURGENCE
144
      As discussed in Section 1, the scale of modern diffusion models has motivated a new paradigm
145
      in which updates to pretrained models are incrementally composed to avoid retraining models from
146   scratch. These updates broadly take the form of one of two interventions: either the model is updated
147   to learn a new concept, or it is updated to “unlearn” an unwanted concept. The standard procedure
148   for learning new concepts is to curate a dataset of images representing the new concept of interest and
149   fine-tune the model on this dataset. Similarly, to unlearn an unwanted concept(s), an “unlearning”
150   algorithm will typically update the weights of the pretrained model in an attempt to ensure that the
151   model no longer generates content associated with that concept. These two steps may be repeatedly
152   composed over the lifetime of a deployed model. This paradigm raises an important question:
153
                      To what extent is concept erasure robust to compositional updates?
154
155
      We begin our investigation with Stable Diffusion v1.4, and apply MACE to a pair of unlearning
156   tasks: celebrity erasure and object erasure. We describe these tasks in detail below. For each task,
157   we fine-tune the model on a random set of in-domain concepts after MACE has been applied. For
158   example, in the context of celebrity erasure — where the goal of the erasure task is to “unlearn” the
159   ability to generate images of a particular celebrity — we further fine-tune the resulting model on a
160   random set of celebrity images (which exclude the unlearned celebrity). This is intended to simulate
161   the real world paradigm of composing unlearning with unrelated fine-tuning steps, the latter of which
      are intended to help the model learn new concepts or otherwise improve performance. In particular,

      we do not fine-tune the model on adversarially chosen concepts, as our goal is to understand whether
163   benign updates can degrade or otherwise alter performance. For work on adversarial attacks and/or
164   jailbreaking of text-to-image diffusion models, see Ma et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024); Dong et al.
165   (2024).

      Via these experiments, we uncover a phenomenon we term concept resurgence: composing unlearn-
167
      ing and fine-tuning may cause a model to regain knowledge of previously erased concepts. Below
168
      we provide additional detail on each of these tasks and quantify the degree of concept resurgence.
169
170   Celebrity Erasure. Following Lu et al. (2024), the first benchmark we consider is inducing the
171   model to forget how to generate certain celebrities (the “erase set”) while retaining the ability to
172
      generate others (the “retain set”). We benchmark Stable Diffusion v1.4 on four subtasks in which we
      apply MACE to unlearn 1, 5, 10 or 100 celebrities, and then evaluate whether the model succeeds
173
      in generating images of these celebrities (e.g., after being prompted with “A portrait of [erased
174
      celebrity name]”). To ensure consistency, both the subtasks and prompts are identical to those in Lu
175   et al. (2024); the full set of celebrities in each subtask, along with the prompts use to evaluate the
176   model, are provided in Appendix C.1. We quantify model performance by separately computing the
177   mean top-1 accuracy of the Giphy Celebrity Detector (GCD) (Hasty et al., 2019) on both erased and
178   retained celebrities.1 Additionally, we quantify the model’s ability to continue to generate general
179   concepts using the CLIP Score (i.e. the cosine similarity between the prompts and generated images)
180   computed on a random sample of 5000 captions from MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2014) (COCO-5K)
181   and the FID between the generated images from pretrained Stable Diffusion and those from our
182   experiments for COCO-5K.
183   Object Erasure. Following Lu et al. (2024), the second benchmark we consider is inducing the
184   model to forget how to generate certain types of objects from the CIFAR10 dataset (the “erase set”)
185   while retaining the ability to generate others (the “retain set”). We apply MACE to Stable Diffusion
186   v1.4 across four subtasks, where we unlearn individual objects (automobile, ship, bird) as well as a
187   set of five objects (automobile, ship, bird, cat, and truck). Next, we evaluate whether the model can
188   generate images of these objects and their synonyms (e.g., after being prompted with “a photo of
189
      the [erased object]”). Both the full set of erased objects and retained objects, along with the prompts
      used to evaluate the model, are provided in Appendix C.1. As in the celebrity erasure task, we
190
      adopt the set of concepts to be erased, evaluation prompts and other hyperparameters from Lu et al.
191
      (2024).2 We quantify model performance by computing the CLIP accuracy on the set of evaluation
192   prompts. Following Lu et al. (2024), we do not compute FID-5K and CLIP-5K for the object erasure
193   task; COCO-5K is itself composed of common objects, and the goal of this evaluation is to assess
194   performance on generic concepts unrelated to the erasure task.
195
      As discussed above, object erasure and celebrity erasure are two of the four tasks considered in Lu
196
      et al. (2024). We discuss our choice to exclude the other two (artistic style and explicit content
197
      erasure) in Appendix A.

      4     A LGORITHMIC FACTORS D RIVING C ONCEPT R ESURGENCE
306
307
      The compositional updating pipeline involves several algorithmic choices that can either contribute
308
      to or mitigate the risk of concept resurgence. We focus on common choices made across most
309   concept unlearning algorithms for diffusion models, using MACE as our baseline technique to assess
310   their impact. These choices include regularization in the cross-attention refinement update, mapping
311   the embedding of the concept to be unlearned to more general concepts, and the selection of the
312   algorithm used for fine-tuning. In the following two sections we focus our attention on the erase
313   10 celebrities and erase ship tasks as we vary other components of the training pipeline.
314
      At a high level, we hypothesize that resurgence occurs because unlearning does not update the model
315   parameters to be sufficiently “far away” from the pretrained weights. Thus, although unlearning may
316   initially suppress the generation of unwanted concepts, even a modest degree of fine-tuning tends to
317   shift model weights towards their initial state, and thus reintroduce seemingly erased concepts. To
318   validate this hypothesis, we first assess the impact of three algorithmic factors on concept resurgence:
319   regularization, choice of mapping concept, and the fine-tuning algorithm.

438   The second component of the pipeline we investigate is the data the unlearned models are fine-tuned
439   on. As discussed in Section 3, we choose not to focus on adversarial dataset constructions (including

      et al. (2024); Dong et al. (2024). Instead, we consider the kind of dataset constructions that occur as
442
      part of common and benign use. For example, the end user of an open source diffusion model may
443
      want to fine-tune the model to acquire new concepts which were excluded from the pretraining set
444   and/or improve its performance on particular tasks of interest. In Section 3, we demonstrated that
445   fine-tuning on random in-domain concepts (e.g., fine-tuning on randomly chosen celebrities after
446   unlearning) can lead to concept resurgence. In this section, we seek to further investigate the role
447   of dataset construction in concept resurgence. First, we consider fine-tuning on in-domain concepts
448   with varying levels of similarity to the concepts which are initially unlearned. Intuitively, it may
449   be that fine-tuning on more related concepts can exacerbate the degree of concept resurgence. We
450   then turn to fine-tuning on out-of-domain concepts; e.g., fine-tuning on images of randomly chosen
451   objects after a celebrity unlearning task. We describe these experiments in more detail below.
452
453   5.1   CLIP D ISTANCE
454
455
      First, we consider fine-tuning on unrelated but in-domain concepts as described above. We further
      segment these concepts by thresholding the CLIP distance to the unlearned concepts, which we use
456
      as a proxy for how “related” the fine-tuning dataset is to the unlearned concepts. In particular, for
457
      each task we find a publicly available dataset with hundreds of concepts in the same domain (e.g.,
458   for the celebrity unlearning task, this is a dataset of celebrity images). We describe these datasets in
459   detail in Appendix C.2. For each concept in the corresponding dataset, we compute its maximum
460   CLIP cosine similarity over the set of unlearned concepts. We then partition this fine-tuning set into
461   three evenly sized subsets based on the terciles of the CLIP cosine similarity. Finally, we randomly
462   sample 10 concepts from each tercile to create different fine-tuning datasets which vary in their
463   degree of “relatedness” to the unlearned concepts. We present these results in Figure 9.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
510
511
      The scale of generative models introduces new challenges, including the risk of learning concepts
512   that are unsuitable or undesirable for certain downstream applications. Ideally, unlearning meth-
513   ods would allow model developers to precisely and permanently remove unwanted concepts while
514   preserving the model’s overall utility. Unfortunately, reality is more complex.
515
      Our work uncovers a critical limitation of current unlearning methods, which we termed concept
516
      resurgence. We demonstrate this phenomenon through rigorous empirical evaluations, highlighting
517
      the practical limitations of state-of-the-art unlearning techniques. These findings emphasize the
518   need to rethink current approaches to concept erasure, especially in contexts where maintaining the
519   integrity of model updates is essential.
520
521
      Our investigation opens up several important avenues for future work. For example, we do not
      provide a theoretical characterization of concept resurgence, nor do we present a strategy designed
522
      to prevent it from happening. Both developments could help to enhance the robustness of unlearning
523
      methods. Additionally, though our evaluations focus on well-known academic benchmarks, further
524   research is necessary to assess the prevalence of concept resurgence in real-world deployments.
525
526   Concept resurgence also raises important questions about responsibility for downstream harms. De-
527
      spite a developer’s best efforts to sanitize a model using these techniques, a downstream user who
      fine-tunes a published model might be surprised to discover that guardrails put in place by the de-
528
      veloper no longer exist. This creates a dilemma: is the developer obligated to permanently and
529
      irrevocably erase problematic concepts, or does responsibility shift to the downstream when they
530   (inadvertently) reintroduce them?
531
532   Despite these challenges, concept unlearning remains a valuable tool for model developers. By
533
      identifying its vulnerabilities, our work aims to drive the development of erasure techniques that
      remain robust throughout a model’s life-cycle or develop tools that can help developers anticipate
534
      when concept resurgence is likely to happen. Addressing these weaknesses will be essential for
535
      ensuring the safety and alignment of generative models as they are fine-tuned and adapted for diverse
536   applications.

      A     E XCLUDING A RTISTIC S TYLE AND E XPLICIT C ONTENT E RASURE
757
758
      As discussed in Section 3, celebrity and object erasure are two of the four benchmarks considered
759   in Lu et al. (2024). The other two considered are artistic style erasure — unlearning the ability
760   to generate images in the style of specific artists, e.g., due to copyright concerns — and explicit
761   content erasure, particularly to suppress nudity. We exclude artistic style erasure due to the diffi-
762   culty of quantifying the degree of a particular style in an image. In particular, unlike celebrity or
763   object erasure, artistic style is not localized to specific regions in an image, and instead is a holis-
764   tic (and partially subjective) property of the model output. For example, Drouillard et al. (2024)
765   note that “courts have emphasized the importance of considering the ‘total concept and overall feel’
766   [for determining whether copyright infringement has occurred], rather than relying on mechanical
767
      dissection or quantitative measures alone.” Characterizing artistic style replication (and copyright
      infringement more broadly) is rich topic in its own right, and we refer to Tenenbaum & Freeman
768
      (1996); Somepalli et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2023b); Casper et al. (2023) for additional background.
769
770   We further exclude the explicit content benchmark due to the sensitive and unpredictable nature of
771   the images which may be generated by the model, the lack of agreed upon standards for conducting
772   such evaluations responsibly, and recent well publicized examples of the real-world harm that can
773
      result from synthetic but realistic nude images (Lapowsky, 2023; Grose, 2024). Instead, we use the
      object and celebrity erasure tasks as representative but benign benchmarks on which to conduct our
774
      evaluations.
