009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, text-to-speech (TTS) technology has witnessed impressive advance-
012            ments, particularly with large-scale training datasets, showcasing human-level
013            speech quality and impressive zero-shot capabilities on unseen speakers. However,
014            despite human subjective evaluations, such as the mean opinion score (MOS),
015            remaining the gold standard for assessing the quality of synthetic speech, even
016            state-of-the-art TTS approaches have kept human feedback isolated from training
017            that resulted in mismatched training objectives and evaluation metrics. In this
018
               work, we investigate a novel topic of integrating subjective human evaluation into
               the TTS training loop. Inspired by the recent success of reinforcement learning
019
               from human feedback, we propose a comprehensive sampling-annotating-learning
020
               framework tailored to TTS optimization, namely uncertainty-aware optimization
021            (UNO). Specifically, UNO eliminates the need for a reward model or pairwise
022            preference data by directly maximizing the utility of speech generations, while
023            considering the uncertainty that lies in the inherent variability in subjective human
024            speech perception and evaluations. Experimental results of both subjective and
025            objective evaluations demonstrate that UNO considerably improves the zero-shot
026            performance of TTS models in terms of MOS, word error rate, and speaker similar-
027            ity. Additionally, we present a remarkable ability of UNO that it can adapt to the
028            desired speaking style in emotional TTS seamlessly and flexibly.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Artificial intelligence-generated content (AIGC) has attracted a surge of interest in both academia
      and industry, revolutionizing the way we acquire and generate information (Cao et al., 2023). In
034
      this context, learning from human feedback plays a pivotal role in calibration—it aims to align the
035
      generative models with human preferences. For instance, the reinforcement learning from human
036   feedback (RLHF) technique can effectively help large language models (LLMs) to avoid generating
037   harmful and toxic content (Achiam et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022), which is crucial to the success
038   of helpful systems like ChatGPT. Similar methods have recently been employed in text-to-image
039   generation (Lee et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
040
      As an important task of AIGC, text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis technology is undergoing rapid develop-
041
      ment driven by deep learning models (Betker, 2023; Ren et al., 2020). Recent TTS works (Łajszczak
042   et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a) with extensive text-speech training
043   pairs exhibit remarkable zero-shot capacity which generates high-quality speech for speakers unseen
044   during training. However, unlike the widespread application of RLHF in LLMs’ calibration, aligning
045   synthetic speech generation with human preferences remains challenging and has not yet been adopted
046   in practice (Zhang et al., 2024). This contradicts the use of human subjective evaluation, such as the
047   mean opinion score (MOS), as the gold standard for assessing TTS performance, and results in a
048   clear mismatch between TTS training and evaluation. Motivated by this, we raise our basic research
049   question: Can we integrate human feedback into the TTS learning loop?
050   The technical barrier of this topic stems from both sides of “TTS” and “human”. (1) First, existing
051   TTS models typically learn a mapping function from input word/phoneme sequences to either mel-
052   spectrograms or discrete audio tokens followed by high-resolution waveform generation based on
053   supervised training (Wang et al., 2023a). However, this learned mapping hardly provides diverse yet
      plausible generations based on the same text and speech prompt, which hinders the formulation of


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      pairwise preference data required by widely used LLMs alignment methods such as direct preference
055   optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) (more discussion in Appendix B). (2) Second, human
056   evaluations of speech quality are subjective and inherently personalised. Each individual’s acoustic
057   perception is unique and influenced by their physical state and cognitive biases, thereby resulting in
058   uncertainty in the human feedback of each sample.
059
      To address the above challenges, we propose a pioneering method named uncertainty-aware optimiza-
060
      tion (UNO), which aims to enhance zero-shot TTS performance with (simulated) human feedback.
061   Inspired by the recent success of RLHF, UNO encompasses a sampling-annotating-learning pipeline,
062   but its original design tailored to TTS lies in these three sub-steps:
063
064          • Sampling with diversity. To obtain representative training examples, UNO performs zero-
065            shot TTS sampling with different speech prompts. It significantly contributes to the diversity
066            in self-generated speech samples, thereby reducing the potential bias that arises from
067            unrepresentative or skewed data collection.
068          • Annotating with uncertainty. UNO eliminates the dependency on preference data based
069            on the same input. Instead, it allows a more flexible and tolerant data annotation: only a
070            binary signal is required for whether the generated speech is desirable or not. Furthermore,
071
               since speech evaluation is subjective and personalised, this step encompasses the uncertainty
               caused by the individual differences among human annotators.
072
073          • UNO treats human feedback as a form of supervision with inconsistent labels to mitigate the
074            mismatch between the TTS training objectives and MOS-like subjective evaluation metrics.
075
               This learning approach directly maximizes the utility of generations from TTS sampling,
               instead of relying on a reward model or maximizing the log-likelihood of preferences.
076
077   Experimental results show that UNO comprehensively enhances the performance of zero-shot TTS
078   models, including speaker similarity (SIM), word error rate (WER), and pseudo-MOS estimated by
079   three pre-trained models. For validation, we conduct subjective human listening tests in the form of
080   naturalness MOS scoring and side-by-side A/B testing. The results of human evaluation confirm that
081   the TTS model optimized through our method significantly outperforms the baseline (3.38 → 4.06)
082   and sounds equally good compared to the ground truth speech. Through both token-level and
083   utterance-level visualization, it is observed that UNO provides effective supervised signals, resulting
084   in the distribution of generated content being closer to the ground truth distribution. Furthermore,
085
      UNO exhibits flexible scalability through adjusting optimization objectives. By changing the selection
      criteria in sampling selection, the method can be seamlessly extended to emotional TTS.
086
087   Our contributions are summarised as follows: (1) We present a comprehensive framework tailored to
088   zero-shot TTS optimization, where human feedback is taken into account in the training objectives
089   to alleviate the mismatch between training and evaluation. (2) By delving into the characteristics
090   of speech synthesis, UNO eliminates the dependence on preference data and accommodates the
091
      uncertainty in human subjective evaluations. This provides a new perspective for high-dimensional
      modality generation and alignment. (3) Intensive experiments demonstrate that UNO brings remark-
092
      able performance gain to zero-shot TTS models, especially in avoiding most failed cases. Additionally,
093
      UNO can be seamlessly extended to emotion TTS, demonstrating its scalability and practical value.

098   Text-to-speech as language modelling. Inspired by the success of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020),
099
      formatting TTS task as next token prediction has gained remarkable popularity in recent years (Borsos
      et al., 2023; Rubenstein et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). Under this setup, the prior step is to convert
100
      speech waveform into a sequence of learnable and discrete units based on vector quantization (Défos-
101
      sez et al., 2022; Zeghidour et al., 2021). SpeechTokenizer (Zhang et al., 2023b) and RepCodec (Huang
102   et al., 2023) enhance the semantic tokenization by adding self-supervised embedding prediction-based
103   losses (Mohamed et al., 2022). With discrete acoustic tokens, TortoiseTTS (Betker, 2023) pioneers to
104   combine a speech-code language model with a diffusion decoder to achieve few-shot TTS. VALL-
105   E (Wang et al., 2023a) and Spear-TTS (Kharitonov et al., 2023) scales to use 60k training data using a
106   pre-trained neural codec model (Défossez et al., 2022), which exhibits remarkable zero-shot capacity
107   to synthesize speech for unseen speakers with speech prompt. VALL-E X (Zhang et al., 2023c) and
      VioLa (Wang et al., 2023b) extent this framework to cross-lingual TTS, and later works (Lyth & King,


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2024; Ji et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023a) control the style of speech synthesis based
109   on the neural codec. More recent work (Peng et al., 2024) extends the TTS framework to address
110   speech editing tasks, BaseTTS (Łajszczak et al., 2024) built the first a billion-parameter TTS model
111   based on a decoder-only structure. RALL-E (Xin et al., 2024) presents a robust language modeling
112   approach for zero-shot TTS.
113
      Learning from Human Feedback. Human feedback has been widely used in language models
114
      for NLP tasks, such as text translation (Kreutzer et al., 2018), instruction-following (Ouyang et al.,
115   2022), and summarization (Stiennon et al., 2020). The advancements in the RLHF framework have
116   contributed to training helpful and harmless AI agents aligning with human preference in the past
117   years (Christiano et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023). Moreover, recent
118   works like DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) shift in favour of closed-form losses that directly operate on
119   preference data. Different from typical preference-based RL (Jain et al., 2013; Busa-Fekete et al.,
120   2014), DPO-style works remove the explicit reward model learning and provide the same alignment
121   effect (Zhou et al., 2023; Amini et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) with typical RLHF.
122   Moreover, (Chen et al., 2024b; Yuan et al., 2024) propose to calibrate LLMs in a “self-rewarding”
123
      manner, and KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) relieves the dependence on preference data and optimises
      LLMs using prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 2013).
124
125   Summary. To align TTS systems with human preference, we propose to consider the uncertainty of
126   human subjective evaluations (Maniati et al., 2022)—assessing speech has more perspectives and
127   subjectivity than text (van Heuven & van Bezooijen, 1995; Wiebe et al., 2004). Moreover, UNO
128   eliminates the dependence on pairwise preference data and does not require any corresponding ground
129
      truth speech (different from SpeechAlign (Zhang et al., 2024)). Combining these factors, we regard
      UNO as a step towards incorporating human evaluation signals in TTS training and contributing to
130
      developing more powerful and versatile speech synthesis.

136

137   function π parameterized by θ as s = πθ (t). In this work, we regard the TTS problem as a conditional

140   downsampled utterance length and n is the number of residual vector quantization (RVQ) codebooks.
141   After quantization, the neural codec decoder is able to reconstruct the waveform.

179   Variability in Human Evaluations. Variability is a unique aspect of real-world human evaluation.
180   Individual variations in physical states, cognitive biases, and personal experiences can lead to
181   subjectivity in perceptual quality assessment (e.g. TTS quality assessment). Instead of solely relying
182   on mean opinions, we propose incorporating the variability present in human evaluation, which
183
      helps mitigate potential biases and promotes fairness and inclusivity. Prior approaches for modelling
      variability in human annotations can be broadly grouped into two types. The first approach explicitly
184
      models the behaviours of different annotators using different individual models (Fayek et al., 2016;
185
      Chou & Lee, 2019; Davani et al., 2022), which is not scalable when the number of annotators
186   increases. The second approach approximates subjective probability distributions using Markov chain
187   Monte Carlo with people (Sanborn & Griffiths, 2007; Harrison et al., 2020), which requires human
188   annotators to be dynamically involved in the process. In this work, a meta-learning framework is
189   adopted for zero-shot human annotation distribution estimation. Given a synthesized utterance si and
                                               (m)

195   training samples. The deep neural network model then serves as a distribution estimator to allow
196   efficient generation of human-like annotations.

202   In order to acquire representative data, we introduce a simple sampling strategy that can encourage
203   more diversified zero-shot TTS generation. Specifically, for each target transcript x, we sample a
204   batch of speech prompts {p1 , p2 , . . . , pb } with the size of b from an unseen speaker pool. Then x

206   model by sk = πθ (t, pk ), k ∈ {1, 2, . . . , b}. Note that pk acts as a prefix sequence in autoregressive
207   decoding and significantly contributes to the diversity of target speech sk .

272   Why is DPO Eliminated? To optimize the KL-constrained RLHF objective given in Eqn. equation 4,
273   DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) presents an approach with mathematical equivalence by maximizing the
274   margin between the preferred and unpreferred generations based on the same input. Especially, the
275   training criterion can be written as follows under the TTS formulation:
276
                                                           πθ (sw |t, v)           πθ (sl |t, v)

277              LDPO-TTS (πθ , πref ) = E − log σ β log                   − β log                    , (11)
                                                           πref (sw |t, v)         πref (sl |t, v)
278
279   where sw and sl are supposed to be preferred and unpreferred speech obtained using the same
280   transcript and speech prompt (t, v). The sampling strategy introduced in Sec. 4.1 fails to provide
281   pairwise sw and sl required by Eqn. equation 11, as the data point in both Ppos and Pneg are generated
282
      using different speech prompts. In fact, due to the lack of diversity, existing TTS models struggle
      to generate paired sw and sl using fixed target transcripts and speech prompts. A solution recently
283
      proposed in (Zhang et al., 2024) recalls ground truth speech as sw while treating the generated speech
284
      by the model as sl . A difficulty of this approach lies in the fact that the TTS model’s outputs are
285   not necessarily unpreferred, and the ground truth may not always be accessible in practice. Another
286   solution is adjusting the sampling hyper-parameters or using beam search strategy. However, it results
287   in low data efficiency, and is exposed to less unseen speakers compared with same sampling times.
288

      were tasked with rating the naturalness of each audio sample on a 5-point Likert scale, ranging from
342
      1 (very unnatural) to 5 (completely natural). Furthermore, these 120 speech samples are randomly
343   assigned to ten listeners for side-by-side A/B testing (12 samples per person). After listening to
344   two samples with the same speech content, listeners were asked to decide which one sounded more
345   natural, or if they were too close to call, indicating a tie.
346
      Baselines. In addition to the well-trained VoiceCraft model by typical supervised learning, we
347
      reproduce the following optimization approaches based on VoiceCraft system for comparison:
348
349
             • SpeechAlign-DPO: Proposed by (Rafailov et al., 2024), it adapts the DPO algorithm to the
350            TTS task and achieves better performance than other alignment methods.
351
352          • SpeechAlign-ODPO: (Amini et al., 2024) presents a enhanced version of DPO with con-
353            sidering offset. We use the difference between the estimated MOS of ground truth and the
354            MOS of generated speech as “offset" to achieve ODPO optimization.
355
             • PPO-SDP: We apply PPO optimization by directly employing MOSNet as the reward model
356            and the mean of MOS as the reward signal. Furthermore, as standard deviation is available,
357            we implement the Standard Deviation-Based Penalty method proposed in (Yang et al., 2024).
358
359          • GroundTruth: Since ground truth waveforms of the evaluation set are available, we calculate
360            their corresponding metrics for TTS reference.
361
362
      Notably, SpeechAlign-DPO and SpeechAlign-ODPO require ground truth to serve as positive samples
      (yw ), thus resulting in an unfair comparison with our approach.

      6.4   E XTENSION ON R EVERSE I NFERENCE .
487
488   Due to the superior performance of the 830M model, we increase the sample size to obtain enough
489   negative samples. At the same time, we face the issue of selecting positive samples, as the large
490   number of positive examples lack distinctiveness. To address this, we propose a selection technique
491   called reverse inference (UNO-RI) based on Bayes’ theorem. We use the generated positive samples
492   as prompts to synthesize the original prompt speech. This approach is used to test whether the
493   synthesized speech can be input for a second inference, similar to the real speech. The results show
494
      that only 54% of the positive samples can produce synthesized speech with a MOS score above
      3 when used as prompt speech. By adopting this positive sample selection strategy, we find that
495
      UNO-RI further improves the performance of the 830M model, achieving WER of 2.0 and MOS of
496
      4.55, with failed cases reduced to less than 1%.
497
498   This results on UNO-RI provide two insights on zero-shot TTS. (i) While the synthesized speech
499   may sound indistinguishable from real speech, when used as prompt speech input in a zero-shot TTS
500
      system, a noticeable gap emerges between the two. This suggests that their audio codec distributions
      still differ, and RLHF method can align the distribution of synthesized speech closed to real speech.
501
      (ii) The remarkable results of UNO-RI demonstrate that in preference optimization, the model is
502
      better suited than humans listener for selecting speech samples. Some highly fine-grained differences
503   in speech are imperceptible to the auditory system of human. This stands in clear contrast to the
504   approach of RLHF in the NLP domain.
505
506   6.5   E XTENSION ON E MOTIONAL TTS.
507
508   In addition to MOS, we extend UNO to align with other human preferences, allowing for the
509   customization of TTS synthesis in different emotions. In practice, we establish the objective of
510   optimization for emotional TTS by manipulating samples in the Ppos and Pneg based on emotional
511   state model (Mehrabian, 1995).
512   Valence. We first utilize valence v ∈ (0, 1) as a metric to prompt the TTS model to generate speech
513   with pleasant emotion, which is equivalent to maximizing the valence in generations while keeping
514   high MOS m to ensure the quality. Specifically, our corresponding experimental adjustment consists
515   of two parts. (1) We sample speech prompts from “happy (high v)” and “sad (low v)” categories from
516   an emotional ESD dataset (Zhou et al., 2021) to encourage the diversity of v in sampling generations.
517   (2) We feed the samples with high valence and high MOS (v+, m+) into Ppos , and samples with
518
      low valence and high MOS (v−, m+) into Pneg . More details are illustrated in the Appendix F. The
      corresponding statistics for v and m are reported in the left part of Table 3, and the evaluation result
519
      based on “happy" prompts shows that UNO effectively achieves an absolute improvement of 0.12
520
      (0.55 → 0.67). Surprisingly, 0.67 is even higher than the average v̄ in Ppos (0.65), which shows UNO
521   captures the desirable speech style to align with human preference.
522
523   Arousal. We also utilize arousal ā to guide model generate speech with surprise emotion where
524
      speech prompts are samples from the “surprise” and “neural” categories of ESD dataset. Since they
      are not in opposite emotions, the average ā in Pneg is only 0.48. However, UNO effectively improves
525
      the a from 0.62 to 0.71 for evaluation, as shown in the right part of Table 3.

528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   This paper presents a novel optimization method UNO tailored to zero-shot TTS models. UNO
531   effectively integrates human feedback into the TTS learning objective using hundreds of self-generated
532   samples, which are annotated by deep neural network models with desirable/undesirable pseudo
533
      labels and their corresponding label uncertainty. The subsequent optimization directly maximizes
      the utilization of these samples in an uncertainty-aware manner. Experimental results demonstrate
534
      the remarkable efficacy of UNO in terms of both objective metrics and subjective metrics scored
535
      by human evaluation. Meanwhile, we explore the extension of UNO on reverse inference and the
536   emotional TTS tasks. We believe this work can provide unique insights and inspiration for leveraging
537   human feedback to enhance the high-dimensional data generation performance of AIGC.

      Question1: What is the limitation of this work?
786
787   Though UNO demonstrates promising efficacy of the neural codec TTS model, it is important to note
788   that TTS is a highly prevalent task encompassing a wide range of different architectures. For instance,
789   models with zero-shot TTS capability, such as those based on the probabilistic diffusion model,
790
      have also demonstrated remarkable performance of speech synthesis (Ju et al., 2024). Therefore, we
      may not have fully captured the potential versatility and broader applicability of human feedback
791
      in TTS optimization. However, in the realm of image generation, recent studies have shown that
792
      conditional diffusion models can also be aligned with human preferences using direct preference
793   optimization (Wallace et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b), where the Eqn (11) can be applied in diffusion
794   model training. Thus, it is theoretically feasible for UNO to optimize diffusion-based TTS models as
795   well, and the first relevant work is proposed in (Chen et al., 2024a).
796
      Question2: Why UNO can avoid the failed cases of original VoiceCraft?
797
798   The failed cases in VoiceCraft mostly stem from the autoregressive generation property in TTS
799   inference, where the TTS model does not learn a strict alignment between speech and text during
800   its training. However, such failed cases are not stubborn and can be avoided by multiple sampling
801   or CoT-like technique introduced in (Xin et al., 2024). In UNO, the Pneg covers different kinds of
802
      failed cases, and their corresponding implicit reward is suppressed during optimization. In human
      evaluation, listeners found that UNO can avoid all failed cases of repetition and interruption that
803
      happened in baseline, only remaining with some small errors such as mispronunciation or omission.
804

      Question6: Can UNO handle the data imbalance in Ppos and Pneg ?
849   Yes, UNO can handle the case when I (Ppos ) and J (Pneg ) are imbalance. Specifically, when the ratio
850   of positive to negative samples is set to 1:4 (I = 50, J = 200), the MOS performance only decreased
851   by 0.11 (4.31 → 4.20), while when the ratio is set to 4:1 (I = 200, J = 50), the MOS decreased
852   by 0.30 (4.31 → 4.01), but still significantly surpasses baseline (3.65). This is because, for a 330m
853   model, a sufficient number of samples are required to cover failed cases. Furthermore, UNO does
854
      not rely on large amount of training data, we increase the K to 10k (I and J are both 5k) but the
      performance gain is less than 0.1 (4.31 → 4.40 by MOSNet) without hyper-parameter tuning. Since
855
      each sample should have been annotated by human, we set the K to a few hundred to ensure that it is
856
      easy to implement in practice.

      Recent TTS research mostly concentrates on enhancing the model’s zero-shot capacity: given a short
885
      reference recording of the unseen voice (a.k.a, speech prompt), zero-shot TTS is framed as a transcript-
886   conditioned speech continuation task, where the synthetic speech is expected to keep consistent with
887   speech prompt. However, unlike humans who can speak a transcript with various styles, existing TTS
888   models struggle to generate diverse speech (e.g., speed, prosody, emotion, etc) based on the same
889   transcript and speech prompt. Beam search and Monte-Carlo dropout can introduce randomness
890   into neural codec modelling TTS system, however, humans can not easily distinguish preference
891   for speech generations like text, as shown in the right part of Figure 5. Therefore, considering the
892   significant impact of the speech prompt on generated speech, this work directly varies the speech
893   prompt during zero-shot TTS sampling. Though this approach hinders the formulation of pairwise
894
      preference data based on the same input, it highly encourages diversity in generated speech, thereby
      reducing the bias in data collection and benefiting the subsequent optimization process.
895
896   In general, we summarize the difference between designing a TTS optimization method and typical
897   LLMs-based RLHF: (i) RLHF mostly serves as a role of calibrator in LLMs generation. However,
898   TTS optimization requires learning from human evaluation to mitigate the absence of such supervised
899   information during training. (ii) Compared with LLMs, TTS systems fail to produce diverse and
900
      representative samples based on the same input, thus TTS optimization requires eliminating the
      dependence of pairwise preference data. (iii) Subjective evaluation of synthetic speech is not as
901
      straightforward as text. For instance, it is hard to judge if one synthetic speech misses some words
902
      but another is at an unnatural pace, but it frequently happens in zero-shot TTS.

      MOS Data. To simulate the human annotation, we utilize the SOMOS dataset (Maniati et al., 2022)

938   17.9 annotations per segment.

940
      I-CNF and EDL. For both two models, we utilize a frozen WavLM4 as an upstream backbone and
      the weighted sum of the outputs of all intermediate Transformer encoder blocks are used as the speech
941
      embeddings feeding into the downstream model. The weights are jointly trained with the downstream
942
      model which contains two Transformer encoder blocks followed by two fully connected layers. For
943   I-CNF, three real NVP blocks are used for the invertible flow model and 50 samples are drawn for
944   each input speech segment. Theses two model are utilized to label samples with in terms of predicted
945   mean of MOS, as well as providing variance as uncertainty during annotating process.
946
      MOSNet 5 is a pre-trained model based on wav2vec2 for MOS prediction (Cooper et al., 2022).
947
      Trained from the dataset for VoiceMOS challenge 6 , it has good generalization ability on out-of-
948
      domain speech assessment. This is the reason we employ it for MOS estimation in UNO experiments.
949   As an evaluator, MOSNet is not involved in data selection and annotation.
950
951   UNO training. We finetune all parameters of pre-trained VoiceCraft models, which are downloaded
952
      from Huggingface 7 . Due to the constraint of the reference model, it will not result in over-fitting.
      The learning rate is set as 1e-5, and the batch size is 2. We employ AdamW as an optimizer and only
953
      train for 1 epoch, the training iteration depends on the number of samples. We set sampling times K
954
      as 400, and use the I-CNF and EDL to classify them to 200 of Ppos and 200 of Pneg . For 400 samples,

       H         B ROADER I MPACTS
1060
       On the positive side, by incorporating human feedback into the training process, the TTS model
1061
       is improved to provide a better user experience in various applications, such as virtual assistants,
1062
       accessibility tools for the visually impaired, and language learning platforms. Moreover, this approach
1063   can enhance the adaptability of TTS systems to diverse linguistic and cultural contexts, fostering
1064   greater inclusivity and accessibility in technology. Overall, the integration of human feedback in TTS
1065   optimization can contribute to the development of more sophisticated, user-friendly, and versatile
1066   speech synthesis technologies.
1067
       Despite the potential benefits, considering that our model demonstrates a high degree of speaker
1068
       similarity in synthesized speech, it poses potential risks related to misuse, such as spoofing voice
1069   identification or impersonating specific individuals. Our experiments were conducted under the
1070   premise that the user consents to be the target speaker in speech synthesis. To mitigate these risks, it
1071   is imperative to develop a robust synthesized speech detection model and establish a comprehensive
1072   system for individuals to report any suspected instances of misuse.
