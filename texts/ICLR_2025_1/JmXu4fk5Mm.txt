                                                 A BSTRACT
034
035            3D completion represents a critical task within the vision industries. Traditional
036            diffusion-based methodologies have achieved commendable performance; however,
037
               they are hindered by several issues. Firstly, these methods primarily depend on
               models such as CLIP or BERT to encode textual information, thereby making them
038
               incapable of supporting detailed and complex instructions. Moreover, their model
039
               sizes usually increase rapidly when the scene is larger or the voxel resolution is
040            higher, making it impossible to scale up. Witnessing the significant advancements
041            in multi-modal understanding capabilities facilitated by recent developments in
042            large language models (LLMs), we introduce Volume Patch LLM (VP-LLM),
043            designed to execute user-friendly conditional 3D completion and denoising using a
044            token-based single-forward pass approach. To integrate a 3D model into the textual
045            domain of the LLM, the incomplete 3D model is initially divided into smaller
046            patches—a process we refer to as “patchification”—in a way that each patch can
047            be independently encoded, analogous to the tokenization configuration utilized by
048
               LLMs. These encoded patches are subsequently concatenated with the encoded
               text prompt sequence and inputted into an LLM, which is fine-tuned to capture the
049
               relationships between these patch tokens while embedding semantic meanings into
050
               the 3D object. Our findings indicate a robust ability of LLMs to interpret complex
051            text instructions and comprehend 3D objects, surpassing the quality of results
052            produced by state-of-the-art diffusion-based 3D completion models, especially
053            when complex text prompts are given.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   3D modeling serves as a pivotal component in a multitude of 3D vision applications including robotics
058   and virtual reality, where the quality of 3D data critically influences model performance. Despite the
059   advancement in 3D scanning technology, the raw data acquired are often noisy, clustered, and may
060   contain large portions of missing data due to occlusion, complex real-world scenes and restricted
061   camera angles, resulting in incomplete 3D acquisition. This necessitates robust pre-processing to
062
      recover or complete the 3D objects, which can enhance the efficiency of subsequent 3D vision tasks.
063   Current approaches for 3D shape completion typically operate on a depth map or partial point
064   cloud, converting it into a voxel representation or sampling points to restore the original 3D objects.
065   While Wu et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2021) showcase advancements, they are confined to specific
066   categories and lack the ability of cross-object generation. Although efforts have been made Yan et al.
067   (2022); Yu et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2018); Wen et al. (2021) to create a unified model that handles
068
      multi-category 3D completion, these models often overlook the inclusion of textual input in guiding
      the completion process, leading to uncertainty when the input is ambiguous, as well as degradation
069
      of feasibility when given captions deviate from the training set. Consequently, methods are needed
070
      to generate a completed shape aligning precisely with the provided text description. Some attempts
071   like Cheng et al. (2023); Kasten et al. (2023) mimic the 2D diffusion method or score distillation
072   sampling (SDS) to incorporate text guidance in the 3D completion tasks, but they cannot be precisely
073   controlled when the description is complicated, and are very time-consuming to generate the results.
074
      To this end, we propose Volume Patch Large Language Model (VP-LLM), which achieves 3D
075
      completion with precise textual control. Inspired by the recent progress in 3D multi-modality
076
      models (Yin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023c), we believe that Large Language
077   Models (LLMs) can underpin our approach by decoding the complex associations between 3D
078   structures and textual descriptions. LLMs, pretrained on large-scale text datasets, have the capability
079   to process long sequences and comprehend complex human languages, while 3D models represented
080   by voxel grids can be straightforwardly converted into a one-dimensional format through flattening.
081   Therefore, we investigate how to enable LLMs to understand a 3D model by decoding complex
082   correlations between 3D structures and textual descriptions, or “translating” it into a “sentence”.
083   For seamless incorporation of 3D data into the LLM tokenization framework, 3D models are initially
084   segmented into smaller patches, facilitating independent encoding and decoding. Different from most
085   previous methods that manage the 3D object as a unified, this idea of patchification is more scalable
086   and extendable. The patchified 3D voxel volume can be processed as a sequence and fed into the
087   LLM with the textual description after alignment. The LLM can fuse the 3D and textural features
088   into its hidden latents, which are finally decoded into complete 3D models.
089   Our whole pipeline is presented in Fig. 1. The 3D volumes are first patchified into individual patches
090   and processed by a patch-wise Variational Autoencoder (VAE) to encode individually. The encoded
091   patches are then projected and concatenated with user-specified text conditions to the LLM. Finally,
092   the output projection layer extracts the features generated by the LLM and lets the VAE decode back
093   each patch individually.
094
      In summary, the major contributions of our papers are:
095
096
            1. We proposed a patchification method, which enables a scalable integration of 3D volumes
097
               into the LLM, which is akin to LLM’s tokens, solving the difficulty in handling high-
098
               resolution voxel grids faced by existing works.
099
100
101
            2. VP-LLM is the first work leveraging LLM to achieve 3D completion with precise text-control,
               which outperforms existing state-of-the-art text-conditioned 3D completion works.
102
103
104         3. Our work serves as an interactive unified agent that performs 3D understanding, completion
105
               and denoising for multiple categories with detailed text control.
106
107   Thus, this experimental paper offers insights and lessons learned, providing the first LLM solution to
      text-guided 3D object completion. Codes and data will be made public upon the paper’s acceptance.

      2.1   M ULTIMODALITY L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
111
112   The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has significantly accelerated advancements in natural
113   language processing. Several studies like Jiang et al. (2023b); Touvron et al. (2023); Team et al. (2024)
114   have demonstrated the capabilities of LLM in comprehending long contexts, ensuring scalability
115   and adaptability, and facilitating the understanding and generation of natural language. Benefiting
116   from these advantages of LLM, many works have already employed LLM across different modalities,
117   including images (Wang et al., 2023b; OpenAI et al., 2024; Alayrac et al., 2022), motion (Jiang
118   et al., 2023c), and video (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Recently, several works combined
119   LLM with 3D data. For example, Yin et al. (2023) leverages a 3D-aware VQ-VAE (van den Oord
120
      et al., 2018) and integrates its codebook into the LLM’s vocabulary, enabling the LLM to generate
      and understand 3D objects. But the codebook size may bottleneck the capability of LLM to tackle
121
      3D objects with more complex and various structures. LLM-Grounder (Yang et al., 2023) carefully
122
      designs LLM prompt to translate the instructions into regular sub-tasks and instructs some pre-trained
123   3D grounders Kerr et al. (2023); Peng et al. (2023); Qi et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2023); Hong et al.
124   (2023) for 3D reasoning, where 3D models are not integrated into the LLM. Octavius (Chen et al.,
125   2023b) adopts the object detector to first discover candidate regions, followed by the application
126   of pre-trained point cloud encoders for extracting features at the instance level. These features are
127   then aggregated and mapped into an LLM for diverse 3D understanding tasks. However, this process
128   reduces the entire 3D model to a single feature, thereby omitting crucial detailed information. In
129   contrast, VP-LLMemploys a VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013) combined with projection layers,
130   capable of effectively aligning the 3D latent space with the LLM’s text space, thus enhancing the
131
      model’s generalizability, especially for out-of-distribution data.
132
133   2.2   T EXT- TO -3D G ENERATION
134
135   Prior to the era of machine learning, primitive works attempted to retrieve 3D assets from large
136
      databases, such as Chang et al. (2014; 2015a). With the rise of GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014),
      attempts such as Text2Shape (Chen et al., 2019) started to dominate the 3D generation field. Recently,
137
      due to promising advancements in text-to-image generation, research focus on text-control 3D
138
      generation has shifted to diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020). Some works Liu et al. (2023a); Sanghi
139   et al. (2022); Jain et al. (2022) adopt CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to align the rendered images with
140   the input text, thus ensuring the semantic meaning of the 3D model, while others like Poole et al.
141   (2022); Wang et al. (2024; 2023a); Chen et al. (2023a); Lorraine et al. (2023); Babu et al. (2023)
142   leverage pre-trained 2D diffusion models to provide text control and score distillation sampling to
143   improve the 3D consistency. Although text-to-3D generation serves as an inspiration for text-guided
144   3D completion, none of the existing methods employ large language models for 3D-text interaction
145   to guide the completion results.
146
147   2.3   3D C OMPLETION
148
149   3D completion is a crucial process in various industries, enabling accurate and efficient design and
150   production, and enhancing the overall quality of products and projects. Early works such as Choy
151   et al. (2016); Dai et al. (2017); Girdhar et al. (2016); Han et al. (2017); Stutz & Geiger (2018; 2020);
152   Wu et al. (2015) that use 3D convolutions with structured representation require high memory usage
153
      and compute. Followed by Yuan et al. (2018), many works An et al. (2024); Tchapmi et al. (2019);
      Yu et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2020) that adopt point clouds as 3D representation for shape completion
154
      were proposed. For example, An et al. (2024); Tchapmi et al. (2019); Yu et al. (2022) generate the
155
      final shape in an auto-regressive manner, while Wu et al. (2020) uses GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014)
156   to complete the model. But none of these offer satisfactory user control. With the introduction of
157   DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), works such as Li et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2023b); Luo & Hu (2021);
158   Vahdat et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2024); Rao et al. (2022); Chu et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2021); Li et al.
159   (2023a) have advanced the 3D shape completion pipelines conditioned on labels. Notably, Cheng
160   et al. (2023) adopts a 3D diffusion model with VAE to achieve 3D completion with multi-type control,
161   and Kasten et al. (2024) uses score distillation to perform test-time optimization. However, neither
      of them can perform completion tasks at a larger scale where model details are required, nor even

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
183
184
      Given an incomplete 3D model and user-supplied textual description of the target 3D model, our
185   model aims to recover the underlying 3D model aligned with the input text. First, the incomplete 3D
186   model undergoes patchification, where it is split into small patches, and each patch is independently
187   encoded by our Variational Autoencoder (VAE). Next, a shared-weight linear layer maps the patch
188   features to the embedding space of the LLM, which are then combined with the textual description
189   and input into the LLM. The LLM, concatenated with our specially-designed output projection layer,
190   generates the features of patches at all positions, allowing for the separate decoding and subsequent
191   assembly, or de-patchification, to the underlying complete 3D model.
192
193   3.1   PATCHIFICATION
194
195   The first step of our method is to divide the 3D models into small patches, dubbed patchification.
196
      Figure 2 demonstrates the process of patchification. For a 3D object represented in voxel V ∈
      {0, 1}H×W ×D , V (x, y, z) = 1 if the position x, y, z is occupied and 0 otherwise. Patchification
197
      uniformly partitions the 3D voxel volume into p small patches of the same size, each containing a
198
      local region of the entire object. For each patch Pi,j,k ∈ {0, 1}h×w×d , the coordinate for position
199   (x, y, z), 0 ≤ x ≤ h, 0 ≤ y ≤ w, 0 ≤ z ≤ d is:
200
201                           Pi,j,k (x, y, z) = V (i · h + x, j · w + y, k · d + z).                      (1)
202
      Thus, p = ⌊H/h⌋ · ⌊W/w⌋ · ⌊D/d⌋. In our experiments, we set H = W = D = 64 and
203
      h = w = d = 8.
204
205   After patchification, a patch Variational Autoencoder (VAE) is adopted to extract the feature for each
206   patch independently. Our patch VAE consists of an encoder E and a decoder D, where E encodes a
207
      patch into a Gaussian distribution N (µ, σ) where µ and σ are mean and variance, respectively, and
      D recovers the original patch from this distribution. The VAE training loss for a single patch P is
208
      defined as
209
210                           LVAE (P ) = LBCE (P, D(E(P ))) + βLkld (E(P )),                              (2)
211
212
      where LBCE is the binary cross entropy loss, Lkld is the KL-divergence and β is a hyperparameter.
213   Benefiting from this patchification structure that allows for independent encoding and decoding of
214   each patch, VP-LLM ensures that when the completions of certain patches are undesired, they do
215   not affect the performance of other well-performed patches, solving the problems faced by previous
      works that encode or decode the entire scene collectively.

239   3.2   M ASK S TRATEGY
240
241   To enhance the understanding of incomplete 3D models, we designed three different strategies to
242   mask out different parts of the original 3D input, aiming to mimic the possible user inputs during the
243   inference stage. Specifically, the following three strategies will be applied randomly with the same
244   possibility:
245
246         1. Random Mask: Given the input 3D model in p patches, we randomly set mr · p patches to 0
247            (unoccupied), where mr is a mask ratio sampled within a pre-defined range;
248
            2. Plane Mask: Given the input 3D model represented in voxel V (x, y, z), we first project
249
               the model onto x-axis and find the first and last occupied voxels, denoted as x1 and x2 ,
250
               along x-axis. Next, a plane parallel to yOz is sampled with x-coordinate between [x1 , x2 ].
251            Intuitively, such a plane cuts the 3D model into two parts, and we then discard one of them
252            by setting all voxels to be 0 (unoccupied) to simulate large portions of missing data in real
253            capture;
254
255         3. Random Noise: Since real-world 3D models usually contain noises and artifacts, we mimic
256            this situation by randomly inverting voxel occupancy (setting occupied voxels to be unoccu-
257            pied, and vice versa). The noise level is also sampled in a pre-defined range indicating how
258            many voxels to invert.
259
260
      3.3   I NPUT P ROJECTION L AYER T RAINING
261
262
      The input project layer, which can map the VAE latent space into the LLM input embedding space,
263
      is a single linear model operated on each VAE latent patch. After patchifying and encoding the
264   incomplete 3D model, each patch, represented by its respective µ and σ, is first reparameterized into
265   a single feature f by sampling from the Gaussian distribution N (µ, σ). The feature (or each encoded
266   patch) going through the input projection layer becomes a 3D token, which then can be understood
267   by the LLM. To train the input projection layer, the LLM is instructed to predict the caption of the
268   incomplete 3D model, given the prompt “Given the incomplete 3D input, describe the 3D model by
269   text.” We use the training loss in Radford et al. (2019) which is the negative log-likelihood on the
      caption. We use the training loss from the LLM to supervise this stage of training.

                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      3.4   O UTPUT P ROJECTION L AYER T RAINING
271
272   The LLM, with the output projection layer appended, takes as input the tokenized se-
273   quence of the underlying incomplete 3D model, the user-supplied caption of the com-
274   plete model as well as the instructions for completion, and outputs the complete
275   3D model aligned with the textual description.                To be specific, we formulate the
276   prompt for LLM as “Given the caption, recover the incomplete 3D model,
277   <tokenized incomplete 3D sequence>, <Caption>”. The output projection layer ar-
278
      chitecture employs a transformer consisting of a 2-layer encoder and a 2-layer decoder, translating
      the LLM hidden states into a sequence of separable latent codes. We observe that to sufficiently
279
      explore the highly fused information in the LLM, more layers of hidden states are necessary. Thus,
280
      we select 5 layers in our experiments, which balances the amount of information with computational
281   complexity. To ensure the length of the generated sequence, we set an additional learnable token
282   sequence as the target. We then utilize Multi-layer Perceptrons (MLPs) to individually map each
283   token to the desired 3D token. The final result is obtained through the concatenation of the mapped
284   tokens.
285
      During training, the input projection layer is frozen, while the LLM is finetuned with LoRA Hu et al.
286
      (2021), while the output projection layer is trained from scratch. We use mean-squared error (MSE)
287   loss between the output projection layer output and the VAE latent of ground-truth 3D model patches
288   to update our model.

      3.5   I NTERACTIVE W ORKING F LOW WITH D ETAILED I NSTRUCTION
291
292   In order to demonstrate the 3D understanding ability of our LLM, we also provide an interactive
293   completion and denoising interface, where the user initially inputs the incomplete model to VP-LLM,
294   and our LLM, combined with our trained input projection layer, can provide potential completion
295   options. The user is afforded the flexibility to either select from these options or input their own
296   control instructions. Ultimately, they obtain the desired completed results, which is the output of our
297   entire pipeline.
298   As illustrated in Fig. 1, after the user inputs half of a guitar, our LLM responds with two options
299   to either complete as a guitar with asymmetrical body or axe-like body. With the user choosing
300   the second one, the model outputs the corresponding results. We will provide more examples
301   demonstrating our model’s detailed controllability in the experiment section, where our model can
302   distinguish between subtle differences in text instructions and understand them precisely.

      4.1   DATASET
307
308   We train our model on a subset of ShapeNet (Chang et al., 2015b) dataset, comprising over 3000
309   objects. To obtain the detailed textual description of 3D models in human languages, we adopt
310   Cap3D (Luo et al., 2024), which leverages BLIP to predict and GPT-4 to refine captions for 3D
311   models.In our experiments, the resolutions of the 3D voxels and patches are respectively set as
312   64 × 64 × 64 and 8 × 8 × 8, while we explore the capability of the model to handle higher resolution
313   formats in Sec. 5.2.
314   To improve the robustness of our model, we apply data augmentation during training. For the 3D
315   models, we rotate them along one random axis with an arbitrary angle making the order of sequence
316   different, while for the captions of the 3D model, we adjust the GPT configurations in Cap3D.
317   Only 3D data augmentation is used in input projection training, while both 3D data and caption
318   augmentation are used in output projection training. More details of the data augmentation can be
319   found in Appendix C.

490   5     A BLATION S TUDY
491
492   5.1   C OMPARISON OF DIFFERENT LLM ARCHITECTURES
493
      LLM is one of the most important components in our model, whose ability to capture multi-modal
494
      semantic information and token relations may greatly affect the performance of the entire pipeline. To
495
      investigate the effect of different LLMs on the performance of our model, we re-trained our method
496   on Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023a) and Gemma-2B (Team et al., 2024). To ensure a fair comparison,
497   we keep the LoRA ranks for the two models to be the same. From Tab. 5.1, we find that under this
498   setting, the two LLMs demonstrate similar results on mask completion, while the performance of
499   Gemma-2B is better than Mistral-7B on denoising tasks while slightly worse in completion from
500   masked inputs. Higher performance can be expected if larger LLM models or higher LoRA ranks are
501   deployed.
502
503   5.2   S CALABILITY ON H IGHER R ESOLUTION VOXEL VOLUMES
504
505   Our VAE encodes and decodes each patch of the 3D model individually, thus enabling our model
506
      to scale to higher voxel resolutions. To demonstrate the scalability, we have expanded upon our
      previous experiments by setting H = W = D = 64, and further increasing the voxel resolution to
507
      H = W = D = 72, while maintaining the patch size at 8. By fixing the patch size, we can ensure
508
      that the fine details of the data remain consistent as we increase the input scale. After this operation,
509   the sequence length of each 3D object will increase from 512 to 729 (around 42% increase). We
510   can see from Tab. 6 that the two resolutions have comparable results, indicating our method can
511   successfully generalize to higher voxel volume resolutions.
512
      On the other hand, we would like to point out that the resolution 643 is already the highest among
513
      voxel grid works. The most recent top conference works use much smaller resolutions, such as Rao
514
      et al. (2022) uses 323 , and Liu & Liu (2021); Tu et al. (2023) both use 403 . Moreover, considering
515   most of the modern small LLM models (even 2B models like Gemma-2B) can handle at least 4K-8K
516   context length, our method can perfectly handle resolutions to 1283 (which requires 4K context
517   length). This is way more than common requirements and it does not make sense to demand model
518   to handle even higher resolutions.
519
520
521
      6     L IMITATION
522
      Our method has been proven effective on small LLMs. However, due to the limitation of computa-
523
      tional resources, LLMs with stronger capability to understand long sequences, such as 70B or larger
524
      scale models, are not employed in our model. Thus, huge potential of our method is still left to probe.
525   Additionally, though we verify the effectiveness of our method by patchification on voxel volumes,
526   which can be intuitively extended to point clouds and SDFs, it is still very hard to employ it on those
527   nascent 3D representations like NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl
528   et al., 2023) that encoded 3D in an implicit (MLP weights for NeRF and Gaussians for 3DGS). More
529   investigations on how to patchify such representations are left in future works.
530
531
      7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we present VP-LLM, which combines text and 3D through LLMs to achieve text-guided
534   3D completion with detailed, precise semantics of texts captured. We introduce a novel approach
535   called patchification to incorporate 3D models into LLMs, and adopt a two-stage training process
536   that allows LLMs to understand input incomplete 3D models and generate entire 3D models. Such an
537   approach also allows an independent encoding and decoding process, thereby ensuring the scalability
538   of our method. Experiments on the ShapeNet dataset validate that our method surpasses the state-
539   of-the-art methods in the 3D completion task. Moreover, our model can achieve satisfactory results
      from noisy 3D inputs with an interaction interface, a practical issue in real 3D data capture.

842   Training configuration We train our patch VAE on 2 RTX 4090 cards for 100 epochs and batch size
843   using ShapeNet voxels volumes with resolution 643 and adopt the same VAE for voxels with different
844   resolutions. This training takes approximately 2 hours. The model is trained with AdamW Loshchilov
845
      & Hutter (2017) optimizer with a learning rate 3e−4 .
846   Our input projection and output projection models are trained on 8 RTX 6000 Ada GPU cards until
847   converge, for around 100 and 500 epochs respectively. For Mistral-7B, these processes take around 1
848   hour and 18 hours, respectively, while for Gemma-2B, the numbers go down to 20 minutes and 8
849   hours. Both stages are trained with AdamW optimizer as well, with input projection training using a
850   learning rate of 3e−4 and output projection training using 5e−4 or 5e−5 , depending on the LLM size.
