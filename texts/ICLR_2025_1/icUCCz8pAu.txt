011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown impressive performance across var-
014            ious tasks, yet they still face significant safety and trustworthiness challenges,
015            such as robustness, fairness, and truthfulness. Addressing these challenges is crit-
016            ical for the reliable deployment of LLMs. Directly fine-tuning LLMs to enhance
017            safety can degrade their performance and is challenging to balance across multiple
018
               safety perspectives due to the forgetting phenomenon. In this paper, we propose
               MultiTrust, a novel and scalable framework designed to enhance LLM safety from
019
               multiple safety perspectives. In particular, MultiTrust first generates challenging
020
               training data through adversarial optimizations, focusing on LLMs trustworthiness
021            perspectives, such as robustness, fairness, and safety. MultiTrust then separately
022            train safety auxiliary models for each perspective using supervised fine-tuning and
023            Direct Preference Optimization (DPO). MultiTrust augments a base model with
024            these safety auxiliary models on the fly through dynamic routing and logit en-
025            sembling, significantly boosting the performance across different trustworthiness
026            metrics for the base model while preserving its helpfulness. Notably, MultiTrust
027            introduces an effective perplexity-based inference-time router to seamlessly inte-
028            grate these safety auxiliary models by averaging the logit outputs of the selected
029
               safety auxiliary model and the base model, which enhances the stability of the
               final performance. Moreover, MultiTrust’s flexible design allows for the augmen-
030
               tation with new safety auxiliary models for different perspectives without necessi-
031
               tating additional training or adaptation. Extensive experimental results show that
032            MultiTrust, which trains a series of 7B safety auxiliary models, significantly im-
033            proves the trustworthiness of the base LLM across different sizes (7B and 13B).
034            For instance, MultiTrust increased the average performance of Llama2-13B from
035            35.54% to 51.14%, and Vicuna-13B from 29.91% to 52.82%, outperforming mod-
036            els with similar and even larger sizes across different perspectives. These results
037            underscore the effectiveness and scalability of MultiTrust in enhancing the safety
038            and reliability of LLMs.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated unprecedented capabilities in a wide range of
044   applications, setting new benchmarks for complex tasks and frequently achieving human-like pro-
045   ficiency in various domains (OpenAI, 2022; Wang et al., 2023b). Despite these advancements, sig-
046   nificant concerns about their safety and trustworthiness persist (Wang et al., 2021; 2023a; Mazeika
047   et al., 2024). For instance, LLMs often exhibit vulnerabilities in different trustworthiness perspec-
048   tives such as robustness, fairness, privacy, and truthfulness, which can severely limit their deploy-
049   ment in sensitive or safety-critical environments (Driess et al., 2023).
050   Traditionally, approaches to enhance model safety have addressed these issues in isolation, focusing
051   on optimizing one perspective of trustworthiness at a time, typically through continuous fine-tuning
052   on domain-specific datasets. While this approach may resolve isolated issues, it often meets the
053   “model forgetting” phenomenon, where improvements in one trustworthiness perspective may inad-
      vertently cause performance degradation in others (Dou et al., 2023). Furthermore, such sequential

054
      training can reduce the overall benign performance or helpfulness of the models, limiting their de-
055   ployment in real-world scenarios (Dou et al., 2023).
056
057   In contrast to methods that sequentially enhance various perspectives of model trustworthiness, some
058
      existing work has attempted to integrate multiple functionalities into a single framework (Ilharco
      et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2024). However, these approaches generally lack flexibility; they are
059
      tailored to specific base models and configurations, which is hard to adapt to diverse base models
060
      and emerging new perspectives. Such frameworks typically require extensive fine-tuning or the
061   training of additional modules, making them computationally expensive and less scalable (Bansal
062   et al., 2024).
063
064
      In response to these challenges, we introduce MultiTrust, a novel framework that treats different
      trustworthiness perspectives as distinct functionalities, allowing for dynamic model safety alignment
065
      based on the specific needs of each task. MultiTrust proposes a unique alignment technique that
066
      dynamically aligns the output of a base LLM with the output of specialized safety auxiliary models.
067   This is achieved through a perplexity-based inference-time router, which intelligently selects the
068   most appropriate safety model during inference based on the input query, and combines the logits
069   from the base and safety models, thereby improving the trustworthiness of the base LLM without
070   necessitating additional training or fine-tuning.
071
      We have conducted extensive experiments and demonstrated the effectiveness of MultiTrust in sig-
072
      nificantly enhancing the trustworthiness of LLMs. By applying our framework to align base mod-
073   els of different sizes, we enhance their performance across different trustworthiness perspectives,
074   without compromising their general helpfulness. For instance, MultiTrust elevated the average per-
075   formance score of Llama2-13B from 35.54% to 51.14% and Vicuna-13B from 29.91% to 52.82%,
076   surpassing both similar and larger-sized models. We also observe interesting findings, for example,
077   we find that learning from the differences between answers using DPO is more effective than SFT.
078   We also notice that datasets that focus on different trustworthiness perspectives may interact with
079   other perspectives, helping to improve the model performance in other perspectives.
080   Our contributions are threefold: 1) Challenging Data Generation. We provide novel methods to
081   generate data that challenge model robustness using various adversarial attack algorithms, and cre-
082   ate fairness-sensitive datasets with balanced representation across sensitive attributes. 2) Flexible
083   Model Safety Alignment. Our perplexity-based router enables the seamless integration of new
084   trustworthy models without additional training or fine-tuning, aligning model outputs by combining
085   logits from the base and selected safety auxiliary models. 3) Enhanced Trustworthieness Per-
086   formance. MultiTrust consistently improves the performance of a given base LLM across different
087
      trustworthiness perspectives, demonstrating its effectiveness, flexibility, and scalability in enhancing
      the safety and trustworthiness of LLMs.

091   2   M ETHOD
092
093   Addressing the safety and trustworthiness of Large Language Models (LLMs) presents several crit-
094   ical challenges. For example, retraining or extensively fine-tuning the base model to enhance safety
095   can be prohibitively expensive and time-consuming. To circumvent this, MultiTrust trains addi-
096   tional, specialized safety auxiliary models rather than modifying the existing base LLM directly.
097   This approach significantly reduces the computational overhead associated with continuously up-
098   dating the base LLM. Besides, the need to flexibly consider different safety perspectives and adapt
099
      to new ones as they emerge presents a significant challenge. To address this, we propose a novel
      perplexity-based router that enables the seamless integration of new safety auxiliary models without
100
      additional training. Moreover, balancing the trade-off between maintaining good performance in
101
      benign scenarios and ensuring safety and truthfulness across various trustworthiness perspectives
102   is challenging. We mitigate such trade-offs by proposing an effective logit-based alignment ap-
103   proach that aligns the base LLM with different safety auxiliary models flexibly. The pipeline of our
104   MultiTrust is shown in Figure 1, which consists of 3 components, 1) creating challenging data for
105   different trustworthiness perspectives, 2) training a safety auxiliary model for each trustworthiness
106   perspective, and 3) base LLM alignment with safety auxiliary models. In this section, we will intro-
107   duce the details of different components, demonstrating how to obtain safe and trustworthy LLMs
      given a pretrained base LLM.

124   2.1   G ENERATING CHALLENGING DATA FOR DIFFERENT TRUSTWORTHINESS PERSPECTIVES
125
126   MultiTrust is a general framework that can be leveraged to enhance the model performance in differ-
127   ent perspectives. Here we mainly focus on the following 3 trustworthiness perspectives: adversarial
128   robustness, fairness, and safety, following the categorization provided in existing benchmarks (Liang
129   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a).
130   Adversarial robustness. To defend the adversarial attacks and make our model more robust to
131   input manipulations, we identify the vulnerabilities in existing models and collect our challenging
132   dataset by adding adversarial perturbations to existing datasets. Specifically, we generate diverse
133   adversarial data against LLMs (Wang et al., 2021), and consider the following five most representa-
134   tive and challenging tasks: Sentiment Analysis (SST-2), Duplicate Question Detection (QQP), and
135   Natural Language Inference (NLI, including MNLI, RTE, QNLI). We sample the training sets of
136
      these tasks, reformulate the data into the instruction-following format, and perform word-level ad-
      versarial attacks to generate our challenging robustness dataset. In our experiments, we consider
137
      the following five kinds of word-level perturbations: typo-based perturbation (TextBugger (Li et al.,
138
      2018)), embedding-similarity-based perturbation (TextFooler (Jin et al., 2020)), context-aware per-
139   turbation (BERT-ATTACK (Li et al., 2020)), knowledge-guided perturbation (SememePSO (Zang
140   et al., 2019)), and semantic-optimization-based perturbation (SemAttack Wang et al. (2022)) to per-
141   form white-box attacks against Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023) model. These attacking strategies
142   are originally designed to attack BERT-like models, which have different classification protocols
143   from GPT-like models. We propose to adapt and modify the adversarial optimization process and
144   use the conditional probabilities of (adversarial) candidate labels given the prompt to optimize the
145   adversarial sentences. After optimizing the input prompt, we collect a ground truth answer and a
146   wrong answer. For tasks with two labels, we select the two labels as the ground truth and the wrong
147
      answer, respectively. For tasks with three labels, such as MNLI, we select the opposite label as the
      wrong answer when the ground truth is ‘yes’ or ‘no’, and we randomly select ‘yes’ or ‘no’ as the
148
      wrong answer when the ground truth is ‘maybe’. We will release our generated adversarial dataset
149
      for public evaluation.
150
151   Fairness. To develop a fair model and close the performance gap of the model on different groups
152   with different sensitive attributes, we construct a fair distributed dataset by considering various sen-
153
      sitive attributes. Specifically, we leverage two commonly used fairness evaluation tabular datasets
      Adult (Asuncion & Newman, 2007) and Crime (Redmond, 2009), and transform the tabular data in
154
      these dataset into language descriptions. Each instance in the Adult dataset includes 14 attributes
155
      of a person (e.g., age and education level) as input. The task is to predict whether the income of
156   the person is over $50k per year. We consider multiple sensitive attributes, including sex (white and
157   black), race (male and female), and age (above average and below average). In the Crime dataset,
158   each instance has 10 attributes of a community, such as education level and unemployment rate. We
159   construct different queries based on controlled protected variables (e.g., demographic attributes).
160   The protected variable in the Crime dataset is selected as the race (e.g., the portion of white in the
161   community). We follow common practice (Wang et al., 2023a) to split the dataset, and we only
      sample data in the training split to avoid potential contamination. Based on these two datasets, we


                                                                        3

162
      construct a fair distributed training dataset. For each instance in the dataset, we first clone a dupli-
163   cate and then flip the sensitive attribute of the cloned instance. In this way, we hope to reduce the
164   model’s dependence on these sensitive attributes and make more fair predictions. We convert the
165   ground truth label to the true answer and use the opposite label as the wrong answer.
166
      Truthfulness and safety. To encourage the model to generate truthful content, we leverage the
167
      GRATH dataset (Chen et al., 2024) to improve the truthfulness of our models. GRATH dataset is
168
      collected by iteratively prompting a pre-trained LLM to give a correct and an incorrect answer to
169   a given question. The two answers are used to fine-tune the pre-trained LM. Then the fine-tuned
170   model can be prompted again to generate pair-wise correct and incorrect answers of higher quality.
171   In our experiments, we adopt the data collected by GRATH in the first two iterations.

174
175   Based on our generated challenging data for different safety perspectives, we independently train
176   a distinct safety auxiliary model for each perspective. Our methodology comprises a two-phase
177   training process. Initially, we conduct supervised fine-tuning (SFT) of the pre-trained LM on the
178   collected datasets. Subsequently, we apply Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to refine the
179
      performance by learning from the differences between correct and incorrect responses.

183   model is trained to maximize the likelihood of the correct answer with the loss function defined as:

223
224   Models. We fine-tune all our safe models based on Llama-2-Chat-7B Touvron et al. (2023), a
225   widely-used open-source 7B LLM. We use the trained safe models to augment three different models
226   with different sizes: Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) (7B and 13B) and Llama-2-Chat (13B). We com-
227   pare MultiTrust against a variety of open-access models featured on LLM Safety Leaderboard (Wang
228   et al., 2023a) and Open LLM Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023), with parameters sizes ranging
229   from 7B to 33B, including Gemma-it (Team et al., 2024), Zephyr (Tunstall et al., 2023), Qwen-
230   Chat (Bai et al., 2023), Mistral-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023), Llama-3-Instruct (AI@Meta, 2024), and
231
      Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023).
232   Datasets. To rigorously evaluate the effectiveness of MultiTrust, we leverage various datasets, fo-
233   cusing on both trustworthiness and general performance. Our selection of trustworthiness datasets
234   includes: 1) DecodingTrust (Wang et al., 2023a): This safety benchmark is designed to assess dif-
235   ferent perspectives of model trustworthiness. For the purposes of our experiments, we specifically
236   focus on the adversarial robustness and fairness sections to assess the corresponding capabilities
237
      of MultiTrust. 2) TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2021): Designed to measure the truthfulness of a lan-
      guage model’s responses, this dataset helps us evaluate how accurately MultiTrust generates truthful
238
      answers in response to various questions. Our selection of general tasks includes: 1) AI2 Reason-
239
      ing Challenge (ARC) (Clark et al., 2018): a dataset of grade-school science questions; 2) Hel-
240   laSwag (Zellers et al., 2019): a dataset for commonsense inference; 3) MMLU (Hendrycks et al.,
241   2020): a dataset to measure a text model’s multitask accuracy. 4) Winogrande (Sakaguchi et al.,
242   2021): an adversarial and difficult Winograd benchmark at scale, for commonsense reasoning.
243
      Implementation details. During robustness data generation, we adopt similar hyper-parameters
244
      used in different attack algorithms to attack a pre-trained Llama-2 model, resulting in 78, 715 adver-
245
      sarial examples across 5 different tasks. For fairness data generation, we sample 3, 000 data from
246   Adult dataset and 1, 000 data from Crime dataset, which lead to 8, 000 data in total after balancing
247   the sensitive attributes. The truthfulness dataset we leveraged contains 2, 184 examples generated
248   using Llama-2 model. During safety auxiliary model learning, we first SFT the model on the corre-
249   sponding dataset for 1 epoch, then run DPO on the answer pairs for 1, 000 steps. All experiments
250   are conducted using A100 GPUs.

      3.3   I N - DEPTH ANALYSIS OF M ULTI T RUST
299
300
      In this section, we conduct a comprehensive exploration and analysis of MultiTrust. We begin
301   by evaluating the common practice of using SFT on trustworthy datasets. Our findings confirm that
302   while SFT is a straightforward method, it typically leads to sub-optimal performance. Besides, when
303   considering new safety perspectives, SFT usually leads to the model forgetting phenomenon, where
304   improvements in one area may cause regressions in another. We proceed by comparing the impacts
305   of SFT and DPO within our two-step safety auxiliary model learning process. We find that DPO is
306   more helpful to enhance the model performance compared to SFT. And integrating both methods
307   significantly improves the overall trustworthiness of the models. We then explore the inference-time
308   routing mechanism introduced in MultiTrust. This part of our analysis focuses on how effectively
309
      the router selects the most appropriate safety auxiliary model based on the input query, thereby opti-
      mizing the model trustworthiness in the responses. Finally, we investigate the interactions between
310
      different trustworthiness perspectives within the framework. This exploration helps us understand
311
      how improvements in one aspect of trustworthiness may influence others, providing insights into the
312   complex dynamics of model safety and reliability.
313
314   Continue fine-tuning leads to model forgetting. One intuitive approach to incorporate new trust-
315
      worthiness perspectives into a model is to continue fine-tuning it on datasets corresponding to each
      perspective. However, our findings indicate that this approach results in significant model forgetting.
316
      In our experiments, we initially train the model on the robustness dataset, followed by sequential
317
      fine-tuning on fairness and truthfulness datasets. After each training phase, we evaluate the robust-
318   ness performance of the model. The results of these evaluations are depicted in Figure 2, which
319   also includes the baseline performance of the pre-trained Llama-2-7B-Chat model for reference. We
320   observed an initial improvement in robustness scores following fine-tuning on the robustness data.
321   However, subsequent fine-tuning on the fairness dataset led to a decline in robustness scores, and
322   this decline continued as the model was further fine-tuned on the truthfulness dataset, eventually
323   nearing the baseline performance of the pre-trained model. These results clearly demonstrate that
      sequential fine-tuning on different datasets causes the model to forget previously learned tasks.

350   model trained on the mixed dataset was 38.14, which falls below the average score of 44.98 achieved
351   by models trained separately on each dataset. These findings underscore the limitations of gener-
352   alized fine-tuning approaches and highlight the effectiveness of our safety auxiliary model training
353   methodology.
354
      Learning from the difference between answers improves model trustworthiness In the safety
355   auxiliary model training phase of MultiTrust, we consider a two-step learning process to enhance
356   model trustworthiness. Initially, we employed Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to train the model us-
357   ing the correct answers from the dataset. Subsequently, we employed Direct Preference Optimiza-
358   tion (DPO) to further refine the model’s ability to discern between correct and incorrect responses.
359   We compare our combined method with two methods separately. The results are shown in Table 3.
360   Our experimental findings reveal that DPO training substantially outperforms SFT in enhancing
361   the trustworthiness of the models. For example, the DPO-trained model gets 64.78 averaged score
362   across three perspective, while the SFT-trained model only gets 55.50. This improvement is likely
363
      attributable to the DPO’s focus on learning from the contrasts between the correct and incorrect
      answers, which sharpens the model’s judgment and decision-making capabilities. Moreover, when
364
      combining the SFT and DPO, we observed the highest improvement in performance. This com-
365
      bination leverages the foundational alignment provided by SFT with the discriminative refinement
366   offered by DPO, resulting in optimal trustworthiness and reliability in model outputs. The resulting
367   model after SFT and DPO achieves 70.07 averaged score across all perspectives. These findings
368   underscore the efficacy of our dual-phase training approach in MultiTrust, demonstrating it to be an
369   effective strategy for developing reliable and safe LMs.

      4    R ELATED WORK
448
      Safety and trustworthiness in LLMs. As LLMs are deployed across increasingly diverse domains,
449
      concerns are simultaneously growing about their trustworthiness. Zou et al. (2023) studies the safety
450
      of LLMs by introducing GCG algorithm to optimize a suffix string that can jailbreak LLMs. Chen
451   et al. (2024) improves the model’s trustworthiness by leveraging a gradual self-truthifying method
452   to iteratively optimize the model. However, they only focus on a single specific trustworthiness
453   perspective and can not extend to other perspectives. There are also many datasets and benchmarks
454   evaluating the trustworthiness of LLMs. For example, AdvGLUE (Wang et al., 2021) and Prompt-
455   Bench (Zhu et al., 2023) evaluate the adversarial robustness of language models. Recently, Liang
456   et al. (2022) and Wang et al. (2023a) also focus on evaluating multiple perspectives of the model’s
457   trustworthiness.
458   Multi-task learning Existing work do multi-task learning either through model augmentation or
459   model merging. Model augmentation improves the LM by incorporating or introducing new mod-
460   ules (Bansal et al., 2024), which is not flexible and requires re-training additional modules when aug-
461   ment with different models. Model merging aims to merge different models into a single model (Il-
462   harco et al., 2022; Muqeeth et al., 2023; Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024). However, they require the LMs
463   are trained from the base model and merged with it. They does not allow model merging with differ-
464   ent initial parameters or different architectures. In MultiTrust, we do not require the safety auxiliary
465
      models to be trained from the base model, and we can augment models with different parameters
      and architectures, leading to better adaptability and real-world applications.
466
467
468   5    C ONCLUSION
469
470   In this paper, we introduced MultiTrust, a novel framework designed to enhance the safety and
471   trustworthiness of Large Language Models (LLMs) from multiple perspectives. By leveraging a
472   perplexity-based routing mechanism, MultiTrust dynamically aligns the output of the base LLM
473   with the outputs of the safety auxiliary models during inference, ensuring robust performance across
474   a variety of trustworthiness metrics. Our experimental results demonstrate significant improvements
475   in model trustworthiness without compromising on general performance, highlighting the frame-
476   work’s effectiveness and practical utility. Despite MultiTrust represents a significant advancement
477
      in the development of safer and more reliable LLMs, there are several limitations to consider. The
      current implementation relies heavily on the quality and diversity of the training data used for each
478
      safety model. In scenarios where data may be biased or insufficient, the effectiveness of the safety
479
      auxiliary models could be compromised. Additionally, the performance of the routing mechanism
480   depends on the quality of the safety auxiliary models. The routing accuracy will be limited if the
481   safety auxiliary models are not well-trained. The broader impacts of MultiTrust are twofold. By en-
482   hancing the safety and trustworthiness of LLMs, MultiTrust makes these models more viable for a
483   range of critical applications, from automated decision-making in healthcare and finance to real-time
484   monitoring systems in security-sensitive environments. Additionally, the framework encourages the
485   adoption of new emerging trustworthiness perspectives into current LLMs, encouraging the com-
      munity toward more responsible and sustainable AI development.
