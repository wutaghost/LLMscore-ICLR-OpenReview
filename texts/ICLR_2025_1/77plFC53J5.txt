011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Spiking neural networks (SNNs) have the potential advantage of building large-
014            scale energy-efficient network. However, the high training cost caused by mul-
015            tiple time steps currently limits the application of SNNs. To address this, we
016            break away from the traditional approach of reducing the number of time steps
017            and investigate feature redundancy between time steps. By jointly unfolding the
018            computational process of SNNs across both temporal and spatial dimensions, we
019            are the first to discover the Feature Overlapping Phenomenon, providing new in-
020
               sights for improving SNNs training paradigms. Our Temporal Differential Decou-
               pling (TDD) method successfully separates dynamic and static features, reducing
021
               redundant computations. By transforming the feature space into the differential
022
               domain, it addresses the issue of the original computational domain’s inability to
023            effectively filter sensitive information. In the differential domain, we propose the
024            Gradient Sensitivity Criterion (GSC), which helps further reduce training costs
025            and avoids the loss of important feature information. This paper introduces the
026            Differential Domain Low-Sparsity Approximation (DDLA) algorithm, which sig-
027            nificantly reduces computational resource consumption while maintaining com-
028            putational accuracy by adjusting the filtering ratio. Experimental results show that
029            we achieved up to an 80.9% reduction in the number of spikes per timestep and a
030            total spike count reduction of up to 57.8%, significantly reduce the inference cost
031
               of SNNs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) process all spiking events to process and represent information,
038   offering advantages such as low power consumption and high biological fidelity compared to Arti-
039
      ficial Neural Networks (ANNs) Allen et al. (2009); Guo et al. (2017). As a third-generation neural
      network model, they have garnered widespread attention Kasabov & Capecci (2015); Zhang et al.
040
      (2013). However, existing SNN networks rely on rate coding to encode features into spike se-
041
      quences, compensating for quantization errors through joint expectations over multiple time steps
042   Yang et al. (2021). This results in computational cost that increases exponentially with the number
043   of time steps, posing significant challenges for inference and training in large-scale networks, which
044   hinders the application of SNNs.
045
      Currently, to address the issue of high computational cost over time steps, existing work primarily
046
      focuse on reducing the number of time steps. Li et al. Li et al. (2023) proposed SEENN-I and
047
      SEENN-II, which explore fine-grained adjustments to the number of time steps during the inference
048   phase. Yao et al. Kim et al. (2023) studied the dynamics of temporal information, redistributing
049   time information to achieve delay-adaptive decision-making. However, these works mainly focus
050   on reducing the number of time steps through dynamic configuration Shen et al. (2024). Given
051   that the current SNNs already operate within five time steps, further reducing time steps results in
052   a significant loss of accuracy. Therefore, we believe that to address the high computational cost
053   of large-scale SNNs, the solution should not be to directly reduce time steps but rather to focus on
      whether there is redundancy between time steps.

054
      Through experiments, we found that between consecutive time steps, due to weight sharing and
055   identical image encoding, the firing patterns of neurons are often very similar. This led us to the
056   following thought:
057
058          • Are there redundant features in the multiple time steps of an SNN?
059
060          • How can we decouple effective features and redundant features?
061          • How can we reduce the computation associated with redundant features?
062
063   We present a frst-of-its-kind study to discover that certain feature information in the same time
064   sequence repeatedly appears across time steps, a phenomenon we define as ”feature shadowing”.
065   Previous works focus only on feature encoding and information representation capabilities. Due to
066   the inability to decouple redundant features, they believe that indiscriminate parallel computation
067   across multiple time steps is necessary for rate coding. Through clever Temporal Differential De-
068   coupling (TDD), we discover feature repetition and achieve redundancy separation. This provides
069   new possibilities for handling challenge of high training and inference costs in large-scale SNNs.
070   We propose the TDD method, which effectively decouples dynamic and static features. The features
071   vary with the time steps, are defined as dynamic features, representing the information gain for the
072   current time step. Transforming the feature space into the differential domain, making it possible
073   to filter out key features. In the original computational domain, crucial dynamic information could
074   be overlooked due to interference from static features. However, in the differential domain, sensi-
075   tive features are clearly identified, which avoids the negative impact of irrelevant features on result
076
      accuracy. We propose the Gradient Sensitivity Criterion (GSC) to filtering Sensitivity Features.
      Based on TDD and GSC, we propose Differential Domain Low-sparsity Approximation (DDLA)
077
      algorithm. By adjusting the filtering ratio, our algorithm is possible to achieve any desired level of
078
      computational precision while dramatically lowering the consumption of computational resources.
079
080   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
081
082
            1. We are the first to discover the Feature Overlapping Phenomenon, which not only reveals
               the issue of computational redundancy in SNN training paradigms but also provides new
083
               insights for improving computational efficiency.
084
085         2. Temporal Differential Decoupling method effectively decouples dynamic and static fea-
086            tures by transforming the feature space into the differential domain, which allows for better
087            filtering of key features. We propose Differential Domain Low-sparsity Approximation al-
088            gorithm, which significantly reduces computational resource consumption while ensuring
089
               computational precision.
090         3. We validated the effectiveness of our approach through comprehensive testing on both static
091            and event-based datasets across various network architectures. While ensuring accuracy
092            comparable to baseline models, we achieved up to an 80.9% reduction in the number of
093            spikes per timestep and a total spike count reduction of up to 57.8%, significantly enhancing
094            computational efficiency.

      2.1   ANN- TO -SNN CONVERSION
100
      The ANN-to-SNN conversion is a widely used method for obtaining SNNs by leveraging pre-trained
101
      ANNs Ho & Chang (2021); Wang et al. (2024). This approach converts an ANN with ReLU acti-
102   vation functions into an SNN with spike-based activations, typically involving scaling the ANN’s
103   weights and biases to ensure compatibility with the spiking characteristics of SNNs Wang et al.
104   (2022). Conversion methods often include scaling operations, such as weight normalization and
105   threshold balancing, to better align the dynamic range of activations in SNNs with those in ANNs
106   Hwang & Kung (2024); Kundu et al. (2021). However, these conversion methods are challenged
107   by significant conversion errors and inference delays, which make it difficult to effectively handle
      highly dynamic or deep neural network architectures.

      2.2   D IRECT TRAINING
109
110   Direct training of SNNs, without relying on pre-trained ANNs, has gained considerable attention
111   in recent years Lee et al. (2016); Yin et al. (2021), particularly for its advancements in reducing
112   time steps Bohte (2011), improving computational efficiency Sengupta et al. (2019), and enhancing
113   network performance Zheng et al. (2021). This approach typically involves using surrogate gradient
114   methods to overcome the challenges of gradient computation due to the discontinuity and non-
115   differentiability of spiking neurons Fang et al. (2021). Recent developments have made it possible
116
      to achieve efficient training with fewer time steps by optimizing surrogate gradient, firing thresholds
      and loss functions Fan et al. (2023). However, despite the reduction in time steps, the computational
117
      cost per time step remains high. Reducing the computational cost is a critical focus for future
118
      research.

      2.3   DYNAMIC T IME S TEP
122
      The computational cost of SNNs increases exponentially due to their multiple steps processing of
123   spiking events Dominguez-Morales et al. (2018). To address this issue, current research has pri-
124   marily focused on two approaches: dynamically adjusting the number of time steps Li et al. (2023)
125   and dynamically redistributing temporal information Kim et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2024). Methods
126   that adjust the number of time steps aim to dynamically modify the time steps of SNNs based on
127   the characteristics of the input samples Meng et al. (2022). For example, SEENN-I uses confidence
128   scores, and SEENN-II employs a policy network to determine the inference time steps. Another
129   set of methods use Bayesian learning through deep ensembles or variational inference to aggregate
130   information across time steps. However, these approaches primarily focus on configuring time steps
131
      and offer limited reductions in computational costs. We believe that, with the number of time steps
      recently reduced to fewer than five, the focus should shift to targeting redundancy in computations
132
      across time steps.

349   5.2   OVERALL A LGORITHM
350
      We propose a novel Differential Domain Low Sparsity Approximation algorithm (DDLA) based on
351   TDD , complemented by GSC. The process begins with the transformation of feature maps from
352   SNNs into the differential domain, where the focus is primarily on the differential increments of
353   information. These increments are crucial as they represent the additional information that can in-
354   fluence subsequent layers. Our method specifically targets sensitive neurons that have a significant
355   impact on the outputs of subsequent layers, while ignoring those with minimal influence. The ratio-
356   nale behind this approach is that if the output of a layer is insensitive to the next layer’s response, it
357   implies that any output from the current layer does not affect the subsequent layer, allowing us to set
358   the output of insensitive neurons to zero to reduce computational load. Additionally, the discrepancy
359
      between the actual and expected outputs at each layer is incorporated into the overall loss function
      for optimization. Through iterative refinement, the algorithm ensures that the output from each layer
360
      significantly affects the following layer, thereby enhancing the overall efficiency and performance
361
      of the network. The detailed algorithm 1 is shown in the appendix.

518   8   D ISCUSSION
519
520   In this paper, we explore and analyze the phenomenon of feature superposition in SNNs, revealing
521   the distribution patterns of features within the current SNNs computational frameworks. We propose
522   a novel approach of shifting the network’s focus from the feature domain to the temporal difference
523   domain. This shift is predicated on the importance of feature dynamics over static features in SNNs,
524   which not only reduces the number of spikes and conserves energy but also enhances the network’s
525   focus on changes between features. Concurrently, we introduce a preliminary method for constrain-
526   ing spike count in the temporal difference domain that aims to minimize spike emissions without
527   compromising accuracy. It is important to note that our method is not the optimal solution but of-
528
      fers a new perspective and constitutes a modest step forward in this field. Our experiments were
      conducted on smaller datasets and network architectures, and the results demonstrate the efficacy of
529
      our approach. We encourage further research into the computational mechanisms of SNNs from the
530
      perspective of the temporal difference domain.
