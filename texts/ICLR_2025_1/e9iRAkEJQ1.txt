                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
               Previous works have demonstrated the universal approximation capability of residual
012            networks through their continuous idealization as flow maps of dynamical systems.
013            However, informative results on their approximation rates in terms of depth (corre-
014            sponding to time) are generally lacking. From the viewpoint of approximation theory, a
015            major difficulty in addressing this gap lies in identifying an appropriate target space for
016            the approximation problem. In this paper, we introduce a restrictive but useful target
017            function space comprised of solutions to the eikonal equations, a type of first-order non-
018            linear partial differential equation, to investigate the approximation rates of flow map
019            families. We provide an estimate of the approximation error within this space, which
020
               is notably different from classical rate estimates based directly on the smoothness of
               target functions. This theoretical result further inspires a new learning-based algorithm
021
               for solving the eikonal equation. Experimental results validate the effectiveness of our
022
               proposed algorithm, including its robustness to spatial resolution and solution regularity,
023            as well as transferability among similar problems.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Flow maps of dynamical systems play a crucial role in machine learning, offering both practical applica-
029   tions and theoretical insights. They are particularly valuable in generative modeling due to their inherent
030   dynamical structure and invertibility (Chen et al., 2018; Song et al., 2020; Papamakarios et al., 2021).
031   Furthermore, deep neural networks with residual connections can be idealized as controlled dynamical
032   systems (Weinan, 2017; Haber and Ruthotto, 2017), where the input-output mapping corresponds to a flow
033
      map and the network depth relates to the maximal time horizon of the flow. This perspective underscores
      the significance of understanding the approximation capabilities of flow map families in deep learning
034
      theory.
035
036   In the context of machine learning, approximation theory studies how well functions in a target space
037   T can be approximated using a hypothesis space H, which is determined by the choice of model
038   architectures. Two main questions arise in approximation theory: density and approximation rate. The
039
      former concerns whether H is dense in T in some topology, while the latter involves estimating the
      approximation error for a function f ∈ T in relation to the model’s complexity. For example, when H is
040
      the space of polynomials, the Stone-Weierstrass theorem (Stone, 1948) establishes its density in the space
041
      of continuous functions. Additionally, Jackson’s theorem (Jackson, 1930) provides an approximation
042   rate estimation, indicating that the error in approximating an order-r smooth function f using degree
043   m polynomials is bounded by C(r, f )m−r , where C(r, f ) is a constant depending on both r and f .
044   This approximation rate tells us the number of polynomial terms required to achieve a specific accuracy
045   in approximating f . Consequently, approximation rate results are more informative and practical than
046   density results, as they offer a quantitative measure of the cost for approximation, it also tells us which
047   functions can be approximated easily.
048   Despite established density results for flow map families over a broad range of dynamical systems (Li
049   et al., 2022; Tabuada and Gharesifard, 2022; Ruiz-Balet and Zuazua, 2023; Cheng et al., 2023), the


                                                         1

      approximation rate in terms of the maximal time horizon T (analogous to network depth) is not well-
051
      understood. Existing results suffer from the curse of dimensionality or are limited to one-dimension case.
052   For instance, Ruiz-Balet and Zuazua (2023) showed that flow maps of continuous-time ResNet
053

056   approximation rate of O(T − d2 ) in L2 over bounded subsets of Rd , but this rate deteriorates rapidly as
057   the dimension d increases. Similarly, Li et al. (2022) provided rate estimates for d = 1, but their results
058   do not generalize to higher dimensions. Consequently, it remains unclear when a deeper flow (larger T )
059   offers advantages over a shallower one for a given target function in general. From the perspective of
060   approximation theory, identifying an appropriate target space is crucial in bridging this gap.
061   In this paper, instead of establishing approximation rate in a general target space, our purpose is to focus
062   on a specific yet practically relevant setting. Specifically, we identify a novel target space comprising
063   solutions to the eikonal equation, a type of first-order nonlinear partial differential equation. By observing
064   that solutions to the eikonal equation can be represented via the flow map of a static vector field, we
065   propose a flow-based hypothesis space and develop an approximation architecture that first approximates
066   the underlying flow map and then computes its flow-based representation to approximate the solution.
067   This architecture resembles a flow idealization of recurrent networks with residual connections, where
068   each layer incorporates specific information about the solution. We establish an approximation rate
069
      estimate within this hypothesis space, providing a quantitative measure of the approximation error with
      respect to the network depth T . Unlike classical smoothness-based approximation results DeVore and
070
      Lorentz (1993), our estimate depends on the dynamical structures of the solutions, offering a precise
071
      setting where increasing depth yields provable performance improvements.
072
073   Moreover, our approximation results motivate a novel deep learning method, which we call the finite flow
074   method, for solving the eikonal equation. This method numerically implements our proposed flow-based
075
      hypothesis space using a deep neural network. The flow-based representation further allows us to train
      the network by minimizing the variational loss of the represented solution, which is a more efficient way
076
      than minimizing the equation loss. Experimental comparisons with the fast marching method Sethian
077
      (1999), a state-of-the-art finite difference solver, and existing physics-informed neural network (PINN)
078   methods bin Waheed et al. (2021); Grubas et al. (2023) demonstrate the effectiveness of our algorithm,
079   highlighting advantages in robustness to spatial resolution, solution regularity, and transferability among
080   similar problems.
081
      In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows:
082
083          • By identifying the solutions to the eikonal equation as a target space, we construct a flow map
084            based hypothesis space and provide an approximation rate estimate in the space (Theorem 2.1).
085            While the target family is restrictive, the resulting approximation rate does not suffer from the
086            curse of dimensionality.
087          • We show that, different from the classical approximation theory where the approximation error is
088            estimated by the smoothness of the target, our approximation rate result depends on dynamical
089            structures of the solution to the eikonal equation.
090          • We further show that while the target space is restrictive, it is useful. We develop the finite flow
091            method for solving the eikonal equation by numerically implementing our hypothesis space.
092            Our method demonstrates effectiveness compared to state-of-the-art finite difference solvers and
093            existing PINN methods, offering advantages in certain scenarios.

      Approximation rate results of deep neural networks As a fundamental aspect in deep learning theory,
098   the expressive capability of deep neural networks has been extensively studied. The universal approxima-
099   tion results of different types of deep architectures have been established in the literature (Hornik et al.,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


100
      1989; Kidger and Lyons, 2020; Li et al., 2022; Yun et al., 2019). Compared to the universal approximation
101
      results, the approximation rates of deep neural networks are less understood. For deep fully connected
102   neural network with ReLU-type activation functions, a series of non-asymptotic approximation rate
103   results are obtained, e.g. Yarotsky (2018); Shen et al. (2019); Lu et al. (2021). Recently, Chenghao et al.
104   extends similar results to ResNet with one-dimension output. These results all suffer from the curse of
105   dimensionality, since their target space are all general smooth function spaces. Some artificial function
106   classes are introduced for curse-of-dimensionlality-free results Montanelli (2021); Poggio et al. (2017);
107   He (2023), but these function spaces lack a clear connection to practical applications. For the family
108   of flow maps, there are fewer results on the approximation rate. By idealizing deep residual networks
109   as the flow map of a continuous-time dynamical system, the authors in Ruiz-Balet and Zuazua (2023)
                                                C
110   provide an approximation rate of O(T − d2 ) for deep residual networks with depth T in the target space
111   of all square integrable functions over a bounded set in Rd . This result suffers significantly from the
112
      curse of dimensionality, limiting its practical insights. In Li et al. (2022), a nearly optimal rate for the
      continuous-time ReLU ResNet is obtained in one-dimension case. Although the approach is difficult to
113
      extend to higher dimensions, this results reveals a difference of the flow-based approximation to classical
114
      smoothness based approximation result.
115
116   Numerical methods for solving the eikonal equation The eikonal equation, a first-order nonlinear
117   partial differential equation, appears in various applications such as geometric optics, computer vision
118
      and seismology Sethian et al. (1999). There are two well-known finite difference methods for the eikonal
      equation: the fast marching method (FMM) Chopp (2001); Sethian (1999) and the fast sweeping method
119
      (FSM) Fomel et al. (2009); Zhao (2005). FMM computes solutions at grid points based on the logic
120
      Dijkstra’s algorithm, whereas FSM solves the eikonal equation by iteratively sweeping through the
121   grids. Both methods are renowned for their efficiency and accuracy, making them widely adopted in
122   practical applications. The physics-informed neural network (PINN) Raissi et al. (2019) has also been
123   applied to solve the eikonal equation bin Waheed et al. (2021); Grubas et al. (2023); Smith et al. (2020).
124   This approach involves training a neural network to approximate solutions to the eikonal equation by
125   minimizing the equation loss. Several factorization and regularization techniques have been introduced in
126   these works to enhance performance.

      The approximation rate in Theorem 2.1 differs significantly from classical smoothness-based approxima-
239   tion results. For instance, in polynomial approximation, the error in approximating an order-r smooth
240   target function F using polynomials of degree ≤ n is bounded by Cn−r |F |W r,p , where |F |W r,p denotes
241   the Sobolev norm of F in W r,p . This implies that functions that are easy to approximate using polyno-
242   mials are those with higher order smoothness and small derivatives in each order. Conversely, in our
243   approximation result, the maximal time T is bounded by the ratio between the maximal and minimal
244   gradient norm of u. However, any smoothness assumptions on u or |∇u| cannot solely guarantee a bound
245   for Cu , highlighting a fundamental difference from smoothness-based approximation.
246   Moreover, when T exceeds the maximal hitting time, the approximation rate in equation 10 is related to the
247   smoothness of Ṽu if W is a smoothness-based approximation family. However, the smoothness of Ṽu and
248   u can differ greatly. For instance, for any u ∈ Σ of the form u = α(|x − xs |), where α : [0, ∞) → [0, ∞)
249   is an increasing function, we have Ṽu (x) = x − xs , which is infinitely differentiable. Nevertheless, by

      Let us now also contrast this with currently known rate estimates concerning the depth T of flow maps.
271
      In Ruiz-Balet and Zuazua (2023), an estimation of the approximation rate by continuous-time ResNet
272
      with activation function ReLU for general L2 integrable target functions is provided. However, their result
273   suffers from the curse of dimensionality, and evaluating the complexity measure of the target function is
274   challenging. Compared to their setting, which approximates general target functions, our approach focuses
275   on a specific class of target functions with dynamical structures. Despite this restrictive setting, we obtain
276   a rate estimate which is curse-of-dimensionality-free with respect to time T , indicating when a deeper
277   network should be used over a shallower one and the corresponding error we can expect. Additionally, our
278   identified target functions have the property that their gradient norms are bounded both above and below,
279   with the ratio of these bounds estimating T . This setting is analogous to the 1D ReLU flow approximation
280   in Li et al. (2022), where target functions that can be approximated by flow within a finite time horizon
281
      have derivatives bounded both above and below. These connections can offer valuable insights into the
      properties target functions should possess for effective approximation by flow maps.
282
283   In summary, our approximation result identifies a restrictive but precise setting where the advantage of
284   using a deeper network can be quantified. Within such a setting, the approximation results by flows
285   are shown to be quite different to the smoothness-based approximation. This phenomenon provides
286   further evidence that smoothness should not be the correct measure of complexity for flow approximation.
287
      Moreover, the common feature of our target functions to the previous results may provide valuable insights
      into the key properties of target functions that can be effectively approximated by flow maps and deep
288
      neural networks.

430   4.2   ROBUSTNESS TO SOLUTION REGULARITY COMPARED TO PINN
431
432   In this part, we provide a comparison to existing PINN methods for the eikonal equation, NES-OP (Grubas
433
      et al., 2023) and PINNeik (bin Waheed et al., 2021).
434   The key difference of finite flow method to PINN is that finite flow method uses a network to formulate
435   the vector field Vu = −∇u/|∇u| and represent the solution using the flow-based representation, while
436   the PINN directly use the network output to represent the solution u. Intuitively, this implies that PINN is
437   suitable to problems where u is regular, while the finite flow method will perform well when ∇u/|∇u| is
438   regular. In general, when u is smooth enough, ∇u/|∇u| will should also be smooth. However, as we
439
      discussed in previous sections, the converse does not hold. Therefore, we may expect that in cases when
      u and Vu are both regular, both PINN and finite flow method can work well. Moreover, there should
440
      also exist cases when ∇u/|∇u| is simple but u itself is not, such that the finite flow method will perform
441
      better.
442
443   To validate this understanding, we test the performance of the three methods for two cost functions f1 and
444   f2 , see Appendix C.3 for the form of f1 and f2 . Here, f1 is a simple smooth cost function without sharp
445
      spatial change, while f2 is f1 plus a perturbation with relatively small scale but a sharp spatial oscillation.
      This perturbation does not change the vector field for f1 too much. The results of the three methods are
446
      compared in Table 1. We can see that for f1 , the best case of all the three methods perform well. PINNeik
447
      achieves the highest accuracy in the best case, while finite flow method is more stable in the typical case.
448   In the case of f2 , finite flow method outperforms both the PINN methods. Both PINNeik and NES-OP
449   show a significant drop in performance for f2 compared to f1 . The result validates that comparing to

451
      regularity of the vector field Vu is still good. This shows a potential of the finite flow method to handle
452   equations with highly heterogeneous cost functions, which is commonly faced in the applications of
453   geophysics (Virieux and Operto, 2009).

      5   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
471
      Currently, our theory and proposed numerical solver only handles the strong solution case. In the general
472
      case when the characteristic lines of the eikonal equation have intersections, the network in the finite
473   flow method may not learn the correct directions near these intersections. However, according to the
474   variational form of the solution, the flow-based representation is still valid in this case, except that the
475   vector field Vu can have discontinuities over a measure-zero set. In this direction, a direct future work
476   is to design algorithm for weak solution case of the eikonal equation. Moreover, we believe the idea of
477   identifying target space through solutions of PDEs can be generalized to a broader setting, especially for
478   the Hamilton-Jacobi equations (Arnol’d, 2013). In the future work, we will explore the approximation
479   results for other non-linear target spaces derived from non-linear PDEs with inspired learning algorithms
480   for solving the PDE.
