011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Using pre-trained models has been found to reduce the effect of data heterogeneity
014            and speed up federated learning algorithms. Recent works have investigated the
015
               use of first-order statistics and second-order statistics to aggregate local client data
               distributions at the server and achieve very high performance without any training.
016
               In this work we propose a training-free method based on an unbiased estimator of
017
               class covariance matrices. Our method, which only uses first-order statistics in
018            the form of class means communicated by clients to the server, incurs only a frac-
019            tion of the communication costs required by methods based on communicating
020            second-order statistics. We show how these estimated class covariances can be
021            used to initialize a linear classifier, thus exploiting the covariances without actu-
022            ally sharing them. When compared to state-of-the-art methods which also share
023            only class means, our approach improves performance in the range of 4-26% with
024            exactly the same communication cost. Moreover, our method achieves perfor-
025            mance competitive or superior to sharing second-order statistics with dramatically
026
               less communication overhead. Finally, using our method to initialize classifiers
               and then performing federated fine-tuning yields better and faster convergence.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Federated learning (FL) is a widely used paradigm for distributed learning from multiple clients or
032   participants. In FL, each client trains their local model on their private data and then send model
033   updates to a common global server that aggregates this information into a global model. The ob-
034   jective is to learn a global model that performs similarly to a model jointly trained on all the client
035   data. A major concern in existing federated optimization algorithms (McMahan et al., 2017) is the
036   poor performance when the client data is not identically and independently distributed (iid) or when
037   classes are imbalanced between clients (Zhao et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Acar et al., 2021; Karim-
038   ireddy et al., 2020a). Luo et al. (2021) showed that client drift in FL is mainly due to the drift in
039
      client classifiers which optimize to the local data distribution, resulting in forgetting knowledge from
      other clients from previous rounds (Legate et al., 2023b; Caldarola et al., 2022). Another challenge
040
      in FL is the partial participation of clients in successive rounds (Li et al., 2019), which becomes
041
      particularly acute with large numbers of clients (Ruan et al., 2021; Kairouz et al., 2021). To ad-
042   dress these challenges, recent works have focused on algorithms to better tackle data heterogeneity
043   between clients (Luo et al., 2021; Tan et al., 2022b; Legate et al., 2023a; Fanı̀ et al., 2024).
044
045
      Motivated by results from transfer learning (He et al., 2019), several recent works on FL have stud-
      ied the impact of using pre-trained models and observe that it can significantly reduce the impact of
046
      data heterogeneity (Legate et al., 2023a; Nguyen et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2022; Qu
047
      et al., 2022; Shysheya et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2021; Tan et al., 2022a). An important finding in sev-
048   eral of these works is that sending local class means to the server instead of raw features is more ef-
049   ficient in terms of communication costs, eliminates privacy concerns, and is robust to gradient-based
050   attacks (Chen et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2019). Tan et al. (2022b) used pre-trained models to compute
051   and then share class means as the representative of each class, and Legate et al. (2023a) showed
052   that aggregating local means into global means and setting them as classifier weights (FedNCM)
053   achieves very good performance without any training. FedNCM incurs very little communication
      cost and enables stable initialization. Recently, the authors of Fed3R (Fanı̀ et al., 2024) explored the

      2   R ELATED W ORK
088   Federated learning. FL is a rapidly-growing field and focuses on neural network training in fed-
089   erated environments (Zhang et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2023). Initial works like FedAvg (McMahan
090   et al., 2017) focuses on the aspect of distributed model training and communication. Later works
091   focus more on non-iid settings, where data among the clients is more heterogeneous (Li et al., 2019;
092   Kairouz et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021). FedNova (Wang et al., 2020) normal-
093
      izes local updates before averaging to address objective inconsistency. Scaffold (Karimireddy et al.,
      2020b) employs control variates to correct drift in local updates. FedProx (Li et al., 2020) introduces
094
      a proximal term in local objectives to stabilize the learning process. Reddi et al. (2020) proposed
095
      use of adaptive optimization methods, such as Adagrad, Adam and Yogi, at the server side. While
096   CCVR (Luo et al., 2021) proposed a classifier calibration by aggregating class means and covari-
097   ances from clients, (Li et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2022; Oh et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2024) proposed
098   using a fixed classifier motivated by the neural collapse phenomenon (Papyan et al., 2020). Af-
099   ter federated training with fixed classifiers, FedBABU (Oh et al., 2021) proposed to finetune the
100   classifiers and SphereFed (Dong et al., 2022) proposed a closed-form classifier calibration.
101
      FL with pretrained models. Recently, there has been increasing interest in incorporating pre-
102   trained, foundation models into federated learning. Multiple works (Nguyen et al., 2023; Tan et al.,
103   2022b; Chen et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2022; Shysheya et al., 2022) propose using pre-trained weights
104   which reduces the impact of client data heterogeneity and achieves faster model convergence.
105   FedFN (Kim et al., 2023) recently combined feature normalization with FedAvg to enhance per-
106   formance and highlighted that using pre-trained weights can sometimes negatively affect in highly
107   heterogeneous settings. FedNCM (Legate et al., 2023a) share clients’ class means for global clas-
      sifier initialization without any training. Recently, Fed3R (Fanı̀ et al., 2024) to initialize the global

521     6                  C ONCLUSION
522
523    In this work we proposed FedCOF, a novel training-free approach for federated learning with pre-
524
       trained models. By leveraging the statistical properties of distribution client class sample means, we
       show that second-order statistics can be estimated using only class means from clients, thus reducing
525
       communication costs. We derive a provably unbiased estimator of population class covariances, en-
526
       abling accurate estimation of a global covariance matrix. After applying shrinkage to the estimated
527    class covariances and removing between-class scatter matrices, we show that the server can effec-
528    tively use this global covariance to initialize the global classifier. Our experimental results show
529    that FedCOF outperforms FedNCM (Legate et al., 2023a) by significant margins while maintaining
530    the same communication costs. Additionally, FedCOF delivers competitive or even superior results
531    to Fed3R (Fanı̀ et al., 2024) across various model architectures and benchmarks while substantially
532    reducing communication costs. Moreover, we empirically demonstrate that our approach can serve
533    as a more effective starting point for improving the convergence of standard federated fine-tuning
534    and linear probing methods.
535    Limitations. The quality of our estimator depends on the number of clients, as shown in Fig. 8a
536    where using multiple class means per client helps with fewer client settings. Another limitation is
537    the assumption that samples of the same class are iid across clients, which is however an assumption
538    underlying most of federated learning. Finally, our method assumes the existence of a pre-trained
539    network. If the domain shift with the client data is sufficiently large, this is expected to impact the
       performance.

1011     A PPENDIX I                                 C ONVERGENCE A NALYSIS
1012
1013    In our work, we claim that FedCOF initialization achieves faster and better convergence based on
1014    our empirical results (Fig. 6) using multiple datasets with 100 classes (CIFAR100) and 200 classes
1015    (ImageNet-R, CUB200). We propose how to initialize the classifiers before performing federated
1016    optimization methods like FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) and FedAdam (Reddi et al., 2020) which
1017
        have already established the theoretical guarantees of convergence in their respective works. Unlike
        gradient-based FL methods, our method is training-free. Similar to Fed3R (Fanı̀ et al., 2024) and
1018
        FedNCM (Legate et al., 2023a), the proposed FedCOF does not depend on assumptions like bounded
1019
        variance of stochastic gradients or smoothness of clients objectives. While we do not propose any
1020    federated optimization step, we propose a training-free method that can be also used for initializing
1021    federated fine-tuning. We would also like to highlight that all existing works in Federated learning
1022    with pre-trained models (Tan et al., 2022b; Nguyen et al., 2020; Fanı̀ et al., 2024; Legate et al.,
1023    2023a; Chen et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2022; Shysheya et al., 2022) focus only on empirical observations
1024    assuming that the theoretical guarantees of existing federated optimization methods holds true when
1025    using pre-trained models. A more exhaustive study on convergence analysis for FL with pre-trained
        models would be an interesting direction to explore in future works.

       A PPENDIX L         P RIVACY C ONCERNS ON SHARING C LASS - WISE S TATISTICS
1141
       Our method requires transmitting class-wise statistics to compute the unbiased estimator of the pop-
1142
       ulation covariance (Eq. 14) and classifier initialization, similar to other methods in federated learn-
1143
       ing (Legate et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2021). In general, transmitting the class-wise statistics may raise
1144   privacy concerns, since each client could potentially expose its class distribution. Inspired by dif-
1145   ferential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006), we propose perturbing the class-wise statistics of each client
1146   with different types and intensities of noise, before transmission to the global server. This analysis
1147   allows us to evaluate how robust FedCOF is to variations in class-wise statistics and whether noise
1148   perturbation mechanisms can effectively hide the true client class statistics. Specifically, we propose
1149   perturbing the class-wise statistics as follows:
1150
                                            ek,c = max(nk,c + σϵnoise , 0)
                                            n                                                               (26)
1151
1152   where σϵnoise is noise added to the statistics, and ϵ is a parameter representing the noise intensity. The
1153   max operator clips the class statistics to zero if the added noise results in negative values, which
1154   is expected to happen in federated learning with highly heterogeneous client distributions. When
1155   clipping is applied, the client does not send the affected class statistic and class mean, and the server
1156   excludes them from the computation of the unbiased estimator.
1157   We consider three types of noise:
1158
1159          • Uniform noise: σϵunif ∼ U(−(1 − ϵ)nk,c , +(1 − ϵ)nk,c ), proportional to the real class
1160            statistics.
1161          • Gaussian noise: σϵgauss ∼ N (0, 1ϵ ), independent of the real class statistics.
1162
              • Laplacian noise σϵlaplace ∼ L(0, 1ϵ ), which is also independent of the real class statistics.
1163
1164   Lower ϵ values correspond to higher levels of noise in the statistics.
1165
1166   In Figure 11, we show that the performance of FedCOF is robust with respect to the considered
1167
       noise perturbation, varying the intensity of ϵ ∈ {0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9}. These results suggest that
       a differential privacy mechanism can be implemented to mitigate privacy concerns arising from the
1168
       exposure of client class-wise frequencies. In Figure 12, we provide a qualitative overview of how
1169
       the proposed Laplacian and uniform noise perturbation affect class-wise distributions.
