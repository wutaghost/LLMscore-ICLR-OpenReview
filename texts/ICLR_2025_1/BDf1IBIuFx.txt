011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            During the acquisition of satellite images, there is generally a trade-off between
014            spatial resolution and temporal resolution (acquisition frequency) due to the on-
015
               board sensors of satellite imaging systems. High-resolution satellite images are
               very important for land crop monitoring, urban planning, wildfire management
016
               and a variety of applications. It is a significant yet challenging task to achieve high
017
               spatial-temporal resolution in satellite imaging. With the advent of diffusion mod-
018            els, we can now learn strong generative priors to generate realistic satellite images
019            with high resolution, which can be utilized to promote the super-resolution task
020            as well. In this work, we propose a novel diffusion-based fusion algorithm called
021            SatDiffMoE that can take an arbitrary number of sequential low-resolution satel-
022            lite images at the same location as inputs, and fuse them into one high-resolution
023            reconstructed image with more fine details, by leveraging and fusing the comple-
024            mentary information from different time points. Our algorithm is highly flexi-
025            ble and allows training and inference on arbitrary number of low-resolution im-
026
               ages. Experimental results show that our proposed SatDiffMoE method not only
               achieves superior performance for the satellite image super-resolution tasks on a
027
               variety of datasets, but also gets an improved computational efficiency with re-
028
               duced model parameters, compared with previous methods.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Satellite imaging is a very useful technique for monitoring the natural phenomena and human activ-
034   ities on the surfaces of the Earth. Lots of applications rely on satellite images such as crop moni-
035   toring, weather forecasting, urban planning, wildfire management and so on Khanna et al. (2024);
036   Burke et al. (2021); Ayush et al. (2020; 2021); Beck et al. (2007); Wang et al. (2018a); M Rustowicz
037   et al. (2019); Li et al. (2019). However, the acquisition of satellite images can be very expensive and
038   the spatial and temporal resolution (the frequency that a satellite image is captured) may be limited
039
      due to the physical constraints of sensors Khanna et al. (2024). In addition, the high temporal reso-
      lution may come with trade-off in spatial resolution. Recent advance in satellite imaging technology
040
      enables us to capture the same area with a high-revisit frequency, but the spatial resolution is often
041
      limited. For instance, two Sentinel-2 satellites with resolutions from 10m to 60m can capture all
042   land surfaces every five days Cornebise et al. (2022); Cong et al. (2022); Tarasiewicz et al. (2023);
043   Van Etten et al. (2018), but to perform land crop monitoring or wildfire management, this resolution
044   is not sufficient. On the other hand, some very high-resolution satellite images such as SPOT6 or
045   WorldView can have resolution better than 1.5m Christie et al. (2018), but it is extremely difficult
046   to collect those images for a large area, and these images cannot be captured as frequently as the
047   Sentinel images. The limited temporal resolution severely limits downstream applications in urban
048   planning, object detection, or continuous monitoring of crop or vegetation covers.
049   To solve the aforementioned challenges, super-resolution algorithms have been introduced to pre-
050   dict the high-resolution (HR) satellite images from a bunch of corresponding low-resolution (LR)
051   satellite images Luo et al. (2018; 2023), so that to obtain more fine details. For example, given
052   the low-resolution satellite images (10m) from Sentinel-2 of a specific location, the super-resolution
053   algorithms are developed to predict the high-resolution (1.5m) SPOT6 satellite image of the same
      location at a specific time. Nevertheless, solving remote sensing super-resolution problems is still

125   Latent diffusion models (LDM) Rombach et al. (2022) have been proposed for faster inference
126   and training with a reduced computational burden. By applying an autoencoder to reduce data
127   dimension, LDMs train the diffusion model in a compressed latent space, and then decode the latent
128   code into signals. This method enables high-quality high-resolution image synthesis benefited from
129   its compressed latent space, which is an ideal fit for satellite images due to the large image size.
130
      Nevertheless, it is still challenging to perform image restoration accurately with LDMs Song et al.
      (2024); Rombach et al. (2022). Various works have tried to extend LDMs for high-dimensional or
131
      high-resolution signal synthesis, such as video generation Blattmann et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023);
132

133   Saharia et al. (2022); Voleti et al. (2022). However, few works apply LDMs for image fusion yet. It is
134   an open problem to sample high-resolution images conditioning on multiple similar low-resolution
135   images with LDMs.
136
137   Single-image super-resolution Single-image super-resolution (SISR) focuses on reconstructing
138   the high-resolution image from one corresponding low-resolution image. In the era of deep learn-
139   ing, super-resolution problem has become a popular research question and many data-driven meth-
140   ods have been proposed. In past few years, state-of-the-art methods apply techniques such as Con-
141   volutional neural network (CNN), Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), and Transformers for
142   restoring the high-resolution images Kingma & Welling (2013); Creswell et al. (2018); Haris et al.
143   (2018); Wang et al. (2018b); Isola et al. (2017); Vaswani et al. (2017). These methods usually learn
144
      the mapping from low-resolution image to high-resolution image through a data-driven way using
      the pair data to train the neural network.
145
146   However, many of these methods output blurry or inaccurate reconstructions since they are learning a
147   direct mapping between LR images and HR images without considering the distribution of possible
148   HR images Song et al. (2024; 2023). Due to the ill-posedness of the super-resolution problem,
149   there may exist multiple HR images corresponding to one single LR image. A direct regression-
150
      based approach may let the network learn an average of all possible HR images, which leads to
      blurry output. Diffusion models address this issue by learning a strong generative prior that can
151
      perform posterior sampling Chung et al. (2023) instead of direct regression. This sampling method
152
      outputs realistic images, and leads to better image perceptual quality. One line of work assumes the
153   degradation operator is known, and focuses on inference-time posterior sampling with the diffusion
154   prior without retraining Chung et al. (2023); Kawar et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Song et al.
155   (2024; 2021). The other line of work assume the degradation operator is unknown. They concat the
156   LR image into the noise vector or conditional networks and then retrain or fine-tune the diffusion
157   model Zhang et al. (2023); Khanna et al. (2024). Both lines of work show good ability to model
158   complex high-resolution image distributions and outperform CNN approaches in image perceptual
159   quality. However, all these works focus on obtaining HR images from a single LR image.

201   from all these previous works, we aim to propose a more flexible and robust fusion algorithm that can
202   take an arbitrary number of low-resolution inputs with corresponding time information to generate

      Instead of conditioning on multiple low-resolution images as a concatenated input Khanna et al.
209
      (2024), we propose a novel fusion algorithm: condition on each image and then fuse each score
210   (the output of the conditional diffusion model) into a high-resolution image reconstruction. First,
211   in order to embed the temporal information, we introduce a new method that trains a conditional
212   diffusion model using one LR image and the relative time difference (between the input LR image
213   and the target HR image) as inputs to reconstruct the HR image. Then we propose a novel inference-
214   time algorithm that modifies the intermediate outputs in the reverse sampling procedure based on
215   the assumption that HR reconstruction of LR images at the same location should look similar. Our
      method is illustrated in Fig. 2.

      5   C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this work, we present “SatDiffMoE”, a novel framework for satellite image super-resolution with
525   latent diffusion models. We first present a novel training mechanism that conditions on relative time
526   difference of LR images and HR images. Then, we propose a novel inference-time algorithm that
527   fuses the reverse sampling trajectory from inputs of different LRs at the same location but different
528   times. Our method is highly flexible that can adapt to an arbitrary number of low-resolution inputs
529   at test-time and requires fewer parameters than diffusion-based counterparts. One of our limitation
530   is we do not impose physical measurement constraint in our reconstruction process, which we will
531   leave as future work.

      compute the dti for each LRi .

714   fMoW We obtain the high-resolution images from Christie et al. (2018), and the paired Sentinel-2
715   images from Cong et al. (2022). We first identify the area of interest on the HR images as given by
716   Christie et al. (2018), and then crop out other areas. Then we crop the corresponding LR images
717   following the pre-processing steps given by Cong et al. (2022). We crop the HR images into patches
718
      of 512 × 512, and align LR images into patches based on each HR image patch. Then we resize each
      LR image into the size of 512 × 512 in accordance with the HR image. We consider 6 categories:
719
      airport, amusement parks, car dealership, crop field, educational institution, electric substation for
720
      training and testing. For training, we filter out HR images that do not have a corresponding LR, and
721   those do not have three channels. We take the same training and validation split from Christie et al.
722   (2018). When training, we consider all images from Christie et al. (2018), and when testing, we
723   pick the first 100 images from each selected category of the validation set of Christie et al. (2018).
724   We also extract dti from the metadata of fmow provided by Christie et al. (2018), and the metadata
725   of Sentinel-2 data provided by Cong et al. (2022).
726
727   Model Training We take the pretrained checkpoint (SD1.2) provided by Rombach et al. (2022),
728   and then fine tune on the processed Christie et al. (2018) and Cornebise et al. (2022) datasets. We
729   rescale every LR image and HR image to the scale of [0,1]. Then, we use a learning rate 1e−5 , and a
730
      batch size of 4 for both datasets. We stop training when the FID of sampled images stops improving.
      For Cornebise et al. (2022) dataset, we only train 800 iterations (partly due to the small dataset size).
731
      For Christie et al. (2018) dataset, we train 4400 iterations. We then take the model for downstream
732
      inference tasks. For WorldStrat dataset, we use the prompt “Satellite images” for training.
733
734
      Model Inferencing We use 50 DDIM steps with η = 0 for inferencing. We perform optimization
735   every 5 steps for computational efficiency, otherwise, we just perform conditional sampling. We set
736   λ = 0.1 and α = 0.2 for both datasets.
737
738   CLIP zero-shot classification We use the pretrained CLIP model to compute the image embed-
739   ding and text embedding. For each class of fMoW, we take the reconstructed images from each
740   methods and compute its CLIP embedding using the ViT-B/32 model, which gives an embedding of
741   shape (1,512). Then we use the text encoder to compute the embedding, which gives a shape of (77,
742   512). The prompt for each class is given by “a satellite image of {class} from an overhead view”.
743   Then we commpute the cosine similarity of the image embedding and the text embeddings, which
744   takes the mean of the text embedding over dimension 0. Then we pick the class with the highest
745
      cosine similarity.

777   of 32, and on Christie et al. (2018) we take 100000 iterations with a batch size of 32.

779   MSRResNet We follow the original codebase of Wang et al. (2018b), where we train the MSR-
780   ResNet model on both Christie et al. (2018) and Cornebise et al. (2022) datasets. During training,
781   we randomly pick a LR image and its paired HR image. We tune the hyperparameters of the loss
782   function based on validation set performance. We train for 160000 iterations for both Cornebise
783   et al. (2022) and Christie et al. (2018) datasets with a batch size of 16.
784
785   DBPN We follow the original codebase of Haris et al. (2018), where we train the MSRResNet
786   model on both Christie et al. (2018) and Cornebise et al. (2022) datasets. During training, we
787
      randomly pick a LR image and its paired HR image. We tune the hyperparameters of the loss
      function based on validation set performance. We train for 100 epochs for both Cornebise et al.
788
      (2022) and Christie et al. (2018) datasets with a batch size of 16.
789
790

      Pix2Pix We follow the original codebase of Isola et al. (2017), where we train the MSRResNet
      model on both Christie et al. (2018) and Cornebise et al. (2022) datasets. During training, we
792
      randomly pick a LR image and its paired HR image. We tune the hyperparameters of the loss
793
      function based on validation set performance. We train for 30 epochs for Christie et al. (2018) and
794   100 epochs for Cornebise et al. (2022) with a batch size of 16.
795
796
      ControlNet We follow the original codebase of Zhang et al. (2023). During training, we randomly
797   pick a LR image and its paired HR image. We tune the hyperparameters of the loss function based
798   on validation set performance. We train for 12500 iterations with a batch size of 16 for Cornebise
799   et al. (2022), and 21500 iterations with a batch size of 16 for Christie et al. (2018).
800
801   DiffusionSat We implemented the 3D ControlNet architecture as mentioned in Khanna et al.
802   (2024). Then, we take the RGB band and the SWIR, NIR band from LR image for training 3D
803   ControlNet. We tune the hyperparameters of the loss function based on validation set performance.
804   We train for 12500 iterations with a batch size of 16 for Cornebise et al. (2022), and 20000 itera-
805   tions with a batch size of 16 for Christie et al. (2018). We observe that further training worsened
806   FID scores on both datasets.
807
808   TR-MISR We use the repo from https://github.com/Suanmd/TR-MISR/. We set the learning rate
809   to be 1e−4 which we observe to give the best performance, and then we use the l-2 loss. We train
      for 100 epochs for both datasets with early stopping if the validation loss stops decreasing.
