011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               Modern machine learning algorithms, especially deep learning-based techniques,
014            typically involve careful hyperparameter tuning to achieve the best performance.
015            Despite the surge of intense interest in practical techniques like Bayesian opti-
016            mization and random search-based approaches to automating this laborious and
017            compute-intensive task, the fundamental learning-theoretic complexity of tuning
018            hyperparameters for deep neural networks is poorly understood. Inspired by this
019            glaring gap, we initiate the formal study of hyperparameter tuning complexity in
020            deep learning through a recently introduced data-driven setting. We assume that
021            we have a series of deep learning tasks, and we have to tune hyperparameters to do
022
               well on average over the distribution of tasks. A major difficulty is that the utility
               function as a function of the hyperparameter is very volatile and furthermore, it
023
               is given implicitly by an optimization problem over the model parameters. This
024
               is unlike previous work in data-driven design, where one can typically explicitly
025            model the algorithmic behavior as a function of the hyperparameters. To tackle
026            this challenge, we introduce a new technique to characterize the discontinuities
027            and oscillations of the utility function on any fixed problem instance as we vary the
028            hyperparameter; our analysis relies on subtle concepts including tools from differ-
029            ential/algebraic geometry and constrained optimization. This can be used to show
030            that the learning-theoretic complexity of the corresponding family of utility func-
031            tions is bounded. We instantiate our results and provide the sample complexity
032            bounds for concrete applications—tuning a hyperparameter that interpolates neu-
033
               ral activation functions and setting the kernel parameter in graph neural networks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Developing deep neural networks that work best for a given application typically corresponds to a
039
      tedious selection of hyperparameters and architectures over extremely large search spaces. This pro-
      cess of adapting a deep learning algorithm or model to a new application domain takes up significant
040
      engineering and research resources, and often involves unprincipled techniques with limited or no
041
      theoretical guarantees on the effectiveness. While the success of pre-trained (foundation) models
042   have shown the usefulness of transferring effective parameters (weights) of learned deep models
043   across tasks (Devlin, 2018; Achiam et al., 2023), it is less clear how to leverage prior experience of
044   “good” hyperparameters to new tasks. In this work, we develop a principled framework for tuning
045   continuous hyperparameters in deep networks by leveraging similar problem instances and obtain
046   sample complexity guarantees for learning provably good hyperparameter values.
047
      The vast majority of practitioners still use a naive “grid search” based approach which involves se-
048   lecting a finite grid of (often continuous-valued) hyperparameters and selecting the one that performs
049   the best. A lot of recent literature has been devoted to automating and improving this hyperparameter
050   tuning process, prominent techniques include Bayesian optimization (Hutter et al., 2011; Bergstra
051   et al., 2011; Snoek et al., 2012; 2015) and random search based methods (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012;
052   Li et al., 2018). While these approaches work well in practice, they either lack a formal basis or
053   enjoy limited theoretical guarantees only under strong assumptions. For example, Bayesian opti-
      mization assumes that the performance of the deep network as a function of the hyperparameter can


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      be approximated as a noisy evaluation of an expensive function, typically making assumptions on
055   the form of this noise, and requires setting several hyperparameters and other design choices includ-
056   ing the amount of noise, the acquisition function which determines the hyperparameter search space,
057   the type of kernel and its bandwidth parameter. Other techniques, including random search methods
058   and spectral approaches (Hazan et al., 2018) make fewer assumptions but only work for a discrete
059   and finite grid of hyperparameters.
060
      We approach the problem of hyperparameter tuning in deep networks using the lens of data-driven
061   algorithm design, initially introduced in the context of theory of computing for algorithm configu-
062   ration (Gupta & Roughgarden, 2016; Balcan, 2020). A key idea is to treat a parameterized family
063   of algorithms as the hypothesis space and input instances to the algorithm as the data, reducing
064   hyperparameter tuning to a learning problem. While the approach has been successfully applied
065   to tune fundamental machine learning algorithms including clustering (Balcan et al., 2018b; 2019),
066   semi-supervised learning (Balcan & Sharma, 2021), low-rank approximation (Bartlett et al., 2022),
067   regularized linear regression (Balcan et al., 2022a; 2024a), decision tree learning (Balcan & Sharma,
068   2024), among others, our work is the only one to focus on analyzing deep network hyperparameter
069
      tuning under this data-driven paradigm. A key technical challenge that we overcome is that varying
      the hyperparameter even slightly can lead to a significantly different learned deep network (even for
070
      the same training set) with completely different parameters (weights) which is hard to characterize
071
      directly. This is very different from a typical data-driven method where one is able to show closed
072   forms or precise structural properties for the variation of the learning algorithm’s behavior as a func-
073   tion of the hyperparameter (Balcan et al., 2021a). We elaborate further on our technical novelties in
074   Section 1.1. We note that our theoretical advances are potentially useful beyond deep networks, to
075   algorithms with a tunable hyperparameter and several learned parameters.
076
      We instantiate our novel framework for hyperparameter tuning in deep networks in some funda-
077
      mental deep learning techniques with active research interest. Our first application is to tuning an
078   interpolation hyperparameter for the activation function used at each node of the neural network.
079   Different activation functions perform well on different datasets (Ramachandran et al., 2017; Liu
080   et al., 2019). We analyze the sample complexity of tuning the best combination from a pair of acti-
081   vation functions by learning a real-valued hyperparameter that interpolates between them. We tune
082   the hyperparameter across multiple problem instances, an important setting for multi-task learning.
083   Our contribution is related to neural architecture search (NAS). NAS (Zoph & Le, 2017; Pham et al.,
084   2018; Liu et al., 2018) automates the discovery and optimization of neural network architectures,
085   replacing human-led design with computational methods. Several techniques have been proposed
086
      (Bergstra et al., 2013; Baker et al., 2017; White et al., 2021), but they lack principled theoretical
      guarantees (see additional related work in Appendix A), and multi-task learning is a known open re-
087
      search direction (Elsken et al., 2019). We also instantiate our framework for tuning the graph kernel
088
      parameter in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2017) designed for more effectively
089   deep learning with structured data. Hyperparameter tuning for graph kernels has been studied in the
090   context of classical models (Balcan & Sharma, 2021; Sharma & Jones, 2023), in this work we pro-
091   vide the first provable guarantees for tuning the graph hyperparameter for the more effective modern
092   approach of graph neural networks.

      1.1    T ECHNICAL CHALLENGES AND INSIGHTS
124
      To analyze the pseudo-dimension of the utility function class U, by using our proposed results (The-
125   orem 3.1), the key challenge is to establish the relevant piecewise structure of the dual utility function
126   class u∗x . Different from typical problems studied in data-driven algorithm design, u∗x in our case is
127   not an explicit function of the hyperparameter α, but defined implicitly via an optimization problem
128   over the network weights w, i.e. u∗x (α) = maxw∈W fx (α, w). In the case where fx (α, w) is piece-
129   wise constant, we can partition the hyperparameter space A into multiple segments, over which the
130   set of connected components for any fixed value of the hyperparameter remains unchanged. Thus,
131   the behavior on a fixed instance as a function of the hyperparameter α is also piecewise constant and
132   pseudo-dimension bounds follow. It is worth noting that u∗x cannot be viewed as a simple projection
133
      of fx onto the hyperparameter space A, making it challenging to determine the relevant structural
      properties of u∗x .
134
135   For the case fx (α, w) is piecewise polynomial, the structure is significantly more complicated
136   and we do not obtain a clean functional form for the dual utility function class u∗x . We first
137   simplify the problem to focus on individual pieces, and analyze the behavior of u∗x,i (α) =
138   supw:(α,w)∈Rx,i fx,i (α, w) in the region Ri where fx (α, w) = fx,i (α, w) is a polynomial. We
139   then employ ideas from algebraic geometry to give an upper-bound for the number of local extrema
140   w for each α and use tools from differential geometry to identify the smooth 1-manifolds on which
141
      the local extrema (α, w) lie. We then decompose such manifolds into monotonic-curves, which have
      the property that they intersect at most once with any fixed-hyperparameter hyperplane α = α0 . Us-
142
      ing these observations, we can finally partition A into intervals, over which u∗x,i can be expressed as
143
      a maximum of multiple continuous functions for each of which we have upper bounds on the num-
144   ber of local extrema. Putting together, we are able to leverage a result from Balcan et al. (2021a) to
145   bound the pseudo-dimension.
146
147   Paper positioning. Our setting requires technical novelty compared to prior work in statistical
148   data-driven algorithm hyperparameter tuning (Balcan et al., 2017; 2020a;b; 2021b;a; 2022a; Bartlett
149   et al., 2022; Balcan & Sharma, 2024). As far as we concern, in most prior research (Balcan et al.,
150   2017; 2020a; 2021a; 2020b; 2021b; Bartlett et al., 2022), the hyperparameter tuning process does
151   not involve the parameter w meaning that given any fixed hyperparameter α, the behavior of the
152   algorithm is determined. In some other cases that involves parameter w, we can have a precise ana-
153   lytical characterization of how the optimal parameter behaves for any fixed hyperparameter (Balcan
154
      et al., 2022a), or at least a uniform approximate characterization (Balcan et al., 2024a). However, our
      setting does not belong to those cases, and requires a novel proof approach to handle the challenging
155
      case of hyperparameter tuning of neural networks (see Appendix B for a detailed discussion).

160   Setup. We introduce a novel data-driven hyperparameter tuning framework for algorithms with
161   trainable parameters. Our objective is to optimize a hyperparameter α ∈ A = [αmin , αmax ] ⊂ R for
      an algorithm that also involves model parameters w ∈ [wmin , wmax ]d ⊂ Rd . For a given problem

162
      instance x ∈ X , we measure the model’s performance as f (x, w; α), where w represents the model
163   parameters and α the hyperparameter. We then define a utility function uα (x) to quantify the algo-
164   rithm’s performance with hyperparameter α on problem instance x: uα (x) = maxw∈W f (x, w; α).
165   This formulation can be interpreted as follows: for a given hyperparameter α and problem instance
166   x, we determine the optimal model parameters w that maximize performance.
167
      In the data-driven framework, we assume an underlying, application-specific problem distribution D
168
      over X . The best hyperparameter α∗ for D can be defined as α∗ ∈ arg maxα Ex∼D [uα (x)]. How-
169   ever, since the problem distribution D is unknown, we instead use a set S of N problem instances
170   at hand, S = {x1 , . . . , xN } drawn from D. The hyperparameter α̂ERM is then chosen to maximize
171                                               PN
      the empirical utility: α̂ERM ∈ arg maxα N1 i=1 uα (xi ).
172
173   Main question. Our goal is to answer the learning-theoretic question: How good is the tuned
174   hyperparameter compared to the best hyperparameter, for algorithms with trainable parame-
175   ters? Specifically, we aim to provide a high-probability guarantee for the difference between
176   the performance of α̂ERM and α∗ , expressed as: |Ex∼D [uα̂ERM (x)] − Ex∼D [uα∗ (x)]| . Let
177   U = {uα : R → [0, H] | α ∈ A} be the utility function class. Classical theory suggests that the
178   learning-theoretic question at hand is equivalent to analyzing the pseudo-dimension (Pollard, 2012)
179   or Rademacher complexity (Wainwright, 2019) (see Appendix C for further background) of the
180   function class U. However, this analysis poses significant challenges due to two primary factors:
181
      (1) the intricate structure of the function class itself, where a small change in α can lead to large
      changes in the utility function uα , and (2) uα is computed by solving an optimization problem
182
      over the trainable parameters, and its explicit structure is unknown and hard to characterize. These
183
      challenges make analyzing the learning-theoretic complexity of U particularly challenging.
184
185   In this work, we demonstrate that when the function f (x, w; α) exhibits a certain degree of
186   structure, we can establish an upper bound for the learning-theoretic complexity of the utility
187
      function class U. Specifically, we examine two scenarios: (1) where f (x, w; α) possesses a
      piecewise constant structure (Section 4), and (2) where it exhibits a piecewise polynomial (or
188
      rational) structure (Section 5). These piecewise structures hold in hyperparameter tuning for
189
      popular deep learning algorithms (Section 6).
190
      Remark 1. Note that our bounds on the learning-theoretic complexity of the dual utility function
191
      class implies bounded sample complexity for ERM, but the algorithmic question of actually imple-
192
      menting this ERM efficiently is left open for future research.

      via analyzing its dual functions. Specifically, for each problem instance x, we define the dual utility

199   Our key technical contribution is to demonstrate that when fx (α, w) := f (x, w; α) exhibits a
200   piecewise structure, u∗x (α) also admits favorable structural properties, which depend on the specific

204   Oscillations and its connection with pseudo-dimension. When the function class U = {uρ :
205   X → R | ρ ∈ R} is parameterized by a real-valued index ρ, Balcan et al. (2021a) propose a conve-
206   nient way of bounding the pseudo-dimension of H, via bounding the oscillations of the dual function
207   u∗x (ρ) := uρ (x) corresponding to any problem instance x. We recall the notions of oscillation and
208   its connection with the pseudo-dimension of the dual function class.
209   Definition 1 (Oscillations, Balcan et al. 2021a). A function h : R → R has at most B oscillations
210   if for every z ∈ R, the function ρ 7→ I{h(ρ)≥z} is piecewise constant with at most B discontinuities.
211
      An illustration of the notion of oscillations can be found in Figure 1. Using the idea of oscillations,
212   one can analyze the pseudo-dimension of parameterized function classes by alternatively analyzing
213   the oscillations of their dual functions, formalized as follows.
214
      Theorem 2.1 (Balcan et al. 2021a). Let U = {uρ : X → R | ρ ∈ R}, of which each dual function
215   u∗x (ρ) has at most B oscillations. Then Pdim(U) = O(ln B).

230   We first establish connection between the number of oscillations in a piecewise continuous function
231   and its local extrema and discontinuities. It serves as a general tool to upper-bound the pseudo-
232   dimension of function classes via analyzing the piecewise continuous structure their dual functions.
233   Lemma 3.1. Let h : R → R be a piecewise continuous function which has at most B1 discontinuity
234   points, and has at most B2 local maxima. Then h has at most O(B1 + B2 ) oscillations.
235
236   Proof Sketch. The proof can be found in Appendix D. The idea is to bound the number of solutions
237
      of h(ρ) = 0, which determines the number of oscillations for h. We show that in each interval
      where h is continuous, we can bound the number of solutions of h(ρ) = 0 using the number of local
238
      maxima of h. Aggregating the number of solutions across all continuous intervals of h yields the
239
      desired result.

      7   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
525
526
      In this work, we establish the first principled approach to hyperparameter tuning in deep networks
527
      with provable guarantees, by employing the lens of data-driven algorithm design. We integrate sub-
528   tle concepts from algebraic and differential geometry with our proposed ideas, and establish the
529   learning-theoretic complexity of hyperparameter tuning when the neural network loss is a piecewise
530   constant or piecewise polynomial function of the parameters and the hyperparameter. We demon-
531   strate applications of our results in multiple contexts, including tuning graph kernels for graph con-
532   volutional networks and neural architecture search.
533
      This work opens up several directions for future research. While we resolve several technical hur-
534   dles to handle the piecewise polynomial case, it would be useful to also study cases where the piece-
535   wise functions or boundaries involve logarithmic, exponential, or more generally, Pfaffian functions
536   (Khovanski, 1991). We study the case of tuning a single hyperparameter, a natural next question is to
537   determine if our results can be extended to tuning multiple hyperparameters simultaneously. Finally,
538   while our work primarily focuses on providing learning-theoretic sample complexity guarantees,
539   developing computationally efficient methods for hyperparameter tuning in data-driven settings is
      another avenue for future research.

      A    A DDITIONAL RELATED WORK
757
758
      Learning-theoretic complexity of deep nets. A related line of work studies the learning-theoretic
759
      complexity of deep networks, corresponding to selection of network parameters (weights) over a
760   single problem instance. Bounds on the VC dimension of neural networks have been shown for
761   piecewise linear and polynomial activation functions (Maass, 1994; Bartlett et al., 1998) as well
762   as the broader class of Pfaffian activation functions Karpinski & Macintyre (1997). Recent work
763   includes near-tight bounds for the piecewise linear activation functions (Bartlett et al., 2019) and
764   data-dependent margin bounds for neural networks (Bartlett et al., 2017).
765
766   Data-driven algorithm design. Data-driven algorithm design, also known as self-improved algo-
767   rithms (Balcan, 2020; Ailon et al., 2011; Gupta & Roughgarden, 2020), is an emerging field that
768   adapts algorithms’ internal components to specific problem instances, particularly in parameterized
769   algorithms with multiple performance-dictating hyperparameters. Unlike traditional worst-case or
770
      average-case analysis, this approach assumes problem instances come from an application-specific
      distribution. By leveraging available input problem instances, this approach seeks to maximize em-
771
      pirical utilities that measure algorithmic performance for those specific instances. This method has
772
      demonstrated effectiveness across various domains, including low-rank approximation and dimen-
773   sionality reduction (Li et al., 2023; Indyk et al., 2019; Ailon et al., 2021), accelerating linear system
774   solvers (Luz et al., 2020; Khodak et al., 2024), mechanism design (Balcan et al., 2016; 2018c),
775   sketching algorithms (Bartlett et al., 2022), branch-and-cut algorithms for (mixed) integer linear
776   programming (Balcan et al., 2021b), among others.
777
778   Neural architecture search. Neural architecture search (NAS) captures a significant part of the
779   engineering challenge in deploying deep networks for a given application. While neural networks
780   successfully automate the tedious task of “feature engineering” associated with classical machine
781   learning techniques by automatically learning features from data, it requires a tedious search over
782   a large search space to come up with the best neural architecture for any new application domain.
783   Multiple different approaches with different search spaces have been proposed for effective NAS,
784
      including searching over the discrete topology of connections between the neural network nodes, and
      interpolation of activation functions. Due to intense recent interest in moving from hand-crafted to
785
      automatically searched architectures, several practically successful approaches have been developed
786
      including framing NAS as Bayesian optimization (Bergstra et al., 2013; Mendoza et al., 2016; White
787   et al., 2021), reinforcement learning (Zoph & Le, 2017; Baker et al., 2017), tree search (Negrinho
788   & Gordon, 2017; Elsken et al., 2017), gradient-based optimization (Liu et al., 2019), among others,
789   with progress measured over standard benchmarks (Dong & Yang, 2020; Mehta et al., 2022). Li et al.
790   (2021) introduce a geometry-aware mirror descent based approach to learn the network architecture
791   and weights simultaneously, within a single problem instance, yielding a practical algorithm but
792   without provable guarantees. Our formulation is closely related to tuning the interpolation parameter
793   for activation parameter in NAS approach of DARTS Liu et al. (2019), which can be viewed as a
794   multi-hyperparameter generalization of our setup. We establish the first learning guarantees for the
795
      simpler case of single hyperparameter tuning. Note that we are considering a simplified version of
      DARTS Liu et al. (2019), where we consider a linear interpolation hyperparameter of activation in
796
      each node, while DARTS uses a probabilistic interpolation instead.
797
798
      Graph-based learning. While several classical (Blum & Chawla, 2001; Zhu et al., 2003; Zhou
799
      et al., 2003; Zhu, 2005) as well as neural models (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Velic kovic et al., 2018;
800
      Wu et al., 2019; Gilmer et al., 2017) have been proposed for graph-based learning, the underlying
801   graph used to represent the data typically involves heuristically set graph parameters. The latter
802   approach is usually more effective in practice, but comes without formal learning guarantees. Our
803   work provides the first provable guarantees for tuning the graph kernel hyperparameter in graph
804   neural networks.

806   A detailed comparison to Hyperband (Li et al., 2018). Hyperband is one of the most notable
807   work in hyperparameter tuning. Specially, the paper provides a theoretical guarantees for the hyper-
808   parameter tuning process, but under strong assumptions. Here, we provide a detailed comparison
809   between guarantees presented in Hyperband and our results, and explain how Hyperband and our
      work are not competing but complementing each others.

810
            1. Hyperparameter configuration settings: Theoretical results (Theorem 1, Proposition 4)
811            in Hyperband assumes finitely many distinct arms and guarantees are with respect to the
812            best arm in that set. Even their infinite arm setting considers a distribution over the hyper-
813            parameter space from which n arms are sampled. It is assumed that n is large enough to
814            sample a good arm with high probability without actually showing that this holds for any
815            concrete hyperparameter loss landscape. It is not clear why this assumption will hold in
816            our cases. In sharp contrast, we seek optimality over the entire continuous hyperparame-
817            ter hyperparameter range for concrete loss functions which satisfy a piecewise polynomial
818            dual structure.
819         2. Guarantee settings: The notion of “sample complexity” in Hyperband is very different
820            from ours. Intuitively, their goal is to find the best hyperparameter from learning curves
821            over fewest training epochs, assuming the test loss converges to a fixed value for each hy-
822            perparameter after some epochs. By ruling out (successively halving) hyperparameters that
823
               are unlikely to be optimal early, they speed up the search process (by avoiding full training
               epochs for suboptimal hyperparameters). In contrast, we focus on model hyperparameters
824
               and assume the network can be trained to optimality for any value of the hyperparameter.
825
               We ignore the computational efficiency aspect and focus on the data (sample) efficiency
826            aspect which is not captured in Hyperband analysis.
827
828
            3. Learning settings: Hyperband assumes the problem instance is fixed, and aims to acceler-
               ate the random search of hyperparameter configuration for that problem instance with con-
829
               strained budgets (formulated as a pure-exploration non-stochastic infinite-armed bandit).
830
               In contrast, our results assume a problem distribution D (data-driven setting), and bounds
831            the sample complexity of learning a good hyperparameter for the problem distribution D.
832
833   Conclusion. The Hyperband paper and our work do not compete but complement each other, as
834   the two papers see the hyperparameter tuning problem from different perspectives and our results
835   cannot be compared to theirs.

       Our definition of dual utility function u∗x (α) = maxw∈W fx (α, w) implicitly assumes an ERM
       oracle. As discussed in Appendix B, this ERM oracle assumption makes the function u∗x (α) well-
1971
       defined and simplifies the analysis. However, one may argue that assuming the ERM oracle will
1972
       make the behavior of tuned hyperparameters much different, compared to when using common
1973   optimization in deep learning. The difference potentially stems from the fact that the global optimum
1974   found by ERM oracle might have a sharp curvature, compared to the local optima found by other
1975   optimization algorithms, which tend to have flat local curvature due to their implicit biases.
1976
