011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Low-rank adaptations (LoRAs) have revolutionized the finetuning of large foun-
014            dation models, enabling efficient adaptation even with limited computational re-
015            sources. The resulting proliferation of LoRAs presents exciting opportunities for
016
               applying machine learning techniques that take these low-rank weights themselves
               as inputs. In this paper, we investigate the potential of Learning on LoRAs (LoL),
017
               a paradigm where LoRA weights serve as input to machine learning models. For
018
               instance, an LoL model that takes in LoRA weights as inputs could predict the per-
019            formance of the finetuned model on downstream tasks, detect potentially harmful
020            finetunes, or even generate novel model edits without traditional training meth-
021            ods. We first identify the inherent parameter symmetries of low rank decom-
022            positions of weights, which differ significantly from the parameter symmetries
023            of standard neural networks. To efficiently process LoRA weights, we develop
024            several symmetry-aware invariant or equivariant LoL models, using tools such as
025            canonicalization, invariant featurization, and equivariant layers. We finetune thou-
026            sands of text-to-image diffusion models and language models to collect datasets
027
               of LoRAs. In numerical experiments on these datasets, we show that our LoL
               architectures are capable of processing low rank weight decompositions to predict
028
               CLIP score, finetuning data attributes, finetuning data membership, and accuracy
029
               on downstream tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Finetuning of pretrained models such as Large Language Models (Devlin et al., 2019; Brown et al.,
035
      2020; Dubey et al., 2024) and Diffusion models (Ho et al. (2020), Rombach et al. (2022)) for im-
036   proved performance on tasks such as generating images in a specific style or creating text for math-
037   ematical proofs has become an extremely common paradigm in deep learning. While full finetuning
038   of all weights effectively boosts model capabilities, it requires a large amount of memory and compu-
039   tation time. In recent years, Low Rank Adapation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), a method for finetuning
040   where a learnable low rank decomposition is added to each weight matrix (Wi 7→ Wi + Ui Vi⊤ ),
041   has been used as an alternative to other finetuning methods thanks to its increased efficiency. LoRA
042   finetuning and its variants have become widespread; there are now software packages (Mangrulkar
043   et al., 2022; Han & Han, 2023), paid services (Replicate, 2024), and online communities (Civitai,
044
      2024) in which countless LoRAs are trained and shared.
045   Given the ubiquity of LoRA weights, one can imagine treating them as a data type. As in recent
046   works in the emerging field of weight-space learning, we can process the weights of input neural
047   networks by using other neural networks (often termed metanetworks (Lim et al., 2023a), neural
048   functionals (Zhou et al., 2024b), or deep weight space networks (Navon et al., 2023a)).
049   In this work, we are the first to extensively study applications, theory and architectures for Learn-
050   ing on LoRAs (LoL), which encompasses any tasks where LoRA weights are the input to some
051   predictive model. An LoL model acting on a LoRA could predict various useful properties of the
052   underlying finetuned model. For instance, given LoRA weights of a finetuned model, an LoL model
053   could predict the downstream accuracy of the model on some task, predict properties of the (po-
      tentially private) training data used to finetune the model, or edit the LoRA to work in some other

055   weights, for some specific applications and a few types of models or learning algorithms.

099   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
100
101
      LoRA background and symmetries Hu et al. (2021) introduced the LoRA method, which is a
102   parameter-efficient method for finetuning (typically large) models (Mangrulkar et al., 2022; Han
103   et al., 2024). Consider a weight matrix W ∈ Rn×m of some pretrained neural network. Directly
104   finetuning W would require training nm parameters on additional data, which could be quite expen-
105   sive. LoRA instead trains low-rank matrices U ∈ Rn×r and V ∈ Rm×r so that the new finetuned
106   weight matrix is given by W + U V ⊤ . This only requires tuning (n + m)r parameters, which is
107   efficient as the rank r is taken to be significantly lower than n or m (for instance a rank of r = 8 can
      be sufficient to finetune a 7B-parameter language model with n = m = 4096 (Liu et al., 2024)).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      As mentioned in the introduction, for any invertible matrix R ∈ GL(r), W + (U R)(V R−⊤ )⊤ =
109   W +U V ⊤ , so the LoRA update given by (U R, V R−⊤ ) is functionally equivalent to the one given by
110   (U, V ). As a special case, when Q ∈ O(r) is orthogonal, (U Q, V Q) is also functionally equivalent.
111   Many variants of the original LoRA work have been proposed, and they often have comparable
112   symmetries that can be handled similarly in our framework (Mangrulkar et al., 2022; Han et al.,
113   2024); we discuss LoRA variants and their symmetries in Appendix E.
114
      Prior works have also studied continuous symmetries (Thomas et al., 2018; Bogatskiy et al., 2020;
115   Satorras et al., 2022; Lim et al., 2023b; Pearce-Crump, 2023; Lawrence & Harris, 2024) such as
116   rotation and scaling symmetries, especially for applications in the chemical and physical sciences.
117   However, they study different classes of symmetries, such as O(n), E(n), and SP(n), none of
118   which contain GL(n). To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to study GL(n) equivariant and
119   invariant architectures.
120
121   Weight-space learning and metanetworks Several neural network architectures have been de-
122   veloped that take in neural network weights as input (Unterthiner et al., 2020; Eilertsen et al., 2020;
123   Metz et al., 2022; Peebles et al., 2022; Navon et al., 2023a;b; Andreis et al., 2023; Shamsian et al.,
124   2024). In many tasks, equivariance or invariance to parameter transformations that leave the network
125   functionally unchanged has been found to be useful for empirical performance of weight-space net-
126
      works (Navon et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024b; Lim et al., 2023a; Kalogeropoulos et al., 2024;
      Tran et al., 2024). However, these works are not specialized for LoRA weight spaces, since they
127
      consider different symmetry groups: many such works focus only on discrete permutation symme-
128
      tries (Navon et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024b; Lim et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024a), or scaling
129   symmetries induced by nonlinearities (Kalogeropoulos et al., 2024; Tran et al., 2024). As covered
130   in the previous section, LoRA weights have general invertible symmetries, which include as special
131   cases certain permutations and scaling symmetries between U and V ⊤ . While LoRAs contain inner
132   permutation symmetries of the form U P T P V T = U V ⊤ , we emphasize that they generally do not
133   have outer permutation symmetries of the more recognizable form P1 U V T P2 , which would resem-
134   ble those of standard weight spaces (e.g. MLPs or CNNs) more. This is an important difference; for
135   instance, the outer permutation symmetries significantly harm linear merging of models trained from
136   scratch (Entezari et al., 2022; Ainsworth et al., 2023; Lim et al., 2024), whereas finetuned models
137
      can be merged well with simple methods (Wortsman et al., 2022; Ilharco et al., 2023).
138   Recently, two works have explored the weight space of LoRA finetuned models for certain tasks.
139   Salama et al. (2024) develop an attack that, given the LoRA weights of a finetuned model, predicts
140   the size of the dataset used to finetune the model. Inspired by correlations between finetuning
141   dataset size and singular value magnitudes, they define a very specific type of LoL model that takes
142   the singular values of dense, multiplied-out LoRA weights σ1 (Ui Vi⊤ ), . . . , σr (Ui Vi⊤ ) as input. In
143
      contrast, we study more tasks, consider the problem of general LoL model design, and develop more
      expressive and efficient LoL models in our paper. In another context, Dravid et al. (2024) study the
144
      LoRA weight space of finetuned personalized text-to-image diffusion models. They do not define
145
      LoL models, but instead study operations such as linear edits in the principal component space of
146   their rank-one LoRA weights.

      3.2    MLP-BASED M ETHODS WITH F EATURIZATION
190
191   MLP: Simple MLP on LoRA Weights The simplest of our models is a simple MLP that takes
192   the flattened LoRA weights as input: MLPθ (U1 , V1 , . . . , UL , VL ). This method is not invariant to
193   the LoRA parameter symmetries, but it is fully expressive, and it is efficient in that it does not need
194   to form the n-by-m dense matrices U V ⊤ .
195
196   MLP + O-Align: Alignment for Canonicalization One method for designing invariant or equiv-
197   ariant networks is to canonicalize the input by using a symmetry-invariant transformation to convert
198   it into a canonical form (Kaba et al., 2023; Dym et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). For example, to canon-
199   icalize a dataset of point clouds with respect to rotations, one might choose one specific point cloud
200   and then rotate all other point clouds in the dataset to it to maximize their relative similarity. After
201
      this alignment, any standard architecture can process the point clouds in an invariant manner with-
      out taking rotation into account. This kind of rotation alignment, known as O(r) canonicalization,
202
      admits a closed from solution and is significantly simpler than GL(r) canonicalization.
203
204   We canonicalize LoRA weights Ui , Vi into O(r)-invariant representatives Ui Qi , Vi Qi as follows.
205   First, we select template weights Ui , Vi of the same shape as Ui and Vi (in our experiments we
206   randomly select Ui and Vi from our training set of LoRAs). Then we align Ui , Vi to the templates
207
      by finding the orthogonal matrix Qi s that most closely match them in the Frobenius norm:
208                                                                  2                     2
                                   Qi = arg min ∥Ui Qi − Ui ∥F + ∥Vi Qi − Vi ∥F .                                        (3)

471   5.4   G ENERALIZATION TO U NSEEN R ANKS
472
473   In the previous sections, each LoL model was trained on LoRA weights of one fixed rank (e.g.
474   rank 4 for the LLMs, rank 32 for the Imagenette models). In practice, model developers can choose
475   different LoRA ranks for finetuning their models, even when they are finetuning the same base model
476
      for similar tasks. Thus, we may desire LoL models that are effective for inputs of different ranks.
      In this section, we explore an even more difficult problem — whether LoL models can generalize to
477
      ranks that are unseen during training time.
478
479   The MLP + Dense and GL-net models can directly take as input models of different ranks. We also
480   use MLP + SVD on inputs of different ranks, by parameterizing the model for inputs of rank r, then
481   truncating to top r singular values for inputs of rank greater than r, and zero-padding to length r for
482
      inputs of rank less than r.
483   In Figure 4, we train LoL models on inputs of rank 4, and then test performance on inputs of ranks
484   between 1 and 32. On the CelebA attribute prediction task, we see that MLP + Dense and GL-net
485   mostly generalize very well to different ranks that are unseen during training (except not as well for
      r = 1), while MLP + SVD does not generalize as well.

518         5.5                        RUNTIME AND S CALING TO L ARGE M ODELS
519
520        Previous weight-space models generally take in small networks as inputs, e.g. 5,000 parameters (Un-
521        terthiner et al., 2020), 80,000 parameters (Lim et al., 2023a), or sometimes up to 4 million param-
522        eters (Navon et al., 2023b). However, many of the most impactful neural networks have orders of
523        magnitude more parameters. Thus, here we consider the runtime and scalability of LoL models as
524
           we increase the model size. We will consider all of the language model sizes in the GPT-3 fam-
           ily (Brown et al., 2020), which range from 125M to 175B parameters. We assume that we finetune
525
           rank 4 LoRA weights for one attention parameter matrix for each layer.
526
527        In Figure 5, we show the time it takes for data preprocessing of 64 input networks, and forward
528        passes for 512 input networks (with batch size up to 64). Even at the largest scales, it only takes
529        at most seconds to preprocess and compute LoL model forward passes for each input LoRA. Every
530
           LoL model besides MLP + Dense scales well with the model size: forward passes barely take longer
           at larger model sizes, and data preprocessing is still limited to less than a second per input network.

534
535        In this work, we introduced the Learning on LoRAs framework, and investigated architectures,
536        theory, and applications for it. There are many potential applications and future directions for using
537        LoL models to process finetunes. For instance, future work could explore equivariant tasks, such as
538        those that involve editing or merging LoRAs. Additionally, future work could consider learning an
539        LoL model that can generalize across different model architectures, or different base models of the
           same architecture.
