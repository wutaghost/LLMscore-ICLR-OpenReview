009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Biological foundation models hold significant promise for deciphering complex biological
012            functions. However, evaluating their performance on functional tasks remains challenging
013            due to the lack of standardized benchmarks encompassing diverse sequences and func-
014            tions. Existing functional annotations are often scarce, biased, and susceptible to train-test
015            leakage, hindering robust evaluation. Furthermore, biological functions manifest at mul-
016            tiple scales, from individual residues to large genomic segments. To address these limi-
017
               tations, we introduce the Diverse Genomic Embedding Benchmark (DGEB), inspired by
               natural language embedding benchmarks. DGEB comprises six embedding tasks across
018
               18 expert curated datasets, spanning sequences from all domains of life and encompassing
019
               both nucleic acid and amino acid modalities. Notably, four datasets enable direct compar-
020            ison between models trained on different modalities. Benchmarking protein and genomic
021            language models (pLMs and gLMs) on DGEB reveals performance saturation with model
022            scaling on numerous tasks, especially on those with underrepresented sequences (e.g. Ar-
023            chaea). This highlights the limitations of existing modeling objectives and training data
024            distributions for capturing diverse biological functions. DGEB is available as an open-
025            source package with a public leaderboard at URLhiddenforanonymity.
026
027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Biological sequences encode complex molecular, evolutionary and biophysical information that govern bi-
030   ological function. Deep learning models have been proposed as promising methods for extracting biologi-
031   cally relevant functional information from sequence data. The promise of ”biological foundation models”
032
      enabling functional interpretation of sequences has resulted in many modeling efforts in protein (Rives et al.,
      2021; Madani et al., 2023; Elnaggar et al., 2022) and genomic (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023; Hwang et al., 2024;
033
      Nguyen et al., 2024) sequence modalities. While the field has seen major advances in AI-enabled structure
034
      prediction of protein sequences (Jumper et al., 2021; Baek et al., 2021), validated successes for AI-enabled
035   function prediction remain limited (Li et al., 2024). Slow progress in function prediction of sequences can
036   be attributed to the following main challenges:
037
038         1. Unlike for structural prediction tasks, objective measurements of function do not exist. Struc-
039            ture prediction tasks benefit from objective evaluation metrics based on quantifiable atomic dis-
040            tances (Mariani et al., 2013). However, biological function is inherently multifaceted and context-
041
               dependent, making direct quantitative assessment difficult.
042         2. Functional labels are sparse, biased, and prone to leakage. Labels are heavily biased towards
043            model organisms (e.g. Human), therefore performance on species-specific evaluation tasks are not
044            guaranteed to transfer to other organisms. Furthermore, functional annotations in databases are
045            rarely standardized in format, necessitating careful curation (e.g. unification of synonymous text
046            labels requires expert knowledge). Critically, all biological sequences are related through evolu-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
               tion. Without carefully designed parameters, train-test leakage can frequently occur, resulting in
048
               unreliable evaluation results (Fang, 2023).
049
050         3. Biological function takes place across diverse scales. Single nucleotide polymorphisms can have
051            phenotypic effects, while entire segments of genomes can be coordinated to carry out singular
052
               functions (e.g. biosynthetic gene clusters). These challenges innate to biological data have led to
               the lack of diverse benchmarks, resulting in independent evaluations of models on biased sets of
053
               ”in-house” tasks, preventing comprehensive and objective model comparisons.
054
055   The Diverse Genomic Embedding Benchmark (DGEB) is inspired by text embedding evaluation bench-
056   marks that have advanced the field of natural language modeling. DGEB aims to span diverse types of
057   downstream embedding tasks, scopes of function, and taxonomic lineages. DGEB consists of 18 datasets
058   covering 117 phyla across all three domains of life (Bacteria, Archaea and Eukarya). Similar to Massive
059
      Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) (Muennighoff et al., 2023), DGEB evaluates embeddings using six
      different embedding tasks: Classification, BiGene mining, Evolutionary Distance Similarity (EDS), Pair
060
      classification, Clustering, and Retrieval. DGEB focuses on evaluating the representations of higher-order
061
      functional and evolutionary relationships of genomic elements, and is designed to complement existing
062   benchmarks that focus on residue-level representations ((Notin et al., 2023) (Marin et al., 2024)).
063
064   We provide DGEB as an open source software, facilitating the evaluation of custom models, and enabling
065
      the addition and revision of datasets. Biological labels for function are limited and rely on careful curation
      by domain experts. DGEB provides a much-needed infrastructure for allowing experts to contribute new
066
      benchmarks and revise datasets upon acquisition of new knowledge. Community driven efforts to collect
067
      and standardize diverse datasets will move the emerging interdisciplinary field of Machine Learning and
068   Biology forward.

072   2.1 NATURAL LANGUAGE EMBEDDING BENCHMARKS
073   Embedding benchmarks (e.g. SentEval (Conneau & Kiela, 2018); BEIR (Thakur et al., 2021); MTEB
074   (Muennighoff et al., 2023)) in natural language processing (NLP) aim to evaluate how the structure of
075   word/sentence representations match the geometric structure of their semantics. For natural language, tasks
076   are typically either zero-shot or few-shot; examples of such tasks range from distance-based matching of
077
      translated texts to classifying tweets based on the labeled sentiment. NLP benchmarks highlight the need
      for holistic evaluation of models through a diverse set of tasks, as model performance can vary significantly
078
      across tasks and datasets.
079
080   2.2 B IOLOGICAL SEQUENCE AND LANGUAGE MODELS
081
      Biological sequence language models are unsupervised models trained on biological sequence data such
082   as proteins or genomic segments. Protein language models (pLMs) have been shown to encode features
083   for protein structure prediction (Lin et al., 2023), enzyme function prediction (Yu et al., 2023) and remote
084   homology search (Liu et al., 2024). More recently, genomic language models (gLMs) have been evaluated
085   on classification of various genomic motifs (e.g. regulatory elements, chromatin features, splicing) (Dalla-
086   Torre et al., 2023) and mutation fitness prediction (Nguyen et al., 2024).

159   3   T HE DGEB B ENCHMARK
160   3.1 D ESIGN CHOICES
161
      DGEB is built on the desiderata previously outlined by NLP benchmarks, in particular, MTEB. While bio-
162   logical sequences and their functional labels are fundamentally different from natural language, these design
163   choices allow for a scalable and flexible framework that can be expanded and optimized as the field matures.
164
165
           a) Diversity: We aim to cover sequences derived from phylogenetically diverse lineages of biology
              (Fig. 1). Existing functional benchmarks largely consist of human or E.coli K-12 sequences. Data
166
              imbalance in biology is a critical problem when training biological sequence language models
167
              (Ding & Steinhardt, 2024) and prevents the models from learning features transferable to underrep-
168           resented sequences. Benchmarks that only utilize sequences from highly overrepresented sequences
169           in the training set perpetuate this problem of data imbalance, hindering the progress towards AI-
170           enabled characterization, discovery and design of diverse biological sequences.
171
           b) Simplicity: DGEB provides a simple API that can be used with any custom model that encodes
172
              biological sequences into vectors.
173
174        c) Extensibility: Given the complexity of biological function, no single dataset can fully capture its
175           diversity, and existing functional annotations must be continuously refined and expanded. DGEB
176           supports simple extension of tasks and datasets. New or revised datasets can be uploaded to the
177
              HuggingFace Hub and new evaluation tasks can easily be added through GitHub pull requests.
178        d) Reproducibility: We version both the software and the datasets and include versioning in the
179           results, making the benchmark results fully reproducible.

      3.2 TASKS AND E VALUATION
182   DGEB consists of 18 datasets that are evaluated using one of the six task types (Fig. 2). The tasks and their
183   evaluation schemes are described below:
184   BiGene Mining BiGene Mining is inspired by Bitext Mining tasks in NLP, where the tasks typically
185   consist of matching translated sentences between two languages using cosine similarity. For BiGene Mining,
186   we curated functionally analogous sequences found in two phylogenetically distant taxa (e.g. Bacteria and
187   Archaea) or interacting pairs in sets of orthologous sequences. For each gene in the first set, the best match

                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


188
      in the second set is found using the cosine similarity. F1 serves as the primary metric for BacArch BiGene
189
      Mining, while recall@50 is used as the primary metric for ModBC BiGene Mining due to the difficulty of
190   the task; accuracy and precision are also reported.
191
192   Evolutionary Distance Similarity (EDS) This task evaluates how accurately models learn evolutionary
193
      relationships between sequences. We compute the correlation between pairwise embedding distances and
      their phylogenetic distances (sum of branch lengths connecting the two leaves of the calculated phylogenetic
194
      tree). Larger phylogenetic distance represents more evolutionary time since divergence. Pearson correlations
195
      are calculated and the top correlation score across three distance metrics (cosine, euclidean, and manhattan)
196   is reported as the primary metric.
197
198   Classification Classification tasks measure the model’s ability to map from embeddings to discrete func-
199   tional classes with few-shot supervision. For multiclass single-label classification, a logistic regression
200
      classifier is trained with up to 1000 iterations. For multiclass multi-label classification, a k-nearest neigh-
      bor (kNN) classifier is trained. Test performance on the test set is measured using F1 as the main metric;
201
      accuracy and average precision scores are also reported.
202
203   Pair Classification Pair classification tasks evaluate model understanding of functional relationships be-
204   tween pairs of sequences. Inputs are pairs of sequences, where labels are binary variables denoting the
205   existence of some particular functional relationship between the pair. Sequences are embedded and the
206
      distances between the pairs are calculated cosine similarity, dot product, euclidean distance and manhattan
      distance. The best binary threshold accuracy, average precision, F1, precision, and recall are calculated. The
207
      primary metric is the average precision score calculated using cosine similarity.
208
209   Clustering Clustering tasks evaluate zero-shot separability of embeddings over discrete classes. Inputs are
210   sets of sequences with labels, and a mini-batch k-means model is trained on their embeddings. The primary
211   metric is v-measure (Rosenberg & Hirschberg, 2007).
212   Retrieval Retrieval tasks evaluate how well a query embedding can retrieve functionally analogous se-
213   quences. Dataset consists of a corpus and queries, where the objective is to rank the embeddings in the
214   corpus by cosine similarity to each query sequence. Correct retrieval is determined by matching functional
215   labels. An example of a retrieval task is retrieving a bacterial homolog given an archaeal query sequence.
216   nDCG@k, MRR@k, MAP@k, precision@k and recall@k are calculated for k=5, 10, 50. MAP@5 is used
217   as the primary metric.
218
      3.3 DATASETS
219
220
      Datasets are divided into three categories: single-element, inter-element, and multi-element, where an el-
      ement refers to a protein/gene or noncoding RNA. Each element can be represented in amino acid and/or
221
      nucleotide sequence modalities. Some datasets support multiple sequence modalities (AA and NA), allow-
222
      ing direct comparison between protein and genomic language models. Statistics for each dataset are found in
223   Appendix B. All datasets are dereplicated at sequence identity thresholds of 70% using CD-hit (Huang et al.,
224   2010), to remove sampling biases. For tasks requiring train and test splits, datasets are split with a maximum
225   sequence identity of 10%. For tasks requiring multiple classes, we conduct class-balanced random sampling.
226   Detailed preprocessing steps are found in Appendix A.
227
      Single Element Datasets (SE) For SE datasets, each genomic element (protein/gene, noncoding RNA) is
228
      individually embedded, with an associated label. SE datasets in DGEB include:
229
230          • RNA Clustering: rRNA, sRNA, and tRNA features predicted using RFam (Kalvari et al., 2021)
231            genomes across diverse taxa. We cluster the sequence embeddings and assess how well they match
232
               the RNA class assignments.
233          • MopB Clustering: The dimethyl sulfoxide reductase (or MopB) family is a functionally diverse set
234            of enzymes found across Bacteria and Archaea. Sequences are sampled from Wells et al. (2023),


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


235
               where the sequence’s catalytic functions are assigned using phylogenetic analysis. We assess how
236
               well the embeddings cluster with their catalytic function.
237
238         • EC Classification: Enzyme commission (EC) numbers are assigned to protein sequences. For each
239           EC class, one sequence is randomly selected for testing, and four sequences from the corresponding
240
              class that have less than 10% sequence identity to each other and test sequence are selected for
              training.
241
242         • Convergent Evolution Classification: Examples of convergent evolution in proteins include en-
243           zymes that have different evolutionary history but have converged in the enzymatic reaction that
244           they confer. We identify such convergent enzymes by curating a set of enzymes that have no se-
245           quence similarity to any of the other sequences in the train set with the same EC designation.
246         • Archaeal Retrieval: Given the corpus of bacterial protein sequences in SWISS-PROT (Bairoch &
247           Apweiler, 2000), where the label is the corresponding text annotation, we query archaeal sequences
248           with string match annotations in the bacterial corpus. We retrieve k nearest neighbors in bacterial
249           corpus embedding space and look for matching labels to calculate the metrics@k.
250         • Eukaryotic Retrieval: Given the corpus of bacterial protein sequences in SWISS-PROT, we query
251           eukaryotic sequences with string match annotations in the bacterial corpus. Metrics are calculated
252           as above.
253
254
      Inter-element datasets (IE) Understanding biological function relies on understanding the evolutionary
      and functional relationships between sequences. For IE datasets, a label is assigned for each pair of genomic
255
      embeddings. IE datasets include:
256
257         • BacArch BiGene: Similar to matching translated sentences between two languages, we curated
258           functionally analogous pairs of sequences in a bacterial genome (Escherichia coli K-12) and an
259           archaeal genome (Sulfolobus acidocaldarius DSM 639 ASM1228v1).
260         • ModBC BiGene: Identifying interacting pairs of ModB and ModC from sets of orthologs is a
261           challenging task. ModB and ModC are interacting subunits of an ABC transporter. This dataset
262           consists of pairs of ModB and ModC that are found to be interacting in the same genome. The
263           goal is to correctly find the interacting ModC for each ModB given a set of orthologous ModC
264           sequences (found in different genomes).
265         • E.coli Operonic Pair Classification: Given a pair of adjacent proteins, the label is assigned based
266           on whether they belong to the same transcription unit in Escherichia coli K-12 substr. MG165.
267
            • Vibrio Operonic Pair Classification: Same as E.coli Operonic Pair Classification except with Vibrio
268
              cholerae O1 biovar El Tor str. N16961.
269
270         • Cyano Operonic Pair Classification: Same as Ecoli Operonic Pair Classification except with Syne-
271           chococcus elongatus PCC 7942.
272         • FeFeHydrogenase Phylogeny: Fe-Fe hydrogenases are complex enzymes that carry out important
273           metabolic functions across diverse organisms. They carry out divergent and specific functions
274           including H2 production, H2 sensing, H2 uptake, and CO2 reduction. Identifying the specific func-
275           tion of these hydrogenases often requires constructing a phylogenetic tree that reconstructs the
276           evolutionary history of the catalytic, or large, subunit. This dataset includes the phylogenetic dis-
277           tances (sum of tree branches connecting the leaves) calculated for all pairs of Fe-Fe hydrogenase
278           sequences.
279         • RpoB Bacterial Phylogeny: RpoB is a ribosomal protein conserved across bacteria and archaea.
280           They are essential single-copy genes and not frequently horizontally transferred, and therefore are
281           often used as phylogenetic marker genes. The RpoB gene is also significantly longer than the

312   4.1 M ODELS
313   We focus on evaluating self-supervised models pretrained on either amino acid (AA) or nucleic acid (NA)
314   sequences. These are ”foundation models” that are not fine-tuned for specific tasks, and we evaluate how
315   well the pre-trained embeddings capture various aspects of biological function. For AA models, we evaluate
316   the ESM2 (Lin et al., 2023) series, ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024) open model, the ProGen2 (Madani et al.,
317   2023) series, and the ProtTrans (Elnaggar et al., 2022) models. For NA models, we evaluate the DNABERT-
318
      2 (Zhou et al., 2024), Nucleotide Transformer (NT) (Dalla-Torre et al., 2023) series and the Evo (Nguyen
      et al., 2024) models. Notably, we include both masked language models (MLM) and causal language models
319
      (CLM) in our evaluation for both data modalities. To extract sequence-level embeddings, each model’s
320
      hidden layer is mean-pool across the sequence dimension, resulting in a fixed-size representation. Model
321   information is found in Appendix C. Additionally, we provide one-hot baselines for AA and NA sequences,
322   where the sequence is represented as one-hot vectors per position (Appendix H.

      4.2.3 D IRECT COMPARISON OF AMINO ACID AND NUCLEIC ACID MODELS
406
407
      While both AA and NA sequences can be used to represent coding sequences, little work has been conducted
      on directly comparing the quality of NA-based model representations against AA-based model representa-
408
      tions on the same task and data. DGEB includes four datasets that support both modalities as input for a
409
      given coding region of the sequence. For all such tasks, we find that NA sequence derived representations
410   perform poorly in capturing biological function and evolutionary relationships of coding sequences (Fig.
411   5). This suggests that AA sequences are a more compute efficient input modality for learning functional
412   information of coding sequences.

414   5   L IMITATIONS
415

416

417

421   confidence (due to the inherent uncertainty in reconstructing the ground-truth phylogeny). However, this
422   task provides a useful starting point for comparing model performance, and will be important for evaluating

457   6   C ONCLUSION
458
459   We developed DGEB to assess how well learned embeddings of biological sequences capture various aspects
460   of biological function. Our expert-curated datasets feature diverse sequences spanning all three domains and
461   major phyla in the tree of life. We benchmarked 20 models that are trained on either AA or NA sequences.
462
      Our results demonstrate that there is no single model that performs well across all tasks. Importantly, there
      are many tasks where performance does not scale with model size for existing models, particularly in tasks
463
      that feature poorly represented sequences (e.g. Archaeal genes), or tasks that assess functions that require
464
      large context lengths (e.g. biosynthetic gene cluster product class classification, operon prediction). For
465   many tasks, there is large headroom for improvement (e.g. ModBC matching, convergent enzyme classi-
466   fication). DGEB also supports direct comparison of models trained on AA and NA data modalities, and
467   our results show that NA models are yet to learn important aspects of biological function. We open-source
468   DGEB to facilitate community-driven dataset addition and revision. We hope that DGEB and the leader-
469   board allow transparent comparison of biological foundation models and drive the field forward.

       A PPENDIX H         O NE - HOT BASELINE DETAILS
1132
1133   We introduce a one-hot vector representation of biological sequences as a baseline method to compare
1134   model performance. This baseline represents each residue or nucleic acid as a one-hot vector. The one-
1135   hot representation is mean-pooled across the sequence dimension, like all evaluated models (Section 4.1).
1136   After mean-pooling, the one-hot baseline results in a representation equivalent to amino-acid composition for
1137   AA tasks and nucleic-acid composition for NA tasks. Previous work has shown that amino-acid composition
1138   is highly predictive of biological tasks such as transmembrane β-barrel protein identification (Garrow et al.,
1139   2005) and microbial growth conditions (Barnum et al., 2024). The baseline representation is evaluated in
1140   the same way as model embeddings, such as logistic regression for single-label classification tasks.
