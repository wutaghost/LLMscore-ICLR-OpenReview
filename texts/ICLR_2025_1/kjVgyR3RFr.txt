011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite the rapid progress and outstanding performance of Large Vision-Language
014            Models (LVLMs) in recent years, LVLMs have been plagued by the issue of hal-
015            lucination, i.e., LVLMs tend to generate responses that are inconsistent with the
016
               corresponding visual inputs. To evaluate the degree of hallucination in LVLMs,
               previous works have proposed a series of benchmarks featuring different types
017
               of tasks and evaluation metrics. However, we find that the quality of the exist-
018
               ing hallucination benchmarks varies, with some suffering from problems, e.g.,
019            inconsistent evaluation results under repeated tests, and misalignment with hu-
020            man evaluation. To this end, we propose a Hallucination benchmark Quality
021            Measurement framework (HQM), which leverages various indicators to assess the
022            reliability and validity of existing hallucination benchmarks separately. Specifi-
023            cally, for reliability we explore test-retest reliability and parallel-forms reliability,
024            while for validity we examine criterion validity and coverage of hallucination types.
025            Furthermore, we construct a High-Quality Hallucination Benchmark (HQH) for
026            LVLMs, which demonstrates superior reliability and validity under our HQM
027
               framework. We conduct an extensive evaluation of over 10 representative LVLMs,
               including GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro, to provide an in-depth analysis of the
028
               hallucination issues in existing models. Our benchmark is publicly available at
029
               https://github.com/HQHBench/HQHBench.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, the rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) has led to a great revolution in the
035
      field of artificial intelligence. Building on the success of LLMs, Large Vision-Language Models
036   (LVLMs), sometimes referred to as Large Multimodal Models (LMMs), have made remarkable
037   advancements. These models usually use LLMs as the foundational architecture and align features
038   from other modalities accordingly, demonstrating exceptional capabilities across various multimodal
039   tasks, such as image captioning and visual question answering (VQA). Despite their outstanding
040   performance, LVLMs are significantly plagued by the issue of hallucination, which could lead to
041   harmful consequences, particularly when users without sufficient domain knowledge over-rely on the
042   models.
043   The original concept of hallucination is introduced for LLMs and categorized into factuality hal-
044   lucination and faithfulness hallucination (Huang et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Rawte et al., 2023).
045   Factuality hallucination occurs when the generated content is inconsistent with real-world facts,
046   while faithfulness hallucination refers to the discrepancy between the generated content and the
047   context provided by the input instruction or output content itself. Compared to LLMs, hallucination
048   in LVLMs is defined as inconsistency of the generated textual content and the visual input (Bai et al.,
049   2024; Liu et al., 2024), emphasizing the multimodal inconsistency.
050   To assess the degree of hallucination in LVLMs, previous studies have proposed a series of hallucina-
051   tion benchmarks, supporting evaluation of closed-ended tasks and open-ended tasks. Closed-ended
052   tasks include yes-or-no questions and multiple-choice questions, while open-ended tasks contain
053   image captioning and free-form VQA. However, we find that some benchmarks suffer from quality
      issues, such as inconsistent evaluation results under repeated tests, misalignment with human eval-

081   it is necessary to measure the quality of existing hallucination benchmarks.
082
      Inspired by psychometrics (Furr, 2021; Raykov & Marcoulides, 2011; Rust & Golombok, 2014),
083
      we propose a framework of quality measurement for hallucination benchmarks from the perspective
084
      of reliability and validity. An overview of our quality measurement framework is illustrated in
085   Figure 1. For reliability, we assess test-retest reliability and parallel-forms reliability, examining
086   whether the evaluation results are consistent under repeated tests and parallel tests. For validity, we
087   measure criterion validity, i.e., whether the evaluation results are aligned with human evaluation,
088   and the coverage of hallucination types. Through detailed analysis, we summarize the strengths and
089   limitations of existing benchmarks as follows. Firstly, we argue that benchmarks of closed-ended
090   tasks offer efficient automated evaluation but exhibit certain deficiencies in reliability since LVLMs
091   are susceptible to response bias (Tjuatja et al., 2023) introduced by task settings, such as acquiescence
092   bias and dissent bias in yes-or-no questions (Yifan Li & Wen, 2023; Fu et al., 2023), position bias in
093
      multiple-choice questions (Zheng et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023). Such bias manifests as the tendency to
      answer "yes" or "no" to yes-or-no questions and select a specific option in multiple-choice questions.
094
      In contrast, benchmarks of open-ended tasks avoid response bias by allowing more freedom in
095
      responses, but they primarily suffer from validity issues, with more severe misalignment between
096   their evaluation and human evaluation.
097
098   Considering the balance between reliability and validity, we opt to build our hallucination benchmark
099
      on open-ended tasks, specifically free-form VQA. We collect images from the validation set of
      Visual Genome (Krishna et al., 2017) dataset and design image-instruction pairs covering compre-
100
      hensive types of hallucination, including attribute, action, counting, environment, (spatial) relation,
101
      comparison, OCR, and existence. To ensure the data quality, we conduct a manual review of all
102   image-instruction pairs and remove low-quality samples. As for metric, existing free-form VQA
103   benchmarks use hallucination score, which leverages external LLMs like GPT (OpenAI, 2022) to
104   assign a specific score to the hallucination level of model response. We think such scoring-based
105   metrics are too difficult for current LLMs, resulting in inconsistent scores across repeated or parallel
106   tests, as well as inaccurate scores that are misaligned with human evaluation. Instead, we employ a
107   simplified process: given detailed image information, the model only needs to determine whether
      the response is hallucinated. Thus, the hallucination rate can be computed as the evaluation metric.

      2.1   L ARGE V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
125   Built on the success of LLMs, LVLMs have rapidly developed, demonstrating strong capabilities.
126   Researchers have constructed a series of advanced LVLMs using various methods. For example,
127   BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023b) adopts a lightweight Q-Former architecture and uses cross-attention mecha-
128   nisms to align textual and visual representations. InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2024) incorporates textual
129   instructions into the Q-Former, enhancing the model performance. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b) is the
130   first to introduce instruction tuning techniques to the multimodal field, forming the most mature open-
131   source multimodal model. The emergence of other open-source models such as MiniGPT-4 (Zhu
132   et al., 2023), Otter (Li et al., 2023a), Shikra (Chen et al., 2023), and Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023) have
133
      further propelled the development of LVLMs. Additionally, many powerful closed-source LVLMs,
      including Gemini-1.5-Pro (Team et al., 2023) and GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), have publicly released
134
      their APIs, promoting the development of downstream applications. In this paper, we use these
135
      open-source LVLMs as test models under our HQM framework, and benchmark them along with
136   several closed-source models on our HQH.

      2.2   H ALLUCINATION B ENCHMARKS FOR LVLM S
139
140   In the context of LVLMs, hallucination refers to the inconsistency of the generated textual content
141   and the visual input (Bai et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). To evaluate the degree of hallucination
142   in LVLMs, various hallucination benchmarks have been proposed, which can be divided into two
143   categories, closed-ended tasks and open-ended tasks. For closed-ended tasks, previous works design
144   yes-or-no questions or multiple-choice questions (Lu et al., 2023), using accuracy as evaluation
145
      metric. For example, POPE (Yifan Li & Wen, 2023) constructs yes-or-no questions based on dif-
      ferent polling strategies to detect whether the responses contain non-existent objects. Following
146
      works like AMBER (Wang et al., 2023a) extend yes-or-no questions to other types of hallucination.
147
      HallusionBench (Guan et al., 2023) manually constructs yes-or-no pairs with an innovative struc-
148   ture by human experts, further measuring more fine-grained hallucination. For open-ended tasks,
149   existing works often employ image captioning or free-form VQA. One kind of evaluation metric
150   is CHAIR (Rohrbach et al., 2018) and its variants (Jing et al., 2023; Ben-Kish et al., 2024), which
151   calculates the proportion of hallucinated objects to all objects mentioned in the response and is mostly
152   used for image captioning. For instance, OpenCHAIR (Ben-Kish et al., 2024) leverages OCH, which
153   expands CHAIR to an open vocabulary, to evaluate the hallucination in image descriptions. Another
154   kind of metric hallucination score utilizes external LLMs like GPT (OpenAI, 2022) to grade the
155   degree of hallucination and give exact scores to the generated responses, which is relatively more
156
      popular in free-form VQA benchmarks like MMHal (Sun et al., 2023) and GAVIE (Liu et al., 2023a).
      We select several representative benchmarks to conduct quality measurement.

160
161   Inspired by psychometrics (Rust & Golombok, 2014; Raykov & Marcoulides, 2011; Furr, 2021),
      we propose a hallucination benchmark quality evaluation framework HQM. Psychometrics, i.e., the

      science of psychological assessment, has been long utilized to measure the psychological construct
163   of human (Rust & Golombok, 2014). To some degree, AI benchmarks for evaluating model capabil-
164   ities have similarities with psychological tests used to assess human psychological constructs like
165   intelligence. Therefore, the integration of psychometrics into AI evaluation has received increasing
166   attention (Wang et al., 2023b; Pellert et al., 2023). Our framework is guided by the systematic test
167   quality assessment approaches in psychometrics, focusing on both the reliability and validity of

170   3.1   R ELIABILITY
171
172   Reliability refers to the consistency or stability of a test (Rust & Golombok, 2014; Wang et al., 2023b).
173   We leverage two reliability indicators, test-retest reliability and parallel-forms reliability (Rust &
174   Golombok, 2014), to quantify the reliability of a hallucination benchmark.
175   Test-retest Reliability. We use test-retest reliability to reflect the consistency of evaluation results
176   under repeated tests, also known as replicability. Specifically, for each benchmark, we conduct two
177   repeated tests on the same set of test models with different random seeds. The Pearson correlation
178   coefficient (Galton, 1877) between the two sets of results is calculated as the test-retest reliability:
179
180
                                                                        Cov(S, Sretest )
181                        Test-retest Reliability = r(S, Sretest ) =                    ,                 (1)
182                                                                       σS σSretest
183
184   where S represents the original evaluation results, Sretest represents the retest results, Cov denotes
185
      covariance, and σ denotes standard deviation. We expect the two sets of results to be at a consistent
      level, without significant fluctuations. Higher test-retest reliability indicates that the benchmark is
186
      less affected by random factors introduced during the evaluation process, such as the random seed
187
      used in the test.
188
189   Parallel-forms Reliability. Parallel-forms reliability is utilized to illustrate the consistency of
190   evaluation results across parallel tests, which is somewhat analogous to robustness. For each
191
      benchmark, we generate its parallel version by constructing equivalent prompts. In detail, yes-or-no
      questions are rewritten into questions with opposite ground truth answers, the order of options
192
      in multiple-choice questions is randomly shuffled, and instructions for captioning and free-form
193
      VQA are rephrased into synonymous expressions. Examples of the rewritten prompts are shown in
194   Appendix B. Similar to test-retest reliability, we test the two parallel-forms benchmarks on the same
195   models and calculate their Pearson correlation coefficient to obtain the parallel-forms reliability:
196
197

201   where S represents the original evaluation results and Sparallel represents the results of the parallel
202   form. Higher parallel-forms reliability suggests that the benchmark is less influenced by the response
203   bias introduced by specific task settings.

205   3.2   VALIDITY
206
207   Validity indicates how well a test measures what it is designed to measure (Rust & Golombok, 2014).
208   To assess the validity of a hallucination benchmark, we leverage the criterion validity (Whitely, 1983)
209   and the coverage of hallucination types.
210   Criterion Validity. Criterion validity measures the extent to which evaluation results correlate
211   with a criterion result. Although automated metrics have advantages in scalability, we consider the
212   results of manual evaluation to be more reliable and suitable as a criterion reference. For efficiency,
213   we randomly sample 100 image-instruction pairs from each benchmark and manually review the
214   responses of all models, obtaining the human evaluation results as the criterion. More details about
215   human evaluation can be found in Appendix C. Criterion validity is quantified via the correlation
      between the automated benchmark evaluation results and human evaluation results:

238   Coverage of Hallucination Types. We examine whether a hallucination benchmark comprehensively

248   3.3      Q UALITY M EASUREMENT
249
250   We select 6 representative publicly available hallucination benchmarks, POPE (Yifan Li & Wen,
251   2023), AMBER (including AMBER-d and AMBER-g) (Wang et al., 2023a), HallusionBench (Guan
252   et al., 2023), OpenCHAIR (Ben-Kish et al., 2024), MMHal (Sun et al., 2023), and GAVIE (Liu

254
      Regarding the evaluation metrics, POPE, AMBER-d, and HallusionBench use accuracy on Yes-or-No

255
      image descriptions based on all mentioned objects; OpenCHAIR uses OCH, a variant of CHAIR,
256
      which expands CHAIR to support an open vocabulary; MMHal and GAVIE adopt hallucination score,
257   leveraging GPT to assess the degree of hallucination in model responses. Due to cost considerations,
258   all benchmarks requiring GPT assistance are conducted using GPT-3.5.

      different benchmarks. The performance and rankings of models vary from one benchmark to another,

301   ings due to the response bias of models towards specific task settings, including acquiescence bias,
302   dissent bias, and position bias. In the evaluation of POPE, AMBER-d and HallusionBench, we
303   calculate the yes-ratio of each model, which denotes the proportion of model responses answering
304   "yes" to the given yes-or-no questions. As shown in Table 2, we find that MiniGPT4-LLaMa-2
305   and Otter suffer from significant acquiescence bias, i.e., the tendency to answer "yes", with much
306   higher yes-ratios than ground truth. Meanwhile, MiniGPT4-Vicuna-7B and Qwen-VL encounter
307   great dissent bias, i.e., the tendency to answer "no", exhibiting apparently lower yes-ratios. Such bias
308   affects the reliability of these benchmarks, making it unclear whether the low accuracy is caused by
309
      hallucination or the inherent response bias of models themselves. In open-ended benchmarks, the
      parallel-forms reliability of AMBER-g and OpenCHAIR, which are built on image captioning, is
310
      also unsatisfactory. This is because, in captioning tasks, the response lengths of certain models are
311

312   "Describe the image." in original test and "Provide a description of the image." in parallel test, the
313   average response lengths of some models fluctuates greatly. Empirically, the longer the response,
314   the higher the likelihood of generating hallucination. Therefore, the differences in response length
315   undermine the stability of the evaluation results.
316

317

321   evaluation is that the model provides the correct answer, but there exists hallucination in the analysis
322   which is contradictory to the answer. Meanwhile, the evaluation of open-ended tasks encounters more
323   significant criterion validity issues. In image captioning benchmarks AMBER-g and OpenCHAIR,

366
367   Considering that closed-ended settings inevitably introduce response bias to some models as illustrated
368   in Section 3.3, our HQH is built on open-ended tasks. Since the evaluation results of captioning
369   tasks fluctuate significantly with different prompts, leading to reliability issues, we opt to conduct our
370   evaluation through free-form VQA.
371

372   instruction patterns that cover various types of hallucination, including attribute, action, counting,
373   environment, (spatial) relation, comparison, OCR, and existence—8 types in total. Ground truth
374   answers are automatically extracted from the image annotations in Visual Genome based on a set of
375   rules tailored to each hallucination type, generating candidate image-instruction pairs. To address
376   potential annotation noise and ensure data quality, we conduct a manual review of all candidate
377   image-instruction pairs, removing low-quality samples. Specifically, we filter out instances where

470   To minimize the impact of these inherent biases in GPT, we refine the GPT-assisted evaluation process

479   By extracting the binary hallucination judgments from GPT’s responses, we calculate the hallucination
480   rate as the evaluation metric.

503   5   D ISCUSSION
504
      Benchmark Quality. There are already studies raising serious concerns about the reliability and
505
      validity of current AI benchmarks (Mitchell, 2023). Reliable and valid benchmarks are the foundation
506
      of trustworthy evaluation for AI models. To the best of our knowledge, HQM is the first framework
507   aimed at measuring the quality of AI benchmarks. While it is developed for hallucination benchmarks,
508   the principles are general and can be extended to other benchmarks with slight modifications. We
509   believe this framework will help to discover potential reliability and validity issues in existing
510   benchmarks and inspire their improvement.
511
      AI & Psychometrics. Psychometrics is the science of how to maximize the quality of psychological
512
      assessments (Rust & Golombok, 2014). As mentioned in Section 3, psychological tests in psycho-
513   metrics share commonalities with AI evaluation benchmarks. The integration of psychometrics
514   into AI may bring new opportunities for AI evaluation (Wang et al., 2023b; Pellert et al., 2023),
515   such as the possibility of using construct-oriented paradigms from psychometrics to evaluate the
516   latent constructs of general AI (Wang et al., 2023b). Our work focuses on a different aspect, mainly
517   adapting the quality measurement methods of psychological tests to AI benchmarks. There are many
518   other potential combinations that deserve further exploration.
519   Hallucination Mitigation. Our HQH enables fine-grained hallucination analysis. Specifically, while
520   many models show progress in alleviating some widely discussed hallucination, such us attribute,
521   other overlooked hallucination like comparison remain critical issues. Therefore, we suggest targeted
522   optimization during model training, e.g., incorporating more data for these specific tasks, to mitigate
523   hallucination issues and enhance overall model robustness.
524
      Limitations. Our proposed HQM framework represents an initial attempt to assess benchmark quality,
525   focusing on reliability and validity, without yet considering other dimensions that may influence
526   benchmark quality. We aim to explore more extensive measures to refine the framework in the future.
527   Additionally, our HQH benchmark only focuses on improving existing free-form VQA benchmarks,
528   while improvements to other benchmarks are equally worth exploring. We plan to further integrate
529   these improved benchmarks to create a more comprehensive benchmark, as it is an effective way to
530   merge community effort.
531
532   6   C ONCLUSION
533
534   We introduce a quality measurement framework for hallucination benchmarks (HQM), utilizing
535   various indicators to assess their reliability and validity. Under our proposed HQM framework,
536
      we construct a new high-quality hallucination benchmark (HQH), which is more reliable, valid,
      and comprehensive. An extensive evaluation of over 10 representative LVLMs, including GPT-
537
      4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro, is conducted on our HQH, illustrating that there is still substantial room
538
      for improvement. We anticipate that our research will inspire future work in the field of LVLM
539   hallucination.
