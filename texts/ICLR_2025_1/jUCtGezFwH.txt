011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Online Continual Learning (OCL) enables machine learning models to adapt to
014            sequential data streams in real time, especially when only a small amount of data
015
               is available. However, applying OCL to physiological data such as electroen-
               cephalography (EEG) and electrocardiography (ECG) is often complicated by
016
               inter-subject variability, which can lead to catastrophic forgetting and performance
017
               degradation. Existing OCL methods are currently unable to effectively address
018            this challenge, leading to difficulties in retaining previously learned knowledge
019            while adapting to new data. This paper presents Online Prototype Weighted Ag-
020            gregation (OPWA), a novel method specifically designed to address the problem
021            of catastrophic forgetting in the presence of inter-subject variability through the
022            use of prototypical networks. OPWA facilitates the retention of knowledge from
023            past subjects while adapting to new data streams. The OPWA method uses an
024            innovative prototype aggregation mechanism that fuses intra-class prototypes into
025            generalized representations by accounting for both within-class and inter-class
026
               variation between subjects. Extensive experiments show that OPWA consistently
               outperforms existing OCL methods in terms of fast adaptation and mitigation of
027
               catastrophic forgetting on different physiological datasets with different modali-
028
               ties, and provides a robust solution for learning on sequential data streams.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION

033   Intelligent machines equipped with artificial intelligence (AI) are increasingly becoming indispens-
034   able partners for humans in various sensitive and time-critical domains, ranging from search and res-
035   cue missions to space exploration Layton (2021); El Alami et al. (2023). The dynamics of real-world
036   environments present complex tasks that can significantly increase the human workload and impair
037   decision-making capabilities. To optimize human-machine collaboration Shively et al. (2018), AI
038   systems need to understand human preferences so that they can adapt their behaviour to the mental
039
      workload of their human counterparts. Non-invasive technologies Gu et al. (2021), such as wearable
      devices, offer a promising approach to collect implicit feedback with minimal distraction.
040
041   Non-invasive technologies capture physiological signals such as electroencephalography (EEG) and
042   electrocardiography (ECG), which offer valuable insights into human mental workload and enable
043   real-time monitoring of stress levels. However, classical machine learning (ML) requires large,
044   annotated datasets for effective stress prediction, which are often not available in the real world
045
      for various reasons. For instance, publicly available datasets are usually collected in specific en-
      vironments using specialized emotion elicitation techniques which often cannot be generalized to
046
      all situations Khare et al. (2024). In addition, these datasets usually represent a limited number of
047
      classes Duan et al. (2024b). Dynamic and time-critical environments lead to different levels of stress
048   in individuals, which can vary considerably from one environment to another. This limitation makes
049   it necessary to train models online on continuous data streams, a process known as Online Con-
050   tinual Learning (OCL). Unlike continual learning, which requires multiple passes over data, OCL
051   processes data incrementally in real-time, observing each sample or mini-batch only once to adapt
052   to dynamic scenarios Soutif-Cormerais et al. (2023). Data arrives incrementally in OCL, which can
053   be either domain incremental (where the input distribution changes over time) or class incremental
      (where new classes are introduced over time). Existing applications of physiological signals can

089   In this work, we propose a robust OCL approach to mitigate the problem of catastrophic forgetting
090
      in the presence of inter-subject variability. Inspired by prototypical networks Snell et al. (2017), we
      develop Online Prototype Weighted Aggregation (OPWA), a novel approach to ensure the retention
091
      of previous knowledge while adapting the model to the new subjects. Existing approaches Wei et al.
092
      (2023); De Lange & Tuytelaars (2021) that incorporate prototypical loss in OCL use momentum-
093   based strategies to update class prototypes based on the mean embeddings of the current data stream.
094   However, such naive prototype updates often result in catastrophic forgetting, as inter-subject vari-
095   ability in OCL causes prototypes to drift away from representations of past data. To address this, our
096   approach introduces intra-class prototype weighting, a key innovation that goes beyond traditional
097   momentum-based updates. While methods like CoPE De Lange & Tuytelaars (2021) incrementally
098   adapt prototypes to new data streams, they fail to account for distributional shifts across subjects,
099   increasing the risk of forgetting. In contrast, our method employs a dynamic weighted aggregation
100   scheme that adapts to these shifts, ensuring more robust and stable prototype representations. This
101
      dynamic weighting, determined by the distances between prototypes, prioritizes prototypes that are
      more stable and reflect the true data distribution. To the best of our knowledge, this weighted ag-
102
      gregation strategy has not been previously employed in the context of OCL. Our contributions are
103
      summarized as follows: (1) We propose OPWA to address the challenge of inter-subject variability
104   in OCL, introducing a robust prototype aggregation mechanism that synthesizes global prototypes
105   from cross-subject prototypes. These global prototypes act as generalized class anchors that serve
106   as the centroids of embedding vectors and improve the prototypical loss for OCL.(2) Our approach

      2    R ELATED W ORK

118   Continual learning frameworks are designed to learn from data streams that may undergo signifi-
119   cant distribution shifts. Recently, several approaches have been introduced to tackle catastrophic
120   forgetting caused by these shifts, which can be categorized into three main types.
121
      Regularization-based approaches apply regularization terms to control the process of parameter up-
122   dates and can be further categorized into weight regularization Kirkpatrick et al. (2016); Ritter et al.
123   (2018); Schwarz et al. (2018) and function regularization Dhar et al. (2018); Hung et al. (2019); Qin
124   et al. (2021); Miao et al. (2022). The weight regularization selectively controls the changes in net-
125   work parameters. Alternatively, function regularization approaches focus on the intermediate or final
126   outputs of the prediction function. Replay-based methods have also been proposed that store a lim-
127   ited number of samples from previously observed distributions and use them for KD or joint training
128   with current data. Replay-based methods include GEM Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017), iCARL Re-
129   buffi et al. (2017), reservoir sampling Vitter (1985) and variants of reservoir sampling Aljundi et al.
130
      (2019a;b). Most of recent works in OCL have focused on computer vision (CV) tasks, particularly
      on image classification. However, when applied to physiological datasets, these approaches may not
131
      perform well, as these datasets present unique challenges Nakisa et al. (2018). For example, OnPro
132
      Wei et al. (2023) introduced prototype equilibrium to prevent shortcut learning, a common problem
133   in image data where models can learn background features to discriminate between classes.
134
135   Very few studies specifically address the classification of physiological data Duan et al. (2024b;a). A
136
      recent study Duan et al. (2024b) proposed AMBM, a meta-learning approach to tackle catastrophic
      forgetting and facilitate rapid adaptation in the presence of subject shifts in EEG signals. This
137
      method is based on the Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) Finn et al. (2017). In the base
138
      loop, a contrastive loss Khosla et al. (2020) is applied to the current data streams for fast adaptation.
139   During the meta-phase, memory replay using reservoir sampling Vitter (1985) is used for rehearsal,
140   with an adaptive learning rate to mitigate forgetting. However, a significant drawback of this method
141   is the overfitting of the current data stream, as the contrastive loss is explicitly applied to the current
142   data stream for several steps in the inner loop, leading to forgetting of the knowledge gained on
143   previous subjects. In addition, AMBM is computationally intensive due to its bilevel optimization
144   that separates adaptation and generalization processes, resulting in higher computational demands.
145   In contrast, our approach efficiently preserves past knowledge while adapting to the new data stream,
146   treating both objectives together in a single process. We incorporate a prototypical loss that utilizes
147
      enhanced prototypes derived from an effective prototypes aggregation mechanism. Another work
      Duan et al. (2024a) proposes to maintain a balanced representation across subjects by considering
148
      data volume and informativeness, using clustering to detect subject shifts, and selectively replacing
149
      less informative or overrepresented samples in the memory buffer. However, the use of gradient
150   norms to measure informativeness may be less effective in dealing with noisy or non-stationary
151   data, which could lead to suboptimal memory representation in highly dynamic scenarios.
152
153
      Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) Kirkpatrick et al. (2016) is a domain adaptation technique
      that helps prevent catastrophic forgetting by penalizing changes to important parameters using the
154
      Fisher Information Matrix (FIM). However, its effectiveness depends on the amount of data, as the
155
      Fisher information requires a sufficient amount of data for accurate estimation, and it incurs high
156   computational costs as the FIM needs to be updated frequently, which makes it impractical in OCL
157   settings. CLUDA Ozyurt et al. (2022) is a contrastive learning based domain adaptation approach
158   designed for time series data which aligns contextual representations between source and target
159   domains through adversarial training and contrastive learning. Although it is effective for domain
160   adaptation tasks, its application in OCL is challenging because there is no static source domain.
161   Although the memory buffer could serve as a pseudo-source domain, its dynamic and limited nature
      makes it difficult to achieve robust generalization and retain knowledge across sequential subjects.

      The most relevant works to ours are OnPro Wei et al. (2023) and CoPE De Lange & Tuytelaars
163   (2021), as they also use prototypes, but in the context of CIL. OnPro introduced prototype equilib-
164   rium to prevent shortcut learning in image data by applying contrastive loss between the prototypes
165   of the original data and those of the augmented views. On the other hand, CoPE employs proto-
166   type evolution, which allows the prototypes to evolve naturally using a momentum-based approach
167   but does not consider subject shifts, leaving a potential bias towards new subjects. In contrast, our
168   method specifically addresses subject shifts by deriving generalized prototypes that represent the
169   entire data distribution, ensuring generalizability in the presence of cross-subject variability.

171   3     M ETHOD
172
173   In this section, we present OPWA, a novel approach that effectively addresses the challenge of
174   inter-subject variability in OCL. The core strength of our method lies in the integration of prototype
175   aggregation with normalized weights, ensuring that subject-specific prototypes contribute appro-
176   priately to the overall class representation without being biased by any single prototype. While
177   momentum-based methods, such as CoPE De Lange & Tuytelaars (2021), gradually update proto-
178   types to accommodate new data streams, they often drift away from representations of past subjects
179   over time, increasing the risk of forgetting. In contrast, our approach incorporates a weighted ag-
180
      gregation scheme that adapts to distributional shifts between subjects and ensures a more robust
      representation of class prototypes. This dynamic weighting, determined by the distances between
181
      prototypes, allows us to prioritize those that are more stable and reflect the true data distribution.
182
      OPWA proposed method provides a refined approach for managing prototypes, especially in scenar-
183   ios with significant subject shifts, and represents a substantial advancement over existing techniques.

      4   E XPERIMENTS

327   Dataset: We used four publicly available datasets to evaluate our proposed method, across differ-
328   ent subjects, conditions and data collection protocols. The datasets include AMIGOS Correa et al.
329   (2017), DEAP Koelstra et al. (2012), PPB-EMO Li et al. (2022) and BCI-IV-2a Tangermann et al.
330   (2012).While BCI-IV-2a focuses on motor imagery classification(left hand, right hand, tongue and
331   both feet), the other datasets focus on emotion classification based on valence and arousal. These
332   data sets capture various physiological signals from subjects while watching videos of specific du-
333   ration (seconds) that are designed to elicit specific emotional responses characterized by valence
334   (high/low) and arousal (high/low). Each data set consists of different channels and is recorded at
335
      different sampling rates. For example, the pre-processed AMIGOS data is downsampled to 128
      Hz. We create 5-second segments for each trial with overlap between consecutive segments. Data
336
      are annotated on the basis of subjects’ ratings of valence and arousal on a defined scale. More de-
337
      tails on the data sets can be found in Appendix A.1. Our evaluation considered both EEG and ECG
338   modalities with a four-class classification task to categorize valence and arousal. For example, in the
339   AMIGOS dataset, subjects rate their emotional state on scales of 0 to 9 for both valence and arousal.
340   Ratings above 5.5 indicate high valence or high arousal, while ratings below 4.5 indicate low valence
341   or low arousal. Intermediate ratings are categorized as neutral emotions. Using this methodology,
342   we can categorize emotions into four distinct classes: High Valence Low Arousal (HVLA), High Va-
343   lence High Arousal (HVHA), Low Valence High Arousal (LVHA), and Low Valence Low Arousal
344   (LVLA). Neutral emotions are excluded from the dataset.
345   Baselines: The baseline methods included in this study fall into four categories: (1) Offline Learn-
346   ing: demonstrates the upper bound performance of the model reached through joint learning, where
347   the data of all subjects is available simultaneously and the model can learn offline. (2) Domain
348   Adaptation: We employed EWC Kirkpatrick et al. (2016) as an additional baseline. However, since
349   EWC cannot be directly applied in an OCL setting, we adapted it by using the memory buffer as a
350   pseudo-source domain. Specifically, the model is trained on the memory buffer before adapting to
351   the next subject. Moreover, we incorporated CLUDA Ozyurt et al. (2022) as an additional baseline.
352
      CLUDA represents domain adaptation approach that relies on adversarial training and contrastive
      learning to align contextual representations across source and target domains, making it particularly
353
      effective for tasks involving temporal data. To adapt CLUDA for OCL setting, where no explicit
354
      source domain is available, we utilized the memory buffer as a pseudo-source domain. The memory
355   buffer, populated during sequential subject learning, served as the labeled source domain for the
356   adaptation process. (3) Online Learning Techniques: We consider two online learning methods,
357   OnPro Wei et al. (2023) and CoPE De Lange & Tuytelaars (2021), both of which employ prototyp-
358   ical loss functions within their frameworks. Although these techniques are primarily designed for
359   image classification tasks, we use them as comparative benchmarks to evaluate the performance of
360   our method against existing solutions as they utilize prototypical loss functions. (4) Bi-level Meta
361   Learning: We incorporate the AMBM Duan et al. (2024b), specifically tailored for the online learn-
362   ing of EEG data in a sequential manner. This approach leverages a bilevel optimization framework,
363
      enabling rapid adaptation to new data in the inner loop through a contrastive loss mechanism and
      effectively mitigates forgetting in the meta-loop. Finally, we establish a baseline, called OCL, which
364
      applies data augmentation to the mini-batch D and employs cross-entropy loss.
365
366   Model architecture and settings: A convolution neural network (CNN) is designed for the feature
367   extractor f which consists of a 1D convolution layer featuring 32 filters with a kernel size of 7 and
368   a stride of 2, followed by batch normalization. Next, the model includes three residual blocks, each
369
      comprising two convolution layers with kernel sizes (15, 21, and 43), followed by batch normaliza-
      tion. A max pooling layer with a kernel size of 4 and a stride of 4 is then applied followed by an
370
      attention layer. The feature extractor concludes with three fully connected layers containing 1024,
371
      512, and 256 units, respectively. A projection head, g ,is applied with embedding dimension of 128,
372   followed by a linear classifier ϕ. Relu activation functions are applied for non-linearity and Soft-

503   Discussion: The OPWA method introduces a prototype memory buffer alongside the memory re-
504   play buffer to store the mean prototypes for each subject. Although this incurs additional storage
505   costs, the memory requirement for prototypes is significantly lower than that of the replay buffer, as
506   prototypes are lower-dimensional representations compared to the original data samples. Therefore,
507   the total storage requirement remains manageable. The additional storage costs are justified by the
508
      advantage of preserving and aggregating compact representations of past subjects, which allows for
      better generalization without significantly increasing storage requirements.
509
510   The OPWA method computes pairwise distances between intra-class prototypes during the weighted
511   aggregation process, which makes it more computationally intensive than baseline methods such as
512   OCL, CoPE and OnPro. This pairwise distance calculation introduces quadratic complexity as it
513   involves iterating over all pairs of prototypes within the same class. As a result, the computational
514
      cost of OPWA depends on both the number of classes and subjects in the dataset. However, for
      datasets with relatively few subjects and classes, such as the PPB-EMO dataset (up to 40 subjects
515
      and 4 classes), this complexity remains manageable. To reduce computational complexity and mem-
516
      ory requirements in OPWA, one possible approach is to remove similar prototypes from memory. If
517   two prototypes have similar weights, one of them can be discarded by analyzing the weight matrix.
518   This reduces memory requirements and improves generalization by eliminating redundant proto-
519   types. OPWA is more computationally efficient as compared to methods like EWC, CLUDA and
520   AMBM. EWC requires computation of Fisher Information Matrix (FIM), which contains second-
521   order information and is computationally intensive. Even with approximations thorugh first order,
522   the complexity remains high due to the large number of model parameters. CLUDA increases the
523   complexity with multiple loss functions, including source and target domain losses and a discrimina-
524   tor loss, which increases the computational demands. AMBM, with its bilevel optimization process
525
      involving multiple inner- loop adaptation steps and a meta-loop for generalization, also incurs sig-
      nificant computational costs due to repeated gradient updates in both loops.

529   5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      Our proposed method,OPWA, addresses the challenges of catastrophic forgetting in the presence of
532
      inter-subject variability in OCL, especially for physiological data. By leveraging the principles of
533
      prototypical networks, OPWA effectively retains the knowledge of previous subjects while adapting
534   to new data streams. Our approach incorporates a robust prototype aggregation mechanism based on
535   intra-class distance considerations, which ensures that the generalized prototypes accurately reflect
536   the entire data distribution. This innovation goes beyond traditional momentum-based methods, pro-
537   viding a more accurate and reliable representation of class prototypes, especially in environments
538   with significant subject shifts. The experimental results demonstrate the superior performance of
539   OPWA in both AAA accuracy and FMA across different datasets, ensuring that the method is scal-
      able to different modalities and different types of data sets.

727   A.2   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
728
729   In our experiments, we utilize the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0001, along with a
730   learning rate scheduler that employs a step size of 30 and a decay rate of 0.9. The batch size is set to
731
      32 samples, and an equal number of samples are fetched from the memory buffer using a reservoir
      sampling technique. For prototype evolution in CoPE and within-subject training in OPWA, we
732
      use a momentum of 0.9. Additionally, the parameter σ in the Gaussian kernel is set to 1. Affine
733
      transformations are applied for data augmentation in CoPE and OnPro for contrastive losses.
734
735   After each subject shift, OPWA stores the prototypes of the current subject in the prototype memory
736   and performs an aggregation over the prototypes of the previously seen subjects. We work with a
737
      subject-aware setting where the subject shifts are known. The subject-agnostic setting, where the
      shifts are unknown, is reserved for future work. However, our method can be easily integrated with
738
      existing subject-agnostic approaches Duan et al. (2024b) that use a detection mechanism to identify
739
      subject shifts during online learning.

743   Figure 4 illustrates the comparison between the embedding spaces learned by the AMBM and
744   OPWA approaches. Both models were trained in an OCL setting using the AMIGOS EEG dataset.
745   At the end of the training phase, the embedding vectors of the test data were extracted from the
746   trained models and visualized using UMAP dimensionality reduction. In the embedding space
747   learned by AMBM, the vectors of the different classes are densely packed, leading to significant
748   overlap between them. This lack of separation poses a challenge for the classifier, as the absence
749   of clear class boundaries makes accurate classification difficult. In contrast, the embedding space
750
      generated by OPWA shows clear and distinct class boundaries, with samples from different classes
      well separated from each other. This improved separation facilitates better discrimination between
751
      classes and highlights OPWA’s superior performance in learning discriminative and separable em-
752
      beddings space.
