009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we explore the possibility of training high-parameter 3D Gaussian
012            splatting (3DGS) models on large-scale, high-resolution datasets. We design a
013            general model parallel training method for 3DGS, named RetinaGS, which uses a
014            proper rendering equation and can be applied to any scene and arbitrary distribution
015            of Gaussian primitives. It enables us to explore the scaling behavior of 3DGS in
016            terms of primitive numbers and training resolutions that were difficult to explore
017            before and surpass previous state-of-the-art reconstruction quality. We observe a
018
               clear positive trend of increasing visual quality when increasing primitive numbers
               with our method. We also demonstrate the first attempt at training a 3DGS model
019
               with more than one billion primitives on the full MatrixCity dataset that attains a
020
               promising visual quality.

043   3D scene reconstruction with Gaussian Splatting (GS)(Kerbl et al., 2023) has drastically improved
044   rendering quality and rendering speed over previous neural 3D representation(Mildenhall et al., 2021;
045   Zhang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022). However, this success has been largely limited to reconstructing
046   scenes with limited image or video resolution (typically <= 1600 pixels wide), data volume, and
047   view distance. Viewing the scene at high resolution or close range remains an unsolved challenge. To
048   achieve imaging effects flawless to the human retina, which we refer to as the goal of retina-level
049   reconstruction, we would desire to train the GS models with higher spatial resolution, larger datasets,
050
      and more varying viewing distances, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (right).
051   Despite the success in scaling up machine learning models (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al.,
052   2019; Brown et al., 2020). Training of 3DGS based reconstruction models have largely been limited
053   to a single GPU. However, in Fig. 1 we can see that the time and memory footprint needed for
      3DGS training on moderately size scene quickly grows infeasible for even the best single GPUs. To

      overcome the limitation of single GPUs, recent works (Lin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) resort to
055   approximately distributed training for specific data distribution, i.e., the bird’s-eye view city data.
056   These methods train independent 3DGS models in parallel on multiple GPUs, where each submodel
057   is responsible for one subspace of the dataset. These subspaces are carefully partitioned to minimize

059   rendering output can be concatenated to represent a large-scale scene. For bird’s-eye view datasets,
060   planar cells exist as a convenient choice of the partition. However, when the subspace partition
061   for “one subspace per ray” is infeasible, as shown in Fig. 2, these approximate distributed training

      In this paper, we present a method for distributed training of GS models that retains the exact
080
      equivalence to the existing single-GPU training scheme, while not relying on a certain data distribution.
081
      We start with the fact that the model space can be divided into a set of non-overlaping convex subspaces
082   and we can identify one subset of splats for each subspace so that all the subsets collectively form an
083   overlapping cover of the set of all splats. We then show that with a proper subset assignment strategy,
084   each ray’s original iterative alpha-blending process can be formulated in a hierarchical manner. In
085   this formulation, which applies to any distribution of splats and model space, we can first compute
086   partial color and alpha values for each subset and then obtain the rendered pixel color value by orderly
087   merging these partial values.
088   We derive our training method based on this formulation and designate one worker for each subset.
089   In the forward process, all workers compute subset-level color and alpha values for all rays in
090   parallel. The partial values, sufficient to compute rendered pixel values, can then be merged through
091   cross-worker communication with a minimal message size. After computing the reconstruction loss
092   and its gradients w.r.t. the partial values, we can distribute the corresponding gradients to each worker
093   and run the backward process in parallel. We further improve training efficiency by utilizing KD-tree
094   to produce subspaces that induce subsets with more even cardinality.
095   We validate the training method on multiple 3D reconstruction datasets with high resolutions training.
096   The trained GS models outperform single GPU trained 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) and several baseline
097   distirbuted 3DGS training approaches on Mip-NeRF360(Barron et al., 2022), Mega-NeRF (Turki
098   et al., 2022), ScanNet++(Yeshwanth et al., 2023) and MatrixCity(Li et al., 2023b) datasets. Through
099   scaling study, we observe a clear tendency to improve visual quality when the GS models grow in
100   splat numbers. Finally, we demonstrate to our knowledge the first attempt at training GS models
101   with billion-scale primitives on city-level dataset with 140k images, resulting in unprecedented visual
102
      experience using million-scale primitives.

106   Distributed Neural Radiance Fields. NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) has revolutionized 3D scene
107   reconstruction and novel viewpoint generation with its photorealistic rendering capabilities, sparking
      a series of subsequent innovations focused on enhancing various aspects of the technology. These

      advancements strive to improve rendering quality (Zhang et al., 2020; Baumgartner et al., 2004;
109   Barron et al., 2021; 2022), optimize training speed (Sun et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022; Chen
110   et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2022), increase memory efficiency (Reiser et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Rho
111   et al., 2023) and expand the scale of reconstructed scenes (Zhenxing & Xu, 2022; Xiangli et al., 2022;
112   Tancik et al., 2022; Turki et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Song & Zhang, 2023; Wu
113   et al., 2023). While these fields might benefit from larger models or enhanced computational power, it
114   is primarily the pursuit of scaling scene sizes that has driven researchers to explore ways to speed up
115   or scale up NeRF models. Switch-NeRF (Zhenxing & Xu, 2022) utilizes a Mixture of Experts (MoE)
116   to increase the capacity of NeRF models, enabling them to represent urban-scale scenes effectively.
117
      Bungee-NeRF (Xiangli et al., 2022) employs a hierarchical assembly of submodels, granting the
      model extensive multiscale representational capabilities. Recognizing the limitations imposed by
118
      GPU computational power and memory on model scale and training velocity, some researchers have
119
      begun deconstructing models into smaller components, adopting distributed approaches to address
120   these challenges. Block-NeRF (Tancik et al., 2022) segments cities into multiple overlapping blocks,
121   each represented by its own NeRF model, and uses neural networks to fuse the outputs of multiple
122   NeRF models in image space, achieving seamless visual results. Mega-NeRF (Turki et al., 2022)
123   introduces a simple geometric clustering algorithm and partitions training pixels into various NeRF
124   submodules, which can then be trained in parallel. All the distributed methods mentioned above only
125   perform 2D planar cell partitioning on the ground, which is suitable for flat urban scenes. However,
126   for detailed rendering of typical indoor scenes and scenes that have a combination for multiple view
127   types, large model capacity is required in both the vertical and horizontal directions, making these
128
      methods insufficient for the task.
129   Distributed Point-Based Representation. NeRF utilizes a volumetric rendering approach that
130   inherently limits their inference speed, which makes it challenging to achieve real-time performance.
131
      Several studies (Yu et al., 2021; Reiser et al., 2023; Yariv et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023) have focused
      on optimizing inference speeds, yet achieving both real-time performance and high-quality rendering
132
      remains elusive. In contrast, the 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) method, which
133
      employs a point-based scene representation, achieves state-of-the-art rendering quality and speed and
134   has significant advantages in training speed. However, the "shallow and wide" structure of the 3DGS
135   model and its explicit representation lead to a larger parameter count and increased training memory
136   footprint. VastGaussian (Lin et al., 2024) is the first attempt to large-scale scene reconstruction
137   with 3DGS. It divides the ground plane into a series of 2D cells with one splat subset for each cell.
138   Unlike our method, all subsets are mutually exclusive and considered as independent 3DGS models in
139   training. To mitigate the deviation from the original 3DGS rendering equation at subspace boundaries,
140   VastGaussian introduces additional nearby cameras and Gaussian primitives to assist in training at the
141   cost of increased compute and memory. CityGaussian (Liu et al., 2024) uses a similar partitioning
142
      strategy but trains an additional coarse GS model as the baseline to regularize submodels. However,
      artifacts caused by deviation from the proper rendering equation could still intensify at certain view
143
      angles and overcome the mitigations. As a result, these approaches mostly assume bird-eye-view
144
      urban scenarios due to their simplicity in view angles and splat distribution. Our method is based
145   on an equivalent form of the 3DGS rendering equation. Thus it can be applied to any scene with
146   arbitrary splat distribution.
147
      Distributed Deep Learning. Our work is also related to distributed deep learning, which aims to
148   scale up the training system for deep neural network models (Dean et al., 2012; Abadi et al., 2015; Li
149   et al., 2020). Early approaches revolve around training multi-GPU convolutional neural networks
150   (CNNs) (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) on image (Jia et al., 2014) and video (Wang et al., 2015) datasets,
151   with data parallelism being highly efficient thanks to CNNs’ high compute to bandwidth ratio. Recent
152   development of large language models presented new challenges in distributing model parameters
153   and larger clusters, resulting in dedicated model parallelism (Krizhevsky, 2014) approaches for
154   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) models, such as tensor-parallelism (Shoeybi et al., 2019),
155   pipeline parallelism (Huang et al., 2018), or hybrid parallelism (Shoeybi et al., 2019; Liu & Abbeel,
156
      2023; Lepikhin et al., 2020). Fully sharded data parallelism (FairScale authors, 2021), a type of
      redundancy-free data parallelism (Rajbhandari et al., 2020), also works great with LLM training. Our
157
      method is best described as model parallelism as it partitions GS model parameters onto multiple
158
      workers to distribute workload and incurs communication cost only for Gaussian on the subspace
159   boundaries. This is well suited to GS models’ relatively low compute-to-parameter ratio1 , which
160   makes data parallelism less efficient.

281   3.4.1   P RIMITIVE I NITIALIZATION
282
      In the original 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) approach, initialization and densification processes
283   involved numerous heuristic strategies and hyperparameters to ensure that Gaussian primitives were
284   appropriately positioned. Such strategies made it difficult to control the number of primitives,
285   impeding our ability to effectively scale the model size and leverage the advantages of distributed
286   modeling to enhance rendering fidelity. Furthermore, we found that the densification process
287   does not always promote an increase in primitives in high-resolution training, which makes it
288   counterproductive.
289   In this paper, we adopt a simple yet effective strategy for initialization as shown in Fig 3 and do
290   not adjust the number afterwards. We performed Multi-View Stereo (MVS) on all training data to
291   obtain depth estimations for all training viewpoints, which were then transformed into dense 3D point
292   clouds. These point clouds could be flexibly sampled and initialized as Gaussian primitives. This
293   streamlined approach allowed us to control the number of primitives as desired. It is also easier to
294   balance workers’ workloads thanks to a predefined number of primitives.

      Datasets. Our performance evaluation spanned 4 datasets that comprising indoor and outdoor
299   scenes. These datasets include all scenes from full-resolution MipNeRF-360 (Barron et al., 2022). We
300   extended our analysis to high-resolution scenarios based on the ScanNet++ dataset (Yeshwanth et al.,
301   2023), with a focus on scenes labeled 108ec0b806 and 8133208cb6. After distortion correction, two
302   scenes provided 863 and 476 high-resolution images respectively (8408 pixels wide). Furthermore,
303   we conducted tests in large-scale environments, including the Residence, Building, and Rubble
304   datasets from Mega-NeRF (Turki et al., 2022), as well as the entire MatrixCity Small City Aerial
305   dataset (referred to as MatrixCity-Aerial, 1920×1080), consisting of 6,362 images. We also utilized
306   the MatrixCity Small City Dense Street dataset, sampling 5 angles every meter along the centerline of
307
      streets to gather 135,290 images (1000×1000). From the Small City Dense Street dataset, we selected
      a focused test set of 2,480 images (referred to as MatrixCity-M). By combining all images from Aerial
308
      and Dense Street (referred to as MatrixCity-ALL), we conducted a comprehensive Billion GS level
309
      reconstruction. We followed the official Train/Test splits for Mega-NeRF and MatrixCity-Aerial, and
310   used every eighth image for testing in other datasets as recommended by MipNeRF-360. To obtain
311   superior MVS initial points, we reran Colmap’s sparse reconstruction based on full-resolution images
312   of MipNeRF-360 and ScanNet++ to obtain poses (using official provided poses of Mage-NeRF and
313   MatrixCity), and subsequently performed dense reconstruction (Schönberger et al., 2016) on all
314   datasets except MatrixCity-ALL. For MatrixCity-ALL, we replaced depth estimations of MVS with
315   official provided depth estimations to avoid super long time cost of Colmap’s dense reconstruction
316   (using MVS for MatrixCity-M and MatrixCity-Aerial).
317   Baselines and Metrics. Our comparisons include 3DGS and NeRF-related works. For fair com-
318   parisons, results from an equal number of iterations from our own 3DGS runs are also presented.
319   We primarily assessed the rendered image quality using three metrics consistent with 3DGS: PSNR,
320   SSIM, and LPIPS.
321   Implementation Details. Our method is based on 3DGS. We extended the number of training
322   iterations to 60k on MipNeRF-360, ScanNet++ and Maga-NeRF and 20 epochs on all MatrixCity
323   datasets for both 3DGS and ours to ensure adequate convergence. We do not adjust the number
      of primitives during training. Since the primitives are initialized with relatively accurate position

      Initialization and Densification. In practice, we observed issues with the original 3DGS’s point-
417   growing strategy, as shown in Appendix.Fig 12. Excessive iterations lead to deteriorating results.

420   stable training run and better model quality. By simply initializing using MVS with more primitives,
421   our model surpasses the original 3DGS even with careful tuning.
422
      Validity of Distribute Rendering we devise a simple test to validate the correctness of the underlying
423   rendering equation in our method. We precisely positioned the camera’s optical axis on the dividing
424   plane between two subspaces sharing this plane, ensuring that each ray passes through only one
425   subspace. In this case, each image pixel should only be rendered by primitives on one side of the
426   plane. If we do not perform the step, we would expect a crisp boundary between color pixels and
427   completely dark pixels on the partially rendered images from the two subspaces. As shown in Fig. 7,
428   our method exhibits the expected behavior while baseline partition approaches (Lin et al., 2024; Liu
429   et al., 2024) that deviates from the proper rendering equation fails the test.

488   Our distributed modeling method enables the GS model to scale up to extremely large sizes. Typically,
489   scenes in previous work are trained on a single GPU using 10 to 200 million pixels with a GS model

492   over 148 billion pixels. There have been no previous reports of successful training at this scale of

533   6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
534   In this paper, we study the problem of distributed training of 3DGS models. We devise a model
535   parallelism-based training method that utilizes a proper rendering equation to avoid artifacts. This
536   allows us to significantly expand the model scale in terms of primitive numbers and seamlessly
537   support large-scale scene reconstruction and detailed rendering. Although our method allows for
538   improving the model’s capacity by simply increasing the number of splats, this also results in a
539   higher computational load per ray. We believe that an effective hierarchical Level-of-Detail (LOD)
      description will address this issue, leading to enhancements in performance and rendering quality.

      A.3    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF T RAINING B ILLION -S CALE 3DGS
965
966   For the initialization of primitives, we employed depth maps officially provided by MatrixCity, where
967   each aerial view image contributes approximately 1/64 million primitives, and each street view image
968   contributes about 1/128 million primitives (excluding pixels exceeding the maximum depth). Due
969   to the application of anti-aliasing on the depth maps, the directly generated initialization points are
970   accompanied by noise. Such noise in the initialization can lead to floters in the training results,
971   potentially compromising the visual quality of the model. To obtain a clean point cloud, we utilized
      DBSCAN for noise filtration, treating aerial and street views separately. For aerial views, the epsilon


                                                                  18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       and minimum number of points were set to 1 m and 15, respectively, while for street views, these
973    parameters were established at 1 m and 20, respectively. Following the noise reduction process, an
974    additional 2% of primitives were added to represent the sky (initialized as hemispheres at a fixed
975    distance from the city center, with the fixed distance being twice the length of the city), culminating
976    in a total of 1 Billion primitives.
977
       We spatially partitioned the initial primitives to 64 sub-models and do not adjust the number of
978
       primitives during training. Since the primitives are initialized with accurate geometrical parameters,
979    we reduce the learning rate for the position parameters from 1.6 × 10−7 to 1.6 × 10−9 with a
980    exponential decay function. Besides, we reduce the learning rate for the scale parameters from
981    5 × 10−4 to 5 × 10−6 with a exponential decay function to avoid unusually large primitives.
