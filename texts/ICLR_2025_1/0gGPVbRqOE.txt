009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Wavelet shrinkage is a powerful tool in neural signal processing. It has been ap-
012            plied to various types of neural signals, such as non-invasive signals and extracel-
013            lular recordings. For example, in Parkinson’s disease (PD), β burst activities in
014            local field potential signals indicate pathological information, which corresponds
015            to strong signal with higher wavelet coefficients. However, it has been found that
016            there also exists weak signal that should not be ignored. This weak signal refers
017            to the set of small coefficients, which corresponds to the non-burst/tonic activity
018            in PD. While it lacks the interpretability of the strong signal, neglecting it may
019            result in the omission of movement-related information during signal reconstruc-
020
               tion. However, most existing methods are mainly focused on strong signals while
               ignoring weak signals. In this paper, we propose Splitted Wavelet Differential In-
021
               clusion, which is provable to achieve better estimation of both the strong signal
022
               and the whole signal. Equipped with an ℓ2 splitting mechanism, we derive the
023            solution path of a couple of parameters in a newly proposed differential inclusion,
024            of which the sparse one can remove bias in estimating the strong signal and the
025            dense parameter can additionally capture the weak signal with the ℓ2 shrinkage.
026            The utility of our method is demonstrated by the improved accuracy in a numerical
027            experiment and additional findings of tonic activity in PD.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Neural signals are fluctuations in neuronal activity that play a crucial role in brain function and
033   communication Buzsáki et al. (2012). To effectively preprocess these signals, wavelet analysis has
034   emerged as a powerful tool and has been widely applied across various neuroscience analysesPavlov
035   et al. (2012), as wavelet functions proficiently characterize transient fluctuations in rhythmic neural
036   oscillations Luo et al. (2018); Donoghue et al. (2020). A typical example is to extract these neu-
037   ral oscillations at different frequency bands (e.g., α (8-12 Hz), β (12-35 Hz), and γ (35-100 Hz)
038   bands) that are particularly important in neuropsychiatric disorders Mathalon & Sohal (2015) and
039
      movement disorders Yin et al. (2021). For such tasks, wavelet analysis facilitates the detection and
      quantification of temporal dynamics among neuronal populations, as observed in epilepsy Kalbhor
040
      & Harpale (2016) and Parkinson’s Disease (PD) Lofredi et al. (2023); Tinkhauser et al. (2017b).
041
042   Of particular paramount application of wavelet analysis is Wavelet Shrinkage Donoho & Johnstone
043   (1994; 1995; 1998); Donoho (1995), which is often employed to neural signal denoising Baldazzi
044   et al. (2020). It projects the noisy data into the wavelet domain, followed by a hard or soft thresh-
045
      olding method to force noisy coefficients to zeros. Apart from denoising, Wavelet Shrinkage is also
      employed in decoding brain states from neural signals, particularly in subthalamic nucleus (STN)
046
      local field potential (LFP) signals in PD Khawaldeh et al. (2020); Tinkhauser et al. (2017b). These
047
      findings also support improved clinical efficacy in PD by an adaptive deep brain stimulation algo-
048   rithm involving thresholds in Wavelet Shrinkage Nie et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2024).   To select the
                                                                                        √
049   threshold parameter, many methods have been proposed, such as Universal (σ 2 log n) Donoho
050   & Johnstone (1994), Minimaxi Verma & Verma (2012), SureShrink Donoho & Johnstone (1995),
051   Bayesian Shrinkage Do & Vetterli (2002); Simoncelli & Adelson (1996); JOHNSTONE & SIL-
052   VERMAN (2005), non-parametric shrinkage Antoniadis & Fan (2001); Gao (1998) such as SureLet
053   Luisier et al. (2007) and Neigh Shrink Sure Chen et al. (2005). Most of these methods only selected
      large coefficients since they are assumed to be all the information contains in the signal Atto et al.

054
      (2011). However, it has been found that there may exist small coefficients Donoho & Johnstone
055   (1994), such as the non-burst activity in PD Khawaldeh et al. (2020).
056
057   In this paper, we call the signal composed by such small (resp. large) coefficients as the weak
058
      (resp. strong) signal. Although the strong signal typically has more semantic meaning and is more
      interpretable than the weak signal, ignorance of the latter can lead to the loss of information in
059
      reconstruction. For example, exaggerated β oscillations (10-35 Hz), especially low β oscillations
060
      (10-20 Hz) in STN are significant biomarkers in the Parkinson’s study Kühn et al. (2006); Lofredi
061   et al. (2023), but the non-burst activity restricts the capacity of encoding physiological informa-
062   tion in the basal ganglia network Khawaldeh et al. (2020); Brittain & Brown (2014); Mallet et al.
063   (2008). At the mesoscopic scale, strong beta activities essentially come from the prolonged synchro-
064   nized state between neurons Tinkhauser et al. (2017a) and these non-burst activities coming from
065   low-frequency synchronized states might contain more information on motor movement Cole et al.
066   (2017); Khawaldeh et al. (2020). Therefore, it is desirable to identify the strong signal as a semantic
067   part and meanwhile estimate the whole signal (including both strong and weak signals) well.
068   Towards this goal, existing methods with too small or too large thresholds either suffer from failing
069   to eliminate noise components in estimating the strong signal or ignoring the weak signal part in
070   estimating the whole signal. Specifically, the methods with too large threshold values (e.g., Univer-
071   sal, or Minimaxi) could eliminate the noise components, but they could induce large bias/errors in
072   estimating the strong signal; moreover, they might over-smooth the weak signal part with small co-
073   efficients. On the other, the methods with smaller thresholds (e.g. SureShrink Donoho & Johnstone
074   (1995)) might fail to eliminate noise components in estimating the strong signal for interpretation.
075   To resolve these problems, we propose a new method from the perspective of differential inclusion,
076   dubbed as Splitted Wavelet Differential Inclusion(SWDI), which is provable to achieve better es-
077   timation than Wavelet Shrinkage on both the strong signal and the whole signal. Specifically, we
078   introduce an ℓ2 splitting mechanism appended to the signal reconstruction loss, which couples a
079   sparse parameter and a dense parameter to estimate the strong signal and the whole signal, respec-
080   tively. To update these parameters, we propose a differential inclusion with sparse regularization,
081
      i.e., a dynamic approach with a unique closed-form solution. Equipped with an early stopping mech-
      anism on this dynamics, the sparse parameter can recover the strong signal without bias, while the
082
      dense parameter can additionally capture the weak signal via ℓ2 shrinkage. We also provide a dis-
083
      cretization method that can efficiently generate the solution path, even if the wavelet decomposition
084   matrix is not orthogonal. The utility and effectiveness of our method are demonstrated in a numeri-
085   cal experiment and the effectiveness study of dopaminergic medication impact on PD. Particularly,
086   the signal recovered by our method is more significantly correlated to the medication, which can be
087   explained by the non-burst activity found by our method.
088
      To summarize, our contributions are listed as follows:
089
090          • We propose the SWDI, which involves a dual parameter to simultaneously estimate the
091            strong and the whole signal.
092          • We theoretically show that the closed-form solution path can achieve better estimation than
093            Wavelet Shrinkage on both the strong signal and the whole signal. For the strong signal,
094            our estimation is bias-free, while for the whole signal, our dense parameter can accurately
095            capture the weak signal via an ℓ2 shrinkage.
096          • We apply our method to neural signal recognition in PD and identify the non-burst activity
097            that has been recently found to be also responsive to the medication.

528   7   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose the SWDI for neural signal recovery, which can simultaneously remove
531   bias in estimating the strong signal and capture weak components in estimating the whole signal.
532   Our method has a unique closed-form solution and can achieve better estimations than Wavelet
533   Shrinkage. Besides, we provide an efficient discretization algorithm that can efficiently obtain the
534   whole solution path. In PD analysis, our method identifies the non-burst/tonic activity in the low β
535   band, which has been recently found to be responsive to medical treatment.
536   Limitations and future work. We only discuss the signal recovery in a non-adaptive way. However,
537   the sub-band and spatially adaptive wavelet decomposition can achieve better reconstruction results.
538   While saying so, we have shown promising results in real applications. Moreover, by considering
539   the strong and the weak signals on each sub-band, our method can be potentially applied to adaptive
      decomposition and will be carefully investigated in the future.

      A    R ELATED WORK OF D IFFERENTIAL I NCLUSION IN S IGNAL R ECOVERY
811
812
      The differential inclusion method in signal recovery was proposed in Osher et al. (2016), which is
813   called (Linearized) Bregman Inverse Scale Space and can be viewed as a continuous dynamics of
814   the Linearized Bregman Iteration (LBI) proposed in Osher et al. (2005); Yin et al. (2008) for image
815   denoising. From the perspective of differential inclusion, Osher et al. (2016) firstly showed the
816   model selection consistency property, recovering the true signal set under irrepresentable conditions.
817   The Huang & Yao (2018) later generalized this result to from the linear model to a general convex
818   function. Moreover, Huang et al. (2016; 2020) proposed the variable splitting method which leads
819   to better model selection consistency. The Sun et al. (2017) further applied them to Alzheimer’s
820   Disease and found there exists another type of lesion feature (which they called “procedural bias”)
821
      that can help disease diagnosis. Our method is motivated by differences from this method in the
      Wavelet Denoising scenario, in which our primary goal is to reconstruct the signal. Besides, thanks
822
      to the orthogonality of the Wavelet Matrix, our method has a closed-form solution, which leads to
823
      theoretical advantages over Wavelet Shrinkage.
