009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Offline reinforcement learning (RL) relies heavily on densely precise reward sig-
012            nals, which are labor-intensive and challenging to obtain in many real-world
013            scenarios. To tackle this challenge, offline imitation learning (IL) extracts optimal
014            policies from expert demonstrations and datasets without reward labels. However,
015            the scarcity of expert data and the abundance of suboptimal trajectories within
016
               the dataset impede the application of supervised learning methods like behav-
               ior cloning (BC). While previous research has focused on learning importance
017
               weights for BC or reward functions to integrate with offline RL algorithms, these
018
               approaches often result in suboptimal policy performance due to training insta-
019            bilities and inaccuracies in learned weights or rewards. To address this problem,
020            we introduce Trajectory-level Data Generation with Better Alignment (TDGBA),
021            an algorithm that leverages alignment measures between unlabeled trajectories
022            and expert demonstrations to guide a diffusion model in generating highly aligned
023            trajectories. The aforementioned trajectories allow for the direct application of
024            BC in order to extract optimal policies, negating the necessity for weight or re-
025            ward learning. In particular, we define implicit expert preferences and, without
026            the necessity of an additional human preference dataset, effectively utilise expert
027
               demonstrations to identify the preferences of unlabeled trajectories. Experimental
               results on the D4RL benchmarks demonstrate that TDGBA significantly outper-
028
               forms state-of-the-art BC-based IL methods. Furthermore, we illustrate the efficacy
029
               of implicit expert preferences, which represents the inaugural application of the
030            benefits of preference learning to offline IL.

033   1    I NTRODUTION
034
035   Reinforcement Learning (RL) has achieved remarkable success in a diverse range of domains,
036   including video games (Silver et al., 2017), large-scale language modeling (Ouyang et al., 2022),
037   protein structure prediction (Lutz et al., 2023), among others. While current RL techniques typically
038   require dynamic interactions with an environment and immediate reward feedback for policy learning,
039
      such online interactions can be costly, time-consuming, and risky in many practical scenarios. To
      mitigate these challenges, offline RL (Lutz et al., 2023) leverages pre-collected datasets with densely
040
      annotated rewards to derive optimal policies (Kostrikov et al., 2021; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Kumar
041
      et al., 2020). Although offline RL has led to significant advancements in various applications, the
042   design of accurate and robust reward functions remains a complex, laborious task, posing substantial
043   difficulties in real-world settings.
044
045
      To address this issue, with a small set of expert demonstrations and the offline datasets where no reward
      signals are provided, offline imitation learning (IL) (Rashidinejad et al., 2021) has been proposed
046
      to extract policies by utilizing behavior cloning (BC) (Pomerleau, 1988) or inverse reinforcement
047
      learning(IRL) (Jarboui & Perchet, 2021). However, their performance is closely related to the quality
048   of the datasets and the expert demonstrations. For the BC-based approaches, the prevalence of
049   suboptimal trajectories in offline datasets may hinder their ability to extract optimal policies due to
050   the distributional shift problem (Ross et al., 2011). To alleviate this problem, distribution divergence
051   measures (e.g., f -divergence (Ghasemipour et al., 2020)) are employed to align learned policies with
052   behavioral policies and a discriminator is trained to determine the optimal importance weights for
053   weighted BC (Ma et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022; 2021). However, such distribution matching schemes
      can incur training instability (Pomerleau, 1988) and over-conservatism (Lyu et al., 2024). Some work


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      utilizes expert demonstrations to learn intermediate reward functions (Lyu et al., 2024; Zolna et al.,
055   2020; Luo et al., 2023), which are then incorporated with existing offline RL methods. However,
056   offline RL is inherently challenging due to its sensitivity to hyperparameters, and learning accurate
057   transition-wise rewards is also difficult.
058
      It has been demonstrated that when training data includes sufficient expert knowledge, learning
059
      an effective policy becomes relatively easy (Xu et al., 2022). While some studies have explored
060
      expanding expert data through data augmentation, these methods often face challenges such as
061   overfitting, stemming from the limited availability of expert data and the insufficient diversity and
062   fidelity of generated samples (Sun et al., 2023). Furthermore, a significant amount of unlabeled
063   trajectory data often remains underutilized. Recently, Flow-to-Better (FTB) (Zhang et al., 2023)
064   introduced a method that combines human preferences across diverse trajectories with a trajectory
065   diffuser to iteratively refine and enhance trajectories, improving both the quality and diversity of
066   training data. However, collecting task-specific human preference data is inherently time-consuming
067   and resource-intensive. This raises an intriguing question: Could we construct an expert-like
068   preference from limited expert data to guide trajectory generation efficiently?
069   Based on the above introduction, we propose a method named Trajectory-level Data Generation with
070   Better Alignment (TDGBA). Firstly, we propose a simple and efficient alignment measurement based
071   on optimal transport theory and Wasserstein distance. Trajectories with small distances are referred
072   to as high-alignment trajectories, and those with large distances are referred to as low-alignment
073   trajectories, with the alignment measurement defined as implicit expert preference. Then, we adopt
074   the trajectory optimization approach proposed in FTB, which trains a trajectory diffuser to refine low-
075
      alignment trajectories, gradually aligning them with expert trajectories. The generated diverse and
      near-expert trajectories are then used to enable the agent to learn policies through a straightforward
076
      behavior cloning (BC) method. The main contributions are as follows:
077
078   1) We present a novel framework for offline IL that can learn optimal policies directly from generated
079   trajectories without the need of learning reward functions or behavior weights;
080   2) By introducing the implicit expert preference, high-fidelity and diverse trajectories can be generated
081   with a trajectory diffuser, even with only a single expert trajectory;
082
      3) Experimental results show that TDGBA outperforms previous offline IL methods across multiple
083
      tasks. Additionally, the effectiveness of our method provides the first evidence of the potential and
084
      feasibility of applying the advantages of preference learning to the offline IL domain.

087   2    R ELATED W ORK
088
089
      Offline Imitation learning learns policies from few expert demonstrations without real rewards.
090
      By using supervised learning, Behavioral Cloning (BC)(Pomerleau, 1988) is one of the simplest
091   IL methods. However, in datasets with insufficient expert demonstrations, challenges such as
092   compounded errors and inadequate generalization capabilities often arise. To alleviate these problems,
093   BCND (Sasaki & Yamashina, 2020) reuses other BC policies as target weights for the original BC
094   policy. Recently, DICE (distribution divergence measure)-related methods (Ma et al., 2022; Kim
095   et al., 2022; 2021) have garnered attention by transforming the min-max problem in adversarial IL
096   into a convex optimization problem. However, these methods usually suffer from training instability
097   and excessive conservatism. Another line of the offline IL research is offline inverse RL(IRL) (Jarboui
098   & Perchet, 2021), which learns reward functions from expert demonstrations and then existing
099
      offline RL algorithms can be used for the policy training. Some typical work of IRL includes ORIL
      (Zolna et al., 2020), CLARE (Yue et al., 2023) and IQ-learn (Garg et al., 2021), where ORIL (Zolna
100
      et al., 2020) applies the online inverse RL method GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016) to the offline setting,
101
      CLARE introduces the maximum entropy inverse reinforcement learning framework to the offline
102   setting, and IQ-learn (Garg et al., 2021)implicitly learns rewards and policies by learning the Soft-Q
103   function. By decoupling reward learning and policy optimization, the optimal transport distance
104   or the nearest neighbor distance is used in OTR (Luo et al., 2023) and SEABO (Lyu et al., 2024)
105   as rewards. However, the reward distribution learned by these methods often significantly differs
106   from the actual reward distribution, making policy learning susceptible to inaccuracies in rewards
107   and sensitivity to hyperparameters. In recent years, several offline preference reinforcement learning
      methods have alleviated the challenge of reward design by introducing preference learning. Methods


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      such as ORPL(Shin et al., 2023) and PT(Kim et al., 2023) assign rewards to transitions by learning a
109   score model. Additionally, FTB(Zhang et al., 2023) proposes a trajectory optimization scheme based
110   on the trajectory diffuser and trajectory preferences, achieving state-of-the-art performance in many
111   tasks. However, these methods rely on the availability of an additional, carefully designed human
112   preference dataset, which imposes a critical limitation. Due to this requirement, the advantages of
113   preference learning cannot be extended to offline imitation learning scenarios where only a small
114   number of expert demonstrations are available. To address this issue, we propose the key concept
115   of implicit expert preferences and a method for measuring them. By integrating these with the
116   trajectory optimization approach in FTB(Zhang et al., 2023), we achieve significant breakthroughs in
117
      performance of offline IL. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to successfully transfer the
      advantages of preference learning to offline imitation learning. We believe that TDGBA will offer
118
      researchers a promising new direction for exploration.

      4.1   C OMPUTE A LIGNMENT M EASUREMENT
210
211   For an unlabeled trajectory in the dataset, it is clear that its alignment measurement with expert
212   demonstrations determines the quality in BC-based policy learning. However, to our best knowledge,
213   how to measure the alignment in a trajectory level is still unsolved in offline IL. Recent works have
214   computed the Wasserstein distance between trajectories and assigned rewards to each transition, which
215   are then used to learn policies via reinforcement learning methods, resulting in some performance
      improvement (Luo et al., 2023)(Dadashi et al., 2020). However, this reward assignment approach has


                                                                                            4

216

217   incorrect reward labeling (Fu et al., 2024)(Liu et al., 2024). Specifically, in the experimental section,

219   comparisons. We hypothesize that the root cause of these issues lies in the fact that Wasserstein

505   6     D ISSUSION
506
507   TDGBA and FTB In this section, we provide additional discussion on TDGBA in comparison to
508   FTB(Zhang et al., 2023). To the best of our knowledge, FTB is one of the most state-of-the-art works
509   in the field of offline preference reinforcement learning. Its approach of combining preference learning
510   with trajectory diffusers has demonstrated great potential and has provided significant inspiration for
511
      our work. We would like to clarify that although TDGBA adopts methods from FTB for trajectory
      optimization and policy learning, the core contribution of our work lies in successfully combining the
512
      advantages of preference learning with offline imitation learning for the first time. To the best of our
513
      knowledge, all previous offline preference learning methods require an additional human preference
514   dataset, whereas our approach can effectively leverage the advantages of preference learning even
515   with only one expert trajectory. Furthermore, it is worth emphasizing that collecting accurate human
516   preference datasets and labeling them, as well as training task-specific preference models, is inherently
517   time-consuming and labor-intensive. TDGBA offers a simpler and more practical advantage in this
518   regard. In the future, we plan to explore combining implicit expert preferences with other preference
519   learning methods and further improving them to continue exploring their value in this field.
520
521   Limitations In this section, we highlight some limitations of TDGBA. First, both our approach
522   and most of the baselines are primarily validated on state-based tasks. Applying TDGBA directly
523
      to image-based tasks presents challenges, such as high computational costs due to the training of
      the Trajectory Diffuser and the large dimensionality of images. Additionally, handling multi-modal
524
      expert demonstrations may introduce potential complications. To address the above issues, we will
525
      explore better alignment measures and investigate more effective preference learning methods to
526   further leverage the advantages of the implicit expert preference concept.

529   7     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose Trajectory-level Data Generation with Better Alignment (TDGBA), a novel
      framework for offline IL without the need for learning behavior weights or reward functions. TDGBA
532
      uses the Wasserstein Distance to calculate the alignment measure between unlabeled trajectories
533
      and expert demonstrations. Then, TDGBA leverages a small number of expert demonstrations to
534   introduce implicit expert preferences, further guiding the preference-based Trajectory Diffuser to
535   generate high-quality, expert-aligned trajectories. With the generated high-alignment trajectories, we
536   can utilize BC directly to derive the optimal policy. Experimental results on the D4RL benchmarks
537   show that TDGBA significantly outperforms SOTA offline IL methods. Additionally, we demonstrate
538   that incorporating implicit expert preferences effectively combines the advantages of preference
539   learning with scarce expert demonstrations, offering a promising new research direction for the offline
      imitation learning domain.

      B.1   COMPARISON TO GENERATIVE MODEL IN RL
765
766   In this section, we compare our approach with currently popular generative model applications
767   in reinforcement learning (RL). Traditional methods primarily use generative models as planners,
768   predicting sequences of trajectories with high returns and implementing corresponding actions (Chen
769   et al., 2021; Janner et al., 2021; 2022; Ajay et al., 2022). However, these approaches require the
770   ground-truth rewards during training. On the other hand, FTB (Zhang et al., 2023) utilizes an
771   additional human preference dataset to train a task-specific score model, which calculates preferences
772
      for unlabeled trajectories. Yet, collecting extensive human preference data is labor-intensive and
      even challenging. To our knowledge, we are the first to extract optimal strategies using generative
773
      models under the conditions of no ground-truth rewards or the dataset with human preference labels.
774
      Unlike previous works, we generate high-quality trajectories using only a few expert demonstrations
775   or even a single expert trajectory. We present the performance comparison results with these methods
776   in Table 9, revealing that our approach demonstrates strong competitiveness and highlighting its
