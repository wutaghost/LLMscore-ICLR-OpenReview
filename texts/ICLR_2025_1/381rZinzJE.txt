                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
               Missing values in measurements for carbon dioxide emissions on drained peat-
015            lands remains an open challenge for training forecasting techniques to achieve net
016            zero. Existing methods struggle to model CO2 emissions to fill gaps at the field
017            scale, especially in nighttime measurements. We propose novel Physics-Informed
018            Autoencoders (PIAEs) for stochastic differential equations (SDEs), which com-
019            bine the generative capabilities of Autoencoders with the reliability of physical
020            models of Net Ecosystem Exchange (NEE) that quantify CO2 exchanges between
021            the atmosphere and major carbon pools. Our method integrates an SDE describing
022            the changes in NEE and associated uncertainties to fill gaps in the NEE measure-
023
               ments from eddy covariance (EC) flux towers. We define this SDE as a Wiener
               process with a deterministic drift term based on day and night time NEE physics
024
               models, and stochastic noise term. In the PIAE model, various sensor measure-
025
               ments are encoded into the latent space, and a set of deterministic decoders ap-
026            proximate the SDE parameters, and a probabilistic decoder predicts noise term.
027            These are then used to predict the drift in NEE and thereby the optimal NEE fore-
028            cast at the next time instance using the SDE. Finally, we use a loss function as a
029            weighted sum of the Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Maximum Mean Discrep-
030            ancy (MMD) between the measurements and the reconstructed samples and the
031            associated noise and drift. PIAE outperforms the current state-of-the-art Random
032            Forest Robust on predicting nighttime NEE measurements on various distribution-
033            based and data-fitting metrics. We present a significant improvement in capturing
034
               temporal trends in the NEE at daily, weekly, monthly and quarterly scales.
035
036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and removals can be monitored at various local and country-wide
039   levels. At the local scale, flux towers using eddy covariance (EC) systems measure Net Ecosystem
040   Exchange (NEE), Latent Heat (LE), and Sensible Heat (H) among other atmospheric scalars Zhu
041   et al. (2022). However, measurements often contain gaps due to power shortages, device malfunc-
042   tions, or other issues, ranging from half-hourly to several months. Gap-filling methods are employed
043   to enhance data quality for forecasting and analysis. Additional information can be obtained by in-
044
      corporating complementary measures from other tools, such as satellite observations through remote
      sensing.
045
046   Initial gap-filling strategies leveraged flux covariance with meteorological variables and temporal
047   auto-correlation, but they struggle with gaps longer than 12 days Reichstein et al. (2005). A com-
048   prehensive study by Moffat et al. (2007) evaluated 15 techniques across different gap scenarios,
049   finding that Non-Linear Regression (NLR), Look-Up Table (LUT), Marginal Distribution Sampling
050
      (MDS), and the Semi-Parametric Model (SPM) performed well overall, though challenges remain
      for gaps up to one month.
051
052   To address longer gaps, the Random Forest Robust (RFR) method was introduced, improving R2
053   values for NEE by 30% compared to MDS and reducing uncertainty by 70% Zhu et al. (2022).
      While effective for longer gaps, RFR struggles with nighttime measurements.

070   This research aims to build on gap duration agnostic approaches, improving performance and inter-
071   pretability in modeling NEE measurements based on physical laws. We propose Physics-Informed
072   Autoencoders (PIAE), which integrate NEE physics models for day and night with a stochastic
073   differential equation (SDE) to predict NEE values and fill data gaps. Our method also provides fore-
074   casting capabilities and enhances performance on NEE gap-filling by accurately learning the NEE
075   distribution and associated parameters.
076   In summary, PIAE combines empirical nighttime and daytime NEE models with a noise model
077   White & Luo (2008) to improve robustness and accuracy in filling gaps and forecasting NEE. Our
078   key contributions include:
079
080          • Introducing a stochastic differential equation for NEE measurements combining daytime
081            and nighttime models with Gaussian noise.
082
083
             • Introducing an interpretable PIAE model that accurately forecasts the NEE at next time
               stamps as a combination of deterministic drift and noise terms, guided by an SDE.
084
085          • Demonstrating that PIAE improves gap-filling robustness compared to state-of-the-art
086            methods, handling gaps from months to years.
087
088          • Showing significant improvements in NEE distribution learning validated by better Maxi-
089            mum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), Wasserstein distance, and Kullback-Leibler (KL) diver-
090            gence.
091
             • Accurately predicting SDE parameters, enhancing interpretability.

      Since we consider physical measurements, we need to consider two aspects: the drift of NEE (deter-
112
      ministic part) and the noise of measurements (assumed to be Gaussian) White & Luo (2008). Thus,
113
      we introduce the dynamics of NEE over time t, modeled as a Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE)
114   incorporating a Wiener process. Firstly, we define a simpler drift of the NEE model as a function
115   of time-based on the Arrhenius-type law for NEE Lasslop et al. (2010) using the temperature and
116   radiation models. We then introduce the diffusion coefficient to represent the noise and complete
117   the SDE. Finally, the SDE is incorporated into the PIAE architecture (discussed in Section 4). The
118   small number of parameters of these dynamics makes it easier to use in the architecture.
119
120   3.1     T HE N ET E COSYSTEM E XCHANGE NIGHTTIME AND DAYTIME MODELS
121
122   The Net Ecosystem Exchange (NEE) represents the net exchanges of CO2 between the ecosys-
123   tem and the atmosphere Lasslop et al. (2010); Keenan et al. (2019). NEE accounts for ecosystem
124
      respiration, the release of CO2 from biological activity, and photosynthesis, the uptake of CO2 to
      produce energy for a plant. Thus, NEE is decomposed as the difference between ecosystem res-
125
      piration (Reco,t ) and the Gross Primary Product (GPP). GPP represents photosynthesis, following
126
      the convention that negative fluxes indicate the removal of CO2 from the atmosphere Lasslop et al.
127   (2010), for every time t,
128                                          NEEt = Reco,t − GPPt                                    (1)
129
130   where Reco,t and GPPt are parameterized by temperature and radiation values over time t. Since,
131   the SDE models NEE dynamics over time, time derivatives of temperature and radiation become
132   key components. The individual models for temperature and radiation are provided in the Appendix
133
      sections A.2 and A.3.
134

      5.1   PARAMETER E STIMATION E0 , rnight/day , α, β
379
380   The flux tower data does not provide ground-truth values for the day and nighttime model parameters
381   E0 , rnight/day , α, β introduced in the models section 3.1. Therefore, we use REddyProc partitioning
382   algorithm to estimate from the flux tower measurements Wutzler et al. (2018). REddyProc is a non-
383   linear regression method that estimates E0 , rnight/day , α, β based on the methodology of Reichstein
384   et al. (2005) for the nighttime, and of Lasslop et al. (2010) for the daytime. In the work from
385   Lasslop et al., we have existing prior knowledge on the range of E0 the temperature sensitivity
386
      (E0 ∈ [50, 400]), rnight/day the base respiration at reference temperature Tref (rday , rnight > 0), α
387
      the canopy light utilisation efficiency (α ∈ [0, 0.22)) and β the maximum CO2 uptake (β ∈ [0, 250))
      Lasslop et al. (2010).
388
389   We use two different methods for calculating the parameters for nighttime and daytime models. As
390   explained in Section 3.1, the GPP value at night time is assumed to be zero. Therefore we can
391   assume NEE to be calculated directly from Reco (see Equation 2). As such, for nighttime parameters
392   E0 and rnight/day , we follow a method based on the flux partitioning described in Reichstein et al.
393
      (2005): we divide the nighttime data into groups of data points representing each night (for points
      with radiation values greater than 10 W.m−2 ). For each group, we estimate values for E0 and
394
      rnight/day by applying the Lloyd-and-Taylor model Lloyd & Taylor (1994) by fitting to the scatter
395
      of NEE and Tair,t using non-linear regression.
396
397   For the daytime model, because of the introduction of GPP in the equation of NEE (see Equations
398   1 and 3), we calculate E0 , r, α, β. We follow a method based on the flux partitioning defined by
399
      Lasslop et. al Lasslop et al. (2010). Here, for each daytime data group, we use the same E0
      values estimated from the nighttime data of the respective day (previously calculated). We first
400
      estimate values for α and β by fitting them to the scatter of GPP and Rg using non-linear regression.
401
      Consequentially, with the estimated values for E0 , α, β and NEE values for each daytime data group,
402   we use Equation 3 to calculate rnight/day for each data point in the daytime data group.

477   6   D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
478
479   In this study, we introduced a Physics-Informed Autoencoder (PIAE) to address the forward problem
480   of Net Ecosystem Exchange (NEE) gap-filling, utilizing a Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE)
481   to enhance the quality of CO2 measurements from flux towers at the agricultural field scale. This
482
      approach not only improves the data quality for training Net Ecosystem Exchange forecasting meth-
      ods but also integrates deterministic models for nighttime and daytime Net Ecosystem Exchange
483
      alongside stochastic components, such as Gaussian noise.
484
485   In Section 3, we outlined the Net Ecosystem Exchange models that account for both determinis-
      tic phenomena and stochastic uncertainties in the measurements. Section 4 detailed our Physics-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Informed Autoencoder, which effectively addresses the forward problem by estimating model pa-
487   rameters. In Section 5, we demonstrated that Physics-Informed Autoencoder outperforms state-
488   of-the-art methods by approximately 22% in R2 score and 52% in MMD score for nighttime Net
489   Ecosystem Exchange gaps and captures trends across daily to quarterly scales more effectively.
490
      Our method’s effectiveness is further validated by comparing it with a standard Autoencoder (AE),
491
      particularly for nighttime data, where Physics-Informed Autoencoder significantly outperforms Au-
492
      toencoder due to the integration of the Stochastic Differential Equation. Additionally, Physics-
493   Informed Autoencoder performs gap-filling in a duration-agnostic manner, similar to the Random
494   Forest Robust (RFR) method, but with the added advantage of incorporating physical laws through
495   Stochastic Differential Equation. Furthermore, Physics-Informed Autoencoder offers forecasting
496   capabilities by predicting Net Ecosystem Exchange at the next time instance enhancing its utility
497   beyond gap-filling.
498
      In conclusion, the use of Physics-Informed Autoencoder for Net Ecosystem Exchange stochastic
499   dynamics has successfully filled gaps ranging from half-hourly to yearly in Net Ecosystem Exchange
500   measurements from the flux tower in East Anglia fields, with satisfactory uncertainty levels for both
501   day and night. This method is ready for deployment and will serve as a robust example for Digital
502   Twin projects, such as AI4NetZero, aimed at climate change monitoring.
