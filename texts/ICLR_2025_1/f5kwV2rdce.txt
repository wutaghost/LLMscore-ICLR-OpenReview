012
013            Event cameras provide a compelling alternative to traditional frame-based sen-
014            sors, capturing dynamic scenes with high temporal resolution and low latency.
015            Moving objects trigger events with precise timestamps along their trajectory, en-
016
               abling smooth continuous-time estimation. However, few works have attempted
               to optimize the information loss during event representation construction, impos-
017
               ing a ceiling on this task. Fully exploiting event cameras requires representations
018
               that simultaneously preserve fine-grained temporal information, stable and char-
019            acteristic 2D visual features, and temporally consistent information density—an
020            unmet challenge in existing representations. We introduce Labits: Layered Bidi-
021            rectional Time Surfaces, a simple yet elegant representation designed to retain all
022            these features. Additionally, we propose a dedicated module for extracting active
023            pixel local optical flow (APLOF), significantly boosting the performance. Our ap-
024            proach achieves an impressive 49% reduction in trajectory end-point error (TEPE)
025            compared to the previous state-of-the-art on the MultiFlow dataset. The code will
026            be released upon acceptance.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As an emerging visual modality, event cameras offer unique and practical advantages. Compared to
032   conventional frame-based cameras, they provide higher temporal resolution, greater dynamic range,
033
      higher efficiency, and lower latency (Gallego et al. (2020)). Furthermore, under stable lighting, event
      cameras are primarily sensitive to the edges of moving objects, naturally filtering out stationary
034
      objects while tracking moving ones. Their ultra-high temporal resolution also enables smoother and
035
      more continuous target tracking. In recent years, numerous papers leveraging this feature of event
036   cameras have addressed topics such as feature tracking (Messikommer et al. (2023)), optical flow
037   generation (Wan et al. (2024)), and video interpolation (He et al. (2022)) based on events.
038
039
      From an event camera’s perspective, each moving point generates a discrete trajectory in the xyt
      space, with each triggered event representing a sampled point on this trajectory, along with its times-
040
      tamp. The instantaneous velocity at any point on the trajectory can be calculated from the relative
041
      positions and times of these events using straightforward mathematical formula v = ∆x/∆t, align-
042   ing with intuitive understanding. While real-world factors like rotations, depth movements, and
043   multiple moving objects complicate pixel-wise speed, the trajectory information persists embedded
044   within the event streams. Our target is to unearth these hidden treasures.
045
      Currently, there are two main approaches to utilizing events. One is to directly construct events into a
046
      graph in the xyt space and input it into a GNN (Graph Neural Network) (Li et al. (2021)) or treat each
047
      event as a spike to be input into an SNN (Spiking Neural Network) (Kosta & Roy (2023)). The other
048   is to first convert events into a dense representation and then input them into an Artificial Neural
049   Network (ANN). Although GNNs appear efficient, constructing the graph is computationally and
050   memory-intensive with increasing events, and GNNs suffer from over-smoothing as network depth
051   increases (Chen et al. (2020)). Additionally, the graph conversion discards fine-grained spatial and
052   temporal information, making position-sensitive scenarios problematic. These limitations restrict
053   GNN applications in event-based vision, especially for low-level tasks. On the other hand, while
      SNNs offer high energy efficiency, they are more difficult to train and are less robust (Lee et al.

080   of neuromorphic hardware is insufficient to support large-scale, high-precision models in practical
081
      applications (Nunes et al. (2022)). These factors limit the widespread use of SNNs.
082   The mainstream approach in event-based vision remains converting the event stream into a dense
083   representation before inputting it into an ANN for inference (Ye et al. (2023), wan2024event). How-
084   ever, existing representations have limitations that prevent event cameras from fully leveraging their

086   (2017)) and event counts (Maqueda et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2018b)) entirely remove the temporal
087
      dimension by projecting all events onto a 2D plane. Event frames sum the polarities at each pixel,
      while event counts simply tally the events. Similarly, voxel grid (Zhu et al. (2019)) applies temporal
088
      quantization discards fine-grained temporal information, projecting events onto the nearest temporal
089
      grid using a bilinear sampling kernel. Although representations like time surfaces (Lagorce et al.
090   (2016)) and TORE volumes (Baldwin et al. (2020)) preserve some temporal information, each pixel
091   retains only the timestamps of the most recent events, ignoring earlier ones triggered by different
092   objects. This leads to temporal occlusion and limits the ability to extract continuous, dense, and
093   temporally consistent local motion information in the spatio-temporal (xyt) domain.
094
      Dense trajectory estimation requires pixel-level movement sensitivity, stable and sharp 2D visual
095
      features, and consistent information density throughout the tracking duration. The final requirement
096   is to ensure the representation does not excessively focus on events near the end of the time period
097   while neglecting earlier occurrences. To fully exploit the potential of events for dense trajectory
098   estimation, a representation that preserves all these characteristics is essential. In this paper, we
099   propose Labits: LAyered BIdirectional Time Surfaces, as a simple and elegant solution to these
100   challenges. The impact of an effective event representation is significant. By simply switching
101   to Labits, it can yield a 13% improvement on TEPE, while incorporating an additional APLOF
102   extractor can provide an extra 30% reduction in error for dense trajectory estimation.
103   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: 1. We proposed Labits, a novel synchronous
104   event representation that is aware of event camera’s asynchronous characteristic and keeps rich local
105   movement trend information. 2. We trained a corresponding active pixel local optical flow estimator
106   based on Labits layers, which utlizes intermediate motion information. 3. We achieved the SOTA
107   performance on event-based dense trajectory estimation task, and the TEPE of our method decreased
      49% compared to the previous SOTA.

123   Event Camera: Event cameras contain a bio-inspired dynamic vision sensor, where each pixel
124   unit works asynchronously and triggers an event instantly when it detects a log-intensity change
125   over a predefined threshold. Each event e = (t, x, y, p) records the spatial coordinate (x, y) of the
126   corresponding pixel position on the image sensor plane, the microsecond-level shooting timestamp
127   t, and a binary polarity value p that indicates the direction of brightness change (Zhang et al. (2024)).
128   Event cameras are widely used in various computer vision and robotics tasks. They excel in motion-
129
      centric tasks like optical flow estimation and object or human pose tracking, as demonstrated in
      numerous studies (Hu et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2024); Chamorro Hernández et al. (2020); Zhang
130
      et al. (2023)). Their high temporal resolution and event-driven operation also enable innovative
131
      video processing techniques, including frame interpolation (Tulyakov et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2023);
132   Liu et al. (2024)) and motion deblurring (Chen et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024); Kim et al. (2024)).
133   These applications leverage the unique characteristics of event cameras for detailed, dynamic scene
134   analysis without the high data demands of traditional high-speed video recording.
135
      Event Representations: In event-based vision, mainstream methods convert the event stream into
136
      a dense representation, then pass it to ANNs for various tasks (Bardow et al. (2016)). However,
137
      existing representations often fail to fully leverage the unique capabilities of event cameras.
138
139   Early representations attempt to convert event streams into intensity frames, highlighting moving
140   edges and mimicking 2D features of traditional cameras. The event count representation (Maqueda
141
      et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2018b)) sums the number of events per pixel within a time window, while
      the event frame (Rebecq et al. (2017)) sums event polarities. Both discard temporal information, ob-
142
      scuring events over time. The voxel grid representation (Zhu et al. (2019)) quantizes time and maps
143
      events to temporal grids using bilinear sampling. While better than event count, time information
144   retention is still limited, and temporal obscuring persists.
145
146   Time surface-style representations form another key branch. The original time surface, or Surface of
147
      Active Events (SAE, Benosman et al. (2013)), encodes only the most recent event’s timestamp per
      polarity at each pixel, disregarding prior events, no matter the scene’s complexity. This leads to poor
148
      2D pattern capture and temporal occlusion, where newer events overwrite earlier ones. Variants like
149
      the averaged time surface (Sironi et al. (2018)) reduce noise and mitigate occlusion, but reintroduce
150   temporal obscuring and ambiguity. TORE volume (Baldwin et al. (2022)) preserves the most recent
151   K events per pixel, creating multi-layer time surfaces. However, redundancy arises when the same
152   object repeatedly triggers recent events at the same pixel, perpetuating temporal occlusion. This is
153   partly due to the complex textures of real-world objects, which introduce numerous small edges that
154   trigger events. All aforementioned representations, and others, are summarized in Table 2.
155
      Trajectory Estimation: Continuous-time trajectory estimation was initially proposed for rolling
156   shutter compensation Kerl et al. (2015). More closely related to our work is the regression of pixel
157   trajectories, aligned with high-speed feature tracking in event cameras Gehrig et al. (2020); Alzu-
158   garay & Chli (2020). This work connects to methodologies described in Gehrig et al. (2024), where
159   features are continuously tracked via the integration of Bézier curves, correlation map sequences,
160   and image data. However, unlike our approach, their solution emphasizes visual pattern-based cor-
161   relation while neglecting the fine-grained temporal information inherent in raw events, often leading
      to erroneous trajectory estimations.

      Event cameras have distinct advantages over traditional frame-based cameras due to their asyn-
165
      chronous nature. This characteristic enables event cameras to provide highly precise timestamps at
166   the microsecond level, making them ideal for capturing instantaneous motion velocity. However, ex-
167   isting event representations fail to fully exploit these advantages. Most approaches convert the sparse
168   and discrete event streams into frame-like structures that highlight 2D visual features such as moving
169   edges and patterns, thus making the event modality compatible with conventional computer vision
170   models. The transformation essentially forces event cameras to conform, fitting themselves into the
171   framework dominated by frame-based cameras, rather than fully leveraging their own strengths and
172   showcasing the unique advantages that conventional cameras cannot replicate.
173   Building event representations inevitably involves information loss. It’s almost a trilemma to faith-
174   fully preserve the original fine-grained timestamp information, compile meaningful 2D visual pat-
175   terns, and maintain historical movement information at intermediate times simultaneously. How-
176   ever, all three aspects are essential for accurate, dense, continuous-time trajectory estimation. Fine-
177   grained event-level timestamp information is the unique strength of the event modality in movement
178   prediction. 2D visual patterns form the basis for correlation-based tracking mechanisms, while inter-
179   mediate movement information ensures stable estimation accuracy throughout the entire trajectory.
180   Therefore, we designed Labits: Layered Bidirectional Time Surfaces to meet these demands. The
181   logic behind this representation is straightforward: a time-surface-like structure is essential for re-
182   taining the microsecond-level timestamp features. The accumulation time range of events should
183   be strictly controlled to avoid issues with visual feature blurriness, so splitting the target time range
184   into a series of smaller time bins is essential. Stopping at this stage creates a layered time surface.
185   However, under this scheme, only the last events’ information is utilized within each time bin at
186   each pixel, which is unpreferable. Moreover, when estimating the local speed at each time bin inter-
187   section, only past movement information is considered, so the prediction here is based on backward
188   difference, where the error estimation is known to be O(δt). Further reducing this error estimation
189   is nothing difficult: simply consider future event if no event is observed in the past δt search range.
190   This change in representation is simple yet powerful: First, pixels ahead of the moving edges’ direc-
191   tion often don’t have triggered events in the near past, by incorporating future events, these originally
192
      empty pixels are assigned a meaningful value, thereby increasing the information density of the re-
      sulting representation. Second, the first and last event within each time bin at each pixel are both
193
      fully utilized. Especially when the time range is small, this sampling strategy is highly representa-
194
      tive. Third, considering near past and future events simultaneously changes the basic representation
195   unit from time bin to intermediate probe times. Local speed estimations at these probe times use
196   central difference, reducing the estimation error to O(δt2 ) (detailed in the Supplementary Material).

      Furthermore, the flexibility of Labits enables its use with varying numbers of probe time points and
248
      time bin sizes, without the need to retrain the model used to predict instantaneous optical flows.
249   This adaptability stems from the fact that corresponding APLOF are generated separately for each
250   Labits layer, and variations in time bin duration just introduce a scaling factor to the perceived speed.
251   Subsequent deep learning models can readily adjust to any scaling effects introduced by variations
252   in the generated local optical flows. This combination of adaptability and precise flow prediction
253   enhances Labits’ utility as a versatile and powerful tool for event-based computer vision tasks.
254
      In conclusion, Labits overcomes the limitations of existing event representations by providing a
255   highly accurate, temporally precise, and robust way to capture motion information from event
256   streams. Its ability to handle both near-past and near-future events, coupled with its stability against
257   time normalization, allows it to deliver superior performance in dense trajectory estimation. By fully
258   harnessing the hardware advantages of event cameras, Labits sets a new benchmark for event-based
259   continuous dense trajectory estimation.

265   Event cameras are highly sensitive to motion, particularly instantaneous or local motion. In this
266   context, “local” refers to both spatial and temporal locality, aspects not captured by conventional
267   optical flow techniques. For instance, in the MultiFlow dataset, the optical flow ground truth at each
268   intermediate probe time is relative to the initial reference time point, representing cumulative motion
269   rather than instantaneous speed. In contrast, events triggered by the same moving object within a
      short time frame are closely tied to the object’s instantaneous speed, based on the variation in their


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      timestamps, rather than cumulative displacement. Therefore, to fully leverage event cameras for
271   tracking, local speed must be taken into account.
272
273   Labits retains substantial local motion information at active pixels both before and after the probe
274
      time points. Compared to voxel grids, the information encoded in Labits enables the main model
      to predict trajectories with greater accuracy. However, due to the sparsity of events, noise, and
275
      temporal occlusion, it can be challenging for a downstream task model to effectively decode this
276
      implicit information without any dedicated guidance.
277
278   To fully leverage Labits, we propose a general-purpose model component named the Labits-to-
279   APLOF Net, where APLOF stands for “Active Pixel Local Optical Flow.” This model component
280
      is specifically designed to utilize a single Labits layer as input for three main reasons: First, each
      Labits layer, generated at an intermediate probe time point, already contains sufficient information
281
      to infer the corresponding APLOF. Second, as we mainly focus on spatiotemporal locality, using a
282
      single layer and a shallow network naturally preserves that while keeping the model efficient and
283   enhancing generality. Finally, since the number of time bins in Labits can be freely adjusted by
284   the user, maintaining a single-channel input increases flexibility. This design makes the Labits-to-
285   APLOF Net a versatile module compatible with Labits.
286
      The Labits-to-APLOF Net is implemented based on U-Net (Ronneberger et al. (2015)). To improve
287
      both stability and efficiency, we employ instance normalization within each block, rather than batch
288
      normalization. This adaptation arises from shifting the channel dimension of Labits to the batch
289   dimension during inference, enabling the network to more effectively handle Labits while keeping
290   the single input channel. Since Labits contains rich information primarily around active pixels, local
291   speed estimation is most reliable in these areas. Thus, we apply an active pixel mask (APM) to
292   the generated APLOF features, considering only Labits pixels with absolute values below a certain
293   threshold β as active pixels. To ensure alignment between the low-resolution (LR) APLOF features
294   at the bottleneck and the pixel coordinates, we incorporate an auxiliary output head during training.
295   This head is trained to predict an LR version of the local speed flow based on the bottleneck features.
296

359
      feature maps. The time period corresponding to the events is evenly divided into B segments, each
      lasting milliseconds. Of these, the first M − 1 segments provide additional reference information,
360
      while the remaining B − M + 1 segments constitute the target time period, referred to as the context
361
      Labits block. If available, the RGB frame at the reference time is concatenated to the context Labits
362   block to enrich the feature set. Using a sliding window approach, the total B Labits layers are
363   grouped into J correlation Labits blocks, each containing M layers. Each block is also referred to
364   as a “view,” as it represents a distinct timestamp.
365
      In part two, correlation volumes are computed between the first view and subsequent views to
366
      facilitate the spatial-temporal correspondence search for initial pixels. Additionally, if RGB frames
367
      are provided, a correlation volume between the reference and target frames is also calculated. These
368   volumes are then utilized to iteratively refine the Bézier parameters.
369
370   In part three, the goal is to find a set of Bézier control points, P, for each initial pixel at the refer-
371
      ence time, tr . Initially, all control points are set to zero, positioning each pixel’s default trajectory
      as a straight line along the time axis. This part primarily involves a ConvGRU, which processes
372
      the context and correlation information, along with the Bézier parameters, and outputs the resid-
373

374   context volumes and APLOF features. During each subsequent iteration, the Bézier parameters are
375   progressively refined to achieve optimal estimation. The Bézier curve is defined as follows:

412   5        R ESULTS
413
414   Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. Our model is trained and evaluated on the MultiFlow dataset
415   (Gehrig et al. (2024)), which comprises 10,100 training and 2,000 test sequences, each including
416   paired RGB images, events, and optical flows relative to a reference time. With optical flow ground
417
      truths recorded every 10 ms, we can generate intermediate local optical flows for training the Labits-
      to-APLOF net, and ensure accurate supervision of the intermediate Bézier parameters. MultiFlow
418
      is currently the only available event dataset that supports dense trajectory prediction and active pixel
419
      local optical flow estimation simultaneously. The ground truth for pixel trajectories is provided rela-
420   tive to a reference time of 0.4 seconds (tref ), with trajectories calculated at 10-millisecond intervals
421   between tref (0.4 seconds) and ttar (0.9 seconds). Events occurring outside this range provide ad-
422   ditional context. To evaluate the trajectory-level estimation accuracy, beyond the standard EPE and
423   AE metrics, we also adopt Trajectory End-Point Error (TEPE) and Trajectory Angular Error (TAE).
424
      Labits-to-APLOF Net Results. The Labits-to-APLOF Net is supervised by high- and low-
425
      resolution APLOF ground truth calculated via the Equation 6, with a single Labits layer as its basic
426   input unit. This lightweight network (0.524M parameters) efficiently captures local movement in-
427   formation from Labits layers, exhibiting high-quality APLOF estimation. In the main trajectory
428   estimation pipeline, only the encoder part is used, reducing the parameter count to 0.293M. The
429   Labits-to-APLOF net is trained using a StepLR scheduler for 60 epochs and 75k iterations. Training
430   on an NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU took 35 hours, achieving a low error rate. The model converged
431   with a minimal total loss (Equation 7) of 0.084, consisting of LR APLOF L1 loss (0.056) and HR
      APLOF L1 loss (0.028). Additional qualitative results are detailed in the Supplementary Material.

      To evaluate the effectiveness of our approach, we compare it with four previously published base-
      lines: RAFT (Teed & Deng (2020)), RAFT-GMA (Jiang et al. (2021)), E-RAFT (Gehrig et al.
460
      (2021b)), and DCT-RAFT (Gehrig et al. (2024)). Specifically, RAFT and RAFT-GMA are frame-
461
      based approaches utilizing the RAFT architecture, whereas E-RAFT and DCT-RAFT are recent
462   event-based approaches. DCT-RAFT also has a version that takes both frames and events as input.
463   We select these baselines for comparison because they are all derived from the RAFT framework,
464   providing a consistent basis for evaluating our method’s performance.
465
      As presented in Table 2, our method, Labits-RAFT, outperforms other methods across all metrics
466
      by a remarkably large margin. Our proposed method reduces the TEPE by 49%, from 1.29 to
467
      0.66, compared to the previous state-of-the-art. Similarly, the Trajectory Angular Error (TAE) is
468   reduced by 49%, from 3.35 to 1.72. These results indicate that approaches directly predicting pixel
469   displacements are not suitable for accurate pixel trajectory estimation (e.g., RAFT, RAFT + GMA,
470   E-RAFT). While incorporating Bézier estimation into E-RAFT improves trajectory and two-view
471   metrics, the estimated flow still has room for improvement. DCT-RAFT, which integrates image
472   frames and introduces correlation features, effectively reduces errors in both trajectory and two-
473   view metrics. However, DCT-RAFT’s reliance on voxel grids leads to a significant loss of fine-
474   grained temporal information, representing a critical limitation. Finally, our method also achieves
475   improvements on two-view metrics (52% on EPE and 55% on AE), additionally demonstrating its
476
      superiority in accumulative optical flow estimation.

478   in dense continuous-time trajectory estimation: the novel Labits event representation and the Labits-
479   to-APLOF net. We carry out two sets of experiments: one excluding the Labits-to-APLOF network
480   and another further replacing Labits with voxel grids. The results, shown in the last four rows of
481   Table 2, demonstrate significant performance declines without these modules in both pure event and
482
      event+image scenarios, confirming the effectiveness of both Labits and APLOF features.

      Implementation Details. We implemented our models in PyTorch, training them on the MultiFlow
      dataset from scratch with AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter (2019)), gradient clipping in the
507
      range of [-1, 1], and a OneCycle learning rate scheduler. We adopted trajectory loss and two-view
508
      loss as detailed in (Gehrig et al. (2024)), supervising the training with 10 flow maps and employing
509   a Bézier curve of degree 10 for precise trajectory modeling. Our model configuration utilizes J = 6
510   correlation Labits blocks across time intervals from 0.4 to 0.9 seconds, quantizing time into N = 41
511   and M = 25 bins for context and correlation, respectively. To ensure consistency in evaluation, we
512   adopt the same setup as described in (Gehrig et al. (2024)). Training spanned 100 epochs and 250k
513   iterations, requiring approximately 40 hours on four NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPUs.
514
      Efficiency Analysis. In our systematic performance evaluation, Labits emerged as an exceptionally
515   efficient representation, adept at capturing dense and detailed motion information with minimal
516   computational delay. We assessed the processing times for various representations, as shown in
517   Figure 1, utilizing randomly sampled 500 event packets from the MultiFlow validation set, with each
518   packet focusing on the initial 100 milliseconds of data. Labits achieved an average execution time
519   of 0.220s, demonstrating comparable efficiency to the Voxel Grid (0.225s) and surpassing the more
520   computationally intensive TORE Volume (7.624s) and Time Surface (0.358s). Notably, simplest
521   Event Frame recorded the fastest execution time at 0.062s, followed by Event Count at 0.102s. All
522   the testing is conducted on the same device under the same condition. Although Labits is not the
523
      quickest, it offers a superior balance of speed and data richness compared to the others.

525   6   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we introduced Labits, a novel event representation that simultaneously retains fine-
528   grained temporal information, meaningful 2D visual patterns, and local speed cues. Labits is the
529   first to achieve this combination. We also developed the Labits-to-APLOF net, which accurately
530   converts Labits into active pixel local optical flows. Together, these innovations significantly im-
531   prove performance on event-based continuous-time dense trajectory estimation, with a remarkable
532
      49% reduction in trajectory end-point error compared to the top baseline models. The results high-
      light that, beyond model architecture, event representations also have a transformative impact on
533
      the final outcomes. While Labits’ strengths are most apparent in tasks that utilize intermediate mo-
534
      tion information, its adaptability to other event-based vision tasks remains an open area for future
535   research. This versatility positions Labits as a valuable asset for advancing event-based vision, offer-
536   ing numerous possibilities for further exploration and development. Detailed analysis of limitations
537   and future research directions could be found in the Supplementary Material.

1104

1105   level movements at numerous intermediate moments within a scene. These movements information,
1106   which we refer to as local motion anchors, play a crucial role in tasks that demand detailed motion

1109   is O(δt), while the one for central difference is O(δt2 ), meaning central difference provides higher
1110   accuracy.

       A.6.1    L IMITATIONS
1224   The proposed solution has specific use cases. Labits demonstrates excellent performance in dense
1225   continuous-time trajectory estimation tasks. However, this is not only due to our novel event repre-
1226   sentation and a well-designed model pipeline, but also because the task definition and the selected
1227   dataset inherently leverage Labits’ ability to preserve rich intermediate temporal information. In
1228   this task, where continuous motion trajectories of each pixel over the target time span are evaluated,
1229   and these intermediate states are included in the metrics, our solution significantly improves results.
1230   Conversely, for tasks less sensitive to intermediate states and focused only on final outcomes, such
1231   as object detection or optical flow estimation, Labits may not outperform some well-established rep-
1232
       resentations and solutions. In these cases, the additional fine-grained intermediate information may
       even become irrelevant noise. Different tasks require different types of information, and while our
1233
       solution is not a one-size-fits-all approach, it can exhibit substantial advantages for suitable tasks.
1234
1235   Labits omits event density information. Labits is designed to maximize the extraction and reten-
1236   tion of motion information at intermediate time points. However, this design sacrifices event density
1237   information, which might be crucial for certain computer vision tasks. Future work could explore
1238
       combining multiple representations to create a more generalizable framework that balances these
       trade-offs.
1239
1240   We did not conduct extensive adaptation experiments across various event camera-based vi-
1241   sion tasks. This paper focuses on providing an exceptional solution for the specific task of dense
       continuous-time trajectory estimation, rather than proposing a universal representation. Applying


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       Labits is not as simple as directly replacing previously used representations in various tasks. Instead,
1243   it requires tailored feature fusion designs for Labits, APLOF, and the tasks themselves. Redesign-
1244   ing and conducting training, testing, and ablation studies for diverse tasks is beyond the scope of a
1245   single conference paper and was not our intention. This work remains centered on the specific task
1246   highlighted in the title.

1249   A.6.2    F UTURE R ESEARCH D IRECTIONS
1250
       This paper introduces Labits along with a series of models and structures that fully exploit its ca-
1251
       pabilities. We have demonstrated its unparalleled suitability for dense continuous-time trajectory
1252   estimation and validated the effectiveness of Labits and its associated structures through compre-
1253   hensive ablation studies. Potential future research directions include:
1254
1255
       Expanding Labits Applications to Diverse Tasks Adapting Labits and its associated models to
       other similar tasks, such as human keypoint tracking, object trajectory tracking, or event-based video
1256
       interpolation. These adaptations would undoubtedly require redesigned model structures tailored to
1257
       each task’s specific needs.
1258
1259   Targeted Tracking Focused on Active Pixels Leveraging the properties of active pixels proposed
1260   in this work to generate representations for selective regions rather than globally. This approach
1261
       could enable more efficient tracking by focusing on active pixel clusters.
1262   Refinement of Labits Combining Labits with traditional event representations like Voxel Grid to
1263   achieve complementary strengths. Labits captures fine-grained temporal information for extracting
1264   local motion states at intermediate moments but lacks event density information. Conversely, Voxel
1265   Grid provides event density information but discards the fine-grained temporal precision that event
1266   cameras excel at. Combining these representations may open new possibilities for many tasks.
1267   Utlizing Polarity Information Labits, for efficiency and information density considerations, does
1268   not differentiate between events of different polarities. Exploring more optimized handling of
1269   polarity-specific events may further enhance its performance in relevant computer vision tasks.
1270
       In conclusion, the innovations proposed in this paper are versatile and can be extended or improved
1271   upon. We hope more researchers will contribute to developing better event representations, unlock-
1272   ing the full potential of event cameras.
