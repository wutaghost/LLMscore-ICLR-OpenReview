009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Precision medicine, such as patient-adaptive treatments utilizing medical images,
012            poses new challenges for image segmentation algorithms due to (1) the large vari-
013            ability across different patients and (2) the limited availability of annotated data for
014            each patient. In this work, we propose a data-efficient segmentation method to ad-
015            dress these challenges, namely Part-aware Personalized Segment Anything Model
016            (P2 SAM). Without any model fine-tuning, P2 SAM enables seamless adaptation
017            to any new patients relying only on one-shot patient-specific data. We introduce
018
               a novel part-aware prompt mechanism to select multiple-point prompts based on
               part-level features of the one-shot data, which can be extensively integrated into
019
               different promptable segmentation models, such as SAM and SAM 2. To fur-
020
               ther promote the robustness of the part-aware prompt mechanism, we propose
021            a distribution-similarity-based retrieval approach to determine the optimal num-
022            ber of part-level features for a specific case. P2 SAM improves the performance
023            by +8.0% and +2.0% mean Dice score within two patient-specific segmentation
024            tasks, and exhibits impressive generality across different domains, e.g., +6.4%
025            mIoU on the PerSeg benchmark. Code will be released upon acceptance.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Advances in modern precision medicine and healthcare have emphasized the importance of person-
031   alized treatment, aiming at adapting to the specific patient (Hodson, 2016). For instance, in radiation
032   therapy, patients undergoing multi-fraction treatment would benefit from longitudinal medical data
033
      analysis that helps timely adjust treatment planning specific to the individual patient (Sonke et al.,
      2019). To facilitate the treatment procedure, such analysis demands timely and accurate automatic
034
      segmentation of tumors and critical organs from medical images, which has underscored the role
035
      of computer vision approaches for medical image segmentation tasks (Hugo et al., 2016; Jha et al.,
036   2020). Despite the great progress made by previous works (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Isensee et al.,
037   2021; Dumitru et al., 2023), their focus remains on improving the segmentation accuracy within a
038   standard paradigm: trained on a large number of annotated data and evaluated on the in-distribution
039   validation set. However, personalized treatment presents unique challenges for segmentation algo-
040   rithms: (1) the large variability across different patients, and (2) the limited availability of annotated
041   training data for each patient. Overcoming these obstacles requires a segmentation approach that
042   can reliably generalize to out-of-distribution patients, in a data-efficient manner.
043   In this work, we address the unmet needs of the patient-specific segmentation by formulating it
044   as an in-context segmentation problem, leveraging the promptable segmentation mechanism inher-
045   ent in Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023). Under this objective, our method
046   seamlessly adapts to any new (out-of-distribution) patients relying only on one-shot patient-specific
047   prior data without requiring additional training, thus in a data-efficient manner. Moreover, such data
048   can be obtained in a standard clinical protocol (Chen et al., 2023), which will not burden clinical
049   researchers. To this end, we propose P2 SAM: Part-aware Personalized Segment Anything Model.
050   In the original prompt mechanism of SAM, as illustrated by Figure 1, a single-point prompt may
051   result in ambiguous prediction, indicating the limitation in both in-domain and out-of-domain appli-
052   cations (Zhang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). To alleviate the ambiguity problem, following the
053   statement in SAM, ”ambiguity is much rarer with multiple prompts”, we propose a novel part-aware
      prompt mechanism to meticulously select multiple-point prompts based on part-level features of

      On the other hand, when the number of parts is sub-optimal, either more or less, the chance of
      encountering outlier prompts may increase. An extreme case is to cluster each image patch into
079
      different groups, which renders a lot of outlier prompts (Liu et al., 2023). To make the part-aware

082   between the foreground feature of the prior data and the result obtained under the current part count.
083   This principle is motivated by the fact that tumors and normal organs manifest in distinct distribu-
084   tions within medical imaging technologies (Garcı́a-Figueiras et al., 2019).
085
      With the aforementioned designs, P2 SAM addresses a general challenge—ambiguity—in prompt-
086
      able segmentation models through a simple yet effective approach, benefiting both medical and
087   natural image domains. The key contributions of this work lie in three-fold:
088
089         1. We formulate the patient-specific segmentation as an in-context segmentation problem,
090            resulting in a data-efficient segmentation method, P2 SAM, which operates with only one-
091            shot prior data and requires no model fine-tuning.
092         2. We propose a novel part-aware prompt mechanism that meticulously selects multiple-point
093            prompts based on part-level features, combined with a distribution-similarity-based re-
094            trieval approach to determine the optimal number of part-level features for each case. These
095            two designs effectively mitigate the ambiguity problem in promptable segmentation models
096            and enable P2 SAM to adapt across different tasks, models, and domains.
097         3. Our method largely benefits real-world applications like patient-specific segmentation, one-
098            shot segmentation, and personalized segmentation. Experiment results demonstrate that
099            P2 SAM improves the performance by +8.0% and +2.0% mean Dice score in two patient-
100            specific segmentation tasks and achieves a new state-of-the-art result, i.e., 95.7% mIoU on
101
               the personalized segmentation benchmark PerSeg.

105   Segmentation Generalist. Over the past decade, various segmentation tasks including semantic
106   segmentation (Strudel et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023a), instance segmentation (He et al., 2017; Li et al.,
107   2022a; 2023b), and panoptic segmentation (Carion et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2021b;a; Li et al.,
      2022b) have been extensively explored for the image and video modalities. Motivated by the suc-

      cess of foundational language models (Radford et al., 2018; 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
109   2023), the computer vision research community is increasingly paying attention to developing more
110   generalized models that can tackle various vision or multi-modal tasks, or called foundation mod-
111   els (Li et al., 2022b; Oquab et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a;b; Kirillov et al., 2023).
112   Notably, Segment Anything model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) and its successor, SAM 2 (Ravi
113   et al., 2024) introduces a promptable model architecture, including the positive- and negative-point
114   prompt; the box prompt; and the mask prompt. SAM and SAM 2 emerge with an impressive zero-
115   shot interactive segmentation capability after pre-training on the large-scale dataset. Given the re-
116   markable generalization capacity, researchers within the medical image domain have been seeking
117
      to build foundational models for medical image segmentation (Wu et al., 2023; Wong et al., 2023;
      Wu & Xu, 2024; Zhang & Shen, 2024) upon them. Certain approaches (Ma et al., 2024a;b) have al-
118
      ready shown promising results: MedSAM (Ma et al., 2024a) has exhibited significant performance
119
      across various medical image segmentation tasks after fine-tuning SAM on an extensive medical
120   dataset. MedSAM 2 (Ma et al., 2024b) incorporates SAM 2 to segment a 3D medical image vol-
121   ume as video. However, whether these methods can achieve zero-shot performance as impressive as
122   SAM and SAM 2 remains an open question that requires further investigation (Ma et al., 2024b).
123
      In-Context Learning First introduced as a new paradigm in natural language processing (Brown
124
      et al., 2020), in-context learning allows the model to adapt to unseen input patterns with a few
125
      prompts and examples, without the need to fine-tune the model. Similar ideas (Li et al., 2023b;
126   Sonke et al., 2019; Rakelly et al., 2018) have been explored in other fields. In computer vision,
127   few-shot segmentation (Rakelly et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019b; Liu et al., 2020; Leng et al., 2024),
128   like PANet (Wang et al., 2019b), aims to segment new classes with only a few examples; in adaptive
129   therapy (Sonke et al., 2019), several works (Wang et al., 2019a; Elmahdy et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
130   2020; Chen et al., 2023) attempt to leverage limited patient-specific data to adapt a model to new pa-
131   tients, but these methods still require model fine-tuning in different manners. Recent advancements,
132   such as Painter (Wang et al., 2023a) and SegGPT (Wang et al., 2023b) pioneer novel in-context
133   learning approaches for vision tasks, enabling the timely segmentation of images based on speci-
134
      fied image-mask prompts. SEEM (Zou et al., 2024) further explores this concept by investigating
      different prompt modalities. More recently, PerSAM (Zhang et al., 2023) and Matcher (Liu et al.,
135
      2023) have utilized SAM to tackle few-shot segmentation through the in-context learning fashion.
136
      PerSAM introduces a novel task, known as personalized object segmentation (Zhang et al., 2023),
137   which aims at adapting SAM to new views of a specific object. However, PerSAM prompts SAM
138   with only a singular prompt, leading to the ambiguity problem (Kirillov et al., 2023) in the segmen-
139   tation results. On the other hand, Matcher enhances segmentation accuracy by utilizing multiple
140   sets of point prompts. However, Matcher’s prompt generation mechanism is based on patch-level
141   features. This mechanism makes Matcher dependent on DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) to generate
142   prompts, which is particularly pre-trained under a patch-level objective. Despite this, Matcher still
143   generates a lot of outlier prompts. Thus, Matcher relies on a complicated framework and lacks
144   flexibility and robustness when integrated into other vision backbones, including SAM.

157   only one-shot patient-specific prior data. As shown in Figure 2, such data can be obtained in a
158   standard clinical protocol, either from the initial visit of radiation therapy or the first frame of medi-

160   object. Given a target image, IT , our goal is to predict its mask MT , without additional human anno-
161   tation costs or model training burdens. This setting is also suitable for object-specific segmentation,
      where the target image represents a new view of the same object depicted in the prior data.

231   Retrieve the Optimal Number of Parts. Improvements of the part-aware prompt mechanism are
232   illustrated in Figure 4. The proposed approach can naturally avoid the ambiguous prediction intro-
233   duced by SAM (e.g., robot) and also improve precision (e.g., can). However, this approach may
234   occasionally result in outliers, as observed in the segmentation example in Figure 5, n=3. There-
235   fore, we propose a distribution-similarity-based retrieval approach to answer the question, ”How
236   many part-level features should we choose for each case?”. We assume the correct target fore-
237   ground feature FTf = FT ◦ MT , and the reference foreground feature FRf should belong to the same
238   distribution. This assumption is grounded in the fact that tumors and normal organs will be reflected
239   in distinct distributions by medical imaging technologies (Garcı́a-Figueiras et al., 2019), also ob-
240   served by the density of Hounsfield Unit value in Figure 5. To retrieve the optimal number of parts
241
      for a specific case, we first define N different part counts, n ∈ {1, · · · , N }, and obtain N sets of
242   part-aware target foreground features {{FTf }n }Nn=1 . Following WGAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017), we
243
      utilize Wasserstein distance to measure the distribution similarity between the reference foreground
244   feature FRf and each target foreground feature {FTf }n . We determine the final number of part-level
245
      features, n, with the smallest distance. The smaller distance value for the correct prediction in
      Figure 4 indicates this approach can be further extended to natural images.

248   3.3   A DAPT SAM TO M EDICAL I MAGE D OMAIN IF N EEDED
249
250   Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) is initially pre-trained on the SA-1B dataset.
251
      Despite the large scale, a notable domain gap persists between natural and medical images. In more
      realistic medical scenarios, clinic researchers could have access to certain public datasets (Aerts
252
      et al., 2015; Jha et al., 2020) tailored to specific applications, enabling them to fine-tune the model.
253
      Nevertheless, even after fine-tuning, the model can still be limited to generalize across various out-
254   of-distribution medical data from different institutions because of the large variability in patient
255   population, demographics, imaging protocol, etc., as mentioned in Section 1. P2 SAM can then be
256   flexibly plugged into the fine-tuned model to enhance robustness on testing cases.
257
      Specifically, when demanded, we utilize in-distribution datasets (Aerts et al., 2015; Jha et al.,
258
      2020) to adapt SAM into the medical image domain. We try full fine-tune, and Low-Rank adapta-
259
      tion (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) for further efficiency. During the fine-tuning, similar to Med-SA (Wu
260   et al., 2023), we adhere closely to the interactive training strategy outlined in SAM to maintain
261   the interactive ability. Details can be found in Appendix B. Then, we employ out-of-distribution
262   datasets (Bernal et al., 2015; Hugo et al., 2016) obtained from various institutions to mimic new
263   patient cases. Note that there is no further fine-tuning on these datasets.

      4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
271
272
      Dataset. We utilize a total of four medical datasets, including two in-distribution (i.d.) datasets: The
273
      NSCLC-Radiomics dataset (Aerts et al., 2015), collected for non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)
274   segmentation, contains data from 422 patients. Each patient has a 3-dimensional computed tomog-
275   raphy volume along with corresponding segmentation annotations. The Kvasir-SEG dataset (Jha
276   et al., 2020), contains 1000 labeled endoscopy polyp images, with different resolutions ranging
277   from 332 × 487 to 1920 × 1072. Two out-of-distribution (o.o.d) datasets from different institutions:
278   The 4D-Lung dataset (Hugo et al., 2016), collected for longitudinal analysis, contains data from 20
279   patients, within which 13 patients underwent multiple visits, 3 to 8 visits for each patient. For each
280   visit, a 3-dimensional computed tomography volume along with corresponding segmentation labels
281   is available. The CVC-ClinicDB dataset (Bernal et al., 2015), contains 612 labeled polyp images
282
      selected from 29 endoscopy videos, with a resolution of 384 × 288. i.d. datasets serve as the training
      dataset to adapt SAM to the medical domain, while o.o.d. datasets serve as unseen patient cases.
283
284   Patient-Specific Segmentation Tasks. We test P2 SAM under two patient-specific segmentation
285   tasks: NSCLC segmentation in the patient-adaptive radiation therapy and polyp segmentation in the
286   endoscopy video. For NSCLC segmentation, medical image domain adaptation will be conducted
287   on the i.d. dataset, NSCLC-Radiomics. For P2 SAM, experiments are then carried out on the o.o.d.
288
      dataset, 4D-Lung. We evaluate P2 SAM on patients who underwent multiple visits during treatment.
      For each patient, we utilize the image-mask pair from the first visit as the patient-specific prior
289
      data. For polyp segmentation, domain adaptation will be conducted on i.d. dataset, Kvasir-SEG.
290
      For P2 SAM, experiments are then carried out on o.o.d. dataset, CVC-ClinicDB. For each video, we
291   utilize the image-mask pair from the first stable frame as the patient-specific prior data.
292
293   Implementation Details. All experiments are conducted on A40 GPUs. For the NSCLC-Radiomics
294
      dataset, we extract 2-dimensional slices from the original computed tomography scans, resulting in
      a total of 7355 labeled images. As for the Kvasir-SEG dataset, we utilize all 1000 labeled images.
295
      We process two datasets following existing works (Hossain et al., 2019; Dumitru et al., 2023). Each
296
      dataset was randomly split into three subsets: training, validation, and testing, with an 80:10:10
297   percent ratio (patient-wise splitting for the NSCLC-Radiomics dataset to prevent data leak). The
298   model is initialized with the SAM’s pre-trained weights and fine-tuned on the training splitting using
299   the loss function proposed by SAM. We optimize the model by AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov &
300   Hutter, 2017) (β1 =0.9, β2 =0.999), with a weight decay of 0.05. We further penalize the SAM’s
301   encoder with a drop path of 0.1. We fine-tune the model for 36 epochs on the NSCLC-Radiomics
302   dataset and 100 epochs on the Kvasir-SEG dataset with a batch size of 4. The initial learning rate is
303   1e-4, and the fine-tuning process is guided by cosine learning rate decay, with a linear learning rate
304   warm-up over the first 10 percent epochs. More details are provided in Appendix C.
305   Summary. We test P2 SAM on o.o.d. datasets with three different SAM backbones: 1. SAM
306   pre-trained on the SA-1B dataset (Kirillov et al., 2023), denoted as Meta. 2. SAM adapted on
307   i.d. datasets with LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and 3. full fine-tune, denoted as LoRA and Full-Fine-
308   Tune, respectively. We compare P2 SAM against various methods, including previous approaches
309   such as the direct-transfer baseline; fine-tune on the prior data (Wang et al., 2019a; Elmahdy et al.,
310   2020; Wang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023); the one-shot segmentation method, PANet (Wang et al.,
311
      2019b); and concurrent methods that also utilize SAM, such as PerSAM (Zhang et al., 2023) and
      Matcher (Liu et al., 2023). For PANet, we utilize its align method for one-shot segmentation. For
312
      Matcher, we adopt its setting of FSS-1000 (Li et al., 2020). It is important to note that all baseline
313
      methods share the same backbone model as P2 SAM does for fairness.

497   Distribution Similarity Measurements (n=4). The cornerstone of our retrieval approach lies in
498   distribution similarity measurements. To evaluate the efficacy of various algorithms, in Table 7,
499   we juxtapose two distribution-related algorithms, namely Wasserstein distance (Rüschendorf, 1985)
500
      and Jensen–Shannon divergence (Menéndez et al., 1997), alongside a bipartite matching algorithm,
      Hungarian algorithm. Given foreground features from the reference image and the target image,
501
      we compute: 1. Wasserstein distance following the principles of WGAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017); 2.
502
      Jensen-Shannon divergence based on the first two principal components of each feature; 3. Hun-
503   garian algorithm after clustering these two sets of features into an equal number of groups. All
504   algorithms exhibit improvements in segmentation performance compared to the w.o. retrieval base-
505   line, while the Wasserstein distance is better in our context. Note that, the efficacy of the Jensen-
506   Shannon divergence further corroborates our assumption that foreground features from the reference
507   image and a correct target result should align in the same distribution, albeit it faces challenges when
508   handling the high-dimensional data.

512   to better results. This result aligns with current conclusions (Mazurowski et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
513   2024): In medical image analysis, the huge SAM may occasionally be outperformed by the large
514   SAM. On the other hand, for the PerSeg dataset, even utilizing the base SAM, P2 SAM achieves
515   higher accuracy compared to PerSAM with the huge SAM. These findings further underscore the
516   robustness of P2 SAM, particularly in scenarios where the model exhibits weaker representation, a
517
      circumstance more prevalent in medical image analysis.

519   5   C ONCLUSION
520
521
      We propose a data-efficient segmentation method, P2 SAM, to solve the patient-specific segmen-
522
      tation problem. With a novel part-aware prompt mechanism and a distribution-similarity-based
523   retrieval approach, P2 SAM can effectively integrate the patient-specific prior information into the
524   current segmentation task. P2 SAM demonstrates promising versatility in enhancing the backbone’s
525   generalization across various levels: 1. At the task level, P2 SAM enhances performance across dif-
526   ferent patient-specific segmentation tasks. 2. At the model level, P2 SAM can be integrated into
527   various promptable segmentation models, such as SAM, SAM 2, and SAM after domain adaptation.
528   3. At the domain level, P2 SAM performs effectively in both medical and natural image domains. We
529   discuss a potential limitation of P2 SAM in Appendix E. P2 SAM may face challenges when multiple
530   similar objects are present, a difficulty also encountered by other methods. While this scenario is
531
      uncommon in most patient-specific segmentation settings, we acknowledge this limitation and pro-
      pose a potential solution. In this work, to meet clinical requirements, we choose to adapt SAM to
532
      the medical imaging domain with public datasets. We opted not to adopt SAM 2, as it requires video
533
      data for fine-tuning, which is more costly. Additionally, treating certain patient-specific segmenta-
534   tion tasks as video tracking is inappropriate. In contrast, approaching patient-specific segmentation
535   as an in-context segmentation problem offers a more flexible solution for various patient-specific
536   segmentation tasks. Moreover, P2 SAM has demonstrated advantages when integrated with SAM 2
537   for polyp video segmentation even before domain adaptation, suggesting its potential to enhance
538   performance in methods of segmenting medical video and in methods of segmenting 3D medical
539   volumes as video. Further exploration of this potential is left for future work. We hope our work
      brings attention to the patient-specific segmentation problem within the research community.

821   A    SAM R EVIEW
822
823
      Overview. Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) comprises three main compo-
824   nents: an image encoder, a prompt encoder, and a mask decoder, denoted as Enc I , Enc P , and
825   Dec M . As a promptable segmentation model, SAM takes an image I and a set of human-given
826   prompts P as input. SAM predicts segmentation masks Ms by:
827
828
                                       Ms = Dec M (Enc I (I), Enc P (P ))                                (3)
829   During training, SAM supervises the mask prediction with a linear combination of focal loss (Lin
830   et al., 2017) and dice loss (Milletari et al., 2016) in a 20:1 ratio. When only a single prompt is pro-
831   vided, SAM generates multiple predicted masks. However, SAM backpropagates from the predicted
832   mask with the lowest loss. Note that SAM returns only one predicted mask when presented with
833   multiple prompts simultaneously.
834   Prompt Encoder Details. Enc I and Dec M primarily employ the Transformer (Vaswani, 2017;
835   Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) architecture. Here, we provide details on components in Enc P . Enc P
836   supports three prompt modalities as input: the point, box, and mask logit. The positive- and negative-
837   point prompts are represented by two learnable embeddings, denoted as Epos and Eneg , respectively.
838   The box prompt comprises two learnable embeddings representing the left-up and right-down cor-
839   ners of the box, denoted as Eup and Edown . In cases where neither the point nor box prompt is
840   provided, another learnable embedding Enot-a-point is utilized. If available, the mask prompt is
841   encoded by a stack of convolution layers, denoted as Emask ; otherwise, it is represented by a learn-
842
      able embedding Enot-a-mask .
843   Interactive Training. SAM employs an interactive training strategy. In the first iteration, either
844   a positive-point prompt, represented by Epos , or a box prompt, represented by {Eup , Edown }, is
845   randomly selected with equal probability from the ground truth mask. Since there is no mask prompt
846   in the first iteration, Epos or {Eup , Edown } is combined with Enot-a-mask and fed into Dec M . In
847   the follow-up iterations, subsequent positive- and negative-point prompts are uniformly selected
848
      from the error region between the predicted mask and the ground truth mask. SAM additionally
      provides the mask logit prediction from the previous iteration as a supplement prompt. As a result,
849
      {Epos , Eneg , Emask } is fed into Dec M during each iteration. There are 11 total iterations: one
850
      sampled initial input prompt, 8 iteratively sampled points, and two iterations where only the mask
851   prediction from the previous iteration is supplied to the model.

854   B    SAM A DAPTATION D ETAILS
855
856   In Section 3.3, we propose to adapt SAM to the medical image domain when it is needed, with full
857
      fine-tune (Full-Fine-Tune) and LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) (LoRA). For Full-Fine-Tune, we fine-tune all
      parameters in SAM backbone. For LoRA, we insert the LoRA module in the image encoder Enc I
858
      and only fine-tune parameters in the LoRA module and the mask decoder Dec M . Our fine-tuning
859
      objectives are as follows:
860
861         1. The model can accurately predict a mask even if no prompt is provided.
862
            2. The model can predict an exact mask even if only one prompt is given.
863
            3. The model maintains promptable ability.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      The training strategy outlined in SAM cannot satisfy all these three requirements: 1. The mask
865   decoder Dec M is not trained to handle scenarios where no prompt is given. 2. The approach to
866   resolving the ambiguous prompt by generating multiple results is redundant as we have a well-
867   defined segmentation objective. Despite that, we find a simple modification can meet all our needs:
868
869         1. In the initial iteration, we introduce a scenario where no prompt is provided to SAM. As a
870            result, {Enot-a-point , Enot-a-mask } is fed into Dec M in the first iteration.
871         2. To prevent Enot-a-point and Enot-a-mask from introducing noise when human-given
872            prompts are available, we stop their gradients in every iteration.
873
874
            3. We ensure that SAM always returns an exact predicted mask. As a result, the ambiguity
               problem does not exist in the model after fine-tuning.

901   The final number of positive-point and negative-point prompts is determined by our distribution-

903
      For LoRA and Full-Fine-Tune, the retrieval range is determined based on the validation set of the i.d.
904
      datasets. We uniformly sample positive-point and negative-point prompts on the ground-truth mask
905

906   becomes marginal, at which point this maximum number is set as the retrieval range for o.o.d. test

908   for these two types of models. This decision is informed by conclusions from previous works (Ma
909   et al., 2024a; Huang et al., 2024), which suggest that the background and semantic target can appear
910   very similar in CT images, and using too many negative-point prompts may confuse the model.
911

912   domain gap (Matsoukas et al., 2022) compared to SAM’s pre-trained dataset. Therefore, for Meta,
913   we use the same retrieval range as the Full-Fine-Tune large model. In contrast, on the 4D-Lung
914   dataset, CT images are in grayscale, leading to a significant domain gap (Matsoukas et al., 2022)
915   compared to SAM’s pre-trained dataset. Consequently, we set the retrieval range for positive-point
916   prompts to 2 to avoid outliers and fixed the number of negative-point prompts to a large constant
917   (i.e., 45) rather than a range, to ensure the model focuses on the semantic target. These values were
      not further tuned.

997
       In this section, we want to discuss a potential limitation of P2 SAM. P2 SAM demonstrates improve-
998
       ments in the backbone’s generalization across domain, task, and model levels. At the task level,
999
       we have already shown how P2 SAM enhances performance for NSCLC segmentation in patient-
1000   adaptive radiation therapy and polyp segmentation in endoscopy videos. However, when addressing
1001   specific tasks that involve multiple similar targets, P2 SAM may fail. Although this scenario is un-
1002   common in patient-specific segmentation, we acknowledge that P2 SAM faces the same challenge
1003   of handling multiple similar objects as other methods (Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). In Fig-
1004   ure 15, we present an example of single-cell segmentation on the PhC-C2DH-U373 dataset (Maška
1005   et al., 2014), which goes beyond the patient-specific setting. In Figure 15, the second row illustrates
1006   that P2 SAM fails to segment the target cell due to the presence of many similar cells in the field of
1007   view. However, given the slow movement of the cell, we can leverage its previous information to
1008
       regularize the current part-aware prompt mechanism. The third row in Figure 15 demonstrates that
       when using the bounding box from the last frame, originally propagated from the reference frame,
1009
       to regularize the part-aware prompt mechanism in the current frame, P2 SAM achieves strong per-
1010
       formance on the same task. Since the bounding box for the first frame can be generated from the
1011   ground truth mask, which is already available, this regularization incurs no additional cost. Utilizing
1012   such tailored regularization incorporating various prompt modalities, we showcase our approach’s
1013   flexible applicability to other applications.

       Interactive Segmentation for Medical Images. Complementary to the works discussed in Sec-
1018
       tion 2, several studies (Butoi et al., 2023; Wong et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a;b; Wu & Xu, 2024)
1019
       have aimed to develop promptable segmentation models specifically for medical image segmenta-
1020   tion. UniverSeg (Butoi et al., 2023) utilizes a support set to provide additional information to the
1021   model during the test time. In this work, we did not include UniverSeg as a baseline method be-
1022   cause our problem setting provides only a single image-mask pair, and UniverSeg’s performance
1023   significantly declines under such conditions. Moreover, UniverSeg employs a different backbone
1024   model and training objective, making it challenging to test on our dataset. Other methods, such
1025   as ScribblePrompt (Wong et al., 2023), One-Prompt (Wu & Xu, 2024), and MedSAM2 (Ma et al.,
       2024b), primarily focus on interactively segmenting medical images. In contrast, our work presents


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       an effective approach that leverages patient-specific prior data to address segmentation for out-of-
1027   distribution patient samples that lie outside the training distribution. Among them, we have chosen
1028   MedSAM Ma et al. (2024a) for comparison in Table 3, as it was pre-trained on a large-scale medical
1029   image dataset and supplemented with a human-given box prompt during inference. It is worth noting
1030   that other methods either utilized smaller pre-training datasets or were not available at the time this
1031   work was conducted. On the other hand, utilizing other prompt modalities, such as scribble, mask,
1032   and box, presents challenges for solving the patient-specific segmentation problem, as it is difficult
1033   to represent prior data in these formats. In this work, we adopt a more flexible prompt modality:
1034   point prompts. Although it may be possible to convert our multiple-point prompts into a scribble
1035
       prompt by connecting them together, we leave the exploration of this direction for future work.
1036   MedSAM as a Strong Baseline. At the outset, we would like to clarify that this paper focuses
1037   on the task of external validation (o.o.d.), where the testing dataset differs from the distribution of
1038   the training dataset. In this scenario, the model’s generalization ability becomes critical for achiev-
1039   ing better performance. We acknowledge that MedSAM is widely used as a baseline across many
1040   benchmarks (Antonelli et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2022). However, these comparisons primarily focus
1041
       on internal (i.d.) validation. MedSAM has the potential to outperform many models on external
       validation sets due to its pre-training on a large-scale medical image dataset. While there is no
1042
       direct evidence to confirm this, DuckNet (Dumitru et al., 2023) (Table 1 v.s. Table 5) suggests
1043
       that large-scale pre-trained models generally outperform others on external validation sets, even if
1044   they lag behind on internal validation. The 4D-Lung dataset (Hugo et al., 2016) is a relatively new
1045   benchmark for longitudinal data analysis, and no standard benchmark for comparison was avail-
1046   able at the time this work was conducted. The results from MedSAM in Table 3 could serve as a
1047   strong baseline, particularly when supplemented with human-provided box prompts. Therefore, we
1048   consider MedSAM a reliable baseline for comparison, especially for external validation, given its
1049   generalization ability.
1050   Different SAM Adaptation Methods. The main purpose of P2 SAM is to leverage one-shot patient-
1051   specific prior data to address segmentation for o.o.d. patient samples. Under this objective, the SAM
1052   adaptation is an optional and orthogonal procedure that can be employed when limited labeled data
1053   is available to further enhance the pre-trained model. In this work, we test full fine-tune method and
1054   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) for parameter-efficient fine-tuning. When compared with other parameter-
1055   efficient fine-tuning strategies like Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019) and Prompt-Tuning Li & Liang
1056   (2021), LoRA integrates the learned parameters directly into the original model, ensuring no ad-
1057   ditional latency during inference. Since P2 SAM can be integrated with any backbone model that
1058
       supports the point-prompt modality, it is compatible with various parameter-efficient fine-tuning
       methods, such as Adapter or Prompt-Tuning, as adopted in Med-SA (Wu et al., 2023).
