011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have achieved significant success in processing
014            graph-structured data but often lack interpretability, limiting their practical ap-
015            plicability. We introduce the Graph Distributional Analytics (GDA) framework,
016
               leveraging novel combinations of scalable techniques to enhance GNN explain-
               ability. The integration of Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) graph kernels with distribu-
017
               tional distance analysis enables GDA to efficiently quantify graph data distribu-
018
               tions, while capturing global structural complexities without significant computa-
019            tional costs. GDA creates high-dimensional embeddings employing WL kernels,
020            measures the distribution of distances from measures of categorical central ten-
021            dency, and assigns distribution scores to quantify each graph’s deviation from
022            this vector We evaluate GDA on the ENZYMES, ogbg-ppa, and MalNet-Tiny
023            datasets. Our experiments demonstrate GDA not only accurately characterizes
024            graph distributions but also outperforms baseline methods in identifying specific
025            structural features responsible for misclassifications. This comprehensive analysis
026            provides deeper insights into how training data distributions affect model perfor-
027
               mance, particularly with out-of-distribution (OOD) data. By revealing the under-
               lying structural causes of GNN predictions through a novel synergy of established
028
               techniques, GDA enhances transparency and offers a practical tool for practition-
029
               ers to build more interpretable and robust graph-based models. Our framework’s
030            scalability, efficiency, and ability to integrate with various embedding methods
031            make it a valuable addition to the suite of tools available for GNN analysis.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful framework for learning from graph-
037   structured data, with applications ranging from social network analysis to molecular biology (Wu
038   et al., 2020). While GNNs excel at learning complex, non-Euclidean relationships, their decision-
039   making processes remain largely opaque (Bodria et al., 2023). This lack of interpretability poses sig-
040   nificant challenges, especially in critical domains like bioinformatics and healthcare, where model
041   decisions must be both accurate and explainable (Sanchez-Lengeling et al., 2020; Adoni et al., 2020).
042   Explainability is crucial for understanding why models make specific predictions, detecting potential
043   biases, and improving model generalization (Ju et al., 2024).
044   In recent years, efforts have been made to develop explainability tools tailored for GNNs. However,
045   most existing methods focus on explaining node- or edge-level predictions, relying on gradient-
046   based or perturbation-based approaches (Rajabi & Kafaie, 2022). While useful in some contexts,
047   these methods struggle with graph-level predictions, particularly in datasets where structural diver-
048   sity within categories complicates classification (Georgousis et al., 2021). Furthermore, many of
049   these tools are computationally expensive and require significant customization to adapt to different
050
      datasets, limiting their scalability and practical applicability (Agarwal et al., 2022).
051   This paper introduces Graph Distributional Analytics (GDA), a novel framework designed to ad-
052   dress the limitations of existing GNN explainability methods. GDA leverages Weisfeiler-Leman
053   graph kernels to embed graph structures into high-dimensional vector spaces, allowing for the char-
      acterization of structural distributions within graph categories. By analyzing the distribution of


                                                        1
054
      graph embeddings, GDA provides insights at both population and sample levels, offering a scalable
055   and interpretable approach to understanding GNN model behavior.
056
057   Through a series of experiments on the ENZYMES, MalNet-Tiny, and ogbg-ppa datasets, we
058
      demonstrate how GDA can reveal structural anomalies and distributional shifts that contribute to
      model underperformance. Unlike previous explainability methods, GDA not only identifies poten-
059
      tial sources of confusion within the dataset but also offers a preemptive tool for improving data
060
      splits and detecting out-of-distribution samples. The contributions of this paper are threefold: (1)
061   the introduction of GDA for graph-level explainability, (2) validation of GDA’s utility through case
062   studies on real-world datasets, and (3) a discussion of how GDA can inform future research into
063   scalable, interpretable GNN models.

071
072
073   Demystifying neural network decision-making is a Herculean task, particularly for non-Euclidean
074   data, where complex interconnections stymie explainability (Adoni et al., 2020). Current explain-
075   ability methods demand significant computational resources (Sanchez-Lengeling et al., 2020), and
076   the explanations offered by these tools tend to be approximate and difficult to interpret (Li et al.,
077   2022). If explainability tools are difficult to explain, their usefulness is questionable. Nearly all
078   methods require extensive tailoring to specific datasets and models which reduces practicability for
079   diverse, real-world environments (Agarwal et al., 2022). Despite increased community interest, most
080
      libraries have prioritized accuracy benchmarks over explainability (Agarwal et al., 2023).
081   Gradient-based explainers, such as Grad (Simonyan et al., 2014), Grad-CAM (Pope et al., 2019),
082   GuidedBP (Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019), and Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017),
083   highlight specific feature importances by backpropagating gradients. These methods work well for
084   image or node classification but struggle with graph-level predictions (Kakkad et al., 2023), which
085   are critical for datasets like MalNet-Tiny, where node and edge features are absent.
086   Perturbation-based explainers, such as GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019), PGExplainer (Luo
087   et al., 2024), SubgraphX (Yuan et al., 2021), and GraphLIME (Huang et al., 2023), evaluate how
088   model predictions change in response to input perturbations. D4Explainer (Chen et al., 2024) of-
089   fers a more efficient method by using a discrete denoising diffusion model tailored towards in-
090   distribution explanations. While these methods offer valuable insights, they are computationally
091   expensive and typically explore only a fraction of possible perturbations, often missing the struc-
092
      tural nuances that impact graph-level decisions (Munikoti et al., 2023).
093   Surrogate-based explainers, like PGMExplainer (Vu & Thai, 2020), use simpler models such as
094   Bayesian networks to approximate GNN behavior. Although they provide interpretability, they face
095   scalability challenges when dealing with high-dimensional probability distributions, and they require
096   substantial tuning to capture relevant patterns in large datasets (Zhu et al., 2022).
097   Several gaps remain in GNN explainability. First, most approaches focus on node- and edge-level
098   tasks, neglecting graph-level explainability crucial for structural prediction problems (Pope et al.,
099   2019). Additionally, their computational inefficiency renders them impractical for dynamic, large-
100   scale graph environments (Kazemi et al., 2020). Finally, due to the difficulty of characterizing
101   graph data, the literature primarily focuses on explainability for Euclidean data, which leaves non-
102   Euclidean datasets like MalNet-Tiny underexplored (Kakkad et al., 2023).
103   Graph Distributional Analytics (GDA) addresses these issues by offering scalable, dataset-agnostic
104   explanations for both population- and sample-level predictions with linear time complexity, making
105   it suitable for real-world applications. GDA also tackles the challenge of out-of-distribution (OOD)
106   detection, enabling better interpretation of model behavior on unseen data by characterizing graph
107   distributions. GDA fills this gap by analyzing graph structures without relying on node or edge
      features, providing a more versatile approach for complex, graph-structured data.

213   3.2   O UTLIER D ETECTION AND D ISTRIBUTION A NALYSIS
214
215   In the context of Graph Distributional Analytics (GDA), we define outliers as graphs whose struc-
      tural properties significantly deviate from the central tendency of their class. Using the embeddings


                                                           4
216
      generated by the Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) kernel, we quantify these deviations based on cosine sim-
217   ilarity between the graph embeddings and the mean embedding of the class. Additionally, we assess
218   abnormal distributions within classes using kurtosis, a measure of how heavily tailed a distribution
219   is compared to a normal distribution.
220
221   3.2.1    O UTLIER D ETECTION
222
223   We define outliers as graphs whose similarity score falls significantly below the average similarity
224
      for the class. Specifically, a graph G ∈ GC is considered an outlier if:
225
226                                             Sc (G) < µSc − ασSc ,
227
228   where µSc and σSc are the mean and standard deviation of cosine similarity scores across all graphs
229
      in class C, and α is a user-defined threshold (typically set to 2 or 3) that determines how many
      standard deviations away from the mean qualifies as an outlier. This method ensures that graphs
230
      with significantly lower similarity to the class mean are identified as outliers, which can help explain
231
      misclassifications or structural anomalies in the dataset.
232
233

234
235   To detect abnormal distributions within a class, we use kurtosis, ξ(Gc ) which measures the ”tailed-
236   ness” of the distribution of cosine similarity scores. High kurtosis indicates that the distribution has
237   heavy tails, meaning there are more extreme outliers than expected under a normal distribution. Low
238   kurtosis suggests that the distribution has lighter tails, indicating fewer extreme values.
239   Kurtosis for a set of cosine similarity scores {Sc (G) | G ∈ GC } is defined as:

      3.3     P OST-H OC S TRUCTURAL ATTRIBUTION FOR M ISCLASSIFIED G RAPHS
256
257   For misclassified graphs, GDA provides a post-hoc explanation by tracing structural deviations.
258   By rerunning the WL kernel with degree sequence tracking, we can identify specific substructures
259   responsible for classification errors. The process involves examining how node labels evolve through
260   each iteration, enabling us to pinpoint graph substructures that deviate from the class norm. This
261   structural attribution mechanism allows for an interpretable analysis of misclassifications, offering
262   insights into how structural features influence GNN predictions.

      for explaining GNN performance. GDA provides insights into model behavior and data distribu-

      4.2     R ESULTS
359
360   In this section, we present a comprehensive analysis of how Graph Distributional Analytics (GDA)
361   was applied across multiple datasets to examine structural anomalies and their impact on model per-
362   formance. The experiments focus on understanding population-level distribution shifts and sample-
363   level misclassifications in the ENZYMES, MalNet-Tiny, and ogbg-ppa datasets. All experiments
364   were conducted using the GraphSAGE and GIN architectures to maintain consistency and compa-
365   rability across datasets.
366
367   4.2.1    P OPULATION L EVEL A NALYSIS
368
369
      The population-level analysis begins with the ENZYMES dataset, where GDA was applied to detect
      abnormal distribution patterns within the graph embeddings. A key observation in this dataset was
370
      the bimodal distribution found in Category 1 that presented a high kurtosis value, corresponding
371
      to the transferase enzymes. Transferases are a functional definition, not a structural definition, so
372   while assigned to the same category, they exhibit significant structural heterogeneity. The existence
373   of two distinct clusters within this category suggested that the enzyme structures were quite different,
374   despite their shared functional role. This bimodal distribution, which was confirmed by the structural
375   representations are shown in Figure ??. This finding is consistent with biochemical literature that
376   highlights the variability within enzyme families (Giegé et al., 2012; Breton et al., 2006). The
377   structural diversity in transferases led to underperformance in this category, with the GNN models
      struggled to capture the dual signals introduced by these two clusters.

399

      accuracy. This observation aligns with prior work showing that structural complexity and variability
400
      within functional groups can lead to model confusion (Chen & Wu, 2022; Ji et al., 2021).
401
402   Moving to the MalNet-Tiny dataset, we found that three out of five categories exhibited relatively
403   normal distributions with cosine similarities clustered near 1 and low kurtosis. However, two cate-
404   gories showed significant divergence, with some graph embeddings nearly orthogonal to their class
405
      mean vectors, as seen in Figure 2. These outliers indicated the presence of structurally distinct
      graphs that deviated from the majority of samples in their respective categories. Our hypothesis
406
      was that these outliers were injecting noise into the model, leading to poorer performance in those
407
      categories. To test this, we removed the outliers and retrained the models. While the improvements
408   were modest, the reduction in false positives was notable. We hypothesize that the model was al-
409   ready learning to ignore these outliers during training in the baseline runs, as their removal only
410   reflected an equivalent reduction in false negative samples. This observation reinforces the idea
411   that models can effectively disregard noisy samples under certain conditions, but identifying and
412   removing them still contributes to cleaner classification metrics.
413
      A related experiment involved analyzing distribution shifts between the training, validation, and test
414   sets in the MalNet-Tiny dataset. GDA revealed significant shifts in the distributions, with the train-
415   ing set embeddings clustering more tightly around the mean compared to the test and validation sets.
416   These shifts, shown in Figure 3, likely caused overfitting, as the model was able to perform well on
417   the more homogeneous training set but struggled to generalize to the more diverse test and validation
418   samples. To address this, we restructured the dataset splits to ensure more consistent distributions
419   across the sets. After retraining the models, we observed an average 4.3% improvement in perfor-
420   mance over the baseline across 10 runs, using both GraphSAGE and GIN architectures. These re-
421   sults emphasize the critical importance of ensuring distributional consistency between dataset splits
422
      to prevent overfitting and improve generalization, as noted in previous studies on distributional shifts
      in machine learning (Ding et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2024).

475   5      C ONCLUSION
476
477
      Graph Distributional Analytics (GDA) offers a novel and scalable framework for understanding
478
      the structural complexities that influence model performance in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs).
479
      By leveraging Weisfeiler-Leman graph kernels to embed graphs and analyze their distributions,
480   GDA provides insights at both population and sample levels. The experiments conducted on the
481   ENZYMES, MalNet-Tiny, and ogbg-ppa datasets demonstrate that GDA can effectively identify
482   structural anomalies, distributional shifts, and misclassified samples that traditional methods may
483   overlook. While GDA does not prescribe specific solutions, it offers a powerful tool for preemptive
484   data analysis, improving dataset splits, and detecting out-of-distribution samples. Future work will
485   focus on integrating GDA with more systematic model refinement strategies and further exploring
      its applications in real-world GNN tasks.

699   A.4     T IME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS OF GDA
700
701   A detailed analysis of the time complexity for the Graph Distributional Analytics (GDA) process is
      essential for understanding its computational efficiency.


                                                     13
702
      A.4.1    W EISFEILER -L EMAN E MBEDDING P ROCESS
703
704   For each graph Gi = (Vi , Ei ) in the dataset H, the Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) embedding process is
705   performed. The embedding involves iterating over the graph’s nodes, refining their labels through
706   the WL kernel, and aggregating these labels to form the final graph embedding. The time complexity
707   for this embedding process is given by:
708
709                                         TWL (Gi ) = 5h · |Vi | + 1                                   (4)
710
711
      where: - h is the number of iterations of the WL kernel, - |Vi | is the number of nodes in graph Gi .
712   This accounts for the operations required to hash and sort node labels during each iteration.
713
714   A.4.2    M EAN V ECTOR C ALCULATION
715
716
      The calculation of mean vectors for each classification category involves summing the embeddings
      of all graphs within the same category. Let: - m represent the number of classification categories, -
717
      |H| represent the total number of graphs in the dataset H.
718
719   The time complexity for computing the mean vectors is:
720
721                                             Tmean = m + |H|                                          (5)
722
723   This reflects the time required to iterate over all graphs and categories to compute their respective
724   mean embeddings.
725
726
      A.4.3    C OSINE S IMILARITY C ALCULATION
727   Determining the cosine similarity for each graph with respect to its classification category mean
728   vector is a key step in GDA. The time complexity for this operation across all graphs is:
729
730
                                               Tcosine = m + 2|H|                                        (6)
731
732   This accounts for the multiplication and summation operations necessary to compute the cosine
733   similarity between the graph embeddings and the category mean vectors.
734
735   A.4.4    D ISTRIBUTION S CORE C ALCULATION
736
      Calculating the distribution score for each graph involves standardizing its cosine similarity relative
737
      to the category’s standard deviation. The time complexity for this step is:
738
739
740                                            Tscore = m + 3|H|                                         (7)
741
      This reflects the additional operations required for calculating and applying the standard deviation
742   to each graph’s cosine similarity.
