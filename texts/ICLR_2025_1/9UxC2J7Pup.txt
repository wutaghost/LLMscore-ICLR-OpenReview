010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            One explanation for the strong generalization ability of neural networks is implicit
013            bias. Yet, the definition and mechanism of implicit bias in non-linear contexts
014            remains little understood. In this work, we propose to characterize implicit bias
015            by the count of connected regions in the input space with the same predicted label.
016
               Compared with parameter-dependent metrics (e.g., norm or normalized margin),
               region count can be better adapted to nonlinear, overparameterized models, because
017
               it is determined by the function mapping and is invariant to reparametrization.
018
               Empirically, we found that small region counts align with geometrically simple
019            decision boundaries and correlate well with good generalization performance.
020            We also observe that good hyper-parameter choices such as larger learning rates
021            and smaller batch sizes can induce small region counts. We further establish the
022            theoretical connections and explain how larger learning rate can induce small
023            region counts in neural networks.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      One mystery in deep neural networks lies in their ability to generalize, despite having significantly
029
      more learnable parameters than the number of training examples (Zhang et al., 2017a). The choice of
030   network architectures, including factors such as nonlinearity, depth, and width, along with training
031   procedures like initialization, optimization algorithms, and loss functions, can result in vastly diverse
032   generalization performances (Sutskever et al., 2013; Smith et al., 2017; Wilson et al., 2017; Li et al.,
033   2019). The varied generalization abilities exhibited by neural networks are often explained by many
034   researchers through the theory of implicit bias (Brutzkus et al., 2017; Soudry et al., 2018). Implicit
035   bias refers to inherent tendencies of how the network learns and generalizes from the training data,
036   even without explicit regularizations or constraints.
037   The implicit bias of linear neural networks has been extensively studied. One of the classical setting is
038   linear classification with logistic loss. Brutzkus et al. (2017); Soudry et al. (2018); Arora et al. (2019)
039   show that the parameter converges to the direction that maximizes the L2 margin. For regression
040   problems, it is proved that gradient descent or stochastic gradient descent converges to a parameter
041   that is closest to the initialization in terms of L2 norm (Gunasekar et al., 2018a). The results from the
042   linear regression model can be extended to deep linear neural networks by generalizing the definition
043   of min-norm and max-margin solutions (Ji & Telgarsky, 2018a; Vaskevicius et al., 2019; Woodworth
044
      et al., 2020).
045   Compared to linear models, defining implicit biases in non-linear networks poses significant chal-
046   lenges. One line of work studies homogeneous networks and demonstrates that gradient flow solutions
047   converge to a KKT point of the max-margin problem (Lyu & Li, 2019; Ji & Telgarsky, 2020; Wang
048   et al., 2021; Jacot et al., 2022). Further research extends this analysis, showing that gradient flow
049   converges to a max-margin solution under various norms (Ongie et al., 2019; Chizat & Bach, 2020).
050
      Other studies focus on describing the implicit bias of neural networks using sharpness, such as (Foret
      et al., 2020; Montúfar et al., 2022; Andriushchenko et al., 2023).
051
052   We note that previous definitions of implicit bias in neural networks mostly focus on certain metrics
053   of network parameters. Such approaches enable explicit analyses of training trajectories, but face
      new challenges when applied to nonlinear networks: reparametrization of the network may preserve

      Motivated by the above studies, we instead focus on leveraging the decision boundaries in the input
      space to characterize implicit bias. Our research identifies a metric called region count, which is
074
      defined by the average number of regions in the predictor’s decision boundary (See Figure 1). We
075
      select a low-dimensional space, use the neural network to predict the labels of all points within this
076   space, and define the number of connected components with the same label as the region count in
077   that subspace. This definition differs from previous studies on the number of linear regions (Hanin
078   & Rolnick, 2019a;b; Safran et al., 2022) which is defined as the set of inputs that correspond to the
079   same activation pattern in the network. We find that this region count has a strong correlation with
080   the generalization gap, defined as the difference in percentage between the training and test errors.
081   The experiments in Figure 1 suggests that models with fewer region counts tend to generalize better.
082   Furthermore, we show that neural networks trained with large learning rate or small batch size, which
083   are typically deemed as beneficial for generalization, are biased towards solutions that have small
084   region counts. Therefore, region count empirically serves as an accurate generalization metric as well
085   as an implicit bias indicator. We also provide theoretical analyses to explain this phenomenon. We
086   prove that for two-layer ReLU neural networks, gradient descent with large learning rate induces a
087   small region count, which accords well with our empirical findings.
088   The main contributions of this paper are listed as follows:
089
090         1. We introduce a novel measure of implicit bias via the region count in the input space.
091            Through extensive experiments, we verify a strong correlation between region count and
092            the generalization gap. This correlation remains robust across different learning methods,
093            datasets, training parameters, and counting methods.
094         2. We assess the factors that induce small region count, discovering that training with larger
095            learning rates and smaller batch sizes typically results in fewer regions. This provides a
096            possible cause for the implicit bias in neural networks.
097         3. We conduct theoretical analyses on region counts, and show that for two-layer ReLU neural
098            networks, gradient descent with large learning rate induces a small region count.

103   Implicit Bias of Linear Neural Network The implicit bias in linear neural networks are thoroughly
104   investigated in recent works. For linear logistic regression on linearly separable data, full-batch
105   gradient descent converges in the direction of the maximum margin solution (Soudry et al., 2018).
106   This foundational work has various follow-ups, including extensions to non-linearly-separable data (Ji
107   & Telgarsky, 2018b; 2019), stochastic gradient descent (Nacson et al., 2019), and other loss functions
      and optimizers (Gunasekar et al., 2018a).

108
      These findings in linear logistic regression are generalized to deep linear networks. For fully-
109   connected neural networks with linear separable data, Ji & Telgarsky (2018a) show that the direction
110   of weight also converges to L2 max-margin solution. For linear diagonal networks, the gradient
111   flow maximizes the margin with respect to a specific quasi-norm that is related to the depth of
112   network (Gunasekar et al., 2018b; Woodworth et al., 2020; Pesme et al., 2021), leading to a bias
113   towards sparse linear predictors as the depth goes to infinity. This sparsity bias also exists in linear
114   convolutional networks (Gunasekar et al., 2018b; Yun et al., 2020).
115
116   Implicit Bias of Non-linear Neural Network The non-linearity of modern non-linear neural
117   networks pose challenges to studying its implicit bias. Initial works in this area (Lyu & Li, 2019; Ji &
118   Telgarsky, 2020) focus on homogeneous networks. These studies show that with exponentially-tailed
119   classification losses, both gradient flow and gradient descent converge directionally to a KKT point
120   in a maximum-margin problem. Further studies, for instance in (Wang et al., 2021), consider a more
121   general setup that includes different optimizers and prove that both Adam and RMSProp are capable
122   of maximizing the margin in neural networks while Adagrad is not. Ongie et al. (2019); Chizat
123   & Bach (2020) showcased a bias towards maximizing the margin in a variation norm for infinite-
124
      width two-layer homogeneous networks. Lyu et al. (2021); Jacot et al. (2022) identified margin
      maximization in two-layer Leaky-ReLU networks trained with linearly separable and symmetric
125
      data. More recent investigations into non-linear neural networks, such as (Jacot, 2022), focus on
126
      the homogeneity of the non-linear layer, demonstrating an implicit bias characterized by a novel
127   non-linear rank.
128
129
130   Region Counts of Neural Network Many previous works focus on calculating the linear regions
131   of neural networks (Hanin & Rolnick, 2019a;b). A linear region is a set of inputs that share the same
132
      activation pattern in the network. Safran et al. (2022) proves that for a two-layer ReLU network
      with width r, gradient flow will converge directionally to a network characterized by no more than
133
      O(r) linear regions. Serra et al. (2018) and Cai et al. (2023) explain that maximizing the number
134
      of linear regions can lead to better-performing networks, and they explore how network structures
135   can be designed to achieve more linear regions. The number of linear regions is independent of
136   the network’s label output, focusing more on its representational capacity rather than generalization
137   ability. In contrast, we define decision regions as connected areas in the input space that correspond
138   to the same label, and our work explores the relationship between the number of regions and the
139   generalization gap.
140
      The paper (Nguyen et al., 2018) is the most relevant to our work, as it similarly defines decision
141   regions corresponding to label predictions of neural networks. They define decision regions in
142   the entire input space, whereas our definition and counting method focus on a subspace. Their
143   conclusion states that, under certain conditions, each label has only one connected decision region in
144   the entire space. However, two points being connected in the entire space does not imply they are
145   connected in a subspace. For instance, two points may be connected in a three-dimensional space,
146   but a two-dimensional cross-section may not provide a connecting path. Thus, we observe multiple
147   regions in 1D or 2D subspaces and find a strong correlation with generalization ability.

      The definition of margin may also be improved to the input-space margin, i.e., the ℓ2 distance of
194   input data x to decision boundary defined by the classifier, which is able to characterize the quality
195   and robustness of the classifier. This metric is invariant to reparameterization and therefore more
196   intrinsic to the underlying classifier. However, due to the highly nonconvex loss landscape, the
197   input-space margin is NP-complete to compute and even hard to approximate (Katz et al., 2017;
198   Weng et al., 2018). Therefore, quantitatively analyzing the decision boundary of a neural network
199   and characterizing its implicit bias remains a challenge.
200   Our motivation can be summarized by a simple idea: although the margin in the input space is hard
201   to compute, we can quantify the regions split by the decision boundary. This measure is invariant
202   to model reparametrization and can also capture the complexity of the decision boundary. This
203   motivates us to consider the region counts as an implicit bias metric.

      6.1   T HE B IAS FROM T RAINING H YPERPARAMETERS
342
343   Training neural networks requires careful selection of many hyperparameters, such as learning rates,
344   batch sizes, optimizers, epochs and so on. Here, we primarily focus on learning rate and batch size,
345   and study their impact on the region count.
346
347   Learning Rates. We provide the relationship between the learning rate and the region count in
348   Figure 5. Our findings indicate that a larger learning rate tends to simplify the decision boundary and
349   results in a smaller region count in the hyperplane. This accords well with real practices, where large
350   learning rates of 0.1 or 0.01 are often favored for better generalization.
351
352   Batch Sizes. Similarly, the training batch size can impact the number of regions. As shown in
353   Figure 5, smaller batch sizes lead to a model with fewer regions. This result reveals the advantage of
354   small-batch training, which leads to better generalization accuracy.
355   Previous studies (Keskar et al., 2016; Jastrzkebski et al., 2017; Hoffer et al., 2017; Novak et al., 2018)
356   find that certain hyperparameters, such as a large learning rate and a small batch size, can improve
357   the generalization of the neural network. Our observations provide a possible explanation: these
358   good hyperparameter choices lead to a reduced region count. Such simplicity bias can decrease the
359   generalization gap of neural networks.

528   8    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
529
530   This paper introduces a novel approach to characterizing the implicit bias of neural networks. We study
531   the region counts in the input space and identify its strong correlation with generalization gap in non-
532   linear neural networks. These findings are consistent across various network architectures, datasets,
533   optimizers. Our analysis offers a new perspective to quantify and understand the generalization
534   property and implicit bias of neural networks.
535   Our paper suggests several promising directions for future research. Firstly, our analyses of why large
536   learning rate induces small region counts mainly focus on a simplified setup. The analyses for more
537   general settings remain open. Secondly, extending the definition of region count to non-classification
538   tasks, such as natural language generation, would be a worthwhile direction. Lastly, region count
539   can be leveraged to design new architectures or regularization, that can potentially improve the
      generalization performance of neural networks.

787   In this section, we study different methods to calculate the region count, and discuss their impact on
788
      the results. Since it is impossible in practice to calculate the predictions of an infinite number of data
      points on the hyperplane, we select grid points from the hyperplane to calculate the region count.
789
790   Assume we have divided a region of the hyperplane into several equidistant small squares. We can
791   use an algorithm similar to breadth-first search to calculate the number of connected components
792   within these small squares, thereby determining the number of regions. Here, we use a 2-dimensional
793   hyperplane as an example (the 1-dimensional case can be considered a degenerate version of this
794
      algorithm). The algorithm for calculating the number of regions in this setup is given in Algorithm 1.
795   Therefore, it is necessary to determine the granularity of splits for the plane. We experimented
796   with different setups of splitting parameters, and the results averaged by 100 independent trials are
797   presented in Table 4. From the results, using 200 grid points in the 1D case and 30x30 grid points
798   in the 2D case is an optimal choice. Splitting the plane into fewer points results in an inadequate
799   approximation of regions, while increasing the number of points does not significantly enhance
800
      accuracy but incurs greater computational costs. Therefore, in our experiments, we split the plane
      into 200 grid points for the 1D case and 30x30 grid points for the 2D case.

803   We experiment with different numbers of hyperplanes, and the results are presented in Table 5. From
