009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Handling lengthy context is crucial for enhancing the recognition and understand-
012            ing capabilities of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) in applications
013            such as processing high-resolution images or high frame rate videos. The rise in
014            image resolution and frame rate substantially increases computational demands
015            due to the increased number of input tokens. This challenge is further exacerbated
016            by the quadratic complexity with respect to sequence length of the self-attention
017            mechanism. Most prior works either pre-train models with long contexts, over-
018            looking the efficiency problem, or attempt to reduce the context length via down-
019
               sampling (e.g., identify the key image patches or frames) to decrease the con-
               text length, which may result in information loss. To circumvent this issue while
020
               keeping the remarkable effectiveness of MLLMs, we propose a novel approach
021
               using a hybrid transformer-MAMBA model to efficiently handle long contexts
022            in multimodal applications. Our multimodal model can effectively process long
023            context input exceeding 100k tokens, outperforming existing models across vari-
024            ous benchmarks. Remarkably, our model enhances inference efficiency for high-
025            resolution images and high-frame-rate videos by about 4 times compared to cur-
026            rent models, with efficiency gains increasing as image resolution or video frames
027            rise. Furthermore, our model is the first to be trained on low-resolution images or
028            low-frame-rate videos while being capable of inference on high-resolution images
029            and high-frame-rate videos, offering flexibility for inference in diverse scenarios.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recent efforts have extended large language models (LLMs) to incorporate multiple modalities,
035   leading to breakthroughs in various multimodal tasks (Liu et al., 2024d;c;b; Luo et al., 2024a; Chen
036   et al., 2023b). Despite these advances, existing MLLMs still struggle with tasks that require long
037   input sequence, e.g. granular visual recognition (Tong et al., 2024), high frame rate videos and
038   long videos. For example, many well-trained models (e.g., GPT-4V) produce hallucinations when
039
      identifying small and occluded objects in images (Tong et al., 2024) while the same also happens to
      video MLLMs (Ma et al., 2023), a limitation that hinders the practical application of MLLMs.
040
041   Various methods have been explored to improve multimodality processing in different domains by
042   incorporating lengthy context, including increasing the resolution of input images (Liu et al., 2024c;
043   Tong et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024b), in-
044   creasing frame rate of input videos and increasing frame sampling rate for long videos. Although
045
      better performance is achieved by incorporating extra lengthy context, increasing context length
      directly can significantly escalate computational demands. For instance, increasing the resolution
046
      of images to 448×448 pixels raises the computational complexity of models like LLaVA by ap-
047
      proximately 1.4 times compared to the default 336×336 resolution. Moreover, pre-trained vision
048   encoders in current MLLMs typically do not support long contexts due to their fixed context length
049   (Liu et al., 2024d;b; Alayrac et al., 2022), making training unstable with significantly increased
050   resolution. Consequently, most prior works have pre-trained models with long contexts (Li et al.,
051   2023a), overlooking efficiency issues. Besides, some works divide the high-resolution images into
052   smaller patches and then encode them independently (Liu et al., 2024b;c), which might lose the spa-
053   tial information and also lead to quadratic computational cost increase. Additionally, some methods
      shorten the context length (Hong et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024b) by inject high-resolution image

054
      features into the hidden layers of LLMs, which leads to varying degrees of visual information loss
055   and thus reduces effectiveness.
056
057   To address these issues, our work proposes using a hybrid transformer-mamba model (e.g., Jamba
058
      (Lieber et al., 2024)) to overcome the quadratic complexity associated with transformer models.
      The hybrid architecture enables our multimodal model to efficiently process the long context re-
059
      sulting from high resolutions and frame rates without compromising effectiveness. For instance, it
060
      operates 4× faster than current open-source models (e.g. LLaVA-Next-13B) when the resolution is
061   4368*4368. In addition, we also propose a train-on-short-infer-on-long recipe, enabling our model
062   to be trained on inputs with a short context (e.g. low-resolution images) for better training efficiency
063   and do inference on inputs with longer contexts (e.g. high-resolution images) for better perfor-
064   mance. This approach addresses both the efficiency and effectiveness problems simultaneously. To
065   summarize, our contributions include:
066
067          • We introduce a hybrid transformer-mamba multimodal model, MMJAMBA, optimized for
068            efficiently processing lengthy contexts from high resolutions and frame rates. Our ”train-
069            on-short-infer-on-long” strategy allows the model to train on short-context inputs (e.g.,
070
               low-resolution images) for efficiency and infer on long-context inputs (e.g., high-resolution
               images) for enhanced performance.
071
072          • We conducte experiments on both images and videos across 18 benchmark datasets fo-
073            cusing on images and videos. Our results show that MMJAMBA achieves state-of-the-art
074            performance compared to open-source and proprietary models, consistently outperforming
075            LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024c) and Gemini Pro 1.0 (Team et al., 2023), occasionally
076
               matching or surpassing proprietary models like GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023). More impor-
               tantly, we show that our model achieves the best efficiency when processing high-resolution
077
               images and high frame rate videos.
078
079          • We conduct comprehensive model analyses, including ablation and case studies, to eluci-
080            date the inner workings and demonstrate the model’s performance in real-world scenarios.

082   2   R ELATED W ORKS
083
084
      Multimodal Large Language Models. Most current multimodal large language models (MLLMs)
085   integrate large language models (LLMs) with vision encoders, such as ViT (Dosovitskiy, 2020)
086   and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), by incorporating image representations into the LLMs (Liu et al.,
087   2024d;b;c). The LLM then performs various vision-language (VL) tasks in an autoregressive man-
088   ner. Within this framework, MLLMs primarily differ in their approaches to combining text and
089   image representations. Most works employ a modular architecture that utilizes an intermediate net-
090   work to map visual features into the text token embedding space of the LLM. Prominent examples,
091   such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024d), often use a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) layer to link the visual
092   encoder with the LLM. Alternatively, other approaches use sampler-based modules to bridge the
093
      visual encoder and the LLM (Li et al., 2023c; Dai et al., 2023). While these sampler-based mod-
      ules effectively reduce the number of visual tokens, they typically require large-scale pre-training to
094
      achieve satisfactory performance.
095
096   MLLMs with High-Resolution Images. MLLMs often use pre-trained visual encoders for vision-
097   dependent tasks. However, these encoders typically rely on low resolutions, such as 224 × 224 or 336
098   × 336 pixels, which limits their ability to perceive small or blurry objects. This limitation can lead
099
      to failures in tasks that require clear and recognizable details for fine distinctions between objects,
      such as OCR and document understanding (Tong et al., 2024). Recently, various methods have been
100
      introduced to incorporate high-resolution inputs to enhance the capabilities of MLLMs by providing
101
      more fine-grained visual features (Luo et al., 2023a; Hong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al.,
102   2024c; Tong et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). Notable examples include LLaVA-Next (Liu et al.,
103   2024c), LLaVA-HR (Luo et al., 2024b), OtterHD (Li et al., 2023a), InternLM-XComposer2-4KHD
104   (Dong et al., 2024), and InternVL 1.5 (Chen et al., 2024b). Among them, many methods (Hong
105   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b;c) divide high-resolution images into smaller patches and encode them
106   independently. These patches are subsequently concatenated for further processing. While this
107   approach can enhance performance, it also results in a quadratic increase in computational cost due
      to the extended context length.

          3.1         P RELIMINARIES

134       Traditional structured State Space Models (SSMs) (Gu et al., 2021; Gu et al.) describe the evolution
135       of system states through time, defined as (∆, A, B, C). A ∈ RN ×N describes how current states
136       evolve into next states, B ∈ RN ×1 determines how external inputs influence the evolution, C ∈
137       R1×N transforms hidden system states to observed measurements, and ∆ is a time-scale parameter
138       that helps transform A and B into discrete-time parameters Ā and B̄. The discretized form of the
139       state space model can be obtained as:

149       Based on the above structured SSM, the selective SSM–Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) is further intro-
150       duced to endow the model with the ability to selectively propagate or forget information according
151       to the sequential input tokens. Specifically, the selective SSM achieves the content-aware reasoning
152       by introducing input-dependent coefficients matrix B(x) and C(x), as well as the parameter ∆(x).
153       Despite advances at handling long-distance relationships, Mamba still falls short in comparison
154       with similarly sized Transformer models. Jamba (Lieber et al., 2024) combines the strengths of
155       both by stacking Transformer layers and Mamba layers in a specific ratio, improving efficiency and
156       performance over the original Transformer and Mamba models.

158       3.2         A RCHITECTURE
159
160       MMJAMBA adopts the standard MLLM architecture, comprising a visual encoder, an MLP adapter,
161       and a backbone LLM. Detailed design and implementation of the components are provided below.
          Figure 1 illustrates the architecture.

162
      Vision Encoder Our vision encoder is responsible for encoding images or videos. We adopt the
163   AnyRes approach (Liu et al., 2024c) to process images with varying aspect ratios. Specifically, we
164   allow a maximum of 4 tiles during training. Consequently, this set includes all 8 possible combina-
165   tions of aspect ratios formed by 1 to 4 tiles, such as {1:1, 1:2, 2:1, 2:2, 3:1, 1:3, 1:4, 4:1}. Then,
166   each image is independently encoded to a sequence of visual features by a transformer-based Visual
167   Encoder. For videos, we adopt a consistent frame sampling approach that extracts a fixed number
168   of frames from each video. The extracted frames are then fed into the visual encoder. We use
169   CLIP-ViT-Large (Radford et al., 2021), with the final layer removed, as the vision encoder. During
170   inference, we adopt an AnyFrame mechanism which samples any number of frames from the input
171
      videos for further encoding. More details are in the appendix (See A.1).
172   Modality Adapter The adapter is a simple learnable module that aligns the feature of vision and
173   text by transforming the dimension of the original visual representation to the dimension of the
174   tokens of language model. We train separate adapters for the image understanding task and the
175   video understanding task.
176   LLM Backbone We employ a hybrid transformer-mamba model as the LLM backbone. In particu-
177   lar, we adopt, Jamba, a hybrid decoder architecture that mixes Transformer layers with Mamba lay-
178   ers. The largest pretrained Jamaba model leverages a mixture-of-experts (MoE) architecture, scaling
179   up to 52 billion parameters with 12 billion active. We chose this model for the following reasons:
180   1) To the best of our knowledge, it is the largest public available model with a hybrid transformer-
181   MoE architecture at the time of doing this work; 2) it demonstrates strong performance on language
182   understanding and reasoning benchmarks; 3) The MoE architecture makes it more computationally
183
      efficient compared to models of similar scale.
184
185   3.3   M ODEL T RAINING
186
187
      We have two training stages, with the vision encoder remaining frozen throughout.
188   1st stage - Adapter Pretraining: Following (Liu et al., 2024d), we perform modality alignment
189   between images (or videos) and text by pre-training the adapter. All other components except for
190   the adapter are frozen in this stage.
191   2nd stage - Visual Instruction Fine-Tuning: Here, we activate all parameters except the vi-
192   sion encoder. The model is trained by minimizing the causal language modeling loss: l =
         P|y|                                                    ′
193   − i=1 logpθ (yi |ŷ1:i−1 , q), where θ ← (θLLM , θv , θ ) represents the model’s trainable parame-
194   ters, yi is the ground-truth target, and ŷ1:i−1 denotes the i-1 preceding tokens of the output yi .
195
196
      Train-Short-Inference-Long Due to the recurrent nature of Mamba layer in our Jamba LLM back-
      bone, our model could adopt a long context length during inference while using a short context
197
      length during training, which could achieve good efficiency at both training stage and inference
198
      stage. Therefore, for image understanding, we use low resolution images during training for shorter
199   context length and thus better training efficiency. During inference, we use high resolution im-
200   ages for longer context length, which contains more fine-grained visual information. For video
201   understanding, we use a low resolution and lower frame number during training for better training
202   efficiency. During inference, we use a high resolution and higher frame number for longer context
203   length, allowing to capture more fine-grained and richer visual information.

      7     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we introduce MMJAMBA, a multimodal instruction tuned model utilizing the hybrid
529   state space model–Jamba. MMJAMBA aims to effectively process long context input brought up by
530   the higher resolutions of the images and more frames of the videos, thereby enhancing multimodal
531
      visual comprehension and recognition. We propose a train-on-short-inference-on-long recipe, which
      could enable our model to be trained on inputs with a short context (e.g. low-resolution images)
532
      and tested on inputs with longer contexts (e.g. high-resolution images). Extensive experiments
533
      on 12 benchmarks on images and 6 benchmarks on videos validate the efficacy of MMJAMBA.
534   We compared our models with both open-source models, such as LLaVA-NeXT, and closed-source
535   models, such as Gemini Pro 1.0 and GPT-4V, demonstrating the superiority of our approach. More
536   importantly, we show that our model achieves the best efficiency when processing high-resolution
537   images, e.g. 4 times faster than current open-source models with a comparable number of activated
538   parameters. Additionally, we conducted extensive model analyses to understand the efficiency of
539   our model and effectiveness of our train-short-inference-long recipe and demonstrate its capability
      in solving real-world multimodal tasks.

792   A.1   V ISION E NCODING
793
794   Image Encoder Utilizing a static input image size for processing images or videos with varying
795
      aspect ratios, is neither efficient nor effective. To overcome this limitation, we utilize the AnyRes
      approach (Liu et al., 2024c), as shown in Figure 1. Our method segments the image into smaller
796
      patches, while trying to maintain the integrity of the original image’s aspect ratio. More specifically,
797
      we dynamically match the optimal aspect ratio from a pre-defined set of aspect ratios. Due to
798   limited computational resources, we allow a maximum of 4 tiles during training. Consequently, this
799   set includes all 8 possible combinations of aspect ratios formed by 1 to 4 tiles, such as {1:1, 1:2, 2:1,
800   2:2, 3:1, 1:3, 1:4, 4:1}. During the matching process, for each input image, we calculate its aspect
801   ratio and compare it with the 8 pre-defined aspect ratios by measuring the absolute difference. Then
802   the model is sliced into multiple patches, whose resolution is same with the resolution that the vision
803   encoder is pre-trained on. To keep the overall layout information, the raw image is also resized to the
804   low-resolution one as the global image. Then, each image is independently encoded to a sequence
805   of visual features by a transformer-based Visual Encoder.
806   Video Encoder For videos, we adopt a consistent frame sampling approach that extracts a fixed
807   number of frames from each video. The extracted frames are then fed into the visual encoder. We
808   use CLIP-ViT-Large (Radford et al., 2021), with the final layer removed, as the vision encoder.
809   During inference, we adopt an AnyFrame mechanism which samples any number of frames from
      the input videos for further encoding.
