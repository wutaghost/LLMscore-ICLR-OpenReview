011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Zero-shot voice conversion (VC) aims to transform the source speaker timbre into
014            an arbitrary unseen one without altering the original speech content. While re-
015
               cent advancements in zero-shot VC methods have shown remarkable progress,
               there still remains considerable potential for improvement in terms of improving
016
               speaker similarity and speech naturalness. In this paper, we propose Takin-VC,
017
               a novel zero-shot VC framework based on jointly hybrid content and memory-
018            augmented context-aware timbre modeling to tackle this challenge. Specifically,
019            an effective hybrid content encoder, guided by neural codec training, that lever-
020            ages quantized features from pre-trained HybridFormer and WavLM is first pre-
021            sented to extract the linguistic content of the source speech. Subsequently, we
022            introduce an advanced cross-attention-based context-aware timbre modeling ap-
023            proach that learns the fine-grained, semantically associated target timbre features.
024            To further enhance both speaker similarity and real-time performance, we uti-
025            lize a conditional flow matching model to reconstruct the Mel-spectrogram of the
026
               source speech. Additionally, we advocate an efficient memory-augmented mod-
               ule designed to generate high-quality conditional target inputs for the flow match-
027
               ing process, thereby improving the overall performance of the proposed system.
028
               Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed Takin-VC method surpasses
029            state-of-the-art zero-shot VC systems, delivering superior performance in terms
030            of both speech naturalness and speaker similarity.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Zero-shot voice conversion (VC) refers to the task of modifying the timbre of a source speech to
036   match that of a previously unseen speaker, while preserving the original phonetic or linguistic con-
037   tent. This technology has found broad applications in various practical domains Gan et al. (2022);
038   Tomashenko et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2021).
039   In recent times, zero-shot VC has witnessed great progressions, with numerous state-of-the-art
040   (SOTA) approaches (Li et al., 2023a; Hussain et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2023; Anastassiou et al.,
041   2024; Li et al., 2024; Luo & Dixon, 2024) exhibiting impressive results in converting natural and
042   realistic utterances. The key idea behind these methods is to factorize speech into distinct elements,
043   such as linguistic content and timbre elements, and then leverage the source speech content along-
044   side the target speaker timbre to synthesize the desired target speech. In this paradigm, the quality
045
      of content and timbre representations, as well as the quality of their disentanglement, significantly
      impact their final performance. Consequently, numerous studies have sought to improve VC perfor-
046
      mance by designing more advanced modules (Wu et al., 2020; Wu & Lee, 2020; Tang et al., 2022;
047
      Wang et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022a; Huang et al., 2023), information disentanglement approaches
048   (Zhao et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022; Dang et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024b) and so forth. However,
049   achieving high-quality decoupling of utterances into distinct components remains a challenging task
050   (Pan et al., 2023; 2024a;c; Yao et al., 2024a), and existing zero-shot VC systems still exhibit subpar
051   performance when handling unseen speakers primarily due to the underlying issues. First, current
052   methods cannot fully mitigate the influence of source speaker timbre during the extraction of lin-
053   guistic content features, a problem commonly referred to as ”timbre leakage.” Second, they normally
      employ pre-trained speaker-verification (SV) models to capture target timbre features and cast them

054
      as globally time-invariant representations. However, as highlighted in (Jiang et al., 2024), the timbre
055   representations may vary with the linguistic content, rendering the performance of these approaches
056   less optimal. Recently, the advances in large-scale speech language models (Wang et al., 2023c;
057   Borsos et al., 2023) have tried to tackle this issue by leveraging robust in-context learning capabil-
058   ities for predicting target speech from concise utterances as prompts. Nevertheless, these methods
059   may suffer from stability issues and error accumulation due to their auto-regressive nature, which
060   can gradually degrade conversion quality.
061   To address the aforementioned limitations, we introduce Takin-VC, an effective VC framework with
062   advanced modeling of content, timbre and audio quality in a zero-shot fashion. Specifically, we pro-
063   pose a hybrid content encoder guided by neural codec training that integrates the phonetic posterior-
064   grams (PPGs) features and quantized self-supervised learning (SSL) representations from two pre-
065   trained models, i.e., HybridFormer (Yang et al., 2023b) and WavLM (Chen et al., 2022), so as to
066   precisely capture the linguistic content. For speaker timbre modeling, we first propose a content-
067   aware timbre modeling method that employs cross-attention (CA) to leverage the target voiceprint
068   (VP) features extracted from a pre-trained speaker verification (SV) model (Wang et al., 2023b), with
069
      the captured source content. This integration enables our proposed approach to learn target timbre
      representations associated with source content. Additionally, to further enhance speaker similarity,
070
      we advocate a memory-augmented module capable of generating high-quality conditional target in-
071
      puts for a conditional flow matching (CFM) model Tong et al. (2023b), ultimately culminating in
072   the synthesis of the target speech using a pre-trained vocoder (Lee et al., 2022).
073
074   To evaluate the performance of the Takin-VC system, we conduct extensive experiments on the both
075
      large-scale 500k-hour multilingual (Mandarin and English) and publicly available LibriTTS Zen
      et al. (2019) datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that Takin-VC consistently outperforms
076
      state-of-the-art (SOTA) zero-shot VC methods in terms of both speaker similarity and speech nat-
077
      uralness. Notably, Takin-VC achieves significant improvements in both subjective and objective
078   metrics compared with all baseline systems, further validating its effectiveness. For more detailed
079   speech samples, please visit our demo page 1 .

084               of generating semantically coherent target timbre representations for unseen speakers, re-
085               sulting in improved speaker similarity and enhanced naturalness/intelligibility.

                • We introduce a hybrid linguistic content encoder that leverages the PPGs and quantized

                  posed method.

097   2       BACKGROUND
098
099   Zero-shot Voice Conversion.
100
      In contrast to previous few-shot Wang et al. (2020); Gabryś et al. (2022) and one-shot Tang et al.
101
      (2022); Li et al. (2023b) VC approaches, zero-shot VC presents a more challenging task, as it re-
102
      quires the model to generalize to unseen speakers without any additional training or fine-tuning. In
103   recent years, advancements in deep learning techniques, such as SSL speech models and diffusion
104   models, have led to significant progress in zero-shot VC. SEF-VC Li et al. (2024) learns speaker tim-
105   bre from reference speech using a CA mechanism and reconstructs waveforms from HuBERT Hsu
106   et al. (2021) tokens. Choi et al. (2023) introduced Diff-HierVC, a diffusion-based hierarchical VC
107
          1
              https://anonymous.4open.science/w/takin-vc-0CD8/

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      method that uses XLS-R Babu et al. (2021) for content extraction and employs two diffusion mod-
109   els to generate high-fidelity converted pitch and Mel-spectrograms. The utilization of robust SSL
110   features, which are rich in phonetic and paralinguistic nuances, has led to improved performance
111   in these methods compared to prior works Fang et al. (2018); Kaneko et al. (2019). Despite these
112   impressive results, SSL-based zero-shot VC approaches Dang et al. (2022); Hussain et al. (2023);
113   Li et al. (2023a) may still encounter the timbre leakage problem, as SSL features do not explic-
114   itly disentangle timbre information, while diffusion-based VC methods Popov et al. (2021); Choi
115   et al. (2024) often struggle with poor real-time performance. Another cutting-edge zero-shot VC
116   paradigm Zhang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023c); Baade et al. (2024) involves decoupling speech
117
      into semantic and acoustic tokens using neural codecs (Défossez et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023a;
      Pan et al., 2024b) and SSL speech models Chen et al. (2022); Baevski et al. (2020), subsequently
118
      leveraging language models to generate the converted speech. However, these methods still possess
119
      great potential for improvement regarding speaker similarity and naturalness/intelligibility.
120
121   Flow Matching-based Generative Models.
122   Recently, flow matching-based generative models Lipman et al. (2022); Tong et al. (2023c;a) have
123   garnered considerable attention in the realm of generative tasks, particularly in the image generation
124   task Ho et al. (2020); Saharia et al. (2022); Ruiz et al. (2023). These methods focus on approxi-
125   mating the transport probability path from random noise to the target distribution by estimating the
126   associated vector field. By employing a neural ordinary differential equation (ODE), these models
127   learn the optimal transport trajectory and establish a direct link between noise and target samples,
128   which greatly reduces the required number of sampling steps. In contrast to diffusion-based meth-
129
      ods Bartosh et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2023), flow matching offers improved
      training stability and real-time performance.
130
131   Influenced by this wave of innovation, the speech processing domain has begun to explore flow
132   matching-based generative systems as well. For instance, SpeechFlow Liu et al. (2023) leverages a
133   pre-trained generative model using flow matching and masked conditions on extensive untranscribed
134   speech data, enabling effective adaptation to various downstream tasks like speech enhancement,
135
      separation, and so forth. ELaTE Kanda et al. (2024) is a zero-shot TTS system that generates
      natural laughter by mimicking voice characteristics from an audio prompt and precisely controlling
136
      laughter timing and expression through specific input cues. P-Flow Kim et al. (2024) utilizes speech
137
      prompts for speaker adaptation, integrating a speech-prompted text encoder that generates speaker-
138   conditional representations with a flow matching generative decoder to achieve high-quality, real-
139   time speech synthesis. Nevertheless, the application of flow matching in zero-shot voice conversion
140   (VC) tasks is still in its developmental phase, indicating the urgent need for a stable and efficient
141   flow matching-based zero-shot VC framework.

289   3.3.2     M EMORY- AUGMENTED T IMBRE M ODELING
290
291   Since we use a CFM model to reconstruct the source Mel-spectrograms, obtaining high-quality
292   conditional target inputs is quite essential, as they provide key guidance for training the CFM model.
293   To this end, we design an efficient memory-augmented module that adaptively integrates the Mel-
294
      spectrogram and VP features of the reference speech, as outlined in Fig. 4. To be specific, our
      proposed memory-augmented module initially use a Conv1d layer to project the xref to a latent
295
      feature space. Subsequently, we incorporate multiple SA blocks, each containing several group
296
      normalization, multi-head SA, and 1D Conv layers, followed by a Conv1d layer and a LeakyReLU
297   activation layer. This design effectively leverages these features in a stable and learnable manner. At
298   the end of memory module, we compute the average vector of the obtained representations across
299   the time dimension to produce the final output xtcond . Finally, xtcond is input into the Memory
300   Fusion Layer (a combination of the Gated Activation Layer and FiLM Layer Perez et al. (2018))
301   within the flow matching network to reconstruct the Mel-spectrogram.

      4.2     E VALUATION M ETRICS
352
353
      To evaluate the performance of our proposed Takin-VC and baseline systems, both subjective and
354   objective metrics are introduced. For subjective metrics, we employ naturalness mean opinion score
355   (NMOS) to evaluate the naturalness of the generated samples and similarity mean opinion scores
356   (SMOS) to evaluate the speaker similarity. We invite 20 professional participants to listen to the
357   generated samples and provide their subjective perception scores on a 5-point scale: ’5’ for excellent,
358   ’4’ for good, ’3’ for fair, ’2’ for poor, and ’1’ for bad. For objective metrics, we employ word error
359   rate (WER), UTMOS, and speaker embedding cosine similarity (SECS) to evaluate the intelligibility,
360   quality, and speaker similarity. Specifically: 1) We use a pre-trained CTC-based ASR model4 to
361   transcribe the generated speech and compare with ground-truth transcription; 2) We use a MOS
362
      prediction system that ranked first in the VoiceMOS Challenge 20225 to estimate the speech quality
      of the generated samples; 3) We use the WavLM-TDCNN speaker verification model6 to measure
363
      speaker similarity between generated speech and target speech.

      4.4     M ODEL C ONFIGURATION
391
392
      For the content encoder part, in the first stage, we used the 12-layer HybridFormer-base model
393   trained on a large dataset of 500K hours. For the wavlm part, we used the output features of the 6th
394   layer. In the VQ part, we adopted a single-layer 8200 codebook with a hidden dimension of 1024,
395   trained for 1 million steps on 100K hours of data. The fusion layer, as described in Sec. 3.2, is a
396   simple process of conv1d, activation layer, and weighted summation. The Decoder adopts the same
397   structure and configuration as Hificodec Yang et al. (2023a).
398
      In the part of timbre modeling and flow matching restoration, both the context-aware timbre module
399   and the memory module use a transformer layer with 8 heads, 6 layers, and a hidden size of 1024,
400   with only the form of attention being different. The main structure of flow matching uses a design
401   of 10-layer Unet plus 3 layers of resblock, with a hidden size of 1280. A Memory Fusion Block is
402   inserted into the 10-layer Unet to enhance the timbral similarity of the generated audio.
403
      For the small-data experiments, we used four A800 GPUs, whereas the large-data experiments were
404
      conducted on eight A800 servers. The batch size on each card was set to 16, and the AdamW
405   learning rate was set to 1e-4. In the inference section, experiments typically took 15 to 50 steps,
406   with the final table uniformly adopting the results of 50 steps. The Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG)
407   coefficient ranged from 0.1 to 1.0, with 0.7 used in the table. The specific experimental results will
408   be detailed later.

      5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
487
488   We conduct ablation experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of each component in our proposed
489   system for generating natural-sounding samples and accurate timbre modeling. As shown in Table
490   3, NMOS and WER results degrade when we replace the proposed hybrid content encoder with
491   a conventional ASR encoder. This suggests that the conventional ASR encoder is less capable of
492   disentangling linguistic content from the necessary paralinguistic information, underscoring the im-
493   portance and effectiveness of our hybrid encoder in extracting linguistic content. Additionally, we
494
      observe a notable decline in speaker similarity when the voice print is removed from the attention
      module. We believe the voice print introduces a stronger timbre bias, which helps the attention
495
      module focus on capturing timbre information. Furthermore, when we remove the memory module,
496
      SMOS and SECS scores show significant degradation compared to the original Takin-VC, demon-
497   strating the critical role of the memory module in improving timbre modeling. These ablation results
498   demonstrate the effectiveness of each component proposed in our Takin-VC.

      6     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
513
514
      Takin is an effective and data-efficient zero-shot VC system that achieves comparable naturalness
      and speaker adaptation performance to its large-scale, autoregressive counterparts. The core of this
515
      approach lies in the neural codec training based hybrid linguistic content encoder, which captures
516
      high-quality speaker-agnostic content representations, and the introduction of both context-aware
517   timbre modeling and memory-augmented modules to enhance speaker similarity performance. In
518   many ways, our work provides a strong foundation for future studies, as we demonstrate that state-
519   of-the-art performance in this task can be achieved without relying on complex training setups,
520   representation quantization steps, or costly autoregressive models.
521
      This work primarily focuses on zero-shot capabilities for speech generation, while zero-shot ca-
522
      pabilities for speech editing remain limited and are a subject for future exploration. Additionally,
523   while high-quality zero-shot VC has great potential, it can also lead to negative social impacts, such
524   as voice impersonation of public figures and non-consenting individuals. We highlight this as a
525   potential misuse of the technology to raise awareness of its ethical implications.

      7     CONCLUSIONS
529
530   In this study, we propose a novel framework called Takin-VC, designed to achieve high quality and
531
      similarity in zero-shot VC. We introduce an effective neural codec training guided hybrid content en-
      coder that leverages quantized features from both pre-trained HybridFormer and WavLM to extract
532
      the linguistic content of the source speech. This hybrid content encoder improves the naturalness
533
      and intelligibility of the converted speech. Additionally, we present an advanced cross-attention-
534   based, context-aware timbre modeling approach that captures fine-grained, semantically associated
535   target timbre features. Furthermore, we employ a conditional flow-matching model to efficiently
536   reconstruct the Mel-spectrogram of the source speech and propose an efficient memory-augmented
537   module for the flow-matching process, enhancing the overall performance of the generated samples.
538   Experimental results demonstrate that Takin-VC outperforms all baseline systems in naturalness and
539   speaker similarity on benchmark datasets. Ablation studies also confirm the effectiveness of each
      component in our approach.
