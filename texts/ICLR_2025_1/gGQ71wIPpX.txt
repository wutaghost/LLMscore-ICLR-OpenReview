009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce nested diffusion models, an efficient and powerful hierarchical gen-
012            erative framework that substantially enhances the generation quality of diffusion
013            models, particularly for images of complex scenes. Our approach employs a series
014            of diffusion models to progressively generate latent variables at different semantic
015            levels. Each model in this series is conditioned on the output of the preceding
016            higher-level model, culminating in image generation. Hierarchical latent variables
017            guide the generation process along predefined semantic pathways, allowing our
018
               approach to capture intricate structural details while significantly improving image
               quality. To construct these latent variables, we leverage a pre-trained visual encoder,
019
               which learns strong semantic visual representations, and apply a series of compres-
020
               sion techniques, including spatial pooling, channel reduction, and noise injection,
021            in order to control the information capacity at each level of the hierarchy. Across
022            multiple benchmarks, including class-conditioned generation on ImageNet-1k and
023            text-conditioned generation on the COCO dataset, our system demonstrates notable
024            improvements in image quality, as reflected by FID scores. These improvements
025            incur only slight additional computational cost, as more abstract levels of our
026            hierarchy operate on lower-dimensional representations. Our method also enhances
027            unconditional generation, narrowing the performance gap between conditional
028            generation and unconditional generation that leverages neither text nor class labels.

113

128   2    R ELATED WORKS
129
130   Hierarchical Generative Model: A hierarchical generative model has been proposed to improve
131   generation quality by progressively refining the prior through multiple nested generation steps. In
132   this line of research, hierarchical variational autoencoders (HVAE) (Vahdat & Kautz, 2020; Zhao
133   et al., 2017; Child, 2020; Takida et al., 2023), which extend the latent space of VAEs (Kingma, 2013)
134   to include multiple latent variables, demonstrate improved generation quality. However, HVAE is
135
      known to suffer from high variance and collapsed representations, where the top-level variables may
      be ignored (Vahdat & Kautz, 2020; Child, 2020). To address this issue, Luhman & Luhman (2022)
136
      introduced a layer-wise scheduler and network regularization to enhance stability, while Hazami et al.
137
      (2022) proposed a simplified architecture.
138
139   Recent work has sought to build hierarchical generative systems by freezing the latent variables and
140   leveraging powerful generative models such as diffusion models and autoregressive models. For
141
      example, Ho et al. (2022); Gu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024) trained a set of diffusion models to
      handle images at different resolutions, and Tian et al. (2024) trained a hierarchical autoregressive
142
      model to predict the residuals between tokenized representations at adjacent resolutions. However,
143
      none of these approaches involve training semantic hierarchical representations.
144
145   Conditional generation: A conditional diffusion model aims to parameterize the prior as a complex
146   joint distribution conditioned on an input, rather than using a simple Gaussian prior, which signif-
147
      icantly enhances the model’s capacity to capture intricate data patterns. For images with complex
      scenes, generation conditioned on image captions Gu et al. (2022); Kang et al. (2023); Reed et al.
148
      (2016) has shown notable improvements in both quality and controllability. Zhang et al. (2023); Rom-
149
      bach et al. (2022) extended this conditioning approach to multi-modality, incorporating inputs such as
150   segmentation maps, depth maps, and human joint positions. Another direction in this field is learning
151   the conditional variable itself. Models like DiffAE (Preechakul et al., 2022), SODA (Hudson et al.,
152   2024), and Abstreiter et al. (2021) train an encoder to produce low-dimensional latent variables to
153   assist the generation process, and these works also demonstrate that the encoder can learn meaningful
154   image representations.
155
      Generation with semantic visual representation: State-of-the-art generative models, such as
156   diffusion models and autoregressive models, can be viewed as denoising autoencoders that inherently
157   learn meaningful data representations. Research by Yang & Wang (2023); Tang et al. (2023); Zhang
158   et al. (2024) demonstrates that diffusion models capture semantic visual representations, which
159   can be directly applied to various downstream tasks (Baranchuk et al., 2021; Karazija et al., 2023).
160   Additionally, Zhang & Maire (2023) highlights that the discriminator in GANs also learns strong
161   image representations. Studies like Li et al. (2023a); Jiang et al. (2024) show that incorporating
      representation learning objectives into the generative framework can further enhance generation

163   representations learned by the encoder to improve generation quality even more.

      3.1   N ESTED DIFFUSION MODELS
192
193   Our proposed nested diffusion models can be seen as a hierarchical generative framework comprising
194   L levels, each employing a diffusion model Dθl . As illustrated in Figure 2, the model at each level
195   l is responsible for generating its corresponding latent variables zl . Here zl ∈ Rdl and dl ≤ dl+1 ,
196   indicating decreasing amount of information when l increases. At the shallowest level of the hierarchy,
197   level 0, the latent variables correspond directly to the data samples, that is, z0 = x.
198
      Diffusion with semantic hierarchy: Our design explicitly directs the generation process to follow a
199
      semantic hierarchy, where top-level (larger l) corresponds to increasing levels of semantic abstraction,
200   while the bottom level (smaller l) correspond to fine-grained detailed information. This is essential
201   for preserving image semantic structures and producing realistic samples in generative models. In
202   contrast, the latent variable in standard diffusion models, x(t) , is a linear transformation of the input
203   data x with added Gaussian noise. This means that information abstraction in standard diffusion
204   models occurs at the raw pixel level, through the addition of noise to images, making it challenging
205   for the diffusion models to maintain semantic structure in the generated output.
206
      Markovian generation: At each hierarchical level l, we follow the diffusion model framework and

                                          (t)                                              (t)

254   during training, complicating the process.
255
      Extraction of features: We initialize {zl }L−1
                                                  l=1 using features from a pre-trained encoder and freeze
256
      them during training. Specifically, we use features from pre-trained models like DINO or CLIP
257
      because they learn strong semantic representations and these representations have been shown to
258   significantly enhance the quality of generative models, including GANs (Casanova et al., 2021) and
259   diffusion models (Hu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b). Alternatively, other recent methods propose to
260   construct hierarchical diffusion models using VAE bottleneck representations, which offer highly
261   compressed feature maps. In our experiments, we observed a substantial improvement in generation
262   quality when using semantically rich features.
263
      Hierarchical compression: A challenge with using DINO or CLIP features described above is that
264   they often result in highly redundant feature maps. For example, DINO’s VIT-B model produces
265   a 14x14x768 feature map, which has the same spatial dimensions as the input image (224x224x3).
266   Such overcomplete representations force the generative model to capture unnecessary correlations,
267   degrading the quality of generated samples. Moreover, this redundancy can disrupt the hierarchical
268   system. If zl contain sufficient information to perfectly reconstruct the original data x, then the
269   lower-level latent variables {zl′ }l′ <l would be meaningless because they do not provide additional
      information for x.

      Therefore, designing an effective progressive compression scheme is critical for managing high-
271   dimensional features and constructing meaningful hierarchical latent variables. Our compression
272   routine involves three key steps:
273
274
      1. Spatial dimensionality reduction via average pooling: We begin by reducing the spatial
      dimensions of the feature map through average pooling. This strategy has been used in previous
275
      hierarchical models based on original images and VAE bottleneck representations. However, we find
276
      that spatial pooling alone is insufficient, as it does not address redundancy in the feature channels.
277
278   2. Feature channel reduction via singular value decomposition (SVD): To tackle redundancy in
279   the feature channels, we apply SVD along the feature dimension and retain only the top components
280
      as hierarchical features. SVD orders the feature channels by importance based on their singular
      values, allowing us to conveniently form hierarchical representations by trimming less important
281
      channels. To prevent the model from neglecting the trailing channels, we standardize the features to
282
      have zero mean and unit variance.
283

285   level of feature abstraction, we introduce Gaussian noise to zl , represented as ẑl ∼ N (zl , σl2 I) for

      We present the setup and results of our experiments, where we evaluate the performance of our nested
301   diffusion model across various tasks. Our primary focus is to explore the model’s effectiveness in
302   both conditional and unconditional image generation scenarios using the COCO-2014(Lin et al.,
303   2014) and ImageNet-100 datasets (Russakovsky et al., 2015), with additional large-scale experiments

305
306   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
307
308   Nested Diffusion Models. We utilize U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023), a ViT-based UNet model with an
309   encoder-decoder architecture, as the foundation of our nested diffusion model. This model employs
310   skip connections and performs diffusion in the latent space of a pre-trained VAE, reducing the input
311   size from 256x256x3 to 32x32x4, which enables efficient handling of high-resolution images. We
312   use the default diffusion scheduler, sampler, and hyperparameters from U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023).
313   For constructing the nested diffusion model, we instantiate the U-ViT model at each hierarchical
314   level, maintaining consistent configurations across all levels, except for the input data shape zl and
315   the conditional feature ẑl+1 . The higher hierarchical levels progressively reduce the dimensionality
316   of zl , resulting in minimal additional computational overhead despite an increase in parameters. We
317   defer further optimizations in parameter efficiency to future work.
318   To incorporate conditional features ẑl+1 , we use deconvolutional layers to upsample them to match the
319   resolution of zl . These features are then concatenated as tokens every two attention blocks, followed
320   by two fully connected layers. During training, we randomly drop the conditional features with a
321   50% probability to facilitate classifier-free guidance (CFG) Ho & Salimans (2022) for improving
322   image generation quality. We use model configurations from U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023) and utilize the
323   ViT-small, ViT-medium, and ViT-large configurations for COCO, ImageNet-100, and ImageNet-1k,
      respectively. Unless stated otherwise, all models are trained for 1000 epochs.

350   4.2   U NCONDITIONAL I MAGE G ENERATION
351
352   To generate realistic images in an unconditional setting, a generative model must recognize the
353   semantic structures of the images effectively. This is particularly challenging when images during the
354   generation process are heavily corrupted, often by Gaussian noise or random masking. Traditional
355   training objectives, usually based on pixel-wise distance, treat each pixel independently and provide
356   no direct structural guidance in the output space, requiring the model to learn these structures
357
      internally in its latent space. If the model struggles to capture these semantic structures, the resulting
      output is likely to lack coherence. Our proposed approach addresses this challenge by introducing
358
      explicit semantic guidance via an external encoder that learns visual semantic representations, thus
359
      reducing the complexity of the task of the generative model.
360
361   We initially assessed the performance of the nested diffusion model on unconditional image generation
362   tasks using the COCO-2014 and ImageNet-100 datasets. For COCO-2014, we follow the text-to-
363
      image evaluation protocol, calculating the FID between 30K generated images and those from the
      validation set. For ImageNet-100, where the validation set contains only 5K images - insufficient
364
      for reliable FID statistics - we use all 50K training images as a reference and compute FID on 50K
365
      generated images. We adopt the default hyperparameters for classifier-free guidance, as outlined
366   in Bao et al. (2023), for conditional generation, substituting the ground truth text or class labels
367   with our generated hierarchical latent variables ẑl . We report our results in multiple depths of the

372   minimal, particularly with deeper models. It is notable that as we add more hierarchical levels, the

409   4.3   C ONDITIONAL I MAGE G ENERATION
410
411   We also evaluated our model on conditional generation tasks, including conditional text and class
412   generation. Text, compared to class labels, offers more detailed information, making the generation
413   process easier. However, there are still gaps in the transfer of information, such as the shape and
414   texture of the object, between the conditional input and the generated images. Our approach addresses
415   these gaps through hierarchical generation, leading to improved performance.
416   For this experiment, we use the same setup as in the unconditional generation tasks, with results
417   presented in Table. 2. Similar to the unconditional generation results, we observe clear performance
418   improvements with hierarchical levels L = 2, and the selection of σ2 remains crucial to overall
419   performance.
420   However, the additional conditional ground truth input causes the performance gains from increasing
421   model depth to grow more slowly compared to the unconditional task. This can be attributed to the
422   overlap in functionality between the conditional input and the higher levels of the deeper nested
423   diffusion models, both of which capture abstract representations.
424
      Choices of visual representations. We examine the effect of different visual representation sources
425
      on constructing the latent variable, with the results shown in Table. 4. Instead of utilizing the encoder’s
426   representation, we experimented with using the bottleneck from a VAE. The same procedure and
427   hyperparameters were applied to construct the hierarchical latent variable {zl }L  l for L = 3. Although
428   VAE learns a compact bottleneck representation, it does not capture strong semantic information.
429   Consequently, when the hierarchical latent variable is constructed by downsampling the feature
430   dimensions, the latent space does not retain coherent semantic structures. As a result, the generation
431   quality with L = 3 for VAE-based representations is inferior to our approach using MoCo-v3 in both
      conditional and unconditional tasks.

      5    C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this work, we introduced the nested diffusion model, a hierarchical generative framework that
478
      effectively generates images by following a semantic hierarchy. Our approach builds upon a series of
479
      hierarchical latent variables derived from pre-trained visual encoders, followed by feature compression
480   techniques. These latent variables guide the generative process, enabling the model to capture
481   detailed structural information while preserving high image quality. By progressively abstracting
482   and compressing feature representations at multiple levels, we achieve significant improvements
483   in generation performance with minimal computational overhead. Our results demonstrate that
484   this structured, hierarchical design outperforms traditional diffusion models in both conditional
485   and unconditional generation tasks. Rather than solely scaling model parameters, we advocate for
      a rethinking of generative model design that emphasizes structural organization. Future research
