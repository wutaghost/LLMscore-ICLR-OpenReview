011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) is a crucial component of unsupervised tasks, en-
014            abling the learning of general feature representations without the need for labeled
015            categories. However, our understanding of SSL tasks remains limited, and it is
016
               still unclear how SSL models extract key features from raw data. Existing inter-
               pretability methods are heavily reliant on downstream tasks, requiring information
017
               from these tasks to explain SSL models. This reliance blurs the line between in-
018
               terpreting the SSL model itself and the downstream task model. Moreover, these
019            methods often require additional samples beyond the target of interpretation, in-
020            troducing extra information that complicates the interpretability process. In this
021            paper, we propose three fundamental prerequisites for the interpretability of SSL
022            tasks and design the Self-Supervised Learning Attribution (SSLA) algorithm that
023            adheres to these prerequisites. SSLA redefines the interpretability objective by
024            introducing a feature similarity measure, reducing the impact of randomness in-
025            herent in SSL algorithms, and achieving more stable interpretability results. Ad-
026            ditionally, SSLA abstracts the interpretability process, making it independent of
027
               specific neural network architectures. To the best of our knowledge, SSLA is the
               first SSL interpretability method that does not rely on downstream tasks. We also
028
               redesign a more reasonable evaluation framework and establish baselines for com-
029
               parative assessment. The source code for our implementation is publicly available
030            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SSLA-EF85.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The advent of the big data era has brought about an abundance of unlabeled data, presenting both
036   challenges and opportunities for the field of machine learning (Gandomi & Haider, 2015; L’heureux
037   et al., 2017). To harness the potential of these vast unlabeled data resources, researchers have pro-
038   posed the Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) paradigm (Jing & Tian, 2020). SSL leverages cleverly
039   designed pretext tasks to enable models to automatically extract meaningful feature representations
040   from unlabeled data, thereby improving performance on downstream tasks. This learning approach
041   has demonstrated immense potential in various domains, including computer vision, natural lan-
042   guage processing, and speech recognition (Liu et al., 2021; Kolesnikov et al., 2019).
043   In computer vision, SSL has been applied to tasks such as image classification, object detection,
044   and semantic segmentation (Doersch et al., 2015; Kolesnikov et al., 2019). In natural language
045   processing, it has provided new avenues for language model pretraining, text classification, and
046   machine translation (Conneau & Lample, 2019). In the realm of speech recognition, SSL contributes
047   to enhancing the robustness and generalization capabilities of acoustic models (Schneider et al.,
048   2019). Furthermore, SSL is progressively showcasing its unique advantages in emerging research
049   areas like multi-modal learning, graph neural networks, and time series analysis. Notably, SSL
050
      algorithms have also given rise to multi-modal feature extraction methods such as CLIP, which has
      become an essential tool for modality unification (Radford et al., 2021).
051
052   Despite the significant advantages of SSL in leveraging unlabeled data, reducing labeling costs,
053   and enhancing model generalization, the interpretability of SSL tasks remains relatively unex-
      plored (Guo et al., 2020; Cabannes et al., 2023). Interpretability research aims to unveil the reasons


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and evidence behind model decisions, thereby increasing model transparency and trustworthiness.
055   Attribution methods are particularly crucial in the context of SSL tasks. Compared to traditional
056   interpretability methods, attribution methods can establish a correspondence between model out-
057   puts and individual dimensions of input features, allowing for a more granular explanation of the
058   decision-making process (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Pan et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2024c;b;a).
059
      The absence of human annotations in SSL tasks renders the model’s learning process and decision
060
      basis more obscure, potentially harboring biases and risks. By delving into the internal mechanisms
061   of SSL models through attribution and other interpretability methods, we can gain a deeper under-
062   standing of how models learn from unlabeled data, identify potential biases and vulnerabilities, and
063   ultimately improve model controllability and safety (Han et al., 2021). Moreover, interpretability
064   research can help uncover potential flaws in SSL task design, guiding the development of more ef-
065   fective and robust SSL algorithms, thereby further advancing the development and application of
066   SSL.
067   Current SSL interpretation methods are often confined to specific downstream tasks or rely on partic-
068   ular model architectures (Gur et al., 2021). When interpretations are based on specific downstream
069   tasks, information from these tasks is inevitably introduced, leading to conceptual confusion in dis-
070   tinguishing whether we are interpreting the downstream task or the SSL task itself. Furthermore,
071   interpretations tailored to specific downstream tasks tend to be limited in their applicability to other
072   tasks. This contradicts the original intention of SSL methods, which is to extract general-purpose
073   features, and fails to explain why SSL tasks can produce such excellent feature representations. Sim-
074   ilarly, interpretation methods that depend on specific structures (e.g., Transformers) cannot account
075
      for the success of encoders based on other architectures, such as convolutional neural networks.
      Given that SSL tasks are designed with an abstraction of the encoder structure, an ideal interpre-
076
      tation algorithm should also possess sufficient abstraction to be broadly applicable across various
077
      model architectures.
078
079   We observe that the essence of SSL tasks lies in finding invariant features amidst variations. For
080   instance, an encoder should extract similar features when a sample undergoes various transforma-
081
      tions that do not alter its semantic information. An encoder with such capability can, to some extent,
      capture the semantic meaning of the sample. Based on this observation, we design an evaluation
082
      function S(x, fθ,Z ) (specific details and symbol definitions are provided in Section 3.3), which is
083
      used to assess whether a sample has been well-trained. This function balances the influence of
084   randomness during SSL task training and reflects the model’s ability to identify invariance amidst
085   variations. Subsequently, we propose the complete SSLA algorithm. SSLA can comprehensively
086   capture the changes in S(x, fθ,Z ) caused by variations in the sample x, thereby observing the con-
087   tribution of each feature of the sample to the SSL model’s ability to extract semantic information,
088   and ultimately obtaining attribution results. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
089
090         1. We summarize three prerequisites for SSL task interpretation, reconstruct the objective of
091
               SSL tasks, and design the attribution method SSLA. To the best of our knowledge, SSLA
               is the first SSL interpretation algorithm that does not rely on any downstream tasks.
092
093         2. We systematically analyze the existing problems in the evaluation of SSL interpretation
094            algorithms and propose a more reasonable evaluation method for interpretability.
095         3. We provide open-source code to facilitate reproducibility and further research by the com-
096            munity.

      2.1   S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING
101
102   SSL aims to learn representations from large amounts of unlabeled data without the need for manual
103   annotations, which can then be utilized to facilitate the training of downstream tasks. As a popular
104   approach in SSL, contrastive learning (CL) learns representations by minimizing the distance be-
105   tween different augmented views of the same image, while maximizing the distance between views
106   of different images. SimCLR proposes a simple contrastive learning framework that achieves com-
107   parable performance to supervised learning without using specialized negative pairs (Chen et al.,
      2020). MoCo-v3 employs a momentum encoder and a queue to maintain a large number of negative


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      samples, further improving the performance of contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2021). BYOL in-
109   troduces a self-distillation-based contrastive learning method that can learn effective representations
110   without negative pairs (Grill et al., 2020). SimSiam further simplifies the BYOL framework and
111   demonstrates through experiments the importance of stop-gradient operations in preventing model
112   collapse (Chen & He, 2021).
113
      In addition to contrastive learning, masked image modeling (MIM) has emerged as another signif-
114
      icant branch of SSL, exemplified by MAE (He et al., 2022), which achieves scalable visual repre-
115   sentation learning by randomly masking image patches and reconstructing the missing parts. MIM
116   methods heavily rely on the Transformer architecture, and their strong performance can be partly
117   attributed to the self-attention mechanism of Transformers. However, our method focuses on the
118   interpretability of general self-supervised methods that do not depend on specific model architec-
119   tures or parameters. Nevertheless, we also present experimental results on MAE, which demonstrate
120   remarkable effectiveness, showcasing its capability in visual representation learning.
121
122   2.2   I NTERPRETABILITY OF T RADITIONAL M ODELS
123
124   Several interpretability methods based on Shapley value, such as SHAP, exist for traditional mod-
125   els (Zhou et al., 2021). While theoretically appealing, they often fall short in practical applications
126
      (Lundberg & Lee, 2017). Particularly when dealing with complex models like deep neural net-
      works, their computational overhead becomes prohibitive, hindering their real-world use. RISE,
127
      on the other hand, assesses feature importance by randomly masking input features and observing
128
      the changes in model output (Petsiuk et al., 2018). However, this method also faces computational
129   bottlenecks when handling high-dimensional inputs such as images. Moreover, these methods en-
130   counter difficulties when interpreting SSL tasks. The inherent randomness in SSL training and the
131   absence of explicit downstream task labels make it challenging for these methods to provide accurate
132   attributions for the model’s representational capabilities.
133
      Attribution techniques play a crucial role in the interpretability research of deep learning models,
134
      aiming to reveal the contribution of input features to the model’s prediction results. Compared to
135   other interpretability methods, attribution methods adhere to two important axioms: Sensitivity and
136   Implementation Invariance (Sundararajan et al., 2017). The Sensitivity axiom emphasizes that if
137   an input differs from the baseline in only one feature, leading to a different prediction outcome,
138   then this differing feature should be assigned a non-zero attribution. The Implementation Invariance
139   axiom requires that two functionally equivalent neural networks, despite potentially different imple-
140   mentations, should have consistent attribution results. These axioms provide essential guidance for
141   the design of attribution methods, helping to ensure the rationality and reliability of the attribution
142   results.
143   However, most current attribution methods (Pan et al., 2021; Hesse et al., 2021; Erion et al., 2021;
144   Kapishnikov et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2024c;b;a) face applicability challenges in SSL tasks. Firstly,
145   these methods are typically task-specific, requiring design tailored to particular downstream tasks
146   (e.g., classification). The characteristic of SSL is its pre-training stage’s independence from specific
147   task labels, making it difficult to directly apply these methods. Secondly, the SSL training process
148   often involves a large number of random operations, such as data augmentation and negative sample
149
      selection in contrastive learning. Existing attribution methods have limited capabilities in handling
      such randomness, making it difficult to provide stable attribution results. Therefore, given the unique
150
      nature of SSL tasks, developing attribution methods that can analyze the model’s representation
151
      learning ability without specific task labels and handle the randomness in the training process is a
152   research topic of significant importance.
153
154
      2.3   I NTERPRETABILITY OF SSL
155
156   Despite the fact that SSL does not rely on specific task labels during the pre-training phase, research
157   on the interpretability of SSL models is still in its infancy. The Attribution Guided Factorization
158   (AGF) method achieves visual interpretation of both supervised and SSL models by combining
159   gradient and attribution information (Gur et al., 2021). However, when interpreting SSL tasks,
160   this method requires the incorporation of specific downstream tasks. This dependency confines the
161   attribution results to the specific task, failing to reflect the general-purpose features extracted by SSL
      models, and thus creating a conflict with the objectives of SSL. On the other hand, a hierarchical


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      latent variable model has been employed for in-depth interpretability analysis of MAE (Kong et al.,
163   2023). Nevertheless, this method is primarily applicable to Transformer architectures and cannot
164   explain the excellent performance achieved by current non-Transformer SSL models. Therefore,
165   developing interpretation methods that are applicable to various SSL model architectures and can
166   effectively explain the representation learning ability of SSL models without relying on specific
167   downstream tasks remains a crucial challenge that needs to be addressed.

      Let fθ : Rn → Rd denote the encoder learned through SSL, which transforms an input sample
174
      x ∈ Rn into a latent feature representation z ∈ Rd , where d ≪ n. This process is expressed as
175   z = fθ (x), with θ representing the parameters of the encoder.
176
177   In practical applications, the extracted latent feature z is fed into a downstream task gϕ : Rd → Rm .
178
      This is denoted as ỹ = gϕ (z), where ϕ represents the parameters of the downstream task model,
      typically obtained by fine-tuning after θ is trained. In the case of a classification task, m equal to the
179
      number of classes c. We utilize gϕ (z) in the evaluation of SSLA.
180
181   Furthermore, we use T to represent the data augmentation techniques employed during SSL train-
182   ing. In this paper, these techniques include color jitter, Gaussian blur, grayscale conversion, random
183   resized crop, and solarization. It is important to note that the T function is stochastic in nature.
184
      S : Rd × Rd → R denotes the function for calculating the distance between latent feature represen-
      tations.
185
186   The objective of attribution is to construct a function A : x × f → Rn that transforms the input
187   sample x into an attribution result of the same dimensionality. This process is represented as a =
188   A(x, f ). Here, ai signifies the importance of xi (the i-th dimension) to fθ . A larger ai implies that
189   xi is more important for fθ .

191   3.2   P REREQUISITES FOR S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING ATTRIBUTION
192
193   Before introducing the SSLA algorithm, we outline the following three prerequisites that need to be
194   satisfied in our attribution design process, along with the rationale behind them.
195
196   Prerequisite 1: The interpretation of SSL should not introduce information from downstream
197   tasks. (Avoid interference from downstream training information.) The evaluation of SSL
198   often involves downstream tasks. For example, a common practice in visual SSL is to freeze the
199
      parameters of fθ , introduce gϕ with only one linear layer, and train the parameters ϕ to observe
      whether fθ can extract linearly separable features (e.g., SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020)). However,
200
      during the interpretation of SSL tasks, gϕ or its parameters ϕ should not be introduced. The reason
201
      is that ϕ is trained separately after the SSL task, and we cannot be certain whether the importance
202   in the final attribution result a comes from gϕ or fθ . Some SSL interpretability methods introduce
203   information from ϕ during the interpretation process (Gur et al., 2021). More importantly, once
204   information from gϕ is introduced, the interpretability results are limited to the downstream task,
205   rather than providing an explanation of the SSL task itself.
206
207   Prerequisite 2: The interpretation process should not introduce samples other than the current
208   sample. Similar to the reasoning behind Prerequisite 1, sampling additional samples introduces
209   extraneous information. The attribution results are influenced by the sampling process and the in-
210   formation from the introduced samples. Moreover, the attribution results are biased towards the
211   interaction between samples, rather than the model’s focus on the current sample.
212
213   Prerequisite 3: The interpretation process should not be restricted to specific model archi-
214   tectures. SSL algorithms abstract the encoder structure during their design, which implies that
215   SSL algorithms themselves do not heavily rely on specific model architectures. Specifically, some
      interpretability methods that utilize the self-attention mechanism for importance analysis cannot ex-

233   3.3        S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING ATTRIBUTION (SSLA)
234
      The core idea of SSL is to find invariant features amidst variations. For example, after applying
235
      multiple transformations τ1 , τ2 ∼ T to an input sample x, we expect the encoder fθ to extract similar
236
      feature representations. Typically, we use cosine similarity as the evaluation metric for similarity,
237   i.e., we desire a higher value for cos(fθ (τ1 (x)), fθ (τ2 (x))).
238
239   Different SSL methods may have different training strategies, such as sampling negative samples
240
      during self-supervised training (e.g., SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020)) or using multiple encoders with
      similar functionality (e.g., BYOL (Grill et al., 2020), where one θ adopts a similar θ′ ). This core
241
      idea can also be extended to reconstruction-based methods like MAE (He et al., 2022), where the
242
      variation lies in the random masking process, and the expectation is that the model can reconstruct
243   the same image regardless of which regions are masked. The effectiveness of the model on a sample
244   can be evaluated by Eτ1 ,τ2 ∼T [cos(fθ (τ1 (x)), fθ (τ2 (x)))]. However, when interpreting SSL mod-
245   els, we need to maintain this core idea while reducing the influence of randomness and ensuring
246   that the interpretability algorithm can directly operate on the original sample. Therefore, we adopt
247   Eτ ∼T [cos(fθ (x), fθ (τ (x)))] for evaluation (Figure 1 provides a better illustration of this process).
248   As shown in Figure 1, features are extracted from both the original image and its augmented ver-
249   sions, and then the cosine similarity between the features of these augmented images and the original
250   image is calculated. The similarity of these features obtained from different augmentations should
251
      be as close as possible to the features of the original image.
252   Essentially, all transformations are based on the premise of not destroying the semantic information
253   represented by the input, meaning that the same semantic information should yield the same output.
254   Therefore, interpreting an SSL-learned model involves explaining the contribution of each feature
255   to preserving the semantic information of the sample. In this process, we observe the impact of
256   modifying each feature on Eτ ∼T [cos(fθ (x), fθ (τ (x)))].
257   Since the purpose of fθ (τ (x)) is to compute similarity with the original sample, it remains un-
258   changed during feature modification, eliminating the need for repeated calculations. We can use
259   Z = [z1 , z2 , · · · , zN ] for caching, where zi = τ (x), τ ∼ T . Thus, the evaluation of whether the
260   model is effective on a sample can be transformed into:

      With S(x, fθ , Z), the design of SSLA becomes straightforward. The core of the SSLA algorithm
276

366   4.2   C HOICE OF S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING M ETHODS
367
368   In our experiments, we selected five representative SSL methods: BYOL (Grill et al., 2020), Sim-
369
      CLR (Chen et al., 2020), SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021), MoCo-v3 (Chen et al., 2021), and MAE (He

370
      bootstrapping positive sample pairs; SimCLR relies on a large number of negative samples for con-
371
      trastive learning; SimSiam adopts a Siamese network architecture and trains solely on positive sam-

      applicability and effectiveness of SSLA across various SSL models.

      4.3   E VALUATION M ETHOD
379
380   Traditional interpretability evaluation methods, such as Insertion score, Deletion score, and INFD
381   score (Petsiuk et al., 2018; Yeh et al., 2019), are impractical when applied to the evaluation of
382   interpretability methods for self-supervised tasks. The fundamental reason lies in the necessity of
383   introducing the concept of a baseline in these traditional methods. The baseline is typically set as an
384   all-zero image or an image with added perturbations (usually Gaussian blur) (Sturmfels et al., 2020).
385   However, a successfully established baseline should, in essence, not provide any guidance to the
386
      current model’s decision-making. That is, replacing features in the input with their corresponding
      values from the baseline should decrease the model’s decision-making capability. However, for SSL
387
      tasks, replacing with zeros or adding perturbations to the sample has minimal impact on the decision.
388
389   As previously mentioned, the essence of SSL lies in seeking invariance through transformations.
390   Therefore, the traditional process of replacing baselines can also be viewed as a form of transforma-
391   tion. For example, in a trained SSL model, operations like Gaussian blur have minimal impact on
392
      the final similarity calculation. This is because data augmentation methods such as Gaussian blur
      are already incorporated as part of the transformation set (T ) during SSL training. Unfortunately,
393
      there is currently no baseline that can both consistently affect SSL tasks and align with intuitive
394
      understanding. Hence, there is an urgent need to propose new evaluation methods specifically for
395   interpretability in SSL tasks.
396
397   Our core idea is to evaluate the potential of different regions in influencing the decision-making
398
      of SSL tasks. In simple terms, if a feature is more important for the decision-making of an SSL
      task, then fixing this feature and modifying other features should have a smaller impact on the
399
      model’s output, and vice versa. Under this assumption, we need to clarify two basic definitions for
400
      evaluation:
401
402         1. How to define the impact on the model’s output.
403
            2. How to ensure that modifying other features is definitely effective.
404
405
      First, let’s define the impact on the model’s output. As mentioned earlier, cosine similarity is used
406   in SSL tasks to assess the similarity of outputs, with the expectation of finding invariant features
407   amidst variations. Following this line of thought, we can use cosine similarity to quantify the degree
408   of change in the output. Specifically, we use cos(fθ (x), fθ (x̃)) to evaluate the change in decision-
409   making caused by transforming sample x into x̃. A smaller value indicates a larger impact on the
410   output. When x̃ = x, the cosine similarity is constantly 1, indicating no impact on the output.
411   Adopting this evaluation approach avoids the selection of a baseline, thereby eliminating the bias
412   introduced by baseline selection in previous evaluation methods.
413   Next, we address the issue of ensuring the effectiveness of modifying other features. This leads to
414   our core hypothesis:
415
416   Theorem 2. ∀m ∈ Rn and mj ∈ {0, 1}, there exists an update direction m·sign ∂ cos(fθ∂(x̃),f
                                                                                               x̃
                                                                                                   θ (x))


417   that guarantees the effectiveness of feature modification, i.e., cos(fθ (x), fθ (x̃)) ≤ 0.
418
419   The mask is created by selecting the features with the highest attribution scores and setting the
420
      corresponding proportion of mj to 0. The proof of Theorem 2 is provided in Appendix D. To
      assess the model’s sensitivity, we modify the features outside the masked region. If the model is
421
      less affected by these modifications, it indicates that the features within the masked region are more
422
      important; conversely, if the model is significantly affected, it suggests that the masked features are
423   less critical.

      4.5      R ESULTS
458
459
      In this experiment, as we are the first to propose an attribution method that does not utilize down-
460
      stream task information, we lack a direct baseline for comparison. We choose to compare random
461
      masking (Random Mask) with masking based on the ranking of attribution values computed by
462   SSLA. By setting different Mask Rates, we mask both important and unimportant features and ob-
463   serve the changes in similarity between the attacked samples and the original images.
464
465
      When important features are masked, we apply a first-order adversarial attack to the unmasked re-
      gions. A higher similarity between the attacked sample and the original image indicates a greater
466
      impact of the masked features on the model’s decision-making. As shown in Table 1, the SSLA
467
      method excels in this strategy, accurately identifying features that play a crucial role in the model’s
468   decisions. Specifically, at a Mask Rate of 50%, SSLA outperforms Random Mask across all SSL
469   methods, with the highest similarity score observed for MAE, reaching 0.89 for SSLA when mask-
470   ing important features, compared to 0.80 for Random Mask. Similar trends are seen for BYOL,
471   SimCLR, SimSiam, and MoCo-v3, with SSLA achieving similarity scores of 0.81, 0.76, 0.81, and
472   0.73, respectively, for masking important features, while Random Mask scores are comparatively
473   lower at 0.74, 0.70, 0.72, and 0.68.
474   Furthermore, when unimportant features are masked, SSLA consistently produces lower similarity
475   scores between the attacked and original samples, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing the
476   influence of unimportant features. For example, in the case of MoCo-v3 at a 50% Mask Rate,
477   the similarity score drops to 0.65 when masking unimportant features using SSLA, as opposed to
478   0.68 for Random Mask. Similarly, for BYOL and MAE, SSLA achieves similarity scores of 0.73
479   and 0.76, while Random Mask results in higher similarity scores of 0.74 and 0.80, respectively.
480   These results validate the stability and reliability of SSLA in both highlighting crucial features and
481   minimizing interference from unimportant regions.
482   Additionally, as the Mask Rate increases, the SSLA method continues to outperform Random Mask
483   in masking important features. At a 90% Mask Rate, SSLA achieves the highest similarity score for
484   MAE at 0.99, while Random Mask remains slightly behind at 0.96. For BYOL, SimCLR, SimSiam,
485   and MoCo-v3, the SSLA method consistently reaches similarity scores of 0.98, 0.97, 0.98, and 0.97,
      respectively, compared to Random Mask scores of 0.95, 0.92, 0.95, and 0.93.

494
495
      In this work, we proposed the Self-Supervised Learning Attribution (SSLA) method, designed to
      address the unique challenges of interpreting SSL models. Through extensive experiments on the
496
      ImageNet dataset using a variety of SSL methods, including BYOL, SimCLR, SimSiam, MoCo-v3,
497
      and MAE, we demonstrated the robustness and applicability of the SSLA method. The evaluation
498   results indicate that SSLA can effectively attribute features, distinguishing between those that are
499   critical and those that are less important to the model’s decision-making process. This capability
500   is consistent across different SSL models and varying mask rates, highlighting SSLA’s adaptabil-
501   ity. Moreover, our experiments showed that SSLA outperforms random masking strategies, provid-
502   ing more accurate and stable attributions. The method successfully guides models to focus on the
503   regions that genuinely influence decision-making, thereby enhancing both the interpretability and
504   trustworthiness of SSL models.
