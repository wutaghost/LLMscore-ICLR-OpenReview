009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we present UniG, a view-consistent 3D reconstruction and novel
012            view synthesis model that generates a high-fidelity representation of 3D Gaus-
013            sians from sparse images. Existing 3D Gaussians-based methods usually regress
014            Gaussians per-pixel of each view, create 3D Gaussians per view separately, and
015            merge them through point concatenation. Such a view-independent reconstruction
016
               approach often results in a view inconsistency issue, where the predicted positions
               of the same 3D point from different views may have discrepancies. To address this
017
               problem, we develop a DETR (DEtection TRansformer)-like framework, which
018
               treats 3D Gaussians as decoder queries and updates their parameters layer by layer
019            by performing multi-view cross-attention (MVDFA) over multiple input images.
020            In this way, multiple views naturally contribute to modeling a unitary represen-
021            tation of 3D Gaussians, thereby making 3D reconstruction more view-consistent.
022            Moreover, as the number of 3D Gaussians used as decoder queries is irrespective
023            of the number of input views, allow an arbitrary number of input images without
024            causing memory explosion. Extensive experiments validate the advantages of our
025            approach, showcasing superior performance over existing methods quantitatively
026            (improving PSNR by 4.2 dB when trained on Objaverse and tested on the GSO
027
               benchmark) and qualitatively.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   3D object reconstruction and novel view synthesis (NVS) are pivotal in computer vision and graph-
032   ics, converting 2D images into detailed 3D structures in various applications such as robotics, aug-
033   mented reality, virtual reality, medical imaging, archaeology, and more. Neural Radiance Fields
034   (NeRF) attempts (Xu et al., 2024; Mildenhall et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021a; Chen et al., 2021; Yu
035   et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024a; Xiong et al., 2024) are notable in 3D fields recently. However, their
036   progress is impeded by slow rendering speeds due to the implicit. Recently, as a semi-implicit repre-
037   sentation, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3D GS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) has achieved remarkable optimization
038
      speed and high-quality novel view rendering performance in representing objects or scenes.
039   However, many recent methods based on 3D GS techniques encounter the challenge of view incon-
040   sistency. This issue arises due to imprecise depth estimations from each views leading to duplicated
041   representations of the same object regions within the 3D reconstructions from different perspectives.
042   For instance, MVGamba (Yi et al., 2024) treats images and 3D Gaussians as sequences in Mamba
043   (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024) which leads to input view order induced view inconsistency.
044
      Splatter Image (Szymanowicz et al., 2024) and LGM (Tang et al., 2024a) predict pixel-aligned 3D
      Gaussians for each input view in the camera space of the corresponding input view. Then these 3D
045
      Gaussians are transformed from camera spaces of each view to the world space and naively merged
046
      together to obtain the ultimate 3D Gaussians, as depicted in fig. 1(a).
047
048   However, such dimension lifting from 2D images to 3D Gaussians in different views are indepen-
049   dent and lacks interactions among different views, thus it may result in a single object point being
050
      represented by multiple 3D Gaussians at different positions, leading to the aforementioned view-
      inconsistency issue (Yang et al., 2024; Dong & Wang, 2024).
051
052   To address this issue, we propose a Unitary 3D Gaussians (UniG) representation. Inspired by De-
053   formable DETR (Liu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Li et al.,
      2023a) that treats the position and properties of bounding box (Bbox) as queries of the Transformer

      ically, MVDFA utilize camera modulation techniques (Karras et al., 2019; Hong et al., 2024) to
072

073   view, with the weights and bias trained from camera parameters. Such operation gives each view
074   its corresponding camera pose information. The view-specific queries are then used for performing
075   deformable attention over corresponding images. Although similar to DFA3D (Li et al., 2023a) and
076   BEVFormer (Li et al., 2022) in employing deformable attention in 3D with a point projection strat-
077   egy, our model prioritizes multi-view distinctions (different qureies in different views) to achieve a
078   more precise 3D representation. Further elaboration is available in section 2.
079   As the number of 3D Gaussians is usually very large, e.g. over 10,000, the self-attention operation
080   in a deformable Transformer decoder layer will demand a significant memory and computational
081   cost. To improve the efficiency, inspired by (Wang et al., 2021b), we introduce a 3D Spatial Effi-
082   cient Self-Attention (SESA) approach, leveraging Fast Point Sampling (FPS) (Qi et al., 2017a) to
083   downsize the number of keys and values while preserving the number of queries. Moreover, directly
084   regressing the positions of 3D Gaussians may lead to convergence challenges (see appendix A.4).
085   To address this problem, we utilize a coarse-to-fine framework, where a direct lift from 2D to 3D is
086   employed for every pixel in randomly selected input views at the coarse stage. Then, the 3D Gaus-
087
      sians from this stage serve as the initialization for the deformable Transformer-based refinement
      network, facilitating meaningful projected positions and aiding in convergence.
088
089   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
090
091          • We propose UniG, a novel 3D object reconstruction and NVS algorithm which utilizes
092
               a unitary set of 3D Gaussians as queries in deformable Transformer. Such an approach
               allows all input views to contribute to the same 3D representation and effectively addresses
093
               the view inconsistency issue and supports arbitrary number of input views.
094
095          • We propose to use MVDFA for tackling the multi-view fusion challenge, SESA for mini-
096
               mizing the memory usage in self-attention, and a coarse-to-fine framework for mitigating
               the convergence issue when directly regressing world coordinates of 3D Gaussians.
097
098          • Both quantitative and qualitative experiments are conducted for evaluation. Our proposed
099            method achieves the state-of-the-art performance on the commonly-used benchmark.

107   diffusion models require longer time to generate 3D with multi-step denoising process, thus limiting
      their applicability in real-time scenarios. Recent methodologies that rely on a single forward pro-

127
128   Deformable Transformer in 3D DFA3D (Li et al., 2023a) and BEVFormer (Li et al., 2022) are
129   introduced to address the feature-lifting challenge in 3D detection and autonomous driving tasks.
130   They achieve notable performance enhancements by employing a deformable Transformer to bridge
131   the gap between 2D and 3D. DFA3D initially uses estimated depth to convert 2D feature maps to 3D,
132   sampling around reference points for deformable attention in each view. However, the 3D sampling
133
      point design causes all projected 2D points to represent a singular point, neglecting view variations.

134
      the feature onto each input view. The Spatial Cross-Attention facilitates the fusion of BEV and
135
      image spaces, though challenges persist sampling 4 height values per pillar in the BEV feature for
136   selecting 3D reference points may limit coverage, posing challenges in accurate keypoint selection
137   for the model. When contrasting DFA3D and BEVFormer with our MVDFA, a commonality lies
138   in projecting onto 3D regression targets to extract data from various image perspectives. However,
139   our model diverges by employing camera modulation to differentiate queries across views, enabling
140   more specific information retrieval.

145   3.1   P RELIMENARIES OF 3D GS
146
147
      3D GS (Kerbl et al., 2023) is a novel rendering method that can be viewed as an extension of point-
      based rendering methods (Kerbl et al., 2023; Chen & Wang, 2024). Hence, 3D Gaussians can serve
148
      as effective 3D representations for efficient differentiable rendering. Each 3D Gaussian ellipsoid
149
      can be described by G = {SH, µ, σ, R, S}. The color of 3D Gaussians is represented by spherical
150   harmonics (SH ∈ R12 ) while the geometry is described by the center positions µ ∈ R3 , shapes
151   (covariance matrix Σ), and opacity (σ ∈ R) of ellipsoids (Zwicker et al., 2001; Kerbl et al., 2023).
152   Especially, the covariance matrix can be optimized through a combination of rotation and scaling
153   for each ellipsoid as Σ = RSST RT , where R ∈ R4 (represented by quaternion) represents the
154   rotation and S ∈ R3 contains the scales in three directions.

194   3.2.1   F EATURE EXTRACTOR
195
196   To extract image features from multi-view input, we utilize UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Song
197   et al., 2021b), a widely employed feature extractor in 3D reconstruction tasks, as demonstrated in
198   Tang et al. (2024a); Szymanowicz et al. (2024). To enhance the network’s understanding of the
199
      complete 3D object, multi-view cross-attention is employed to transfer information among views
      right after the UNet block, activated when the number of input views exceeds one. In this config-
200
      uration, each input view acts as queries, while the concatenation (post-flattening) of the remaining
201
      views serves as keys and values. To efficiently enable cross-attention across all views, we employ
202   shifted-window attention, as introduced in the Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021). This mechanism
203   reduces interactions by focusing on tokens within a local window, effectively reducing memory us-
204   age for large input sequences. By processing tokens within a fixed window, shifted-window attention
205   effectively lowers the computational complexity, thereby enhancing the overall efficiency.
206

      3.2.2   V IEW- AWARE QUERY REFINEMENT DECODER

209
      Decoder structure In the decoder module, we employ a fixed number of queries Q ∈ RN ×C
210   with N and C denote the number of Gaussians and the hidden dimension to model 3D Gaussians by
211   associating queries with 3D Gaussian ellipsoid parameters G, including the center µ, opacity σ, ro-
212   tation R, scaling S, and Spherical Harmonics SH. As depicted in fig. 2, the queries navigate through
213   multiple decoder layers, each including a multi-view deformable attention (MVDFA) (section 3.2.2)
214   mechanism to leverage image features, a spatial efficient self-attention (SESA) (section 3.2.3) layer
215   for inter-Gaussian interactions, and a feed-forward network (FFN). The functionality of a decoder
      layer can be summarized by eq. (1), where F represents image features from different views and Pl

224   Multi-view deformable attention (MVDFA) The goal of MVDFA is to enhance the unified
225   queries and Gaussian representations by integrating multi-view image features. Following origi-
226   nal design of DFA, trainable sampling points are employed on the image features to sample the most
227   relevant image features as values (Carion et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021). The remaining problem
228   lies in determining the sampling points and attention scores. Specifically, we project the 3D queries
229   onto each image view and adjust it using camera modulation to account for view discrepancies. The
230   sampling offsets and attention scores are subsequently obtained from the view-specific queries.
231   By leveraging the center µ of each 3D Gaussian from the previous layer, along with the correspond-
232   ing camera poses πi and intrinsic parameters Ki for the i-th image, we can compute UV coordinates
233   Pi by projecting the center coordinates of each 3D Gaussian onto the image plane of the i-th in-
234   put image using the pinhole camera model (Forsyth & Ponce, 2003; Hartley & Zisserman, 2003):
235   Pi = Ki πi µ. In this context, both matrices Ki and πi are expressed in homogeneous form. These
236   UV coordinates in Pi then function as the reference points for 2D deformable attention.
237   As depicted fig. 3 (a), in 3D, we have a set of queries associated with 3D Gaussian paremeters G
238   while the queries for each image planes should to be adjusted to suit each view individually. To
239   tackle this issue, we start by using camera modulation with the adaptive layer norm (adaLN) (Hong
240   et al., 2024; Karras et al., 2019; 2020; Viazovetskyi et al., 2020) to generate view-specific queries.
241   More information on this modulation is provided in fig. 3(b). Subsequently, a linear layer to predicts
242   the sampling offsets ∆s for retriving images features as values and another linear layer to predicts
243   the attention scores α of the sampling points s. Following (Zhang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2021),
244
      we compute attention scores directly from queries, omitting keys to streamline calculations. Then,
      we apply the grid sampling algorithm with bilinear interpolation to extract image features at these
245
      sampling points, which act as the values v for cross attention.
246
247   Finally, for each input view, we compute the updated queries for each view using the attention
248   scores α and sampled values v. The ultimate unitary queries are then computed as a weighted sum
249   of individual view queries, with the weights calculated using an linear layer on the view-specific
250
      queries. Detailed pseudo code for our multi-view deformable cross-attention is available in fig. 3(b).
251
252   3.2.3   S PATIAL EFFICIENT SELF - ATTENTION (SESA)
253   Our multi-view deformable cross-attention mechanism demonstrates a superior efficiency in terms
254   of computational cost and memory usage. However, self-attention is computationally expensive,
255   especially with numerous 3D Gaussians. Updating each Gaussian with information from all oth-
256   ers may not always be essential, as neighboring Gaussians often contain similar information. To
257   tackle this problem, drawing inspiration from Wang et al. (2021b), we introduce a method to re-
258   duce the number of keys and values while keeping the number of queries unchanged during self-
259   attention. This selective update strategy enables each query to be updated with a subset of related
260   queries, effectively enhancing the information exchange efficiency. To ensure crucial information
261
      flow, we leverage the Fast Point Sampling (FPS) algorithm from point cloud methodologies (Qi
      et al., 2017a;b). By utilizing Gaussian centers µ to identify distant points for querying, we opti-
262
      mize memory usage while guaranteeing essential information sharing among Gaussians. Additional
263
      details are in appendix A.1.

333   4.1       DATASET AND EXPERIMENT SETTINGS
334
335   Dataset We utilized a refined subset of the Objaverse LVIS dataset (Deitke et al., 2023) for train-
336   ing and validation. The training dataset comprised two sets of rendered images: one set featured 12
337   random camera poses, while the other included input rendering images captured from fixed view-
338   points (front, back, left, right). Supervision was provided from 32 random views spanning elevations
339   between -30 to 30 degrees. The resolution of the rendered images was downscaled to 128 × 128.
340   To evaluate our model, we conducted tests on the Google Scanned Objects (GSO) benchmark. Two
341   test sets were utilized: one with fixed-view inputs (e.g., front, left, back, right) at 0 degrees elevation,
342   tested on 32 random views with elevations ranging from 0 to 30 degrees, and the other includes
343   25 random views with corresponding camera poses. Importantly, there are no constraints on the
344   elevation of the rendered views. We refer to these test sets as GSO-random and GSO-fixed in our
345   subsequent analysis. More details for dataset can be found in appendix A.2.
346
347   Evaluation metric We compute the peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR), structural similarity index
348   (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004), and perceptual distance (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018b) between the ren-
349   dered images and the ground truth. Additionally, we offer visual representations of both the rendered
350
      images and the 3D Gaussian centers as a point cloud. More details are provided in appendix A.2.
351

511   4.5     A PPLICATIONS IN 3D GENERATION
512
513   Image-to-3D conversion represents a fundamental application in 3D generation. Following the
514
      methodology of LGM and InstantMesh (Tang et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024), we initially leverage
      a multi-view diffusion model, ImageDream (Wang & Shi, 2023), to generate four predetermined
515
      views. Subsequently, our model is utilized for 3D Gaussian reconstruction. A comparative analysis
516
      with LGM and InstantMesh is detailed in appendix A.3.2. We also showcase the quality results of
517   our model on both the GSO dataset and in-the-wild images in appendix A.3.1.
518
519   Our model can also do the Text-to-3D task. To evaluate quality, we utilize MVDream (Shi et al.,
520
      2024) to generate a single image from a text prompt. Subsequently, a diffusion model is employed
      to produce multi-view images, which are then processed by our model to derive a 3D representation.
521
      A qualitative comparison of the text-to-3D generation is presented in appendix A.3.1.

524   5      C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
525
526   In this paper, we have introduced a novel sparse view 3D reconstruction and novel view synthe-
527   sis method. Initially, a fixed number of 3D Gaussians with predefined properties are initialized,
528   and each Gaussian ellipsoid is projected onto input image features extracted by a feature extractor.
529
      We propose the MVDFA block to integrate image features surrounding the projected 3D Gaussians
      from each view to refine the 3D Gaussians, employing a coarse-to-fine strategy to ensure robust
530
      model convergence. Additionally, we develop a spatially efficient self-attention mechanism to min-
531
      imize computational costs, tackling view inconsistency and computational inefficiency. Our model
532   accommodates an arbitrary number of views as input and showcases its effectiveness through quan-
533   titative and qualitative experiments compared to state-of-the-art methods trained on Objaverse and
534   tested on the GSO dataset. Furthermore, with the aid of an off-the-shelf diffusion model, our model
535   undertakes generation tasks such as image-to-3D and text-to-3D conversions. We present an ab-
536   lation study elucidating the significance of each model component. While our model signifies a
537   notable advancement in sparse view 3D reconstruction, there are inherent limitations. Presently,
538   user-provided camera parameters, both camera poses and intrisics, are necessary for projecting 3D
539   Gaussians onto images, presenting potential challenges in 3D reconstruction. Addressing this issue
      stands as a focal point for future research.

835
836   A.1   S PATIAL EFFICIENT SELF ATTENTION (SESA)
837
838
      While our 3D-aware deformable attention mechanism is notably efficient, the computational cost
      and memory occupation mainly arises in the self-attention component, particularly when dealing
839
      with a large number of 3D Gaussians. However, updating each 3D Gaussian with information from
840
      all others is not always necessary because those neighbouring 3D Gaussians usually carry similar
841   information.
842
843   To mitigate this issue, as depicted in fig. 6 and drawing inspiration from Wang et al. (2021b), we
844
      introduce a technique aimed at reducing the size of the key and value components while maintain-
      ing the query component unaltered within the self-attention process. The core concept behind this
845
      approach is that while each 3D Gaussian requires updating, not every other 3D Gaussian needs to
846
      contribute to this update. We achieve this by selectively updating each query solely with a subset of
847   corresponding queries linked to other 3D Gaussians.
848
849   To retain crucial information flow, we leverage the Fast Point Sampling (FPS) algorithm commonly
850
      used in point cloud methodologies like PointNet (Qi et al., 2017a) and PointNet++ (Qi et al., 2017b).
      Specifically, we employ the Gaussian centers µ to identify the most distantly located points and use
851
      these points to index the queries. By implementing this strategy, we significantly reduce the model’s
852
      overall memory footprint while preserving essential information exchange among the Gaussians.

897   Experiment setting We train our model on the setting of 4 views, each time we randomly select
898   4 views as input and all the views for supervision. In the coarse stage, we train the model with less
899   views (i.e. 2 views) with resolution 128 × 128 and generate 16384 3D Gaussians as initialization
900
      of the fine stage. In the fine stage, We use 19600 3D Gaussians to represent the 3D object. For
      the 3D Gaussians from the coarse stage, we use the mask to remove the background points and
901
      padding the number of 3D Gaussians to 19600 by copying some of the remaining 3D Gaussians.
902
      The selected 3D Gaussians are then utilized to project queries onto image plane in the refine stage.
903   In each deformable attention layer, we utilize 4 sampling points for each projected 3D Gaussian
904   reference point to sample values on the image.
905
906
      We use 4 decoder layers and the hidden dimension is 256. Moreover, when training the fine stage,
      we finetune both the coarse stage and the encoder. We use a mixed-precision training (Narang et al.,
907
      2018) with BF16 data type. We train our model with Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) optimizer and the
908
      learning rate is 0.0001. We take 300K iteration with batch size 4. For the coarse stage, we train it
909   on 8 3090 GPUs (24G) for 5 days and for the fine stage, we train it on 8 A100 (80G) for 3 days.

       Hyperparameter selection As previously highlighted, the memory bottleneck of our model lies
1281   in the pointwise self-attention mechanism. To address this, we implement a spatially efficient self-
1282   attention technique to alleviate memory consumption. Illustrated in fig. 18 (left), as we augment
1283   the downsampling rate of the key and value in the self-attention mechanism, the memory overhead
1284   diminishes linearly, while the PSNR reduction is not as rapid. Consequently, we opt for a down-
1285   sampling rate located at the inflection point, which we determine to be 0.01, balancing memory
1286   efficiency with reconstruction quality. Similarly, we select the number of Gaussians as 19600 as
1287   shown in fig. 18 (right).
1288   In table 11, we opted for 4 decoder layers over 6, as the latter offers marginal improvement but
1289   demands significantly more computational resources. Additionally, we experimented with using the
1290   fine stage initialized with the coarse stage as the encoder and tested the efficacy of fine-tuning both
1291   stages. Our findings indicate that fine-tuning both stages yields the best results.

1353   the respective camera spaces and subsequently merge them in the global world space. Despite
1354   pixelSplat’s integration of cross-view-aware features through an epipolar Transformer, accurately
1355   forecasting a dependable probabilistic depth distribution based solely on image features remains
1356   a formidable task (Chen, Yuedong and Xu, Haofei and Zheng, Chuanxia and Zhuang, Bohan and
1357   Pollefeys, Marc and Geiger, Andreas and Cham, Tat-Jen and Cai, Jianfei, 2024). This limitation
1358   often translates to pixelSplat’s geometry reconstruction exhibiting comparatively lower quality and
1359
       plagued by noticeable noisy artifacts (Chen, Yuedong and Xu, Haofei and Zheng, Chuanxia and
       Zhuang, Bohan and Pollefeys, Marc and Geiger, Andreas and Cham, Tat-Jen and Cai, Jianfei, 2024).
1360
       Upon examination, we observed that even after isolating points within a visual cone and eliminating
1361
       background Gaussians, the geometry fails to convey meaningful information, yielding unsatisfactory
1362   results.
1363
1364   In contrast, MVSplat adopts a design that incorporates a cost volume storing cross-view feature
1365
       similarities for all possible depth candidates. These similarities offer crucial geometric cues for
       3D surface localization, leading to more substantial depth predictions. However, akin to Splatter
1366
       Image, which assigns each pixel a Gaussian and thereby generates a planar representation rather than
1367
       the object itself, MVSplat’s approach may obscure object details due to occlusion by background
1368   Gaussians from other viewpoints, resulting in suboptimal outcomes.
1369
1370   To address this issue, we selectively mask the positioning of Gaussians on background pixels, fo-
1371

1372
