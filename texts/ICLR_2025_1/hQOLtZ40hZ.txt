008                                                         A BSTRACT
009
010            Offline reinforcement learning is important in many settings with available observational data but
011            the inability to deploy new policies online due to safety, cost, and other concerns. Many recent
012            advances in causal inference and machine learning target estimation of “causal contrast” functions
013            such as CATE, which is sufficient for optimizing decisions and can adapt to potentially smoother
014            structure. We develop a dynamic generalization of the R-learner (Nie et al., 2021; Lewis & Syrgka-
015
               nis, 2021) for estimating and optimizing the difference of Qπ -functions, Qπ (s, a) − Qπ (s, a0 ), for
               potential discrete-valued actions a, a0 , which can be used to optimize multiple-valued actions with-
016
               out loss of generality. We leverage orthogonal estimation to improve convergence rates, even if Q
017
               and behavior policy (so-called nuisance functions) converge at slower rates and prove consistency of
018            policy optimization under a margin condition. The method can leverage black-box estimators of the
019            Q-function and behavior policy to target estimation of a more structured Q-function contrast, and
020            comprises of simple squared-loss minimization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORK
025   Learning optimal dynamic treatment rules, or sequential policies for taking actions, is important, although often only
026   observational data is available. Many recent works in offline reinforcement learning develop methodology to evaluate
027   and optimize sequential decision rules, without the ability to conduct online exploration.
028   Offline reinforcement learning is closely connected to causal inference. An extensive literature on causal inference and
029   machine learning establishes methodologies for learning causal contrasts, such as the conditional average treatment
030   effect (CATE) (Wager & Athey, 2018; Foster & Syrgkanis, 2019; Künzel et al., 2019; Kennedy, 2020), the covariate-
031   conditional difference in outcomes under treatment and control, which is sufficient for making optimal decisions.
032   A key “inductive bias” motivation is that the causal contrast (i.e. the difference that actions make on outcomes)
033   may be smoother or more structured (e.g., sparser) than what the main effects (what happens under either action by
034   itself). Therefore methods that specifically estimate these contrast functions could potentially adapt to this favorable
035   structure when it is available. A classically-grounded and rapidly growing line of work on double, orthogonal, or
036
      debiased machine learning (Kennedy, 2022; Chernozhukov et al., 2018) derives improved estimation procedures for
      these targets. Estimating the causal contrast can be statistically favorable, while sufficient for decision-making.
037
038   In this work, building on recent advances in heterogeneous treatment effect estimation, we focus on estimating anal-
039   ogous causal contrasts for offline reinforcement learning, namely τtπ (s; a, a0 ) = Qπt (s, a) − Qπt (s, a0 ), for possible
040   actions a, a0 in the action space A. We focus on the case of two actions, though the method generalizes to multi-
041   ple actions. This is closely related to, but different from advantage functions in reinforcement learning, defined as
042
      Qπ (s, a) − V π (s), the advantage of taking action a beyond the policy. For brevity, to describe statistical behavior, we
      denote the binary-action difference-of-Q τtπ (s) = Qπt (s, 1) − Qπt (s, 0), with the understanding that our analysis in
043
      the sequel is a tool for the multiple-action setting as well.
044

046   from sparsity patterns induced by the joint (in)dependence of rewards and transition dynamics on the (decompositions
047   of) the state variable. A number of recent works point out this additional structure (Wang et al.; 2022), for example
048
      of a certain transition-reward factorization, first studied by (Dietterich et al., 2018), that admits a sparse Q-function
      contrast (Pan & Schölkopf, 2023). (Zhou, 2024b) proposes a variant of the underlying blockwise pattern that also
049
      admits sparse optimal Q functions and policies. Section 1 illustrates how both of these structures have very different
050
      conditional independence assumptions. Methods designed assuming one model is correct may not perform well if it
051   is not. However, both structures imply the same property on the difference-of-Q functions: sparsity in the endogenous

069   The contributions of this work are as follows: We develop a dynamic generalization of the R-learner (Nie & Wager,
070
      2021; Lewis & Syrgkanis, 2021) for estimating the Q-function contrast. We show theoretical guarantees of improved
      convergence rates. Therefore our method leverages behavior policy estimation to improve estimation without suffering
071
      from unstable propensity weights. We illustrate benefits of adapting to structure in synthetic examples.

073   Related work for our approach. There is a large body of work on offline policy evaluation and optimization in offline
074   reinforcement learning (Jin et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021), including approaches that leverage importance sampling or
075   introduce marginalized versions (Jiang & Li, 2016; Thomas et al., 2015; Kallus & Uehara, 2019a; Liu et al., 2018). For
076
      Markov decision processes, other paperse study statistically semiparametrically efficient or doubly-robust estimation,
      but of the averaged policy value E[V1π (S1 )], rather than MSE convergence of the difference-of-Q function as we
077
      do here (Kallus & Uehara, 2019a;b; Xie et al., 2023; Kallus & Zhou, 2022). The literature on dynamic treatment
078
      regimes (DTRs) studies a method called advantage learning (Schulte et al., 2014), although DTRs in general lack
079   reward at every timestep, whereas we are particularly motivated on sparsity implications that arise jointly from reward
080   and transition structure. In particular, beyond policy value estimation, we aim to recover the entire contrast function.

      Advantage functions are widely studied in RL and dynamic treatment regimes (Neumann & Peters, 2008; mur, 2003).
      However, policy optimization is hard because which contrast it evaluates is time-t policy dependent when optimizing
083
      at time t. Our difference-of-Q functions are independent of candidate time-t policies when optimizing at time t.
084
085   (Pan & Schölkopf, 2023) note the analogy with causal contrast estimation and derive a Q-function independent esti-
086   mator, but in the online setting. After preparing an initial version of this paper, we became aware of the recent work of
087   Pan & Schölkopf (2024) in the offline setting. While their method elegantly avoids estimating future Q functions, it re-
088
      quires a nonconvex constraint on the action-average of advantages, which is computationally and statistically difficult.
      The motivations are different; the methods are complementary. (See appendix for more discussion.) Our identification
089
      argument is different and we focus on improved statistical guarantees.

093   generalize structural nested-mean models (SNMMs) by estimating “blip-to-zero” functions but only consider hetero-
094
      geneity based on a fixed initial state and dynamic treatment regimes with terminal rewards.Consequently, our analysis
      is similar at a high level. Overall, a closely related work with a similar goal of estimating contrast functionals in rein-
095
      forcement learning is that of (Shi et al., 2022), which derives a pseudo-outcome for estimating the Q-function contrast
096
      in the infinite horizon setting. We focus on the finite-horizon setting and have a different estimation strategy building
097   on the R-learner Note that the (single-stage) R-learner loss function is an overlap-weighted (Li et al., 2019) regression
098   against the doubly-robust score (DR-learner (Kennedy, 2020)). (See (Morzywolek et al., 2023; Chernozhukov et al.,

      We study the improved statistical rates of convergence from orthogonal estimation for policy evaluation (Theorems 1
      and 2) and show that this implies convergent policy optimization (Theorem 3). While Theorems 1 and 2 are applica-
243
      tions of orthogonal statistical learning to our new estimand, we establish Neyman-orthogonality of our loss function
244
      (eq. (6)). Policy optimization is more challenging; the novelty of Theorem 3 is showing that estimation error from
245   policy-dependent nuisance functions is of higher-order than the evaluation rates. Our analysis generally proceeds
246   under the following assumptions.

       Extending to nonlinear settings: details on MINE. Sample-based MI estimation methods with upper bounds (Cheng
1226
       et al., 2020) and lower bounds (Belghazi et al., 2018b) provide an alternative solution by shuffling the sample from the
1227
       joint distribution in the batch scope. In practice, we can use the statistics network to exploit the mutual information
1228   neural estimator (MINE) and optimize the absolute value in case of minor estimation errors. While mutual information
1229   is non-negative as a measure, the neural network estimates can be negative. Thus, we optimize the absolute value of the
1230   mutual information as our objective. This treats both positive and negative values consistently, driving the objective to
1231   zero and making the variables independent, as desired. Therefore, although we appreciate the concern that minimizing
1232   a function using a lower bound is less clear than maximizing it, our approach provides a pragmatic solution to the
1233   problem at hand, and our results confirm its effectiveness.
