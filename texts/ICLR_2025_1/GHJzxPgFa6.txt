010                                                    A BSTRACT
011
012                Effective research ideation is a critical step for scientific research. However, the
013                exponential increase in scientific literature makes it challenging for researchers to
014                stay current with recent advances and identify meaningful research directions. Re-
015
                   cent developments in large language models (LLMs) suggest a promising avenue
                   for automating the generation of novel research ideas. However, existing methods
016
                   for idea generation either trivially prompt LLMs or directly expose LLMs to ex-
017
                   tensive literature without indicating useful information. Inspired by the research
018                process of human researchers, we propose a Chain-of-Ideas (CoI) agent, an LLM-
019                based agent that organizes relevant literature in a chain structure to effectively
020                mirror the progressive development in a research domain. This organization facil-
021                itates LLMs to capture the current advancements in research, thereby enhancing
022                their ideation capabilities. Furthermore, we propose Idea Arena, an evaluation
023                protocol that can comprehensively evaluate idea generation methods from dif-
024                ferent perspectives, aligning closely with the preferences of human researchers.
025                Experimental results indicate that the CoI agent consistently outperforms other
026
                   methods and shows comparable quality as humans in research idea generation.
                   Moreover, our CoI agent is budget-friendly, with a minimum cost of $0.50 to gen-
027
                   erate a candidate idea and its corresponding experimental design1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Idea generation is a crucial aspect of scientific research for driving technological innovations and
033   breakthroughs. Traditionally, this process has been predominantly human-driven, necessitating ex-
034   pert researchers to review extensive literature, identify limitations in existing solutions, and propose
035   new research directions. However, the complexity and vastness of scientific literature, coupled with
036   rapid technological advancements, have rendered this task increasingly challenging for researchers.
037
      Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024;
038   Yang et al., 2024a) have enabled these models to exceed human experts in various scientific tasks,
039   including mathematics (Yu et al., 2023), theorem proving (Yang et al., 2023), and coding (Chen
040   et al., 2021). Building on this robust scientific foundation, one may hypothesize that LLMs could
041   support a more abstract and creative research idea-generation task. Notably, Si et al. (2024); Kumar
042   et al. (2024) have validated this hypothesis, highlighting its substantial potential to expedite the
043   discovery of novel concepts and uncharted research avenues.
044   Existing methods seek to address two key challenges to improve the quality of generated ideas:
045   curating pertinent literature for LLMs to gain inspiration and ensuring the novelty of generated ideas.
046   To address the first challenge, previous research enhances traditional academic retrieval systems,
047   which typically depend on textual similarity, with academic knowledge graphs (Baek et al., 2024;
048   Wang et al., 2023). For the second challenge, existing approaches either apply predefined criteria
049   such as novelty to guide the idea generation process (Baek et al., 2024) or iteratively refine ideas
050   until they demonstrate low embedding similarities with existing papers (Wang et al., 2023).
051   However, in existing attempts, LLMs are presented with an extensive volume of research literature
052   when asked to generate ideas. This makes LLMs vulnerable to the influence of less relevant works,

074   it into GoT framework (Besta et al., 2024) to generate what they interpret as a “novel idea”. However,
075   the resultant idea conflates two concepts: GoT is a prompting method while GraphGPT is a fine-
076   tuning method leveraging graph neural network architecture (Zhou et al., 2020). In contrast, human
077   researchers often trace the evolution of a research field by analyzing its progression from founda-
078   tional works to the most recent advancements. This comprehensive perspective provides valuable
079   insights into the key factors driving developments within the domain. Such an understanding enables
080   researchers to critically assess the limitations of earlier studies while identifying emerging trends.
081
      Therefore, they are better grounded in devising innovative and impactful research ideas.
082   Motivated by the human practices in conducting research, we introduce a novel Chain-of-Ideas (CoI)
083   agent framework to address the previously identified logical inconsistencies in the ideation processes
084   of LLMs. As shown in the bottom part of Figure 1, CoI agent aims to provide a clear landscape of
085   current research topics by systematically selecting and organizing the relevant papers and their ideas
086   in a chain structure. CoI agent offers several distinctive advantages: Firstly, it minimizes the risk
087
      of interference from less relevant literature via carefully selecting papers (i.e. from CoT (Wei et al.,
      2022) to GoT). Second, LLMs are demonstrated with human practice to craft a novel idea. For
088
      example, SC (Wang et al., 2022) emerges as a novel idea derived from CoT. This can be viewed
089
      as a form of few-shot prompting strategy, which has been proven to enhance the overall LLM’s
090   generation capability (Brown et al., 2020). Third, CoI exemplifies a global progression in research
091   development. As a result, LLMs can gain a deep understanding of the motivations behind these
092   developmental trends, facilitating the identification of promising future research directions.
093
      Specifically, CoI agent first retrieves an anchor paper of the given research topic. Instead of indis-
094
      criminately aggregating all papers within the citation network of the anchor, as done in (Baek et al.,
095
      2024), we construct the CoI by selecting relevant and important literature from both the anchor’s
096   references and its subsequent works, thereby extending the chain backward and forward from the
097   anchor. We then prompt the constructed CoI to an LLM for idea generation and experiment design.
098   During idea generation, we require the LLM to predict possible future trends. This prognostic result
099   facilitates the gradual consolidation of the idea, beginning with the motivation for the proposed idea,
100   progressing through an assessment of its potential impact, and culminating in the realization. How-
101   ever, as the evolution of scientific discovery can emerge from multiple perspectives, a single CoI
102   may be insufficient to capture the most promising direction. Additionally, there is no guarantee that
103   the generated ideas will be novel. To address these issues, we construct multiple CoI branches for
104
      different perspectives of a research topic. Additionally, a novelty-checker agent iteratively evaluates
      the draft idea against existing literature and refines it if substantial similarity is identified.
105
106   We compare our CoI agent against existing baselines on idea generation in the artificial intelligence
107   (AI) field. To do this, we develop an arena-style evaluation framework called Idea Arena where
      participant methods compete in pairs, which demonstrates high agreement with human evaluation.

145   2.1      F RAMEWORK : C HAIN - OF -I DEAS AGENT
146
      In this section, we detail our CoI agent framework, as illustrated in Figure 2, which consists of
147
      three stages: (1) CoI Construction, (2) Idea Generation, and (3) Experiment Design. First, given a
148   research topic, the CoI agent constructs multiple CoIs from existing literature, reflecting different
149   trends within the domain. Then, for each CoI, the LLM predicts future research directions, and
150   crafts ideas through step-by-step consolidation and iterative novelty checks. The best idea is then
151   selected. Lastly, the LLM generates and refines an experiment design to implement the final idea.
152
153   2.2      C O I C ONSTRUCTION
154
155   Generating novel research ideas requires a profound comprehension of the respective research do-
156   main, coupled with a rigorous reasoning process. Previous endeavors (Lu et al., 2024; Baek et al.,
157   2024) have sought to augment LLMs with relevant papers to facilitate the ideation process. However,
158
      these methods simply mix these papers into the prompt without effective organization. This scenario
      is akin to dropping an LLM at a chaotic intersection with no map in sight, leaving it uncertain about
159
      which path to take. To address this issue, we propose a Chain-of-Ideas agent framework.
160
161   As shown in Figure 2, a CoI, represented as {I−M → · · · → I0 → · · · → IN }, is a sequence consist-
      ing of M + N + 1 ideas extracted from M + N + 1 research papers respectively, where they together


                                                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      show the evolution progress within a given research field. Specifically, given an initial research
163   topic, we prompt the LLM to generate multiple queries, [q 1 , . . . , q K ], that reflect K different per-
164   spectives of this topic. The prompt is given in Table 7 of Appendix. Unless otherwise specified, all
165   prompts of our framework are presented in the Appendix tables. The K queries are used to construct
166   K branches of CoI. This reduces the reliance on a single CoI that may be insufficient to capture the
167   most significant development and direction. For each query q k , we use it to retrieve a top-ranked
168   paper, which we call anchor paper Pk0 . In Figure 2, ToT (Yao et al., 2024) is an illustrative example
169   of an anchor paper. An anchor paper serves as the foundation for constructing a CoI. Specifically,
170   a CoI is constructed by extending from the corresponding anchor paper to related papers in both
171   directions: forward, tracing the progression of ideas, and backward, tracing their origins.
172   In the forward direction, starting from Pk0 , we identify subsequent papers that directly cite it by
173   leveraging the Semantic Scholar API2 . We use OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-large3 to rank
174   these papers based on their cosine similarities to the concatenation of the initial research topic and
175   the abstract of the anchor paper. Subsequently, we select the highest-ranked paper as Pk1 to extend
176   the CoI in the forward direction (e.g. GoT in Figure 2). This process is repeated iteratively from Pki
177   to Pki+1 , until either the length of the CoI reaches a preset value or the LLM finds that there is no
178   valuable follow-up work (Table 8).
179
      In the backward direction, starting from the anchor paper Pk0 , we instruct an LLM to thoroughly
180
      review the full paper and to identify candidate references based on the following criteria: 1) refer-
181
      ences that Pk0 directly built upon, 2) references that serve as baselines in Pk0 , and 3) references that
182
      tackle the same topic as Pk0 . With those candidate references, we ask the LLM to determine the most
183
      relevant one to the anchor paper (Tables 9 and 10), denoted as Pk−1 (e.g. SC in Figure 2), to extend
184
185
      the CoI backward. This backward extension is also carried out iteratively from Pk−i to Pk−(i+1) to
186
      identify preceding papers (e.g. tracing backward from SC to CoT in Figure 2). It terminates when
      the length of CoI reaches a preset value or we encounter a milestone paper (defined as one with over
187
      1,000 citations), indicating that the idea from the milestone paper could serve as a strong starting
188
      point for the CoI. Additionally, we instruct the LLM to terminate the search if no reference relevant
189   to the original research topic is found (Table 8).
190
191   After we collect K paper chains, denoted as {Pk−M k → · · · → Pk0 → · · · → PkN k }Kk=1 , we ask the
192   LLM to extract ideas from these papers and inherit the progressive relation of the paper chains to
193   form our CoIs {Ik−M k → · · · → Ik0 → · · · → IkN k }K
                                                           k=1 (Tables 9 and 10). Then for each CoI, we
194   ask the LLM to summarize the existing research trends by analyzing the evolution between any two
195
      adjacent ideas (Table 11). For example, the upper part of Figure 2 shows the evolution process from
      CoT to GoT step-by-step. Additionally, we extract experiment designs and the definition of key
196
      entities from these papers (Tables 9 and 10). The above information including CoIs and the derived
197
      knowledge will be used in the following idea generation and experiment design stages.

200   2.3     I DEA G ENERATION
201
202
      In this section, we use the above-constructed CoIs and their developing trends to guide the generation
      of a novel idea. For each generated CoI, the first step is to predict possible future trends. As shown
203
      in the lower-left section of Figure 2, we prompt the LLM with the CoI, the developing trends of
204
      existing works, and the key entities extracted from existing literature, as described in Sec. 2.2
205   (Tables 12 and 13). These entities comprise relevant datasets and potential baseline models, which
206   are important to clarify the concepts mentioned in the existing literature. After obtaining the future
207   trend, we continue to prompt the LLM to articulate its motivation, novelty, and methodology, finally
208   consolidate the idea (Tables 14 and 15). Through this step-by-step manner, COI can produce a more
209   detailed idea. Following the previous practice (Wang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), we also use a
210   novelty-check agent to evaluate candidate ideas. It retrieves relevant papers and prompts another
211   LLM to assess the similarity between the generated idea and the retrieved papers (Table 16). Based
212   on this assessment, our framework determines if another round of generation is necessary. Finally,
213
      we pairwisely compare the generated ideas from all CoI branches and select the one with the highest

      2.4      E XPERIMENT D ESIGN
220
221   While our primary goal is to generate novel ideas, it is also useful to develop experimental plans that
222   help users implement these ideas. Thus, we extended the CoI agent to include experiment design.
223   As shown in the lower-right of Figure 2, we prompt the LLM with experiments from existing works
224   obtained from Sec. 2.2 as few-shot examples, along with the proposed idea and key entities, to guide
225   the LLM in designing experiments for our ideas (Table 17).
226   We also employ a review agent to assess the candidate experiment designs. Its main role is to
227   evaluate the clarity and comprehensiveness of the protocol, ensuring all key elements—such as
228   datasets and models—are clearly specified. Additionally, it checks if the design provides enough
229   detail for practical implementation (Table 18). The review agent provides critical feedback on these
230   aspects, subsequently utilizing this information to conduct further searches for relevant literature
231   (Table 19) to help the LLM refine and enhance its previous experiment design (Table 20). Through
232   this iterative process of review and refinement, we arrive at a final experiment design.

      3.2      DATA
246
      To evaluate our CoI agent’s ability to generate novel ideas, we collect recent research topics from
247   Hugging Face’s Daily Papers4 , known for its timely updates and the high quality of the featured
248   papers. We select papers submitted between August 1 and September 15, 2024, ensuring that the
249   topics are sufficiently new and the time frame is after the data cutoff of the LLM. We ask 10 skilled
250   researchers (All have publications in top-tier conferences and major in AI-related topics, such as
251   computer vision, embodied intelligence, and natural language processing) to identify papers that
252   capture their interests. Subsequently, we prompt GPT-4o to extract research topics, proposed ideas,
253   and their corresponding experiment designs from these selected papers (Tables 21, Table 22 and
254   23). The extracted topics will then be returned to the researchers for validation, ensuring that the
255
      extracted topics are valid and reasonable within their research domains. The extracted ideas and
      experiment designs will be utilized as our Real Paper baseline, as described in Section 3.3. Due
256
      to the substantial costs associated with generating and evaluating ideas and experiment designs, we
257
      adhere to the assessment scale of Lu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023) to collect 50 research topics in
258   total for evaluation.

      3.4       E VALUATION : I DEA A RENA
299
300   Model-based Evaluation. The open-ended nature of idea generation poses challenges for automatic
301   evaluation. Prior work primarily uses LLM-based Likert scale system to score ideas (Baek et al.,
302   2024; Lu et al., 2024). However, Si et al. (2024) show this method poorly aligns with human
303   preferences. Instead, they show LLMs perform better in ranking ideas. To obtain reliable scores for
304   evaluation, we propose Idea Arena, a pairwise evaluation system using a Round-Robin tournament
305   to compute ELO scores for each idea-generation method. For a given topic, we require the LLM
306
      judge to rank the ideas generated by any pair of methods (Table 24). We evaluate each pair twice
      with order reversed to reduce the position bias. To comprehensively evaluate an idea from multiple
307
      perspectives, we incorporate criteria from ICML 2020 review guidelines 5 , and those in Si et al.
308
      (2024), which consist of Novelty, Significance, Clarity, Feasibility, and Expected Effectiveness.
309   Finally, the resultant win-loss-tie records are utilized to calculate the ELO scores for each method,
310   following the practices outlined in Zheng et al. (2024); Zhao et al. (2024). We also evaluate the
311   experiment design in the same pairwise way, focusing on Feasibility, Technical Quality, and Clarity.
312   Refer to Definitions for all metrics in Tables 5 and 6 of the Appendix.
313
      Human Evaluation. The 10 AI researchers who review the extracted topics are asked to rank two
314
      ideas and experiment designs based on the same pairwise criteria as the model-based evaluation. To
315   ensure fairness, we anonymize the source of the ideas by concealing the method identity.

495   Scientific Research Idea Generation. Idea generation is a fundamental step in scientific research.
496   Due to its innovative nature, idea generation has been primarily a human-driven activity. However,
497   recent studies indicate that LLMs can generate plausibly novel and feasible ideas as those of human
498   researchers (Si et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2024). To investigate the potential of LLMs in idea gen-
499   eration, prior works begin with the task of scientific hypothesis discovery (Yang et al., 2024b; Qi
500   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), which aims to elucidate relationships between two scientific vari-
501
      ables. Despite its utility, scientific hypothesis discovery may not fully capture the complexity and
      multifaceted nature of real-world problems. To address this limitation, projects like GPT-Researcher
502
      (Assafelovic, 2023) and ResearchAgent (Baek et al., 2024) have adopted a more open-ended idea
503
      generation scenario including the underlying methodologies and experimental designs. They lever-
504   age agent-based systems to enhance the quality of idea generation. Beyond ideation, numerous
505   studies also explore the use of LLMs for executing experiments (Huang et al., 2024; Tian et al.,
506   2024) or combining both idea generation and experimental execution (Li et al., 2024; Lu et al.,
507   2024). However, these approaches often make minor modifications to existing ideas for drafting
508   their ideas, which often lack depth and creativity.
509
      Align LLMs with Human Cognitive Patterns. As LLMs are trained with vast amounts of human
510   data (Brown et al., 2020), this may enable them to internalize human cognitive patterns. Firstly,
511   CoT (Wei et al., 2022) indicates that LLMs can enhance their reasoning abilities when provided
512   with step-by-step guidance. Further research supports this notion by showing that simply prompting
513   LLMs to engage in step-by-step reasoning can trigger better reasoning capability (Kojima et al.,
514   2022). Additionally, Fu et al. (2022) reveals that in-depth reasoning of LLMs can be achieved with
515   more elaborate prompts. As a result, a prompting strategy that closely emulates human cognition is
516   likely to elicit more insightful responses from these models. Motivated by this, we propose CoI to
517   better mimic the progressive cognitive patterns of humans when generating new research ideas.

521
522   The misuse of AI-generated research ideas could present a risk to our society. We believe this is a
523   fundamental limitation inherent in all generative models, not just an issue specific to our CoI. Con-
524
      sequently, we advocate for the continuation of safety research specifically focused on the academic
      domain. As for this paper, our primary goal is to enhance effectiveness, while safety issues are re-
525
      ally out of this scope. Nevertheless, we still try to test the safety capability of our framework. The
526
      analysis, detailed in Appendix A.2, shows that CoI does not compromise the safety alignment of
527   existing LLMs, thereby making it a safe and reliable framework for idea generation.

530   7   C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce Chain of Ideas (CoI) agent, a framework designed to enhance the capa-
533
      bility of LLMs in generating research ideas. The CoI agent offers a promising and concise solution
534   by organizing ideas into a chain structure, effectively mirroring the progressive development within
535   a given research domain. It facilitates LLMs to digest the current advancements in research, thereby
536   enhancing their ideation capabilities.p To comprehensively evaluate the capability of automated idea
537   generation methods, we also propose Idea Arena, an evaluation system that requires the participant
538   methods to compete in pairs about their generated ideas for the research topics, which demonstrates
539   high agreement with human evaluation. Experimental results indicate that the CoI agent consistently
      outperforms other methods and is capable of generating ideas comparable to human creativity.
