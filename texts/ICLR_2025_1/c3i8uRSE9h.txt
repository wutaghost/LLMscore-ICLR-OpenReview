009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Diffusion models have dominated the field of large, generative image models, with
012            the prime examples of Stable Diffusion and DALL-E 3 being widely adopted.
013            These models have been trained to perform text-conditioned generation on vast
014            numbers of image-caption pairs and as a byproduct, have acquired general knowl-
015            edge about natural image statistics. However, when confronted with the task of
016            constrained sampling, e.g. generating the right half of an image conditioned on
017            the known left half, applying these models is a delicate and slow process, with
018            previously proposed algorithms relying on expensive iterative operations that are
019            usually orders of magnitude slower than text-based inference. This is counter-
020
               intuitive, as image-conditioned generation should rely less on the difficult-to-learn
               semantic knowledge that links captions and imagery, and should instead be achiev-
021
               able by lower-level correlations among image pixels. In practice, inverse models
022
               are trained or tuned separately for each inverse problem, e.g. by providing parts
023            of images during training as an additional condition, to allow their application
024            in realistic settings. However, we argue that this is not necessary and propose
025            an algorithm for fast-constrained sampling in large pre-trained diffusion models
026            (Stable Diffusion) that requires no expensive backpropagation operations through
027            the model and produces results comparable even to the state-of-the-art tuned mod-
028            els. Our method is based on a novel optimization perspective to sampling under
029            constraints and employs a numerical approximation to the expensive gradients,
030            previously computed using backpropagation, incurring significant speed-ups.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The recent state-of-the-art in image generation has been dominated by diffusion-based, text-to-image
036   models (Rombach et al., 2022), which excel in translating input text prompts into images. However,
037   by training on millions of text-image pairs, these models have acquired some general knowledge
038   about the natural image space. Thus, besides from text-to-image generation, these models should
039   also be useful in other image-based inference tasks, such as inpainting, super-resolution etc., given
040   that their knowledge about image statistics can be induced.
041
      Such efforts on utilizing text-to-image models on image-based inference tasks have focused on de-
042   vising ways to adapt pre-trained, text-guided models to the target task. The simplest approach is to
043   fine-tune the text-based model on the new image-based task (Xie et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024),
044   with the downside of fine-tuning being unnecessarily expensive in smaller-scale scenarios. Alterna-
045   tively, a different set of algorithms has been developed that utilize only the pre-trained text-to-image
046   diffusion model, and modify the sampling process to infer the missing information (Chung et al.,
047   2023; Rout et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2024). These sampling-based approaches however come with
048   a significant increase in the computations needed.
049   As a practical example, in Figure 1 we select an image from the web and pose the task of inferring
050   the right half given the pixels in the left half. Based on the Stable Diffusion 1.5 model, we first
051   utilize a fine-tuned variant, trained to be conditioned on images and inpainting masks. Although the
052   inference is fast (4s) and relatively accurate with textures being correctly replicated on the right side
053   and no visible seams, this model required 440k steps of additional inpainting training, on top of the
      initial SD 1.5 weights.

079
      there is a fast numerical approximation that speeds up this optimization significantly. Our work
080   aims to make inference under constraints practical both by improving the synthesized images and
081   reducing the inference times to a reasonable range.

087   Denoising diffusion models were proposed for image generation in Ho et al. (2020), where their
088   ability to generate diverse and high-quality samples was first showcased in the image space. The
089   original formulation views the training and inference process as a hierarchical latent variable model
090   xT → xT −1 → · · · → x1 → x0 , where the final latent is distributed normally xT ∼ N (0, I) and
091   p(x0 ) represents the data distribution. After choosingqa noise schedule at that defines the forward
092   transitions xt → xt+1 , usually Gaussian centered at aat+1    t
                                                                      xt and with variance (1 − aat+1
                                                                                                   t

093

094
095   Further iterations of denoising diffusion introduced class conditioning (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021)
096   and classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022), which culminated in the development of Latent
097
      Diffusion Models (Rombach et al., 2022). Latent diffusion proposed the large-scale training of text-
      to-image diffusion models in the latent space of an image autoencoder, x0 = D(E(x0 )). We note
098
      that although we use latent diffusion models for all our experiments, we do not require additional
099
      steps during inference to ensure consistency between the pixel and latent spaces, as is performed in
100   previous works (Rout et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2024).

      4.1    I NVERSE P ROBLEMS
286
287   We validate our approach on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) by performing inpainting and super-
288   resolution. Following Chung et al. (2024), we randomly choose 1000 images from the 10k test
289   images of the ctest10k split. For the diffusion model, we use SD v1.5, which is pre-trained on
290   the LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022) text-image dataset. All experiments were done on a single
291
      NVIDIA RTX A5000 24GB GPU.
292   We use Stable Diffusion 1.5, a latent diffusion model, which spatially compresses the images by a
293   factor of 8. To demonstrate the versatility of our approach, we approach linear inverse problems from
294   two different angles. For inpainting, instead of decoding the latent into an image and comparing
295   it to the measurement in pixel space, we opt to dilate the pixel-level masks and directly perform
296   inpainting in the latent space. That means we ’discard’ some of the information in the given image
297
      by only keeping 8 × 8 patches that do not overlap with the masked pixels. Consequently, we apply
      the masking and un-masking operators, A and AT respectively, in the latent space and inpaint latent
298
      values instead of pixels. Even with fewer pixels, our method performs better than existing methods
299
      in inferring the missing information. We utilize the 10 − 20% free-form masking from Saharia et al.
300   (2022) as the method of masking pixels.
301
302   For superresolution, we cannot work in the latent space as the operation of downsampling pixels
303
      does not correspond to downsampling latents. Therefore, in order to compute the error direction
      e we backpropagate the pixel-level constraint cost (AD(xt ) − y)T (AD(xt ) − y) through the
304
      decoder network and utilize the resulting gradient w.r.t. the latent as the error direction. We note
305
      that by setting e = −∂C(D(xt )/∂xt we only require backpropagation through the decoder model,
306   which is significantly less expensive than backpropagating through the denoiser network, as is done
307   in previous works. For both degradations, we also include additive white Gaussian noise with σy =
308   0.05.
309
      In both experiments, we also find it useful to perform warm restarts of our algorithm. After running
310
      Algorithm 1 from t = 1000 to t = 0, we reset the inferred x0 by adding the appropriate noise to
311
      When superresolving, we add an additional perturbation to the gradient to avoid local minima. We
312   find that our approach quickly converged to blurry images, which satisfy completely the superres-
313   olution constraint, but do not contain the desired details and texture. A simple perturbation of the
314   gradient with random noise around the current x̂0 seems to be adequate in solving this issue. For
315   inpainting, this is not a problem, as the textures and overall fidelity of the inferred image are dictated
316   by the given regions.
317
      In Figure 4 we showcase the qualitative performance of our algorithm compared to similar recent
318   works. In the case of inpainting our results show greater coherence with the rest of the image.
319   For superresolution, our method seems to introduce more new textures in the generated image,
320   which may not always align perfectly with the shown content (e.g. artifacts). In Table 1 we present
321   quantitative results by measuring PSNR, LPIPS and FID scores. Inpainting shows a clear advantage
322   for our approach, whereas superresolution struggles to improve significantly. It is important to
323   consider that for our results, the inference time is around 2 minutes, including the warm restarts,
      whereas competing methods always require more than 4 times longer for a single image.

      4.2   I MAGE LAYER INFERENCE
354
355
356   Our approach allows for fast inference of missing regions in a given image. With that in mind, we
357   go beyond inpainting and propose a new inference problem for pre-trained diffusion models, layer
358   inference. Given an input image x0 , we aim to generate two new images x10 , x20 and a pixel-wise
359   mask m that will satisfy x0 = mx10 + (1 − m)x20 . We want the model to generate a possible
360   ’decomposition’ of the input image into two layers, e.g. foreground and background along with the
361   blending mask.
362   We propose a simple algorithm to perform this task. The assumption is that pixels originating
363   from the same layer should have stronger correlations than pixels from different layers. To probe
364   this correlation given a soft mask m, we generate multiple possible images by sampling a binary
365   mask from the (0,1) values of m and performing inpainting. After running multiple iterations of
366   inpainting, we can then compute the likelihood of each pixel in the original image to belong in one
367   of the layers. We choose to model a layer image as Gaussian with per-pixel means and variances
368
      dictated by the generated samples in that layer.
369   Instead of performing the full sampling process, we find that we can substitute each layer image with
370   the predicted x̂0 (xt ). To induce more variety to the x0 estimates, we perturb xt and help generate
371   multiple inpainting variations without running the full inference process. The mask is randomly
372   initialized with values in (0,1) and we choose to sample K = 5 images per layer.
373   In Figure 5 we provide qualitative results by running the layer inference algorithm on images from
374   the web. To guide each layer we also condition the denoiser x̂0 (xt ) on layer-specific text prompts
375   that describe the image content we want to select. The results show that we can control the separation
376   between the layers and get two images that describe different characteristics of the source image.
377   The key is the speed of our method in inferring the missing pixels; this inference would have been
      computationally infeasible to perform with previous sampling-based approaches.

409   5   C ONCLUSION
410
411   In this work, we presented a new algorithm for fast inference in pre-trained diffusion models under
412   constraints. Our novel approach exploits a different gradient update that has different qualitative
413   results and requires no expensive backpropagation operations through the model. Our method pro-
414   duces results comparable to the state-of-the-art with significantly less inference time. We also intro-
415   duce a new layer inference task, which is enabled by our fast and high-quality constrained sampling
416
      algorithm. We believe that a fast and accurate method to sample from large pre-trained generative
      image models, under any condition, can have the potential to enable countless downstream applica-
417
      tions that rely on utilizing a strong image prior.
