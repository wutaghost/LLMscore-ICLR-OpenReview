009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            QR codes have become ubiquitous in daily life, enabling rapid information ex-
012            change. With the increasing adoption of smart wearable devices, there is a need
013            for efficient, and friction-less QR code reading capabilities from Egocentric point-
014            of-views. However, adapting existing phone-based QR code readers to egocentric
015            images poses significant challenges. Code reading from egocentric images bring
016
               unique challenges such as wide field-of-view, code distortion and lack of visual
               feedback as compared to phones where users can adjust the position and fram-
017
               ing. Furthermore, wearable devices impose constraints on resources like com-
018
               pute, power and memory. To address these challenges, we present EgoQR, a
019            novel system for reading QR codes from egocentric images, and is well suited
020            for deployment on wearable devices. Our approach consists of two primary com-
021            ponents: detection and decoding, designed to operate on high-resolution images
022            on the device with minimal power consumption and added latency. The detection
023            component efficiently locates potential QR codes within the image, while our en-
024            hanced decoding component extracts and interprets the encoded information. We
025            incorporate innovative techniques to handle the specific challenges of egocentric
026            imagery, such as varying perspectives, wider field of view, and motion blur. We
027
               evaluate our approach on a dataset of egocentric images, demonstrating 34% im-
               provement in reading the code compared to an existing state of the art QR code
028
               readers.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Quick Response (QR) codes have revolutionized information exchange, enabling rapid data transfer
034   across various applications. By encoding diverse data types such as URLs, plain text, and contact
035   information, QR codes have achieved widespread adoption, becoming ubiquitous in daily life from
036   marketing to payment systems Shin et al. (2012); Tiwari (2016). QR code decoding on smartphones
037   relies on user interaction, allowing for adjustments in focus, positioning, and orientation until a
038   successful scan is achieved. In fact, in these devices users receive real-time feedback, allowing them
039
      to optimize the scanning conditions actively. However, the growing adoption of smart wearable
      devices, requires building a frictionless QR code reading system with minimal feedback from the
040
      user.
041
042   Integrating QR code functionality into wearable devices poses significant challenges. Unlike smart-
043   phones, wearable devices capture images in a single shot, without user adjustment or feedback. User
044   expectations also differ significantly between smartphones and wearable devices. Smartphone users
045
      are accustomed to actively framing the QR code within the camera view, whereas with wearables,
      users expect detection and decoding of QR codes within their field of vision. This expectation of
046
      ”seeing what I see” presents unique challenges for egocentric captured images. Users may view
047
      QR codes from various angles and distances, resulting in distorted perspectives unlike the typically
048   straight-on view achieved with smartphones. Natural head movements can introduce motion blur,
049   which the algorithm must compensate for. The QR code may not always be in the primary focus area
050   of the image, requiring more robust detection methods. Varying lighting conditions can affect the
051   binarization process in the decoding process, causing uneven illumination. Additionally, wearable
052   devices must handle a wide range of lighting conditions and backgrounds as users move through dif-
053   ferent environments, introducing environmental variability. This shift in user expectations presents
      both opportunities and challenges. While it promises a more intuitive and frictionless interaction, it


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      also demands more robust and adaptable QR code reading algorithms capable of handling subopti-
055   mal imaging conditions (Figure 8). Additionally, running the QR code reader on edge devices poses
056   unique challenges due to limited resources, including processing power, memory, and battery life,
057   requiring efficient algorithms to balance performance and resource utilization.
058
      Existing QR code reading systems struggle to address these challenges, where not only they are not
059
      optimized to run in limited resource settings but also they not meant to handle egocentric images,
060
      thus resulting in reduced code reading success; leaving efficient, real-time solutions remain elusive.
061   In light of these challenges and evolving user expectations, this paper presents a novel, lightweight
062   algorithm designed to address these specific challenges, aiming to enhance the QR code reading
063   experience in egocentric settings. Our approach focuses on efficient yet more robust detection and
064   decoding components that can operate on high-resolution images with minimal power consumption
065   and added latency. Specifically, we developed a light weight code detection model paired with a
066   enhanced decoding algorithm on top of ZXing Crossing”) library and could improve the decoding
067   success rate by 34% compared to state of the art code readers on a ego-centric dataset collected for
068   the purpose of this work.

070   2   P REVIOUS WORKS
071
072
      Early QR code detection methodologies relied on traditional computer vision techniques, which pri-
073   marily sought to identify distinct geometric features of QR codes. One prominent method proposed
074   a fast approach for detecting QR codes in arbitrarily acquired images, aiming to minimize computa-
075   tional demands while maintaining a high detection rate Belussi & Hirata (2011). This work laid the
076   foundation for subsequent research efforts by providing a solution to detect QR codes in real-world,
077   uncontrolled conditions. In a related effort, a component-based approach was introduced to further
078   enhance detection accuracy by breaking the QR code into recognizable parts—such as finder and
079   alignment patterns—thereby improving detection rates in environments with complex backgrounds
080   Belussi & Hirata (2013a). Despite their effectiveness under controlled conditions, these methods
081
      had difficulty generalizing to diverse environments due to their reliance on manually engineered
      features that struggled with variability in image capture conditions.
082
083   The introduction of machine learning approaches revolutionized QR code detection by enhancing
084   robustness and reducing dependency on handcrafted features. The use of convolutional neural
085   networks (CNNs) for automating feature extraction marked a significant step forward Chou et al.
086   (2015b). By allowing models to learn features directly from data, these techniques avoided the lim-
087
      itations of traditional methods, improving adaptability to distortions and variations in lighting or
      perspective. In another advancement, a local feature-based QR code detection approach was devel-
088
      oped to emphasize the extraction of unique patterns, helping to enhance detection in cluttered or
089
      challenging scenes Belussi & Hirata (2013b). Complementing these developments, a focus on QR
090   code detection in high-resolution images Szentandrási et al. (2012) provided important insights into
091   balancing detection speed with improved accuracy, demonstrating how streamlining the pipeline
092   could yield faster results without sacrificing performance.
093
      Further progress came through optimization strategies aimed at faster and more reliable detection.
094
      An algorithm designed for rapid detection reduced the computational burden while maintaining
095
      robustness, making it particularly useful for real-time applications Zhang et al. (2017). Enhance-
096   ments based on the parallel line features of QR codes were also introduced, showing how geometric
097   properties could be leveraged to strengthen detection accuracy across varying conditions Klimek &
098   Vámossy (2013). Another innovative approach used BING and AdaBoost-SVM techniques to boost
099   performance in detection Yuan et al. (2019), while a binary large object-based detection method
100   focused on effectively segmenting image components to enhance accuracy in uncontrolled settings
101   Lopez-Rincon et al. (2017). These strategies collectively contributed to making QR code detection
102   faster and more adaptable to different environments.
103   Challenges associated with low-resolution images have long been a significant limitation in QR code
104   recognition, as they often result in poor detection rates. To mitigate these issues, super-resolution
105   techniques have been applied to improve the quality of low-resolution QR code images, thus enhanc-
106   ing detection reliability. A deep learning-based super-resolution method was proposed to enhance
107   QR code clarity, enabling detection systems to perform effectively even when presented with low-
      quality inputs Shindo et al. (2022). Another similar effort utilized the inherent structural features


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of QR codes to reconstruct high-quality images from low-resolution captures, thus boosting the ef-
109   fectiveness of subsequent detection steps Kato et al. (2011). These approaches have demonstrated
110   the potential for pre-processing techniques to play an important role in enhancing recognition in
111   challenging situations, thereby broadening the practical applicability of QR code detection systems.
112
      The application of deep learning techniques to QR code detection continued to evolve, with com-
113
      prehensive evaluations demonstrating the superiority of these models over traditional methods. A
114
      study evaluating various deep learning architectures showed that properly trained models could sig-
115   nificantly outperform conventional approaches under a wide array of image conditions Blanger &
116   Hirata (2019). Additional advancements in the use of CNNs sought to address issues of distortion
117   and extreme angles, thereby extending the practical use of QR code detection systems to cases in-
118   volving perspective challenges Chou et al. (2015a). These improvements were pivotal for enabling
119   detection systems to function accurately regardless of camera angles or image orientations.
120
      The integration of sophisticated detection models further enhanced QR code detection systems.
121   Faster-RCNN, combined with Feature Pyramid Networks (FPNs), was used to detect QR codes in
122   complex environments Peng et al. (2020). FPNs allowed the model to extract and process features
123   across multiple scales, thus improving detection performance across a wide variety of QR code sizes
124   and environments with significant background clutter. This multi-scale capability was important for
125   differentiating QR codes from other visual elements. Additionally, precise localization of QR codes
126   using deep neural networks was explored, which helped address the challenge of distinguishing QR
127   codes from other patterns that might exist in natural scenes Grósz et al. (2014).
128   Several foundational libraries have supported QR code detection and processing. Libraries such as
129   ZXing Crossing”), ZBar ZBar, and Quirc Quirc”) have been pivotal in providing robust tools for
130   reading QR codes, each offering unique strengths. ZXing stands out for its versatility and wide
131   adoption, ZBar is notable for its lightweight design and ability to detect multiple codes within a sin-
132   gle image, while Quirc focuses on efficiency and high-performance scanning. OpenCV’s QRCod-
133   eDetector opencv has offered an accessible, integrated approach to QR code detection by including
134   functions for enhancement, localization, and decoding. Additionally, Dynamsoft’s SDK Reader pro-
135
      vides a commercial-grade solution for recognizing QR codes, focusing on optimized algorithms for
      increased reliability.

139
140
      Figure 1 depicts the overall architecture of our flow. The process commences with image downsizing
141
      for efficient QR code detection. This module identifies QR code bounding boxes within the image
142   and rescales them to their original size. Subsequently, the code reader extracts each QR code and
143   applies necessary image processings to the code patch, then attempting decoding until successful.
144   The decoded codes, potentially multiple, are then forwarded to the fulfillment module. Leveraging
145   user input signals, such as pointing gestures, this module identifies the most relevant QR code.
146   Finally, the selected code is transmitted to the application for display to the user.

      3.1   D ETECTION
149
150   To efficiently detect QR code patches, we employ a detection model that processes a 576 × 432
151   thumbnail image, optimized for memory and latency constraints. Our approach leverages the Faster
152   R-CNN framework Ren et al. (2015), with tailored anchor box distributions specifically designed
153   for QR code detection. The model is trained on a dataset of approximately 15,000 images, achieving
154   94% recall and 95% precision at a 0.5 IOU threshold.
155
      However, two primary challenges arise in QR code detection. Stylistic QR codes pose a significant
156   challenge due to their limited representation in our training data. While our model excels at de-
157   tecting traditional square-shaped QR codes with black patterns, it exhibits reduced robustness when
158   encountering stylistic variations. Figure 2.a illustrates examples of these cases. Small QR codes also
159   prove difficult to detect due to the egocentric nature of the images. Specifically, QR codes occupying
160   15 × 15 pixels or less in the thumbnail image are challenging to identify. Notably, the majority of
161   false positives arise from small patches. Figure 2.b demonstrates the scale of a 0.8-inch QR code
      within the captured image, highlighting the detection challenges.

199   3.2   D ECODING
200
201   While detection focuses on identifying QR code patterns, decoding relies on precise pixel-level
202
      information, making it more susceptible to image quality issues. To improve decoding success rates,
      we developed a multi-trial preprocessing algorithm that enhances the image quality of detected QR
203
      codes. After applying our high-recall detection model to the full image, we extract each detected
204
      QR code from the high-resolution grayscale image, adding a margin around the code. We then apply
205   the following image enhancement techniques iteratively:
206
207         – Color Inversion: We process both the original and inverted color versions of the code. The
208           inversion is performed as:
209                                             Iinv (x, y) = 255 − I(x, y)                           (1)
210           where I(x,y) is the intensity of the pixel at (x,y).
211         – Multi-scale Processing: We process the code at four different scale values, including up-
212           scaling and downscaling.
213
            – Unbalanced Contrast Handling: a) OTSU Binarization: We use Otsu’s method Otsu (1979)
214           to find the optimal threshold (t) that minimizes the intra-class variance:
215

217             σ12 and σ22 are the variances of these two classes. b) CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive
218             Histogram Equalization Pizer et al. (1987)): We apply CLAHE with two clip limit values

233   After each enhancement step, decoding is attempted. The process terminates once decoding is

      successful or all enhancement techniques have been applied. It’s important to note that while some of

235
      overall latency. All steps except the last one are based on fast computer vision algorithms. The super-
236
      resolution step, requiring approximately 20ms to run, is applied last due to its higher computational
237   cost.

239   3.2.1    S UPER R ESOLUTION
240
241   We utilize a custom implementation of the LRSRNGankhuyag et al. (2023) model, adapted for our
242   specific use case, to perform super-resolution. This was chosen for its favorable latency characteris-
243
      tics. The model was trained on approximately 700,000 pairs of low-resolution to high-resolution QR
      code patches, with the high-resolution patches primarily sourced from MetaClip datasetsXu et al.
244
      (2024). To generate low-resolution images, we used simulation techniques to mimic camera noise.
245
      The super-resolution step improved our decoding success rate by 2.6% as shown later. The super-
246   resolution model aims to learn a mapping function fSR that minimizes the difference between the
247   generated high-resolution image and the ground truth:
248

251   high-resolution ground truth, and θ are the model parameters. An example QR code passed through
252   this model is shwon in figure 3.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND P RACTICAL I MPLICATIONS
453
454
      In this paper, we presented a novel, lightweight architecture for reading QR codes in egocentric
      settings, specifically designed to run on wearable devices with minimal battery and latency impacts.
455
      Our approach addresses the unique challenges posed by wearable devices, including limited com-
456
      putational resources, single-shot image capture, and the complexities of egocentric imagery such
457   as potential motion blur and wider field of view. Our key contributions include the development
458   of an efficient QR code detection component that can quickly locate potential QR codes within
459   high-resolution egocentric images, and an enhanced decoding component that successfully extracts
460   and interprets encoded information from QR codes captured in challenging egocentric conditions.
461   When evaluated on a comprehensive dataset of egocentric, high-resolution images, our algorithm
462   demonstrates a 34% improvement in QR code reading compared to existing state-of-the-art read-
463   ers. These advancements pave the way for more seamless integration of QR code functionality in
464   wearable devices, enhancing user experience across various applications, from augmented reality to
465
      accessibility assistance.
466   The rising prominence of multi-modal AI models Bordes et al. (2024) is reshaping our interaction
467   with the world, making it increasingly seamless. In this context, the ability to efficiently read and
468   interpret QR codes becomes important. QR codes serve as a bridge between the physical and digital
469   worlds, providing quick access to additional information that can enrich AI-driven experiences.
470   Consider a scenario where a wearable camera user encounters a restaurant menu presented as a QR
471
      code, a trend that has gained popularity, especially in the wake of recent global health concerns. Our
      enhanced QR code reading capability could allow the AI model to quickly scan and interpret the
472
      menu, potentially offering personalized recommendations based on the user’s dietary preferences
473
      or restrictions, without the need for manual input or smartphone interaction. In scenarios where
474   minimizing physical contact is preferred, such as public transportation or retail environments, hands-
475   free QR code scanning offers a hygienic alternative to touchscreen interactions.
476
477
      While our work represents a significant step forward in QR code reading for wearable devices, sev-
      eral avenues for future research and development remain. Leveraging multiple frames to improve
478
      QR code reading ability could enhance performance, especially in challenging lighting or motion
479
      conditions. This approach could involve techniques such as super-resolution or temporal noise re-
480   duction. Addressing the challenges posed by small QR codes remains important, with future work
481   focusing on improving both detection and decoding of these codes, particularly when pixel contrast
482   diminishes due to size constraints. Developing better detection models these problematic codes and
