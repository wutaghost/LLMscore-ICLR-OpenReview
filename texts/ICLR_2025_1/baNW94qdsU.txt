009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the past few years, multimodal foundation models, e.g., CLIP, learned from
012            a massive amount of paired multimodal data, emerged and exhibited impressive
013            cross-modal ability in many applications. Yet collecting high-quality paired data
014            is generally costly or even infeasible in certain cases, and the amount of paired
015            multimodal data is several orders fewer than that of unpaired unimodal data, i.e.,
016
               data without any correspondence. Our work focuses on alleviating the exces-
               sive demand for paired language-image data by leveraging the abundant unpaired
017
               data. We introduce a new approach for vision-language alignment, which we call
018
               Language-Image Self-Training (LIST). LIST consists of two key ingredients that
019            function in a synergistic loop: i) a captioner model trained alternatively with the
020            augmented paired data and the unpaired data with synthetic captions, both derived
021            from the data engine, and ii) a data engine that synthesizes a diverse spectrum of
022            captions for both paired and unpaired images with the captioner, integrating syn-
023            thetic captions with the web-scraped ones to enhance the quality of paired data
024            using off-the-shelf Large Language Models. We observe that the LIST method-
025            ology not only significantly improves the alignment between vision and language
026            representations across multiple major benchmarks—zero-shot image classifica-
027
               tion, image-text retrieval, and compositional evaluation—but also demonstrates
               strong generalization to audio-language representation alignment.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Over the last decade, we have witnessed remarkable leaps in the realm of multi-modal foundation
033   models (Radford et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Alayrac et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Radford
034   et al., 2023). These models, with their ability to process and integrate inputs from different modali-
035   ties, including but not limited to image, language, and audio, have unveiled a realm of unprecedented
036   possibilities. Notably, empowered by contrastive language-image pretraining (CLIP) (Radford et al.,
037   2021), diffusion denoising objective (Ho et al., 2020), and Transformers architecture (Vaswani et al.,
038   2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), multi-modal foundation models have demonstrated remarkable ca-
039
      pabilities. They can generate visually appealing images from textual descriptions (Ramesh et al.,
      2021; Rombach et al., 2022), respond to human instructions conditioned on input images (Li et al.,
040
      2022; Alayrac et al., 2022), and synthesize audio from unseen text description (Huang et al., 2023),
041
      showing astonishing scalability relative to the volume of data and compute.
042
043   However, a caveat accompanies these advances. Multi-modal foundation models depend extensively
044   on paired multi-modal data, as evidenced by the use of vast quantities of image-text pairs, reaching
045
      into the millions (Radford et al., 2021) or billions (Schuhmann et al., 2022), for training models like
      CLIP. This reliance poses significant challenges in data acquisition at scale. In practice, the avail-
046
      ability of paired multi-modal data is substantially overshadowed by the abundance of unpaired uni-
047
      modal data, making the former more challenging to collect and access. In the context of image-text
048   datasets, while web-scraping can somewhat ease the collection process, the resulting data often suf-
049   fers from noise and necessitates extensive efforts in data cleaning. Several studies have emerged to
050   refine the image-text dataset quality. Radenovic et al. (2023) proposed a filtering method to remove
051   noisy image-text pairs, Fan et al. (2023) explored rewriting captions using large language models,
052   and Nguyen et al. (2023); Lai et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2024) suggested enhancing raw captions with
053   those synthesized by a specialized captioner. However, these approaches still fundamentally rely on
      the assumption of having access to a substantial volume of data pairs beforehand.

073
      This study aims to mitigate the limitations inherent in relying on paired data, aligning different
074
      modalities by leveraging the untapped potential of unpaired data. We present Language-Image Self-
075
      Training (LIST), a new methodology for vision-language alignment. As depicted in Fig. 1, LIST
076   comprises two essential elements, the captioner model and the data engine, that work in a dynamic,
077   synergistic loop:
078
079   1. Captioner model. This model, instantiated by Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy
080      et al., 2020), is alternately trained using two types of data. First is a small-scale paired data,
081      augmented by the data engine with the help of LLMs, e.g.LLaMa (Touvron et al., 2023). The
082      second one is the unpaired data, each of which is uniquely paired with multiple synthetic cap-
083      tions synthesized by the data engine. Both data types are sourced from our sophisticated data
084      engine, providing diverse and comprehensive training supervision.
085   2. Data engine. This engine is tasked with generating a wide array of captions for both paired and
086      unpaired images, using the captioner model. In addition, for the paired data, the data engine inte-
087      grates synthetic captions with those scraped from the web. This integration process is supported
088      by advanced off-the-shelf LLMs, ensuring high-quality and contextually appropriate captions.
089
090   Together, these components enable LIST to effectively incorporate unpaired data to train vision-
091   language models, requiring only a small amount of data pairs to warm up the training and allowing
092
      for the explicit control of the captions synthesis. In addition, since LIST does not have any design
      specific to the vision input, it can be easily generalized to broader scenarios where paired data are
093
      scarce. For instance, much like the transition from CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to CLAP (Elizalde
094
      et al., 2023), LIST demonstrates strong generalization beyond the vision-language tasks to the audio-
095   language setting, where typically only a few thousand paired examples are available.
096
097   In summary, this paper makes several significant contributions to multi-modal alignment:
098
       • We introduce Language-Image Self-Training (LIST), a generic framework designed to harness
099
         the untapped potential of unpaired data for enhancing multi-modal representation alignment.
100
         LIST exhibits astonishing performance across multiple benchmarks, including zero-shot im-
101      age/audio classification, image-text retrieval, and compositional evaluation.
102
103    • Our approach demonstrates how the integration of a captioner model with a data engine, operat-
104
         ing in a synergistic loop within LIST, can lead to concurrent improvements in both the model’s
         performance and the quality of the data.
105
106    • The data engine in LIST is able to generate a diverse range of captions for both paired and
107      unpaired images. By leveraging LLMs, it effectively integrates the information of web-scraped
         and synthetic captions, thereby enhancing the quality of the paired data.

      Multimodal foundation models. Multimodal foundation models (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al.,
      2021; Yu et al., 2022; Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023) have exhibited remark-
112
      able capabilities in understanding and generating outputs across various modalities. CLIP (Radford
113
      et al., 2021) uniquely processes images and text as parallel data streams, enabling seamless con-
114   nections between visual and textual content. This approach is further advanced by models like
115   DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021) and StableDiffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), which extend the con-
116   cept to generate intricate images from textual descriptions, and (Guzhov et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
117   2023) that extend it to audio understanding. These models rely heavily on extensive paired datasets
118   for training and have demonstrated notable proficiency in diverse cross-modal tasks, including im-
119   age captioning, text-to-image synthesis, and audio-visual correlations. This surge in multimodal
120   learning highlights the importance of large-scale, diverse datasets. However, the challenge lies in
121   the labor-intensive process of curating high-quality image-text pairs, especially at scale. This work
122
      seeks to mitigate the reliance on paired data by capitalizing on the abundance of readily available
      unpaired data, utilizing self-training techniques to bridge this gap.
123
124   Improving multi-modal datasets. Multi-modal datasets, sourced primarily through internet crawl-
125   ing, are susceptible to noise and biases due to limited human moderation, often reflecting a nar-
126   row spectrum of human interests. Recent research has focused on improving the quality of vision-
127   language pretraining datasets by caption filtering (Radenovic et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023), caption
128
      rewriting (Fan et al., 2023), using synthetic caption (Li et al., 2022; 2023a; Santurkar et al., 2022),
      raw-synthetic caption mixing (Nguyen et al., 2023), and image synthesis (Tian et al., 2023) with
129
      text-to-image diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022). Closer to LIST, concurrent methods, e.g.,
130
      CapsFusion (Yu et al., 2024), VeCLIP (Lai et al., 2023), and ALIP (Yang et al., 2023a), also ex-
131   plored generating synthetic captions and then merging them with the web-scraped ones. However,
132   they are highly dependent on external VLMs trained on additional paired data, while LIST does not
133   have access to any implicit source of data pairs. These enhancements have shown that optimized
134   datasets can significantly boost model performance, making it possible for models trained on fewer,
135   but higher-quality, text-image pairs to match or even surpass those trained on larger datasets. LIST
136   stands apart in its fundamental aim to leverage unpaired data rather than enhancing paired data, an
137   approach that ideally complements the existing methodologies.
138   Self-training. Self-training (Scudder, 1965; Fralick, 1967; Blum & Mitchell, 1998), a semi-
139   supervised learning technique, has emerged as a significant approach for its effective use of unla-
140   beled data to boost model performance. This methodology, which entails generating pseudo-labels
141   for unlabeled data using the model itself, has demonstrated considerable improvements in perfor-
142   mance. It proves beneficial not only in areas with limited labeled data (Broder, 1997; Lee et al.,
143   2013) but also in larger-scale applications (He et al., 2019; Yalniz et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020).
144   Recent advancements in self-training have extended its application to various domains, such as lan-
145
      guage modeling (Huang et al., 2022a; Dong et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023), LLMs alignment (Li
      et al., 2023b), and object detection (Zoph et al., 2020), making it invaluable where labeled data ac-
146
      quisition is challenging or costly. LIST aligns with this paradigm but distinguishes itself through
147
      its task for vision-language alignment and the unique caption refinement process that incorporates
148   external knowledge from LLMs. In a related context, UCM (Yang et al., 2023b) similarly pro-
149   poses a self-training method for vision-language BERT, albeit with a different emphasis than vision-
150   language alignment. Nonetheless, UCM’s methodology is heavily dependent on an external object
151   detector, while LIST is a self-reliant framework, functioning independently even in the absence of
152   the LLMs–the sole external component LIST utilizes. Furthermore, leveraging LLMs enables LIST
153   to integrate open-set knowledge, which is a significant enhancement over the object detector used in
154   UCM that is constrained by a predefined set of close-set classes.

212   Model architecture. LIST is a generic framework and is agnostic to the specific architecture of the
213   vision-language model M. Throughout this paper, we opt for the Contrastive Captioner (CoCa) (Yu
214   et al., 2022) to instantiate M because of its simplicity and its capability to generate descriptive cap-
215   tions for vision-language learning (c.r. (Nguyen et al., 2023)). M compromises of three components
      (depicted in Fig. 4 of Appendix).

      In our empirical observations, initiating training of the model M from scratch often led to severe
225   overfitting, a phenomenon especially marked in scenarios with limited initial paired data, such as
226   only a few hundred thousand pairs in our case. However, the extensive availability of high-quality
227   unpaired unimodal data provides a beneficial alternative. This plentiful resource enables us to em-
228   ploy unimodal pretrained models as an effective countermeasure against overfitting. Our approach
229   aligns with the LiT methodology (Zhai et al., 2022), in which we opt for a pretrained, frozen vision
230   encoder, specifically DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023). This is complemented by a randomly initial-
231   ized, trainable attentional pooling layer (Yu et al., 2022) atop the pretrained encoder, forming our
232   standard configuration. In the event of the audio-language setting, we simply replace the vision
233
      encoder with an audio encoder pretrained by AudioMAE (Huang et al., 2022b) and keep the other
      audio-irrelevant designs unchanged. Furthermore, considering the language modeling aspect of our
234
      model, we initiate the language segments Et , Dt of M with a pretrained T5 encoder-decoder (Raffel
235
      et al., 2020) and use an averaging pooling when extracting the global language embedding with the
236   language encoder. Subsequently, we update the weights of these segments through gradient descent,
237   thereby fine-tuning the model for the tasks of captioning and contrastive learning.

286   Captions refinement. The data engine E augments the existing captions for the paired data with

292   The captioner model M has the capability to supplement the existing knowledge found in web-
293   scraped captions with novel insights. As extensively analyzed by Nguyen et al. (2023), synthetic
294   captions, as opposed to raw, web-scraped ones, exhibit distinct characteristics. Synthetic captions
295   typically demonstrate greater consistency and coherence with the visual content but lack diversity.
296   On the other hand, raw captions, while offering semantically richer context, are often susceptible to
297
      noise, a common byproduct of web-scraping processes. To harness the strengths of both types, we
      propose instructing LLMs to adeptly integrate the valuable elements from each, thus creating more
298
      comprehensive and enriched captions.
299
300   Our process begins with the collection of a few integration examples using ChatGPT (OpenAI,
301   2023), a more capable LLM. We commence by randomly selecting 20 captions from E(Dp ; G)
302   along with their corresponding synthetic captions. For each pair, we generate a merged caption
303
      using a prompt like“Combine a web-scraped caption with a synthesized one, giving precedence
      to the former.” These merged samples then serve as in-context examples. Coupled with the task
304
      description, “From a web-scraped caption || a synthesized caption, create a new caption after ->,
305
      favoring the web-scraped details and carefully adding from the synthesized one”, and the specific
306   query, they are used to prompt the relatively smaller LLM G, LLaMa 2 (Touvron et al., 2023) in our
307   case, to integrate the captions. An illustration of this prompting technique is provided in Fig. 2.

      4.5     C OMPOSITIONALITY EVALUATION
462
463   Recent studies (Thrush et al., 2022; Yuksekgonul et al., 2022; Hsieh et al., 2023) have raised ques-
464   tions about the compositional capabilities of vision-language models, uncovering a tendency for
465   these models to behave more like bag-of-words systems rather than fully understanding attributes,
466   relationships, and the order of objects. In this context, we explore the compositional understanding
467   of LIST using the SugarCrepe dataset (Hsieh et al., 2023), which is carefully crafted by altering
468   captions through objects/attributes replacements, swaps, and additions and relations replacements.
469   As shown in Tab. 5, LIST is compared with baseline models under various configurations, demon-
470   strating that our method significantly outperforms the baselines with an equivalent amount of paired
471
      data. Notably, LIST, even when trained with only 160K paired data, surpasses CoCa trained with a
      much (∼ 20×) larger dataset of 2.8 million pairs in 4 out of 7 entries, and matches it in one. This
472
      highlights the exceptional ability of LIST to effectively utilize unpaired data, thereby enhancing the
473
      compositional understanding in vision-language models.

             This paper introduces the Language-Image Self-Training (LIST) framework, a novel methodology
533
             for multi-modal alignment. LIST’s distinctive approach, which adeptly leverages the untapped po-
534          tential of unpaired data, mitigates the traditional reliance on large-scale, paired multi-modal datasets.
535          This framework, characterized by its synergistic cycle of model training and data synthesis, and fur-
536          ther enhanced by the integration of Large Language Models, significantly improves both data quality
537          and model performance. Comprehensive evaluations on a wide range of standard zero-shot classi-
538          fication, retrieval, and compositionality benchmarks not only demonstrate LIST’s effectiveness in
539          enhancing vision-language alignment but also highlight its capability to generalize to new modali-
             ties, e.g., audio-language.

      We perform all our experiments on the OpenCLIP codebase (Ilharco et al., 2021) with Py-
889
      Torch 2.0 (Paszke et al., 2019) and the automatic mixed precision training. The input image un-
890
      dergoes a weak augmentation, i.e., random flip, random crop, and is then resized to 224 × 224.
891   The input text is tokenized by a SentencePiece tokenizer (Kudo & Richardson, 2018), with a max-
892   imal length of 40 tokens to avoid computation burden. In our experiments, we use the base-size
893   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), i.e., ViT-Base/14 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) pretrained by DI-
894   NOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) for the vision encoder Ev and T5-Base (Raffel et al., 2020) for the
895   language encoder-decoder Et , Dt . The model is trained using the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter,
896   2017) optimizer, with a batch size of 2,048 for both images and texts, a weight decay set to 0.2, an
897   initial τ set to 1/0.07 (Ilharco et al., 2021), and the cosine annealing learning rate decay (Loshchilov
898   & Hutter, 2016). We keep the image encoder fixed, except for the attentional pooling layer. The hy-
899
      perparameters α and β in Eq. 8 are set to 1 and 2, respectively, following (Yu et al., 2022).
900   During the training process, we observed that the language decoder Dt necessitates a larger gradient
901   update step compared to the language encoder. In light of this, specifically for effective caption
902   synthesis, we opt to train the model for 128 epochs with a learning rate of 0.002. Additionally, we
903   adjust the training process by scaling down the gradient of the text encoder by a factor of 0.1. For
904   the evaluation phase, involving both synthetic and original data, the models are trained for a shorter
905
      duration of 32 epochs, employing a reduced learning rate of 0.0005, in line with the standard setting
      of OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021). To facilitate future research, code and synthesized data will be
906
      made publicly available concurrently with the official release of this paper.

       A notable limitation of the LIST framework lies in its reliance on a small, initial paired dataset,
1019
       potentially restricting the diversity of concepts learned and thereby limiting its ability to generalize
1020   diverse captions for unpaired data. This reliance might affect the model’s scalability and adaptability,
1021   particularly in new domains or complex tasks that differ significantly from the initial training data.
1022   However, it’s important to note that LIST does employ pretrained language encoder-decoders and
1023   off-the-shelf LLMs, which were trained on large-scale and diverse unimodal data. This integration
1024   enables the introduction of new and unseen concepts into the framework. This aspect of LIST
1025   potentially aids in mitigating some of the generalization limitations by infusing a wider range of
       knowledge and concepts beyond the initial dataset.
