009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning (FL) is a promising paradigm for training decentralized machine learning
012            models with privacy preservation. However, FL models are biased, leading to unfair model
013            outcomes towards subgroups with intersecting attributes. To address this, we propose
014            LipFed, a subgroup bias mitigation technique that leverages Lipschitz-based fairness
015            constraints to mitigate subgroup bias in FL. We evaluate LipFed’s efficacy in achieving
016
               subgroup fairness across clients while preserving model utility. Our experiments on
               benchmark datasets and real-world datasets demonstrate that LipFed effectively mitigates
017
               subgroup bias without significantly compromising group fairness or model performance.

021   1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Federated learning (FL) trains a global model using decentralized edge devices’ private data without collecting
024   their data centrally, promoting collaborative learning while preserving data privacy McMahan et al. (2017).
025   This makes FL suitable for privacy-sensitive applications such as medical diagnosis Feki et al. (2021); Ku
026   et al. (2022), gender prediction Krishnan et al. (2020), next-character prediction Sun et al. (2022), and activity
027   recognition Ek et al. (2020); Ouyang et al. (2021); Sozinov et al. (2018). Despite collaborative learning and
028
      privacy preservation benefits, FL inevitably learns undesired biases from statistically heterogeneous clients’
      data Abay et al. (2020). For instance, a crime detection FL algorithm may predict crime suspects based on skin
029
      color Courtland (2018), leading to the wrongful prediction of who goes to jail Polonski (2018). Unchecked
030
      biases in FL can erode user trust and negatively impact user experiences, affecting FL adoption and acceptance.
031   Recent FL research has focused on addressing bias, target-
032   ing individual bias Li et al. (2019a); Mohri et al. (2019);
033   Deng et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020); Hu et al. (2022); Hor-
034   vath et al. (2021) and group bias Yue et al. (2021); Cui et al.
035   (2021); Papadaki et al. (2022). Individual bias techniques

      Example 1. In a hypothetical scenario with race (black and white) and gender (male and female) as groups,
048
      consider a classifier predicting positive outcomes only for black men or white women. This classifier appears
049   fair across groups, predicting positively for both men and women 50% and both black and white groups 50%
050   of the time. However, examining subgroups like black and white women violates statistical parity fairness.
051   For instance, black women may be disproportionately labeled unfavorably, causing an unfair disadvantage
052   for this intersectional subgroup. This example demonstrates Simpson’s Paradox Pearl (2022) in fairness
053   evaluation, where seemingly fair techniques for groups become unfair for their fine-grained subgroups.
054
      In FL, subgroup bias arises because subgroups across clients fail to be independent and identically distributed
055   (IID). This deviation from IID-ness happens because subgroups across clients can have diverse feature
056   distributions due to factors such as geographical location, weather, and data collection devices Hsieh et al.
057   (2020); Lyu et al. (2020). For example, images of black and white female faces can vary dramatically
058   worldwide due to their skin color. Images of black and white female faces can also look very different given
059   the quality of their collection devices; low vs. high-quality collection devices, etc. Subgroup fairness is vital
060   because it reveals hidden biases within intersecting groups, as illustrated in the previous example. Ensuring
061   fairness across both intersectional subgroups and broad groups is necessary to avoid biases. Based on this
062   requirement, our research question is: how can FL models achieve subgroup fairness without compromising
063
      overall group fairness and model utility?
064   To address subgroup fairness in FL, we propose Lipschitz Fair Federated Learning (LipFed), a novel frame-
065   work applying the Lipschitz property Dwork et al. (2012) to decentralized FL. While Lipschitz constraints have
066   been used before (§B.1), our approach uniquely adapts them to ensure equitable model performance across
067   diverse subgroups on different devices. LipFed leverages a distance metric to measure subgroup similarity
068   and performance distributions across clients, overcoming the complexity of decentralized data(§4.1).
069   Contributions. In summary, we make the following contributions:
070     1. We identify the subgroup bias problem in FL (§3), focusing on bias at the subgroup level rather than
071        statistical bias across fixed demographic groups, addressing intersectional biases more comprehensively,
072        ensuring fairness, and reducing discrimination based on intersecting attributes.
073     2. We propose LipFed, leveraging the Lipschitz property to train subgroup fair models in FL, ensuring
074        minor changes in sensitive features lead to minor changes in model predictions, thus promoting fair
075        subgroup outcomes in FL models.
076
        3. We conduct theoretical analysis and establish precise bounds for subgroup and statistical fairness. By
           providing clear bounds (§4.2, §C.1), our work promotes a more transparent and accountable approach
077
           to addressing subgroup and statistical fairness challenges, fostering trust and reliability in FL.
078     4. We apply the LipFed across datasets (§5), reducing subgroup bias by up to 49% without degrading
079        model utility, though with some trade-offs in statistical fairness, clarified through our theoretical analysis
080        (§C.2). LipFed also improves other existing FL methods, by up to 25% in mitigating subgroup bias.

084   This section reviews methods in FL fairness, focusing on those related to subgroup fairness. While subgroup
085
      fairness is recognized in centralized learning, we address the unique challenges of FL and highlight the
      limitations of existing approaches. Due to space constraints, additional methods are discussed in Appendix D.

087   FL algorithms aimed at achieving a globally fair model are typically classified into three distinct categories,
088   including client-fairnessLi et al. (2019a); Mohri et al. (2019); Deng et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020); Hu et al.
089   (2022); Horvath et al. (2021), group-fairnessYue et al. (2021); Cui et al. (2021); Papadaki et al. (2022);
090   Selialia et al. (2023), and robustness techniques Lee et al. (2022); Karimireddy et al. (2020).
091   Client fairness. Ensuring fairness among clients in FL is essential to mitigate biases from non-IID data
092   distributions across devices. Techniques such as Federated Fair Averaging (FedFV) Wang et al. (2021)
093   adjust gradient directions and magnitudes to balance model average performance based on client contribu-

      tions Papadaki et al. (2022), while GIFair-FL Yue et al. (2023) dynamically modifies model updates with a
095
      fairness-aware aggregator to reduce average loss. FjORD Horvath et al. (2021) employs ordered dropout
096   to customize model sizes to client capacities, enhancing both fairness and accuracy. Additionally, Agnostic
097   Federated Learning (AFL) Mohri et al. (2019) tailors the global model to any client distribution mix, q-FFL Li
098   et al. (2019a) reweights losses to favor lower-performing devices, and Tilted Empirical Risk Minimization

101
      Group fairness. Recent advancements in FL emphasize addressing group fairness and biases against protected
102   groups. FairFed Ezzeldin et al. (2023) uses fairness-aware aggregation and local debiasing to enhance group
103   fairness under heterogeneous data conditions. FedMinMax Papadaki et al. (2022) employs alternating
104   optimization for minimax fairness across demographic groups, showing competitive performance. FCFL Cui
105   et al. (2021) combines algorithmic fairness and performance consistency, achieving Pareto optimality via
106   gradient-based methods and outperforming existing models in fairness and utility.
107
      Limitations of existing techniques. While valuable, current bias mitigation techniques in FL do not ensure
108   fairness for subgroups with overlapping characteristics. According to Simpson’s Paradox Pearl (2022),
109   seemingly group-fair techniques may still exhibit unfair outcomes towards fine-grained subgroups. The
110   following section explores these deviations and their implications through empirical studies.

152   Lipschitz fairness. Achieving individual-level fairness across similar entities x and x′ , where the similarity

157                                        D(hθ (x), hθ (x′ )) ≤ ϵ · d(x, x′ ).                                (3)

      measure, improving subgroup fairness without significantly sacrificing utility, as delineated by our theoretical

674   A.1    L IMITATIONS
675
676   Despite the effectiveness of the LipFed framework in mitigating subgroup bias, several limitations remain.
677
      Firstly, the reliance on the Lipschitz property to ensure subgroup fairness introduces constraints that may not
      universally apply across all types of models or datasets. There is a possibility that different models exhibit
678
      varying degrees of sensitivity to Lipschitz constraints, which could lead to inconsistent results when applied
679
      to non-IID data distributions. Second, the effectiveness of our method is influenced by the proper selection of
680   the hyperparameter ϵ that governs the Lipschitz constraint. Finding the optimal balance between subgroup
681   and group fairness may require extensive tuning and could differ based on the specific characteristics of the
682   datasets being used.
683
      Furthermore, while our approach shows improvements over existing methods, the trade-off between subgroup
684
      and group fairness necessitates careful calibration, which may not be straightforward. As subgroup variance
685
      decreases, the potential for bias to still emerge in certain groups remains a challenge. Lastly, the additional
686   computational overhead of enforcing Lipschitz constraints during the optimization process may not be feasible
687   for all practical applications, especially in resource-constrained environments.
688
689   A.2    F UTURE W ORK
690
691   Further empirical studies are needed to evaluate the performance of LipFed in diverse real-world scenarios,
692   including applications beyond image classification, such as text and audio data. Investigating the scalability
693   of our method in federated learning environments with a large number of clients and significantly diverse data
694   distributions would also be beneficial.
695   Moreover, it would be valuable to explore dynamic tuning mechanisms for the hyperparameter ϵ, potentially
696   through adaptive methods that can adjust to the evolving characteristics of the data during the training process.
697   This would facilitate achieving a more nuanced balance between subgroup and group fairness.

      B.2   F EDERATED L EARNING S UBGROUP FAIRNESS VS . C ENTRALIZED L EARNING S UBGROUP
715
            FAIRNESS
716
717
      Subgroup fairness in FL differs significantly from centralized learning. In centralized learning, all data is
718
      aggregated in one location, making it easier to apply fairness constraints uniformly across subgroups. However,
719   FL operates on decentralized data distributed across multiple clients, where non-IID data distributions pose
720   significant challenges. Achieving subgroup fairness in FL requires ensuring that each client contributes
721   equitably to the global model despite these variations. This decentralized setup demands sophisticated model
722   aggregation techniques to maintain subgroup fairness, as direct access to all client data is not possible.
723
724
725   B.3   S UBGROUP FAIRNESS VS . FAIRNESS ACROSS MULTIPLE SENSITIVE ATTRIBUTES
726
727   Fairness across multiple sensitive attributes, discussed in MultiFairTian et al. (2024), ensures that fairness
728   constraints are satisfied for each sensitive attribute individually (regardless of their number) without neces-
729   sarily focusing on their intersections. Consider a loan approval algorithm that aims to ensure fairness. The
730   algorithm might be designed to approve loans at the same rate for men and women (gender fairness) and at
731   the same rate for people of different ages (age fairness). Each attribute (gender, age) is treated separately to
732
      ensure fairness, but the algorithm might not specifically check if it’s fair to, for instance, young women or
      older men. Subgroup fairness (intersectional attributes focus) and multiple sensitive attributes (individual
733
      attribute focus) have some overlap, but they are not closely related. The distinction between these approaches
734
      is well-recognized in the literature Kearns et al. (2018). In centralized learning, there is a clear separation
735   between ensuring fairness for individual attributes and addressing fairness at the intersection of multiple
736   attributes (subgroup fairness). As noted in the paper Kearns et al. (2018), the need to ensure fairness across
737   intersectional subgroups is paramount to avoid fairness gerrymandering, where a model appears fair across
738   individual attributes but fails at the intersection of these attributes.
739
740
741   B.4   A DDITIONAL C AUSES FOR S UBGROUP FAIRNESS
742
743   Several factors contribute to subgroup unfairness, one of the most prominent being differences in group
744   sizes. This issue is commonly referred to as label distribution skew, where imbalances in the distribution of
745   labels across groups lead to biased outcomes. This challenge has been extensively studied in recent federated
746   learning fairness research Yue et al. (2023); Kearns et al. (2018).
747   In contrast, our work Lipfed deliberately focuses on a less explored yet equally important issue: the same
748   label, different features phenomenon. This refers to instances where subgroups that share the same label
749   exhibit significantly different feature distributions, leading to unfair treatment across those subgroups. By
750   addressing this underexamined factor, our work provides new insights into the complexities of achieving
751   subgroup fairness in FL.

      C.4.1    D IFFERENTIAL P RIVACY IN L IP F ED
975
      Differential privacy ensures that the inclusion or exclusion of a single data point (or client) does not sig-
976   nificantly affect the outcome of the computation, thereby protecting sensitive data. LipFed integrates DP
977   by adding Laplace noise to the local subgroup losses, ensuring privacy in the exchange of fairness-related
978   metrics between clients and the server.
979
980
      Definition of Differential Privacy. A randomized algorithm A satisfies ϵ-differential privacy if, for any
981

       D.1   C LIENT FAIRNESS IN F EDERATED L EARNING
1035
1036   Ensuring fairness among clients in FL is vital to counteract biases from non-IID data distributions across
1037   devices. Techniques like the Federated Fair Averaging (FedFV)Wang et al. (2021) adjust gradient directions
1038   and magnitudes to balance model average performance based on each client’s conflict level and contri-
1039   butionPapadaki et al. (2022). GIFair-FL Yue et al. (2021) dynamically adjusts model updates using a
1040   fairness-aware aggregator to reduce average loss across clients, while FjORD Horvath et al. (2021) employs
1041   ordered dropout to tailor model sizes to clients’ device capacities, enhancing fairness and accuracy.
1042   Additional approaches that build upon these fairness-enhancing techniques include Agnostic Federated
1043   Learning (AFL)Mohri et al. (2019), which optimizes the global model against any potential target distribution
1044   by accommodating unknown distribution mixes among clients. q-FFLLi et al. (2019a) addresses data
1045   heterogeneity by reweighting losses to prioritize devices with poorer performance, promoting uniform model
1046   accuracy across devices. Tilted empirical risk minimization (TERM) Li et al. (2020) adjusts the influence
1047   of outliers and balances class representation through a flexible tilt hyperparameter. These methods enhance
1048   average performance in FL systems operating in heterogeneous environments.

1050   D.2   G ROUP FAIRNESS IN F EDERATED L EARNING
1051
1052   Recent advancements in FL have highlighted the importance of addressing fairness concerns, particularly
1053
       group fairness, where biases against protected demographic groups are mitigated. Ezzeldin et al. (2023)
       introduced FairFed, a strategy that ensures fair model training by employing a fairness-aware aggregation
1054
       method. In FairFed, each client performs local debiasing using their own dataset to maintain decentralization
1055
       and privacy. Clients evaluate the global model’s fairness in each FL round, and aggregation weights are
1056   adjusted in collaboration with the server based on the mismatch between global and local fairness metrics.
1057   This method, supported by secure aggregation protocols, enhances group fairness under heterogeneous
1058   data conditions and allows for client-specific debiasing techniques, showing significant improvement over
1059   traditional fairness approaches in FL settings. FairFed’s empirical validation confirms its effectiveness in
1060   achieving group fairness, with plans for future enhancements to accommodate various application scenarios
1061   and integrate broader fairness concepts, such as collaborative and client-based fairness.
1062   In a parallel effort, Papadaki et al. (2022) explore group fairness in FL through their FedMinMax algorithm,
1063   which is crafted to establish minimax fairness across demographic groups, an approach that differs from tradi-
1064   tional methods aimed at equalizing performance across clients. FedMinMax strategically employs alternating
1065   optimization techniques—projected gradient ascent for optimizing weights and stochastic gradient descent
1066   for the model—tailoring the learning process to balance fairness among demographic groups effectively. This
1067   method has demonstrated competitive or superior performance against established benchmarks in various
1068   FL setups, showcasing its capability to uphold group fairness robustly. Simultaneously, Cui et al. (2021)
1069   propose the FCFL framework, which addresses both algorithmic fairness and performance consistency across
1070
       distributed data sources in FL. Derived from a constrained multi-objective optimization perspective, FCFL
       aims to maximize the utility of the least advantaged client while meeting fairness constraints, achieving Pareto
1071
       optimality via gradient-based methods. Theoretical and empirical validations of FCFL underscore its ability
1072
       to outperform existing models in ensuring fairness and consistent performance across clients, making it a
1073   viable solution for real-world applications where these attributes are crucial. These developments collectively
1074   signal a shift towards more ethical and equitable federated learning environments, emphasizing the necessity
1075   for continuous innovation in fairness-oriented methodologies within the field.
