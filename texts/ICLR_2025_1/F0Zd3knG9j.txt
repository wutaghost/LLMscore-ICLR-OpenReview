009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the learning process and the embedded computation in transform-
012
               ers is becoming a central goal for the development of interpretable AI. In the
               present study, we introduce a hierarchical filtering procedure for generative mod-
013
               els of sequences on trees, allowing us to hand-tune the range of positional cor-
014
               relations in the data. Leveraging this controlled setting, we provide evidence
015            that vanilla encoder-only transformers can approximate the exact inference al-
016            gorithm when trained on root classification and masked language modeling tasks,
017            and study how this computation is discovered and implemented. We find that cor-
018            relations at larger distances, corresponding to increasing layers of the hierarchy,
019            are sequentially included by the network during training. Moreover, by compar-
020            ing attention maps from models trained with varying degrees of filtering and by
021            probing the different encoder levels, we find clear evidence of a reconstruction of
022            correlations on successive length scales corresponding to the various levels of the
023
               hierarchy, which we relate to a plausible implementation of the exact inference
               algorithm within the same architecture.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Transformer-based large language models have revolutionized natural language processing, and have
028
      notably demonstrated their capacity to perfectly assimilate the grammatical rules of the languages
029
      they are trained on. While this evidence shows that transformers can handle and exploit the subtle
030   long-range correlations that emerge in natural language, their inner workings remain largely unclear.
031
032   Due to the complexity of the standard transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), understanding
033
      what strategy is precisely implemented via the attention mechanism to solve a given problem has
      been limited so far to very simple tasks (Weiss et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2024; Behrens et al., 2024).
034
      Nonetheless, significant results have been obtained by studying transformers on simplified models of
035
      language known as Context-Free Grammars (CFGs). Through probing of the so-called parsing tree
036   of CFGs, evidence has notably pointed towards transformers trained on predicting masked symbols
037   implementing the optimal dynamic programming algorithm to reconstruct the hidden structure of
038   the grammar, but alas without finding a fully plausible implementation within the architecture (Zhao
039   et al., 2023; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023). On the other hand, when tasked with reconstructing the most
040   probable parsing tree in the context of probabilistic CFGs, transformers may struggle to match the
041   optimal algorithm if ambiguity is high (Khalighinejad et al., 2023).
042   Beyond language models, the significance of data structure in machine learning applications is well
043   recognized yet remains poorly understood. CFGs represent a data structure characterized by hier-
044   archical correlations (Mossel, 2016). In general, understanding how standard deep networks can
045   take advantage of this hierarchical structure in their training is an important research question. To-
046   wards this objective, simplified hierarchical models of structured data on fixed trees have proved
047   very useful in understanding the effectiveness of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (Cagnetta
048   et al., 2024), for which there are now formal results supporting the idea that the optimal Belief Prop-
049   agation (BP) algorithm can be approximately implemented (Mei, 2024). Unfortunately, while the
050
      implementation of the hierarchy in CNNs is made quite transparent by the hierarchical structure of
      their convolutional filters, this is not true for transformers, and one can therefore not straightfor-
051
      wardly transpose this interpretation to other architectures (Cagnetta & Wyart, 2024).
052
053   In this work, we present a complementary study to those described above, which allows us to under-
      stand further how transformers approach optimal inference in a structured data model.

087   Our contributions. We propose a controlled hierarchical model of discrete sequences, in which
088   we can easily tune the strength of correlations between tokens thanks to a “filtering” parameter k,
089   illustrated in Fig. 1(a). This tree-based probabilistic graphical model gives us access to the exact
090   inference algorithm for reconstructing any symbol on the tree, Belief Propagation (BP) (Mézard &
091   Montanari, 2009). Leveraging this context, we show that
092
             • Transformers not only approach optimal performance in root classification and Mask Lan-
093
               guage Modeling (MLM) tasks, but they spontaneously do so in a calibrated way—i.e., by
094            predicting probabilities that approximate those yielded by the BP oracle even on out-of-
095            sample inputs, see Fig. 1(b)—which provides evidence of an equivalence in computation
096            to the exact inference algorithm.
097
             • When trained with stochastic gradient descent, transformers sequentially discover the exis-
098            tence of higher hierarchical correlation levels (i.e., longer-range correlations), progressively
099            aligning with the prediction of algorithms that impute only parts of the full correlation
100            structure, see Fig. 1(c)-(d). In other words, our simplified setting allows us to understand
101            how transformers learn from structured data in time.
102          • Well-trained transformers reconstruct the correct hierarchical structure through the suc-
103            cession of attention blocks. Matching the number of transformer layers to the number of
104            layers in the generative tree, we find that the attention maps are compatible with a natural
105            implementation of BP within the architecture, see Fig. 1(e). We verify this affinity through
106            probing experiments, providing strong clues on how transformers learn from our struc-
107            tured data in “space”, thereby explaining the effectiveness of unsupervised pre-training for
               supervised classification tasks, illustrated in Fig. 1(f).

115   2     A MODEL WITH FILTERED HIERARCHICAL CORRELATIONS

117   2.1   T HE FULL HIERARCHICAL MODEL
118
      We consider a tree-based generative process producing structured sequences of discrete symbols.
119
      We here focus on the fixed tree topology case, allowing for direct control over the effective range
120   of the hierarchical correlations induced in the generated sequences (2.2), and enabling exact and
121   efficient inference through Belief Propagation (2.4).
122
123
      The “full” hierarchical generative process shown in the first row of Fig 1(a) can be described as
      follows. The chain starts from an initial symbol x0 , which we will refer to as the root of the tree,
124
      sampled with probability p0 from a vocabulary X = {1, . . . , q}. Then, the first layer of the tree
125
      is drawn randomly using a transition tensor M, which assigns the probability of generating some
126   children—from the same vocabulary X —given a parent (here x0 ). In this work, we will restrict
127                                                                       q×q×q
      ourselves to binary trees for simplicity. We therefore have M ∈ R+        , with Mabc the probability
128   of generating the pair (b, c) given a parent a. Since   its elements are transition probabilities, this
                                                         P
129   tensor should satisfy Mabc ∈ [0, 1] ∀ a, b, c and bc Mabc = 1 ∀ a. The process, with the same
130   tensor M, is then repeated independently for each of the newly created children nodes for a total
131   of ℓ generations, eventually yielding a sequence of 2ℓ symbols {xi }i=1,...,2ℓ . We will refer to the
132   symbols in the sequence as the leaves of the generative tree.
133
      The class of transition tensors M that we use is defined precisely in Appendix A. In short, we
134   will resort to randomly sampled log-normal transition probabilities, yielding complex long-range
135   correlations along the sequences. Importantly, we will only consider tensors with non-overlapping
136   entries, such that: if Mabc > 0, then ∀a′ ̸= a Ma′ bc = 0. As a result, the production rules of
137   our unfiltered generative model are non-ambiguous in the sense that a pair of children symbols can
138   only have a single parent. Given all the symbols on the leaves, one can therefore deterministically
139   reconstruct the underlying generative tree, all the way up to the root.

141   2.2   F ILTERING HIERARCHICAL CORRELATIONS
142
      We develop a filtering tool that enables control over the correlation structure in the generated se-
143
      quences. In particular, we consider a family of generative models, indexed by an integer k ≤ ℓ, with
144
      hierarchical correlations truncated at a given depth k of the tree.
145
146   In the k = 0 case described in the previous paragraph, all children generated at any level of the

148

149

171   2.3   R ELATED DATA MODELS
172
      Context-free grammars. Our hierarchical model can be considered as an instance of a simplified
173
      probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) with log-normally distributed transition rates (De Giuli,
174
      2019). The simplification is two-fold. Standard CFGs typically include two distinct sets of symbols,
175   non-terminals and terminals, representing parts of speech—i.e. nouns, verbs etc.—and actual words
176   respectively, plus a root symbol. Here, instead, we consider a single vocabulary X for all the symbols
177   in the tree, including the root—which allows us to define a root classification task. Moreover, the
178   parsing trees underlying CFGs are not fixed: terminals can be produced at different levels and the
179   sequence length can vary. Instead, we assume a fixed parsing tree for our model, where the 2ℓ leaves
180   are collected from the last layer—which allows us to define a filtering procedure based on removing
181   layers of hidden symbols above the leaves.
182
183   The Random Hierarchy Model. Our model is closely related to the recently introduced Random
184   Hierarchy Model (RHM) of Cagnetta et al. (2024), which was studied to improve the understanding
185   of the effect of hierarchical structures on generative diffusion (Sclocchi et al., 2024) or last token
186   prediction (Cagnetta & Wyart, 2024). The main differences to our formulation are that in the RHM
187
      the allowed transitions have uniform transition rates—while we consider a log-normal distribution—
      and that the production rules depend on the layer—while we here consider a single transition tensor
188
      throughout the tree. Correlations between the leaves arise in the RHM when some children pairs
189
      cannot be produced, leading to a reduced entropy of viable sequences. Having non-uniform transi-
190   tions in our model similarly limits the entropy, while leading to a significantly different correlation
191   structure. One should for instance notice that the staircase decrease of the correlations as a function
192   of the distance between leaves presented in Cagnetta & Wyart (2024) is not visible in our case.
193

      2.4   E XACT INFERENCE
195
196   A key advantage of generating sequences through a tree-based process is that we can perform exact
197   inference efficiently using a dynamic programming approach. Moreover, the fixed tree topology
198   allows us to consider a simplified version of the general inside-outside algorithm (Baker, 1979),
199   which can be written in a message-passing form within the Belief Propagation (BP) formalism (Sato,
200   2007; Mézard & Montanari, 2009). Assuming that the transition tensor M and root probabilities p0
201   are known, with BP one can compute the exact marginal probabilities for all the symbols at any
202
      position in the tree, with a computational cost linear in the size of the tree. Without going into detail
      on the derivation, let us describe the BP scheme for the filtered tree graphs we are considering.
203
204   We start by randomly initializing an upgoing and downgoing message—each one being a vector
205   in Rq that represents a probability distribution over the q possible symbols—for each edge in the
206   generative tree. In the following, we denote with νj→α a message going from a so-called variable
207   node j (shown by a circle in the sketches) to a factor node α (shown by a full or empty square in the
208
      sketches), and with ν̂α→j the message in the opposite direction. Wherever there is a known variable
      one should then fix νj→α [xj ] = δxj ,a , where a is the known value e.g. of the leaf.

253   3.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
254
255   We will focus on the encoder-only variant (Devlin et al., 2019) of the celebrated “vanilla” trans-
256   former architecture, introduced in Vaswani et al. (2017). A full recap of this parametrization is
257   given in Appendix B.
258   In a nutshell, each of the sequence elements xi ∈ {1, . . . , q} is first converted to a positionally-
259                      (0)
      informed token xi ∈ Rd . For our experiments, we consider d = 128 and the standard sinusoidal
260
      positional encoding of Vaswani et al. (2017). Each transformer block in the network then maps
261   the previous encoded sequence onto a new sequence of tokens with the same length and embedding
262   dimension, through a concatenation of a self-attention layer and a fully connected layer, with residual
263   connections and layer normalization. The self-attention layer importantly introduces some mixing
264   between the different tokens in the sequence, represented by what we will refer to as an attention
                       ℓ   ℓ
265   matrix A ∈ R2+ ×2 . We take the fully connected layer to be a standard 2-layer network with relu
266   activations and hidden dimension d′ = 2048. Following these operations, repeated nL times to
267   obtain the full encoder, we obtain a position-dependent high-dimensional representation of each of
268   the original symbols in the sequence. What is finally done with this sequence of tokens depends
269   on the task at hand: we consider root classification in Sec. 3.2 and masked language modeling in
      Sec. 3.3.

358   3.3   M ASKED L ANGUAGE M ODELING
359
      We now turn to self-supervised training, where the model learns from a dataset of P unlabeled
360
      sequences. In simple terms, the Masked Language Modeling (MLM) training procedure consists of
361   randomly masking parts of the sequences and asking the model to recover them from the context.
362   This is closer to what is done in practice to train large language models, see e.g. Devlin et al. (2019);
363   Liu et al. (2019). While in principle one could mask several symbols simultaneously in training, we
364   focus on single-symbol masking—at a random position in the sequence—in the following, given
365   the limited length of our sequences (a single symbol representing already 6.25% of the sequence for
366   ℓ = 4). Contrary to the root inference task, in MLM perfect accuracy cannot be achieved even in the
367   fully hierarchical case, because of the stochastic nature of the branching process in the generative
368   tree. The optimal performance is still yielded by the BP matched to the test data.
369   To reconstruct the masked symbol, we now feed a single token, selected from the final transformer
370   encoding at the positions associated with the masked element, to a linear layer producing a vector
371   of logits. The network is then trained by minimizing the cross-entropy loss between these logits and
372   the one-hot encoding of the masked element in the sequence.

521   5   C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   By using a simple, tunable, hierarchical model of structured sequences, we were able to shed some
524   light on the inner workings of transformer encoders and better understand how they achieve optimal
525   inference on both supervised and self-supervised tasks. The modularity of our data model also
526   allowed us to uncover how transformers sequentially implement longer-range correlations during
527   the learning dynamics, compatible with similar controlled studies (Rende et al., 2024) and with the
528
      general understanding of LLMs trained on natural language (Kaplan et al., 2020). This mechanism
      could perhaps be exploited to shape theory-driven curriculum learning strategies for NLP, where
529
      curating the presentation order of training examples was already proven effective (Campos, 2021).
530
531   Generalizing our filtering-based interpretative tool to the case of variable sequence lengths (Allen-
532   Zhu & Li, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023)—where the topology of the parsing tree is not known a priori—is
533   a challenging but promising direction for approaching a more detailed understanding of the learning
534
      dynamics and the embedded computation in transformer trained on natural language. On the other
      hand, while the idealized model of structured sequences studied in the present work might be less
535
      suited for modeling natural language compared to standard CFGs, the agnostic nature of the ap-
536
      proach could open connections to other related fields, like protein sequences analysis (Zhang et al.,
537   2023) and immunology (Meynard-Piganeau et al., 2024). It could finally be interesting to undertake
538   a similar investigation on the way transformers learn in other problems where optimal inference can
539   also be achieved via dynamic programming (Mossel et al., 2014; 2023).

647   better sample efficiency, which seems to indicate that more flexible models require less data to reach

837   by the network for each token and their correct ancestor label, then averaged on all the sequence
838   elements. We present another experiment, where the cross-entropy is measured only with the first
839   and the last token embeddings of the sequence, just below. The readout is trained on 100 epochs in
840   all cases, which we found to be sufficient for the relatively small training set size we used.

849   representations, but with models trained on factorized data. As visible in the right panel of Fig. 14,

852   only predict ancestors up to level 2 (two ancestry layers above the leaves - above that, the tree

854   ancestors right above the leaves - for the same reason). This is exactly what could be expected from
855
      the attention maps of Fig. 6. As before, we are probing the hidden representations of individual
      tokens, so this happens because the attention must provide mixing between ∼ 2ℓ−k elements of the
856
      sequence in order for individual tokens to carry information up to the level k of the fully hierarchical

      generative model.
