009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) has traditionally relied on models with
012            large parameters to capture extended temporal dependencies. However, time se-
013            ries data, unlike high-dimensional images or text, often exhibit strong periodicity
014            and low-rank structures, especially in long forecasting horizons. This charac-
015            teristic can lead many models focusing on redundant patterns, resulting in inef-
016            ficient use of computational resources. In this paper, we introduce TimeBase,
017
               an ultra-lightweight network with fewer than 0.4k parameters, designed to har-
               ness the power of minimalism in LTSF. TimeBase extracts core periodic features
018
               by leveraging full-rank typical period representations under orthogonality con-
019
               straints, enabling accurate prediction of future cycles. Extensive experiments
020            on real-world datasets demonstrate that TimeBase not only achieves minimal-
021            ism in both model size and computational cost, reducing MACs by 35x and
022            parameter counts by over 1000 times compared to standard linear models, but
023            also wins state-of-the-art forecasting performance, ranking Top1-Top5 in all 28
024            prediction settings. Additionally, TimeBase can also serve as a very effective
025            plug-and-play tool for patch-based forecasting methods, enabling extreme com-
026            plexity reduction without compromising prediction accuracy. Code is available at
027            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TimeBase-fixbug.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031   Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) has been studied with significant interest in various do-
032   mains, ranging from energy management, traffic accident preservation, and extreme disaster warn-
033
      ing Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2023b); Lin et al. (2023a); Wang et al. (2023). With the rapid
      advancement of deep learning, an increasing number of models have been proposed, including
034
      MLP-based Liu et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2024a), RNN-based Lin et al. (2023b), and Transformer-
035
      based Liu et al. (2021a); Zhang & Yan (2023), approaches, all of which employ thousands to millions
036   of parameters to capture long-range dependencies and forecast future outcomes.
037
038   Generally, a higher number of parameters increases the model capacity, which can lead to better pre-
039
      dictive performance Zhou et al. (2023a). In the fields of computer vision (CV) and natural language
      processing (NLP), large models have achieved significant success He et al. (2016); Liu et al. (2023).
040
      For instance, Vision Transformers (ViT) Dosovitskiy (2020) have demonstrated outstanding capa-
041
      bilities in image recognition, while large language models (LLM) have made breakthrough advances
042   across various language tasks Devlin (2018); Radford et al. (2019). Recently, large models are be-
043   ing explored for LTSF to capture complex temporal patterns and long-range dependencies Jin et al.
044   (2023). For example, some LLM-based methods are proposed with tens of billions of parameters.
045   However, despite their impressive performance on specific forecasting tasks, these models suffer
046   from high computational costs, interpretability challenges, and resource-intensive requirements.
047
      In fact, images and text, as high-dimensional data, contain multiple dependencies and complex
048   underlying physical rules Liu et al. (2021b), which necessitate the use of more parameters to model
049   their rich semantic structures. However, as shown in Figure 1(a), one-dimensional time series data
050   is typically much more regular, exhibiting obvious periodicity. Moreover, in long-term time series
051   data, this regularity can even manifest as low-rank characteristics Liu et al. (2012), as different
052   cycles often exhibit similar temporal patterns Jones & Brelsford (1967); Hochreiter & Schmidhuber
053   (1997). This raises an important question: Is it truly necessary to employ such a large number of
      parameters to learn these regular time series patterns Tan et al. (2024)?

096   Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) aims to predict future sequences of considerable length
097   using extended historical windows. The advancement of deep learning has significantly enhanced
098   the accuracy of LTSF, with various foundational models, such as Transformers Zhou et al. (2021);
099   Zhang & Yan (2023), Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) Luo & Wang (2024), and Recur-
100   rent Neural Networks (RNNs) Lin et al. (2023b), being employed to design long-term forecasting
101   networks.
102   These models are designed based on the inherent properties of time series, such as series decompo-
103   sition Wu et al. (2021), frequency domain Xu et al. (2024), and periodic characteristics Wu et al.
104   (2023). As to series decomposition, for instance, Autoformer Wu et al. (2021) introduces a se-
105   ries decomposition block that utilizes moving average techniques to decompose complex temporal
106   variations into seasonal and trend components, each undergoing separate time series modeling. Ad-
107   ditionally, FEDformer Zhou et al. (2022) further enhances the representation capabilities of the
      series decomposition block by employing multiple kernels moving average to decompose data at


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      various granularities, thereby improving forecasting performance. Considering the frequency do-
109   main characteristics of time series, FITS Xu et al. (2024) operates on the principle that time series
110   can be manipulated through interpolation in the complex frequency domain, achieving performance
111   comparable to state-of-the-art models for time series forecasting. On the other hand, periodicity
112   is a significant factor considered by many LTSF methods. TimesNet Wu et al. (2023)proposes
113   the use of Fourier Transform to capture multiple periodic lengths of time series, expanding one-
114   dimensional time series into several two-dimensional components, which are processed through
115   two-dimensional networks to handle high-dimensional data. CrossGNN Huang et al. (2024b) em-
116   ploys moving average techniques based on periodicity to expand single-granularity time series data
117
      into multi-granularity data, enriching the information contained within the dataset. SparseTSF Lin
      et al. (2024) directly utilizes the prior periodicity of time series to transform LTSF at the time-point
118
      level into segmented prediction tasks at the periodic level, thereby reducing the scale of network
119
      parameters.
120
121   On the other hand, many MLP-based models have emerged, aiming to achieve lightweight forecast-
122   ing solutions Wang et al. (2024a). DLinear Zeng et al. (2023) introduces a linear model based on
123
      trend and seasonal decomposition, whose competitive forecasting performance empirically demon-
      strated the feasibility of using MLPs for LTSF. Following this, TiDE Das et al. provides theoretical
124
      proof that the simplest linear analogue could achieve near-optimal error rates for linear dynami-
125
      cal systems. Later, numerous Mixer-based works emerge, such as MTS-Mixer Li et al. (2023),
126   TSMixer Ekambaram et al. (2023) and HDMixer Huang et al. (2024a), which stack standard MLP
127   layers to efficiently capture correlations across different dimensions of multivariate time series. Fur-
128   thermore, Koopa Liu et al. (2024b) addresses the challenge of dynamic and unstable time series
129   systems by disentangling time-variant and time-invariant components using Fourier filters and de-
130   signing a Koopman Predictor to advance the respective dynamics. TimeMixer Wang et al. (2024b)
131   tackles the issue of different granularity levels in micro and macro series by proposing mixing
132   blocks, fully leveraging disentangled multi-scale series in both past extraction and future prediction
133   phases. These works represent efficient time series forecasting models based on MLP structures
134
      (1.03 M ∼ 31.07 M). However, it still remains challenging when faced with stricter deployment
      constraints on edge devices and higher efficiency demands Chatfield (2013). To address this, We
135
      propose TimeBase to sigficantly reduce data complexity by extracting basis periods, transforming
136
      time-step-level prediction tasks into segment-level (or period-level) forecasting tasks. Our approach
137   requires only 0.06 k ∼ 0.39 k parameters to achieve competitive predictive performance. More
138   detailed differences between TimeBase and MLP-based models is summarized at Appendix A

142   3.1 P ROBLEM D EFINITION
143   In LTSF, the objective is to predict future values over an extended time horizon based on very long
144   look-back windows. Formally, let X = [x1 , x2 , ..., xT ] ∈ RT denote the historical time series
145   data, where T ≫ 1 is the length of look-back window. The goal is to forecast the future values
146   Y = [xT +1 , xT +2 , ..., xT +L ] ∈ RL with a forecasting horizon L ≫ 1. However, the exceptionally
147   long horizon scale T and L substantially increases model size, leading to a rapid and considerable
148   growth in the number of parameters, which may be unnecessary for time series data that follow
149
      simple and regular patterns. Consequently, our focus shifts to designing models that not only deliver
      robust and efficient performance but also remain extremely lightweight.
150
151
      3.2 T IME BASE
152
153
      In practical, regular time series often exhibit prominent periodic patterns Lin et al. (2024), with
      approximate low-rank structures across these periods Jones & Brelsford (1967). For example, traffic
154
      flow typically follows a daily period, with similar patterns recurring each day. To effectively leverage
155
      period characteristics and accomplish efficient forecasting, we propose TimeBase, implemented
156   through Basis Extraction and Period Forecasting by two extremely small-scale linear layers. This
157   approach drastically reduces the model parameters to the hundred level while maintaining state-
158   of-the-art (SOTA) forecasting performance. Most existing multivariate time series are homogeneous,
159   meaning that each sequence within the dataset exhibits similar periodicity. This characteristic allows
160   them to be organized as a unified multivariate time series. Based on this property, we employ
161   the Channel Independence Nie et al. (2023) to simplify the forecasting of MTS data into separate
      univariate forecasting tasks. An overview of TimeBase is shown in Figure 2.

176   3.2.1 BASIS P ERIOD R ECONSTRUCTION
177   First, we extract the period P from the time series, which can be categorized into two scenarios: (1)
178   The time series has a predefined prior period: For instance, in domains like electricity or traffic,
179   the cyclic patterns often follow a daily periodicity, allowing us to directly assign P = 24 as the
180   known period from hourly sampled data. (2) The time series lacks significant prior knowledge
181
      of its period: In this case, we can perform a period analysis, i.e., Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT),
      on the available dataset, such as the training set Xtrain , to determine the corresponding period Wu
182
      et al. (2023). Based on the period P , we segment the one-dimensional time series X ∈ RT into
183
      N = PT sub-sequences, denoted as Xhis = [X1 , X2 , ..., XN ] ∈ RN ×P , each of length P . When

184   the length of XN is insufficient to meet P , the corresponding values from XN −1 will be used to fill
185   in the gaps. The segmentation operation can be represented as:
186
187                                          Xhis = Segment[N,P ] (X)                                       (1)
188
      where N and P in Segment[N,P ] (·) represent the number of rows and columns of the transformed
189
      2D matrix. The maximized rank of the matrix Xhis is Rmax = min(N, P ). Given that typical
190   time series exhibit similar temporal patterns across periods, we have R ≪ min(N, P ) Jones &
191   Brelsford (1967). However, directly utilizing low-rank time-series data for prediction can result in
192   unnecessary resource consumption and an oversized model. Fortunately, in structured time series
193   data, a representative periodic pattern can be identified, referred to as the basis period Xbasis ∈ RR×P
194   which can be utilized to capture compact information and minimize the model size. Just as any
195   vector in a coordinate system can be represented as a linear combination of its basis vectors, the
196   combination of basis periods in specific time series can represent its any periodic pattern Hochreiter
197   & Schmidhuber (1997). Conversely, we can approximate the full-rank basis periods using the low-
198
      rank temporal periods:

      Implementation Details In TimeBase, Channel Independence Nie et al. (2023) is involved to
325   simplify the multivariate forecasting process to univariate time series forecasting. For datasets with
326   shorter periodicity, such as ETTh1, ETTh2, Electricity, and Traffic, we use a prior period P = 24 for
327   subsequence segmentation and set the number of basis periods R = 6 for efficient basis extraction.
328   For datasets with extremely long periods (which exceed the look-back window), such as ETTm1,
329   ETTm2, and Weather, we use smaller P values and larger R values for effective basis extraction.
330   For the orthogonality loss weight λorth , due to varying loss scales across datasets, we perform hy-
331   perparameter searches over λorth ∈ [0.04, 0.08, 0.12, 0.16, 0.2] for each dataset. Due to TimeBase’s
332   extremely small parameter count, and the need to reconstruct the basis period based on respectively
333
      longer historical information, we choose a larger input length , i.e., L = 720. Section 5.4 presents
      the sensitivity analysis on the number of basis periods R and the orthogonality loss weight λorth .

      To deploy long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) models in more realistic scenarios such as edge
531
      computing and mobile devices, we focus on exploring lightweight forecasting methods. Given the
532   periodicity of long time series and the approximate low-rank nature resulting from similar patterns
533   between adjacent cycles, we propose TimeBase. This model employs basis period extraction to
534   identify representative periodic features, transforming step-level long-term time series forecasting
535   into period-level forecasting. Theoretical analysis and extensive empirical results demonstrate that
536   this approach not only ensures prediction accuracy but also significantly reduces the model size,
537   achieving the lightest time series forecasting model to date, with only 0.39K parameters.
