009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The production of high-fidelity images by generative models has been transforma-
012            tive to the space of artificial intelligence. Yet, while the generated images are of
013            high quality, the images tend to mirror biases present in the dataset they are trained
014            on. While there has been an influx of work to tackle fair ML broadly, existing
015            works on fair image generation typically rely on modifying the model architec-
016            ture or fine-tuning an existing generative model which requires costly retraining
017            time. In this paper, we use a family of tractable probabilistic models called prob-
018            abilistic circuits (PCs), which can be equipped to a pre-trained generative model
019
               to produce fair images without retraining. We show that for a given pre-trained
               generative model, our method only requires a small fair reference dataset to train
020
               the PC, removing the need to collect a large (fair) dataset to retrain the generative
021
               model. Our experimental results show that our proposed method can achieve a
022            balance between training resources and ensuring fairness and quality of generated
023            images.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In recent years, generative models have seen an explosion of interest and advancements and are
029
      being applied in a wide range of real-world domains. They have broad applications, including but
030   not limited to image, text, audio, video, and medical data. Some popular examples of image-based
031   generative models are variational autoencoders (Kingma, 2013), generative adversarial networks
032   (GAN) (Goodfellow et al., 2020), flow-based generative models (Dinh et al., 2014), and diffusion
033   models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015). Similarly, text-based generative models such as ChatGPT
034   and LLAMA (Touvron et al., 2023) have rapidly gained the interest of academia, industry, and the
035   broader public.
036   However, while generative models have demonstrated success across many domains in producing
037   realistic samples, fair generation has been a relatively less explored area. Fair generation is a difficult
038   challenge as it is a direct consequence of using biased data in training generative models. Yet, as
039   generative models become integrated into everyday life, techniques to ensure fair generation must
040   be established. They are also being used to generate simulated data to train downstream ML models,
041   again with fairness implications.
042   One of the first challenges to address in fairness-aware machine learning is determining the appro-
043   priate notion of fairness for the domain. For example, for fair classification, there exist numerous
044   types of technical formulations of fairness, often concerning the output (prediction) of the classifier
045   and sensitive/protected attributes such as race, gender, or other demographic features. On the other
046   hand, for image generation, there is no particular output/target variable with respect to which to de-
047   fine fairness. Rather, the users and ML practitioners for downstream tasks may be interested in the
048   distribution of the generated samples and ensuring that it is not biased with respect to some sensitive
049   attributes. Furthermore, this notion of fairness could simply enforce that the sensitive attributes of
050
      the generated images follow a uniform distribution (e.g., equal probability of generating a female
      or male image); however, it may be more appropriate in certain domains to rather enforce that the
051
      generated distribution matches the population distribution. As such, this work focuses on ensuring
052
      that the distribution of generated images follows a given reference fair distribution.
053
      This paper makes the following key contributions:

      2     R ELATED W ORK
065
066   Discussions regarding fair image generation have grown in the machine-learning community due
067   to the increased utilization of generative models. Previous approaches to fair image generation
068
      involved transfer learning (Teo et al., 2023), generating fair synthetic data (Van Breugel et al., 2021),
      and learning without sensitive labels (Um & Suh, 2023; Jalal et al., 2021). Pioneering work in this
069
      area includes (Choi et al., 2020a), which uses a weakly-supervised approach to learn a generative
070
      model based on an importance reweighing scheme. Tan et al. (2020) also tackles the problem of fair
071   image generation, specifically focusing on generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow
072   et al., 2020) after first seeing empirically that bias within training data is amplified by the GAN
073   model. They propose the use of “latent distribution shifting” by learning a Gaussian mixture model
074   (GMM) over a set of fair latent codes conditioned on a specific attribute value.
075
      We differ from these ideas in that we utilize a more expressive model (Probabilistic Circuit) to learn
076
      the distribution of a reference fair dataset. This allows us to integrate directly into a variety of pre-
077   trained generative models similar to (Tan et al., 2020); however, the increase of expressivity allows
078   us to more easily represent complex latent distributions over the fair subset of attributes.

083   3.1   N OTATION
084
085   Throughout this paper, random variables are denoted by uppercase letters (X) and their assignments
086   by lowercase letters (x). We use bold uppercase (X) and lowercase letters (x) for sets of variables
087
      and their assignments, respectively. P r represents probability, p and q represent probability mass
      functions, and E[.] represents expected value.

089
090   3.2   FAIR G ENERATION
091
092   In fair generation, the goal is to have a model capable of generating samples with distribution pfair (x)
093
      where pfair (x) = Es∼Sf air P r(X = x|s) and Sf air is the distribution of the sensitive attributes
      according to a usually small reference dataset, called Df air . The reference dataset may (but not
094
      necessarily will) follow a uniform distribution with respect to the sensitive attributes. For example, in
095
      terms of gender equality, usually z ∼ Bernoulli(0.5), meaning that the gender has equal probability
096   for female and male. Generative models need a large dataset (called Dbias ) to produce high-quality
097   images that mirror the inductive biases of the dataset. However, the learned inductive biases usually
098   lead to biases against minority groups. So, they tend not to follow the Sf air distribution. The goal
099   is to use Df air and Dbias to train a generative model that is both expressive and fair.
100

      3.3   P ROBABILISTIC C IRCUITS
102
103   Probabilistic Circuits (PCs) (Choi et al., 2020b) are a class of tractable probabilistic models (TPMs)
104   such as sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos, 2011), Einsum networks (Einets) (Peharz et al.,
105   2020), Cutset networks (Rahman et al., 2014), and arithmetic circuits (Darwiche, 2002). With
106   tractability defined as the ability to compute marginal probabilities in polynomial time. Probabilistic
107   circuits can also be seen as deep mixtures of probability distributions (Darwiche, 2003). We will
      define PCs as the following,

      4    P ROPOSED M ETHOD
136
137
      Assuming that there exists a pre-trained generative model having some kind of encoder-decoder
138   pair—e.g., an autoencoder or a flow-based model, etc.—we use a probabilistic model to intervene
139   on its latent distribution. That is, we will guide the latent variables, such that the images generated by
140   decoding those latent variables will follow a fairer distribution with respect to a reference dataset.
141   In different image generation paradigms, one may also work with a generator such as a GAN, as
142   seen in (Tan et al., 2020). The details regarding specific choice of model for our implementation
143   is provided in Section 5. According to Figure 1, the encoder can be modeled by qϕ (z|x) where x
144   is the input image, z is the latent vector, aka embedding, and ϕ represents all the parameters in the
145   encoder. Similarly, the decoder is modeled by pθ (x̃|z) where x̃ denotes the reconstructed image,
146
      and θ the decoder parameters.
147   Probabilistic circuits have shown excellent capabilities in expressively learning a distribution while
148   maintaining tractability. It gives them the ability to perform marginal and conditional queries in
149   polynomial time with respect to the circuit size. Probabilistic circuits are also data and model ef-
150   ficient; thus there is no need for a large dataset for their training. In this work, a PC learns the
151   distribution of latent variables for Df air . In other words, pψ (z) = P r(Z = z; ψ) is learned and
152
      with the help of the decoder, the distribution of the output, x̃, is fair. We only manipulate the distri-
      bution of z and do not fine-tune the generative model. As shown in the experiments, the proposed
153
      method is fast due to only manipulating the latent variables.
154
155   Given an ideal encoder and decoder pair, meaning that they are not affected by the biases in Dbias ,
156   one tempting procedure is to learn the distributions of the latent variables when the fair dataset
157   (Dbias ) is fed to the encoder (see Figure 1). Considering this scenario, a PC learns the latent space
158
      distribution i.e. pf air (z).
