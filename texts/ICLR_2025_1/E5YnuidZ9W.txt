009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural network minima are often connected by curves along which train and test
012            loss remain nearly constant, a phenomenon known as mode connectivity. While
013            this property has enabled applications such as model merging and fine-tuning, its
014            theoretical explanation remains unclear. We propose a new approach to explor-
015            ing the connectedness of minima using parameter space symmetry. By linking
016
               the topology of symmetry groups to that of the minima, we derive the number of
               connected components of the minima of linear networks and show that skip con-
017
               nections reduce this number. We then examine when mode connectivity and linear
018
               mode connectivity hold or fail, using parameter symmetries which account for a
019            significant part of the minimum. Finally, we provide explicit expressions for con-
020            necting curves in the minima induced by symmetry. Using the curvature of these
021            curves, we derive conditions under which linear mode connectivity approximately
022            holds. Our findings highlight the role of continuous symmetries in understanding
023            the neural network loss landscape.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Among recent studies on the loss landscape, a particularly interesting finding is mode connectivity
028
      (Draxler et al., 2018; Garipov et al., 2018), which refers to the phenomenon that distinct minima
029
      found by stochastic gradient descent (SGD) can be connected by continuous, low-loss paths through
030   the high-dimensional parameter space. Mode connectivity has significant implications for other
031   aspects of deep learning theory, including the lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle et al., 2020) and
032   the analysis of loss landscapes and training trajectories (Gotmare et al., 2018). Additionally, mode
033   connectivity has inspired applications in diverse fields, including model ensembling (Garipov et al.,
034   2018; Benton et al., 2021; Benzing et al., 2022), model averaging (Izmailov et al., 2018; Wortsman
035   et al., 2022), pruning (Frankle et al., 2020), improving adversarial robustness (Zhao et al., 2020),
036   and fine-tuning for altering prediction mechanism (Lubana et al., 2023).
037   Despite extensive empirical validation, mode connectivity, especially linear mode connectivity, re-
038   mains largely a theoretical conjecture (Altintas et al., 2023). The limited theoretical explanation
039   suggests a need for new proof techniques. In this paper, we focus on parameter symmetries, which
040   encode information about the structure of the parameter space and the minimum. Our work in-
041   troduces a new approach towards understanding the topology of the minimum and complements
042   existing theories on mode connectivity (Yunis et al., 2022; Freeman & Bruna, 2017; Nguyen, 2019;
043   2021; Kuditipudi et al., 2019; Shevchenko & Mondelli, 2020; Nguyen et al., 2021).
044   Discrete symmetry is well-known to be related to mode connectivity. In particular, the neural net-
045   work output, and hence the minimum, is invariant under neuron permutations (Hecht-Nielsen, 1990).
046   Various algorithms have been developed to find the optimal permutation for linear connectivity
047   (Singh & Jaggi, 2020)(Ainsworth et al., 2023), and Entezari et al. (2022) conjecture that all minima
048   found by SGD are linearly connected up to permutation. Compared to discrete symmetry, the role
049   of continuous symmetry, such as positive rescaling in ReLU, on shaping loss landscape remains less
050
      well studied.
051   We explore the connectedness of minimum through continuous symmetries in the parameter space.
052   Continuous symmetry groups with continuous actions define positive dimensional connected spaces
053   in the minimum (Zhao et al., 2023). By relating topological properties of symmetry groups to
      their orbits and the minimum, we show that both continuous and discrete symmetry are useful in

054
      understanding the origin and failure cases of mode connectivity. Additionally, continuous symmetry
055   defines curves on the minimum (Zhao et al., 2024). This enables a principled method for deriving
056   explicit expressions for paths connecting two minima, a task that previously relied on empirical
057   approaches.
058
      In this paper, we focus on the complete set of minima, instead of restricting to those reachable by
059
      SGD. Our main contributions are:
060
061          • Providing the number of connected components of full-rank linear regression with and
062            without skip connections, by relating topology of symmetry groups to topology of minima.
063
064
             • Proving mode connectivity up to permutation for linear networks with invertible weights.
065          • Deriving examples where the error barrier on linear interpolation of minima is unbounded.
066
             • Deriving explicit low-loss curves that connect minima related by symmetry, and bounding
067
               the loss barrier on linear interpolations between minima using the curvature of these curves.

070   2   R ELATED W ORK
071
072   Mode connectivity. Garipov et al. (2018) and Draxler et al. (2018) discover empirically that the
073   global minimum of neural networks are connected by curves on which train and test loss are almost
074   constant. It is then observed that SGD solutions are linearly connected if they are trained from pre-
075   trained weights (Neyshabur et al., 2020) or share a short period of training at the beginning (Frankle
076   et al., 2020). Additionally, neuron alignment by permutation improves mode connectivity (Singh
077
      & Jaggi, 2020) (Tatro et al., 2020). Subsequently, Entezari et al. (2022) conjecture that all minima
      found by SGD are linearly connected up to permutation. Following the conjecture, Ainsworth et al.
078
      (2023) develop algorithms that find the optimal alignment for linear mode connectivity, and Jordan
079
      et al. (2023) further reduce the barrier by rescaling the preactivations of interpolated networks.
080
081   It is worth noting that linear mode connectivity does not always hold outside of computer vision.
082   Language models that are not linearly connected have different generalization strategies (Juneja
083
      et al., 2023). Lubana et al. (2023) further show that the lack of linear connectivity indicates that the
      two models rely on different attributes to make predictions. We derive new theoretical examples of
084
      failure cases of linear mode connectivity (Section 5.2).
085
086
087
      Theory on connectedness of minimum. Several work explores the theoretical explanation of
      mode connectivity by studying the connectedness of sub-level sets. Freeman & Bruna (2017) show
088
      that the minimum is connected for 2-layer linear network without regularization, and for deeper lin-
089
      ear networks with L2 regularization. Futhermore, they show that the minimum of a two-layer ReLU
090   network is asymptotically connected, that is, there exists a path connecting any two solutions with
091   bounded error. Nguyen (2019) proves that the sublevel sets are connected in pyramidal networks
092   with piecewise linear activation functions and first hidden layer wider than 2N , where N is the
093   number of training data). The width requirement is later improved to N + 1 (Nguyen, 2021).
094
      Others prove connectivity under dropout stability. Kuditipudi et al. (2019) prove the existence of a
095
      piece-wise linear path between two solutions for ReLU networks, if they are both dropout stable,
096   or both noise stable and sufficiently overparametrized. Shevchenko & Mondelli (2020) generalize
097   this proof to show that wider neural networks are more connected, following the observation that
098   SGD solutions for wider neural network are more dropout stable. Nguyen et al. (2021) give a new
099   upper bound of the loss barrier between solutions using the loss of sparse subnetworks that are
100   optimized, which is a milder condition than dropout stability. We approach the theoretical origin of
101   mode connectivity via continuous symmetries in the parameter space, a connection that has not been
102   previously established.
103   A few papers propose theoretical explanations for linear mode connectivity using different tools.
104   Yunis et al. (2022) explain linear mode connectivity through finding a convex hull defined by SGD
105   trajectory endpoints. Ferbach et al. (2023) use optimal transport theory to prove that wide two-layer
106   neural networks trained with SGD are linearly connected with high probability. Singh et al. (2024)
107   explain the topography of the loss landscape that enables or obstructs linear mode connectivity.
      Zhou et al. (2023) show that the feature maps of each layer are also linearly connected and identify


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      conditions that guarantee linear connectivity. Altintas et al. (2023) analyze effects of architecture,
109   optimization algorithm, and dataset on linear mode connectivity empirically.
110
111   Symmetry in the loss landscape. Discrete symmetries have inspired a line of work on loss land-
112   scapes. Brea et al. (2019) show that permutations of a layer are connected within a loss level set. By
113   analyzing permutation symmetries, Şimşek et al. (2021) characterize the geometry of the global min-
114   ima manifold for networks without other symmetries and show that adding one neuron to each layer
115   in a minimal network connects the permutation equivalent global minima. Continuous symmetries
116   have also attracted recent attention. By removing permutation and rescaling symmetries, Pittorino
117   et al. (2022) study the geometry of minima in the functional space. Zhao et al. (2023) find a set of
118
      nonlinear continuous symmetries that partially parametrizes the minimum. Zhao et al. (2024) use
      symmetry induced curves to approximate the curvature of the minimum. Our paper explores a new
119
      application of parameter symmetries – explaining the connectedness of the minimum.

      3.1   C ONNECTED COMPONENTS
129   Consider two topological spaces X and Y . A map f : X → Y is continuous if for every open
130   subset U ⊆ Y , its preimage f −1 (U ) is open in X. If X and Y are metric spaces with metrics dX
131   and dY respectively, this is equivalent to the delta-epsilon definition. That is, f is continuous if at
132   every x ∈ X, for any ϵ > 0 there exists δ > 0 such that dX (x, y) < δ implies dY (f (x), f (y)) < ϵ
133   for all y ∈ X.
134
      A topological space is connected if it cannot be expressed as the union of two disjoint, nonempty,
135   open subsets. A topological space X is path connected if for every p, q ∈ X, there is a continuous
136   map f : [0, 1] → X such that f (0) = p and f (1) = q. Path connectedness implies connectedness.
137   The converse is not always true (Lee, 2010), but counterexamples are often specifically constructed
138   and unlikely to be encountered in the context of deep learning. Path connectedness can therefore
139   help develop intuition for connectedness, for practical purposes.
140
      The following theorem is the main intuition of this paper and will appear frequently in proofs.
141
      Theorem 3.1 (Theorem 4.7 in Lee (2010)). Let X, Y be topological spaces and let f : X → Y be
142
      a continuous map. If X is connected, then f (X) is connected.
143
144   A map f is a homeomorphism from X to Y if f is bijective and both f and f −1 are continuous. X
145   and Y are homeomorphic if such a map exists. A (connected) component of a topological space X is
146   a maximal nonempty connected subset of X. The components of X form a partition of X. The next
147   two corollaries of Theorem 3.1 show that connectedness and the number of connected components
148   are topological properties. That is, they are preserved under homeomorphisms.
149   Corollary 3.2. Let f : X → Y be a homeomorphism from X to Y , and let U ⊆ X be a subset of
150   X with the subspace topology. Then U is connected if and only if f (U ) ⊆ Y is connected.
151   Corollary 3.3. Let X be a topological space that has N components. Let Y be a topological space
152   homeomorphic to X. Then Y has N components.
153
      Another consequence of Theorem 3.1 is the following upper bound on the number of components
154   of the image of a continuous map.
155
      Proposition 3.4. Let f : X → Y be a continuous map. The number of components of the image
156   f (X) ⊆ Y is at most the number of components of X.
157
158   Let X1 , ..., Xn be topological spaces. The product space is their Cartesian product X1 × ... × Xn
159   endowed with the product topology. Denote π0 (X) as the set of connected components of a space
160   X. The following proposition provides a way to count the components of a product space.
161   Proposition

      3.2    G ROUPS
163
164   A group is a set G together with a composition law, written as juxtaposition, that satisfies associa-
165   tivity, (ab)c = a(bc) ∀ a, b, c ∈ G, has an identity 1 such that 1a = a1 = a ∀ a ∈ G, and for all
166   a ∈ G, there exists an inverse b such that ab = ba = 1. An action of a group G on a set S is a map
167   · : G × S → S that satisfies 1 · s = s for all s ∈ S and (gg ′ ) · s = g · (g ′ · s) for all g, g ′ in G and
168   all s in S. The orbit of s ∈ S is the set O(s) = {s′ ∈ S | s′ = gs for some g ∈ G}.
169   A topological group is a group G endowed with a topology such that multiplication and inverse
170   are both continuous. A recurring example is the general linear group GLn (R), with the subspace
                                  2
171   topology obtained from Rn . The group GLn (R) has two connected components, which correspond
172   to matrices with positive and negative determinant.
173
174
      The product of groups G1 , ..., Gn is a group denoted by G1 × ... × Gn . The set underlying
      G1 × ... × Gn is the Cartesian product of G1 , ..., Gn . The group structure is defined by identity
175
      (1, ..., 1), inverse (g1 , ..., gn )−1 = (g1−1 , ..., gn−1 ), and multiplication rule (g1 , ..., gn )(g1′ , ..., gn′ ) =
176
      (g1 g1′ , ..., gn gn′ ).

179   3.3    C ONNECTEDNESS OF GROUPS , ORBITS , AND LEVEL SETS
180
      From Theorem 3.1, continuous maps preserve connectedness. Through continuous actions, we study
181
      the connectedness of orbits and level sets by relating them to the connectedness of more familiar
182
      objects such as the general linear group. Establishing a homeomorphism from the group to the set
183   of minima requires the symmetry group’s action to be continuous, transitive, and free. Here we only
184   assume the action to be continuous and try to bound the number of components of the orbits.
185
186
      As an immediate consequence of Proposition 3.4, an orbit cannot have more components than the
      group.
187
188   Corollary 3.6. Assume that the action of a group G on S is continuous. Then the number of
189   connected components of orbit O(s) is smaller than or equal to the number of connected components
190   of G, for all s in S.
191
192
      Let X be a topological space and L : X → R a continuous function on X. A topological group G is
      said to be a symmetry group of L if L(g · x) = L(x) for all g ∈ G and x ∈ X. In this case, the action
193
      can be defined on a level set of L, L−1 (c) with a c ∈ R, as G × L−1 (c) → L−1 (c). If the minimum
194
      of L consists of a single orbit, Corollary 3.6 extends immediately to the number of components of
195   the minimum.
196
197   Corollary 3.7. Let L be a function with a symmetry group G. If the minimum of L consists of a
198
      single G-orbit, then the number of connected components of the minimum is smaller or equal to the
      number of connected components of G.
199
200

201   connectedness of the orbits does not directly inform the connectedness of the level set. Neverthe-

203   components of the space.

      5.3   L INEAR MODE CONNECTIVITY OF ORBITS
379
380   Symmetry accounts for a large part of the set of minima. In particular, given a known minimum x,
381   the orbit of x defines a set of points that are also minima. Although not all minima are on the same
382   orbit of known symmetries, each orbit often contains a nontrivial set of minima. In this section,
383   we examine the error barrier of linear interpolations of minima restricted to an orbit of parameter
384   symmetries.
385   When the architecture contains a multiplication of two weight matrices W2 W1 , where W2 ∈
386   Rm×h , W1 ∈ Rh×n , there is a GLh symmetry that acts on (W1 , W2 ) by g · (W1 , W2 ) =
387   (gW1 , W2 g −1 ) for g ∈ GLh . The following proposition states that a point on the linear inter-
388   polation of two points in the same orbit can be far away from the orbit.
389   Proposition 5.5. Let A ∈ Rn×n be an invertible matrix. Let set S = {(W1 , W2 ) : W1 , W2 ∈
390   Rn×n , W1 W2 = A}. For any positive number b > 0, there exist W ′ , W ′′ ∈ S and 0 < α < 1, such
391   that minŴ ∈S ∥ ((1 − α)W ′ + αW ′′ ) − Ŵ ∥2 > b.
392
393   The structure in the form of W1 W2 is not uncommon in deep learning architectures. Notably, the pa-
394   rameter matrices for queries and keys in the attention function are multiplied directly in this manner
395   (Vaswani et al., 2017), thus admitting the GLh symmetry and having orbits with properties given by
396   Proposition 5.5.
397   While the error barrier in the linear interpolation of two minima can be unbounded (Proposition
398   5.3), this typically occurs when the parameters are allowed to be arbitrarily large. Constraining
399   the parameters to remain bounded ensures that the loss barrier is bounded above. The following
400   proposition makes this intuition precise for the set of minima consisting of a particular orbit.
401   Proposition 5.6. Consider the loss function with the same set of assumptions in Proposition 5.3. Let
402   W ∈ L−1 (0) be a point on the minimum. Consider the multiplicative group of positive real numbers
403   R+ that acts on L−1 (0) by g · (W1 , ..., Wl ) = (W1 , ..., Wl−2 , gWl−1 , Wl g −k ), where g ∈ R+ .
404   Then there exists a positive number b > 0, such that for all 0 < α < 1 and W ′ ∈ Orbit(W )
405   with ||Wi′ ||2 < c for all i and some c > 0, the loss value for points on the linear interpolation
406   L ((1 − α)W + αW ′ ) < b.
407
408
      Proposition 5.5 and 5.6 are two examples where the knowledge of parameter symmetry enables
      analysis of the linear connectivity of subsets of minima. As more continuous symmetries are char-
409
      acterized (e.g. the nonlinear symmetries in Zhao et al. (2023)), these analysis can potentially be
410
      extended to even larger parts of the set of minima.

      7   D ISCUSSION
518
519
      In this work, we study topological properties of the loss level sets by relating their topology to the
520
      topology of symmetry groups. We derive the number of connected components of full-rank multi-
521
      layer networks with and without skip connections, and prove mode connectivity up to permutation
522   for full-rank linear regressions. Using symmetry in the parameter space, we construct an explicit
523   expression for curves that connect two points in the same orbit. The explicit expressions allow us to
524   obtain the curvature of these curves, which are useful to bound the loss barrier on linear interpolation
525   between minima.
526
      While symmetry appears to be a useful tool for studying the loss landscape, our current results
527
      rely on the existence of a homeomorphism between symmetry groups and the minimum. A future
528   direction is to explore the possibility of removing this assumption. Another interesting direction
529   is to investigate additional links between different architecture choices, such as normalization, and
530   connectedness of the minimum. The impact of these results can also benefit from further study on
531   the connection between the topology of minimum and generalization ability of neural networks.
532
      The connectedness results obtained from symmetry raise a number of interesting questions related
533
      to mode connectivity. For example, it would be interesting to understand when and why there is no
534   significant change in loss on the linear interpolation between two minima. One possible explanation
535   is that there always exists a γ defined in the way above that is close to the line formed by the linear
536   interpolation. Another possible reason is that the dimension of minimum is usually high, and a
537   significant part of the linear interpolation is within the minimum with high probability. Moreover, it
538   has been observed that the train and test accuracy are both near constant on the paths that connect
539   different SGD solutions (Garipov et al., 2018). If these paths are induced by a group action, this
      implies that the group action’s dependence on data is weak.

706
      This section contains additional background in general topology and proofs for statements in Section
707
      3. We refer readers to Lee (2010) for a more detailed introduction to this topic.

709
      A.1    C ONNECTED COMPONENTS
710
711   Consider two topological spaces X and Y . A map f : X → Y is continuous if for every open
712   subset U ⊆ Y , its preimage f −1 (U ) is open in X. If X and Y are metric spaces with metrics dX
713   and dY respectively, this is equivalent to the delta-epsilon definition. That is, f is continuous if at
714   every x ∈ X, for any ϵ > 0 there exists δ > 0 such that dX (x, y) < δ implies dY (f (x), f (y)) < ϵ
715   for all y ∈ X.
716
      A topological space is connected if it cannot be expressed as the union of two disjoint, nonempty,
717   open subsets. A topological space X is path connected if for every p, q ∈ X, there is a continuous
718   map f : [0, 1] → X such that f (0) = p and f (1) = q. Path connectedness implies connectedness,
719   but the converse is not true (Lee, 2010). Nguyen (2019) studies the path connectedness of sublevel
720   sets of loss functions.
721
      The following theorem is the main intuition of this paper and will appear frequently in proofs.
722
723
      Theorem A.1 (Theorem 4.7 in Lee (2010), Theorem 3.1 in the Main Text). Let X, Y be topological
      spaces and let f : X → Y be a continuous map. If X is connected, then f (X) is connected.
724
725   A map f is a homeomorphism from X to Y if f is bijective and both f and f −1 are continuous. X
726   and Y are homeomorphic if such a map exists. A (connected) component of a topological space X is
727   a maximal nonempty connected subset of X. The components of X form a partition of X. The next
728   two corollaries of Theorem A.1 show that connectedness and the number of connected components
729   are topological properties. That is, they are preserved under homeomorphisms.
730   Corollary A.2. Let f : X → Y be a homeomorphism from X to Y , and let U ⊆ X be a subset of
731   X with the subspace topology. Then U is connected if and only if f (U ) ⊆ Y is connected.
732
733   Proof. By the definition of homeomorphism, f and f −1 are continuous. From Theorem A.1, if U ∈
734   X is connected, then f (U ) ∈ Y is connected. Similarly, if f (U ) is connected, then f −1 (f (U )) = U
735   is connected.
736

777   A.2    G ROUPS
778
779   A group is a set G together with a composition law, written as juxtaposition, that satisfies associa-
780   tivity, (ab)c = a(bc) ∀ a, b, c ∈ G, has an identity 1 such that 1a = a1 = a ∀ a ∈ G, and for all
781
      a ∈ G, there exists an inverse b such that ab = ba = 1. An action of a group G on a set S is a map
      · : G × S → S that satisfies 1 · s = s for all s ∈ S and (gg ′ ) · s = g · (g ′ · s) for all g, g ′ in G and
782
      all s in S. The orbit of s ∈ S is the set O(s) = {s′ ∈ S | s′ = gs for some g ∈ G}.
783
784   A topological group is a group G endowed with a topology such that multiplication and inverse
785   are both continuous. A recurring example is the general linear group GLn (R), with the subspace
                                  2
786   topology obtained from Rn . The group GLn (R) has two connected components, which correspond
787   to matrices with positive and negative determinant.
788   The product of groups G1 , ..., Gn is a group denoted by G1 × ... × Gn . The set underlying
789   G1 × ... × Gn is the Cartesian product of G1 , ..., Gn . The group structure is defined by identity
790   (1, ..., 1), inverse (g1 , ..., gn )−1 = (g1−1 , ..., gn−1 ), and multiplication rule (g1 , ..., gn )(g1′ , ..., gn′ ) =
791   (g1 g1′ , ..., gn gn′ ).
