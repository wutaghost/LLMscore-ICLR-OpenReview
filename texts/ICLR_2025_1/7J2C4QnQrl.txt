009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning (RL) has the potential to transform power grid operations
012            by providing adaptive, scalable controllers essential for decarbonization and grid re-
013            silience. However, despite their promise, today’s RL methods struggle to deal with
014            complex dynamics, aleatoric uncertainty, long-horizon goals, and hard physical
015            constraints, hindering their application in power grids and other real-world settings.
016
               In this work, we present RL2Grid, a benchmark representing realistic power grid
               operations that aims to foster the maturity of RL methods. This work builds upon
017
               Grid2Op, a power grid simulation framework developed by RTE France, to provide
018
               standardized tasks, state and action spaces, and rewards within a common inter-
019            face, and thereby provide a common basis for monitoring and promoting progress.
020            We evaluate and compare widely adopted RL algorithms across the increasingly
021            complex grid settings represented within RL2Grid, establishing reference perfor-
022            mance metrics and offering insights into the effectiveness of different approaches
023            (including pure RL approaches and hybrid approaches incorporating heuristics).
024            Our findings indicate that power grids present substantial challenges for modern
025            RL, underscoring the need for novel methods capable of dealing with complex
026            real-world physical systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Power grids play a key role in efforts to combat climate change, necessitating a rapid transition to
031   low-carbon energy and improved robustness against climate-induced extremes. This requires power
032   grids to operate under increasing speed, scale, and uncertainty, due in large part to evolving supply and
033   demand profiles resulting from the integration of variable renewable energy sources and distributed
034   devices (Li et al., 2023b). This integration creates significant challenges for human operators of
035   power grids (Marot et al., 2022b), further exacerbated by the limitations of traditional power system
036   solvers in addressing realistic systems (Chauhan et al., 2023). Deep reinforcement learning (RL)
037   offers a promising approach to reshaping power grid operations, having demonstrated impressive
038   performance in simulated environments such as Atari and Starcraft (Mnih et al., 2013; Papoudakis
039
      et al., 2021). However, there remain many open challenges that impede the practical application of
      RL in real-world environments–such as dealing with complex dynamics and aleatoric uncertainty,
040
      learning long-horizon goals, and satisfying hard physical constraints. We argue that power grids
041
      encompass many of these challenges, which are also open research questions in RL. For these reasons,
042   investigating realistic power grid tasks from an RL perspective could yield substantial benefits for
043   both society and the RL research community. However, progress in relevant RL methodologies is
044   hindered by a lack of standardized benchmarks that can help promote and monitor progress, identify
045   bottlenecks, and develop insights to address real-world challenges.
046
      To address this gap, we present RL2Grid, an RL benchmark representing realistic power grid
047
      operations. RL2Grid captures a diverse, standardized set of increasingly complex power grid tasks
048   that entail dealing with the combinatorially large number of possible actions available in typical
049   grid operations. These tasks are presented within a standard Gymnasium-based interface, alongside
050   common rewards, state spaces, and action spaces, in order to provide a common basis for comparison.
051   To ensure the realism of the tasks represented, we build this benchmark upon Grid2Op (Donnot,
052   2020), a well-regarded simulation framework for sequential decision-making in realistic power grids.
053   We additionally provide a comprehensive empirical comparison of widely adopted RL algorithms
      on the tasks captured within RL2Grid. In particular, we evaluate several model-free RL algorithms,

086   2.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING F ORMALIZATION
087
088   We consider power grid problems that can be defined as a Markov decision process (MDP), modeled
089   as a tuple (S, A, P, ρ, R, γ); S and A are the finite sets of states and actions, respectively, P :
090   S × A × S → [0, 1] is the state transition probability distribution, ρ : S → [0, 1] is the initial
091   uniform state distribution, R : S × A → R is a reward function, and γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor.
092
      In policy optimization algorithms, agents learn a parameterized stochastic policy π : S × A → [0, 1],
      modeling the probability of taking an action at ∈ A in a state st ∈ S at a certain step t. We can also
093
      design value-based algorithms by defining state and action value functions Vπ and Qπ , which model
094
      the expected discounted return when starting from a state s (and action a for Qπ ) and following the
095   policy π thereafter as:

                               t                                          t
          Vπ (s) = Eπ         γ R(st , at )|s0 = s , Qπ (s, a) = Eπ      γ R(st , at )|s0 = s, a0 = a .
098                      t=0                                           t=0
099
      Given the current state and action, we can also measure how much better or worse the agent performs
100   compared to its expected performance using the advantage function Aπ (s, a) = Qπ (s, a) − Vπ (s).
101   In these contexts, agents typically use a greedy policy over the action value or the advantage function
102   (i.e., they take the action corresponding to argmax over the values). The goal is to find a policy that
103   maximizes the expected discounted return.

      clarify the setting our benchmark addresses, Figure 1 illustrates a simplified power grid scenario.
109   This grid consists of four substations interconnected by transmission lines (edges), with two power
110   generators and two loads connected to buses within each substation. Generators produce power to
111   meet the demands (loads); the power flows through transmission lines, which also leads to power
112   losses due to resistive heat on the lines; and substations (which may contain multiple buses) can act
113   as “switches” to direct power flows to an extent. All of these electrical components have physical
114   limitations; for instance, generators have ramping limits that prevent arbitrary instantaneous changes
115   in power output, and transmission lines have maximum carrying capacities, with prolonged overloads
116   potentially causing permanent damage and disconnections. RL has the potential to address such
117
      disruptions in real time, for instance by considering the two following categories of actions:
118
             • Topology optimization (Figure 1 top) involves identifying substations where a bus-split
119            action—the type of topological action we consider—can mitigate the overload by adjusting
120            the grid topology (i.e., how elements are currently interconnected in the grid). This ap-
121            proach is cost-effective for grid operators as it typically involves simple switch activation.1
122            However, determining the “optimal” topology from the combinatorial number of possible
123            configurations is typically infeasible using existing optimization-based solvers.
124          • Re-dispatch or curtailment (Figure 1 bottom) deals with adjusting the power flow by re-
125            dispatching or curtailing the power output of fossil and renewable power generators (respec-
126            tively). However, this method is often economically demanding as it disrupts the normal
127            operations of third parties controlling the generators and can lead to additional power costs.

129   2.3   G RID 2O P
130
131   Grid2Op is an open-source simulator designed by RTE France (France’s transmission system opera-
132   tor) to model sequential decision-making on a power grid (Donnot, 2020). It allows for the testing
133
      of various control algorithms, including RL policies, in relatively realistic scenarios. In particular,
      Grid2Op models important complexities in the power grid, including realistic non-linear dynamics,
134
      uncertainty deriving from time-varying renewable energy sources, and the massive amount (combina-
135
      torially large number) of grid configurations and actions that exist even in moderately-sized grids.
136   It then presents various scenarios that simulate typical grid operations, requiring that grids are kept
137   operational for long horizons in a way that is robust to contingency events (i.e., unexpected failures),
138   as well as adhering to physical and operational constraints. Contingencies include transmission
139   line disconnects, deterministic maintenance, and stochastic (“adversarial”) events such as overloads
140   (potentially caused by extreme weather conditions). With respect to operational constraints, Grid2Op
141   imposes operational constraints such as: (i) cooldown periods to prevent immediate reconnection of
142   disconnected lines, and limits on the frequency of actions on the same line to avoid asset degradation;
143   (ii) limited thermal capacity of transmission lines; (iii) ramp rates on generators that restrict how
144
      much power generation can change between time periods; and (iv) adherence to AC power flow
      constraints. Moreover, using external packages like chronix2grid (Marot et al., 2020a), Grid2Op’s
145
      scenarios include time series data that model load demands and generator outputs necessary to satisfy
146
      cumulative demand under ideal conditions without line capacity limits.
147
148   To date, Grid2Op has been primarily used as the computation engine for the “Learning to Run a
149   Power Network” (L2RPN) competitions (Marot et al., 2020b; 2021; 2022a). While a number of
150
      RL-based methods have been proposed over the years for L2RPN, they fail to provide a common
      ground to foster advancements in RL methodologies. For example, each method employs different
151
      grid features as input for the agent and different action spaces of (very) limited size, often without
152
      providing sufficient evidence on how and why these action spaces were considered. For these reasons,
153   to date, there is no standardized solution that allows RL researchers to easily get started in this field
154   and compare over an established benchmark. To address this gap, our work builds on Grid2Op
155   to provide a benchmark with standardized tasks, state and action spaces, and rewards, as well as
156   comprehensive evaluation of strong baseline methods; these are critical to provide a common basis
157   for assessing advances in RL methods (Papoudakis et al., 2021) as well as to improve accessibility to
158   RL practitioners who may have limited prior knowledge of power systems. (See also Appendix A for
159   further discussion on the relationship between L2RPN and our RL2Grid benchmark.)

      3     RL2G RID B ENCHMARK
176
177   In this section, we present RL2Grid, a benchmark for RL in power grid operations. We discuss the
178   main features of the power grid environments presented as part of RL2Grid (which we wrap within a
179   standardized Gymnasium interface), as well as our approach to standardizing the action and state
180
      spaces and the reward function for these environments in order to create a standardized set of tasks.
      We further discuss the set of widely-adopted RL algorithms that we assess as baselines on these tasks,
181
      as well as presenting a heuristic module that enables basic grid operations to be incorporated within
182
      the training loop of existing RL algorithms.

185   3.1    RL2G RID TASKS
186
187   The tasks presented within RL2Grid are designed on top of 7 main “base environments” from
188   Grid2Op. Each of these “base environments” has a double bus system, meaning that every
189   electrical component (i.e., generator and load) has two possible connections within a substa-
190   tion. Table 1 summarizes the base environments, including their features and the number
191
      of electric components. These environments include various types of contingencies such as
      (i) Maintenance (M): Scheduled events that the agent is
192
      aware of (included in the state). During maintenance, a
193
      line is disconnected for a specified period and cannot be
194   reconnected until maintenance is complete. (ii) Opponent
195   (O): Unforeseen events (e.g., weather conditions) that cause
196   a random line to disconnect (Omnes et al., 2021). The agent
197   does not know about these events in advance and must react
198   in real-time, using topological or re-dispatching/curtailment
199   actions. Once a line is disconnected, it enters a “cooldown”
200   state, during which it cannot be reconnected for several steps.
201   Environments may also include storage units (batteries (B)) Figure 2: IEEE 14-bus sample grid.
202
      that can act as both generators and loads. Batteries can store
      a given amount of energy, which can be discharged as needed.
203
204   Action spaces. For each base environment, we consider two types of tasks based on their action
205   spaces, resulting in a total of 39 tasks.
206   (i) Topology (T): Agents can take discrete actions related to topological changes. These include
207   disconnecting or reconnecting a line, or changing the bus to which an electrical component (load,
208   generator, battery, or transmission line) is connected in a substation. These actions are virtually free
209   since they only involve remotely activating a switch. However, they represent a significant challenge
210   for grid operations, as the number of discrete actions scales exponentially with the number of elements
211   connected to the substation. Human operators currently modify the grid topology manually based on
212   historical behaviors; there is no tractable approach to obtain optimal topology optimization solutions
213   (at scale) as of yet. As analyzed by (Chauhan et al., 2023), the number of topological re-configurations
214
      N for a double bus substation comprising of Nlines lines, Ng generators, Nl loads, is:
215
                                      N = 2Nlines +Ng +Nl −1 − 2Ng +Nl − 1.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      For instance, substation #5 of the bus14 grid (Figure 2) has 2 generators, 1 load, and 4 lines (7
217   elements), resulting in 55 possible actions. In more complex tasks like bus36 and bus118, a single
218   substation can have over 65,000 possible topologies.
219
220
      Given the large discrete action space, we create different versions of the topology environments
      (“difficulty levels”), in which different numbers of topology actions are available to the agent.2 We
221
      selected the action spaces for these difficulty levels through extensive simulations (48 hours on the
222
      computer cluster detailed in Section 4) in which we ranked the full set of discrete actions based on
223   their survival rate for the grid. The survival rate represents how long the grid operates in normal
224   conditions over an episode—the normalized number of steps for which an action does not cause a
225   grid collapse (i.e., because the total demand is not satisfied or parts of the grid become disconnected).
226   Specifically, we uniformly sampled actions from the topological space, and counted the number of
227   times these actions did not cause a grid collapse over the simulation. After ranking the actions by
228   survival rate, we take the first Nactions from the ordered action space, where Nactions differs at each
229   difficulty level. Each increasing level of difficulty thus features a higher-dimensional discrete action
230   space. Appendix C summarizes the difficulty levels with the corresponding total number of actions.
231
      Considering these levels, RL2Grid has a total of 32 topology-based environments. To motivate our
      action ranking method, we also visually analyze the resultant action spaces in Appendix C.1.
232
233   (ii) Redispatching and curtailment (R): Agents can take continuous actions related to costly dispatch-
234   ing changes. Costs arise from altering the planned generation schedule of power plants, increased
235   fuel costs, and financial compensation for renewable energy producers, to name a few examples.
236   Redispatching actions apply to fossil fuel-based generators, while curtailment actions apply to renew-
237
      able energy-based generators. Batteries, if present, are also considered generators and add continuous
      actions for charging/discharging operations. This action space is relatively tractable for RL algorithms
238
      since it involves one continuous action per generator (i.e., N = Ng ). Thus, we present a total of
239
      7 continuous action-based training environments (one per base environment, in which all possible
240   redispatching and curtailment actions are available to the agent).
241
242   State space. Agents have access to the state of the power grid at each time step. The state includes
243
      common grid features such as production at each generator, load demands, status, capacity, and
      cooldown of transmission lines, as well as the current step. Additional features are provided based on
244
      the environment’s characteristics (e.g., maintenance, opponent events, batteries) and the action space.
245
      For example, in the topological case, the state includes the topological vector, the connection status
246   of lines, overflow status, and substation cooldowns. In the continuous case, the state includes target
247   and actual dispatches, curtailment, and generator ramping limits. An exhaustive list and description
248   of the features that comprise the state is discussed in Appendix D.
249
      Reward. The reward function is designed to encourage the agent to keep the grid operational for as
250
      long as possible while minimizing: (i) the capacity of the transmission lines (i.e., how much they
251
      are used), (ii) changes to the topology (only for the topology action space); (iii) costs related to
252   re-dispatching operations. Appendix C.2 provides an exhaustive description of this reward function.

      3.2    RL2G RID BASELINES
255
256   We assess the performance of a number of RL methods on the above tasks. In particular, we select a
257   set of methods that are commonly used in the RL literature and serve as building blocks for more
258   complex algorithms. These methods are discussed in more further depth in Appendix B.
259
      DQN (Mnih et al., 2013) approximates the Q-function, using an ϵ-greedy policy at training time.
260   Due to its value-based nature, a DQN agent can only consider discrete (topological) actions.
261
262   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) directly approximates a policy by learning its parameters using a
263
      computationally tractable clipped objective. By learning different probability distributions, a PPO
      agent can deal with continuous (re-dispatching) and discrete (topological) actions.
264
265   SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) uses different networks to learn a policy and two value functions that
266   mitigate positive bias in value estimates. An SAC agent can deal with the same action types as PPO.

      3.3   H EURISTIC - GUIDED RL
286
287   Given the complexity of the topological actions, we introduce two baseline heuristics to assist RL
288   agents’ operations. These heuristic-guided approaches have been employed in various forms in
289   previous work (Donnot, 2020; Marot et al., 2020b), but there is no widely adopted solution that:
290   (i) can be easily used by RL practitioners, and (ii) has been benchmarked across different tasks to
291   establish a standard for comparison and evaluation.
292   We refer to the first heuristic as “idle,” as it does not perform any operation on the grid when
293   line capacities are below the safety threshold of 95%. The second heuristic is a “recovery policy,”
294   (Figure G.3) as it restores the grid’s original topology or performs idle actions when line capacities are
295   below the safety threshold of 95%. In more detail, when line capacities exceed the safety threshold,
296   the RL agent selects and executes an action based on the current state to bring the grid back to normal
297   operation. When the grid operates normally (i.e., all line flows are under the safety threshold), the
298   recovery policy takes over. If the grid is in its original topology, the heuristic performs an idle action
299   to proceed with the simulation without changes. Otherwise, the heuristic calculates the actions needed
300
      to revert each substation to its original configuration. Considering realistic operational limits (i.e.,
      change at most one substation per step), the heuristic first recovers the substation that is most different
301
      from its original configuration.
302
303   Importantly, the recovery actions do not disrupt RL agent training; instead, they emulate typical
304   human operator behavior. Under normal conditions, system operators prefer to maintain the starting
305   topology. Therefore, it is challenging for an RL agent to learn to restore the original topology in
306
      high-dimensional action spaces. For this reason, we anticipate that RL baselines augmented with the
      recovery policy will drastically improve performance and sample efficiency.

388   7     TACKLING THE C HALLENGES OF P OWER G RIDS WITH RL
389
390   Applying RL in power grids presents numerous open problems, each offering significant opportunities
391   for advancing both grid operations and RL methodologies (Marot et al., 2022b). While we address a
392   subset of these challenges via our benchmark, there remains ample room for future work.
393
394   7.1   RL M ETHODOLOGIES OF I MPORTANCE FOR P OWER G RIDS
395
396   Different RL techniques have the potential to be beneficial in addressing open problems in power
397   grids. However, there are also potential risks – e.g., with respect to safety, reliability, and robustness –
398
      that are important to address. In the following, we summarize interesting avenues for future research
      to both realize the potential of RL in power grid operations and mitigate its risks.
399
400   Safe RL. Safety is paramount in power grid operations. Safe RL methods aim to ensure that learning
401   and control policies adhere to strict safety constraints, preventing actions that could lead to blackouts
402   or equipment damage (Donnot, 2020). Ensuring safety while optimizing performance is a critical area
403   of research (Garcıa & Fernández, 2015). In particular, incorporating Constrained Markov Decision
404
      Process (C-MDP) representations, which explicitly handle constraints, can be particularly beneficial
      for ensuring that solutions adhere to physical and operational limits (Liu et al., 2021).
405
406   Human-in-the-loop. Effective grid management often requires human expertise and intervention.
407   Incorporating human supervision, interaction, and feedback into RL systems allows for a synergistic
408   approach where human operators and AI systems work together to optimize grid operations (Marot
409   et al., 2022b). This collaboration can enhance decision-making and build trust in AI-driven solutions.
410   Hierarchical control and multi-agent learning. Power grids operate across multiple hierarchical
411   levels, from individual substations to entire regions. Effective coordination within and across these
412   levels is crucial for maintaining efficient and reliable grid operations. Hierarchical RL methods can be
413   developed to manage these multi-level control tasks, in a way that addresses the scale and complexity
414   inherent in grid operations (Pateria et al., 2021). Another promising direction is the use of multi-agent
415   representations. Given the vast and distributed nature of power grids, scalability can be enhanced by
416
      dividing the grid into distinct areas or agents, each responsible for its own operations. Multi-agent
      RL (MARL) frameworks can enable these agents to learn and coordinate actions (Papoudakis et al.,
417
      2021), to improve overall grid performance while managing local contingencies more effectively.
418
419   Robust RL. The integration of renewable energy sources introduces significant variability and
420   uncertainty into power grids, leading to non-stationary environments. RL algorithms need to adapt to
421   these evolving dynamics to ensure stable and efficient grid operations despite fluctuating supply and
422
      demand profiles. Handling non-stationarity is thus a critical research direction (Moos et al., 2022).
423   Model-based RL. Model-based RL methods leverage models of the grid dynamics to improve
424   learning efficiency and policy performance. These methods can provide more accurate predictions
425   and better generalize across different scenarios, leading to faster and more robust solutions (Luo et al.,
426   2022). Additionally, the AlphaZero algorithm, which combines tree search with deep learning, has
427   shown remarkable success in games like chess and Go and could offer new strategies for handling
428
      complex, sequential decision-making tasks with high-dimensional spaces (Liu et al., 2023).
429   Better representations. Improving model representations for RL in power grids can also lead to more
430   efficient learning and better policy performance. Leveraging Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) offers
431   a potential avenue for advancement. Power grids can be naturally represented as graphs, with nodes
      representing buses and edges representing transmission lines. GNNs can effectively model these


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      structures, capturing the spatial and topological dependencies inherent in power grids. Integrating
433   GNNs with RL algorithms can enhance the representation and learning of grid dynamics.
434
435   Non-RL approaches. While RL holds great promise, it is also essential to consider non-RL ap-
436
      proaches such as optimization solvers, which are relevant particularly for problems with well-defined
      optimization objectives and constraints. In addition, exploring hybrid methods that combine RL with
437
      traditional optimization techniques can yield powerful tools for complex grid management tasks.
438
439
      7.2   I MPROVING R EALISM OF P OWER G RID E NVIRONMENTS
440
441   While RL2Grid aims to promote initial advancements in RL methodologies of relevance to power
442   grids, it is important to acknowledge that this is only a first step. Notably, building on these
443   advancements to develop “last-mile” deployed solutions will require further improvements in the
444   realism of power grid environments. We highlight several important directions in this regard.
445
      Scalability. Realistic power systems akin to those managed by operators like RTE France, National
446
      Grid ESO, and 50Hertz may capture hundreds to thousands of buses. To ensure that RL solutions are
447   applicable to real-world scenarios, improving the size and scale of grid environments is essential.
448
449   Real data. Grid2Op (and thus, RL2Grid) relies on realistic but synthetic data, which already provide
450
      significant challenges for RL. After scaling up RL to deal with the challenges provided by RL2Grid,
      future environments should address current privacy issues and publicly release real grid data to design
451
      to bridge the gap with real power grid operations.
452
453   N-1 security. In real operations, grid operators must ensure the system can withstand failure of any
454   single component. Rather than modeling failures via random opponents, environments should handle
455   this exhaustively and/or through adversarial agents tailored specifically to the method being tested.
456   Topology vs re-dispatch. Different grid operators handle the relationship between re-dispatch
457   and topology optimization differently. Future benchmarks should reflect this heterogeneity in how
458   different power grids are managed. Moreover, Grid2Op’s current approach of disconnecting lines
459   after unaddressed overloads does not fully capture real-world practices, where operators attempt to
460   prevent overheating at all costs. Incorporating more realistic consequences for unaddressed overloads,
461   such as system costs, can improve the fidelity of benchmarks. Additionally, grid operators cannot
462
      switch every element to every busbar, and there are limits on the number of connected components
      per substation. Reflecting these constraints can lead to more practical and applicable RL solutions.
463
      Storage assets also play an increasingly important role in grid operations. Future benchmarks should
464
      accurately model storage and clarify the extent of control grid operators have over these assets.
465
466   Phase-shift transformers. Phase-shift transformers, currently modeled as integer variables in the
467   action space, should be represented more accurately to reflect their operational impact. Maintenance
468
      activities also vary significantly, with Type A involving physical presence at the site and Type B
      allowing remote interventions. Differentiating these types of maintenance activities in benchmarks
469
      can provide a more accurate representation of real-world constraints.

472   8     C ONCLUSIONS
473
474   Power grids are essential in combating climate change, requiring a transition to low-carbon energy and
475   enhanced resilience against climate-induced extremes. The integration of variable renewable energy
476   sources introduces complexities and uncertainties in grid operations, posing significant challenges for
477
      human operators and traditional power system solvers. Our work aims to foster progress towards
      these challenges by introducing RL2Grid, a benchmark designed to bridge the gap between current
478
      grid management practices and methodological research in RL. RL2Grid provides a standardized
479
      interface for power grid environments, featuring common rewards, state spaces, and action spaces
480   across a pre-designed set of diverse and complex grid tasks in order to provide a common ground for
481   monitoring and promoting progress. We perform a comprehensive evaluation of the performance of
482   DQN, PPO, SAC, and TD3 on RL2Grid tasks, including versions augmented with domain-informed
483   heuristics aimed at improving performance and sample efficiency, and find that there is still significant
484   room for improvement in the performance of these methods. By offering a standardized platform
485   for RL research in the context of power grids, RL2Grid aims to accelerate algorithmic innovation
      towards improving power grid operations amidst the evolving challenges posed by climate change.

      A    R ELATIONSHIP OF RL2G RID TO L2RPN TASKS AND SOLUTIONS
595
596
597   In this section, we clarify the relationship of the tasks presented within RL2Grid, as well as the
598   baseline methods evaluated, to the tasks and solutions presented within the Learning to Run a Power
599
      Network (L2RPN) competition series.
600   Tasks. RL2Grid employs all the main Grid2Op “base environments” (which are likewise employed
601   in L2RPN). However, the solutions developed for L2RPN relied on different customized components.
602   Every competition relied on different time series, making effective comparisons far from trivial. For
603   these reasons, on top of the standardization proposed in our work, we have made some underlying
604   changes to the base environments to better reflect the current and future challenges of RL research.
605
      Examples include (i) episodes with longer horizons (i.e., an RL2Grid episode models a month of grid
      operations, ∼8000 steps, compared to weekly episodes of most prior work); (ii) making the tasks as
606
      uniform as possible (i.e., by integrating curtailment operations in all Grid2Op tasks); (iii) enabling
607
      simulation steps inside the Gymnasium interface (a feature added in our code revision, which is
608   not currently available in Grid2Op). These decisions were driven by our goal of ensuring that our
609   benchmark is accessible, standardized, and provides a clear starting point for researchers who may
610   not be familiar with the nuances of these competitions and power grids.
611
      Baselines. Due to the different choices of input features and action spaces considered by different
612
      methods submitted to the L2RPN challenges, it was not possible to directly benchmark these
613
      specific methods on the RL2Grid tasks. However, the baselines chosen are representative of the
614   methods submitted to past L2RPN competitions, in addition to representing commonly-used methods
615   within the RL community as a whole. In particular, within the L2RPN submissions, a common
616   approach was to incorporate heuristics. These heuristics varied significantly between methods and
617   pushed us to design one that mimicked human operations in real grid operations. We developed
618   this heuristic in collaboration with power system operators who have contributed to our work,
619   incorporating fundamental insights from previous solutions while keeping the focus on standardization
620   and benchmarking.

622
623   B    RL BASELINES
624
625   In this section, we briefly introduce the baseline RL algorithms employed in our evaluation, referring
626   to the original papers for exhaustive details about these methods (Mnih et al., 2013; Schulman et al.,
627   2017; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Fujimoto et al., 2018).
628
      DQN (Mnih et al., 2013). A DQN agent uses a neural network to approximate the action value
629
      function Q by taking as input the state of the environment and outputting Q-values for every possible
630   action. During training, the agent uses an ϵ-greedy policy to select random actions or follow the
631   greedy policy on these Q-values, according to a linearly decaying probability ϵ. The Q network is
632   thus updated to minimize the difference between predicted Q-values and a target derived from actual
633   rewards and future Q-values. To deal with overestimation, we use Double-DQN (van Hasselt et al.,
634   2016) and decouple action selection from action evaluation using a target Q network. Due to its
635   value-based nature, a DQN agent can only consider discrete (topological) actions.
636   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). A PPO agent uses its neural network to directly approximate a policy.
637   The agent learns the policy parameters by simplifying the TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015) algorithm,
638   using a computationally tractable clipped objective. This clipping mechanism prevents large changes
639   to the policy that could destabilize the training. At a high level, such a surrogate objective balances
640   policy improvement and limits the divergence between policy updates. To drive the policy training,
641   PPO also learns an advantage function to determine how much better (or worse) taking an action is
642   compared to the expected value. By employing different probability distributions as a policy, a PPO
643   agent can deal with both continuous (re-dispatching) and discrete (topological) actions.
644   SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018). Similarly to PPO, a SAC agent learns different networks to maintain
645   a policy and two value functions that mitigate positive bias in value estimates. Overall, the agent
646   maximizes both the expected return and the entropy of the policy. The entropy term encourages ex-
647   ploration by promoting stochastic policies, which helps prevent premature convergence to suboptimal
      policies. In terms of actions, the SAC agent can deal with the same action types as PPO.


                                                       12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018). A TD3 agent learns multiple networks similarly to SAC. However,
649   unlike the stochastic policies learned by PPO and SAC, TD3 learns a deterministic policy and can
650   only deal with continuous actions. To encourage exploration, the agent does not maximize the entropy
651   of the policy but adds noise to the output of the policy network.
