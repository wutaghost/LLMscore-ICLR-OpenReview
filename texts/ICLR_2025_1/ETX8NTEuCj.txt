009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Score-based diffusion models have achieved remarkable progress in various do-
012            mains with an ability to generate new data samples that do not exist in the training
013            set. In this paper, we examine a hypothesis that this phenomenon manifests an inter-
014            polation effect caused by a smoothing of the empirical score function. Focusing on
015            settings where the training set lies in a one-dimensional linear subspace, we take a
016            distribution-agnostic perspective and study the interplay between score smoothing
017            and the denoising dynamics with mathematically solvable models. We demonstrate
018            how score smoothing can lead to the generation of samples that interpolate among
019
               the training data within the subspace while avoiding a full memorization of the
               training set.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   In the past years, score-based diffusion models have emerged as an important pillar of generative
026   modeling in a variety of domains from image and video generation to drug discovery (Sohl-Dickstein
027
      et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Ramesh et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2024). For
      instance, after being trained on datasets of actual images or existing molecular configurations, such
028
      models are capable of transform noise samples into high-quality images or chemically-plausible
029
      molecules. Importantly, the generated samples do not belong to the original training set, indicating an
030   exciting potential of such models to generalize beyond the training data and, in a sense, be creative.
031
032   The creativity of score-based diffusion models has not been fully understood from a theoretical point
033
      of view. At the core of these models is the training of neural networks (NNs) to fit a series of target
      functions – called the empirical score functions (ESF) – which will be used to drive the denoising
034
      process. The precise form of these functions are determined by the training set and can be computed
035
      exactly in principle (though inefficient in practice). However, when equipped with the exact precise
036   ESF instead of the approximate version learned by NNs, the model will end up generate data points
037   that already exist in the training set (Yi et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), a phenomenon often called
038   memorization. This suggests that, for the models to generalize fresh samples beyond the training
039   set, it is crucial to have certain regularizations on the score function (perhaps implicitly through NN
040   training) that prevent the ESF to be learned exactly. In particular, Scarvelis et al. (2023) hypothesized
041   that smoothing the ESF results in a tendency of the generated samples to interpolate among training
042   data points, but it remains unclear mathematically how score smoothing leads to interpolation.
043   Meanwhile, a different yet related phenomenon of generative models is hallucination, in which
044   they generate samples that are qualitatively (and often undesirably) different from the training set.
045   Fundamentally, both generalization and hallucination concern scenarios where models generate
046   samples that are distinct from the original training set, with their difference being whether the the new
047   samples lie within or out of the support of the underlying target distribution. Hence, the hallucination
048   phenomenon is also related to the tendency of diffusion models to interpolate among or extrapolate
049   from the training set. Indeed, the recent work of Aithal et al. (2024) proposed “mode interpolation” as
050
      the essence of hallucination and demonstrates its empirical presence when the NN learns a smoother
      version of the ESF, but its mathematical mechanism remains unclear as well.
051
052   In this work, we will illustrate mathematically how score smoothing in diffusion models could result
053   in data interpolation and subspace recovery. Focusing on examples where the training data belong to
      a one-dimensional subspace, we consider a simple type of local smoothing on the ESF and derive


                                                         1
054
      that it can lead the denoising dynamics to recover a non-singular density that is supported on the
055   one-dimensional subspace, thus recovering the subspace from training data.

      while in the latter case the terminal distribution is still spread over all d dimensions and equivalent to
337
      adding Gaussian noise to the training data points. In either case, the terminal distribution has infinite
338
      KL-divergence from any smooth density supported on the one-dimensional subspace.

      of smoothing in higher dimensions that is invariant to coordinate rotations. We discuss one such
343

428   5    D ISCUSSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
429
430   The main motivation of the work is not to propose new designs for diffusion models (an example in
431   this regard is Scarvelis et al. 2023) but to understand why existing ones can produce new samples
      different from the training set. We focus on one hypothesis, that the ability comes from a smoothing


                                                          8
432
      of the score function when learned by NNs, and have provided mathematical and empirical evidence
433   that score smoothing is indeed sufficient to induce such an ability through an interpolation effect on
434   the outcome of the denoising dynamics. At the core of this phenomenon are two properties of the
435   smoothed ESF: reducing the speed of convergence towards training data along the tangential direction
436   (to avoid memorization) while preserving it along the normal direction (to ensure a convergence
437   onto the subspace). The decomposition of the score function into tangential and normal components
438   when data belong to subspaces was also studied by Chen et al. (2023b); Wang & Vastola (2023b). To
439   our knowledge, our work is novel in showing that the distinct effect of score smoothing on the two
440   components leads to data interpolation along the subspace.
441
442   Limitations. To further validate of this hypothesis, another important question is whether (and
443
      how) score smoothing is achieved in practice, which is not the focus of the current work. There has
      been empirical evidence that NNs indeed tend to learn smoother versions of the ESF (Aithal et al.,
444
      2024), though theoretical understandings are still lacking and, admittedly, there is no justification as
445
      to why the smoothing effect caused by NN training should be similar to the type of local smoothing
446   studied in this paper. We nevertheless hope that our analysis can help to elucidate the phenomenon
447   and provide intuitions for future work along this line. Another limitation of the present work is the
448   vastly simplified setup compared to real-world scenarios, which enables us to obtain insights through
449   mathematically solvable models. It would be very interesting to extend our theory to cases where
450   training data belong to multi-dimensional and nonlinear manifolds as well as to more general types of
451   diffusion models (De Bortoli et al., 2021; Albergo et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
452

      6    R ELATED WORKS
454
455   Generalization vs memorization in diffusion models. Several works have studied empirically the
456   generalization vs memorization behaviors in diffusion generative models, observing the transition
457   from the former to the latter when the model capacity increases relatively to the training set size (Gu
458   et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie et al., 2024). Li et al. (2024) showed that
459   learning the ESF well does not result in generalization, which stresses the discrepancy between it
460   and the score functions of the true underlying density. When provided with an oracle estimator for
461   the latter, it has indeed been proved that diffusion models can produce accurate distributions (Song
462   et al., 2021a; Lee et al., 2022; De Bortoli, 2022; Chen et al., 2023a;c; Benton et al., 2024; Huang
463
      et al., 2024), but the question remains as to how to estimate the true score functions well. When
      assuming that the ground truth density or its score function belongs to certain restricted classes (e.g,
464
      densities being Gaussian mixtures, or sub-Gaussian, or belonging to certain smoothness classes; their
465
      score functions being Lipschitz or belonging to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces), prior works have
466   constructed score estimators with guaranteed sample complexity and sometimes statistical optimality
467   (Block et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Wibisono et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024;
468   Gatmiry et al., 2024). Due to their assumptions, these results do not typically apply to the scenario
469   considered in Section 3.4 where the data are supported on low-dimensional sub-manifolds. A more
470   fundamental distinction is that, whereas these results concern the estimation of densities from i.i.d.
471   samples, our work does not assume underlying densities and make the finite training set a starting
472   point of analysis. Both perspectives have advantages: the former setting is suitable for deriving
473   sample complexity bounds (but needs assumptions on the underlying distribution), whereas the latter
474
      allows us to exploit the local geometry and derive analytical solutions of the denoising dynamics. In
      future works, it will be interesting to also extend our analysis to the density estimation perspective.
475
476
477
      Diffusion models and the manifold hypothesis. An influential hypothesis in machine learning
      is that high-dimensional real-world data often lie in low-dimensional sub-manifolds (Tenenbaum
478
      et al., 2000; Fefferman et al., 2016), and it has been argued that diffusion models are able to estimate
479
      their intrinsic dimensions (Stanczuk et al., 2024) or learn manifold features in order of descending
480   variance (Wang & Vastola, 2023a;b). Assuming conditions on the score estimator, Pidstrigach (2022);
481   De Bortoli (2022) studied the convergence of diffusion models in the case of manifold data. Oko
482   et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023b) proved sample complexity guarantees for score estimation using
483   certain NN models under the manifold hypothesis. Nonetheless, the error bound by Oko et al. (2023)
484   is in Wasserstein distance and hence less informative about the memorization phenomenon than
485   KL divergence (e.g., unlike Corollary 5, a finite Wasserstein distance bound does not exclude the
      distribution produced by the model from being fully singular). Meanwhile, Chen et al. (2023b)


                                                          9
486
      focused on a regime where the denoising dynamics is stopped at a fixed tmin > 0 while n → ∞,
487   and hence unlike Corollary 5, their result does not guarantee the recovery of a density restricted to
488   the subspace with finite training data. The intricate interplay between n and t and their effect on
489   memorization have been nicely studied by Biroli et al. (2024) in the case of Gaussian mixture data.
490
491   Score smoothing. Aithal et al. (2024) showed empirically that NNs tend to learn smoother versions
492   of the ESF and argued that this leads to a mode interpolation effect that underlies model hallucination.
493   Scarvelis et al. (2023) designed alternative closed-form diffusion models by smoothing the ESF,
494   although the theoretical analysis therein is limited to showing that their smoothed score function is
495   directed towards certain barycenters of the training data. Inspired by their work, in this paper we
496   further analyze mathematically the effect of score smoothing on the denoising dynamics.
497   In summary, our work shows mathematically how smoothing the ESF leads the denoising dynamics
498   to produce distributions that interpolate among the training data on their subspace, elucidating a
499   mechanism of potential relevance to both the generalization ability and the hallucination phenomenon
500   of score-based diffusion models.
