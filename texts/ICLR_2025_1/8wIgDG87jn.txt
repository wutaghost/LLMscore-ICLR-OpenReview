011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Model (LLM) based multi-agent systems (MAS) have shown
               promise in tackling complex tasks, but often rely on predeﬁned roles and central-
014
               ized coordination, limiting their adaptability to evolving challenges. This paper
015
               introduces M ORPH AGENT, a novel framework for decentralized multi-agent col-
016            laboration that enables agents to dynamically evolve their roles and capabilities.
017            Our approach employs self-evolving agent proﬁles, optimized through three key
018            metrics, guiding agents in reﬁning their individual expertise while maintaining
019            complementary team dynamics. M ORPH AGENT implements a two-phase process:
020            a warm-up phase for initial proﬁle optimization, followed by a task execution
021            phase where agents continuously adapt their roles based on task feedback. Our
022
               experimental results show that M ORPH AGENT outperforms traditional static-role
               MAS in terms of task performance and adaptability to changing requirements,
023
               paving the way for more robust and versatile multi-agent collaborative systems.

028
      The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
      2023b) has ushered in a new era of artiﬁcial intelligence, enabling the creation of sophisticated AI
029
      agents capable of tackling complex tasks across various domains (Nakajima, 2023; Torantulino,
030   2023). As these AI systems become more intricate, there is a growing need for effective collabora-
031   tion mechanisms that allow multiple agents to work together. This collaborative approach, known as
032   Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) (Han et al., 2024), has shown great promise in addressing challenges
033   that are too complex or diverse for single-agent systems (Hong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023).
034
      While existing MAS implementations have shown promising results, they often rely on predeﬁned
035   roles (Li et al., 2023), centralized coordination (Guo et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024), or rigid orga-
036   nizational structures (Wang et al., 2024b; Hong et al., 2024). These approaches limit cooperative
037   resilience within MAS (Chacon-Chamorro et al., 2024), which focuses on robustness and adapt-
038   ability in dynamic, unpredictable environments. Figure 1 presents two examples to illustrate the
039   real-world challenges with details elaborated below:
040   Example 1.1 (Domain shift). Domain shift refers to a change in the characteristics or requirements
041   of a task as it progresses through different phases or contexts, presenting new challenges and requir-
042   ing different skill sets. For instance, a scientiﬁc research project could begin with literature review,
043   move to experiment design, and conclude with result analysis and paper writing. These transitions
044   demand a ﬂexible and adaptive multi-agent system that can seamlessly adjust its collaborative strate-
      gies and agent roles as the task progresses.
045
046   Example 1.2 (Node Failure). In real-world applications, the reliance on centralized coordination in
047   many existing MAS approaches (Chen et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024) introduces a potential single
      point of failure. Consequently, the single point of failure can lead to cascading failures in MAS,
048
      where the entire system collapses if the central coordinator becomes unavailable. This vulnerability
049
      highlights the need for more robust, decentralized approaches to multi-agent collaboration (Chacon-
050   Chamorro et al., 2024).
051
052   In this paper, we address these above challenges through a fully decentralized approach, where
053   agents autonomously adapt their roles and strategies. However, a naive implementation of fully
      decentralized MAS presents inefﬁciencies for role assignment since the agents require many interac-

110   LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam
111   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a) has led to LLM-based autonomous agents capable of tackling
112   complex tasks, like BabyAGI (Nakajima, 2023) and AutoGPT (Torantulino, 2023). However, sin-
113   gle LLM agents often struggle with cooperative work, such as software engineering (Jimenez et al.,
114   2024). To address these limitations, recent study have proposed LLM-based multi-agent systems
115   (MAS) (Han et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023), where multiple AI agents collaborate on complex
116
      tasks. Current approaches often rely on predeﬁned roles, centralized coordination, or rigid orga-
      nizational structures. CAMEL (Li et al., 2023) and ChatEval (Chan et al., 2023) employ agents
117
      with predened roles through role-playing, but struggle to adapt to tasks requiring unforeseen skills.
118   MegaAgent (Wang et al., 2024b) introduces autonomous task splitting with centralized coordination,
119   but this approach can create bottlenecks in large-scale systems and be damaged by single points of
120   failure in real-world environments.. MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024) implements human workﬂow in
121   rigid organizational structures, showing improvements in code-generation but lacking generalization
122   to other domains. Our work addresses these limitations by initializing agents homogeneously with-
123   out predeﬁned roles or structures, allowing them to naturally develop cooperation and specialization
124
      through interaction.
125
126   Organization Optimization for MAS Recent research in LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems
127   (MAS) has focused on optimizing organizational structures (Guo et al., 2024; Zhuge et al., 2024)
128   and enhancing agent performance (Zhang et al., 2024) to reduce communication costs and increase
129   team efciency. Approaches like AgentVerse (Chen et al., 2024), Criticize-Reﬂect (Guo et al., 2024)
130
      and MegaAgent (Wang et al., 2024b) rely on centralized mechanisms, where a single role or a subset
      of agents monitor and evaluate the system’s overall trajectory. While effective in certain scenarios,
131
      these centralized methods may face scalability issues and potential bottlenecks in large-scale MAS.
132   Our research proposes a decentralized approach, leveraging LLM-based agents’ self-reﬂection
133   capabilities (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Renze & Guven, 2024). Agents dynamically
134   adjust their responsibilities based on context, enabling better scalability and mitigating context
135   overload risks.
136
137
      Standard Operating Procedure based MAS Another signicant strand of research has explored
138   more structured and controlled methodologies in LLM-based multi-agent systems. Standard Operat-
139   ing Procedure (SOP) based approaches like AgentCoder (Huang et al., 2023) and MetaGPT (Hong
140   et al., 2024) have shown performance gains through standardized pipelines. GPTSwarm (Zhuge
141   et al., 2024) extends this by conceptualizing agents as subnets of action nodes. While effective for
142   speciﬁc tasks, these approaches lack ﬂexibility in dynamic environments. Our framework com-
143   bines multi-agent collaboration with autonomous planning capabilities of advanced LLM-based
144
      agents (Huang et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Instead of rigid SOPs, it enables
      dynamic development of collaborative strategies and efﬁcient role adaptation, enhancing overall
145
      performance and robustness.

157   3.1   S YSTEM OVERVIEW
158
159   Complex tasks require adaptive problem-solving approaches that go beyond traditional centralized
160   systems, which are vulnerable to bottlenecks and failure points. In centralized systems, a single coor-
161   dinator is responsible for breaking down tasks and assigning them to individual agents. However, if
      the coordinator fails or communication is disrupted, the entire system risks collapsing. Additionally,

162
      ﬁxed roles within such systems prevent agents from adjusting to shifting task requirements, resulting
163   in inefﬁciencies.
164
165   To develop a novel decentralized system, where multiple autonomous agents collaborate on complex
      tasks without predeﬁned roles or centralized coordination, we introduce a fully decentralized multi-
166
      agent system, M ORPH AGENT comprising three key components—namely, (i) Autonomous Agent,
167
      (ii) Auxiliary Agent, and (iii) Environment—each playing a crucial role in enabling autonomous,
168   decentralized collaboration, as detailed in the following subsections.
169
170   (i) Autonomous Agent. The autonomous agent is built upon a LLM, e.g., GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
171   2023), which provides it with advanced reasoning and language understanding capabilities. It is pri-
172   marily composed of a dynamic proﬁle and utilizes the ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2022) combined
173   with Reﬂexion (Shinn et al., 2023) to guide its behavior. The dynamic proﬁle, which deﬁnes each
174   agents roles and responsibilities, is central to how agents update and adapt, and will be discussed in
175   detail in Section 3.2. Here, we focus on the operational mechanics of the agent.
176   Each agent follows the O BSERVE-T HINK-ACT cycle, where it ﬁrst gathers information from the
177   environment and other agents in the O BSERVE phase. In the T HINK phase, it uses its reasoning
178   abilities to process the information and plan its next move as presented in Algorithm 1’s Line 13. Fi-
179   nally, in the ACT phase, the agent decides to either EXECUTE or SKIP a task based on its current
180   capabilities and the tasks requirements. After executing a task, the agent reﬂects on the outcomes,
181
      incorporating feedback into its memory for future decisions. This process is detailed in Algo-
      rithm 1’s Line 18. The continuous cycle enables agents to adapt over time, reﬁne their strategies
182
      based on feedback, and collaborate more effectively to address the speciﬁc problem at hand.
183
184
      (ii) Auxiliary Agent. The auxiliary agent serves as a middleware component that facilitates inter-
185   action between autonomous agents and the environment, without directly contributing to problem-
186   solving. This design allows for efﬁcient management of agent interactions and environmental feed-
187   back while maintaining the decentralized nature of our system. By handling technical details, the
188   auxiliary agent allows the autonomous agents to focus on high-level problem-solving strategies.
189
      The main function of the auxiliary agent is to translate agent decisions into environmental operations
190   and relays feedback from the environment back to the agents. For example, when an agent decides
191   to execute Python code, the auxiliary agent runs the code in the environment, captures the output or
192   errors from the environmental feedback, and provides this feedback to the multi-agent system. Thus
193   the auxiliary agent ensures that autonomous agents can concentrate on strategic problem-solving
194   while it manages execution and feedback processes.
195
196   (iii) Environment. The environment receives the instructions from users, processes them into mes-
197   sages, and the auxiliary agent is responsible for collecting and forwarding these messages within the
198
      environment to autonomous agents. Additionally, the environment is equipped with a mechanism
      to evaluate agent proﬁles, providing feedback that agents can use to reﬁne and adjust their roles
199
      and strategies. This process ensures that agents continuously improve their task alignment and spe-
200   cialization, leading to more efﬁcient collaboration and better overall system performance as task
201   conditions evolve.

203   3.2   DYNAMIC P ROFILE -BASED C OLLABORATION M ECHANISM
204
205   Traditional multi-agent systems (MAS) (Hong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023) with predeﬁned roles
206   and static structures struggle to adapt to domain shift, leading to suboptimal performance when
207
      faced with unexpected changes or new challenges. To address this, we introduce a dynamic proﬁle
      mechanism, as prensented in Algorithm 1’s Line 4 and 22, enabling agents to continuously adjust
208
      their roles and skills based on interaction and task requirements. We begin with the concept of agent
209   proﬁles, followed by the key metrics used to evaluate and optimize these proﬁles.
210
211   Proﬁle. The concept of agent proﬁles encapsulates an agent’s evolving role, capabilities, and exper-
212   tise, serving as a dynamic representation of the agent’s identity within the multi-agent system (Wang
213   et al., 2024a). Proﬁles play a crucial role in MAS by facilitating effective task allocation, fostering
214   collaboration, and enabling adaptive behavior (Sun et al., 2024). Unlike static role assignments,
215   dynamic proﬁles allow agents to continuously reﬁne their capabilities, leading to more ﬂexible col-
      laboration and efﬁcient problem-solving.

357     iterations are completed.
358   • Task Execution Phase: Observation, Action Decision, and Proﬁle Updates. After proﬁle
359     optimization, the system moves to the task execution phase. In this phase, agents observe the
360
        environment and task state, make decisions based on their current proﬁles, and either execute or
        skip tasks. If an agent chooses to execute, the results are recorded and logged. This phase is also
361
        iterative, allowing proﬁles to be updated based on the execution outcomes and the current state of
362     the task. The agents continuously adapt to changing conditions and reﬁne their proﬁles, ensuring
363     that actions remain aligned with both individual and collaborative goals.

      4.3   ROBUSTNESS TO N ODE FAILURE
458
459   To evaluate the robustness of our decentralized approach compared to centralized methods, we con-
460   ducted experiments simulating potential node failures which we mentioned in Example 1.2. Specif-
461   ically, in each round of interaction, one agent in the multi-agent system (MAS) has a certain prob-
462   ability of failing to respond. We compared our method with AgentVerse (Chen et al., 2024), a
463   centralized approach that relies on a central evaluator. For both AgentVerse and our method, all
464
      agents, including the central evaluator in AgentVerse, have an independent probability of failure in
      each interaction. For this experiment, we used the gpt-4o-mini model and varied the failure
465
      probability of each agent from 0.3 to 0.7, simulating environments with different levels of risk.
466
467   As shown in Figure 4, our decentralized approach demonstrates superior robustness compared to
468   AgentVerse across all failure probabilities, providing the following insights:
469    • Fault Tolerance: Our method maintains relatively stable performance across different failure
470      probabilities. Even at a high failure probability of 0.7, our method maintains competitive perfor-
471      mance, with accuracies of 63.81%, 47.37%, and 53.00% for code generation, general reasoning,
472      and mathematical reasoning tasks respectively.
473    • Decentralized Architecture: Our method’s distributed nature allows it to continue functioning
         even when individual agents fail, unlike centralized approaches that may collapse entirely if the
474
         central node fails.
475
       • Adaptive Role Adjustment: The dynamic proﬁle updating and role differentiation in our method
476      enable the system to reassign tasks and responsibilities when certain agents fail, maintaining
477      overall system robustness.
478
      These insights underscore the importance of fully decentralized, adaptive approaches in creating
479
      robust multi-agent systems, particularly in high-risk environments or for critical applications where
480
      node failures are a signiﬁcant concern.

525   5   L IMITATION , F UTURE W ORK , AND C ONCLUSION
526
527   Limitation and Future Work. In this work, we have presented a novel decentralized multi-agent
528   system that leverages dynamic proﬁle-based collaboration to enhance problem-solving capabilities
529   in complex tasks. While our approach demonstrates promising results across various benchmarks,
530   there are some opportunities for future work. Our method utilizes continuously updating and
531
      evaluating agent proﬁles, which may incur computational overhead. Future work could explore
      efﬁcient decentralized mechanisms to reduce computational costs. Besides, future work should
532
      explore more efﬁcient, peer-to-peer communication strategies that maintain the beneﬁts of our
533   approach while reducing computational costs.
534
535   Conclusion. In this paper, we introduced M ORPH AGENT, a decentralized multi-agent system that
536   employs dynamic, proﬁle-based collaboration to improve problem-solving in complex tasks. By
537   incorporating proﬁle evaluation and optimization, we present a ﬂexible approach to role adaptation,
538   addressing the limitations of predeﬁned roles in traditional MAS and the vulnerability of centralized
539   systems to node failures. M ORPH AGENT offers a promising foundation for developing resilient,
      self-organizing multi-agent systems capable of responding to unforeseen challenges.

1136   LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam
1137   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a) has led to LLM-based autonomous agents capable of tack-
1138   ling complex tasks similar to humans, like BabyAGI (Nakajima, 2023) and AutoGPT (Torantulino,
1139   2023). However, single LLM agents often struggle with cooperative work, such as software engi-
1140   neering (Jimenez et al., 2024). To address these limitations, recent study have proposed LLM-based
1141   multi-agent systems (MAS) (Han et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023), where multiple AI agents collabo-
1142
       rate on complex tasks.
1143   However, current approaches often rely on predeﬁned roles, centralized coordination, or rigid organi-
1144   zational structures, which may limit their ﬂexibility and adaptability. For instance, CAMEL (Li et al.,
1145   2023) and ChatEval (Chan et al., 2023) employ agents with predeﬁned roles through role-playing to
1146
       effectively complete different tasks and achieve common goals. While this approach shows effective
       cooperation, it can struggle to adapt to tasks that require unforeseen skills. MegaAgent (Wang et al.,
1147
       2024b) introduces autonomous task splitting and execution in centralized coordination, demonstrat-
1148
       ing how multi-agent systems can outperform single agents by leveraging collective capabilities. Nev-
1149   ertheless, this centralized approach can create bottlenecks in large-scale systems and be damaged
1150   by single points of failure in real-world environments. Recent works like MetaGPT (Hong et al.,
1151   2024) introduce human workﬂow in rigid organizational structures, organizing agents in a manner
1152   similar to a software company showing signiﬁcant improvements in code-generation benchmarks
1153   but such rigid structures cannot generalize effectively to other domains.
1154   Our work addresses these limitations by focusing on a more general setting, where all agents are ini-
1155   tialized homogeneously without predeﬁned roles or organizational structures. This approach aims to
1156   examine how agents learn to cooperate and specialize over time through interaction and experience
1157   to tackle diverse and evolving challenges.
1158
1159   Organization Optimization for MAS Recent research in LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems
1160   (MAS) has focused on optimizing organizational structures (Guo et al., 2024; Zhuge et al., 2024)
1161   and enhancing agent performance (Zhang et al., 2024) to reduce communication costs and increase
1162   team efﬁciency. Approaches like AgentVerse (Chen et al., 2024), Criticize-Reﬂect (Guo et al., 2024)
1163
       and MegaAgent (Wang et al., 2024b) rely on centralized mechanisms, where a single role or a subset
       of agents monitor and evaluate the system’s overall trajectory. While effective in certain scenarios,
1164
       these centralized methods may face scalability issues and potential bottlenecks in large-scale MAS.
1165
1166   Our research proposes a paradigm shift towards a fully decentralized approach, leveraging the in-
1167   herent capabilities of LLM-based agents for self-reﬂection and self-correction (Madaan et al., 2023;
1168
       Shinn et al., 2023; Renze & Guven, 2024). In this decentralized framework, agents can dynamically
       adjust their responsibilities (proﬁle) based on the current context and their evolving understanding of
1169
       the task environment. As agents learn to specialize and collaborate without central coordination, the
1170   system can scale more effectively to handle increasingly complex tasks and larger agent populations,
1171   mitigating the risk of context overload for central coordinating agents.
1172
1173
       Standard Operating Procedure based MAS Another signiﬁcant strand of research has explored
1174   more structured and controlled methodologies in LLM-based multi-agent systems. Among these,
1175   Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) based approaches like AgentCoder (Huang et al., 2023) and
1176   MetaGPT (Hong et al., 2024), have demonstrated considerable performance gains. These works
1177   deﬁne a standardized pipeline for agents, which provides a determined framework for task execution
1178   and inter-agent communication. GPTSwarm (Zhuge et al., 2024) further extends this concept by
1179   conceptualizing each agent as a subnet composed of action nodes, framing agent collaboration as
1180
       a graph of action nodes. This approach enables efﬁcient task-solving for speciﬁc and well-deﬁned
       task by identifying the optimal action sequence for information ﬂow and task execution.
1181
1182   While SOP-based approaches provide an efﬁcient method for coordinating complex tasks by follow-
1183   ing predeﬁned procedures in speciﬁc scenarios, they lack ﬂexibility. When the established pipeline
1184   does not ﬁt the current task, the system is unable to adjust. Consequently, such rigid frameworks
       cannot adapt effectively to dynamic environments.
1185
1186   In our work, we propose a more ﬂexible framework that combines the strengths of multi-agent col-
1187   laboration with the autonomous planning capabilities of advanced agents. Instead of enforcing rigid
       SOPs, our framework dynamically develops and reﬁnes collaborative strategies using the inherent


                                                        22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       planning abilities of advanced LLM-based agents (Huang et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2023; Wang
1189   et al., 2023). Moreover, our approach fosters agents to efﬁciently and optimally adapt their roles to
1190   the evolving demands of the task, enhancing overall performance and robustness.
