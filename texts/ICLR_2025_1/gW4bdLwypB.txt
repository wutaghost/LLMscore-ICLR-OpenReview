009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The need for training multilingual multi-task automatic speech recognition (ASR)
012
               models is increasingly evident. However, a significant challenge arises from the
013
               conflicts among multiple objectives when using a single model. Multi-objective
014            optimization (MOO) can address this challenge by facilitating the optimization
015            of multiple conflicting objectives and aligning the gradient updates in a common
016            descent direction. While MOO helps avoid conflicting gradient update directions,
017            a critical issue is that when there are many objectives such as those in multilingual
018            multi-task ASR, it is often impossible to find such common descent directions.
019            Therefore, an interesting question is: would it be more effective to separate highly
020            conflicting objectives into different optimization levels or keep them in one level?
021            To address this question, this paper investigates three multi-objective ASR training
022
               formulations, which we refer to as objective soup recipes. These formulations use
               MOO at different optimization levels to mitigate potential conflicts among all objec-
023
               tives. We conduct an extensive investigation using the LibriSpeech and AISHELL
024
               v1 datasets for ASR, along with the CoVoST v2 dataset for both ASR and speech-
025            to-text translation (S2TT) tasks, to determine the highly conflicting objectives and
026            the optimal training recipes among these three MOO training algorithms.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) technology is crucial across various applications such as
033   virtual assistants and voice search (Graves et al., 2013b; Hinton et al., 2012). Its importance
034   extends to multilingual environments, where there is a growing demand for ASR systems capable
035   of processing multiple languages efficiently. Multilingual ASR systems find use in international
036   communication and language learning platforms (Toshniwal et al., 2018; Yadav & Sitaram, 2022).
037   Ideally, those multilingual systems can perform multiple tasks like transcription and translation
038   simultaneously (Chen & Mak, 2015). Unified speech models that handle multiple tasks across diverse
039
      languages have emerged as a promising solution (Schultz & Kirchhoff, 2006; Bourlard et al., 2011),
      simplifying maintenance efforts and reducing system complexity. However, training a unified model
040
      for multilingual multi-task learning is challenging due to language diversity, task heterogeneity, data
041
      scarcity, and model complexity (Kim et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022).
042
043   A common approach to tackling these challenges is to introduce different objective functions that
044   represent different performance metrics and integrate them into the ASR training process. For
045
      example, to overcome data scarcity, one can introduce both the self-supervised learning (SSL) loss
      and the supervised learning loss in ASR tasks (Oord et al., 2018; Schneider et al., 2019; Baevski
046
      et al., 2019; 2020; Hsu et al., 2021); to address multilingual phonetic diversity, one can introduce
047
      separate objective functions for each language and also enforce fairness across languages. We call this
048   methodology as objective soup. In this context, multilingual multi-task learning naturally presents a
049   multi-objective learning problem, but the caveat is that different objectives may conflict with each
050   other – improvement of some objectives degrades others. Notably, a related work to ours is rewarded
051   soups (Rame et al., 2024), where the goal is to achieve the Pareto-optimal alignment for foundation
052   models by a weighted combination of diverse reward model parameters. The resulting reward model
053   provides an objective for alignment. Different from this, we consider a broader range of MOO
      methods to tackle objective conflicts.

068   To handle conflicts among multiple objectives, recent studies have leveraged multi-objective opti-
069   mization (MOO) to tackle multilingual multi-tasking ASR problems. Roughly speaking, there are
070   three primary MOO formulations, specified in Section 3, to model multilingual multi-tasking ASR
071   problems: i) the single-level vector optimization method; ii) the bilevel hybrid vector optimization
072   method; and, iii) the multi-level optimization (MLO) method (Miettinen, 1999). However, which
073   method is most suitable for multilingual multi-task ASR remains unclear, especially when the ob-
074   jectives exhibit conflict. Selecting an optimal solution from this front involves making challenging
075   decisions regarding acceptable trade-offs, which can negatively impact the model’s performance.
076
      Therefore, identifying the most appropriate MOO-based algorithm for multilingual multi-task ASR
      remains a significant research challenge.
077
078   In this context, we aim to thoroughly investigate these three MOO-based algorithms for multilin-
079   gual multi-task acoustic modeling and determine their effectiveness in handling higher conflicting
080   objectives. By evaluating their performance, we hope to identify the algorithm that best balances the
081   trade-offs and enhances the overall model performance.
082   Our findings and contributions. We conduct extensive experiments on various widely-used bench-
083   mark datasets, including LibriSpeech, AISHELL, and CoVoST v2, and across models with different
084   sizes. We find consistent performance gains through MLO of self-supervised and supervised objec-
085   tives for ASR and S2TT tasks across multiple languages. The findings are summarized below.
086
087
             F1: MOO methods mitigate gradient conflicts in pre-training (PT) and fine-tuning (FT),
                 thus improving the performance. Compared to traditional PT+FT methods that are either
088
                 implemented in a two-stage manner or through static weighting, the MOO method with
089
                 dynamic weighting to handle conflicting gradients performs better. This is because MOO
090              methods mitigate conflicting multilingual multitask objectives through optimization along
091              common descent directions. On average, the use of MOO (VC-ASR1 ) improves the ASR
092              and S2TT performance over Joint PT+FT without MOO by 3.8% and 4.8%, respectively.
093              See results in Tables 1 and 2.
094          F2: Hierarchical objectives enhance ASR performance. Introducing appropriate hierarchy
095              in multilingual multi-task ASR objectives consistently improves ASR performance. In
096              particular, the MLO method consistently outperforms both single-level and bilevel optimiza-
097              tion methods. This suggests that separating highly conflicting objectives across multiple
098              optimization levels effectively mitigates conflicts. On average, MLO (VM-ASR2 ) improves
099
                 ASR and S2TT performance by 5.6% and 5.9%, respectively, compared to VC-ASR. Refer
                 to Tables 1 and 2 for details.
100
             F3: Task-based hierarchy outperforms language-based hierarchy in both efficiency and
101
                 accuracy. In MLO, a task-based hierarchy requires fewer levels compared to a language-
102
                 based hierarchy, thereby reducing the overall complexity of the optimization algorithms.
103              Moreover, the task-based hierarchy achieves superior accuracy, as task-related objective
104              conflicts tend to be more significant than language-related objective conflicts. Refer to
105              Figure 3 for an illustration of gradient conflicts.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
116
117
      In this section, we review existing works on multilingual ASR and S2TT, aiming to identify the
118   current research landscape in these areas.
119
120   Multilingual ASR and S2TT. Earlier works in multilingual ASR used deep neural networks, hidden
121
      Markov models, and multilayer perceptron models (Heigold et al., 2013; Thomas et al., 2010; Tüske
      et al., 2013; Ghoshal et al., 2013). Later studies showed Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models
122
      to be more effective for multilingual ASR (Graves et al., 2013a; Zhou et al., 2017). Recently, Seq2Seq
123
      models with hybrid attention/CTC algorithms and transformer-based models have achieved state-of-
124   the-art results (Watanabe et al., 2017; Toshniwal et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2018). Multilingual S2TT
125   tasks have also gained attention, primarily using transformer-based models with SSL pre-training
126   (Li et al., 2020; Bapna et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2020). Despite advancements, these systems lack
127   multi-tasking capabilities, a longstanding challenge in developing a single model for multiple speech-
128   related tasks. This line of work is orthogonal to the current paper and can potentially be combined
129   with our multi-objective training recipes.
130   Multi-task learning for speech recognition. Multi-task learning for joint ASR and S2TT tasks has
131   been explored in various studies, yet challenges remain in optimizing shared representations and
132   reducing task interference. The first algorithm for joint ASR and S2TT decoding was introduced
133   by (Anastasopoulos & Chiang, 2018). Subsequent models improved this by using word embedding
134   intermediates and two-stage models (Chuang et al., 2020; Sperber et al., 2019). A transformer-based
135   dual encoder-decoder architecture with separate decoders for each task was also applied (Le et al.,
136   2020). The Whisper (Radford et al., 2023) model was trained on large-scale audio dataset for
137   multitask learning. The Mu2 SLAM model (Cheng et al., 2023) pre-trains on multilingual speech,
138
      text, and supervised data. Cross-modality learning from multiple self-supervised and supervised
      subtasks establishes a robust multi-task algorithm (Tang et al., 2022). Joint pre-training and fine-
139
      tuning is also explored in ASR and multilingual multitask speech-to-text tasks to reduce training
140
      complexity (Bai et al., 2022; Saif et al., 2024; Talnikar et al., 2021). Although these approaches
141   address multilingual multi-task learning using static weighting or constrained optimization, they do
142   not explicitly tackle conflicting objectives such as using a conflict-avoidant update direction, which
143   may lead to suboptimal results.
144
      In this paper, we investigate conflicting objectives in multilingual multitask speech-to-text tasks and
145
      propose MOO-based algorithms to mitigate these conflicts. Our approach demonstrates a significant
146
      improvement over baseline methods, highlighting the effectiveness of MOO in multilingual multitask
147   speech-to-text tasks.

183   3.2    T HREE MOO FORMULATIONS OF MULTILINGUAL MULTI - TASK ASR
184
185
      We adopt a joint PT+FT training approach for our MOO-based multilingual multitask speech-to-
      text algorithms, facilitating the sequential optimization of PT and FT objectives. This results in
186
      locally matched optima that enhance model convergence and overall performance (Saif et al., 2024).
187
      Unlike (Saif et al., 2024), which employs SSL objective as a lower-level constraint to establish
188   a feedback loop between PT and FT, we leverage SSL objective to narrow the search space for
189   identifying the most suitable Pareto optimal point in the Pareto optimal front. Additionally, we
190   implement MOO to address conflicts between objectives, an aspect not explored in their work.
191
      In our formulation, let Θ := [θ; ϕ], where θ is the parameter of the backbone and ϕ is that of a
192
      language/task-dependent layer of a model. For pre-training shared backbone parameters θ, we use
193
      SSL loss, lu (θ). For language and task specific parameters ϕt,n , we use supervised classification loss,
194   lctc (θ, ϕt,n ), where t ∈ [T ] and n ∈ [N ] represents different languages and tasks, respectively. This
195   self-supervised loss and multiple supervised losses form the MOO objectives for multilingual ASR.
196   Note that, for multi-objective ASR, we can represent all the objectives as a vector, L(Θ) containing
197   supervised losses from different languages and tasks such as multilingual ASR and S2TT where
198   Θ := [θ, ϕ1,1 , · · · , ϕT,N ]; that is, L(Θ) := [lctc (θ, ϕ1,1 ), . . . , lctc (θ, ϕT,N )].
199

201   parameters while avoiding conflicting gradient directions we formulate three MOO ASR problems.

352   multi-task ASR experiments. The first model has 10 Conformer blocks with a hidden dimension of
353   612 units and 12 attention heads; the second model has 8 blocks with 512 hidden dimensions and 8
354   attention heads. Each attention head has a dimension of 51 for the first model and 64 for the second
355   model. Both configurations use a convolutional kernel size of 31 to capture temporal dependencies
356   and distinct classification heads with varying output sizes. For VC-ASR, the initial penalty parameter
357   η is 0, increasing by 0.02 per epoch. For VM-ASR, there are three optimization levels. The penalty
358
      constant η1 used for the second level starts at 0.1, increasing by 0.02 per epoch, while the lower-level
      penalty constant η2 starts at 0 and increases by 0.02 per epoch. We use learning rates of α = 5 × 10−4
359
      for backbone parameters and β = 5 × 10−5 for classification parameters.
360
361   Training time and memory complexity: Our proposed MOO ASR models exhibit higher training
362   time and memory complexity compared to traditional PT+FT models. Specifically, the PT+FT model
363   uses an average of 8.7 GB of GPU memory and takes approximately 2.25 hours per epoch, while
364
      the MOO ASR models require about 11.6 GB of GPU memory and around 2.8 hours per epoch.
      This increased memory consumption and training time are primarily due to the additional gradient
365
      calculations needed for computing dynamic weights. However, the higher training cost is justified
366
      by significant performance gains. Moreover, our MOO approach demonstrates scalability as the
367   number of tasks increases, and in the long run, a single MOO model reduces resource demands during
368   deployment, making it a more efficient solution overall G.

520   6     C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
521   In conclusion, our study highlights the substantial advantages of integrating self-supervised loss
522   as constraining objectives within a multilevel multi-objective optimization (MOO) structure for
523   multilingual multi-task ASR training. Our findings strongly indicate that segregating highly con-
524   flicting objectives into different optimization levels yields significant benefits for ASR and S2TT
525   tasks. This approach not only enhances the effectiveness of MOO but also underscores its potential
526   for optimizing complex tasks across diverse linguistic boundaries. While our results are based on
527
      extensive simulations, further theoretical analysis would be an interesting direction for future research.
528
529   7     F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      Our study demonstrates the effectiveness of MOO methods in addressing conflicting objectives for
      multilingual, multi-task ASR and S2TT tasks. However, there remain areas for further exploration.
532
      Specifically, we hypothesize that when objectives are highly conflicting, their optimal solutions are
533
      far apart in the parameter space, resulting in a large and spread-out Pareto front that represents diverse
534   trade-offs. Investigating this hypothesis and its implications for optimization strategies, particularly in
535   highly conflicting scenarios, would provide deeper insights into managing such trade-offs effectively.

735   After formalizing the three training algorithms in 3.2, our subsequent objective is to devise a
736   gradient-based algorithm capable of addressing large-scale, high-dimensional multilingual multi-task
737   challenges while ensuring guaranteed convergence to Pareto stationary solutions. We will focus on
738
      the algorithm development of VC-ASR, as this can be easily extended to the other two methods
      (VS-ASR, VM-ASR). To achieve a gradient-based algorithm for VC-ASR that can avoid conflicting
739
      update directions, we leverage recent advances in unconstrained MOO (Chen et al., 2023) and
740
      employ a penalty-based approach to convert the constrained MOO problem in 3 into an unconstrained
741   MOO problem. This approach simultaneously conducts self-supervised pre-training and supervised
742   multi-objective learning, as defined in Equation (3); that is,
743
744                   min      Lη (Θ) :=[lctc (θ, ϕ1,1 ) + ηlu (θ), · · · , lctc (θ, ϕ1,N ) + ηlu (θ), . . . ,        (9)

750   Limitation of static weighting. To guarantee Pareto stationary for supervised objectives, we can
751   employ either static or dynamic weighting MOO methods. In static weighting, we optimize the
752   (weighted) average of the multiple objectives (Kurin et al., 2022; Xin et al., 2022). This method is
753   simple but it may suffer from conflicting objectives where gradients of multiple objectives may have
754   conflicting directions. For instance, considering lt,n (Θ) = lctc (θ, ϕt,n ) + ηlu (θ) and lt′ ,n′ (Θ) =

991

992

1110   In this section, we outline the dataset, models, hyper-parameters, and data pre-processing techniques
1111   employed in evaluating our VS-ASR, VC-ASR, and VM-ASR algorithms.
1112
1113
       Dataset. We evaluate our training algorithms on a combined dataset of LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al.,
       2015), AISHELL v1 (Bu et al., 2017), and CoVoST v2 (Wang et al., 2020). LibriSpeech is an English
1114
       speech dataset consisting of 960 hours of data along with transcripts. AISHELL v1 is a 178-hour
1115
       multi-channel Mandarin speech corpus designed for various speech/speaker processing tasks. We
1116   have combined these two datasets to create a single multilingual dataset. Our approach involved
1117   splitting the LibriSpeech dataset, allocating 860 hours for self-supervised pre-training and using
1118   the 100-hour train-clean-100 subset for supervised training. The trained models are tested on the
1119   AISHELL test dataset and the LibriSpeech test-clean dataset. During training using CoVoST dataset,
1120   we use equal batch sizes across all languages and tasks to ensure balanced training. For high-resource
1121   En, we fix a subset of data (top 50% from the provided CSV), while applying upsampling for low-
1122   resource languages—4x for Ca and Es and 2x for Fr and De. The same En subset is consistently used
1123   across all runs to maintain fairness.
1124   In the first experiment, we use combined LibriSpeech and AISHELL multilingual dataset and train a
1125   multi-head conformer for multilingual ASR tasks. In the second experiment, we use the CoVoST v2
1126   training dataset for multilingual ASR and S2TT training. The CoVoST v2 test set is used to evaluate
1127   the trained models. CoVoST v2 is a widely-used benchmark multilingual S2TT corpus covering
1128   translations from 21 languages into English and from English into 15 languages.
1129   Models. We use two configurations of the Conformer model (Gulati et al., 2020), each with a different
1130   number of Conformer blocks and hidden units. The first model has 10 Conformer blocks with a
1131   hidden dimension of 612 units and 12 attention heads; the second model has 8 blocks with 512 hidden
1132   dimensions and 8 attention heads. Each attention head has a dimension of 51 for the first model and
1133   64 for the second model. Both configurations use a convolutional kernel size of 31, enhancing the
       model’s ability to discern temporal dependencies and capture long-range dependencies in the input

1154   rate of β = 5 × 10−5 , with a scheduler reducing the learning rate by a factor of 0.1 if the test loss
1155   does not improve within 10 epochs. All MOO models (VS-ASR, VC-ASR, and VM-ASR) and joint

1157   fine-tune it for an additional 100 epochs. A batch size of 256 and AdamW optimizer are used for

1163   0.02 per epoch, while the lower-level penalty constant η2 starts at 0 and also increases by 0.02 per
1164   epoch. The increase for both penalty constants stops once they reach a maximum value of 1.5. A
1165   higher increase rate for the lower level ensures equal importance of both upper-level and lower-level
1166   objectives.

1261
1262   In our multilingual multi-task ASR experiments, we investigated the effects of different penalty
1263   parameter increase rates to balance the ASR and S2TT tasks. We tested two configurations:
1264
1265          • A lower increase rate of 0.002, which led to worse WER/BLEU score for lower-level tasks,

1269
1270   Choice of capped value for the penalty parameter: We capped the penalty parameter at 1.5 based
1271   on our observed trade-off between upper- and lower-level tasks. A penalty higher than 1.5 could
1272   have improved lower-level performance further, but it would have significantly degraded upper-level
1273
       metrics. Thus, 1.5 was chosen as an optimal balance point.
1274   Post-Maximum Penalty Effects: The penalty parameter reached its maximum value of 1.5 after
1275   75 epochs, but training continued for another 25 epochs. During this time, we observed further
1276   improvements in lower-level WER/BLEU scores, while upper-level performance deteriorated. This
1277   reinforces the critical role that penalty parameter selection plays in balancing competing objectives.

1281
       This section addresses the question: How does a single MOO model reduce resource demands
1282
       during deployment, making it a more efficient solution overall?
1283
1284          • Reduced Storage Requirements: A single MOO model is highly memory-efficient due
1285            to parameter sharing across tasks, see Table: 9. The largest MOO model used in our
1286            experiments has a size of 654.4 MB, comprising an encoder (~100M parameters) shared
1287            across all objectives and five lightweight classification heads (~0.5M parameters each) for
1288            five different tasks (considering the ASR for five languages). In contrast, deploying five
1289            separate models for these tasks would require 5× more backbone parameters, resulting
1290            in significantly higher storage demands. Assuming each single-objective model uses an
1291            encoder of similar size, the total storage requirement for separate models would reach
1292
                approximately 3.2 GB.
1293          • Efficient Inference: The MOO model also minimizes latency and computational overhead
1294            during inference. In our system, it takes only 0.27s to load the single MOO model, whereas
1295            loading five separate models takes 1.25s (5 × 0.25 s). This reduction in loading time directly
                translates to faster response times and improved computational efficiency.
