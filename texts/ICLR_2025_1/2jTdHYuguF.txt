009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
                   This paper introduces MMMU-Pro, a robust version of the Massive Multi-
012                discipline Multimodal Understanding and Reasoning (MMMU) benchmark.
013                MMMU-Pro rigorously assesses multimodal models’ true understanding and rea-
014                soning capabilities through a three-step process based on MMMU: (1) filtering out
015                questions answerable by text-only models, (2) augmenting candidate options, and
016                (3) introducing a vision-only input setting where questions are embedded within
017                images. This setting challenges AI to truly “see” and “read” simultaneously, test-
018                ing a core human cognitive skill of seamlessly integrating visual and textual infor-
019                mation. Results show that model performance is substantially lower on MMMU-
020
                   Pro than on MMMU, ranging from 16.8% to 26.9% across models. We explore
                   the impact of OCR prompts and Chain of Thought (CoT) reasoning, finding that
021
                   OCR prompts have minimal effect while CoT generally improves performance.
022
                   MMMU-Pro provides a more rigorous evaluation tool, closely mimicking real-
023                world scenarios and offering valuable directions for future multimodal research.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recent advances in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have led to remarkable progress
      in tackling complex reasoning tasks that combine textual and visual information (Yin et al., 2023a;
030
      Jin et al., 2024). Models like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a) have achieved impressive results, e.g., on
031
      the Massive Multi-discipline Multimodal Understanding and Reasoning (MMMU) benchmark (Yue
032   et al., 2024), reaching an accuracy of 69.1% on college-level questions that integrate text and images.
033
034   While these achievements are significant, they raise a critical question: Do the current benchmark
035
      results truly reflect a deep, multifaceted understanding of diverse subjects, or are these models
      exploiting subtle shortcuts and statistical patterns to arrive at correct answers without genuine
036
      comprehension and reasoning?
037
038   This question has profound implications for the development and deployment of AI systems in real-
039   world applications. If models rely on superficial cues rather than true multimodal understanding (Du
040   et al., 2023; Yuksekgonul et al., 2023), we risk overestimating their capabilities and potentially
041
      deploying systems that fail in unpredictable ways when faced with novel scenarios (Wu & Xie,
      2024; Tong et al., 2024b).
042
043   To address this concern and push the boundaries of multimodal AI evaluation, we introduce
044   MMMU-Pro, a more robust and challenging version of the MMMU benchmark. MMMU-Pro is
045   designed to more accurately and rigorously assess a model’s true multimodal understanding and
046   reasoning capabilities across a wide range of academic disciplines. The development of MMMU-
047
      Pro is motivated by key observations, including the text-only solvability of some benchmark ques-
      tions, limited option space in multiple-choice formats (Wang et al., 2024), and the need to challenge
048
      models’ ability to jointly understand different modalities in a more integrated way.
049
050   MMMU-Pro employs a rigorous three-step construction process (as shown in Figure 1) that builds
051   upon MMMU (Yue et al., 2024): (1) filtering out questions answerable by text-only language mod-
052   els, (2) augmenting candidate options to reduce the effectiveness of guessing based on the options,

066   The introduction of the vision-only input setting is particularly crucial, as it tests a fundamental
067   human cognitive ability: the seamless integration and switching between visual and textual infor-
068   mation. This setting challenges models to develop the capability to truly “see” and “read” simul-
069   taneously, mirroring how humans effortlessly process complex scenes where text and images are
070   intertwined. This ability is crucial for tasks ranging from interpreting scientific diagrams (Li et al.,
071   2024d) to navigating graphical user interfaces (Liu et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024; Koh et al.,
072   2024). Moreover, this approach aligns with how users naturally interact with AI systems, often
073   sharing screenshots or photos rather than meticulously separating text and images.
074   Our experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of MMMU-Pro in providing a more rigor-
075   ous evaluation of multimodal models. We observe significant performance drops across all tested
076   models when compared to the original MMMU benchmark, with decreases ranging from 16.8% to
077   26.9%. These results highlight the limitations of current state-of-the-art models in true multimodal
078   understanding and reasoning. Furthermore, our analysis reveals that while CoT (Wei et al., 2022)
079   prompting generally improves performance, the benefits vary across models and settings.
080   Interestingly, we find that explicit OCR prompts do not significantly impact performance for most
081   models, suggesting that advanced multimodal models have already developed robust text extraction
082   capabilities from images. However, this result also underscores that simple OCR is insufficient for
083   the challenges presented by MMMU-Pro’s vision-only input setting. Our further qualitative analysis
084   indicates that when text is embedded within images, it significantly increases the overall complexity
085   of the visual input, requiring models to not only recognize text but also understand its context,
086
      relationship to visual elements, and relevance to the question. These findings not only provide a
      more accurate assessment of current multimodal AI capabilities but also highlight the need for more
087
      sophisticated multimodal reasoning abilities.

151   2.2   M ETHODS
152
      To address these issues and build a more robust benchmark, we implemented a three-step approach.
153
154
      Filtering Questions: We begin by filtering out questions that can be answered by text-only LLMs.
155
      We select four strong open-source LLMs: Llama3-70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), Qwen2-72B-
156   Instruct (Yang et al., 2024), Yi-1.5-34B-Chat (Young et al., 2024), and Mixtral-8×22B-Instruct (gpt-
157   4o)—and task them with answering the MMMU questions without access to images. The models
158   are required to provide answers even when they indicate that visual input is necessary. We repeat
159   this process ten times for each model, considering a question as “answerable” if a model correctly
160   answers it more than five times. We then exclude any question where at least three out of the four
161   models answer correctly across the majority of trials. We randomly sample 1800 questions from the
      remaining pool, evenly distributed across 30 subjects (60 questions per subject).

191   cognitive abilities of humans. In everyday scenarios, users often take shortcuts by simply capturing
192   screenshots of questions that include both text and images, rather than inputting text separately.
193   This habit of feeding integrated visual-textual content reflects a natural human tendency to process
194   information holistically. Humans excel at interpreting and understanding information when both text
195   and images are presented together, and our goal with this setting is to push models toward achieving
196   a similar level of comprehension. By mimicking this real-world behavior, the vision-only input
197   setting not only adds realism to the benchmark but also ensures that models are better equipped to
198
      handle the complexities of multimodal tasks as they appear in practical applications.
199   After these three steps, we finally obtain a total of 3460 questions (1730 samples are in the standard
200   format, and the other 1730 are in screenshot or photo form).

289   3.2      OVERALL R ESULTS
290
291   We presented the overall results of MMMU-Pro of different models in Table 1.
292   Effect of Increased Candidate Options: The shift from 4 to 10 candidate options (∆1 ) reveals a
293   significant drop in performance for all models. GPT-4o (0513) experienced a decrease of 10.7%,
294   from 64.7% to 54.0%. This indicates that increasing the number of options effectively reduces the
295   likelihood of models guessing the correct answer, forcing them to engage more deeply with the
296   multimodal content.
297   Impact of Vision-Only Setting: The introduction of the vision-only input setting further challenges
298   models, as evidenced by the additional drop in performance when comparing the vision-only results
299   to the 10-option standard (∆2 ). For instance, GPT-4o (0513) dropped another 4.3% in accuracy
300   when evaluated in the vision-only setting, and LLaVA-OneVision-72B saw a dramatic 14.0% de-
301   crease. This suggests that the vision-only setting successfully tests the models’ ability to integrate
302   visual and textual information, highlighting their limitations when the text is not explicitly provided.
303   Combined Effects on MMMU-Pro: The overall ∆3 , representing the difference between MMMU-
304   Pro and MMMU (Val), shows a significant decrease across the board. For instance, models like
305   Gemini 1.5 Pro (0801) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet exhibited declines of 18.9% and 16.8%, respectively,
306   while more drastic drops were seen in models like VILA-1.5-40B with a 26.9% decrease.
307
      This significant reduction in accuracy across the board suggests that MMMU-Pro successfully miti-
308   gates the shortcuts and guessing strategies that models could exploit in the original benchmark.

312
313   Figure 5 examines the effectiveness of Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting in enhancing model
314   performance on the MMMU-Pro benchmark, both in the Standard and Vision Input settings. Across
315   both settings, the introduction of CoT prompts generally led to improved performance. However,
316   the extent of improvement varied significantly among models. For instance, Claude 3.5 Sonnet
317   demonstrated a substantial increase in the Standard setting, improving from 42.7% to 55.0%. In
318   contrast, models like LLaVA-OneVision-72B showed only minimal improvement.
319   Interestingly, we observed a significant performance drop for some models, such as VILA1.5-40B.
320   This decline might be attributed to challenges in instruction-following abilities. When a model
321   struggles to follow instructions accurately, generating CoT explanations becomes more difficult.
322   Additionally, these models may face issues with maintaining the correct response format, leading
323   to what is known as “boiled response format” problems. These findings highlight the potential of
      CoT to enhance model performance in complex, real-world tasks that require nuanced reasoning

521

522   on MMMU-Pro Vision. A potential reason is the lack of training data with text-rich images in
523   reasoning-intensive contexts. To address this, we developed a tool leveraging the MMMU-Pro Vi-
524   sion human annotation process. This tool processes a JSON file with questions and images and
525   outputs screenshots embedding both. Such tools can further generate similar datasets at scale, en-
526   hancing models’ ability to integrate visual and textual information in real-world scenarios.
527

534   with its predecessor MMMU. Our results demonstrate MMMU-Pro’s effectiveness in exposing the
535   limitations of current state-of-the-art multimodal models, with significant performance drops across
536   all tested systems. MMMU-Pro opens up several important avenues for future research: 1) Devel-

      to address the increased complexity of MMMU-Pro questions.

974
       Multimodal Large Language Models. Recent progress in multimodal AI has been marked by
975    innovative training approaches (Lu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
976    2021; Li et al., 2020; Alayrac et al., 2022; Awadalla et al., 2023). Inspired by the success of large
977    language models, researchers have developed various models with improved instruction-following
978    capabilities (Liu et al., 2023c;b; 2024a; Li et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Zhang
979    et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023a;b; Zhao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Monajatipoor
980    et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Lin et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). Proprietary
981    models such as GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023), GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a), Gemini (Team et al., 2023),
982    and Claude-3.5 (Anthropic, 2024) have demonstrated strong performance across various vision-
983
       language tasks. However, a significant challenge remains in accurately evaluating the capabilities of
       these advanced LMMs, highlighting the need for more robust and comprehensive benchmarks.
984
985    MLLM Benchmarks. The rise of more advanced multimodal pre-training and instruction tun-
986    ing has exposed the limitations of earlier benchmarks like VQA (Antol et al., 2015; Goyal et al.,
987    2017), OK-VQA (Marino et al., 2019), and MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2014), which no longer suffice
988    to evaluate the full spectrum of LMMs capabilities. To address this, recent benchmarks such as
989
       LAMM (Yin et al., 2023b), LVLM-eHub (Xu et al., 2023), SEED (Li et al., 2024b), MMBench (Liu
       et al., 2023d),CV-Bench (Tong et al., 2024a), MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2024), Mantis (Jiang et al., 2024),
990
       and BLINK (Fu et al., 2024) have emerged, covering aspects from basic perception to hallucination
991
       detection (Cui et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a). However, existing benchmarks often fall short in
992    evaluating expert-level domain knowledge and complex reasoning (Lu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al.,
993    2024b). While MMMU (Yue et al., 2024) made strides by incorporating multimodal, college-level
994    questions, it still permits text-only models to find shortcuts (Lu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2024b).
995    To address these limitations, we introduce MMMU-Pro, a more robust benchmark that removes
996    text-only answerable questions, expands candidate options, and includes a vision-only input setting
997    to better reflect real-world multimodal scenarios.

       Expanding the number of answer options naturally increases the difficulty of the benchmark, but
1191   its effectiveness relies heavily on the quality, diversity, and contextual relevance of these additional
1192   options. To ensure this, we implemented a rigorous multi-stage validation process, combining auto-
1193   mated and human efforts to produce high-quality results.
1194
       Initial Model-Based Option Augmentation and Filtering. We began by leveraging large language
1195
       models (LLMs) to automate the initial generation and filtering of expanded options. Specifically,
1196
       GPT-4o was used to generate additional options, while Claude 3.5 acted as a preliminary filter to
1197   remove options that were contextually irrelevant or logically inconsistent. This step significantly
1198   reduced the workload for human reviewers by pre-screening the candidates.
1199
1200
       Two Rounds of Human Review. To further enhance quality and eliminate potential issues, we
       conducted two rounds of meticulous human validation:
1201
1202
       • First Round of Review: Individual reviewers assessed the expanded options for each question.
1203     They ensured that the options were diverse, logically distinct, and free from ambiguity. If any
1204     flaws were identified, reviewers were instructed to correct the issues or create new options to
1205     maintain the integrity of the question.
1206
       • Second Round of Review: A double-check process followed, involving two additional human ex-
1207     perts who cross-validated each question and its options. This iterative step eliminated any residual
1208     inconsistencies or errors and provided an additional layer of assurance.
1209
1210   By combining automated methods with multi-stage human validation, we ensured that each ex-
1211   panded option met high standards of quality, robustness, and alignment with the intended challenges
1212   of the benchmark. This approach not only addressed potential weaknesses in automated generation
1213   but also significantly improved the reliability of the dataset.
