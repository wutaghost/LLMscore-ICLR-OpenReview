011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated learning (FL) emerges as a promising paradigm for training machine
014            learning models on decentralized data sources while preserving privacy. How-
015
               ever, the presence of not independent and identically distributed (non-IID) data
               among the clients introduces high variance in gradient updates, posing a signifi-
016
               cant challenge to the global model’s performance in terms of accuracy and con-
017
               vergence. To mitigate the adverse effects of data heterogeneity, we propose a
018            novel momentum-based partial variance reduction technique. Our approach ad-
019            justs the gradient updates for the final classification layers of the client’s neural
020            network by leveraging the gradient differences between local and global mod-
021            els. This adjustment aims to effectively capture and mitigate client drift, a key
022            challenge arises from the presence of non-IID data distributions across clients.
023            We systematically explains client drifts and conduct extensive experiments on
024            three widely-used datasets, demonstrating that our method significantly enhances
025            global model accuracy while reducing the communication rounds needed for con-
026
               vergence. Notably, our momentum-based partial variance reduction technique
               provides a robust mechanism, rendering more efficient and effective in scenar-
027
               ios with inherently non-IID and heterogeneous data distributions. By address-
028
               ing the critical challenge of data heterogeneity in FL, our proposed approach
029            paves the way for more reliable and accurate model training while preserving
030            the privacy of decentralized data sources. The code is available at the following
031            link https://anonymous.4open.science/r/FedPMVR-33C1.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a effective solution to privacy concerns in centralized model
037   training, enabling multiple clients to collaboratively build while keeping their raw data decentralized
038   and private (McMahan et al., 2017), (Guo et al., 2021), (Park et al., 2021). In FL, clients perform
039
      local training on their data and only share model updates (weights) with a central server, which
      aggregates these updates to refine the global model. While federated training offers significant ad-
040
      vantages in preserving user privacy, it faces a practical obstacle in the form of data heterogeneity (Li
041
      et al., 2020a). Diverse user behaviors across different clients can lead to significant heterogeneity in
042   their local data, resulting in unstable convergence, slow training progress, and suboptimal or even
043   detrimental model performance (Zhao et al., 2018), (Karimireddy et al., 2020). FedAvg (McMahan
044   et al., 2017), the widely adopted FL algorithm, often encounters challenges in achieving optimal
045   accuracy and convergence, particularly in scenarios with heterogeneous data distributions across
046   clients. This difficulty arises from client drift, a phenomenon resulting from the varying nature of
047   data among participating clients. Client drift occurs when local models diverge significantly from
048   the global model, causing aggregated updates to become less effective or detrimental to overall per-
049   formance (Karimireddy et al., 2020). Addressing data heterogeneity and client drift is crucial for
050
      harnessing the full potential of FL in real-world applications with decentralized and diverse data.
051   Previous research efforts have aimed to address the issue of client drift by introducing penalties
052   for the divergence between client and server models (Li et al., 2020a), (Li et al., 2021a) or by em-
053   ploying variance reduction approaches during the client model update process (Karimireddy et al.,
      2020), (Acar et al., 2021). Techniques like CCVR (Luo et al., 2021) first reported that classification


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      layers are solely accountable for client drift and addressed data heterogeneity through re-training
055   classifiers using virtual features derived from an approximate Gaussian mixture model (GMM).
056   However, creating representative datasets or features spanning multiple clients can be challenging,
057   requiring domain knowledge and raising privacy concerns. Recent research has highlighted how
058   biased classifiers adversely affect the performance of federated training on heterogeneous and long-
059   tailed data (Shang et al., 2022). To mitigate this issue, Luo et al. (2021) proposed a method that
060   addresses long-tailed and heterogeneous data by re-training the classifier on the server using learn-
061   able features from client models. Additionally, researchers have investigated gradient variability
062   across clients, especially in deeper or classification layers, using metrics like drift diversity. In par-
063
      ticular, Li et al. (2023) found that client drift predominantly originates from the classification layers
      and proposed a partial variance reduction technique using control variates, though this approach can
064
      lead to increased communication costs.
065
066   Building on prior researches (Refer to Appendix A for the detailed related works), we introduce
067   Federated Partial Momentum Variance Reduction (FedPMVR) that integrates a momentum-based
068   variance reduction technique that selectively targets the classification layers of client neural net-
069
      works. Our approach employs standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in the initial layers to
      capture diverse representations from heterogeneous client data while incorporating a momentum
070
      term in the classification layers to enhance gradient alignment. By maintaining a local momentum
071
      term for each client, FedPMVR captures the drift between local and global models, efficiently ad-
072   dressing client drift in non-IID data settings. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work
073   to leverage such a selective momentum-based regularization, achieving improved performance by
074   balancing representation learning with variance reduction. The key contributions are summarized as
075   follows:
076
077          • We propose FedPMVR, which employs momentum terms to reduce divergence between
078            the classification layers of client and global models, effectively addressing client drift from
079            non-IID data distributions.
080          • Momentum term incorporation in FedPMVR allows aligning local models with the global
081            model, facilitating faster convergence to the global optimum and accelerating convergence.
082
             • We provide theoretical convergence guarantees for FedPMVR in convex and non-convex
083
               settings, demonstrating its limited reliance on measures of data heterogeneity.
084
085          • Experimental evaluations consistently show that FedPMVR outperforms state-of-the-art
086
               approaches across diverse datasets with varying levels of data heterogeneity.

171   2.2     F ED PMVR: F EDERATED PARTIAL M OMENTUM VARIANCE R EDUCTION
172
173   Our proposed method, FedPMVR, incorporates momentum correction for the last classification lay-
174   ers of the neural network during the local model updation. The core concept is to harness the benefits
175
      of momentum-based optimization on the local client side to mitigate the effects of non-IID data dis-
      tribution across clients, which can lead to better performance. Details of the two-step process are
176
      given below.
177
178
      2.2.1    C LIENT U PDATE S TEP
179
180   In the client update step, each client performs the following operations:
181
182
              • Local Model Initialization: To ensure a consistent starting point for local training, each
                client initializes its local model by setting the weight parameters to those of the global
183
                model received from the central server. This initialization process guarantees that all clients
184
                start their local optimization from an identical model state.
185
              • Local Model Training: For a predefined number of epochs, the client trains the local
186
                model using their local dataset. This training process involves updating the model weights
187
                using SGD or a variant of SGD, such as Adam or RMSprop.
188
              • Gradient Computation: Upon completing local model training, the client calculates the
189
                gradients of the updated local weights in relation to the initial global weights. These gradi-
190
                ents indicate the necessary weight adjustments to minimize the loss function for the client’s
191             local data.
192
              • Momentum Update: For the last few layers of the neural network, the client updates the
193
                corresponding momentum terms using an exponential moving average of the gradients.
194             Specifically, for each weight in these layers, the momentum term is updated as follows:

218   Incorporating momentum terms into the FL framework, particularly at the client level, presents a
219   robust solution to mitigate the issue of client drift due to data heterogeneity. It stabilizes gradient
220   updates by accumulating past gradient information, which counters sudden changes and reduces
221   variance caused by non-IID data across clients. This stabilization leads to more reliable updates
222   and mitigates client drift. Additionally, momentum accelerates convergence by maintaining the
223   gradient direction and reducing oscillations, allowing clients to overcome local minima and saddle
224
      points more effectively. An example of integrating the proposed approach into the standard FedAvg
      algorithm is provided below, demonstrating how partial momentum variance reduction effectively
225
      mitigates client drift caused by heterogeneous data distributions.
226

524   5     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we investigated data heterogeneity within the traditional FL framework, focusing on
527   enhancing the accuracy and convergence speed of the final global model. We conducted an in-depth
528   analysis of client drift in FL, which arises from data heterogeneity, causing local models to di-
529   verge from the global model and reducing the effectiveness of aggregated updates. To address this,
530   we introduced FedPMVR, which employs a straightforward yet effective partial momentum vari-
531
      ance reduction technique to stabilize training, accelerate convergence, and improve overall global
      model performance. The core advantage of FedPMVR lies in its ability to mitigate client drift
532
      through momentum-based variance reduction, ensuring stable gradient updates without increasing
533
      much communication overhead. Our comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets confirm
534   FedPMVR’s superior performance, particularly in highly non-IID data scenarios, demonstrating sig-
535   nificant improvements in both convergence speed and model accuracy. Importantly, FedPMVR re-
536   quires only a minor modification to the existing FedAvg, offering an efficient and scalable solution
537   for real-world FL applications. Additionally, while FedPMVR introduces minimal computational
538   overhead at the client side for calculating the momentum term, future work will focus on exploring
539   strategies to further mitigate this cost. We also aim to extend our approach to more complex scenar-
      ios, such as asynchronous updates and the integration of advanced privacy-preserving techniques.

682   A    R ELATED W ORK
683
684   Various approaches have been extensively explored to address the challenges arising from data het-
685   erogeneity in the context of FL. These methodologies can be broadly classified into three main
686   categories: (1) client drift mitigation strategies (Li et al., 2021a), (Karimireddy et al., 2020), (Sahoo
687
      et al., 2024b), (Li et al., 2023) (Sahoo et al., 2024a), which modify the local objectives of clients to
      better align their models with the global model; (2) aggregation scheme enhancements (Hsu et al.,
688
      2019), (Lin et al., 2020), (Wang et al., 2020b), (Wang et al., 2020a), which focus on improving the
689
      server-side fusion mechanism for model updates; and (3) personalized FL techniques (Fallah et al.,
690   2020), (Sattler et al., 2020), (Bui et al., 2019), which aim to train personalized models for individual
691   clients. Since our work centers on minimizing client-server model divergence to mitigate client drift,
692   we primarily discuss client drift mitigation strategies.
693
      FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) is the most widely adopted optimization method in FL, but data het-
694
      erogeneity often results in subpar performance. To tackle this issue, methods like FedProx (Li et al.,
695
      2020a) have introduced a proximal regularization term at the client side to manage divergence; how-
696   ever, it still falls short in effectively aligning the optimal points (Acar et al., 2021). FedNova (Wang
697   et al., 2020b) tackled the issue of objective inconsistency caused by client heterogeneity in feder-
698   ated optimization by introducing a normalized averaging method, which effectively mitigates this
699   inconsistency and accelerates error convergence. Li et al. (2021b) used local batch normalization
700   to mitigate feature shifts, and demonstrated faster convergence. Acar et al. (2021) proposed a dy-
701   namic regularizer aimed at bridging the gap between local and global minima, facilitating better
      alignment of local solutions with the global model. Li et al. (2021a) introduced a model-contrastive


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      framework that aligns local representations with the global model using contrastive loss, enhanc-
703   ing consistency and convergence, particularly in non-IID settings. Similarly, FedMut (Hu et al.,
704   2024) generates intermediate models through global model mutation, which avoids sharp solutions,
705   improves generalization in heterogeneous data, and outperforms FedAvg in handling data hetero-
706   geneity. Stochastic variance reduction (SVR) techniques, such as SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013),
707   SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014), and their variants, have been explored to mitigate challenges posed
708   by data heterogeneity. These methods leverage control variate to reduce the variance inherent in
709   traditional stochastic gradient descent (SGD), enabling linear convergence rates for strongly convex
710   optimization problems. SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020) and DANE (Shamir et al., 2014)
711
      have incorporated variance reduction techniques for the entire model on convex problems, but their
      performance in non-convex setups has not been extensively explored. While these approaches have
712
      the potential to be beneficial, they incur higher communication costs due to the transmission of ad-
713
      ditional control variates (Halgamuge et al., 2009). Moreover, existing methods have demonstrated
714   rapid convergence in simpler models, but their effectiveness on deep neural networks (DNNs) (He
715   et al., 2015), (Huang et al., 2018) remains largely unexplored. FedPVR (Li et al., 2023) revis-
716   its the performance of FedAvg in DNNs and reveals significant diversity in the final classification
717   layers. By proposing variance reduction solely on the final layers, FedPVR outperforms several
718   benchmarks, addressing the limitations of existing approaches and demonstrating the effectiveness
719   of targeted variance reduction techniques. Several momentum-based techniques have been explored
720   to enhance FL convergence (Das et al., 2022). Liu et al. (2020) incorporated Momentum Gradi-
721   ent Descent (MGD) into the local update step, accelerating convergence, deriving an upper bound
722
      on the convergence rate, and identifying conditions where it outperforms standard FL. Building on
      this, Cheng et al. (2023) demonstrated that momentum improves FedAvg and Scaffold, allowing
723
      FedAvg to converge without assuming bounded data heterogeneity, even with a constant local learn-
724
      ing rate. They also showed that momentum accelerates Scaffold’s convergence under partial client
725   participation, leading to new variance-reduced extensions with state-of-the-art convergence rates.
726   Further, Sun et al. (2024) introduced a general framework for server momentum in FL, accom-
727   modating diverse momentum schemes, stagewise hyperparameter scheduling, and handling system
728   heterogeneity and asynchronous local computing.

      C.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
867
868   For our experiments with MNIST and FMNIST, we have utilized the well-established LeNet (LeCun
869   et al., 1998) neural network, commonly employed in FL research (Duan et al., 2023). For the
870   CIFAR10 dataset, we constructed a custom 5-layer convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture
871   from scratch to serve as the base encoder, following the approach presented in (Li et al., 2021a).
872   This base encoder composed of two convolutional layers with (5 × 5) kernels, each succeeded by
873   a (2 × 2) max pooling layer. Subsequently, the architecture includes two fully connected (FC)
874   layers with ReLU activation functions, the first containing 120 and the second containing 84 units of
875
      neurons. Following the methodology outlined in (Duan et al., 2023), we applied variance reduction
      techniques to the last two layers of the model to tackle the impact of data heterogeneity. We followed
876
      the configurations reported in prior studies (Li et al., 2023) and (Yu et al., 2022) by involving 10
877
      participating clients per communication round and employing a batch size of 32. Each local client
878   performed two local epochs of model updating during each round. We have employed a maximum
879   of 300 server-client communication rounds with the early stopping criteria. To evaluate the global
880   model’s performance, a test set, unseen during training, is held out at the server. We utilize the actual
881   test split from the original dataset for this purpose. To determine the optimal client learning rate for
882   each experiment, we conducted a grid search over 0.05, 0.01, 0.2, 0.3. For all datasets and methods,
883   the optimizer used was SGD with a learning rate of 0.01, a weight decay of 1e−6, and a momentum
884   value of 0.9. we conducted a grid search over 0.001, 0.1, 0.3, 0.6, 0.8 to choose the learning rate
885   for momentum update α. We employed hyperparameters (if any) similar to those specified in the
886
      original papers for all baseline methods. We have run each experiment three times with different
      seed values and reported the average of the performance with the standard deviation.

      C.2   E FFECT ON TEST ACCURACY FOR DIFFERENT VALUES OF HYPERPARAMETER α
919
920   The hyperparameter α controls the degree of variance reduction in the classification layers by mod-
921   ulating the momentum update. α can take values between 0 and 1. To gain insights into the nature
922   of this hyperparameter, we experimented with a range of values from lower to higher magnitudes.
923   As depicted in Fig. 10, higher values of α lead to lower accuracy, while values closer to zero yields
924   higher accuracy. A possible explanation for this observation is that when α is set to a higher value,
925   instead of mitigating client drift, it may inadvertently exacerbate drift by aggressively reducing the
926
      weight magnitudes in an attempt to align with the global model for achieving the global optimum.
      Conversely, lower values of α closer to zero effectively curb client drift, resulting in improved accu-
927
      racy. These findings suggest that α is an easily tunable hyperparameter, with lower values close to
928
      zero proving to be the most effective in enhancing the performance of our proposed approach.

955   To assess the scalability, we evaluated the performance of FedPMVR and baselines by varying the
956   number of participating clients, as depicted in Fig. 11. As the number of clients increased from 10
957   to 50, FedPMVR exhibited a significantly slower decline in accuracy compared to the baselines.

959   accuracy even as the number of participating clients grows. Notably, FedPMVR achieved the highest
960   test accuracy among all baselines in all evaluated cases, further underscoring its robust performance
961   and ability to handle increasing client populations effectively. This scalability is a desirable attribute,
962
      particularly in real-world FL scenarios involving large numbers of clients, where our method can
      deliver consistent and reliable results.

965
966   While this work primarily investigates data heterogeneity in terms of label and quantity skewness,
967   the concept of momentum-based variance reduction could be expanded to address other forms of

969   mentation of FedPMVR incurs some computational overhead due to the use of momentum terms for
970   last-layer updates on the client side, an issue we plan to investigate further in future work to mitigate
