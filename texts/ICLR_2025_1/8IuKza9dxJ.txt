009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Unsupervised graph domain adaptation (GDA) addresses the challenge of transfer-
012            ring knowledge from labeled source graphs to unlabeled target graphs. However,
013            existing methods primarily implement spatial message-passing operators, which
014            are limited by the neglect of the unique roles of spectral signals in unsupervised
015            GDA. In this paper, we initially conduct an experimental study and find that the
016            low-frequency topology signals signify the shared cross-domain features, while
017            the high-frequency information indicates domain-specific knowledge. However,
018
               how to effectively leverage the above findings persists as a perplexing conundrum.
               To tackle this issue, we propose an effective framework named Synergy Low-High
019
               Frequency Cross-Domain Network (SnLH) for unsupervised GDA. Specifically,
020
               we disentangle the low- and high-frequency components in the original graph,
021            extracting global structures and local details to capture more discriminative in-
022            formation and enhance the graph-level semantics. For the low-frequency com-
023            ponents, we design an optimization objective to maximize the mutual informa-
024            tion among low-frequency features, promoting the model to learn more general-
025            ized low-frequency information. To further mitigate domain discrepancy, we in-
026            troduce high-frequency information cross-domain contrastive learning to impose
027            constraints on the domains. By effectively leveraging both low and high-frequency
028            information, the learned features turn out to be both discriminative and domain-
029
               invariant, thereby attaining effective cross-domain knowledge transfer. Extensive
               experiments demonstrate the superiority and effectiveness of the proposed frame-
030
               work across various state-of-the-art unsupervised GDA baselines.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Graph data has been widely applied in various fields due to its ability to naturally express complex
036
      relationships in the real world, such as social network analysis (Fan et al., 2019), drug discovery
037   (Abbasi et al., 2019; Bongini et al., 2021), and traffic flow prediction (Li & Zhu, 2021). In real-
038   world applications, graph data from different domains typically encounters the issue of domain shift
039   (Wu et al., 2022b). As a result, graph domain adaptation (GDA) methods have emerged (Ding
040   et al., 2018), aiming to transfer knowledge from the source domain to the target domain (Lin et al.,
041   2023; Liu et al., 2023). GDA effectively alleviates the challenges posed by differences in data
042   distribution during cross-domain learning of complex graph-structured data. However, traditional
043   graph domain adaptation methods (Qiao et al., 2023) typically rely on supervised learning and fail
044   when there is a severe lack of labeled data in the target domain. Data with rich labels is scarce
045
      or difficult to obtain in real situations and it always takes a lot of effort and costs to have a little.
      Therefore, unsupervised graph domain adaptation (UGDA) is proposed to address the above issue.
046
      The advantage of unsupervised graph domain adaptation is that it can learn an effective cross-domain
047
      transfer model without any labeled data from the target domain. UGDA addresses the limitation
048   of traditional GDA which relies on labeled data and enhances the model’s generalization ability
049   across different graph domains. Among existing UGDA works, adversarial learning-based methods
050   (Wu et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2024) attempt to reduce distribution differences between domains
051   through adversarial training. However, this strategy is limited by cross-domain feature differences
052   and performs poorly on the target domain. Graph neural network-based methods (Yin et al., 2023)
053   aim to align the distribution of domain data within the generated representation space. As it is
      difficult to learn a reliable representation, the alignment is not effective in some cases.

066   Although these UGDA methods have made significant progress in their respective application sce-

068   domain features, relying heavily on spatial information while overlooking the impact of cross-
069   domain features in the spectral domain. This results in the loss of specific information in the spectral
070   domain, making it difficult to effectively align cross-domain features, leading to poor model perfor-
071   mance in the target domain. An experimental analysis of the influence of spectral signal is shown
072
      in Figure 1. The graphs from different domains exhibit astonishing similarity in the low-frequency
      components of the same class of graphs in the spectral space while showing significant differences in
073
      the high-frequency components. This discovery prompted us to reassess the critical role of low- and
074
      high-frequency information in addressing cross-domain issues and to further explore the potential
075   of low- and high-frequency information in unsupervised graph domain adaptation.
076
077   Inspired by the interesting findings above, we propose a novel and effective UGDA framework,
078
      named the synergy low-high frequency cross-domain network (SnLH). The framework concurrently
      optimizes both low-frequency and high-frequency information to alleviate the negative influence of
079
      domain dissimilarities in cross-domain circumstances. Specifically, we propose to use meticulously
080
      designed low- and high-frequency filters to separately extract low- and high-frequency information
081   at the graph level for both the source and target domains, thereby enhancing the semantic repre-
082   sentation of the graphs. For cross-domain low-frequency components, we employ a cross-domain
083   mutual information constraint strategy to maximize the interaction between low-frequency infor-
084   mation across domains, improving the model’s ability to learn from cross-domain low-frequency
085   information. However, relying solely on optimizing low-frequency information is insufficient to
086   narrow and fully reduce domain differences. Therefore, for cross-domain high-frequency com-
087   ponents, we introduce a cross-domain contrastive learning mechanism, aimed at finely tuning the
088   differences in high-frequency components between positive and negative samples across domains,
089
      thereby strengthening the model’s ability to distinguish cross-domain feature differences. Overall,
      our model captures and jointly optimizes both low- and high-frequency information across different
090
      domains, ensuring that the model learns representations that are both domain-invariant and discrimi-
091
      native. Our method has achieved significant improvements on benchmark datasets for graph domain
092   adaptation through extensive experimental validation, outperforming existing methods. We summa-
093   rize the contribution points as follows:
094
095
      • We conduct an experimental study and discover some distinctive advantages of spectral signals in
096     unsupervised graph domain adaptation tasks. Drawing inspiration from these findings, we have
097     devised corresponding filters to extract low- and high-frequency information at the graph level
098     from both the source and target domains. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to study
099     the spectral signal on the graph-level UGDA task.
100
101   • We propose a synergistic low-high frequency network (SnLH) that leverages cross-domain low
102     and high-frequency information. By imposing constraints on low- and high-frequency informa-
103     tion, SnLH effectively mitigates the impact of domain discrepancies on the model, enhancing its
104     generalization capability on the target domain.
105
106   • Extensive experiments demonstrate that spectral domain information plays an important role in
107     unsupervised graph domain adaptive tasks, our model achieves significant improvement on bench-
        mark datasets and outperforms state-of-the-art baselines.

      2.1   T YPICAL D OMAIN A DAPTION
111
112   Domain adaptation (DA), as a technique of transfer learning, is to improve the model’s ability to
113   generalize in scenarios with a different data distribution or label scarcity (Ben-David et al., 2006).
114   However, real-world datasets often lack reliable labels (Achituve et al., 2021), making unsupervised
115   domain adaptation a hot research topic. Current UDA methods are mainly divided into the following
116   categories: The maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) method (Sun & Saenko, 2016) aligns the
117   distributions by minimizing the MMD distance between the source and target features in a specific
118   kernel space, but it fails to align the high-order distribution differences; adversarial-based methods
119   (Ganin et al., 2016) distinguish the features of the source domain and the target domain through
120
      the domain discriminator. However the training process is unstable, which requires careful tuning
      of the discriminator and generator; the pseudo-label-based method (French et al., 2017) generates
121
      pseudo-labels for supervised learning of the target domain, while the quality of the pseudo-labels is
122
      difficult to guarantee, resulting in excessive deviation of the model training. These methods have
123   undoubtedly achieved outstanding accomplishments in their respective fields, but there are still some
124   unsolved challenges in these methods.
125
126
      2.2   U NSUPERVISED G RAPH D OMAIN A DAPTATION
127
128
      In practice, while existing unsupervised domain adaptation methods (Sun et al., 2017; Long et al.,
129   2018) have achieved remarkable success in computer vision and natural language processing, there
130   is a lack of unsupervised domain adaptation methods specifically designed for graph structure data
131   because of the unique nature of graph structure data. Current unsupervised GDA approaches (Lin
132   et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022a) primarily focus on transferring information (Yin et al., 2022) from the
133   source domain to the target domain using spatial operators of graph neural networks combined with
134   domain alignment techniques (Luo et al., 2023). However, most of these methods (Guo et al., 2022;
135   Zeng et al., 2024) overlook the significance of frequency domain information in graph domain adap-
136   tation (Yin et al., 2023). Therefore, in this paper we first explore the influence of frequency domain
137
      information and effectively leverage this knowledge to mitigate domain discrepancies, resulting in a
      significant improvement in the accuracy of the graph classification task.

201   4     M ETHODOLOGY
202
203   In this section, we explore the problem of UGDA from a spectral domain perspective and propose a
204   novel model called synergy low-high frequency cross-domain network. Specifically, inspired by the
205
      characteristics of low- and high-frequency graph information in cross-domain tasks, we designed
      a low and high-frequency filter that disentangles the semantic information of the graph into low
206
      and high-frequency components. To fully exploit these information, we imposed a mutual informa-
207
      tion maximization constraint on cross-domain low-frequency information and enhanced the model’s
208   ability to distinguish cross-domain feature differences through a cross-domain high-frequency con-
209   trastive learning mechanism. In the following, we will introduce each module in detail.
210

494
495   In this subsection, to further illustrate the influence of low- and high-frequency information on the
496   cross-domain task, we replace the input of the cross-domain mutual information maximization mod-
497
      ule and the cross-domain contrastive learning module with different signals, respectively. (1) L-F:
      Cross-domain low-frequency information is used for mutual information maximization constraint,
498
      and cross-domain mixed information is used for contrastive learning; (2) L-H: Cross-domain low-
499
      frequency information is used for mutual information maximization constraint, and cross-domain
500   high-frequency information is used for contrastive learning; (3) F-L: Cross-domain mixed infor-
501   mation is used for mutual information maximization constraint, and cross-domain low-frequency
502   information is used for contrastive learning; (4) F-H: Cross-domain mixed information is used for
503   mutual information maximization constraint, and cross-domain high-frequency information is used
504   for contrastive learning; (5) H-L: Cross-domain high-frequency information is used for mutual in-
505   formation maximization constraint, and cross-domain low-frequency information is used for con-
506   trastive learning; (6) H-F: Cross-domain high-frequency information is used for mutual information
507   maximization constraint, and cross-domain mixed information is used for contrastive learning. We
508
      tested and analyzed them on the datasets of Mutagenicity and NCI1. The experimental results are
      shown in Figure 3. The results show that low- and high-frequency information play different roles in
509
      the cross-domain task. Just as previous experimental study found for graph datasets from different
510
      domains, the low-frequency components of the same category of graphs in the spectral space show
511   apparent similarities, while the high-frequency components show significant differences. This may
512   be the core reason for the model to perform well in cross-domain tasks.

      5.5   H YPERPARAMETER S ENSITIVITY
515
516   In this subsection, to evaluate the influence of hyperparameter low- and high-frequency ratio λ on
517   model performance, we conduct experiments on Mutagenicity and NCI1 as shown in Figure 4. We
518   limit the range of the hyperparameter λ to [0-1] and fine-tune the hyperparameter with a span of 0.1.
519   The experimental results show that when the parameter is gradually increased from 0.0 to 1.0, the
520   overall accuracy of the model gradually increases and tends to be stable. When the proportion is
521
      small, high-frequency information accounts for the main part. According to our analysis, the reason
      is that high-frequency information cannot capture the global information between cross-domains
522
      well, which leads to the degradation of model performance. It shows that with the fusion ratio
523
      of low- and high-frequency information, and λ is 0.8 (the low-frequency ratio is 0.8 and the high-
524   frequency ratio is 0.2), the performance of the model reaches the best. At this time, the model can not
525   only capture more valuable global information by virtue of cross-domain low-frequency information
526   but also capture rich cross-domain local high-frequency information. Then, the contrastive learning
527   module is used to eliminate the difference of cross-domain high frequency.

653   We represent the overall algorithmic flow of the model as follows. Furthermore, the time complexity

655
656   A.1   T IME C OMPLEXITY OF L OW-H IGH F REQUENCY S IGNAL D ISENTANGLEMENT
657
      For the low- and high-frequency filter module, the number of nodes N , the number of edges |E|, the
658
      feature dimension F of each graph, and the operation of each layer are considered when calculating
659

      the time complexity. For the operation of each layer, the time complexity is O(|E| +  N × F 2 ) , and
660
      the model has L layers, so the overall time complexity is O L × (|E| + N × F 2 ) . It can be seen
661   that the overall time complexity of the low- and high-frequency filter module is mainly related to
662   the structure of the graph, that is, it is positively correlated with the number of nodes and the feature
663   dimension.
664
665   A.2   T IME C OMPLEXITY OF L OW-F REQUENCY I NTRA -C LASS C ONSISTENCY
666
667   For the part of low-frequency intra-class consistency, the calculation of time complexity mainly
668   involves the number of samples Ns and Nt of the source domain and the target domain, and the
669   number of classification classes C of the task, and the overall time complexity is O(Ns ×C+Nt ×C).
670
671   A.3   T IME COMPLEXITY OF HIGH - FREQUENCY CONTRASTIVE LEARNING
672
      For high-frequency contrastive learning, the computational time complexity mainly involves calcu-
673
      lating the similarity matrix and the cyclic traversal to find positive and negative samples. For the
674
      number of source domain and target domain graphs are Ns and Nt respectively, the time complexity
675   of computing the similarity matrix is O(Ns × Nt × F ), and the time
676
                                                                             complexity of cyclic traversal
      of positive and negative samples is O (Ns + Nt ) × max(Ns , Nt ) .
