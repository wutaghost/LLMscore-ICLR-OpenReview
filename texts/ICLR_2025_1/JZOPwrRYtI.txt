010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Temporal link prediction aims to forecast future link existence in temporal graphs,
013            with numerous real-world applications. Existing methods often rely on designing
014
               complex model architectures to parameterize the interaction patterns between nodes.
               Instead, we re-think the interaction dynamics in temporal graphs (which we call
015
               “interaction rhythms”) by addressing a fundamental research question: Is there a
016
               strong yet prevalent latent interaction rhythm pattern across different temporal
017            graphs that can be leveraged for temporal link prediction? Our introduced empiri-
018            cal analyses reveal that there indeed exists temporal clustering in node interaction
019            rhythms, where for a specific node, interactions tend to occur in bursts. Such
020            observation leads to two key insights for predicting future links: (i) recent historical
021            links that carry the latest rhythm pattern information; and (ii) the inter-event times
022            that further illuminate temporal dynamics. Building on these empirical findings,
023            we propose TG-Mixer, a novel method that explicitly captures temporal cluster-
024            ing patterns to advance temporal link prediction. TG-Mixer samples the most
025
               recent historical links to extract surrounding neighborhoods, preserving currently
               invaluable interaction rhythms while avoiding massive computations. Additionally,
026
               it integrates a carefully designed silence decay mechanism that penalizes nodes’
027
               long-term inactivity, effectively incorporating temporal clustering information for
028            future link prediction. Both components ensure concise implementations, leading
029            to a lightweight architecture. Exhaustive experiments on seven benchmarks against
030            nine baselines demonstrate that TG-Mixer achieves state-of-the-art performance
031            with faster convergence, stronger generalization capabilities, and higher efficiency.
032            The experimental results also highlight the importance of explicitly considering
033            temporal clustering for temporal link prediction.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Temporal graphs can model the dynamic graph-structured data in many real-world scenarios, where
039   objects are represented as nodes and timestamped interactions between them are depicted as temporal
040   links Wang et al. (2021d); Tian et al. (2024a). To effectively capture the dynamic nature of such
041
      graphs, researchers have developed Temporal Graph Networks (TGNs) Souza et al. (2022); Chen
      & Ying (2024). These networks effectively explore the temporal and topological information inside
042
      temporal graphs, thereby facilitating representation learning. Various downstream tasks have been
043
      studied based on existing TGNs already Li et al. (2023); Su et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b).
044   Among them, temporal link prediction, aiming to forecast the future link existence between potential
045   interaction node pairs Tian et al. (2024b), has extensive applications in various real-world systems,
046   such as forecasting users’ purchasing actions of items on recommender systems to enhance user
047   experiences Yin et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2024), and predicting the potential transaction between two
048   parties on payment platforms to prevent money laundering activities Duan et al. (2024).
049   Existing TGNs always focus on exploring various model architectures to parameterize the interaction
050   patterns in temporal graphs, like stacking temporal graph convolutions Zhang et al. (2023; 2024a),
051   encoding temporal random walks Wang et al. (2021c); Jin et al. (2022), or applying sequential
052   models Tian et al. (2024b). Although powerful, existing TGNs often invest considerable effort in
053   approximating their parameterized interaction patterns through extensive computations. However,
      such procedure tends to overlook the potentially heuristic, realistic patterns inherent in the temporal

      2     P RELIMINARIES
118   In this section, we first define the temporal link prediction task and then provide the definitions of the
119   key terminologies introduced in this paper.
120
121   2.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
122   If the interactions (or links) of a graph are associated with timestamps, we name it a temporal graph.
123
124
      Definition 1. Temporal Graph. Given node set V, a temporal graph defined based on it can be
      represented as a sequence of chronological temporal links G = {(u1 , v1 , t1 ), (u2 , v2 , t2 ), ...} where
125
      0 ≤ t1 ≤ t2 ≤ .... Each link (u, v, t) ∈ G corresponds to an interaction between a pair of interaction
126
      nodes u ∈ V and v ∈ V at timestamp t. Each node u ∈ V involves node feature xu ∈ RdN and each
127   link (u, v, t) attaches link feature etuv ∈ RdL , where dN and dL are the feature dimensions of the
128   nodes and links, respectively. If the graph is non-attributed, we simply let both of the node and link
129   features to zero vectors, i.e., x∗ = 0 and et∗∗ = 0.
130
      We define temporal link prediction on the batch scale.
131
132   Definition 2. Temporal Link Prediction. Given a batch size B ∈ Z+ , a set of interaction nodes
                 B                       B                                                 B
133   {ub ∈ V}b=1 and {vb ∈ V}b=1 , and corresponding timestamps {tb > 0}b=1 , temporal link predic-
134   tion aims to predict whether each node pair (ub , vb ) interacts at timestamp tb based on the historical
135   links {(u′ , v ′ , t′ )|t′ < tb } ⊆ G, i.e., predicting the existence of the future link (ub , vb , tb ).
136
137
      2.2   K EY T ERMINOLOGIES
138   Now, we define the introduced concept of node interaction rhythms among temporal graphs.
139
      Definition 3. Node Interaction Rhythm. Node interaction rhythms indicate the interaction dynamics
140
      for a specific node in temporal graphs. Given a temporal graph G, for node u ∈ V, we can derive
141
      a sequence of u’s interaction timestamps Tu = {t′1 , t′2 ...} where 0 ≤ t′1 ≤ t′2 ≤ .... Each t′i ∈ Tu
142   records “when” the node u is involved in an interaction. The distribution patterns among Tu
143   encapsulate the dynamics of u’s interactions, which we refer to as the interaction rhythms of node u.
144
145
      Temporal clustering investigates the concentration pattern of node interaction rhythms.
146   Definition 4. Temporal Clustering. Temporal clustering in node interaction rhythms reveals the
147   following two interaction patterns among temporal graphs: (i) a specific node’s interactions tend
148   to occur frequently within certain time periods; and (ii) its interactions at other times are relatively
149   few. This results in dense interaction rhythms during specific intervals while remaining sparse at
150   other times, leading to a clustered pattern in the temporal dimensions. Therefore, we refer to such
151
      interaction patterns in temporal graphs as temporal clustering.

248   powerful node interaction rhythms, promotes the inclusion of temporal clustering information in
249   predictions. Both insights require straightforward implementation and rapid computation. To this
250   end, we could develop a lightweight model that explicitly considers temporal clustering, facilitating
251   effective and efficient temporal link prediction.

      The architecture of the TG-Mixer is depicted in Figure 4. Given node u at timestamp t, we first sample
      its most recent historical links to preserve fresh interaction rhythm patterns and obtain link sequences
256
      Sut . Then, we encode and pad the information of those links in Sut , resulting in three unified encodings.
257
      Furthermore, we concatenate these encodings and feed them into the TG-Mixer encoder, where we
258   introduce a silence decay mechanism for fully capturing temporal clustering information. Finally, our
259   temporal link decoder derives the temporal node representations for temporal link prediction.
260
261
      Sampling the most recent historical links. Most existing TGNs employ neighbor selection strategies
      to extract nodes’ surrounding neighborhoods for representation, such as “sampling multi-hop most
262
      recent links” or “extracting all 1-hop links”. For example, models like TGN Rossi et al. (2020a)
263
      sample the multi-hop most recent historical links while other models like DyGFormer Yu et al. (2023)
264   extract all 1-hop historical links. Although both strategies could help preserve temporal clustering
265   patterns among neighborhoods, they may risk incorporating spurious or noisy information either
266   from high-order connections Rossi et al. (2020b) or long-outdated histories Zhang et al. (2023).
267   Besides, they could suffer from high inefficiency and complexity due to handling a massive number
268   of selected historical neighbors Cong et al. (2023). To address these issues, we exclusively sample
269   the most recent 1-hop historical links, preserving the latest relevant temporal clustering patterns
      while ensuring a conceptually and technically efficient neighbor selection strategy. Formally, for

451   5.3                         B ENEFITS OF T EMPORAL C LUSTERING FROM S ILENCE D ECAY M ECHANISM
452   Now, we validate the benefits of explicitly incorporating temporal clustering by our silence decay
453   mechanism, and summarize two insightful observations as follows:
454
      Temporal clustering can provide a highly discriminative training signal in TG-Mixer. To under-
455   stand the effectiveness of temporal clustering, we visualize the complementary decay coefficients in
456   Equation 9, 1 − g(stu ), from both positive and negative links during binary classification training, as
457   described in Section B.3. A small value indicates a weak temporal clustering, resulting in more severe
458   decay produced by the silence decay mechanism. Similar to the micro-level analyses in Section 3, we
459   visualize the complementary coefficients between positive and negative links over individual time
460   steps in Figures 6 & 10. Our silence decay mechanism provides a highly discriminative training signal
461   between positive and negative links: Compared to positive links, the interaction nodes of negative
462   links exhibit significantly lower coefficients, leading to greater decay and more severe penalties. This
463
      is because the interaction nodes of negative links tend to exhibit weaker temporal clustering, thus
      producing more easily distinguishable interaction rhythm signals compared to positive links. As a
464
      result, TG-Mixer can capture these dominant signals from temporal clustering, thus generating more
465
      expressive representations for prediction. This capability may be the key reason for its state-of-the-art
466   performance with a lightweight model architecture.
467
      Temporal clustering can be easily adopted to boost existing sequential TGNs. Silence decay
468
      mechanism is versatile and can be easily integrated into existing sequential TGNs. This is because
469   these methods learn from nodes’ 1-hop historical links and we can decay their neighbor information
470   directly. The detailed implementations can be found in Section C.10 of the Appendix. We integrate
471   the silence decay mechanism into TCL Wang et al. (2021a), GraphMixer Cong et al. (2023), and
472   DyGFormer Yu et al. (2023), and present the temporal link prediction results in Tables 3 & 13. We
473   find all three models demonstrate certain performance improvements after considering temporal
474   clustering information in prediction. Additionally, the most significant performance improvements
475   are observed in some datasets with extremely strong temporal clustering patterns revealed in our
476   empirical analyses, such as LastFM and Flights. This observation further validates the effectiveness
477
      and importance of temporal clustering. We emphasize that TG-Mixer still outperforms, highlighting
      the necessity of the well-designed temporal mixer. This module allows TG-Mixer to better extract
478
      temporal clustering information compared to directly decaying neighbor information.

531   6      C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we observe a prevalent yet strong pattern of temporal clustering in node interaction
533
      rhythms among temporal graphs and utilize this interesting phenomenon for advancing temporal
534   link prediction. We propose TG-Mixer, an effective and efficient method that explicitly considers
535   temporal clustering information by learning from the most recent historical links and penalizing
536   the nodes’ long-term inactivity in a decaying manner. Extensive experimental results demonstrate
537   the superiority of TG-Mixer and the benefits of temporal clustering, motivating us to re-think the
538   importance of incorporating interaction dynamics for temporal link prediction. In the future, not
539   limited to temporal link prediction, we will extend our algorithms for different downstream tasks,
      such as evolving node classification.

758   In this section, we provide the theoretical analysis of our proposed silence decay mechanism using
759   the theory of Hawkes Process. Hawkes Process Brémaud & Massoulié (1996) is a stochastic process
760   that allows the occurrence of an event to increase the probability of future events. The occurrence of
761   an event raises the probability of a new event occurring within a short period afterward. This self-
762   excitation makes the Hawkes Process particularly suitable for describing sequences of interactions
763   characterized by temporal clustering. We first present the basic definition of the Hawkes Process.
764   Definition 1. Hawkes Process. For K ∈ N and [K] = 1, . . . , K, the point process can be desribed as
765   N = (N1t , · · · , NK  t

767   λt = (λt1 , · · · , λtK ), which is informally the infinitesimal rate of an event conditionally on the past of
768   the process. In the nonlinear Hawkes model, the intensity process has the following form:

775   describes the cascade of historical influences on the dynamic network system Crane & Sornette
776   (2008). This process captures how previous attention from one individual’s history can spread to the
777   present time and become the cause that triggers their future attention. Therefore, if a given entity
778   whose interests make it susceptible to a certain recent interaction, it may trigger action through a

899
900   Tasks and Metrics. We conduct the experiments through the temporal link prediction task. Following
901   Zhang et al. (2023), we employ two settings: the transductive setting, which predicts future links
902   between nodes that have been observed during the training phase, and the inductive setting, which
903
      involves link prediction between unseen nodes. Moreover, we use Average Precision (AP) and Area
      Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC) as our evaluation metrics. It is
904
      important to note that we follow the same configures as Yu et al. (2023), and thus, we maintain the
905
      table numbers of baselines reported in its publication.
906
907   Training and Evaluation. We follow Yu et al. (2023) and adopt a mini-batch training process.
908   Specifically, we identify the links within each mini-batch as positive and generate an equal number
909
      of negative links by randomly sampling node pairs within the training data. In practice, to improve
      consistency, we fix the source nodes and sample an equal number of the destination nodes to construct
910
      negative links. Therefore, the negative edges share the same source nodes as the positive edges.
911
      Subsequently, we can utilize our temporal link decoder mentioned in Section 4 for binary classification
912   to predict the link existence among these node pairs. Additionally, recall that we maintain a rhythm
913   vector globally for all nodes and update it along the timestamps. For batch processing, we maintain a
914   rhythm matrix with a size corresponding to the batch size, which is dynamically updated throughout
915   training. We train the models for 100 epochs and employ an early stopping strategy with a patience
916   of 20. For all results, we utilize Adam Kingma & Ba (2014) for model optimization and standardize
917   the learning rate and batch size across all models and datasets to 0.0001 and 200, respectively. To
      ensure reliability, we run the models five times with seeds ranging from 0 to 4 and report the averaged

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      performance to minimize variations. All experiments are conducted on a single server equipped with
919   72 cores, 32GB of memory, and an Nvidia Tesla V100 GPU.

924

       C.2           A DDITIONAL E XPERIMENTS U NDER D IFFERENT N EGATIVE S AMPLING S TRATEGIES
973
974    Inspired by recent studies Poursafaei et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2024) that provide the suggestion of
975    robust evaluation in temporal link prediction, we adopt more challenging evaluation scenarios with
976    different negative sampling strategies and rank-based evaluation metrics to carefully assess model
977    performance. Based on this motivation, we conduct additional experiments to evaluate the models
978    under random negative sampling, historical negative sampling, inductive negative sampling, and
979    degree-aware negative sampling using the MRR metric.
980    In addition to the random negative sampling strategy used in the main experiments, we follow
981    Poursafaei et al. (2022) and employ historical negative sampling (sampling negative links that have
982    been observed before but are absent in the current step) and inductive negative sampling (sampling
983    negative links are not observed during training). To further mitigate potential biases from popular
984    nodes during evaluation, we construct negative links by sampling negative destination nodes based on
985    their historical degree distribution. Specifically, we sample a destination node with the probability of
986
       d/N , where d is the node’s current degree and N denotes the total number of historical interactions.
       For the evaluation metrics, we follow TGB Huang et al. (2024) and sample 100 negative edges per
987
       positive edge and employ Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) as our evaluation metric.
988
989    From the results shown in Figure 7, we find that: (i) TG-Mixer consistently demonstrates strong
990    performance across all negative sampling strategies under the MRR metric, further proving its
991    robustness and effectiveness for temporal link prediction. (ii) All models experience some degree of
992
       performance degradation under different negative sampling strategies. Such challenging evaluation
       scenarios also amplify the differentiation in model performance. (iii) Inductive negative sampling
993
       tends to cause the most performance drop. This is likely because this strategy samples unseen nodes
994
       to construct negative links, making it more challenging for models to accurately distinguish between
995    positive and negative links.

1010   C.3           A DDITIONAL E XPERIMENTS U NDER D IFFERENT BATCH S IZES
1011
       In this section, we evaluate model performance under different batch sizes. The batch-based training
1012
       approach, introduced by Rossi et al. (2020a), has been widely adopted in the temporal graph
1013
       community. This method processes interactions in batches following their chronological order.
1014   Specifically, it first sorts all interactions by timestamp and then groups them into batches using
1015   the predefined batch size. In this way, Back Propagation Through Time (BPTT) within each batch
1016   allows the models to update in chronological order. Despite its efficiency, a fundamental issue with
1017   this approach is that all predictions within a given batch share the same model state, which may be
1018   outdated for later interactions in the batch. It is particularly problematic for memory-based methods,
1019   as they always explicitly maintain an up-to-date memory component, such as the rhythm vector in
1020   TG-Mixer. While the memory state for the first interaction in the batch is up-to-date (as it incorporates
1021   information from all previous interactions), the memory state for the last interaction in the batch is
1022
       out-of-date (as it does not include information from previous interactions within the same batch).
1023   Based on the above motivation, we conduct additional experiments to evaluate model performance
1024   under different batch sizes. From the results in Figure 8, we find that: (i) TG-Mixer continues
1025   to achieve the best performance across various batch sizes, further validating its effectiveness and
       robustness for temporal link prediction. (ii) Although the performance ranking of models remains


                                                                                                                                                  19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       unchanged, memory-based methods (e.g., TG-Mixer and TGN) tend to perform better with smaller
1027   batch sizes. This underscores the limitations of the existing batch training approach when applied to
1028   larger batch sizes. It also highlights the need for a more effective parallel processing method, which
1029   is beyond the scope of this work and we leave this as a future research direction.

1309   D.1                            R ELATED W ORK
1310
1311   Temporal Graph Networks (TGNs). According to the granularity of temporal information, dynamic
1312   graphs can be categorized into two primary types: Discrete-Time Dynamic Graphs (DTDGs), also
1313   known as Discrete Graphs, and Continuous-Time Dynamic Graphs (CTDGs), also referred to as
1314   Temporal Graphs Liang et al. (2023). Discrete graphs are characterized by multiple graph snapshots
1315   taken at fixed time intervals, where each snapshot represents a static graph, and all snapshots are
1316
       ordered chronologically Xue et al. (2022); Kazemi et al. (2020). Existing methods for handling
       discrete graphs typically apply static graph techniques to each snapshot individually, with RNNs
1317
       Pareja et al. (2020) or attention mechanisms Sankar et al. (2020); Cong et al. (2021) to capture
1318
       temporal dependencies across different snapshots.
1319
1320   In contrast, temporal graphs Wen & Fang (2022); Tian et al. (2024b); Xiang et al. (2023) encapsulate
1321   more granular temporal information, with each edge explicitly annotated with interaction timestamps.
1322
       This fine-grained temporal information allows the graph to evolve continuously over time. Conse-
       quently, temporal graphs present more challenges and opportunities. For capturing the dynamics and
1323
       topologies within temporal graphs, Temporal Graph Networks (TGNs5 ) have been widely studied in
1324
       recent years Goyal et al. (2020); Fan et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021e); Rossi et al. (2020b); Gao
1325   & Ribeiro (2022); Chang et al. (2020); Pareja et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024);
1326   Zhang et al. (2024b). Most existing TGNs try to parameterize the time-dependent interaction patterns
1327   via their designed complex architectures, such as temporal graph convolutions Wang et al. (2021d;b);
1328   Alvarez-Rodriguez et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022); Luo & Li (2022); Zhang et al. (2024a), temporal
1329   random walks Wang et al. (2021c); Souza et al. (2022); Jin et al. (2022), and sequential models Wang
1330   et al. (2021a); Cong et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023); Tian et al. (2024b). Although powerful, these
1331   methods overlook high-level temporal correlations and rhythm patterns among node interactions in
1332   raw data. In this paper, we introduce some empirical analyses to observe temporal clustering within
1333
       node interaction rhythms and leverage this interesting phenomenon for advancing temporal link
       prediction. To this end, we propose a novel method to show the necessity of explicitly considering
1334
       temporal clustering, which is achieved by two designs: a neighbor selection strategy and a temporal
1335
       mixer with a silence decay mechanism.
1336
1337   Existing Studies on Bursty Behavior. We notice that some studies explore the effects of bursty
1338   interaction patterns in temporal networks Karsai et al. (2018); Hiraoka et al. (2020); Jo (2023).
1339
       However, these works focus on designing statistical models to analyze the burst events and their
       impacts on the distribution characteristics Karsai et al. (2011), correlations Lambiotte et al. (2013);
1340
       Min & Goh (2013), and propagation processes of inter-event times Barabasi (2005); Sheng et al.
1341
       (2023). Instead, this paper leverages bursty interaction patterns to design a deep learning model that
1342   facilitates representation learning in temporal graphs. Additionally, these works are outside the ML
1343   community and primarily published in the field of physical sciences, which is definitely outside the
1344   scope of our paper.
1345
       Difference Discussions. To provide a clearer understanding of the concept of temporal clustering
1346
       introduced in our paper, we briefly differentiate between “temporal clustering” and “clustering tasks
1347
       among temporal graphs” Liu et al. (2023).
1348
          5
1349        For consistency, we follow Souza et al. (2022) in using “TGNs” to refer to a family of models for temporal
       graph representation learning. This term differs from the specific model “TGN” proposed by Rossi et al. (2020a).

1350
       Temporal clustering introduced in our paper reflects a distinctive dynamics of interaction patterns
1351   within temporal graphs: node interactions tend to occur in bursts, leading to a concentration or
1352   clustering of occurrence along the temporal dimensions. Different from temporal clustering, clustering
1353   tasks Yao & Joe-Wong (2021) are defined based on the topological or connectivity tendencies of
1354   nodes among graphs. In these tasks, nodes within a cluster are densely connected, while nodes across
1355   different clusters exhibit sparse connectivity. Recently, clustering tasks among temporal graphs Li
1356   et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023; 2024) have emerged as a significant research trend in graph clustering.
1357   These studies often focus on adapting static clustering techniques to temporal graphs. For example,
1358   TGC Liu et al. (2023) investigates and introduces deep temporal graph clustering, which proposes a
1359
       clustering method to suit the interaction sequence-based batch-processing of temporal graphs.
1360   In this paper, we incorporate the high-level dynamics of interaction patterns (i.e., temporal clustering)
1361   for the temporal link prediction task, presenting a lightweight solution that achieves both effectiveness
1362   and efficiency. Our work is fundamentally different from the above clustering tasks in temporal
1363   graphs or other tasks that leverage topological connectivity density for performance improvements.
1364   Therefore, the above methods fall outside the scope of our paper, and we do not discuss their details.

1366   D.2    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
1367
1368   In addition to the experimental evaluation of model complexity in Section 5.2, we also provide
1369
       a theoretical complexity analysis of the main components in TG-Mixer and selected baselines.
       Specifically, we choose three baselines: TGN Rossi et al. (2020a), CAWN Wang et al. (2021c), and
1370
       DyGFormer Yu et al. (2023), as they represent the most representative method in temporal graph
1371
       convolution models, temporal walk models, and sequential models, respectively.
1372
1373   Let L represent the number of interaction sequences, D denote the average node degree, K indicate
1374   the number of encoding layers, and m depict the sample size in the neighbor selection strategy. To
1375
       simplify the calculations, we assume that the models’ input, hidden, and output dimensions are
       uniformly set to d. TG-Mixer generates node representations by sampling the most recent historical
1376
       links and then using  a token mixer and a temporal mixer, leading to a computational complexity of
1377
       O mLd + mLd2 . TGN samples multi-hop most recent historical links, updates nodes’ memory
1378   with RNNs, and employs a temporal attention mechanism      to summarize link information, resulting

1379   in an overall complexity of O Ld2 + m2 KLd . CAWN encodes temporal walks with RNNs and
1380   aggregates these walks using an attention mechanism, which results in a computational complexity
1381   of O nKLd2 + n2 Ld where n represents the number of temporal walks. DyGFormer extracts all
1382   1-hop historical links and employs a multi-layer Transformer
                                                                     encoder to aggregate link information,
1383   resulting in a complexity of O KLD2 d + KLd .

1385   D.3    L IMITATIONS
1386
1387   One potential limitation of TG-Mixer is the cold start issue for learning temporal clustering at the
1388   beginning of the training (discussed in Section 5.2). However, after several epochs, TG-Mixer
1389   effectively captures and leverages the powerful predictive signals derived from explicitly considering
1390
       temporal clustering, and achieves outstanding performance compared to the baselines.
1391   Another potential limitation of TG-Mixer is its neighbor selection strategy that samples from 1-hop
1392   historical links. It may be suboptimal when handling certain scenarios in which nodes’ high-order
1393   relationships are crucial. However, simply incorporating multi-hop links into TG-Mixer would
1394   significantly increase computational costs and weaken its ability to capture temporal clustering. There
1395   is significant potential in designing both efficient and effective methods to capture nodes’ high-order
1396
       relationships for better temporal link prediction.
