011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            While language models have demonstrated impressive capabilities across a range
014            of tasks, they still struggle with tasks that require complex planning and reasoning.
015            Recent studies have proposed training language models on search processes rather
016
               than optimal solutions, resulting in better generalization performance even though
               search processes are noisy and even suboptimal. However, these studies overlook
017
               the value of optimal solutions, which can serve as step-by-step landmarks to guide
018
               more effective search. In this work, we explore how to leverage optimal solutions
019            to enhance the search and planning abilities of language models. To this end, we
020            propose guided stream of search (GSoS), which seamlessly incorporates optimal
021            solutions into the self-generation process in a progressive manner, producing high-
022            quality search trajectories. These trajectories are then distilled into the pre-trained
023            model via supervised fine-tuning. Our approach significantly enhances the search
024            and planning abilities of language models on Countdown, a simple yet challenging
025            mathematical reasoning task. Notably, combining our method with RL fine-tuning
026            yields further improvements, whereas previous supervised fine-tuning methods do
027
               not benefit from RL. Furthermore, our approach exhibits greater effectiveness than
               leveraging optimal solutions in the form of subgoal rewards.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Transformer-based language models have achieved remarkable success, demonstrating human-level
      performance across a wide range of natural language tasks, including conversation, code generation,
034
      and mathematical problem-solving (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Roziere et al., 2023;
035
      Li et al., 2023; Lightman et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024). Their impressive performance is primarily
036   attributed to auto-regressive training on high-quality, internet-scale data. However, language models
037   still face challenges with tasks that require complex planning and reasoning (Pallagani et al., 2023;
038   Valmeekam et al., 2023). Models trained using next-token prediction often result in the snowballing
039   of errors over long sequences, making it difficult for them to maintain consistent plans over multiple
040   steps. Furthermore, teacher-forcing, where models are given the correct sequence of previous tokens
041   for each prediction, exacerbates this problem by encouraging them to learn shortcuts rather than truly
042   understanding the underlying structure of the task (Bachmann & Nagarajan, 2024).
043   A growing body of literature attempts to improve the planning and reasoning capabilities of language
044   models through prompt-based strategies, allowing them to perform chain-of-thought reasoning, self-
045   correction, and planning with symbolic search algorithms (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Shinn
046   et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023). While these methods are successful in certain tasks, they have inherent
047   limitations. They only assist the model during inference without updating its internal weights, which
048   significantly constrains performance to that of the base model. Moreover, their success heavily relies
049   on the quality of prompt design, and a poorly constructed prompt sometimes degrades performance
050
      (Huang et al., 2024).
051   To address these limitations, recent studies have shifted toward directly improving the planning and
052   reasoning abilities of language models during the training phase (Lehnert et al., 2024; Gandhi et al.,
053   2024). This approach, known as stream of search (SoS), involves training a model to predict search
      trajectories that encompass the entire decision-making process of finding solutions through trial and

074   that models trained on search trajectories, despite being noisy and even suboptimal, achieve superior
075   generalization performance to those trained to imitate optimal action sequences via behavior cloning
076   (BC) (Ross et al., 2011). They have also demonstrated that fine-tuning on self-generated data further
077
      improves the search and planning capabilities of the models to some extent. However, this leads to a
      critical question: if models trained on search trajectories perform better, does this imply that optimal
078
      solutions are unnecessary? Optimal action sequences can serve as valuable landmarks, or subgoals,
079
      within the search process, guiding the model toward more effective search strategies. However, prior
080   studies have underexplored the role of optimal solutions, focusing exclusively on fully self-generated
081   data to improve the model, which is inherently limited.
082
083
      In this work, we explore how optimal solutions can be leveraged to enhance the search and planning
      abilities of language models pre-trained on search trajectories. We observe that the pre-trained model
084
      successfully discovers solutions by searching when prompted with partial optimal solutions as hints,
085
      even when these solutions are unseen during pre-training. While this guidance produces high-quality
086   data for supervised fine-tuning, it often has a low likelihood under the pre-trained model, potentially
087   causing negative effects on the model’s search and planning capabilities. To address this problem, we
088   propose guided stream of search (GSoS), which seamlessly incorporates optimal solutions to direct
089   the self-generation process and produces trajectories that have both high quality and likelihood. The
090   key idea of GSoS is to integrate each intermediate action from the optimal solution into the trajectory
091   in a step-by-step manner using unsuccessful search traces as contexts, as illustrated in Figure 1. We
092   then fine-tune the pre-trained model to predict these augmented trajectories.
093   We evaluate our approach on Countdown (Gandhi et al., 2024), a mathematical reasoning benchmark
094   that requires complex search and planning. Notably, our approach achieves an accuracy gain of 13%
095   compared to the pre-trained model. When further applying RL fine-tuning tailored to handle the long
096   contexts of search trajectories, this improvement increases to 20%, substantially outperforming both
097   supervised and RL fine-tuned baselines. In contrast, standard supervised fine-tuning methods, which
098   rely solely on self-generated data, do not benefit from RL fine-tuning. We conduct a comprehensive
099   analysis of how unsuccessful search trajectories play an important role in generating trajectories with
100
      both high quality and likelihood. Moreover, we demonstrate that our approach is more effective than
      leveraging optimal solutions as subgoal rewards during RL fine-tuning (Lightman et al., 2023).

105   2.1      P ROBLEM SETUP
106
107   In this paper, we consider a sequential decision-making problem derived from the program synthesis
      literature, which requires strong search and planning abilities (Devlin et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2024).


                                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Each instance of this problem is defined as a tuple x = (I, O), where I denotes the input state and O
109   denotes the output state. We are given a set of operations P, where each operation P ∈ P maps one
110   state to another, producing an intermediate state. There exists an optimal sequence of operations ŷ =
111   (P1 , P2 , . . . , PN ) that transforms the input state into the output state, i.e., ŷ = PN ◦ · · · ◦ P2 ◦ P1 (x).
112   Given a training set D, our goal is to train a model fθ to generate optimal solutions for a test set.
113
114   2.2    S TREAM OF SEARCH
115
116   One straightforward approach to this problem is to apply imitation learning through BC (Ross et al.,
117   2011), which trains the model to directly predict optimal solutions from input-output pairs. However,
118
      prior studies have demonstrated that BC struggles to generalize to unseen test examples (Yang et al.,
      2022; Lehnert et al., 2024; Gandhi et al., 2024).
119
120   To address this, SoS introduces an approach that leverages the search process rather than the optimal
121   solution (Gandhi et al., 2024). This method reformulates the problem as a tree search, navigating the
122   search tree through trial and error with operations until the solution is reached. Each node in the tree
123   represents a state, and each edge represents an operation between two states. SoS expresses primitive
124
      operations for the tree search in language, including node generation, exploration, backtracking, and
      verification. It then generates search trajectories using symbolic search algorithms, including depth-
125
      first search (DFS) and breadth-first search (BFS), representing them as sequences of tokens. Finally,
126
      it trains a language model to predict these trajectories from input-output pairs used as prompts:
127
                                      max Ex∼D,y∼symbolic(x) [log fθ (y | x)] .
128                                       θ
129   Note that the SoS model is not specifically designed to find solutions, as symbolic search algorithms
130   may produces suboptimal trajectories with a limited search budget. See Appendix A for more details.

132   2.3    S UPERVISED FINE - TUNING
133
134
      The pre-trained language model, not originally designed for particular tasks, requires alignment with
      a downstream task to enhance its performance. One approach to achieving this alignment is to apply
135
      supervised fine-tuning with self-generated data, referred to as self-taught reasoner (STaR) (Zelikman
136
      et al., 2022; Gulcehre et al., 2023). This method generates trajectories using the model from prompts
137   and filters them based on a task-specific metric M that evaluates quality. It then fine-tunes the model
138   to predict these filtered trajectories:
139                           max Ex∼D,y∼fθ (·|x) [1 [M (y | x) > τ ] · log fθ (y | x)] ,                 (1)
140                              θ
141   where τ is the threshold that controls the ratio of filtered trajectories. This process can be performed
142   iteratively to further refine the model.
143
144   2.4    R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING FINE - TUNING
145
      Another approach to aligning the pre-trained language model is to perform RL fine-tuning (Stiennon
146
      et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). This method uses the model as a policy πθ and fine-tunes the policy
147   to maximize a task-specific reward R, while minimizing the KL divergence from the reference policy
148   πref . The problem is formulated as a token-level finite-horizon Markov decision process (MDP). The
149   state space S is the set of all possible token sequences, and the action space A is the set of all tokens.
150   The initial state s0 ∈ D is a randomly sampled prompt. Each state sh ∈ S is the concatenation of the
151   prompts and the previously generated tokens, with the transition function p : S × A → S appending
152   the action ah ∈ A to the state. Given a trajectory generated by the policy with horizon H ∈ Z+ , the
153   policy is trained to optimize the following objective:
                                            "H                                                              #

155         max Es0 ∼D,(sh ,ah )∼πθ (·|s0 )      R(sh , ah ) − β · (log πθ (ah | sh ) − log πref (ah | sh )) ,
             θ
156                                           h=0

157   where β > 0 is the coefficient that controls the influence of the KL divergence. Typically, the reward
158
      is provided at the end of the trajectory.
159   A common algorithm for training this policy is proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al.,
160   2017). This method optimizes the policy using the clipped surrogate objective, which is designed to
161   limit policy changes. Simultaneously, it trains a value function Vϕ : S → R to predict the multi-step
      returns computed by the generalized advantage estimator (GAE) (Schulman et al., 2016).

      2.5   C OUNTDOWN BENCHMARK
163
164   To evaluate the search and planning abilities of language models, we use Countdown as a benchmark
165   (Gandhi et al., 2024). Each problem consists of input numbers and a target number, where the goal
166   is to transform the inputs into the target using the four basic arithmetic operations. This problem has
167   a high branching factor of O(k 2 ) in the search tree, where k is the number of remaining inputs. To
168   ensure a tractable level of difficulty, we set the number of initial inputs to 4, following the setup in
169   Gandhi et al. (2024). See Appendix B for more details on Countdown.
170
171   3     M ETHODS
172
173   3.1   M OTIVATION
174
175   Gandhi et al. (2024) show that training a language model using noisy, suboptimal search trajectories
176   leads to better generalization compared to clean, optimal solutions. That said, optimal solutions can
177   still offer valuable guidance during generation. If the SoS model has the ability to continue the search
178   from arbitrary incomplete trajectories, we can leverage this to generate high-quality trajectories. By
179
      providing the model with partial optimal solutions alongside the prompts as hints, we can guide it to
      continue the search within a reduced search space, increasing the probability of finding a solution.
180
181   To examine whether the SoS model possesses this ability, we conduct an experiment on Countdown.
182   For each problem with an optimal solution of N operations, we define the partial optimal path as the
183   trajectory generated by applying the first n operations in the search tree. We then append this path to
184   the initial prompt. Finally, we generate search trajectories using the SoS model from these modified
185
      prompts and evaluate correctness. Table 1 shows the ratio of successful trajectories with varying the
      length of partial optimal solutions for 200,000 training problems. The model successfully discovers
186
      solutions starting from these paths, despite not having encountered them during training. Moreover,
187
      increasing their length greatly improves the correctness of the resulting trajectories. This encourages
188   us to use these high-quality, self-generated data for supervised fine-tuning, distilling the knowledge
189   of optimal solutions into the model.
190
191
      However, this guidance results in low likelihood under the model. Table 1 presents the cross-entropy
      loss of the trajectories, showing that using longer partial optimal paths results in higher loss values.
192
      Fine-tuning on these trajectories may lead to significant changes in the model’s weights, potentially
193
      degrading its search and planning abilities. Therefore, it is crucial to explore methods for effectively
194   integrating optimal solutions to produce trajectories that maintain both high likelihood and quality.
195

      3.2   G UIDED STREAM OF SEARCH
205
206   In this subsection, we introduce guided stream of search (GSoS), a supervised fine-tuning approach
207   that seamlessly incorporates optimal solutions into the self-generation process and effectively distills
208   them into the model. The key idea of GSoS is to leverage an unsuccessful search trajectory as context
209   for each intermediate step of the optimal solution. This approach effectively mimics how the model
210   discovers the solution through the search procedure, producing a trajectory that has a high likelihood
211   under the SoS model. Moreover, by providing an exploratory context for reaching each intermediate
212
      step, it facilitates distilling the optimal solutions into the model (Yang et al., 2022).
213   Before delving into our approach, we establish some notations. We define subgoal nodes as the non-
214   leaf nodes along the optimal path in the search tree. An optimal solution consisting of N operations
215   contains N −1 subgoal nodes. We define the generation and exploration lines of a node as sequences
      of tokens that represent the primitive operations for generating and exploring the node, respectively.

      Note that while we include the root node as a subgoal node for simplicity, it is always explored prior
      to the search since it is already present in the prompt.
238
239   GSoS incorporates each subgoal into a trajectory in a progressive manner. Consider an unsuccessful
240   search trajectory that has reached the (n − 1)-th subgoal but fails to navigate its subtree, thereby not
241   reaching the n-th subgoal. First, it selects the k-th explored child node from the (n − 1)-th subgoal
242   node. Subsequently, it replaces this child node with the n-th subgoal node, modifying its generation
243
      and exploration lines within the trajectory. While multiple exploration lines may exist, only the first
      one is modified. Finally, it prunes the trajectory beyond the modified exploration line, allowing the
244
      model to restart the search from the subgoal node. An example of the resulting trajectory is provided
245
      in Figure 12 in Appendix C. GSoS uses this trajectory as a prompt to generate a new search trajectory
246   using the SoS model. This procedure, called subgoal augmentation, is summarized in Figure 2 and
247   Algorithm 1. It is repeated until all subgoals are incorporated into the trajectory.
248

260   Note that the exploration history from the first to the (k−1)-th explored child node is used as context
261   for the subgoal node, where k determines the size of this context. We consider three node selection
262   strategies to control the context size and use random node selection as the base strategy:
263
264            • First: This strategy selects the first explored child node, providing minimal context.
265            • Rand: This strategy randomly selects an explored child node, providing varied context.
266            • Last: This strategy selects the last explored child node, providing maximal context.

269   model on the filtered data, resulting in the GSoS model. Algorithm 2 outlines the overall procedure

      3.3                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      O PERATION - LEVEL RL FINE - TUNING
303   Building on the GSoS model, we apply RL fine-tuning to further enhance its performance. However,
304   this poses challenges since the model generates search trajectories that are much longer than optimal
305   paths. The extremely long sequences, combined with the sparse rewards given only at the end, hinder
306   the effective propagation of the reward signal to earlier timesteps, even when multi-step returns with
307   GAE are used for training the value function.
308
      To address this, we formulate the problem as an operation-level MDP, which significantly reduces its
309   effective horizon. The action space consists of the primitive operations for tree search defined in SoS,
310   separated by newline tokens within the trajectory, as shown in Figure 3. The state space includes all
311   possible operation sequences ending with a newline token. Accordingly, the value function is trained
312   to predict values exclusively at these newline tokens, as illustrated in Figure 3. This operation-level
313   approach reduces the effective horizon to less than 5%, facilitating better reward signal propagation.
314   See Appendix D for a more detailed explanation.

360      For unsupervised pre-training, we create 500,000 training problems and generate search trajectories
361      using heuristic-guided DFS and BFS. For supervised fine-tuning, we generate trajectories from the
362      first 200,000 training problems with a temperature of 0.8, filtering them based on their correctness.
363      We perform this fine-tuning for three iterations, following Gandhi et al. (2024). For RL fine-tuning,
364      we generate trajectories from 25,600 randomly sampled training problems with a temperature of 1.0.
365      We use the same reward function as Gandhi et al. (2024), which takes into account both correctness
366      and efficiency. See Appendices E.1 to E.4 for more details.
367      For evaluation, we follow the procedure outlined in Gandhi et al. (2024). We create 10,000 problems
368      for each of the two test cases: (1) seen targets and (2) unseen targets. We generate trajectories using
369      greedy decoding and measure accuracy based on their correctness. To evaluate models fine-tuned on
370      self-generated data, which involves randomness in the data generation process, we run experiments
371      with three different seeds and measure the mean and standard deviation. See Appendix E.1 for more
372      details on the test set.
373      We compare our methods with the symbolic method, SoS, and the two baseline fine-tuning methods
374      introduced in Gandhi et al. (2024): (1) SoS+STaR and (2) SoS+PPO. Although Gandhi et al. (2024)
375      employ iterative APA as the base RL algorithm, but this approach necessitates resetting the reference
376      policy, which introduces additional costs for model selection (Zhu et al., 2023). We find that a single
377      iteration of PPO outperforms iterative APA. See Appendix F for a more detailed comparison. Unless
         otherwise specified, we use operation-level PPO for all RL fine-tuning experiments.

      5   R ELATED WORKS
493
      Searching with language models Yang et al. (2022) introduce the idea of using search procedure
494
      for sequential decision making. They train a neural network to imitate not only actions generated by
495   an expert MCTS policy but also the search procedures involved in determining the actions. However,
496   their approach primarily focuses on utilizing search procedures to enhance imitation learning rather
497   than improving the search and planning capabilities of the model. Recent studies focus on enhancing
498   these capabilities using language models. Lehnert et al. (2024) train a T5 model to imitate A∗ search
499   traces and optimize it to generate shorter traces through supervised fine-tuning (Raffel et al., 2020).
500   Gandhi et al. (2024) train a GPT-Neo model to imitate DFS and BFS search trajectories and improve
501   its performance through supervised or RL fine-tuning. Our work differs from these prior studies in
502   that it utilizes optimal solutions throughout the self-data generation process rather than relying solely
503
      on fully self-generated data.
504
      Fine-tuning on self-generated data Anthony et al. (2017); Silver et al. (2017) introduce the idea
505
      of iteratively distilling self-generated data into neural networks to enhance their performance. They
506
      generate high-quality trajectories using an expert MCTS policy and imitate them to improve a neural
507   network-based policy. However, their work is restricted to small-scale convolutional neural networks
508   and the narrow domain of gaming. Recent studies extend this idea to fine-tune language models for
509   various downstream tasks, including theorem proving, question answering, and machine translation
510   (Polu et al., 2022; Zelikman et al., 2022; Gulcehre et al., 2023). While Zelikman et al. (2022) utilize
511   optimal solutions as prompts to generate higher-quality data, our approach differs by first generating
512   trajectories without any prior knowledge of optimal solutions and then integrating optimal solutions
513   into them. Furthermore, we incorporate each intermediate subgoal of optimal solutions step by step,
514   in the same spirit as Lightman et al. (2023).
515
516   RL fine-tuning with higher-level MDP Verma et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2024) address the long-
517
      horizon problem in multi-turn conversation tasks by defining each single-turn utterance as an action.
      However, their work focuses exclusively on off-policy RL algorithms. Furthermore, their approaches
518
      require an additional value network for baseline estimation and a target network to improve training
519
      stability. Our work differs from these prior studies by successfully extending the idea of higher-level
520   MDP to on-policy RL algorithms without introducing any additional networks. Although Ahmadian
521   et al. (2024) explore sequence-level optimization using REINFORCE (Williams, 1992), their focus
522   is limited to tasks with short horizons, where the average generation length is under 100.
523
524
      6   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this work, we explore the role of optimal solutions in enhancing the search and planning abilities
527
      of language models. We identify that optimal solutions can serve as step-by-step subgoals, providing
528   valuable guidance to models pre-trained on search trajectories. We propose guided stream of search
529   (GSoS), which seamlessly integrates optimal solutions into the self-generation process step by step,
530   producing high-quality trajectories for supervised fine-tuning. Our method achieves state-of-the-art
531   performance on the challenging Countdown benchmark. Furthermore, we find that GSoS possesses
532   the intriguing property of working in tandem with RL fine-tuning, which is not observed in standard
533   supervised fine-tuning.
534   Our work is not without limitations. Transformer models trained on search trajectories face inherent
535   challenges as they require long context lengths. Given their quadratic memory and time complexity,
536   this significantly raises the computational demands for both training and inference. This issue can be
537   further exacerbated as tasks grow more complex and require even longer context lengths. Exploring
538   the search and planning capabilities of more efficient language models, such as Mamba (Gu & Dao,
539   2023), is a promising direction for future work. Alternatively, training a model to implicitly perform
      search through knowledge distillation also presents a valuable approach (Deng et al., 2023).

      A        S TREAM OF SEARCH
721
      SoS trains a language model to predict search trajectories generated by symbolic search algorithms
722
      (Gandhi et al., 2024). For each problem with the input state I and the output state O, its search tree is
723
      structured with the root node representing the input state, non-root nodes representing intermediate
724   states, and edges representing operations between nodes. A leaf node is reached when no additional
725   operations can be applied. Formally, each nodes and edge in the tree are defined as follows:
726
727               • Node: Each node vα at depth d, where α = (i1 , i2 , . . . , id ), represents a sequence of d − 1
728                 operations and the resulting intermediate states, along with the output state.
729               • Edge: Each edge ev,v′ represents an operation that maps the node v to the node v ′ .
730
731   SoS defines the following primitive operations for symbolic search algorithms in langauge:
732
733
                  • Generation: Given a node vα at depth d, this action generates the id+1 -th child node vα′
                    at depth d + 1 by applying an operation, where α′ = (α, id+1 ).
734
735               • Exploration: Given a node vα at depth d, this action transitions to the previously generated
736
                    id+1 -th child node vα′ at depth d + 1, where α′ = (α, id+1 ).
737               • Backtracking: Given a node vα at depth d, if exploring its child nodes is unlikely to yield
738                 a solution, this action transitions to a previously generated node vα′ at depth d′ .
739               • Verification: Given a leaf node vα at depth D, where no further operations can be applied,
740                 this action verifies whether the node represents the solution.
741
742   Finally, it generates search trajectories using diverse symbolic search algorithms. Since all primitive
743
      operations are expressed in language, search trajectories are also represented in language. Figure 10
      illustrates an example of a search tree and its corresponding search trajectory. Note that an optimal
744
      solution can be represented as a path in a search tree.
745
746   One major challenge in training language models on these search trajectories is their context length.
747   Symbolic search algorithms can generate excessively long search trajectories via exhaustive search.
748   However, language models, such as Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), have a fixed context length,
749
      preventing them from capturing or learning from sequences beyond this length. To address this, SoS
      uses heuristic-guided search algorithms to generate shorter search trajectories, albeit with reduction
750
      in performance. See Appendix E.1 for more details on these algorithms.

1016   For RL fine-tuning, we implement PPO based on TRIL (Chang et al., 2023), a library that supports
1017   distributed RL training with transformers using Hugging Face Accelerate and Microsoft Deepspeed
1018   (Gugger et al., 2022; Rasley et al., 2020). However, we identify several critical issues in the original
1019   implementation and make the following modifications:
1020
1021            • The original code always passes a gradient accumulation step of 1 to the Deepspeed plugin,
1022              ignoring the intended setting. We modify the code to pass the correct value.
1023            • The original code uses separate networks for the policy and value function but updates both
1024              networks simultaneously using a single optimizer. This hinders the gradient clipping, as the
1025              larger gradient overshadows the smaller gradient. We modify the code to employ separate
                  optimizers, preventing the interference between the networks during the gradient clipping.

1058   F     C OMPARISON OF ITERATIVE APA WITH PPO
1059
1060   Gandhi et al. (2024) adopt iterative APA for RL fine-tuning instead of PPO. In this approach, a policy
1061   is fine-tuned using APA over multiple iterations, with the best-performing policy from each iteration
1062   serving as the reference for the subsequent iteration. However, this method can be cumbersome, as
1063
       it requires periodically saving and evaluating checkpoints to determine the best-performing policy.
1064   We observe that a single iteration of PPO achieves better performance than iterative APA with fewer
1065   rollouts, as shown in Table 9. Notably, SoS+PPO in the operation-level MDP outperforms iterative
1066   APA, with an accuracy gain of 4% for both seen and unseen targets. For a fair comparison, we train
1067   the SoS model using the same dataset and architecture as in the original paper. Therefore, we choose
1068   operation-level PPO over iterative APA as the base RL algorithm.
