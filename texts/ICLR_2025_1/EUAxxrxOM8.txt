010
011            We consider the discrete time infinite horizon average reward restless markovian
012            bandit (RMAB) problem. We propose a model predictive control based non-
013            stationary policy with a rolling computational horizon τ . At each time-slot, this
014            policy solves a τ horizon linear program whose first control value is kept as a con-
015            trol for the RMAB. Our solution requires minimal assumptions and quantifies the

               loss in optimality in terms
017            optimality gap is O(1/ N ) in general, and exp(−Ω(N )) under a local-stability
018            condition. Our proof is based on a framework from dynamic control known as
019
               dissipativity. Our solution is easy to implement and performs very well in prac-
               tice when compared to the state of the art. Further, both our solution and our proof
020

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   This work investigates the sequential decision making problem of Restless Multi-Armed Bandits
027   (RMAB for short) over an infinite discrete time horizon. In this problem there are N statistically
028   identical arms. At each time step, the decision maker must choose for each arm if they would like
029   to pull the arm or leave it as is. The decision maker has a constraint αN on the maximal number of
030   arms that they may pull at each time instance. Each arm has a known state belonging to a common
031   finite state space and upon choosing an action, produces a known state and action dependent reward.
032   Next, the arms evolve to a new state independently according to a known state-action dependent
033
      transition kernel. These arms are only coupled through the budget constraint on the number of arms
      that maybe pulled at each time instance. The state and reward are both revealed to the decision maker
034
      before the next decision needs to be made. The objective of the decision maker is to maximize the
035
      long term time average reward.
036
037   This problem was first proposed by Whittle (1988). Over the years RMABs have been used to model
038   a number of practical problems. These applications include web-crawling, queuing, communication
039
      systems, scheduling problems and many more, (Veatch & Wein, 1996), (Dance & Silander, 2019),
      (Nino-Mora, 2002), (O’Meara & Patel, 2001). The problem of choosing a subset of tasks to per-
040
      form among a larger collection of tasks under resource constraints shows up time and time again in
041
      various resource constrained control problems. For a comprehensive review on RMABs and their
042   applications, the interested reader is directed towards Niño-Mora (2023). While the existence of
043   an optimal policy for RMABs straightforward, Papadimitriou & Tsitsiklis (1999) showed that the
044   exact solution to this problem is PSPACE-hard. Consequently, most work focused on designing
045   approximate solutions with good performance guarantees.
046
      In the seminal paper, Whittle (1988) suggested that under a condition known as indexability, an index
047
      can be associated with each state. This index is now referred to as the Whittle’s index (WI) and it was
048   conjectured that a priority policy based on this index would be an optimal solution for this problem.
049   This setting naturally lends itself to mean field approximations where one may replace the N armed
050   problem with a dynamical system in order to find these approximate solutions. (Weber & Weiss,
051   1990) were the first to point out that under a (hard to verify) condition on the dynamical system
052   known as uniform global attractor property (UGAP), the (WI) was asymptotically optimal. Recently,
053   many of the results in the RMAB literature have focused on two major aspects of the problem.
      Firstly, how quickly do proposed asymptotically optimal policies converge to the optimal solution

054
      as a function of the number of arms. Secondly, can the underlying assumptions such as indexability
055   and UGAP be made less restrictive to obtain more general conditions under which optimal solutions
056   can be found. In the former category, under the assumptions of Weber & Weiss (1990), Gast et al.
057   (2023a) showed that the WI policy is exponentially close to the optimal solution. More recently
058   still, several works have been able to show an exponential order of convergence, Gast et al. (2023b);
059   Hong et al. (2024a) under less restrictive assumptions. On the other hand, in the latter category,
060   Hong et al. (2023) showed an asymptotic convergence result under a far less restrictive assumption
061   known as the synchronization assumption. Similar works following this steering dynamics include
062   Yan (2024) and Hong et al. (2024b) to show asymptotically optimal algorithms. Furthermore, Hong
063
      et al. (2024a) loosened the restrictions further to not only show asymptotic convergence but describe
      fundamental conditions in order to achieve exponential convergence rates for any algorithm. A
064
      more comprehensive view of recent results can be found in Appendix A with descriptions of both
065
      assumptions and the algorithm.
066
067   In this work, we return to the dynamical control perspective but do not look to steer our system
068   towards an optimal fixed point. Instead, we focus on the notion of dissipativity, to make the case for
069
      a well known set of policies known as model predictive control. We show that a simple MPC with a
      finite planning horizon produces an asymptotically optimal solution to the RMAB problem without
070
      any need to relax constraints. In doing so, not only do we bring a new perspective to this decades old
071
      problem but also describe a simple tractable algorithm that performs exceptionally well in practice
072   with a small planning horizon.
073   Main Contributions The main contribution of our paper is the proof that a very natural model
074   predictive control (LP-update) provides both an algorithm that works well in practice as well as the-
075   oretical guarantees under very weak and easy to verify assumptions on the system parameters only,
076   using the framework of dissipativity. The idea of resolving an LP for finite-horizon restless bandits
077   already exists in the literature, however, all the papers that proposed to use this idea either analyzed
078   the algorithm in the finite-horizon case or used the hard to verify UGAP assumption(uniform global
079
      attractor property) on the system. This is primarily because without the framework of dissipativity,
      analyzing how a finite horizon policy translates to the infinite horizon reward seems to be infeasible.
080
      The use of this framework is one of the key technical novelties of our approach. A greater exposi-
081
      tion on our work’s place in the current literature is relegated to the Related works section, Appendix
082   A due to lack of space. Apart from the main result, we make several fresh observations about the
083   nature of RMAB problems:
084
085         1. Our proof is of independent interest since it uses a new framework known as dissipativity.
086            Dissipativity is a closely studied phenomenon in the model predictive literature and is used
087            to study how a policy drives a system towards optimal fixed points Damm et al. (2014).
088         2. Returning to the dynamics around the fixed point, we can tighten the rate of convergence
089            to e−cN under a local stability condition.
090
            3. Perhaps the most helpful portion of our results, for practical purposes; the MPC algorithm
091            works well in practice and is easy to implement. It performs well both in terms of the
092            number of arms N as well as the computational time horizon T beating state of the art
093            algorithms in our bench marks.

      7                                C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we study the problem of constructing an efficient policy for restless multi-armed
532   bandit for the average reward case. We show that under quite general condition, a simple model-
533   predictive control √algorithm provides a solution that achieves both the best known upper bound
534   up to know (O(1/ N ) or exp(−ΩN ) for stable non-degenerate problems), but also works very
535   efficiently in practice. Our paper provides the first analysis of this policy for the average reward
536   criterion. Our paper uses a novel framework based on dissipativity that helps up to make a crisp
537   connection between the finite- and the infinite-horizon problems and we are the first to us it in this
538   context. We believe that this framework is what makes our analysis simple and easily generalizable
539   to other settings. As an example, we discuss in Appendix B.2 potential generalization to the multi-
      action multi-constraint bandits, that is an interesting direction for future work.

      A A DDITIONAL L ITERATURE R EVIEW
649
      The study of bandit problems dates back at least to the 1930’s in the form of sequential design of
650   experiments, (Thompson, 1933), (Wald, 1947). However, the more modern perspective of Marko-
651   vian bandits in terms of MDPs is largely credited to the monograph by Bellman et al. (1957). This
652   monograph brought to light the combinatorial difficulty of this problem even over a finite horizon.
653   The rested variant of the multi-armed bandit problem was famously resolved through Gittins (1979)
654   which proposed a remarkably simple structure to the solution by allotting indices to the states of the
655   bandit arms.
656   In his seminal work Whittle (1988) generalized the problem to the restless multi-armed bandit prob-
657   lem in its modern form and conjectured that under a condition known as indexability, an index
658   policy is asymptotically optimal (in the number of arms) for the RMAB problem. Weber & Weiss
659   (1990) showed a counter example to this conjecture, further going on to show in the same work
660   that the conjecture did hold under a global attractor (UGAP assumption) and ergodicity condition
661   of a single-arm. This result was generalized to the multi-action setting by Hodge & Glazebrook
662   (2015). On the other hand, Verloop (2016); Gast et al. (2023b) generalized the set of index policies
663   by constructing a set of priority policies known as LP priority index while maintaining the UGAP
664
      assumption. More surprisingly, Gast et al. (2023a;b) showed these priority policies are in fact expo-
      nentially close to being optimal, under three conditions: uniform global attractor property (UGAP),
665
      non-degeneracy of the fixed point and global exponential stability Gast et al. (2023a). The restric-
666
      tion of indexability is removed in Gast et al. (2023b) and the condition of UGAP was first removed
667   Hong et al. (2024a).
668
669   Gast et al. (2023a;b) observed that under the conditions of non-degeneracy, there exists a local neigh-
670
      borhood around the fixed point where the priority policy set is affine. This is the key observation
      which leads to the exponentially small error bounds under simple local stability conditions. This
671
      observation around the fixed point drove several works (Hong et al., 2023; 2024a;b; Goldsztajn &
672
      Avrachenkov, 2024; Yan, 2024) to shift their perspective towards policies that look to steer the dy-
673   namical system towards the optimal fixed point. Hong et al. (2023) created a virtual system that
674   was driven to the fixed point. To transform this into an asymptotically optimal policy, they required
675   the real system to synchronize with the virtual system, hence, they developed the synchronization
676   assumption. Unlike Hong et al. (2023) which passively allowed arms to align with each other even-
677   tually, Hong et al. (2024a) used a two set policy to actively align non aligned arms to the fixed point.
678   In contrast to this form of control, Yan (2024) designed the align and steer policy to steer the mean
679   field dynamical control system towards the fixed point. Yan (2024)’s policy is asymptotically opti-
680   mal under the assumptions of controllability of the dynamical system to more directly move the state
681
      of the system towards the fixed point over time. In parallel to this work, Goldsztajn & Avrachenkov
      (2024) used a similar idea of mean field dynamical control for weakly coupled systems under a
682
      relaxed but generalized constraint set if the single arm process is unichain and aperiodic.
683
684   In contrast to these approaches our algorithm is not explicitly designed at the outset to steer the
685   corresponding dynamical system towards a fixed point. Rather, we show that the model predictive
686   control algorithm is designed to solve an equivalent rotated cost minimization problem. In doing
687
      so, it produces policies that are close to optimal to the infinite horizon average reward problem.
      We thus produce a policy that is easily generalizable to the multi-action, general constraint setting
688
      without relaxing the constraints using ideas from Whittle’s relaxation. Further, when the fixed point
689
      is unique, minimizing the rotated cost problem will coincide with steering the dynamical control
690   system towards the fixed point, allowing us to recover exponentially close asymptotic optimality
691   bounds.
692
693
      There are also a lot of recent papers on RMABs for the finite-horzon reward criteria Hu & Frazier
      (2017); Zayas-Caban et al. (2019); Brown & Smith (2020); Ghosh et al. (2022); Zhang & Frazier
694
      (2021); Gast et al. (2023a), or the infinite-horizon discounted reward Zhang & Frazier (2022); Ghosh
695
      et al. (2022). Our results are close in spirit to those since our policy shows how to use a finite-horizon
696   policy to construct a policy that is asymptotically optimal for the infinite-horizon rewards. Finally,
697   along the same lines, it is worth noting that a recent result Yan et al. (2024), developed in parallel
698   to our own showed an order O(1/N ) result for the degenerate finite time horizon problem using a
699   diffusion approximation instead of a mean field approximation. Unfortunately, this finite horizon re-
700   sult cannot immediately be compared to the infinite horizon average reward result primarily because
701   unlike the mean field solution which can directly be translated as the solution to (5) the diffusion
      dynamic has no immediate corresponding equivalent.
