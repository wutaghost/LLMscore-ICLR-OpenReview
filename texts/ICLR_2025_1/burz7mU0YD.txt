009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                We present OpenMU-Bench, a large-scale benchmark suite for addressing the data
012                scarcity issue in training multimodal language models to understand music. To
013                construct OpenMU-Bench, we leveraged existing datasets and bootstrapped new
014                annotations. OpenMU-Bench also broadens the scope of music understanding
015                by including lyrics understanding and music tool usage. Using OpenMU-Bench,
016                we trained our music understanding model, OpenMU, with extensive ablations,
017
                   demonstrating that OpenMU outperforms baseline models such as MU-LLaMA.
                   Both OpenMU and OpenMU-Bench are open-sourced to facilitate future research
018
                   in music understanding and to enhance creative music production efficiency1 .

021   1       I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have successfully extended large language models
024   (LLMs) by enabling them to perceive, process, and understand data in modalities beyond text (Tsim-
025   poukelli et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023; McKinzie et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023;
026   Gong et al., 2024), such as images, videos, and audio. However, there has been limited effort (Gard-
027   ner et al., 2024) focused on constructing MLLMs capable of effectively understanding the music
028   modality or addressing Music Information Retrieval (MIR) tasks. MIR is a research field focusing
029   on modeling, understanding and interpreting data relevant to music, aiming to improve the efficiency
030
      of music production (Serra et al., 2013). Conventional machine learning algorithms (Wang, 2003;
      Casey et al., 2008) sparked the success in music searching. Subsequently, deep learning models ex-
031
      panded the success to music tagging (Won et al., 2020), transcription (Gardner et al., 2021; Toyama
032
      et al., 2023) and representation learning (Castellon et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024; Won et al., 2024).
033
034   We aim to contribute to the MIR field by training an MLLM, dubbed OpenMU, for understanding
035   music clips. Building on the versatile capabilities of LLMs and pretrained audio encoders, OpenMU
036
      effectively comprehends and reasons about input music clips, producing relevant answers accord-
      ingly. We also enable OpenMU to leverage well-established music tools to encourage synergies
037
      between OpenMU and creative practitioners through cooperation. OpenMU is expected to greatly
038
      improve music production efficiency. Creative practitioners can instruct OpenMU to describe a
039   music clip’s contents and features, saving minutes of time compared to listening to the full track.
040
041   The major obstacle we faced when training and evaluating OpenMU was the issue of data scarcity in
042
      the music modality (Serra et al., 2013; Seeger, 2003; Holzapfel et al., 2018). To address this issue,
      we construct OpenMU-Bench, a large-scale benchmark for training and evaluating MLLMs in
043
      music understanding. To construct OpenMU-Bench, we bootstrap new datasets using GPT-3.5, and
044
      leverage existing datasets when available. As a result, OpenMU-Bench comprises approximately
045   one million training examples, covering various aspects of music understanding, such as music
046   captioning, reasoning, multi-choice question answering, lyrics understanding and music tool using.
047   To the best of our knowledge, no large-scale open-sourced benchmark comparable to OpenMU-
048   Bench currently exists, and we hope it will advance future research and development of MIR.
049
      In summary, our contributions include: (1) Proposing OpenMU for music understanding. OpenMU
050
      is an MLLM dedicated to the music modality, outperforming baseline models such as MU-
051   LLaMA (Liu et al., 2024) in tasks like music captioning, reasoning, and multiple-choice question an-
052   swering. We carefully evaluate various design choices for OpenMU and provide extensive ablations

060   2   R ELATED WORK
061
062
      Understanding music goes beyond recognizing objective attributes of music such as tempo (Böck
063   et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2021) or instrumentation (Gururani et al., 2019; Zhong et al., 2023). It is
064   also subjective and highly context-dependent, like determining music genres (Kereliuk et al., 2015)
065   or moods (Bogdanov et al., 2019; Koutini et al., 2019). Researchers succeeded in understanding
066   music by classifying music clips into predefined tags (Li et al., 2024; Won et al., 2024). Recently,
067   music captioning (Manco et al., 2021) and reasoning (Gardner et al., 2024) tasks, where natural
068   language descriptions are employed to describe music clips, have earned increasing attention. Also,
069   the ability of selecting correct answers in multi-choice question answering is included in music un-
070   derstanding Weck et al. (2024). However, there has been limited exploration into enabling MLLMs
071
      to utilize external digital tools (i.e., established music tools) for music analysis. We hypothesize
      that a music understanding model can further boost the workflow of creative practitioners by deeply
072
      integrating the set of widely adopted music tools. Last but not least, lyrics information processing
073
      (Watanabe & Goto, 2020), such as semantic lyrics understanding (Zhang et al., 2022) enhances the
074   understanding of a music clip. Therefore, we integrate it in OpenMU-Bench. Overall, we broaden
075   the scope of music understanding by considering two extra aspects beyond music captioning and
076   reasoning: Music tool using and lyrics understanding.
077
      Foundation models for music understanding. Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) (Tsimpoukelli et al.,
078
      2021; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023; McKinzie et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2024) fuse non-textual
079
      information into LLMs (Liang et al., 2022) to solve real-world tasks requiring the ability of per-
080   ceiving data in different modalities. The scope of MLLMs is recently expanded to include music.
081   MU-LLaMA (Liu et al., 2024) and MusiLingo (Deng et al., 2024) narrowed down their scope to
082   music captioning and question answering (QA); other critical aspects of music understanding, e.g.,
083   key and chord recognition, are not covered. Perhaps the closest to ours is Llark (Gardner et al.,
084   2024). However, neither the model itself nor the music understanding datasets from Llark have been
085   released. None of these models is capable of using music tools, an important ability to interact with
086   creators. In this paper, we propose OpenMU-Bench and OpenMU to advance the field of music un-
087   derstanding. OpenMU-Bench holistically measures various aspects of music understanding, while
088
      OpenMU achieves state-of-art performance on the benchmark. Both OpenMU-Bench and OpenMU
      are released to facilitate the future research and development in this field.
089
090   Music understanding datasets. The proliferation of LLMs has spurred the development of bench-
091   marks designed to holistically measure the genuine capabilities of LLMs. Benchmarks have been
092   designed for NLP tasks (BIG-bench authors, 2023; Hendrycks et al., 2021), and vision-language
093   tasks (Liu et al., 2023c; Fu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023). MMMU (Yue et al., 2023) included the mu-
094
      sic modality into evaluation but at a very narrow scope (334 entries of sheet music). Researchers are
      striving to address the data scarcity challenge of music: Doh et al. (2023) introduced LP-MusicCaps,
095
      associating LLM-augmented captions with music clips from MusicCaps (Agostinelli et al., 2023).
096
      Similarly, Liu et al. (2024) developed MusicQA, containing QA and captioning tasks for music
097   clips from MusicCaps, MagnaTagATune (Law et al., 2009b), and MTG-Jamendo (Bogdanov et al.,
098   2019). Concurrently, Deng et al. (2024) proposed MusicInstruct, which targets QA and captioning
099   for clips in MusicCaps. Weck et al. (2024) create MuChoMusic as a music understanding benchmark
100   containing 1,187 multiple-choice questions for evaluation. Building on existing datasets, we con-
101   struct OpenMU-Bench by additionally bootstrapping new datasets using GPT-3.5. OpenMU-Bench
102   contains about one million examples for training and evaluation across various music understand-
103   ing tasks. We also standardize evaluation metrics to ensure consistency2 in reporting results on
104   OpenMU-Bench. Table 1 provides the statistics for OpenMU-Bench.

127   3       C ONSTRUCTING O PEN MU-B ENCH
128   This section outlines the construction of OpenMU-Bench. We introduce the five types of tasks in-
129   cluded in OpenMU-Bench and explain the dataset construction procedures for each type. In addition
130   to incorporating existing music understanding datasets, we generate new annotations for music clips
131   from datasets that do not contain natural language annotations. Our goal is to integrate as many
132   datasets as possible to enable OpenMU-Bench to comprehensively and systematically evaluate mu-
133   sic understanding models. Furthermore, we specify the recommended evaluation metrics to ensure
134   consistent and fair benchmarking. Table 2 shows examples of different OpenMU-Bench task types.
135
136   3.1      O PEN MU-B ENCH TASK TYPES
137
138   Music captioning tasks a model with generating textual descriptions capturing musical contents
139   and key features of a music clip. A music understanding model excels at captioning can improve
140   the efficiency of music production by generating music descriptions in a short time, eliminating the
141   needs of listening to the entire music track by creators. Music reasoning, as defined by Gardner et al.
142
      (2024), tasks the model with answering questions in two aspects. First, it examines the interaction
      between different elements of a music clip, such as how a fast tempo is likely to correspond with a
143
      high energy level. Second, it explores how the real-world can interact with the music clip, e.g., how
144
      a creator can increase the energy level of a music clip by using faster tempos (see Table 2).
145
146   Tool using. The MIR community has developed a wide range of music technology tools for various
147   tasks, such as tempo estimation, key detection, chord recognition, and instrument identification3 .
148
      Unlike Llark (Gardner et al., 2024), which aims to address many MIR tasks using only the LLM,
      OpenMU takes a different view. We aim for OpenMU to integrate and leverage the well-established,
149
      rigorously tested MIR tools to solve practical, real-world problems. This approach is motivated by
150
      the limitations of current LLMs, which often struggle with tasks like arithmetic (Qin et al., 2023). By
151   combining the strengths of established MIR tools (e.g., tempo estimator) with LLM-based method,
152   OpenMU aims to provide a more robust solution to music understanding.
153
154
      Lyrics understanding. Lyrics, which carry rich semantic content, are often used to convey moods
      and emotions (Watanabe & Goto, 2020). We incorporate a lyrics understanding task into OpenMU-
155
      Bench. We consider a model that excels at understanding lyrics to be capable of producing inter-
156
      pretations similar to those of humans, conditioned on the music input. This task is framed as a text
157   generation problem.
158
159   Multiple-choice questions. Text generation models are inherently difficult to evaluate due to their
160
      open-ended nature (Celikyilmaz et al., 2020). This challenge extends to LLM-based music under-

181   3.2     I NDIVIDUAL DATASETS
182
      As introduced in §2, a few datasets already exist for music understanding. We incorporate these
183
      datasets and create new annotations to ensure that OpenMU-Bench has both a large scale and broad
184
      coverage. We describe each of the datasets, along with the applied modifications aligning them with
185   the OpenMU-Bench task types. We adhere to existing train/test splits of the datasets when available
186   (c.f. §A.4); Appendix §A.2 details the preprocessing and annotating details of OpenMU-Bench; we
187   highlight only the key information here.
188
      MusicCaps, created by Agostinelli et al. (2023), is pivotal for the music captioning task. It contains
189
      approximately 5.5K 10-second music clips sourced from AudioSet (Gemmeke et al., 2017), with
190
      corresponding gold-standard text captions written by professional musicians. We incorporate Mus-
191   icCaps into OpenMU-Bench as part of the captioning task. LPMusicCaps & LPMusicMTT (Doh
192   et al., 2023) extend MusicCaps and the MagnaTagATune (Law et al., 2009a) dataset by generating
193   additional textual descriptions. The authors prompt GPT-3.5 to “write”, “summarize”, “paraphrase”,
194   and “predict attributes” new captions to the music clips. We integrate4 approximately 8K LPMus-
195   icCaps and 51K LPMusicMTT training captions into OpenMU-Bench. MusicInstruct (Deng et al.,
196   2024) also extends MusicCaps by creating question-answer pairs for the MusicCaps clips using
197   GPT-4. This dataset contains approximately 60K question-answer pairs, which are categorized into
198   two versions: a short version (MI-short) focusing on musical content such as tempo and genre, and
199
      a long version (MI-long) that paraphrases the MusicCaps captions. We integrate MusicInstruct into
      OpenMU-Bench as a captioning task, and report performance on both versions separately.
200
201   MusicQA, developed by Liu et al. (2024) by prompting MPT (MosaicML-NLP-Team, 2023), is em-
202   ployed to train their MU-LLaMA. MusicQA is composed of MusicCaps clips for pretraining, Mag-
203   naTagATune (Law et al., 2009a) clips for finetuning, and MTG-Jamendo (Bogdanov et al., 2019)
204   clips for testing. We incorporate MusicQA-Finetune and MusicQA-Test into OpenMU-Bench, while
205
      MusicQA-Pretrain, which contains the test split of MusicCaps, is excluded to prevent potential train-
      test leakage (Deng et al., 2024). Following Liu et al. (2024), we separate MusicQA into captioning
206
      and reasoning parts.
207
208   Music4all, developed by Pegoraro Santana et al. (2020), consists of approximately 100K music clips
209   with rich metadata, including attributes like energy, valence, and genre. Based on this metadata, we
210   prompt GPT-3.5 to generate annotations for both the captioning and reasoning tasks. The prompts
211
      used for these annotations are provided in the Appendix §A.3. GTZAN, developed by Tzanetakis
      & Cook (2002), contains approximately 1K 30-second music clips, each labeled with genre tags
212
      and we create extra tempo tags with Madmom (Böck et al., 2016). Based on these tags, we generate
213
      captioning and reasoning annotations with prompting. MusicNet (Thickstun et al., 2017) contains 1

236   3.3     E VALUATION METRICS
237
238   OpenMU-Bench leverages common evaluation metrics for text generation tasks: captioning, rea-
239   soning, and lyrics understanding. BLEU-1, BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002)6 , Meteor (Banerjee &
240   Lavie, 2005), Rouge-1, and Rouge-L (Lin, 2004) measure an answer’s textual overlap with the gold
241   standard, while BertScore (Zhang et al., 2020) measures similarity in the semantic representation
242   space of a pretrained BERT model. For all evaluations, we report the scores computed using the
243
      F-measure. We report accuracy for the task of multiple-choice questions.

      4.1     M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
248
      Encoding music clips. We use AudioMAE (Huang et al., 2022) to encode an input music clip
249
      into vector representations. Specifically, we use the “ViT-B AS-2M pretrained + finetuned” version
250   of AudioMAE, which is a Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) initially pretrained with
251   a masked auto-encoding reconstruction loss (He et al., 2022), followed by finetuning on tagging
252   tasks (Gemmeke et al., 2017), both using the AudioSet2M (Gemmeke et al., 2017) dataset. The
253   choice of using AudioMAE over other music encoders, such as MERT (Li et al., 2024) or Jukebox-
254   5B (Dhariwal et al., 2020; Castellon et al., 2021), is motivated by two primary reasons. First,
255   more than half of the audio clips inAudioSet2M7 consist of music or musical instrument recordings,
256   resulting in approximately 3,137 hours of music data (compared to the 910 hours in the MERT-95M-
257   public model (Li et al., 2024)). Audio encoders pretrained on AudioSet have shown competitive
258
      performance in music tagging tasks (Koutini et al., 2021; Niizumi et al., 2022). Second, the size
      of the multimodal encoder is not a performance bottleneck (McKinzie et al., 2024). Instead, the
259
      smaller number of parameters in ViT-B (86M) facilitates more efficient training.
260
261   LLM. We use the open-sourced Llama3-8B-instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) as our LLM. Compared to
262   previous Llama models (Touvron et al., 2023), Llama3 has been trained on higher-quality datasets
263   and at larger scales, achieving GPT-4-level performance (Achiam et al., 2023) on numerous tasks.
264   Music-language projector links the representation space of the music encoder with the LLM. Stud-
265   ies (McKinzie et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a) have shown that the architecture of the projector itself
266   has little impact on downstream task performance, while the number of tokens from the multimodal

      4.2     T RAINING DETAILS
291
292   Dataset preprocessing. When processing the music clips, we limit their maximum length to 30
293   seconds and zero-pad those shorter than 30 seconds. All music clips are resampled to 16 kHz
294   and then converted to a 128-bin Mel-spectrogram with a 25-ms hann window and 10-ms hop size.
295   Consequently, each music clip is represented as a mel-spectrogram with a shape of (3072, 128).
296   Since AudioMAE is trained to encode inputs of up to 10 seconds, we segment each mel-spectrogram
297   into three parts, encode them separately, and then concatenate the results. As a result, each 30-second
298   music clip is encoded by 1536 tokens, with each token having a shape of (1, 768).
299   Throughout our experiments, we used between 8 and 16 A100 40GB GPUs, depending on the
300   experimental setup (c.f. §5). In all experiments, we set the maximum context length of the LLM to
301   2048 tokens. We utilized DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) and FlashAttention2 (Dao
302   et al., 2022) to enable fast and efficient training. It took approximately three days to train OpenMU
303   on the captioning and reasoning subsets of OpenMU-Bench (around 1 million data examples).
304   Training setup of OpenMU-Bench largely follows the common practice of MLLM training (Yin
305   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; McKinzie et al., 2024), consisting of:
306
307         Stage (1) Captioning. We train OpenMU to generate captions, conditioned solely on the input
308         music clip. The goal of Stage (1) training is to align the representation spaces of AudioMAE and
309         Llama3, with the only trainable module in this stage being the music-language projector. We use
310         the captioning subset of OpenMU-Bench for training in this stage. A key configuration is the
311         number of music tokens fed into the LLM, which we discuss in detail in §5.1. The remaining
312
            hyperparameters largely follow Liu et al. (2023b) and are provided in the Appendix §A.1.
313         Stage (2) Instruction Tuning. After aligning the music and text representation spaces, Stage (2)
314         training enables OpenMU to follow various instructions in the music domain, such as inferring
315         music genres or reasoning about the content of a music clip. In this stage, LoRA adapters
316         (Hu et al., 2022) are incorporated into OpenMU’s LLM, followed by fine-tuning on OpenMU-
317
            Bench’s captioning and reasoning tasks. We focus on two critical research questions in this stage.
            First, we extensively evaluate OpenMU’s task performance with respect to its LoRA parameters
318
            (see §5.2). Second, we investigate in-depth OpenMU’s use of music information. Given the
319
            large-scale pretraining data of OpenMU’s LLM, we hypothesize that OpenMU might be able
320         to make correct predictions for knowledge-intensive questions even without relying on musical
321         information within a music clip. We test this hypothesis and show that in order to achieve
322         higher performance, OpenMU indeed relies on information from the music clip, demonstrating
323         OpenMU’s genuine ability to understand music.

353   5     D ESIGNING O PEN MU AND DISCUSSIONS
354
355   In this section, we explore and discuss the critical factors involved in training OpenMU. We aim for
356   these detailed analyses to contribute to the research and development of future foundation models
357   for music understanding.

      5.1       N UMBER OF MUSIC TOKENS
361
362   McKinzie et al. (2024) illustrate that the number of image tokens is more significant than the archi-
363
      tecture of the vision-language projector in vision-language MLLMs. To the best of our knowledge,
      no prior research has addressed this critical aspect in the context of training foundation models for
364
      music understanding. This is particularly important because music clips can often be lengthy, lead-
365
      ing to a large number of music tokens. For instance, the AudioMAE encoder outputs 1536 tokens
366   for representing a 30-second music clip. While using all available tokens ensures the maximum use
367   of music modality information, it may hinder training efficiency and limit the utility of the context
368   window of the LLM (2048 in our case). In this section, we extensively evaluate the impact of the
369   number of music tokens when training OpenMU.
370
      Figure 2 displays the training trajectories (log-scale) of both Stage (1) and Stage (2) training, where
371
      we apply mean-pooling to every 2–128 music tokens output by AudioMAE. For instance, mean-
372   pooling every 8 tokens means using only 1536/8 = 192 tokens to represent the 30-second input music
373   clip. The difference among mean-pooling 2, 8, and 32 tokens is small, suggesting that there may be
374   redundancies in the representations of the encoded music clip. Although aggressively mean-pooling
375   128 tokens significantly reduces the overall training time (7.5 hours when pooling 128 tokens vs.
376   20 hours when mean-pooling 2 tokens), the setting results in a weaker convergence. As a result,
377   we empirically focus on model variants with mean-pooling 8 tokens in the next sections to balance
      between model convergence and training efficiency.

      5.2   L O RA, TASK PERFORMANCE , AND MUSIC INFORMATION UTILITY
396
397   As introduced in §4.2, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA; Hu et al. (2022)), is employed in Stage (2)
398   training to efficiently adapt OpenMU’s LLM for following instructions in the music domain. Given
399   an LLM weight parameter matrix W P Rdˆk , instead of directly modifying W , LoRA introduces
400   and trains two matrices, B P Rdˆr and A P Rrˆk for adapting W to a downstream task:
401                                                           α
402
                                                 W Ð W ` BA.
                                                              r
403   The LLM weight matrix W remains unchanged; the LoRA rank r determines the number of train-
404   able parameters by controlling the size of B and A. The matrix multiplication result, BA, repre-
405   sents the changes introduced by adaptation to a downstream task, scaled by αr . Here, α is a hyper-
406   parameter, and typically r ă α. For OpenMU, we fix α “ 128 following Liu et al. (2023b;a), while
407   varying the value of r. Intuitively, a smaller rank r imposes a stricter bottleneck on B and A, requir-
408   ing the learned parameter differences, represented by BA, to rely on fewer trainable parameters to
409   capture concise and genuine information about the downstream task, which are subsequently scaled
410   by a larger αr . In contrast, a larger r introduces more trainable parameters, which may be prone to
411
      learning shortcuts, redundant information, or noise (Geirhos et al., 2020) during adaptation to the
      downstream task, subsequently scaled by a smaller αr . In this section, we investigate how LoRA
412
      configurations affect Stage (2) training, as well as reporting the evaluation results of OpenMU on
413
      OpenMU-Bench.

417
      the macro-average performance of each OpenMU variant across all subtasks in OpenMU-Bench.
      Additionally, Figure 4 (left) displays the evaluation results using BertScore (Zhang et al., 2020) as
418
      the metric. Several observations can be made. First, the number of music tokens plays a critical role

423   lost when mean-pooling 32 tokens. Second, the effectiveness of LoRA parameters show limited
424   impacts on task performance, similar to the findings in Gong et al. (2024). As a result, we will focus

      We presented OpenMU-Bench, a large-scale benchmark suite containing approximately one mil-
532
      lion examples for training and evaluating LLM-based music understanding models. We construct
533
      OpenMU-Bench by creating new annotations as well as leveraging existing datasets. We trained
534   our music understanding model, OpenMU, with extensive ablations and demonstrated that it outper-
535   forms baseline models such as MU-LLaMA. Both OpenMU and OpenMU-Bench are open-sourced
536   to facilitate future research in music understanding and enhance the efficiency of creative music
537   production. Future work may explore extending OpenMU to support multiple music clips as input
538   and enable in-context learning for music understanding. Another promising direction is enabling
539   OpenMU to integrate more MIR tools, combining the strengths of LLMs and established tools for
      deeper music understanding.

823   A.1       T RAINING DETAILS AND HYPERPARAMETERS
824
825   In this section, we describe the detailed settings and hyperparameters used for training OpenMU.
826   All experiments were conducted using 8-16 A100 40GB GPUs, with BF16 enabled to ensure stable
827   training. We use DeepSpeed ZERO-3 (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) and Flash Attention 2 (Dao et al.,
828   2022) to reduce the memory consumption. We utilized the Adam optimizer and a cosine learning
829   rate scheduler, with a 30% warm-up ratio.
830
      For Stage (1) training, we pretrained OpenMU for 15 epochs on the captioning subtask of OpenMU-
831
      Bench, which consists of approximately 275K pairs of music clips and corresponding captions.
832   Stage (1) training took approximately 10 hours for the checkpoint we evaluated (i.e., mean-pooling
833   every 8 music tokens as illustrated in Figure 2. The initial learning rate was set to 1e-3, with a batch
834   size of 8 per GPU.
835
      For Stage (2) training, we extended pretraining of OpenMU for 10 epochs on the captioning and
836
      reasoning subtasks of OpenMU-Bench, which comprise roughly one million training examples. The
837
      initial learning rate was set to 2e-5, with the same per-GPU batch size of 8. Stage (2) required
838   approximately 40 hours due to the increased size of training data.
839
840   For the lyrics understanding subtask, we trained OpenMU for 20 epochs, reusing the hyperparame-
841
      ters from Stage (2). Similarly, for the tool using subtask, we reused the Stage (2) hyperparameters
      but reduced the number of epochs to 5 due to the smaller dataset size for this task.

923
924   Based on the metadata of each music clip (§A.2), we prompt GPT-3.5 to generate examples for the

926   modifications tailored to the available metadata of different OpenMU-Bench subsets and subtasks.

928
929

930                  You are an expert AI assistant that is knowledgeable about music
931                  production, musical structure, music history, and music styles, and
                     you are hearing audio of a short clip or loop extracted from a piece
932                  of music. What you hear is described in a JSON-format shown below,
933                  describing the same audio clip you are listening to. This description
                     is provided in a JSON dictionary, where the keys and values represent
934                  attributes of the music clip. The JSON contains a set of fields
935                  describing various features of the music clip.
936                  Specifically, the JSON will contain:
937                      - "tempo": the tempo of this music clip.
                         - "energy": the energy level of the music clip. High energy means
938                        fast, loud, and noisy. For example, death metal has high energy,
939                        while a Bach prelude scores low on energy. Perceptual features
                           contributing to this attribute include dynamic range, perceived
940                        loudness, timbre, onset rate, and general entropy.
941                      - "valence": the valence level of the music clip. Valence measures
                           the musical positiveness conveyed by the song. High valence
942                        value means the song is more positive (e.g. happy, cheerful,
943                        euphoric), otherwise the song is more negative (e.g. sad,
                           depressed, angry).
944                      - "danceability": danceable level of this music clip. Danceability
945                        measures how suitable a song is for dancing based on a
                           combination of musical elements including tempo, rhythm
946                        stability, beat strength, and overall regularity.
947
                     Optionally, the JSON may contain the following OPTIONAL annotations of
948                  the song:
949                      - "genre": a list of genres of the music clip.
                         - "mood": a list of possible moods of the music clip.
950                      - "instrument": a list of possible instruments used by the music
951                        clip.
                         - "others": other informative factors of the music clip.
952
953                  You should only use the optional annotations when provided. Otherwise,
                     you MUST NOT mention them in the description.
954
955                  Based on the JSON, **provide a detailed musical description of the
                     clip, from the perspective of a musical expert describing the clip as
956                  they hear it being played**. Use your knowledge about music to make
957                  sure to describe the musical style and contents, and any unique
                     features of the music clip.
958
959                  Describe the music clip using a tone of describing facts. DO NOT USE
                     languages such as "I hear ...". Just provide musical captions to the
960                  clip.
961
                     Only provide details that are based on the provided metadata or your
962                  background knowledge of music as an intelligent AI assistant. Explain
963                  any musical concepts that would be unfamiliar to a non-musician. Do
                     not specifically reference the provided metadata in the response;
964                  instead, respond as if you are hearing the song and reporting a rich
965                  description of what you hear. Keep in mind that the music clip is only

       In this section, we provide details about the train/test splits of the OpenMU-Bench subtasks. Specif-
993
       ically, for MusicCaps, MusicInstruct, LPMusicCaps, LPMusicMTT, MusicQA, MusicNet, BART-

997
       For Music4All, we start with the 800 music clips from BART-Fusion as the initial test set. We then
998

999

1000   the music clips from folds 90 to 99 of the original dataset (Bogdanov et al., 2019) serve as the test
1001   data, while the remaining clips and their annotations are treated as training data. For tool using, we
1002   randomly sample 80% examples for training and 20% for testing.
