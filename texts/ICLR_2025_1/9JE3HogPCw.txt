009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Activation functions are one of the key components of a deep neural network.
012            The most commonly used activation functions can be classed into the category
013            of continuously differentiable (e.g. tanh) and piece-wise linear functions (e.g.
014            ReLU), both having their own strengths and drawbacks with respect to downstream
015            performance and representation capacity through learning (e.g. measured by
016            the number of dead neurons and the effective rank). In reinforcement learning,
017            the performance of continuously differentiable activations often falls short as
018
               compared to piece-wise linear functions. We provide insights into the vanishing
               gradients associated with the former, and show that the dying neuron problem
019
               is not exclusive to ReLU’s. To alleviate vanishing gradients and the resulting
020
               dying neuron problem occurring with continuously differentiable activations, we
021            propose a Hadamard representation. Using deep Q-networks, proximal policy
022            optimization and parallelized Q-networks in the Atari domain, we show faster
023            learning, a reduction in dead neurons and increased effective rank.

087   summarized as follows:
088
089               • We show that, in reinforcement learning, dying hyperbolic tangents are a phenomenon of a
090                 similar scale as the dying ReLU problem, but have a more profound effect on performance.
091

093

      We consider an agent acting within its environment, where the environment is modeled as a discrete
102   Markov Decision Process (MDP) defined as a tuple (S, A, T, R, γ). S is the state space, A is the
103   action space, T : S × A → S is the environment’s transition function, R : S × A → R is the
104   environment’s reward function and γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor. A replay buffer B is used to
105   store visited states st ∈ S that were followed by actions at ∈ A and resulted in the rewards rt ∈ R
106   and the next states st+1 . One entry in B contains a tuple of past experience (st , at , rt , st+1 ). The
107   agent’s goal isPto learn a policy π : S → A that maximizes the expectation of the discounted return
                        ∞
      V π (s) = Eτ [ t=0 γ t R(st , at ) | st = s], where τ is a trajectory following the policy π.

113   3             AUGMENTING H YPERBOLIC TANGENTS
114
115   Continuously differentiable activations such as the hyperbolic tangent (tanh) and the sigmoid (σ)
116   activations are fundamentally different than the ReLU or its piece-wise linear descendants, which are
117   non-symmetric and have a large part of the input space mapped to zero (leading to sparsity). The
118   hyperbolic tangent and the sigmoid output values in the ranges [−1, 1] and [0, 1], respectively. These
                                            x   −x
119   functions are defined as tanh(x) = eex −e                    1
                                              +e−x and σ(x) = 1+e−x .
120
      Both functions have the advantage of being differentiable everywhere, as well as being bounded.
121   Furthermore, the sigmoid is well suited for output probabilities, while the tanh is convenient when
122   requiring a zero-centered symmetrical output. However, both functions exhibit the vanishing gradient
123   problem for saturating activations (Glorot & Bengio, 2010; Goodfellow et al., 2016).
124
125
      DYING H YPERBOLIC TANGENTS
126
127   Although common literature has focused on the dying ReLU problem (He et al., 2015; Lu et al., 2019;
128   Gulcehre et al., 2022; Sokar et al., 2023), we find that hidden layers activated by hyperbolic tangents
129   similarly show strong dying neuron behavior. When using any activation function in a deep neural
130   network, a single neuron αi , i ∈ Rw , with w the layer dimension, is saturated or dying if:
131
132
133                                                                        αi ≈ Ω ∀ st ∈ B                                                                                              (1)
134
135   Where Ω represents the saturation value and st is an observation in buffer B. In practice, a mini-batch
136
      of observations is evaluated instead of the whole dataset in B. For the hyperbolic tangent, given that it
      is an asymptotic function near its saturation point, an approximate equality must be considered, as the
137
      classification of its saturation will always remain qualitative (|αi | ≠ 1 ∀ st ∈ B) . To approximate
138
      the condition given in Eq. 1, the amount of dying hyperbolic tangents is calculated by using a kernel
139   density estimation (KDE) (Silverman, 1986) on the activations αi , i ∈ Rw of each individual neuron
140   in the activation layer. In order to visualize activations in a hidden layer, a fixed subset of the KDE’s
141   of the neurons αi is taken. A clear visualization of dying hyperbolic tangents during training in the
142   Atari Breakout environment can be seen by analyzing sixteen individual neuron KDE’s in Fig. 3.

      H ADAMARD R EPRESENTATIONS (HR)
179
180   As Fig. 3 indicates that the activation of zt with a hyperbolic tangent leads to saturation and dying
181   neurons, an augmentation of the representation architecture is proposed. In the conventional encoder
182   setting, the networks’ final hidden layer is defined as z enc (x) = f (A1 x + B1 ), with Ai and Bi
183   representing weight and bias parameters, the function f () representing a nonlinear activation function
184   while x is the set of activations from the previous layer. In order to reduce the information dependence
185   on a single set of neurons, we propose using a Hadamard representation that augments the original
186   representation with a parallel representation layer z ∗ . This can be interpreted as using a single
187
      highway layer with a closed carry gate (Srivastava et al., 2015), or as an augmented version of the
      Gated Linear Unit (GLU) (Dauphin et al., 2017). The final representation is defined as the Hadamard
188
      product between the aforementioned activations z(x) = z enc (x)·z ∗ (x), where z ∗ (x) = f (A2 x+B2 ).
189
      A visualization of the proposed architecture can be found in Fig. 4.
190

      P REVENTING DYING N EURONS
192
193   Our key hypothesis is that the Hadamard representation can prevent saturation, hence alleviating
194   vanishing gradients and dying neurons (See Eq. 1). To support our hypothesis, we investigate the
195   derivative of a product of two functions. For the product of two arbitrary functions g(x) · h(x), the
196   derivative is defined as g ′ (x)h(x) + g(x)h′ (x). In the context of using a sigmoid activation function
197   for f (x), the derivative of z(x) becomes:
198                       z ′ (x) = A1 σ(A1 x + B1 )(1 − σ(A1 x + B1 ))σ(A2 x + B2 )
199
                                 +A2 σ(A1 x + B1 )σ(A2 x + B2 )(1 − σ(A2 x + B2 ))
200
201
202
      If a neuron from f (A1 x + B1 ) = 0 ∀ x, the gradient of the product becomes 0 while if a positive
      saturation is experienced i.e. f (A1 x + B1 ) = 1 ∀ x, z ′ (x) can remain nonzero. For a product of two
203
      hyperbolic tangent functions, the derivative is defined as:
204
205        z ′ (x) = A1 sech2 (A1 x + B1 ) tanh(A2 x + B2 ) + A2 sech2 (A2 x + B2 ) tanh(A1 x + B1 )
206
207   In this context, sech2 is the derivative of the hyperbolic tangent function. Unlike the sigmoid, the
208   hyperbolic tangent saturates to nonzero values, ensuring that if and only if both parts are saturated,
209   product saturation occours. Thus, when g(x) saturates, h(x) still keeps a non-trivial gradient in
210   the product, providing a mechanism to avoid vanishing gradients. We visualize the kernel densities
211   during training with a Hadamard representation in Fig. 5. The individual representations before
212
      taking the Hadamard product can be found in Appendix B.
213   Taking a more formal approximation of neuron collapse, we start by defining the probability of
214   a single neuron saturating as p. Furthermore, in the case of a sigmoid or hyperbolic tangent, we
215   assume symmetric saturation probabilities to both ends, defining the probability of a neuron saturating
      to one end of the spectrum as 0.5p. Lastly, we make an independence assumption between two

      Hyperbolic Tangent: In the case of the hyperbolic tangent, product saturation only occurs if strictly
      both neurons are saturated. This results in a probability of p · p = p2 . Taking a product of hyperbolic
239
      tangent activated neurons thus reduces the probability of neuron saturation from p to p2 .
240
241
      Sigmoid: For the sigmoid function, product saturation occurs in two scenarios: Either one of the
242   neurons is saturated towards zero or both neurons are saturated towards 1. The probability that a
243   single neuron does not saturate towards zero is (1 − 0.5p), and subsequently the probability that
244   neither neuron saturates towards zero is (1 − 0.5p)2 . The probability that at least one of the two
245   neurons saturates to zero is therefore 1 − (1 − 0.5p)2 = p − 0.25p2 . Adding the probability that both
246   neurons saturate towards 1, which is (0.5p)2 = 0.25p2 , the final probability of the neuron product
247   saturation is p − 0.25p2 + 0.25p2 = p. Taking a product of sigmoid activated neurons therefore
248   does not reduce the probability of neuron collapse.
249
250   Rectified Linear-Unit: In the case of a ReLU activation, we also assume the probability of a single
251
      neuron dying to be p. As we look at the product of two neurons, the probability that one of the two
      neurons does not saturate is therefore 1 − p, and the probability that both neurons do not saturate is
252
      (1 − p)2 . The probability that at least one neuron saturates is thus equal to 1 − (1 − p)2 = 2p − p2 .
253
      As the ReLU saturation results in strict zeroes, this results in the product also being zero. Taking a
254   product of ReLU activated neurons therefore increases the final neuron saturation probability from p
255   to 2p − p2 . For an overview, we refer the reader to both Table 1 and the corresponding empirical
256   evidence in Appendix C.3.

      When dying neurons occur in ReLU-activated layers, it basically prunes these neurons and the
433   associated weights to the next layer. However, in hyperbolic tangent activated layers, dying neurons
434   lead to an unintended phenomenon where weights associated with dead neurons effectively become
435   biases.

468   5    R ELATED W ORK
469
470   Network Capacity in RL. Liu et al. (2019) investigated the need for sparse representations in
471   the continuous control domain. Gulcehre et al. (2022) analyzed network expressiveness in RL by
472   measuring the effective rank (Kumar et al., 2021) of the compressed representation, and found that
473   hyperbolic tangent representations generally maintain high rank while not suffering strongly from
474   rank decay as training continues. Related work used normalization techniques and action penalization
475   to counteract high variance in pixel-based robotic control (Bjorck et al., 2022). Other work by Lyle
476   et al. (2022) investigated capacity loss in RL and similarly found that, as training progresses, the
477
      inherent network capacity of RL algorithms decays. Further research by Nikishin et al. (2022) used
      network resets to counteract the primacy bias and Sokar et al. (2023) evaluated and mitigated the
478
      dying ReLU phenomenon in DQN, both operating in the sample efficiency setting. Nikishin et al.
479
      (2023) further studied plasticity injection for long-term training and Delfosse et al. (2024) applied
480   rational activations (Molina et al., 2019) in RL to increase plasticity. Concurrent work by Dohare
481   et al. (2024) used continual back-propagation to further alleviate plasticity loss. In another related
482   direction, recent work has investigated network sparsity in RL, showing that a large part of network
483   capacity might be unnecessary when training reinforcement learning (Arnob et al., 2021; Graesser
484   et al., 2022; Sokar et al., 2022; Tan et al., 2023; Obando-Ceron et al., 2024). This provides further
485   insights into why a ReLU can achieve strong performance despite resulting in a significant number of
      dead neurons.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Network Architecture in RL The origin of network optimization problems with hyperbolic
487   tangents and sigmoids were empirically investigated by Glorot & Bengio (2010), where, according
488   to the authors, a lot of mystery still surrounds the subject. Work by Srivastava et al. (2015) in
489   supervised learning first looked at the idea of using products of hidden layers together with a ’gate’
490   that determined the amount of information flow (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). Using these
491   ideas, the Resnet was invented (He et al., 2016) and also showed strong performance in combination
492   with RL (Espeholt et al., 2018). Further work by Henderson et al. (2018) showed differences in RL
493   performances over different network architectures and nonlinear activations. Work by Abbas et al.
494   (2023) successfully applied ReLU concatenation (Shang et al., 2016) to improve continual learning
495
      while keeping a similar performance when training from scratch. Finally, recent work by Grooten
      et al. (2024) investigated raw pixel masking for distractions in RL using a parallel CNN input layer.
496
497
498   6    C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSION
499
500   This paper analyzed issues with continuously differentiable activations in RL and demonstrated that
501   these activation functions also suffer from the dying neuron problem. Based on this analysis, we
502   propose a novel representation architecture, the Hadamard Representation (HR), which enhances
503   an encoder’s final hidden layer by taking the Hadamard product with a parallel, independently
504
      parameterized activation layer. We further discussed and empirically showed that applying the
      Hadamard representation with hyperbolic tangents reduces the occurrence of dead neurons in the
505
      representation and increases layer expressiveness. In DQN, PQN and PPO, this approach significantly
506
      improved performance in Atari games compared to both standard representation parameterizations
507   and merely increasing the representation dimension. Finally, we have given insights into the ability
508   of the ReLU activation to be less affected the symptoms of dying neurons, as opposed to non-zero
509   saturating activations such as the hyperbolic tangent. Future work could focus on further identifying
510   the intricacies of the effects that different activation functions have on the resulting representation,
511   in an attempt to push the potential of non piece-wise linear activations in reinforcement learning
512   or even in a supervised learning setting (see Fig. 2). Also, we believe that an implementation of
513   Hadamard-style architectures in a continual learning setting as in Abbas et al. (2023) or in Delfosse
514   et al. (2024) could be promising.
515
516   7    L IMITATIONS
517
518   Using a Hadamard representation will double the incoming weights connected to a hidden layer.
519   However, in Fig. 10, a comparison against the CReLU is shown, which equally doubles the network’s
520   parameters. Furthermore, recent work shows that a scaling baselines in Atari often leads to reduced
521   performance (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024; Obando Ceron et al., 2024). Second, due to the vast
522   amount of activation functions and the theoretical sound candidacy for the hyperbolic tangent, we
523   have mostly focused our research on the combination of hyperbolic tangents. Further research into
524
      multiplicative representations could find even better activation combinations. A hyperparameter
      search could also give more insights into the strengths of the Hadamard Representation, since the
525
      baselines are specifically tuned for the ReLU activation. Finally, as this research performs experiments
526
      on DQN, PPO and PQN, integration of a Hadamard representation into more complex algorithms and
527   architectures such as Rainbow (Hessel et al., 2018), Impala (Espeholt et al., 2018) or Schwarzer et al.
528   (2021) would be interesting.

      H ADAMARD I MPLEMENTATION
865
866   Constructing a Hadamard representation is a straightforward process that only requires an addi-
867   tional, parallel linear layer. Starting from the flattened convolutional features in DQN, the Pytorch
868   pseudocode is defined as follows:
869
870
      flattened_features = conv_features.flatten(1)
      representation1 = nn.Tanh(linear1(flattened_features))
871
      representation2 = nn.Tanh(linear2(flattened_features))
872
      hadamard = representation1 * representation2
873   Q-values = linear_q(hadamard)
874
875   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
876
877   In DQN, the action at is chosen following an ϵ-greedy policy. With probability ϵ, a random action is
878   selected, and with (1 − ϵ), the action maximizing the Q-value is chosen. The target Yt is defined as:
879                                Yt = rt + γQ′ (zt+1 , arg maxQ(zt+1 , a)),                              (5)
880                                                          a∈A
881
      where Q′ (z, a) denotes the target Q-network, an auxiliary network that stabilizes the learning by
882
      providing a stable target for Q(z, a). The parameters of Q′ are updated less frequently to enhance
883   learning stability. The loss function for training the network is:
884
                                                                    2
885                                          LQ = Yt − Q(zt , a) .                                         (6)
886
887   Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) operates on a different principle, utilizing policy gradient
888   methods for policy improvement. PPO seeks to update the policy by maximizing an objective
889   function while preventing large deviations from the previous policy through a clipping mechanism in
890   the objective’s estimator. The clipped policy gradient loss LCLIP is defined as:
891
                                          h                                            i
                          LCLIP (θ) = E min(rt (θ)Ât , clip(rt (θ), 1 − ϵ, 1 + ϵ)Ât ) ,             (7)
892
893
894   where rt (θ) represents the ratio of the probabilities under the new policy versus the old policy, and
895   Ât is the advantage estimate at timestep t. This clipped surrogate objective ensures gradual and stable
896   policy updates.

1300   We normalize performance with respect to the ReLU baseline in cleanrl Huang et al. (2022).
1301   The minimum and maximum score of the ReLU baseline are taken for each environment, and the
1302   normalized score for each environment is calculated as follows:
1303                                                     Score − Min Score
1304                             Normalized Score =                                            (8)
                                                      Max Score − Min Score
1305
1306
       where Score refers to the raw performance score of the model being evaluated, Min Score is a single
       value representing the lowest score obtained by the ReLU baseline (usually equivalent to random
1307
       policy or even slightly worse), and Max Score is a single value representing the highest score achieved
1308
       by the ReLU baseline in the same environment. To average, we sum the normalized scores for every
1309   run and take the mean.
1310
1311   The more official Human-Normalized Score, as referenced in Mnih et al. (2015), is calculated
1312
       similarly but using human and random performance benchmarks:
1313                                                                   Score − Random Score
                          Human-Normalized Score =                                                         (9)
1314                                                                 Human Score − Random Score
1315
       where Human Score and Random Score refer to the scores recorded by human players and random
1316
       agents, respectively. Calculating our performance according to the Human-Normalized Score leads to
1317   the plot seen in Fig. 19. Due to taking a subset of the Atari domain in DQN and PPO, the VideoPinball
1318   environment is extremely dominant in the Human-Normalized Score calculation. For a more realistic
1319   comparison of the methods, we therefore decided to use baseline-normalized scores in the main
1320   paper.
