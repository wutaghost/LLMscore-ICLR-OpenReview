009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual instruction tuning is the key to building large vision language mod-
012            els (LVLMs), which can greatly improve the task generalization and solving capa-
013            bilities by learning a mixture of instruction data from diverse visual tasks. Previ-
014            ous work mostly collects multiple existing visual instruction datasets via heuristic
015            ways for training (even more than a million instructions), which may introduce
016            data redundancy and enlarge the training cost. To investigate this issue, we con-
017            duct a series of empirical studies, which reveal a significant redundancy within the
018
               visual instruction datasets, and show that greatly reducing the amount of instruc-
               tions from several tasks even do not affect the performance. Based on the findings,
019
               we propose a high-value data selection approach TIVE, to eliminate redundancy
020
               within the visual instruction data and reduce the training cost. In TIVE, we first
021            estimate the instance influence score on its corresponding task, and the task dif-
022            ficulty score, based on the gradient-based influence functions. Then, we leverage
023            the two kinds of scores to determine the task proportion within the selected visual
024            instruction subset, and select high-value instances for each task, respectively. Ex-
025            periments on various LVLMs show that our approach using only about 15% data
026            can achieve comparable average performance to the full-data fine-tuned model
027            across eight benchmarks, even surpassing it on four of the benchmarks. Our code
028            and data will be publicly released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The advent of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron
      et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023b) has marked significant advancements in the field of artificial in-
034
      telligence (AI), exhibiting excellent capabilities in human instruction following, world knowledge
035
      utilization, and complex reasoning. A surge of recent studies (Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;
036   Dai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a) equip LLMs with the vision encoder to empower the capability of
037   processing visual information. Through vision-language alignment pre-training and visual instruc-
038   tion tuning, Large Vision Language Models (LVLMs) are created to extend the application of LLMs
039   into multimodal tasks and scenarios.
040
      Visual instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2023b; Dai et al., 2023) is the key technique for improving the
041
      task generalization and instruction following capabilities of LVLMs, which relies on a set of visual
042   instructions for fine-tuning. Therefore, the construction of visual instruction datasets is very crucial
043   for LVLMs. Typically, there are two widely used ways to construct visual instructions: synthesizing
044   instructions based on LLMs (Liu et al., 2023b) or transforming existing vision-language datasets
045   into visual instructions (Dai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a). To achieve better performance, exist-
046   ing LVLMs generally combine a mixture of visual instructions from different domains or tasks, to
047   compose a large-scale visual instruction dataset. The LVLMs fine-tuned on these mixtures of visual
048   instructions have shown remarkable performance on massive downstream multimodal benchmarks.
049   However, such a mixture of instructions may also introduce significant data redundancy, leading to
050   increased training costs and potentially overfitting risk. To investigate the redundancy issue, we first
051   conduct an empirical study on the visual instruction dataset of state-of-the-art open-source LVLM,
052   i.e., LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a), by reducing the instruction amount of a certain task and then
053   evaluating the performance. The results show that the reduction of instruction data only leads to
      slight or even no performance decline across most benchmarks, indicating that there exists redun-

139   2.2                 R ESULTS AND F INDINGS
140
      According to the results in Figure 2, we list the main findings as follows:
141
142   First, there exists a significant redundancy in visual instruction datasets. We can observe that de-
143   creasing the amount of instruction data only leads to slight performance drop in most cases. For
144   example, reducing the number of VC would not significantly affect the model’s performance across
145   all benchmarks, and even lead to improvement on ScienceQA using 50% of data. It indicates that
146
      not all the used instruction datasets are indispensable.
147   Second, for each task, the redundancy degree of different instruction datasets differs. For OE-
148   VQA and MC-VQA, reducing their instruction number leads to relatively significant performance
149   degradation, e.g. 8% on MME-P and 7% on MMBench using a pruning ratio of 87.5%, respectively.
150   While pruning task instructions from VC leads to minimal decline on most of the benchmarks.
151   It indicates that different task instructions contribute to the model’s final performance differently.
152
      Therefore, it is necessary to estimate the value of each task, for helping set a more proper pruning
      ratio and mixing proportion for all the tasks.

156
157   In this section, we present our approach TIVE, to reduce the redundancy of visual instruction data.
158   Based on the findings in section 2, it is necessary to consider the contribution degree to the learning
159   of diverse tasks during fine-tuning LVLMs. Specially, we consider measuring both task difficulty
160   and instance influence scores for helping select visual instruction data. Based on the two kinds of es-
161   timated scores, we design the data selection process, to sample a small high-value visual instruction
      subset for efficiently and effectively fine-tuning LVLMs. We show the details of TIVE in Figure 3.

189   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
190
191   The elimination of dataset redundancy aims to select a high-quality subset from a large dataset
192
      suffering the redundancy issue. The selected subset should contain relatively few but informative
      samples, to ensure the performance of the models trained on it. In this work, we focus on reducing
193
      the redundancy of the visual instruction data pool D = {D1 , ..., Dn } , which is a mixture of multiple
194
      highly diverse instruction datasets from different tasks. Each dataset comprises a set of instruction
195   samples, denoted as Di = {s1 , ..., sn } . Our goal is to select a data subset DT from the visual
196   instruction data pool for fine-tuning LVLMs. We use |DT | to denote the target size of the selected
197   subset.
198
      Specially, we select the data subset from two perspectives, with the help of a pre-learned reference
199
      model trained on the sampled small set of the visual instruction data. First, we estimate the value of
200
      each task and rely on its difficulty to determine their proportions within the final subset DT . Second,
201   we estimate the value of each instance within each task Di to select the most useful instances for
202   this task.
203

255   An Efficient Estimation for Both Data Values In addition to the above estimation methods, we
256   develop a more efficient implementation for TIVE, TIVE-efficient, to reduce the time cost for gra-
257
      dient computation across all samples. To achieve this, we uniformly sample a small amount of

258
      difficulty and instance influences using the above algorithm. Then, using the instance influences of
259
      these samples, we train a small model to predict the instance influences for remaining samples. In
260   this way, we significantly reduce the time cost for gradient computation, while slightly compromis-
261   ing estimation accuracy. TIVE-efficient can serve as an effective alternative when computational
262   resources are limited.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Visual Instruction Tuning. Visual instruction tuning is a crucial part of the construction of
489   LVLMs, which aims to enhance the model’s ability on instruction following. The collection of
490   visual instructions is essential for visual instruction tuning. Early studies often employ LLMs to
491   synthesize visual instructions. LVLMs trained on these instructions demonstrate promising capabil-
492   ities in visual conversation and instruction following, but fail to achieve satisfactory performance on
493   academic benchmark (Goyal et al., 2017; Schwenk et al., 2022; Marino et al., 2019). Subsequent
494   studies (Liu et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023) have usually mixed the synthesized
495   visual instructions and instructions from existing academic datasets together as the final instruc-
496   tion data. LVLMs trained on these mixtures of instructions demonstrate exceptional performance
497
      in both understanding and generation scenarios. Despite the success, these efforts solely combine
      all instructions in a simple way, neglecting the potential redundancy within the instructions from
498
      different tasks. We investigate the redundancy in existing visual instruction datasets and propose a
499
      measurement for data value based on instance influence and task difficulty to reduce redundancy.
500
501
      Data Selection for Instruction Tuning. With the advancement of LLMs, the significance of data
502   selection has become increasingly prominent due to the high training costs. As for instruction tun-
503   ing, LIMA (Zhou et al., 2024) is the first to demonstrate that instruction tuning can be accomplished
504   with only a small amount of data. Chen et al. (2023a) further explores the potential of low data us-
505   age in task-specific models. Subsequent efforts focus on estimating the importance of an instruction
506   sample. The importance can be estimated based on certain prior characteristics (e.g. length, com-
507   plexity, diversity) (Liu et al., 2023c; Cao et al., 2023), with the assistance of language models (Jain
508   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Li et al., 2023c), by human efforts (Zhuo et al., 2024; Muennighoff
509   et al., 2023), or using the gradient-based influence estimation on the validation set of the target
510
      benchmark (Xia et al., 2024). Compared to the data selection approach for language instruction
      tuning, our approach doesn’t only rely on prior characteristics of texts, but considers the importance
511
      of visual instructions from a holistic perspective of both image and text. Compared to LESS (Xia
512
      et al., 2024), our approach doesn’t require data from downstream benchmark, thereby achieving
513   better generalization ability.
514
515   Data Selection for Visual Instruction Tuning. Fewer studies have been focusing on data-efficient
516   visual instruction tuning. To the best of our knowledge, there are only two studies currently con-
517   ducted in this area. Among these studies, InstructionGPT-4 (Wei et al., 2023) selects high-quality
518   instructions based on several metrics designed in their studies and SELF-FILTER (Chen et al., 2024)
519   proposes selecting instruction data with higher diversity and difficulty by training a score-net. Com-
520   pared to these studies, We are the first to study data selection for a highly complex mixture of visual
521   task instructions, which provides much better results than the candidate datasets from these stud-
522
      ies. To handle such complex visual instructions, we propose a gradient-based approach to estimate
      data value for efficient and effective task learning. With our approach, we accomplish better results
523
      compared to previous studies on data selection for visual instruction tuning with our selected data.
524
525
526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we focus on the redundancy issue within a mixture of visual instruction datasets that
529   have been widely used for fine-tuning LVLMs. Through our empirical studies, we find that a sig-
530   nificant redundancy exists in the mixed visual instruction datasets, with varying redundancy degrees
531
      across different task instructions. To eliminate redundancy, we design a novel method namely TIVE,
      which first estimates data value based on instance influence and task difficulty, then determines
532
      the instruction task proportion and selects representative instances to compose a smaller visual in-
533
      struction subset for training. Experimental results indicate that, with the help of our data selection
534   method, using only about 15% data can achieve comparable performance as the full-data fine-tuned
535   model across eight benchmarks, even surpassing it on some of the benchmarks.

946   F.2    TIVE’ S PERFORMANCE AT DIFFERENT SAMPLING RATIO
947
948   To verify whether TIVE can exhibits enhanced performance at higher sampling rates and to identify
949   the optimal sampling rate. We conduct extensive experiments of TIVE at various sampling rates,
950   including 15%, 30%, 50%, and 100%. The results are presented in Table 9.
951   As we can observe, at a sampling rate of 50%, TIVE exhibits the most superior average task perfor-
952   mance, achieving comparable or better performance relative to the full-data baseline across nearly all
953   benchmarks. Furthermore, we discover that the performance of the model on different downstream
954   benchmarks varies with increasing sampling rates. We observe a consistent performance improve-
955   ment on MME-P and SEED-I, while on MMBench and SQA-I, the model’s performance exhibits
956   a trend of initial increase followed by a decline. We posit that this phenomenon is attributable to
957   the characteristics of the downstream tasks. For tasks that demand more on visual perception (such
958   as MME-P and SEED-I ), the benefits of improved visual perception capability from increased data
959
      size outweigh the negative impact of redundancy. However, for tasks that demand more on inference
      (such as MMBench and SQA-I), a small amount of data can help the model learn basic inference
960
      patterns in visual scenarios while the risk of potential overfitting caused by increased data size may
961
      interfere with its inference process, causing a significant negative impact. From the perspective of
962   average performance across all tasks, a sampling rate of around 50% appears to be ideal. However,
963   in practical scenarios, the optimal choice of sampling rate needs to consider the specific task type,
964   as well as the trade-off between performance and time cost.

1017
1018
       G.1     TIVE’ S SCALABILITY TO OTHER INSTRUCTION DATASETS
1019
       Our experiments on three datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of TIVE on instruction datasets
1020   ranging from 0.5M to 2M. Ideally, our method can be adapted to any visual instruction dataset.
1021   However, practically, the computational cost of gradient calculations for extremely large instruction
1022   datasets (reaching the scale of 10M or even 100M) is substantial, necessitating a more efficient
1023   implementation (even more efficient than TIVE-efficient) to make the application of TIVE on these
1024   instruction datasets feasible. Simultaneously, when our target instruction dataset encompasses a vast
1025   number of tasks (over 1000), and the differences between each task are not particularly distinct, the
       effectiveness of our approach warrants further exploration.


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       G.2   TIVE’ S APPLICABILITY TO DIFFERENT TYPES OF MULTIMODAL TASKS
1027
1028   Although We have evaluate TIVE across multiple downstream benchmarks, we still acknowledge
1029   the insufficiency in our understanding TIVE’s impact on the model’s generalized performance across
1030   different tasks. Intuitively, the influence of data redundancy on the learning of various model ca-
1031   pabilities is different. For highly visual-related abilities, such as entity recognition or OCR, the
1032   learning of these abilities necessitates a large amount of data. In this case, pruning the original in-
1033   struction dataset through TIVE could easily lead to a decline in these specific abilities. Conversely,
1034
       for some reasoning tasks, as these abilities primarily derive from LLMs, they do not require a sig-
       nificant amount of data for learning. Therefore, the removal of redundancy has little to no impact on
1035
       these abilities, and may even yield improvements. Our method eliminates redundancy from a com-
1036
       prehensive perspective (aiming at improving average overall performance), but overlooks how to
1037   establish an optimal parameter (such as sampling rate, temperature) for dataset selection in specific
1038   scenarios, with the aim of retaining the most effective specific capabilities.
1039
1040   G.3   OTHER POTENTIAL LIMITATION
1041
1042   Firstly, theoretically speaking, our method can be applied to any training scenario, not just limited
1043   to visual instruction tuning. However, we have not sufficiently discussed these types of scenarios in
1044   our paper. Secondly, we address the redundancy issue in a large dataset composed of a vast amount
1045   of highly diverse instruction data combinations. but our approach necessitates the use of existing
1046
       task labels to categorize these different data points. For current instruction dataset, most of these
       labels are accessible and correct, so TIVE is able to effectively reduce redundancy on both task-level
1047
       and instance-level. However, in other scenarios where these labels are either non-existent or inaccu-
1048
       rate, we may need to manually categorize these different data using methods such as clustering. In
1049   such circumstances, whether TIVE remains effective worths further exploration. Thirdly, given the
1050   requirement for gradient computation, TIVE entails a relatively high time cost. Although we can
1051   optimize the calculation of task difficulty and instance influence with TIVE-efficient, these strate-
1052   gies may introduce a certain degree of estimation deviation. While these losses are relatively minor,
1053   there is still room for better implementation.
