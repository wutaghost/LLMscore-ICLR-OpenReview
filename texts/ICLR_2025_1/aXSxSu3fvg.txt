009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Semi-supervised learning (SSL), especially when combined with deep learning
012            (DL) models, is a useful technique when there is a substantial amount of unlabeled
013            data. This is particularly relevant in healthcare applications, such as mHealth,
014            where data is often collected through smartphones. Labels are typically obtained
015            via self-reported questions delivered by the device and tend to have a high rate of
016            non-response i.e., missing labels. Despite its benefit, there is a lack of objective
017            methodology on how to train semi-supervised deep learning (SSDL) models. In
018
               this study, we propose a framework for early-stopping in SSDL that terminates
               learning to prevent overfitting and before the performance starts to deteriorate.
019
               Our approach focuses on three aspects: model stability, generalizability, and high-
020
               confidence pseudo-label (i.e., label assigned to unlabeled data during SSL). We
021            first monitor changes in learned weights of the model to assess convergence, us-
022            ing weight stabilization. We also track cross-entropy loss, identifying which itera-
023            tion of the SSL algorithm minimizes validation loss and improves generalizability.
024            Lastly, we use a sliding window method to assess our confidence in the pseudo-
025            labels, retaining only the most reliable labels during training. Combining these
026            criteria, this SSDL framework can be used to train deep learning models in the
027            context of SSL with an objective criteria that prevents overfitting and improves
028            generalizability. We apply this SSDL training strategy to mHealth data (device
029
               sensor data and self-reported data) collected from participants in a clinical trial,
               which consists of 4,700 observations, 62% of which are unlabeled. Using this ob-
030
               jective early stopping criteria for training, we achieve improvements in accuracy
031
               and F1 scores, compared to the benchmark model where the early stopping criteria
032            is not applied.

035   1     I NTRODUCTION
036
037   1.1   F ROM S UPERVISED TO S EMI -S UPERVISED D EEP L EARNING
038
039   The early stage of deep learning (DL) models primarily focused on supervised learning, used in
040   the case where fully-labeled data are accessible in the training step. For example, supervised DL
041   models achieved success in detecting diabetic retinopathy from retinal images (Gulshan et al., 2016)
042   as accurately as human specialists. In another example, supervised DL has been also applied to
043   predict cardiovascular risk from echocardiogram video data (Ouyang et al., 2020), which provided
044
      more accurate and real-time assessments so that it could fully support clinical decision-making.
045   There are many instances in healthcare applications where fully-labeled data is unavailable, e.g.,
046   only highly trained experts can manually annotate symptoms or diagnostic labels for medical im-
047   ages. Another example of unlabeled data in healthcare applications is in mHealth studies (involving
048   smartphones) which is the context of this work. Prediction models with mHealth data often have
049   prediction targets as user self-reports, also known as Ecological Momentary Assessments or EMA,
050
      which suffer from non-response from participants (Stone et al., 2023). Such non-responses in EMAs
      can be considered as unlabeled data.
051
052   Semi-supervised deep learning (SSDL) is an alternative, to include unlabeled data in the prediction
053   model as excluding them can lead to a biased and less generalizable prediction model. For example,
      Bai et al. (2017) and Yu et al. (2019) demonstrated better performances with image segmentation of


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      cardiac MRI and 3D left atrium data, respectively. In electronic health record (EHR) analysis, Miotto
055   et al. (2018) increased the performance of predicting a large volume of patients’ health outcomes
056   such as the onset of diseases in long-term tracking.
057
058
      One significant remaining challenge in SSDL is is the lack of an objective methodology on how
      to train such models and when to stop training. While stopping rules have been proposed for DL
059
      models, it remains unclear how they should be implemented in semi-supervised learning (SSL)
060
      which iterates on predicting initial labels for unlabeled data (pseudo-labeling) and updating the
061   pseudo-labels through pre-training and fine-tuning (Li et al., 2019). Thus, errors in predicted pseudo-
062   labels from earlier iterations can accumulate and propagate through subsequent training steps—a
063   phenomenon known as error propagation.. This propagation undermines the effectiveness of feature
064   extraction by deep networks, which in turn impacts the model’s final classification performance (Wu
065   & Prasad, 2017; Arazo et al., 2020; Nishi et al., 2021). Furthermore, SSDL is known to tend to be
066   overfitting on small initial pseudo-labels so that robust training strategy is demanding.
067
068   1.2   C ONTRIBUTION
069
070   We propose three heuristic criteria, each serving as an individual early stopping rule for training an
071   SSDL, with the objective of preventing overfitting and error propagation. By incorporating various
072   metrics of model performance and convergence in the training phase, such as, stabilization of the
073   weights of the DL model, criterion to improve generalizability and approach on the confidence of
074
      the pseudo-labels of unlabeled data. This is distinct from existing methods that only rely on a single
      metric, such as accuracy, loss function, or mean squared error (MSE) (Yalniz et al., 2019; Ouali
075
      et al., 2020).
076
077   We demonstrate the application of the heuristic stopping criteria in the SSDL framework and illus-
078   trate how each criterion works under various scenarios. Rather than relying solely on regularization
079   or uniform criteria for model training (Sohn et al., 2020; Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017), this approach
080
      improves performance by tailoring the stopping point to specific training conditions. This scheme
      can be used regardless of the complexity of the task, confidence of pseudo-labels, domain-specificity
081
      of the data.

086   2.1   DATA OVERVIEW
087
088   The data utilized in this study was collected by our research center1 . The participants of this study
089   undergo psychotherapy for depression for 9 weeks and are given a smartphone to monitor their
090   behavior and augment the psychotherapy. The dataset consists of two types of measures: active and
091   passive data.
092   First, active data includes patients’ daily self-reported responses to instruments such as the Pho-
093   tographic Affect Meter (PAM, Pollak et al. (2011)), the Patient Health Questionnaire (PHQ), and
094   homework compliance. Homework refers to psychotherapy-related tasks, which are central to Cog-
095   nitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) (Hofmann et al., 2012). These structured tasks, discussed during
096   therapy sessions, are to be completed between sessions. Predicting non-compliance with these tasks
097   allows for targeted interventions. For compliant participant, encouraging nudges can be sent, while
098
      non-compliant participant may receive reminders or additional support to improve task completion.
099   Second, passive sensing data are multivariate streams of data recorded via the sensors of the mHealth
100   device. Some examples are step counts, travel distance from GPS locations, sleep patterns (e.g.,
101   sleep duration percentage of REM sleep hours, etc.), and time spent in human conversation (inferred
102   from microphone). These passive observations are aggregated as daily measures so that align with
103   the frequency of the active data.
104   In addition to these active and passive data, we incorporate demographic information of the partici-
105   pant for supporting contextual analysis. The dataset consists of 4,700 observations (43 participants

114   2.2   DATA P REPROCESSING
115
116   The passive and active data from mHealth devices need significant preprocessing as device use
117   status (i.e., carrying the smartphone on person or wearing the wearable) is unknown which can
118   result in bias in the prediction model. To this end, we conduct preprocessing in two steps. First, we
119   use 2SpamH (Zhang et al., 2024), a preprocessing algorithm for passively sensed mHealth data, to
120
      estimate days when device use is low, which are considered as missing values; this step addresses
      the bias due to device non-use inherent in passive sensing data from mobile devices (Zhang et al.,
121
      2023). Second, we use the R package missForest (Stekhoven & Bühlmann, 2012) to impute the
122
      missing data, with estimated values derived from neighboring information (i.e., similar days with
123   high device use). This completes our preprocessing and produces a cleaned and imputed dataset
124   where the prediction target, i.e., homework compliance, is measured for 4,700 person-days. Next,
125   we construct features from passive and active data for each person-day of homework compliance
126   by constructing a 7-day and 2-day look-back window for a particular person to capture both long-
127   term and short-term trends. During these look-back windows, we construct features that include the
128   mean, standard deviation, and trends for each feature. All features are then standardized.
129   We perform cross-validation (CV) in a longitudinal participant-specific manner, such that we learn
130   a participant’s behavior through passive and active sensing for the first 21 days and provide per-
131   sonalized predictions after 21 days to the end of the study, which is typically 9 weeks or 63 days.
132   This setup mirrors a real-world use case, where digital interventions are pushed based on predicted
133   homework compliance. The first 7 days (out of 9 weeks) of the participant’s homework compliance
134   data (target of prediction) is not utilized as it is used to engineer features for the prediction model
135   starting at day 8. For the CV, the data is randomly divided into 4-folds at participant level, i.e.,
136   each fold has approximately 10 participants and their entire active and passive data collected over
137
      9 weeks. Then, the longitudinal data of each participant is divided into two blocks, the first block
      from days 0 to 21 (or 3 weeks) is retained only for training and the second block from days 22 -
138
      63 is used for test data in the cross-validation. For each fold in the CV (e.g., Fold 1), the model is
139
      trained using Block 1 data from that fold and Block 1 and 2 data from the remaining folds. Block 2
140   from Fold 1 is used as the test data. This process is repeated for all folds, ensuring that each fold’s
141   Block 2 data serves as the test set once. Figure 1 provides a visual representation of how the dataset
142   is split into folds and blocks for the CV process.

      2.3   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
157
158   The model architecture of the proposed SSDL model is detailed in the Appendix (Figure A1). Each
159   row in the dataset consists of 187 variables used as predictors for predicting a participant’s home-
160   work compliance status on a specific day. The input layer of the model consists of 187 units, with
161   each unit corresponding to one of these variables. Dropout with a rate of 0.3 is applied across the
      input layer, as well as the first and second hidden layers, to randomly deactivate 30% of the neurons


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      during training (i.e., their corresponding weights are not updated). This prevents overfitting by en-
163   suring that the model does not become overly reliant on any single feature (Srivastava et al., 2014).
164   In a neural network (NN) model, the weights are scalars representing the strength of connections be-
165   tween neurons across different layers. These weights are updated after each epoch using the Adam
166   optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2017), in our context, which adapts the learning rate for each parameter.
167
      The first hidden layer has 25 neurons and uses the hyperbolic tangent (Tanh) activation function. The
168
      second hidden layer consists of 13 neurons and uses the the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation
169   function. The number of neurons in the first and second hidden layers was selected through hyper-
170   parameter tuning using Bayesian optimization via KerasTuner in Python (O’Malley et al., 2019) to
171   optimize the model’s performance while maintaining an appropriate level of complexity.
172
      Finally, the output layer consists of a single neuron with a sigmoid activation function, which pro-
173
      duces a probability between 0 and 1 to predict the likelihood of a binary outcome. By applying
174
      dropout throughout the model and tuning the number of neurons in each layer, the architecture is
175   designed to prevent overfitting.

335   2.5.4     C OMBINED C RITERIA
336
      We determine an appropriate point to halt the training of our SSDL model by integrating three key
337
      criteria: model convergence, generalizability, and high-confidence of pseudo-labels. To select a
338
      suitable iteration, we follow a process that integrates these criteria in tandem.
339
340   First, we select the top s(< S) candidate iterations based on each specific criterion: model stability,
341   generalizability, and high-confidence pseudo-labels. These s candidates are selected from the S SSL
342
      iterations, where S represents the total number of training iterations. Here, s can be chosen to ensure
      enough iterations are considered for evaluation. Once we have the top s iterations for each criterion,
343
      we consider the overlap between them. This ensures that the iterations we focus on achieve balanced
344
      performance across multiple metrics. For the overlapping iterations, we compute an average rank
345   based on how well each iteration performs in each criterion. Instead of using the raw performance
346   values, we rank the iterations for each criterion. For instance, if a particular iteration ranks 10th in
347   model convergence criterion, 12th in generalizability, and 2nd in pseudo-label quality, its average
348   rank would be the mean of these rankings: 10+12+2  3     = 8. This approach allows us to aggregate
349   performance across criteria; weighted mean can also be used when favoring specific metrics over
350   others. Finally, we select the iteration with the lowest average rank, prioritizing iterations that
351   perform well across all criteria.

385   Combining all criteria results in a model results in the best AUC, high accuracy and F1 score,
386   moderate sensitivity and NPV, and good specificity and PPV. Thus the combined-criteria approach
387   produces a well-rounded model that maximizes performance across all metrics without major trade-
388
      offs.
389
390   4   D ISCUSSION
391
392   Developing and optimizing our SSDL framework requires careful adjustment of various parameters
393   to achieve the best performance. In deep learning architecture (Figure A1), parameters such as
394   the number of layers, units per layer, activation functions, learning rate, and epochs play a crucial
395   role. For example, model complexity, determined by layers and units, must be balanced to avoid
396   underfitting or overfitting. This parameter tuning is compounded when a SSL framework is used
397
      with deep learning. In our SSDL framework (Algorithm 1), there are additional key parameters that
      include the maximum number of iterations S, the confidence threshold for pseudo-labels θ, and the
398
      sliding window size w in 5 – 7. When proposing an early stopping rule based on a combination
399
      of three criteria—model stability, generalizability, and pseudo-label confidence—we introduce an
400   additional parameter, s, which refers to the top iterations selected based on performance across
401   these criteria (see Section 2.5.4). A small s risks insufficient overlap between performance with
402   respect to the three criteria, while a large s could potentially add noise to the selection of the best
403   performing iteration. In summary, a unified framework to effectively evaluate this complex training
404   procedure is needed.
405
406
      5   C ONCLUSION
407
408
      This study presents a framework for training a SSDL model tailored for healthcare applications, ad-
409   dressing the challenge of limited labeled data. By integrating heuristic criteria—model convergence,
410   generalizability, and confidence of pseudo-label—our approach aims to enhance the robustness of
411   the model.
412
      Each criterion contributed uniquely to evaluating the model’s performance. The model convergence
413
      criterion improved sensitivity and overall accuracy, while the generalizability criterion exhibited
414
      strengths in specificity and positive predictive value. Our results suggest that combining these crite-
415   ria resulted in a more balanced and stable model, yielding high performance across multiple metrics.
416
417   While the approach outlined in this paper serves as an heuristic guide for developing SSDL models,
418
      it is by no means a definitive solution. We aim to provide a framework that encourages further inno-
      vative research in the field of SSDL, particularly as the high volume of unlabeled data is a common
419
      challenge across many real-world applications. By adapting these principles to diverse contexts, this
420
      framework has the potential to offer valuable insights across a wide range of applications.
