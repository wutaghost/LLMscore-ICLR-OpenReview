011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Antibodies offer great potential for the treatment of various diseases. However, the
014            discovery of therapeutic antibodies through traditional wet lab methods is expen-
015            sive and time-consuming. The use of generative models in designing antibodies
016            therefore holds great promise, as it can reduce the time and resources required.
017            Recently, the class of diffusion models has gained considerable traction for their
018
               ability to synthesize diverse and high-quality samples. In their basic form, how-
               ever, they lack mechanisms to optimize for specific properties, such as binding
019
               affinity to an antigen. In contrast, the class of offline Reinforcement Learning
020
               (RL) methods has demonstrated strong performance in navigating large search
021            spaces, including scenarios where frequent real-world interaction, such as interac-
022            tion with a wet lab, is impractical. Our novel method, BetterBodies, which com-
023            bines Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) with offline RL guided latent diffusion,
024            can generate novel sets of antibody CDRH3 sequences from different data distri-
025            butions. Furthermore, we reflect biophysical properties in the VAE latent space
026            using a contrastive loss and add a novel Q-function based filtering to enhance
027            the affinity of generated sequences. Using the Absolut! simulator, we demon-
028            strate that BetterBodies generates sequences with improved binding affinity to
029
               the SARS-CoV spike receptor-binding domain and matches or outperforms the
               state-of-the-art method Generative Flow Network (GFlowNet). In conclusion, our
030
               method has the potential for great implications in real-world biological sequence
031
               design, where the generation of novel high-affinity binders is a cost-intensive en-
032            deavor.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Antibodies are a class of proteins with great potential for treating diseases such as cancer (Kaplon
039
      et al., 2023; Norman et al., 2020; Robert et al., 2022). However, the discovery of therapeutic antibod-
      ies in classical wet lab experiments is constrained by high costs and low throughput (Angermueller
040
      et al., 2019; Angermüller et al., 2020; Shanehsazzadeh et al., 2023). Computational antibody design
041
      using generative models, therefore, holds immense potential for reducing the time and resources
042   needed (Shanehsazzadeh et al., 2023).
043
044   Diffusion models have recently received considerable attention due to their ability to generate di-
045
      verse and high-quality data (Murphy, 2023). Their versatility makes them applicable to numerous
      tasks in the realm of protein design, including protein structure prediction (Anand & Achim, 2022),
046
      protein-protein docking (Ketata et al., 2023), and protein design (Chen et al., 2024; Watson et al.,
047
      2023). However, basic diffusion is not capable of optimizing for a desired property such as binding
048   affinity to an antigen. In contrast, RL methods have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in identifying
049   solutions in large search spaces (Silver et al., 2016). In the domain of offline RL, the objective is to
050   learn an optimal policy from a pre-collected dataset without any real-world interaction. This is well-
051   suited to antibody sequence design, where direct access to a wet lab is not feasible. Consequently,
052   the combination of diffusion models and (offline) RL methods has great potential for the field of
053   computational antibody design. Wang et al. (2023) demonstrated that RL methods can be utilized to
      guide continuous diffusion models toward optimal regions within the explored space.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In our work, we integrate several of these recent advances into an approach we term BetterBodies
055   to design novel diverse antibody CDRH3 sequences with improved binding affinity given a set of
056   training sequences. We introduce a VAE to map amino acids (AAs) into a continuous latent repre-
057   sentation, allowing us to apply the work by Wang et al. (2023) to the AA sequence design task. The
058   offline RL policy, represented by a continuous diffusion model guided by a learned Q-function, is
059   then trained to stepwise diffuse AAs in this latent space to generate novel sequences. This stepwise
060   approach facilitates the stitching of parts of suboptimal sequences contained in the training data to
061   create improved sequences (Kumar et al., 2022). Furthermore, we demonstrate how to improve the
062   affinity of generated sequences by exploiting properties of both the learned AA latent space and the
063
      learned Q-function. Therefore, we introduce a contrastive loss (Khosla et al., 2020) to shape the VAE
      latent space to reflect biophysical properties and show that the learned Q-function can be used as a
064
      discriminator to sort out low-affinity sequences. In experiments on the Absolut! benchmark (Robert
065
      et al., 2022), we demonstrate that BetterBodies can learn from a variety of distributions of sequences
066   and affinity values, including random sequences, sequences generated by an RL agent, and murine
067   antibody sequences. Thereby, sequences generated using BetterBodies match or outperform those
068   generated by the state-of-the-art algorithm GFlowNet Jain et al. (2022).

      2.1   A NTIBODY S EQUENCE D ESIGN
078
079
      Antibodies are a class of proteins, consisting of a sequence of AAs, utilized by the immune system to
080
      recognize and bind foreign molecules (antigens) with high specificity (Norman et al., 2020; Robert
081   et al., 2022). Due to their favorable binding properties, they have become the leading class of new
082   drugs developed (Lu et al., 2020; Norman et al., 2020).
083
084
      Given that there are 20 possible AAs to be placed at each sequence position, the total search space
      for sequences of length L contains 20L sequences. However, it has been demonstrated that specific
085
      regions of the antibodies, the so-called Complementarity Determining Regions (CDRs), contain the
086
      majority of antigen-binding AAs (Norman et al., 2020). Furthermore, the third CDR of the heavy
087   chain (CDRH3) has been shown to have the largest influence on the antibodies’ specificity (Xu &
088   Davis, 2000). Consequently, we utilize the design of CDRH3 sequences as a proxy for the design
089   of complete antibodies. The Absolut! software, which we employ to approximate antibody CDRH3
090   binding affinity to an antigen, fixes the length of this region to 11 positions. Thus, in this work, we
091   will set the length of designed sequences to L = 11, resulting in approximately 205 trillion possible
092   sequences. This vast space precludes exhaustive search, thereby underscoring the potential impact
093   of computational antibody design on wet labs.

      2.2   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS
097
098   Autoencoders are encoder-decoder networks trained to minimize a reconstruction loss between their
099
      input x and reconstructed input dρ (eω (x)), where eω is the encoder network and dρ is the decoder
      network represented by their learnable parameters ω and ρ. The VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014) is
100
      a specific type of autoencoder in which the continuous latent representation, denoted by z = eω (x),
101
      follows a probabilistic distribution pω (z|x). The latent representation z ∼ N (µxω , σωx ) is typically
102   defined as a Gaussian distribution with a learned mean µω and standard deviation σω . In addi-
103   tion to the reconstruction loss, the VAE is regularized such that the latent distribution minimizes
104   the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence to a Gaussian distribution N (0, I), facilitating a dense latent
105   space. In our setting, we use VAEs to encode AAs classes into a two-dimension latent space and use
106   a Binary Cross Entropy loss for reconstruction. Furthermore, VAEs can be regularized to cluster in-
107   puts belonging to the same class by pulling them together in embedding space, while simultaneously
      pushing apart clusters of inputs from different classes (Khosla et al., 2020).

111   into noisy data and learn a reverse process, or denoising process, to undo the corruption. A trained
112   model can thus be used to generate high-quality data from noise (Murphy, 2023).
113

119   cess gradually adds Gaussian noise to x0 according to a noise schedule β 1 , ..., β N , over N steps (Ho

134   2.4   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
135
      In RL, tasks are typically formulated as Markov Decision Processs (MDPs). We define a determin-
136
      istic MDP as a tuple ⟨S, S0 , A, P, R⟩, where S is the set of possible states, S0 is the set of initial
137   states S0 ∈ S, A is the set of possible actions a executable in s ∈ S, P is a deterministic transition
138   function P (s, a) : S × A 7→ S, and R is a deterministic reward function R(s, a) : S × A 7→ R.
139
140
      In this work, we address the task of designing discrete AA sequences, representing antibody CDRH3
      sequences targeting a specific antigen. We choose to frame the task as a stepwise generation process
141
      where the AAs are placed in the sequence one after the other. To evaluate the binding affinity of
142
      designed sequences given a specific antigen, we utilize Absolut! (Robert et al., 2022) which sets
143   the length of a complete sequence to 11. Thus, we define the set of states S as the set of all possible
144   AA sequences up to length 11, including the empty sequence. We define the set S0 as an empty
145   sequence. The set A is then defined as the set of 20 natural AAs. To facilitate notation, the symbol a
146   is used to refer to the action, the AA it represents, and its two-dimensional VAE latent representation.
147   Consequently, we define P (s, a) = s ∥ a as the concatenation of the sequence generated thus far
148   with the next AA, extending the sequence by one more AA. The reward function R(s, a) is defined
149   corresponding to the predicted free energy using the Absolut! software. As sequences of length
150   shorter than 11 can not be evaluated, the reward function is sparse, returning the evaluated free
151
      energy Absolut! (s∥a|antigen) for sequences of length 11 and a reward of 0 for all shorter sequences.
152   The objective in RL is to learn a policy π that maximizes the expected sum of rewards. The action-
153   value function Q represents this expected sum starting from a given state st . We define it as follows:
154                                                        10−t

      The policy π should thus select the action a that maximizes Q for each state s. As the search space
      of CDRH3-sequences is enormously huge, we estimate π and Q with function approximations πθ
159
      and Qϕ (s, a), parameterized by θ and ϕ respectively.

161   In this work, we focus on the offline RL setting, where the agent is trained using a pre-collected
      dataset, which we consider to be more realistic for the antibody design task, as interactive access to

      a wet lab is not feasible. The offline setting presents its own set of challenges (Levine et al., 2020),
163   mainly erroneous assignment of high Q-values to state-action pairs outside the provided dataset and
164   a resulting distribution shift in the policy. There are multiple approaches to prevent these issues.
165   Our method falls in the class of policy regularization, providing an incentive to remain close to the
166   provided dataset.

      3   R ELATED W ORK
170
171   In recent years the field of protein sequence design has been approached with a variety of generative
172
      models and from a multitude of perspectives. Some (Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022; Khan et al., 2022;
      Vogt et al., 2023) approach the task in an online setting, where the policy has continuous access to
173
      the evaluation metric and can freely explore the design space to find a high-reward sequence using
174
      RL or bayesian optimization methods. In other settings, which are sometimes referred to as active
175   learning settings, the generative policy is trained from pre-collected offline datasets for multiple
176   rounds where generated sequences can be evaluated and might be added to the datasets in between
177   rounds (Angermueller et al., 2019; Angermüller et al., 2020; Jain et al., 2022). In such settings, en-
178   sembles of evolutionary algorithms (Angermüller et al., 2020), RL algorithms (Angermueller et al.,
179   2019), and GFlowNets (Jain et al., 2022) have been employed as generative models. Lastly, the task
180   can also be approached from a purely offline perspective, where the generative policy is trained only
181   once on a pre-collected offline dataset and then evaluated (Chen et al., 2024; Gruver et al., 2024;
182   Jain et al., 2022).
183   We approach the task from a purely offline perspective and will present related work from that
184   domain in more detail here. In some of their experiments, Jain et al. (2022) utilize GFlowNets to
185   tackle the design of DNA sequences and protein sequences in the offline setting. They thereby utilize
186   a learned reward model to explore beyond the offline data. In addition to generating samples that
187   optimize a desired property, the networks are also trained to generate samples with high uncertainty
188   according to the learned reward model. The choice of GFlowNets, which are trained to generate
189
      samples with likelihoods proportional to their reward fraction in the dataset, intuitively allows for
      the generation of high-reward samples. In practice, this class of networks is hard to train, due to
190
      oversampling of low reward trajectories, and the rewards need to be non-linearly scaled to achieve a
191
      good performance (Jain et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2023).
192
193   In their approach, Chen et al. (2024) utilize a continuous diffusion model to generate entire antimi-
194   crobial peptide (AMP) sequences in an ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2023) latent space. The choice of dif-
195
      fusion models, which are capable of modeling complex multi-modal distributions (Ho et al., 2020;
      Wang et al., 2023), appears well suited for the complex circumstances underlying AA sequence
196
      design. They demonstrated that generated peptides exhibited similar physicochemical properties to
197
      natural peptides and aligned closely with respect to AA diversity, which highlights the expressive
198   power of their method. However, they do not employ any technique guiding the diffusion process
199   towards improved sequences. In contrast, Gruver et al. (2024) employ discrete diffusion, whereby
200   sequences are directly diffused in the discrete sequence space. They propose guiding the diffusion
201   model by a learned value function. However, their formulation requires the diffusion model and the
202   value function to share some of their hidden layers and requires the value function to be trained on
203   corrupted inputs (Gruver et al., 2024). Furthermore, wet lab experiments were conducted on gener-
204   ated antibody sequences, which indicated that some of the designed sequences may have improved
205   real-world binding affinity.
206   RL methods have been demonstrated to identify solutions in large search spaces (Silver et al., 2016)
207   and to be applicable to the sequence design task (Angermueller et al., 2019; Cowen-Rivers et al.,
208   2022; Vogt et al., 2023). Moreover, it has been shown that learned Q-functions, employed in RL, can
209   be utilized to guide continuous diffusion models towards high rewards in offline RL (Wang et al.,
210   2023).
211   In our work, we apply recent advances in offline RL to the protein sequence design task. Similar
212   to Jain et al. (2022) but in contrast to Gruver et al. (2024) and Chen et al. (2024) we choose to
213   phrase the protein sequence as a stepwise AA generation task, conditioned on the sequence generated
214   thus far, instead of generating entire sequences at once. Such an approach facilitates the use of Q-
215   functions which are able to stitch together improved sequences from suboptimal ones (Kumar et al.,
      2022). We enable continuous diffusion of discrete AAs by training a VAE encoding discrete AA into

      a latent space and decoding generated continuous vectors back to the discrete AAs. Furthermore,
217   we show how biophysical properties can be injected into the latent space to improve performance.
218   Finally, we propose a novel filtering method based on learned Q-values to enhance the average
219   affinity in the set of returned sequences.

251               P ROPERTIES

253   In contrast to discrete diffusion (Gruver et al., 2024), which is designed to directly generate categor-
254   ical values in the reverse process, our policy π generates a continuous latent vector. This continuous
255
      vector facilitates guiding the diffusion model and shaping the latent space to incorporate biophys-
      ical properties but requires representing the categorical AAs as continuous vectors. We choose to
256
      represent them using the two-dimensional latent vectors of a VAE which we train and freeze before
257
      training the diffusion model. The latent vectors generated by π are then transformed back to discrete
258   AAs using the VAE’s decoder network. To train the VAE, each AA a is represented as a one-hot
259   vector and mapped to a two-dimensional latent z = eω (a) ∼ N (µaω , σωa ) using the encoder network
260   eω . The decoder network dρ (z) then maps the latent vector z back to a probability distribution over
261   discrete AAs. Consequently, the VAE can be trained end-to-end by minimizing the Binary Cross
262   Entropy (BCE) loss between the input a and the decoder’s output. Additionally, the distribution of
263   latent variables z is regularized to minimize the KL divergence to the Gaussian distribution. The
264   loss function of the VAE is then given as L(a) = BCE(a, dρ (eω (a))) + KL(N (µaω , σωa ), N (0, I)).
265   Furthermore, the latent space utilized in our method also allows for incorporating additional biases.
266   As a proof of concept, we chose to regularize the VAEs latent space to represent AA properties.
267   Specifically, we group the AAs according to their side chains’ properties, based on classification by
268   Garrett & Grisham (2010) with some modifications (cp. Appendix Section A.3). In this modification
269   of our method, which we refer to as BetterBodies-C(ontrastive), we added a supervised contrastive
      loss to the VAE training objective to realize this grouping in latent space (Khosla et al., 2020).

511   7   C ONCLUSION
512
513   We presented BetterBodies a novel method for antibody CDRH3 sequence design, demonstrating the
514   applicability of guided latent diffusion for successive AA sequence design. Our method successfully
515   generates novel, diverse, and high-affinity sequences towards the SARS-CoV spike receptor-binding
516
      domain given three different sequence and affinity distributions, evaluated using the Absolut! soft-
      ware. We demonstrated that Q-value guidance and our novel filtering and contrastive latent methods
517
      enhance the affinity of generated sequences when compared to basic diffusion. We further demon-
518
      strate that our methods match or exceed the affinity scores of GFlowNets, but sometimes generates
519   less diverse sequence sets. In conclusion, methods such as ours have the potential to have great
520   implications for real-world biological sequence design, where the generation of novel high-affinity
521   binders is a cost-intensive endeavor (Norman et al., 2020; Shanehsazzadeh et al., 2023).
522
523
524
      8   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE W ORK
525
      In this work, we proposed a novel method for protein sequence generation using diffusion mod-
526
      els and RL. One of the main drawbacks of diffusion models is the relatively high computational
527
      time, especially for higher N . This could presumably be reduced using methods by Kang et al.
528   (2024), Nichol & Dhariwal (2021), or Song et al. (2021), which would increase the training and in-
529   ference speed of our method. Additionally, there are many recent methods proposed in the offline RL
530   community (Levine et al., 2020) which could be used instead of clipped double Q-learning (Fujimoto
531   et al., 2018). Finally, our method could be extended to the model-based and active learning setting
532   and subsequently be evaluated using the sequence tasks proposed by Jain et al. (2022) and Trabucco
533   et al. (2022).

      To reduce the effect of the latent space’ structure on the reported results, we share the pre-trained
740   VAE between all datasets for a given seed. Due to the large computational burden, we chose N = 5
741   diffusion steps for our experiments, even though we found that N = 50 leads to better results for
742   η = 0. This finding is analogous to (Wang et al., 2023). We follow (Wang et al., 2023) for the choice
743   of β noise schedule to train our diffusion model.
744
      As in the implementation by Wang et al. (2023) we generate 50 actions using the Diffusion Model
745
      per step and sample the final action via a softmax distribution over the respective Q-weights.
746
747   Note, that we choose to represent s for the Policy π and Q-function as a concatenation of one-hot
748   encodings, which represent the previous AAs, due to its simplicity. In theory, a concatenation of
749
      VAE latent vectors, or a latent vector representing the entire sequence, could also be used.
