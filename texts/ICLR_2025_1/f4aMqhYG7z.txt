                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012
               Conventional supervised single-image dehazing methods, which are trained with
               substantial synthetic hazy-clean image pairs, have achieved promising performance.
013
               However, they often fail to tackle out-of-distribution hazy images, due to the
014
               domain shift between source and target scenarios (e.g., between indoor and outdoor,
015            between synthetic and real). In this work, we observe the opportunity for improving
016            such dehazing models’ generalization ability without modifying the architectures
017            or weights of conventional models by adopting the diffusion model to transfer the
018            distribution of input images from target domain to source domain. Specifically, we
019            train a denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) with source hazy images
020            to capture prior probability distribution of the source domain. Then, during the
021            test-time the obtained DDPM can adapt target hazy inputs to source domain in the
022            reverse process from the perspective of conditional generation. The adapted inputs
023
               are fed into a certain state-of-the-art (SOTA) dehazing model pre-trained on source
               domain to predict the haze-free outputs. Note that, the whole proposed pipeline,
024
               termed Diffusion-based ADaptation (DiffAD), is model-agnostic and plug-and-
025
               play. Besides, to enhance the efficiency in real image dehazing, we further employ
026            the predicted haze-free outputs as the pseudo labels to fine-tune the underlying
027            model. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that our DiffAD is effective,
028            achieving superior performance against SOTA dehazing methods in domain-shift
029            scenarios.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Hazy images often suffer from low contrast, poor visibility, and color distortion (Tan, 2008), impos-
035   ing a negative impact on the downstream high-level vision tasks, such as object detection, image
036   classification, and semantic segmentation. According to the atmospheric scattering model (ASM)
037
      (Narasimhan & Nayar, 2002; 2003), the hazing process is commonly formulated as:
038                                     I(x) = J(x)t(x) + A(1 − t(x)),                                   (1)
039
      where I(x) is the observed hazy image and J(x) denotes the clean image of the same scene. A and
040
      t(x) are the global atmospheric light and the transmission map, respectively.
041
042   With the advancement of deep learning, various methods have been proposed to solve this highly
043   ill-posed problem (Wu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024). Among them, well-designed architectures
044   based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) or transformers try to learn the dehazing priors from
045
      large-scale synthetic hazy-clean pairs and reach state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. Such dehazing
      priors are particularly effective for synthetic hazy images with similar distribution to training data.
046
      However, the domain shift caused by different scenarios (indoor and outdoor) or different haze modes
047
      (synthetic and real) makes it challenging to generalize the learned dehazing priors from one specific
048   domain (i.e., source) to another. For example, a model trained on indoor datasets fails to achieve
049   desirable results in outdoor scenes as shown in Fig. 1. Previous methods (Shao et al., 2020; Chen
050   et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022) attempt to bridge this domain gap via generative
051   adversarial networks (GANs) or unsupervised losses. These methods struggle to produce visually
052   pleasing results. On one hand, GANs are difficult to train and may generate artifacts in the results.
053   On the other hand, when the handcrafted priors (that unsupervised losses rely on) fail, the dehazing
      results may be biased. Moreover, these methods achieve domain adaptation by updating parameters

      effectively leverage such dehazing priors in another unseen domain (biased from source domain)

079   Recently, diffusion probabilistic models (DDPM) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020)
080   have gradually surpassed GANs and exhibited great success in various tasks, such as image gen-
081   eration (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), image editing (Meng et al., 2022) and image restoration (Fei
082
      et al., 2023; Özdenizci & Legenstein, 2023). Given the powerful capacity for modeling complicated
      data distributions and generating high-quality images, DDPM can achieve domain translation by
083
      adding Gaussian noise and then gradually denoising (Meng et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022; Peng et al.,
084
      2023). Such property inspires a new research direction for effectively leveraging the learned dehazing
085   prior in another unseen domain. One intuitive and feasible idea is to project hazy images from the
086   target domain to the source domain by DDPM, and then perform dehazing through the dehazing
087   model trained on the source domain. Since the weights are frozen after training, the dehazing priors
088   encapsulated in the dehazing model remain intact and can be fully leveraged. However, as a kind
089   of generative model, DDPM tends to slightly alter the image content during the domain translation
090   process, introducing cumulative errors into the subsequent dehazing model. In addition, another
091   drawback lies in the efficiency problem. How to maintain the fidelity after domain translation and
092   how to enhance the efficiency are key challenges existing in this idea.
093   Based on the above discussions, we propose a novel Diffusion-based ADaptation paradigm (i.e.,
094   DiffAD) to explore the domain shift problem in image dehazing. DiffAD acts on the input hazy
095   images to adjust the distribution. First, we train a DDPM with source hazy images to capture the
096   prior probability distribution of the source domain. A source-Gaussian-source loop is built in this
097   step and given a hazy image from the target domain (e.g., real-captured hazy image), we can adjust
098   the distribution to make it align with the source domain (e.g., synthetic hazy image). A novel loss
099
      function is designed by considering the fidelity and quality to guide the generation during the reverse
      process (preventing the generative output from structure distortion and color variation). Then, the
100
      adapted hazy image can be directly fed into a certain SOTA dehazing model (e.g., AECRNet (Wu
101
      et al., 2021), Dehazeformer (Song et al., 2023), and FocalNet (Cui et al., 2023)) pre-trained on source
102   domain to predict the haze-free output. The SOTA dehazing model is used for inference and will
103   not change its weights and architecture. Therefore, the crucial dehazing priors can be fully explored
104   and exploited. Finally, due to the absence of clean ground-truth images from target domain, we
105   employ the haze-free outputs from our DiffAD as pseudo labels (with some necessary modifications)
106   to fine-tune the underlying SOTA model. Surprisingly, the fine-tuned model no longer requires the
107   DDPM, leading to a significant improvement in efficiency. In summary, our main contributions are as
      follows:

109            domain shift problem in image dehazing. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first

124   2    R ELATED WORK
125
126   Single Image Dehazing. Early efforts (Fattal, 2008; Tan, 2008; He et al., 2010; Fattal, 2014; Zhu
127   et al., 2015; Berman et al., 2016) made in image dehazing relies on ASM and primarily focus on
128   handcraft priors observed from both hazy and haze-free images. These methods achieve promising
129   results but fail in scenes that do not satisfy their assumptions. The advent of deep learning has
130   revolutionized image dehazing by freeing it from handcrafted priors. A variety meticulously designed
131
      architectures (Cai et al., 2016; Ren et al., 2016; Li et al., 2017; Zhang & Patel, 2018; Liu et al., 2019;
      Dong et al., 2020; Dong & Pan, 2020; Qin et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022; Hong et al.,
132
      2022; Ye et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; He et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang
133
      et al., 2024) has been proposed to learn image dehazing from the large-scale synthetic datasets (Li
134   et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2021). For example, Qin et al. (2020) introduce attention mechanisms to CNNs
135   and significantly improve the dehazing performance. Song et al. (2023) propose a transformer-based
136   architecture to further promote image dehazing. Although these learning-based methods achieve
137   impressive results, they tend to over-fit the training set and demonstrate poor generalization ability on
138   unseen hazy images.
139
      Domain Adaptation for Image Dehazing. To address the domain shift when encountering unseen
140   hazy images, some studies (Li et al., 2019; Shao et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022;
141   Li et al., 2022) attempt to improve the generalization ability of dehazing models through domain
142   adaptation. For instance, a representative solution (Shao et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022) involves utilizing
143   GANs to perform translation between the source and the target domain, followed by adapting model
144   to the target domain. (Li et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022) start from physical priors
145   and adapt the dehazing model to the target domain in an unsupervised manner. However, due to the
146   updating of model parameters, these methods struggle to preserve the well-learned dehazing priors
147   from large-scale synthetic datasets.
148   Diffusion models. Recently, denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al.,
149   2015; Ho et al., 2020) have attracted widespread attention from researchers as a type of generative
150   model. DDPM gradually converts simple Gaussian noise to complex data distribution by a Markov
151   chain. Many studies have demonstrated the superiority of DDPM across various tasks (Dhariwal &
152   Nichol, 2021; Vahdat et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2022; Su et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2022;
153   Fei et al., 2023; Özdenizci & Legenstein, 2023; Peng et al., 2023). In image dehazing, a prevalent
154
      way to utilize DDPM is mapping the hazy image to the clear one in a conditional manner (Özdenizci
      & Legenstein, 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Different from previous works, in this paper,
155
      we employ DDPM to project the hazy image from the target to the source domain, aiming to preserve
156
      well-learned dehazing priors of the source domain.

      4.1.2      C USTOM LOSS FUNCTION
271
272   The loss function L(x̃0 , y) works in test time by guiding the projection from HT to HT →S in terms
273   of fidelity and quality. Accordingly, the total loss can be divided into two items: fidelity loss F(x̃0 , y)
274   and quality loss Q(x̃0 , y). The former contains a spatial consistency loss Lsc and a color consistency
275   loss Lcc . The latter contains a white balance loss Lwb and a region-aware DCP loss Lrdcp .
276   Fidelity Loss. We design the fidelity loss F(x̃0 , y) from the perspective of preventing both structure
277   deformation and color distortion to ensure the fidelity of the projected image HT →S . In general
278   circumstances, we don’t need to consider the issue of fidelity, since constraints have been imposed
279   by image distance losses (e.g., MSE). However, in our DiffAD pipeline, MSE may fail the image
280   adaptation (x̃0 and y should exhibit distinct distributions). Thus, we adopt the spatial consistency
281   loss Lsc from Guo et al. (2020), which encourages spatial coherence of HT →S through preserving
282
      the structural gradient (rather than intensity) between x̃0 and y:

336   4.2      D IFF AD FOR REAL IMAGE DEHAZING
337
338   Due to the difficulty in obtaining a large-scale hazy-clean image pairs under real-world scenarios,
339   improving the model’s (pre-trained on synthetic hazy-clean pairs) generalization ability is a promising
340   research direction. A typical application of our DiffAD is to remove the haze of real-captured images,
341   which are unlabeled. Although DiffAD provides an effective solution for domain shift problem in
342   real image dehazing, it is highly time-consuming due to the iterative reverse process. To enhance the
343
      efficiency, we collect some real hazy images and generate corresponding high-quality pseudo labels
      with a pre-trained SOTA dehazing model Φ and our DiffAD.
344
345   As illustrated in Fig. 4, we design a fully automatic pipeline to generate the pseudo label J. The
346   output J ∗ of the pre-trained dehazing model Φ is also embedded to avoid catastrophic forgetting.
347   Specifically, a high-quality pseudo label must satisfy simultaneously with (a) visibility within dense
348   haze regions, and (b) artifact-free. Benefiting from the controllable nature of our DiffAD, we can
349   easily obtain pseudo label J˜ that satisfy property (a) by adopting relatively larger λdcp . To further
350
      fulfill the property (b), we find the the output J ∗ of the pre-trained dehazing model Φ quite fits. As
      illustrated in Fig. 5, we first compute the weight map W by adding the sky mask WS and depth map
351
352
      WD estimated from J ∗ . Then, the W is utilized to fuse J˜ and J ∗ in a weighted addition manner to
      generate the refined pseudo label J. In our implementation, the methods described in (Zou et al.,
353
      2022) and (Yang et al., 2024) are adopted to estimate WS and WD , respectively.

      Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. We employ three widely-used synthetic datasets as the source
402   domains, including ITS dataset (Li et al., 2018), OTS dataset (Li et al., 2018) and Wu’s dataset (Wu
403   et al., 2023). For domain shift (1), two synthetic datasets are selected for quantitative assessment:
404   SOTS-indoor dataset (Li et al., 2018), SOTS-outdoor dataset (Li et al., 2018). For domain shift (2),
405   we adopt O-HAZE (Ancuti et al., 2018a; Kar et al., 2021) and I-HAZE (Ancuti et al., 2018b; Kar
406   et al., 2021) datasets as target real domains. In addition, we adopt PSNR and SSIM as evaluation
407   metrics.
408   Implementation Details. As illustrated in Fig. 2, DiffAD contains three main steps. For step one, We
409   select AECRNet (Wu et al., 2021), Dehazeformer (Song et al., 2023), and FocalNet (Cui et al., 2023)
410   as our base networks. We re-train their models on source domains with public codes and default
411   settings if their pre-trained models are not available. In step two, we train three denoising diffusion
412   probabilistic models (DDPMs) from scratch on ITS (Li et al., 2018), OTS (Li et al., 2018) and Wu’s
413   dataset (Wu et al., 2023) (only the hazy images are employed for training). Each diffusion model
414   is trained for 50k iterations using the Adam optimizer with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999 and learning rate
415   is set to 2e−5 . We randomly crop images into 256 × 256 patches in the training phase. Following
416
      DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), we adopt linear noise schedule and set the number of diffusion steps as
      T = 1000. In step three, we empirically set the guidance scale g to 0.8 × HW for stable guidance,
417
      where H and W denote height and width of the input image, respectively. In the reverse process, we
418
      set k = 10, λsc = 1, λcc = 0.1, and λwb = 1 for all three DDPMs. λdcp is used to control the extent
419
      of dehazing, and in our implementation, we set it to a fixed value (i.e., 5e−5 ).
420
421   Scene Type Adaptation (between indoor and outdoor). We evaluate the performance of scene type
422   adaptation of our DiffAD between indoor and outdoor domains. Specifically, we choose the model
423
      pre-trained on OTS (source domain) to test the performance on SOTS-indoor (target domain). We
      denote this setting as “OTS / SOTS-indoor”. “ITS / SOTS-outdoor” indicates the opposite setting. We
424
      equip our DiffAD with three selected state-of-the-art dehazing methods (i.e., AECRNet (Wu et al.,
425
      2021), Dehazeformer (Song et al., 2023), FocalNet (Cui et al., 2023)) to explore the domain shift

430   DiffAD significantly enhance FocalNet’s scene type adaptation performance on “OTS / SOTS-indoor”

      6    L IMITATION AND CONCLUSION
528
529   Limitation. By studying our DiffAD, we observe some difficulties that are urgent to be addressed. (1)
530   We find it’s hard to properly evaluate the dehazing performance by current metrics, especially in real
531   image dehazing where the ground-truth is not available. (2) It is sub-optimal to fix hyper-parameters
532   when generating pseudo labels. We plan to make them input-adaptive in future.
533
      Conclusion. In this paper, we propose a novel Diffusion-based ADaptation paradigm (i.e., DiffAD)
534   to explore the domain shift problem in image dehazing. We train a denoising diffusion probabilistic
535   model (DDPM) with source hazy images to capture the prior probability distribution of the source
536   domain. A source-Gaussian-source loop is built and given a hazy image from the target domain (e.g.,
537   real-captured hazy image), we can adjust the distribution to make it align with the source domain.
538   Then, the adapted hazy image can be directly fed into a certain SOTA dehazing model pre-trained on
539   source domain to predict the haze-free output. The proposed DiffAD can be successfully applied to
      real image dehazing by employing the predicted haze-free outputs as the pseudo labels.

902   A.6      D ETAILED A RCHITECTURE OF SFT L AYER
903
904   Considering haze is highly related to the scene depth, we embed the depth imformation into the
905   encoder of the dehazing network to guide the dehazing process. Specifically, for hazy features Fhazy
906   extracted in each level of the dehazing encoder, we first utilize convolution layer to extract the depth
907   features Fdepth with the same dimensions from the estimated depth map. Then, we utilize SFT
908   layer (Wang et al., 2018) to achieve effective modulation of Fhazy and Fdepth . The structure of SFT
909
      layer is illustrated in Fig. 16. Two groups of different convolution layers are adopted to predict scale
      parameter γ and shift parameter β. Transforming the hazy features Fhazy with predicted parameters,
910
      we can obtain the modulated features Fout :
911
912
