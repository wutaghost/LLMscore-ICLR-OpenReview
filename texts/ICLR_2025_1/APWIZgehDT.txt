011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Human decision-making in cognitive tasks and daily life exhibits considerable
014            variability, shaped by factors such as task difficulty, individual preferences, and
015
               personal experiences. Understanding this variability across individuals is essen-
               tial for uncovering the perceptual and decision-making mechanisms that humans
016
               rely on when faced with uncertainty and ambiguity. In this study, we present a
017
               counterfactual-based approach to investigate the subject-level decision-making
018            behaviors and reveal the underlying perceptual mechanisms by synthesizing vi-
019            sual stimuli. First, we developed an efficient generative model that samples
020            along an artificial neural network (ANN)’s perceptual boundary, generating im-
021            age samples designed to induce high variability in human perception. Using these
022            generated samples, combined with behavioral data from 246 human participants
023            across 116,715 trials, we constructed the varMNIST dataset. Then, we presented
024            a subject-specific fine-tuning approach to align the perceptual variability of ANNs
025            with that of humans. It allows us to successfully predict human decision-making
026
               behaviors on varMNIST. Finally, we verified the ability to selectively manipu-
               late individual behaviors by generating tailored controversial stimuli, which high-
027
               lighted significant inter-subject perceptual variability. Together, our work illu-
028
               minated key distinctions between human and machine perceptual variability and
029            established an effective strategy for manipulating individual decision-making be-
030            haviors. This study paves the way for artificial intelligence models with personal-
031            ized perceptual capabilities.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   A core goal of cognitive science is to establish models that reflect the relationship between external
037   stimuli and human internal experiences. The development of ANNs has significantly contributed to
038   this goal, particularly through the latent representations of ANNs that have shown a strong corre-
039
      lation with human psychological representations (Wei et al. (2024a;b); Muttenthaler et al. (2022a);
      Mahner et al. (2024); Zheng et al. (2019); Hebart et al. (2020); Muttenthaler et al. (2022b)). How-
040
      ever, individuals may have markedly different internal experiences when presented with the same
041
      external stimulus (Snyder et al. (2015); Partos et al. (2016); Floridou et al. (2022). For instance,
042   when different people observe the same handwritten digit, they may recognize it as different num-
043   bers (see Figure 1 (left)). This variability in human percepts has been inadequately explored within
044   both cognitive science and computer science. We hypothesize that there is a correspondence be-
045   tween the perceptual boundaries of ANNs and human perceptual classification boundaries and im-
046   ages generated along these boundaries would evoke different internal experiences in humans. This
047   hypothesis can be tested by human experiments with visual stimuli sampled along the perceptual
048   boundaries of ANNs.
049   In recent years, counterfactual generation methods have made significant progress in revealing the
050   differences between human and machine perception. Veerabadran et al. (2023); Zhou & Firestone
051   (2019); Elsayed et al. (2018) highlighted that while ANNs exhibit brittleness in response to small
052   perturbations, these perturbations can also bias human perceptual choices under specific conditions.
053   Similarly, Gaziv et al. (2024) found that by enhancing ANN models, low-norm image perturbations
      could be generated that significantly disrupt human percepts, demonstrating their potential as pre-

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110
      Researchers have extensively used synthetic images generated by ANNs to study human percep-
111   tual space, uncovering differences between model and human perception while refining generation
112   techniques to enhance their influence on human cognition. For instance, Golan et al. (2020; 2023)
113   utilized controversial stimuli to highlight classification discrepancies between neural networks. Sim-
114   ilarly, Veerabadran et al. (2023) demonstrated that adversarial perturbations could simultaneously
115   influence ANN classifications and human perceptual choices, revealing shared sensitivities. How-
116   ever, Gaziv et al. (2024) found that while standard ANN perturbations fail to impact human percep-
117   tion, robustified ANN models can generate low-norm perturbations that significantly disrupt human
118   percepts.
119   Other studies have approached this problem from different angles. For example, works like Feather
120   et al. (2023; 2019); Nanda et al. (2022; 2023) investigated model metamers, revealing fundamental
121   mismatches between model activations and human recognition. Extending beyond perceptual dis-
122   crepancies, Fu et al. (2023) introduced DreamSim, a perceptual metric leveraging synthetic data and
123   human experimental data to better reflect human similarity judgments and address shortcomings in
124   conventional perceptual metrics. Building on such synthetic data and behavioral insights, recent
125
      efforts have sought to align vision models with human perceptual representations by incorporat-
      ing human-like conceptual structures, resulting in improved alignment and enhanced performance
126
      across diverse tasks Muttenthaler et al. (2024); Sundaram et al. (2024).
127
128   To study the variability of human perception, it is essential that generated images significantly influ-
129   ence human cognition. Given that we sample from the perceptual boundaries of ANNs, which often
130   contain high noise levels, better methods are needed to ensure that the generated images appear
131
      natural. Recently, the fields of adversarial examples and counterfactual explanations in machine
      learning have adopted effective techniques to help deal with this problem, such as Jeanneret et al.
132
      (2023), Wei et al. (2024b), Chen et al. (2023), Jeanneret et al. (2022), Vaeth et al. (2023), and
133
      Atakan Bedel & Çukur (2023). These studies use diffusion models with training-free guidanceYu
134   et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024) as regularizers to introduce prior distributions,
135   thereby enhancing the naturalness of generated images and their impact on human perception.

145   The image perturbations that significantly affect ANN perception also influence human perception
146   ( Gaziv et al. (2024); Veerabadran et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2024a); Muttenthaler et al. (2022a)),
147   suggesting that ANNs and humans may share similar perceptual boundaries. Based on this, we
148   hypothesize that samples on these boundaries (which exhibit high perceptual variability for ANNs)
149   may also lead to ambiguous perception in humans, resulting in different internal experiences for the
150   same stimuli.
151   We adopted two guidance strategies: uncertainty guidance and controversial guidance. Uncertainty
152   guidance aims to generate images that lie near the decision boundaries of classifiers. Its loss function

157   for two categories (e.g., “3” and “5”), resulting in high-uncertainty images. Controversial guidance
158   generates images that cause conflicting predictions between two classifiers. Its loss function is
159   defined as:
160
                                  L = H(p1 (y|x), q1 (y)) + H(p2 (y|x), q2 (y)),
161   where p1 (y|x) and p2 (y|x) are the predicted probability distributions of classifiers 1 and 2, and
      q1 (y) and q2 (y) are their corresponding target distributions. The target distributions ensure that

274   3.3.2    Q UANTITATIVE ANALYSIS OF VAR MNIST
275
276   Evaluation metrics. To comprehensively evaluate the guiding effectiveness of the generation
277   method, we define three types of guidance outcome, as illustrated in Figure 3a: success, bias,
278   and failure. For the guidance targets o1 and o2 , let p1 and p2 represent the probabilities of par-
279
      ticipants choosing o1 and o2 , respectively.A result is considered success if p1 + p2 ≥ 80% and
      min(p1 , p2 ) ≥ 10%, indicating the generated stimuli guide participants to make a balanced choice
280
      between the two targets. A result is labeled as bias if p1 + p2 ≥ 80% but min(p1 , p2 ) < 10%, in-
281
      dicating a strong bias toward one target. A result is classified as failure if p1 + p2 < 80%, meaning
282   the stimuli fail to guide participants effectively. These definitions allow us to evaluate and compare
283   the performance of different guidance strategies and classifiers.
284
285   ANN variability can arouse human variability. To evaluate whether the images generated by
286   sampling on the perceptual boundaries of ANNs can arouse human perceptual variability, we first
287   calculated the entropy of participants’ choice probabilities in the digit recognition experiment. As
288   shown in Figure. A.10 (bottom left), the entropy values for more than half of the generated images
289   were significantly greater than zero, indicating substantial variability in human choices. This sug-
290   gests that the generated images successfully elicited human perceptual variability. Furthermore, as
291   illustrated in Figure. 3b, the average sum of success rate and bias rate across all generated images
292
      was close to 80%. This indicates that, in the majority of cases, human choices aligned with either
      both or one of the guidance targets. This demonstrates that the generation method effectively guided
293
      human digit recognition behavior.
294
295
      Guidance strategy, classifier and target influence the guidance outcome. We further analyzed
296
      the influence of different guidance strategies, classifiers used for guidance, and guidance targets
297
      on the guidance outcome. First, for the two guidance strategies, uncertainty guidance and contro-
298   versial guidance, the success rate of controversial guidance is higher, while the bias rates of both
299   methods are similar (Figure. 3b). This indicates that controversial guidance is more effective in
300   guiding participants. Second, for the five classifiers used in uncertainty guidance, CORNet achieved
301   the highest success rate and the lowest failure rate, indicating that CORNet is the most effective
302   guidance classifier (Figure. 3b). This result aligns with the fact that CORNet incorporates more
303   human visual priors. For the ten adversarial classifier combinations used in controversial guidance,
304   the combination of VGG and CORNet achieved the highest success rate and the lowest bias rate,
305   demonstrating that VGG and CORNet have strong guiding capabilities, with comparable guidance
306
      strength. Additionally, when VGG and CORNet were combined with other classifiers, their bias
      rates were relatively high, with the combination with LRM exhibiting the highest bias rate. This
307
      suggests that other classifiers are less effective compared to VGG and CORNet, with LRM being
308
      the least effective in guidance strength. Finally, for the ten guidance targets (digits 0-9), the results
309   showed significant variability in guidance outcomes (Figure. A.12). For example, digit pairs (1, 7),
310   (1, 2), and (4, 9) achieved the highest success rates, exceeding 0.35. In contrast, digit pairs (1, 8),
311   (2, 9), and (7, 8) exhibited the lowest success rates, below 0.03. These findings highlight that the
312   guidance outcome is strongly influenced by the specific digit pairs being guided.

393   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL PARADIGM
394
395   Building on varMNIST and alignment experiments, we designed a paradigm to test whether individ-
396   ually fine-tuned models can amplify perceptual differences and guide decision-making (Figure 5a).
397   This experiment evaluates the ability of targeted stimuli to reveal individual variability and achieve
398   precise manipulation of perceptual outcomes, highlighting the potential of personalized modeling in
399   understanding human perception. For the first round of experiments, we initially selected around 500

479   6    D ISCUSSION
480
481   Using recently developed counterfactual-based approach that generates synthetic visual stimuli
482   along the perceptual boundaries of neural networks, our work provides new insight into variabil-
483   ity of human percepts. First, we demonstrated that sampling along the perceptual boundaries of
484   ANNs allows for the generation of stimuli that evoke diverse internal experiences among human
485   observers. Second, through human experiments, we validated the effectiveness of our model in
      capturing the nuances of human perception. Third, by utilizing carefully designed controversial


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      stimuli, we selectively manipulated individual behavior, unveiling significant inter-individual differ-
487   ences in perceptual variability. Our study not only uncovers specific differences between humans
488   and machines in the variability of their perceptual experiences but also offers effective tools for
489   manipulating and predicting individual category judgments.
490
      From the perspectives of cognitive science and neuroscience, our method significantly enhances the
491
      utility and flexibility of generated images in the study of human perception. Unlike the methods em-
492
      ployed by Golan et al. (2020) and Feather et al. (2023), which reveal the disparities between model
493   and human perception by generating images that strongly affect ANNs while having minimal impact
494   on human cognition, our method is capable of influencing both models and human perception simul-
495   taneously. This dual impact allows for a nuanced counterfactual examination of the subtle differ-
496   ences in perceptual variability between the two. In contrast to the approaches taken by Veerabadran
497   et al. (2023) and Gaziv et al. (2024), which focus on improving ANNs to produce images that can
498   influence human perception, our use of diffusion models with prior distributions allows for broader
499   applicability across various ANN models and perturbation methods. This also expands the range of
500   image sampling, enabling sampling from high-noise areas like perceptual boundaries. Moreover, the
501
      incorporation of prior distributions ensures that our generated images more closely resemble natural
      images, enhancing their effectiveness in influencing human perception. With this significant im-
502
      provement in the usability and flexibility of generated images, we successfully explored individual
503
      differences in human perception and opened the door for personalized manipulation, increasing the
504   efficiency and scope of human perception studies.
505
506   From the perspective of computer science methodology, we have made significant improvements
507
      upon existing methods, opening new avenues for fields of AI for science and AI-human alignment.
      Drawing on the controversial stimuli from Golan et al. (2020) and adversarial perturbations from
508
      Veerabadran et al. (2023), we integrated these concepts with diffusion model priors to create two
509
      new loss-guiding methods: controversial guidance and uncertainty guidance. This enhancement
510   increases the naturalness of the generated images and their influence on human perception. Addi-
511   tionally, inspired by works such as Jeanneret et al. (2023), Wei et al. (2024a), Wei et al. (2024b),
512   Chen et al. (2023), Jeanneret et al. (2022), Vaeth et al. (2023), Atakan Bedel & Çukur (2023), we
513   introduced counterfactual methodologies into the study of human perceptual variability, allowing us
514   to explore this relatively under-researched area in greater depth. Our experiments demonstrate that
515   the varMNIST dataset we generated significantly evokes human perceptual variability, providing
516   a novel approach for aligning AI and human by harmonizing their perceptual variabilities. Fur-
517   thermore, varMNIST can reveal individual differences among humans, enabling the generation of
518
      customized images that reflect these differences through ANNs aligned with individual participants.
519   Despite our progress in exploring human perceptual variability, several limitations remain. Our
520   varMNIST dataset, generated by sampling along ANN perceptual boundaries, cannot fully capture
521   human variability, especially influences like culture, as some ANNs are trained on data from spe-
522   cific groups. To address this, we plan to include participants from diverse cultural backgrounds for
523   a more comprehensive understanding. Furthermore, the dataset’s focus on handwritten digits, while
524
      effective for evoking perceptual variability, limits the exploration of broader visual phenomena. Ex-
      panding beyond object recognition to tasks like similarity judgments, emotion recognition, visual
525
      attention, and scene memory could offer deeper insights. However, exploring such complex tasks re-
526
      mains challenging given the limited number of trials available in individual behavioral experiments.
527   Future work will incorporate natural images to better capture the complexity of human perceptual
528   variability in more diverse and ecologically valid contexts.
529
530
      In terms of aligning AI with humans, although ANNs finetuned with individual behavioral data
      showed a notable improvement in predicting perceptual variability, there remains a significant gap
531
      when compared to their performance in standard classification tasks. This indicates that percep-
532
      tual variability is a promising but underexplored method for AI-human alignment, with ample room
533   for improvement. To address this, we propose incorporating optimal experimental design Rainforth
534   et al. (2024); Foster et al. (2019; 2021) into human experiments, using ANNs finetuned with individ-
535   ual behavioral data to generate customized images that maximize individual variability. These new
536   behavioral data could then be fed back into the training of ANNs, dramatically improving AI-human
537   alignment with fewer experimental trials. This approach would significantly increase the efficiency
538   of human behavior data collection, reduce the cost of AI-human alignment, and accelerate the ad-
539   vancement of both cognitive science and artificial intelligence.

752   In previous studies that employed generated images to investigate model and human perception
753   ( Golan et al. (2020); Gaziv et al. (2024); Veerabadran et al. (2023); Feather et al. (2023)), a common
754   issue was that the generated images lacked sufficient naturalness and failed to significantly influence
755   human perception. This issue is particularly crucial within the context of our research objectives.
      Using previous methods often resulted in images that were unrecognizable to human participants,

756
      leading to nearly random classification results (see Figure. A.2). Recent advances in adversarial
757   examples and counterfactual explanations in machine learning (Jeanneret et al. (2023); Wei et al.
758   (2024b); Chen et al. (2023); Jeanneret et al. (2022); Vaeth et al. (2023); Atakan Bedel & Çukur
759   (2023)) have addressed this issue by employing diffusion models as regularizers to introduce prior
760   information. This technique allows for the generation of natural images capable of influencing
761   human perception.
762
      Inspired by these advances, we utilize a classifier-free diffusion model as the core of image gen-
763   eration process. By sampling noise from the target dataset (MNIST) distribution and feeding it
764   into the diffusion model for denoising, we effectively incorporate prior information. This approach
765   enhances the naturalness of the generated images, making them more reflective of the real distri-
766   bution of handwritten digits and thereby increasing their impact on human perception, as shown in

769   A.2.3    E DITING EXISTING DATASETS VS . GENERATING DATA FROM SCRATCH
770
      The process of adding MSE loss to the loss function can be seen as editing existing datasets. The
771
      MSE loss is used to constrain the pixel space of the stimuli. Without the MSE loss, the model is
772   more likely to sample from distributions of stimuli that are very similar in pixel space within a certain
773   class. We aim to enforce a constraint in the pixel space that encourages the stimuli to be closer to
774   the original distribution of randomly sampled samples from the MNIST dataset. This approach is
775   intended to enhance the diversity of the generated stimuli. We define α as the pixel-level restraint
776   scale.
777
      For uncertainty guidance with MSE constraint, we have:
778
                                     ℓ(f (xt ), y) = H(y|xt ) + α||xt − xref ||2 ,
779
      where H represents the entropy, α represents the strength of the MSE loss. For controversial guid-
780
      ance with MSE constraint, we have:
781
                           ℓ(f (xt ), y) = DKL (p(y1 |xt ), p(y2 |xt )) + α||xt − xref ||2 ,
782
783
      where DKL represents the KL divergence between two distributions. We conducted experiments on
      five models. For uncertainty sampling, we generated stimuli for each model, resulting in five groups
784
      of stimuli. In the controversial sampling experiments, we pitted the models against each other in
785
      pairs, creating ten groups of stimuli. However, this approach can be perceived as manipulating one
786   class into another, which is similar to our goal of sampling along the decision boundaries of ANNs,
787   but not exactly the same. In generation, the guidance scale is set to 0.1, resampling steps is set to 5,
788   the inference steps is set to 50, and α is set to 50.
789
790   A.2.4    TARGETED GUIDANCE VS . UNTARGETED GUIDANCE
791
792
      Untargeted guidance focuses solely on increasing the variability of the generated images, without
      considering the overall distribution of the images. We adopted an untargeted guidance method to
793
      generate stimuli for the digit recognition experiment. To sample at the decision boundary of the
794
      model, we drew on previous research and proposed two guidance methods: uncertainty guidance
795   and controversial guidance. Uncertainty guidance ensures that the generated images are as close as
796   possible to the model’s perceptual boundary by maximizing the entropy of the classification proba-
797   bility distribution of a single ANN model for the generated images, thereby obtaining images with
798   high perceptual variability for the model. For uncertainty guidance, this can be represented as:
799                                             ℓ(f (xt ), y) = H(y|xt ),
800   Where H is the entropy, y is the output probability of the neural network. Controversial guidance,
801   on the other hand, utilizes two different ANN models and generates images that maximize the KL
802   divergence between their classification probability distributions, thereby maximizing perceptual dif-
803   ferences between the models. For controversial guidance, this can be represented as:
804                                   ℓ(f (xt ), y) = DKL (p(y1 |xt ), p(y2 |xt )),
805   Where DKL is the KL divergence, p(y1 |xt ) is the output probability of the first neural network,
806   p(y2 |xt ) is the output probability of the second neural network. In generation the guidance scale is
807   set to 0.1, resampling steps is set to 5, and the inference steps is set to 50. Targeted and untargeted
808   guidances are compared in Figure.A.4. Losses with and without MSE are also compared in Fig-
809   ure. A.3. The formula for the targeted guidance can be found at section 3.1 . The losses compared
      in this figure are untargeted losses.

813
814   Configuration of DiT. The Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie (2023)) is a Transformer-
815   based diffusion model tailored for generative tasks. In our configuration, the model processes 28 ×
816
      28 grayscale images using a patch size of 2 × 2, resulting in patch embeddings transformed into
      sequences of hidden size 128, with 1 input channel and 10 output classes. The architecture includes
817
      4 Transformer layers with 8 attention heads per layer and an MLP ratio of 4.0.
818
819   DiT incorporates Patch Embedding, Timestep Embedding, and Label Embedding modules. These
820   embeddings are combined with fixed sinusoidal positional encodings to provide spatial and temporal
821   context. AdaLN (Adaptive Layer Normalization) layers condition the model on timestep and label
822
      embeddings, with zero-initialized manipulation for training stability.
823   The model outputs spatial predictions through a final linear layer followed by an unpatching op-
824   eration, restoring the input image dimensions. Classifier-free guidance is supported by computing
825   conditional and unconditional outputs, enabling control over generated samples.
826
827   Training of Diffusion Model. For prior diffusion model, we use the MNIST dataset as the training
828   dataset. The dataset consists of grayscale images of size 28 × 28, which are directly used without
829   further resizing. The training process is conducted using a single GPU (NVIDIA GeForce RTX
830   4090) with the Adam optimizer.
831   The data is loaded into the training pipeline using a PyTorch DataLoader with a batch size of 128,
832   andthe number of worker threads for data loading is set to 128. The model is trained for 150 epochs,
833   with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and an unconditional training rate of 0.1, and the weight decay is not
834   applied. Dropout is applied to the class embedding with a probability of 0.1, while the model does
835   not learn the variance (sigma).

888   handwritten digits.
889
890
891   Task paradigm Before the formal experiment, participants will first complete a pre-experiment.
892   Each round of the pre-experiment consists of two stages. (1) Selection Stage: A test image appears at
893   the center of the screen, with two buttons labeled "True" and "False" displayed below it. Participants
894   are required to judge whether the image represents a number. (2) Feedback Stage: After making their
895   choice, participants will receive feedback below the image indicating whether it is a number. The
896   pre-experiment includes a total of 10 rounds, after which participants will proceed to the formal
897
      experiment. In formal experiment, participants performed multiple rounds of a choice task (see
      Figure. A.7). Each trial consisted of two phases: (1) Fixation Phase: A black cross was displayed
898
      at the center of the screen for 300 ms to direct participants’ attention to the center. (2) Selection
899
      Phase: A test image appears at the center of the screen, with two buttons labeled "True" and "False"
900   displayed below it. The positions of the buttons were fixed and remained unchanged throughout
901   the trials. Participants were asked to judge whether the image represents a figure by selecting the
902   corresponding button with the mouse or pressing the key on the keyboard (A represents True and
903   D represents False). There was no time limit for responding. Each session of formal experiment
904   comprised 500 trials, divided into two types: (1) Sentinel trials (n = 10), in which participants
905   are shown a set of 10 pre-selected MNIST images, i.e., the correct response should be True. We
906   screened participants based on their accuracy in the sentinel trials to ensure high-quality responses.
907   (2) Random Trials (n=490), where images were randomly selected from the dataset, excluding the
908
      fixed images. The two trial types were presented in a random alternating order. No feedback was
      provided after participants made their selection. The experiment was programmed using JSPsych,
909
      with stimuli presented via the JSPsych-Psychophysics component.
910
911
912   Human data collection The experiment got ethics approval from the local University. We re-
913   cruited participants (N=400) and collected data through the NAODAO platform. Prior to the ex-
914   periment, participants read an informed consent form detailing any potential risks associated with
915   participation. Participants were allowed to withdraw from the experiment at any time. No personal
916   identification information was collected. We only included data from participants with sentinel trial
917   accuracy greater than 70%, resulting in data from 276 participants and 135240 trials involved in the
      following analyses.
