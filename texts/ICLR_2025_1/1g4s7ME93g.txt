                                                      A BSTRACT
008
009
               Learning a single model for multiple robotic manipulation tasks, particularly high-
010
               precision tasks, has been a long-standing challenge in robotics research due to un-
011
               certainties inherent in both the model and the data. These uncertainties, namely
012            epistemic uncertainty arising from model limitations and aleatoric uncertainty
013            stemming from data variability, hinder precise control. While the Robot View
014            Transformer (RVT) improves performance by re-rendering point clouds from fixed
015            viewpoints and processing structured 2D virtual images, it still suffers from oc-
016            clusion artifacts in rendering and limited action precision due to resolution con-
017            straints. To address these limitations, we propose the Super Robot View Trans-
018            former (S-RVT) framework, which integrates three novel components: the Super
019            Point Renderer (S-PR), the Super-resolution Multi-View Transformer (S-MVT),
020
               and the Hierarchical Sampling Policy (HSP). The S-PR enhances the render-
               ing process to mitigate occlusion artifacts, while the S-MVT integrates super-
021
               resolution to the output heatmaps, enabling finer-grained manipulation. The HSP
022
               efficiently samples multi-view heatmaps in 3D space to obtain accurate 3D poses.
023            These innovations collaboratively mitigate the challenges of occlusion and pre-
024            cision in manipulation tasks. Our experimental results demonstrate that S-RVT
025            achieves a success rate of 87.8 % across 18 manipulation tasks, surpassing the
026            state-of-the-art of 81.4 %. Notably, for high-precision manipulation tasks, S-RVT
027            exhibits nearly a two-fold improvement over existing methods, underscoring its
028            effectiveness in precise control scenarios. Our code and trained models will be
029            released to support further research.

051   1   I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Learning diverse manipulation tasks is fundamental and necessary for building intelligent embodied
      agents. Multi-task learning in robotic manipulation has drawn significant interest in the community


                                                               1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and made considerable progress, thanks to large-scale Imitation Learning (IL) demonstrations and
055   Reinforcement Learning (RL) simulators James et al. (2020); Mees et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2020);
056   Gu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Gong et al. (2023); Padalkar et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024).
057   These simulators and datasets fuel the development of Embodied Artificial Intelligence (EAI) by
058   enabling researchers to build and test algorithms in controlled environments that closely mimic real-
059   world conditions. In this work, we investigate the problem of multi-task imitation learning for robot
060   manipulation.
061   Multi-task Imitation Learning challenges robots to acquire diverse skills described by natural lan-
062   guage through human demonstrations. This approach encompasses tasks requiring semantic scene
063   understanding (e.g., take the steak off the grill), high-precision (e.g., put the ring on the azure spoke)
064   or long-horizon planning (e.g., put the item in the top drawer). Different from previous methods
065   like Diffusion Policy (DP) Chi et al. (2023), Action Chunking with Transformers (ACT) Zhao et al.
066   (2023) and their variations Fu et al. (2024); Ze et al. (2024); Ke et al. (2024); Ha et al. (2023);
067   Chen et al. (2023a); Xian et al. (2023), which learn task-specific policies, multi-task learning aims
068   to develop a single policy for various tasks. Multi-task Imitation Learning generally falls into two
069
      categories: key-state based methods Shi et al. (2023); Xian et al. (2023); Gervet et al. (2023); Goyal
      et al. (2023; 2024); Shridhar et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023b) and key-trajectory based methods Ke
070
      et al. (2024); Brohan et al. (2022; 2023); Huang et al. (2023). Key-state based methods decompose
071
      demonstrations into critical discrete key poses, teaching the robot to predict the subsequent key pose
072   based on current observations. Key pose typically represents the gripper’s translation, rotation, and
073   state. Key-trajectory methods directly imitate the demonstrated trajectories. This study focuses on
074   key-state based methods for robotic manipulation.
075
      Previous methods learn the key pose distribution from colored point cloud observation. Per-
076
      Act Shridhar et al. (2023) employs a voxel-based representation to predict the next key pose, while
077
      Act3D Gervet et al. (2023) introduces multi-resolution 3D feature field. Other methods, such as
078   Robot View Transformer (RVT) Goyal et al. (2023), tackle this problem by predicting multi-view
079   heatmaps from rendered RGB-D observations. These approaches have demonstrated impressive re-
080   sults on training and inference speed, semantic understanding and long-horizon planning. However,
081   they fall short in high-precision tasks like peg insert or shape place. RVT-2 Goyal et al. (2024)
082   addresses challenging precise manipulation tasks by a zoom-in process, and demonstrates promising
083   results in extensive experiments.
084   We advance beyond previous methods by addressing uncertainties inherent in both the model and
085   the data Kendall & Gal (2017); Kendall et al. (2018). These uncertainties are broadly categorized
086   into epistemic uncertainty, arising from limitations in the model, and aleatoric uncertainty, stem-
087   ming from inherent variability in the data. For instance, in the task of put the ring on the azure
088   spoke, the former uncertainty refers to the fact that the robot may misjudge the exact position of
089   the spoke due to insufficient or biased training data. The latter uncertainty indicates the robot fails
090   to predict the next key pose due to occlusions. The original RVT framework discretizes the action
091   space; however, such a coarse-grained action space is insufficient for accomplishing high-precision
092
      manipulation tasks, which contributes to epistemic uncertainty. Additionally, the points of interest
      on the manipulated objects are often occluded by the robot arm, making it difficult for the model to
093
      infer the next key pose based on the current observation, thus increasing aleatoric uncertainty. To
094
      address these uncertainties, we introduce the Super Robot View Transformer (S-RVT), a multi-task
095   framework designed for high-precision manipulation tasks.
096
097   Our S-RVT framework comprises three key modules: the Super Point Renderer (S-PR), the Super-
098
      resolution Multi-View Transformer (S-MVT), and the Hierarchical Sampling Policy (HSP). The
      S-PR mitigates aleatoric uncertainty by addressing observational uncertainties, particularly catas-
099
      trophic occlusion, where critical visual obstructions impede task completion. The S-MVT and HSP
100
      work together to reduce epistemic uncertainty: S-MVT enhances model expressivity by generat-
101   ing super-resolution heatmaps with strong supervision, while HSP samples multi-view heatmaps in
102   3D space to obtain accurate 3D poses. By integrating these modules, S-RVT effectively reduces
103   both epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties, advancing the state-of-the-art in high-precision robotic
104   manipulation tasks. Notably, our method is a general boosting framework for virtual view-based
105   approaches. Thus, we integrate it with both the RVT Goyal et al. (2023) and RVT-2 Goyal et al.
106   (2024), yielding promising results across 18 challenging tasks in the RLBench benchmark James
107   et al. (2020). For RVT, our S-RVT improves the average success from 0.629 to 0.734. Furthermore,
      our S-RVT2 achieves a success rate of 0.878, surpassing the state-of-the-art 0.814. In tasks requiring

109   relative 115 % improvement over the state-of-the-art performance of 0.40. We also demonstrate our

118   2.1   T RANSFORMERS FOR M ANIPULATION
119
      In the domain of robotic manipulation, transformer architectures Vaswani et al. (2017) have gained
120
      significant traction, demonstrating their efficacy in enhancing control capabilities Johnson et al.
121
      (2021); Chaplot et al. (2021); Clever et al. (2021); Yang et al. (2021). These models excel in pro-
122   cessing diverse sensory inputs Dasari & Gupta (2021); Jangir et al. (2022); Kim et al. (2021); Liu
123   et al. (2022); Zhao et al. (2023); Goyal et al. (2023); Singh et al. (2023); Simeonov et al. (2023),
124   leveraging their inherent ability to handle heterogeneous data streams. This multi-modal adapt-
125   ability has been further expanded through the integration of transformer backbones with diffusion
126   models Team et al. (2024); Chi et al. (2023); Ze et al. (2024); Xian et al. (2023), facilitating com-
127   plex, long-horizon motion planning. Notably, transformer-based approaches have shown remarkable
128   prowess in feature extraction from various input modalities. However, a recurring theme in exist-
129   ing literature is the necessity for extensive training datasets, typically encompassing hundreds of
130
      task-specific demonstrations, to achieve robust performance. This data-intensive requirement un-
      derscores the challenges associated with transformer deployment in robotic systems.

133   2.2   M ULTI - TASK L EARNING IN ROBOTICS
134
      Recent years have witnessed growing interest in the robotics community towards developing mod-
135
      els capable of performing multiple tasks using a single model. Some researchers have focused on
136   achieving multi-task generalization by learning generalizable task or action representations Brohan
137   et al. (2022); Gubbi et al. (2020); Guhur et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2021); Lee et al. (2019). How-
138   ever, the limitations of these representations often restrict generalization to specific task categories.
139   More recent studies have expanded the scope by using language to specify a broader range of tasks,
140   while training policies on large-scale, pre-collected datasets Jang et al. (2022); Rohmer et al. (2013);
141   Schoettler et al. (2020); Shi et al. (2023); Simeonov et al. (2023); Radosavovic et al. (2023b); Nair
142   et al. (2022); Xiao et al. (2022); Radosavovic et al. (2023a); Wu et al. (2024); Black et al. (2024);
143   Gu et al. (2024). Concurrent research efforts have explored various approaches to enhance model
144
      capabilities, including learning more effective visual representations through masked image model-
      ing Xiao et al. (2022); Radosavovic et al. (2023b) or contrastive learning Nair et al. (2022); Sermanet
145
      et al. (2018), as well as developing world models Seo et al. (2023); Hafner et al. (2020).

      2.3   H IGH -P RECISION M ANIPULATION
149
      High-precision manipulation is crucial in tasks with stringent requirements for action accuracy. To
150   develop high-precision strategies, previous studies have employed various sensory modalities and
151   data-intensive learning algorithms. Initial investigations utilize proprioceptive data for learning peg-
152   in-hole task through imitation learning Gubbi et al. (2020). Subsequently, (Schoettler et al., 2020)
153   and (Tang et al., 2023) apply reinforcement learning algorithms to accomplish insertion tasks us-
154   ing visual sensory input or proprioceptive data. To enhance execution accuracy further, researchers
155   incorporate tactile feedback, including torque sensors Lee et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2020) and vision-
156   based tactile sensors Dong et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2023). However, these approaches rely on al-
157   gorithms requiring substantial training data (e.g., reinforcement learning or imitation learning from
158
      hundreds of demonstrations) and are limited to learning a single model per task.
159   In contrast to prior methods, we adopt the multi-task learning paradigm of Robot View Transformer
160   (RVT) Goyal et al. (2023), which employs structured representations such as point cloud renderings
161   of virtual views. While RVT enables robots to acquire more robust skills with fewer demonstra-
      tions, it falls short in tasks requiring high precision due to uncertainties in both observation and

254   3.3   S UPER - RESOLUTION M ULTI V IEW T RANSFORMER
255
256   The task descriptions are processed through the CLIP (ResNet-50) text encoder to extract features,
257   which, together with the rendered multi-view images, are then fed into the Super-resolution Multi-
258   View Transformer (S-MVT). As shown in Figure 2, S-MVT generates super-resolution heatmaps;
259   we denote the super-resolution factor as sr. Additionally, S-MVT outputs the rotation and gripper
260   opening predictions for the next key pose. Specifically, the virtual images and language features
261
      are processed through an MVT structure similar to that in RVT, producing feature maps. These
      feature maps undergo upsampling to produce an sr-fold super-resolution heatmap, representing the
262
      probability distribution of possible 3D poses projected onto the 2D plane. Our upsampling employs
263
      an Efficient Up-convolution Block (EUCB), which uses Depthwise Separable Convolution (DWC)
264   to reduce computational cost and parameter count while improving output resolution and preserving
265   feature details. To predict the rotation and gripper state, we sample features from the image patch
266   corresponding to the projected 3D position of the predicted next key pose on the virtual view. These
267   sampled features are then processed through an MLP to estimate the rotation and gripper opening.
268   This conditional sampling approach is employed because the gripper’s rotation and opening are
269   intrinsically linked to its translation, thereby yielding more plausible predictions. The details of our
      model architecture are discussed in Appendix A.1.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this paper, we address the limitations of previous virtual view-based methods, focusing on occlu-
530   sion problems and resolution constraints in the action space. To overcome these challenges, we in-
531
      troduce the Super Robot View Transformer (S-RVT). Our approach incorporates a Super Point Ren-
      derer that re-renders 3D point cloud into virtual images from fixed viewpoints, reducing information
532
      loss due to occlusion. The Super-resolution Multi View Transformer (S-MVT) then processes these
533
      images to generate high-resolution heatmaps representing 3D key pose translation probabilities on
534   2D planes. Finally, we employ a Hierarchical Sampling Policy (HSP) to sample and determine the
535   3D key pose. Our method demonstrates substantial improvements across 18 tasks, increasing perfor-
536   mance from 81.4% to 87.8% compared to the previous state-of-the-art. Notably, for tasks requiring
537   high-precision manipulation, our approach achieves a two-fold improvement in effectiveness. While
538   current virtual view-based methods typically predict discrete key poses for the gripper, our future
539   work will explore the potential for continuous trajectory prediction, further advancing the field of
      robotic manipulation.

795   A.1     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE OF S-MVT
796
797
      As illustrated in Figure 6, our Super-resolution Multi-View Transformer maps the input multi-view
798   virtual images and task description to the translation, rotation, and gripper state of the next key pose.
799   Initially, we patchify the multi-view virtual images and process them through four attention layers
800   to obtain multi-view image features. We then concatenate these features with language features
801   extracted by CLIP text encoder. The resulting combined feature vector is processed through another
802   four attention layers to generate heatmap features. This heatmap features undergo super-resolution
803   upsampling to produce multi-view super-resolution heatmaps.
804   To determine the rotation and gripper state of the next key pose, we employ conditional sampling.
805   During the training phase, we project the ground-truth 3D translation onto the heatmap plane and
806   select the heatmap feature corresponding to the projected 2D coordinates. This feature is then pro-
807   cessed through two separate MLPs to output the rotation and gripper state, respectively. In the
808   inference phase, we utilize the Hierarchical Sampling Policy (HSP) to predict the 3D translation,
809   which serves as a condition for sampling the feature. This sampled feature is subsequently pro-
      cessed through MLPs to obtain the rotation and gripper state.

            1. Point Cloud Augmentation: We apply random transformations to the input RGB point
               cloud to enhance the model’s learning of interest point positions relative to language in-

               structions and the rotation of subsequent key poses. These transformations include:

                • Random translation with a magnitude of 0.125 relative to the point cloud dimensions.
886             • Random rotation around the z-axis up to 45 degrees.

888            tions to the pixel values. Specifically, we add a random noise term ϵ to each pixel value,
889            where:

907   A.6   DATA E FFICIENCY
908
909   Data scarcity and heterogeneity are major challenges in current manipulation tasks. To address
910   heterogeneity, researchers have experimented with uniquely designed model architectures that train
911   small-parameter networks for each specific embodiment while keeping the backbone network pa-
912   rameters fixed. This approach offers a pathway to mitigating data heterogeneity. Nevertheless, data
913   scarcity is a more fundamental problem. Unlike the era of internet AI where large datasets are read-
914
      ily available online, robotics researchers cannot easily obtain vast amounts of data from the internet.
      Consequently, there is a growing interest in how to train robust and highly generalizable robots using
915
      limited data.
916
917   Existing methods like Diffusion Policy (DP) generate robust actions through denoising processes
      but require hundreds of human demonstrations to achieve convergence. Similarly, Action Chunking


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      with Transformers (ACT) collects human demonstrations through specialized mechanical setups,
919   yet it still needs dozens of demonstrations to perform tasks robustly. In contrast, our proposed S-
920   RVT framework addresses data efficiency by transforming raw observations into point cloud space
921   and applying translation and rotation augmentations, significantly enhancing the diversity of virtual
922   images. This allows our model to achieve convergence with as few as 10 demonstrations per task in
923   real-world experiments. Moreover, the model learns correlations across multiple tasks, presenting
924   possibilities for scaling up.

928   In future work, we aim to address camera occlusion in real-world scenarios from both temporal
929
      and spatial perspectives. Temporally, since objects are not continuously occluded throughout the
      sequence, enabling S-RVT to retain memory of past observations is valuable. Spatially, deploying
930
      multiple cameras from different viewpoints help mitigate occlusion. Regarding the limited num-
931
      ber of demonstrations, generating additional demonstrations from synthetic data could enable the
