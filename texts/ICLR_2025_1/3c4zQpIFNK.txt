009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) are measured on numerous bench-
012            marks like image captioning, visual question answer, and reasoning. However,
013            these benchmarks often include overly simple or uninformative samples, making it
014            difficult to effectively distinguish the performance of different MLLMs. Addition-
015
               ally, evaluating models across many benchmarks creates a significant computational
               burden. To address these issues, we propose LIME (Less Is More for MLLM Eval-
016
               uation), a refined and efficient benchmark curated using a semi-automated pipeline.
017
               This pipeline filters out uninformative samples and eliminates answer leakage by
018            focusing on tasks that require image-based understanding. Our experiments show
019            that LIME reduces the number of samples by 76% and evaluation time by 77%,
020            while it can more effectively distinguish different models’ abilities. Notably, we
021            find that traditional automatic metrics like CIDEr are insufficient for evaluating
022            MLLMs’ captioning performance, and excluding the caption task score yields a
023            more accurate reflection of overall model performance. All code and data are
024            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LIME-49CD.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In order to better understand the model’s capabilities and guide addressing the shortcomings of
030   MLLMs, researchers develop numerous benchmarks for various tasks (Antol et al., 2015; Wei et al.,
031   2023; Fu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a). These benchmarks thoroughly explore the
032   capabilities of MLLMs in various tasks such as image captioning, image question answering, and
033   multimodal retrieving.
034
      However, existing MLLM benchmarks and unified evaluation frameworks cannot effectively and
035
      efficiently reflect the ability of MLLMs. Current benchmarks include numerous relatively simple
036   samples (i.e., how many chairs are in the picture) and some incorrect questions caused by annotation
037   issues. Most MLLMs consistently perform on these samples (i.e., all correct or all wrong). Therefore,
038   those benchmarks cannot fully reflect the gap between different MLLMs and across various tasks.
039   Besides, the current unified multimodal evaluation frameworks require significant computational
040   resources, necessitating integrating much evaluation data from various benchmarks. The selection of
041   effective evaluation data is largely overlooked by current researchers.
042   As shown in Figure 1, to address the aforementioned issues, we propose to use a general data process
043   pipeline and curate a LIME, which contains 9403 samples and is refined across 10 tasks within 6
044   domains. We select six major tasks in the multimodal domain and use 9 MLLMs to refine those 10
045   benchmarks within the corresponding domain. To eliminate bias introduced by individual models,
046   we choose 9 models as judges and filter samples based on their performance. On the one hand, we
047   remove samples that most models answer correctly due to the fact that they cannot distinguish the
048   capabilities among different models. On the other hand, we use a method that combines humans
049   and MLLMs to filter out some abnormally difficult samples. Meanwhile, we use LLMs to filter out
050
      samples that can be answered directly from the context of the question. After that, we obtain a smaller
      yet higher-quality unified bench (i.e., LIME).
051
052   We conduct various experiments on LIME using both MLLMs and LLMs on different input settings,
053   such as QA + image inputs, QA input (text-only input), and the QA + image description experiment.
      We make several valuable findings:

      2    M ETHOD
072
073   Most benchmarks contain low-quality, noisy data. Figure 2 shows the statistics of different
074   subtasks within our LIME benchmark. It is worth mentioning that the proportion of easy and wrong
075   samples exceeds 30Out of the 10 subtasks, 6 have proportions exceeding 50%. Notably, for the
076   POPE dataset, 95% of the data can be classified as noisy or erroneous. This indicates that existing
077   benchmarks are filled with a large amount of low-quality data, which does not accurately reflect the
078   true capabilities of MLLMs.
079   Inspired by MMStar (Chen et al., 2024a), we utilize open-source MLLMs and LLMs as the judges for
080   filtering, specifically, we remove the existing annotation errors. The overall pipeline consists of three
081   main stages: (1) Using open-source models as judges, (2) A semi-automated screening process, and
082   (3) Eliminating answer leakage. Our approach aims to improve existing benchmarks by removing
083   inaccurate and oversimplified data.

129   2.1     OPEN - SOURCE MODELS AS JUDGES
130
131   To avoid potential biases that may exist in individual MLLMs, we select ten different types of open-
132   source models as judges. To categorize the difficulty of each sample, we analyze the performance of
133   all judge models on each question and label the difficulty based on the number of models that answer
134   correctly. We define N as the number of models that correctly answer the sample. If N ≥ 6, the
135
      question is classified as the easy set. If 3 ≤ N ≤ 5, it is classified as the middle set. Conversely, if
      N ≤ 2, it is classified as the hard set.
136
137
138   2.2     S EMI - AUTOMATED SCREENING PROCESS
139
140   Easy samples do not effectively differentiate the capabilities of various models, as most models can
141   answer them correctly. Therefore, we remove the easy samples to assess model performance better.
142   Furthermore, we find that some questions are not correctly answered by any model, which can be
143   due to potential errors in the question design. To mitigate these potential errors and filter out totally
144   incorrect questions, we implement a semi-automated screening process, which consists of two stages.
145   In the first stage, all questions with zero passes are reviewed by GPT-4V to assess their correctness
146   in terms of logic and meaning. In the second stage, questions deemed correct by GPT-4V are then
147   manually screened. This strategy helps us eliminate meaningless or erroneous data from the dataset,
148   thereby reducing its size and improving its quality.
149
150   2.3     E LIMINATING A NSWER L EAKAGE
151
152   Although the previous two stages have filtered out potential errors and assessed the quality of the
153   questions, we still need to address the potential issue of ANSWER LEAKAGE. Multimodal Answer
154   Leakage can be summarized into two main categories: 1.Text Answerable Questions: The textual
155   information contains all the necessary details to answer the question, making the corresponding visual
156   information redundant. 2.Seen Questions: The MLLMs have encountered a specific question during
157   training and has memorized the question along with its corresponding ground truth.
158   As for the Seen Questions, it has been removed in the Filtering Easy Sample module in Sec. 2.2.
159   Therefore, we conduct a text-only check using pure text LLMs to eliminate the ANSWER LEAK-
160   AGE. Specifically, based on LLMs’ responses, we remove the samples that can be directly answered
161   without using the image. After that, we proportionally sample 1,200 samples from these categories
      based on their difficulty levels. For benchmarks with fewer than 1,200 entries, we adapt all samples.

233   4.1      E XPERIMENT S ETTING
234
      To evaluate the quality of LIME, we conduct a series of experiments across various open-source and
235
      closed-source models. These experiments primarily encompass the following three settings:
236
237   Main experiment: To demonstrate the performance of LIME, we evaluate mainstream open-source
238   and closed-source models using a standardized process to reflect their overall performance differences.
239   Text-only set: To prevent potential data leakage issues, we conduct validation experiments using
240   text-only QA pairs. This verifies whether LLMs can correctly answer questions based on text-only
241   information.
242
      Text-only question with Image Description set: Image Description (ID) refers to simple descriptions
243   of images that represent superficial information contained within them. For most MLLMs, questions
244   containing only superficial information are easy to answer; however, questions requiring complex
245   visual inference are significantly more challenging. To further validate whether LIME can reflect
246   the capabilities of MLLMs, we input text-only QA pairs combined with ID into LLMs and test their
247   ability.
248
249   4.2      BASELINES
250
251   We select LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a;b), LLaVA-1.6 (Liu et al., 2024), Tinny-LLaVA (Zhou et al.,
252   2024), MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024), Idefics-2 1 , Deepseek-VL(Lu et al., 2024), CogVLM (Wang
253   et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023), XComposer-4KHD (Zhang et al., 2023), Mantis (Jiang et al., 2024),
254
      InternVL-1.5 and InternVL-2 (Chen et al., 2023; 2024b) as our MLLMs baseline, and LLaMA3,
      Yi, Yi-1.5 (AI et al., 2024), Qwen-1.5 (Bai et al., 2023a) and Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024) as LLMs
255
      baseline. To ensure fairness in the evaluations, we use the unified evaluation framework provided
256
      by lmms-eval (Zhang et al., 2024b) to conduct evaluation experiments on LIME. For models not
257   supported by lmms-eval, we refine the inference code provided by the model developers to make it
258   compatible with the new models for the sake of aligning the results of different models.
259
260   Metrics For most tasks included in LIME, we reference the metrics computation methods used in
261   lmms-eval. Specifically, for tasks such as AI2D, ScienceQA, OCRBench, and POPE, we calculate
262   the accuracy of the extracted responses. For tasks such as OK-VQA and TextVQA, we calculate the
263   metric scores based on the overlap between the response and the candidate answers. For tasks like
264   TextCaps and COCO-Caption2017, we use CIDEr as the score. The ANLS metric is used for the
265   infoVQA task, and the Relaxed Overall metric is used for the ChartQA task.
266   We calculate the sub-scores for each task category by taking a weighted average of the subtask scores,
267   and then compute the overall score by weighted averaging the scores of all tasks except for the caption
268   tasks. The details of the metrics calculation are provided in Tab 7.

      As shown in Tab 2, we evaluate both open-source and closed-source MLLMs using our LIME
305
      benchmark. Overall, for closed-source models, GPT-4O achieves the best performance with a score
306   of 52%, while for open-source models, models with larger parameter sizes and newer versions tend to
307   have higher overall scores. InternVL-1.5, InternVL-2-Large (26B, 40B), and LLaVA-OneVision-7B
308   achieve the best overall performance, with their overall scores all surpassing 60%. The performance
309   of InternVL-2-Small (1B-8B), the CogVLM series, and the Cambrian series follows, with their
310   overall scores ranging from 45% to 60%.
311
      Comparing the overall scores of LIME and Origin benchmarks, we observe that certain model
312   series, such as Cambrian and LLaVA-1.5, experience a decline in overall scores. Conversely, the
313   CogVLM and LLaVA-OneVision series show an improvement, with CogVLM2 and XComposer-
314   4KHD experiencing significant increases of 4% and 6%, respectively.
315
      Tab 6 provides more detailed experimental results. Regarding caption subtasks, most models demon-
316
      strate good performance. These tasks involve generating or assessing descriptions of the content in
317
      images, which indicates that current MLLMs possess strong image content recognition capabilities.
318   As for the VQA task, current MLLMs perform relatively well on TextVQA, ChatQA, and ScienceQA,
319   where the questions directly ask about facts in the picture. However, their performance is relatively
320   lower on OK-VQA, infoVQA, and AI2D, which require additional commonsense knowledge or
321   complex reasoning to answer the questions. This demonstrates that current MLLMs exhibit significant
322   image content recognition capabilities but are limited in their ability to perform complex reasoning
323   using additional knowledge. We believe this limitation may be due to constraints in the language
      model component of MLLMs.

353   5.2   C ORRELATION A NALYSIS
354
      Figure 4 illustrates the correlation between the various sub-tasks in LIME and WildVision Bench.
355
      Most tasks in LIME exhibit a strong positive correlation with WildVision Bench. Six subtasks
356   have correlation scores exceeding 80%. Additionally, the overall score of LIME correlates at 91%
357   with WV-Elo, which is higher than any individual sub-task and the original bench’s correlations,
358   demonstrating that the overall score of LIME provides a more comprehensive reflection of MLLMs’
359   capabilities.
360
361   Automated evaluation metrics (e.g., CIDEr) cannot effectively assess the performance of
362   MLLMs in captioning tasks. As an early foundational problem, the captioning task is exten-
363   sively studied, and MLLMs demonstrate exceptional ability in this task. For instance, earlier models
364   like InstructBlip perform exceptionally well on captioning tasks, and there is a broad presence of
365   training data for image captioning in MLLMs’ training processes. However, the captioning task
366
      shows a negative correlation with all other sub-tasks. This indicates that previous metrics (e.g., BLEU,
      CIDEr) only focus on the overlap between the model-generated responses and the ground truth, but
367
      do not consider that MLLMs might generate content that is semantically close to the ground truth
368
      (i.e., the model-generated response may be semantically similar to the ground truth but expressed
369   differently, or the model may generate more detailed content about the image). Consequently, we
370   exclude it from the overall score calculation.
371
372   There is a certain degree of correlation between the sub-tasks in LIME. On the one hand, the
373   relevance of TextVQA, InfographicVQA, and OCRBench is relatively high. As shown in Fig. 4, the
374   correlation of these tasks all surpasses 85%, and these two VQA tasks require MLLMs to understand
375   fine-grained content in images to answer questions. This demonstrates that OCR tasks also rely on
376   the ability of MLLMs to perceive fine-grained objective facts in images. On the other hand, POPE,
377   ChartQA, and InfographicVQA all require reasoning abilities using extra commonsense knowledge.
      The correlation scores of these tasks are all above 70%, and POPE requires the model to use extra


                                                                         7

      knowledge to solve the hallucination of MLLMs. We assume that ChartQA and infoVQA may also
379   necessitate the use of additional common knowledge by the models to solve problems.

479
480   To further investigate the gap between LIME and real-world users’ queries, we construct a similarity
481   search system that compares them. MixEval (Ni et al., 2024) uses SentenceTransformers(Reimers,
482   2019) as the retrieval model, while Uniir (Wei et al., 2023) employs multimodal models like CLIP
483   and BLIP. We use WildVision-Chat as the query data source, which contains 45.2k high-quality user
484   questions, and employ SentenceTransformers to retrieve the top 10 most similar samples from LIME.
485   To fully incorporate image information, we combine the question and image description as the query
      input. Additionally, we utilize Qwen2-72B to ensure a high level of relevance in the final results. As

      6   R ELATED WORK
499
500   In recent years, there has been increasing attention on establishing evaluation benchmarks to assess
501   the performance of MLLMs in different scenarios to guide the development of MLLMs. Early
502   multimodal evaluation benchmarks primarily focused on single tasks, such as Visual Question
503   Answering (VQA)(Antol et al., 2015; Goyal et al., 2017; Kafle & Kanan, 2017; Singh et al., 2019;
504   Marino et al., 2019), Image Captioning(Agrawal et al., 2019), and Information Retrieval (Wei et al.,
505   2023). As MLLMs develop, simple benchmarks are no longer sufficient to evaluate the versatile
506   capabilities of these models comprehensively, since most MLLMs demonstrate exceptional ability on
507
      those benchmarks. Consequently, numerous more difficult and diverse benchmarks have emerged
      in recent years to assess the capabilities of MLLMs comprehensively. For instance, MMMU (Yue
508
      et al., 2024) and CMMMU (Zhang et al., 2024a) are comprehensive benchmark tests for university-
509
      level multidisciplinary multimodal understanding and reasoning. MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c)
510   has developed a comprehensive evaluation pipeline that offers fine-grained capability assessment
511   and robust evaluation metrics. MMRA (Wu et al., 2024b) systematically establishes an association
512   relation system among images to assess the multi-image relation mining ability of MLLMs.
513
      However, those benchmarks cannot distinguish the performance gaps among different models ex-
514
      cellently, as they still contain some too simple or difficult samples that most models yield the same
515
      results on. Furthermore, training datasets across different models may contain the samples of those
516   benchmarks, which results in data leakage issues (Fu et al., 2023). Mmstar (Chen et al., 2024a)
517   and MMLU Redux (Gema et al., 2024) have identified several issues within current benchmarks.
518   Mmstar (Chen et al., 2024a) proposes an automated pipeline to filter benchmark data, aiming to detect
519   potential data leakage, while MMLU Redux (Gema et al., 2024) focuses on correcting annotation
520   errors. However, there is still a pressing need for a comprehensive pipeline that fully addresses the
521   challenges posed by multimodal datasets. In response to this, we introduce LIME: LESS IS MORE
522   FOR MLLM EVALUATION. We have carefully selected six task types from existing mainstream
523   benchmarks and scaled them down according to clear guidelines. This streamlined version retains the
524
      core elements of mainstream MLLM benchmarks, providing a more efficient and focused evaluation.
525
526
527   7   C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   As MLLMs continue to advance, a notable absence of convenient and high-quality multimodal
531
      benchmarks has emerged. In response to this, we propose a pipeline aimed at semi-automatically
      refining existing benchmarks to enhance their quality, culminating in the development of LIME,
532
      which comprises 9,403 evaluation samples across 6 types of tasks and 10 different benchmark
533
      datasets. By refining the original benchmarks to filter question difficulty and eliminate potentially
534   problematic items, LIME offers a more rigorous evaluation for MLLMs, necessitating a deeper
535   understanding of image information. The outcomes of our evaluation experiments demonstrate the
536   heightened challenge posed by LIME for MLLMs. We anticipate that our approach will contribute
537   to the advancement of MLLM evaluation systems, and we are committed to continually enriching
538   LIME with an expanded array of datasets through regular updates and expansions. Our ultimate goal
539   is to provide the community with a simpler, more efficient, and more accurate evaluation method and
      suite for MLLMs.

       The original dataset contains noise data. In the following figure, we categorize the problematic data
1083   into three types and present specific examples from different datasets.
1084
1085   Text Answerable Questions: Some questions can be answered without the need for visual infor-
1086   mation, mainly focusing on the AI2D and ScienceQA datasets. As shown in figs. 30 and 31, AI2D
1087   and ScienceQA emphasize knowledge in the field of science while overlooking the importance of
1088   visual information. Given the background of domain knowledge, some LLMs are able to provide
1089   answers even without requiring visual input.
1090
1091   Annotation Error Questions: Most benchmarks are manually curated, which inevitably leads to
1092   annotation errors. Problematic questions exist in almost all benchmarks. It can be found in figs. 32,
1093   33 and 39 to 44.
1094
1095   Repeated Question: Some benchmarks also contain a significant amount of duplicate data, where
1096
       the question content and image content are completely identical. This issue is mainly found in the
       POPE dataset, as shown in the figs. 34 to 38.
