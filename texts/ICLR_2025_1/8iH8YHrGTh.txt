011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Grokking is the intriguing phenomenon of delayed generalization: networks ini-
014            tially memorize training data with perfect accuracy but poor generalization, then
015            transition to a generalizing solution with continued training. While reasons for this
016
               delayed generalization, such as weight norms and sparsity, have been discussed,
               the influence of network structure, particularly the role of subnetworks, remains
017
               underexplored. In this work, we link the grokking phenomenon to the lottery ticket
018
               hypothesis to investigate the impact of inner network structures. We demonstrate
019            that using lottery tickets obtained at the generalizing phase (termed ‘grokking
020            tickets’) significantly reduces delayed generalization on various tasks, including
021            multiple modular arithmetic, polynomial regression, sparse parity, and MNIST.
022            Through a series of controlled experiments, our findings reveal that neither small
023            weight norms nor sparsity alone account for the reduction of delayed generalization;
024            instead, the presence of a good subnetwork structure is crucial. Analyzing the
025            transition from memorization to generalization, we observe that rapid changes
026            in subnetwork structures, measured by the Jaccard distance, correlate strongly
027
               with improvements in test accuracy. We further show that pruning techniques
               can accelerate the grokking process, transforming a memorizing network into a
028
               generalizing one without updating the weights. Finally, we confirm the emergence
029
               of periodic inner-structures, indicating that the model discovers internally good
030            structures (generalizing structures) suited for the task.

      Understanding the mechanism of generalization is a central question in understanding the efficacy
036   of neural networks. Recently, Power et al. (2022) unveiled the intriguing phenomenon of delayed
037   generalization (grokking); neural networks initially attain a memorizing network Cmem with the perfect
038   training accuracy but poor generalization, yet further training transitions the solution to a generalizing
039   network Cgen . This phenomenon, which contradicts standard machine learning expectations, is
040   being studied to answer the question: what underlies the transition between memorization and
041   generalization? (Liu et al., 2022; 2023a)
042   Regarding the relationship between generalization and deep learning in general, it is well known
043   that structure of networks significantly impacts generalization performance. For instance, image
044   recognition performance has greatly improved by leveraging the structure of convolution (Krizhevsky
045   et al., 2012). Moreover, as shown in Neyshabur (2020), incorporating β-Lasso regularization into fully
046   connected MLPs facilitates the emergence of locality—resembling the structures in CNNs—leading
047   to improved performance in image tasks. From a slightly different perspective, Frankle & Carbin
048   (2019) proposed the lottery ticket hypothesis (LTH), which suggests that good subnetworks (good
049   structure) help to achieve better performance with better sample efficiency (Zhang et al., 2021). Also,
050
      Ramanujan et al. (2020) shows that exploring structures alone can achieve performance comparable
      to weight updates, suggesting that good subnetworks are enough to achieve generalized performance.
051
052   While the importance of structure is well known in general, its connection to the phenomenon of
053   grokking has not been investigated enough. Similar to our study, several prior works connect the
      grokking phenomenon to the property of networks, e.g., weight norm and sparsity of networks. For

      To investigate the role of structure in the grokking phenomenon, we first demonstrate that when
084
      using the lottery ticket obtained at generalizing solution (referred to as grokking tickets), delayed
085   generalization is significantly reduced. Figure 1 (left) illustrates that the train and test accuracy
086   increase almost simultaneously with grokking tickets, unlike randomly initialized dense networks
087   where delayed generalization occurs. As will be shown later, this result is related to the pruning rate,
088   with proper pruning rates resulting in less delay. We conducted further experiments from several
089   perspectives to understand why delayed generalization is significantly reduced with grokking tickets.
090   First, as illustrated in Figure 1 (right), we decompose the differences between the grokking ticket
091   and a randomly initialized dense network into three elements: (1) small weight norms, (2) sparsity
092   and (3) good structure. We investigate which of these elements contribute to the significant reduction
093
      of delayed generalization. For (1) weight norms, we find that dense networks with the same initial
      weight norms as the grokking ticket do not generalize faster, indicating weight norm is not the cause.
094

095
      et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019; Tanaka et al., 2020) also do not generalize faster, indicating sparsity is
096   not the cause. These results suggest that (3) good structure is essential for understanding grokking.
097
098   Subsequently, based on the above results, we analyze whether good structure exploration truly occurs
099
      during the transition phase. Using the jaccard distance (Paganini & Forde, 2020) as the metric

100

101

102   generalization. We employ the edge-popup algorithm (Ramanujan et al., 2020), which finds a good

126   2    BACKGROUND
127
128   Grokking is a phenomenon where generalization happens long after overfitting the training data
129   (as shown in Figure 1 (left)). The phenomenon was initially observed in the modular addition task
130   ((a + b) mod p for a, b ∈ (0, · · · , p − 1)), and the same phenomenon has been observed in more
131   complex datasets, encompassing modular arithmetic (Gromov, 2023; Davies et al., 2023; Rubin
132   et al., 2023; Stander et al., 2023; Furuta et al., 2024), semantic analysis (Liu et al., 2023a), n-k
133   parity (Merrill et al., 2023), polynomial regression (Kumar et al., 2023), hierarchical task (Murty
134   et al., 2023) and image classification (Liu et al., 2023a; Radhakrishnan et al., 2022). This paper
135
      mainly focuses on grokking in the modular arithmetic tasks commonly used in prior studies.
136   To understand grokking, previous works proposed possible explanations, including the slingshot
137   mechanism (Thilak et al., 2022), random walk among minimizers (Millidge, 2022), formulation of
138   good representation (Liu et al., 2022), the scale of weight norm (Liu et al., 2023a; Varma et al., 2023),
139   simplicity of Fourier features (Nanda et al., 2023) and sparsity of generalizing network (Miller et al.,
140   2023). Among those, one of the dominant explanations regarding how the network changes during
141
      the process of grokking are the simplicity of the generalization solution, particularly focusing on
      the weight norms of network parameters ∥θ∥2 . For example, the original paper (Power et al., 2022)
142
      posited that weight decay plays a pivotal role in grokking, i.e., test accuracy will not increase without
143
      weight decay. Liu et al. (2023a) analyzed the loss landscapes of train and test dataset, verifying that
144   grokking occurs by entering the generalization zone defined by L2 norm, with models having large
145   initial values θ0 . More recently, Varma et al. (2023) demonstrated that the generalization solution
146   could produce higher logits with smaller weight norms. In this paper, we examine the changes in
147   the network’s structure and demonstrate that the network is not simply decreasing its overall weight
148   norms but searching for good structures within itself.
149
      Several studies have proposed that acquiring good representations is the key to understanding
150   grokking. For example, Power et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022) explained that the topology of the ideal
151   embeddings tends to be circles or cylinders within the context of modular addition tasks. Nanda
152   et al. (2023) identified the trigonometric algorithm by which the networks solve modular addition
153   after grokking and showed that it grows smoothly over training. Gromov (2023) showed an analytic
154   solution for the representations when learning modular addition with MLP. Zhong et al. (2023) show,
155   using modular addition as a prototypical problem, that algorithm discovery in neural networks is
156   sometimes more complex. These studies support the quality of representation as key to distinguishing
157   memorizing and generalizing networks; however, these studies do not explain what is happening
158
      within the network’s structure.
159   The lottery ticket hypothesis proposed by Frankle & Carbin (2019) has garnered attention as an
160   explanation for why over-parameterized neural networks exhibit generalization capabilities (Allen-
161   Zhu et al., 2019). Informally, the lottery ticket hypothesis states that randomly initialized over-
      parameterized networks include sparse subnetworks that reach good performance after train, and

162
      the existence of the subnetworks is key to achieving good generalization in deep neural networks.
163   This claim was initially demonstrated experimentally, but theoretical foundations have also been
164   established (Frankle et al., 2020; Sakamoto & Sato, 2022). More formally, the process involves the
165   following steps:

169

170
171         3. Reset the weights of the network to their initial values. θ0 and get a subnetwork fθ0 ⊙mkt ,

173
174   After the discovery of the lottery tickets, Ramanujan et al. (2020) show that there exist strong
175   lottery tickets, which achieve good performance without weight update. They use the edge-popup
176   algorithm (Ramanujan et al., 2020) to selects subnetworks based on a score s (s ∈ R|θ0 | ). In other
177   words, when pruning a certain proportion k of weights from the given weights θ0 , the model predicts
178   using edges with the top (1 − k) scores in a forward pass. For a detailed description of edge-popup,

302   Figure 2 indicates that grokking ticket facilitates generalization. To better understand the inner
303   workings, we decouple the potential benefits of lottery tickets into (1) small weight norms, (2) higher
304   sparsity, and (3) good structure, as shown in Figure 1 (right). We conduct a series of controlled
305   experiments using a grokking ticket to decouple each effect. Firstly, we find that dense networks with
306
      the same initial weight norms as the grokking ticket do not generalize faster. Additionally, compared
      to networks with the same sparsity as grokking tickets, we show mere sparsity also does not promote
307
      generalization. These results indicate that the reason why lottery tickets significantly accelerate the
308
      generalization process is better attributed to the structure of networks, rather than small weight
309   norm and sparsity.

478   analysis of the network weights after generalization reveals a clear periodicity (see the Base model

482   a good structure, we introduce Fourier Entropy (FE) as follows. In general, the discrete Fourier

502   6    D ISCUSSION AND R ELATED W ORKS
503
504   In this paper, we conducted a set of experiments to understand the mechanism of grokking (delayed
505   generalization). Below is a summary of observations. (1) In subsection 3.2, the use of the lottery
506   ticket significantly reduces delayed generalization. (2) In section 4, good structure is a more important
507   factor in explaining grokking than the weight norm and sparsity by comparing it with the same weight
508   norm and sparsity level. (3) In Figure 7, good structure is gradually discovered, corresponding to
509
      improvement of test accuracy. (4) In Table 1, pruning without updating weights from a memorizing
      solution increases test accuracy. (5) In Figure 8, finding a good structure corresponds to the acquisition
510
      of representations. Our work also contributes to a deeper understanding of grokking and may inspire
511
      a more rigorous approach to deep learning.
512
513
      Weight norm reduction Liu et al. (2023a) suggests that the reduction of weight norms is crucial
514   for generalization. In Figure 5, our results go further to show that, rather than simply reducing weight
515   norms, the network discovers good structure (subnetwork), resulting in the reduction of weight norms.
516
517   Representation learning Liu et al. (2022); Nanda et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023a); Gromov (2023)
518   showed the quality of representation as key to distinguishing memorizing and generalizing networks.
519   Figure 8 demonstrates that good structure contributes to the acquisition of good representation,
520   suggesting the importance of inner structure (network topology) in achieving good representations
521
522   Sparsity and Efficiency Merrill et al. (2023) argued that the grokking phase transition corresponds
523   to the emergence of a sparse subnetwork that dominates model predictions. While they empirically
524   studied parse parity tasks where sparsity is evident, we are conducting tasks (modular arithmetic,
525
      MNIST) commonly used in grokking research and architecture (MLP, Transformer). Furthermore,
      Figure 5, we demonstrate not only sparsity but also that good structure is crucial.
526
527
      Regularization Weight decay (Rumelhart et al., 1986) is one of the most commonly used regu-
528
      larization techniques and is known to be a critical factor in grokking (Power et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
529
      2023a). In Appendix E, we show if the good structure is discovered, the network generalizes without
530   weight decay, indicating that weight decay works as a structure exploration, which suggests that
531   weight decay contributes not to reducing weight but to exploring good structure. In addition, we
532   tested pruning as a new force to induce generalization (Table 1).
533
534   Lottery ticket hypothesis The lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019) suggests that good
535   structures are crucial for generalization, but it remains unclear how these structures are acquired during
536   training and how they correspond to the network’s representations. To the best of my knowledge,
537   our paper is the first to connect grokking and the lottery ticket hypothesis, demonstrating how
538   good structures emerge (subsection 5.1) and contribute to effective representations (subsection 5.3).
539   Building on this, we further show that while faster generalization is a well-documented property of
      winning tickets, our work goes beyond by exploring how the discovery of good structures.

1164   I   L IMITATION
1165
1166   First, our study uses the framework of the lottery ticket hypothesis, so neural network models are
1167   within its scope. Therefore, grokking phenomena in non-neural networks, as seen in studies Levi
1168
       et al. (2023); Miller et al. (2023), are outside the scope and are considered future work.
1169   Additionally, although we have verified various experimental settings (Modular Arithmetic, MNIST
1170   classification, Polynomial regression, Sparse parity), the tasks and networks used are still simple, as
1171   in previous grokking studies. The lottery ticket hypothesis has been studied in more practical cases,
1172   so experiments in more practical scenarios are interesting and remain as future work.
1173   In addition, our paper shows that structural changes correspond to generalization ability, but it does
1174   not propose specific metrics for what structural characteristics lead to good representations. Research
1175   on the network characteristics of good structures remains in future work.

       J     G ROKKING T ICKETS IN NLP TASKS
1189
1190
       Following Liu et al. (2023a), we conducted a sentiment analysis task on the IMDb dataset (Maas
1191   et al., 2011), which consists of 50,000 movie reviews classified as either positive or negative. The
1192   data was pre-processed by extracting the 1,000 most frequent words and tokenizing each review into
1193   an array of token indices, with less frequent words ignored and each review padded to a length of
1194   500. For classification, we utilized a two-layer LSTM model (Hochreiter, 1997) with an embedding
1195   dimension of 64 and a hidden dimension of 128. The model was trained using the Adam optimizer
1196   with a learning rate of 0.001 and weight decay of 0.001 to minimize the binary cross-entropy loss,
1197   and 25% of the dataset was reserved for testing.
1198   In our sentiment analysis task, which involves two-class classification with a chance rate of 50%, we
1199   observe distinct learning dynamics between the base model and the grokking ticket. The base model
1200   rapidly achieves 100% training accuracy; however, its test accuracy remains low until approximately
1201   10,000 optimization steps, after which it begins to improve. On the other hand, the grokking
1202   ticket demonstrates a different behavior, with test accuracy improving almost simultaneously with
1203   training accuracy from the very beginning of the optimization process. These results suggest that the

       L    S IGNIFICANCE OF P ERIODICITY IN THE M ODULAR A DDITION TASK
1297
1298
       The modular addition task, defined as predicting c ≡ (a + b) mod p, inherently involves periodicity
1299   due to the modular arithmetic structure. Nanda et al. (2023) demonstrated that transformers trained
1300   on modular addition tasks rely on periodic representations to achieve generalization. Specifically, the
1301   networks embed inputs a and b into a Fourier basis, encoding them as sine and cosine components of
1302   key frequencies wk = 2kπ p for some k ∈ N. These periodic representations are then combined using
1303   trigonometric identities within the network layers to compute the modular sum.
1304
       Mechanism of Periodic Representations
1305
1306   Nanda et al. (2023) reverse-engineered the weights and activations of a one-layer transformer trained
1307   on this task and found that the model computes:
1308                      cos(wk (a + b)) = cos(wk a) cos(wk b) − sin(wk a) sin(wk b),
1309
1310                       sin(wk (a + b)) = sin(wk a) cos(wk b) + cos(wk a) sin(wk b),
1311   using the embedding matrix and the attention and MLP layers. The logits for each possible output c
1312   are then computed by projecting these values using:
1313
                  cos(wk (a + b − c)) = cos(wk (a + b)) cos(wk c) + sin(wk (a + b)) sin(wk c).
1314
1315   This approach ensures that the network’s output logits exhibit constructive interference at c ≡ (a + b)
1316   mod p, while destructive interference suppresses other incorrect values.
1317   Given this mechanism, it can be inferred that the model internally utilizes periodicity, such as the
1318   addition formulas, to perform modular arithmetic.
