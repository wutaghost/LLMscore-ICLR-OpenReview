011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) are trained on extensive datasets that often contain
014            sensitive, human-generated information, raising significant concerns about privacy
015            breaches. While certified unlearning approaches offer strong privacy guarantees,
016            they rely on restrictive model assumptions that are not applicable to LLMs. As
017
               a result, various unlearning heuristics have been proposed, with the associated
               privacy risks assessed only empirically. The standard evaluation pipelines typically
018
               randomly select data for removal from the training set, apply unlearning techniques,
019
               and use membership inference attacks (MIAs) to compare the unlearned models
020            against models retrained without the to-be-unlearned data. However, since every
021            data point is subject to the right to be forgotten, unlearning should be considered
022            in the worst-case scenario from the privacy perspective. Prior work shows that
023            data outliers may exhibit higher memorization effects. Intuitively, they are harder
024            to be unlearn and thus the privacy risk of unlearning them is overlooked and un-
025            derestimated in the current evaluation. In this paper, we leverage minority data
026            to identify such a critical flaw in previously widely adopted evaluations. We sub-
027            stantiate this claim through carefully designed experiments, including unlearning
028
               canaries related to minority groups, inspired by privacy auditing literature. Using
               personally identifiable information (PII) as a representative minority identifier, we
029
               demonstrate that minority groups experience at least 20% more privacy leakage
030
               in most cases across six unlearning approaches, three MIAs, three benchmark
031            datasets, and two LLMs of different scales. Given that the right to be forgotten
032            should be upheld for every individual, we advocate for a more rigorous evaluation
033            of LLM unlearning methods. Our minority-aware evaluation framework represents
034            an initial step toward ensuring more equitable and thorough assessments of LLM
035            unlearning efficacy.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039   Large Language Models (LLMs) are trained on vast and diverse datasets, often sourced from public
040   content on the web, much of which is generated by humans (Touvron et al., 2023; Ouyang et al.,
041   2022). This practice raises significant ethical concerns, particularly when the data includes sensitive
042   information, leading to potential privacy violations. Individuals whose data has been used may seek
043   to exercise their "right to be forgotten", a protection guaranteed by regulations such as the General
044
      Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Krzysztofek, 2018).
045   The ideal approach to fulfilling such a request is to retrain the LLM from scratch, excluding the data to
046   be removed. However, this solution is prohibitively expensive and impractical for large-scale models.
047   To address this, the concept of machine unlearning has emerged as a promising alternative. Machine
048   unlearning seeks to efficiently modify the LLM so that it becomes statistically indistinguishable from
049   a model retrained from scratch, without the data subject to removal. In this way, no adversary could
050
      confidently determine whether a model has undergone a proper unlearning process or been retrained,
      ensuring compliance with the “right to be forgotten”.
051
052   Unfortunately, it remains an open problem to enforce the formal unlearning guarantee for deep neural
053   networks and LLMs. Despite recent progress in theoretical unlearning literature (Guo et al., 2020;
      Sekhari et al., 2021; Neel et al., 2021; Ullah et al., 2021; Chien et al., 2023; Ullah & Arora, 2023;


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Chien et al., 2024a;b), they are still far from ready to be applied to deep neural networks and LLMs
055   due to their restrictive assumptions. Meanwhile, researchers have developed efficient unlearning
056   heuristics and verified the efficacy of unlearning empirically (Golatkar et al., 2020a;b; Graves et al.,
057   2021; Liu et al., 2024a;c; Yao et al., 2024). Recent advancements in LLM unlearning often measures
058   the unlearning performance by comparing the performance of unlearned models against models
059   retrained from scratch (Pawelczyk et al., 2024a). A common approach to empirically evaluating
060   unlearning efficacy is to employ membership inference attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2017), where
061   an attacker attempts to identify if a model is from retraining or not. For instance, MUSE (Shi et al.,
062   2024b) is a representative benchmark study that leverages the ROC-AUC metric from MIAs to
063
      quantify privacy leakage.
064   We identify a critical pitfall in the aforementioned LLM unlearning efficacy evaluation pipeline.
065   Literature indicates that memorization levels in LLMs vary significantly across individual training
066   samples (Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Carlini et al., 2022). However, the current LLM unlearning
067   efficacy evaluation only captures an ’average-case’ performance, where removed data is randomly
068   sampled from the training set. This approach neglects the privacy risk of data that are hard to unlearn
069
      which does not account for situations where privacy must be rigorously protected in the worst-case
      scenario (Steinke & Ullman, 2020; Aerni et al., 2024). It neglects the principle that every individual’s
070
      right to be forgotten should be upheld equally, where the privacy risk of data from minority groups is
071
      overlooked. They are often considered as outliers and may be more resistant to unlearning efforts
072   due to the aforementioned stronger memorization effect. Therefore, standard unlearning evaluation
073   significantly underestimates privacy risks associated with these groups within the training set, which
074   also misses the broader social responsibilities.
075
      To verify our claim, we first conduct a careful synthetic experiment of unlearning injected canaries
076
      pertaining to minority groups, which is motivated by the privacy auditing literature (Jagielski et al.,
077
      2020; Steinke et al., 2024). We choose Personally Identifiable Information (PII) as a representative
078   minority identifier, while our idea extends to broader cases. We show that minorities suffer from at
079   least 20% more of privacy leakage in most cases across combinations of six unlearning approaches,
080   three variants of MIAs, three benchmark datasets, and two LLMs of different scales. It highlights
081   the prevalence of the claimed issue in practical settings. Our results call for a more careful LLM
082   unlearning efficacy evaluation, particularly in regard to privacy risk for minority groups. We propose
083   a minority-aware LLM unlearning evaluation protocol (Figure 1) as an initial step toward this goal.
084   We further benchmark existing unlearning approaches and investigate the effect of the forget set size
085   as well as unlearning complexity with our minority-aware evaluation. This provides a more holistic
086
      understanding of different LLM unlearning approaches for practitioners. Importantly, we observe that
      Langevin Unlearning—the only approach incorporating noise—achieves a favorable privacy-utility
087
      trade-off. This suggests that incorporating noise may play a crucial role in effective unlearning.

091   Privacy auditing is a fundamental yet challenging aspect of LLM unlearning due to the difficulty of
092
      distinguishing training samples effectively (Duan et al., 2024). Various privacy-related metrics, such
      as exposure (Carlini et al., 2019), mean reciprocal rank (Wu et al., 2023), extraction likelihood (Jang
093
      et al., 2022), and truth ratio (Maini et al., 2024), have been proposed to probe privacy leakage. Among
094
      these, MIAs remain one of the most crucial tools for evaluating machine unlearning methods (Liu
095   et al., 2024b). Standard MIAs typically involve training numerous shadow models independently to
096   empirically approximate the distribution (Carlini et al., 2022). This approach has also been adopted
097   for LLM unlearning, as seen in the NeurIPS 2023 Machine Unlearning Challenge1 (which compares
098   the point-wise output distributions of multiple unlearned and retrained models to perform MIAs)
099   and Kurmanji et al. (2024); Pawelczyk et al. (2024b). Hayes et al. (2024) further highlights the
100   limitations of average-case evaluations and proposes the specialized MIA method for unlearning
101   evaluation to date. Their work focuses on unlearning a randomly selected subset of training data
102   with a sample-dependent MIA. By contrast, our work targets unlearning cases involving minority
103
      populations within the training data, using a fixed MIA for evaluation.
104   A major downside of MIA approaches involving shadow models is their computational expense, as
105   they require training a large number of LLMs independently (Liu et al., 2024b). To address this

142   3     P RELIMINARIES
143
      Machine unlearning (Cao & Yang, 2015; Bourtoule et al., 2021) has emerged as an important direction
144   in trustworthy language models. It was initially motivated by privacy due to “the right to be forgotten”
145   from GDPR and later on extended to other legal and ethical concerns, including copyright (Yao et al.,
146   2024), biased or outdated information mitigation (Liu et al., 2024b), hallucination removal (Yao et al.,
147   2023), entity forgetting (Maini et al., 2024) and data poisoning removal (Pawelczyk et al., 2024a). In
148   this work, we focus on the privacy aspect of the problem, albeit our methodology extends to other
149   cases whenever the indistinguishability to the retrained model is an appropriate metric.
150   We briefly state the generic machine unlearning setting for privacy. Assume a training dataset Dtrain
151   and a holdout test set Dtest are given. Let Mlearn ← A(M0 , Dtrain ) be the language model train on
152   Dtrain starting from an initial model M0 via the training algorithm A, which may be either a pre-
153   trained language model or random initialization. Once the model is trained, we receive data removal
154   requests that partition the training set Dtrain = Dforget ∪ Dkeep into a subset to be forgotten later Dforget
155   and a keep set Dkeep 2 . An unlearning algorithm U takes Mlearn , Dforget and Dtrain as input to return an
156   updated model Munlearn ← U(Mlearn , Dforget , Dtrain ). It is worth noting that Munlearn depends on the
157   choice of Dforget . The gold standard to adhere “the right to be forgotten” is retraining without Dforget ,
158   namely Mretrain ← A(M0 , Dtrain \ Dforget ). We say U achieves good unlearning efficacy if Munlearn
159   and Mretrain are indistinguishable in their behavior m(Munlearn , D) ≈ m(Mretrain , D) on any corpus

165   3.1   E FFICIENT M EMBERSHIP I NFERENCE ATTACKS FOR LLM U NLEARNING
166   As discussed in the previous section, the effectiveness of unlearning methods can be measured by their
167   indistinguishability in behavior compared to a retrained model. Membership Inference Attack (MIA)
168   is often leveraged to determine whether a specific sample is part of the training set and is widely
169   applied to audit training data privacy leakage. Therefore, to evaluate the efficacy of an unlearning
170   approach, we consider the PrivLeak (PL) metric (Shi et al., 2024b) defined as follows:
171
                                     AUC(Munlearn ; Dforget , Dtest ) − AUC(Mretrain ; Dforget , Dtest )
172              PrivLeak (PL) =                                                                         ,           (1)
                                                    AUC(Mretrain ; Dforget , Dtest )
173
174   where AUC is the AUC-ROC score of an MIA Ye et al. (2022) that tries to discriminate samples
175   from Dforget and Dtest based on the output statistics (e.g. loss) of a given model M . By normalizing
176   the difference in AUC scores between Munlearn and Mretrain using the AUC of Mretrain , the metric
177   accounts for the inherent difficulty of distinguishing the forget and test sets. Note that for an
178   effective unlearning method, the metric should be around zero since the behavior of Munlearn , Mretrain
179   are indistinguishable. A larger magnitude of the PL metric implies a greater amount of privacy
180
      information that has been leaked under the tested MIA. A positive value indicates that the sample has
      not been fully forgotten, as the attacker has a higher AUC for Munlearn than Mretrain . Conversely, a
181
      negative metric value suggests over-forgetting, which still indicates that Munlearn differs from Mretrain
182
      and thus may cause privacy breaches. Finally, note that an effective unlearning solution should lead
183   to a small PL metric for any choices of MIA. In this work, we consider three popular efficient MIAs
184   and report the corresponding PL metric simultaneously.
185
186   • lossMIA (Yeom et al., 2018): Determine membership of a sample based on its loss ℓ(M ; x).
187
      • zlibMIA (Carlini et al., 2021): Determines membership of a sample based on the sample loss
188     normalized by its zlib compression size, ℓ(M ; x)/zlib(x).
189
      • Min-K% (Shi et al., 2023): Selects the lowest K% of token likelihoods and leverages the corre-
190
        sponding negative log-likelihood for membership inference.

196   we estimate the privacy leakage of an unlearning method U via some evaluation m, it is important to
197   account for potential high-risk partitions of Dforget to ensure a comprehensive assessment of privacy
198
      risk. Unfortunately, the current LLM unlearning evaluation pipeline overlooks this critical aspect,

199

200

203   rization compared to common training samples (majorities) (Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Carlini et al.,
204   2022). Intuitively, a similar phenomenon persists for unlearning. See Figure 1 for an illustration of
205   our experimental design.

      5    U NLEARNING M ETHODS
280   We test the following popular unlearning approaches for privacy in the literature. With a slight abuse
281   of notation, we denote M for both the model and its parameter for simplicity.
282
283
      • Random Labels (RL) (Golatkar et al., 2020a; Yao et al., 2024): In next-token prediction, the
        method randomly selects from all possible token sets in disturb training on Dforget and try to
284
        maintain performance on Dkeep . The intuition for this method is that the next-token prediction
285
        of a model not seeing Dforget should act as random guessing. However, this intuition may be
286     inappropriate in some cases as argued in Yao et al. (2024).
287
      • Exact Unlearning (EUk) and Catastrophic Forgetting (CFk) (Goel et al., 2022): Exact unlearning
288
        can be done by retraining the entire model from scratch on Dkeep , albeit is prohibitively expansive
289
        in practice. Goel et al. (2022) propose EUk method, which retrains only the last k layers of the
290     model while freezing the other layers. As a result, it is computationally cheaper than retraining the
291     entire model. They also propose the CFk method, which continues training the last k layers on the
292     Dkeep without retraining from scratch, while freezing the other layers.
293
      • Gradient Ascent (GA) (Golatkar et al., 2020a; Graves et al., 2021; Jang et al., 2023): Gradient
294     ascent is arguably the most popular heuristic for machine unlearning. It seeks to remove the
295     influence of the forget set Dforget from the trained model by reversing the gradient updates associated
296     with Dforget . Notably, researchers have reported that gradient ascent can lead to significant model
297     utility degradation in some cases (Ilharco et al., 2023; Pawelczyk et al., 2024a).
298   • NegGrad+ (Kurmanji et al., 2024): NegGrad+ is a combination of gradient ascent on Dforget and
299     gradient descent on Dkeep . It finetunes the current model by optimizing the following loss function:
300
301                             β · Êx∼Dkeep [ℓ(M ; x)] − (1 − β)Êx∼Dforget [ℓ(M ; x)],
302
        where β ∈ (0, 1) is a hyperparameter and Ê is the empirical expectation. The intuition is to “review”
303
        the information from Dkeep in order to prevent the model degradation due to the gradient ascent.
304
305   • SCRUB (Kurmanji et al., 2024): SCalable Remembering and Unlearning unBound (SCRUB) is
306
        a state-of-the-art unlearning method that leveraging a student-teacher framework. It updates the
        model by optimizing the objective function:
307
308              Êx∼Dkeep [KL(Mlearn (x)∥M (x)) + ℓ(M ; x)] − Êx∼Dforget [KL(Mlearn (x)∥M (x))],
309
310
        where KL is the Kullback-Leibler divergence. SCRUB shares a similar intuition with NegGrad+,
        which can also be viewed as a combination of gradient ascent on Dforget and descent on Dkeep .
311
        Nevertheless, instead of directly employing the original loss ℓ, SCRUB leverages KL divergence to
312
        the original model Mlearn . It provides a different regularization compared to NegGrad+.
313
314   All the above unlearning methods focus on the design of unlearning algorithm U and are agnostic
315   to the learning algorithm A. This makes them compatible with any training pipeline. On the other
316   hand, there are unlearning solutions that design (A, U) jointly. For instance, Bourtoule et al. (2021)
317   propose a sharding-based learning-unlearning framework SISA, which achieves exact unlearning by
318   design. However, SISA requires training multiple models independently on partitions of the training
319   set, which not only deviates significantly from standard machine learning pipelines but also incurs
320
      substantial memory overhead.
321    Chien et al. (2024a;b) recently proposed Langevin Unlearning, which leverages noisy gradient
322   descent for machine unlearning. During the training process A, it replaces the common gradient
323   descent with DP-SGD (Abadi et al., 2016). For unlearning process U, it finetunes the model on
      Dkeep with DP-SGD as well. Chien et al. (2024a) establish a smooth theoretical connection between

      differential privacy and unlearning and show that Langevin Unlearning can provide a formal privacy
325   guarantee for non-convex problems. Unfortunately, they mentioned that the resulting privacy bound
326   is too loose to be applied in practice. We test Langevin Unlearning empirically in our experiments.

328   5.1 E NFORCING THE S AME C OMPUTATION B UDGET FOR U NLEARNING M ETHODS
329   We categorize all methods into three groups: those that only require the forget set (RL, GA), those
330   that only require the keep set (EUk, CFk, Langevin), and those that require both the forget and keep
331   sets (NegGrad+, SCRUB). Since machine unlearning is about the trade-off between privacy-utility-
332   efficiency (Guo et al., 2020; Chien et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024c), we carefully ensure a similar
333   computational complexity for all tested unlearning methods when demonstrating the privacy-utility
334
      trade-off. We define a Complexity Unit as the gradient computation budget of one training epoch
      on |Dforget | samples and limit all unlearning methods to a maximum of 10 Complexity Units. Since
335
      |Dforget | = U is roughly 1% of |Dtrain | throughout our experiments, all unlearning methods are indeed
336
      much more efficient than retraining from scratch under our setup.
337
338   For unlearning approaches leverage Dforget only, they can unlearn for at most 10 epochs. For those
339   leverage Dkeep only, we randomly subsample it to size U for each epoch and unlearn for at most 10
340
      epochs. For methods that leverage both Dforget and Dkeep simultaneously, we limit their maximum
      unlearning epoch to 5. The situation is slightly more complicated for EUk and CFk approaches since
341
      only the last k layers are trained to save computation. We randomly select U/r samples from the keep
342
      set in each epoch, where r is the ratio of trainable parameters in the last k layers compared to the total
343   number of parameters in the model. Our setup ensures that all tested unlearning approaches exhibit
344   a similar unlearning computational complexity for a fair comparison. We optimize the unlearning
345   epoch for each methods under the 10 Complexity Unit constraint by the following criterion: if the
346   perplexity of the unlearned model on Dtrain increases by more than 1 point compared to that of the
347   initial model (No Unlearn), we stop at the first epoch where this condition is met; otherwise, we use
348   the checkpoint from the last epoch.

350   6    E XPERIMENTS
351   Datasets. We conduct our LLM unlearning evaluation pipeline on two representative PII datasets:
352   Enron (Klimt & Yang, 2004) and ECHR (Chalkidis et al., 2019). The Enron dataset consists of
353   corporate emails by employees that were released to the public by the Federal Energy Regulatory
354   Commission. The ECHR dataset includes information about legal cases from the European Court of
355   Human Rights. In our experiments, we consider specific PIIs based on their distributions within each
356   dataset: for Enron, we consider phone numbers (Enron-Phone) and email domains (Enron-Email);
357   for ECHR, we consider the year of judgment (ECHR-Year). We define data minorities based on
358   these corresponding PIIs in each dataset. Detailed statistics for each dataset can be found in App. A.1.
359
      Note that our study focuses on instance-level unlearning, with each individual as a single record.
360   General Settings. We focus on the fine-tuning scenario, where the initial model M0 is a pretrained
361   LLM (GPT-2 (117M) (Radford et al., 2019) or Llama-2 7B (Touvron et al., 2023)). The fine-tuned
362   model Mlearn is obtained by training M0 on a dataset Dtrain for 5 epochs. In the GPT-2 experiments,
363   both the training and test sets contain 10,000 samples, subsampled from the full dataset. For Llama-2,
364   we employ efficient fine-tuning using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) with a rank of 16 and an alpha scaling
365
      factor of 32; both the training and test sets consist of 50,000 samples, subsampled from the entire
      dataset. In all cases, the forget set size is set to 100. The models are optimized using the AdamW
366
      optimizer with a constant learning rate of 10−5 and a batch size of 32, following the settings described
367
      in Shi et al. (2024b). During the unlearning process, all unlearning methods are constrained to the
368   same computational budget—not exceeding 10 complexity units—as detailed in Section 5.1. We
369   ensure that the unlearning complexity of each method is similar to allow for a fair comparison.
370   Our ultimate goal in machine unlearning is to achieve a superior privacy-utility-efficiency trade-off.
371   We utilize MIA to estimate the empirical privacy risk measured by the PL metric as described in
372   Section 3.1. For evaluating the utility of the LLMs, we report the perplexity following standard
373   practices in the literature (Radford et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022), where a lower perplexity indicates
374   that the model is more confident in its predictions. Additional details are provided in App. B.

      7    C ONCLUSIONS
528   In this paper, we highlight a major limitation in the typical evaluation pipeline for LLM unlearning
529   efficacy: privacy risks of minority groups in the training data are usually underestimated. We
530   support this assertion with carefully crafted experiments, incorporating unlearning canaries linked to
531   minority groups, drawing inspiration from privacy auditing research. By using personally identifiable
532   information (PII) as a proxy for minority identifiers, we show that minority groups experience at least
533   20% more privacy leakage in general. Since the right to be forgotten must apply to all individuals, we
534   call for more stringent evaluations of LLM unlearning techniques. We further benchmark existing
535   unlearning solutions with our minority-aware unlearning evaluation for LLMs, where the popular
536
      heuristic, gradient ascent, is found to be unstable and suffers from model utility degradation in some
      cases. Approaches such as SCRUB and Langevin Unlearning are found to be more robust.

814
      In this paper, we examine two representative PII datasets: Enron and ECHR, described as follows:
815
816   • Enron (Klimt & Yang, 2004). The Enron dataset consists of 536,000 authentic emails from 158
817     employees of the Enron Corporation, made publicly available by the Federal Energy Regulatory
818     Commission following an investigation. Each email typically includes the sending timestamp,
819     sender and recipient information, a greeting, the main content, and a footer containing the sender’s
820     personal details.
821
      • ECHR (Chalkidis et al., 2019). The ECHR dataset comprises case records from the European
822
        Court of Human Rights. Each record contains a series of factual lists that detail the specifics of a
823
        case. In our experiments, we further decompose these cases into individual facts, with each fact
824     forming a distinct sample, averaging around 80 tokens in length. In total, the dataset includes around
825     118,000 samples.

      A.3    P REPROCESSING AND DATASET S PLIT
850
851
      Dataset Preprocess. In our experiments, since the average token length of Enron samples is
852   approximately 770, we controlled the token length of each fact to ensure the model could effectively
853   memorize the samples. We randomly selected three coherent sentences from each sample, and if the
854   sample contained specific PIIs of interest, we prioritized selecting sentences around them. We will
855   keep the original samples for the ECHR dataset.
856
      Dataset Construction. We begin by searching the dataset for occurrences of specific PIIs and
857
      analyzing their distribution. To form the minority set used in our Minority setting, we select 100
858   samples containing the least frequent PIIs; this set serves as our forget set. In the Random setting,
859   we construct the forget set by randomly selecting 100 samples containing PIIs. To create the canary
860   set, we replace the PIIs in the forget set (Random setting) with the least frequent PII found in the
861   dataset. From the remaining data, we randomly select samples to create the training and test sets. For
862   experiments with GPT-2 (117M), we uniformly at random selected 10,000 samples each for both the
863   training and test sets. For Llama-2 7B, we uniformly at random selected 50,000 samples for both the
      training and test sets.

871   B.2    U NLEARNING E XPERIMENT S ETUP
872
873   Unlearning Algorithms. For all unlearning methods, we use a constant learning rate of 10−5 and a
874   batch size of 32, consistent with the fine-tuning stage. Note that some unlearning algorithms require
875   additional hyperparameters. We follow the common designs from previous literature (Pawelczyk
876   et al., 2024a) and detail the hyperparameter selection as follows:
877
      • EUK and CFK. In our experiments, we set the number of retrained layers to k = 3 for both
878
        GPT-2 and Llama-2 (LoRA) models. For GPT-2, the unfrozen trainable parameters account for
879     approximately 16% of the total parameters, while for Llama-2 7B, the unfrozen parameters account
880     for around 10%.
881
      • NegGrad+. As noted in the main text, the hyperparameter β balances samples between Dforget and
882
        Dkeep . In these experiments, we set β = 0.999.
883
884   • SCRUB. In the SCRUB method, three hyperparameters are used to balance the loss function on the
885     keep set and the KL regularizers on both the keep and forget sets. According to the definition of the
886
        objective function in Section 5, three terms are weighted sequentially by setting: α = 0.5, β = 1,
        and γ = 0.01.
887
888   • Langevin Unlearning. The Langevin Unlearning method leverages noisy gradient descent to
889     unlearn samples from the forget set. In our experiments, we set the Gaussian noise scale to
890
        σ = 5e − 4 (for GPT-2) and σ = 5e − 3 (for Llama-2), and the clipping norm to 1.
891
      Unlearning Epoch Selection. As outlined in Section 5.1, all unlearning methods are constrained
892
      to a maximum of 10 complexity units, and the optimal epoch for each method is selected based on
893   whether the perplexity of the unlearned model on Dtrain increases by more than 1 point. Under our
894   computational budget, methods that only require the forget set (RL, GA) are run for 10 epochs, while
895   methods requiring both the keep and forget sets (NegGrad+, SCRUB) are limited to 5 epochs, due
896   to the equal-sized cycling between the two sets. For methods that only require the keep set (EUK,
897   CFK, Langevin), we use 10 epochs, with varying sample sizes for EUK and CFK, as some model
898   parameters remain frozen. The selected epoch for each method in each experiment is detailed in
899   Table 6.
