011                                                             A BSTRACT
012
013                  Visual Language Models (VLMs) are vulnerable to adversarial attacks, especially
014                  those from adversarial images, which is however under-explored in literature. To
015
                     facilitate research on this critical safety problem, we first construct a new laRge-
                     scale Adervsarial images dataset with Diverse hArmful Responses (RADAR),
016
                     given that existing datasets are either small-scale or only contain limited types
017
                     of harmful responses. With the new RADAR dataset, we further develop a novel
018                  and effective iN-time Embedding-based AdveRSarial Image DEtection (NEAR-
019                  SIDE) method, which exploits a single vector that distilled from the hidden states
020                  of VLMs, which we call the attacking direction, to achieve the detection of ad-
021                  versarial images against benign ones in the input. Extensive experiments with
022                  two victim VLMs, LLaVA and MiniGPT-4, well demonstrate the effectiveness,
023                  efficiency, and cross-model transferrability of our proposed method. Our code is
024                  included in the supplementary file and will be made publicly available.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
028
      Vision Language Models (VLMs), such as BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a),
029
      MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024) and GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023), have attained remarkable success over
030   various vision-language tasks (Dai et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024). Besides improving performances,
031   ensuring the safety of responses is just as important in the development of VLMs. Compared with
032   classic Large Language Models (LLMs) that take in discrete textual inputs, VLMs that accept both
033   textual and visual inputs are more susceptible to “jailbreaking”, wherein malicious users manipu-
034   late inputs to elicit harmful outputs, due to the continuous and high-dimensional nature of visual
035   inputs (Qi et al., 2024a). This issue, which has posed persistent safety challenges in classical vision
036   models (Chakraborty et al., 2018), also presents intrinsic difficulties for developing safe VLMs.

      have been conducted to protect VLMs against adversarial images. These methods either seek to
055   detect adversarial images based on the responses’ discrepancy (Zhang et al., 2023b), or to purify
056   noised-images (Qi et al., 2024a) with diffusion models (Nie et al., 2022), achieving promising effec-
057   tiveness. However, the first approach is computation-intensive and time-consuming, as it requires
058   sampling multiple responses for the same input; the other approach, in addition to the computational
059   cost issue, may even suffer degraded performance when dealing with less perceptible noises.
060
      According to previous studies (Subramani et al., 2022; Turner et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023a; Rimsky
061   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b) (see Sec. 2.2), the behaviors of LLMs can be mod-
062   ulated to generate texts towards certain specific attributes, such as truthfulness, by exploiting a set
063   of steering vectors (SVs) that can be directly extracted from LLMs’ hidden states. In adversarial at-
064   tacks, the victim VLMs are manipulated by adversarial inputs to generate harmful responses, where
065   the VLMs’ behaviors change from harmlessness to harmfulness. We can calculate the SV that can
066   account for VLMs’ behavior change given the adversarial inputs, which is named the attacking di-
067   rection, and exploit it to detect the existence of adversarial samples by assessing whether the inputs’
068   embedding has high similarity to the attacking direction.
069   However, existing datasets for investigating adversarial attacks for VLMs, as shown in Tab. 1,
070   are small-scale and contain limited harm types, significantly restricting the thorough evaluation of
071   VLMs defending against adversarial attacks. Therefore, we construct RADAR, a dataset of laRge-
072   scale Adervsarial images with Diverse hArmful Responses, for comprehensively evaluating VLMs
073   against adversarial images. In RADAR, we generate adversarial images to attack two widely-used
074   VLMs, MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), based on a wide diversity of
075
      harmful contents. Each sample consists of an adversarial and a benign sample, with each containing
      a query, an adversarial/benign image and corresponding response of VLMs. In total, RADAR con-
076
      tains 4,000 samples, which is the most large-scale so far. For high sample quality, we apply filtering
077
      operations to ensure harmlessness and harmfulness of responses to benign and adversarial inputs
078   respectively. It will be released to the public to facilitate related research in the community.
079
080   With RADAR, we further propose a novel iN-time Embedding-based AdveRSarial Image DEtection
081
      (NEARSIDE) method, which leverages the attacking direction to detect adversarial images to defend
      VLMs. Specifically, we first extract the attacking direction from VLMs by calculating the average
082
      difference between the benign input and the adversarial input in the embedding space of VLMs.
083
      With the obtained attacking direction, we classify an input as an adversarial input if the projection
084   of its embedding to the attacking direction is larger than a threshold; otherwise the input is classified
085   as a benign input. Once the adversarial image is detected with the proposed NEARSIDE method,
086   further actions can be taken to protect the VLMs, such as overwriting outputs with a predefined
087   harmless response or purifying the adversarial images by diffusion models.
088
      We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate our NEARSIDE method on the new RADAR dataset.
089
      It is demonstrated that NEARSIDE achieves detection accuracy of 83.1% on LLaVA and 93.5% on
090   MiniGPT-4, indicating impressive effectiveness. Furthermore, we experimentally verify the cross-
091   model transferability of the attacking direction in our method. At inference, we compare the effi-
092   ciency between our method and the baseline method, showing that our method takes an average of
093   0.14 seconds to complete a detection on LLaVA that is 40 times faster than the best existing method.
094

112   Adversarial attack is maliciously manipulating inputs to compromise performance of the targeted
113   model (Chakraborty et al., 2021; Ponnuru et al., 2023). The manipulated inputs are referred to as
114   adversarial samples. Formally, adversarial samples are generated by minimizing the negative log-
115   likelihood loss of an adversarial target:

      2.2   S TEERING VECTORS IN LLM S
132
      According to the previous research (Subramani et al., 2022; Turner et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023a;
133
      Rimsky et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b), the behaviors of LLMs can be modulated to
134   generate texts towards certain specific attributes, such as truthfulness, by exploiting a set of steering
135   vectors (SVs) that can be directly extracted from LLMs’ hidden states. To extract the SV for a
136   certain behavior of LLMs, pairs of contrastive prompts (p+ , p− ) are used, where p+ , p− involve the
137   same question or request, but p+ adds words to encourage LLMs to possess the behavior while p−
138   represents the opposite. Formally, given a set D of (p+ , p− ), the SV is calculated by
139
                                         1 X
140                              SV =                  LLM(p+ ) − LLM(p− )                                 (2)

142   where LLM(p+ ), LLM(p− ) ∈ Rd are d-dimension vectors that represent LLMs’ embedding for
143   the ith prompt p+ and p− respectively. Through Eqn.(2) that takes the mean difference of the em-
144   beddings, the SV can be easily acquired, which can specify a tendency, or direction, in LLMs’
145   embedding space regarding the model behavior. That means, simply adding or subtracting such a
146   direction in LLMs’ activations can noticeably control LLMs’ behavior to generate text with certain
147   attributes. For example, given a direction of “truthfulness”, adding this direction can encourage
148   LLMs to generate more truthful responses (Zou et al., 2023a; Rimsky et al., 2024).

183   3.2      A DVERSARIAL IMAGE GENERATION
184
185   According to Eqn. (1) and Sec. 2.1, we optimize a continuous noise that is added to the benign
186   image to maximize the probability of the harmful text, in order to generate an adversarial image. The
187   optimization of noises is implemented using PGD (Madry et al., 2018). In particular, for samples
188   from HH-rlhf harm-set and Harm-Data, we optimize − log(p(yi |Iˆadv ) in Eqn. (1), where Iˆadv denotes
189   the noised adversarial image and the query, and yi denotes the harmful response. Note that when
190   optimizing − log(p(yi |Iˆadv ) on D-corpus, Iˆadv refers to only the noised adversarial image, and yi is
191   the harmful sentence. To generate the adversarial images, we use the open-sourced code 1 and leave
192
      implementation details and hyper-parameters to Appx. A.
193
194   3.3      S AMPLE GENERATION AND FILTERING
195
      We then use the benign and adversarial images obtained as aforementioned to generate the samples
196
      constituting the proposed RADAR dataset. In particular, we input each benign or counterpart adver-
197   sarial image plus a corresponding query to the victim VLM, i.e. MiniGPT-4 or LLaVA, respectively,
198   and obtain a response. For D-corpus, we utilize the harmful sentence as the query. The response
199   is then judged by two models to assess its safety. The first model is a classifier called HarmBench-
200   Llama-2-13b-cls2 (Mazeika et al., 2024), which is fine-tuned from the Llama2-13b (Touvron et al.,
201   2023) and classifies that whether a pair of (query, response) is harmful or not. The other model is
202   GPT-4o mini (OpenAI, 2024), which are guided with carefully designed prompts to make judge-
203   ments following (Qi et al., 2024b) and (Zeng et al., 2024). Concretely, we prompt GPT-4o mini
204   to provide a score ranging from 1 to 5 for each tuple of (query-response), where the scores of 1, 2
205
      indicate a harmless response, the score of 3 indicates borderline, and the scores of 4, 5 indicate a
      harmful response. Please refer to Appx. B for more details. It is expected that for each pair of benign
206
      and adversarial images, the responses given by the victim VLM should be judged as harmless for
207
      the benign input while harmful for the adversarial input by both models simultaneously. We take
208   this as the criterion to determine whether the quintuple of (query, benign input, harmless response,
209   adversarial input, harmful response) will be included in our RADAR.
210
211
      In practice, we find that quite a number of responses are harmful given benign images and harmless
      given adversarial images. As also reported in Qi et al. (2024a), the success of adversarial attack is far
212
      from 100%. When constructing our RADAR, we use the two models to judge the responses’ harm-
213
      fulness. Such filtering operations significantly lift the quality of samples in the proposed dataset.

229   3.4     S TATISTICS ANALYSIS
230
      With the above construction pipeline, the resultant RADAR contains 4,000 samples in total, attack-
231
      ing two victim VLMs, i.e. MiniGPT-4 and LLaVA. For each VLM, RADAR provides one training
232   set and three test sets, with 500 samples per set. Division of train and test sets is based on the source
233   of images and queries. Samples built using images from COCO validation set and queries from the
234   train set of HH-rlhf harm-set are grouped into the train set in RADAR; samples built using images
235   from COCO test set and queries from the test set of HH-rlhf harm-set, D-corpus, and Harm-Data
236   are grouped to three test sets, respectively. Training and tests sets use different images. Different
237   harmful texts are used in the four sets to ensure no information leakage and a reliable result.
238   A comparison of our RADAR with previous datasets used for investigating adversarial attack for
239   VLMs is provided in Tab. 1. Our RADAR features four advantages compared with previous ones.
240
241            • Large-scale: As shown in Tab. 1, RADAR greatly surpasses the existing datasets in scale.
242              It contains up to 4,000 samples while the previous largest dataset, i.e. from (Zhang et al.,
243              2023a), contains only 200 samples, facilitating a reliable evaluation of VLMs’ safety.
244            • Diversity of harmful types: RADAR covers a favorable diversity of harmful queries and
245              responses, enabling a comprehensive evaluation of VLMs’ performance on understanding
246              and defending various adversarial attacks. Recent research on safety of VLMs (Wang et al.,
247              2023; Dai et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2023a) provides taxonomies about the potential harms in
248              queries or responses, e.g. asking for guidance to make bombs or for providing private
249
                 information. During the construction of RADAR, we purposely increase such diversity.
250            • Open-source: RADAR will be open-sourced to facilitate future research on VLMs defend-
251              ing against adversarial attacks.
252            • High sample quality: We apply filtering operations during the construction of our RADAR
253              with two models to ensure that the response to a benign input is harmless and that to an
254              adversarial input is harmful. In comparison, the other datasets are built by specifying the
255              harmfulness of the input before feeding it to victim models, while neglecting the reliability
256              of responses, given the success ratio that adversarial images attack VLMs is not 100%.

      4.3   C ROSS - MODEL TRANSFERABILITY
325
326   The Platonic Representation Hypothesis (Huh et al., 2024): “Neural networks, trained with dif-
327   ferent objectives on different data and modalities, are converging to a shared statistical model of
328   reality in their representation spaces.”
329
      The proposed NEARSIDE is supposed to use the attacking direction extracted from one VLM to
330   detect the adversarial samples for the same VLM. According to the above Platonic Representation
331   Hypothesis, we can assume that the learnt attacking direction and effectiveness of our detection
332   method NEARSIDE are transferable across different models. That is, our NEARSIDE can use the
333   attacking direction extracted from one VLM to detect the adversarial samples for other VLMs. The
334   reason behind the assumption of the cross-model transferrability in our method is that, although
335   different VLMs are trained from different data, the patterns regarding safety in these data should be
336   similar. However, the embedding spaces between two VLMs do have a gap. We thus propose to

363
364   We conduct extensive experiments on RADAR to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed NEAR-
365   SIDE in detecting adversarial images. We first compare our method with strong baseline and then
366   analyze its cross-model transferability, followed by the efficiency test.

368   5.1   E XPERIMENTS SETUP
369
370   Victim VLMs. We adopt MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a) as the
371   victim VLMs. MiniGPT-4 is built upon Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) and LLaVA is built upon
372   Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023). Regarding the visual encoder, MiniGPT-4 utilizes the same pre-
373   trained vision components of BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a) consisting of pre-trained ViT followed by a
374
      Q-Former, while LLaVA only adopts a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021).
375   Implementation. For each victim VLM, as stated in Sec. 3, RADAR constructs one training set
376   and three test sets. NEARSIDE learns the attacking direction and threshold from the hidden states
377   of the VLM on the training set. Here, the hidden states refer to the embedding of the last token
      of the input from the LLM decoder’s final layer. Then, we test the detection performance with the

      obtained attacking direction on the three test sets. Regarding the cross-model transferability, we
379   collect 5,000 pairs of benign images and queries to train the PCA model for each VLM. We set 2048
380   as the dimension of the embedding after PCA.
381
382
      Baseline. We use JailGuard (Zhang et al., 2023a) as our baseline, which is the state-of-the-art
      model for this task. To detect adversarial visual inputs, JailGuard mutates input images to generate
383
      variants and calculates the discrepancy of VLMs’ outputs given different variants to distinguish the
384
      adversarial and benign inputs. There are 18 mutation methods, and we use the best-performing

      Evaluation metrics. Since adversarial detection is a binary classification task, we adopt Accuracy,

      P recision, Recall and F 1 score as the evaluation metrics.

      6     R ELATED WORKS
500
501   6.1   V ISION LANGUAGE MODELS
502
      Vision Language Models (VLMs) is equipped with a visual adapter to align the visual and textual
503
      representations in LLMs. Notable examples are BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a),
504
      MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024), QWen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023), and Gemini
505   (Anil et al., 2023), demonstrating impressive performance across various vision-language tasks (Dai
506   et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024). These VLMs vary in the design of their adapters (Liu et al., 2023a; Li
507   et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024). For instance, BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a) proposes Q-Former to align
508   vision features with LLMs; MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a) further add
509   a linear transformation, and Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023) uses a single-layer cross-attention module.
510
511   6.2   S AFETY OF LANGUAGE MODELS
512
513   Safe LLMs should behave in line with human intentions and values (Soares & Fallenstein, 2014;
514   Hendrycks et al., 2021; Leike et al., 2018; Ji et al., 2023b) which are measured as being Helpful,
515   Honest, and Harmless (Askell et al., 2021). Alignment has emerged as a nascent research field
516   aiming to align LLMs’ behaviors with human preferences, and there are two widely adopted align-
517
      ment techniques, i.e. Instruction Fine-tuning and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
      (RLHF). In instruction fine-tuning, LLMs are given examples of (user’s query, desired output) and
518
      trained to follow user instructions and respond the expected output (Taori et al., 2023; Wei et al.,
519
      2022). In RLHF, LLMs update output probabilities, i.e., the response policy, by reinforcement learn-
520   ing, which are rewarded for generating responses that align with human preferences and otherwises
521   penalized (Russell & Norvig, 2016; Bai et al., 2022a; Rafailov et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022).
522
523
      Two types of strategies can defend language models from adversarial attacks: detection and purifi-
      cation. For instance, Zhang et al. (2023b) detects adversarial examples by calculating responses’
524
      discrepancy; Qi et al. (2024a) uses diffusion models (Nie et al., 2022) to purify the noised-images.
525
      Other techniques such as the adversarial training (Bai et al., 2021) can also improve the robustness of
526   models to adversarial attacks. Though effective on classical image classifiers, these methods remain
527   unexplored on large models like LLMs and VLMs and may disturb the optimization.
528
529
530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this work, we propose RADAR, the first large-scale adversarial image dataset with diverse harm-
533   ful responses to facilitate research on safety of VLMs. With RADAR, we further develop NEAR-
534   SIDE that exploits the idea of attacking direction to detect adversarial inputs. We demonstrate with
535   the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed NEARSIDE by comparing it to the state-of-the-art
536
      on RADAR, and also highlight its cross-model transferability.
537   VLMs can generate open-ended responses, posing a persistent challenge to complete evaluation of
538   the potential harms (Ganguli et al., 2022). RADAR is built from a diverse array of datasets but may
539   fall short of covering all harmful contents. NEARSIDE is intended to detect the adversarial samples
      we examine in this work and is a demonstration of our idea of exploiting the attacking direction.
