009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Inspired by recent success of LLMs, the field of human motion understanding has
012            increasingly shifted towards the development of large motion models. Despite
013            some progress, current works remain far from achieving truly generalist mod-
014            els, largely due to the lack of large-scale, high-quality motion data. To address
015            this, we present MotionBase, the first million-level motion generation benchmark,
016            offering 15 times the data volume of the previous largest dataset, and featuring
017            multimodal data with hierarchically detailed text descriptions. By leveraging this
018
               vast dataset, our large motion model demonstrates strong performance across a
               broad range of motions, including unseen ones. Through systematic investiga-
019
               tion, we underscore the importance of scaling both data and model size, with
020
               synthetic data and pseudo labels playing a crucial role in mitigating data acqui-
021            sition costs. Moreover, our research reveals the limitations of existing evalua-
022            tion metrics, particularly in handling out-of-domain text instructions — an is-
023            sue that has long been overlooked. In addition, we introduce a 2D lookup-free
024            approach for motion tokenization, which preserves motion information and ex-
025            pands codebook capacity, further enhancing the representative ability of large
026            motion models. The release of MotionBase and the insights gained from this
027            study are expected to pave the way for the development of more powerful and
028            versatile motion generation models. Our code and database will be released at
029
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/MotionBase.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Motion generation is an emerging field with diverse applications in video games, filmmaking, and
034
      robotics animation. At the forefront of this area is text-to-motion generation (T2M) (Ahn et al.,
035
      2018; Ahuja & Morency, 2019), which plays a crucial role in translating natural language into human
036   motions. State-of-the-art T2M models typically rely on a combination of the motion quantization
037   methods (e.g., VQ (Van Den Oord et al., 2017)), along with a text encoder (e.g., CLIP (Radford
038   et al., 2021)) and decoder (e.g., GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019)) to generate motion sequences from
039   detailed textual instructions. Despite the availability of a few high-quality datasets (Guo et al.,
040   2022a; Lin et al., 2024) curated in recent years, their limited size restricts current methods to a
041   narrow range of scenarios, creating performance bottlenecks when addressing diverse or unseen
042   motions, as illustrated in Figure 1 (RIGHT).
043   The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023a) in multimodal
044   learning has been significantly bolstered by the availability of vast data resources (Zheng et al.,
045   2024; Xu et al., 2024). In contrast, the volume of motion data remains considerably smaller than that
046   of visual-text data, as illustrated in Figure 1 (LEFT). This disparity primarily arises from the high
047   costs associated with motion data collection, which often requires specialized wearable devices and
048   substantial human labor for annotation. Consequently, developing a state-of-the-art (SoTA) large
049   motion model based on LLMs presents a significant challenge and remains an unresolved issue.
050
      While some recent efforts (Jiang et al., 2023) have explored this direction, the effectiveness of large
      motion models has yet to be fully demonstrated.
051
052   In this paper, we aim to address the question: “Can a large motion model be a promising direction
053   for motion generation?” To tackle this, we have developed a systematic data collection scheme that
      led to the creation of MotionBase, the first large-scale dataset containing over one million motion

      Building on the solid foundation of MotionBase, we can now conduct a comprehensive investiga-
077
      tion into the effectiveness of large motion models. This research aims to firstly identify key factors
078
      driving their advancement and offer valuable insights for future model design, including: ❶ scal-
079   ing both data and model size significantly reduces joint prediction errors on critical metrics while
080   improving generalization to novel motions. ❷ Despite observable domain gaps, synthetic and static
081   data, as well as pseudo motion labels are becoming increasingly essential and effective, especially
082   given the high cost of acquiring ground truth motion data. ❸ Existing metrics show limitations when
083   faced with out-of-domain text instructions. Notably, the widely used metric, FID, fails to accurately
084   capture the alignment between ground truth and generated motions. Our findings highlight the need
085   for a more robust and equitable evaluation framework that enhances open-set generalization.
086   In addition to these factors, we argue that large motion models are further constrained by inad-
087   equate motion representation. Most approaches rely on transforming motion into discrete tokens
088   via vector quantization (VQ), which are then processed by autoregressive models to generate mo-
089   tion sequences. While these methods have produced impressive results, they suffer from two major
090   drawbacks. ❶ Information loss: The current VQ process inevitably leads to the loss of critical
091   information. Given a motion clip with D-dimensional features M = {m1 , m2 , ..., mT }, where
092   mi ∈ RD , VQ compresses it into a list of 1D embeddings of size ⌊T /α⌋ × d, where α is the tempo-
093
      ral downsampling ratio and d is the codebook dimension. Unlike images, which consist of uniform
      RGB pixel values, each motion state mi contains a set of distinct features (e.g., joint position, ve-
094
      locity, foot-ground contact). Using a single 1D embedding to represent such complex motion states
095
      is insufficient. This not only results in the loss of vital information but also limits the model’s ability
096   to flexibly generate motion at a part-level. ❷ Limited Codebook Size: Existing VQ are limited
097   by a small codebook, meaning that all possible human motions must be selected from these limited
098   options. Consequently, these 1D embeddings fail to capture the vast diversity of human motion.
099
      To address this issue, we propose treating a motion clip as a 2D image with a single channel, rep-
100
      resented as M ∈ RT ×D×1 . By expanding the dimensionality of the motion clip from 1D to 2D,
101
      we enhance the encoder’s capacity, improving its ability to represent complex motions while retain-
102   ing more critical information after tokenization. Although increasing the size of the codebook is a
103   straightforward way to enhance its expressiveness, this approach often leads to “codebook collapse,"
104   particularly when training samples are scarce. To mitigate this, we introduce a finite scalar quan-
105   tizing method inspired by Mentzer et al. (2023), which enables learning a large motion vocabulary
106   without requiring a lookup for corresponding tokens in the codebook for each entry. As a result,
107   we expand the motion codebook by at least two orders of magnitude, boosting its representational
      capacity while maintaining efficiency.

      We summarize our main contributions as follows. (1) MotionBase: We introduce MotionBase, the
109   first large-scale motion generation benchmark containing over one million motions with detailed
110   textual descriptions, significantly advancing the capability to effectively train motion generation
111   models. (2) Key Insights: Our research identifies critical factors affecting the effectiveness of large
112   motion models, emphasizing the importance of scaling both data and model size. Additionally, we
113   uncover limitations in the current evaluation metrics, particularly when handling diverse and unseen
114   motions. (3) Novel Motion Quantization: We propose a novel motion quantization approach that
115   represents motion clips as 2D images and constructs a finite-scale codebook without requiring token
116   lookups. This method retains essential information and expands the capacity of the motion encoder,
117
      enhancing the ability of large motion models to leverage large-scale motion data.

121   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS AND M ULTI -M ODALITY
122
123   Substantial advancements have been made in enhancing LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Raffel et al.,
124   2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022) with the ability to understand and respond to human instructions,
125   through a technique known as instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022). Recent research has extended
126
      these capabilities to the multimodal domain (Ye et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023), with notable work
      by Liu et al. (2023), who pioneered visual instruction tuning to create a highly adaptable visual
127
      assistant. Additionally, Li et al. (2023a) integrated multimodal context directly into instruction
128
      data to further enhance model performance. Subsequent studies (Zhang et al., 2023b; Zhao et al.,
129   2023) expanded this research by scaling up instructional datasets and incorporating image-rich text.
130   Notably, Dai et al. (2023) developed InstructBLIP, based on BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023b), which features
131   an advanced visual feature extraction mechanism to improve performance across vision-language
132   tasks. Despite these breakthroughs, the application of multimodal models to human motion remains
133   less competitive compared to current state-of-the-art (SoTA) methods, although recent initiatives are
134   beginning to explore this domain (Jiang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b).
135
136   2.2   V ECTOR Q UANTIZATION
137
138   Vector quantization (VQ) has been highly successful in generating high-quality images (Van
139
      Den Oord et al., 2017) and videos (Gupta et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2021). VQ-VAE first converts
      images into discrete representations and autoregressively models their distribution. Building on
140
      this, Lee et al. (2022) introduced residual quantization (RQ), which encodes images into a stacked
141
      map of discrete codes, efficiently reducing the spatial resolution of features. You et al. (2022) further
142   developed hierarchical vector quantization (HQ), employing a pyramid scheme with two-level codes
143   for image encoding. Most existing motion generation approaches have adopted VQ or its variants to
144   quantize human motions. However, the small codebook size in traditional VQ methods limits their
145   ability to generalize and accurately represent the diversity of human motions. Although increas-
146   ing the codebook size can improve representational capacity, it often leads to codebook collapse.
147   Recently, Mentzer et al. (2023) demonstrated that discrete codes can be obtained via scalar quanti-
148   zation, where each scalar entry is independently quantized to the nearest integer through rounding.
149   Similarly, Yu et al. (2023) introduced a lookup-free codebook that maps videos into compact discrete
150
      tokens, utilizing all codes without auxiliary losses and expanding the codebook size.
151

      tion. Recently, Guo et al. (2024) proposed integrating residual quantization and masked modeling

188   3   M OTION BASE DATASET
189
190   Data is the foundation of large motion models. With advancements in fields like human pose detec-
191   tion, we are now able to extract high-quality motion sequences from vast amounts of online videos,
192   including datasets like InternViD (Wang et al., 2023) and WebVid (Bain et al., 2021). In its initial
193   public release, our MotionBase contains over one million motion clips, each annotated with fine-
194
      grained automatic pseudo labels. A comparison with existing benchmarks is presented in Table 1.
      Our data collection pipeline involves the following key steps in order.
195
196   ❶ Source Video Collection and Cleaning: We begin by collecting over 20 million videos from
197   publicly available datasets and online platforms such as YouTube. To ensure quality and relevance,
198   we filter out videos that do not contain human figures.
199   ❷ 2D-3D Keypoint Estimation: Keypoints are essential for capturing the skeletal structure of
200   human motion. Initially, we estimate whole-body 2D keypoints with confidence scores using a
201   pretrained model (Xu et al., 2022). To further enhance motion accuracy, we estimate precise 3D
202   keypoints with another pretrained model (Sárándi et al., 2023) trained on large 3D datasets, Fol-
203   lowing the method of Lin et al. (2024), we apply temporal smoothing and enforce 3D bone length
204   constraints during triangulation, improving the stability and consistency of the keypoint estimations.
205   ❸ Incorporating Additional Modalities: A comprehensive understanding of human motion ben-
206   efits from the inclusion of diverse modalities such as RGB and depth data. To enrich MotionBase,
207   we provide annotations for these additional modalities. Furthermore, MotionBase includes videos
208   featuring multi-person scenarios, with each motion sequence grounded in its corresponding video
209   through object-level bounding boxes. Although this paper primarily focuses on the text-to-motion
210   task, these additional modalities open avenues for future research in other areas.
211   ❹ Local-Global Pose Estimation: We begin by registering the body model SMPL-X (Pavlakos
212   et al., 2019) for each frame in MotionBase, which leverages keypoints based on progressive learning-
213   based mesh fitting method (Lin et al., 2024). Specifically, we predict SMPL-X parameters using a
214   pretrained body mesh recovery method, OSX (Lin et al., 2023), followed by iterative optimization
215   to fit the parameters to the target 2D and 3D joint positions. After fitting, we apply global motion
      optimization based on Yuan et al. (2022) to refine both global motions and camera poses simulta-

284   4.2   2D L OOKUP - FREE M OTION Q UANTIZATION
285
      Similar to visual tokenization, motion tokenization is a process that compresses motion signals into
286
      a series of discrete tokens, typically involving an encoder E, a decoder D and a codebook C. We
287   propose a 2D lookup-free quantization method as a key component for building large motion models.
288
289   2D Motion Quantization. Traditional motion quantizers use 1D embeddings to represent motion
290
      at each timestamp, which inevitably results in the loss of crucial information. Furthermore, this
      approach limits the quantizer’s ability to generate and interpret part-level motions. To address these
291
      limitations, we treat the motion sequence M = {m1 , m2 , ..., mT } as a single-channel image, rep-
292
      resenting each motin sequence as M ∈ RT ×D×1 . Each motion embedding mi is divided into P
293   components, capturing distinct features of motion, such as root orientation, joint rotation and foot
294   contact. Our motion encoder then converts M into a feature map E(M) ∈ R⌊T /α⌋×P ×d , where α
295   denotes the temporal downsampling ratio. This approach ensures that each body part is tokenized
296   separately, allowing for more granular, part-level motion encoding and decoding.
297
      Lookup-Free Quantization. Traditional motion quantizers are often constrained by small code-
298
      book sizes, restricting their ability to capture the full diversity of human motion. A common ap-
299
      proach is to expand the motion vocabulary. However, excessively enlarging the codebook can result
300   in “codebook collapse”, where only a small subset of tokens in the codebook is used, offering min-
301   imal performance improvements. In some cases, an overly large vocabulary can even degrade the
302   model’s overall performance. To address this, a more effective way is to reduce the dimensionality
303   of code embeddings (Mentzer et al., 2023), which limits the representational capacity of individual
304   tokens and encourages more efficient learning across a larger vocabulary. Similar to Yu et al. (2023),
305   we reduce the embedding dimension of the codebook to zero by replacing the codebook C ∈ RK×d
306   with an integer set C with |C| = K. Specifically, C is the Cartesian product of single-dimensional
307   variables C =×di=1 Ci , where Ci = {−1, 1} and d is equal to log2 K. Given a feature vector
308   z ∈ Rd , our quantizer Q(·) converts each dimension of the quantized representation into:

      Evaluation Metrics. For the motion generation task, we employ the following metrics in our
330
      experiments following Guo et al. (2022a). (1) Frechet Inception Distance (FID): This metric assesses
331   overall motion quality by measuring the distributional difference between the high-level features of
332   generated motions and real motions. (2) Motion-retrieval Precision (R-Precision) and Multimodal
333   Distance (MMDist): These metrics evaluate the semantic alignment between the textual input and
334   generated motions. R-Precision measures the top-1/2/3 retrieval accuracy, while MMDist computes
335   the distance between matched text and motion pairs. Additionally, we validate our motion tokenizer
336   by conducting experiments on the motion reconstruction task. This is measured using both Mean
337   Per Joint Position Error (MPJPE) and FID. MPJPE quantifies the average distance (in millimeters)
338   between the predicted joint positions and the ground truth positions across all joints in the skeleton.
339   Implementation Details. For the motion tokenizer, we implement a VQ codebook C ∈ R1024×512
340   with an embedding dimensionality of d = 512, and the resulting discrete codes are incorporated as
341   additional vocabulary for the LLM. In comparison, our lookup-free codebook has a size of 216 =
342   16384, where the least frequently used tokens from the LLM’s codebook are mapped to represent
343   motion codes. The motion encoder E operates with a temporal downsampling rate of α = 4. We
344   experiment with four LLM architectures to build our large motion model: GPT2-medium (Radford
345
      et al., 2019), Llama-2-7b, Llama-2-13b (Touvron et al., 2023b), and Llama3.1-8b (Dubey et al.,
      2024). The motion tokenizer is trained with a learning rate of 1e-4 and a batch size of 256 over
346
      300K iterations. For training the large motion model, full parameter tuning is performed on 8×A800

      GPUs, with a batch size of 1024, over 300 epochs. The learning rate is set to 2e-4 for GPT2-medium

492   5.4    L IMITATION OF AUTOMATED M ETRIC
493
494   As mentioned earlier, the FID scores in Table 2
495   and Table 5 yield unexpected results. Specifically,
496   when evaluating on Motion-X and UNSEEN-90K,
497
      FID achieves its best performance when trained
      on Motion-X, significantly outperforming both the
498
      smaller HumanML3D and the larger-scale Motion-
499
      Base. In this section, we aim to investigate this
500   anomaly. FID, a standard metric widely used for
501   generation tasks, is typically measured by a pre-
502   trained evaluator. In traditional image generation,
503   FID is calculated using a well-trained, robust visual
504   encoder like InceptionNet (Szegedy et al., 2015),
505   which is trained on millions of images. However, the
506   evaluator currently used to compute FID for motion Figure 5: Comparison of codebook utiliza-
507   generation is a simple motion autoencoder with a tion for different motion quantization.
508
      very small parameter scale (Guo et al., 2022a). Since
      this motion autoencoder is trained on limited data consisting of only 20K motions, we argue that it
509
      may lack the generalization needed for robust performance, leading to difficulties in reliably cap-
510
      turing the complex semantic alignment between text and motion.Similar unexpected results occur
511   in motion reconstruction as well. As show in Table 6, the FID score on HumanML3D is two or-
512   ders of magnitude higher when comparing 2D-LFQ and VQ-VAE, despite the former achieving a
513   much lower MPJPE. When tested on MotionBase, 2D-LFQ obtains the highest FID score even while
514   achieving the best MPJPE. We observe the same issue with other metrics like MMDist, as discussed
515   in Appendix C.1. Notably, Voas et al. (2023) have mentioned that existing metrics are sensitive to
516   the quality of the embedding space and do not always align with human perception. These findings
517   highlight the need for a more robust and fair metric for large motion models moving forward.

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we explore how to advance the field of large-scale motion generation. To this end, we
533
      introduce a large-scale motion dataset named MotionBase, which includes detailed text descriptions
534   and rich modality annotations, providing a strong foundation for effectively training large motion
535   models. Our research highlights key findings, such as the impact of scaling both data and model size.
536   Additionally, we identify potential limitations in the current evaluation metrics, particularly when
537   assessing diverse and unseen motions. To enhances the benefits large motion models can derive from
538   extensive motion data, we propose a novel motion quantization approach that treats motion clips as
539   2D images and constructs a finite-scale codebook, eliminating the need for token lookups. We hope
      that this research offers valuable direction for future work in large-scale motion generation.

911
912   Due to space limitations in the main paper, we provide the overview of our model architecture in
913   Figure 11 in this appendix. Following most LMMs, our large motion model consists of two stages:
914   pre-training and fine-tuning. During the pre-training stage, we train a motion encoder, a motion
915   decoder, and a motion codebook to represent motions using discrete tokens. With this motion to-
916   kenizer, we fine-tune an autoregressive language model to predict motion tokens. In the inference
917   stage, the input text is processed by the language model to generate motion tokens in an autoregres-
      sive manner, which are then decoded into natural motion by the pre-trained motion decoder.

       D     DATASET C ONSTRUCTION P IPELINE
1243
1244
1245
       Our data collection pipeline is a multi-stage process designed to curate a large-scale, high-quality,
       and richly annotated multimodal motion dataset. The detailed steps are outlined below:
1246
1247   Video Data Collection and Cleaning: We amass over 20 million videos from publicly available
1248   datasets like InternVid and WebVid, as well as online platforms such as YouTube. To maintain data
1249   relevance and quality, we employ a pretrained human detection model to filter out videos lacking
1250   human presence.
1251   2D and 3D Keypoint Estimation: We estimate 2D human keypoints and their corresponding con-
1252   fidence scores using the pretrained VitPose model (Xu et al., 2022). To further refine motion in-
1253   formation, we leverage a pretrained 3D keypoint estimation model (Sárándi et al., 2023) trained on
1254   extensive 3D datasets. Following the methodology of Lin et al. (2024), we apply temporal smooth-
1255   ing and 3D bone length constraints during triangulation to enhance the stability and consistency of
1256   the keypoint estimations.
1257   Multimodal Information Integration: For a more comprehensive understanding of human motion,
1258   MotionBase incorporates RGB, depth data, and annotations for multi-person scenarios. In multi-
1259   person sequences, each motion is grounded to its respective video via object-level bounding boxes.
1260   While this work primarily focuses on text-to-motion tasks, these additional modalities pave the way
1261   for future research in related areas.
1262   Local-Global Pose Estimation: We fit the SMPL-X body model (Pavlakos et al., 2019) to each
1263   frame in MotionBase using a progressive learning-based mesh fitting approach (Lin et al., 2024).
1264   Specifically, we predict SMPL-X parameters using the pretrained OSX method (Lin et al., 2023),
1265   followed by iterative optimization to align the parameters with the target 2D and 3D joint positions.
1266   Subsequently, we apply a global motion optimization technique based on Yuan et al. (2022) to refine
1267   both global motions and camera poses, ensuring consistency with the video evidence. Finally, for
1268   motion sequences with noisy or occluded input data, we employ RoHM (Zhang et al., 2024a) to
1269
       reconstruct complete and plausible motions.
1270   Single-Frame Pose Expansion: To enhance dataset diversity and scale, we expand single-frame
1271   pose data into multi-frame sequences. We achieve this using the PHC (Luo et al., 2023) strategy
1272   and the pre-trained motion completion model MotionGPT (Jiang et al., 2023). The PHC strategy
1273   ensures the physical plausibility of the generated motion sequences, while MotionGPT provides
1274   motion priors to enhance naturalness and fluidity.
1275   Hierarchical Motion Descriptions: MotionBase features hierarchical text annotations to address
1276   limitations in existing dataset descriptions. Leveraging the Gemini-1.5-pro large language model
1277   (Reid et al., 2024) and a carefully crafted prompt format, we generate detailed descriptions for
1278   individual body parts (e.g., left arm, right leg), dedicating a sentence to each. Furthermore, we sum-
1279   marize the overall body movement with 1-3 sentences, providing a more holistic motion description.

       To ensure dataset quality, we conduct multifaceted evaluations of the motion data.
1287   Refinement using a Reinforcement Learning-based Strategy: We use PHC to train a reinforce-
1288   ment learning-based policy model that refines the raw motion data, ensuring conformity to physical
1289   laws and enhancing realism. This policy takes raw motion sequences as input, treats them as target
1290   poses, and generates new motion sequences satisfying physical laws in a simulated environment,
1291   thereby eliminating issues such as jitter and foot sliding. While this strategy may encounter chal-
1292
       lenges with drastic movements, it effectively improves data quality for most motion sequences.
1293   Data Diversity: A key advantage of the MotionBase dataset is its scale and diversity. We collect
1294   over one million motion sequences from multiple sources (including InternVid and internet videos),
1295   encompassing a wide range of motion types. This diversity supports the training of more generaliz-
       able motion models.
