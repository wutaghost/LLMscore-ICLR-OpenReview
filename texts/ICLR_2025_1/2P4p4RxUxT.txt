009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We develop confidence sets which provide spatial uncertainty guarantees for the
012            output of a black-box machine learning model designed for image segmentation.
013            To do so we adapt conformal inference to the imaging setting, obtaining thresh-
014            olds on a calibration dataset based on the distribution of the maximum of the
015            transformed logit scores within and outside of the ground truth masks. We prove
016            that these confidence sets, when applied to new predictions of the model, are guar-
017            anteed to contain the true unknown segmented mask with desired probability. We
018
               show that learning appropriate score transformations on an independent learning
               dataset before performing calibration is crucial for optimizing performance. We il-
019
               lustrate and validate our approach on polyps colonscopy, brain imaging and teeth
020
               datasets. To do so we obtain the logit scores from deep neural networks trained for
021            polyps, brain mask and tooth segmentation segmentation. We show that using dis-
022            tance and other transformations of the logit scores allows us to provide tight inner
023            and outer confidence sets for the true masks whilst controlling the false coverage
024            rate.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep neural networks promise to significantly enhance a wide range of important tasks in biomed-
030   ical imaging. However these models, as typically used, lack formal uncertainty guarantees on their
031   output which can lead to overconfident predictions and critical errors (Guo et al., 2017; Gupta et al.,
032   2020). Misclassifications or inaccurate segmentations can lead to serious consequences, includ-
033
      ing misdiagnosis, inappropriate treatment decisions, or missed opportunities for early intervention
      (Topol, 2019). Without uncertainty quantification, medical professionals cannot rely on deep learn-
034
      ing models to provide accurate information and predictions which can limit their use in practical
035
      applications (Jungo et al., 2020).
036
037   In order to address this problem, conformal inference, a robust framework for uncertainty quan-
038   tification, has become increasingly used as a means of providing prediction guarantees, offering
039
      reliable, distribution-free confidence sets for the output of neural networks which have finite sample
      validity. This approach, originally introduced in Papadopoulos et al. (2002); Vovk et al. (2005),
040
      has become increasingly popular due to its ability to provide rigorous statistical guarantees without
041
      making strong assumptions about the underlying data distribution or model architecture. Conformal
042   prediction methods, in their most commonly used form - split conformal inference - work by cali-
043   brating the predictions of the model on a held-out dataset in order to provide sets which contain the
044   output with a given probability, see Shafer & Vovk (2008) and Angelopoulos & Bates (2021) for
045   good introductions.
046
      In the context of image segmentation, we have a decision to make at each pixel/voxel of an im-
047
      age which can lead to a large multiple testing problem. Traditional conformal methods, typically
048   designed for scalar outputs, require adaptation to handle multiple tests and their inherent spatial
049   dependencies. To do so Angelopoulos et al. (2021) applied conformal inference pixelwise and per-
050   formed multiple testing correction on the resulting p-values, however this approach does not account
051   for the complex dependence structure inherent in the images. To take advantage of this structure, in
052   an approach analogous to the False discovery rate (FDR) control of (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995),
053   Bates et al. (2021) and Angelopoulos et al. (2024) sought to control the expected risk of a given
      loss function over the image and used a conformal approach to produce outer confidence sets for

054
      segmented images which control the expected proportion of false negatives. Other work considering
055   conformal inference in the context of multiple dependent hypotheses includes Marandon (2024) and
056   Blanchard et al. (2024) who established conformal FDR control when testing for the presence of
057   missing links in graphs.
058
      In this work we argue that bounding the segmented outcome with guarantees in probability rather
059
      than on the proportion of discoveries is more informative, avoiding errors at the borders of potential
060
      growths/tumors. This is analogous to the tradeoff between familywise error rate (FWER) and FDR
061   control in the multiple testing literature in which there is a balance between power and coverage
062   rate, (a correspondence which we formalize in Section A.10). The distinction is that in medical im-
063   age segmentation making mistakes can have potentially serious consequences Under-segmentation
064   might cause part of the true mask to be missed, potentially leading to inadequate treatment (Jalalifar
065   et al., 2022). Over-segmentation, on the other hand, could result in unnecessary interventions, in-
066   creasing patient risk and healthcare costs (Gupta et al., 2020; Patz et al., 2014). Confidence sets are
067   instead guaranteed to contain the outcome with a given level of certainty. Since the guarantees are
068   more meaningful the problem is more difficult and existing work on conformal uncertainty quan-
069
      tification for images has thus often focused on producing sets with guarantees on the proportions
      of discoveries or pixel level inference rather than coverage (Bates et al. (2021), Wieslander et al.
070
      (2020), Mossina et al. (2024)) which is a stricter error criterion.
071
072   In order to obtain confidence sets we use a split-conformal inference approach in which we learn
073   appropriate cutoffs, with which to threshold the output of an image segmenter, from a calibration
074   dataset. These thresholds are obtained by considering the distribution of the maximum logit (trans-
075
      formed) scores provided by the model within and outside of the ground truth masks. This approach
      allows us to capture the spatial nature of the uncertainty in segmentation tasks, going beyond simple
076
      pixel-wise confidence measures. By applying these learned thresholds to new predictions, we can
077
      generate inner and outer confidence sets that are guaranteed to contain the true, unknown segmented
078   mask with a desired probability. As we shall see, naively using the original logit scores to do so can
079   lead to rather large and uninformative outer confidence sets but these can be greatly improved using
080   distance transformations.

086   Let V ⊂ Rm , for some dimension m ∈ N, be a finite set corresponding to the domain which
087   represents the pixels/voxels/points at which we observe imaging data. Let X = {g : V → R} be
088   the set of real functions on V and let Y = {g : V → {0, 1}} be the set of all functions on V taking
089   the values 0 or 1. We shall refer to elements of X and Y as images. Suppose that we observe a
090   calibration dataset (Xi , Yi )ni=1 of random images, where Xi : V → R represents the ith observed
091   calibration image and Yi : V → {0, 1} outputs labels at each v ∈ V giving 1s at the true location
092   of the objects in the image Xi that we wish to identify and 0s elsewhere. Let P(V) be the set of all
093
      subsets of V. Given a function f : X → X , we shall write f (X, v) to denote f (X)(v) for all v ∈ V.
094   Let s : X → X be a score function - trained on an independent dataset - such that given an image
095   pair (X, Y ) ∈ X × Y, s(X) is a score image in which s(X, v) is intended to be higher at the v ∈ V
096   for which Y (v) = 1. The score function can for instance be the logit scores obtained from applying
097   a deep neural network image segmentation method to the image X. Given X ∈ X , let M̂ (X) ∈ Y
098   be the predicted mask given by the model which is assumed to be obtained using the scores s(X).
099   In what follows we will use the calibration dataset to construct confidence functions I, O : X →
100   P(V) such that for a new image pair (X, Y ), given error rates α1 , α2 ∈ (0, 1) we have
101
                                  P (I(X) ⊆ {v ∈ V : Y (v) = 1}) ≥ 1 − α1 ,                              (1)
102
103                              and P ({v ∈ V : Y (v) = 1} ⊆ O(X)) ≥ 1 − α2 .                          (2)
104   Here I(X) and O(X) serve as inner and outer confidence sets for the location of the true segmented
105   mask. Their interpretation is that, up to the guarantees provided by the probabilistic statements (1)
106   and (2), we can be sure that for each v ∈ I(X), Y (v) = 1 or that for each v ̸∈ O(X), Y (v) = 0.
107   Joint control over the events can also be guaranteed, either via sensible choices of α1 and α2 or by
      using the joint distribution of the maxima of the logit scores - see Section 2.3.

206   2.4   O PTIMIZING SCORE TRANSFORMATIONS
207
208   The choice of score transformations fI and fO is extremely important and can have a large impact
209   on the size of the conformal confidence sets. The best choice depends on both the distribution of the
210   data and on the nature of the output of the image segmentor used to calculate the scores. We thus
211   recommend setting aside a learning dataset independent from both the calibration dataset, used to
212
      compute the conformal thresholds, and the test dataset. This approach was used in Sun & Yu (2024)
      to learn the best copula transformation for combining dependent data streams.
213
214   In order to make efficient use of the data available, the learning dataset can in fact contain some
215   or all of the data used to train the image segmentor. This data is assumed to be independent of
      the calibration and test data and so can be used to learn the best score transformations without


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      compromising subsequent validity. The advantage of doing so is that less additional data needs to be
217   set aside or collected for the purposes of learning a score function. Moreover it allows for additional
218   data to be used to train the model resulting in better segmentation performance. The disadvantage is
219   that machine learning models typically overfit their training data meaning that certain score functions
220   may appear to perform better on this data than they do in practice. The choice of whether to include
221   training data in the learning dataset thus depends on the quantity of data available and the quality of
222   the segmentation model. We do not recommend using the training data as part of the learning dataset
223   if there is a large amount of data available as doing so may not lead to the optimal transformation.
224   A score transformation that we will make particular use of in Section 3 is based on the distance
225   transformation which we define as follows. Given A ⊆ V, let E(A) be the set of points on the
226   boundary of A obtained using the marching squares algorithm (Maple, 2003). Given a distance
227   metric ρ define the distance transformation dρ : P(V) × V → R, which sends A ∈ P(V) and v ∈ V
228   to
229                             dρ (A, v) = sign(A, v) min{ρ(v, e) : e ∈ E(A)},
230   where sign(A, v) = 1 if v ∈ A and equals −1 otherwise. The function dρ is an adapation of
231   the distance transform of Borgefors (1986) which provides positive values within the set A and
232   negative values outside of A. Moreover define the Hausdorff distance between two sets A, B ⊆ V
233   as Hρ (A, B) = max{supa∈A inf b∈B ρ(a, b), supb∈B inf a∈A ρ(b, a)}, The following result shows
234   that transforming the scores using the distance transformation ensures that accurate segmentation

      provides precise confidence sets. See Section A.3 for a proof.

244   2.5   C ONSTRUCTING CONFIDENCE SETS FROM BOUNDING BOXES
245
246
      Existing work on conformal inner and outer confidence sets, which aim to provide coverage of
      the entire ground truth mask with a given probability, has primarily focused on bounding boxes
247
      (de Grancey et al., 2022; Andéol et al., 2023; Mukama et al., 2024). These papers adjust for mul-
248
      tiple comparisons over the 4 edges of the bounding box, doing so conformally by comparing the
249   distance between the predicted bounding box and the bounding box of the ground truth mask. These
250   approaches provide box-wise coverage by aggregrating the predictions over all objects within all of
251   the calibration images, often combining multiple bounding boxes per image. However, as observed
252   in Section 5 of de Grancey et al. (2022), doing so violates exchangeability which is needed for valid
253   conformal inference, as there is dependence between the objects within each image. Instead image-
254   wise coverage can be provided without violating exchangeability by treating the union of the boxes
255   as the ground truth image (de Grancey et al., 2022; Andéol et al., 2023).
256   We establish the validity of a version of the image-wise max-additive method of Andéol et al. (2023)
257   (adapted to provide coverage of the ground truth) as a corollary to our results, see Appendix A.4. In
258   this approach we define bounding box scores based on the chessboard distance transformation to the
259   inner and outer predicted masks and use these scores to provide conformal confidence sets. Validity
260   then follows as a consequence of the results above as we show in Corollaries A.6 and A.7. Using the
261   bounding box scores thus provides the same confidence sets as those used in Andéol et al. (2023).
262   We compare to this approach in our experiments below. Targeting bounding boxes does not directly
263
      target the mask itself and so the resulting confidence sets are typically conservative.

287   3.1   C HOOSING A SCORE TRANSFORMATION
288
289   In order to optimize the size of our confidence sets we set aside 298 of the 1798 polyps images
290   to form a learning dataset on which to choose the best score transformations. Importantly as the
291   learning dataset is independent of the 1500 images which we set aside, we can study it as much as
292
      we like without compromising the validity of the follow-up analyses in Sections 3.2. In particular
      in this section we shall use the learning dataset to both calibrate and study the results, in order to
293
      maximize the amount of important information we can learn from it.
294
295   The score transformations we considered were the identity (after softmax transformation) and dis-
296   tance transformations of the predicted masks: taking fI (s(X), v) = fO (s(X), v) = dρ (M̂ (X), v),
297   where ρ is the Euclidean metric. We also compare to the results of using the bounding box transfor-
298   mations fI = bI and fO = bO which correspond to transforming the predicted bounding box using
299
      a distance transformation based on the chessboard metric and are defined formally in Appendix A.4.
      For the purposes of plotting we used the combined bounding box scores defined in Definition A.5.
300
301   From the histograms in Figure 1 we can see that thresholding the logit scores at the inner threshold
302   well separates the data. However this is not the case for the outer threshold for which the data is bet-
303   ter separated using the distance transformed and bounding box scores. Figure 2 shows PraNet scores
304   for 2 typical examples, along with surface plots of the transformed scores and corresponding 90%
305
      marginal confidence regions (with thresholds obtained from calibrating over the learning dataset).
      From these we see that PraNet typically assigns a high score to the polyps regions which decreases
306
      in the regions directly around the boundary before returning to a higher level away from the polyps.
307
      This results in tight inner sets but large outer sets as the model struggles to identify where the polyps
308   ends. Instead the distance transformed and bounding box scores are much better at providing outer
309   bounds on the polyps, with distance transformed scores providing a tighter outside fit. Additional
310   examples are shown in Figures A8 and A9 and have the same conclusion.
311
      Based on the results of the learning dataset we decided to combine the best of the approaches for
312
      the inner and outer sets respectively for the inference in Section 3.2, taking fI to be the identity and
313
      fO to be the distance transformation of the predicted mask in order to optimize performance. We
314   can also use the learning dataset to determine how to weight the α used to obtain joint confidence
315   sets. A ratio of 4 to 1 seems appropriate here in light of the fact that in this dataset identifying where
316   a given polyps ends appears to be more challenging than identifying pixels where we are sure that
317   there is a polyps. To achieve joint coverage of 90% this involves taking α1 = 0.02 and α2 = 0.08.

      6            D ISCUSSION
509
510
511   In this work, we have developed conformal confidence sets which offer probabilistic guarantees for
512   the output of a black box image segmentation model and provide tight bounds. Our work helps to
513
      address the lack of formal uncertainty quantification in the application of deep neural networks to
      medical imaging which has limited the reliability and adoption of these models in practice. Con-
514
      fidence sets provide informative spatial bounds on the expected output and ensure that we are not
515
      overconfident about our model predictions.
516
517   The use of the distance transformed scores was important in providing tight outer confidence bounds
518   in all applications considered as the original neural network is by itself unable to reliably determine
519
      where the true masks end with certainty. The distance transformation penalizes regions away from
      the predicted mask, allowing the true mask to be distinguished from the background. In other
520
      datasets and model settings, other transformations may be appropriate. We saw for instance that
521
      smoothing the scores can be beneficial and allow the model to boost power using spatial information.
522   As such we strongly recommend the use of a learning dataset to learn the best transformation and
523   maximize the precision of the resulting confidence bounds.
524
525
      We have shown (Theorems 2.8 and A.4) that an increase in the quality of the predictions of the image
      segmentation model leads to more precise confidence sets when using the distance transformed
526
      scores. Such a relationship does not hold for the untransformed logit scores. This is well illustrated
527
      in the brain imaging application, see Figure A20, in which the distance transformed scores allow
528   for very tight uncertainty bands but the confidence sets obtained based on the logit scores are very
529   uninformative. Metrics for each model used are shown in Appendix A.9 and the model (HDBET)
530   with the highest performance on these metrics has the most precise confidence bands. Further
531   refinements to the segmentations within these bounds could be acheived using biological information
532   on the shapes of the ground truth masks. In this work we have assumed that a ground truth mask is
533   known for each subject. However, in some applications mutliple raters provide true masks for each
534   segmentation. Future work could look at incorporating the uncertainty in the rating process into the
535   conformal prediction algorithm to better infer on model uncertainties.
536   Matlab code to implement the methods of this paper and a demo on a downscaled version of the
537   data is available in the supplementary material. The code is very fast: calculating inner and outer
538   thresholds (over the 1000 images in the calibration set) requires approximately 0.03 seconds on the
539   downscaled polyps data on a standard laptop (Apple M3 chip with 16 GB RAM) and 2.64 seconds
      for the original polyps dataset.

840   A.5   W RITING THE TEST TIME STEPS AS AN ALGORITHM
841
842   In order to clarify what is done at test time we include the following algorithm which demonstrates
843   this for the polyps data application.
844
845   Algorithm 1 Test time application of the methods (for the polyps data application)
846   Require: Inner and outer alpha levels α1 and α2 and thresholds λI (α1 ) and λO (α2 ) obtained as
847       in equations (3) and (5) from the calibration dataset with fI the identity and fO the distance
848       transformed scores. A test time observation Xn+1 and a score function s(Xn+1 ) obtained by
849       an image segmenting model and a distance metric ρ.
850    1: Compute the predicted mask as M̂ (Xn+1 ) = {v ∈ V : s(Xn+1 , v) > 0}
851    2: Calculate the set of points E(M̂ (Xn+1 )) on the boundary of the predicted mask M̂ (Xn+1 )
852       using the marching squares algorithm.
853    3: Compute dρ (M̂ (Xn+1 ), v) = sign(M̂ (Xn+1 ), v) min{ρ(v, e) : e ∈ E(M̂ (Xn+1 ))}, i.e. the
854       distance transformed scores, for each v ∈ V.
855    4: Let I(Xn+1 ) = {v ∈ V : s(X, v) > λI (α1 )}.
856    5: Let O(Xn+1 ) = {v ∈ V : −dρ (M̂ (Xn+1 ), v) ≤ λO (α2 )}.
857    6: return I(Xn+1 ) and O(Xn+1 ).
858
859   For the brain imaging application, λI (α1 ) and λO (α2 ) are instead computed from the calibration
860   dataset with both fI and fO being the distance transformed scores. Then line 4 of the algorithm
861   at test time is replaced by: Let I(Xn+1 ) = {v ∈ V : dρ (M̂ (Xn+1 ), v) > λI (α1 )}. For the teeth
862   segmentation problem the inner scores are analogously replaced but with scores smoothed using an
863   isotropic Gaussian kernel with FWHM 2 pixels.

       A.10     R ELATIONSHIP WITH MULTIPLE TESTING ERROR RATES
1634
1635   FAMILY-W ISE E RROR R ATE (FWER)
1636
1637   In traditional multiple hypothesis testing Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER) is the probability of mak-
1638   ing at least one false discovery across a set of considered hypotheses. Given a multiple testing
1639   problem in which m hypotheses are tested and a multiple testing algorithm M, let V (M) denotes
1640   the resulting set of false discoveries, R(M) the set of rejected hypotheses and T be the set of true
1641
       rejections. Then the FWER is defined as:
1642                                     FWER(M) := P(|V (M)| ≥ 1).
1643
1644
       Then, given α > 0, if we can guarantee that FWER ≤ α then it follows that R(M) ⊆ T with
       probability at least 1 − α. This statement is thus analogous to the coverage guarantees which
1645
       we provide in Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 in the sense that a probabilistic gaurantee on the inclusion
1646
       probability is provided.
1647
1648
       FALSE D ISCOVERY R ATES AND P ROPORTIONS
1649
1650   Instead the false discovery proportion in the multiple testing setting for an algorithm M is given by:
1651
1652

                                       FDP(M) :=              · 1|R(M)|>0
1653                                                  |R(M)|
1654   and the False Discovery Rate is given by:
1655
1656                                         FDR(M) = E [F DP ] ,
1657   where 1|R(M)|>0 is an indicator function that is 1 when |R(M)| > 0 and 0 otherwise. Controlling
1658   the FDP in probability is thus analogous to the proportion of the true mask that lies within the
1659   discovered sets, as in Bates et al. (2021) whilst controlling the FDR is instead analogous to the risk
1660   control discussed in Angelopoulos et al. (2021) in which conformal inference is used to control the
1661   expected proportion of the true mask which is discovered.
