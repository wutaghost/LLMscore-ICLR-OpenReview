                                              Abstract
009
              Diffusion models are relatively easy to train but require many steps to
010
              generate samples. Consistency models are far more difficult to train, but
011
              generate samples in a single step.
012
              In this paper we propose Multistep Consistency Models: A unification
013
              between Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023) and TRACT (Berthelot
014           et al., 2023) that can interpolate between a consistency model and a diffusion
015           model: a trade-off between sampling speed and sampling quality. Specifically,
016           a 1-step consistency model is a conventional consistency model whereas a
017           ∞-step consistency model is a diffusion model.
018           Multistep Consistency Models work really well in practice. By increasing
019           the sample budget from a single step to 2-8 steps, we can train models
020           more easily that generate higher quality samples, while retaining much of
021           the sampling speed benefits. Notable results are 1.4 FID on Imagenet 64
022           in 8 sampling steps and 2.1 FID on Imagenet128 in 8 sampling steps with
023           consistency distillation, using simple losses without adversarial training.
024
              We also show that our method scales to a text-to-image diffusion model,
              generating samples that are close to the quality of the original model.

      1   Introduction
028
029
      Diffusion models have rapidly become one of the dominant generative models for image,
030   video and audio generation (Ho et al., 2020; Kong et al., 2021; Saharia et al., 2022). The
031   biggest downside to diffusion models is their relatively expensive sampling procedure: whereas
032   training uses a single function evaluation per datapoint, it requires many (sometimes hundreds)
033   of evaluations to generate a sample.
034
      Recently, Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023) have reduced sampling time significantly,
035
      but at the expense of image quality. Consistency models come in two variants: Consistency
036   Training (CT) and Consistency Distillation (CD) and both have considerably improved
037   performance compared to earlier works. TRACT (Berthelot et al., 2023) focuses solely on
038   distillation with an approach similar to consistency distillation, and shows that dividing the
039   diffusion trajectory in stages can improve performance. Despite their successes, neither of
040   these works attain performance close to a standard diffusion baseline.
041
      Here, we propose a unification of Consistency Models and TRACT, that closes the perfor-
042   mance gap between standard diffusion performance and low-step variants. We relax the
043   single-step constraint from consistency models to allow ourselves as much as 4, 8 or 16
044   function evaluations for certain settings. Further, we generalize TRACT to consistency
045   training and adapt step schedule annealing and synchronized dropout from consistency
046   modelling. We also show that as steps increase, Multistep CT becomes a diffusion model.
047   We introduce a unifying training algorithm to train what we call Multistep Consistency
048   Models, which splits the diffusion process from data to noise into predefined segments. For
049   each segment a separate consistency model is trained, while sharing the same parameters.
050
      For both CT and CD, this turns out to be easier to model and leads to significantly improved
      performance with fewer steps. Surprisingly, we can perfectly match baseline diffusion model
051
      performance with only eight steps, on both Imagenet64 and Imagenet128.
052

085   Diffusion models are specified by a destruction process that adds noise to destroy data:

166   step consistency converges to a standard diffusion training in the limit. Finally, we develop
167   a deterministic sampler named aDDIM that corrects for the missing variance problem in
168   DDIM.

353   4   Related Work
354
355   Multistep Consistency Models are a direct combination of (Song et al., 2023; Song &
356   Dhariwal, 2023) and TRACT (Berthelot et al., 2023). Compared to consistency models, we
357
      propose to operate on multiple stages, which simplifies the modelling task and improves
      performance significantly. On the other hand, TRACT limits itself to distillation and uses
358
      the self-evaluation from consistency models to distill models over multiple stages. The
359
      stages are progressively reduced to either one or two stages and thus steps. The end-goal of
360   TRACT is again to sample in either one or two steps, whereas we believe better results can
361   be obtained by optimizing for a slightly larger number of steps. We show that this more
362   conservative target, in combination with our improved sampler and annealed schedule, leads
363   to significant improvements in terms of image quality that closes the gap between sample
364   quality of standard diffusion and low-step diffusion-inspired approaches.
365
      Earlier, DDIM (Song et al., 2021a) showed that deterministic samplers degrade more gracefully
366   than the stochastic sampler used by Ho et al. (2020) when limiting the number of sampling
367   steps. Karras et al. (2022) proposed a second order Heun sampler to reduce the number of
368   steps (and function evaluations), while Jolicoeur-Martineau et al. (2021) studied different
369   SDE integrators to reduce function evaluations. Progressive Distillation (Salimans & Ho,
370   2022; Meng et al., 2022) distills diffusion models in stages, which limits the number of model
371   evaluations during training while exponentially reducing the required number of sampling
372   steps with the number stages.
373   Other methods inspired by diffusion such as Rectified Flows (Liu et al., 2023a) and Flow
374   Matching (Lipman et al., 2023) have also tried to reduce sampling times. In practice however,
375   flow matching and rectified flows are generally used to map to a standard normal distribution
376   and reduce to standard diffusion. As a consequence, on its own they still require many
377   evaluation steps. In Rectified Flows, a distillation approach is proposed that does reduce
      sampling steps more significantly, but this comes at the expense of sample quality.

400   Adversarial distillation Distillation to a few steps was very difficult to do using only
401   simple distance metrics. Therefore, many works resort to a form of adversarial training. For
402   example Luo et al. (2023) distill the knowledge from the diffusion model into a single-step
403
      model and Zheng et al. (2023) use specialized architectures to distill the ODE trajectory
      from a pre-created noise-sample pair dataset. A very similar approach to ours is Consistency
404
      Trajectory Models (CTMs) (Kim et al., 2023), which are trained to arbitrarily integrate to a
405
      given timestep. This is implemented by modifying the inputs of the denoising network to
406   include an endpoint of the integration. Although CTMs produce very high quality image
407   samples in a few steps, their performance relies on adversarial training: Without it, CTMs
408   cannot produce great samples and have a considerable gap in FID score. In contrast, our
409   Multistep CMs can be trained with simple distance metrics and still achieve very good FID
410   scores under a few sampling steps. A possible explanation is that it is much easier to learn a
411   handful of fixed integration trajectories (Multistep CMs) instead of every possible integration
412   with arbitrary endpoints (CTMs). Another advantage of Multistep CMs is that the inputs
413   to the denoising network are not changed, making fine-tuning of existing diffusion models to
414
      Multistep CMs very straightforward.

      6     Conclusions
527
528
      In conclusion, this paper presents Multistep Consistency Models, a simple unification between
      Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023) and TRACT (Berthelot et al., 2023) that closes the
529
      performance gap between standard diffusion and few-step sampling. Multistep Consistency
530
      gives a direct trade-off between sample quality and speed, achieving performance comparable
531   to standard diffusion in as little as eight steps. The main limitation of multistep consistency is
532   that one pays the price of several function evaluations to generate a sample. Here, adversarial
533   approaches generally perform better when only one or two evaluations are permitted, but
534   they come the cost of more difficult training dynamics.
535
536   Broader Impacts This paper proposes a method to speed up sampling from diffusion
537   models. Although generative models may be used for positive applications such as enhancing
538   human creativity or drug discovery, they may also be used to create deepfakes or misinfor-
539   mation. Hence, enabling faster sampling may amplify both the positive and the negative
      impacts of generative modelling.

651
652   In this paper we follow the setup from simple diffusion (Hoogeboom et al., 2023). Following
653   their approach, we use a standard UViTs. These are UNets with MLP blocks instead of
654   convolutional layers when a block has self-attention, making the entire block a transformer
655   block. This contains the details for the architecture and how to define diffusion process.
656   There are some minor specifics which we share per experiment below. All runs are initialized
657   using the parameters of a pretrained diffusion models.
658
659   Multistep Consistency Hyperparameters For all ImageNet runs (small/large,
660   1 through 16 step) we use a log-linear interpolated schedule from 64 teacher
661
      steps to 1280 teacher steps, annealed over 100000 training iterations which means
      Nteacher (i) = exp(log 64 + clip(i/100.000, 0, 1) · (log 1280 − log 64)). The batch size is 2048.
662
      We use a xvar_frac of 0.75 for aDDIM. And we use a huber epsilon of 1e-4. The model is
663
      trained for 200000 steps. The interpolation starts quite low and takes a long time, and these
664   settings are somewhat excessive for the larger student step models such as the 8- or 16-step
665   model. However, fixing these settings for the model allowed for clean comparisons. These
666   runs anneal the teacher steps using
667
      For the text-to-image model, we ran consistency distillation where we kept the teacher steps
668
      fixed at 256 and used an xvar_frac of 0.75. Note that the xvar_frac should always be
669
      computed on the conditional output, not the guided output (so guidance zero). We used a
670   huber epsilon of 1, which is essentially a scalar-scaled l2 squared loss for the normalized [-1,
671   1] domain of interest. We train these models for 30000 steps at a batch size of 2048.
672

679   all transformers albeit with a lower factor of 0.1. The network is trained with an interpolated
680   cosine schedule from noise resolution 32 to 64 at a resolution of 64 (this is practically identical
681
      to a normal cosine schedule). The small and large model have 394M and 1.23B parameters,

      Fig. 5 compares various samplers including the 2nd order Heun sampler. Additionally, a
744   stochastic version of DDIM is included (noise DDIM) where we add random Guassian noise
745   directly to the model prediction. This direct noise injection breaks the determinism of
746   DDIM and is therefore not a useful sampler for consistent distillation. However, it behaves
747   very similarly to the aDDIM which seems to indicate that our heuristic noise correction is
748   accurately simulating the positive effects of noise injection in the sampler.
749
      Interestingly, we observe a significant difference in the relative quality of various sampling
750   methods depending on the noise schedule used at evaluation. The Heun sampler favors the
751   schedule introduced by Karras (Karras et al., 2022) while the noisy methods seem to work
752   better with a standard cosine schedule. One possible explanation is that the asymptotic
753   behavior of the cosine schedule favours the noise injection methods. Previous work has
754   indicated that the asymptotic behavior a noise schedule is important to fully capture the data
755   distribution (Lin et al., 2024). We consider investigating the interaction between schedules
      and samplers and interesting opportunity for future work.
