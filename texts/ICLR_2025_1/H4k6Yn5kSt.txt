009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We extend the Differential Privacy (DP) framework to Hadamard manifolds, the
012            class of complete and simply connected Riemannian manifolds with non-positive
013            sectional curvature. Inspired by the Cartan–Hadamard theorem, we introduce
014            Exponential-Wrapped Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms to achieve ε-DP, (ε, δ)-
015            DP, Gaussian DP (GDP), and Rényi DP (RDP) on these manifolds. Our approach
016            employs efficient, straightforward algorithms that circumvent the computationally
017            intensity Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) methods. This work is the first to
018
               extend (ε, δ)-DP, GDP, and RDP to Hadamard manifolds. We further demonstrate
               the effectiveness of our methodology through simulations on the space of Symmet-
019
               ric Positive Definite Matrices, a frequently used Hadamard manifold in statistics.
020
               Our findings reveal that our Exponential-Wrapped mechanisms surpass traditional
021            MCMC-based approaches, which require careful tuning and extensive diagnostics,
022            in both performance and ease of use. Additionally, our methods achieve compara-
023            ble utility to the Riemannian Laplace mechanism with enhanced utility for smaller
024            privacy budgets (ε) and operate orders of magnitude faster computationally.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The proliferation of AI and machine learning technologies has catalyzed the exploration of more
030   complex types of data. Notably, nonlinear manifold data, which frequently emerge in fields such as
031   medical imaging (Pennec et al., 2019; Dryden, 2005; Dryden et al., 2009), computer vision (Turaga
032   & Srivastava, 2015; Turaga et al., 2008; Cheng & Vemuri, 2013), pattern recognition (Nielsen, 2013;
033
      Hettiarachchi & Peters, 2015), signal processing (Barachant et al., 2010; Zanini et al., 2018), and
      geometric deep learning (Belkin et al., 2006; Niyogi, 2013), pose unique challenges.
034
035   As data becomes increasingly complex, the task of safeguarding privacy also becomes more chal-
036   lenging and intricate. Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006b), a leading mathematical
037   framework, has been widely recognized for its ability to quantify and ensure privacy protection.
038   While numerous mechanisms have been developed to achieve DP (McSherry & Talwar, 2007; Barak
039
      et al., 2007; Wasserman & Zhou, 2010; Reimherr & Awan, 2019), these traditional mechanisms,
      primarily designed for linear data, often fall short when applied to complex nonlinear data. For
040
      instance, the commonly adopted extrinsic method embeds nonlinear data into Euclidean space, allow-
041
      ing the application of standard differential privacy (DP) mechanisms. However, as Reimherr et al.
042   (2021) demonstrated, leveraging the intrinsic properties of nonlinear data can significantly enhance
043   data utility while maintaining privacy. This underscores the necessity for privacy mechanisms that
044   integrate differential geometry to effectively address the complexities of nonlinear data and fully
045   leverage its geometric structure.
046
      The differential privacy framework was first extended to general manifolds by Reimherr et al. (2021),
047
      who introduced the Riemannian Laplace mechanism on Riemannian manifolds to achieve ε-DP.
048   However, the development of other differential privacy variations for broader manifold applications
049   remains limited. Utpala et al. (2023a) extended (ε, δ)-DP only to a specific manifold – the Symmetric
050   Positive Definite Matrices (SPDM) space – using the log-Euclidean metric instead of the usual
051   Rao-Fisher affine invariant metric. When equipped with the log-Euclidean metric, the SPDM space
052   becomes geometrically flat (Arsigny et al., 2007), which simplifies the approach but at the expense
053   of generality. In a similar vein, Jiang et al. (2023) expanded Gaussian Differential Privacy (GDP)
      to general manifolds, although their calibration algorithm is confined to constant curvature spaces

054
      and demands considerable computational resources. The sampling methods required for both the
055   Riemannian Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms, as described by Reimherr et al. (2021) and Jiang
056   et al. (2023), involve computationally intensive Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) processes,
057   particularly in complex, high-dimensional spaces such as the SPDM space. This highlights an
058   ongoing need for broader extensions of differential privacy variations to manifolds and the creation
059   of more computationally efficient mechanisms.
060
      In this research, we significantly advance the application of differential privacy to Hadamard Rie-
061   mannian manifolds, showcasing robust mechanisms alongside empirical validations. Our main
062   contributions are outlined below::
063
064          • We are the first to extend (ε, δ)-DP, GDP, and RDP to Hadamard Riemannian manifolds
065            through the introduction of Exponential-Wrapped Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms. Im-
066            portantly, this is the first extension for RDP and broadens the scope of privacy frameworks
067            to general manifold settings.
068          • We develop fast and efficient implementations of these mechanisms that avoid the computa-
069            tionally intensive MCMC sampling methods. This development facilitates more feasible
070            and practical applications of differential privacy in real-world scenarios.
071          • Through comprehensive numerical experiments, our results demonstrate that our mecha-
072            nisms perform comparably to the traditional Riemannian Laplace mechanism. Notably,
073            when achieving GDP, our Exponential Gaussian mechanism exhibits superior performance
074            in scenarios with small privacy budgets.

076
      Differential Privacy. Subsequently, we introduce the Exponential-Wrapped Distribution and detail its
077
      calibration to achieve (ε, δ)-DP, GDP, and RDP. We then explore the task of releasing differentially
078   private Fréchet means and derive theoretical utility bounds for our mechanisms. Finally, we present
079   numerical simulations to demonstrate the effectiveness of our methods.

      The primary focus of this paper is on Hadamard manifolds. A simply connected complete Riemannian
116
      manifold of non-positive curvature is called a Hadamard Manifold. It is named after the famous
117
      Cartan-Hadamard theorem which states that for any d-dimensional Hadamard manifold M, it is
118   differomorphic to Rd and more precisely, at any point p ∈ M, the exponential mapping Expp :
119   Tp M → M is a diffeomorphism and thus Logp is defined everywhere on M. This property
120   enables us to develop the Exponential-Wrapped mechanisms in Sections 3.2 and 3.3. Another
121   important property of the Hadamard manifold is that Logp is a contraction for any p ∈ M. That is,
122   ∥ Logp q1 − Logp q2 ∥ ≤ d(q1 , q2 ) for any p, q1 , q2 ∈ M. For more technical details on Hadamard
123   manifolds, please refer to Petersen (2006); Shiga (1984).

125   2.2   D IFFERENTIAL P RIVACY
126
127   Definition 2.1 ((Dwork et al., 2006a)). A data-releasing mechanism M is said to be (ε, δ)-DP with
128   ε ≥ 0, 0 ≤ δ ≤ 1, if for any adjacent datasets, denoted as D ≃ D′ , differing in only one record, we
129   have Pr(M (D) ∈ A) ≤ eε Pr (M (D′ ) ∈ A) + δ for any measurable set A in the range of M . For
130   δ = 0, M is said to be ε-DP.
131
      Since (ε, δ)-DP is a well-defined concept on any measurable space (Wasserman & Zhou, 2010), it
132
      can be readily extended to any Riemannian manifold equipped with the Borel σ-algebra.
133
134   One relaxation of ε-DP is the Rényi DP, which is based on Rényi divergence. It shares many important
135   properties with ε-DP while allowing tighter analysis of composite heterogeneous mechanisms.

137   (RDP) of order α, or (α, ϵ)-RDP for short, if Dα (M (D)∥M (D′ )) ≤ ϵ for all neighboring datasets
138   D ≃ D′ , where the Rényi divergence of a finite order α ̸= 1 is defined as

144   Another way of extending the differential privacy definition is through the viewpoint of the statistical

179
      space probability via the exponential map. For manifold M with dimension d > 1, wrapping a
180   density around the manifold involves volume distortion. This occurs because the exponential map
181   typically does not preserve the area between the Lebesgue measure on the tangent space and the
182   reference measure on the manifold.
183

184

186

187

482   6       C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
483
484   In this paper, we develop Exponential-Wrapped Laplace and Gaussian mechanisms and apply them
485   to achieve ε-DP, (ε, δ)-DP, RDP, and GDP on Hadamard manifolds, the class of simply connected
      complete manifolds with non-positive curvature. We provide simple and fast algorithms for these


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      mechanisms. Finally, through simulations, we demonstrate that our Exponential-Wrapped Gaussian
487   mechanism has comparable results and better results for small privacy budgets compared to the
488   Riemannian Laplace mechanism for achieving GDP while having a magnitude of degree faster
489   computational time.
490
      For future research, the initial step should be to identify the optimal selection of the footpoint p0 in
491
      both of our mechanisms. In non-constant curvature spaces, certain choices of p0 may more effectively
492
      reduce the volume distortion introduced by the Riemannian exponential map. Since this paper focuses
493   on manifolds of non-positive curvature, another direction would be to extend various differential
494   privacy mechanisms to manifolds with non-negative curvature. Finally, instead of releasing privatized
495   Fréchet means, we aim to extend our work to more complex tasks such as principal geodesic analysis
496   (Huckemann et al., 2010; Fletcher et al., 2003; Zhang & Fletcher, 2013) and regression on manifolds
497   (Cheng & Wu, 2013).

      A.4     B ROADER I MPACTS
959
      Differential privacy on manifolds ensures robust privacy protections for datasets with complex
960   geometric structures, such as those found in healthcare, geography, and neuroscience. By tailoring
961   privacy mechanisms to the unique properties of manifolds, we reduce re-identification risks, improve
962   data utility and promote ethical data use. This approach builds public trust, supports open data
963   initiatives, and drives innovation by enabling secure analysis of manifold-based data.
964
      The challenge with differential privacy on manifolds lies in grasping and applying the manifold
965
      concept itself. Adapting privacy mechanisms to these intricate geometric structures requires a deep
966   understanding, which might not be readily accessible, particularly for smaller organizations. This
967   complexity could slow down the adoption of these methods, potentially delaying progress in fields
968   such as healthcare, geospatial analysis, and scientific research, where manifold-based data holds
969   significant potential.
