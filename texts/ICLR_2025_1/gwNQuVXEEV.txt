                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               We propose Bayesian Tree-dependent Factorization (BTF), a novel probabilistic
               representation learning model that uncovers hierarchical, continuous latent fac-
011
               tors in complex datasets. BTF constructs a tree-based model that discovers in-
012
               terpretable factorizations of the data wherein each factor has a conditional rela-
013            tionship to its parent, allowing it to capture both global and local effects. This
014            approach is particularly well-suited for biological data, where traditional methods
015            like PCA fail to capture higher-order dependencies and hierarchical structure. A
016            significant contribution of this work is the multi-view extension of BTF, which al-
017            lows for the joint analysis of multiple data modalities. By learning shared loadings
018            across views while maintaining distinct factors for each modality, multi-view BTF
019            improves performance and enables deeper insights into the relationships between
020            different data types. We demonstrate the performance of BTF in simulations as
021
               well as in a real-world application to gene expression and clinical data in breast
               cancer patients, revealing biologically and clinically meaningful patient trends,
022
               and showing that BTF is a valuable representation learning tool for analysis and
023
               hypothesis generation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Complex data are often comprised of both global effects that apply broadly across a significant
029
      portion of the data as well as local effects that may be detectable only in small subsets. This is es-
030
      pecially the case in biological datasets; for example, single cell RNA sequencing counts are widely
031   influenced by effects such as cell size or cell cycle phase, but certain subsets of samples may ex-
032   hibit specific properties related to more targeted effects such as cell-type-specific stress responses.
033   Similarly, breast cancer patients are all subject to certain common biological effects such as tumor
034   growth, but certain subtypes of patients may be particularly affected by biological responses related
035   to specific biological or clinical markers such as immunohistological status. There may be even fur-
036   ther effects conditioned on interactions between immunohistological status and other processes such
037   as metastasis, stage or particular immune response. A significant challenge presented by these types
038   of data is that while it can be useful to discretize certain categories or sub-groups of samples, many
039
      of the significant latent effects are inherently continuous in nature. These considerations motivate a
      highly interpretable approach that learns a dependent structure of continuous factors so that we are
040
      able to jointly infer and understand the continuous effects present in the data and their relationships
041
      to each other.
042
043   Common factor analysis approaches such as Principal Component Analysis and Independent Com-
044   ponent Analysis often do not provide interpretable factors, even when sparse; every principal com-
045
      ponent typically captures many different sources of variance to different degrees, without accounting
      for higher order dependencies between effects. These approaches also fail to account for any kind
046
      of hierarchical structure and instead impose strong orthogonality or independence constraints. More
047
      recent methods attempt to address these weaknesses by directly inferring hierarchical structure un-
048   der certain strong constraints. One such approach, Tree-Dependent Component Analysis (Bach &
049   Jordan, 2012), relaxes the orthogonality assumptions made by more classic factorization methods
050   by inferring a set of components that are well fit by a tree-structured graphical model. Even more
051   recently, hierarchical approaches to matrix factorization have come into increased focus. These also
052   do not impose orthogonality constraints, but they often make discrete assumptions of hierarchical
053   structure membership that do not allow for fully continuous effects. (Sugahara & Okamoto, 2024;
      Almutairi et al., 2021; Li et al., 2019) One relevant example of this approach is eTrees (Almutairi


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2021), which generates tree-structured embeddings that explicitly encode a hierarchical struc-
055   ture by assuming discrete binary indicators of category and subcategory-membership. Other com-
056   mon approaches to the unsupervised discovery of hierarchical structure in data often rely on two
057   broad techniques: recursively constructed sampling-based approaches such as hierarchical Dirichlet
058   processes (Teh et al., 2004); or post-hoc modeling of the hierarchical structure using agglomerative
059   (or similar) clustering of the results of factorization methods with desirable properties. Most of these
060   approaches also assume that the underlying hierarchy leads to discrete groupings and do not capture
061   the kinds of continuous effects we previously described.
062   We propose a Bayesian approach to a unified factor analysis of hierarchically structured yet contin-
063   uous effects. In contrast to all the previously described methodologies, Bayesian Tree-Dependent
064   Factorization infers a dependent structure that models continuous effects and also generates an ex-
065   plicit interpretation of each component’s effect given their parents. Notably, one common machine
066   learning approach that incorporates non-linear dependencies in a hierarchical fashion is the decision
067   tree (more specifically, classification and regression trees)(Breiman et al., 1984). Decision trees can
068   be used for both supervised and unsupervised learning tasks, but usually rely on discrete splits and
069
      are prone to overfitting. To address these problems, we typically rely on ensembles of decision trees
      (or random forests). (Breiman, 2001) While these ensembles also leverage hierarchical structure in
070
      order to make predictions and are very robust, they sacrifice the interpretability of the decision tree.
071
      This challenge is one of the motivations of the tree-based approach to factorization that we explore
072   here.
073
074   A key contribution of this work is its emphasis on flexibility and interpretability. Because we have
075
      taken a Bayesian approach, we can place priors on the factor loadings as well as the factors them-
      selves, and generate posterior estimates of uncertainty and likelihood. We also place certain con-
076
      straints on the factor loadings which allow us to more easily interpret factor dependencies as condi-
077
      tional weights.
078
079   Finally, we introduce a multi-view extension to BTF. Multi-view datasets are becoming increasingly
080   prevalent, particularly in genomics and healthcare applications, where the integration of different
081
      dependent modalities can be crucial to understanding the complex underlying mechanisms. By
      leveraging the structure present in multiple modalities of data, we can increase the robustness of our
082
      estimates across the views. The relationships between the modalities also give us the opportunity to
083
      interpret what we learn in one view in the context of another. We demonstrate that this interpretabil-
084   ity can significantly improve our understanding of the latent structure in clinical and biological data,
085   and that we can leverage it to observe detailed mechanistic relationships between the signals in each
086   view and formulate powerful hypotheses.

089   2    M ETHODS
090
091
092   BTF is a Bayesian approach which extends the probabilistic PCA (Tipping & Bishop, 1999) formu-
093
      lation with a hierarchical loading structure. As in probabilistic PCA, samples are drawn from Gaus-
      sian distributions with means generated from a linear combination of independently drawn Gaussian
094
      factors and a shared tolerance or noise parameter σ. As such, the probabilistic graphical model for
095
      BTF (shown in Fig. 1) closely resembles that of probabilistic PCA. In contrast to probabilistic PCA,
096   we formulate the loadings on the factors in BTF such that they are dependent on one another. Each
097   loading is the product of sub-loadings z, some of which they share with other loadings. A binary
098   tree determines the sub-loadings used to construct a given factor’s loading and the way in which they
099   are combined, as depicted in Fig. 2. Crucially, the position of a node sub-loading in relation to its
100   parent dictates whether it is multiplied by the parent’s sub-loading zp or the complement (1 − zp ),
101   indicating that right and left child factors describe conditional effects whose distributions are posi-
102   tively and negatively correlated with the effect distributions of their parent, respectively. As a result,
103   we hypothesize that the BTF model is well-suited to data where hierarchical dependencies involving
104
      up- or down-regulation are of particular relevance (as is often the case in biological data).
105   The depth of the binary tree is specified a priori and may be specific to the chosen application. We
106   further constrain the values of the sub-loadings such that they fall between 0 and 1, which lends
107   itself to a simple interpretation of a sample’s factor sub-loading z as the conditional weight of that
      factor in that sample given the weight of its parent factor.

      2.2   M ULTI -V IEW BAYESIAN T REE -D EPENDENT FACTORIZATION
181
      To increase the stability and interpretability of BTF, we also formulate a multi-view extension to
182   the model. One of the possible challenges to factorization problems and to BTF in particular is
183   identifiability; in particular, the large number of sub-loadings specified in the BTF model means that
184   there are many more degrees of freedom in the parameter space than there are factors. Due to the
185   Bayesian nature of the model, stronger or additional priors may be added to alleviate these concerns.
186
      In particular, we integrate multiple views of data by jointly optimizing the sum of the log likelihoods
187
      of multiple factorizations across a common set of samples with different data types. We constrain
188   the loadings across views such that they are equal. As such, we learn one set of loadings, but as many
189   sets of factors as there are views. This significantly constrains the loading parameters and ensures
190   that the factors across views are related, which further assists in our interpretation. Additionally,
191   we incorporate weight parameters on each of the individual model log likelihoods which may be
192   specified a priori in order to up-weight the effect of any given view.

      3     S IMULATIONS
195
196
      In order to test the ability of the BTF to recover a ground truth hierarchical structure, we first applied
197
      BTF to data from two different simulations with three levels of effects. We devise simulations
198   by randomly sampling 7 sets of sub-loadings from a uniform distribution between 0 and 1 and
199   then composing the loadings from the sub-loadings in the hierarchical fashion specified by the BTF
200   model. To induce hierarchical structure in the data, we mask each set of sub-loadings such that the
201   right-child sub-loadings of any given parent loading are only non-zero when the parent loading is
202   greater than 0.5, and left-child sub-loadings are non-zero only when their parent loading is less than
203   0.5. We sample N sub-loadings at each level and combined these with arbitrarily chosen factors that
204   demonstrate spread along D dimensions to produce a simulation of N samples in D dimensions.
205   We demonstrate this generative process in an easy-to-visualize simulation in two dimensions (Fig.
206
      3). All experiments with simulated data are run on simulations of 1,000 samples.
207   We ran BTF on the simulated data and then compared the inferred loadings to those recovered by
208   three baseline methods: Principal Component Analysis (PCA), Independent Component Analysis
209   (ICA) (Lee & Lee, 1998), and Tree-dependent Component Analysis (TCA). PCA and ICA are the
210   most common approaches to factorization and make basic assumptions regarding orthogonality or
211   independence of the components, respectively. TCA relaxes these assumptions by allowing for a
212
      set of components that are well fit by a tree-structured graphical model. As such, we consider it a
      comparable approach for the purposes of inferring an unknown hierarchical structure.
213
214   One of the advantages of BTF is that it is able to learn an arbitrary number of factors without
215   being constrained by the rank of the data. PCA and the commonly used FastICA (Hyvarinen, 1999)
      algorithm are each only able to generate a maximum of r factors where r is the rank of the given

      4.1   E VALUATION A PPROACH
351
352
      To evaluate our single and multi-view results, we used a combination of visualization and gene
353
      set enrichment analysis. The clinical factors are easily visualizable and interpretable due to the
354   low dimensionality of the data. The scaled clinical factors of our multi-view analysis are shown
355   in Supplementary Fig. 12. In order to interpret the gene factors we used PreRanked Gene Set
356   Enrichment Analysis (Subramanian et al., 2005), which evaluates a predefined ranking to determine
357   whether different sets of genes are over-represented at either end of the scale defined by the ordered
358   ranking when compared to a randomly permuted baseline. We treated the factors as rankings of
359   the genes. Because lower factor values contribute less signal in a non-negative factorization of the
360   data, we only considered significant enrichments where the Normalized Enrichment Score (NES)
361   was greater than 0, limiting our analysis to gene sets that are over-represented in the higher factor
362
      values. Enrichments were evaluated for significance using 1,000 permutations and were corrected
      for multiple hypothesis testing using the Benjamini Hochberg approach at α = 0.1.
363
364   To evaluate the performance of SV-BTF, we compared the number of unique biological enrichments
365   of MSigDB canonical pathways (Liberzon et al., 2011) captured by the learned BTF factors to those
366   captured by three baseline methods (PCA, ICA and NMF). We also evaluated the reconstruction
367   errors of the 4 methods.
368   We evaluated the improvement of MV-BTF over SV-BTF by comparing the number of MSigDB
369   oncogenic geneset enrichments (Liberzon et al., 2011) learned in each application. We also did a
370   qualitative examination of the enrichments discovered by the multi-view approach as well as how
371   they relate to patient trends in the learned hierarchical structure of the clinical view.
372   To better understand the degree to which transfer learning occurs in MV-BTF, we ran additional
373   experiments wherein we added varying levels of Gaussian noise to the data. To this end, we learned
374   8 individual multi-view and single-view models with added Gaussian noises of σ = 0.1, 0.2, 0.5 and
375   1 added in each of the two views. All noised data was rescaled to ensure a fair comparison between
376   views. In all analyses, models were fit 10 times with distinct random seeds so as to account for
377   variability due to initialization. Additional information on our choice of model hyperparameters can
      be found in the Supplement.

      5   D ISCUSSION
509
510
      Commonly used factorization approaches fail to account for the complexities of many real world
511   datasets containing both local and global effects with higher order dependencies between factors. We
512   show that Bayesian Tree-dependent Factorization is a Bayesian approach capable of interpretably
513   and effectively discovering dependent, hierarchical structure that captures both specific and broader
514   effects. One advantage of BTF is that it is highly flexible and allows for the specification of various
515   priors on the latent factors. An additional strength of BTF is the continuous but constrained nature
516   of the factor loadings, which allow the user to interpret the learned factors as continuous effects
517   with loadings that indicate their conditional weight given their parents. We also demonstrate that
518   we can construct a multi-view formulation of BTF that allows for integrative discovery of common
519
      mechanisms across modalities. Furthermore, our applications of BTF to breast cancer patient data
      demonstrate that BTF and MV-BTF are capable of uncovering and aiding in the interpretation of
520
      the real hierarchical mechanisms underlying complex datasets and thus could be valuable tools for
521
      investigation and hypothesis generation in contexts such as drug target discovery.
522
523   BTF is not without limitations. In particular, the model makes very strong assumptions about the
524   nature of the relationships between dependent factors and how the hierarchy of these factors is
525
      structured. Due to the number of possible configurations of the underlying latent hierarchies, con-
      sideration of all the potential structures quickly becomes intractable. The binary tree learned by
526
      BTF is unlikely to always reflect the true underlying structure of the data, and care must be taken
527
      not to interpret the factor relationships too literally. Additionally, BTF requires that the user specify
528   the depth of the learned factor tree a priori. Future work may attempt to relax these assumptions
529   by automatically inferring properties of the tree structure. Similarly, our multi-view formulation of
530   BTF makes the very strong assumption that the loadings across views be identical; future work could
531   relax this assumption, for example by implementing a weighted likelihood that prefers factorizations
532   with loadings that are highly correlated or otherwise related across views.
533
      Broadly speaking, there are many opportunities for advancement in hierarchical factorization meth-
534   ods. In particular, many methods (including BTF) rely on prescriptive assumptions regarding the
535   form of the higher order dependencies between factors. Future work could attempt to incorpo-
536   rate a deep learning approach or other highly non-linear functional forms in order to relax these
537   assumptions as much as possible and accommodate additional complexity. Overall, we expect that
538   hierarchical approaches like BTF that prioritize the interpretability of higher-order dependencies and
539   latent mechanistic structure will continue to serve an important function in real world applications,
      particularly in critical decision making tasks.

      6.1   P REPROCESSING OF THE METABRIC DATA
664
665   Before applying BTF, we pre-processed the data. We log-transformed the quantile-normalized ex-
666   pression data and then generated approximate log2 gene expression intensities from the means of all
667   probe intensities occuring within the gene bodies. We filtered to the top 10% most variably express-
668   ing genes over samples (2,011 genes). We also made sure to include all available genes from the
669   PAM50 set (Bernard et al., 2009), which are commonly used as molecular markers for breast cancer
670   subtype. We imputed 22 missing values using a nearest neighbors approach with five neighbors.
671   In the clinical data, we converted categorical values to one-hot encoding and then chose a subset of
672   the features based on relevance and low degrees of missingness. Among the features included were
673   histological subtype, PAM50 subtype, receptor status, stage, grade, tumor size, cellularity, age at
674   diagnosis and tumor size. We also included survival status and the right-censored time to survival in
675   days. The full set of features used in this analysis is shown in Fig. 12.
676   We removed patient samples of the Normal-like PAM50 subtype to reduce the odds of confounding
677   the other breast cancer subtypes; the true nature of this subtype is not well understood and it was
678   originally defined using normal breast tissue samples for the purpose of quality control. (Bernard
679   et al., 2009)
680
      Clinical data were scaled using a min-max scaler to the range [0, 1] so as to ensure positive values
681   for the purpose of the multi-view analysis.

      6.2   M ODEL HYPERPARAMETERS
684
685   We ran all formulations of BTF with the following hyperparameter settings:

687
                                                        θ=1
688                                              β0 = (10, 10)
689                                                 σ0 = 0.01

      Multi-view models were run with model weights of 1 and 15 on the expression and clinical views
