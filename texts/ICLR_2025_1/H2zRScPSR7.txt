009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Saddle point optimization is a critical problem employed in numerous real-world
012            applications, including portfolio optimization, generative adversarial networks, and
013            robotics. It has been extensively studied in cases where the objective function is
014            known and differentiable. Existing work in black-box settings with unknown objec-
015            tives that can only be sampled either assumes convexity-concavity in the objective
016
               to simplify the problem or operates with noisy gradient estimators. In contrast, we
               introduce a framework inspired by Bayesian optimization which utilizes Gaussian
017
               processes to model the unknown (potentially nonconvex-nonconcave) objective
018
               and requires only zeroth-order samples. Our approach frames the saddle point
019            optimization problem as a two-level process which can flexibly integrate existing
020            and novel approaches to this problem. The upper level of our framework produces
021            a model of the objective function by sampling in promising locations, and the lower
022            level of our framework uses the existing model to frame and solve a general-sum
023            game to identify locations to sample. This lower level procedure can be designed
024            in complementary ways, and we demonstrate the flexibility of our approach by
025            introducing variants which appropriately trade off between factors like runtime,
026            the cost of function evaluations, and the number of available initial samples. We
027
               experimentally demonstrate these algorithms on synthetic and realistic datasets,
               showcasing their ability to efficiently locate local saddle points in these contexts.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   We consider the problem of finding saddle points for smooth two-player zero-sum games of the form
033            P LAYER 1: min f (x, y)       P LAYER 2: min −f (x, y)       x ∈ Rn x , y ∈ Rn y      (1)
034                            x                               y

035   with an unknown, nonconvex-nonconcave objective f . We assume that we can draw noisy zeroth-order
036   samples of f via a possibly expensive process given coordinates (x, y), where x ∈ Rnx , y ∈ Rny .
037   Saddle points are points at which the function f is simultaneously a minimum along the x-coordinate
038   and a maximum along the y-coordinate. Such points specialize the well-known Nash equilibrium
039   concept to the setting of two-player, zero-sum games. Saddle point optimization (Tind, 2009) is
040   widely used in real-world applications like economics (Luxenberg et al., 2022), machine learning
041   (Goodfellow et al., 2020), robotics (Agarwal et al., 2023), communications (Moura & Hutchison,
042   2019), chemistry (Henkelman et al., 2000), and more.
043   Zero-sum games have been widely studied for known and differentiable objective functions. However,
044   this assumption does not encompass numerous real-world situations with nonconvex-nonconcave
045   objectives which may be unknown and can only be sampled. Such objectives are often referred
046   to as “black-box.” For example, in robust portfolio optimization, the goal is to create portfolios
047   resistant to stock market fluctuations (Nyikosa, 2018), which are inherently random and difficult
048   to model but can be sampled in a black-box fashion through trial and error. Similar problems arise
049   in various physical settings, such as robotics (Lizotte et al., 2007) and communication networks
050
      (Qureshi & Khan, 2023). Motivated by these real-world examples in nonconvex-nonconcave black-
      box settings, we present a flexible framework that seeks to identify a saddle point, (x∗ , y ∗ ), such that
051
      f (x∗ , y) ≤ f (x∗ , y ∗ ) ≤ f (x, y ∗ ), for all x, y in its neighborhood.
052
053   Most previous research in this area has focused on solving minimax problems (Bogunovic et al., 2018;
      Fröhlich et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022), which take the form minx maxy f (x, y). The difference


                                                          1
054
      between minimax and saddle points is subtle: a minimax point achieves the best worst-case outcome
055   for the minimizer (i.e., a Stackelberg equilibrium). In contrast, at a saddle point, the best worst-case
056   and worst best-case outcomes coincide (i.e., a Nash equilibrium). Solutions to minimax problems
057   in general nonconvex-nonconcave settings are not necessarily Nash, and encode an leader-follower
058   hierarchy which is not present for the saddle point concept. In settings like racing, chess, and resource
059   allocation, where rational, adversarial actors make decisions simultaneously, equilibria are best
060   described as saddle points.
061   Many previous works in saddle point optimization assume convex-concave objectives (v. Neumann,
062   1928; Korpelevich, 1976; Tseng, 1995; Nemirovski, 2004), for which every minimax point is a
063   saddle and vice versa because the best worst-case and worst best-case always coincide. However,
064   this equivalence does not hold in general nonconvex-nonconcave settings. Notably, some prior
065   works addressing black-box convex-concave settings use zeroth-order samples (Maheshwari et al.,
066   2022). Lastly, finding global saddle points remains an open problem in general settings, so our work
067   specifically focuses on discovering local saddle points, as detailed in Remark 3.5.
068   In contrast to previous works, we approach this problem in the spirit of bilevel Bayesian optimization:
069   at a high-level, we use Gaussian processes to build a surrogate model for the black-box function
070   f (x, y) by sampling points (x, y) at promising locations, and at a low-level, we identify these sample
071   points by solving general-sum games defined on the surrogate model. Specifically, the low-level
072   game selects these samples by seeking local Nash points (Defn. 3.2) of these two-player general-sum
073   games. The high-level optimizer then aims to ensure that in the limit, these samples converge to local
074   saddle points of the black-box problem. We present our contributions as follows.
075   1. We present the first black-box technique for saddle point optimization on nonconvex-nonconcave
076
         objectives based on zeroth-order information. While prior works exist that find saddle points in
         black-box settings or on nonconvex-nonconcave objectives or with zeroth-order information, our
077
         work is the first, to our knowledge, that achieves all three simultaneously.
078
      2. We use our framework to propose a set of algorithms for the lower-level game, with each vari-
079      ant catering to a different real-world case based on uncertainty and sampling cost. Thus, our
080      approach allows for versatility in trading off between factors like ease of sampling, exploration
081      and exploitation, and provides a template for future work in this area.
082   3. We experimentally demonstrate our algorithms’ effectiveness on a variety of challenging synthetic
083      and realistic datasets. As with prior work in black-box optimization, a key limitation of our
084      method is that it is appropriate for only relatively low-dimensional spaces.

086   2   R ELATED W ORK AND P RELIMINARIES
087
088   Saddle Point Optimization: Saddle point problems are widely studied in the game theory (Başar &
089   Olsder, 1998; Cherukuri et al., 2017), optimization (Dauphin et al., 2014; Pascanu et al., 2014), and
090   machine learning communities (Benzi et al., 2005; Jin et al., 2021). We note three previous varieties
091   of algorithms in the area of nonconvex-nonconcave saddle point optimization which guarantee
092   convergence to local saddle points rather than stationary points. Of these, Adolphs et al. (2019) and
093   Gupta et al. (2024) introduce algorithms which solve for saddle points in deterministic settings and
094   neither approach handles unknown objectives. Mazumdar et al. (2019) introduces local symplectic
095   surgery (LSS), a method that, when it converges, provably does so to a local saddle point given
096
      access to first-order and second-order derivative measurements by the sampler. However, derivative
      information is not always available in systems of interest. Moreover, while zeroth-order samplers
097
      can estimate noisy first-order (and second-order) derivatives via finite differencing, the extensive
098
      sampling requirements for such an approach is prohibitive when sampling is expensive. By contrast,
099   our work proposes an extensible zeroth-order framework for black-box saddle point optimization,
100   and we provide extensive experimental evaluation of several variants tailored to distinct settings.

120   Bayesian Optimization (BO) with Gaussian Processes: Močkus (1975); Brochu et al. (2010b);
121   Shahriari et al. (2016), is a sequential search method for maximizing an unknown objective function
122   f : Rk → R with as few evaluations as possible. It starts with initializing a prior over f and uses an
123   acquisition function to select the next point xt given the history of observations, f (x1 ), . . . , f (xt−1 ).
124

      current estimate of f . Typically, f is modeled as a GP, and the GP prior is updated with new
125
      samples. One common acquisition function, used in the GP-UCB algorithm, is the Upper Confidence
126
      Bound UCBt (x) = µt (x) + βt σt (x). GP-UCB (Srinivas et al., 2010) has been used in a variety of
127   settings including robotics (Deisenroth et al., 2013), chemistry (Westermayr & Marquetand, 2021),
128   user modeling (Brochu et al., 2010a), and reinforcement learning (Cheung et al., 2020). A high βt
129   parameter in UCBt implies a more optimistic maximizer (i.e. favoring exploration) in the presence
130   of uncertainty. The UCB acquisition function combines the estimated mean, µt (x), and the estimated
131   standard deviation, σt (x), of the unknown objective function f at point x at iteration t. Analogously,
132   we can also define the Lower Confidence Bound (LCBt (x) = µt (x) − βt σt (x)).

136   Problem Setup: We consider the two-player, zero-sum game in (1), and focus on the case in
137   which the objective f is an unknown function defined on the domain Rnx × Rny , and can only be
138   realized through (possibly expensive, noisy) evaluations. That is, we query the objective
                                                                                                at a point
139   (x, y) ∈ Rnx × Rny and observe a noisy sample r = f (x, y) + z, where z ∼ N 0, σz2 . Although f
140   itself is unknown, we will assume that it is smooth and can be differentiated twice. Our goal is to find
141   Local Saddle Points (LSPs) of f .
142   Our proposed framework will consist of two stages: at the lower level, we will solve a general-sum
143   game defined on a GP surrogate model to identify (local) Nash points, and at the high level we will
144   sample f at those points and refine the GP surrogate model. To frame this problem formally, we
145   must discuss the relationship between Nash points which solve general-sum games and saddle points
146   which solve zero-sum games. For a two-player general-sum game, where player 1 is minimizing
147   function f1 , and player 2 is minimizing function f2 , a Nash point, (x∗ , y ∗ ), is defined as:
148   Definition 3.1 (Global Nash Point (GNP) for Two-Player General-Sum Game). (Başar & Olsder,
149   1998, Defn. 2.1) Point (x∗ , y ∗ ) is a global Nash point of objectives f1 , f2 if, for all x, y ∈ Rnx × Rny ,
150                              f1 (x∗ , y ∗ ) ≤ f1 (x, y ∗ ), f2 (x∗ , y ∗ ) ≤ f2 (x∗ , y).                   (3)
151   At a GNP, variables x and y cannot change their respective values without achieving a less favorable
152   outcome. Finding a GNP is computationally intractable in nonconvex settings (as in global nonconvex
153   optimization), so we seek a Local Nash Point (LNP), where this property need only hold within a
154   small neighborhood. A LNP is characterized by first- and second-order conditions.
155   Definition 3.2 (LNP for Two-Player General-Sum Game). (Ratliff et al., 2016, Defn. 1) Let ∥ · ∥
156   denote a vector norm. A point, (x∗ , y ∗ ), is a local Nash point of cost functions f1 and f2 if there
157   exists a τ > 0 such that for any x and y satisfying ∥x − x∗ ∥ ≤ τ and ∥y − y ∗ ∥ ≤ τ , we have (3).
158   Proposition 3.3 (First-order Necessary Condition). (Ratliff et al., 2016, Prop. 1) For differentiable
159   f1 and f2 , a local Nash point (x∗ , y ∗ ) satisfies ∇x f1 (x∗ , y ∗ ) = 0 and ∇y f2 (x∗ , y ∗ ) = 0.
160   Proposition 3.4 (Second-order Sufficient Condition). (Ratliff et al., 2016, Defn. 3) For twice-
161   differentiable f1 and f2 , if (x, y) satisfies the conditions in Prop. 3.3, ∇2xx f1 (x, y) ≻ 0, and
      ∇2yy f2 (x, y) ≻ 0, then it is a strict local Nash point.

162
      Remark 3.5 (Nash Point is a Saddle Point when f1 = −f2 ). If f = f1 = −f2 , then the point (x∗ , y ∗ )
163   is a global saddle point of f when Defn. 3.1 holds and a local saddle point when Defn. 3.2 holds. A
164   local saddle point is characterized by the same first- and second-order conditions defined in Prop. 3.3
165   and Prop. 3.4, respectively. Henceforth, we use the term saddle point to refer to Nash points in
166   zero-sum games and refer to Defns. 3.1 and 3.2 and Props. 3.3 and 3.4 for Nash and saddle points.

      estimates improve with iterations, the loop in Alg. 2 is expected to terminate at a local saddle point.

295   Next, we provide an intuitive explanation of why sampling at local Nash points of confidence bounds
296   in (5) will lead to local saddle points of f in (1). As we sample local Nash points, the variance in
297   the confidence bounds at those points will decrease, and LCB/UCB will get closer to each other, as
298
      shown by Lemma A.1 and Remark A.2. As we keep sampling, the variance will eventually become
      the noise variance, σz . As such, ∇σ → 0 and therefore ∇LCB, ∇UCB → ∇µ → ∇f , and therefore
299
      finding local Nash points of the confidence bounds will eventually lead to first-order saddle points
300
      of µ and consequently of f . To confirm that we find a saddle, we verify the second-order condition
301   (Prop. 3.4) for the final saddle point returned by Alg. 2. If this point does not satisfy the second-order
302   conditions, we reinitialize our algorithm from a new initial point. We find that, in practice, our
303   algorithms find LSPs on the first initialization more frequently than baseline methods.
304
305   4.4      VARIANTS OF BSP
306
307   BSP Expensive: Our BSP method in Alg. 2 aims to minimize queries of the function f by taking
308   multiple Newton steps per query of f . However, the algorithm may become unstable if the confidence
309   bounds UCBt+1 , LCBt+1 do not accurately approximate f . Additionally, it is possible that querying
310   f can often be inexpensive, for example, in the case of Reinforcement Learning in simulated
311
      environments (Sutton & Barto, 2018). In this case, we query f during each iteration of Alg. 2 after
      taking a single Newton step, in contrast to the multiple steps taken in Alg. 1 (lines 2-3). This approach
312
      is referred to as BSP-expensive since we make more queries of f , while our original algorithm
313
      in Alg. 2 is referred to as BSP-efficient. We demonstrate in our results that this variant can more
314   effectively and efficiently solve complex scenarios than baseline methods under these conditions.
315
316   Exploration and Exploitation: In Alg. 2, we encourage more exploration by using LCB for
317
      minimization and UCB for maximization. Since the value of unexplored regions has high variance
      and thus a lower LCB value, the minimization procedure will explore those regions first (vice-versa
318
      for UCB and maximization). As such, we refer to our original proposed method, as BSP-explore.
319
      Alternatively, we can use LCB for maximization and UCB for minimization, thus promoting more
320   exploitation by our algorithm. We will refer to this variant as BSP-exploit. In real-world scenarios,
321   this approach might be suitable when optimizing a well-understood process, fine-tuning known
322   models, or when domain knowledge allows for confidently focusing on exploitation.

526   6    C ONCLUSION
527
      We present a BO-inspired framework for identifying local saddle points for an unknown objective
528   function, f , with zeroth-order samples. We frame the problem of finding local saddle points for an
529   unknown objective function as a two-level procedure. A low-level algorithm constructs a general-sum
530   game from a Gaussian process which approximates the unknown function f , and solves for the local
531   Nash points of this game by finding the roots of a system of nonlinear algebraic equations. A high-
532   level algorithm queries the points returned by the low-level algorithm to refine the GP estimate and
533   monitor convergence toward local saddle points of the original problem. We validate the effectiveness
534   of our algorithm through extensive Monte Carlo testing on multiple examples.
535   Limitations and Future Work: While our proposed framework shows promising results, we plan
536   to address certain limitations in future work. First, we intend to directly incorporate second-order
537   sufficient conditions (Prop. 3.4) to enhance the performance of our approach within the general-sum
538   low-level game. Second, to further demonstrate our framework’s adaptability, we will test our
539   algorithm with other acquisition functions, such as knowledge gradient (Ryzhov et al., 2012) and
      entropy search (Hennig & Schuler, 2012).

784
785   In this section, we delve into the convergence properties of our proposed algorithms. As mentioned
786
      in Sec. 4.3, when we sample local Nash points obtained by Alg. 1, the variance in the confidence
      bounds at that local Nash point decreases, and LCB/UCB converge towards each other eventually at
787
      the local Nash point. In Lemma A.1, we demonstrate that with zero observation noise, the confidence
788
      bounds at a sampled point are equal, i.e., LCB = UCB. Subsequently, in Remark A.2, we explore
789   the more general case involving non-zero observation noise, positing that as we repeatedly sample
790   in close proximity to the same point, the variance of the sampled point becomes predominantly
791   dependent on the observation noise, resulting in LCB ≈ UCB. Lastly, in Appendix A.2, we provide
792   experimental evidence to corroborate the decrease in the variance of the sampled point during our
793   algorithm’s execution.
794
      Consequently, these results attest that as we sample the local Nash point, the variance in the GP at
795   that point will decrease, and eventually becomes a observation noise variance. As such, the gradient
796   of the variance, ∇σ → 0, and therefore ∇LCB, ∇UCB → ∇µ. As stated in Sec. 4.3, since ∇µ will
797   become a reliable estimator of the true gradients of the unknown function, ∇f , finding local Nash
798   points of the confidence bounds will eventually lead to local saddle points of µ and consequently of
799   f . This convergence property is a key feature of our proposed algorithms, ensuring that the method
800   converges to a solution that represents a local saddle point of the underlying unknown function.

857   Remark A.2 (Approximate equality of UCB and LCB at sampled points under observation noise).
858   Consider the upper confidence bound, UCBt , and the lower confidence bound, LCBt , for a given
859   time step t. In the presence of observation noise, i.e., zt ∼ N 0, σz2 , when the same point is
860   sampled repeatedly, or nearby points are sampled, the predictive variance σ(x) approaches the
861   observation noise σz2 . This is based on the fact after repeated sampling, the only uncertainty left
862   about the sampling point will be due to the observation noise. As such, as the predictive variance
863   becomes smaller due to repeated sampling, the confidence bounds at the sampled point x will have
      UCBt (x) ≈ LCBt (x).

      J(xt , yt ) must be non-singular. Therefore, at each new iterate, we need to verify the invertibility
901   of J(xt , yt ). A common way to ensure Hessian invertibility is by adding a constant factor λI to
902   the diagonal. Gill & King (2004) offers a concise overview of alternative methods for inverting the
903   Hessian matrix.

      2. Line-search: Newton’s method alone (with a unit step length) does not guarantee convergence
905
      to the root unless the starting point is sufficiently close to the solution. To enhance robustness, we
906
      employ line-search, using the merit function M as the criterion for sufficient decrease. The use of
907   line search is standard practice, as discussed and explained in (Nocedal & Wright, 2006, Ch.3).

963   B.6   ROBUST MPC EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS
964
965   In this section, we provide the details of the Robust MPC experiments. In this experiment, we
966   demonstrated that the MPC parameters found at the end of our zero-sum game between the ARIMA
967   antagonist player and the MPC protagonist player will be more robust to out-of-distribution data. Our
968
      algorithm will converge to a local saddle point of this zero-sum game, where both players will be in
      equilibrium. The ARIMA antagonist player cannot find more adversarial parameters for MPC, while
969
      MPC cannot get better at tracking ARIMA generated forecasts.
970
971   Specifically, for the experiment, we compared a nominal MPC with a robust MPC (found using our
      method) on in-distribution data and out-of-distribution data. To generate in-distribution data, we


                                                          18
972
       sampled 500 ARIMA time series forecasts, sF , with α, β ∈ [−1, 1]. We chose out-of-distribution
973    ARIMA parameters similar to the final ARIMA antagonist player parameters. Although the parame-
974    ters were constrained to be within [−1, 1] during the actual algorithm, we selected out-of-distribution
975    parameters α = −0.1 and β = −1.2 and sampled 500 ARIMA time series forecasts for these
976    parameters. This choice enabled us to evaluate the robustness of the MPC against data that deviates
977    from its original training distribution. Finally, we picked nominal MPC parameters, A, B, by fitting
978    the MPC parameters to in-distribution data using supervised learning, i.e., the best A, B to minimize
979    the MPC tracking cost for the in-distribution data. We chose robust MPC parameters as the final
980    MPC protagonist player at the convergence of the zero-sum game. By comparing the performance
981
       of the nominal and robust MPCs on both in-distribution and out-of-distribution data, we aimed
       to demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in finding robust MPC parameters that can handle
982
       deviations from the original training data distribution better than the nominal MPC.
983
984    In Fig. 2, we compared the MPC tracking costs of both the MPCs on in-distribution data and out-
985    of-distribution data. As expected, the nominal MPC performs better on in-distribution data since
986    it is trained on this data. However, the robust MPC significantly outperforms the nominal MPC
987
       on out-of-distribution data by achieving a 27.6% lower mean MPC cost. Additionally, the poor
       performance of the nominal MPC indicates that the final ARIMA antagonist player parameters are
988
       indeed challenging. These results suggest that our algorithm has successfully identified robust MPC
989
       parameters and adversarial ARIMA parameters.
