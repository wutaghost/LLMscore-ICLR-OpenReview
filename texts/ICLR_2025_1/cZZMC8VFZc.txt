011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            As large language models (LLMs) increasingly underpin technological advance-
014            ments, the privacy of their training data emerges as a critical concern. Differential
015            Privacy (DP) serves as a rigorous mechanism to protect this data, yet its integration
016            via Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent (DP-SGD) introduces sub-
017
               stantial challenges, primarily due to the complexities of per-sample gradient clip-
               ping. Current explicit methods, such as Opacus, necessitate extensive storage for
018
               per-sample gradients, significantly inflating memory requirements. Conversely,
019
               implicit methods like GhostClip reduce storage needs by recalculating gradients
020            multiple times, which leads to inefficiencies due to redundant computations. This
021            paper introduces FlashDP, an innovative cache-friendly method that consolidates
022            necessary operations into a single task, calculating gradients only once in a fused
023            manner. This approach not only diminishes memory movement by up to 50% but
024            also cuts down redundant computations by 20%, compared to previous methods.
025            Consequently, FlashDP does not increase memory demands and achieves a 90%
026            throughput compared to the Non-DP method on a four-A100 system during the
027            pre-training of the Llama-13B model, while maintaining parity with standard DP-
028
               SGD in terms of accuracy. These advancements establish FlashDP as a pivotal
               development for efficient and privacy-preserving training of LLMs.

033   The transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) has revolutionized fields like natural language
034   processing (Gao et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2023), embodied AI (Song et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2022;
035   Xu et al., 2024), and AI-generated content (AIGC) (Cao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023), with Large
036
      Language Models (LLMs) demonstrating exceptional abilities in text generation, complex query re-
      sponses, and various language tasks due to training on massive datasets. These models, exemplified
037
      by ChatGPT, are applied across diverse areas, including healthcare, where they enhance diagnosis
038
      and drug discovery by analyzing medical data (Toma et al., 2023; Ali et al., 2023; Sheikhalishahi
039   et al., 2019; Sallam, 2023; Biswas, 2023). However, the extensive capabilities of LLMs raise signif-
040   icant privacy concerns, particularly as they can inadvertently expose or generate sensitive informa-
041   tion, owing to their potential to memorize data from large training sets (Pang et al., 2024; Nasr et al.,
042   2023; Carlini et al., 2023; Ippolito et al., 2022; McCoy et al., 2023; Tirumala et al., 2022; Zhang
043   et al., 2023; Ashkboos et al., 2023).
044
      Differential Privacy (DP) ensures privacy by adding noise during data processing, such that any sin-
045   gle data point’s influence on outcomes is minimal (Dwork, 2006). As the most commonly adopted
046   methods for ensuring DP in deep learning models, Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient Descent
047   (DP-SGD) based methods (Abadi et al., 2016) adapt traditional stochastic gradient descent by clip-
048   ping gradients per sample and adding noise. Although DP-SGD’s application in LLMs is increasing,
049   recent research (Li et al., 2022; Bu et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2022; Hoory et al., 2021) primarily tar-
050   gets the fine-tuning phase, providing privacy only for fine-tuned data. While some studies (Lee &
051   Kifer, 2021; Li et al., 2022; Bu et al., 2023) have applied DP-SGD to pre-training, they typically use
052   shorter sequence lengths, not maximizing the benefits of longer sequences used in modern LLMs.
053
      This limitation arises from the high computational and memory demands of DP-SGD, especially
      with long sequences typical in LLM pre-training.

140   2   R ELATED W ORK
141   Improving Time and Memory Complexities of DP-SGD. The transition from standard stochas-
142   tic gradient descent to DP-SGD introduces substantial modifications in memory and computational
143   demands. In conventional settings, parameter updates are efficiently computed by aggregating gradi-
144   ents across all samples within a batch. This approach is both memory-efficient and computationally
145   straightforward. In contrast, DP-SGD mandates that each sample’s gradients be preserved, clipped,
146   and subsequently aggregated to uphold privacy guarantees. Recent innovations in DP-SGD have
147
      primarily concentrated on ameliorating its computational and memory inefficiencies. TF-Privacy
      vectorizes the loss to calculate per-sample gradients through backpropagation, which is efficient in
148
      terms of memory but slow in execution Abadi et al. (2015). Opacus Yousefpour et al. (2021) and
149
      Rochette et al. (2019) enhance the training efficiency by employing the outer product method Good-
150   fellow (2015), albeit at the cost of increased memory usage needed to store per-sample gradients.
151   This memory overhead is mitigated in FastGradClip Lee & Kifer (2020) by distributing the space
152   complexity across two stages of backpropagation, effectively doubling the time complexity. Addi-
153   tionally, ghost clipping techniques Goodfellow (2015), Li et al. (2021), Bu et al. (2022) allow for
154   clipping per-sample gradients without full instantiation, optimizing both time and space, particularly
155   when feature dimensions are constrained. Furthermore, Bu et al. (2023) introduces a ’book-keeping’
156   (BK) method that achieves high throughput and memory efficiency while falling short in handling
157   the long sequence lengths typical in LLM training.
158   While these methodologies have made significant strides in mitigating the extensive computational
159   and memory demands typically associated with managing per-sample gradients in DP-SGD, they
160   have not addressed the optimization of DP training from the perspective of GPU architecture and
161   memory access. Additionally, the approaches detailed thus far do not cater effectively to the training
      of today’s long-sequence LLMs. FlashDP aims to enhance the efficiency and feasibility of training

      LLMs with long sequences under the constraints of differential privacy, ensuring both high perfor-
163   mance and adherence to privacy standards.

      4.2 A DAPTIVE K ERNEL I MPLEMENTATION
351
352
      The implementation of the FlashDP algorithm leverages the robust and versatile capabilities of the
      PyTorch framework Paszke et al. (2019), which is renowned for its intuitive handling of automatic
353
      differentiation and dynamic computational graphs. One of the critical features of our implemen-
354
      tation involves customizing PyTorch’s autograd functionality to accommodate the specific needs
355   of differential privacy during the training of deep neural networks. To this end, operators that ne-
356   cessitate trainable parameters are intricately defined by wrapping them within PyTorch’s autograd
357   function.
358
      However, implementing the Block-wise All-Reduce algorithm has presented unique challenges, pri-
359
      marily due to the limitations of CUDA’s programming model in facilitating block-wise synchroniza-
360
      tion. Block-wise synchronization is essential in our algorithm; without it, clip operations might be
361   executed prematurely, while the inter-block reduce operation is still incomplete, leading to numeri-
362   cal inaccuracies in the computation of per-sample gradients’ norm squares. There are two primary
363   methods to implement synchronization: 1. cooperative groups (CG) 1 and 2. adaptive kernel. We
364   opted for the second method because the grid synchronization required by CG necessitates launching
365   all blocks simultaneously, which is impractical for DP applications.
366
      To address this limitation, FlashDP’s implementation employs an adaptive approach. Instead of
367   relying on a monolithic kernel to perform the entire Block-wise All-Reduce operation, the process is
368   split across different kernels, which are executed iteratively over the batch dimension. This iterative
369   approach allows for synchronization points between the execution of kernels, using the inherent
370   block synchronization that occurs at kernel launch and completion.
371
      The execution flow in FlashDP is as follows: (1) Intra-block Reduction: Each block computes
372
      the norms of its gradients and performs an HRA-based reduction within the shared memory. This
373   step employs a shuffle-reduce mechanism, optimizing intra-block operations by minimizing memory
374   footprint and synchronization overhead. This results in a single norm value per block. (2) Inter-
375   block Reduction: Each block transfers the outcome of its intra-block reduction to the HBM. This
376   transfer is facilitated through atomic operations for several reasons. Firstly, the result of the intra-
377

379   the atomic operation on this element. This approach minimizes potential bottlenecks, as the differing
380   execution speeds across blocks prevent serious serialization issues. Secondly, atomic operations
381   benefit from acceleration by the hardware instruction set, ensuring that these operations are executed
382   swiftly and efficiently. (3) Inter-kernel Synchronization: After the completion of the inter-block
383   reduction, FlashDP leverages the termination of the kernel as a natural synchronization point. At
384   this juncture, all blocks have finished their individual reductions. (4) Iterative Kernel Launch: For
385   each batch element, a new kernel is launched serially, maintaining synchronization across kernels.
386   This approach involves broadcasting operations where source operands are dimensionally disparate,
387
      ensuring uniform data handling across computational units.
388   This implementation strategy, while divergent from the ideal single-kernel solution, allows FlashDP
389   to function effectively within the current constraints of CUDA. It underscores FlashDP’s adaptability
390   and represents a practical solution to the block synchronization challenge, ensuring accurate gradient
391   norm calculations essential for maintaining the model’s differential privacy. Our analysis on memory
392   and access in Appendix C shows the utility of this implementation.

      6      C ONCLUSION
527   In this paper, we introduce FlashDP, a novel approach for integrating differentially private SGD
528   (DP-SGD) into the training of large language models (LLMs) while enhancing memory efficiency
529   and computational throughput. By optimizing GPU input/output operations, FlashDP significantly
530   reduces the memory transaction overhead, allowing it to achieve near-non-private throughput levels
531
      while maintaining strict privacy standards. Central to FlashDP’s strategy is the Block-wise All-
      Reduce algorithm, which integrates several critical operations into a unified kernel execution. To
532
      achieve this, we propose a Hierarchical Reduction Architecture (HRA), which encompasses more
533
      than just reduction operations. Moreover, we employ an adaptive kernel approach to implement
534   HRA, which addresses the limitations of CUDA’s programming model in facilitating block syn-
535   chronization. Our experiments demonstrate that FlashDP reduces memory usage to levels compara-
536   ble with non-private methods and increases throughput, making the efficient training of substantial
537   models like the Llama 13B feasible on modern hardware. The minimal interference in the training
538   process and the maintenance of computational precision suggest that FlashDP could significantly
539   advance the adoption of DP in sectors where privacy is crucial, making secure and efficient machine
      learning more accessible for a wider range of applications.
