009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We focus on the design of algorithms for finding equilibria in 2-player zero-sum
012            games. Although it is well known that such problems can be solved by a single
013            linear program, there has been a surge of interest in recent years, for simpler algo-
014            rithms, motivated in part by applications in machine learning. Our work proposes
015            such a method, inspired by the observation that the duality gap (a standard metric
016            for evaluating convergence in general min-max optimization problems) is a con-
017            vex function for the case of bilinear zero-sum games. To this end, we analyze a
018
               descent-based approach, variants of which have also been used as a subroutine in
               a series of algorithms for approximating Nash equilibria in general non-zero-sum
019
               games. In particular, we analyze a steepest descent approach, by finding the direc-
020
               tion that minimises the directional derivative of the duality gap function and move
021            towards that. Our main theoretical result is that the derived algorithms achieve a
022            geometric decrease in the duality gap and improved complexity bounds until we
023            reach an approximate equilibrium. Finally, we complement this with an experi-
024            mental evaluation. Our findings reveal that for some classes of zero-sum games,
025            the running time of our method is comparable with standard LP solvers, even with
026            thousands of available strategies per player.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Our work focuses on the design of algorithms for finding Nash equilibria in 2-player bilinear zero-
032   sum games. Zero-sum games have played a fundamental role both in game theory, being among the
033
      first classes of games formally studied, and in optimization, as it is easily seen that their equilibrium
      solutions correspond to solving a min-max optimization problem. It has also been long known that
034
      a max-min strategy (i.e., an equilibrium strategy) for each player can can be actually tackled via
035
      linear programming. Even further, solving zero-sum games is in fact equivalent to solving linear
036   programs, as properly demonstrated in Adler (2013).
037
038   Despite these positive news and the fact that a single linear program (and its dual) suffices to find a
039
      Nash equilibrium, there has been a surge of interest in recent years, for simpler algorithms, motivated
      in part by applications in machine learning. One reason for this is the fact that we may have very
040
      large games to solve, corresponding to LPs with thousands of variables and constraints. A second
041
      reason could be that e.g., in learning environments, the players may be using iterative algorithms
042   that can only observe limited information, hence it would be impossible to run a single LP for the
043   entire game. As an additional motivation, finding new algorithms for such a fundamental problem
044   can provide insights that could be of further value and interest.
045
      In parallel to the above considerations, there has also been a stream of works on finding approximate
046
      Nash equilibria in general non-zero-sum 2 player games. This is a more difficult problem compared
047
      to linear programming, and the efforts so far have led to a variety of algorithms, culminating to the
048   constant factor approximation guarantees achieved in Tsaknakis & Spirakis (2008) and Deligkas
049   et al. (2023). What is interesting about these algorithms is that they start with a descent-based
050   approach by computing the directional derivative of the maximum regret of the two players (i.e., the
051   maximum gain a player can have by deviating). In non-zero-sum games, this does not guarantee
052   that one can reach an approximate equilibrium, with always a small approximation, and therefore
053   the descent method has to be combined with an additional subroutine in order to come up with a
      desired strategy for each player.

      The approaches mentioned above naturally give rise to the question of how would they perform if
055   applied to zero-sum games with the duality gap as the objective function. More specifically, we are
056   interested in evaluating just the descent-based part of such approaches. An indication that this may
057   be promising for zero-sum games is the observation that the duality gap is a convex function for
058   zero-sum games. The duality gap function is simply the sum of the regrets of the two players in a
059   given strategy profile. Therefore, to obtain a δ-approximate equilibrium, it suffices that we find a
060   strategy profile where the duality gap is at most δ.
061
062   1.1   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
063
064   We analyze a method for finding δ-approximate Nash equilibria in zero-sum games, for any con-
065   stant δ. The method is essentially an adaptation of the algorithms in Tsaknakis & Spirakis (2008);
066   Deligkas et al. (2023; 2017) which are for general games, tailored to the case of zero-sum games
067   and having the duality gap as the objective function. Inspired by the convexity of the duality gap
068   function, the method is applying a steepest descent approach, where we find in each step the di-
069
      rection that minimises the directional derivative of the duality gap function and move towards that.
      Our main theoretical result is that the derived algorithm achieves a geometric decrease in the duality
070
      gap until
                we reach  an approximate equilibrium. This implies that the algorithm terminates after at
071
                1       1
072   most O ρ · log δ       iterations with a δ-approximate equilibrium and ρ a fixed constant parameter
073   of our algorithm. Finally, we complement this with an experimental evaluation. Even though the
074   method does need to solve a linear program in each iteration, this turns out to be of much smaller
075   size on average (in terms of the number of constraints) than solving the linear program of the entire
076
      game. As a result, our findings reveal that for some classes of zero-sum games the running time of
      our method is comparable with standard LP solvers, even with thousands of available strategies per
077
      player. We therefore conclude that the overall approach deserves further exploration, as there are
078
      also potential ways of accelerating its running time, discussed in Section 6.

081   1.2   R ELATED W ORK
082
      As already mentioned, conceptually, the works most related to ours are Tsaknakis & Spirakis (2008);
083
      Deligkas et al. (2023; 2017). Although these papers do not consider zero-sum games, they do utilize
084
      a descent-based part as a starting point. The main differences with our work is that first of all, their
085   descent is performed with respect to the maximum regret among the two players, whereas we use
086   the duality gap function. Furthermore the descent phase of their algorithms does not suffice for more
087   general games, and hence their focus is less on the decent phase itself and more on utilizing further
088   procedures to produce approximate equilibria.
089
      There is a plethora of algorithms for linear programming and zero-sum games, which is impossible
090
      to list here, but we comment on what we feel are most relevant. When focusing on optimization
091   algorithms for zero-sum games, Hoda et al. (2010) use Nesterov’s first order smoothing techniques
092   to achieve an ϵ-equilibrium in O(1/ϵ) iterations, with added benefits of simplicity and rather low
093   computational cost per iteration. Following up on that work, Gilpin et al. (2012) propose an iterated
094                                                                    ||A||
      version of Nesterov’s smoothing technique, which runs within O( δ(A)   ·ln(1/ϵ)) iterations. However,
095
      while this is a significant improvement, the complexity depends on a condition measure δ(A), with
096   A being the payoff matrix, not necessarily bounded by a constant.
097
098   Apart from the optimization viewpoint, there has been great interest in designing learning algorithms
099
      for zero-sum games, given their relevance in formulating GANs in deep learning Goodfellow et al.
      (2014) and also other applications in machine learning. Some of the earlier and standard results in
100
      this area concern convergence on average. I.e., it has been known that by using no-regret algorithms,
101
      such as the Multiplicative Weights Update (MWU) methods Arora et al. (2012) the empirical average
102   of the players’ strategies over time converges to a Nash equilibrium in zero-sum games. One could
103   also utilize the so-called Gradient Descent/Ascent (GDA) algorithms (which differ from ours since
104   in GDA each person is applying gradient descent to her utility function, which is not convex). In the
105   recent years, there has also been a great interest in the more robust notion of last-iterate convergence.
106   This means that the strategy profile (xt , yt ), reached at iteration t of an iterative algorithm, converges
107   to the actual equilibrium as t → ∞. In fact, it was shown in Bailey & Piliouras (2018) that several
      MWU variants do not satisfy last-iterate convergence. Motivated by this, there has been a series of

516   6      C ONCLUSIONS
517
518   We have analyzed a descent-based method for the duality gap in zero-sum games. In this work our
519
      goal has been to demonstrate our method, based on the descent on the duality gap, as a proof of
      concept. We expect that our method can be further optimized in practice and find this a promising
520
      direction for future work. In particular, one idea to explore is whether we can reuse the LP solutions
521
      we get in Algorithm 2 from one iteration to the next (since we only change the current solution
522   slightly by a step of size ϵ). Exploring such warm start strategies (see e.g. Yildirim & Wright
523   (2002)) could provide significant speedups. An additional idea for acceleration is to utilize results
524   regarding low support approximate equilibria, hence reducing further the size (the number of LP
525   constraints, and thus number of participating rows and columns in each iteration of Algorithm 2).
526   We can for example reduce ρ according to some schedule so that the best response sets BR become
527   smaller in size.
