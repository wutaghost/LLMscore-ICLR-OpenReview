011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Hallucination, a phenomenon where multimodal large language models (MLLMs)
014            tend to generate textual responses that are plausible but unaligned with the image,
015
               has become one major hurdle in various MLLM-related applications. Several
               benchmarks have been created to gauge the hallucination levels of MLLMs, by
016
               either raising discriminative questions about the existence of objects or introducing
017
               LLM evaluators to score the generated text from MLLMs. However, the discrimi-
018            native data largely involve simple questions that are not aligned with real-world
019            text, while the generative data involve LLM evaluators that are computationally
020            intensive and unstable due to their inherent randomness. We propose LongHalQA,
021            an LLM-free hallucination benchmark that comprises 6K long and complex hal-
022            lucination text. LongHalQA is featured by GPT4V-generated hallucinatory data
023            that are well aligned with real-world scenarios, including object/image descriptions
024            and multi-round conversations with 14/130 words and 189 words, respectively,
025            on average. It introduces two new tasks, hallucination discrimination and hallu-
026
               cination completion, unifying both discriminative and generative evaluations in
               a single multiple-choice-question form and leading to more reliable and efficient
027
               evaluations without the need for LLM evaluators. Further, we propose an advanced
028
               pipeline that greatly facilitates the construction of future hallucination benchmarks
029            with long and complex questions and descriptions. Extensive experiments over
030            multiple recent MLLMs reveal various new challenges when they are handling
031            hallucinations with long and complex textual data.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Dai et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024; ?; Bai et al.,
037   2023; Liu et al., 2023b; 2024a; Zhu et al., 2023) have achieved great progress in understanding
038   multi-modal contents, by generating detailed descriptions of images, conducting sophisticated,
039
      consecutive conversations with humans, etc. Despite the remarkable advancements, MLLMs often
      experience severe hallucination problems (Yin et al., 2023; Leng et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Zhu
040
      et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024) by generating textual responses that are not aligned
041
      with the corresponding image contents. While hallucinations significantly compromise MLLMs’
042   reliability and applicability in various vision-language tasks and applications, effective and efficient
043   measurement of the hallucination level of MLLMs has become a prerequisite for diagnosis and
044   mitigation of hallucination in MLLMs.
045
      Several related benchmarks (Yifan et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b;
046
      Lovenia et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; 2024a) have been proposed to gauge the hallucination level
047
      of MLLMs in two representative approaches. The first approach conducts discriminative evaluations,
048   where MLLMs are queried with simple questions about whether some objects exist in the image, as
049   illustrated in the upper part of Fig. 1. The second approach conducts generative evaluations, which
050   first apply MLLMs to describe the image and then adopt LLM evaluators to examine whether MLLMs
051   generate hallucinatory content. However, most existing benchmarks share several constraints: 1)
052   Most discriminative benchmarks merely require a yes-or-no answer, which is often too simple to
053   tell much on the cause of hallucinations. 2) Discriminative benchmarks usually come with very
      short questions like "Is there an {object} in the image?" which are oversimplified and insufficient for

      We design LongHalQA, an LLM-free hallucination benchmark that comprises 6K long and context-
      rich hallucination questions. LongHalQA is built from GPT4V-generated hallucinatory data that is
100
      well aligned with various real-world scenarios. It features two multiple-choice-question (MCQ) tasks,
101
      namely, hallucination discrimination and hallucination completion as illustrated at the bottom of
102   Fig. 1. Specifically, hallucination discrimination requires MLLMs to determine whether the given
103   text contains hallucinations and pick the right causes of the hallucinations. Hallucination completion
104   instead transforms generative evaluations into a discriminative task, asking MLLMs to continue
105   the text and pick the right option that does not contain hallucinations. LongHalQA thus unifies
106   discriminative and generative evaluations into the same MCQ form, assessing MLLMs’ understanding
107   of hallucinations and their tendency to generate hallucinations concurrently. LongHalQA queries
      come in three data formats as illustrated in the middle of Fig. 1, including object-level descriptions,

      image-level descriptions, and multi-round conversation, which are much longer and complex, covering
109   a wide range of 12 types of hallucinations. Such long and complex questions allow LongHalQA to
110   gauge the hallucination levels of MLLMs in more practical applications and scenarios. Compared
111   to existing generative benchmarks, we demonstrate that our MCQ hallucination completion task
112   exhibits similar trends to free-form generative evaluation. Additionally, LonghalQA achieves much
113   higher speed in evaluating MLLMs generative hallucination, especially for extremely large models,
114   facilitating the expansion of evaluation samples and the need for fast testing and evolution of MLLMs.
115   Additionally, we propose LongHallGen, an automated pipeline for Long-context Hallucination Data
116   Generation. LongHallGen is featured with a set of prompt templates for GPT4v that allow generating
117   hallucination data and converting the generated data into multiple-choice-questions automatically. By
118   modifying the prompt sets, LongHallGen can adjust the type of generated hallucinations, content
119   topics, and data formats. We believe that LongHallGen will serve as a strong basis while creating new
120   or expanding existing hallucination datasets for evaluating and training MLLMs in future research.
121   Based on LongHalQA, we evaluate ten mainstream MLLMs on long-context hallucinations and
122   provide a comprehensive analysis. The evaluations reveal constraints of MLLMs in discerning
123   and explaining hallucination in long texts, as well as in generating hallucinatory content when
124   completing long texts. Additionally, we observe that the Chain-Of-Thought (COT), a simple but
125   effective hallucination mitigation method (Jiang et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024), is effective for short
126   queries and generative hallucinations but degrades the performance of most MLLMs on long-context
127   hallucinations discrimination in LongHalQA, especially for those with small sizes, This suggests that
128   COT may be limited by MLLMs’ capability on long context processing. We believe that LongHalQA
129
      will serve as a basis for mitigating long-context hallucinations in various real-world MLLM tasks.

131   2     R ELATED W ORKS
132
133   Hallucination Benchmarks for MLLMs. Various benchmarks have been proposed to measure
134   the hallucination level of MLLMs, including both discriminative and generative benchmarks. For
135   discriminative benchmarks, POPE (Yifan et al., 2023), CIEM (Hu et al., 2023), AMBER (Wang
136   et al., 2023), NOPE (Lovenia et al., 2023), and MME (Fu et al., 2023) query MLLMs with simple
137
      questions about the existence or attributes of specific objects in images. PhD (Liu et al., 2024b)
      and Hal-Eval (Jiang et al., 2024) introduce more types of intrinsic hallucinations into evaluations,
138
      such as multi-modal conflicting or event hallucinations. Most of these benchmarks feature simple
139
      and short questions and seek solely simple binary "yes" or "no" answers. Generative evaluations
140   introduce LLM evaluators to analyze hallucinations in MLLM-generated text. Various generative
141   benchmarks (Jiang et al., 2024; Kaul et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023) are proposed to
142   improve the scope of evaluated hallucinations and the efficiency and accuracy of LLM evaluators.
143   Our LonghalQA takes a different perspective on long-context hallucinations and encompasses both
144   discriminative and generative evaluations in a unified and efficient MCQ format.

149   text: hallucination discrimination and hallucination completion. This section presents the task format
150   in Sec. 3.1, the data format and distributions in Sec. 3.2, and the evaluation metrics in Sec. 3.3.

189   Hallucination Completion. Previous generative benchmarks require MLLMs to generate descrip-
190   tions for the image and employ LLM evaluators to score descriptions. To obviate the slow generation
191
      process and costly LLM evaluations, we transform generative evaluations into MCQ format, as shown
      in Fig. 1. Specifically, we provide an image and a related incomplete description or conversation and
192
      ask MLLMs to continue the text. Four answer choices of possible completing sentences are provided,
193
      with one correct choice and three hallucinatory choices. Compared to generative benchmarks based
194   on LLM evaluators, the format of generative MCQ significantly reduces evaluation costs and allows
195   for more detailed annotation and analysis of hallucination data. LongHalQA comes with 2149
196   samples for the hallucination completion task.
197
      The MCQ hallucination completion task simulates the sampling process in MLLM inference, where
198
      MLLMs first generate several potential outputs and then select the most appropriate one free from
199
      hallucinations. Furthermore, the hallucination completion task can be adapted into a free-form
200   continuation task, where MLLMs are prompted to freely continue long-context data. Our experiments
201   demonstrate that the MCQ format of the hallucination completion task and the free-form generation
202   format yield similar trends in evaluating generative hallucinations of MLLMs.

205   3.2     DATA F ORMAT AND D ISTRIBUTION
206
207   This section presents the format of long-context hallucination data from two aspects, namely, data
208   formats and types of hallucinations, more details to be elaborated in the ensuing subsections.
209
      Data Formats. As shown in Tab. 1, LongHalQA consists of three formats of image-text hallucinatory
210   data, including 1370 Object-level Description, 1372 Image-level Description, and 1604 Multi-round
211   Conversation. Specifically, Object-level Description describes a specific object in the image, such as
212   its attributes, states, or relations with other objects. Image-level Description covers the main contents
213   and more details of an image, such as objects, background, weather, etc, in one paragraph. For the
214   Multi-round Conversation, we simulate a human user who communicates with an assistant, querying
215   the image content. The three types of data formats are highly compatible with the actual application
      scenarios of MLLMs and thus can better simulate real hallucination situations.

255   3.3   E VALUATION M ETHODS AND M ETRICS
256
257   We adopt both binary and multiple-choice settings for the Hallucination Discriminiation task, and
258
      multiple-choice setting for Hallucination Completion task. For binary answers, we use Accuracy,
      Precision, and "Yes" ratios as metrics following previous practices (Yifan et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,
259
      2024). For multiple-choice setting, we adopt (mc-)accuracy (Liu et al., 2023d) as the evaluation
260
      metric, which requires MLLMs to generate the letter (e.g., A, B, C, or D) of the answer option. We
261   randomly shuffle the order of the four options for each MCQ to reduce the impact of option order.

      Image Collection and Filtering. To generate informative hallucinatory data, the first step involves
288   selecting images that contain rich content and discarding those with overly simple scenes or rare
289   objects. This can be simply achieved by leveraging off-the-shelf image understanding techniques. We
290   adopt images from the validation set of VisualGenome (Krishna et al., 2017) and Objects365 (Shao
291   et al., 2019) to avoid images being used for training MLLMs, and then analyze and filter them based
292   on dataset annotations and GroundingDINO (Liu et al., 2023c). This straightly leads to 1200 images
293   with complex contents for further hallucinatory data generation.
294   Positive Data Generation. With selected images, long-context data can be generated by leveraging
295   state-of-the-art MLLMs. This process involves designing certain prompts according to specific
296   tasks and domains. We adopt GPT4V to generate long-context texts. Unified prompt templates are
297   designed to allow adjusting the format and scope of generated texts, as illustrated in Appendix B.
298   Note current MLLMs, even GPT4V, suffer from severe hallucinations while generating long-context
299   data. A hallucination check process is required to ensure the quality of the generated data.
300   Hallucination Check. The MLLM-generated data are then examined comprehensively for detecting
301   any inherent hallucinations. The examination can be achieved in three steps. First, the generated
302   data undergo a per-sentence self-check by GPT4V, where each piece of data is checked twice to
303   reduce randomness. Second, names of objects present in the data are extracted, and certain image
304   understanding tools such as GroundingDINO (Liu et al., 2023c) are then conducted with detection
305   results feeding to GPT4V for further checking. Finally, the summarized analysis are revised manually,
306   and the revised analysis can serve for the generation of hallucination-explanation data pairs.
307   Though MLLMs such as GPT-4V can generate lengthy descriptions, they tend to generate numerous
308   hallucinations as well. Take LongHallQA as an example. Among its generated descriptions for the
309   500 images on image-level descriptions, 394 descriptions (78.8%) contain at least one hallucination.
310   The ratio goes up to 82.4% for the generated conversations. One major cause of the high hallucination
311   rate is due to the increased length of the generated descriptions. In addition, most selected images
312   in LongHalQA have complex scenes, further boosting the possibility of descriptive hallucinations.
313   Nevertheless, such data realistically simulate the hallucinations in actual applications of MLLMs.
314   Hallucination-Explanation Pair Generation. Two different prompts are formulated to construct
315   hallucination-explanation (HE) data pairs with the checking outcome. For the data without hallucina-
316   tions detected, GPT4V is prompted to modify the data to produce a misleading error within the range
317   of hallucination types suggested in the prompt. For the data containing hallucinations, GPT-4V is
318   prompted to modify the data to contain only one error to form HE pairs. The generated HE pairs are
319   then adopted to construct MCQs for MLLM evaluations.
320   Question and Answer Generation. With the generated HE pairs, MLLMs such as GPT-4V can be
321   employed to generate questions for tasks such as hallucination discrimination and hallucination com-
322   pletion in LongHalQA. For discriminative tasks, questions like ’Does the following {Hallucination
323   Data} match the image?’ can be formulated to prompt MLLMs to generate four candidate options
      with explanations. For completion tasks with questions like ’Complete the following {Hallucination

452   We conduct a detailed analysis of different types of hallucinations in the discrimination task as shown

      From the perspective of MLLMs, MiniCPM-V2-2B and Qwen-VL-Chat show balanced strength

      across different types of hallucinations. Fuyu-8B is competitive across multiple types, but struggles

458   types of hallucination, especially for object location (H6) and Text/Sign semantic meanings (H8).

      5.5   P ROMPT A NALYSIS
461
462   We additionally examine the Chain-Of-Thought (COT) on LongHalQA, which has been verified in
463   previous benchmarks (Qian et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) for mitigating hallucinations. We modify
464   the prompt to guide MLLMs to think step by step, and provide more instructions, such as possible
465   types of hallucinations and suggestions for per-sentence verification (Refer to Appendix C for details).
466   As shown in Tab. 7, apart from GPT-4o, most MLLMs experience a drop in performance across
467   different tasks when using Chain of Thought (COT)—notably, the larger the language model, the
468   smaller the performance drop. Qwen2-VL-72B only experienced a drop in the multiple-choice task
469   for hallucination Discrimination, with an average increase of 2.18 accuracy. We conjecture that this is
470
      largely due to the limited capability of MLLMs to interpret long-context data. Besides, the modified
      prompts improve short query discrimination and hallucination completion the most but have little
471
      effect on discriminate hallucinations in long texts.

525   7   C ONCLUSION
526
527   This paper presents LongHalQA, a novel benchmark with long-context data for evaluating MLLMs’
528
      level of hallucinations in more practical scenarios. LongHalQA consists of 6.4k question-answer
      pairs with long-context data covering 12 types of hallucination. It features two multiple-choice
529
      tasks: hallucination recognition and hallucination completion, implementing both discriminative and
530
      generative hallucination evaluation in one unified format. It also offers additional assessments of
531   the causes of hallucinations without involving LLM evaluators as in existing benchmarks. We also
532   propose an automated pipeline for generating long-context hallucination data. Extensive tests reveal
533   the constraints of existing MLLMs in handling long-context hallucinations, showing the necessity for
534   more research on robust MLLMs with respect to long-context hallucinations.

      We present the definitions of different types of hallucinations as follows. Some examples are shown

           H1 (Non) Existent Objects. The described objects do not exist in the given image.

           H2 Object Attributes. The appearances (shape, pattern, etc.), types, or other attributes of
              objects are incorrectly described.
658
659        H3 Object Color. The colors of objects are incorrectly described. (Due to the high number
660           of hallucinations of object color descriptions, we list "object color" separately from object
661
              attributes.)
662        H4 Object States. The states of objects, such as movement, orientation, the actions of the
663           person, etc, are incorrectly described.
664        H5 Number of Objects. The number of objects is incorrectly stated.
665        H6 Object Locations. The locations of objects in the image are incorrectly described.
666
           H7 Object Relationships. The relations or relative positions between two or multiple objects

672           for example, indoors, outdoors, rural, urban, bookstore, food market, etc.
673       H10 Background Description. The descriptions of activities, scenes, objects, etc., in the
674           background of the image are hallucinatory. Examples of descriptions include the presence of
675           mountains, buildings, skyscrapers people in the background, or the description of no visible
676           people in the background.

678           judgment of the time without clear evidence.
