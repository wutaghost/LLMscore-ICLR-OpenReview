009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning trustworthy and reliable offline policies presents significant challenges
012            due to the inherent uncertainty in pre-collected datasets. In this paper, we propose
013            a novel offline reinforcement learning method to tackle this issue. Inspired by the
014            concepts of Lyapunov stability and control-invariant sets from control theory, the
015            central idea is to introduce a restricted state space for the agent to operate within.
016            This approach allows the learned models to exhibit reduced Bellman uncertainty
017            and make reliable decisions. To achieve this, we regulate the expected Bellman
018
               uncertainty associated with the new policy, ensuring that its growth trend in subse-
               quent states remains within acceptable limits. The resulting method, termed Lya-
019
               punov Uncertainty Control (LUC), is shown to guarantee that the agent remains
020
               within a low-uncertainty state enclosure throughout its entire trajectory. Further-
021            more, we perform extensive theoretical and experimental analysis to showcase the
022            effectiveness and feasibility of the proposed LUC.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Offline reinforcement learning (RL) allows policy learning from historical data without real-world
028   interaction. However, ensuring reliable sequential decision-making from offline data poses a signifi-
029   cant challenge in practical applications. For example, in healthcare (Tang & Wiens, 2021), a reliable
030   diagnostic agent requires avoiding unfamiliar approaches that may introduce errors in subsequent
031   procedures. Similar requirements exist in fields such as autonomous driving (Kiran et al., 2022),
032   robotics control (Lobbezoo et al., 2021), and others.
033   The reliability of offline RL is undermined by the risk of deviating from the scoped safe regions,
034   i.e., stable safe control (Kang et al., 2022) from offline data. More precisely, our aim is to stop the
035   offline-learned agent from entering areas that could cause severe consequences after deployment.
036   Specifically, in practical applications, the safety requirements for decision making are extremely
037   strict (Jiang et al., 2023), demanding that every decision made by the agent at each step be safe.
038   Meanwhile, current methods like the pessimistic and DRRL methods fall short in handling this
039
      issue. For instance, Pessimistic methods such as MOPO (Yu et al., 2020), PBRL (Bai et al., 2022)
      and RORL (Yang et al., 2022) mainly concentrate on making the agent act in accordance with the
040
      demonstrations in the dataset. Although these methods are good at quantifying the OOD data, they
041
      may not entirely fulfill the previously mentioned safety requirements. On the other hand, Robust
042   RL methods (Panaganti et al., 2022; Shi & Chi, 2024) aim to improve the agent’s capacity to deal
043   with distributional shift by establishing the uncertainty set of the transition function and optimizing
044   the lower bound of the policy’s long term returns under the worst case scenario within this set.
045   Unfortunately, Robust RL also fails to take into account the safety issue described earlier. In other
046   words, they are unable to prevent the learned agent from straying from the safe region.
047
      Inspired by control-invariant sets in control theory (Kerrigan, 2000; Richter & Roy, 2017), where a
048   closed region is delineated in the state space to offer a reliable working environment for the agent,
049   a recent method named Lyapunov Density Model (Kang et al., 2022) defines a region based on
050   data density distribution to ensure adequate data support for the agent during operation. However,
051   setting a reliable region based on a common and pessimistic density criterion overlooks the issue of
052   performance imbalances in complex environments, where data requirements for achieving a certain
053   performance level may vary across regions. For example, in autonomous driving, data needs for
      learning on a smooth highway differ significantly from those on a rugged mountain road. Instead,

075

076   uncertainty regions that serve as safe zones. Drawing inspiration from the control Lyapunov func-

078   We implement our Lyapunov Uncertainty Control (LUC) method using a standard deviation-based
079   uncertainty measure that relies on Q-ensembles, as described in Bai et al. (2022). Theoretically,
080   we demonstrate that our approach can confine the learned agent to operate within a low-uncertainty
081   state enclosure, resulting in secure and reliable trajectories. Furthermore, in certain scenarios, it
082   can enhance the minimum performance bound of the new policy. Finally, we conduct extensive ex-
083   periments to showcase the effectiveness and feasibility of LUC across various tailored benchmarks,
084
      including out-of-distribution (OOD) benchmarks and those with poor demonstrations.
085   The subsequent sections, after a brief review of related works, Section 3 present a concise overview
086   of the fundamental concepts in offline RL. Section 4 elaborates on the LUC methodology, providing
087   a detailed theoretical analysis of its effectiveness and implementation insights. Section 5 presents

089   summarizes the findings and contributions, along with a brief discussion on limitations.

092
093
      Offline RL algorithms. Dealing with distributional shift poses a significant challenge for offline
094
      RL algorithms. Previous works, including CQL (Kumar et al., 2020), BEAR (Kumar et al., 2019),
095
      and BRAC (Wu et al., 2019), have aimed to tackle this issue by integrating conservative principles to
096   prevent out-of-distribution (OOD) actions. However, these methods mainly concentrate on reducing
097   the disparity between the new policy and the behavior policy that gathered the dataset. On the
098   other hand, approaches like Implicit Q-Learning (IQL) (Kostrikov et al., 2022) entirely avoid OOD
099   queries for actions during TD target estimation. Nonetheless, these methods heavily depend on the
100   action distribution of the behavior policy, leading to a lack of generalization capability.
101
102   Pessimistic offline RL. Pessimistic offline RL algorithms introduce Bellman uncertainty quan-
103   tification (Jin et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021) to determine reliable actions for generalizing to OOD
104   data. This method has exhibited significant efficacy in model-based offline RL algorithms such as
105   Model-based Offline Policy Optimization (Yu et al., 2020) and MOdel-Bellman Inconsistency penal-
106   ized offLinE Policy Optimization (MOBILE)(Sun et al., 2023), as well as in model-free algorithms
107   like Pessimistic Bootstrapping for offline RL (PBRL)Bai et al. (2022), Ensemble-Diversified Actor
      Critic (EDAC)An et al. (2021) and Robust Offline RL (RORL)(Yang et al., 2022). However, this

108
      study reveals that solely managing uncertainty at the current step is insufficient to ensure reliability
109   and safety, given the short-sightedness regarding the decision’s potential outcomes.
110
111   Consequence-driven offline RL. Consequence-driven offline RL methods have been developed
112   to address the state distributional shift issue utilizing the concept of state recovery(Zhang et al.,
113   2022; Jiang et al., 2023). State Deviation Correction(Zhang et al., 2022) involves pre-training a
114   forward dynamics model to facilitate the recovery process, whereas Out-of-sample State Recovery
115   (OSR) (Jiang et al., 2023) employs an inverse dynamics model to implicitly execute the concept.
116   While these techniques assist in rectifying the agent’s behavior deviations from reliable regions,
117   their unreliability in OOD states may hinder their success rates. In contrast, our LUC approach can
118   be thought of as a more robust method which not only considers the reliability of the immediate
119
      consequence of executing a policy but that of its long term consequence.

122   Robust Reinforcement Learning. Robust RL’s pessimism stems from penalizing with uncer-
123   tainty in the outcome predictions of actions, to deal with the problem of distributional shift(Panaganti
124   et al., 2022; Shi & Chi, 2024). However, if some behaviors that could lead to the deviation from the

126
      its effectiveness due to that the uncertainty in outcome predictions would be tiny, thereby exacerbat-

127
      to prevent the learned agent from straying from the safe region, i.e., the safety requirements.

      4.1   O PERATING WITHIN R ELIABLE R EGIONS BY LYAPUNOV P OLICY
163
164   In this section, we formally define our conceptual framework for Lyapunov Uncertainty Control.
165   Specifically, analogous to control-invariant sets in control theory, we first define reliable regions in
166   the state space where the policy π can operate effectively. Previous methods defined these regions
167   using density models (Kang et al., 2022); however, as mentioned earlier, a local region may not
168   be reliable even with high density due to the complexity and nonlinearity of the underlying envi-
169   ronment. Instead, we introduce a measurement based on epistemic uncertainty, denoted as ζf (s, a).
170
      The precise computational method for ζf (s, a) will be presented later; however, it is a positive scalar
      that is closely related to the agent’s current knowledge, reflecting the generalization capability of the
171
      learned value function f at the input data (s, a). If f generalizes well, ζf (s, a) will be small; con-
172
      versely, if f does not generalize well, ζf (s, a) will be large.
173
174   Using ζf (s, a), we can evaluate whether an induced policy π can make reliable decisions at a given
175   state s by assessing the uncertainty of the learned value function f . Furthermore, we derive the
176
      following definition of the f − π reliable region in Definition 1:
177   Definition 1. (f − π reliable region). Given an arbitrary value function f , policy π and a threshold
178   c, we define the f − π reliable region over the state space,
179                                            Gf (π) = {s|ζf (s, π) ≤ c}                                  (2)
180
      where ζf (s, π) = Ea∼π(·|s) ζf (s, a).
181
182   As shown in Figure 1, where ’low-uncertainty region’ illustrates the agent’s f − π reliable regions.
183   If the agent operates beyond its reliable regions, it would accumulate decision errors, finally failing
184   the task. On the other hand, if an agent with policy π always operate within its f −π reliable regions,
185   we call this policy a reliable policy, defined in Definition 2.
186   Definition 2. (Reliable policy). A policy π is reliable if it satisfies that ∀s ∈ D ∩ Gf (π), if ∀t, st ∈
187   supp(P (st |s0 = s, π)), then st ∈ Gf (π).
188
      Furthermore, a Lyapunov policy, as defined in Definition 3, not only manages uncertainty at the
189
      current step but also addresses the tendencies of these uncertainty one step ahead. In other words, a
190
      Lyapunov policy is capable of controlling current step uncertainty to encompass reliable regions over
191   the state space, while also restricting one-step forward uncertainty to ensure trajectory reliability.
192

343   important to control the one-step forward growth tendency of the Bellman uncertainty along with
344   the whole trajectory, which is the main contribution of this paper. This helps the learned value
345   function to be more likely to converge to the fixed point of the empirical Bellman operator, which is
346   hence for controlling the performance lower bound of the learned agent.

      6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This paper aims to identify a reliable operational region for the agent based on offline data. To
534   achieve this, we introduce the Lyapunov Uncertainty Control (LUC) algorithm in an offline, model-
535   free manner. Theoretically, in deterministic MDPs or when the dataset fully covers all dynamic
536   modes, LUC can confine the agent’s operations within low-uncertainty areas, thereby enhancing
537   decision-making reliability. Empirically, LUC-trained agents demonstrate superior robustness and
538   reliability in high-risk scenarios compared to various other methods. In future works, LUC can
539   serve as a versatile tool in diverse offline reinforcement learning frameworks, including model-based
      approaches, potentially paving the way for new research opportunities.

1204   C.2   D IFFERENCES BETWEEN LUC AND ROBUST RL
1205
1206   Robust RL methods, although they seem to share a similar form to our method (Lyapunov uncer-
1207   tainty control), it is crucial to note the fundamental differences between them and some technical
1208   tips:
1209
       1) Objective and motivation. Robust RL still falls short of addressing the safety issue we mentioned
1210   before. Specifically, the pessimism of Robust RL stems from penalizing with uncertainty in the
1211   outcome predictions of actions, to deal with the problem of distributional shift. However, if some
1212   behaviors that could lead to the deviation from the safe regions are supported well by dataset, the
1213   penalty loses its effectiveness due to that the uncertainty in outcome predictions would be tiny,
1214   thereby exacerbating the risk of entering high-uncertainty regions. While in our method, we aim to
1215   learn the policy to satisfy the Lyapunov reliable properties, of which we has shown the effectiveness
1216   in stable safe control in both theoretical and experimental ways.
1217   2) Penalization mechanism. Robust RL’s penalty is reward-driven, aiming to maximize the expected
1218   cumulative return under the worst case to deal with transitioned distributional shift. On the other
1219   hand, our method places greater emphasis on the safety of the agent’s decisions, aiming to minimize
1220   the risk of deviation from the safe regions under the worst case to achieve stable safety control for the
1221   agent. From this view, we can conclude that the Robust methods focus on the agent’s generalization
1222   performance in solving offline RL problems, while our method prioritizes stable control of the agent
1223   to meet safety requirements in practical applications.
1224
1225
       C.3   D IFFERENCES BETWEEN LUC AND EDAC
1226
1227
       There are several key differences between our method, LUC, and the EDAC (An et al., 2021) method
1228
       in terms of motivation, implementation, and effectiveness, summarized as follows:
1229
1230   1) Motivation: The motivation behind the EDAC method lies in enhancing the sensitivity of Q-
1231   ensembles to out-of-distribution (OOD) data. Therefore, the EDAC could be seen as a traditional
1232   pessimistic method. Pessimistic methods that focus only on having the agent behave like the demon-
1233
       strations present in the dataset - while helpful in the quantification for the OOD data, do not fully
       meet the safety requirements as mentioned before. This is because these methods often focus on
1234
       an average effect, biasing the agent towards regions with higher data coverage or better model per-
1235
       formance, without strictly constraining the agent’s activities within the safety region. This can be
1236   observed from Theorem 1 in this paper, where both rules defined in Definition 3 are essential for
1237   Lyapunov reliability: controlling the action uncertainty of current step and the growth of uncertainty
1238   in the future. Unfortunately, previous pessimistic methods only control the former, while ignoring
1239   the problem of uncertainty accumulation, failing to satisfy the Lyapunov reliability. While in our
1240   method, Q-ensembles serve as a tool to help us measure the safety of the model at certain data
1241   points, scoping a safe region for the agent to operate within. These two methods address completely
       different issues.


                                                          23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       2) Implementation: While the EDAC method, similar to traditional pessimistic methods, primarily
1243   penalizes the agent for selecting OOD actions, our method not only penalizes such actions but
1244   also considers whether choosing a specific action would lead to an increase in uncertainty, thereby
1245   preventing the agent from entering regions of high uncertainty (unreliability).
1246
       3) Effectiveness: Experimental results indicate that compared to traditional pessimistic methods like
1247
       EDAC (such as PBRL, RORL, etc.), our method demonstrates greater reliability when facing highly
1248
       stochastic real-world environments (see Section 5 of our paper).

1251   C.4   D IFFERENCES BETWEEN LUC AND CQL
1252
1253   When comparing our method to conservative approaches like CQL (Kumar et al., 2020), several key
1254   differences can be identified:
1255   1) OOD quantification: Conservative methods like CQL lack the ability to quantitatively measure
1256   the degree of out-of-distribution (OOD) data, leading to the rejection of all unseen data and reducing
1257   their generalization capabilities. In contrast, our method evaluates OOD data based on uncertainty,
1258   assessing the reliability of the model at that specific OOD data point, thus enabling it to generalize
1259   effectively on OOD data.
1260
       2) Safety assessment of consequences/long-term trends: CQL still focuses on penalizing OOD ac-
1261   tions without considering the outcomes’ safety of these actions, making it challenging to ensure
1262   the agent’s operation regions in practical deployment and potentially resulting in trajectory devia-
1263   tions (Zhang et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2022). On the other hand, our method defines
1264   a closed region where the agent can operate stably, suitable for scenarios requiring higher reliability
1265   and safety in decision-making, such as autonomous driving and healthcare applications. This could
1266   also be seen in the experimental results in our paper, where we have compared our method LUC
1267   with CQL from various benchmarks.
