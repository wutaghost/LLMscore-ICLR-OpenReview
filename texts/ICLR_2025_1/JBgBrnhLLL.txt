009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose zero order diffusion guidance, a method that allows using a diffusion
012            model to solve inverse problems without access to the gradients of the process
013            we seek to invert. Our method employs a zero-order gradient estimator combined
014            with a novel differentiable dimensionality reduction strategy to approximate true
015            gradients during guidance while keeping the task computationally tractable in
016            thousands of dimensions. We apply our method to model inversion and demonstrate
017            how it can be used to reconstruct high-quality faces in a realistic scenario where the
018
               adversary has only black-box access to face embeddings. Across a range of inverse
               problems—including synthetic experiments and JPEG restoration—we show that
019
               access to gradients is not necessary for effective guidance. Our black-box method
020
               matches white-box performance, thus expanding the scope of inverse problems
021            that can be solved with diffusion-based approaches.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Diffusion models have emerged as powerful generative models capable of generating realistic images,
027   audio, and text (Rombach et al., 2021). Central to their success is their controllability, i.e., their
028   ability to use gradient information from a differentiable guidance function to steer the output towards
029   a set of desirable properties (Ho & Salimans, 2022). This capability is important for solving inverse
030   problems (e.g., colorization (Saharia et al., 2022), MRI processing (Kazerouni et al., 2023), 3D
031   reconstruction (Anciukevičius et al., 2023)) as well as for generative design (e.g., producing biological
032
      molecules with desired properties) (Chung & Ye, 2022; Rombach et al., 2021; 2022; Lugmayr et al.,
      2022; Poole et al., 2022).
033
034   However, obtaining gradient information useful for guidance may be challenging. In many inverse
035   problems, the process that we seek to invert may be complex, non-differentiable, or available only as
036   a black box. For example, in security applications, we may be working with sensitive information
037   from data obfuscated by a black-box program (Duong et al., 2020). In generative design settings, the
038
      data we work with may be discrete (e.g., language, proteins, DNA), and thus the underlying guidance
      process does not have gradients (Sahoo et al., 2024; Austin et al., 2021).
039
040   Here, we introduce zero order diffusion guidance, an algorithm that only assumes input-output
041   access to a guidance function, and not its gradients. Our key idea is to approximate classifier-based
042   guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) by using zero-order gradient estimators.
043   Out of the box, zero-order methods are too slow for guidance: we propose two types of estimators
044   combined with novel dimensionality reduction and error correction strategies that make zero-order
045   methods practical for classifier-based guidance without an excessive increase in computation time.
046   The resulting method Zero ORder Optimization for classifier guidance (ZORO) is simple, easy-to-
047   implement, and matches the performance of white-box guidance.
048   We demonstrate the applicability of ZORO across two application areas: black-box inverse problems
049   and non-differentiable guidance. We first use our method to study the security of face recognition
050   systems and propose a strategy for inverting a black-box face embedding model to recover human
051   faces from their embeddings (Duong et al., 2020). We show that zero order guidance with a custom
052   sampler yields an inversion attack that is both effective (i.e., it recovers high-quality images) and
053   easier to stage than previous, GAN-based, black-box inversion methods, and that sheds light on
      vulnerabilities of face embedding models. Additionally, we utilize ZORO for JPEG restoration (Li

228   3.3   ZORO: Z ERO -O RDER O PTIMIZATION FOR C LASSIFIER -BASED G UIDANCE
229
230   Motivating Example. Consider the specific case where c is the output of a face embedding model
231   f , and x is an unknown image of a face. In this setting, searching for the image with an embedding
232   that minimizes the loss ℓ(f (x), c) = − cos(f (x), c) should, in theory, let us recover the face from
233   its embedding. Classifier-based diffusion guidance has been quite successful at solving such inverse
234   problems (Rombach et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2022a). However, this specific task of facial embedding
235   inversion is more challenging because (3) isn’t directly applicable. This is due to the intractability of
236
      computing the classifier gradients ∇x log qϕ (c|x) = ∇x cos(f (x), c) when we only have black-box
      access to the embedding model f (x).
237
238
239   Zero-Order Classifier Guidance. To address the aforementioned challenge, we propose ZORO,
240
      that uses Zero ORder Optimization for classifier guidance in diffusion models. Our method approxi-
      mates the classifier gradients using zero-order methods as detailed in Equation 9. ZORO combines
241
      zero order classifier-based guidance with classifier-free guidance as follows:
242
243                                                                           ˆ x log qϕ (c|x),
                 ∇x log pt (x|c) ≈ αu ∇x log pθ (x) + αf ∇x log pθ (x|c) + αc ∇                           (10)
244
245   where αu , αf , αc ∈ R denote the strengths of the unconditional, classifier-free, and classifier-
246   based guidance terms respectively and ∇     ˆ x log qϕ (c|x) denotes the zero order approximation of the
247   true gradient ∇x log qϕ (c|x). This formulation allows for the simultaneous use of both traditional
248   classifier-free guidance and our zero order classifier-based guidance, without the need to compute any
249   gradients. Our approach deviates from the conventional diffusion guidance methods, which typically
250   use either classifier-based or classifier-free guidance exclusively. As shown in Table 6, we empirically
251
      demonstrate that combining both types of guidance is essential for achieving optimal performance.

337   4.2   FACIAL EMBEDDING INVERSION
338
339   We next evaluate our method in a real-world scenario, focusing on reconstructing facial im-
340   ages from their embeddings using a face embedding model. For this experiment, we selected
341   the ElasticFace-Cos model, referred to as ElasticFace, from Boutros et al. (2022). ElasticFace
342   is one of the most widely used open-source models for facial recognition and provides both an
343   open-source implementation and pre-trained model weights. Like other popular models, ElasticFace
344   is trained to maximize the cosine similarity between embeddings of facial images from the same
345   individual.
346   To create our test dataset, we embedded all images from the FFHQ dataset using ElasticFace, resulting
347   in pairs of original images and their corresponding embeddings (x, f (x)). We train our diffusion
348   model to generate x conditioned on f (x). Following Ho & Salimans (2022), we replace f (x) with ∅
349   with 10% probability during training to aid classifier free guidance. In preliminary experiments on
350   a small batch of images, we found that setting αf = 10 and αc = 1000 in (10) produced the best
351   cosine similarity.
352   We compare the performance of our method against several baselines: the oracle, the classifier-based
353   guidance diffusion approach of Kansy et al. (2023), the unconditional generation from a diffusion
354   model as in Karras et al. (2022), and the embedding inversion method of Vendrow & Vendrow
355   (2021), which uses a Generative Adversarial Network (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Detailed metrics are
356   provided in Table 2. We observe that our method outperforms all the baselines and achieves results
357   comparable to those of the oracle, even in this challenging setting.
358   In Table 6, we ablate different components of ZORO. First, we observe that using downsampling +
359   δ correction (9) as a dimensionality reduction technique outperforms using an autoencoder. Next

480   5   R ELATED W ORK
481
482   Diffusion models for inverse problems. Diffusion models have been successfully applied to
483   solve a variety of inverse problems, such as text-to-image generation (Rombach et al., 2021; 2022),
484   inpainting (Chung et al., 2022b), colorization (Chung et al., 2022a), and super-resolution (Chung et al.,
485   2023). Song et al. (2023) use classifier guidance for non-differentiable functions f , but unlike our
      method, they require defining a pseudo-inverse f † for the non-invertible function f . However, such a


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      method is not applicable to tasks like embedding inversion, where the function f is parameterized by
487   a neural network and defining f † is non-trivial.
488
489
      Zero-order methods. Zero-order methods have been proposed for generating adversarial examples
490   for vision classifiers (Chen et al., 2017; Andriushchenko et al., 2020). Tu et al. (2020) show that
491   downsampling the input with an autoencoder can reduce the number of black-box queries required
492   without compromising performance. In contrast to these works, we propose the use of zero-order
493   methods for classifier-based guidance in diffusion models. More recently, zero-order methods
494   have been proposed for other tasks, such as model stealing (Chen et al., 2019) and training large
495   models (Chen et al., 2023). We are the first to apply zero-order methods to provide classifier-based
496   guidance to diffusion models.
497
498   Model inversion. Inverting the representations of neural networks has been widely explored,
499   especially in computer vision (Mahendran & Vedaldi, 2014; Dosovitskiy & Brox, 2016), as discussed
500   in the general case by Morris et al. (2023). Zhmoginov & Sandler (2016) was the first to attempt
501
      inverting embeddings from facial attribute embedding models specifically. Most recently, diffusion
      models with classifier-free guidance have been proposed for solving this problem (Kansy et al., 2023).
502
503
504   6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
505
506   We introduced ZORO, a method that enables classifier guidance for diffusion models when only
507   black-box access to the classifier is available. ZORO approximates the gradients through the black-
508   box classifier using zero-order methods. We propose a novel error correction strategy in Sec. 3.2 that
509   improves the scalability of the zero-order method without the need for additional neural networks,
510   unlike prior works.
511   We demonstrate the application of ZORO in two key areas: first, in solving black-box inverse
512   problems where the gradient ∇x ℓ(f (x), c) is defined but inaccessible (e.g., when the model is only
513   accessible via an API), and second, in providing non-differentiable guidance in JPEG restoration,
514   where ZORO outperforms all baselines and matches the performance of the oracle. Additionally,
515   our method could potentially be useful for guiding diffusion models in scenarios where gradients
516   are undefined—such as with discrete structures like molecules and text (Nisonoff et al., 2024)—as
517   long as some signal is available to guide the model toward generating better solutions. We leave this
518
      exploration for future work.

797   C.1    JPEG R ESTORATION
798
799   By enabling differentiation through previously non-differentiable functions, we can now solve
800   challenging inverse problems, provided we can efficiently sample from them. JPEG restoration
801   serves as a practical example of such a problem. Images are often corrupted by excessive JPEG
802   compression, resulting in degradation that is difficult to reverse. While one could train a purpose-built
803   JPEG decompression algorithm, we propose to instead use a large, powerful, pretrained diffusion
804   model to undo this corruption.
805   In fact, we can adapt ZORO to recover the original image by leveraging the natural image prior
806   embedded within diffusion models. For these experiments, we subtly modify our sampling procedure.
807   Instead of starting the sampling process from timestep t = T = 100 , we begin by corrupting the
808   low-quality JPEG image to timestep t = 30 and then run the sampling process with αc = 1. Because
809   c already contains significant information about the input, it is unnecessary to start the sampling
      process from pure Gaussian noise at T = 100.
