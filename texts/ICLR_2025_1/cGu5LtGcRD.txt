011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent research indicates that using cross-entropy (CE) loss for value function
014
               learning surpasses traditional mean squared error (MSE) loss in performance and
               scalability, with the HL-Gaussian method showing notably strong results. How-
015
               ever, this method requires a pre-specified support for representing the categori-
016
               cal distribution of the value function, and an inappropriately chosen interval for
017            the support may not match the time-varying value function, potentially impeding
018            the learning process. To address this issue, we theoretically establish that HL-
019            Gaussian inherently introduces a projection error during the learning of the value
020            function, which is dependent on the support interval. We further prove that an
021            ideal interval should be sufficiently broad to reduce truncation-induced projec-
022            tion errors, yet not so excessive as to counterproductively amplify them. Guided
023            by these findings, we introduce the Adaptive HL-Gaussian (AHL-Gaussian) ap-
024            proach. This approach starts with a confined support interval and dynamically ad-
025
               justs its range by minimizing the projection error. This ensures that the interval’s
               size stabilizes to adapt to the learning value functions without further expansion.
026
               We integrate AHL-Gaussian into several classic value-based algorithms and evalu-
027
               ate it on Atari 2600 games and Gym Mujoco. The results show that AHL-Gaussian
028            significantly outperforms the vanilla baselines and standard HL-Gaussian with a
029            static interval across the majority of tasks.

      1    Introduction
034
035   Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has achieved significant success across various practical ap-
036   plications (Badia et al., 2020; Shah et al., 2022; Fawzi et al., 2022; Degrave et al., 2022; OpenAI,
037   2022), among which value-based methods (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2017) are the most widely
038   adopted frameworks. Within this framework, value functions are typically approximated using neu-
039
      ral networks and learned to fit the Bellman targets. A common approach for this is to employ mean
      squared error (MSE) as the regression objective (Mnih et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Fujimoto
040
      et al., 2018a). Alternatively, a class of methods (Bellemare et al., 2017; Dabney et al., 2018) mod-
041
      els the value function as a categorical distribution on a finite support, capturing its distributional
042   properties, and employs cross-entropy (CE) loss for learning as a classification objective. Recently,
043   Farebrother et al. (2024) reviewed three typical approaches within this paradigm and revealed that
044   using CE loss rather than MSE loss can significantly improve the training performance and exhibit
045   scaling law as model complexity increases. In particular, Farebrother et al. (2024) found that HL-
046   Gaussian (Imani & White, 2018), a specialized method among the three, which involves projecting
047   Bellman target scalar into a categorical distribution derived from Gaussian distribution, can produce
048   the most remarkable results. These empirical benefits have been attributed to several hypotheses, in-
049   cluding improved gradient stability (Ehsan Imani, 2024), better feature representation (Zhang et al.,
050
      2023), implicit biases (Stewart et al., 2023), and greater resilience to noisy targets and non-stationary
      environments (Farebrother et al., 2024).
051
052   However, HL-Gaussian and analogous approaches that represent value functions through categor-
053   ical distributions share a fundamental limitation: they necessitate a predetermined interval for the
      support, [vmin , vmax ], within which the value functions must be confined. Intuitively, the choice of

074
      Therefore, for HL-Gaussian to be effective in RL and achieve widespread adoption, the current static
075
      method of setting intervals, which relies on task-specific priors, is inadequate. A more promising
076   approach lies in developing a dynamic support adjustment mechanism that is both task-agnostic
077   and value-function-aware. This advancement would enable HL-Gaussian to reach its full potential
078   across a wide range of real-world applications.
079
      In this study, we explore the influence of the support interval [vmin , vmax ] on the learning of the value
080
      function within the HL-Gaussian approach. Initially, we establish that HL-Gaussian introduces
081
      a projection error during value function fitting, arising from the truncation of the Bellman target
082   within [vmin , vmax ] and its discretization into categorical distributions. Subsequently, we demonstrate
083   the relationship between these two types of errors and [vmin , vmax ]: when the Bellman target is within
084   [vmin , vmax ] and away from the boundaries, the overall projection error is minimal. Otherwise, the
085   truncation error is substantial. Additionally, the projection error rises linearly with the expansion
086   of [vmin , vmax ]. Therefore, we conclude that the ideal interval must be wide enough to minimize
087   truncation error but not so broad as to incur counterproductive projection error. Leveraging these
088   theoretical insights, we propose an approach that begins with a confined interval and dynamically
089   adjusts its range by optimizing the projection error. This method is task-agnostic and can adapt
090
      the interval dynamically based on the learning value function. We term this approach Adaptive
      HL-Gaussian (AHL-Gaussian).
091
092   We integrate AHL-Gaussian with several classic value-based algorithms, including DQN (Mnih
093   et al., 2015), SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018), and TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018b), and apply it to tasks
094   with both discrete action spaces (Atari 2600 games) and continuous action spaces (Gym Mujoco).
095   The results consistently show that across the majority of tasks, AHL-Gaussian, without relying on
096
      any prior knowledge of the tasks, greatly improves the performance of the original algorithms it
      is applied to, as well as the HL-Gaussian method that is specially fine-tune for each task. This
097
      performance is attributed to three key characteristics of AHL-Gaussian: (i) the universality of its
098
      mechanism, which is both task-agnostic and value function-aware; (ii) the modularity that facili-
099   tates flexible integration as a plug-in within a variety of algorithms; and (iii) the insensitivity to its
100   remaining parameters associated with HL-Gaussian, thus enhancing robustness.

307

310   when the Bellman targets stabilizes, ξ will also converges at a state that is sufficient to encompass all
311   Bellman targets without the impetus to continue expanding. At this point, the interval has achieved
312   a balance that is neither too large nor too small. Additionally, this method involves optimizing just
313   a single variable, making it computationally efficient and resulting in minimal extra computational
314   cost.

      4.1                       Observation Study of Projection Error

      Consider the interval [−500, 500]. Figure 3(a) demonstrates how the projection error varies as the
327   Bellman target µ shifts. It is evident that the error exhibits two distinct patterns based on µ’s relative
328   position to the interval. The error is minimal when µ is comfortably within [−500, 500]. However,
329   the error climbs as µ nears the interval’s boundaries. Once µ exceeds the interval, the error increases
330   linearly with the distance from the boundaries. This aligns perfectly with our primary finding (i),

437   4.3                         Comparative Study of AHL-Gaussian and Non-learning-based Strategies
438
439   In this section, we explore the possibility of implementing AHL-Gaussian without relying on learn-
440   ing mechanisms. A naive strategy would be to set ξ as the maximum value of all current Bellman
441   targets, multiplied by a coefficient η. This approach, while straightforward, aims to dynamically
442   adapt the interval in response to fluctuations in the value function. However, as shown in Fig-
443   ure 7, for both Ant-v2 and Hopper-v2 tasks, setting η to 1 results in a constrained interval range,
444
      which in turn, triggers substantial projection errors and inferior performance. Upon increasing η
      to 1.1, we observe a significant improvement in Ant-v2’s performance, suggesting that η correlates
445
      well with the escalating trend of the value function. Conversely, on Hopper-v2, this adjustment
446
      causes an unwarranted surge in the value function, leading to significant projection errors and sub-
447   par performance again. This observation implies that the coefficient η, being a hyperparameter, is
448   inherently task-specific and thus lacks the universal applicability that AHL-Gaussian offers across
449   various tasks.

      5                            Conclusion and Future Work
526
527   In this paper, we concentrate on value function learning methods that leverage HL-Gaussian. We
528   demonstrate that a misalignment between the support interval and the value function can result in
529   substantial projection errors, which in turn can compromise the optimality of the resulting policy.
530
      Our analysis further reveals that an ideal interval should be sufficiently broad to reduce truncation-
      induced projection errors, yet not so extensive as to paradoxically amplify them. Motivated by these
531
      findings, we introduce AHL-Gaussian, a novel dynamic interval adjustment mechanism designed
532
      to align with the dynamic evolution of the value function. Empirical results indicate that AHL-
533   Gaussian is compatible with a range of algorithms and can consistently boost performance across
534   both discrete and continuous control tasks.
535
536
      In our future work, we intend to broaden the application of the AHL-Gaussian approach to encom-
      pass more complex tasks. Furthermore, we aim to integrate it with a range of other distributional
537
      RL methods. We are also committed to investigating the adherence of AHL-Gaussian to a scaling
538
      law as the model’s complexity increases.

      A     Related Work
649
650
651   Distributional RL Distributional Reinforcement Learning (Distributional RL) marks a key ad-
652   vancement in reinforcement learning by modeling the distribution of returns instead of the expected
653   return. The C51 algorithm (Bellemare et al., 2017) models the value function using a categorical
654   distribution instead of a scalar and adopts cross entropy loss to learn the value function, yielding im-
655
      proved performance, especially in stochastic environments. Following this, QR-DQN (Dabney et al.,
      2018) introduces a quantile-based approximation, learning specific quantiles via the quantile Huber
656
      loss, thereby offering finer control over the distribution tails. DERL (Rowland et al., 2019) further
657
      advances DRL with expectile regression, enabling the modeling of conditional value at risk (CVaR),
658   which is particularly advantageous for risk-averse applications. On the theoretical front, Wang et al.
659   (2023) provides small-loss bounds for distributional RL, offering stronger convergence guarantees,
660   while Rowland et al. (2023) extends QR-DQN through quantile temporal-difference learning, show-
661   ing its statistical benefits in environments with skewed or heavy-tailed rewards. Distributional RL
662   has also been extended to continuous action spaces through the D4PG (Barth-Maron et al., 2018)
663   and DSAC (Duan et al., 2022), and also demonstrates the scalability and generalization in offline
664   Q-learning across diverse multi-task data (Kumar et al., 2023). The advantage of quantization in
665   distributional RL are also discussed (Bellemare et al., 2017) that it can better handle approxima-
666
      tion errors, reduce chattering caused by policy updates, and mitigate state aliasing, thus improving
      training stability. Additionally, the distribution itself provides a rich set of predictions, allowing the
667
      agent to learn from multiple predictions rather than solely focusing on an expected value. Moreover,
668
      the distributional perspective introduces a more natural inductive bias framework for reinforcement
669   learning, enabling the imposition of assumptions on the domain or the learning problem itself.
670
671   Our proposed AHL-Gaussian method falls within the realm of distributional RL. Yet, it distinguishes
672
      itself from the methods previously discussed by employing HL-Gaussian to project the Bellman tar-
      get’s value onto a discrete distribution when crafting the categorical distribution of the Bellman
673
      target. Building upon the existing limitations of HL-Gaussian, we have further introduced a mecha-
674
      nism for dynamic interval adjustment, which significantly differentiates AHL-Gaussian from current
675   distributional RL methods.
676
677   HL-Gaussian in RL HL-Gaussian is a specialized learning method that utilizes the cross-entropy
678
      loss and constructs a target categorical distribution derived from Gaussian histogram densities. Ini-
      tially proposed for regression tasks by Imani & White (2018) and Ehsan Imani (2024), it was found
679
      to primarily enhance optimization processes. Farebrother et al. (2024) later applied HL-Gaussian to
680
      reinforcement learning (RL), demonstrating significant improvements in training performance and
681   a beneficial scaling effect as the model complexity increases. This pioneering work spurred further
682   exploration. Denis Tarasov (2024) investigated HL-Gaussian in offline RL settings, finding it ca-
683   pable of delivering state-of-the-art results, albeit with occasional fluctuations. Josiah P. Hanna &
684   Harish (2024) applied it to stochastic policy gradient RL, achieving enhanced data efficiency and
685   stability, particularly in continuous control scenarios. Additionally, Yang Zhang (2024) successfully
686   adapted a discrete regression method akin to HL-Gaussian for multi-agent systems.
687   While these studies have straightforwardly integrated HL-Gaussian with existing RL methods, they
688   overlook a critical aspect of RL algorithms: the target function for fitting is in constant flux. Conse-
689   quently, a static support interval is inadequate for fully realizing HL-Gaussian’s potential in RL. To
690   surmount this challenge, we introduced a dynamic interval adjustment mechanism, which we have
691   both theoretically and empirically proven to be effective and universally applicable.

       Approximation Policy Iteration (API) is a popular iterative paradigm to find an approximate solution
1053   to the optimal value function Q∗ . SAC and TD3 can be regared within this framework. It starts
1054   from a policy π0 , and then approximately evaluates that policy π0 , i.e. it finds a Q0 that satisfies
1055   T π0 Q0 ≈ Q0 . Afterwards, it performs a policy improvement step, which is to calculate the greedy
1056   policy with respect to (w.r.t.) the most recent action-value function, to get a new policy π1 . The
1057   policy iteration algorithm continues by approximately evaluating the newly obtained policy π1 to get
1058   Q1 and repeating the whole process again, generating a sequence of policies and their corresponding
1059   approximate action-value functions Q0 → π1 → Q1 → π2 · · · .
1060   Similarly, we can build API for the setting that value functions are learned by HL-Gaussian. Specif-
1061   ically, it also starts from a policy π0 , and then approximately evaluates that policy π0 , i.e. it finds
1062   a categorical distribution p̂(0) that satisfies the Bellman equation Ep(0) [z] ≈ Ep̂(0) [z], where p(0)
1063   comes from projecting T π0 Ep̂(0) [z] on m discrete locations through equation (6). Afterwards, it
1064   performs a policy improvement step, which is to calculate the greedy policy with respect to (w.r.t.)
