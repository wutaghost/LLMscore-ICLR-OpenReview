                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Fine-tuning large-scale text-to-image diffusion models for various downstream
012            tasks has yielded impressive results. However, the heavy computational burdens
013            of tuning large models prevent personal customization. Recent advances have
014            attempted to employ parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques to adapt
015            the floating-point (e.g., FP16) or quantized pre-trained weights. Nonetheless,
016            the adaptation parameters in existing works are still restricted to FP16 arith-
017            metic, hindering hardware-friendly acceleration. In this work, we propose Int-
018            LoRA, to further push the efficiency limits by using integer type (e.g., INT4)
019            low-rank parameters to adapt the quantized diffusion models. By working in the
020
               integer arithmetic, our IntLoRA offers three key advantages: (i) for fine-tuning,
               the pre-trained weights are quantized, reducing memory usage; (ii) for storage,
021
               both pre-trained and low-rank weights are in INT4 which consumes less disk
022
               space; (iii) for inference, IntLoRA weights can be naturally merged into quan-
023            tized pre-trained weights through efficient integer multiplication or bit-shifting,
024            eliminating additional post-training quantization. Extensive experiments demon-
025            strate that IntLoRA can achieve performance on par with or even superior to the
026            vanilla LoRA, accompanied by significant efficiency improvements.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recently, large-scale text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models (Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al.,
032   2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023) have shown promising capabilities for image gener-
033
      ation. Taking advantage of the strong generative prior of pre-trained parameters, a range of down-
      stream adaptation applications have emerged, including subject-driven generation (Ruiz et al., 2023),
034
      style-customized generation (Sohn et al., 2023), and controllable generation (Zhang et al., 2023).
035
      However, fully fine-tuning these large models for personalized customization poses challenges on
036   consumer-level GPUs, as well as the costs of storing the weight for each downstream task.
037
038   To facilitate efficient adaptation, recent advances (Qiu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b) have intro-
039
      duced parameter efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Hu et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019; Jia et al., 2022)
      that focuses on fine-tuning a limited number of parameters for downstream tasks. Despite the re-
040
      duction in trainable parameters, current PEFT methods predominantly work on floating-point (e.g.,
041
      FP16) arithmetic, which can be inefficient for practical applications. For instance, only loading
042   the FLUX.1-dev (BlackForestLabs, 2024) can consume over 23GB GPU memory, let alone subse-
043   quent fine-tuning. Additionally, it also costs huge disk space to store per-task fine-tuned models.
044   Although some PEFT methods propose weight merging to mitigate the overhead associated with
045   additional adapters, they still fall short in accelerated inference.
046
      On the other hand, neural network quantization (Nagel et al., 2021; 2020; Esser et al., 2019), which
047
      can transform the trained FP16 parameters into low-bit integer (e.g., INT4) representations, is a
048   prominent technique for accelerating deep learning models. Thus, integrating PEFT with quantiza-
049   tion techniques holds promise for enhancing the efficiency of downstream adaptations. To this end,
050   some pioneer works (Dettmers et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2024) attempt downstream adaptation by di-
051   rectly fine-tuning the quantized INT4 pre-trained weights with FP16 PEFT parameters. However,
052   it remains an open challenge for quantization-aware adaptation. Specifically, since the adaptation
053   weights are still in FP16, it is inevitable to convert the quantized pre-trained weights back to FP16
      for arithmetic consistency for subsequent weight merge. As a result, it necessitates additional post-

073   To some extent, the primary technical bottleneck in bridging PEFT and quantization arises from
074   the arithmetic inconsistency between the quantized pre-trained weights and the adaptation weights.
075   Specifically, the FP16 adaptation weights necessities the conversion of the quantized pre-training
076
      weights from INT4 to FP16 for the subsequent weight fusion. To address this inconsistency, a po-
      tential solution is to also transfer the adaptation weights to integer arithmetic. In this way, all weights
077
      during fine-tuning are quantized, allowing to store only INT4 weights, as well as convenient weight
078
      merging during inference. Despite these promising properties, it is non-trivial to accurately quantize
079   the adaptation weights for satisfactory performance. For example, while zero initializing low-rank
080   weights are advantageous for fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021), it poses quantization challenges due to
081   substantial quantization errors from small values. Furthermore, the additive form of the original
082   LoRA forces the pre-trained and adaptation weights to share the same quantizer for subsequent
083   weight merging, which restricts the available parameter space for adaptation.
084
      To address the above challenges, we propose IntLoRA, which employs integral low-rank parame-
085   ters to adapt the quantized diffusion models. In detail, we introduce the Adaptation-Quantization
086   Separation (AQS) technique, which employs a task-agnostic auxiliary matrix to enable quantization-
087   friendly low-rank parameters without disrupting the gradient trajectory of the original LoRA. Addi-
088   tionally, we present the Multiplicative Low-rank Adaptation (MLA), which reformulates the math-
089   ematical structure of LoRA from addition to multiplication. This remains mathematically equiva-
090   lent to the original but allows for independent optimization of adaptation weights, eliminating the
091   need to share the same quantizer as the pre-trained weights. Furthermore, we develop the Variance
092   Matching Control (VMC) mechanism that aligns variances of the pre-trained and auxiliary matrices,
093
      controlling the adaptation distribution for more efficient log2-quantization. For implementation,
      we provide two versions of IntLoRA, i.e., IntLoRAMUL , and IntLoRASHIFT . The IntLoRAMUL
094
      learns quantized low-rank parameters and can be seamlessly merged with quantized pre-trained
095
      weight through integer multiplication, while IntLoRASHIFT introduces log2-quantization and oper-
096   ates by bit-shifting the quantized pre-trained weights for downstream adaptation. We evaluate our
097   IntLoRA on prevalent T2I adaptation tasks, including subject-driven generation (Ruiz et al., 2023),
098   style-customized generation (Sohn et al., 2023) and controllable generation (Zhang et al., 2023).
099   Extensive experiments demonstrate that IntLoRA represents a novel diffusion fine-tuning paradigm
100   with impressive efficiency and favorable performance.
101
      The contribution of this work can be summarized as follows: (i) we introduce IntLoRA, which
102   achieves integer PEFT to address the arithmetic inconsistency, thereby advancing the efficiency
103   of diffusion model adaptations; (ii) we propose the adaptation-quantization separation to facilitate
104   quantization-friendly pre-trained weights, and further develop the multiplicative low-rank adapta-
105   tion for independent quantizers, complemented by variance matching control for effective distribu-
106   tion manipulation; (iii) our IntLoRA enables the adaptation of quantized diffusion models through
107   hardware-friendly integer multiplication or bit-shifting, resulting in significant efficiency gains in
      fine-tuning, storage, and inference. Extensive experiments validate the superiority of our method.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning of diffusion models. In order to reduce the fine-tuning cost of
111   large models, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has recently gained great interests (Lian et al.,
112   2022; Chavan et al., 2023; Li & Liang, 2021; He et al., 2021; Jie & Deng, 2023). For example,
113   some pioneering work (Zaken et al., 2021) proposes to fine-tune only a fraction of the pre-trained
114   weights. To achieve better performance, prompt-based methods (Jia et al., 2022) append learnable
115   prompts to modify the input space. Adapter-based methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Chen et al.,
116   2022) employ additional bottleneck structures as bypass branches for adaptation. LoRA (Hu et al.,
117   2021), which adopts low-rank matrices to learn weight updates for downstream tasks, has become a
118   popular pipeline for diffusion model adaptation. Moreover, OFT (Qiu et al., 2023) uses orthogonal
119
      constraints to preserve the pairwise angles between neuron vectors to maintain the hypersphere
      energy. COFT (Liu et al., 2023b) further uses butterfly factorization to formulate denser orthogonal
120
      matrices using fewer parameters. In this work, we mainly focus on LoRA since it has been widely
121
      applied and can be merged into pre-trained weights without increasing the inference cost.
122
123   Network quantization of diffusion models. Quantization (Nagel et al., 2021) is an effective tech-
124   nique to reduce model storage and inference costs, and can be categorized into quantization-aware
125   training (QAT) (Jacob et al., 2018; Li et al., 2024; 2022; Xu et al., 2023a) and post-training quanti-
126   zation (PTQ) (Wang et al., 2023a; Nahshan et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
127   2023a; Huang et al., 2024a). In the context of diffusion model quantization, existing works mainly
128   focus on PTQ because of the significant overhead of retraining diffusion models. For example,
129   PTQ4DM (Shang et al., 2023) makes the first attempt to quantize diffusion models to 8 bits. Af-
130   ter that, Q-Diffusion (Li et al., 2023) further achieves improved performance and lower bit-width.
131
      In addition, PTQD (He et al., 2024) eliminates quantization noise through correlated and residual
      noise correction. EfficientDM (He et al., 2023) introduces LoRA to fine-tune the pre-trained model
132
      to allow comparable performance with QAT. TFMQ-DM (Huang et al., 2024b) observes the impact
133
      of quantization errors on the time-step representation and proposes to quantize the time-embedding
134   layer individually for better performance. However, the network quantization only enables efficient
135   inference at low bit-widths on the original task, failing to handle various downstream tasks.
136
137   Joint adaptation and quantization. The work aims to achieve both parameter-efficient adap-
138   tations as well as storage and inference-efficient quantization, to further push the efficiency lim-
139   its of diffusion model fine-tuning. However, this also poses additional challenges, such as back-
140   propagating gradients in quantized values and optimizing the learnable parameters with quantization
141   restrains. Existing related works have explored mainly LLMs, but it is still far from a satisfactory
142   solution. Specifically, QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024) proposes to quantize the LLMs before fine-
143   tuning the LLMs with LoRA. Despite the reduced GPU usage during training due to the import
144
      of only the quantized model, QLoRA does not maintain quantized at inference since the quantized
      weights need to be converted to FP16 again so that to add the LoRA weights. To solve this problem,
145
      QA-LoRA (Xu et al., 2023b) develops a group-wise quantization through sharing parameters across
146
      channels, but at the cost of impairing the adaptation ability as well as the need to store FP16 LoRA
147   weights. IR-QLoRA (Qin et al., 2024) analyzes the entropy loss of quantization from an information
148   theory view, but it also needs to convert the quantized weights back to FP16 during inference.

      3   P RELIMINARY

      The main idea of low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) comes from the fact that the learned
      incremental matrix during downstream adaptation usually possesses low-rank properties. To this
154
      end, LoRA introduces a low-rank matrix ∆W to learn the weight increments for adapting the pre-
155
      trained weights W ∈ RCout ×Cin to downstream tasks. In implementation, the ∆W is formulated
156   as the matrix multiplication of two low-rank matrices A ∈ RCout ×d and B ∈ Rd×Cin , where the
157   inner dimension d is the pre-defined rank. During fine-tuning, the pre-trained weight W is frozen
158   and only A, B is trainable. Since d ≪ min{Cin , Cout }, the number of trainable parameters can be
159   very small compared to full fine-tuning. The output during the downstream fine-tuning is calculated
160   as y = Wx+λ·(AB)x, where λ is the LoRA scale to adjust the control strength. During inference,
161   the task-specific AB can be naturally merged into the pre-trained weights, i.e., W′ = W + λ · AB,
      without increasing additional computational cost.

      4.1           B RIDGING E FFICIENT- ADAPTATION AND Q UANTIZATION
196
197
      Although parameter-efficient adaptation and network quantization each contribute to improved fine-
198
      tuning and inference efficiency, it remains an unexplored challenge to effectively combine them.
199   Existing work such as QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024) employs the low-rank AB to fine-tune quan-
200   tized weights Ŵ to reduce the memory footprint of loading a full-precision model. After training,
201   the merging between low-rank FP16 parameters and quantized NF4 weights causes the adapted
202   weights W′ to revert to FP16 again. Consequently, additional PTQ on W′ is needed for inference
203   efficiency. When the bit width is low, e.g., 4-bit, this PTQ can significantly degrade performance.
204
      In this work, we attribute the main difficulty in effectively marrying PEFT and quantization to the
205
      arithmetic inconsistency. Specifically, the FP16 AB forces the quantized Ŵ to revert to FP16
206
      during weight merging. To bridge the arithmetic gap, a feasible solution is to convert the low-rank
207                                             ˆ In this way, the same typed Ŵ and AB  ˆ can be seam-
      parameters into integer type to obtain AB.
208
      lessly merged during inference without additional PTQ. However, several technical challenges arise
209
      when performing PEFT on integer arithmetic. First, the AB in the original LoRA is zero-initialized
210   to preserve the knowledge of the pre-trained weights. Although helpful for fine-tuning, this ini-
211   tialization complicates the quantization process. For instance, the all-zero distribution requires a
212   separately designed quantizer at the beginning of tuning, since the scaling factor s = 0 leads to
213   an infinite Xs in Eq. (1). Second, the vanilla LoRA merges the FP16 AB and W using addition.
214   When both AB and W are quantized, it is essential to ensure that they share identical quantization
215   parameters to enable PTQ-free weight merging. This requirement leads to constrained parameter
      space, thus limiting the adaptation ability.

      4.2   I NTEGRAL L OW- RANK A DAPTATION
217
218   To address the above challenges, we propose the integral low-rank adaption, dubbed IntLoRA, which
219   enables PEFT in integer arithmetic. The overall pipeline is shown in Fig. 2.
220
      Adaptation-quantization separation. The vanilla LoRA adopts zero initialization on AB to en-
221
      sure the behavior of the model is similar to the pre-trained one at the beginning of training (Hu
222   et al., 2021). Although this initialization can improve performance, the all-zero distribution is not
223   quantization-friendly, as validated in Sec. 4.1 and Sec. 5.3. To allow accurate quantization while
224   maintaining the correct gradient, we propose the Adaptation-Quantization-Separation (AQS) strat-
225   egy. The key observation is that the adaptation requires gradients from zero-initialized weights while
226   the quantization does not. Therefore, we can split the non-zero initialized adaptation weights into
227   the gradient-aware zero part and the gradient-free nonzero part. Formally, let R be the non-zero
228   auxiliary matrix, Q be the quant-dequant operator, then our AQS can be formulated as:
229                                  W′ = Q[W − sg(R)] + sg(R) + AB,                                     (2)
230   where sg(·) denotes the stop gradient operation. Thanks to the AQS, the AB can be zero-initialized
231   for the same gradient as the original LoRA, while sg(R) + AB facilitate subsequent quantization
232   by specifically designing the auxiliary matrix R as discussed in Sec. 5.3. In the following part, we
233   will ignore the sg(·) notation for clarity.
234   Multiplicative low-rank adaptation. The vanilla LoRA employs additive form W + AB for
235   weight merge. However, it is difficult to seamlessly fuse the quantized Ŵ and AB    ˆ when they are
236   quantized by independent quantizers. To this end, we propose Multiplicative Low-rank Adaptation
237   (MLA) to rewrite the form of the original LoRA into a quantization-friendly multiplication form.
238   Specifically, denote the quant-dequant results as Q(W − R) = s · (Wround − z), then the MLA

      4.3   I MPLEMENTATION OF I NT L O RA
284
285   Benefiting from the quantization-friendly weight distribution produced from the variance matching
286   control, we provide two variants of our IntLoRA according to different quantizers on the adaptation
287   term. The first variant employs the uniform quantizer on the adaptation term, thus enabling direct
288   weight merge between the quantized adaptation term and pre-trained weights through integer mul-
289   tiplication. The second variant introduces log2 quantization to achieve downstream adaptation by
290   bit-shifting the quantized pre-trained weights. More details are given below.
291   Integer multiplication form. We employ uniform affine quantization on the adaptation term, with
292   the scaling factor and zero-point denoted as s̄ and z̄, and the quantized results as Uround , then our

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
312
313   Baselines. Since there is little work studying PEFT of quantized diffusion models in integer arith-
314   metic, we reproduce existing related approaches in LLMs and compare them with the proposed
315   IntLoRA. Specifically, we include the following baselines: i) QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024), which
316   fine-tunes the quantized model using FP16 low-rank matrix. Since the adapted weights are still in
317   FP16, we thus apply additional PTQ on the merged weights for a fair comparison. ii) QA-LoRA (Xu
318   et al., 2023b), which uses group-shared low-rank parameters for seamless weight merge, and iii) IR-
319   QLoRA (Qin et al., 2024), which improves the quantization with information retention as well as

      6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We propose IntLoRA, a novel joint adaptation and quantization framework which can address the
      arithmetic inconsistency by employing integer low-rank parameters, to push the efficiency limits of
532
      diffusion model fine-tuning. We propose the quantization-adaptation separation to allow the coex-
533
      istence of zero-initialized gradient and quantization-friendly distribution. We further introduce the
534   multiplicative low-rank adaptation to achieve a decoupled quantizer of pre-trained and adaptation
535   weights for PTQ-free weight merge, accompanied by the variance matching control to adjust the
536   channel-aware variance for accurate adaptation control. Benefiting from the elegant design, we pro-
537   vide two variants of IntLoRA, which either use int-multiplication or bit-shifting to adapt the quan-
538   tized pre-trained models. Through transferring the adaptation weights to the integer arithmetic, our
539   IntLoRA demonstrates its effectiveness across different pre-trained models and various downstream
      tasks, while exhibiting impressive efficiency across model tuning, storage, and inference.

786   E    T HE SIGNIFICANCE OF ACCELERATED TRAINING
787
788   In the downstream adaptation, since the the pre-trained weights is fixed and only small adapters
789   are updated during fine-tuning, it naturally rises questions of the importance of the speed-up for
790   the training stage in our IntLoRA. Here, we would like to point out that the original LoRA (Hu
791   et al., 2021) only reduces the GPU memory of gradient and optimizer states during training, and
792
      cannot benefit the model weights. Considering that the diffusion models are getting bigger, e.g.,
      just importing FLUX.1-dev to cuda can take up 23.8GB, it makes sense to reduce the size of the
793
      weights during training. Our IntloRA only needs the quantized pre-training weights during training,
794
      further facilitates user-customized diffusion fine-tuning on consumer-level GPUs. In detail, taking
795   SDXL (Podell et al., 2023) as an example, full fine-tuning SDXL consumes 59GB GPU memory, of
796   which 13.5GB comes from the weights. Using FP32 LoRA tuning consumes 28.38GB, indicating
797   30.62GB reduced gradients and optimizer states. However, this LoRA tuning still maintains the
798   weights in FP32. Theoretically, our IntLoRA can further reduce this 13.5GB FP32 weight to
799   3.375GB with the INT8 quantization. Finally, efficient training is only one of the benefits of our
800   IntLoRA, whose another advantage is to obtain downstream quantized models without additional
801   PTQ for efficient inference.

      G    F URTHER EXPLANATION OF THE AUXILIARY MATRIX .
836
837
838   In the proposed method, the auxiliary matrix R is crucial to ensure quantization-friendly distribution
839   and meaningful optimization trajectories. Here, we summarize two aspects of the role of R in the
840   proposed method. First, R can be viewed as an initialization parameter for trainable parameters
841
      AB. Specifically, we use R without gradient in the proposed AQS to guarantee easily quantized
      adaptation distributions while guaranteeing zero-initialized gradient trajectories. Second, the pres-
842
      ence of R in the adaptation term also implies that it can be used to control the distribution of the
843
      adaptation term, thus allowing for more challenging quantizers, such as log2 quantization. For this,
844   we propose the VMC, which makes the expectation of the adaptation term converge to zero, enabling
845   as many log-scale buckets to be used as possible. Despite this indispensable role of R, finding the
846   optimal shape and variance of R remains challenging. In this work, we attempt to solve this from
847   an engineering perspective by ablating different distributions and the variance scales. Experimental
848   results demonstrate the effectiveness of this solution.

851   H    D IFFERENCES FROM QA-L O RA
852
853
854   In this section, we give a detailed discussion about the differences between the proposed IntLoRA
855
      and QALora (Xu et al., 2023b). First, one of the limitations of QA-LoRA is that it requires the
      pre-trained weights and the adaptation weights to share the same quantizer, which leads to a dis-
856
      crete parameter search space, limiting the adaptation capability for downstream tasks. In contrast,
857
      our IntLoRA ensures a more powerful adaptation through the proposed MLA which reformulates
858   the form from additive to multiplicative, thus allowing independent quantizers. Second, the adap-
859   tation weights of QALoRA are still in FP16, leading to storage cost as well as FP16 addition for
860   weight merge during deployment, whereas our IntLoRA only needs to store INT4 weights while
861   fusing weights through hardware-efficient integer multiplication or bit shifting. Third, QALoRA
862   requires additional group quantization in the input channel dimensions, leading to additional GEMM
863   core design, while our approach uses a common quantization strategy, thus facilitating practical
      deployment.

       K    I MPACTS FROM THE AUXILIARY MATRIX
973
974
       In Eq. (2) of the proposed AQS, we introduce an additional auxiliary matrix R to the original pre-
975    trained weight W0 to achieve adaptation-quantization separation. However, this extra R potentially
976    introduces outliers and thus cause quantization error for W. Here, we point out that since the
977    proposed VMC can control the range of R through the variance scaling factor r = σW /σR , the
978    introduction of R in the AQS is ensured not result in additional outliers. For validation, we also
979    give the distribution visualization of the original W and the VMC re-scaled R in Fig. 13. It can be
980    seen that the range of R is effectively controlled within the range of W, thus effectively avoiding
981    the detrimental effect of additional outliers.

       L    J USTIFICATION FOR THE VALUE ORDERS

       A key assumption in the derivation for VMC is that the learned values of low-rank parameters AB
987
       is orders of magnitude smaller than the auxiliaty matrix R. Based on this assumption we ignore
988    AB as an approximation. Here, we give the specific evidence for this approximation. Specifically,
989    we visualize the weights of the trained AB and the distribution of R, as shown in Fig. 13. It can
990    be seen the range of AB is constrained to [−0.0004, 0.0004], while the range of R is [−0.08, 008].
991    Therefore, the experimental visualization above confirms the soundness of our approximation. Since
992    the AB in LoRA is zero initialized, it tends to be distributed around zero with learned small values
993    aiming to not disturb the pre-training weights too much.

995    M     A DVANTAGE D ISCUSSION OF I NT L O RA
996
997    Here, we would like to discuss the advantages of our InLoRA, to highlight the contribution of this
998    work. First, since the downstream fine-tuning is performed on the quantized pre-trained weights,
999    and thus the adaptation pipeline with our IntLoRA only needs to load the quantized weights, reduc-
1000   ing memory footprint from FP16 weights. Second, transferring the adaptation parameters to also
1001   the INT4 arithmetic allows all weights of the model can be stored in INT4 format, reducing the
1002   disk space for storage. Thirdly, thanks to the designed multiplicative adaptation form, our IntLoRA
1003   enables seamless weight merge of quantized pre-trained weights and adaptation weights at test time
1004
       without additional post-training quantization, streamlining the adaptation process while alleviating
       performance drop from quantization.

1007   N    L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORKS
1008
1009   Although the proposed IntLoRA can effectively improve the efficiency of diffusion model fine-
1010   tuning by allowing the adaptation parameters also on the integer arithmetic, the proposed framework
1011   can be further improved in the following aspects. First, although the trainable low-rank weights are
1012
       quantized with STE, these quantized weights are still in FP16 type during tuning to enable accurate
       gradient updates. Therefore, it is promising to specifically design integer-type propagation. Despite
1013
       this seems challenging, it can further reduce the training cost and accelerate the adaptation process.
1014
       Second, although we introduce a feasible way that uses hyperparameter search of the smoothing
1015   factor α to find a compatible σR as well as the appropriate distribution shape of R, it can be more
1016   elegant if we can use advanced mathematical analysis techniques, such as functional analysis, to
1017   find the statistical properties a suitable R should satisfy. Third, this work mainly focuses on the
1018   efficient acceleration of LoRA due to its prevalence among the PEFT techniques. Applications to
1019   other PEFT methods for hardware-efficient adaptation could be interesting future work.

          Question: A robe takes 2 bolts of blue fiber and half that much white fiber. How many bolts

1465      of white fiber is half of the blue fiber, which is 2/2 = 1 bolt. Therefore, the total number of
1466      bolts needed is 2 + 1 = 3 bolts. The answer is 3.

1469      we can use the ratio of 1:2. Since the ratio is 1:2, we can set up the equation 1/2 * 2 = x,
1470
          where x represents the amount of white fiber. Solving for x, we get x = 1. Therefore, the
          robe takes 2 bolts of blue fiber and 1 bolt of white fiber. In total, the robe takes 2 + 1 = 3
1471
          bolts of fiber. The answer is: 3

1476      total. The answer is: 3
