009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The generalization capacity of deep neural networks has been studied in a variety
012
               of ways, including at least two distinct categories of approaches: one based on
013
               the shape of the loss landscape in parameter space, and the other based on the
014            structure of the representation manifold in feature space (that is, in the space of
015            unit activities). Although these two approaches are related, they are rarely studied
016            together explicitly. Here, we present an analysis that bridges this gap. We show
017            that in the final phase of learning in deep neural networks, the compression of the
018            manifold of neural representations correlates with the flatness of the loss around
019            the minima explored by SGD. This correlation is predicted by a relatively simple
020            mathematical relationship: a flatter loss corresponds to a lower upper bound on the
021            compression metrics of neural representations. Our work builds upon the linear
022
               stability insight by Ma and Ying, deriving inequalities between various compression
               metrics and quantities involving sharpness. Empirically, our derived inequality
023
               predicts a consistently positive correlation between representation compression
024
               and loss sharpness in multiple experimental settings. Overall, we advance a dual
025            perspective on generalization in neural networks in both parameter and feature
026            space.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep neural networks’ generalization capacity has been studied in many ways. Generalization is
033   a complex phenomenon influenced by myriad factors, including model architecture, dataset size
034   and diversity, and the specific task used to train a network. Researchers continue to develop new
035   techniques to enhance generalization (Elsayed et al., 2018; Galanti et al., 2023). From a theoretical
036   point of view, we can identify two distinct categories of approach. These are works that study neural
037   network generalization in the context of (a) properties of minima of the loss function that learning
038   algorithms find in parameter space (Dinh et al., 2017; Andriushchenko et al., 2023), and (b) properties
039
      of the representations that optimized networks find in feature space – that is, in the space of their
      neural activation (Rangamani et al., 2023; Papyan et al., 2020).
040
041   One of the most widely studied factors that influence generalization is the shape of the loss landscape
042   in parameter space. Empirical studies and theoretical analyses have shown that training deep neural
043   networks using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with a small batch size and a large learning rate
044   often converges to flat and wide minima (Ma & Ying, 2021; Blanc et al., 2020; Geiger et al., 2021;
045
      Li et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2018b; Jastrzebski et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2019). Flat
      minima refer to regions in the loss landscape where the loss function has a relatively large basin:
046
      put simply, the loss doesn’t change much in different directions around the minimum. Many works
047
      conjecture that flat minima lead to a simpler model (shorter description length), and thus are less
048   likely to overfit and more likely to generalize well (Jastrzebski et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2023; Wu
049   et al., 2018b). However, whether flatness positively correlates with the network’s generalization
050   capability remains unsettled (Dinh et al., 2017; Andriushchenko et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2021). In
051   particular, Dinh et al. (2017) argues that one can construct very sharp networks that generalize well
052   through reparametrization. However, more recent work (Andriushchenko et al., 2023) shows that
053   even reparametrization-invariant sharpness cannot capture the relationship between sharpness and
      generalization.

      In our work, we investigate how the sharpness of the loss function near learned solutions in parameter
055   space influences local geometric features of neural representations. We demonstrate that as this
056   sharpness decreases and the minima become flatter, there is a set of mathematical bounds that imply
057   that the neural representation must undergo at least a specific, computable level of compression. This
058   process, which is related to previous results including the concept of neural collapse (Farrell et al.,
059   2022; Kothapalli et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021; Ansuini et al., 2019; Recanatesi et al., 2019; Papyan
060   et al., 2020), refers to the emergence of a more compact and by some measures lower dimensional
061   structure in the neural representation space. Compression in the feature space enables networks to
062   isolate the most crucial and discriminative features of input data. As a model becomes less sensitive to
063
      small perturbations or noise in the input data, it gains increased robustness against variations between
      training and test data. This simple and direct relationship between compression and robustness creates
064
      a valuable lens into networks’ potential to generalize.
065
066   We find that bounds that apply to two different metrics of compression – volumetric ratio and maxi-
067   mum local sensitivity – include different terms, and therefore predict different levels of compression
068   for each. We also note that local dimensionality is a compression metric of a distinct nature, and
069
      therefore does not necessarily correlate with sharpness. Taken together, this reveals that the im-
      pact of loss function sharpness on the neural representation is more complex than a simple (and
070
      single) compression effect. These effects shed light on the complex link between sharpness and
071
      generalization.
072
073   Throughout, we focus on the second, or final, stage of learning, which proceeds after SGD has already
074   found parameters that give near-optimal performance (i.e., zero training error) on the training data
075
      (Ma & Ying, 2021; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Ratzon et al., 2023). Here, additional learning still
      occurs, which changes the properties of the solutions in both feature and parameter space in very
076
      interesting ways.
077
078   Our work makes the following novel contributions:
079
080         1. The paper identifies two representation space quantities that quantify compression and are
081
               bounded by sharpness – volumetric ratio and maximum local sensitivity (MLS) – and gives
               new explicit formulas for these bounds.
082
            2. The paper conducts empirical experiments with VGG-11, LeNet, MLP, and ViT networks
083
               and finds that volume compression and MLS are indeed strongly correlated with sharpness.
084
            3. The paper finds that only the bound (Proposition 3.10) incorporating all linear weights of
085            the network consistently predicts a positive correlation between both sides of the inequality
086            across various experimental settings.
087
088   In these ways, we help reveal the interplay between key properties of trained neural networks
089   in parameter space and representation space. Specifically, we identify a sequence of inequality
090   conditions for the bounds that link the volume and MLS of the neural representations to the sharpness
091   in parameter space. These conditions help explain why there are mixed results on the relationship
092   between sharpness and generalization in the literature, by looking through the additional lens of the
093
      induced representations. Our findings altogether suggest that allied views into representation space
      offer a valuable dual perspective to that of parameter space landscapes for understanding the effects
094
      of learning on generalization.
095
096   Our paper proceeds as follows. First, we review arguments of Ma & Ying (2021) that flatter minima
097   can constrain the gradient of the loss with respect to network inputs and extend the formulation to
098   the multidimensional input case (Section 2). Next, we prove that lower sharpness implies a lower
099
      upper bound on two metrics of the compression of the representation manifold in feature space: the
      local volume and the maximum local sensitivity (MLS) (Section 3.1, Section 3.2). We conclude
100
      our findings with simulations that confirm our central theoretical results and show how they can be
101
      applied in practice (Section 4).

      et al., 2021) which is also reparametrization-invariant in Appendix G.2. Another more aggressive
353
      reparametrization-invariant sharpness is proposed in Andriushchenko et al. (2023); Kwon et al.
354   (2021), and we again show that it upper bounds input-invariant MLS in Appendix E.2. Therefore, we
355   provide a novel perspective: reparametrization-invariant sharpness is characterized by the robustness
356   of outputs to internal network representations.

      4.1   S HARPNESS AND COMPRESSION : VERIFYING THE THEORY
362
363
      The theoretical results derived above show that, during the later phase of training – the interpolation
364
      phase – measures of compression of the network’s representation are upper bounded by a function
365
      of the sharpness of the loss function in parameter space. This links sharpness and representation
366   compression: the flatter the loss landscape, the lower the upper bound on the representation’s
367   compression metrics.
368
369
      However, it remains to be tested in practice whether these bounds are sufficiently tight to show a
      clear relationship between sharpness and representation collapse. For one such test, we conducted
370
      the following experiment. We trained a VGG-11 network (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015) to classify
371
      images from the CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2009) and calculated the sharpness (Equation (2)),
372   the log volumetric ratio (Equation (7)), and the MLS and NMLS (Equation (11) and Equation (12))
373   during the training phase (Fig 1 and 2). We trained the network using SGD on images from 2 classes
374   (out of 10) so that convergence to the interpolation regime, i.e. zero error, was faster. We explored
375   the influence of two specific parameters that have a substantial effect on the network’s training:
376   learning rate and batch size. For each pair of learning rate and batch size parameters, we computed
377   all quantities at hand across 100 input samples and five different random initializations for network
      weights.


                                                                            7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      In the first set of experiments, we began by studying the link between a decrease in sharpness during
379   the latter phases of training and volume compression (Figure 1). We noticed that when the network
380   reaches the interpolation regime, and the sharpness decreases, so does the volume. Similarly, the
381   MLS decreases. All these results were consistent across multiple learning rates for a fixed batch size
382   (of 20): specifically, for learning rates that yielded lower values of sharpness, volume was lower as
383   well.
384
      We then repeated the experiments while keeping the learning rate fixed (lr=0.1) and varying the
385   batch size. The same broadly consistent trends emerged, linking a decrease in the sharpness to a
386   compression in the representation volume (Figure 2). However, we also found that while sharpness
387   stops decreasing after about 50 · 103 iterations for a batch size of 32, the volume continues to decrease
388   as learning proceeds. This suggests that other mechanisms, beyond sharpness, may be at play in
389   driving the compression of volumes.
390
      We repeat the experiments with an MLP trained on the FashionMNIST dataset (Xiao et al., 2017)
391   (Figure H.9 and Figure H.8). Although the sharpness does not noticeably decrease towards the end of
392   the training, it follows the same trend as MLS, consistent with our bound. The volume continues to
393   decrease after the sharpness plateaus, albeit at a much slower rate, again matching our theory while
394   suggesting that an additional factor may be involved in its decrease.
395
      This characterization of sharpness using compression provides a possible explanation for why
396
      (reparametrization-invariant) sharpness sometimes fails to account for the generalization behavior of
397   the network (Wen et al., 2023; Andriushchenko et al., 2023): Compression of network output is not
398   always desired for generalization. For example, it is observed in our Figure 1, Cohen et al. (2020) and
399   Wu et al. (2022) that learning rate is negatively correlated with sharpness, but Wortsman et al. (2022)
400   shows that a large learning rate can severely hurt OOD generalization performance. More intuitively,
401   consider a scenario where one provides a large language model (LLM) with a long text sequence and
402   instructs it to find a specific piece of information, often called the ”needle in a haystack” test. Even
403   a slight alteration in the instructions (a tiny portion of the input) given to the model should lead to
404   a notable difference in its output depending on the desired information. Therefore, compression of
405
      network output is not a desirable property in this case.

429   4.2      S HARPNESS AND LOCAL DIMENSIONALITY
430
431   Lastly, we analyze the representation’s local dimensionality in a manner analogous to the analysis of
      volume and MLS. A priori, it is ambiguous whether the dimensionality of the data representation


                                                                       8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      should increase or decrease as the volume is compressed. For instance, the volume could decrease
433   while maintaining its overall form and symmetry, thus preserving its dimensionality. Alternatively,
434   one or more of the directions in the relevant tangent space could be selectively compressed, leading
435   to an overall reduction in dimensionality.
436
      Figures 1 and 2 show our experiments computing the local dimensionality over the course of learning.
437
      Here, we find that the local dimensionality of the representation decreases as the loss decreases
438
      to near 0, which is consistent with the viewpoint that the network compresses representations in
439   feature space as much as possible, retaining only the directions that code for task-relevant features
440   (Berner et al., 2020; Cohen et al., 2020). However, the local dimensionality exhibits unpredictable
441   behavior that cannot be explained by the sharpness once the network is near the zero-loss manifold
442   and training continues. Further experiments also demonstrate a weaker correlation of sharpness and
443   local dimensionality compared to other metrics such as MLS and volume (Appendix Figure G.5-G.7).
444   This discrepancy is consistent with the bounds established by our theory, which only bound the
445   numerator of Equation (15). It is also consistent with the property of local dimensionality that
446   we described in Section 3.3 overall: it encodes the sparseness of the eigenvalues but it does not
447
      encode the magnitude of them. This shows how local dimensionality is a distinct quality of network
      representations compared with volume, and is driven by mechanisms that differ from sharpness alone.
448
      We emphasize that the dimensionality we study here is a local measure, on the finest scale around a
449
      point on the “global” manifold of unit activities; dimension on larger scales (i.e., across categories or
450   large sets of task inputs (Farrell et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2017)) may show different trends.
451
452
      4.3   C ORRELATION BETWEEN SHARPNESS AND COMPRESSION
453
454   Besides training dynamics, we also test the correlation between both sides of the bounds that we
455   derive (see Appendix G.2). We find that MLS and the bound over NMLS, introduced in Equation (11)
456   and Equation (12), correlate positively with NMLS in all of our experiments. Although the bound in
457   Proposition 3.4 is loose, log volume correlates well with sharpness and MLS. Moreover, we find that
458   sharpness is positively correlated with the generalization gap, suggesting that little reparametrization
459   effect (Dinh et al., 2017) occurs during training; this indicates that the network weights do not change
460   significantly, aligning with observations from Ma & Ying (2021). Finally, we found that quantities
461
      that only consider a single layer of weights such as relative flatness (Petzka et al., 2021) and the bound
      over MLS (Equation (11)) can exhibit a weak or even negative correlation with the generalization
462
      gap and sharpness in some cases (Figure G.6).

      identify two representation space quantities that are bounded by sharpness – volume compression

519   recent studies have established contradictory results.

      to systematically investigate the implications of our findings, with a key area being further learning
523
      problems, such as predictive learning, beyond the classification tasks studied here. Nevertheless, we
524
      are confident that highly interesting and clarifying findings lie ahead at the interface between the
525   parameter and representation space quantities explored here.

       G.2    C ORRELATION ANALYSIS
1101
1102   We empirically show how different metrics correlate with each other, and how these correlations can
1103   be predicted from our bounds. We train 100 VGG-11 networks with different batch sizes, learning
1104   rates, and random initialization to classify images from the CIFAR-10 dataset, and plot pairwise scatter
1105   plots between different quantities at the end of the training: local dimensionality, sharpness (square
1106   root of Equation (3)), log volume (Equation (7)), MLS (Equation (11)), NMLS (Equation (12)),
1107   generalization gap (gen gap), D (Equation (48)), bound (right-hand side of Equation (12)) and relative
1108   sharpness (Petzka et al., 2021) (see Figure G.5). We only include CIFAR-10 data with 2 labels to
1109
       ensure that the final training accuracy is close to 100%.
1110   We repeat the analysis on MLPs and LeNets trained on the FashionMNIST dataset and the CIFAR-10
1111   dataset (Figure G.6 and Figure G.7). We find that
1112
1113         1. The bound over NMLS, MLS, and NMLS introduced in Equation (12) and Equation (11)
1114
                consistently correlates positively with the generalization gap.
1115         2. Although the bound in Equation (9) is loose, log volume correlates well with sharpness and
1116            MLS.
1117         3. Sharpness is positively correlated with the generalization gap, indicating that little
1118            reparametrization effect (Dinh et al., 2017) is happening during training, i.e. the net-
1119            work weights do not change too much during training. This is consistent with observations
1120            in Ma & Ying (2021).
1121         4. The bound derived in Equation (12) correlates positively with NMLS in all experiments.
1122         5. MLS that only consider the first layer weights can sometimes negatively correlate with the
1123            bound derived in Equation (11) (Figure G.6).
1124
             6. Relative flatness that only consider the last layer weights introduced in (Petzka et al., 2021)
1125
                shows weak (even negative) correlation with the generalization gap. Note that “relative
1126            flatness” is a misnomer that is easier understood as “relative sharpness”, and is supposed to
1127            be positively correlated with the generalization gap.
