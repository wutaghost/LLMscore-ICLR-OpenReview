009                                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011              Contradiction retrieval refers to identifying and extracting documents that explicitly
012              disagree with or refute the content of a query, which is important to many down-
013              stream applications like fact checking and data cleaning. To retrieve contradiction
014              argument to the query from large document corpora, existing methods such as
015              similarity search and crossencoder models exhibit significant limitations. The
016              former struggles to capture the essence of contradiction due to its inherent nature
017              of favoring similarity, while the latter suffers from computational inefficiency,
018
                 especially when the size of corpora is large. To address these challenges, we
                 introduce a novel approach: S PARSE CL that leverages specially trained sentence
019
                 embeddings designed to preserve subtle, contradictory nuances between sentences.
020
                 Our method utilizes a combined metric of cosine similarity and a sparsity function
021              to efficiently identify and retrieve documents that contradict a given query. This
022              approach dramatically enhances the speed of contradiction detection by reducing
023              the need for exhaustive document comparisons to simple vector calculations. We
024              validate our model using the Arguana dataset, a benchmark dataset specifically
025              geared towards contradiction retrieval, as well as synthetic contradictions generated
026              from the MSMARCO and HotpotQA datasets using GPT-4. Our experiments
027              demonstrate the efficacy of our approach not only in contradiction retrieval with
028              more than 30% accuracy improvements on MSMARCO and HotpotQA across
029
                 different model architectures but also in applications such as cleaning corrupted
                 corpora to restore high-quality QA retrieval. This paper outlines a promising
030
                 direction for improving the accuracy and efficiency of contradiction retrieval in
031
                 large-scale text corpora.

      Training sentence embedding for similarity retrieval has been well studied in the literature (Gao et al.
046
      (2021); Xiong et al. (2020); Karpukhin et al. (2020)), where a standard practice is to use contrastive
047
      learning to map those similar sentences together and those dissimilar sentences far from each other.
048   However, these existing sentence embeddings are mainly tailored to similarity retrieval, while as far
049   as we know, there hasn’t been sentence embeddings for non-simlarity based retrieval. In this paper,
050   we study the problem of contradiction retrieval, a typical case of non-similarity based retrieval. Given
051   a large document corpus and a query passage, the goal is to retrieve document(s) in the corpus that
052   contradict the query, assuming they exist. This problem has a large number of applications, including
053   counter-argument detection Wachsmuth et al. (2018) and fact verification Thorne et al. (2018). The
      standard approaches to retrieving contradictions are two-fold. One is to use a bi-encoder Xiao et al.

079   Unfortunately, both methods suffer from limitations. The first approach (cosine similarity search on
080   sentence embeddings) is inherently incapable of representing the “contradiction relation” between the
081
      documents, due to the fact that the cosine metric is transitive: if A is similar to B, and B is similar
      to C, then A is also similar to C. As an example, consider an original sentence and its paraphrase
082
      in Table 8. Both of them contradict the sentence in the third column but they are not contradicting
083
      each other. The second approach, which uses a cross-encoder model, can capture the contradiction
084   between sentences to some extent, but it is much more computationally expensive. Our experiment in
085   Appendix H shows that compared with standard vector computation, running a cross-encoder is at
086   least 200 times slower.
087
      In this paper, we propose to overcome these limitations by introducing S PARSE CL for efficient
088
      contradiction retrieval using sparse-aware sentence embeddings. The key idea behind our approach
089
      is to train a sentence embedding model to preserve sparsity of differences between the contradicted
090   sentence embeddings. When answering a query, we calculate a score between the query and each
091   document in the corpus, based on both the cosine similarity and the sparsity of the difference between
092   their embeddings, and retrieve the ones with the highest scores. Our specific measure of sparsity is
093   defined by the Hoyer measure of sparsity Hurley & Rickard (2009), which uses the scaled ratio of the
094   ℓ1 norm and the ℓ2 norm of a vector as a proxy of the number of non-zero entries in the vector. Unlike
095   the cosine metric, the Hoyer measure is not transitive (please refer to Appendix D for a detailed
096   analysis), which avoids the limitations of the former. At the same time this method is much more
097   efficient than a cross-encoder, as both the cosine metric and the Hoyer measure are easy to compute
098
      given the embeddings. The Hoyer sparsity histogram of our trained embeddings is displayed in
      Figure 3.

101   (2018), which to the best of our knowledge, is the only publicly available dataset suitable for testing

103   documents in MSMARCO Nguyen et al. (2016) and HotpotQA Yang et al. (2018) datasets are
104
      synthetically generated using GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023). Our experiments demonstrate the efficacy
      of our approach in contradiction retrieval, as seen in Table 1. We also apply our method to corrupted
105
      corpus cleaning problem, where the goal is to filter out contradictory sentences in a corrupted corpus
106

141   Counter Argument Retrieval A direct application of our contradiction retrieval task in “counter-
142   argument retrieval”. Since the curation of Arguana dataset by Wachsmuth et al. (2018), there has
143   been a few previous work on retrieving the best counter-argument for a given argument Orbach et al.
144   (2020); Shi et al. (2023). In terms of methods, Wachsmuth et al. (2018) uses a weighted sum of
145
      different word and embedding similarities and Shi et al. (2023) designs a "Bipolar-encoder" and
      a classification head. We believe that our method relying only on cosine similarity and sparsity is
146
      simpler than theirs and produces better results in the experiment. In addition, some analyses in the
147
      counter-argument retrieval papers are specific to the “debate” setting, e.g. they rely on topic, stance,
148   premise/conclusion, and some other inherent structures in debates for help, which may prevent their
149   methods from being generalized to broader scenarios.
150
151   Fact verification and LLM hallucination Addressing the hallucination problem in Large Lan-
152   guage Models has been a subject of many research efforts in recent years. According to the three
153   types of different hallucinations in Zhang et al. (2023b), here we only focus on those so called
154   “Fact-Conflicting Hallucination” where the outputs of LLM contradict real world knowledge. The
155   most straightforward way to mitigate this hallucination issue is to assume an external groundtruth
156   knowledge source and augment LLM’s outputs with an information retrieval system. There have
157   been a few works on this line showing the success of this method Ren et al. (2023); Mialon et al.
158
      (2023). This practice is very similar to "Fact-Verification" Thorne et al. (2018); Schuster et al. (2021)
      where the task is to judge whether a claim is true or false based on a given knowledge base.
159
160   However, as pointed out by Zhang et al. (2023b), in the era of LLM, the external knowledge base can
161   encompass the whole internet. It is impossible to assume that all the information there are perfectly
      correct and there may exist conflicting information within the database. In the context of our paper,


                                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      instead of using a groundtruth database to check an external claim, our goal is to check the internal
163   contradictions between different documents in an unknown corpus.
164
165   Learning augmented LLM and retrieval corpus attack Augmenting large language models with
166   retrieval has been shown to be useful for many purposes. Recently, there have been a few works
167   Zhong et al. (2023); Zou et al. (2024) studying the vulnerability of retrieval system from adversarial
168   attack. In specific, they show that adding a few corrupted data to the corpus will significantly drop the
169   retrieval accuracy. This phenomenon bring our attention to the necessity of checking the factuality
170   of the knowledge database. Note that the type of corrupted documents considered by their papers
171   are different from ours. While they consider the injection of adversarially generated documents, we
172   consider the existence of contradicted documents as a natural part of the corpus. Also their purpose
173
      is to show the effect of adversarial attack, while we provide a defense method for a certain kind of
      corrupted database.

178   Problem Formulation We consider the contradiction retrieval problem: given a passage corpus
179   C = {p1 , p2 , ...pn } and a query passage q, retrieve the “best” passage p∗ that contradicts q. We
180   assume that several similar passages supporting q might exist in the corpus C.
181
182   Embedding based method Judging whether two passages contradict each other is a standard
183   Natural Language Inference task and can be easily tackled by many off-the-shelf language models
184   Touvron et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2022), . However, to retrieve the best candidate from the corpus,
185
      we have to iterate the whole corpus, or at least send the candidates retrieved by similarity search
      to the language model to determine if they constitute contradiction. This is time consuming, given
186
      that there are potentially many similar passages in the corpus. Therefore, in our paper, we mainly
187
      focus on those methods that only rely on their passage embeddings. Specifically, we want to design a
188   simple scoring function F that given the embeddings of two passages, outputs a score between [0, 1],
189   indicating the likelihood that they are contradicting each other.
190
191   Sparse Aware Embeddings Following the idea from counter-argument retrieval papers Wachsmuth
192   et al. (2018), such a score function should be a combination of similarity and dissimilarity functions.
193   Observe that a dissimilarity function is basically a negation of a similarity function, so the authors
194   of Wachsmuth et al. (2018) design several different similarity functions and set the scoring function
195   to maximize one of them and minimize another. Here, instead of enumerating different similarity
196   functions, we consider another notion: the “sparsity” of their embedding differences. The basic
197   intuition is as follows. Suppose that all sentences are represented as vectors in a “semantic” basis,
198
      where each coordinate represents one clearly identifiable semantic meaning. Then a contradiction
      between two passages should manifest itself as a difference in a few coordinates, while other
199
      coordinates should be quite close to each other. The issue, however, is that we do not know how to
200
      construct the appropriate basis, and the sparsity is defined with respect to a fixed coordinate system.
201   Nevertheless, following this intuition, we fine-tune sentence embedding models using contrastive
202   learning, by rewarding the sparsity of the difference vectors between embeddings of contradicting
203   passages. Please see Figure 3 for the Hoyer sparsity histogram of our trained embeddings.
204
205   S PARSE CL We use contrastive learning (Gao et al. (2021); Karpukhin et al. (2020)) to fine-tune
206   any pretrained sentence embedding model to generate the desired sparsity-aware embeddings. The
207   choice of positive and negative examples are exactly the reverse of the choice we make when the
208   training sets are Natural Language Inference datasets. The positive example for a passage is its
209   contradiction passage in the training set. The hard negative example for a passage is its similar
210   passage in the training set. There are also other random in-batch passages as soft negative examples.
211
      The sparsity function we choose here is Hoyer sparsity function from Hurley & Rickard (2009). Let
      h1 and h2 be two sentence embeddings and their embeddings have dimension d. We define

222   Scoring function for contradiction retrieval For the score function for contradiction retrieval,
223   we use a weighted sum of the standard cosine similarity and our sparsity function. Note that the
224   cosine similarity is provided separately by any off-the-shelf sentence embedding model in a zeroshot

228                      F (p1 , p2 ) = cos (E(p1 ), E(p2 )) + α · Hoyer(Es (p1 ), Es (p2 )).

231   contradiction. To get the answer passages, we calculate the score function for all passages and report

      Dataset Arguana is a dataset curated in Wachsmuth et al. (2018), where the author provide a corpus
      of 6753 argument-counterargument pairs, taken from 1069 debates with 15 themes on idebate.org.
246
      For each debate, the arguments are further divided into two opposing stances (pro and con). For each
247
      stance, there are paired arguments and counter-arguments. The dataset is split into the training set
248   (60% of the data), the validation set (20%), and the test set (20%). This ensures that data from each
249   individual debate is included in only one set and that debates from every theme are represented in
250   every set. The task goal is: given an argument, retrieve its best counter-argument.
251

253   via S PARSE CL. To construct our training data, for each argument and counter-argument pair (xi , xci )

255   arguments and counter-arguments from the same debate and stance as xi ’s hard negatives. We
256   fine-tune three pretrained sentence embedding models of different sizes (“UAE-Large-V1” Li & Li
257   (2023), “GTE-large-en-v1.5” Li et al. (2023), and “bge-base-en-v1.5” Xiao et al. (2023)). Please
258
      refer to Table 12 for our training parameters.

      of the datasets in the MTEB Retrieval benchmark, directly searching for the similar passages in the

264   similar argument to each query (Zeroshot). For a fair comparison, we also report the results of
265   fine-tuning these models using standard contrastive learning (CL) on the same dataset used for

      but not for MSMARCO and HotpotQA. Our explanation is that, for MSMARCO and HotpotQA, the
      generated paraphrases are more similar to the query than the contradictions. Therefore fine-tuning
363
      with the standard cosine similarity is unlikely to work.

534   5    C ONCLUSION
535
536   In this work, we introduced a novel approach to contradiction retrieval that leverages sparsity-aware
537   sentence embeddings combined with cosine similarity to efficiently identify contradictions in large
538   document corpora. This method addresses the limitations of the traditional similarity search as well
539   as computational inefficiencies of the cross-encoder models, proving its effectiveness on benchmark
      datasets like Arguana and on synthetic contradictions retrieval from MSMARCO and HotpotQA.

      Complex retrieval tasks Information retrieval is a well-studied area Singhal et al. (2001) and there
740
      have been many benchmarks for testing retrieval performance such as BEIR Thakur et al. (2021),
741
      MTEB Muennighoff et al. (2023), and MIRACL Zhang et al. (2023a). However, most of the datasets,
742   through varying in some degrees, focus only on "retrieving the most similar document". People have
743   noted that there exist some more complex retrieval tasks (e.g. Arguana Wachsmuth et al. (2018)
744   retrieves counter-arguments that refute a query argument), and build retrieval benchmark focusing on
745   complex retrival goals, e.g. BIRCO Wang et al. (2024) and BERRI Asai et al. (2023).
746
      To retrieve according to different instructions, Asai et al. (2023) trains TART, a multi-task retrieval
747
      system with task instructions attached as prompts in front of the query content. However, when
748   answering queries, they are still searching for the most similar sentence embedding, though the prompt
749   is different for different tasks. As far as we know, our paper studies the first non-similarity-based
750   search problem.
