009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011             Despite recent progress, video generative models still struggle to animate human
012             actions from static images, particularly when handling uncommon actions whose
013             training data are limited. In this paper, we investigate the task of learning to
014             animate human actions from a small number of videos—16 or fewer—which is
015             highly valuable in real-world applications like video and movie production. Few-
016
                shot learning of generalizable motion patterns while ensuring smooth transitions
                from the initial reference image is exceedingly challenging. We propose FLASH
017
                (Few-shot Learning to Animate and Steer Humans), which improves motion gen-
018
                eralization by aligning motion features and inter-frame correspondence relations
019             between videos that share the same motion but have different appearances. This
020             approach minimizes overfitting to visual appearances in the limited training data
021             and enhances the generalization of learned motion patterns. Additionally, FLASH
022             extends the decoder with additional layers to compensate lost details in the la-
023             tent space, fostering smooth transitions from the reference image. Experiments
024             demonstrate that FLASH effectively animates images with unseen human or scene
025             appearances into specified actions while maintaining smooth transitions from the
026             reference image. The animated videos are accessible on the anonymous website1 .

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Despite substantial progress (Ho et al., 2022b; Singer et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Guo et al.,
031   2023c; Wang et al., 2023b; Esser et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Liew et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a;
032   He et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024b;a), video generative models still struggle to
033   accurately portray human actions from static images. Even commercial AI video generators, such
034   as Dream Machine2 from Luma AI and KLING AI3 from Kuaishou, encounter difficulty with this
035   task. As shown in Figure 1, both models fail to animate actions such as balance beam jump or
036   shooting a soccer ball from static images. This difficulty arises from the scarcity of training data
037
      that specifically depict the target action. As human actions are diverse and likely follow a long-tailed
      distribution, many highly recognizable human actions, such as those of a niche sport like balance
038
      beam, suffer from limited training data. The data scarcity prevents data-hungry video generative
039
      models from effectively learning such actions.
040
041   In this paper, we explore the task of learning to animate human actions from a small set of videos.
042   Our aim is to transform a static reference image into a short video of a few seconds, which portrays
043
      a specific human action described by a textual prompt. This transformation is learned from a limited
      dataset containing up to 16 videos for each action class, thereby reducing the need for extensive
044
      video data collection. This capability holds the promise to reduce computational cost and broaden
045
      the application domains of video generative models; it is particularly valuable for applications like
046   video and movie production, which needs to animate specific actors performing a wide range of
047   actions, yet each action is only used once or twice. Under such use cases, techniques requiring
048   many example videos for each action become cost-ineffective.
049
      Existing image animation methods encounter considerable difficulties with this task. These ap-
050
      proaches typically rely on large video datasets for training and primarily focus on preserving the

      To tackle this challenge, we propose FLASH (Few-shot Learning to Animate and Steer Humans),
      a method for few-shot human action animation. To learn generalizable motion patterns, FLASH
094
      devise the Motion Alignment Module to align the motion features and inter-frame correspondence
095
      relations between a video and its strongly augmented variant, where the motion remains the same
096   but the appearance differs significantly. By requiring the model to predict the two videos using the
097   two aligned motion signals, this approach encourages learning motion patterns that can generalize
098   across different appearances, reducing overfitting to the appearance in the limited training data. Ad-
099   ditionally, to improve transition smoothness from the reference image, FLASH employs the Detail
100   Enhancement Decoder to propagate the details in the reference image to generated frames, which
101   compensates for the loss of details in the latent space in the decoding process. The overall framework
102   of FLASH is illustrated in Figure 2 (a).
103   Through experiments on 12 atomic human actions selected from HAA500 (Chung et al., 2021), we
104   demonstrate that FLASH accurately animates human actions from diverse reference images while
105   maintaining smooth transitions. It outperforms existing image animation methods across various
106   quantitative metrics and human evaluations, showcasing the effectiveness and superiority of FLASH.
107   Our contributions include: (1) We tackle the practical and challenging task of few-shot human action
      animation, an under-explored area with significant potential for video and film production. (2) We

114   Video Generation. Video generation using diffusion (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b;a) have
115   notably surpassed methods based on GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020), VAEs (Kingma & Welling,
116   2013) and flow techniques (Chen et al., 2019). Diffusion models for video generation can be broadly
117   classified into two groups. The first group generates videos purely from textual descriptions. These
118
      methods extend advanced text-to-image generative models by integrating 3D convolutions, 3D UN-
      ets, and temporal attention modules to capture temporal dynamics in videos (Ho et al., 2022b;a;
119
      Singer et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023c; Wang et al., 2023b).
120
      To mitigate concept forgetting when training on low-quality videos, some methods use both videos
121   and images jointly for training (Ho et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2024). Large Language Models (LLMs)
122   contribute by generating frame descriptions (Gu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b)
123   and scene graphs (Fei et al., 2023) to guide the video generation. Trained on large-scale video-text
124   datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Xue et al., 2022), these methods excel at producing high-fidelity videos.
125   However, they typically lack control over specific frame layouts, such as object positions and human
126   poses. To improve controllability, LLMs are used to predict control signals (Lu et al., 2023; Lian
127   et al., 2023; Lv et al., 2024), but these signals typically offer coarse control (e.g., bounding boxes)
128   rather than fine-grained control (e.g., detailed human motion or object deformation).
129   On top of textual descriptions, the second group of techniques benefit from additional guidance
130   sequences, such as depth maps, motion vectors, optical flows, and bounding boxes (Esser et al.,
131   2023; Yin et al., 2023; Liew et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; He et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b;a),
132   which help control motion and frame layouts. Additionally, several techniques use existing videos
133   as guidance to generate videos with different appearances but identical motion patterns (Wu et al.,
134   2023a; Qi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Geyer et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023c;
135
      Ling et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024b; Park et al., 2024; Jeong et al., 2024). However, these methods
      cannot create novel videos that share the same motion class with the guidance video but differ in the
136
      actual motion, such as human positions and viewing angles, which limits their generative flexibility.
137
138   Image Animation. Image animation involves generating videos that begin with a given reference
139   image. Common approaches achieve this by integrating the image features into videos through
140   cross-attention layers (Wang et al., 2023a; Xing et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023a; Jiang et al., 2023;
141
      Gong et al., 2024), employing additional image encoders (Guo et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024c), or
      incorporating the reference image into noised videos (Zeng et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b; Girdhar
142
      et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024a; Gong et al., 2024). Another line of methods focuses
143
      on learning structural guidance (e.g., motion maps) that aligns with the reference image to guide the
144   generation of subsequent frames (Shi et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2023; Kandala et al., 2024). However,
145   these approaches often require extensive training videos to effectively learn motion or structure
146   guidance. Zhao et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2024) employ a temporal path to learn motion patterns
147   from a few videos and a spatial path to learn appearance from a reference image for animation.
148   However, they require training for each reference image, which limits their adaptability. While
149   Materzynska et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023b); Kansy et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024a) are similar to
150   our work in learning specific motion patterns from a few videos, they primarily use the reference
151   image as an appearance condition and rely on the model to automatically prioritize motion over
152
      appearance. Without explicit supervision for appearance-general motion, their generalizability is
      still limited. In this paper, we propose FLASH, which learns generalizable motion from only a few
153
      videos through explicit supervision, and the learned motion can be applied to reference images that
154
      differ widely in visual attributes like human positions and texture.

199   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
200
201   Image Diffusion Models. Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022), a leading image
202   generative model, comprises four main components: an image encoder E, an image decoder D, a
203   text encoder T , and a U-Net ϵθ . During training, an image x ∈ RH×W ×3 is first encoded into
204   a latent image z0 = E(x) ∈ Rh×w×c , where h, w and c denote the height, width and number
205
      of channels of the latent image, respectively. Next, z0 undergoes a pre-defined√ diffusion√ process
      (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho et al., 2020) to add noise, resulting in zt = ᾱt z0 + 1 − ᾱt ϵt ,
206
      where ϵt ∼ N (0, I), t ∈ [0, T ] denotes the noising step, and ᾱt represents the noise strength. The
207
      U-Net is then trained to predict the noise ϵt from zt . During inference, a latent noise zT is drawn
208   from N (0, I) and progressively denoised into ẑ0 . Finally, the decoder reconstructs the generated
209   image x̂ = D(ẑ0 ).
210

299   3.3   D ETAIL E NHANCEMENT D ECODER
300
301   In LDM, pixel-level details can be distorted when videos are decoded from the latent space, as even
302   minor perturbations within the latent space can lead to noticeable visual artifacts, compromising
303   the intricate details of human actors and smooth transitions. To mitigate this issue, we devise the
304   Detail Enhancement Decoder that extends the image decoder D in LDM with additional layers to
305   propagate multi-scale details from the reference image to the generated frames. Since the motion
306
      between the reference image and generated frame can range from small to large displacements, we
      introduce two branches to handle both short- and long-range motion.
307
308   We define the levels of both the encoder and decoder as l ∈ {0, 1, · · · , L}, with l = 0 representing
309   the pixel space and l = L representing the latent space. At level l, we extract the decoder features
310   of the i-th decoding frame, denoted as hil , and the encoder features of the reference image, denoted
311   as gl1 . gl1 is then propagated to enhance the details in hil through two branches, as shown in Figure
312   2 (c). The first branch, the warping branch, retrieves details from nearby areas in gl1 for each spatial
313   position in hil . It learns the displacements between the two features and warps gl1 into the output
314
      ĝl1 based on these displacements. The second branch, the patch attention branch, retrieves details
      from the global scope of gl1 , complementing the local retrieval of the warping branch. It employs an
315
      attention layer with hil as the query and gl1 as the key and value to produce the output ǧl1 . The two
316
317
      output features are fused using learnable weights wli : h̃il = hil +wli ⊙(ĝl1 + ǧl1 ), where ⊙ represents
318   element-wise multiplication. The fused features h̃il is then passed to the next level. Through detail
319   propagation at each level for each decoding frame, the details in the generated videos are enhanced.
320   We train the Detail Enhancement Decoder to retrieve proper details through reconstructing distorted
321   videos to their ground-truth versions. We first extract gl1 from the first frame of a training video.
322   Next, we distort the video and encode it into a latent video. The decoder is then trained to reconstruct
323   the ground-truth video using this distorted latent video. This approach encourages the decoder to
      retrieve relevant details from the first frame. Further details can be found in Appendix Sec. A.3.

358   4                        E XPERIMENTS
359
360   We conduct experiments on 12 actions selected from the HAA500 dataset (Chung et al., 2021). The
361   selected actions include single-person actions (push-up, arm wave, shoot dance, running in place,
362   and sprint run), human-object interactions (soccer shoot, drinking from a cup, balance beam jump,

368   Metrics. Following Wu et al. (2023a;b); Henschel et al. (2024), we use three CLIP-based metrics:
369
      Text Alignment or the similarity between generated videos and action descriptions, Image Alignment
      or the similarity between generated videos and reference images, and Temporal Consistency or the
370
      similarity between adjacent frames in generated videos. In these metrics, higher scores indicate
371
      better performance. Following Xing et al. (2023), we utilize Fréchet distance to compare generated
372   videos and real ones. To mitigate content bias in the commonly used FVD (Unterthiner et al., 2018),
373   we adopt the CD-FVD (Ge et al., 2024), where a lower distance indicates better performance. To
374   assess the similarity between generated and ground-truth videos in the HAA dataset, we calculate
375   the cosine similarity for each pair of generated and ground-truth videos. We utilize RGB and optical
376   flow to calculate two similarity metrics: Cosine RGB and Cosine Flow. In these metrics, higher
377   similarities reflect better performance. For all metrics, we report the average results across all test
      videos. More details are described in Appendix Sec. A.6.

456
457   We conducted ablation studies on four actions: sprint run, soccer shoot, canoeing sprint, and hugging
458   human. The quantitative and qualitative results are presented in Table 2 and Figure 5, respectively.
459
      Variant #1 serves as the baseline, excluding both the Motion Alignment Module and the Detail
      Enhancement Decoder. Variant #2 uses only strongly augmented videos without any alignment
460
      technique. Variants #3, #4, and #5 progressively incorporate motion feature alignment, inter-frame
461
      correspondence relation alignment, and both, respectively. Lastly, Variant #6 builds upon Variant #5
462   by incorporating the Detail Enhancement Decoder.
463
464   Comparing the quantitative results of Variants #1 and #2, we observe that Variant #2 improves CD-
465
      FVD, Cosine RGB, and Cosine Flow, albeit with a slight decrease in CLIP scores. Qualitative
      results show that Variant #2 improves the fidelity of the generated actions. For example, in the
466
      soccer shooting action, the person’s legs tend to disappear as the action progresses in Variant #1;
467
      however, Variant #2 preserves the leg movements. These results suggests that using augmented
468   videos enhances the quality of generated motion.
469
470   Comparing the quantitative results of Variant #2 with Variants #3, #4, and #5, we find that Variants
471
      #3, #4, and #5 improve CD-FVD, Cosine RGB, and Cosine Flow. Both Variants #3 and #4 enhance
      the Cosine RGB, and Cosine Flow. When combined, Variant #5 yields further enhancements in co-
472
      sine similarity and a 25-point improvements in CD-FVD, without a noticeable drop in CLIP scores.
473
      Qualitative results also indicates improved fidelity in Variants #3, #4, and #5. For instance, motion
474   in Variant #2 appears unrealistic in both actions. In the soccer shooting action, the person’s foot
475   didn’t touch the soccer ball, and the leg appears disconnected in some frames. In the canoe pad-
476   dling action, the hand positions on the paddle are inconsistent across frames. However, these issues
477   are largely mitigated in Variants #3, #4, and #5. These results demonstrate the effectiveness of the
478   Motion Alignment Module in learning accurate motion. By providing explicit guidance for learn-
479   ing appearance-general motion, the module directs the model toward generalizable motion, thereby
480   improving the quality of the generated videos.
481   Comparing the quantitative results of Variant #5 and Variant #6, we observe that Variant #6 notice-
482   ably improves Text Alignment and Temporal Consistency without substantially affecting CD-FVD,
483   Cosine RGB, or Cosine Flow. Qualitatively, Variant #6 enhances some details, like the soccer ball
484   in certain frames in the soccer shooting action, and reduces noise in generated frames. These re-
485   sults suggest that the Detail Enhancement Decoder could compensate for some missing details in
      generated frames, leading to better temporal consistency and alignment with the action descriptions.

      A.2       P RELIMINARIES
841
842   Temporal Attention Layers. To capture temporal dynamics in videos, temporal attention layers are
843   introduced into the U-Net (Ho et al., 2022b; Esser et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023c;b). In a temporal
                                                        ′  ′  ′                                    ′
844   attention layer, the input features Fin ∈ RN ×h ×w ×c are first reshaped to F̃in ∈ RB×N ×c , where
              ′      ′
845   B = h × w . Here, the features at different spatial locations are treated as independent samples.
846   Temporal position encoding are then added, and a self-attention layer is applied to transform F̃in
                                                                  ′  ′  ′
847   into F̃out . Finally, F̃out is reshaped back to Fout ∈ RN ×h ×w ×c as output features. The temporal
848   attention layer integrates information from corresponding spatial locations across frames, enabling
849   the learning of temporal changes.
850
      Noise-Free Frame Conditioning. To preserve the appearance of the reference image in the image
851
      animation task, the noise-free latent reference image is integrated into the U-Net input (Wu et al.,
852   2023b; Ren et al., 2024a). During training, the first latent frame remains noise-free, while noise
853   is added only to subsequent latent frames throughout the noising process. At the noising step t,
854   the latent video Zt = ⟨zti ⟩N                             1   2           N           1
                                   i=1 is modified to Žt = ⟨z0 , zt , · · · , zt ⟩, where zt is replaced by
855   z01 . During inference, a sample ZT is drawn from N (0, 1), and zT1 is substituted with z01 = E(I),
856   where I is the user-provided reference image. The modified latent video ŽT = ⟨z01 , zT2 , · · · , zTN ⟩ is
857   then used for denoising. This technique effectively carries over the features from the first frame to
858   subsequent frames, ensuring that the appearance of the reference image is preserved in the generated
859   video.
860
861   A.3       D ETAIL E NHANCEMENT D ECODER
862
863   We define the levels of both the encoder and decoder as l ∈ {0, 1, · · · , L}, with l = 0 representing
      the pixel space and l = L representing the latent space. At level l, we extract the decoder features


                                                                    16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      of the i-th decoding frame, denoted as hil , and the encoder features of the reference image, denoted
865
      as gl1 . We interpolate gl1 to match the spatial size of hil and use a fully connected layer to adjust gl1
866
      to the same number of channels as hil , resulting g̃k1 as the input of the following two branches.
867
868   Warping Branch. This branch aims to retrieving details from nearby areas in g̃l1 for each position
869   in hil . It takes the channel-wise concatenation of hil and g̃l1 as input and applies four convolutional
870   layers to estimate motion displacements from hil to g̃l1 . These displacements determine the sampling
871   positions in g̃l1 . By warping g̃l1 based on the sampling positions, it outputs ĝl1 .
872   Patch Attention Branch. This branch retrieves details from the global scope of g̃l1 , complementing
873   the local recovery done by the warping branch. It begins by dividing both hil and g̃l1 into patches
874   and transforming each patch into features through a fully connected layer. A cross-attention layer is
875   then applied, using the patch features of hil as the query and the patch features of g̃l1 as the key and
876   value, resulting in a weighted combination of g̃l1 to produce the output ǧl1 .
877
      Feature Fusion. To control the amount of detail added to hil , a two-layer convolutional network is
878
      used to learn the fusion weights. The network takes the channel-wise concatenation of hil and g̃l1 as
879
      input and outputs the fusion weights wli , which has the same spatial size as hil . The fusion is then
880
      performed as:
881
                                          h̃il = hil + wli ⊙ (ĝl1 + ǧl1 ).                              (6)
882
883   The resulting feature h̃il is then passed to the next level. The details in the generated frames are
884   enhanced through the hierarchical detail propagation in each level.
885
      Learning to Reconstruct Distorted Videos. We train the Detail Enhancement Decoder to retrieve
886
      proper details through reconstructing distorted videos to their ground-truth versions. During train-
887   ing, we first extract gl1 using the first frame of a training video. We then intentionally distort the
888   video using random Gaussian blur, random color adjustments on 80% of the selected areas, and ran-
889   dom elastic transformations, and encode it into a latent video. The decoder is trained to reconstruct
890   the ground-truth video with MSE loss. This approach encourages the decoder to retrieve relevant
891   details from the reference image.

      A.6   E VALUATION M ETRICS
939
940   In line with previous works (Wu et al., 2023a;b; Henschel et al., 2024), we use three CLIP-based
941   metrics to assess text alignment, image alignment, and temporal consistency. (1) Text Alignment:
942   We compute the similarity between each frame and the provided text prompt, averaging the scores
943   across all frames. (2) Image Alignment: Similar to Text Alignment, we replace the text prompt with
944   the provided reference image to compute the image alignment score. (3) Temporal Consistency: We
945   calculate the average similarity between consecutive frame pairs to obtain the temporal consistency
946   score. We use ViT-L-14 from OpenAI (Radford et al., 2021) for feature extraction. In these three
947   metrics, higher scores indicate better performance.
948   Following Xing et al. (2023), we utilize Fréchet distance to compare generated and real videos. We
949   use CD-FVD (Ge et al., 2024) to mitigate content bias in the widely used FVD (Unterthiner et al.,
950   2018). We use VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022), pretrained on SomethingSomethingV2 (Goyal et al.,
951   2017), for feature extraction and distance calculation between real and generated videos. In this
952   metric, lower distances reflect better performance.
953   To evaluate the similarity between generated and ground-truth videos in the HAA dataset, we cal-
954   culate the cosine similarity for each pair of the generated and ground-truth videos. (1) Cosine RGB:
955   We extract video features using I3D (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017), pretrained on RGB videos, for
956   both the generated and ground truth videos, calculating cosine similarity for each pair. (2) Cosine
957   Flow: We extract optical flow using RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020) and then use I3D (Carreira & Zis-
958   serman, 2017), pretrained on optical flow data, to extract features for cosine similarity calculation.
959   In these two metrics, higher similarities indicate better performance.
