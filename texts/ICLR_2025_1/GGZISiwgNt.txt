026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Reinforcement Learning is a sequential decision-making framework where an agent learns optimal
029   behavior by iteratively interacting with its environment. At each timestep, the agent observes the
030   current state of the environment, takes an action, receives a reward, and transitions to the next state.
031   While RL has traditionally been studied in stationary environments with time-invariant rewards and
032   state-transition dynamics, this may not always be the case. Consider the examples of a carbon-aware
033
      datacenter job scheduler that tracks the dynamic electricity prices and local weather patterns (Yeh
      et al., 2024) and recommendation systems with evolving user preferences (Chen et al., 2018). Time-
034
      varying environments are also observed in inventory control (Mao et al., 2024), healthcare (Chandak
035
      et al., 2020), ride-sharing (Kanoria & Qian, 2024), multi-agent systems (Zhang et al., 2021a).
036
037   Motivated by these applications, we consider the problem of non-stationary reinforcement learning,
038   modeled by a Markov Decision Process with time-varying rewards and transition probabilities, in the
039
      infinite horizon average reward setting. While many works consider discounted rewards (Chandak
      et al., 2020; Igl et al., 2020; Lecarpentier & Rachelson, 2019), the more challenging average-reward
040
      setting is vital in representing problems where the importance of rewards does not decay with time,
041
      such as in robotics (Mahadevan, 1996; Peters et al., 2003) or scheduling workloads in cloud computing
042   systems (Jali et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022). The key challenges for an agent operating in a dynamic
043   environment are learning an optimal behavior policy that varies with the environment, devising an
044   efficient exploration strategy, and effectively incorporating the acquired information into its behavior.
045
      Current algorithms designed for non-stationary MDPs in the average reward setting can be classified
046
      broadly into model-based and model-free value-based methods. Model-based solutions incorporate
047
      sliding windows, forgetting factors, and confidence interval management mechanisms into UCRL
048   (Cheung et al., 2020; Ortner et al., 2020; Gajane et al., 2018; Jaksch et al., 2010). Model-free value-
049   based methods assimilate restarts and optimism into Q-Learning (Mao et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023)
050   and LSVI (Zhou et al., 2020; Touati & Vincent, 2020). A significant gap in the literature is the absence
051   of model-free policy-based techniques for time-varying environments. The inherent flexibility of
052   policy-based algorithms makes them suitable for continuous state-action spaces, facilitates efficient
053   parameterization in high-dimensional state-action spaces, and enables effective exploration through
      stochastic policy learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018).

071   2    R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Non-Stationary RL. Solutions to the non-stationary RL problem can be categorized into passive
074   and active methods. Active algorithms are designed to actively detect changes in the environment in
075   contrast to passive ones which implicitly adapt to new environments without distinct recognition of the
076
      change. While we focus our attention on passive techniques with dynamic regret as the performance
      metric in this work, a comprehensive survey can be found in Padakandla (2021) and Khetarpal et al.
077
      (2022). Model-based solutions in the infinite horizon average reward setting incorporate into UCRL a
078
      sliding window or a forgetting factor for piecewise stationary MDPs (Gajane et al., 2018), variation
079   aware restarts (Ortner et al., 2020) and a bandit based tuning of sliding window and confidence
080   intervals (Cheung et al., 2020) for gradual or abrupt changes constrained by a variation budget and
081   pessimistic tree search for Lipschitz continuous changes (Lecarpentier & Rachelson, 2019).
082
      In the episodic setting, model-free value based methods assimilate restarts and optimism into Q-
083
      Learning (Mao et al., 2024), LSVI (Zhou et al., 2020; Touati & Vincent, 2020) and sliding window
084
      and optimistic confidence set based exploration into a value function approximated learning (Feng
085   et al., 2023). Further, in the episodic setting, Lee et al. (2024) proposes strategically pausing learning
086   as an effective solution to non-stationarity with forecasts of the future. Wei & Luo (2021) proposed
087   an algorithm agnostic black-box approach that finds a non-stationary equivalent to optimal regret
088   stationary MDP algorithms. Further, Mao et al. (2024) presents an information theoretic lower bound
089   on the dynamic regret for both the episodic and infinite horizon settings and Peng & Papadimitriou
090   (2024) captures the complexity of updating value functions with any change. We note the distinction
091   between the scope of this work and the body of research on adversarial MDPs which often allow for
092   only changes in rewards, study the static regret and work with full information feedback instead of
093
      bandit feedback. We direct readers to Appendix J for a table of comparison of regret bounds across
      the above mentioned algorithms which we omit here due to a paucity of space.
094
095
      Non-Stationary Bandits. A precursor to non-stationary RL, the multi-armed bandit problem with
096
      time-varying rewards was first proposed in Garivier & Moulines (2008). Solutions include UCB with
097
      a sliding window or a discounting factor (Garivier & Moulines, 2008), UCB with adaptive blocks
098   of exploration and exploitation (Besbes et al., 2014), Restart-Exp3 (Besbes et al., 2014), Thompson
099   Sampling with a discounting factor (Raj & Kalyani, 2017) and bandit based sliding window tuning
100   (Cheung et al., 2019). Further, while most existing works assume arbitrarily (constrained by variation
101   budget) changing reward distributions and achieve Оҳ(T 2/3 ) regret, (Jia et al., 2023) achieves an
102   improved O(T 3/5 ) regret when the reward distributions change smoothly. Recent work by Liu et al.
103   (2023a) points out ambiguities in the definition of non-stationary bandits and how the dynamic regret
104   performance metric causes over-exploration, and Liu et al. (2023b) proposes, predictive sampling, an
105   algorithm that deprioritizes acquiring information that loses usefulness quickly.
106
107   Policy Gradient Algorithms for Stationary RL. Wu et al. (2020) was the first to provide a finite
      time analysis of the two timescale Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C) with function approximation to a

226   Challenges due to Non-Stationarity. When running policy-gradient methods in stationary RL, the
227   policy evolves to efficiently learn a fixed environment (P, r). However, in non-stationary case, the
228   environment (Pt , rt ) also changes over time. Therefore, the agent chases a moving target, namely,
229   the time-varying optimal policy ПҖ вӢҶt , resulting in the following unique challenges.
230
      вҖў Explore-for-Change vs Exploit: The agent needs to explore more aggressively than in the stationary
231     setting to adapt to the changing dynamics. As an example, a sub-optimal action at the current
232     timestep may become optimal at a later timestep, necessitating re-exploration. This is in sharp
233     contrast to stationary RL, where sub optimal actions are picked less often as time progresses.
234   вҖў Forgetting Old Environments: The policy and value function estimates must evolve quickly lest
235     they might become irrelevant once the environment changes significantly. However, observations
236     are noisy and the agent needs to collect multiple samples to obtain confident estimates. Hence, the
237     agent has to carefully balance the rate of forgetting the old environment versus learning a new one.

531
      We consider the problem of non-stationary reinforcement learning in the infinite-horizon average-
532
      reward setting and model it as a Markov decision process with time-varying rewards and transition
533
      probabilities. We analyze the first model-free policy-based algorithm, Non-Stationary Natural Actor-
534   Critic (NS-NAC). It is a two-timescale natural policy gradient based method that utilizes learning
535   rates as adapting factors and entropy based exploration to learn the time-varying optimal policy.
536   NS-NAC achieves a dynamic regret that is sublinear in the time horizon thus theoretically validating
537   policy gradient methods often used in practice in continual non-stationary RL. Directions for future
538   work include designing parameter-free algorithms that do not require prior knowledge of the variation
539   budget. Further, we believe a tighter regret bound can be obtained by a more refined analysis of the
      norm of the policy gradient using by leveraging the Fisher Information preconditioner.

2107
2108   Synthetic Environment. We empirically evaluate the performance of our algorithm NS-NAC
2109   on a synthetic non-stationary MDP, comparing it with three baseline algorithms: SW-UCRL2-CW
2110   (Cheung et al. (2023)), Var-UCRL2 (Ortner et al. (2020)), and RestartQ-UCB (Mao et al. (2024)).
2111   The synthetic MDP environment simulates non-stationary dynamics by alternating between two
2112   sets of transition matrices and reward functions over the time horizon T . The switching frequency,
2113   controlled by nswitches , determines the degree of non-stationarity and the variation budget вҲҶP,T for
2114   transitions and вҲҶR,T for rewards. The MDP consists of |S| states and |A| actions per state, with two
2115   sets of transition probabilities and rewards sampled at initialization. Further, to benchmark the effect
2116
       of the dynamic changes, the optimal policy is recalculated at each switching step tswitch by solving a
       linear programming problem (Puterman (2014)).
2117
2118   The environment alternates between these two sets of transitions and rewards, (P1 , r1 ) and (P2 , r2 ),
2119   every T /nswitches steps. The transition probabilities, P1 and P2 , are drawn from a Dirichlet distri-
2120   bution with a concentration parameter set to 0.5, ensuring a moderate degree of randomness in the
2121   state transitions. The first reward matrix r1 is drawn from a Beta distribution with shape parameters
2122
       Оұ = 0.5 and ОІ = 0.5, leading to rewards spread across the interval [0, 1], with a higher probability
       near the extremes of 0 and 1. The second reward matrix r2 is sampled from a Beta distribution with
2123
       shape parameters Оұ = 0.2 and ОІ = 0.9, producing rewards skewed toward lower values, introducing
2124
       diversity in the reward structure. We use 5 random seeds to initialize the matrices, with the standard
2125   deviation capturing the variability across these runs.
2126
2127   Varying T . We evaluate the performance of different algorithms in a synthetic environment with
2128   |S| = 50 and |A| = 4 under varying time horizons T . Specifically, the time horizon T is varied over
2129   the values 50 Г— 103 , 70 Г— 103 , 100 Г— 103 , 150 Г— 103 , 180 Г— 103 , 200 Г— 103 , and 250 Г— 103 . For
2130   each T , we set nswitches = 1000, resulting in a transition variation budget вҲҶP,T вүҲ 300, indicating
2131   significant environmental changes across the time horizon. The reward function is kept stationary (no
2132   switching between r1 and r2 ), and therefore вҲҶR,T = 0.
2133
2134   Varying вҲҶT . We investigate the impact of changing variation budget by adjusting the number of
2135   switches nswitches while keeping the number of states |S| = 50, actions |A| = 4, and the time horizon
2136
       T = 50 Г— 103 constant. The number of switches is varied across 10, 45, 100, and 1000, with both the
       reward function and the transition dynamics being non-stationary. The observed variation in rewards
2137
       вҲҶR,T is 9, 48, 98, and 1000, respectively, and the observed variation in transitions вҲҶP,T is 4, 14, 30,
2138
       and 303, respectively, corresponding to different levels of non-stationarity.
2139
2140   Varying |S|. We study the effect of varying the number of states while keeping the time horizon
2141   T , number of actions, and variation budget вҲҶT constant. Specifically, the time horizon T is fixed
2142   at 50 Г— 103 steps, and the number of states is varied across the values 100, 150, 175, and 200,
2143   corresponding to environments with different state sizes while keeping the number of actions fixed at
2144   4. The nswitches is adjusted to 75, 100, 120, and 150, respectively, in order to maintain a consistent
2145   вҲҶP,T of around 14 for all environments. The reward function is kept stationary (no switching between
2146   r1 and r2 ), and therefore вҲҶR,T = 0.
2147
2148   Varying |A|. We examine the effect of varying the number of actions while keeping the time
2149
       horizon T , number of states, and variation budget вҲҶT constant. Specifically, the time horizon T
       is fixed at 50 Г— 103 steps, and the number of actions is varied across the values 5, 10, 20, and 25,
2150
       corresponding to environments with different action sizes while keeping the number of states fixed
2151
       at 50. The nswitches is kept constant at 45 across all experiments to maintain a consistent variation
2152   budget вҲҶP,T of around 14 for all environments. The reward function is kept stationary (no switching
2153   between r1 and r2 ), and therefore вҲҶR,T = 0.
