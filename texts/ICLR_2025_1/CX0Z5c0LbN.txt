011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            In this study, we propose a novel approach to anomaly detection in the tabular do-
014            main using normalizing flows, leveraging a simple likelihood test to achieve state-
015
               of-the-art performance in unsupervised learning. Although simple likelihood tests
               have been shown to fail in anomaly detection for image data, we redefine the coun-
016
               terintuitive phenomenon and demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, why
017
               this method succeeds in the tabular domain. Our approach outperforms traditional
018            anomaly detection methods by offering more consistent results. Furthermore, we
019            question the practice of fine-tuning parameters for each dataset individually, en-
020            suring fair and unbiased comparisons by adopting uniform hyperparameters across
021            all datasets. Through extensive experimentation, we validate the robustness and
022            scalability of our method, highlighting its practical effectiveness in real-world set-
023            tings.

      Generative models with artificial neural networks are models that learn the distribution of data and
      are actively researched and utilized in diverse applications such as the generation of industrial data,
029
      medical data, etc. An example of such a model is the variational autoencoder (Kingma, 2013), which
030
      adopts Evidence Lower BOund(ELBO) as an objective function and maximizes it to indirectly max-
031
      imize the log-likelihood log p(x) for input data x ∈ Rd , normalizing flow (Dinh et al., 2014); in
032
      addition it utilizes, an invertible function f to transform the distribution of complex input data into
033   a simple distribution such as the Standard Gaussian Distribution, as well as generative adversarial
034   networks (Goodfellow et al., 2020), where a network comprising a discriminator and two generators
035   is trained to minimize/maximize the minimax loss, respectively. Models that can obtain the true
036   likelihood of the input data, such as normalizing flow, can be utilized in the field of anomaly detec-
037   tion or out-of-distribution detection (OOD Detection) through likelihood1 . The simplest approach
038   of anomaly detection using normalizing flow is to assume that normal data x ∈ Rd follows the
039   distribution P of normal data, and anomalous data x′ ∈ Rd follows a distribution P ̸= Q, and to de-
040   termine that a given data xtest ∈ Rd is an anomaly if its likelihood ϕP (x) is lower than a predefined
041   threshold α when tested.
042   This methodology is based on the intuition that anomalous data are less likely to be observed in the
043   distribution of normal data. However, the image domain illustrates that in-distribution data utilized
044   as training data in models that can obtain the likelihood of the input data indirectly or directly, such
045   as normalizing flow, exhibit similar or even lower likelihoods than out-of-distribution data. For
046   example, when CIFAR10 (Alex, 2009) is used as training data (In-distribution) and SVHN (Netzer
047   et al., 2011) is used as the test data (Out-of-distribution) of a model that can obtain the likelihood of
048   input data, SVHN has a higher likelihood than CIFAR10 (Nalisnick et al., 2018). Kirichenko et al.
049
      (2020) analyzes why this counterintuitive phenomenon occurs in anomaly detection for normalizing
      flow and suggests an approach to mitigate this phenomenon. Serrà et al. (2019), and Osada et al.
050
      (2024) experimentally and theoretically demonstrated that normalizing flow tends to assign low
051
      likelihood to complex images, i.e., images with high bit rates and compression algorithms like PNG,

072   In a real unsupervised setting, we train only normal datasets, limiting the optimal hyperparameter
073   selection. However, previous papers that performed tabular anomaly detection under unsupervised
074   settings have selected different hyperparameters for each dataset when evaluating the performance

100   Normalizing flow is one of the generative models that converts input data x ∈ Rd , which follows
101   an unknown distribution called px , into z ∈ Rd ; in addition, it follows a simple distribution pz
102   that typically selected standard Gaussian N (0, Id ) (Dinh et al., 2016), using an invertible function
103   f : Rd → Rd that consists of complex functions such as neural networks (Dinh et al., 2014), such
104   that px can be expressed as a formula expressed in terms of pz . At this point, px is expressed as
105   the determinant of Jacobian of x and z and pz by the change-of-variable rule, and is expressed as
106   Equation 1.

      In general, it learns in the direction of maximizing the likelihood log px (x) of the learning input
109   data, and approximates the distribution of the input data (Caterini & Loaiza-Ganem, 2022). The
110   normalizing flow model can be categorized into a model whose volume has a constant-volume term
111   that is invariant to the input data (e.g. Dinh et al. (2014)) and variant to the input data (e.g. Rezende
112   & Mohamed (2015); Dinh et al. (2016); Kingma & Dhariwal (2018); Behrmann et al. (2019); Chen
113   et al. (2019); Durkan et al. (2019)). When sampling new data, sampling is performed by extract-
114   ing it from the pre-defined pz and inputting it as the input of f −1 . The normalizing flow has the
115   advantage of being able to obtain the actual likelihood of the input data, unlike models such as
116   variational autoencoder and generative adversarial network, it has the advantage of not requiring
117
      the additional likelihood approximate inference techniques (Nalisnick et al., 2018). However, nor-
      malizing flow has two constraints: 1) the computational amount of Jacobian must not become too
118
      large, and 2) the inverse of f must exist. Therefore, the following methodologies were utilized to
119
      ensure the ease of Jacobian calculation and the existence of the inverse f −1 : 1) introducing a special
120   architecture such as a coupling layer (Dinh et al., 2014; 2016; Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018), 2) using
121   a function of a special form (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015), 3) using the approximation method of
122   power series and the Lipschitz constraint (Behrmann et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019), etc. In our
123   study, we demonstrated that NICE (Dinh et al., 2014), a relatively simple model, outperforms other
124   comparison models when performing a simple likelihood test. We refer to this methodology using
125   normalizing flow as NF-SLT (Normalizing Flow with Simple Likelihood Test).

127   2.2   C OUNTERINTUITIVE P HENOMENON OF L IKELIHOOD
128
129   Nalisnick et al. (2018) reported that a counterintuitive phenomenon regarding likelihood assignment
130   occurs in models that can obtain exact likelihood, such as normalizing flow, in the image domain.
131
      This study lays the foundation for identifying the cause of this phenomenon or suggesting solutions.
      Kirichenko et al. (2020); Schirrmeister et al. (2020) improved performance by changing the structure
132
      of the existing flow model. In Addition, the latter proposed a methodology to improve performance
133
      by reflecting the hierarchical structure of data. Serrà et al. (2019) quantified complexity through
134   a general compression algorithm such as PNG, based on experimental results, demonstrating that
135   simple images exhibit higher likelihood, and presented an anomaly score combining the likelihood
136   and complexity terms. Kamkari et al. (2024) used Local Intrinsic Dimension (LID) to measure an
137   image’s simplicity and proposed a dual thresholding method for LID and likelihood to improve per-
138   formance. Morningstar et al. (2021), Osada et al. (2024), and Ahmadian et al. (2021) are improved
139   the disadvantages of using only a single likelihood by estimating the density of a vector that com-
140   bines the likelihood and several statistics (e.g. complexity, determinant of Jacobian). Nalisnick et al.
141   (2019) demonstrated the perspective that detection may fail because in-distribution data are located
142
      in the typicality set (Cover, 1999) and OOD data is in the high density set. Zhang et al. (2021)
      presented the view that the counterintuitive phenomenon occurs due to misestimation of the model.
143
      Le Lan & Dinh (2021) demonstrated that even with a perfect model, simple likelihood-based meth-
144
      ods can fail due to variants in the representation. Ren et al. (2019) improved performance by using
145   the likelihood ratio between the background and semantic models. and Caterini & Loaiza-Ganem
146   (2022) explained the cause of this phenomenon from an entropic perspective and why the likelihood
147   ratio model works well. We define a general counterintuitive phenomenon that can be applied to
148   all domains, which demonstrates that a simple likelihood-based method that adopts a flow model
149   in the tabular domain outperforms other existing methods, showing “counter of counterintuitive”
150   phenomenon in which the counterintuitive phenomenon do not occur or rarely occur unlike image
151   domain through extended experiments and theoretical analysis.

153   3     D EFINITION OF C OUNTERINTUITIVE P HENOMENON
154
155
      Anomaly detection methods based on likelihood tests have revealed surprising results in cer-
156   tain cases, commonly referred to as the counterintuitive phenomenon. This occurs when out-of-
157   distribution (OOD) data are assigned higher likelihoods than in-distribution data, contradicting the
158   expected behavior of likelihood-based models. Nalisnick et al. (2018) first observed this issue when
159   models trained on CIFAR10 images as in-distribution data assigned higher likelihoods to OOD data
160   such as SVHN. These findings challenge the assumption that normal data should have higher likeli-
161   hoods than anomalous data. While the counterintuitive phenomenon has been extensively studied in
      image datasets, its prevalence in tabular data remains uncertain. Earlier research (Kirichenko et al.,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2020) noted instances where in-distribution and OOD data had overlapping likelihoods in tabular
163   datasets, but these findings were limited to only a few datasets and lacked comprehensive compar-
164   isons with other anomaly detection models. To address this gap, we propose a generalized definition
165   of the counterintuitive phenomenon that applies to diverse tabular datasets.
166
      To formalize this phenomenon, we begin by establishing two core assumptions:
167
168   Assumption 1. If a counterintuitive phenomenon occurs, most comparison models should outper-
169   form the generative model on an anomaly detection task.
170   Assumption 2. Even if the above condition is satisfied, the performance gap between the generative
171   model and comparison models must be significant to qualify as a counterintuitive phenomenon. If
172   the gap is small, it cannot be considered counterintuitive.
173
      We now formalize this phenomenon using these assumptions.

208

211   pears in tabular anomaly detection datasets. Some might argue that assigning higher likelihoods to
212   anomalies than to normal data suggests a counterintuitive phenomenon, but this view is incomplete.
213   It focuses only on individual data points rather than overall model performance. The occurrence of a
214   counterintuitive phenomenon must be determined by comparing the generative model’s performance
215   against other models (e.g., k-NN, OCSVM), not simply by an AUROC lower than 100%. Without
      this comparative analysis, it’s difficult to conclusively identify the phenomenon.

      Dataset and Preprocessing The experiment was conducted using the data split protocol in Zong
224   et al. (2018). To explain this protocol, 50% of normal data is used for training, and the remaining
225   50% of normal and abnormal data are used as test data. We used all 47 tabular datasets presented
226   in ADBench(Han et al., 2022), except CV/NLP embedding dataset(e.g. MV-Tec Embedding). To
227   the best of our knowledge, this is the first time we have run an experiment with all the tabular
228   data proposed in ADBench under the data split protocol used in the experiment. This setting was
229   motivated by Shwartz-Ziv & Armon (2022), who criticized that researchers selected datasets with
230   selection bias to make it look like they were performing well; hence, we adopted all of the pro-
231   posed benchmark datasets without selection. All models except the NeuTraLAD model utilized
232
      RobustScaler provided by the Python library Scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) to standardize the
      input data. The reason for excluding NeuTraLAD is that a significant performance decrease was
233
      observed when scaling.
234
235   Fair Hyperparameter Searching Most of the studies in the tabular anomaly detection task follow
236   the dataset splitting protocol of Zong et al. (2018), which is an unsupervised setting with no valida-
237   tion set, so choosing the optimal hyperparameter for each dataset is impossible. However, different
238
      hyperparameters were selected for each datasets to demonstrate performance, against the unsuper-
      vised setting; therefore, we conducted a fair hyperparameter search. This method first selects some
239
      hyperparameters that are considered important for each comparison model (e.g. select k in k-NN),
240
      and then defines a discrete hyperparameter-specific searching space. Then, for each dataset, after
241   experimenting with all combinations in the hyperparameter searching space with 10 repeated exper-
242   iments, the hyperparameter combination with the highest average AUROC (Area Under Receiver
243   Operating Characteristic Curve) for all datasets is selected as the representative hyperparameter
244   combination to demonstrate the performance of the model. The hyperparameter search space for
245   each model is recorded in Appendix B.
246
      Models We compared the performance of 7 shallow AD models and 6 deep AD models. We imple-
247   mented the shallow model using the PyOD (Zhao et al., 2019). The compared shallow models are

249   1999), k-NN (Ramaswamy et al., 2000), COPOD (Li et al., 2020), and ECOD (Li et al., 2022). The
250   compared deep models are DAGMM (Zong et al., 2018), DeepSVDD (Ruff et al., 2018), GOAD

      model uses the statistics of the test data when performing orthogonal projection, which was judged

256
      are considered essential for each comparison model (e.g. select k in k-NN), and then defines a

461   6   L IMITATION
462
463   We demonstrate that counterintuitive phenomena rarely occur in tabular anomaly detection by show-
464   ing that NF-SLT outperforms most shallow and deep models. However, it still fails to beat the per-
465   formance of k-NN, a strong baseline. In addition, there is a limitation in that the experiments were
466   conducted only using relatively simple models, NICE and RealNVP, rather than advanced normaliz-
467   ing flow models. However, our study focuses on that counterintuitive phenomena rarely occur when
468
      performing simple likelihood tests in tabular domains and provides empirical and theoretical expla-
      nations for why ”counter of counterintuitive” phenomena. Therefore, in addition to comparing the
469
      anomaly detection performance with advanced normalizing flow models, future work could be to
470
      propose a flow architecture that can outperform k-NN in the tabular domain compared to the current
471   flow model architecture suitable for the image domain like RealNVP’s masking strategy.
472
473
474   7   C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we investigated whether the counterintuitive phenomenon observed in anomaly de-
477   tection for image data also occurs in the tabular domain. Through both theoretical analysis and
478   extensive experiments, we showed that this phenomenon is rare in tabular data when using simple
479   likelihood tests with normalizing flow. Our results demonstrate that normalizing flow-based meth-
480   ods are highly effective for tabular anomaly detection, outperforming traditional models without
481   encountering the issues seen in the image domain.
482   We also addressed the problem of biased hyperparameter selection in previous studies, proposing
483   a fair and consistent evaluation framework. Based on our findings, future research should focus
484   on developing normalizing flow architectures specifically designed for tabular data and ensuring
485   fair hyperparameter selection in unsupervised settings. Our work provides a strong foundation for
      advancing anomaly detection techniques in structured data domains.
