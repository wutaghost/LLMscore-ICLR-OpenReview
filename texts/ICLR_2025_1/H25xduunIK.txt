                                                  A BSTRACT
012            The rapid development and dynamic nature of large language models (LLMs)
013            make it difficult for conventional quantitative benchmarks to accurately assess their
014            capabilities. We propose Report Cards, which are human-interpretable, natural
015            language summaries of model behavior for specific skills or topics. We develop a
016            framework to evaluate Report Cards based on three criteria: specificity (ability to
017            distinguish between models), faithfulness (accurate representation of model capa-
018            bilities), and interpretability (clarity and relevance to humans). We also propose
019
               an iterative algorithm for generating Report Cards without human supervision and
               explore its efficacy by ablating various design choices. Through experimentation
020
               with popular LLMs, we demonstrate that Report Cards provide insights beyond
021
               traditional benchmarks and can help address the need for a more interpretable and
022            holistic evaluation of LLMs.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
      The generality of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020) admits a near-infinite range of
027
      potential tasks and outputs. This vast possibility space poses significant challenges for evaluation.
028
      While benchmarks such as GLUE (Wang et al., 2018) and BIG-bench (BIG-bench authors, 2023)
029   measure various aspects of model performance, such quantitative metrics often fail to capture the full
030   spectrum of LLM capabilities, limitations, and potential risks. Moreover, the focus on quantifiable
031   leaderboards risks overfitting, thereby invoking Goodhart’s law and undermining the value of these
032   metrics. The black-box nature of many LLMs further complicates the interpretation of their behaviors.
033   Consequently, there is a pressing need for innovative evaluation approaches that provide more holistic,
034   interpretable, and context-rich assessments of LLM performance (Ethayarajh and Jurafsky, 2020;
035   Arnold and et al., 2019; Birhane and et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024).
036   Qualitative assessment emerges as a natural approach, which may be necessary to fully understand
037   model behavior and identify potential failures or biases (Ribeiro et al., 2020; Geva et al., 2022).
038   However, manual inspections of LLM outputs, although insightful, are labor-intensive and can be
039
      limited in scope (Callison-Burch, 2009; OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023).
040   To alleviate the labor-intensive nature of qualitative assessments and to complement quantitative
041   benchmarks with human-interpretable insights, we propose using LLMs to generate Report Cards,
042
      which are interpretable, natural language summaries of model capabilities in relation to specific
      skills or topics. Excerpts from example Report Cards are shown in Figure 1. We generate Report
043
      Cards for various “student” LLMs across multiple skills, focusing on areas with existing quantitative
044
      benchmarks, to assess the supplementary value of Report Cards. We assess the quality of Report
045   Cards from three perspectives: specificity, faithfulness, and interpretability. We quantify specificity
046   using a contrastive metric, which measures how effectively Report Cards can be used to differentiate
047   between models. We assess faithfulness by comparing estimates of model performance derived
048   from Report Cards to those based on direct output comparisons. Lastly, we evaluate interpretability
049   through human annotations of relevance, informativeness, and clarity. Our experiments also include
050   ablation studies investigating how performance disparities between models and various design choices
051   influence Report Card effectiveness.

      Our main contributions are:
095       1. We introduce Report Cards, a novel approach to interpretable, qualitative evaluations of LLM
096          behavior. Report Cards address the limitations of purely quantitative metrics and provide richer
097          insights into model performance.
098       2. We propose a set of metrics to evaluate the specificity, faithfulness, and interpretability of Report
099          Cards, which we use to validate our approach on a variety of LLMs.
100       3. We present PRESS, an iterative algorithm for generating Report Cards that is competitive with
101          less interpretable baselines and robust to test-time paraphrasing. We investigate factors affecting
102
             summary quality through extensive ablation studies.

      2.1      T HE ROLE OF Q UALITATIVE E VALUATION
106
107   Approaches to LLM evaluation span a continuum, trading off between simplicity and comprehen-
      siveness. At one extreme, summary statistics such as validation set accuracy offer concise, easily


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      comparable metrics. This is what is commonly reported on leaderboards. For example, Holistic
109   Evaluation of Language Models (HELM) (Liang et al., 2022) considers statistics such as accuracy,
110   calibration, robustness, and fairness. Any single metric on its own, however, typically has poor
111   robustness to different test distributions (Ethayarajh and Jurafsky, 2020). For instance, Liu et al.
112   (2024) conducted a fine-grained evaluation of math capabilities and found that models with similar
113   overall scores exhibited different fine-grained characteristics. Some models performed better on
114   theoretical versus applied problems, and there were nuances when assessing math abilities in a
115   bilingual context. This makes it difficult to gain a meaningful understanding of model capabilities
116   from benchmark measures, beyond the ordinal ranking of models that they provide.
117   The other extreme is to use the model’s outputs as a way of showing its performance, for example
118   by crudely concatenating the set of questions from a specific topic or benchmark along with the
119   model’s responses. While this extremely verbose approach preserves all the information about the
120
      model’s behavior, it becomes prohibitively difficult for humans to read and understand as the number
      of questions grows. For this reason, the sample-based approach to evaluation is primarily used with a
121
      small number of samples to showcase “surprising” behaviors or capabilities, including failure modes.
122
      Between these extremes, there are qualitative assessments of model behavior, such as the detailed
123
      reports by OpenAI (2023) and Bubeck et al. (2023) on GPT-4’s capabilities. Such assessments strike
124
      a balance between conciseness and clarity, however they are conducted ad hoc and require extensive
125   human inspection. As such, there is no standard approach to qualitative assessment. We propose
126   LLM generated Report Cards to bridge this gap and serve as an automatic and human-interpretable
127   evaluation method. Report Cards summarize an LLM’s behavior with respect to a skill or topic (see,
128   e.g., Figure 1). We design and evaluate Report Cards with the following desiderata in mind:
129      • Specificity: A Report Card should accurately describe unique aspects of model behavior, so that
130        it may be used to distinguish between models.
131      • Faithfulness: The specific behaviors described by a Report Card, taken as a whole, should
132        accurately capture the model’s overall capability with respect to the skill it describes.
133      • Interpretability: A Report Card should be relevant, informative, and clear to humans.
134
135
      We assess these aspects using a combination of different metrics, detailed in Section 2.2. Our
      approach uses LLMs in three distinct roles: the “student” models being evaluated, the evaluator that
136
      drafts the Report Cards, and the guesser or judge that assesses the quality of the Report Cards.
137

      To measure faithfulness, we use an Elo rating (Elo,
163   1978) derived from pairwise comparisons of Report
164   Cards. The Elo system, originally developed for
165   chess player rankings, provides a method to calculate
166   relative skill levels in two-player games, which we
167   adapt here to compare models. For a given set of
168   models, we consider two schemes for determining
169   wins and losses for Elo computation:
170   • Oracle Elo: Given a query q, and completions
171     aMi and aMj from students i and j, the winner is
172     determined by the ground-truth answer if available
173     (such as in MMLU). Otherwise, we use a judge
174     LLM to select the preferred completion.
175   • Card Elo: Given a pair of Report Cards Si and Sj
176     describing students i and j, a judge LLM awards a
177     win to the preferred student.
178   Each scheme is used to produce an Elo rating
179   for each model in a comparison set. If the card- Figure 3: Faithfulness is measured by the

354   3.3   FAITHFULNESS E VALUATION
355
356
      We evaluate the faithfulness of Report Cards—how well they reflect the model’s genuine capabilities—
      by computing the R2 score between the Card Elo and Oracle Elo metrics described in Section 2.2. A
357
      high R2 indicates that the card is faithful to the completions. We focus on MMLU and the open-ended
358
      CN Grammar dataset, on which models display significant capability differences. For MMLU, the
359   results by topic are largely similar, and we report the average R2 score across topics.
360
      Figure 7 compares the faithfulness of Report Cards to two baselines: (a) ChatbotArena Elo (Zheng
361   et al., 2023), which represents each model’s general capability as measured by human annotators,
362   and (b) Few-shot Elo, which represents each model using k samples, as described in Section 3.2. For
363   the few-shot baseline, we present two types of results. The scatter points and solid bars represent
364   “individual faithfulness,” showing the average R2 across ten individual runs, each with a different
365   fixed set of few shot samples. The shaded bars indicate “aggregation improvement,” where we
366   average Elo from all individual runs before computing the R2 faithfulness score, which reduces
367   variance and noise. This uses ten times as many comparisons as Card Elo.
368   Report Cards consistently obtained the highest faithfulness scores, which suggests that they can better
369   represent skill-specific capabilities than general metrics such as ChatbotArena Elo or sample-based
370   representation like the few-shot baseline. Note that while one could represent a model’s capability
371
      using Oracle Elo directly, this requires significantly more comparisons and does not provide an
      interpretable summary of model behavior. Importantly, k-shot completion Elo, using the same
372
      number of comparisons as Card Elo, obtains a significantly worse faithfulness score than Card Elo.

458   4     R ELATED W ORK
459
460
      Our work builds upon several research directions in AI evaluation and transparency. These include
      efforts to document model characteristics and capabilities, automated evaluation methods, and
461
      approaches to generating interpretable summaries of model behavior.
462
463
464   Model documentation and qualitative evaluations Prior work on Model Cards emphasizes the
465   importance of documenting key model details and intended use (Mitchell et al., 2019; Arnold and
466   et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2022). Studies have highlighted the importance of
467   conciseness (Bracamonte et al., 2023) and interactive exploration (Crisan et al., 2022) to improve the
468
      interpretability of such documentation. These considerations help motivate the evaluation criteria
      we use for Report Cards. As compared to Model Cards, Report Cards focus more on context-
469
      specific model capabilities than intended use. Report Cards draw inspiration from existing qualitative
470
      evaluations, such as those in OpenAI (2023); Bubeck et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024), which probe
471   for risky behaviors such as hallucinations and disinformation. Our framework could help identify
472   such risky behaviors if used with datasets like Anthropic’s Advanced AI Risk (Perez et al., 2022).
473
474
475   Automatic and open-ended evaluation Recent work has focused on developing automatic and
476   open-ended evaluation methods for language models. LLMs are increasingly used to assess them-
477
      selves and other LLMs (Ribeiro et al., 2020; Panickssery et al., 2024), offering scalable evaluation
      that often agrees with human judgment (Chiang and Lee, 2023b; Zheng et al., 2023; Hackl et al.,
478
      2023; Chang et al., 2024). For example, approaches like GPTScore (Fu et al., 2023) and G-EVAL
479
      (Liu et al., 2023) use LLMs to score user-defined metrics. Systems based on pairwise comparisons
480   of language model outputs, as used in Chatbot Arena (Zheng et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2024) and
481   AlpacaEval Li et al. (2023), have emerged as key quantitative measurements of LLM capabilities with
482   respect to open-ended prompts. While these methods effectively capture overall model capabilities,
483   they are prone to prompt sensitivity and potential biases such as length bias and automated judges
484   preferring their own responses (Dubois et al., 2024; Panickssery et al., 2024). Our approach with
485   Report Cards complements these quantitative approaches with nuanced qualitative assessments that
      ground the evaluation using interpretable summaries of model completions.


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Fine-grained LLM evaluation Recent research has focused on developing nuanced evaluation
487   methods for LLMs to provide a detailed understanding of capabilities across various skills and
488   contexts. (Li et al., 2024) proposed a framework for fine-grained analysis of LLM performance,
489   while (Zhao et al., 2024) introduced targeted probing tasks for specific domains. (Song et al., 2024)
490   developed a multidimensional framework considering factors like faithfulness and coherence. Chen
491   et al. (2024) proposed the Self-Challenge framework where LLMs identify their own limitations by
492   generating challenging test cases, leading to a benchmark that revealed systematic weaknesses from
493   tokenization issues to logical reasoning that persist across different LLMs. Murahari et al. (2024)
494   introduced QualEval, a framework that improves traditional metrics with qualitative insights and
495
      more fine-grained evaluation. However, they focus on evaluation to improve the model, while we seek
      to generate faithful and interpretable reports for humans. Our work complements prior approaches
496
      by generating interpretable summaries of model behavior and facilitating holistic and interpretable
497
      evaluations of LLMs.
498

      5    C ONCLUSION
501   We introduce Report Cards for qualitatively evaluating LLMs, along with three metrics to measure
502   their effectiveness. Report Cards offer a new tool for understanding and assessing LLM capabilities,
503   and can be used to complement existing quantitative metrics with qualitative insights. Our experiments
504   demonstrate that Report Cards produced using our PRESS algorithm are interpretable, specific, and
505   faithful across various topics and datasets, and showcase our method’s versatility and potential for
506   broad application in the field of LLM research.
507   Our work, while promising, has certain limitations that point to important future directions. The
508   specificity and faithfulness of Report Cards are heavily reliant on the capabilities of both the evaluator
509   and judge (guesser) models; therefore, advancements in these models could significantly improve
510   Report Card generation and assessment. Addressing potential biases in LLM-based evaluations
511
      remains an important challenge to ensure fair and comprehensive assessments: it is conceivable that
      Report Cards while mitigating biases based on stylistic elements, could introduce other biases that
512
      we are not yet aware of. Moreover, our experiments are limited to specific topics and datasets. Future
513
      work should consider applying Report Cards to a wider range of domains—including open-ended
514   tasks like reasoning, creative writing, and emotional understanding. Finally, we collected limited
515   human evaluation for interpretability, and a more extensive human annotation (or an approach to
516   LLM scoring that exhibits improved alignment) could provide more accurate and comprehensive
517   assessments on Report Cards. Future work addressing these challenges would strengthen Report
518   Cards as a holistic and interpretable approach to qualitatively evaluating LLMs.

655   A    R EPORT C ARDS F ORMATS
656
657   In preliminary experiments, we explored three different formats for Report Cards: bullet point
658   (BP), hierarchical bullet point (HIER), and paragraph. Each format offers unique advantages in
659
      presenting information about model capabilities and performance. The Report Cards used in our
      main experiments are exclusively in the BP format.
660
661
      Bullet Point Format The bullet point format decomposes the Report Card into multiple categories
662
      or skills, presenting information in a concise, interpretable, and easy-to-scan list. Each bullet point
663   typically focuses on a particular aspect of the model’s performance, making it easier for readers to
664   quickly identify strengths and weaknesses across various fine-grained criteria.
665
666    {
667          "<criterion_1>": "<description_1>",
668          "<criterion_2>": "<description_2>",
669          "..."
670
       }
671          "..."
672    }
673
674
675
      Hierarchical Bullet Point Format This format builds on the bullet point format, and presents
676
      information in a nested structure. It is inspired by how a teacher might write a report card, providing
677
      an overview followed by more detailed observations. The hierarchical structure allows for both
678   high-level summaries and in-depth analysis within each category. The structure of the hierarchical
679   bullet point format is as follows:
680
681
                {
682          "<criterion>": {
683               "overview": "<general assessment>",
684               "thinking_pattern": "<description of reasoning approach>",
685               "strength": "<model strengths in criterion>",
                  "weakness": "<model weaknesses in criterion>"
686
             },
687          "..."
688    }
689
690
691
      Paragraph Format In this approach, the Report Card is crafted into a single, coherent paragraph.
692
      This narrative encompasses the model’s principal capabilities, strengths, weaknesses, and other
693   pertinent traits. Although this format offers a fluid and natural description, it might pose challenges
694   for quickly locating specific information and capturing nuanced characteristics.
695
696   Our experiments use the bullet point format, as it offers the best balance between brevity and
697   informativeness, as shown in the ablation study of Appendix C.2. This format allows for efficient
698   comparison between models while still providing sufficient detail about their capabilities. The
699   hierarchical bullet point format, while more comprehensive, tended to be longer and potentially more
700   cumbersome for quick reference. The paragraph format, although providing a narrative flow, was
701   empirically less effective for the assessment of model strengths and weaknesses across multiple
      domains.
