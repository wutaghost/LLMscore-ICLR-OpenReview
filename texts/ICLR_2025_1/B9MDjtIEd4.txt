009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We focus on knowledge transfer in offline reinforcement learning (RL), which
012            aims to significantly improve the learning of an optimal policy in a target task
013            based on a pre-collected dataset without further interactions with the environment.
014            Data scarcity and high-dimensional feature spaces seriously pose challenges to of-
015            fline RL in many real-world applications, and knowledge transfer offers a promis-
016            ing solution. We propose a novel and comprehensive knowledge transfer frame-
017            work for offline RL, which carefully considers the relationship between the target
018            and source tasks within the linear Markov decision process (MDP) framework.
019
               This enables efficient knowledge transfer from related source tasks to enhance
               learning in the target task and effectively address data scarcity concerns in offline
020
               RL. Our main contributions include establishing a relationship with the learning
021
               process between the target task and source task, introducing an effective and robust
022            knowledge transfer technique to reduce the suboptimality of the learned policy,
023            and demonstrating the significant effectiveness of the knowledge transfer frame-
024            work through detailed theoretical analysis. Our work significantly contributes to
025            the advancement of offline RL by providing a practical and robust framework for
026            knowledge transfer facilitating more efficient and effective data utilization in var-
027            ious applications.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved remarkable success in various applications, which
033   largely relies on two crucial factors: (i) powerful function approximators, such as deep neural net-
034   works (LeCun et al., 2015; Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2016; Vinyals et al., 2017), that can
035   approximate policies and values with high precision, and (ii) efficient data generators, like simula-
036   tion environments (Bellemare et al., 2013; Todorov et al., 2012), that facilitate the collection of large
037   amounts of data through interactions with the environment. However, in many real-world scenarios,
038   such as robotics and healthcare, obtaining massive interactive data can be extremely costly, time-
039
      consuming, and even pose risks. Therefore, we focus on offline RL, which aims to learn an optimal
      policy based on a pre-collected dataset without further interactions with the environment.
040
041   In today’s rapidly evolving technological landscape, offline reinforcement learning (RL) has
042   emerged as a crucial area of research in data-driven decision-making. It aims to learn optimal
043   policies based on datasets collected a priori, without the need for further interactions with the en-
044   vironment. This is particularly relevant in various domains, such as marketing, healthcare, and
045
      education, where data scarcity and high-dimensional feature spaces pose significant challenges. Un-
      like online RL, offline RL is still relatively less understood from a theoretical perspective (Lange
046
      et al., 2012; Levine et al., 2020), which poses significant challenges in developing reliable algo-
047
      rithms for practical applications. In particular, since active interactions with the environment are not
048   feasible in offline RL, it becomes difficult to exploit the dataset without further exploration fully.
049   Due to the lack of continuous exploration, any algorithm for offline RL may suffer from the prob-
050   lem of insufficient dataset coverage (Wang et al., 2020). Specifically, two main challenges arise (i)
051   the intrinsic uncertainty, where the dataset may fail to cover the trajectory induced by the optimal
052   policy, which contains essential information; (ii) the spurious correlation, meaning that the dataset
053   may accidentally cover a trajectory that is unrelated to the optimal policy, but which can mislead the
      learned policy (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Agarwal et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2020; Gulcehre et al., 2020).

      In the context of offline RL, knowledge transfer offers a promising approach to improving learning
055   efficiency and performance. By transferring knowledge from related source tasks to a target task,
056   we can exploit the relationship between target and source tasks to overcome the data scarcity issue.
057   However, existing literature on knowledge transfer or transfer learning for RL lacks a thorough
058   examination of the theoretical guarantees for value function estimation.
059
      In this paper, we aim to answer the following question:
060
061   Can we develop a knowledge transfer framework that effectively addresses the data scarcity and
062   provides rigorous theoretical guarantees?
063   In conclusion, we propose a novel framework for knowledge transfer in offline RL (KT-RL). The
064   contributions of our work are concluded as follows:
065
066   1. Knowledge Transfer Framework Innovation
067
          • Breaking Conventional Assumptions: We assume that the target data is a linear combination
068         of source data. This assumption provides a novel perspective and method for knowledge transfer
069         in offline RL, departing from the common practices in existing literature.
070
          • Comprehensive Consideration of Task Relationships: Based on the linear Markov Decision
071         Process (MDP) framework, we comprehensively consider the relationships between the target
072         task and source tasks. This approach effectively addresses the data scarcity problem and en-
073         hances the learning performance of the target task.
074
      2. Theoretical Contributions
075
076       • Establishing Theoretical Relationships: We establish a theoretical relationship between the
077         learning processes of the target task and source tasks. By introducing knowledge transfer tech-
078
            niques, we can reduce the suboptimality of the learned policy.
079       • Providing Bounds for Algorithm Evaluation: Through theoretical analysis, we provide an up-
080         per bound on the suboptimality of our algorithm. Additionally, we prove the minimax optimality
081
            of the algorithm, which offers a solid basis for evaluating the performance of the algorithm.
082   3. Algorithm Design Contributions
083       • Efficient Source Data Processing and Integration
084
          – Separate Calculation of Source Data Statistics: For each source task, we calculate statistical
085
             quantities separately. In each step, we define the empirical mean squared Bellman error (MSBE)
086          and calculate the estimated Bellman operator, confidence bound, value function, action-value
087          function, etc. This process fully considers the characteristics of each source task and retains its
088          unique information. Unlike the transfer learning methods in (Chen et al., 2024; Lei et al., 2024),
089          which require aggregating raw data from various sources, our approach only necessitates the
090          sharing of statistical quantities from the model. From this perspective, our algorithm enhances
091          privacy preservation by decentralized sensitive raw data.
092       – Integration of Source Data for Target Task: We integrate the statistical quantities obtained
093          from each source task to calculate the target task. This integration method takes into account
094          the diversity of source data and effectively transfers knowledge. It enhances the flexibility
095
             and effectiveness of the algorithm in handling different source-target task relationships, thus
             improving the accuracy and effectiveness of knowledge transfer.
096
097       • Ensuring Dataset Compliance and Trajectory Independence: In data collection process, we
098
            assume that the dataset complies with the underlying MDP and the trajectories are independent.
            This assumption simplifies the analysis process and ensures the reliability and stability of the
099
            algorithm in practical applications.
100
101
      These contributions together significantly advance the field of offline RL and offer potential for more
102   efficient and effective decision-making in various real-world applications.

      Reinforcement Learning: There is a rich body of literature on offline RL algorithms, such as
109   (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Agarwal et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2020; Gulcehre et al., 2020). These algorithms
110   aim to learn an optimal policy based on a pre-collected dataset without further interactions with the
111   environment. Our proposed algorithm contributes to this area by incorporating transfer learning to
112   enhance learning efficiency. There are several work falls within the realm of batch reinforcement
113   learning, where the goal is to learn an optimal policy from a fixed dataset without further interactions
114   with the environment (Shi et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024).
115   Knowledge Transfer in Reinforcement Learning: Numerous studies have explored transfer learn-
116   ing in the context of reinforcement learning. Chen et al. (2022) investigated the transfer of Q-
117   learning, while Agarwal et al. (2023) focused on the benefits of representational transfer in rein-
118   forcement learning. These works provide valuable insights into how knowledge can be transferred
119   between tasks in the RL domain. Transfer learning in RL aims to leverage data from related source
120   tasks to enhance the learning on a target task (Agarwal et al., 2023). Additionally, our work is
121   related to the broader topic of knowledge transfer in sequential decision-making. Previous studies
122   have investigated utilizing data from existing ventures to navigate high-dimensional feature spaces
123
      and address data scarcity in new ventures (Liu, 2023; Komorowski et al., 2018; Rafferty et al., 2016).
      We extend this idea to the context of offline reinforcement learning, demonstrating how knowledge
124
      transfer can be applied to improve the learning efficiency in this domain. In contrast to the existing
125
      literature on transfer learning (Bastani, 2021; Lei et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022; 2023; Bastani et al.,
126   2022; Tian & Feng, 2023), which typically assumes that source data closely resembles target data,
127   our approach diverges from this assumption.
128
129
      Linear MDPs and High-Dimensional Feature Spaces in RL: Dealing with high-dimensional fea-
      ture spaces is a crucial challenge in offline reinforcement learning. Some works (Bellemare et al.,
130
      2013; Todorov et al., 2012) have focused on developing efficient data generators and function ap-
131
      proximators to address this issue. Our approach builds upon these ideas by proposing a novel transfer
132   learning framework that specifically takes into account the high-dimensional feature spaces in the
133   source and target tasks. Additionally, linear MDPs have been studied in various RL papers (Yang
134   & Wang, 2019; Jin et al., 2020). These studies have shown that linear MDPs can provide a useful
135   framework for analyzing and solving RL problems. In our work, we also utilize the concept of linear
136   MDPs to define the task discrepancy and establish the relationship between target and source tasks
137   and the learning process in the target task.

516   7     C ONCLUSION
517
518   In conclusion, we have presented a novel knowledge transfer framework for offline reinforcement
519   learning. This framework addresses the crucial challenges of data scarcity and high-dimensional fea-
520   ture spaces. By assuming a linear relationship between target and source data and comprehensively
521   considering task relationships within the linear MDP framework, we have introduced innovative
522
      approaches to knowledge transfer. Our theoretical contributions include establishing relationships
      between the learning processes of target and source tasks and providing bounds for algorithm evalu-
523
      ation, demonstrating both the suboptimality upper bound and the minimax optimality. In algorithm
524
      design, we have focused on efficient source data processing and integration, along with ensuring
525   dataset compliance and trajectory independence. Overall, our work significantly contributes to the
526   advancement of offline reinforcement learning, offering a practical and theoretically sound solution
527   for more efficient learning in various applications.
