009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Offline Reinforcement Learning (RL) addresses the problem of sequential decision-
012            making by learning optimal policy through pre-collected data, without interacting
013            with the environment. As yet, it has remained somewhat impractical, because
014            one rarely knows the reward explicitly and it is hard to distill it retrospectively.
015            Here, we show that an imitating agent can still learn the desired behavior merely
016
               from observing the expert, despite the absence of explicit rewards or action labels.
               In our method, AILOT (Aligned Imitation Learning via Optimal Transport), we
017
               involve special representation of states in a form of intents that incorporate pairwise
018
               spatial distances within the data. Given such representations, we define intrinsic
019            reward function via optimal transport distance between the expert’s and the agent’s
020            trajectories. We report that AILOT outperforms state-of-the art offline imitation
021            learning algorithms on D4RL benchmarks and improves the performance of other
022            offline RL algorithms by dense reward relabelling in the sparse-reward tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Over the past years, offline learning has remained both the most logical and the most ambitious
      avenue for the development of RL. On the one hand, there is an ever-growing reservoir of sequential
028
      data, such as videos, becoming available for training the decision-making of RL agents. On the
029
      other hand, these immense data remain largely unlabeled and unstructured for gaining any valuable
030   guidance in the form of a tractable objective for learning the underlying policy, stimulating the
031   development of unsupervised and self-supervised methods (Singh et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Sinha
032   et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Eysenbach et al., 2018; Park et al., 2023b).
033
      Following the success of language-based foundational models, the offline RL community have
034
      also begun to leverage the weakly-labeled data. However, incorporating such offline data into RL
035
      frameworks efficiently remains a challenge (Levine et al., 2020)). Several factors, such as distribution
036   shift, slow convergence when the labels are missing, and the absence of known rewards or task-specific
037   objectives, hinder the development of offline RL (Kumar et al., 2021; Fujimoto et al., 2019).
038
039
      A potential remedy to these issues is found in Imitation Learning (IL), where the explicit reward
      function is not needed. Instead, an imitating agent is trained to replicate the behavior of the expert.
040
      Behavior cloning (BC) (Ross & Bagnell, 2010) frames the IL problem akin to classical supervised
041
      learning, seeking to maximize the likelihood of the actions provided under the learner’s policy.
042   Despite working well in simple environments, BC is prone to accumulating errors in states, coming
043   from different distributions other than that of the expert. Other notable methods include Inverse RL
044   (Torabi et al., 2018; Zolna et al., 2020) and action pseudo-labeling (Kumar et al., 2020), however
045   they are rarely used in practice due to the introduced overhead. Distribution matching is another
046   promising IL paradigm, where the approaches such as DIstribution Correction Estimation ("DICE"),
047   including SMODICE (Ma et al., 2022b), attempt to match the state-occupancy measures between
048   the imitator’s and the expert’s policies. However, these methods posses several limitations: 1) they
049   require a non-zero overlap between the supports of the agent and the expert and 2) they mostly use
050
      KL-divergence (or, generally, f -divergences), which ignores the underlying geometry of the spaces
      where the distributions are defined.
051
052   Another line of development is associated with Computational Optimal Transport, a domain that has
053   garnered significant popularity for addressing diverse machine learning tasks (Peyré et al., 2019).
      Its applications span from domain adaptation to generative modeling (Salimans et al., 2018; Rout


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2021; Korotin et al., 2022), including weakly-supervised and unsupervised methods (Bespalov
055   et al., 2020; 2022). Optimal transport theory was proposed to alleviate the necessity for manual
056   reward engineering (Luo et al., 2023) via establishing an optimal coupling between a few high-
057   quality expert demonstrations and the trajectories of the learning agent, which proved to be useful
058   for Imitation Learning tasks (Haldar et al., 2022; 2023). However, performing OT matching in the
059   high-dimensional unstructured space is difficult, thus limiting the approach to low-dimensional tasks.
060
      The absence of reward labels is not the sole challenge; obtaining access to the expert actions can
061   also be problematic. When no rewards or action labels accompany the abundant demonstrations,
062   one potential remedy is to create a simulator (Krylov et al., 2020; Saboo et al., 2021) that enables
063   access to both actions and rewards. However, adopting such an approach entails substantial additional
064   work. Here, we eliminate the requirement for knowing the rewards or the action labels of the expert
065   altogether. We propose to map the initial state space to the space of intentions, where there is some
066   high-level semantic imaginary goal that the agent needs to achieve to imitate the expert. Aligning
067   intents of the agent with those of the expert via Optimal Transport represents a new pathway for
068   training efficient offline RL models.
069
      Our contribution are as follows:
070
071          • A new intrinsic dense reward relabelling algorithm that ‘comprehends’ the demonstrated
072            dynamics in the environment, on top of which any offline RL method can be used.
073          • We outperform the state-of-the-art models in the majority of Offline RL benchmarks. We
074            do so even without knowing the expert’s action labels and the ground truth rewards.
075          • We report extensive comparison with previous works. We show that our approach enables
076            custom imitation even if the agent’s data are a mix of random policies.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
080
081
      In this study, we broaden the application of offline Reinforcement Learning (RL) to datasets that lack
      both the rewards and the action labels. Despite all the research effort on learning the intrinsic rewards
082
      for RL, most works assume either online RL setting (Brown & Niekum, 2019; Yu et al., 2020; Ibarz
083
      et al., 2018) or the unrealistic setup of possessing some annotated prior data. Moreover, the problem
084   of learning and exploration in sparse-reward environments can be deemed as solved for online RL (Li
085   et al., 2023; Eysenbach et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019); whereas, only a few works exist that consider
086   the offline goal-conditioned setting with reward-free data (Park et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2023;
087   Wang et al., 2023a). The goal of our work is to extract guidance from the expert by forcing optimal
088   alignment of information-rich representations of imaginary goals between the expert and the agent in
089   a shared distance-aware latent space.
090   We build upon the approach introduced in OTR (Luo et al., 2023), extending it by finding a repre-
091   sentative distance-preserving isometry to the shared latent space and forcing alignment via optimal
092   transport in this metric-aware space. In OTR, the authors estimate the optimal coupling between
093   expert and agent trajectories, which enables rewards relabeling for agent’s state transitions by com-
094   puted Wasserstein distance. However, performing optimal transportation matching in this way is
095   sensitive to underlying cost function (e.g Cosine or Euclidean) and final results can vary drastically.
096   Choosing right distance function requires additional knowledge of the environment and extensive
097   search, limiting application to simple tasks.
098   An advantage of our method is in its ability to perform distribution alignment in the space, which
099   captures temporally structured dependencies in the provided datasets, which is completely ignored
100   in previous works. For example, a lot of methods focus on KL divergence, which is agnostic to
101   the distance metric Such temporal structure induces spatial closeness between temporally simil-
102   iar states.Additionally, the method seamlessly integrates with various downstream RL algorithms,
103   providing flexibility in selecting the most suitable training approach.
104   Another recent method called Calibrated Latent gUidancE (CLUE) (Liu et al., 2023), introduces
105   a parallel approach for deriving intrinsic rewards. In this study, the authors employ a conditional
106   variational auto-encoder trained on both expert and agent transitions and compute the euclidean
107   distance between the collapsed expert embedding and the agent trajectory. The expert embedding
      might not collapse into a single point in a multimodal expert dataset, requiring clustering to handle

108
      different skills. Unlike the CLUE method, our approach doesn’t need state-action paired labeled data,
109   eliminating the need for action annotations.

114   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION

      A standard Reinforcement Learning problem is defined as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) with
116   tuple M = (S, A, p, r, ρ0 , γ), where S ⊂ Rn is the state space, A ⊂ Rm is the action space, p is a
117   function describing transition dynamics in the environment p: S × A → P(S), r : S × A → R is a
118   predefined extrinsic reward function, ρ0 is an initial state distribution and γ ∈ (0, 1] is the discount
119   factor. The
                P∞ objective is to learn policy πθ (a|s) : S → P(A) that maximizes discounted cumulative
120   return E[ t=0 γ t r(st , at )]. In contrast, offline RL assumes access to a pre-collected static dataset of
121   transitions D = {(si , ai , s′i , ri )}ni=1 while prohibiting additional interaction with the environment. In
122   this study, we assume access to a dataset of transitions without reward labels Da = {(si , ai , s′i )}ni=1 ,
123   collected from M, and a limited number of ground truth demonstrations from the expert policy
124
      without reward and action labels De = {(si , s′i )}m      i=1 . These demonstrations are collected from the
      same MDP (M) and we assume that several trajectories from De have high cumulative return. The
125
      primary objective is to determine a policy that closely emulates the behavior of the expert and thereby
126
      maximises the cumulative return.

311   5.1      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
312
313   Dense reward relabelling by AILOT is completely decoupled from the offline policy training. In all
314   our experiments, we endow AILOT with Implicit Q-Learning (IQL) (Kostrikov et al., 2021), which is
315   a simple and a robust offline RL algorithm. In our additional experiments, we also test AILOT+IQL
316   against Diffusion-QL (Wang et al., 2022), which is a recent state-of-the-art approach for offline RL.
317   We implement AILOT in JAX (Bradbury et al., 2018) and use the official IQL implementation1 . Our
318   code is written using Equinox library (Kidger & Garcia, 2021). To compute optimal transport, we
319   use implementation of Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi, 2013) from OTT-JAX library (Cuturi et al., 2022).
320   Additional details on chosen hyperparameters and settings are provided in the Appendix A.2. Each
321   dataset includes 106 samples. We do pre-training of the ICVF procedure for 250 ∗ 103 steps. The IQL
322   parameters follow original paper recommendations (particularly 106 train steps with batch size 256).

      Runtime All of the experiments were conducted on a single RTX 3090. The runtime overhead of
342   the application of optimal transport is not greater than extra ∼ 10 minutes (compared to the offline
343   RL algorithm training time). Thus, the total runtime of AILOT+IQL equals ∼ 25 minutes. Finding
344   optimal matrix P ∗ and correspondent internal rewards takes several hundred iterations of Sinkhorn
345   solver, which is relatively fast. Moreover, because the optimal transport is computed in a fixed
346   latent space of intents, the overhead stays constant across broad range of tasks regardless of the state
347   dimensionality. For high-dimensional tasks (e.g., learning from pixels), AILOT is more suitable than
348   OTR due to performing OT in the latent space, accelerating the overall relabelling.

497   6   D ISCUSSION
498
499   In our work, we introduced AILOT – a new non state-occupancy estimation method for extracting
500   expert’s behavior in terms of its intentions and guiding the learning agent to them through an intrinsic
501   reward. We empirically show that it surpasses the state-of-the art results both in the sparse-reward RL
502   tasks and in the offline imitation learning setting. AILOT can mimic the expert behaviour without
503   knowing its action labels, and without the ground truth rewards. Moreover, we show that the intrinsic
504   rewards, distilled by AILOT, could be used to efficiently boost the performance of other offline RL
505   algorithms, thanks to the proper alignment to the expert intentions via the optimal transport.
506
507   Limitations of AILOT include assumption on sufficient access to a large number of unlabeled
508   trajectories of some acceptable quality. In our work, we have focused on the expert behaviours,
509   typically considered in the offline RL publications: popular synthetic environments with some
510
      comprehensible expert movements and, consequently, some sufficiently ‘intuitive’ intentions, which
      can be easily extracted from the provided datasets.
511
512   Another limitation follows from the multi-modality of intents, because the expert can have several
513   goals or performs a vague action. The imitation efficiency could, of course, drop, because the expert
514   intents may no longer be transparent to the agent. While such a trait would be on par with the way
515   humans learn a certain skill by observing an adept, weighing the hierarchy of multiple possible
516
      intents in AILOT could prove useful to further regularize the learning dynamics in such uncertain
      scenarios and is an interesting direction for further research. Other direction of future work is to
517
      venture into the cross-domain imitation. Based on the results observed here, it should be possible to
518
      generalize AILOT to handle the transition shift between the expert and the agent in the presence of
519   larger mismatches pertinent to the different domains.
520
521   In conclusion, the development of AILOT sets a robust benchmark for future generalizations, enhanc-
522
      ing ongoing research in crafting generalist knowledge distillation agents from the offline data.

782   B    D ETAILS ABOUT THE BASELINE M ODELS
783
784
      Performance of AILOT + IQL is compared to the following algorithms:
785
786         • IQL (Kostrikov et al. (2021)) is state-of-the-art offline RL algorithm, which avoids querying
787           out of the distribution actions by viewing value function as a random variable, where upper
788           bound of uncertanity is controlled through expectile of distribution. In our experiments,
789           evaluation is made using ground-truth reward from D4RL tasks.
790
            • OTR (Luo et al. (2023)) is a reward function algorithm, where reward signal is based on
791           optimal transport distance between states of expert demonstration and reward unlabeled
792           dataset.
793
794
            • CLUE (Liu et al. (2023)) learns VAE calibrated latent space of both expert and agent
              state-action transitions, where intrinsic rewards can be defined as distance between agent
795
              and averaged expert transition representations.
796
797         • IQ-Learn (Garg et al. (2021)) is an imitation learning algorithm, which implicitly encodes
798           into learned inverse Q-function rewards and policy from expert data.
799         • Diffusion-QL (Wang et al. (2022)) is an state-of-the-art offline RL algorithm, which models
800           learning policy as conditional diffusion model in order to effectively increase expressiveness
801           and provide more flexible regularization towards behavior policy, which collected dataset.
802
            • SQIL (Reddy et al. (2019)) proposes to learn soft Q-function by setting expert transitions to
803           one and for non-expert transitions to zero.
804
805         • ORIL (Zolna et al. (2020)) utilizes discriminator network which distinguishes between
806
              optimal and suboptimal data in mixed dataset to provide reward relabelling through learned
              discriminator.
807
808         • SMODICE (Ma et al. (2022a)) offline state occupancy matching algorithm, which solves
809           the problem of IL from observations through state divergence minimization by utilizing dual
              formulation of value function.

938   F    D ESCRIPTION OF E VALUATED E NVIRONMENTS
939
940   Gym-MuJoCo locomotion are the most commonly used tasks for the evaluation of performance of
941
      RL algorithms. D4RL provides precollected datasets of different quality, corresponding to training
      policies evaluated after training for certain number of steps.
942
943
      Gym-Locomotion. Locomotion tasks consist of four environments: Hopper, Walker2d, HalfChee-
944
      tah, and Ant. Provided datasets from D4RL come in "-expert", "-replay", and "-random" splits. Where
945
      "-expert" split corresponds to high-reward trajectories, whilst the others contain a mixture of roll-outs
946   from partially trained policies or even some random transitions. All of the datasets are obtained with
947   the SAC algorithm.
948
949   Manipulation In addition to the locomotion experiments, we test the proposed approach on
950   Adroit environments. Adroid involves controlling a 24-DoF Hand robot tasked with opening a
951   door, hammering a nail, picking up and moving a ball. Two types of offline data from D4RL
952   are considered: human demonstrations (with "-human" split), which were obtained from logged
953   experiences of humans and data obtained from a sub-optimal policy (with "-cloned" split), obtained
954   from the imitation policy.
