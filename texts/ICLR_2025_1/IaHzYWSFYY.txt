011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate remarkable performance in various
014            applications, yet their training demands extensive resources and time. Conse-
015
               quently, fine-tuning pre-trained LLMs has become a prevalent strategy for adapt-
               ing these models to diverse downstream tasks, thereby reducing costs. Despite
016
               their benefits, LLMs have vulnerabilities, such as susceptibility to adversarial
017
               attacks, potential for jailbreaking, fairness issues, backdoor vulnerabilities, and
018            the risk of generating inappropriate or harmful content. Since fine-tuned mod-
019            els inherit some characteristics from their original models, they may also inherit
020            these issues and vulnerabilities. In this work, we propose a lightweight frame-
021            work, RootTracker, specifically designed to trace the original models of fine-tuned
022            LLMs. The core idea is to identify a set of prompts that can assess which pre-
023            trained LLM a fine-tuned model most closely resembles. This process is con-
024            ducted in a “knockout tournament” style, where the model is repeatedly tested
025            against pairs of LLMs until the original pre-trained model is identified. To evalu-
026
               ate the effectiveness of our framework, we created 200 distinct fine-tuned models,
               derived from original models including GPT-Neo, GPT-2, TinyLlama, and Pythia.
027
               The results demonstrate that our framework accurately identified the original mod-
028
               els for 85.7% of the fine-tuned versions. Therefore, we advocate for timely up-
029            dates to model versions or deliberate obfuscation of model types when deploying
030            large models.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT (Achiam et al., 2023) and LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
036   2023), have demonstrated exceptional performance across a wide range of applications, from natu-
037   ral language processing to complex decision-making tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2019;
038   Vaswani, 2017). The effectiveness of these models in handling diverse and intricate tasks has led to
039
      their widespread adoption in both academic research and industry applications. However, the train-
      ing of such large models from scratch demands substantial computational resources and time, often
040
      involving hundreds of GPUs and significant energy consumption (Strubell et al., 2020; Schwartz
041
      et al., 2020). Consequently, fine-tuning pre-trained LLMs has become increasingly popular. This
042   approach leverages the general capabilities of an already trained model, adapting it to specific tasks
043   with considerably reduced resource expenditure, thereby broadening the accessibility of LLM tech-
044   nologies for a variety of downstream applications.
045
      Despite their capabilities, LLMs are not without issues. Research indicates that these models can
046
      inadvertently generate biased or harmful content (Welbl et al., 2021; Gallegos et al., 2024; Chu et al.,
047
      2024). Furthermore, even with implemented safety measures, studies reveal “jailbreaking” attacks
048   that can circumvent these protections, potentially inducing the generation of toxic content (Qi et al.,
049   2024; Shayegani et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Deng et al., 2023). Moreover, studies have shown
050   that fine-tuned models may retain the vulnerabilities and biases of their original pre-trained versions
051   (Li et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2021), which can perpetuate or
052   even amplify these issues in downstream applications. Therefore, if an application employs a model
053   fine-tuned from older open-source LLMs, it may inherit the vulnerabilities of those models, making
      it susceptible to attacks. For instance, we used the same jailbreak prompts to successfully attack


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      multiple fine-tuned versions of GPT-2-XL (details provided in Appendix A.1). Additionally, this
055   phenomenon inspires us to consider that carefully designed prompts can be utilized to trace the
056   original model of a fine-tuned version.
057
058
      In this work, we introduce RootTracker, a novel lightweight, extensible, and modular framework de-
      signed to identify the original pre-trained models of fine-tuned LLMs under black-box conditions.
059
      The primary concept of our framework is to use a set of prompts to identify the original models of
060
      fine-tuned models. To achieve this, the framework is divided into four components: Models Prepa-
061   ration, Database Construction, Single Pair Classifier, and Knockout Round. First, Models Prepa-
062   ration leverages prompt tuning to efficiently generate a substantial number of fine-tuned models for
063   training and testing. Database Construction then creates a comprehensive prompt database using
064   multimodal prompts generated by various high-performing LLMs, providing a rich search space
065   for the Single Pair Classifier. This classifier evaluates fine-tuned models to determine their original
066   models, utilizing a search and optimization approach that is significantly less resource-intensive than
067   model tuning. Finally, the Knockout Round, inspired by elimination tournaments in sports, extends
068   the Single Pair Classifier to assess multiple original models, enhancing the overall functionality of
069
      the framework.
070   To evaluate the effectiveness of our framework, we generated 200 distinct fine-tuned models derived
071   from original models including GPT-Neo, GPT-2, TinyLlama, and Pythia. The framework achieved
072   an accuracy rate of 85.7% in identifying multiple original models (utilizing the knockout round).
073   Additionally, an ablation study underscored the unique and indispensable roles of each framework
074   component. We further tested the framework on 11 parameter-tuned LLMs, successfully tracing the
075
      origins of 8 models, demonstrating strong generalization capabilities. The results also show that our
      framework enables accurate tracing of the lineage of these models, facilitating the identification of
076
      associated attack mechanisms. This emphasizes the need for LLM service providers to regularly
077
      update their models or implement obfuscation techniques to mitigate potential vulnerabilities.

      This section describes the architecture of our framework, RootTracker. As illustrated in Figure 1,
099   RootTracker is composed of four principal components: Models Preparation, Database Construc-
100   tion, Single Pair Classifier, and Knockout Round. The subsequent sections will provide a detailed ex-
101   ploration of each component, highlighting their roles and interdependencies within the RootTracker
102   framework.
103
104   2.1      M ODELS P REPARATION
105
106   As shown in Figure 1, this component aims to generate as many fine-tuned models as possible. The
107   reason for this is that, for a single vanilla LLM, it is not straightforward to find enough existing
      fine-tuned models for training and testing. Specifically, we select a range of vanilla original models


                                                                                               2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      that are commonly used as base models for fine-tuning. We then apply fine-tuning—specifically,
109   prompt tuning—to generate various fine-tuned versions. Prompt tuning is chosen for this purpose
110   because it demonstrates performance comparable to parameter tuning while using a significantly
111   smaller number of parameters (Lester et al., 2021).
112
      In addition, prompt tuning offers two primary advantages. Firstly, it significantly reduces training
113
      time since it only involves appending prompts to the original models, facilitating the efficient cre-
114
      ation of multiple fine-tuned versions. Secondly, it lowers storage requirements by saving only the
115   prompts used for tuning rather than the entire set of model parameters. These prompts encapsulate
116   the necessary adjustments to adapt the base model for specific tasks. Additionally, to enhance in-
117   ference speed, we can opt to store not only the prompts but also the output embeddings from the
118   training samples. Compared to storing the entire model, storing embeddings requires significantly
119   less space. By doing so, we bypass the need for re-embedding during the inference phase, thereby
120   accelerating the overall classification process.
121
122   2.2   DATABASE C ONSTRUCTION
123
124   This component focuses on constructing a comprehensive database of prompts used in the search
125   step of the Single Pair Classifier. The database is designed to encompass a wide range of modalities.
126   As illustrated in the Database Construction section of Figure 1, we begin by selecting a variety of
127   high-performing LLMs (such as GPT-4, Claude-3, and Gemini). We then instruct these models to
128   generate multiple prompts. The input instructions for these LLMs are structured as follows: First,
129
      we clearly and thoroughly describe the issue to be addressed with the prompts, emphasizing the
      tracking of the original models of fine-tuned versions. Next, we specify the need for diverse prompt
130
      modalities, including various topics, structures, and lengths, to broaden the range of prompts and ex-
131
      pand the search space for the subsequent search step. Finally, we have each LLM generate multiple
132   rounds of prompts, incorporating human feedback to ensure the production of varied prompt modal-
133   ities. The prompts generated by these LLMs are then consolidated to form the extensive database
134   required for our downstream processes (an example is provided in Appendix A.2).
135
136
      2.3   S INGLE PAIR C LASSIFIER
137
138
      This component serves as the core analytical engine of the framework, classifying the origins of the
139   fine-tuned models. It employs multiple analytical steps to accurately distinguish between the base
140   models used in the fine-tuning process, as illustrated in the Single Pair Classifier section of Figure
141   1. Each Single Pair Classifier functions as a binary classifier. Instead of determining whether the
142   original model of a test model is a specific one, it assesses which of two possible original models
143   the test model more closely resembles. For instance, if the test model is based on GPT and the
144   classifier is designed to differentiate between GPT and Llama, the classifier’s output would lean
145   toward GPT. Similarly, if the same GPT-based test model is evaluated by a classifier designed to
146   distinguish between Claude and Llama, and if GPT’s features are more similar to those of Llama,
147
      the classifier would lean toward Llama.
148   The rationale for employing this pairwise comparison approach instead of a single model that scores
149   similarity to all original models and selects the highest score is twofold. First, considering only two
150   models at a time reduces overlaps and provides clearer boundaries in the high-dimensional numeric
151   space. Second, some prompts may readily distinguish between models A and B but struggle to
152   differentiate between A and C. Including C in the same evaluation as A and B could compromise
153
      the classifier’s performance.
154   Single Pair Classifier is structured as a pipeline that includes the following sequential steps: Search
155   k Seed Prompts, Optimize k Seed Prompts, k Metrics and Vote. Each of these steps will be explained
156   in detail below.
157
158   Search k Seed Prompts. This step focuses on selecting the k best prompts from the prompt
159   database established during the Database Construction phase. The selection process initially screens
160   for prompts that can effectively distinguish between the features of two original models. To quanti-
161   tatively assess the prompts, their outputs are embedded using sentence embeddings—preferable to
      word embeddings for sentences—to convert them into a high-dimensional numeric space.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To evaluate whether a prompt effectively differentiates between two original models, we utilize the
163   fine-tuned models obtained during the Model Preparation phase. First, we classify the fine-tuned
164   models according to their corresponding original models. Next, we select the two sets of models
165   relevant to the Single Pair Classifier. These sets are then divided into training and testing models,
166   ensuring that the number of training models in each set is equal to minimize bias.
167
      In the training phase, a well-performing prompt can be defined as follows: when a prompt is input
168
      into the two sets of models, it produces two sets of outputs. If the outputs within the same set
169   are close together (with distance defined as the cosine distance after embedding) while the outputs
170   between different sets are far apart, then the prompt is considered effective. We employ a loss
171   function as the metric to assess this performance.
172
      The loss function utilized is a variation of the contrastive loss function (Hadsell et al., 2006), specif-
173
      ically tailored for this application. This specialized loss function excels at drawing outputs from the
174
      same model type closer together, while pushing those from different types apart. The details of this
175   loss function are as follows.

201   Optimize k Seed Prompts. This step is designed to enhance the performance of prompts through
202   fine-grained adjustments and to broaden the search space. To improve the performance of the k
203   seed prompts, we implement a modified genetic algorithm (GA) (details are provided in Appendix
204
      A.3). This algorithm is particularly well-suited for solving optimization problems by simulating the
      processes of natural selection and genetics. Our GA operates as follows:
205
206   • Fitness Function: We use the loss function, as described earlier, to evaluate the fitness of each
207     prompt. This function measures how effectively a prompt generates the desired responses.
208   • Genes: The characters that can be used in the prompts—including uppercase and lowercase let-
209     ters, symbols, and spaces—constitute the gene pool for constructing prompt strings.
210   • Crossover: Two existing prompts are combined at random points to produce new prompts, mim-
211     icking biological recombination between genes.
212   • Mutation: With a certain probability, random changes are introduced to the prompts to explore a
213     wider search space. This could involve changing a character in a prompt to another character from
214     the gene pool.
215   • Selection: After evaluating the fitness of the prompts, a subset is selected for breeding the next
        generation based on their fitness scores.

216
      The use of a genetic algorithm is justified as it expands the search space beyond the initial database,
217   increasing the likelihood of finding optimal prompts. This method leverages evolutionary principles,
218   such as selection and variation, to iteratively improve the prompts.
219
220
      k Metrics and Vote. This step aims to evaluate a model from different perspectives using various
221
      prompts and to aggregate the results through a voting mechanism. In the k metrics step, each metric
222   functions as a classifier built with k optimized prompts. For each prompt, we input it into two
223   classes of training models corresponding to different original models and map their outputs to an
224   embedding space. The same procedure is applied to the model under test. We then utilize the k-
225   nearest neighbors (kNN) algorithm to classify the test model’s output based on its proximity to the
226   training embeddings. This process is repeated for all k metrics.
227
      Finally, a specialized voting mechanism aggregates the results from each prompt to determine the
228   predominant classification of the model under test. This voting mechanism tallies the sum of two
229   types of reference points (the k points chosen for decision-making) in each kNN iteration. The final
230   result is determined by comparing the total counts of these two types of reference points across all
231   metrics, with the majority type prevailing. By utilizing the intermediate results of kNN rather than
232   relying solely on the final outcome, this voting method allows for a more comprehensive considera-
233   tion of the overall results, thereby reducing biases that may arise from depending solely on the final
234   kNN results (see Appendix A.4).

236   2.4   K NOCKOUT ROUND
237
238   In scenarios involving multiple model classes (≥ 2 possible original models), multiple pairwise
239   comparisons are necessary, as illustrated in the Knockout Round section of Figure 1. To expedite
240   this process, a knockout tournament mechanism, similar to those used in sports competitions, is em-
241   ployed. This approach reduces the number of comparisons from the combinatorial number C(n, 2)
242
      to just n − 1, thereby demonstrating the scalability of the framework. Furthermore, when new model
      types are introduced, only pairwise comparisons with the existing model types are required.
243
244   The knockout mechanism initially divides the models into two disjoint groups, where they compete
245   in pairwise matches, with the winners advancing to the next round. This setup effectively simplifies
246   the comparison process, as the number of competitors is halved with each round until a final winner
247   is determined (an example is provided in Appendix A.5).
248   In addition, traditional knockout rounds focus solely on the winner, which means that a single mis-
249   judgment (where the correct result loses) can jeopardize the entire outcome of the tournament. To
250   mitigate this issue, we have implemented a double knockout round system that introduces both a
251   winners’ bracket and a losers’ bracket. Initially, all entries start in the winners’ bracket, and any
252   entry that loses is moved to the losers’ bracket. An entry is only eliminated from contention after
253   losing a second time in the losers’ bracket. However, if an entry wins in the losers’ bracket, it has
254
      the opportunity to compete against the winners’ bracket later on. This effectively reduces the risk
      of a single erroneous judgment compromising the entire knockout round (an example is provided in
255
      Appendix A.5).

345   3.4   C OMPARISON
346
347   To the best of our knowledge, there is only one prior study (Foley et al., 2023) that closely aligns
348   with our application scenario. This previous work employed classifiers based on parameter tuning
349   of LLMs, which necessitated substantial computational resources, thereby limiting its extensibility.
350   In comparison, our results show an accuracy of 85.7% in identifying multiple candidate original
351
      models, slightly exceeding the 80% accuracy rate reported in that study, which correctly identified
      8 out of 10 fine-tuned models.
352
353   Additionally, our framework requires significantly less computational power and has lower hard-
354   ware demands, as it utilizes search and optimization strategies rather than relying on model tuning.
355   Moreover, our approach minimizes storage requirements by only necessitating the retention of orig-
356   inal models and their corresponding tuning prompts, rather than the complete storage of fine-tuned
357
      models. Furthermore, our framework is designed to adapt seamlessly to the inclusion of new orig-
      inal models, accommodating the ongoing evolution of large models. Thanks to its modular design,
358
      components within our framework can be easily replaced, facilitating updates with newer method-
359
      ologies.

      3.7   L IMITATION
437
      The main limitation of our approach is that it relies on a predefined pool of original models. Con-
438   sequently, we can only determine which LLM the fine-tuned model closely resembles, rather than
439   identifying its exact origin if it falls outside this pool. However, in real-world attack scenarios, this
440   limitation is less restrictive, as most fine-tuned LLMs are typically derived from well-known open-
441   source models. In addition, due to limitations in computing resources, we evaluated our method
442   using only 1.4B parameter versions of LLMs. However, we ensured that our experiments included
443   the most mainstream 1.4B LLMs. Given the extensibility of our framework, we plan to expand the
444   pool of LLMs as soon as sufficient computing resources become available.

      4     R ELATED W ORK

448
      Model Features and Characteristics Our framework leverages concepts similar to model water-
449
      marking and fingerprinting to identify the pre-trained origins of fine-tuned models. Watermarking
450
      works (Adi et al., 2018; Jia et al., 2021; Uchida et al., 2017; Lao et al., 2022; Clements & Lao,
451   2022; Li et al., 2022; Cong et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022) involve embedding
452   identifiable information within a model to verify its authenticity or ownership, while fingerprint-
453   ing works (Lukas et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
454   2022) capture unique model characteristics that can be used to trace its lineage. By connecting these
455   techniques with model features and characteristics, our framework identifies distinct features that
456   link fine-tuned models to their original pre-trained versions. This approach facilitates the accurate
457   determination of a model’s provenance, enhancing model transparency and accountability. Recent
458   research (Foley et al., 2023) also demonstrates that fine-tuned models retain the characteristics of
459
      their original pre-trained models.
460
461   LLMs Vulnerability LLMs face significant vulnerabilities that challenge their safe use. Some
462   issues include jailbreaking (Qi et al., 2024; Shayegani et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Deng et al.,
463   2023; Lapid et al., 2024), where models bypass safety protocols to generate unethical content, and
464
      susceptibility to adversarial attacks (Qi et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Bailey et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
      2023) that can manipulate outputs through malicious inputs. Additionally, LLMs can be compro-
465
      mised by backdoor attacks (Yan et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024), where specific
466
      triggers can cause the model to behave in unexpected or harmful ways. Furthermore, LLMs may
467   also generate harmful content unintentionally (Zellers et al., 2019; Welbl et al., 2021), such as mis-
468   information or hate speech, which can have serious consequences for individuals and communities.
469   Moreover, these models often reflect biases inherent in their training data (Gallegos et al., 2024; Li
470   et al., 2023b; Chu et al., 2024), perpetuating societal inequalities and reinforcing stereotypes. The
471   combination of these issues significantly impacts the normal functioning of LLMs and can lead to
472   societal harms, such as discrimination and the spread of false information. Therefore, it is crucial
473   that we work actively to address these challenges. Besides, some studies (Li et al., 2021; Zhang
474   et al., 2023; Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2021) have demonstrated that vulnerabilities in pre-trained
475
      models are often inherited by their fine-tuned models. This evidence suggests that identifying the
      original pre-trained models used as the basis for fine-tuning is helpful for analyzing the robustness of
476
      the fine-tuned models. Such identification can also facilitate the detection of bugs and vulnerabilities
477
      in these models.

      A.5   E XAMPLE OF K NOCKOUT ROUND
814
815   Traditional Knockout Round The Knockout mechanism initially employs a series of mutually
816   exclusive classifiers to determine which LLM the LLM under test (LUT) most closely resembles. In
817   the first round, there are no overlapping classifiers such as (LLMa , LLMb ) and (LLMb , LLMc ).
818   For instance, in the first round, the LLM classifiers evaluate the LUT and conclude that it most
819   closely resembles LLM1, LLM4, and LLM5. In the subsequent knockout round, we select the clas-
820   sifier that distinguishes between LLM1 and LLM4, and in the final round, we choose the classifier
821
      for LLM1 and LLM5. Ultimately, it is determined that the LUT most closely resembles LLM5.
822
823
      Double Knockout Round In the initial phase of our testing framework, we employ a set of mu-
      tually exclusive classifiers to evaluate which of several predefined language models (LMs) the lan-
824
      guage model under test (LMUT) most closely resembles. Each classifier is specific to a pair of
825
      models, such as (LMa , LMb ), ensuring no overlaps like (LMb , LMc ) in the first round. Suppose in
826   the first round, the classifiers indicate that LMUT shares similarities with LM1, LM4, and LM5.
827
828   In the double knockout setup, LMUT first faces LM4 in a direct comparison. Let’s say LMUT loses
829
      this round and is moved to the losers’ bracket, while LM4 progresses in the winners’ bracket. In the
      losers’ bracket, LMUT is next pitted against LM1. Winning this round allows LMUT to challenge
830
      another model from the winners’ bracket, providing a chance for redemption.
831
832   Continuing in the losers’ bracket, LMUT then competes against LM5 and wins, setting up a rematch
833   with LM4. In this final round, if LMUT defeats LM4, it faces one last match against LM5, which
834   had been progressing in the winners’ bracket. If LMUT wins this ultimate match, it is determined
835
      that LMUT most closely resembles LM5, completing its journey from an initial setback to ultimate
      validation. This double knockout approach ensures that a single early loss does not eliminate a
836
      potentially strong candidate prematurely and validates the final result through multiple rounds of
837
      testing.
