                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Federated learning (FL) has received widespread attention due to its distributed
               training and privacy protection. However, existing federated learning methods
011
               encounter significant challenges, such as increased communication costs and de-
012
               graded model performance, when processing non-independently and identically
013            distributed (non-IID) data. This paper jointly alleviates these problems by analyzing
014            and exploiting the low-rank properties of global model trajectories.
015
               Primarily, we introduce a streaming subspace update strategy and then propose a
016            general federated learning framework, Ferated Learning in Streaming Subspace
017            (FLSS). In FLSS, local model updates are restricted to the global streaming
018            subspace, resulting in low-dimensional trajectories. The server then aggregates
019            these trajectories to update the global model. Comprehensive experiments verify
020            the effectiveness of our framework. In Cifar100, the FLSS-equipped FL method
021            outperforms the baseline by 2.14% and reduces the communication cost by 80%.
022            FLSS utilizes the early training information of the global model to simultaneously
023            improve the performance and communication efficiency of federated learning.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The remarkable progress made in machine learning is largely due to the availability of abundant and
029   extensive data Cordts et al. (2016); Lin et al. (2014); Russakovsky et al. (2015). Nonetheless, as
030
      data volumes grow, aggregating such data becomes very difficult. Consequently, federated learning
      emerged as a distributed framework for machine learning Li et al. (2021b; 2020a); Yang et al.
031
      (2019). Federated learning leverages data stored on edge devices such as smartphones and PCs to
032
      collaboratively train global models under scheduling by a central server Kairouz et al. (2021); Li
033   et al. (2020a); Mothukuri et al. (2021), which is of great significance in privacy-sensitive applications
034   Bonawitz et al. (2019); Kaissis et al. (2020); Dayan et al. (2021); Hard et al. (2018).
035
036
      Within the domain of federated learning, the communication cost of handling heterogeneous data is a
      significant obstacle KonecnyŐÄ et al. (2016); Wu et al. (2022); Kairouz et al. (2021). This challenge
037
      stems mainly from the essential requirement of transmitting local models or gradients from each client
038
      to a central server Li et al. (2022a). Moreover, in the non-independently and identically distributed
039   (non-IID) scenario, the imbalance of client data further affects the performance of the model Li et al.
040   (2019); Hsu et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2023b). Hence, two pivotal challenges emerge as focal points
041   for research in federated learning: ‚Ě∂ reducing communication overhead KonecnyŐÄ et al. (2016) and
042   ‚Ě∑ mitigating the accuracy degradation problem resulting from data heterogeneity KonecŐĆnyŐÄ et al.
043   (2016).
044
      Numerous studies have been conducted to address these challenges Zhu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021a);
045   Acar et al. (2021); Bernstein et al. (2018). In the context of addressing data heterogeneity, FL methods
046   are primarily categorized into update correction Karimireddy et al. (2020), regularization Li et al.
047   (2020b), model splitting Li et al. (2021a), and knowledge distillation Zhu et al. (2021). However,
048   these methods often do not fully leverage early information from the global model. Regarding
049   communication compression, approaches such as Fetchsgd Rothchild et al. (2020), Signsgd Bernstein
050   et al. (2018); Karimireddy et al. (2019), and STC Sattler et al. (2019) introduce gradient compres-
051   sion techniques like sketching, quantization, and sparsification. Nevertheless, these compression
052   frameworks are randomized and data-independent, which inherently limits their effectiveness. While
053
      existing algorithms are effective in tackling individual issues, they often struggle to simultaneously
      address both communication overhead and data heterogeneity challenges.

      Nonetheless, a key challenge in training federated learning models within subspaces lies in obtaining
070
      a subspace that maintains training performance. Existing subspace extraction solutions primarily
071
      involve random generation or pre-training sampling, which often struggle to cover the complete
072   neural network parameter space. Applying it to federated learning may lead to a loss of accuracy.
073   To address this issue, we design a strategy inspired by subspace tracking algorithms Eftekhari et al.
074   (2019); RŐĆehuŐärŐĆek (2011); Grammenos et al. (2020), which involves real-time monitoring of changes
075   in neural network parameters to ensure that the subspace consistently contains the latest model
076   information. Building upon this subspace, we propose a novel federated learning training scheme
077   named Ferated Learning in Streaming Subspace (FLSS).
078
      In FLSS, the training process involves constraining the local model to a low-dimensional subspace of
079   the global model trajectory, which is equivalent to unifying the local model updates under the global
080   subspace and performing local model fine-tuning, thus mitigating the harm of statistical heterogeneity.
081   The consensus among the models is achieved by aggregating the projection coefficients of the local
082   model update residing in this subspace, resulting in reduced communication costs. To cultivate
083   the streaming subspace on the client side, a subspace tracking method is employed. This method
084   periodically samples the global model trajectory and performs singular value decomposition to capture
085   the knowledge of model changes during the training phase. Extensive experiments show that we
086   obtain better performance by applying FLSS to the traditional FL algorithms. For instance, compared
087
      with traditional FedAvg, after applying the FLSS strategy, we obtained an accuracy improvement of
      1.90 ‚ąľ 2.58% and 0.08 ‚ąľ 1.76% on Cifar10 and Cifar100 with different degrees of heterogeneity,
088
      respectively, and reduced the communication cost by nearly 80%. In summary, the key contributions
089
      of this paper can be summarized as follows:
090
091          ‚ÄĘ We propose FLSS, which limits local model updates to the streaming subspace of global
092            model trajectory to fully exploit global information, and reduces communication costs by
093            transmitting the projection coefficients of model updates in the subspace.
094          ‚ÄĘ We introduce a strategy to extract the streaming subspace for training. This strategy ensures
095            that the subspace always contains the latest information of the model by performing singular
096            value decomposition on the global model trajectory in real-time.
097
             ‚ÄĘ Through multiple datasets, we verify that FLSS can improve the FL method in terms of
098            communication efficiency and model performance. Specifically, with FedAvg, using FLSS
099            reduced communication costs by 80% and increased accuracy by up to 8.15%.

118   Furthermore, STC Sattler et al. (2019) combines top-k sparsification with quantization. Although

120   due to the randomness of compression and insufficient consideration of global information.
121   Training in Tiny Subspace. Many studies have emphasized the inherent low-dimensional character-
122   istics of neural networks Tuddenham et al. (2020); Vinyals & Povey (2012); Gressmann et al. (2020).

124   randomly chosen subspace facilitates parameter compression, albeit potentially at the expense of final
125   accuracy. In subsequent work Li et al. (2022b;c), Li et al. successfully extracted a subspace approxi-
126   mating the entire parameter trajectory through principal component analysis applied to a pre-trained
127   neural network. However, despite the richness of information captured within the aforementioned
128   subspace, its efficacy remains primarily constrained to the early stages of pre-training. Realizing its
129
      full potential often necessitates multiple epochs.

479      5            C ONCLUSION
480
481     To simultaneously address the high communication costs and suboptimal performance of federated
482     learning under statistical heterogeneity, we propose a general strategy called FLSS, with a theoretical
483     guarantee. Specifically, it helps the client better utilize the early information of the global model
484     and reduce the impact of heterogeneity on global performance by limiting local updates to the
485     global model subspace. Notably, FLSS reduces communication costs by transmitting the projection
        coefficients of local model updates within the streaming subspace.

      A.1    F EDERATED L EARNING
678
679   Federated learning is a distributed machine learning framework through iterative communication
680   and computation between servers and clients. FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017) is a well-known FL
681   method and the basic framework of many FL methods. We first introduce its main steps: (1) Server
682   sends the current global model to clients; (2) The clients initialize the current global model as its own
683   local model; (3) The clients train the local model on its own private data and send the trained local
684   models to the server; (4) The server receives the client models and aggregates them to obtain the
685   global model, and then resends it to the clients. However, the above solutions often face the problems
686   of high communication and poor performance in heterogeneous scenarios. Therefore, a lot of work
687
      have been carried out to solve the above problems.
688   Traditional Federated Learning. Federated learning algorithms designed to enhance perfor-
689   mance in heterogeneous environments can be divided into four different types Zhang et al. (2023a):
690   regularization-based FL Li et al. (2020b); Acar et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2022), update correction-
691   based FL Karimireddy et al. (2020); Gao et al. (2022); Niu & Deng (2022), model split-based FL Li
692   et al. (2021a); Jiang et al. (2022), and knowledge distillation-based FL Zhu et al. (2021); Lee et al.
693
      (2022); Gong et al. (2022); Huang et al. (2022). In the field of regularization-based FL, FedProx
      Li et al. (2020b) introduces a proximal term to reduce the Euclidean distance between the global
694
      model and the local model, while FedDyn Acar et al. (2021) adopts dynamic regularization to align
695
      the local optimal point with the minimum value of the global empirical loss. For FL based on update
696   correction, methods such as SCAFFOLD Karimireddy et al. (2020) and FedDC Gao et al. (2022)
697   employ global gradient calibration to mitigate local model drift. However, these methods require
698   the transmission of twice the message size required by FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017). In model
699   split-based FL, MOON Li et al. (2021a) enhances the consistency between local and global model
700   representations by adding a contrastive learning loss. Meanwhile, in knowledge distillation-based FL,
701   FedGen Zhu et al. (2021) utilizes a generator trained on the server to absorb local insights and utilizes
      the synthesized knowledge as an inductive bias to guide the local training process. Furthermore,


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      FedNTD Lee et al. (2022) uses local non-true distillation to solve the problem of forgetting global
703   information during local training.
704
705   Communication-efficient Federated Learning. To address the challenge of communication over-
706
      head in federated learning, many frameworks for gradient compression techniques have been proposed.
      Fetchsgd Rothchild et al. (2020) utilizes sketching techniques to effectively compress local gradients.
707
      Signsgd+EF Karimireddy et al. (2019) combines error feedback with 1-bit quantization, which
708
      reduces communication costs and improves the generalization ability of Signsgd. Furthermore,
709   STC Sattler et al. (2019) is specifically designed for federated learning, combining top-k sparsity
710   and quantization techniques to optimize data transfer. Similarly, DGC Lin et al. (2018) utilizes
711   sparsification to preserve important gradients while minimizing bandwidth in distributed training
712   environments. LBGM Azam et al. (2021) utilizes the low-rank characteristics of gradient space to
713   reduce communication requirements; however, it does not fully consider the relationship between
714   early global model information and local model updates. Although these methods have proven their
715   feasibility in reducing communication load, their effectiveness is often limited in heterogeneous
716   environments. This limitation is due to the stochastic nature of the compression framework and the
717
      fact that the client does not have complete information about the global model.
718
719   A.2    T RAINING IN T INY S UBSPACE
720
721
      Many studies have emphasized the inherent low-dimensional characteristics of neural networks
      Tuddenham et al. (2020); Vinyals & Povey (2012); Gressmann et al. (2020). A seminal study in Li
722
      et al. (2018); Gur-Ari et al. (2018) reveals that training a neural network within a randomly chosen
723
      subspace helps to achieve parameter compression, though the final accuracy may not be as high as
724   that in the original space. The following work Gressmann et al. (2020) improved the training of fixed
725   random subspaces by considering different layers of the network and re-drawing the random subspace
726   at each step. Different from random subspaces, Li et al. Li et al. (2022b;c) successfully extracted
727   a subspace that approximates the entire parameter trajectory by performing principal component
728   analysis on a pre-trained neural network. Efficient dimensionality reduction is achieved by limiting
729   the training process to this subspace.
730   However, although the above subspace contains model information to a certain extent, it is essentially
731   limited to the early stage of pre-training. Often it takes multiple epochs to reach its full potential.
732   In contrast, streaming subspace adapts to data changes by continuously updating the subspace to
733   dynamically capture real-time model information.

       E    F URTHER DISCUSSION
1252
1253
       We found that the global model space of federated learning has low rank properties. In fact, due to
1254   the scarcity of client data, federated learning algorithms face the risk of overfitting. By restricting
1255   the local model to a low dimensional subspace, the degree of freedom in model updates is reduced.
1256   This can be used as a basis for many federated learning algorithms to improve their generalization
1257   capabilities.
1258
       In addition, since the global model update of federated learning can be represented with fewer orthog-
1259
       onal bases, FLSS can also be widely integrated as a compression strategy into various compression
1260   frameworks to further reduce the compression rate. Compared with traditional compression schemes,
1261   FLSS pays more attention to the distribution of model parameters or gradient space. FLSS can
1262   adaptively select appropriate orthogonal bases to represent model updates for different networks and
1263   different scenarios. In other words, the compression of FLSS is data-driven and task-relevant.
1264
       In fact, many algorithms address the heterogeneity problem by considering local and global consis-
1265
       tency. For instance, model parameter consistency is tackled by FedProx, representation consistency
1266   by Moon, and logit consistency by FedNTD. In contrast to these approaches, our method emphasizes
1267   the directional consistency between global and local updates, constraining the update direction by
1268   applying a projection to limit the angle.
