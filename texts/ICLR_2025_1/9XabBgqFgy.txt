009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper delves into the interplay between vision backbones and optimizers,
012            unveiling an inter-dependent phenomenon termed backbone-optimizer coupling
               bias (BOCB). We observe that canonical CNNs, such as VGG and ResNet, exhibit
013
               a marked co-dependency with SGD families, while recent architectures like ViTs
014            and ConvNeXt share a tight coupling with the adaptive learning rate ones. We
015            further show that BOCB can be introduced by both optimizers and certain backbone
016            designs and may significantly impact the pre-training and downstream fine-tuning
017            of vision models. Through in-depth empirical analysis, we summarize takeaways
018            on recommended optimizers and insights into robust vision backbone architectures.
019
               We hope this work can inspire the community to question long-held assumptions on
               backbones and optimizers, stimulate further explorations, and thereby contribute to
020
               more robust vision systems. The source code and models are publicly available.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The past decade has witnessed rapid progress in computer vision, marked by significant strides in
026   network architectures (He et al., 2016; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2024) and optimizers (Sinha
027
      & Griscik, 1971; Kingma & Ba, 2015). From AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012a) to ConvNeXt (Liu
      et al., 2022a), the vision community has pushed the boundaries of pre-trained backbones in terms
028
      of task-specific accuracy, efficiency (e.g., model parameters and inference speed), and other metrics
029   through heuristic architecture designs. Amidst the buzz, however, the impact of optimizers has been
030   largely overlooked - practitioners often default to established ones without systematic justification.
031   For instance, while AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) has emerged as the de facto choice for
032   training Vision Transformers (ViTs), the generality of such optimizer preferences across backbones
033   remains under-explored. This inquiry becomes particularly important as vision models nowadays
034
      are deployed in various real-world applications, where the choice of optimizer can significantly
      impact model generalization (Woo et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2023), robustness to distribution
035
      shifts (Vishniakov et al., 2023), and adaptability in transfer learning (He et al., 2017; Kirillov et al.,
036   2023). Recent studies have explored adapting Adafactor for efficient training scaling in ViTs (Zhai
037   et al., 2022), comparing SGD and AdamW for vision model fine-tuning (Kumar et al., 2022), and
038   investigating general optimizer designs for transformers (Xiong et al., 2020). Thus, understanding
039   the backbone-optimizer interplay may provide critical insights for enhancing model reliability and
040   facilitating vision backbone design and deployment across diverse practical scenarios.
041   In this paper, we explore the relationship between vision backbones and optimizers. Our primary
042   focus is threefold: (i) Does any identifiable dependency exist between existing vision backbones and
043   widely-used optimizers? (ii) If such backbone-optimizer dependencies exist, (how) do they affect the
044   training dynamics and performance of vision models? (iii) Can we identify direct connections between
      these inter-dependencies and specific designs of vision backbone architectures and optimizers?
045
046   To answer these questions, we first revisit different categories of existing vision backbones and
047   optimizers as shown in Figures 1, A1, and Section 2. We then conduct extensive experiments where 20
048
      representative backbones are evaluated against 20 optimizers on mainstream vision datasets, including
      CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), ImageNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012b), and COCO (Lin et al.,
049
      2014). As such, we provide the backbone-optimizer benchmark and thereby observe an interesting
050   inter-dependent phenomenon, which we term backbone-optimizer coupling bias (BOCB). Table 1
051   conveys this evidence: classical Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) like VGG (Simonyan &
052   Zisserman, 2015) and ResNet (Zhang et al., 2022) exhibit a marked co-dependency with SGD (Sinha
053   & Griscik, 1971). In contrast, modern backbones, such as Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy
      et al., 2021) and ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022a), perform better when paired with adaptive learning rate

054
      optimizers (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) (see blue and gray results in Table 1). While most backbones
055   and optimizers are assumed to be unbiased and well-generalized, our findings appear to question it.
056
057
      To dig deep into such interplay, thorough empirical analyses are conducted from both backbone and
      optimizer perspectives. We first examine the performance stability of each backbone with vioplinplots
058
      as shown in Figure 3, which offers intuitive insights into their robustness across different optimizers.
059   We then analyze the hyper-parameter robustness of all benchmarked backbone-optimizer pairs in
060   Figure 4,5, as the well-designed ones are expected to have robust hyper-parameter settings. To further
061   elucidate the rationale behind BOCB, we visualize the layer-wise patterns and PL exponent alpha
062   metrics (Martin et al., 2021) of learned parameters to examine how different network architectures
063   influence the parameter space and optimization complexity, hence potentially inducing the BOCB
064
      phenomenon. As illustrated in Figures 1, 6, and Appendix D, certain stage-wise (hierarchical or
      isotropic) and block-wise (heterogeneous or not) designs can significantly affect parameter space
065
      and hyper-parameters robustness. Interestingly, we further observe that optimizers also introduce
066   bias back to backbones (see Section 4). For example, fine-tuning the AdamW pre-trained backbones
067   with the other ones often leads to significant performance degradation, while this is not present when
068   pre-training the model with SGD variants. Moreover, different pre-training optimizers may even
069   alter the parameter patterns of identical backbones. Overall, our findings suggest that BOCB can
070   be introduced by optimizers and backbones and may significantly impact both the pre-training and
      downstream fine-tuning of vision models, thus limiting their flexibility and practical applications.
071
072   In the following sections, we first provide an overview of vision backbones and optimizers in Section 2.
073   We then present the backbone-optimizer benchmark details and our empirical findings in Section 3.
074   Section 4 offers an in-depth analysis of the rationale behind BOCB and takeaways for recommended
      optimizers and summarized backbone designs. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
075
076       • We explore the crucial yet poorly studied backbone-optimizer interplay in visual representation
077         learning, revealing the phenomenon of backbone-optimizer coupling bias (BOCB).
078       • We provide the backbone-optimizer benchmark that encompasses 20 popular vision backbones,
079         from classical CNNs to recent transformer-based architectures, and evaluate their performance
080         against 20 mainstream optimizers on CIFAR-100, ImageNet-1K, and COCO, unveiling the
081         practical limitations introduced by BOCB in both pre-training and transfer learning scenarios.
082       • From the BOCB perspective, we summarize recommendations of optimizers and insights on more
083         robust vision backbone design. The benchmark results also serve as takeaways for user-friendly
084         deployment. We open-source the code and models for further explorations in the community.

086   2     ROADMAPS OF V ISION BACKBONES AND O PTIMIZERS
087
088   This section provides an overview of most existing vision backbones and optimizers. We first revisit
089   different networks based on their stage-wise macro design (hierarchical or isotropic), building block
090   structures (heterogeneous or not), and core operators (convolution, self-attention, etc.). We then dive
091
      into mainstream optimizers, emphasizing their distinctive approaches to learning rate adjustment and
      gradient handling. This serves two purposes: first, it offers an organized framework for understanding
092   the current landscape; second, it facilitates our subsequent analyses, allowing us to draw connections
093   between experimental results and specific techniques, thereby yielding clear observations and insights.

095   2.1    TAXONOMY OF V ISION BACKBONE A RCHITECTURES

097   Stage-wise Macro Design. As shown in Figure 1 and Table A1, the overall framework of existing
098   vision backbones can be categorized into two groups: (i) Hierarchical architectures: These models
099   (e.g., VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015), ResNet (He et al., 2016), MobileNet.V2 (Sandler

100
      stages, where each stage consists of stacked building blocks with specific designs (e.g., bottleneck,
101   MetaFormer (Yu et al., 2024) or ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022a)) for feature extraction. (ii) Isotropic
102   architectures: In contrast, backbones like Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)
103   and MLP-Mixers (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) employ an isotropic building block stacking, where stand-
104   alone token and channel mixers (e.g., self-attention and MLP) are proposed to capture long-range
105   dependencies with attention-like operators while enabling token prompting for broader applications.
106   Intra-block Micro Design. The building block structures can also be classified into two paradigms:
107   (i) Homogeneous structures: Early CNNs like AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012a) employed a
      straightforward approach of interleaving convolutions and pooling layers for feature extraction. A

      As such, mainstream optimizers can be divided into four principal classes as depicted in Figure A1. (a)
182
      Fixed Learning Rate with Momentum: This category employs static learning rates while modulated
183   by momentum (Sinha & Griscik, 1971; Heo et al., 2021). Its key principle is the accumulation of past
184   gradients to determine the current update, which aids in accelerating convergence in consistent direc-
185   tions while dampening oscillations in high-curvature dimensions. (d) Adaptive Learning Rate without
186   Momentum: Optimizers in this class (e.g., AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011) and RMSProp (Hinton,
187   2012)) adapt learning rates for each parameter based on the historical statistics. While this approach
188
      may introduce extra cost, it allows for larger updates for infrequent parameters and smaller updates for
      frequent ones, providing better adaptability to varying feature scales and data sparsity. (b) Adaptive
189
      Learning Rate with Momentum: This type combines the benefits of momentum with parameter-wise
190   learning rate adaptation (Kingma & Ba, 2015; Reddi et al., 2018), making it well-suited for large
191   datasets or complex neural networks. (c) Estimated Learning Rate with Momentum: These optimizers
192   aim to mitigate the convergence issues of Category (b) through additional constraints or estimations,
193   such as factored moments (Shazeer & Stern, 2018) and bounded learning rates (Luo et al., 2019).

234   image classification on CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) for analysis efficiency, where models are

235

238   DenseNet-121 (DN) (Huang et al., 2017), MobileNet.V2 (MobV2) (Sandler et al., 2018), EfficientNet-

      et al., 2023), MogaNet-S (Moga) (Li et al., 2024), and UniRepLKNet-T (URLK) (Ding et al.,

      2024). We also evaluate MetaFormer baselines (Yu et al., 2024) with IdentityFormer-S12 (IF),

        values less than 2 or greater than 6 have the risk of overfitting or underfitting. The diagonal bars denote
        the BOCB occurring. View discussions in Section 4 and details on the alpha metric in Appendix B.3.
442
443     based on the Vallina Transformer but introduces hierarchical stages and local attention blocks. These
444     designs enhance the model’s ability to capture fine-grained features, leading to better performance
445     and weaker BOCB compared to DeiT-S. Takeaway: Block-wise macro designs aimed at reducing
446     heterogeneity or enhancing homogeneity, combined with hierarchical stages and the integration of
        inductive biases within token mixers, are crucial for ViTs to mitigate BOCB in computer vision tasks.
447
448
        Case 2: Modern CNNs. ConvNeXt, inspired by the macro design of ViTs, introduces a homoge-
        neous block design that integrates two types of operators within a residual connection, potentially
449
        enhancing capacities across various tasks and data scales. The effectiveness of this architecture
450     underscores the need to evaluate network designs beyond standard metrics, especially in the context
451     of real-world optimization challenges. The interaction between backbones and optimizers is crucial
452     for both pre-training and fine-tuning, with different architectures influencing optimization landscapes.
453     BOCB in CNNs is often associated with the FFN designs, which are pivotal in models. These blocks,
454     implemented as point-wise convolutions or inverted bottleneck layers, are susceptible to overfit-
455
        ting without proper regularization. To eliminate this, ConvNeXt.V2 introduces Global Response
        Normalization (GRN) between FFN blocks, similar to RMSNorm, to stabilize training and prevent
456
        model collapse, thereby reducing BOCB. ConvFormer, based on the MetaFormer framework, uses
457     homogeneous blocks with depth-wise and point-wise convolutions, improving training robustness
458     and reducing BOCB risk. Similarly, with the VGG series’ simple and homogeneous architecture,
459     RepVGG’s introduction of training-phase residual connections enhances performance while main-
460     taining stability and weak BOCB (see Figure 7(b)), exhibiting well-behaved training dynamics. In
461     contrast, ConvNeXt and MogaNet, featuring complex operations and heterogeneous blocks, are more
        susceptible to BOCB. UniRepLKNet, however, sidesteps this issue with a more homogeneous design,
462
        highlighting the importance of architectural uniformity in reducing BOCB. Takeaway: For modern
463     CNNs, designs that foster a homogeneous building block structure and incorporate crafted strategies
464     to mitigate model failures are more likely to achieve stable FFN training and reduce the risk of BOCB.
465
466     Case 3: MetaFormer. MetaFormer architecture is distinguished by its hierarchical stage-wise
467     and block-wise design, featuring ResScale, facilitating the flexible integration of various token
468
        mixers. This macro design is crucial for achieving competitive performance while minimizing the
        risk of BOCB. IdentityFormer, without any token mixers, sets a robust baseline for MetaFormer
469
        but may fall short in complex tasks requiring advanced token-wise representations, potentially
470     increasing BOCB risk, as shown in Figure 7(c). PoolFormerV2 (pooling as token mixers) outperforms
471     IdentityFormer but may overlook critical details due to the absence of token-wise aggregation,
472     leading to higher BOCB susceptibility. To achieve high performance while mitigating these risks,
473     selecting an appropriate token mixer is essential. ConvFormer integrates CNN layers to balance local
474     inductive bias in data-limited scenarios, ensuring better convergence and less BOCB. AttenFormer
475
        and CAFormer further explore attention mechanisms, aiming to enhance the representation capacity
        with global receptiveness through improved token interactions. Takeaway: Overall, MetaFormer
476
        architecture’s success hinges on a judicious balance between its hierarchical design and the selection
477     of token mixers, ensuring robust performance across diverse tasks while mitigating the risk of BOCB.

      BOCB property is still kept when transferring to dense prediction tasks for pre-trained backbones.

527
      pre-training and fine-tuning stages. Takeaway: Optimizer selection in pre-training can significantly
      impact models’ transferability, with Categories (b) and (c) optimizers potentially introducing BOCB

      to pre-trained backbones while yielding superior performance. We recommended three superior
529   optimizers and five BOCB indicator optimizers with property evaluation in Appendix D.5.

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534
      This paper explores the interplay of backbone designs and optimizer selections in computer vision.
      We unveil the phenomenon of backbone-optimizer coupling bias (BOCB) and the potential limitations
535
      it poses to vision backbones, for example, the extra fine-tuning time and efforts in downstream tasks.
536   We also discover the underlying rationale behind different network designs and BOCB and thereby
537   provide guidelines for future vision backbone design. Meanwhile, the benchmarking results and
538   released code serve as takeaways for user-friendly deployment and evaluation. Overall, we aim to
539   inspire the computer vision community to rethink the relationship between backbones and optimizers,
      consider BOCB in future studies, and thus contribute to more systematic future advancements.

853   Popular Optimizers. We also summarize popular optimizers with four categories in Figure A1 and
854   Table A2 provide four essential technical designs of 20 widely adopted optimizers, as described in
855   Algorithm 1. We classify these optimizers based on their strategies of the learning rate adjustment
856   (step 2) and the gradient estimation (step 3). Specially, we consider five types of statistics during
857   training: (i) First(-order) moment (gradient): The gradient itself, the first-order partial derivative of the
858
      objective function concerning the parameters. (ii) Estimated first-moment gradient (momentum): An
      exponentially decaying average of past gradients, serving as an estimate of the first-order moment. (iii)
859
      Second(-order) moment (gradient): The second-order partial derivative of the objective function for
860   the parameters, also known as the Hessian matrix, which can be approximated by Nesterov gradient
861   descenting (Reddi et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2023). (iv) Estimated second moment: An exponential
862   moving average (EMA) of the squared gradients, providing an estimate of the second-order moment.
863   (v) Second-order gradient: The Hessian matrix, the second-order partial derivative of the objective
      function to the parameters.

1025   Patterns of learned parameters. To investigate the layer-wise properties discussed in Section 2.1,
       we employed a set of quantitative metrics to analyze the learned parameters of each layer. As shown in


                                                           19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       Section D, these metrics reveal intrinsic topological patterns that reflect the unique characteristics of
1027   different network architectures, such as stage-wise macro designs, building block structures, and core
1028   operators of token-mixers and channel-mixers. We focused on the three key indicators as follows:
1029
       • PL Exponent Alpha: In the context of WeightWatcher (Martin & Mahoney, 2021; Martin
1030     et al., 2021), the Power Law (PL) exponent α quantifies the learned parameter quality of neural
1031     network layers. It is extracted from the tail fit of the layer weight matrix’s Empirical Spectral
1032     Density (ESD) to a truncated Power Law: ρ(λ) ∼ λ−α , ρ(λ) denotes the ESD, and λ represents
1033     the eigenvalues of the weight matrix’s correlation matrix X = W T W . The exponent α reflects the
1034     correlation structure, with lower values indicating enhanced generalization capabilities and higher
1035     values suggesting potential overfitting or underfitting. This metric facilitates the assessment of
1036
         neural network models’ generalization tendencies without the need for training or testing datasets,
         serving as an intrinsic measure of model quality.
1037                                                                                  P
1038
       • Entropy: The information entropy of the learned parameter tensor, H = − pi log(pi ), where pi
         is the probability of each value in the parameter tensor. It is used to measure the randomness of
1039     the parameter distribution. Higher entropy indicates a more uniform or random distribution, while
1040     lower entropy suggests a more patterned distribution. This provides insights into the complexity

1382   D.5                    RULES FOR C OUNTING THE O PTIMIZER R ANKINGS

       We have summarized and analyzed a great number of mixup benchmarking results to compare
1384   and rank all the included mixup methods in terms of performance, applicability, and the overall
1385   capacity. We have conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis of optimizer benchmarking results
1386   to systematically evaluate and rank a diverse array of optimization algorithms across four critical
1387   dimensions: Performance, Hyperparameter Robustness, Backbone Optimizer Coupling Bias (BOCB),
1388   and Computational Efficiency. Our methodology employs a weighted scoring system to synthesize
1389
       these multifaceted evaluations:
1390
                 • Performance (40% weight): This metric quantifies an optimizer’s efficacy across various
1391               backbone architectures, reflecting its paramount importance in algorithm selection.
1392
                 • Hyperparameter Robustness (20% weight): Quantified as the median Manhattan distance from
1393
                   the optimal learning rate and weight decay configurations to the maximum average distance,
1394               this metric assesses the optimizers’ robustness to hyperparameter perturbations.
1395
                 • BOCB (20% weight): Represented as a binary indicator (1 or 0), this factor evaluates the
1396
                   potential for coupling deviation between the optimizer and the backbone architecture.
1397
1398
                 • Computational Efficiency (20% weight): Measured by GPU memory allocation, this dimension
                   quantifies the computational resources required by each optimizer.
1399
1400   The aggregation of these standardized scores yields a comprehensive ranking that serves as a ro-
1401   bust benchmark for optimizer selection in deep learning visual backbone scenarios. This mul-
1402   tidimensional analysis not only elucidates the relative merits of established algorithms such as
1403   AdamW—corroborating its long-standing prevalence in the community—but also highlights the
       potential of emerging optimizers like Adan and LAMB, particularly in contexts where BOCB or

1415   E     L IMITATIONS
1416
1417   This work has several limitations: (1) Although we conduct transfer learning experiments to ImageNet
1418   and COCO, the benchmark is mainly focused on CIFAR-100, which may lead to questionable findings
       for broader downstream tasks. However, all our transfer learning results are consistent with the
1419
       CIFAR-100 findings. Moreover, our released code can be easily extended to other tasks. Users can
1420   thus easily conduct validations with it. (2) BOCB is more complex than current metrics such as
1421   parameters and FLOPs, which may lead to inconvenience in practice. We suggest researchers use
1422   our code, selecting representative optimizers, such as SGD, Adam, and AdamW, for the ridge plot
1423   validation and CIFAR-100 benchmarks, which are practical and resource-efficient. We also call for
1424   further explorations of BOCB in the community to advance vision systems together.
