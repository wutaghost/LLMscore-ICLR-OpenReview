009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Empowering safe exploration of reinforcement learning (RL) agents during training
012            is a critical impediment towards deploying RL agents in many real-world scenarios.
013            Training RL agents in unknown, black-box environments poses an even greater
014            safety risk when prior knowledge of the domain/task is unavailable. We introduce
015            ADVICE (Adaptive Shielding with a Contrastive Autoencoder), a novel post-
016
               shielding technique that distinguishes safe and unsafe features of state-action
               pairs during training, thus protecting the RL agent from executing actions that
017
               yield potentially hazardous outcomes. Our comprehensive experimental evaluation
018
               against state-of-the-art safe RL exploration techniques demonstrates how ADVICE
019            can significantly reduce safety violations during training while maintaining a
020            competitive outcome reward.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Sutton and Barto, 2018) is a powerful machine learning paradigm
026   for solving complex decision-making tasks that has exhibited performance commensurate with the
027
      cognitive abilities of humans in diverse applications, including game-playing (Silver et al., 2017;
      2018; Berner et al., 2019) and robot control (Rudin et al., 2022; Heess et al., 2017). Despite this
028
      huge potential, developing RL-based agents that can explore their environment safely remains a
029
      significant challenge. Exploring unfamiliar, and potentially hazardous, states while learning from
030   the environment, especially in safety-critical domains, like robotics or healthcare, can pose real
031   dangers. Alleviating this entails RL agents capable of synthesising an optimal policy by exploring
032   the policy space adequately while ensuring safe exploration by preventing the execution of unsafe
033   actions (Amodei et al., 2016).
034
      Ensuring safety becomes an increasingly difficult challenge in complex environments typically
035
      characterised by high-dimensional state/action spaces (Dalal et al., 2018). Such environments require
036   a large amount of training time before the agent can consistently complete the task and avoid safety
037   concerns. This issue is further exacerbated in black-box environments where no prior knowledge
038   can be utilised before training; the only information available is the data observed in real time by
039   the RL agent. In such scenarios, the risk associated with exploration increases exponentially as the
040   agent must operate without pre-defined guidelines, rendering typical safe exploration techniques
041   inadequate (Waga et al., 2022).
042   Prior research on safe RL exploration formulates safety constraints as linear temporal (Alshiekh
043   et al., 2018; Könighofer et al., 2023; ElSayed-Aly et al., 2021) and probabilistic logic (Yang et al.,
044   2023) specifications, whose use as a shield protects the agent during training. Shielding techniques
045   are categorised into pre-shielding (restricting action choices to a predefined safe subset) and post-
046   shielding (evaluating and modifying actions post-selection to ensure safety) (Odriozola-Olalde et al.,
047   2023). Despite noteworthy advances, a common challenge across these methods is their reliance
048   on some degree of prior knowledge about the environment, task, or safety concern which may not
049   be generally available. Research targeting safe exploration in black-box environments employs
050
      Lagrangian methods (Stooke et al., 2020; Altman, 1998; Tessler et al., 2018; Achiam et al., 2017), or
      involves a pre-training phase before the shield synthesis (Tappler et al., 2022).
051
052   We present ADVICE (ADaptiVe ShIelding with a Contrastive AutoEncoder), a novel post-shielding
053   technique for the safe exploration of RL agents in black-box environments. The ADVICE shield is
      underpinned by a contrastive autoencoder that effectively learns distinguishing latent representations


                                                        1
054
      between safe and unsafe features (state-action pairs) and a non-parametric classifier that employs
055   these latent representations, allowing new features to be identified as safe, or correcting them with
056   a new safe action when deemed unsafe. Further, ADVICE encompasses an adaptation component
057   that considers the agent’s recent performance to automatically regulate the risk tolerance levels of
058   the shield, thus encouraging exploration when appropriate. We demonstrate ADVICE’s ability to
059   work in highly complex black-box environments and significantly reduce safety violations during
060   training in comparison to state-of-the-art methods, such as Lagrangian multipliers (Lillicrap et al.,
061   2015), discretized shields (Shperberg et al., 2022) and conservative safety critics (Bharadhwaj et al.,
062   2020). To the best of our knowledge, ADVICE is the first research work that investigates shielding
063
      for safe RL exploration in black-box environments with high-dimensional state/action spaces and
      introduces an end-to-end approach for shield synthesis without using any prior knowledge.

066   2    R ELATED W ORK
067
068   Shielding Techniques. Recent research devises techniques for the safe exploration of RL agents using
069   safety shields, allowing the agent to select from a pool of safe actions or correct an action deemed
070   unsafe (Odriozola-Olalde et al., 2023). Existing shielding approaches leverage linear temporal logic
071   (LTL) specifications (Alshiekh et al., 2018; Könighofer et al., 2023; ElSayed-Aly et al., 2021) or
072
      use external hints for constructing the LTL formulae (Waga et al., 2022). LTL specifications can
      be replaced with probabilistic logic programming (PLP) (Yang et al., 2023), extending their use
073
      to continuous deep RL and enabling safety constraints to be differentiable. By utilising logical
074
      neural networks (Kimura et al., 2020), the same logic specifications can be both respected and learnt,
075   providing a more nuanced understanding of safety. Jansen et al. (2020) introduce probabilistic shields
076   to ensure safety, while other approaches implement safety under partial observability (Carr et al.,
077   2023) or use approximate models of the environment to maintain safety (Goodall and Belardinelli,
078   2023). A recurring limitation of these methods is their reliance on explicit prior knowledge of their
079   environment, task, and/or safety concerns. Although they can improve safety and, in some cases,
080   eliminate violations altogether, their applicability is restricted to a narrow set of environments and
081   safety considerations (Turchetta et al., 2020). In contrast, our ADVICE post-shielding method does
082   not need any prior knowledge, using exclusively the information captured in a typical RL problem.
083   Black-Box Safe Exploration Techniques. Other recent research focuses on improving safety in
084   black-box environments, where no prior knowledge is provided to the agent/user. A trivial but
085   effective solution is to record all unsafe features in a tabular format to prevent the agent from
086   repeating them (Shperberg et al., 2022). However, this approach is limited to discrete environments
087   or extremely low-dimensional spaces. Other research collects data in the environment before training,
088   to then utilise a safety layer (Dalal et al., 2018; Srinivasan et al., 2020; Thananjeyan et al., 2021;
089
      Bharadhwaj et al., 2020) or shield (Tappler et al., 2022) to protect the agent. This requires a significant
      amount of data collection, resulting in an increasing number of safety violations. Lagrangian methods
090
      enable modelling the Markov Decision Process as a Constrained Markov Decision Process, leading
091
      to their wide adoption due to their simplicity and effectiveness (Altman, 2021; Garcıa and Fernández,
092   2015). The Lagrangian multiplier can be fixed (Stooke et al., 2020; Altman, 1998), or integrated into
093   the algorithm itself (Tessler et al., 2018; Achiam et al., 2017). Other advances employ uncertainty
094   estimation concepts (Kahn et al., 2017; Jain et al., 2021). Defining safety in terms of uncertainty and
095   propagating it into the RL algorithm during training yields a cautious yet effective agent for reducing
096   safety violations, even in complex environments. Similarly to these approaches, ADVICE needs no
097   prior knowledge; however, ADVICE also entails far less data collection than the previously discussed
098   techniques.

102   Markov Decision Process. A Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Bellman, 1957) is a discrete-time
103   stochastic control process to model decision-making. An MDP is formally defined as a 5-tuple
104   M “ pS, A, P, R, γq, where S is the state space, A is the action space, P is the state transition
105   probability matrix such that P ps1 |s, aq is the probability of transitioning to state s1 from state s
106   using action a, R is the reward function such that Rps, aq is the reward for taking action a in state
107   s, and γ is the discount factor that determines the value of future rewards. A policy π : S Ñ ∆pAq
      is a distribution over actions given a state. MDPs can be solved using dynamic programming

      techniques (e.g., value iteration, policy iteration) which require complete knowledge of the MDP’s
109   dynamics (Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis, 2008).

112
      sequence of decisions by interacting with the MDP, which represents the environment. This machine
      learning technique is used to solve MDPs when full knowledge is “not  ř8available.   The agent’s

115   following the current policy π. Common RL algorithms include Q-learning (Watkins and Dayan,
116   1992), and SARSA (Rummery and Niranjan, 1994). Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) extends
117   traditional algorithms by utilising deep neural networks to approximate the policy π or the value
118   function V when the state/action space is high-dimensional and complex. Actor-critic methods (Sutton
119   and Barto, 2018) are a popular class of algorithms both in traditional and deep RL. Distinctly, the
120   policy (actor) and the value function (critic) are modelled as separate components, allowing for
121   simultaneous updates to both functions. Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) (Lillicrap et al.,

123   Contrastive Learning. Contrastive Learning (CL) (Hadsell et al., 2006) is an unsupervised or
124   semi-supervised machine learning paradigm aiming at distinguishing between similar (positive) and
125   dissimilar (negative) pairs of data. At its core lies a contrastive loss function, which encourages the
126   model to put similar pairs closer together in the embedding space while separating dissimilar pairs.

223
      the RL loop. The constructed shield encourages safe environment exploration, guarding the agent
224   throughout its interaction with the environment by ensuring the execution of safe actions.
225
226   The ADVICE execution and adaptation stage, shown in Algorithm 1 and Figure 1 (bottom), involves
227
      a continuous cycle of action evaluation and decision-making until the maximum number of training

228
      observed the current state st , selects an action at based on its current policy π (line 4). Without
229

233

306
      We evaluate ADVICE 1 against state-of-the-art safe exploration methods using tasks from the Safety
307   Gymnasium test-suite (Ji et al., 2023a), where a robot with Lidar sensors must complete tasks by
308   navigating through obstacle-filled environments. This benchmark is designed to assess performance
309   and safety in complex, high-dimensional scenarios with various safety constraints. Accordingly, the
310   Safety Gymnasium is an ideal benchmark to assess the effectiveness of ADVICE and has been used
311   by comparable techniques (Bharadhwaj et al., 2020). A terminal state is reached when the agent
312   collides with an obstacle, signifying catastrophic damage to the robot and ending the episode. The
313   selected tasks below cover a range of complexities in agent, goal, and obstacle positions:
314   • Semi-random Goal: A standard goal environment with six obstacles and one goal. The obstacles
315     have a static spawn, while the agent and the goal have randomised positions every episode.
316   • Randomised Goal: A similar goal environment, but obstacles have random positions per episode.
317
      • Randomised Circle: The agent has to circle in a given zone in this environment. The aim is to
        maximise speed and distance from the zone’s centre while avoiding the three randomised obstacles.
318
      • Constrained Randomised Goal: A randomised goal variant environment where the obstacles are
319
        hazards that impose a step-wise cost when the agent is inside. The objective is to minimise the cost.
320
321   Further details of these four environments and the corresponding tasks are provided in Appendix A.
322   Figure 7 in this appendix shows a snapshot of the tasks within the Safety Gymnasium. To evaluate the

      evidence that ADVICE, albeit designed to prioritise safety, manages to maintain a competitive
353
      performance (reward) in all tasks. Both DDPG-Lag and ADVICE, indicate an inherent trade-off
354   between reward maximisation and safety prioritisation.
355
356   Since the primary goal of a safety shield is to encourage exploration and learning in a safe way, it
357
      is evident that ADVICE, across all tasks, can significantly reduce safety violations, outperforming
      by a notable margin all other methods. DDPG-Lag also manages to reduce safety violations, but
358
      not to the magnitude of ADVICE, highlighting the effectiveness of ADVICE’s shield. Through all
359
      three tasks, it is noticeable that the conservative safety critic (CSC) vastly underestimates safety.
360   This is due to the sparse data that comes with an unconstrained MDP mixed with the complex
361   safety constraints. Without a consistent cost signal, regular neural network-based safety techniques
362   struggle to keep the agent safe. This insight further highlights the strength of the contrastive learning
363   underpinning ADVICE. We visualise the learnt latent space from an example ADVICE run (Figure 2
364   right), illustrating the power of contrastive learning and validating its effectiveness at learning latent
365   representations that empower the distinction between safe and unsafe features. Considering that
366   ADVICE constructs the shield for the first E “ 1000 episodes and then starts its execution for safe
367   exploration, while DDPG-Lag aims at reducing safety violations from episode E “ 1, the results are
368
      even more affirmative.
369   Despite its conservative prioritisation to reward, ADVICE still reaches the goal (successful episode
370   execution) with a similar frequency to all other methods. In fact, ADVICE completes the task without
371   compromising safety, and the difference in accumulated reward is due to the reward function design
372   and the reduced time the agent has to complete the task using a cautious path. Through additional
373   experiments with an increased number of maximum steps per episode (E 1 “ 2000 – Figure 14 -
374
      Appendix C), we have established that ADVICE’s average episodic return is very similar to the other
      methods. Hence, increasing the episode’s duration yields improved ADVICE-based results.
375
376   These core results corroborate that ADVICE’s contrastive approach to shielding an RL agent from
377   unsafe actions is effective in comparison to other methods. ADVICE significantly reduces safety
      violations without detracting much from the agent’s overall performance.

514   5.5   D ISCUSSION
515
      Despite ADVICE achieving a significantly lower safety violation rate compared to other methods,
516
      there are a few areas that warrant further exploration. Firstly, while ADVICE experiences a cold-start
517
      period due to the need to gather sufficient features for training the contrastive autoencoder, it still
518   outperforms methods that start learning from timestep t0 by significantly reducing violations. This
519   challenge can be mitigated through transfer learning (as seen in Figure 4). Furthermore, although
520   ADVICE increases computational demands due to continuous inference at each timestep, optimizing
521   inference intervals or leveraging more efficient models can help balance performance and resource
522   usage. This adaptation may help extend its applicability to resource-constrained settings without
523   compromising safety. Lastly, in dynamic environments with temporally changing obstacles, ADVICE
524   might benefit from incorporating temporal context through methods like LSTM, which would enhance
525   its ability to handle these situations effectively. This could offer a promising avenue for further
526
      enhancing safety performance, despite the additional computational load.
527
528   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
529
530   We introduced ADVICE, a post-shielding technique for the safe exploration of RL agents operating in
531
      complex black-box environments. ADVICE does not need any prior knowledge and uses a contrastive
      autoencoder to distinguish between safe and unsafe features efficiently. Our evaluation shows that
532
      ADVICE significantly reduces safety violations while maintaining competitive performance against
533
      state-of-the-art methods. Despite its effectiveness, ADVICE has areas for improvement, including
534   mitigating the cold-start issue, reducing high computational demands, and addressing challenges
535   with dynamic obstacles. In future work plan to explore meta-learning techniques (Hospedales et al.,
536   2021) to address the cold-start problem, enabling faster activation of ADVICE without sacrificing
537   performance. Quantisation or pruning could reduce computational demands, enhancing applicability
538   in computationally constrained domains. We envisage that ADVICE serves as a foundational step in
539   using neural network-based shielding for safe RL exploration in complex, black-box environments
      without any prior knowledge.

957   In this section, we present an extended analysis of ADVICE to display the robustness and adaptability
958   of the approach. These experiments were chosen to explore the effects of varying K thresholds, and
959   the timing of ADVICE’s activations E.
960   A user can specify the conservativeness of ADVICE using the safety threshold K. In Figure 12,
961   we evaluate how this parameter affects the model’s safety, and performance. The results are clear,
962   increasing K leads to a more conservative behaviour as hypothesised. The reward decreases a small
963   amount as well as the cumulative goal reaches, however, it also results in fewer safety violations.
964   Conversely, decreasing K allows the underlying DDPG agent more freedom. As a result, average
965   reward and goal reaches are increased at the expense of safety violations. These findings display a
966   clear trade-off between return efficiency and safety assurance.

968   order to work efficiently. We acknowledge that this can affect the performance of ADVICE evidently
