009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011
               Spatial coding is a fundamental function of the brain. Place cells in the hippocampus (HPC)
012            and grid cells in the medial entorhinal cortex (MEC) are two primary types of neurons ac-
013            counting for spatial representation in the brain. These two types of neurons employ different
014            spatial coding strategies and process environmental and motion cues, respectively. In this
015            work, we develop a computational model to elucidate how place and grid cells can comple-
016            ment each other to integrate information optimally and overcome their respective shortcom-
017            ings. Specifically, we build a model with reciprocally coupled continuous attractor neural
018            networks (CANNs), in which a CANN with location coordinate models the place cell en-
019            semble in HPC, and multiple CANNs with phase coordinate model grid cell modules with
020
               different spacings in MEC, and the coupling between place and grid cells conveys the cor-
               relation prior between sensory cues. We theoretically derive that the dynamics of our model
021
               effectively implements the gradient-based optimization of the posterior. Using simulations,
022
               we demonstrate that our model achieves Bayesian optimal integration of the environmental
023            and motion cues, and avoids the non-local error problem in phase coding of grid cells. We
024            hope that this study gives us insights into understanding how place and grid cells complement
025            each other to improve spatial representation in the brain.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Spatial coding is a fundamental function of the brain, which serves as the foundation for the brain to realize many
031
      other advanced cognitive functions. Over decades, experimental studies have unveiled the neural correlates of
032
      spatial coding in the brain, and two primary neuron types central to this functionality are identified, which
033   are place cells in the hippocampus (HPC) (O’Keefe & Dostrovsky, 1971; O’Keefe, 1976) and grid cells in the
034   medial entorihnal cortex (MEC) (Hafting et al., 2005). These two types of cells exhibit very different response
035   characteristics in spatial representation. Specifically, place cells display localized place fields, firing intensely
036   at localized locations in the environment; while grid cells display distributed place fields, forming periodic
037   grid-like firing patterns across the environment (Fig.1a). Experimental data further revealed that place and grid
038   cells are primarily responsible for processing different sensory cues, namely, place cells are primarily driven
039   by environmental cues, such as the visual and/or olfactory cues; while grid cells are primarily driven by the
040   self-motion cue of the animal (McNaughton et al., 2006; Laptev & Burgess, 2019; Chen et al., 2019; Sharp
041
      et al., 1995).
042   The significantly different response characteristics between place and grid cells indicate that they employ dif-
043   ferent strategies to represent space. Simply state, the strategy of HPC is to use intensive number of place cells
044   with localized fields to cover the space compactly (Fig.1a-b), referred to as localized space coding (LSC) here-
045   after; while the strategy of MEC is to use the combination of grid cells’ phases with varying spacing to encode
046   space (Fig.1c-d), referred to as phase space coding (PSC) hereafter. From the information theory point of view,
047
      LSC is robust to noise but not efficient, since the number of neurons it needs increases linearly with the spatial
      size (Abbott & Dayan, 1999; Latham et al., 2003); while PSC is efficient but susceptible to noise, as small
048
      perturbations in grid cells’ activities can induce non-local errors in the space representation (Fiete et al., 2008b;
049
      Sreenivasan & Fiete, 2011) (Fig.1c).
050
051   Given that place and grid cells employ different coding strategies and process different sensory cues, a prompt
052   question is: whether these two coding strategies can complement with each other, such that the brain can
053
      integrate two sensory cues optimally to improve spatial representation? Experimental studies have shown that
      there exist abundant reciprocal connections between HPC and MEC (Manns & Eichenbaum, 2006; Wible,
054
      2013; Bush et al., 2014), which can support the interaction between place and grid cells. In this work, we build
055
      a computational model with reciprocally coupled place and grid cells, and demonstrate that HPC and MEC can
056   interact with each other to integrate the environmental and motion cues optimally for spatial representation.

      The organization of the paper are as follows. In Sec.2, we first review the different coding properties of place and
058   grid cells, and discuss about their respective advantages and disadvantages in spatial representation. In Sec.3,
059   we use a probabilistic inference model to elucidate the information integration between place and grid cells,
060   and present an optimal decoding approach called gradient-based optimization of posterior (GOP). In Sec.4,
061   we build a computational model with reciprocally coupled continuous attractor neural networks (CANNs), in
062   which a CANN with location coordinate models the place cell ensemble in HPC, and multiple CANNs with
063   phase coordinate model the grid cell modules of different spacings in MEC. HPC and MEC receive the location
064   information through the environmental and the motion cues, respectively, and they are reciprocally connected
065   in a congruent manner, which conveys the correlation prior between two sensory cues. We theoretically derive

      integrates two sensory cues optimally in a Bayesian optimal manner, and that it avoids the non-local error

      problem of PSC. In Sec.6, overall conclusions of this study and related works are discussed.

144   2.1   C ODING EFFICIENCY
145
      The efficiency of a coding strategy can be quantified by information rate, which is defined a R = ln L/N , with
146
      L the spatial range and N the number of neurons (Mackay, 2003; Sreenivasan & Fiete, 2011). We can roughly
147
      estimate the efficiency of LSC and PSC as follows. In LSC, the place cell ensemble covers the space compactly.
148   Denote ∆x to be the interval covered by a place cell with its local place field. The whole space range covered
149   by N place cells is L = N ∆x. Thus, the information rate of LSC is given by Rp = log(N ∆x)/N , which
150   becomes very small for large N , indicating that LSC is not efficient, in term of utilizing the resource of neurons.
151   In PSC, the phases of grid cells in a single module cover a spatial range λ (the spacing); beyond that, multiple
152   positions share the same phase value, inducing ambiguity. By employing multiple modules of varying spacing
153   λi , for i = 1, . . . , M , and that {λi } are prime numbers, the spatial range unambiguously represented by the
                                                    QM
154   combination of grid cells’ phases is L = i=1 λi ≈ λ̄M (Fiete et al., 2008a). Thus, the information rate of PSC
155   is given by Rg = log(λ̄M )/M N0 = log λ̄/N0 , with N0 the number of neurons in each module. This shows
156   that with the increased number of modules M (and so does the number of neurons M N0 ), the information rate
157   of PSC is a constant, indicating that PSC is much more efficient than LSC in utilizing the resource of neurons
158   (for more detailed analysis, see Appendix.A).
159
160   2.2   C ODING ACCURACY
161
162   We use Fisher information, whose inverse is the lower bound of decoding variance of any unbiased estimator
163   (commonly known as the Cramér–Rao bound (CRAMÉR, 1946)), to quantify the decoding accuracy of LSC.
164   For the response property of place cells given by Eq. (1), the Fisher information can be analytically calculated,
165   whose value increases linearly with the number of neurons, indicating that LSC can achieve arbitrary accuracy
166   if the number of neurons is sufficiently large (see Appendix.A). The accuracy of PSC is, however, very sensitive
167
      to noises. In PSC, grid cells’ phases are combined to represent spatial location. Due to the periodic mapping
      between phase and location, two points close in the phase plane can represent two far away spatial locations
168
      (see illustration in Fig.1d and details in Appendix.A). This implies that small fluctuations in grid cells’ activ-
169
      ities can induce large non-local errors in spatial representation, a shortcoming of PSC that has been noticed
170   previously (Sreenivasan & Fiete, 2011).

477   5.2                      T HE MODEL ELIMINATING NON - LOCAL ERRORS
478
479   In certain scenarios, such as moving in dark, animals have to locate themselves relying mainly on the motion
480   cue. In our model, this corresponds to the external inputs Ip = 0 and Ig ̸= 0. In such a case, if we only
481   use the responses of grid cells to decode the position, e.g., by maximizing p(rg |ϕ) directly, we will get large
482   non-local errors associated with phase coding (Sreenivasan & Fiete, 2011). On the other hand, if we let the
483   coupled CANNs to decode the location, which is given by the bump position of place cells, non-local errors are
484
      eliminated, as shown in Fig. 4a. The underlying reason is intuitively understandable. In the coupled CANNs,
      although place cells does not receive the environment cue (Ip = 0), through reciprocal connections between
485
      networks, place cells will still generate a bump activity to represent the animal location. As analyzed above, the
486
      dynamics of coupled CANNs effectively implements GOP, whereby the previous bump position of place cells
487   serves as the initial point, which restricts the bump to evolve to a local maximum of the posterior, reflecting
488   the continuity of the animal movement. Hence, no abrupt change in decoding results occurs, which effectively
489   eliminate large non-local errors. Fig. 4b show that as the amplitude of noises in grid cells’ responses increases,
490   the error of MAP based on PSC increases dramatically (due to non-local errors), while the error of coupled
491   CANNs remains largely flat.

      6                C ONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSIONS
494
495
      In this study, we have developed a computational model to elucidate how place and grid cells complement with
496
      each other to improve spatial representation in the brain. We first formulate the interaction between place and
497
      grid cells as a probabilistic inference model for integrating information between environmental and motion cues.
498   We then model the interaction between HPC and MEC using reciprocally coupled CANNs, with the coupling
499   strength conveying the correlation prior between sensory cues. We theoretically derive that the dynamics of
500   coupled CANNs effectively implements gradient-based optimization of the posterior (GOP). Using simulations,
501   we further demonstrate that our model indeed achieves Bayesian optimal integration of two sensory cues, which
502   improves the spatial coding accuracy compared to no cue integration. We also show that when only the motion
503   cue is presented, our network model avoids the non-local error problem faced by phase coding of grid cells.
504
505   6.1                      R ELATED WORKS
506
507   The coding properties of place cells and grid cells have been studied intensively in the literature for long time.
508   For instances, a large volume of theoretical works studied the encoding accuracy and decoding models of place
509
      cells (see e.g. (Wilson & McNaughton, 1993; Seung & Sompolinsky, 1993; Brunel & Nadal, 1998; Abbott &
      Dayan, 1999) and references therein), and researches on grid cells involve calculating the coding efficiency and
510
      addressing the non-local error problem (Fiete et al., 2008b; Sreenivasan & Fiete, 2011; Mathis et al., 2012).
511
      Notably, Sreenivasan et al. proposed a “constrained range” approach to resolve the non-local error of PSC,
512   which restricts the coding range of grid cells to reduce the sensitivity of phase coding to noises (Sreenivasan


                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


513
      & Fiete, 2011), and they built a network model with connected place cells and grid cells to implement this
514   idea. Their method differs from our model significantly. Specifically, by restricting the spatial range to be
515   decoded, the “constrained range” approach avoids large non-local errors, but it also sacrifices the advantage of
516   high coding efficiency of PSC. On the other hand, in our model, the non-local error of phase coding is naturally
517   avoided, since the coupled CANNs effectively implement a gradient-based searching method (GOP), which
518   imposes that the decoding result of the place cell network is history-dependent and only changes smoothly.
519
      There are also a large volume of theoretical works in the literature studying the interactions between place and
520   grid cells. These works include: 1) exploring how grid cell inputs drive place cells to form localized place fields
521   and how the place field remapping occurs (Bush et al., 2014; Agmon & Burak, 2020; Whittington et al., 2020); 2)
522   exploring how place cell inputs shape the hexagonal activity pattern of grid cells (Blair et al., 2008; Fernandez-
523   Leon et al., 2022; Evans & Burgess, 2019); 3) examining the role of the HPC-MEC loop in multisensory
524   processing. For examples, Li et al. proposed that the reciprocal connections between HPC and MEC transmit
525   the prior association between visual and motion cues, enabling the differentiation of multiple environments (Li
526   et al., 2020); Laptev et al. suggested that attractor dynamics in HPC and MEC enable decentralized information
527   integration (Laptev & Burgess, 2019); Agmon et al. modeled the joint network of grid cells and place cells
528
      as coupled CANNs (Agmon & Burak, 2020). Compared to these works, our study has the following novel
      contributions: 1) we formulate the information integration between HPC and MEC as a Bayesian inference
529
      model, and propose GOP as a feasible decoding method; 2) we build a model of coupled CANNs to elucidate
530
      the information integration between HPC and MEC, where the reciprocal connections between CANNs convey
531   the prior correlation between sensory cuses; 3) by both theoretical analysis and simulations, we demonstrate
532   that the coupled CANNs effectively implement GOP, a Bayesian optimal way of information integration, and
533   that the coupled CANNs eliminate non-local errors of phase coding of grid cells.
534
535   6.2   L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
536
537   In the present study, for the purpose of theoretically elucidating the neural mechanism clearly, we have consid-
538   ered the representation of 1D space, while a large amount of experimental data was on the 2D space. Also, the
539   focuses of this work are on the information integration between place and gird cells and eliminating of non-local
540
      decoding errors. Experimental data has shown that place and grid cells can also complement with each other
      on other functions. One example is the construction of a global map of the space (Whittington et al., 2020;
541
      Evans et al., 2016). Based on the environmental cue such as vision, place cells can sense the space precisely but
542
      only locally; while based on the self-motion cue, gird cells sense the space ambiguously but can link far-away
543   locations via path-integration. Thus, in order to construct a global map, it is necessary to combine both environ-
544   mental and motion cues. In future work, we will extend the current model to the 2D space and investigate how
545   place and grid cells complement with each other to construct a global spatial map.
546
      Recent experimental studies have continuously revealed new functions of the HPC-MEC loop. In addition to
547
      processing different sensory cues, it was found that the spatial map formed by place cells is more oriented to the
548
      special layout of the environment; while the spatial map formed by grid cells reflects more the abstract metric
549   information of the environment (Whittington et al., 2020; Evans et al., 2016). This is manifested by that place
550   cells tend to remap their place fields dramatically when the environment changes, while grid cells only realign
551   their place fields slightly (Hafting et al., 2005). Furthermore, it was suggested that in addition to represent the
552   space, the HPC-MEC loop can cooperate to represent some general relationships between objects. It is our
553   future work to extend the current model to explore these issues.

670   A    A PPENDIX A: C ODING PROPERTIES OF LSC AND PSC
671
672   We compare the different coding properties of place cells and grid cells.
673
      Local spatial coding by place cells, also known as ”classical population coding” (Sreenivasan & Fiete, 2011),
674
      is widely found in various brain regions (Schreiner et al., 2000; Georgopoulos et al., 1989; Taube et al., 1990;
675   Vollan et al., 2024). In this coding strategy, each neuron has a local receptive field corresponding to its preferred
676   feature values. The receptive field typically has a bell-shaped curve, with neuronal activity peaking when the
677   stimulus is at the center of the receptive field and gradually decreasing as the stimulus moves away from the
678   center. The receptive fields of different neurons cover different parts of the feature space, together spanning the

811   Non-local errors in PSC. The vast coding range of grid cells, which grows exponentially with the number
812   of modules, comes with the expense of high sensitivity to noise. For instance, consider PSC with two grid
813   cell modules. The phase combination (ϕ1 , ϕ2 ) spans a two-dimensional toroidal space with periodic bound-
814   aries (Figure S1c). A straight line in position space (with position coordinate z) is mapped to parallel line
815   segments in the phase space through the mapping function Eq. (22). The vertical separation between adjacent
816   parallel lines is denoted by dmin . It is important to note that, due to the periodicity of the phase space, two
817   points that are far apart in the position space (the blue and red dots in Figure S1c) can be close in the phase
818   space. Consequently, a small perturbation in the phase space can result in a large shift in the decoded positions
819
      (Figure S1d) (Sreenivasan & Fiete, 2011).
820   Specifically, non-local errors occur when noises cause the phase combination encoded by grid cells to deviate
821   by more than dmin /2 from the true phase combination in the vertical direction. Since the encoding of phase
822   within a single grid module follows “classical population coding,” the error is proportional to the intensity of
823   noises. Therefore, PSC does not produce non-local errors when the noise level is low. However, as the noise
824   level exceeds a certain threshold, non-local errors emerge, leading to a sharp increase in the error variance (as
825
      shown in the numerical simulations in Figure S1e). This indicates that PSC is vulnerable to noise.

1455   E.3   M ODEL C OMPARISON
1456
1457   We compare our model with the ”constrained range model” proposed by Sreenivasan & Fiete (2011) (see
1458   Fig. S5). Both models are capable of eliminating non-local errors when the decoding range is tightly con-
1459   strained (Fig. S5a). However, when the decoding range becomes larger, our model continues to eliminate
1460
       non-local errors, whereas the ”constrained range model” fails to do so (Fig. S5b-c).
1461   The key difference lies in how non-local errors are handled. Our model leverages recurrent currents within
1462   the place cell network to store historical information, enabling robust error correction without relying on range
1463   constraints. In contrast, the ”constrained range model” reduces non-local errors by limiting the coding range,
1464   thereby sacrificing coding capacity. This distinction highlights the advantage of our model: the dynamics of the
1465   place cell network not only ensure robust spatial coding but also preserve coding efficiency.
