011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid advancements in large video models have unlocked new horizons in
014            video understanding, enhancing applications in various domains such as surveil-
015            lance, healthcare, and entertainment. However, these models often compromise
016
               individual privacy by inadvertently revealing sensitive private information such as
               skin color and gender. Existing privacy preservation methods are often limited
017
               in their scope and tailored to specific downstream tasks. Since current methods
018
               directly apply an anonymization function to the input pixel space, they demand
019            extensive computational resources due to the retraining of the utility video model.
020            To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach that shifts privacy-
021            preserving anonymization from the input pixel space to the latent feature space,
022            significantly reducing computational costs and enabling deployment in large foun-
023            dational video models. Our method employs a self-supervised privacy budget in
024            the latent space by minimizing the mutual information between static clip fea-
025            tures. This approach notably allows, for the first time, supervision from down-
026            stream tasks such as anomaly detection and temporal action detection through
027
               collaborative co-training. Furthermore, we introduce a latent consistency loss to
               maintain the utility video model’s multitask generalization capabilities and pre-
028
               vent single task overfitting. Our extensive evaluations demonstrate a significant
029
               (≈29%) reduction in privacy leakage while maintaining near peak (within 1%)
030            utility performance across various downstream tasks: Action Recognition (Ki-
031            netics400, UCF101, HMDB51), Temporal Action Detection (THUMOS14), and
032            Anomaly Detection (UCF-Crime). Moreover, we propose new protocols for as-
033            sessing gender bias in action recognition models, demonstrating that our method
034            effectively mitigates such biases and promotes equitable video understanding.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Video understanding encompasses a wide range of problem formulations, including action recogni-
040   tion, anomaly detection, and temporal action localization. It holds significant potential for real-world
041   applications such as patient behavior monitoring, sports analytics, robotics, industrial automation,
042   surgical videos, and surveillance. Recent advancements in video understanding have aimed at de-
043   veloping single models capable of addressing multiple video tasks, leading to the creation of video
044
      foundational models such as InternVideo Wang et al. (2022), VideoMAEv2 Wang et al. (2023), V-
      JEPA Bardes et al. (2023), NMS Dave et al. (2024) etc. These models offer a deeper understanding
045
      of the intrinsic nature of videos, thereby enhancing their applicability in real-world scenarios.
046
047   However, the utility of video understanding models is accompanied by the risk of privacy breaches.
048   Features extracted from pre-trained video encoders can reveal sensitive information about individu-
049   als in the videos, such as skin color, gender, and clothing Fioresi et al. (2023). This information can
050
      be exploited maliciously, underscoring the urgent need to prevent such privacy leaks from powerful
      contemporary video models.
051
052   Several prior privacy-preserving methods have been proposed to address this issue, including ap-
053   proaches like SPAct Dave et al. (2022b), STPrivacy Li et al. (2023b), TeD-SPAD Fioresi et al. (2023)
      and VITA Wu et al. (2020). These methods excel at learning anonymization functions that maintain


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the utility performance of targeted video downstream task while reducing privacy leakage. However,
055   they encounter several challenges. For instance, after learning the anonymization function, they re-
056   quire full fine-tuning of the utility video encoder model, which demands substantial computational
057   resources and is impractical for scaling to large-scale models such as ViT-H, ViT-L of V-JEPA, and
058   InternVideo. Additionally, these methods often focus on specific downstream video tasks, such as
059   action recognition in SPAct and anomaly detection in TeD-SPAD, limiting their generalizability.
060
      To address the challenges of scalability and generalization in video model anonymization, we intro-
061   duce a novel approach termed SPLAVU, which stands for Self-supervised Privacy-preservation via
062   Latent Anonymization for general Video Understanding. SPLAVU aims to prevent privacy leakage
063   while maintaining downstream performance. Our method operates in the latent space of a utility
064   model, enabling the training of a proposed Anonymizing Adapter Module (AAM) on a frozen util-
065   ity video model. By eliminating the need for full fine-tuning, SPLAVU is feasible for large video
066   models and adaptable to various downstream tasks.
067   We adopt the minimax optimization strategy, a proven method in prior privacy works Dave et al.
068   (2022b); Wu et al. (2020); Fioresi et al. (2023), to the latent space. Our joint optimization strategy
069   balances privacy and proxy-utility branches by minimizing the mutual information between two
070   static clips derived from the same video, following the self-supervised privacy objective Dave et al.
071   (2022b). The latent formulation enables a novel co-training paradigm, where the anonymizer is
072   trained to maintain the performance across multiple downstream video understanding tasks, not just
073   for a single task. Additionally, to preserve the generalization capability of the utility model on
074   unseen tasks, we introduce a latent consistency loss.
075   Our anonymization method integrates seamlessly with multiple state-of-the-art methods for various
076   downstream tasks. We demonstrate that SPLAVU outperforms prior privacy preservation methods
077   by a significant margin in numerous downstream video understanding tasks. Additionally, SPLAVU
078   exhibits data efficiency; even when trained on a small-scale dataset like HMDB51 Kuehne et al.
079   (2011), it generalizes to multiple tasks without compromising downstream performance and privacy
080   tradeoff. Beyond privacy protection, we address the emerging issue of human-attribute related bias
081
      in video understanding, which has been largely unexplored. For instance, a model might predict
      specific actions based on gender stereotypes, such as associating a perceived female subject with
082
      hands near her face as applying makeup or brushing hair, even with nothing in hand. For the first
083
      time, we propose protocols to evaluate gender bias in action recognition models and demonstrate
084   our method’s effectiveness in mitigating such bias.
085
086   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
087
             • We introduce a novel latent privacy preservation method that is effective across various
088
               downstream video understanding tasks using a collaborative multitask co-training protocol,
089
               unlike prior work that focused solely on one specific task.
090
091          • To enable anonymization in the latent space, we propose a static-clip-based self-supervised
092
               privacy budget objective. We also introduce a latent consistency loss to preserve the gener-
               alization capability of the utility model.
093
094          • We conduct extensive evaluations of our method on multiple downstream tasks. Our
095            method achieves notable decrease in privacy leakage by approximately 29%, while pre-
096            serving performance across a range of downstream tasks, including Action Recogni-
097            tion (UCF101, HMDB51, Kinetics400), Temporal Action Detection (THUMOS14), and
098
               Anomaly Detection (UCF-Crime).
099          • Our extensive ablation studies demonstrate the data efficiency of our method when trained
100            on a small-scale dataset and its applicability across various video backbones.
101          • We propose new protocols to assess gender bias in existing action recognition models and
102            demonstrate that our method effectively mitigates this bias.

      Privacy Preservation in Video Understanding Recent efforts in video action recognition have ad-
      dressed visual privacy concerns. Many studies have aimed to protect visual privacy at the time of
142
      data capture by utilizing non-intrusive sensors such as thermal imaging, depth cameras, and event
143
      cameras Luo et al. (2018); Hinojosa et al. (2022); Kim et al. (2022); Ahmad et al. (2022; 2023). In
144   this study, we focus exclusively on models using standard RGB cameras. Initial approaches involved
145   reducing the resolution of input data Ryoo et al. (2017); Dai et al. (2015); Liu & Zhang (2020) or em-
146   ploying object detection for targeted obfuscations Ren et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2021). However,
147   recent research indicates that these methods often fail to balance utility and privacy effectively Wu
148   et al. (2020); Dave et al. (2022b); Fioresi et al. (2023). Wu et al. (2020) showcased an adversarial
149   training framework where a U-Net Ronneberger et al. (2015) modifies input frames to decrease the
150   accuracy of private attribute prediction while preserving action recognition performance. Dave et al.
151   (2022b) proposed a self-supervised variant that focuses on reducing mutual information instead of
152
      relying on sensitive privacy labels. Fioresi et al. (2023) adopts the self-supervised privacy objective
      from Dave et al. (2022b) for the video anomaly detection task.
153
154   Compared to the prior input-level anonymization methods our latent-space anonymization method
155   differs in two key aspects: (1) previous methods are tailored to specific downstream tasks, such as
156   action recognition in Dave et al. (2022b) and anomaly detection in Fioresi et al. (2023), while our
157   approach aims to preserve privacy across various downstream video understanding tasks, (2) unlike
158
      prior methods, our method does not necessitate the retraining of the video model, thus providing
      computational efficiency for anonymizing even large-scale video foundation models.

160   Bias Mitigation Computer vision tasks often struggle with spurious correlations Geirhos et al.
161   (2018; 2020), where models rely on irrelevant information to make decisions, such as using back-
      ground cues for action recognition instead of focusing on subjects’ movements Ding et al. (2022);

      Zou et al. (2023). Unfortunately, biases across a variety of protected demographic attributes, such as
163   perceived gender, skin color, and age Zhao et al. (2017); Stock & Cisse (2017); Buolamwini & Ge-
164   bru (2018); Wilson et al. (2019); Prabhu & Birhane (2020); Tong & Kagal (2020); Steed & Caliskan
165   (2021); Gustafson et al. (2023) have been found in vision-based tasks. These biases not only skew
166   model performance but can also perpetuate harmful stereotypes. One common method for bias mit-
167   igation is to utilize adversarial training Goodfellow et al. (2014); Xie et al. (2017); Zhang et al.
168   (2018). When targeting a sensitive attribute such as perceived gender, these methods involve the use

      giving promise to privacy preservation as a form of debiasing. In contrast to the image domain,
172
      biases in the video domain have not been as extensively studied. While a few papers Choi et al.
173
      (2019); Li et al. (2023a) address and mitigate scene bias in action recognition tasks, they overlook
174   biases related to human attributes. Motivated by this gap, we introduce, for the first time, protocols
175   to assess gender bias in action recognition. Our findings demonstrate that our self-supervised pri-
176   vacy preservation method, even without an explicit bias-related objective, effectively generalizes in
177   mitigating gender bias.

265   This section describes full anonymization framework and training. The framework consists of 3
266
      major components: (1) a frozen video encoder backbone fE , (2) an anonymization function adapter
      fA , which modifies the latent features while retaining the original shape, and (3) a set of utility
267
      classifier heads {fTAR ; fTT AD ; fTAD } ∈ fT ∗ for a predefined set of tasks.
268
269   Network Initialization To start, we initialize fA to act as an identity function. This involves a
      brief pretraining phase where fA learns to reconstruct the latent features from Dreco using an ℓ1

      4     E VALUATION P ROTOCOLS
340
      To ensure that our anonymization method preserves the utility of the original off-the-shelf encoder
341
      across multiple tasks, we evaluate its performance comprehensively. Previous work Fioresi et al.
342
      (2023) has shown that existing anonymization methods, which typically use action recognition as
343   the proxy utility task, significantly degrade performance on alternate downstream tasks. However,
344   the original pretrained models are known to demonstrate strong performance in areas like tempo-
345   ral action detection (TAD) and anomaly detection (AD). Therefore, we assess the learned features
346   across five distinct tasks to thoroughly evaluate their effectiveness post-anonymization.
347
348   4.1   P RIVACY E VALUATION
349
350   First, we utilize an established privacy preservation protocol to ensure that fA removes sensitive-
351   attribute related information. Even though we work with with action-focused video understanding
352
      models, information related to sensitive attributes is still carried through the backbone encoder. This
      is empirically shown through performance on a private attribute prediction task using the VISPR
353
      dataset Orekondy et al. (2017). While this is an image-based protocol, we are still able to use it by
354
      repeating each image to form static clips. A classifier is trained on these representations using the
355   same protocol as previous video privacy works Wu et al. (2020); Dave et al. (2022b); Fioresi et al.
356   (2023). The attribute labels are multi-label, so performance is measured by mean average precision
357   across classes (cMAP). Since our goal is to enhance privacy—not to accurately predict attributes—a
358   lower performance on this task indicates better privacy preservation.
359
360   4.2   U TILITY V IDEO TASK E VALUATION
361
362   Action Recognition Action recognition involves analyzing spatio-temporal features of a video to
363   classify the actions based on a predefined set of categories. Our anonymization framework uses
364   action recognition as its proxy utility task, carried out on multiple different datasets Dreco , namely
365
      Kinetics400 Carreira & Zisserman (2017), UCF101 Soomro et al. (2012), and HMDB51 Kuehne
      et al. (2011). Evaluation is top-1 accuracy on 5 evenly spaced clips from each testing video.
366
367   Temporal Action Detection Temporal action detection (TAD) is a task that involves identifying
368   the specific time intervals within an untrimmed video where particular actions occur. TAD uti-
369   lizes features from a Kinetics-pretrained video encoder model. Given Dtad , fA is used to generate
370   anonymized feature set Ftad = { fA (fE (X (i) )) | ∀X (i) ∈ Dtad }. Our TAD evaluation uses THU-
371   MOS14 Jiang et al. (2014) as Dtad . We choose one of the recent state-of-the-art methods, TriDet Shi
372
      et al. (2023) with default hyperparameters to evaluate using mean Average Precision (mAP).
373   Weakly-Supervised Anomaly Detection Weakly supervised anomaly detection (WSAD) in-
374   volves localizing the timestamps of anomalous (unexpected) events given long, untrimmed videos
375   and only video-level labels. Our evaluation for WSAD uses UCF-Crime Sultani et al. (2018)
376   as Danomaly . Given Danomaly , fA is used to create anonymized feature set Fanomaly =
377   { fA (fE (X (i)) )) | ∀X (i) ∈ Danomaly }. A recent state-of-the-art method MGFN Chen et al. (2023)
      is used with default hyperparameters. Final evaluation is given as frame-level ROC AUC percentage.

380   Proposed NTU Bias Evaluation We further verify the anonymization performance by evaluating on
381   our proposed attribute video bias protocols. The NTU 60 Shahroudy et al. (2016) action recognition
382   dataset is carefully curated to have minimal scene and subject biases as each actor performs each
383   action multiple times in different scenes. Given that each video is carefully labeled with subject ID,
384   we can introduce an artificial bias by controlling the subclass ratios across actions. A gender ratio
385   of 95% is set for all but one action, where the typical gender ratio is inverted to create a spurious
386   shortcut for the model. This is done for each gender, resulting in two subsets: NTU-Bias-F and
387
      NTU-Bias-M. More detailed information about protocol creation can be found in Appendix Sec. A.

389   is less balanced and represents a real-world scenario with elderly individuals performing unscripted
390   daily activities. Each video is labelled with a subject ID, allowing for robust evaluation of perceived
391   gender biases without the need for a pretrained gender classifier. Here, we look at the performance of
392   each gender subclass. A model is considered less biased if the baseline gap between the performance
393
      of each gendered subclass is reduced.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529
      We propose an innovative self-supervised privacy-preserving method via a novel formulation
      of latent-space anonymization called SPLAVU. Our method is the first to enable generalized
530
      anonymization for unprecedented performance across various downstream video understanding
531
      tasks, including action recognition, anomaly detection, and temporal action detection. It employs
532   a self-supervised privacy budget within the latent space, coupled with a latent consistency loss to
533   maintain the powerful generalization capability of the model. Moreover, the latent formulation en-
534   ables learning the anonymizer with gradients from each task for a boost in performance, which is
535   impractical with input anonymization. SPLAVU also eliminates the need for extensive retraining
536   of the utility video model, enabling the anonymization of large foundational models with compu-
537   tational efficiency. Our extensive evaluations and ablation studies validate the effectiveness and
538   data efficiency of our approach. Furthermore, our innovative protocols for assessing gender bias
539   contribute to the development of more responsible and unbiased video understanding models.

      A    DATASET D ETAILS
819
      Kinetics400 Carreira & Zisserman (2017) is a large-scale video action dataset of YouTube videos
820
      which includes 400 human action classes with at least 400 video clips for each action. Each clip
821
      lasts around 10 seconds and is labeled with a single action class. The dataset is widely used for
822   pretraining deep learning models for use in many video understanding tasks.
823   UCF101 Soomro et al. (2012) is an action recognition dataset of realistic action videos consisting
824   of 101 action categories. With over 13,000 videos from various actions and scenes, it provides a
825   diverse set of actions and a broad range of variability in terms of actions, viewpoints, appearances,
826   and backgrounds.
827   HMDB51 Kuehne et al. (2011) is a collection of 6,766 video clips distributed across 51 human
828   action categories, each containing a minimum of 101 clips. The dataset includes a wide range of
829   human actions and is designed for the development and evaluation of action recognition methods.
830
      NTU RGB+D 60 Shahroudy et al. (2016) is a large-scale multi view human action recognition
      dataset complete with RGB video, depth maps, and skeleton joints, and IR sequences. This work
831
      only uses the RGB frames. Each of the 40 subjects are recorded completing 60 daily activities from
832
      3 different cameras.
833   Toyota Smarthome Das et al. (2019) is a challenging real-world activity classification dataset
834   captured from 7 independent Kinect v1 cameras. The clips recorded 18 senior subjects performing
835   31 daily activities in a natural manner. This work only uses the provided RGB frames. The dataset
836   contains high class imbalance, intra-class variation, and duration variance.
837
838
      THUMOS14 Jiang et al. (2014) focuses on temporal action localization in untrimmed videos. It
839   extends the UCF101 dataset with temporal annotations for a subset of the action classes, providing
840   detailed temporal annotations for 20 action classes across 200 validation videos and 213 test videos.
841
842
      UCF-Crime Sultani et al. (2018) is a large-scale dataset of surveillance videos designed for
843
      anomaly detection. It consists of 1,900 long and untrimmed videos for a total of 128 hours. The
844
      videos contain examples of 13 different real-world anomalies, including burglary, robbery, and
845   fighting, among others, making it suitable for training and evaluating video anomaly detection
846   models.
847
848
      VISPR Orekondy et al. (2017) consists of around 22,000 Flickr images annotated with 68 privacy-
849
      related attributes such as gender, age group, skin color, and more. It offers a multi-class classification
850
      protocol for assessing private attribute prediction. Table 7 shows the VISPR attribute split used,
851   which we have adopted from Wu et al. (2020); Dave et al. (2022b); Fioresi et al. (2023).

853
      Proposed NTU Bias Evaluation Details More formal details for the creation of the proposed per-
854
      ceived gender NTU bias protocol are described here. While the original dataset is balanced in terms
855   of scene and actor, the distribution of actor/video counts are not balanced with respect to perceived
856   gender. To properly evaluate bias mitigation, it is essential to ensure that there are no performance
857   differences stemming from the larger number of male subjects and training videos. The subject
858   IDs are used to first restructure the dataset in an effort to maximize fairness across the gender sub-
859   groups. As such, within themselves, the train and test sets should contain both an even number
860   of male and female subjects AND an even number of videos per action. Formally, lets take the
861   set of subjects S = {si }N  i=1 , where NS is the number of subjects in the dataset. For each sub-
                                    S

863   Nm = Nf = N2S , where Nm and Nf are the number of male and female subjects, respectively. Us-
      ing the above notation with DIID abbreviated to D, we define Dm = {(xi , yi , gi ) ∈ D|gi = male}

      B.1   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
899
900   Each video encoder fE model is left unchanged from the original implementation. The fT classifier
901   head is a simple linear layer Linear(d, N ), where d is the feature vector dimension of fE and
902   N is the number of classes in Dreco . For the private attribute prediction task, a 2-layer MLP is
903   used: Linear(d, d) → Linear(d, 7) with a ReLU activation after the first layer. For the
904   fA AAM, we ablate different architecture styles (see Table 8). To break it down, we tried a LoRA-
905   based adapter, standard MLPs of different depths, and self-attention based adapters of different
906   depths. The LoRA adapter features a simple downsample-upsample architecture formulated by:
907   h+ Linear(d, 256) → (256, d), where h is the output feature embedding from fE . Each
908
      MLP layer is composed of a Linear(d, d) followed by a ReLU activation and a BatchNorm1D
      layer, and dropout with a probability of 0.1 during training. The self-attention layers are standard
909
      MulitheadAttention blocks with dim d and 8 heads by default.
910
911
912   B.2   I NPUTS AND AUGMENTATIONS
913
914   All inputs consist of 16 frame clips sampled with consecutive frames, resized to spatial resolution of
915   224 × 224. For training, only random resized crop and random horizontal flip with probability 50%
916   are utilized. In validation, the short edge is resized to 256, then a center crop of 224 × 224 is taken.
917   Standard ImageNet Krizhevsky et al. (2012) mean and standard deviation based normalization is
      applied in both settings. The input and augmentation protocol is consistent for every fE .

      B.3   T RAINING D ETAILS AND H YPERPARAMETERS
919
920   Each AAM variation is trained using an ℓ1 loss to reconstruct the input features for 100 epochs with
921   the AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2017) optimizer and a learning rate of 2e-5. Kinetics400 Carreira
922   & Zisserman (2017) features are used as the train-test set. Privacy evaluation is carried out using
923   supervised training of the predictor MLP for 100 epochs at a learning rate of 1e-3. A learning rate
924   scheduler is based on the loss plateau where it decreases the learning rate to 1/5th.
925   For anonymization training, the base learning is 1e-4 for both fA and fT ∗ , corresponding to a batch
926   size of 512, scaled when necessary according to the linear scaling rule Goyal et al. (2017a). By
927   default, ωLC = 100, ωT = 1, and ωB = 1 (Main Eq. 8). The anonymization training is carried out
928   for 100 epochs.

933   a weighted overall performance metric. Specifically, for each evaluated model, we take the raw
934
      performance scores of each video understanding task and add this with an inverse of the privacy
      scores, then divide across tasks. The overall score function is defined as follows:

939   where S(T ) represents the performance on a given task. To prioritize the importance of the privacy
940   task in comparison to the other tasks collectively, it is assigned a weight of 3. This weighting
941   strategy ensures that the significance of achieving the privacy objective is on par with the aggregate
942   significance of all other tasks combined. This scoring metric is used in Main Table 1.

       Since we are using a completely frozen video encoder model fE , the latent feature embeddings can
       be precomputed for a much faster training process. In this case, only validation augmentations are
1143
       used, and each video clip is only ran through the model forward pass once. There is flexibility in clip
1144
       choice and skip rate. In this work, we opt for a simple skip rate of 1 (consecutive frames), and take
1145   all non-overlapping sequential clips for each video. The computed embeddings are saved for each
1146   video, and a random clip is sampled during training time. The same evenly-spaced 5 video clips are
1147   used for validation. Table 14 shows a comparison between using the raw videos and precomputed
1148   features. Due to the use of weak augmentations in the raw videos, we see an improvement over
1149   using the precomputed. However, using the precomputed features only requires a single forward
1150   pass over the dataset, which takes 4 minutes (HMDB51), then only 1.4 minutes for training.
