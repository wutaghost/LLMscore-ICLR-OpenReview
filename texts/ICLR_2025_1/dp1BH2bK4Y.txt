                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            The Chain-of-Thought (CoT) paradigm has become a pivotal method for solving
013
               complex problems. However, its application to intricate, domain-specific tasks
               remains challenging, as large language models (LLMs) often struggle to accu-
014
               rately decompose these tasks and, even when decomposition is correct, fail to
015
               execute the subtasks effectively. This paper introduces the Re-TASK framework,
016            a novel theoretical model that Revisits LLM Tasks from cApability, Skill, and
017            Knowledge perspectives, drawing on the principles of Bloom’s Taxonomy and
018            Knowledge Space Theory. While CoT offers a workflow perspective on tasks,
019            the Re-TASK framework introduces a Chain-of-Learning view, illustrating how
020            tasks and their corresponding subtasks depend on various capability items. Each
021            capability item is further dissected into its constituent aspects of knowledge and
022            skills. Our framework reveals that many CoT failures in domain-specific tasks
023            stem from insufficient knowledge or inadequate skill adaptation. In response,
024
               we combine CoT with the Re-TASK framework and implement a carefully de-
               signed Re-TASK prompting strategy to improve task performance. Specifically,
025
               we identify core capability items linked to tasks and subtasks, then strengthen
026
               these capabilities through targeted knowledge injection and skill adaptation. We
027            validate the Re-TASK framework on three datasets across the law, finance, and
028            mathematics domains, achieving significant improvements over the baseline mod-
029            els. Notably, our approach yields a remarkable 44.42% improvement with the Yi-
030            1.5-9B model and a 33.08% improvement with the Llama3-Chinese-8b on the le-
031            gal dataset. These experimental results confirm the effectiveness of the Re-TASK
032            framework, demonstrating substantial enhancements in both the performance and
033            applicability of LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

      As the scale of large language models (LLMs), such as the GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini series
      (Achiam et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Team et al., 2023), as well as their open-source counterparts
040
      like the LLaMA, Mistral, and Qwen series (Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Bai et al.,
041
      2023), continues to increase, their general capabilities in natural language processing (NLP) tasks
042   have shown substantial improvements. Despite these advancements, however, these models often
043   struggle with complex reasoning tasks, particularly those that are domain-specific. The Chain-of-
044   Thought (CoT) technique (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023)
045   has emerged as a promising paradigm by decomposing complex tasks into a series of subtasks in a
046   divide-and-conquer manner. Yet, the application of CoT to domain-specific tasks faces significant
047   challenges in both task decomposition (Kambhampati, 2024) and subtask execution (Lightman et al.,
048   2024) due to a lack of domain knowledge and specialized capabilities.
049   The idea that an individual’s capabilities directly influence task performance is well supported by ed-
050   ucational theories, notably Bloom’s Taxonomy (Bloom, 2010) and Knowledge Space Theory (KST)
051   (Doignon & Falmagne, 1985). Bloom’s Taxonomy outlines how educational objectives are achieved
052   through structured instructional activities, each involving specific knowledge and cognitive pro-
053   cesses. Similarly, KST emphasizes the sequential dependencies between learning items, forming
      “learning pathways” that guide learners from foundational knowledge to mastery.

075   Building on these insights into the Chain-of-Learning, we introduce the Re-TASK framework, which
076   revisits LLM tasks through the lenses of capability, skill, and knowledge (see Figure 1(b)). This
077   framework posits that the successful completion of tasks1 depends on sequentially mastering multi-
078   ple capability items, with each item further dissected into its constituent aspects of knowledge and
079   skills. The distinction between knowledge acquisition and skill application also aligns with the per-
080
      spective of Bengio & Hu (2023), who argue that effective reasoning requires both a robust world
      knowledge model and a powerful inference engine capable of generating solutions consistent with
081
      that model.
082
083   Our framework posits that failures in the CoT paradigm, particularly in task decomposition and
084   subtask execution, can be attributed to a lack of corresponding capabilities due to either insufficient
085   knowledge or inadequate knowledge-skill adaptation (or skill adaptation for short). First, LLMs may
086
      lack relevant knowledge because of limited access to proprietary data or issues related to data time-
      liness. Second, even when the knowledge is available, LLMs often struggle to effectively apply it to
087
      solve complex tasks, leading to suboptimal performance. Techniques such as Retrieval-Augmented
088
      Generation (Lewis et al., 2020) can inject knowledge into the context, but models may still under-
089   perform due to inadequate skill adaptation on utilizing the retrieved knowledge, ultimately resulting
090   in task failures.
091
092
      To address these issues, we propose integrating the CoT paradigm with the Re-TASK framework
      to enhance LLM performance, as illustrated in Figure 1. Specifically, we identify core capability
093
      items linked to the overall task and its corresponding subtasks, then strengthen these capabilities
094
      through targeted knowledge injection and skill adaptation using a deliberately designed prompting
095   strategy, Re-TASK prompting (see Figure 2). The capability items represent demonstrations of
096   knowledge-skill adaptation, such as conceptual knowledge understanding and procedural knowledge
097   applying. Notably, knowledge itself can be treated as a special capability item, as in the case of
098   knowledge injection through knowledge recalling or retrieving. We then adopt in-context learning
099   (ICL) techniques (Brown et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2022) to enhance the corresponding capabilities
100   by carefully arranging these demonstrations within the prompt.
101   We conduct comprehensive experiments with open-source LLMs to evaluate the effectiveness of the
102   Re-TASK framework in enhancing CoT performance across the law, finance, and mathematics do-
103   main tasks. By incorporating appropriate capability items that inject relevant domain knowledge or
104   improve skill adaptation, we observe substantial improvements in task performance. Furthermore,
105   we extend our experiments to include LLMs of varying scales, demonstrating that while model ca-
106   pabilities generally increase with scale, our framework can still effectively boost their performance.

125   2.1   E DUCATIONAL T HEORIES
126
127   Bloom’s Taxonomy (Bloom, 2010) provides a foundational framework that links learning objec-
128
      tives with instructional activities. It posits that achieving learning goals requires the completion of
      multiple interconnected activities, categorized along two key dimensions: knowledge and cognitive
129
      processes. The knowledge dimension outlines four types of knowledge: factual, conceptual, pro-
130
      cedural, and metacognitive. The cognitive process dimension establishes a hierarchy of cognitive
131   skills, encompassing six levels—remember, understand, apply, analyze, evaluate, and create—each
132   linked to specific cognitive processes, totaling 19 distinct actions. By integrating these dimensions,
133   Bloom’s Taxonomy serves as a comprehensive guide for educators to design curricula and instruc-
134   tional strategies that foster deeper understanding and encourage higher-order thinking in students.
135
      Knowledge Space Theory (KST) (Doignon & Falmagne, 1985; Falmagne et al., 2013; Cosyn et al.,
136
      2021) offers a mathematical framework for modeling and assessing learners’ knowledge within a
137   specific domain. It identifies various knowledge states, defined as sets of problems or concepts
138   that a learner can successfully solve or understand. The entirety of these possible knowledge states
139   forms the knowledge structure, which delineates the relationships among different states. Learning
140   pathways are the routes learners can take to transition from one knowledge state to another. By
141   utilizing KST, educators can design effective educational interventions and personalized learning
142   experiences, optimizing the learning process for each individual learner.
143   Building on these foundational educational theories, we propose the Re-TASK framework, which
144   elucidates the dependence of tasks on various capability items. Each capability item is further broken
145   down into its constituent aspects of knowledge and skills, highlighting the intricate relationships that
146   contribute to task performance.
147
148   2.2   K NOWLEDGE AND S KILLS IN LLM S
149
150   Several studies have explored LLMs from the perspectives of knowledge and skill. KoLA (Yu et al.,
151   2023) emphasizes the importance of world knowledge for LLMs and establishes a knowledge-
152   oriented evaluation benchmark. In its approach to ability modeling, KoLA simplifies and selects
153
      from Bloom’s learning theories to form four levels of knowledge-capability assessment: knowledge
      memorization, knowledge understanding, knowledge applying, and knowledge creating. Skill-it
154
      (Chen et al., 2024) posits that language models naturally acquire a sequence of skills from training
155
      data and formalizes the notion of a skill and an ordered set of skills in terms of associated data, dif-
156   ferentiating this approach from traditional curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009), which focuses
157   on training models using progressively difficult examples. In continual pre-training experiments,
158   Skill-it’s ordered learning of skills achieves faster convergence of validation loss compared to ran-
159   dom sampling. RA-DIT (Lin et al., 2023) introduces a lightweight fine-tuning methodology that
160   improves retrieval-augmented language models by enhancing both the relevance of retrieved knowl-
161   edge and its effective utilization, marking a specialized form of knowledge and skill enhancement.
      MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) serves as a benchmark designed to measure the possession of world


                                                         3

162
      knowledge and problem-solving abilities. Reflecting on these developments, Bengio & Hu (2023)
163   have emphasized the integration of the world model and the inference machine in current LLMs.
164   They suggest that to reason effectively, a robust world knowledge model and a powerful inference
165   machine are necessary, advocating for their separation and simultaneous development to enhance
166   reasoning capabilities.
167
168   2.3       P ROMPTING S TRATEGIES
169
170   Various prompting strategies have been proposed to enhance model performance in solving complex,
171   domain-specific tasks. One prominent approach is CoT (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022;
172
      Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023), which decomposes a complex task into simpler subtasks,
      utilizing a divide-and-conquer strategy. Another notable method is Retrieval-Augmented Generation
173
      (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2024), which integrates neural language models with retrieval
174
      mechanisms to produce contextually relevant outputs by fetching knowledge from external sources.
175   In-Context Learning (ICL) techniques (Brown et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2022) represent a significant
176   advancement in prompting; ICL utilizes examples within the prompt itself, enabling the model to
177   learn from context without the need for explicit retraining or fine-tuning.
178
      In contrast, we propose Re-TASK prompting, which integrates CoT prompting with the Re-TASK
179
      framework. This approach leverages ICL techniques to enhance corresponding capabilities by care-
180
      fully arranging demonstrations of capability items within the prompt, ultimately leading to improved
181   overall task performance.

      3.1       R E -TASK F RAMEWORK
187
      We begin by defining several key concepts within our framework: tasks, capability items, knowl-
188   edge, and skills. We then elucidate how these elements interconnect to establish the structured
189   Re-TASK framework, as illustrated in Figure 1(b). These concepts parallel Bloom’s Taxonomy,
190   where educational objectives—comparable to tasks in our framework—are systematically achieved
191   through structured instructional activities, analogous to capability items. Each instructional activity
192   involves the acquisition of specific types of knowledge and engages distinct cognitive processes,
193   thereby facilitating knowledge-skill adaptation (or skill adaptation for short). The successful com-
194   pletion of an educational objective, or task, depends on mastering various capability items, each
195   developed through these instructional activities. Knowledge Space Theory (KST) further reinforces
196
      this structured approach by highlighting sequential dependencies among learning items (akin to ca-
      pability items in our framework) and establishing “learning pathways”.
197
198   Definition 1. (Task) A task T is defined as a specific objective that LLMs are designed to achieve,
199
      characterized by a mapping from input x to output y, facilitated by a task instruction I and an op-
      tional context ctx. Formally, this relationship is expressed as T(ctx; I; x) = y, where the semicolon
200
      denotes concatenation of inputs.
201
202   The optional context ctx can be leveraged for knowledge injection or skill adaptation. By identifying
203   corresponding capability items and incorporating a list of capability item demonstrations into ctx,
204   this method aligns closely with in-context learning. In the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) paradigm, a task
205   can be decomposed into a series of subtasks.
206   Definition 2. (Knowledge) A knowledge point K is defined as a text segment containing domain-
207   specific knowledge that is essential for the performance of a task/subtask. In the context of LLMs,
208   the knowledge K can also be implicit knowledge encoded within the model’s parameters.
209
      According to Bloom’s Taxonomy, we consider three types of domain knowledge: factual, concep-
210
      tual, and procedural2 . Each type plays a distinct role in task execution, contributing differently to
211
      the LLM’s ability to process and respond to task-specific demands.
212
213
      Definition 3. (Skill) A skill S corresponds to the cognitive processes in Bloom’s Taxonomy and is
      developed through related instructional activities, including knowledge recalling/retrieving, under-
214
      standing, applying, and others.
215
          2
              Metacognitive knowledge is beyond the scope of LLMs and is not our primary focus.


                                                             4

216
      Definition 4. (Capability Item) A capability item C, corresponding to the concept of instructional
217   activities in Bloom’s Taxonomy, is a specific exercise or demonstration designed to guide LLMs
218   in applying a particular skill S to the relevant knowledge K, thereby facilitating knowledge-skill
219   adaptation.
220
221   Successfully completing a task T requires the sequential mastery of multiple capability items. These
222   items can be conceptualized as a chain of learning, where dependencies among them are clearly de-
223   fined. Figure 1(c) illustrates these dependencies. A task generally involves overall procedural knowl-
224   edge (C01 ), with its resolution corresponding to a capability item (C02 ) that applies this knowledge
225   in a manner akin to a CoT process. This procedural knowledge is further segmented into steps (i.e.,
226   subtasks), each linked to specific knowledge and involving different capability items Cij .
227   Note that the knowledge K can be treated as a special capability item with the default skill of
228   knowledge recalling or retrieving. Consequently, the task and its subtasks depend on two types of
229   capability items: knowledge recalling (the knowledge itself) and knowledge-skill adaptation (e.g.,
230   knowledge understanding and applying).
231
232
      3.2   C APABILITY I TEM C ONSTRUCTION
233
234
      To effectively improve the performance of LLMs in domain-specific tasks, identifying key capability
235   items is crucial. Our primary focus is on retrieving relevant knowledge (i.e., knowledge injection)
236   and enhancing the understanding of conceptual knowledge along with the applying of procedural
237   knowledge (i.e., knowledge-skill adaptation). These capability items are systematically designed
238   to target specific aspects of task performance and are integral to the successful implementation of
239   our Re-TASK framework. Below, we present several exemplary capability items that illustrate this
240   strategic approach:
241   1) Knowledge retrieval: This involves identifying the relevant knowledge points for a given task or
242   subtask and retrieving them from external sources. It may also include recalling internal knowledge
243   points stored within the LLM.
244
      2) Instances of conceptual knowledge: This involves understanding conceptual knowledge in real-
245
      world situations and providing an example that illustrates the conceptual knowledge. This example
246
      can help deepen understanding of the conceptual knowledge.
247
248   3) Execution of procedural knowledge: This capability is crucial for tasks that require following a
249   set of ordered steps or procedures, such as technical troubleshooting, recipe preparation, or complex
250
      calculations. This capability item is an applying case of using procedural knowledge.
251   It is noteworthy that identifying the capability items associated with a task is a complex undertaking.
252   For each given task, we can either manually pinpoint the relevant knowledge points and subsequently
253   identify the corresponding skills that facilitate task resolution, or leverage LLMs to automate the
254   entire process.

      3.3   R E -TASK P ROMPTING
257
258   By combining CoT with the Re-TASK framework, we can identify core capability items linked to the
259   overall task and its corresponding subtasks, subsequently strengthening these capabilities to enhance
260   subtask performance and, ultimately, overall task performance. Specifically, we enhance the capa-
261   bility items through targeted knowledge injection and skill adaptation using a deliberately designed
262   prompting strategy known as Re-TASK prompting, as illustrated in Figure 2. The capability items
263   serve as demonstrations of knowledge-skill adaptation, including knowledge recalling/retrieving,
264   knowledge understanding, and knowledge applying.
265   We carefully arrange the demonstrations within the prompt according to their dependencies, fol-
266   lowing the chain of learning. Specifically, we sequence the capability items Cij for each subtask i.
267   If multiple items are associated with the same subtask, we prioritize the knowledge itself (i.e., the
268   capability item of knowledge retrieval) first, followed by more advanced items such as knowledge
269   understanding and applying. Finally, we include the overall procedural knowledge C01 along with
      its applying C02 at the end of the prompt.

352   4.3    C ONSTRUCTION OF C APABILITY I TEMS
353
354   We utilized large language models (LLMs) to assist in decomposing tasks and generating the ca-
355   pability items involved in Re-TASK. First, we predefined the capability types for each task based
356   on the definition in Section 3.2 and then leveraged larger LLMs, which are presumed to possess
357
      sufficient domain knowledge, to automatically generate these items.
358   We began by employing LLMs to decompose the tasks, which resulted in the identification of the
359   overall procedural knowledge (C01 ). Next, we instructed the LLMs to create a CoT demonstration
360   using the generated knowledge as a knowledge applying capability item (C02 ). Task decomposition
361   also guides the creation of capability items for each subtask. For each subtask i, we instructed the
362   LLMs to generate relevant conceptual or procedural knowledge (Ci1 ). For conceptual knowledge,
363
      we requested illustrative examples to enhance understanding, while for procedural knowledge, we
      sought CoT demonstrations to illustrate effective applying (Ci2 ).
364
365   For financial tasks, we followed the complete procedure to generate capability items for both the
366   overall task and its corresponding subtasks. In contrast, for other tasks, where the subtasks are
367   relatively straightforward and do not require complex knowledge, we only generated capability items
368   for the overall tasks.

477
      In this paper, we introduced the Re-TASK framework, a novel approach that revisits LLM tasks
478
      through the lenses of capability, skill, and knowledge, aiming to address the limitations of the Chain-
479
      of-Thought (CoT) paradigm in complex, domain-specific tasks. By integrating Re-TASK with CoT,
480   we systematically enhanced the performance of LLMs through targeted knowledge injection and
481   skill adaptation using a structured prompting strategy, Re-TASK prompting. Our extensive experi-
482   ments across the law, finance, and mathematics domains demonstrated that the Re-TASK framework
483   significantly improves task performance, achieving substantial gains over baseline models and con-
484   firming the framework’s potential to enhance LLM capabilities across diverse domains. Our research
485   opens new avenues for practitioners to deepen their understanding, evaluation, and improvement of
      LLMs.
