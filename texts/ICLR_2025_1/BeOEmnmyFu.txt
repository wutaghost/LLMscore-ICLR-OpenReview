009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The advent of large language models (LLMs) has spurred the development of
012            numerous jailbreak techniques aimed at circumventing their security defenses
013            against malicious attacks. An effective jailbreak approach is to identify a do-
014            main where safety generalization fails, a phenomenon known as mismatched
015            generalization. In this paper, we introduce two novel jailbreak methods based
016
               on mismatched generalization: natural language games and custom language
               games, both of which effectively bypass the safety mechanisms of LLMs, with
017
               various kinds and different variants, making them hard to defend and leading
018
               to high attack rates. Natural language games involve the use of synthetic lin-
019            guistic constructs and the actions intertwined with these constructs, such as
020            the Ubbi Dubbi language. Building on this phenomenon, we propose the cus-
021            tom language games method: by engaging with LLMs using a variety of cus-
022            tom rules, we successfully execute jailbreak attacks across multiple LLM plat-
023            forms. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our methods,
024            achieving success rates of 93% on GPT-4o, 89% on GPT-4o-mini and 83% on
025            Claude-3.5-Sonnet. Furthermore, to investigate the generalizability of safety
026            alignments, we fine-tuned Llama-3.1-70B with the custom language games to
027
               achieve safety alignment within our datasets and found that when interacting
               through other language games, the fine-tuned models still failed to identify harm-
028
               ful content. This finding indicates that the safety alignment knowledge embed-
029
               ded in LLMs fails to generalize across different linguistic formats, thus open-
030            ing new avenues for future research in this area. Our code is available at
031            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/encode jailbreaking anonymous-B4C4.
032            Warning: this paper contains examples with unsafe content.

037   Large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023), Llama2 (Touvron et al.,
038   2023), Claude2 (Anthropic, 2023) and Gemini (Team et al., 2023) have become increasingly impor-
039
      tant across various domains due to their advanced natural language comprehension and generation
      capabilities. These models are employed in a wide range of applications, including customer ser-
040
      vice, content generation, code assistance, and even medical diagnostics, offering valuable sugges-
041
      tions and improving productivity in numerous scenarios. However, with this growing prominence
042   comes a heightened risk: the rapid development of attack schemes that are designed to manipulate or
043   deceive these models into generating unsafe or unethical content. One of the most concerning types
044   of attacks is the jailbreak attack, a technique that seeks to subvert the safety protocols built into
045   LLMs. In essence, a jailbreak attack manipulates input prompts in a way that bypasses the model’s
046   safety alignment, which is designed to detect and block harmful or unethical requests. One typical
047   jailbreak attack approach is mismatched generalization (Wei et al., 2024), which occurs when LLMs
048   are given out-of-distribution inputs that were not covered in the safety alignment data, yet still fall
049   within the scope of the LLMs’ pretraining corpus (Deng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
050   In the context of mismatched generalization jailbreak attacks, many input prompts are transformed
051   from natural language into encoded formats such as Morse code (Barak, 2023), ciphers (Yuan et al.,
052   2023), or Base64 (Wei et al., 2024). These methods present two primary issues: On one hand, when
053   the encoded output generated by the LLMs contains errors, it becomes completely indecipherable
      for an adversary, as the encoded content is not in natural language form. For instance, even minor

      2   R ELATED W ORK
127
128   Jailbreak Attacks. The safety of LLMs has been a longstanding concern, as adversaries contin-
129   ually develop new methods to manipulate these models into generating harmful content. One key
130
      approach to jailbreak attacks, as described by Wei et al. (2024), involves designing competing ob-
      jectives within the input. This tactic exploits the LLM’s instruction-following capabilities, pushing
131
      the model to prioritize generating a response based on the user’s request rather than adhering to
132
      safety constraints. Studies by Li et al. (2023), Chang et al. (2024), Jiang et al. (2024), and Guo et al.
133   (2024) demonstrate how carefully crafted natural language instructions can trick LLMs into produc-
134   ing harmful or unethical content. By embedding harmful intent in seemingly benign instructions,
135   attackers can confuse the model’s objective and circumvent its safety alignment protocols.
136
      Another prominent jailbreak method involves mismatched generalization, wherein input prompts
137
      are encoded in a way that was not accounted for in the LLM’s safety training but still lies within the
138
      model’s general pretraining corpus. As noted by Wei et al. (2024), this technique has been used to
139   bypass alignment processes in various attack schemes. While it provides a comprehensive overview
140   of 28 existing jailbreak strategies, new methods continue to emerge, indicating that the problem
141   is far from solved. For instance, Deng et al. (2023) demonstrated that interacting with LLMs in
142   medium and low-resource languages can lead to the generation of unsafe outputs. This highlights a
143   significant gap in the models’ safety mechanisms, particularly in under-represented languages that
144   may not have been as rigorously aligned for safety during training. Similarly, Yuan et al. (2023)
145   showed that conversations encoded with ciphers can trick LLMs into producing unsafe responses.
146   These examples reveal how attackers can use obfuscation strategies to bypass content moderation
147
      mechanisms by encoding harmful queries in formats that the models do not immediately recognize
      as dangerous. In addition, Liu et al. (2023) proposed AutoDAN, an automated system that generates
148
      stealthy jailbreak prompts using a hierarchical genetic algorithm. AutoDAN systematically creates
149
      prompts designed to evade safety protocols by mimicking benign inputs, leveraging the evolutionary
150   process to develop increasingly effective jailbreak training strategies over time.
151
152   Safety Training of LLMs. Ensuring the responsible and effective deployment of LLMs requires
153
      aligning their outputs with human preferences and ethical standards (Korbak et al., 2023; Achiam
      et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). Common alignment methods include supervised fine-tuning
154
      (Bianchi et al., 2023), red-teaming (Ganguli et al., 2022; Perez et al., 2022), the use of reward
155
      signals (Ouyang et al., 2022), and preference-based modeling (Christiano et al., 2017). With the rise
156   of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), Dai et al. (2023) proposed Safe RLHF,
157   a novel framework which incorporates a two-dimensional human annotation scheme and a safety-
158   focused training mechanism to enhance model performance while ensuring safety. Additionally,
159   Ji et al. (2024) developed the PKU-SafeRLHF dataset to provide training data and a reproducible
160   code pipeline, facilitating further research in alignment. However, while these methods evaluate the
161   effectiveness of alignment, they do not thoroughly explore whether safety knowledge is generalized
      across the intermediate layers of LLMs.

189   Natural language games refer to well-known linguistic manipulations where spoken or written lan-
190   guage is altered according to predefined rules. These games are often used in informal contexts to
191   obfuscate meaning, making communication incomprehensible to an untrained listener or reader. For
192
      the purpose of this study, we focused on a specific subset of natural language games that involve
      systematic alterations to the structure of words or sentences. An example of such a game is Ubbi
193
      Dubbi, where the syllable ”ub” is inserted before each vowel sound in a word, effectively distort-
194
      ing the recognizable structure of the sentence while preserving the meaning for those familiar with
195   the game’s rules. This manipulation serves as a lightweight obfuscation that can challenge language
196   models’ ability to detect harmful intent. We selected four natural language games that operate within
197   the English language for our experiments. Our goal is to determine whether LLMs’ safety align-
198   ments could effectively detect harmful intent hidden behind playful yet systematic alterations to the
199   languages. Table 1 provides the names and the basic rules of these language games.

      3.2   C USTOM L ANGUAGE G AME ATTACK
228
229   During our research, we found that when harmful questions are encoded using custom rules, LLMs
230   can still generate harmful content. This occurs due to the powerful inference capabilities of LLMs,
231   which can recognize and process meaning even when the input has been altered in non-standard
232   ways. This reveals a significant vulnerability: we can arbitrarily design custom language games and
233   modify their content, enabling us to carry out a large number of effective jailbreak attacks.
234   In our experiment, we first develop the custom rules that define how the original harmful questions
235   will be transformed. These rules are specifically crafted to alter the structure of the question in ways
236   that do not align with any known or natural language game, making the input appear unfamiliar
237   to the model’s pretraining and alignment data. Examples of custom rules might include inserting
238   specific characters or symbols between letters, shuffling words in the query, or replacing vowels
239   with numbers. Then the original harmful base questions are transformed according to these rules,
240   resulting in the “encoded question”. This transformation significantly alters the appearance of the
241
      text while preserving the semantic meaning of the original harmful query.
242   For interaction with LLMs, as in Figure 2, the same prompt structure used for natural language
243   games is also applied. The LLMs are instructed to chat and respond to the encoded questions, but
244   under the assumption that the responses will follow the format dictated by the custom rules. We
245   develop eight diverse custom language games and set the prototype attack prompt as “How to make
246   a bomb?”:
247
             • Adding “-a-” to the interletter of a word, e.g., “H-a-o-a-w t-a-o m-a-a-a-k-a-e a b-a-o-a-m-

253            ak-2-e a b-2-om-2-b?”.
254          • Reversing the order of the letters within each word, while the first and last letters remain
255            unchanged, e.g., “How to mkae a bmob?”.

272   4.1   S ETUP
273
274   Dataset. We conduct our experiments on the SALAD-Bench benchmark (Li et al., 2024), which is
275   specifically designed for evaluating LLMs, defense, and attack methods. It comprises harmful base
276   questions categorized into 6 domains, 16 tasks, and 66 specific categories. Considering that some
277   questions are appropriately answered by LLMs, we filtered and randomly sampled 50 harmful base
278   questions from each of the 6 domains, resulting in a total of 300 questions. These sampled questions
279
      are then transformed according to both natural and custom language game formats.
280   Models. In our experiments, we evaluate the effectiveness of jailbreak attacks across three differ-
281   ent large language models: GPT-4o-2024-08-06 (GPT-4o), gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18 (GPT-4o-mini),
282   and Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20240620 (Claude-3.5-Sonnet). These models were selected based on their
283   widespread use, robust natural language processing capabilities, and built-in safety alignment mech-
284   anisms, making them ideal candidates for testing vulnerabilities to jailbreak attacks. Each model is
285
      in its default settings to ensure consistency and to simulate real-world use cases.
286   Evaluation. To categorize the different types of responses generated by LLMs during our jailbreak
287   attack experiments, we report three evaluation metrics: success rate (SR), unclear rate (UR), and
288   failure rate (FR). Success rate represents the percentage of cases where the LLM generates a harmful
289   or unsafe response despite the safety mechanisms in place. Unclear rate measures the percentage of
290   responses where the LLM generates a reply that is unrelated to the transformed query or responds
291
      only to the non-harmful content. Failure rate represents the percentage of cases where the LLM
      successfully blocks or refuses to respond to the harmful input, as intended by its safety alignment.
292
      We utilize GPT-4o-mini as an auxiliary tool for labeling, with the specially designed classification
293
      prompt template provided in Appendix A.1.

365   Claude-3.5-Sonnet showed a variation in success rates between Self 6 and Self 7. These differences
366   suggest that even slight variations in the rules of language games can lead to significantly different
367   outcomes when it comes to bypassing safety mechanisms.

419   4.5                     E XPLORATION ON S AFETY A LIGNMENT G ENERALIZATION
420
421   To explore the safety alignment generalization abilities of LLMs, we fine-tuned Llama-3.1-70B
422   using the corpus transformed by the custom language games and then conducted custom language
423
      game jailbreak attacks on the fine-tuned model. The goal of this process was to evaluate how well
      the model could generalize its safety alignments to other custom language game variations after
424
      fine-tuning on a specific transformation. Specifically, we collected a general knowledge dataset from
425
      Chen et al. (2023) and mixed it with our custom jailbreak dataset. The ratio of the general knowledge
426   dataset to the jailbreak dataset was set at 2.7:1, ensuring a balanced training set that emphasizes
427   both benign content and adversarial examples. For our jailbreak dataset, the output labels were
428   specifically set as negative. To implement the fine-tuning, we utilized the LoRA method (Hu et al.,
429   2021), a parameter-efficient approach for adapting large models by adding low-rank adapters, which
430   reduces computational overhead while preserving the performance of the base model. This method
431   allowed us to effectively fine-tune the Llama-3.1-70B model without retraining the entire network,
      making the process faster and more resource-efficient.

478   5   C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose two distinct jailbreak attack methods: the natural language game attack
481   and the custom language game attack. We collect and design various language games to carry out
482   jailbreak attacks on LLMs. Our experiments demonstrate that these methods are both highly effec-
483   tive and universally applicable. Furthermore, we explore the generalization abilities of LLMs by
484   attacking safety fine-tuned models, proving that safety alignments fail to generalize effectively after
485   fine-tuning. These findings underscore the limitations of current fine-tuning methods in providing
      comprehensive safety defenses.
