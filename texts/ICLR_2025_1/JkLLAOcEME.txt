009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The growing use of deep learning necessitates efficient network design and de-
012            ployment, making neural predictors vital for estimating attributes such as accuracy
013            and latency. Recently, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and transformers have
014            shown promising performance in representing neural architectures. However, each
015            method has its disadvantages. GNNs lack the capabilities to represent complicated
016            features, while transformers face poor generalization when the depth of architecture
017            grows. To mitigate the above problems, we rethink neural architecture topology
018
               and show that sibling nodes are pivotal while overlooked in previous research. Thus
               we propose a novel predictor leveraging the strengths of GNNs and transformers
019
               to learn the enhanced topology. We introduce a novel token mixer that consid-
020
               ers siblings, and a new channel mixer named bidirectional graph isomorphism
021            feed-forward network. Our approach consistently achieves promising performance
022            in both accuracy and latency prediction, providing valuable insights for learning
023            Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) topology. The code will be released.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep neural networks have demonstrated remarkable success across various applications, high-
029   lighting the significance of neural architecture design. Designing neural architectures can be quite
030   resource-intensive. Evaluating the performance of a model necessitates training on large datasets.
031   Measuring its inference latency and throughput involves multiple steps such as compilation, de-
032   ployment, inference, and latency evaluation on various hardware platforms, incurring substantial
033
      human effort and resources. One strategy to mitigate these challenges is to predict network attributes
      with machine learning predictors. By feeding the network structure and hyperparameters into these
034
      predictors, valuable attributes of the network can be estimated with just a single feedforward pass, e.g.,
035
      accuracy on a validation set or inference times on specific hardware configurations. This predictive
036   approach has been successfully applied in various tasks including neural architecture search (Xu
037   et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2023; 2024) and hardware
038   deployment (Zhang et al., 2021; Kaufman et al., 2021; Dudziak et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Yi et al.,
039   2023; 2024), yielding promising outcomes in improving the efficiency and effectiveness of network
040   design.
041
      Previous neural predictors model the neural architecture as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) (Wen
042   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2020; Dudziak et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Dong et al.,
043   2022; Luo et al., 2023) and utilize Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) or Transformers to extract
044   neural architecture representation. GNNs have emerged as an intuitive solution for learning graph
045   representations (Wen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2020; Dudziak et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
046   2022), which leverage the graph Laplacian and integrate adjacency information to learn the graph
047   topology. GNN-based predictors show strong generalization ability, yet their performance may
048   not be optimal. This is attributed to the structural bias in the message-passing mechanism, which
049   typically relies solely on adjacency information. As illustrated in Figure. 1(a) and (b), GCNs (Kipf
050
      & Welling, 2016) aggregate the forward and backward adjacent nodes without discrimination, and
      GATs (Veličković et al., 2018) aggregate them with dynamic weights. Both of them are limited to
051
      adjacent information.
052
053   With the recent development of transformers, various transformer-based frameworks have been
      introduced (Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2023; 2024). Transformers have strengths in global modeling and

083

084   depth of the input architecture increases to hundreds of layers. For example, NAR-Former (Yi et al.,
085   2023; 2024) has illustrated that transformer predictors with global attention struggle in deep network
086   latency prediction, leading to worse performance than GNNs (Liu et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2024).
087
      To study a more effective neural predictor, we rethink the DAG topology and show that the commonly
088
      used topological information is not suitable for the neural architecture representation. Most of recent
089
      works focus on modeling the relationship of preceding and succeeding operations (Dong et al., 2022;
090   Luo et al., 2023). However, it is essential to recognize the importance of “sibling nodes”, which share
091   a common parent or child node with the current node as shown in Figure. 1(e). They often exhibit
092   strong connections to the current nodes in neural architecture representation. For example, in the
093   accuracy prediction task, parallel branches may extract complementary features, hence enhancing
094   overall model performance. Furthermore, operations that share the same parent or child node can
095   be executed simultaneously, potentially reducing inference latency. On the contrary, long-range
096   dependency might not be crucial, given that features typically propagate node-by-node within the
097   architecture. Previous methods have not explicitly leveraged sibling cues.
098   Based on the analysis above, we introduce a new model for neural architecture representation, named
099   Neural Network transFormer (NN-Former). It leverages the strengths of GNNs and transformers,
100   exhibiting good generalization and high performance. For the token mixing module, we utilize a
101   self-attention mechanism of transformers to extract dynamic weights for capturing complex features.
102   We explicitly learn the adjacency and sibling nodes’ features to enhance the topological information.
103   For the channel mixing module, we use a bidirectional graph isomorphism feedforward network. It
104
      learns strong graph topology information such that the position encoding is no longer necessary.
105   Extensive experiments reveal that 1) our approach surpasses existing methods in both accuracy
106   prediction and latency prediction, demonstrating expressivity and generalization ability, and 2) our
107   method has good scalability on both cell-structured architectures and complete neural networks that
      have hundreds of operations. To the best of our knowledge, this is an original work that leverages

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
114
115
      Neural Architecture Representation Learning. Neural architecture representation learning es-
      timates network attributes without actual training or deployment, resulting in significant resource
116
      savings. Accuracy predictors forecast the evaluation accuracy, avoiding the resource-intensive process
117
      of network training in neural architecture search (Liu et al., 2018; White et al., 2021; Deng et al.,
118   2017; Luo et al., 2018; 2020; Cai et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2020;
119   Chen et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2023; 2024). Additionally, latency
120   prediction can estimate the inference latency without actual deployment, saving time and materials
121   for engineering application (Dudziak et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Kaufman et al., 2021; Liu
122   et al., 2022). Given the complex connections between operations and the one-way message-passing
123   mechanism, the neural network is better described as a DAG, with the connection between nodes
124   represented by the adjacency matrix. Consequently, graph-based (Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020;
125   Shi et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2020) and transformer-based (Lu et al., 2021; Yi
126
      et al., 2023; 2024) predictors have been employed to learn the representation of neural architectures.
      Both methods achieve promising results in neural architecture representation, while they still face
127
      challenges. In this paper, we absorb the strengths of both methods and delve into the topological
128
      relationship.
129
130   Message-Passing Graph Neural Networks. Most contemporary graph neural networks can be
131   expressed within the framework of the message-passing architecture (Gilmer et al., 2017; Kipf &
132   Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017; Veličković et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2018; You et al., 2020). In
133   this framework, node representations are computed iteratively by aggregating the embeddings of
134   their neighboring nodes, and a final graph representation can be obtained by aggregating the node
135   embeddings, such as GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), GAT (Veličković et al., 2018), GIN (Xu et al.,
136   2018), etc. GNN-based models have emerged as a prominent and widely adopted approach for
137   neural network representation learning (Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2020; Chen
138
      et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2020). The straightforward structural characteristics of GNNs contribute to
      strong generalization ability, yet also necessitate further improvement in the performance. Enhancing
139
      topological information and dynamic bi-directional aggregation is a promising approach.
140
141
      Transformers on Graphs. Recently, transformer has been introduced into graph representation
142
      learning (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023), together with
143   network architecture representation learning (Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2023; 2024). TNASP (Lu et al.,
144   2021) inputs the sum of the operation type embedding matrix and Laplacian matrix into the standard
145   transformer. NAR-Former (Yi et al., 2023) encodes each operation and connection information into
146   a token and inputs all tokens into a proposed multi-stage fusion transformer. NAR-Former V2 (Yi
147   et al., 2024) introduced a graph-aided transformer block, which can handle both cell-structured
148   networks and entire networks. However, transformers face challenges of poor generalization when
149   the network goes deeper, with global attention mixing up the far away information (Yi et al., 2024).
150   To address this limitation, we propose a novel predictor that harnesses the strengths of both GNNs
151
      and transformers, allowing it to extract both topology features and dynamic weights. This approach
      enhances the model’s ability to extract valuable insights and maintains good generalization.
152
153
      Neural Networks over DAGs. The inductive bias inherent in DAGs has led to specialized neural
154
      predictors. GNNs designed for DAGs typically compute graph embeddings using a message-passing
155
      framework (Thost & Chen, 2021). On the other hand, transformers applied to DAGs often incorporate
156   the depth of nodes (Kotnis et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023) or Laplacian (Gagrani et al., 2022) as the
157   position encoding, which may seem non-intuitive for integrating structural information into the model.
158   Additionally, transformer-based models frequently use transitive closure (Dong et al., 2022; Luo
159   et al., 2023) as attention masks, leading to poor generalization as mentioned above. Some hybrid
160   methods with GNNs and Transformers are not tailored to neural architecture representation and also
161   face similar challenges (Ying et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021). This paper proposes a novel hybrid model
      with enhanced topological information from sibling nodes.

      3.1      OVERVIEW
185   We adopt a commonly used graph representation of neural architectures (Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al.,
186   2023; 2024; Dong et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022). An architecture with n operations
187   is refered to as a Graph G = (V, E, Z), with node set V , edge set E ⊆ V × V , and node features
188   Z ∈ Rn×d . Each operation is denoted as a node in V such that |V | = n. The edge set E is often
                                                                n×n
189   given in form of an adjacency matrix A ∈ {0, 1}               , where Aij = 1 denotes a directed edge
190   from node i to node j. Each row of Z represents the feature vector of one node, i.e., operation
191   type and hyperparameters, with the number of nodes n and feature dimension d. Unlike previous
192   methods (Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2023), our predictor is strong and position encoding is unnecessary.
193   For simplicity, Z is encoded with one-hot encoding for operation type and sinusoidal encoding for
194   operation attributes as (Yi et al., 2024). The neural architecture representation (Luo et al., 2018; Wen
195
      et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2023; 2024; Liu et al., 2022) utilizes a predictor
      f θ (·) with parameters θ to estimate specific attributes of candidate architectures, e.g., validation
196
      accuracy or inference latency:
197
                                                    ŷ = f θ (Z, A) ,                                           (1)
198
      where ŷ denotes the predicted attribute of the architecture.
199
200   As illustrated in Figure. 2, our approach uses a transformer as the baseline and incorporates dis-
201   criminative topological features to pursue a strong predictor. Previous transformer-based predictors
202   considered adjacent propagation (Lu et al., 2021; Yi et al., 2023; 2024) or transitive closure (Dong
203   et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023) as the graph structure information. Global attention is effective in
204
      shallow network prediction as shown in previous works such as TNASP (Lu et al., 2021) and NAR-
      Former (Yi et al., 2023). However, with the network depth increasing, there is a decrease in the
205
      generalization of global attention as shown in Yi et al. (2024). Global attention may be biased
206
      towards training data and demonstrate poor generalization performance. To build a general neural
207   predictor for the range of all depths, we propose a non-global neural predictor that outperforms
208   the previous methods on both accuracy and latency predictions. As we discussed in Section 1,
209   sibling nodes have a strong relationship with the current nodes and also provide useful information
210   in accuracy and latency prediction. Thus we introduce an Adjacency-Sibling Multi-head Attention
211   (ASMA) in the self-attention layer to learn the local features. As the sibling relationship can be
212   calculated from adjacency matrix A, our ASMA is formulated as:

      3.2   A DJACENCY-S IBLING M ULTIHEAD ATTENTION
230
231   Given a node, we define its sibling nodes as those that share the same parents or children. To identify
232   these sibling nodes, we use the adjacency matrix A and its transpose AT . Specifically, nodes sharing
233   the same parent nodes are indicated by the non-zero positions in the matrix product AAT , reflecting
234   the backward mapping followed by the forward mapping. Similarly, nodes sharing the same children
235   nodes are identified through the matrix product AT A. In this way, we can identify whether there is a
236   sibling relationship between each pair of nodes.
237   To inject topological information, we introduce a novel multi-head attention module. As shown in
238   Figure. 2, we use four-head attention, where each head uses an attention mask indicating a specific
239   topology. These masks include forward adjacency A, backward adjacency AT , siblings with the
240   same parents AAT , and siblings with the same children AT A, respectively. The proposed ASMA is
241   denoted as:

302   4.1     ACCURACY PREDICTION
303
      We conduct accuracy prediction on NAS-Bench-101 (Ying et al., 2019) and NAS-Bench-201 (Dong
304
      & Yang, 2020). Both datasets adopt cell-structured architectures. The NAS-Bench-101 (Ying
305
      et al., 2019) dataset contains 423,624 unique architectures, each comprising 9 repeated cells with
306   a maximum of 7 nodes and 9 edges per cell. Similar to the NAS-Bench-101, the architectures in
307   NAS-Bench-201 (Dong & Yang, 2020) are also built using repeated cells. It presents 15,625 distinct
308   cell candidates, each composed of 4 nodes and 6 edges. We report Kendall’s Tau as the previous
309   methods (Lu et al., 2021; Ning et al., 2020; Yi et al., 2023).
310
      Experiments on NAS-Bench-101. We implemented the configuration outlined in TNASP (Lu
311
      et al., 2021) to train our predictor on subsets of 0.02%, 0.04%, 0.1%, and 1% of the entire dataset.
312   Subsequently, we utilized the complete dataset as the test set and computed Kendall’s Tau to evaluate
313   the performance. The results are detailed in Table 1. Our predictor consistently outperforms baseline
314   methods, such as CNNs, LSTMs, GNNs, Transformers, and hybrid GNNs and Transformers. This
315   result underscores the superior predictive capability of NN-Former in determining neural architecture
316   performance.
317
      Experiments on NAS-Bench-201. We employ a comparable experimental setup to NAS-Bench-101,
318   i.e., training predictors on different subsets of 1%, 3%, 5%, and 10%, and then evaluating them on
319   the complete dataset. The results are depicted in Table 2. NN-Former surpasses other methods in
320   all scenarios except for the 10% subsets. Note that our method aims at unified prediction for both
321   accuracy and latency, it is acceptable that our method achieves comparable results. We outperform
322   NAR-Former V2 (Yi et al., 2024) for all setups, which has a similar unifying motivation. Additionally,
323   neural architecture search prefers high generalization performance with fewer training samples,
      resulting in significant resource savings. More details are discussed in Section B.1.2.

354   4.2   L ATENCY PREDICTION
355
356
357
      We employ NNLQ as the latency prediction task. NNLQ (Liu et al., 2022) includes 20,000 deep-
      learning networks and their respective latencies on the specified hardware. This dataset encompasses
358
      10 distinct network types, with 2,000 networks for each type. The depth of each architecture varies
359
      from tens to hundreds of operations, requiring the scalability of the neural predictor. In line with
360   NNLP, the Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) and Error Bound Accuracy (Acc(δ)) are
361   employed to assess the disparities between latency predictions and actual values.
362
363
      We conduct the experiments on two scenarios following (Yi et al., 2024). In the first in-domain
      scenario, the training and testing sets are from the same distribution. The results are shown in Table 3.
364
      When testing with all test samples, the average MAPE of our methods is 0.62% lower than the
365
      NNLP (Liu et al., 2022) and 0.22% lower than the NAR-Former V2 (Yi et al., 2024). The average
366   Acc(10%) is 2.20% higher than the NNLP and 1.04% higher than the NAR-Former V2. When tested
367   on various types of network data separately, previous methods fail on specific model types, especially
368   on AlexNet, while our method largely mitigates this challenge and obtains a balanced performance
369   on each model type.
370
      The other out-of-domain scenario is more significant, as it involves inferring an unseen network type
371
      during the evaluation. The results in Table 4 indicate that relying solely on FLOPs and memory access
372   data is insufficient for predicting latency. Due to the disparity between kernel delay accumulation and
373   actual latency, kernel-based approaches such as nn-Meter (Zhang et al., 2021) and TPU (Kaufman
374   et al., 2021) exhibit inferior performance compared to GNNs (NNLP (Liu et al., 2022)) and hybrid
375   models (NAR-Former V2 (Yi et al., 2024)). Leveraging enriched topological information, our method
376   achieves the highest MAPE and Acc(10%) among the average metrics of the ten experimental sets.
377   In comparison to the runner-up NAR-Former V2 (Yi et al., 2024), our approach demonstrates a
      substantial 11.61% increase in average Acc(10%).

415   In this section, we perform a series of ablation experiments on the NAS-Bench-101 (Ying et al., 2019)
416   and NNLQ (Liu et al., 2022) datasets to analyze the effects of the proposed modifications. First, we
417   assess the performance of ASMA and BGIFFN over a vanilla transformer baseline. Second, we verify
418   the design for both modules, especially by examining the significance of the topological information.
419
      Ablation on ASMA on latency prediction. To evaluate the generalization ability of ASMA, we
420   conducted experiments on NNLQ (Liu et al., 2022) under the out-of-domain setting. We use NAS-

422   are shown in Table 5a, where we keep the number of heads unchanged ablate on the attention mask.
423   The model with ASMA shows a large performance enhancement of 11.45% Acc(10%) in contrast
424   to the one utilizing global attention. It indicates that global attention presents a noticeable disparity,
425   underscoring the advantages of local features when building a unified neural predictor.
426   Ablation on ASMA and BGIFFN on accuracy prediction. To evaluate the effectiveness of ASMA
427   and BGIFFN, we conducted accuracy prediction experiments using the 0.04% setting of NAS-Bench-

429   vanilla transformer and the result is respectable since it does not incorporate any topology information.

      for each head. Rows 1 and 2 exclusively utilize forward or backward adjacency across all 4 heads.

468   neural architecture representation. Row 5 combines the adjacency and sibling nodes, achieving the
469   highest performance. These results highlight the significance of sibling nodes. It also shows that the
470   design of ASMA is robust and logically sound, showcasing its effectiveness in capturing architectural
471   patterns.
472
      Ablation on Position Encoding (PE). We also explored the impact of PE on our model. Traditionally,
473
      transformers have heavily relied on PE to capture structural information. However, our approach
474   makes this reliance unnecessary because our method inherently incorporates abundant topological
475   information. As shown in Table 6b, we experiment with the inclusion of position encoding in our

477   2023), and Laplacian position encoding in TNASP (Luo et al., 2023). The results suggest that
478   they have no improvement in the performance of NN-Former. It indicates that our method presents
479   exceptional structural learning capabilities.

704   A.1    I MPLEMENTATION FOR ASMA
705
706   We present Python-style code for calculating the attention matrix in the ASMA module in Listing 1.
707   ASMA is motivated by the importance of sibling nodes. In the accuracy prediction, sibling nodes
708   provide complementary features, such as parallel 1x1 and 3x3 convolutions extracting pixel features
709   and local aggregations, respectively. Although the two nodes are neither connected nor reachable
710   through transitive closure, their information can influence each other. This conclusion has been
711
      studied in works such as Inception (Szegedy et al., 2015) and RepVGG (Ding et al., 2021). In latency
      prediction, sibling nodes can run in parallel. For example, if two parallel 1x1 convolutions are merged
712
      into one, it takes only one CUDA kernel and fully utilizes parallel computing. Hence it is reasonable
713
      for ASMA to fuse the sibling nodes information directly.
714

      def attention_matrix(Q, K, A):
717      # Q: query, K: key, A: adjacency matrix
718      # Calculate the attention scores
719      attn = torch.matmul(Q, K.mT) / math.sqrt(Q.size(-1))
720      # Prepare attention masks
         pe = torch.stack([A, A.mT, A.mT @ A, A @ A.mT], dim=1)
721
         pe = pe + torch.eye(L, dtype=A.dtype, device=A.device)
722      # Apply masking
723      attn = attn.masked_fill(pe == 0, -torch.inf)
724      # Softmax operation
725      attn = F.softmax(attn, dim=-1)
         return attn

      A.4   I MPLEMENTATION FOR BGIFFN
838
839   We present Python-style code for the BGIFFN module in Listing 2. BGIFFN is intended to extend
840   Graph Isomorpsim to the bidirectional modeling of DAGs. It extracts the topological features simply
841   and effectively, assisting the Transformer backbone in learning the DAG structure. Various works use
842   convolution to enhance FFN in vision (Guo et al., 2022) and language tasks (Wu et al., 2019). It is
843   reasonable for BGIFFN to assist Transformer in neural predictors.
844
845                                    Listing 2: Calculation for BGIFFN.
846   def bgiffn(x, A, W_1, W_forward, W_backward, W_2):
847      # x: node features, A: adjacency matrix
         # W_1, W_forward, W_backward, W_2: the weight for linear transform
848
         aggregate = torch.cat((A @ x @ W_forward, A.mT @ x @ W_backward),
849          dim=-1)
850      combine = F.relu(x @ W_1 + aggregate) @ W_2
851      return combine

984
       Our ASMA method has less or equal computational complexity than the vanilla attention. On the
985
       dense graph, the vanilla self-attention has a complexity of O(N 2 ) where N denotes the number of
986    nodes. With the sibling connection preprocessed, our ASMA also has a complexity of O(N 2 ). On
987    sparse graphs, the vanilla self-attention is still a global operation thus the complexity is also O(N 2 ).
988    Our ASMA has a complexity of O(N K), where K is the average degree and K << N on sparse
989    graphs. In practical applications, sparse graphs are common thus our method is efficient. The latency
990    prediction experiments in the paper show that our predictor can cover the DAGs from 20 200 nodes,
991    which is applicable for practical use.
