009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances have highlighted the benefits of scaling language models to en-
012            hance performance across a wide range of NLP tasks. However, these approaches
013            still face limitations in effectiveness and efficiency when applied to domain-
014            specific tasks, particularly for small edge-side models. We propose the LoRA-Gen
015            framework, which utilizes a large cloud-side model to generate LoRA parameters
016            for edge-side models based on task descriptions. By employing the reparame-
017            terization technique, we merge the LoRA parameters into the edge-side model
018
               to achieve flexible specialization. Our method facilitates knowledge transfer be-
               tween models while significantly improving the inference efficiency of the spe-
019
               cialized model by reducing the input context length. Extensive experiments show
020
               that LoRA-Gen outperforms the conventional LoRA fine-tuning, which achieves
021            competitive accuracy and a 2.1x speedup with TinyLLaMA-1.1B on common-
022            sense reasoning tasks. Besides, our method delivers a compress ratio of 10.1x
023            with Gemma-2B on intelligent agent tasks.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The principle of scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) demonstrates that increasing the size of Large
029   Language Models (LLMs) can significantly improve cross-task generalization. However, due to
030   the constraints of their enormous size, generic LLMs struggle to achieve a good balance between
031   efficiency and effectiveness when addressing domain-specific tasks or preferences. Accordingly, im-
032   proving smaller edge-side language models which are deployed on edge devices to run locally with
033
      a compact size in specific tasks (Fu et al., 2023; Grangier et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2024) is receiving
      increasing attention in both academic research and industrial applications. Many approaches utilize
034
      parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques (Houlsby et al., 2019; Li & Liang, 2021; Lester et al.,
035
      2021; Hu et al., 2021), particularly LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), to train on specific datasets for special-
036   ization. However, this method may encounter the issue of catastrophic forgetting, which can result
037   in a decrease in performance on other unseen tasks (Feng et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023a).
038
039
      To alleviate knowledge forgetting in specialized training, recent approaches (Dou et al., 2024; Gao
      et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024c; Li et al., 2024a) leverage the flexibility of the Mixture of Experts
040
      (MoE) for LoRA training. Specifically, as shown in Figure 2(b), they integrate a group of multiple
041
      LoRA components as experts within the language model, allowing the language model to control
042   the selection of LoRA components during token generation. However, these methods introduce ad-
043   ditional inference costs due to the extra experts and control units. LoRAHub (Huang et al., 2023b),
044   on the other hand, pre-trains a set of task-specific LoRA components and employs a manually de-
045   signed parameter-free optimization method for selection. Nevertheless, the effectiveness of above
046   mentioned approaches is limited by their model scale, resulting in constrained performance and gen-
047   eralization capabilities on unseen tasks. Therefore, this paper explores a new perspective: utilizing
048   a large cloud-side model to generate parameters for a smaller edge-side model to achieve better
049   specialization.
050   To achieve it, we propose a new LoRA generation framework, termed LoRA-Gen. As shown in
051   Figure 2(c), our method can be divided into two parts: Online LoRA generation and Specialized LM.
052   The former is used to generate LoRA parameters based on the task-defined system prompt, while
053   the latter facilitates efficient batch inference for user input. Specifically, a fine-tuned large language
      model, LLaMA3 (Dubey et al., 2024) and a mixture of LoRA experts are deployed in the cloud.

131   2.2   L O RA M EETS M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
132
133   Leveraging its lightweight nature, LoRA is utilized in Mixture of Experts (MoE) architectures to
134   enhance performance. MoLoRA (Zadouri et al., 2023) incorporates LoRA adapters as experts on
135   top of pre-trained models and uses a router layer to integrate these experts. MOELoRA (Liu et al.,
136   2024a) applies this framework to various medical domain tasks, though it requires task type input
137   for the router. LoRAMoE (Dou et al., 2024) introduces multiple LoRA experts into the feed-forward
138   block to mitigate knowledge forgetting during the instruction-tuning phase. LoraHub (Huang et al.,
139
      2023b) allows a dynamic assembling of LoRA modules on various tasks and even unseen tasks by
      combining adapted LoRA modules. Additionally, MoLA (Gao et al., 2024a) proposes layer-specific
140
      experts, allocating a varying number of LoRA experts to different layers to boost performance.
141
142
143   2.3   C ONTEXT C OMPRESSION
144
145   With the rise of in-context learning (Wei et al., 2022) and agentic pipelines (Yang et al., 2024b),
146   LLMs often need to process thousands of tokens, potentially exceeding their maximum context
147   length. Unlike methods that extend the context window of LLMs, context compression offers an
148   efficient way to reduce the input prompt length. There are two primary methods of context compres-
149   sion: hard prompt and soft prompt. Selective-Context (Li, 2023) and (Jiang et al., 2023) exemplify
150   hard prompt methods by removing low-information content at the lexical level (e.g., sentences,
151   words, or tokens) to shorten the prompt. On the other hand, gisting (Mu et al., 2023), AutoCom-
152
      pressors (Chevalier et al., 2023), ICAE (Ge et al., 2024), and 500xCompressor (Li et al., 2024b)
      represent soft prompt methods that compress input prompts into a small number of special tokens.
153
      In contrast to these approaches, we propose compressing the context into rank-decomposition layers
154
      using LoRA methods.

185   3.2   O NLINE L O RA G ENERATION
186
187   Overview. The mixture of LoRA experts has showcased reasonable performance in fine-tuning
188   for specific tasks. However, there remains a gap in its effectiveness for multi-task learning and the
189   generalization to unseen tasks. Additionally, most LoRA-MoE (Li et al., 2024a; Dou et al., 2024)
190   methods require calculating the expert routing for each token individually, which significantly in-
191
      creases the computational complexity. To address these challenges, we propose a new framework,
      termed LoRA-Gen that generates task-aware LoRA via an online large language model with system
192
      prompts (including few-shot samples, task description, role specification, and the conversation for-
193
      mat) as presented in Figure 3. In the following, we elaborate on our LoRA generation method and
194   the reparameterization of the edge-side language model.
195
196   Cloud-side LM & Meta Token. In adherence to meta-learning (Hospedales et al., 2021; Finn
197   et al., 2017), we construct a unified representation of the task-related information to achieve gen-
198   eralization capabilities for various tasks, relying on cloud-side language models to facilitate this
199   process. Specifically, Given a series of few-shot samples or task-specific system prompts, the cloud-
200   side LM appends L special tokens ⟨meta⟩ behind them and transfers the inherent knowledge into
201   these tokens with causal masks in a single forward pass. We define these tokens as meta tokens
202   {Timeta }Li=1 , where L represents the number of layers of the subsequent edge-side language model.
203
      Each meta token is associated with a transformer layer in the edge-side LM.
204
205
      LoRA Expert Pool. Our initial attempt is to generate LoRA parameters directly through a con-
      tinuous projection on the meta token. However, the expansive parameter space poses optimization
206
      challenges, making the model susceptible to overfitting and hindering generalization, whose analysis
207
      refers to Table 8. Therefore, similar to the previous works (Dou et al., 2024), we adopt an alterna-
208   tive solution by introducing the discrete MoE mechanism. Specifically, as shown in Figure 3, we
209   construct a LoRA expert pool of n experts, whose weights are defined as {Ei }ni=1 . Each LoRA
210   expert contains three LoRA blocks, corresponding to the gate linear layer, up linear layer, and down
211   linear layer in FFN of the edge-side model, respectively. Different from the LoRAHub (Huang et al.,
212   2023b), these experts are trained in an end-to-end manner.
213
214   Routing Module. To control the composition of experts, we propose a routing module using meta
215   tokens. Unlike the token-wise LoRA-MoE (Dou et al., 2024), our MoE is layer-wise. We apply an
      individual MoE for each transformer layer in the edge-side LM, and all tokens in a sequence use

275   introduce a soft constraint with the coefficient of variation as the auxiliary loss, encouraging a more
276   balanced usage of the available experts. Formally, the constraint can be formulated as:

      4.1   DATASETS AND M ETRICS .
297
      Reasoning Tasks. We select eight widely-used benchmarks to assess the reasoning ability of
298   LoRA-Gen across various knowledge domains ranging from natural science to daily life follow-
299   ing counterparts (Dou et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a). One classification task: BoolQ (Clark et al.,
300   2019). Five question-answering tasks: ARC-c (Clark et al., 2018), ARC-e (Clark et al., 2018),
301   OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020) and SocialQA (Sap et al., 2019).
302   One science completion task: Hellaswag (Zellers et al., 2019) and a fill-in-the-blank task: Wino-
303   grande (Sakaguchi et al., 2020).
304
305   Agent Dataset. We utilize the GPT4Tools (Yang et al., 2024b) which provides a benchmark to
306   evaluate the ability of LLM to use tools, to access the effectiveness of LoRA-Gen in the deployment
307   of intelligent agents. GPT4Tools constructs a tool-related instructional dataset, including positive
308   samples, negative samples, and context samples. It consists of 71k instruction-response pairs with
309
      21 tools in the training set and 652 items in the test set with 8 novel tools absent from the training
      set.
310
311
      Metrics. The performance of all reasoning benchmarks is measured with the accuracy metric in
312
      all datasets. To further evaluate the performance in multi-task learning, we utilize two metrics:
313
      the average accuracy (AVE.) and the harmonic mean (HAR.) of all task results. For GPT4Tools,
314   we measure the performance of the method from five aspects: successful rate of thought (SRt ),
315   successful rate of action (SRact ), successful rate of arguments (SRargs ), successful Rate (SR) and
316   IoU according to GPT4Tools.

466   this approach exhibits comparable performance to that achieved through meta tokens on the seen

469

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we propose an online LoRA generation framework, called LoRA-Gen, which utilizes
489   a cloud-side language model to generate task-specific LoRA parameters for edge-side models. Our
490   strategy offers four advantages over previous methods, including context compression for unseen
491   tasks, a reparameterized language model, inference-time specializing, and knowledge transfer. Ex-
492   tensive experiments show that LoRA-Gen achieves competitive results and an impressive speedup
493   on common-sense reasoning tasks. Additionally, our method achieves a compress ratio of 10.1x on
494   zero-shot agent tasks, indicating its potential applicability to more scenarios.
495
496   Limitations. Our method supports diverse application scenarios, such as tool calls, personalized
497   virtual assistants, offline intelligent systems, IoT device control, and tasks necessitating long system
498
      prompts. However, the current paradigm needs to predefine a pair of cloud and edge-side LM. The
      model-agnostic framework leaves an open question for future work.
