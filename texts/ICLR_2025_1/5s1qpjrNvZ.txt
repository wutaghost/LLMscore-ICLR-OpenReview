009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning-based solutions are increasingly being considered as strong
012            alternatives to classical system controllers, despite their significant sample ineffi-
013            ciency when learning controller tasks from scratch. Many methods that address this
014            issue use prior task knowledge to guide the agent’s learning, with several recent
015            algorithms providing a guide policy that is sometimes chosen to execute actions
016            instead of the learner policy. While this approach lends excellent flexibility as it
017            allows the guide knowledge to be provided in any format, it can be challenging to
018
               decide when and for how long to use the guide agent. Current guide policy-based
               approaches typically choose a static guide sampling rate empirically and do not
019
               vary it. Approaches that transfer control use simple methods like linear decay or
020
               require hyperparameter choices that strongly impact the performance. We show that
021            under certain assumptions, the sampling rate of the guide policy can be calculated
022            to guarantee that the mean return of the learning policy will surpass a user-defined
023            performance degradation threshold. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
024            time a performance guarantee has been established for a guided RL method. We
025            then implement a guided RL (GRL) algorithm that can make use of this sample
026            rate and additionally introduce a roll-back feature in guided RL with roll-back
027            (GRL-RB) to adaptively balance the trade-off between performance degradation
028            and rapid transfer of control to the learner. Our approach is simple to implement
029
               on top of existing algorithms, robust to hyperparameter choices, and effective in
               warm-starting online learning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The sample inefficiency of model-free reinforcement learning (RL) remains one of the greatest barriers
035   to the wider adoption of RL-trained models in everyday life (Yu, 2018). Real-world applications of
036   RL are increasing, such as in refining the large language model ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), or for
037   efficient cooling of commercial buildings (Luo et al., 2022). However, real-world environments often
038   translate to complex environments for RL agents (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2021). This can be due to
039
      sparse rewards, high stochasticity and very large observation and action spaces. As such, RL agents
      may require extensive training to be effectively deployed in such environments.
040
041   Many systems currently run by controllers or human operators demonstrate potential performance
042   gains from automation. Often in such systems, there is an available policy that governs the behaviour
043   of the original controller (e.g., a set of fuzzy or crisp rules). Alternatively, in a system controlled by
044   a human operator, data that describes the behaviour running the system could be collected. Rather
045
      than forcing an RL-based controller to commence learning from scratch, such prior policies or expert
      data could be exploited to warm-start an RL agent before deployment in an online environment (Luo
046
      et al., 2022). Additional challenges of real-world environments make a warm-starting solution more
047
      attractive. Firstly, while the sample inefficiency of model-free RL is often handled by training across
048   many parallel environments (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2021), real-world environments cannot be similarly
049   replicated. Further, for many applications, there is a need to ensure a certain level of performance
050   when a model is deployed to the real world, e.g. for robotics (Zhao et al., 2020), (Lobbezoo et al.,
051   2021) and self-driving cars (Maramotti et al., 2022)(Isele et al., 2018). These types of agents would
052   ideally commence at a reasonable performance level, such that they require only fine-tuning in the
053   online environment. It is important that a warm-starting solution can not only accelerate the learning
      of the agent but minimise performance degradation after transfer to the online environment.

054
      In this paper, we focus on a ‘roll-in’ method, where control is gradually shifted from a guide policy
055   to a learning policy in a stepped manner. The focus of our Guided Reinforcement Learning (GRL)
056   method is to allow online learning while reducing performance degradation. We show that given a
057   user-defined acceptable degradation threshold, the online agent can be theoretically guaranteed to
058   maintain an evaluation return above this threshold under certain assumptions. The most challenging
059   of these assumptions is that the agent is able to converge fully between roll-in steps. To ease the
060   challenges in meeting this assumption, guided RL with roll-back (GRL-RB) is introduced to enable
061   tuning of the guide sampling rate, to respect the given threshold. Further, GRL-RB is flexible in
062   the structure of the prior policy (e.g. heuristics, decision trees, a policy learned through imitation
063
      learning/offline RL etc.) and can be implemented on top of existing algorithms. Finally, this method
      gradually transfers full control to the learning agent, to ensure exploration is not hindered by the
064
      prior knowledge and to minimise distribution mismatch between the replay buffer trajectories and the
065
      trajectories induced by the learning agent’s policy (Fujimoto et al., 2019).
066
067   Our contributions are threefold: a) A guided reinforcement learning approach (GRL) that enables
068   the gradual transfer of control to a learning policy, with a guaranteed online performance above
069
      a user-defined threshold. (b) The theoretical derivation and experimental confirmation of guide
      sampling rates for GRL under defined assumptions. (c) A GRL with a roll-back algorithm (GRL-RB)
070
      that helps to retain the performance guarantee of GRL while relaxing its assumptions.

100   2.1   ACTION S ELECTION
101
      Action selection-based methods use either an online learning agent initialised with randomised
102   parameters or a guide policy to choose actions. This method has become increasingly popular due
103   to its inherent flexibility - the guide policy can be of any format - e.g. a pre-trained RL policy, a
104   decision tree, a heuristic or a list of rules (Chang et al., 2015; 2023; Liu et al.; Uchendu et al., 2023).
105   An obvious challenge in any guide policy-based approach is the question of how and when to reduce
106   or remove the influence of the guide agent during the learner’s training. This is especially important
107   when the underlying algorithm is on-policy, such that the data distribution eventually reflects the
      distribution induced by the learner’s policy. We find that most current approaches simply choose the


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      sampling rate empirically, and often do not vary it throughout training. Such a method for supervised
109   learning was used in Daumé et al. (2009), where subsequent ‘guide’ classifiers are created through
110   linear interpolation βh′ + (1 − β)h of the previous guide h′ and current policy h. While the choice
111   of the constant β is discussed in terms of providing a bound on the supervised loss, it is not varied
112   during training. Another linear interpolation uses a constant β to interpolate between a continuous
113   action uM P C (t) from a Model Predictive Control method, and a DDPG policy action a(t).
114
      A number of methods used horizon-based sampling (Chang et al., 2015; 2023; Liu et al.; Uchendu
115   et al., 2023; Daoudi et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024) where a guide policy is
116   sampled until some horizon h is reached, after which the learner probabilistically chooses the actions
117   (or vice versa). Alternatively, each time step may have a probability of sampling the learner. These
118   probabilities may not vary throughout training (Chang et al., 2015; 2023), or may be gradually reduced.
119   In Liu et al., prior to h (sampled randomly), one of many oracles or the learner itself is sampled,
120   depending on a prediction of the oracle’s expertise in that state by their learned value functions.
121   As the learner improves, it will tend to be chosen over the oracles. In Jump-Start Reinforcement
122   Learning (JSRL) proposed by Uchendu et al. (2023), sampling the guide first allows the learner agent
123
      to start closer to its goal near the episode end, giving the agent an easier task to reduce performance
      degradation. While this means that the learner should initially be sampled for short periods that
124
      gradually increase, in practice this depends highly on having constant N . This is because if the
125
      episode runs for longer than N , the learner will be sampled. In some environments, this may leave
126   the agent unable to reach the goal within the environment’s time limit T . If T >> N , the learner
127   may end up being sampled for most of the episode, as shown in Appendix B.
128
129
      Daoudi et al. (2024) instead used multiple local guides. The learner policy is used for action selection
      until a local guide satisfies a confidence function indicating their knowledge of the current state. The
130
      guide then produces an action which is then modified by a bounded, parameterised perturbation. This
131
      was an effective method for continuous action spaces, though the guide is again used throughout
132   learning. In Yang et al. (2022), control in a safety-focused constrained MDP is handed back to the
133   guide when a safety violation occurs. Again, no procedure is implemented to ensure control is fully
134   transferred to the learner. While a linear decay method is implemented, it was not effective compared
135   to the safety-violation based method. Finally, an adaptive sampling approach in (Zhang et al., 2024)
136   produced samples for an imitation-learning based policy by framing sampling as a MDP, with the
137   action space involving either sampling the online learner or expert. However, given the sampler
138   begins as a randomised policy, it would be difficult to make the performance guarantees we are
139   interested in using this method. This is similarly true for Liu et al., where performance would be very
140
      difficult to guarantee while the oracles’ value functions are being learned.

142   2.2   ROLL -BACK
143   The concept of reverting to safe or previous operational states enhances the safety and stability of
144   reinforcement learning systems. (Hans et al., 2008) introduced an approach where safe actions are
145   identified based on a modified Bellman equation that prioritizes minimal future rewards rather than
146   maximal returns. This strategy involves maintaining a backup policy to revert to when the current
147   policy encounters unsafe or suboptimal performance. Actions leading to "fatal" transitions—where
148
      rewards fall below a threshold—are avoided, and the agent reverts to previously validated safe actions
      using a level-based exploration strategy. (Ma et al., 2019) expanded the “roll back" principle to a novel
149
      regret-based mechanism within a navigation agent. This mechanism enhances the navigation strategy
150
      by allowing the agent to revert to better alternatives based on past decisions. This retrospective
151   analysis is supported by heuristic aids and progress estimation, enabling the agent to continuously
152   refine its decision-making process. The recovery mechanism in Dasagi et al. (2019) allows the RL
153   agent to revert to previous, stable versions of its policy upon detecting performance drops using
154   statistical methods like the Mann-Whitney U-test. This recovery strategy helps mitigate the risks
155   associated with aggressive policy updates that can degrade learning progress, ensuring more reliable
156   and continuous improvement over time. GRL-RB relies on a similar principle to the above method,
157   where instead the previous safe policy is the guide policy.

161   The first focus of this work is to enable a πl to explore an environment while sampling a πg with
      sampling rate α, which is carefully chosen to guarantee that performance doesn’t fall below a user-

163   while avoiding the issues with over-sampling πl that were discussed in Section 2. We chose to
164   employ a similar sampling approach to Chang et al. (2015) and Chang et al. (2023), but where πg
165   is sampled with probability α at any time during the episode. Additionally, we track the fractional

171   (GRL) is formally described in Algorithm 1 of Appendix D. To enable the implementation of GRL,

188   3.1.1       M ETHOD : D ERIVATION
189   To simplify the derivation of α, we modify the toy ‘Combination Lock’ MDP (Uchendu et al., 2023).
190   This is an episodic MDP with fixed time horizon H (corresponding to the length of the combination
191   code). The agent must correctly choose the next digit in the combination sequence (the optimal action,
192   a∗), otherwise if the agent chooses the wrong digit (the non-optimal action, a∗ ), the episode ends
193   immediately. All rewards are 0, unless the agent reaches the end of the combination, where it receives
194   a sparse reward of r (see Appendix C for a full description of the environment). Sampling rate α
195   will be derived for three variations of πl , πg and the reward: variation 1 - optimal πg , terminating
196   πl , sparse reward; variation 2 - non-optimal πg , non-terminating πl , sparse reward; and variation
197
      3 - non-optimal πg , non-terminating πl , dense reward. The derived α is then used as input to GRL
      (Algorithm 1), and online training can commence. If the online algorithm is sufficient, it will allow
198
      the learner policy to converge (such that R̂π = R̂πg ). At this point, rather than always choosing
199
      non-optimal action, the learning policy πl now has (at least) a (1 − α) probability of choosing the
200
      optimal action. As such, we can reduce the guide sampling policy by (1 − α), such that the new guide
201   sampling rate is α − (1 − α). We can continue to apply the procedure until the guide sampling rate
202   α = 0, as per Algorithm 1. The assumption of convergence between steps of α is key to the success
203   of GRL. In complex environments, it can be very difficult to guarantee this convergence, as shown
204   in Figure 4. This challenge motivates the introduction of a roll-back mechanism, in our proposed
205   GRL-RB described below.

354   4   G UIDED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING WITH ROLL -BACK (GRL-RB)
355
356   As discussed above, the assumption of convergence between updates of α ensures the success of GRL.
357
      However, if πl has not yet converged such that we can be confident it is sampling the optimal action
      with 1 − α probability, then decreasing α for the next step of training may cause GRL to over-sample
358
      the non-optimal action and cause the return to fall below Equation 2. To help the GRL algorithm
359
      recover from non-convergence, and minimise the number of evaluations that fall below the defined
360   threshold, we introduce the Guided RL with Roll-Back (GRL-RB) (Algorithm 2, described fully in
361   Appendix G). In GRL-RB, if the evaluation return falls below Equation 2, then α is ‘rolled back’ to
362   the value that attained the previous best return. Beyond maintaining good online performance, another
363   benefit of the GRL-RB approach is to speed up the transfer of learning from πg to πl . GRL-RB
364   allows the algorithm to take larger steps of α (and more frequently), while minimising degradation
365   through the roll-back mechanism.
366   As a straightforward demonstration of the value of GRL-RB, we make intentionally poor hyperpa-
367   rameter choices for GRL in the Combination Lock environment. This makes convergence unlikely,
368   and we can immediately see how GRL struggles to respect the chosen threshold (Figure 4). By
369   comparison, GRL-RB gives the learner the opportunity to return to the previous best-performing
370   α, and recover. Additional experimental results in this environment are provided in Appendix F.
371   Experiments with the algorithms in a more challenging environment, Antmaze, are shown in Figure
372   5. We compare GRL-RB with JSRL (Uchendu et al., 2023) and another offline-to-online method,
373   IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022). See Appendix G.1.2 for details. To demonstrate GRL-RB’s flexibility
374
      to transfer hyperparameters, we include a robustness experiment in Figure 9 in Appendix G.1.3.
375   These results shown the roll-back mechanisms effectiveness in helping GRL to maintain the per-
376   formance threshold with non-ideal hyperparameter choices, and in challenging environments. In
377   Figure 4, we show the maximum and minimum score attained across 50 runs to show all threshold

421   5    D ISCUSSION
422
423
      Transferring from πg to πl is a careful balance between speed and managing performance degradation.
      For complex tasks, πl may take time to learn the optimal behaviours in the environment, improving
424
      upon the behaviour of πg . However, an extremely slow transfer may negate some of the benefits of
425
      warm-starting learning using a guide, and so ideally we would like πg to transfer as fast as possible
426   to πl , while assuring some level of performance. In our first contribution, GRL, we introduced an
427   algorithm that enabled a successful transfer from πg to πl . This algorithm is simple to implement
428   on top of existing algorithms (e.g., IQL) and does not require any additional training compared to
429   vanilla RL, as πg stays static throughout learning. As πg is sampled any time throughout an episode,
430   it avoids using the learner for many consecutive time steps, which can take the agent so far from an
431   optimal trajectory that it cannot recover (e.g. Figure 6). The user is free to set the initial sampling rate
      α of GRL, and so in our second contribution, we show how this can be chosen most advantageously.


                                                             8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      πl does need to explore to some extent, which will inevitably result in some level of degradation,
433   however for many applications it is possible to specify an acceptable level of exploration degradation.
434   In our second contribution, we have sought to take advantage of this specified performance degradation
435   threshold. Our theoretical results in Section 3.2 show α can be chosen in certain environments to
436   enable the fastest transfer possible while respecting performance requirements. LD is reasonably
437   effective but can be over-conservative and slow to transfer. The static πg sampling algorithms (S25%
438   and S75%) are clearly unable to converge unless πg is already optimal, so it is surprising that this
439   method continues to be used in the guided policy literature (Section 2). Our analysis has shown that
440   GRL is the only method that respects the performance degradation threshold in all experiments.
441   In an ideal situation, GRL alone would be sufficient to enable knowledge transfer from πg to πl . The
442   result in Figure 8a shows clearly that when the policy is able to fully converge between updates, the
443   roll-back mechanism introduces no additional advantage to the algorithm. When convergence is not
444   assured however, our third contribution, GRL-RB, is able to adaptively adjust its sample rate to better
445   respect the performance degradation threshold. The results in Figure 8 (Appendix G.1.1) show the
446   strong utility of the roll-back mechanism of GRL-RB in alleviating these issues. Specifically, Figure
447
      8c-8h show that rolling back the sample rate allows the performance to recover. In Figure 8d, it is
      clear to see the learner sampling rate is dropped automatically at time steps above 250, due to the
448
      agent’s drop in evaluation score at the corresponding time steps as in Figure 8c. The agent’s score
449
      then gradually increases until it returns to its previous optimal value. On average, GRL-RB is able to
450   maintain performance above the threshold even with obviously poor choices of hyperparameters.
451
452   The AntMaze results in Section 4 further demonstrate the utility of both GRL and GRL-RB, in a
453
      more complex environment. While IQL without any modification does very well when it has a variety
      of data available in its replay buffer (Table 6), it struggles when the replay buffer is cleared for online
454
      learning. This makes it unsuitable to applications without a dataset available (e.g., when πg is a set of
455
      rules). Furthermore, the same policy is shifted from offline to online learning, meaning it must be of
456   the same format (i.e., a neural network). JSRL also gets some reasonable results, however it suffers
457   from the horizon-based action-selection, as discussed in Section 2. It can struggle to recover from
458   the initial overuse of the learner agent in the more challenging environments (medium and large).
459   Conversely, performance degradation does not occur in these environments for GRL-RB or LD, as
460   our percentage-based sampling method ensures the restricted use of the learner. Even in the extreme
461   case presented for the additional robustness experiment in Appendix G.1.3, the GRL-RB method is
462   effective in preventing degradation by constantly rolling back the sampling rate when required.
463   A limitation of GRL-RB is that the roll-back is only triggered once the score has fallen below the
464   threshold. A valuable addition would be a mechanism to predict or detect whether this is about to
465   occur, to prevent the violation altogether. Further, future work could make use of a statistical method
466   to compare the evaluation score distributions (as in Dasagi et al. (2019)), to provide an enhanced
467   measure of evaluation score improvement. Finally, the derivations of α provided above have a fairly
468   limited scope compared to the wide variety of environments and reward schemes used in RL. In
469   future work, we will aim to derive more results for other kinds of environments.
470
471   6    C ONCLUSION
472
473   We introduce two algorithms, GRL and GRL-RB, to enable the transfer of prior knowledge from a
474   guide policy πg to learning policy πl . Our theoretical analysis and experimental confirmation show
475   that for environments with certain reward schemes, an initial sample rate α for the πg can be derived
476
      to ensure the mean evaluation score does not fall below a user-defined threshold. This is important,
      as many systems that currently use classical controllers may require to maintain a certain evaluation
477
      score, due to reasons including safety, cost or user experience. It is thus critical that algorithms
478
      designed to replace such systems have some guarantee of performance. To the best of our knowledge,
479   this is the first time a performance guarantee has been established for a guided RL method.
480
481   The sample rates we derive do have a requirement that the agent is able to fully converge to the original
482
      evaluation score between evaluations, before α is progressed. Choosing the correct hyperparameters
      to assure this can be challenging, so we introduced GRL-RB to allow the agent the opportunity to
483
      return to a previous best α. Results in the Combination Lock and AntMaze environments show
484
      that GRL-RB enables effective self-correction, providing some flexibility for use in more complex
485   environments.

      A     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
649
650
      There are many approaches in the literature to providing an agent with knowledge of a task before
651
      it commences online learning. While the most relevant approaches to our algorithm are discussed
652
      in Section 2, ere we summarise approaches that influence the replay buffer, parameter updates, and
653   initial parameters of an agent.
654
655
656   A.1    R EPLAY B UFFER
657
      The replay buffer approach is primarily found in algorithms using the learning from demonstrations
658
      (LfD) method (Rajeswaran et al., 2017; Vecerik et al., 2017; Hester et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022a;
659
      Nair et al., 2018; Goecks et al., 2019). In LfD, trajectories generated from a teacher (e.g. a human
660   expert, or a partially trained agent) are made available to the agent during training (through the replay
661   buffer) to provide examples of high quality behaviour. This has found to be effective even with a
662   minimalistic application, by using a distribution of (Kakade & Langford, 2002), a single (Salimans &
663   Chen, 2018), or the most significant (Tavakoli et al., 2019) demonstration(s) as states from which
664   to restart agent exploration. These significant states could be even be found through online agent
665   exploration, then archived for learning through LfD methods (Ecoffet et al., 2021). For goal-oriented
666   tasks, a curriculum of starting states can be produced, increasingly further from a single demonstrated
667   state that achieves the goal (Florensa et al., 2017). The demonstrations can help to emphasise the
668
      direction in which the agent’s parameters should be updated to solve its task, or to guide the agent
      to states covered by an optimal (or near-optimal) policy. A unique approach in (Wu et al.) learned
669
      an additional Planner policy, which could sample natural-language based instructions to give to the
670
      Actor as part of the input to its policy network. The rewards in this case for both the planner and actor
671   were also modified to encourage the actor to follow the planner guidance.
672
673
674   A.2    PARAMETER U PDATES
675
      Modifying the parameter update typically involves adding an additional term to the loss or policy
676
      gradient. It is common for Replay Buffer-based methods to also modify the parameter update, to
677
      encourage imitation of the demonstrated trajectories. For example, the (Nair et al., 2018) adds
678   a behaviour-cloning based term to expert actions that appear optimal, (Rajeswaran et al., 2017)
679   introduces a term to weight contributions from advantageous demonstrations to the gradient, and
680   (Hester et al., 2018) uses a margin function to increase the q-values of the expert actions. in
681   Levine & Koltun (2013), Guided Policy Search instead uses a dynamic programming approach to
682   generate multiple guide policies, whose samples are used to update the online learner. An additional
683   importance weighting term is also introduced to better incorporate the off-policy samples to the policy
684   gradient-based algorithm.
685
686   A.3    I NITIAL PARAMETERS
687
688   Methods that influence the initial parameters of the agent’s policy and/or value function typically
689   involving a portion of offline pre-training. This is advantageous when there is a batch of data
690   available, and thus batch RL (Levine et al., 2020) (also known as offline RL) techniques can be used
691   to warm-start learning. While this is an appealing approach - to directly take the behaviours the
692   agent has learned from the restricted batch of data, then continue to improve it online - this approach
693
      faces challenges with performance degradation. This issue was analysed in Wexler et al. (2022) and
      found to be due to overestimation bias, similar to that experienced in offline RL (Fujimoto et al.,
694
      2019)(Fujimoto & Gu, 2021). Thus, such methods typically address this in their parameter update.
695
      For example, in Wexler et al. (2022), a penalty is introduced to the policy objective to reduce the
696   size of gradient updates when their measure of extrapolation error is high. (Xu et al., 2022) uses
697   a min function over Q-values from multiple experts to reduce overestimation of Q-values. The
698   aforementioned (Hester et al., 2018) also uses a pre-training method, and the margin function acts to
699   ensure Q-values of unexplored actions will not be estimated as higher than the experts’ actions. Some
700   methods do not explicitly address performance degradation, but instead load a pre-trained critic along
701   with the policy (Goecks et al., 2019)(Uchendu et al., 2023), which can help to avoid degradation
      (Wexler et al., 2022).


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      The initial parameters could also be influenced using an adaptive rules-based approach. The methods
703   in Chen et al. (2023)(Wang et al., 2022b)(Shi et al., 2022)(Yu et al., 2023) encode the knowledge
704   as rules with adaptive parameters, which can be refined via online RL. (Silva & Gombolay, 2021)
705   instead encodes rules as a differential decision tree, whose parameters are also adaptable through
706   online training. These methods can be useful when a policy can be easily encoded as a rule set with
707   obvious choices for weighting parameters or membership functions, however can be challenging for
708   more complex decision policies.
709
710
      B        E XAMPLE OF JSRL FAILURE M ODE
711
712
      The JSRL horizon-based curriculum, where the learner πl takes over the action-selection from the
713
