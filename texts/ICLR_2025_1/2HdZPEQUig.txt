008                                                      A BSTRACT
009
010                This paper introduces a method for efficiently learning video-level object-centric
011                representations by bootstrapping off a pre-trained image backbone, which we term
012
                   Interpreter. It presents a novel hierarchical slot attention architecture with
                   local learning and an optimal transport objective that yields fully unsupervised
013
                   video segmentation. We first learn to compress images into image-level object-
014
                   centric representations. Interpreter then learns to compress and reconstruct
015                the object-centric representations for each frame across a video, allowing us to
016                circumvent the costly process of reconstructing full frame feature maps. Unlike
017                prior work, this allows us to scale to significantly longer videos without resort-
018                ing to chunking videos into segments and matching between them. To deal with
019                the unordered nature of object-centric representations, we employ Sinkhorn di-
020                vergence, a relaxed optimal transport objective, to compute the distance between
021                unordered sets of representations. We evaluate the resulting segmentation maps on
022                video instance segmentation in both realistic and synthetic settings, using YTVIS-
023
                   19 and MOVi-E, respectively. Interpreter achieves state-of-the-art results
                   on the realistic YTVIS-19 dataset and presents a promising approach of scaling
024
                   object-centric representation learning to longer videos.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Self-supervised object-centric learning has received significant attention within the last few years
030
      (Locatello et al., 2020; Aydemir et al., 2023; Kakogeorgiou et al., 2024). In object-centric learning
031
      the objective is to learn a mapping from observations to sets of vectors such that each vector encodes
032   a semantically distinct part of the observation, e.g. individual or groups of objects in an image. This
033   process is learned in a self-supervised manner, for example through reconstruction of image pixels
034   or feature maps. Recent work has extended object-centric learning from images to video while
035   achieving impressive results on video object segmentation without relying on additional cues such
036   as motion estimation or depth. However, extending slot attention to videos, particularly of high
037   resolution, presents a computational challenge due to the requirement of reconstructing the full
038   feature map of each frame. As a result, prior work (Aydemir et al., 2023) resorts to chunking videos
039   into small segments of a few frames, extracting object-centric representations and segmentations,
040
      and matching these across frames. We submit that a more efficient method is needed to permit full
      processing of the temporal context.
041
042   We present one such solution. Interpreter introduces a novel hierarchical slot attention ap-
043   proach with a local learning objective. We overcome the temporal limitations of prior work by break-
044   ing the problem down into two parts. Interpreter first learns to compress image feature maps
045   into object-centric representations through reconstruction. Next, our proposed method learns to
046
      compress and reconstruct the image-level object-centric representations across entire videos, yield-
      ing a set of video-level frames. However, to implement this approach requires us to answer a few
047
      questions. The first question is how to extend the attention maps between the individual frames and
048
      video-level representations. For this we show that attention maps can be propagated between the im-
049   age and video level slot attention modules to compute a image-to-video attention maps. The second
050   question is what criteria is suitable to measure the distance between the unordered sets that con-
051   stitute the image-level object-centric representations and their reconstructions. This we resolve by
052   considering any two sets of object-centric representations as empirical samples in high dimensional
053

      feature space. Through this we are able to employ Sinkhorn Divergence, an entropy regularized
055   optimal transport distance that is amenable to gradient-based learning.
056
057   To summarize, our contributions are threefold. First, we introduce Interpreter, a novel method
058
      that leverages a hierarchical slot attention architecture with local learning objectives to efficiently
      learn video-level representations from pre-trained image backbones, enabling the processing of en-
059
      tire videos without the need for chunking. Second, we propose a technique to propagate attention
060
      maps between image-level and video-level representations, facilitating the computation of image-
061   to-video level attention maps. Third, we address the challenge of measuring distances between
062   unordered sets of object-centric representations by employing Sinkhorn Divergence, an entropy-
063   regularized optimal transport distance. We validate our approach on both realistic and synthetic
064   datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art video object segmentation performance on YTVIS-19 in
065   terms of mean Intersection over Union.

068   2   R ELATED W ORK
069
070   We consider here two lines of work, object-centric learning and video object segmentation, that we
071   will summarize in brief. We focus on fully unsupervised video object segmentation.
072
073   Object-Centric Learning: The most prevalent method in contemporary object-centric learning is
074   Slot Attention (Locatello et al., 2020), which defines a learnable iterative function that extracts a
075   set of slots of predefined cardinality from an observation, e.g. an image, through an inverted cross-
076   attention mechanism. Subsequent work extended Slot Attention from simple pixel reconstruction on
077   synthetic scenes to real world images by introducing a feature map reconstructive objective using the
078   high quality features from a pre-trained DINO/DINOv2 ViT backbone (Seitzer et al., 2022; Caron
079   et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023). Newer versions of Slot Attention have also been introduced, such
080   as Invariant Slot Attention (Biza et al., 2023), which makes slot attention invariant to translation and
081
      scale, and Implicit Slot Attention (Chang et al., 2022) which significantly improved the robustness
      of training.
082
083
084   Video Object Segmentation: In Video Object Segmentation (VOS) the goal is to accurately seg-
085   ment individual objects throughout the frames of a video. This includes making sure that segmen-
086   tations stay consistent across the sequence, without mix up of identities. While there exist two
087
      common VOS settings, semi-supervised and unsupervised, we choose here to focus on prior work in
      unsupervised VOS as it aligns with our proposed method. In unsupervised VOS training on ground
088
      truth segmentation annotations is permitted, but no supervision is provided at inference time. Re-
089
      cent work, however, has approached VOS fully unsupervised (Xie et al., 2022; Aydemir et al., 2023;
090   Ding et al., 2024), where the only permitted supervision should come from the data itself. Methods
091   such as OCLR (Xie et al., 2022) use optical flow to discover and segment objects. More recently,
092   approaches not reliant on any modality other than the video itself, have been proposed. Two notable
093   examples are SOLV and BA (Aydemir et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024).
094
      SOLV combines per-frame invariant slot attention with learnable queries and a temporal encoder that
095
      enriches slots across frames. SOLV also introduces agglomerative clustering to alleviate the over-
096   clustering that occurs from using a fixed number of slots. BA takes a different approach and makes
097   use of the attention maps of a pre-trained DINO vision encoder and learns an attention mechanism
098   to capture spatio-temporal dependencies, followed by hierarchical clustering to generate coherent
099   object segmentation masks. However, both SOLV and BA are limited by relatively short temporal
100   contexts, e.g. T = 5 and T = 3 frames, respectively, requiring matching between video segments. In
101   contrast, our proposed method, Interpreter, is designed to allow for a more extensive temporal
102   context, making it more effective at maintaining object consistency across longer sequences.

      3.1   I NTERPRETER
109
110   Our proposed method, Interpreter, follows prior works (Seitzer et al., 2022; Kakogeorgiou
111   et al., 2024) by first learning an image-level slot attention auto-encoder to reconstruct high-quality
112   image features from a pre-trained DINOv2 ViT-B/14 backbone. Subsequently, we introduce a sec-
113   ond video-level slot attention module that is trained by reconstructing the image-level object-centric
114   representations from the first level. However, the video-level objective poses a challenge. While the
115   image-level reconstruction benefits from an explicit ordered structure in the feature map, allowing
116
      for structured losses like L2, the image-level object-centric representations form an unordered set,
      thereby lacking explicit structure. Although these representations implicitly contain latent positional
117
      information, they still do not permit the use of a structured loss.
118
119   To effectively use the object-centric representations as reconstruction targets, we require a loss func-
120   tion that is invariant to the ordering of elements within the set. Consider a set of object-centric
121   representations S and a predicted set S̃, both viewed as empirical samples in high-dimensional fea-
122   ture space with equal probability mass assigned to each feature vector. One natural way to measure
123   the discrepancy between S and S̃ is the Wasserstein (optimal transport) distance, which evaluates
124   the minimal ”work” (distance × mass) required to transform S̃ into S. This distance is inherently
125   invariant to the ordering of elements. However, the direct computation of optimal transport is non-
126
      differentiable, complicating its use in gradient-based optimization.
127   Fortunately, an alternative divergence measure, Sinkhorn Divergence, approximates the Wasser-
128   stein distance while being both fast and differentiable (Feydy et al., 2019). Sinkhorn divergence
129   interpolates between the exact Wasserstein distance and an entropy-regularized divergence by in-
130   corporating an entropy term that smooths the problem. This divergence can be computed iteratively
131   via the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm. As the entropy regularization diminishes, Sinkhorn divergence
132
      converges towards the true Wasserstein distance, making it a practical and theoretically sound choice
      for measuring how well the model reconstructs unordered sets in high-dimensional feature space.

135   3.2   A RCHITECTURE AND O BJECTIVE
136
      Interpreter consists of a pre-trained image backbone and two hierarchically stacked slot atten-
137
      tion auto-encoders that are trained with a local objective, independently. For each level we use
138   Implicit Slot Attention (Chang et al., 2022) for more robust training and Flash Attention (Dao
139   et al., 2022) for faster and cheaper transformer attention (Vaswani et al., 2017). For calculating
140   the Sinkhorn divergence we use the GPU accelerated Geomloss (Feydy et al., 2019) package due
141   to its speed and robust implementation.
142

144   without causal masking. A diagram of this stage of training can be seen in figure 1 left. The slot
145   attention module takes image feature maps F ∈ RHW ×D0 , from the pre-trained image encoder and
146   compresses them to object-centric representations S ∈ RN ×D1 , where N is the number of image
147   slots with N ≪ HW , D0 and D1 is the dimensionality of the feature map and slots, respectively.
148   Following this, a transformer decoder maps a set of 2D sine-cosine positionally encoded vectors
149   P0 ∈ RHW ×D0 to a reconstructed feature map F̃ conditioned on the slots S. The objective is then
150   calculated as the L2 loss between the original feature map F and the reconstructed feature map F̃ :

219   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
220
221   Datasets: To evaluate our proposed method on video object segmentation in real-world videos we
222   train and test on YouTube-VIS 2019 (YTVIS-19) (Yang et al., 2019), consisting of a total of 2883
223   videos that are up to 36 frames, corresponding to 6 seconds of video. We follow the training and
224   testing protocol of (Aydemir et al., 2023) and train on the combined train, test and validation sets.
225   Due to the lack of ground truth masks on the train and validation set, we also follow (Aydemir et al.,
226   2023; Ding et al., 2024) by evaluating on the exact same holdout set of 300 videos from the training
227   set. More succinctly, we train on a total of 2583 videos and evaluate on 300 videos. During training
228
      and evaluation we resize the videos and segmentation masks to 336 × 504 resolution and extend
      videos shorter than the maximum length of 36 frames by repeating the sequence to fill the maximum
229
      length and truncate any excess. During evaluation we resize the attention maps after propagation
230
      with bilinear interpolation to match the resolution of the videos and cut off any repeated frames.
231
232   To further test the video segmentation performance we use the synthetic MOVi-E dataset (Greff
233   et al., 2022), consisting of around 10k training videos, and 1k testing videos, at 24 frames each,
234
      corresponding to 2 seconds. MOVi-E features up to 20 in-motion objects combined with linear
      camera motion. We resize the videos to 336 × 336 resolution and consume each 24 frame video in
235
      full without needing to extend due to constant video length. Similar to YTVIS-19, we use bilinear
236
      interpolation to upscale the attention maps during segmentation evaluation. For both YTVIS-19 and
237   MOVi-E we report the video Mean Intersection over Union (mIoU), disregarding the background
238   class. As established by prior methods (Bao et al., 2023; Aydemir et al., 2023; Karazija et al., 2022),
239   we also report the mean per-frame Foreground Adjusted Rand-Index (FG-ARI). Following standard
240   practice, we perform Hungarian matching between the predicted segmentations and the ground truth
241   segmentation maps.
242
243
244   Implementation Details: On the YTVIS-19 dataset we train the first level of interpreter with 32
245   slots and a 4 layer transformer decoder. We keep the dimensionality D1 of the slots equal to that of
246   the 768 dimensional DINOv2 ViT-b embeddings and train with the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter,
247   2018) optimizer for 180 epochs with a batch size of 128, equalling roughly 110k steps. We use a
248   linear learning rate warmup from 1 × 10−4 to a peak of 4 × 10−4 over 5 epochs, followed by cosine
249   annealing down to 2 × 10−6 . For the second level we sample 8 slots with dimension D2 = 768 and
250
      learn a transformer encoder and decoder, each with 4 layers. We also use AdamW for the second
      level, but train with a batch size of 96 for 800 epochs, totalling around 21k steps. We use a slow
251
      linear learning rate warm up from 1 × 10−5 to 2 × 10−4 for 50 epochs followed by a cosine decay
252
      down to 2 × 10−6 . Both levels employ a learnable Gaussian prior in the slot attention module, and
253   are trained with a constant weight decay of 0.005. For the second level we set the clustering distance
254   threshold to 0.15, use a blur value of 0.05 (which controls the level of entropy regularization), and
255   set the scaling to 0.5 (which controls the step size of Sinkhorn Knopp). During training of both
256   levels we only use a simple horizontal flip and no other augmentations.
257
      For the MOVi-E dataset we use nearly the same configuration as for YTVIS-19. We sample 32 slots
258
      at the first level and 20 at the second level. We train the first level for 60 epochs with a batch size of
259   128, totalling around 110k training steps, but warm up the learning rate for only 3 epochs. For the
260   second stage we train for 600 epochs with a batch size of 96, totalling around 47k training steps. We
261   warmup for 50 epochs, and perform consine annealing with the same learning rate configuration as
262   with YTVIS-19. We set the clustering distance threshold to 0.15. During training for both level 1
263   and 2 we use a simple horizontal flip augmentation. Just as with YTVIS-19, we use a blur value of
264   0.05 and a scaling value of 0.5 for the Sinkhorn loss.
265
266
267   Ablations: We perform a small set of ablations on the YTVIS-19 dataset and choose to ablate the
268   number of slots N used at the image level, and the clustering threshold ϵ used during agglomerative
269   clustering at the video level. Note that we train each model for the distance threshold ablation with
      half the number of training steps of the final reported model due to resource constraints.

      4.3   A NALYSIS OF R ESULTS
446
447   We observe a few common failure modes of our proposed method. A common issue on the YTVIS-
448   19 dataset is the spurious clustering of similar entities. This usually presents itself when there are two
449   or more objects of similar appearance, and seems to happen more frequently, though not exclusively,
450   when such objects are in close proximity. Figure 3 shows two examples of this happening on the
451   300 video YTVIS-19 hold-out set. Another failure mode occurs when there are similar entities and
452   significant camera movement. In particular, in cases of rapid camera movement can cause the model
453   to confuse one entity for another due to camera panning. We show an example of this in the bottom
454   row of figure 3.
455   Looking closer at the qualitative results in figure 4 we can see one of the reasons why the perfor-
456   mance suffers on MOVi-E. Ostensibly, the model appears to allocate excess slot capacity at the video
457   level to break up the movement trajectories of objects. Notably, we can see that objects maintain
458   the same slot assignment as they fall through the air, but change assignment as soon as the object
459   hits the ground and motion changes. This is unsurprising considering the compression objective;
460   the model is making use of the allotted representational capacity to best reconstruct the sequence of
461
      image-level object-centric representations. To remedy this the clustering distance threshold ϵ could
      be increased, but this also makes spurious clusterings of adjacent objects more likely. Notably, we
462
      observe qualitatively that MOVi-E is sensitive to under-clustering as ϵ increases, likely due to the
463
      sheer number of objects in each scene.
464
465   The attention maps derived from slot attention have a tendency to over-segment objects, particularly
466   when the background is simple, leading to a signature ”aura” look. An example of this can be
467
      observed in the middle row in figure 2. This might be why our proposed method, which makes use
      of slot attention, falls behind other works such as BA and TimeT (Ding et al., 2024; Salehi et al.,
468
      2023), that do not make use of slot attention, in terms of average per-frame FG-ARI on YTVIS-19.
469
      Supporting this hypothesis is the relatively close performance of prior slot-attention based methods;
470   SMTC, SOLV, and our proposed method score closely at 31.4, 29.1, 28.5 FG-ARI, respectively. On
471   MOVi-E Interpreter scores unexpectedly well in terms of per-frame FG-ARI. This result is
472   consistent with the observations of motion trajectories being segmented into separate parts; while
473   the video-level mIoU score will suffer from segmented motion, the per-frame FG-ARI results should
474   not. Regardless, the performance in terms of FG-ARI on MOVi-E is surprising.
475

      5     C ONCLUSION
477
478
      5.1   S UMMARY
479
480   We have introduced Interpreter, a novel method for efficient video-level representation learn-
481   ing that leverages a hierarchical slot attention architecture with local learning objectives. By build-
482   ing upon pre-trained image backbones and avoiding the reconstruction of full frame feature maps,
483   Interpreter scales effectively to longer videos without the need for chunking and matching. We
484   enable this novel hierarchical approach by introducing a method for propagating attention maps be-
485   tween slot attention layers and a method for learning to reconstruct unordered sets of object-centric
      representations using the Sinkhorn divergence distance metric.


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Experimental results demonstrate that Interpreter achieves state-of-the-art performance on the
487   YTVIS-19 dataset in terms of mean Intersection over Union, outperforming existing methods by a
488   significant margin. While there are limitations observed on synthetic datasets like MOVi-E, where
489   the method underperforms compared to previous works, Interpreter represents a notable ad-
490   vancement in object-centric video representation learning as it offers a scalable and efficient ap-
491   proach for processing entire videos.
492
493   5.2   F UTURE W ORK
494
495   We see a need for better methods of dynamically selecting the number of slots used during training
496   and inference time. The agglomerative clustering approach helps to alleviate over-clustering in some
497
      scenarios, but it can lead under-clustering in others. A better method of deciding the number of slots
      would likely improve our proposed method.
498
499   We empirically find that Interpreter is particularly sensitive to masking. During our experi-
500   ments we tried temporal masking for when videos are too short to fill the context window, such as
501   is the case with YTVIS-19, but found that it often lead to divergence during training. While it is
502   possible to mask, we found that extending the sequence through repetition to be an easier solution
503
      that does not adversely affect results. A future direction of research could try to address this issue.
504   The hierarchical slot attention method we propose is general and could be extended to any depth. It
505   would be interesting to see it applied with more levels than two, which would allow the method to
506   be extended to even longer videos and would result in multi-level segmentation hierarchies.
