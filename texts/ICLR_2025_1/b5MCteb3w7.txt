011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper explores the emergence of in-context learning (ICL) in reinforcement
014            learning (RL) environments, focusing on how transformers can surpass Bayesian
015            inference limitations. We investigate the critical role of task diversity in enabling
016
               transformers to develop advanced learning algorithms for RL. To overcome the
               limitations of existing RL environments in providing sufficient task variety, we in-
017
               troduce a novel benchmark based on the Omniglot dataset, offering unprecedented
018
               task diversity. Through extensive experimentation, we demonstrate that increasing
019            task diversity leads to significant improvements in transformers’ ability to gener-
020            alize to unseen tasks. We examine the effects of model capacity, regularization
021            techniques, and action representation on ICL performance. Our findings reveal
022            that larger model capacities and specific augmentation strategies contribute to en-
023            hanced ICL capabilities. Notably, we observe a clear transition from Bayesian
024            inference-like behavior to more advanced learning paradigms as task diversity
025            increases. This research provides crucial insights into the factors driving general-
026            izable in-context reinforcement learning in transformers and underscores the im-
027
               portance of designing RL environments with qualitatively diverse tasks to unlock
               the full potential of ICL in RL scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), with their widespread applications across numerous fields and
      substantial evidence of effectiveness, have become a cornerstone in modern machine learning. These
034
      models, particularly when pretrained on extensive text corpora, have demonstrated remarkable pro-
035
      ficiency in transferring knowledge to related downstream tasks through finetuning on smaller, task-
036   specific datasets (Devlin et al., 2018; Howard & Ruder, 2018; Radford et al., 2019). A striking
037   capability emerges when these pretrained transformers are exposed to training datasets character-
038   ized by high task diversity: they adapt to new tasks directly through the examples in their context,
039   without the need for further training (Brown et al., 2020). This phenomenon, termed in-context
040   learning (ICL), contrasts starkly with other settings such as supervised learning (SL) and reinforce-
041   ment learning (RL), where acquiring large, diverse datasets poses a significant challenge (Levine
042   et al., 2020).
043   The exploration of in-context learning within supervised learning, particularly in regression and
044   classification, has been extensively documented (Kirsch et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
045   2023; Wies et al., 2023; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023). It has been pro-
046   posed that transformers might implicitly learn various learning algorithms, such as ridge regression,
047   gradient descent, and Bayesian inference, influenced by factors like task diversity and transformer
048   architecture (Xie et al., 2021; Akyürek et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023). Moreover, train-
049   ing regimes that deviate from Bayesian principles have been observed with increased task diversity
050
      (Raventós et al., 2023) or the distribution of training data (Chan et al., 2022).
051   In the realm of reinforcement learning, in-context learning has garnered attention, particularly where
052   models are prompted with trajectories or timestep transitions in the hope of learning from demon-
053   strations. Signs of ICL in reinforcement learning has been shown to emerge through direct training
      (Melo, 2022), prompt tuning (Hu et al., 2023), supervised training (Lee et al., 2023), and leveraging

054
      pretrained large language models (Reid et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). Team et al. (2023) studies task
055   diversity in a closed-source domain where they do not share their trained models nor training code.
056   Furthermore, its online training of its agents renders its results unrelated for many domains that can
057   mostly focus on offline training (e.g., robotics). Lu et al. (2024) is another work that focuses on
058   online training. However, they also note that they achieve limited generalization to unseen control
059   tasks. Kirsch et al. (2023) train their agent as more of a learning-to-RL style on offline trajectories.
060   Their choice of simple augmentation to increase task diversity again results in limited performance
061   on unseen tasks. Raparthy et al. (2023) shows transformers trained on a sequence of expert trajecto-
062   ries can have limited generalization to unseen game levels. Furthermore, this work does not show the
063
      scaling with massive task diversity. Since they only have at most 12-16 tasks, they cannot reliably
      show the scaling as we can show in our experiments for more than 16k tasks. Despite these efforts,
064
      a comprehensive understanding of the generalization capabilities of these models, particularly the
065
      factors contributing to such capabilities, remains elusive. While the Bayesian inference regime has
066   been achieved in some instances (Laskin et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023), the gen-
067   eralization to unseen tasks continues to be a significant hurdle (Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023).
068   Therefore, as far as we are aware, our work is the first that can show this transition with respect to
069   the number of tasks used in the pretraining.
070
      In RL, agents learn by interacting with an environment, where they must make a series of deci-
071
      sions that affect future states and rewards (Sutton & Barto, 2018). This dynamic nature introduces
072   complexities such as temporal credit assignment, where agents must learn which actions lead to
073   rewards over time (Mnih et al., 2015), and the exploration-exploitation dilemma (Auer et al., 2002;
074   Bellemare et al., 2016), which requires balancing the discovery of new strategies against optimizing
075   known ones. The context in RL, therefore, involves understanding not just the immediate outcomes
076   of actions, but also their long-term effects and dependencies (De Asis et al., 2018). This makes in-
077   context learning in RL significantly more challenging, as models must infer not only the immediate
078   consequences of actions but also their impact on future states and rewards. Meta-RL shows that
079   learning new but similar environments can still be quite challenging even with the ability to finetune
080
      the model. Therefore, if the environments are separate enough, we cannot hope to learn new policies
      for those environments quickly in the in-context RL setting as well. Therefore, we utilize a common
081
      idea in the NLP domain for LLMs, prompting with demonstrations. In ICRL, the demonstrations
082
      are expert trajectories and the hope is that the pretrained model will be able to learn from those
083   to more quickly adapt to new environments. ICRL has several desired advantages over Meta-RL.
084   Firstly, the model does not need to be finetuned which is much more computationally expensive
085   than inferencing. Secondly, Meta-RL typically requires many episodes within the new environment
086   to adapt, whereas ICRL has the possibility to learn from a few trajectories, and in our experiments
087   section we show this to be true.
088
      A crucial challenge we identify is the scarcity of task diversity in current RL environments. Our pre-
089   liminary investigations revealed that simple modifications in environments such as MuJoCo, such
090   as altering limb lengths and weights or setting different goal velocities, fall short in providing suf-
091   ficient task diversity for learning new behaviors when prompted. This observation has driven us to
092   develop a new RL environment with a vastly greater range of tasks. Our environment is designed to
093   probe the question, “What enables in-context reinforcement learning?” Through extensive experi-
094   mentation, we observe significant factors that lead to the emergence of non-Bayesian learning in RL
095   settings. Alongside increasing task diversity, we also explore additional dimensions that contribute
096   to this phenomenon, including model architecture and regularization techniques.
097   A critical discovery of our study is the intricate relationship between task diversity and the per-
098   formance of transformers in RL settings. When the task diversity is limited, transformers tend to
099   emulate a form of Bayesian inference or posterior sampling based predominantly on the tasks they
100   have encountered. This approach, while effective in known scenarios, leads to suboptimal outcomes
101   when dealing with out-of-distribution, unseen tasks. However, our research reveals a significant shift
102   in this pattern as we increase task diversity. With a broader spectrum of tasks during the pretraining
103
      phase, transformers demonstrate remarkable improvements in handling unseen tasks, exhibiting en-
      hanced adaptability and efficiency. This observation is not only pivotal in understanding transformer
104
      behavior in RL environments but also illuminates a new pathway for designing RL environments.
105
      By prioritizing task diversity, we can develop environments that nurture more robust and versatile
106   agents, capable of superior performance in a wide array of scenarios.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
125
      Meta-reinforcement learning The aim of meta-learning, also referred to as learning-to-learn, is
126
      designing techniques that yield models that can quickly adapt to unseen tasks (Schmidhuber, 1987;
127   Finn et al., 2017). In reinforcement learning, new tasks can differ by transition probabilities, reward
128   functions, state-action spaces or constraints (Mitchell et al., 2021; Zintgraf et al., 2021; Khattar et al.,
129   2022). The possible ways of adapting meta models include finetuning (Gupta et al., 2018; Sel et al.,
130   2023b; Padalkar et al., 2023), using regressive models to extract latent features from demonstrations
131   (Zintgraf et al., 2019; Dorfman et al., 2021; Gehring et al., 2022; Khattar & Jin, 2023) or Bayesian
132   inference (Humplik et al., 2019; Rothfuss et al., 2021).
133
134   In-context learning. ICL is a form of meta-learning more attributed to autoregressive models such
135   as RNNs (Rumelhart et al., 1986; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and transformers (Vaswani
136
      et al., 2017) underlining the parameter-update-free version of meta-learning. These models, when
      trained on sufficiently diverse and large datasets, can learn new tasks only by being provided exam-
137
      ples into their context (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022; Sel et al., 2023a). Transformers can
138
      also portray the ability to infer tasks when prompted with trajectories (Laskin et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
139   2022; Lee et al., 2023). In this paper, we investigate the emergence of in-context out-of-distribution
140   reinforcement learning through autoregressive pretraining.

277   4     E XPERIMENTS
278
279   In this section, we embark on a comprehensive exploration to investigate the impact of pretraining
280   task diversity on the generalization capabilities of transformers in reinforcement learning (RL) en-
281
      vironments, especially concerning their adaptability to tasks not encountered during training. This
      inquiry is crucial for advancing our understanding of in-context learning (ICL) within the domain of
282
      RL.
283
284   Our experimental framework is designed to address a fundamental question: “How does increas-
285   ing task diversity in RL environments enable transformers to surpass the limitations of traditional
286   learning paradigms?” To explore this, we recognized the necessity of an RL environment capable
287
      of presenting a vast array of distinct tasks and allowing control over their diversity. However, our
      attempts to find or adapt existing RL environments such as MuJoCo (Todorov et al., 2012), follow-
288
      ing a number of prior meta-RL works that studied quick adaptation in similar settings (Nagabandi
289
      et al., 2018; Rakelly et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2020; Pong et al., 2022), proved inadequate. While these
290   environments permit the creation of numerous variations by minor modifications in reward functions
291   or agent model configurations, they fall short in generating the level of task diversity required for
292   our study, leading to suboptimal generalization in unseen tasks during testing (see Figure 2).
293
      Acknowledging this gap, we repurposed the Omniglot dataset, primarily utilized in supervised learn-
294
      ing tasks, to develop a novel RL benchmark. This benchmark is tailored to provide the extensive task
295
      variety essential for our question, enabling us to thoroughly examine how task diversity influences
296   transformers’ ability to learn and generalize beyond their training experiences. Our experiments
297   delve into multiple facets of this phenomenon. We meticulously evaluate how transformers, tradi-
298   tionally bounded by Bayesian inference, evolve in their learning capabilities as they are exposed
299   to an increasingly diverse range of tasks. This is akin to showing the transition from MθF−PS to
300   MθE−PS , where the latter might have been seen as unrealistic. We also investigate the effects of
301   model architecture, regularization techniques, and other relevant factors on this learning process.
302   The effect of action representation, such as continuous or discrete inputs is explored in Appendix
303   B.1 due to space constraints.
304
305   4.1   N OVEL ICRL E NVIRONMENT
306
307   The Omniglot dataset (Lake et al., 2019) serves as the foundation for our novel in-context reinforce-
308   ment learning (ICRL) environment. This dataset is a rich collection of over 19,000 handwritten
309
      character images derived from a diverse range of alphabets and contributors. A unique feature
      of Omniglot is the inclusion of ‘strokes’ data, which provides a sequential representation of how
310
      each character is written. Leveraging this aspect, we have transformed Omniglot into an innovative
311
      benchmark for few-shot ICRL.
312
313   In our setup, we select n examples from each character along with their corresponding stroke se-
314   quences. These are then concatenated to form the input for our transformer model. The primary
315
      task in this environment is to learn the writing of a character based on provided demonstrations.
      The action space for the agent involves deciding where to place strokes on a canvas decided by the
316
      task transition dynamics, and the reward function is defined as the negative Euclidean distance from
317
      the agent’s action to the ground truth stroke placement. Therefore, it is not possible for an agent to
318   write a character correctly when they only view the goal state. However, with provided demonstra-
319   tions, the learner can understand the transition dynamics together with the exact stroke sequence to
320   correctly write any new character. A key aspect of our environment is the control over task diver-
321   sity, which we manage by selecting specific characters for inclusion. As we increase the variety of
322   characters, the diversity of pretraining tasks correspondingly expands. This approach allows us to
323   systematically investigate how varying levels of task diversity impact the in-context learning abilities
      of our models.

      4.2   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
332
333   We use a GPT-2 transformer (Radford et al., 2019) with head and embedding layers removed. Ad-
334   ditionally, for processing images of handwritten characters, we finetune a pretrained ResNet model
335   (He et al., 2016). Our approach utilizes projection matrices for both the input and output actions
336   to match the dimensions of these different modalities, a technique employed commonly (Liu et al.,
337   2024). We show these parts in Figure 1, together with the trajectories are represented.
338
339   Image Embeddings. Our methodology incorporates a finetuned, pretrained ResNet model with
340   configurations of 18, 34, and 50 layers (He et al., 2016). The original pre-trained model lacked the
341   precision needed for accurately embedding the specific locations of strokes within the images. To
342
      address this, we adopted a scaled-down version of our training setup. This involved only four inputs
      to the entire model, consisting of two one-step horizon trajectories with corresponding images and
343
      a random point within the strokes, along with another image matched to the same corresponding
344
      image. Through this fine-tuning process, the ResNet model effectively learns to identify and extract
345   stroke locations from the images.
346
347
      Action Embeddings. Despite the prevalence of individual tokenization of action dimensions in
348   existing literature (Janner et al., 2021; Brohan et al., 2022), we found no significant advantage in
349   applying this technique to our framework (see Figure 9). Instead, we implement a straightforward
350   projection layer to acquire the action embeddings. This involves the use of a single-layer perceptron
351   without an activation function.
352
353   Transformer. For our transformer model, we select a GPT-2 architecture (Radford et al., 2019)
354   and modify it by incorporating 4 to 16 layers with embedding dimensions ranging from 16 to 1024.
355   We exclude the original embedding and token head layers and introduce projection layers tailored
356   for image embeddings, input actions, and output actions. A decision was made against utilizing a
357   pre-trained GPT-2 model. The primary reason is the unavailability of pre-trained GPT-2 models in
358   the various layer and embedding dimension formats required for our study.
359
360   Action heads. We again employ a single-layer perceptron, this time to down-project the final
361   embedding produced by the transformer to derive the actions. The Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss
362
      is used, as it allows us to directly obtain continuous actions without the need for tokenization per
      each action dimension. Other possible choices could include per action dimension outputs (Janner
363
      et al., 2021) or diffusion action heads (Chi et al., 2023).

459   The larger models that possess ICL at test time and the smaller model that do not have similar ICL
460   capabilities during training as shown in Figure 5. This behavior is also seen in large language model

462   transformer is performing worse than the 6-layer transformer. This can be attributed to potential
463   overfitting when pretraining task counts are not the highest in our environment. However, as we
464   increase the layer counts further, we see that models are able to obtain generalization. We believe
465
      this hints at a potential double descent phenomenon.
466   Throughout the models tested, we observe that no model demonstrates ICL with an embedding
467   size smaller than 128. This finding underscores the pivotal role of embedding size. Even with the
468   minimal complexity of a 4-layer model, ICL is attainable with an embedding size of 1024. This
469   indicates that embedding size, rather than layer count, is a more significant factor for achieving ICL
470   in this task.
471   The analysis of the ResNet models tells a parallel story as shown in Figure 6. The pretrained models,
472   while proficient on a general dataset like ImageNet, were not immediately optimal for our environ-
473   ment’s image data. This misalignment necessitated fine-tuning to adjust the models to the specifics
474   of our image data, which includes not just the characters themselves but also the crucial positional
475   information not inherently prioritized in ImageNet training. This is in accordance with the literature
476   hinting at the lack of spatial awareness of vision models simply trained on images and their captions

       C    T HE T RANSITION FROM BAYESIAN I NFERENCE
973
974
       We give the motivation for our proposed RL benchmarks, by visualizing the Bayesian inference
975    on a limited number of tasks in Figure 2 in the MuJoCo HalfCheetah environment (please see the
976    explanation of that figure below). Further, in Figures 3-5 we show this transition from the Bayesian
977    inference on pretraining tasks to a Bayesian inference to a task distribution for the way transformers
978    change their in-context learning method as we increase task diversity by marking the gap in total
979    cumulative reward in training and test tasks.
980
       In order to visualize our main takeaway, we have prompted our models pretrained with task diversity
981    from N = 2 to N = 2048 similar to the ones shown in Figure 3 in our paper, by the English letters.
982    More specifically, we choose two handwritten characters from each letter in the alphabet. We insert
983    the image and strokes sequence of the first one together with the image of the second one into the
984    context of our pretrained models. Then we let it generate the action sequences. We visualize this
985    in Figures 10-11. As can be seen, when the task diversity is lower than 2048, we see it a trajectory
986    generated a character from its pretraining dataset. In that figure, this is most obvious for N = 2,
987    N = 8 and N = 32 since the trajectories for some of them are similar. However, when N = 2048
988    we see a good representation of the true actions to the ones shown by the expert. We believe this is
989
       a clear evidence of the shift in the in-context learning method. The visualizations are also consistent
       with the Figure 3 in our paper, where we a sudden dropout going from N = 512 to N = 2048.
