011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Pretraining and finetuning models has become increasingly popular in decision-
014            making. But there are still serious impediments in Imitation Learning from Obser-
015            vation (ILfO) with pretrained models. This study identifies two primary obstacles:
016            the Embodiment Knowledge Barrier (EKB) and the Demonstration Knowledge
017            Barrier (DKB). The EKB emerges due to the pretrained models’ limitations in han-
018
               dling novel observations, which leads to inaccurate action inference. Conversely,
               the DKB stems from the reliance on limited demonstration datasets, restricting
019
               the model’s adaptability across diverse scenarios. We propose separate solutions
020
               to overcome each barrier and apply them to Action Inference by Maximising
021            Evidence (AIME), a state-of-the-art algorithm. This new algorithm, AIME-NoB,
022            integrates online interactions and a data-driven regulariser to mitigate the EKB.
023            Additionally, it uses a surrogate reward function to broaden the policy’s supported
024            states, addressing the DKB. Our experiments on vision-based control tasks from
025            the DeepMind Control Suite and MetaWorld benchmarks show that AIME-NoB
026            significantly improves sample efficiency and converged performance, presenting a
027            robust framework for overcoming the challenges in ILfO with pretrained models.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   We have been going through a paradigm shift from learning from scratch to pretraining and finetuning,
033   in particular in Computer Vision (CV) (He et al., 2016; Radford et al., 2021; He et al., 2022) and
034   Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al.; Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron
035   et al., 2023a;b) fields due to the increasing availability of foundation models (Bommasani et al.,
036   2021) and ever-growing datasets. However, it is still unclear how to adapt this new paradigm into
037   decision-making, in particular what type of models we need to pretrain and how these models can be
038   adapted to solve downstream tasks. Recent work (Zhang et al., 2023; DeMoss et al., 2023; Sekar
039
      et al., 2020; Rajeswar et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2023a) showed that pretrained latent space world
      models enable successful and efficient transfer to new tasks with either reinforcement learning (Sekar
040
      et al., 2020; Rajeswar et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2023a) or Imitation Learning from Observation
041
      (ILfO) (Zhang et al., 2023; DeMoss et al., 2023). ILfO (Torabi et al., 2018; 2019; Baker et al., 2022;
042   Zhang et al., 2023; DeMoss et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a), especially from videos (Baker et al., 2022;
043   Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a; DeMoss et al., 2023), is a more promising approach in this new
044   paradigm since it does not require a handcrafted reward function which is hard to define for many
045   real-world tasks.
046
      But there are challenges when using pretrained models in ILfO. To quantify these, we introduce
047
      two new barriers, which we call the Embodiment Knowledge Barrier (EKB) and the Demonstration
048   Knowledge Barrier (DKB). The EKB describes the limitation of a pretrained model when confronted
049   with novel observations and actions beyond its training experience. The DKB describes the generali-
050   sation from a limited number of expert demonstrations in imitation learning (Ho & Ermon, 2016).
051   State-of-the-art approaches such as BCO(0) (Torabi et al., 2018) and AIME (Zhang et al., 2023)
052   typically suffer from these two knowledge barriers. First, these algorithms depend on the pretrained
053   model to infer missing actions from observation sequences. Thus, when the model has not seen a
      specific observation before, it may not know enough about the embodiment to infer the correct action.

080   Second, if the policy optimisation is only guided by limited demonstrations, it can lead to a policy
081   that generalises poorly, working well in some states but not in others.
082   To better showcase the two barriers, in Figure 1, we evaluate both AIME (Zhang et al., 2023) and
083   BCO(0) (Torabi et al., 2018) and their oracle versions w.r.t. different number of demonstrations on
084   walker-run task. Both algorithms pretrain a model from a large embodiment dataset and use that to
085   infer the actions for the observation-only demonstrations. The oracle versions remove the need to
086   infer the missing actions by providing the algorithm with the true actions, thus removing the EKB.
087   As we can see from the figure, the two algorithms are always upper-bounded by the corresponding
088   oracle version, and the difference between them represents the EKB. On the other hand, even if given
089
      the true actions of the expert, imitation performance may still be impacted by a limited number of
      demonstrations providing insufficient coverage of the state space. Thus, the difference between the
090
      oracle version and the expert performance represents the DKB.
091
092   In this paper, we study how to overcome these barriers to improve the performance of ILfO approaches
093   with pretrained models, in particular of AIME. For the EKB, we extend the setting from offline to
094   online by allowing the agent to further interact with the environment to gather more data to train the
095
      world model. While for the DKB, we introduce a surrogate reward function to allow the policy to
      essentially train on more data. We demonstrate that the proposed modifications effectively overcome
096
      the two barriers and significantly improve the performance on nine tasks in DeepMind Control Suite
097
      (DMC) (Tunyasuvunakool et al., 2020) and six tasks in MetaWorld (Yu et al., 2021).
098

115

116

117   space, and Ω : S → O is the emission function. The goal is to find a policy       π : S → A which
118

      maximises the expected accumulated reward, or return, i.e. R(π) = Ea∼π [ t rt ]. Since in this work
119   we focus on imitation learning, this oracle reward is not available to the agent. We mainly use this
120   reward to quantify the performance of the learnt policies.
121
      We presume the existence of three datasets of the same embodiment available to our agent. The
122
      embodiment dataset Dbody contains trajectories {o0 , a0 , o1 , a1 . . . } that represent past experiences
123   of interacting with the environment. This dataset provides information about the embodiment for the
124   algorithm to learn a world model. In addition, we also allow the agent to interact with the environment
125   to collect new data in a replay buffer Donline . Note that, although the simulator will give us the
126   reward information, the agent is not allowed to use them, and we only use the reward for evaluation
127   purposes. The demonstration dataset Ddemo contains a few expert trajectories {o0 , o1 , o2 . . . } of the
128   embodiment solving a certain task defined by Rdemo . The crucial difference between this dataset and
129   the other two datasets is that the actions are not provided anymore since they are not observable from
130   a third-person perspective. The goal of our agent is to learn a policy π from Ddemo which can solve
131
      the task defined by Rdemo as well as the expert πdemo who generated Ddemo .

133   2.1   W ORLD M ODELS
134
      A World Model (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) is a generative model which models a probability
135   distribution over sequences of observations, i.e. p(o1:T ). The model can be either unconditioned
136   or conditioned on other factors, such as previous observations or actions. When the actions taken
137   are known, they can be considered as the condition, i.e. p(o1:T |a0:T −1 ), and the model is called
138   embodied (Zhang et al., 2023). In this paper, we consider variational latent world models where
139   the observation is governed by a Markovian hidden state. This type of model is also referred to as
140   a State-Space Model (SSM) (Karl et al., 2017; Hafner et al., 2019b;a; Becker-Ehmck et al., 2019;
141   Klushyn et al., 2021). Such a variational latent world model involves four components, namely
142                encoder zt = fϕ (ot ),                   posterior st ∼ qϕ (st |st−1 , at−1 , zt ),
143                decoder ot ∼ pθ (ot |st ),                   prior st ∼ pθ (st |st−1 , at−1 ).

      3.1   OVERCOMING THE EKB
193
194   The most natural way to overcome the EKB is to allow the agent to further interact with the
195   environment, similar to how we humans practice for a novel skill. New experiences can minimise the
196   error in the pretrained model near the policy πψ and enhance task-specific embodiment knowledge.
197   Torabi et al. (2018) proposed a modified version of BCO(0) called BCO(α) introducing such an
198   interaction phase. However, empirical results show it did not overcome the EKB as a gap remains
199   with the BC oracle when the environment is complex. In fact, as we will show in the following, the
200
      idea of adding online interactions is not straightforward to successfully implement in practice.
201   As shown in recent works in Offline RL, continuing training of an actor-critic from the offline phase
202   in the online phase requires certain measures to combat the shift of objective (Lee et al., 2022; Ball
203   et al., 2023; Nakamoto et al., 2023). A similar story also applies when extending AIME from purely
204   offline to online. The most dominant problem we found is overfitting to the newly collected dataset.
205   As the training progresses alternating between data collection, model training and policy training, in
206   the early phase of training there are only very few new trajectories available for training the model.
207   Because the world model is highly expressive, it may overly favour similar trajectories, especially the
208   action sequence, in the new data, leading to a high ELBO. Normally, this may not be a big problem
209   since, eventually, more and more data will be collected to combat this overfitting. But since AIME
210   also depends on the ELBO to train the policy, it quickly causes the policy training to diverge. That is
211   to say, when the model is extensively trained on a small amount of data, it not only maximises the
212
      conditional likelihood log(o1:T |a0:T −1 ) but also maximises the marginal likelihood log(·|a0:T −1 ),
      which diverges the likelihood-based action inference process.
213
214   In order to address the overfitting issue, we need a regulariser for model learning. Instead of designing
215   ad-hoc methods to regularise the model in the parameter space, we adopt a data-driven approach.
      From the model’s perspective, the overfitting is caused by a sudden shift of the training data from a

217   the most straightforward method is to just append new data to a replay buffer that is pre-filled with

219   is relatively little compared to the big pretraining dataset. Uniformly sampling from the joint replay
220   buffer hence overly limits usage of the new data. Instead, we suggest sampling separately from both
221   datasets. We modify the model-learning objective Jmodel in Equation (2) to

      This finding contradicts Rajeswar et al. (2023) and Hansen et al. (2023a), where the pretrained world

      3.2   OVERCOMING THE DKB
233
234   Based on the discussion from the previous sections, the straightforward way of overcoming the
235   DKB is also to increase the number of demonstrations available to the agent. However, expert
236   demonstrations are difficult and expensive to collect. Increasing the size of the demonstration dataset
237   is not always feasible in real-world applications. In order to propose a more practical solution, we
238   need to look deeper into what is the real cause of the DKB.
239   The policy-learning part of the AIME algorithm is essentially behaviour cloning, and it is only
240   conducted on the demonstration dataset. So for the states covered in the demonstration dataset, the
241   policy is given clear guidance about what to do, while for other states, the behaviour is undefined.
242   AIME solely relies on the generalisation abilities of the learned latent state and the trained policy
243   network to extrapolate the correct behaviour. In particular for small demonstration datasets, this can
244   be unreliable or even impossible. Therefore, if we were able to enlarge the space of the covered states,
245   we should reduce the DKB (Ross et al., 2011).
246   Based on these insights, we propose to introduce a surrogate reward providing a guiding signal for

      NoB compare with state-of-the-art methods on common benchmarks? Q2: How well do the proposed

286   4.1   DATASETS AND TASKS
287
288   For the DMC benchmark, we choose nine tasks across six embodiments following Liu et al. (2022a)
289   and use the same published dataset (Haldar et al., 2022) as the demonstration datasets. Each dataset
290   contains only 10 trajectories to reflect the scarcity of expert demonstrations. For the embodiment
291   dataset, in order not to leak the task information from the pretraining phase, we follow Rajeswar et al.
292   (2023) and run a Plan2Explore (Sekar et al., 2020) agent for each embodiment with 2M environments
293
      steps and use its replay buffer as the embodiment dataset. Different to them taking the model directly
      from the Plan2Explore agent as the pretrained model, we follow Zhang et al. (2023) to retrain the
294
      model for 200k gradient steps to get a better model. When evaluating the performance of the learned
295
      policy on each task, we rollout the policy 10 times with the environment, and report the mean return.
296
297   For vision-based MetaWorld benchmark, we use the data from Hansen et al. (2023a). The embodiment
298   dataset was created from the replay buffer datasets. The open-sourced replay buffer datasets contain
299
      40k trajectories for each of the 50 tasks with only state information. In order to fit to our image
      observation setup, we render the images by resetting the environment to the initial state of each
300
      trajectory and then executing the action sequence. The details can be found in Appendix F.
301
302   With respect to the embodiment dataset, following the idea of not leaking too much about the task
303   information, inspired by the common practice in offline RL benchmarks (Fu et al., 2021), we use the
304   first 200 trajectories from each replay buffer and form a dataset with 10k trajectories in total. We call
305
      this dataset MW-mt50 and we use it for the benchmark on MetaWorld to compare AIME-NoB with
      other algorithms. To further study the out-of-distribution transfer ability of the pretrained model, we
306
      follow the difficulty classification of the tasks from (Seo et al., 2022a) and only use the 39 easy and
307
      medium difficulty tasks to generate the datasets and use the 11 hard and very hard tasks as hold-out
308   tasks. We uniformly sample 250 trajectories from the first 10k trajectories from each of the 39 tasks
309   and form a dataset with 9750 trajectories in total. We refer to this dataset as MW-mt39.
310
311
      For evaluating the algorithms, we choose four hard or very hard tasks, namely disassemble, assembly,
      hand-insert and push; and two medium difficult tasks, namely sweep and hammer. As for the
312
      demonstration datasets, we use the single-task policies open-sourced by TD-MPC2 and collect 50
313
      trajectories for each task. We ensure that every trajectory in the demonstration dataset is successful.
314   For evaluation on each MetaWorld task, due to the noisy nature of the task, we rollout the policy
315   100 times with the environments, and report success only if the very last time step of an episode is
316   marked as successful by the environment (following Hansen et al. (2023a)).

      Q3.5: How do different values of the gradient loss weight β influence the performance? We set
479
      the weight β from [0.0, 0.1, 1.0, 10.0, 100.0] and plot the results in Figure 6c. As the result shows,
480   without the surrogate reward, i.e. β = 0, the agent cannot reach expert performance due to the DKB.
481   Having a small β slows learning progress toward convergence. On the other hand, setting β to a
482   much larger value will improve the sample-efficiency without causing instability. For the sample
483   efficiency, since we only have 10 demonstrations, the DKB dominates over the EKB as shown in
484   Figure 3. Thus, having a larger β will speed up learning. In terms of stability, as we discussed in 3.2,
485   AIME loss and the value gradient loss operate on different regions of the environment states. This
      could make their influence on the policy independent of each other.

      Q3.6: Is surrogate reward all you need? Given the results above that AIME-NoB can work well
487   even when we lower the effect of the AIME loss by making either worse action inference, i.e. set α
488   to low value, or strengthen the value gradient loss, i.e. set β to high value, a natural question to ask is
489   whether the AIME loss is still needed in AIME-NoB or whether surrogate reward only is enough to
490   solve the imitation learning task. The AIME loss is used in two places in the AIME-NoB algorithm
491   – both for pretraining the policy offline and finetuning the policy online. We compare AIME-NoB
492   with variants that removes AIME from these parts. Form the results shown in Figure 6b, removing
493   AIME loss from either pretraining or finetuning will lower the sample efficiency. Removing from
494   both phases causes instability and convergence issues during training. Thus, AIME loss is still crucial
495
      and cannot simply be replaced by surrogate rewards.

497   5    R ELATED W ORK
498
499   Imitation Learning from Observation. ILfO (Torabi et al., 2018; 2019; DeMoss et al., 2023; Li
500   et al., 2023; Baker et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022a) becomes more popular in recent
501   years due to their potential to utilise internet-scale videos for behaviour learning. Most of the previous
502   works (Torabi et al., 2018; 2019; Li et al., 2023; Kidambi et al., 2021) study the problem only with
503   the true state as observation. Recent works (DeMoss et al., 2023; Baker et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
504
      2023; Liu et al., 2022a) have started to shift toward image observations as a more general setting.
      Only few works (Zhang et al., 2023; Torabi et al., 2018) can leverage pretrained models. Our work is
505
      a continuation of this journey and further emphasise the performance benefit from pretrained models.
506
507   Pretrained Models for Decision-Making. Inspired by the tremendous progress made in recent years
508   in CV and NLP fields with the power of pretrained models, the decision-making community is also
509   trying to follow the trend. Most recent works focus on the use of Large Language Model (LLM) for
510
      decision-making. A prompted model is used for producing trajectories and plans (Chen et al., 2024;
      Huang et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022; Di Palo et al., 2023), code (Vemprala et al., 2023; Liang et al.,
511
      2023; Singh et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023) or for modifying the reward (Ma et al.,
512
      2023; Mahmoudieh et al., 2022). There are also other people studying the benefit of pretrained visual
513   models for visuomotor tasks (Shah & Kumar, 2021; Majumdar et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2023b;
514   Parisi et al., 2022) while others try to train large policy networks directly with transformers (Vaswani
515   et al., 2017) and huge datasets (Brohan et al., 2022; Brohan et al.; Reed et al., 2022). However, there
516   is only little attention being put on pretrained world models (Zhang et al., 2023; Rajeswar et al., 2023;
517   Sekar et al., 2020), which are natively developed by the model-based decision-making community
518   and perfectly fit into the pretraining and finetuning paradigm. Our work explores this overlooked
519   domain and showcases its potential.
520
521   6    D ISCUSSION
522
523   In this paper, we identify two knowledge barriers, namely the EKB and the DKB, which limit the
524   performance of state-of-the-art ILfO methods using pretrained models. We thoroughly analyse the
525   underlying cause of each barrier and propose practical solutions. Specifically, we propose to use
526   online interaction with a data-driven regulariser to overcome the EKB and surrogate reward labelling
527   to reduce the DKB. Combining these solutions, we propose AIME-NoB and showcase its supreme
528   efficiency compared to SOTA ILfO methods. Our ablation studies show how each knowledge barrier
529   is addressed by the proposed solution and how different design choices influence the performance.
530   However, there are still limitations for the scope of this study. First, this work is mainly an empirical
531   study. Some theoretical results could enhance the understanding of the knowledge barriers. Second, as
532   suggested by the ablation study, a careful design for a schedule of α could further improve the sample
533   efficiency. Third, due to the high demand of computing resources, we only study the pretrained world
534   model on a rather small scale, i.e. the biggest model has only 20M parameters. It will be interesting
535   to study how well the algorithm scales to larger models. Lastly, although we have shown a single
536
      pretrained world model can be used for multiple tasks, the power of pretrained world models are not
      fully realized. It would be interesting to see how world models can be used to train a multi-tasks
537
      policy. These limitations provide directions for future works.
538
539   We hope our work can shed some light on the future development of ILfO method and bring more
      attention to the great potential of pretrained world models.

954   In the original paper, the authors first pretrain a VQ-GAN (Esser et al., 2021) from a multi-tasks
955
      expert dataset, and then train a GPT-style auto-regressive model in the quantised space for prediction.
      For a fair comparison with other variant, we consider to only train the VIPER model on the single
956
      demonstration dataset for the task. For simplicity of the implementation, in this paper, we consider
957
      training an unconditioned latent world model as in Seo et al. (2022b) to model the VIPER reward.
958   We use the same RSSM architecture of the model learning for DMC, only removing the condition of
959   the actions, and we train the VIPER model for each task separately. Especially during training, we
960   find training such a powerful model from scratch on a small dataset can easily result in over-fitting.
961   Thus, we empirically choose to train the model only for 500 gradient steps for DMC models and
962   1000 gradient steps for MetaWorld models. We show evidence of overfitting in Appendix H.1. Due to
963   the large scale of the ELBO, we also apply symlog (Hafner et al., 2023) when computing the VIPER
964   reward. Another difference with the original VIPER paper is that we do not use intrinsic motivation

991    F    D ETAILS FOR R ESETTING M ETAW ORLD TASKS
992
993    To generate the image observation datasets from the TD-MPC2 replay buffer (Hansen et al., 2023a),
994    we modify the MetaWorld codebase to reset the environment to the initial state of the trajectory from
995    the first observation. Luckily, the starting position of the robot arm is always the same for each task,
996    so that we do not need to apply inverse kinematics to solve for the initial pose of the robot arm. For
997    the object and the target position, for most of the tasks, the internal reset position can be computed by
998    making a constant shift on the object position and the target position in the observations. There are,
999    however, also a few edge cases which we handle differently.
1000   In button-press-topdown and button-press-topdown-wall, the object’s true position only appears in
1001   the observation upon the second time step, presumably due to some simulator delay in the resetting
1002   process. So for these two tasks, the initial state is reset by the second observation.
1003
       For basketball and box-close, it seems like there is some internal collision detection that will alter
1004   the object and robot position after the task is reset, so computing the exact reset value from the
1005   observation is not possible. For these two tasks, we instead resort to a search-based method. To be
1006   specific, we use a gradient-free optimiser from (Liu et al., 2022b) to search over the resetting space
1007   of the object and find the reset position that minimises the L2 distance with the true observation.
1008
       More details of the implementation can be found in the code.

1011   G    D IFFERENCE BETWEEN COMPOUNDING ERROR AND DKB
1012
1013   For DKB, most literature (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Peng et al., 2021; Torabi et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022a)
1014   understand it as compounding error, in which the error is understood as when placing the policy
1015   with the same initial states as the demonstration, the learned policy will gradually diverge from
1016   the demonstrations due to error accumulation. Our DKB is a broader concept than compounding
1017
       error. DKB attributes the gap of perform to the lack of learning signal on unsupported space of
       the demonstrations. In this way, EKB can explain more empirical successes in the literature than
1018
       compounding error can. For example, a recent work MAHALO (Li et al., 2023) shows evidence
1019
       of the importance of the size of the covered space. The authors studied a similar ILfO setup with
1020   embodiment and demonstration datasets. They compared two variants: for one they train an Inverse
1021   Dynamics Model (IDM) from the embodiment dataset and use it to label the demonstration dataset,
1022   while for another they train a reward model from the demonstration dataset by labelling all time steps
1023   with a reward of 1, and then use it to label the embodiment dataset. Finally, they run the same offline
1024   RL algorithm on both labelled datasets. The results show the second variant attains a much better
1025   performance even though the labelling from the reward model is not as meaningful as the actions
       from the IDM.
