009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we study offline reinforcement learning (RL) with zero-shot general-
012            ization property (ZSG), where the agent has access to an offline dataset including
013            experiences from different environments, and the goal of the agent is to train a
014            policy over the training environments which performs well on test environments
015            without further interaction. Existing work showed that classical offline RL fails
016            to generalize to new, unseen environments. We propose pessimistic empirical
017            risk minimization (PERM) and pessimistic proximal policy optimization (PPPO),
018            which leverage pessimistic policy evaluation to guide policy learning and enhance
019
               generalization. Theoretically, our framework is capable of finding a near-optimal
               policy with ZSG. Empirically, our framework demonstrates the ability to enhance
020
               the performance of the base offline RL methods. Our result serves as a first
021
               step in understanding the foundation of the generalization phenomenon in offline
022            reinforcement learning. Our codes are released at this link.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Offline reinforcement learning (RL) has become increasingly significant in modern RL because it
028
      eliminates the need for direct interaction between the agent and the environment; instead, it relies
      solely on learning from an offline training dataset. However, in practical applications, the offline
029
      training dataset often originates from a different environment than the one of interest. This discrepancy
030
      necessitates evaluating RL agents in a generalization setting, where the training involves a finite
031   number of environments drawn from a specific distribution, and the testing is conducted on a distinct
032   set of environments from the same or different distribution. This scenario is commonly referred to as
033   the zero-shot generalization (ZSG) challenge which has been studied in online RL(Rajeswaran et al.,
034   2017; Machado et al., 2018; Justesen et al., 2018; Packer et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2018a;b), as the
035   agent receives no training data from the environments it is tested on.
036
      A number of recent empirical studies (Mediratta et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Mazoure et al., 2022)
037   have recognized this challenge and introduced various offline RL methodologies that are capable of
038   ZSG. Notwithstanding the lack of theoretical backing, these methods are somewhat restrictive; for
039   instance, some are only effective for environments that vary solely in observations(Mazoure et al.,
040   2022), while others are confined to the realm of imitation learning(Yang et al., 2023), thus limiting
041   their applicability to a comprehensive framework of offline RL with ZSG capabilities. Concurrently,
042   theoretical advancements (Bose et al., 2024; Ishfaq et al., 2024) in this domain have explored multi-
043   task offline RL by focusing on representation learning. These approaches endeavor to derive a
044   low-rank representation of states and actions, which inherently requires additional interactions with
045
      the downstream tasks to effectively formulate policies based on these representations. Therefore, we
      raise a natural question:
046
047
                    Can we design provable offline RL with zero-shot generalization ability?
048
049   We propose novel offline RL frameworks that achieve ZSG to address this question affirmatively. Our
050   contributions are listed as follows.
051

053     modifications. Specifically, we prove that if the offline dataset does not contain context information,
        then it is impossible for vanilla RL that equips a Markovian policy to achieve a ZSG property. We

      Offline RL Offline reinforcement learning (RL) (Ernst et al., 2005; Riedmiller, 2005; Lange et al.,
102   2012; Levine et al., 2020) addresses the challenge of learning a policy from a pre-collected dataset
103   without direct online interactions with the environment. A central issue in offline RL is the inadequate
104   dataset coverage, stemming from a lack of exploration (Levine et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020). A
105   common strategy to address this issue is the application of the pessimism principle, which penalizes
106   the estimated value of under-covered state-action pairs. Numerous studies have integrated pessimism
107   into various single-environment offline RL methodologies. This includes model-based approaches
      (Rashidinejad et al., 2021; Uehara and Sun, 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2021b;

109
      et al., 2022; Ghasemipour et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2023), and policy-based strategies (Rezaeifar et al.,
110   2022; Xie et al., 2021a; Zanette et al., 2021; Nguyen-Tang and Arora, 2024). (Yarats et al., 2022) has
111   observed that with sufficient offline data diversity and coverage, the need for pessimism to mitigate
112   extrapolation errors and distribution shift might be reduced. To the best of our knowledge, we are the
113   first to theoretically study the generalization ability of offline RL in the contextual MDP setting.

      Generalization in online RL There are extensive empirical studies on training online RL agents
115
      that can generalize to new transition and reward functions (Rajeswaran et al., 2017; Machado et al.,
116   2018; Justesen et al., 2018; Packer et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2018a;b; Nichol et al., 2018; Cobbe
117   et al., 2018; Küttler et al., 2020; Bengio et al., 2020; Bertran et al., 2020; Ghosh et al., 2021; Kirk
118   et al., 2023; Juliani et al., 2019; Ajay et al., 2021; Samvelyan et al., 2021; Frans and Isola, 2022;
119   Albrecht et al., 2022; Ehrenberg et al., 2022; Song et al., 2020; Lyle et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2020; Lee
120   et al., 2020; Jiang et al.). They use techniques including implicit regularization (Song et al., 2020),
121   data augmentation Ye et al. (2020); Lee et al. (2020), uncertainty-driven exploration (Jiang et al.),
122   successor feature (Touati et al., 2023), etc. These works focus mostly on the online RL setting and
123   do not provide theoretical guarantees, thus differing a lot from ours. Moreover, Touati et al. (2023)
124
      has studied zero-shot generalization in offline RL, but to unseen reward functions rather than unseen
      environments. Addtional related works that have studied zero-shot RL include Park et al. (2024);
125
      Jeen et al. (2023).
126
127   There are also some recent works aimed at understanding online RL generalization from a theoretical
128   perspective. Wang et al. (2019) examined a specific class of reparameterizable RL problems and
129   derived generalization bounds using Rademacher complexity and the PAC-Bayes bound. Malik
130
      et al. (2021) established lower bounds and introduced efficient algorithms that ensure a near-optimal
      policy for deterministic MDPs. A more recent work Ye et al. (2023) studied how much pre-training
131
      can improve online RL test performance under different generalization settings. To the best of our
132
      knowledge, no previous work exists on theoretical understanding of the zero-shot generalization of
133   offline RL.
134
135   Our paper is also related to recent works studying multi-task learning in reinforcement learning
136
      (RL) (Brunskill and Li, 2013; Tirinzoni et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2021; Zhang and Wang, 2021; Lu
      et al., 2021; Bose et al., 2024; Ishfaq et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023), which focus on transferring
137
      the knowledge learned from upstream tasks to downstream ones. Additionally, these works typically
138
      assume that all tasks share similar transition dynamics or common representations while we do not.
139   Meanwhile, they typically requires the agent to interact with the downstream tasks, which does not
140   fall into the ZSG regime.

168   Remark 1 We briefly compare generalizable RL with several related settings. Robust RL (Pinto et al.,
169   2017) aims to find the best policy for the worst-case environment, whereas generalizable RL seeks
170   a policy that performs well in the average-case environment. Meta-RL (Beck et al., 2023) enables
171   few-shot adaptation to new environments, either through policy updates (Finn et al., 2017) or via

      5.2    M ODEL - BASED APPROACH : PESSIMISTIC EMPIRICAL RISK MINIMIZATION
302   Given PPE, we propose algorithms that have the ZSG ability. We first propose a pessimistic empirical
303   risk minimization (PERM) method which is model-based and conceptually simple. The algorithm
304   details are in Algorithm 2. In detail, for each dataset Di drawn from i-th environments, PERM
305   builds a model using PPE to evaluate the policy π under the environment Mi . Then PERM outputs
                                                                                          Pn   π
306   a policy π PERM ∈ Π that maximizes the average pessimistic value, i.e., 1/n i=1 Vbi,1      (x1 ). Our
307   approach is inspired by the classical empirical risk minimization approach adopted in supervised
308   learning, and the Optimistic Model-based ERM proposed in Ye et al. (2023) for online RL. Our
309   setting is more challenging than the previous ones due to the RL setting and the offline setting,
310   where the interaction between the agent and the environment is completely disallowed. Therefore,

527   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   In this work, we study the zero-shot generalization (ZSG) performance of offline reinforcement
530   learning (RL). We propose two offline RL frameworks, pessimistic empirical risk minimization and
531
      pessimistic proximal policy optimization, and show that both of them can find the optimal policy
      with ZSG ability. We also show that such a generalization property does not hold for offline RL
532
      without knowing the context information of the environment, which demonstrates the necessity of
533
      our proposed new algorithms. Currently, our theorems and algorithm design depend on the i.i.d.
534   assumption of the environment selection. How to relax such an assumption remains an interesting
535   future direction.

       Environments We are using the comblock framework that we adapted from (Bose et al., 2024),
1478
       where we directly record the policies and Q-values through state-action pairs instead of recovering
1479
       them linear-algebraically through rich observations. We leverage the PyTorch framework with CPU
1480   device to process the tabular numerical operations. The entire dataset generation and experiment
1481   process is conducted on MacBook Pro with M3 Max chip where the dataset generation process takes
1482   about 2 hours.
1483
       We consider the Combination Lock environment adapted from (Bose et al., 2024). At each timestep
1484
       h, there are three states s0,h , s1,h , s2,h with 5 possible actions; only s0,h , s1,h are considered as
1485
       desirable states that are reachable toward final reward. The environment uniformly and independently
1486   samples 1 out of 5 actions for each desirable state a0,h , a1,h at each timestep, where taking these
1487   actions will result transition to one of the good states s0,h+1 , s1,h+1 with equal probability, otherwise
1488   the transition will deterministically to the bad state s2,h+1 and remains in the bad states for the rest
1489   of the horizon. If the agent is staying in the good states at the end of the horizon, the reward will be 1;
1490   otherwise the agent has 0.5 probability to receive a 0.1 reward.
1491
       Implementation details We consider two experiment settings, one with 5 context environments and
1492   the other with 10 context environments. Each context environment is generated randomly. For the
1493   generation of the offline dataset, as in (Bose et al., 2024), we adopt the Exploratory Policy Search
1494   (EPS) algorithm proposed by (Agarwal et al., 2023) to obtain exploratory policies (not necessarily
1495   optimal) that cover as much of the feature space. For each context environment, 500 exploratory
1496   trajectories are i.i.d. sampled. We compare our proposed PPPO with the previous baseline PEVI Jin
1497   et al. (2021) w.r.t. their average reward. In our experiment, we calibrate the β(δ) parameter for PEVI
1498   to reflect the optimal performance, as well as the β(δ), α parameters for PPPO to reflect near-optimal
1499   performance.
