011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Vision-language contrastive learning based on the CLIP method has been instru-
014            mental in driving recent advancements in computer vision. However, high quality
015            CLIP models are based on very large datasets. This makes them expensive to
016
               train and hampers the scientific analysis of these models. We show how to train
               a CLIP base-size model efficiently for a broad domain on a much smaller amount
017
               of data. We demonstrate this specifically with the automated creation of a dataset
018
               named LivingThings with 8.9M images of animals and plants and 12.2M texts.
019            The dataset is obtained via focused image-search queries of three kinds: entity
020            queries (e.g., “eagle”), entity-attribute queries (e.g., “bushy tail of a fox”), and
021            type-attribute queries (e.g., “insect on a leaf”). The entities and types, as well as
022            some of the texts, are derived from the WordNet and Wikidata knowledge graphs,
023            the attributes are obtained via LLMs. We train a CLIP model from scratch on Liv-
024            ingThings and evaluate it on ImageNet, iNaturalist, and CUB for object classifica-
025            tion and OVAD and CUB for attribute classification. On the broad target domain
026            of animals and plants, our model achieves comparable, and sometimes even much
027
               better performance than models that have orders of magnitude more parameters
               or training data. For instance, our ViT-B-32 model improves over much larger
028
               state-of-the-art CLIP models on the iNaturalist 21 object classification task. We
029
               will publicly release our code and dataset.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) is a popular way to train
035
      Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and is part of a large percentage of contemporary works in com-
036   puter vision. CLIP models learn high-quality visual embeddings and establish a link to the semantic
037   level of brief text descriptions by training on pairs of images and their corresponding text descrip-
038   tions collected from the web. The features and the link between images and text have been used
039   directly for, e.g., zero-shot classification or text-to-image retrieval, and enable dialogues with visual
040   input, such as in the LLaVA family of models (Liu et al., 2023). The link can also be exploited in the
041   opposite direction to enable text-conditional image generation, e.g., Stable Diffusion (Podell et al.,
042   2023).
043   However, pretraining such a model is very expensive, since it requires large amounts of data and
044   compute, with the original CLIP model already training on 400M image-text pairs, and later works
045   scaling the training up even more (Gadre et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024a). Therefore, research on
046   VLMs that requires control over the training of the model is either left to companies or is limited to
047   models of significantly lower quality. Finetuning existing CLIP models can be done with moderate
048   compute, however, then the architecture and pretraining data is fixed. Pretraining allows us to be in
049   full control of all input data, choice of architecture and training algorithm.
050   The goal of this work is to train a VLM from scratch with much less compute while approaching
051   or even surpassing the quality of the largest models. Our strategy focuses on training with less but
052   better data, in order to maximize the performance per datapoint. Li et al. (2024) have explored CLIP
053   “along three dimensions: data, architecture, and training strategies” and they stress the “significance
      of high-quality training data”. For LLMs, Abdin et al. (2024) have shown that data curation brings

      2    R ELATED WORK
109
110
111   Datasets. Many recent works investigate ways of building large-scale datasets for multimodal train-
112
      ing. Radford et al. (2021) train the original CLIP model on a closed set of 400M images, with the
      model weights being released, but not the data. They build their dataset by collecting image-text
113
      pairs, where the text includes frequent terms derived from Wikipedia or WordNet nouns, and ap-
114
      proximately class-balancing the result. As a first approach to create a public dataset of this size,
115   Schuhmann et al. (2021) build a dataset with 400 million image-text pairs by filtering HTML data
116   from Common Crawl (LAION-400M) (Rana, 2010). Their main method of filtering is to remove
117   all image-text pairs that have less than 0.3 similarity estimated by the CLIP model. In a follow-up
118   work, Schuhmann et al. (2022) scale their approach up one order of magnitude with the multilingual
119   LAION-5B dataset. Xu et al. (2024) intend to replicate the original CLIP’s data curation approach.
120   They collect image-text pairs from CommonCrawl and filter them using Wikipedia and WordNet,
121   then balance the results. Gadre et al. (2023) propose DataComp, a filtering challenge with a candi-
122   date pool of up to 13B image-text pairs from CommonCrawl, where the goal is to filter this candidate
123
      pool and run a fixed training pipeline on the resulting data. They propose a baseline DataComp-1B
      dataset with 1.4B pairs filtered with a combination of CLIP score and clustering CLIP embeddings
124
      to find images close to ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) training examples. Fang et al. (2024a) train a
125
      Data Filtering Network on an internal dataset of 357M human-verified image-text pairs and finetune
126   it on a set of public human annotated datasets. They filter 42B candidates into the DFN-5B dataset
127   and train the current top model of the OpenCLIP leaderboard (Ilharco et al., 2021).
128
129
      These large datasets have mostly replaced smaller datasets like ConceptualCaptions12M (CC12M)
      (Changpinyo et al., 2021), which relies on unimodal heuristics as well as Google Cloud Vision APIs
130
      to predict the image-text similarity. Another popular small dataset is Yahoo Flickr Creative Com-
131
      mons 15M (YFCC15M), a subset of 15M image-text pairs obtained from the YFCC100M dataset
132   which is based on Flickr (Thomee et al., 2016). Though many works are mostly concerned on
133   scaling up multimodal datasets and models as much as possible, we aim to improve research on
134   high-quality CLIP models also in scenarios with much less available compute.
135
      Stevens et al. (2024) aim to create a general vision model for organismal biology and curate the
136
      TreeOfLife-10M dataset based on Encyclopedia of Life (EOL, 2018), iNaturalist 2021 (Van Horn
137
      et al., 2018) and BIOSCAN-1M (Gharaee et al., 2023). Their model BioCLIP is trained on a mix
138   of english and latin entity names. For evaluation, they curate the RareSpecies benchmark which
139   tests generalization to 400 species unseen during training. While Stevens et al. (2024) use biological
140   domain knowledge to build their dataset, we instead rely on knowledge graphs and propose a dataset
141   collection method for arbitrary domains.
142
      Training algorithms. Various works are concerned with improving CLIP from the algorithmic side.
143
      Li et al. (2023a) simply train on low resolution first, then finetune on higher resolution later. Li et al.
144   (2023b) additionally mask a substantial portion of the image to further reduce the amount of input
145   during training. Zhai et al. (2023) propose using a sigmoid loss which reduces the computional load
146   especially in big distributed settings. Vasu et al. (2024) enhance their training data with synthetic
147   captions created by an image captioning model and use an ensemble of CLIP teachers to train their
148   model. This way, they can increase the learning efficiency by transferring knowledge from bigger
149   models to their smaller models. Such algorithmic improvements are orthogonal to our research,
150   since they would potentially also improve training on our dataset. In this work, we focus on data
151   improvements and fix the algorithm and architecture choices, since this also allows to easily and
152
      fairly compare to a big set of already trained vanilla Vision Transformer (ViT) based CLIP models.
153   Li et al. (2024) scale down CLIP and analyse the influence of different data, architecture, and training
154   strategies. They find that especially large models need larger datasets, and data quality plays an
155   important role. They create higher quality datasets by applying CLIP filtering to the 3.4B WebLI
156   dataset (Chen et al., 2023), while we aim to use a different dataset collection process.
157   Evaluation. Image classification is a popular way to evaluate vision encoders like CLIP-style mod-
158   els. While established benchmarks like ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) or iNaturalist (Van Horn et al.,
159   2018) provide a solid grounding to evaluate CLIP-style models on object classification, evaluating
160   attribute understanding is more challenging. Attributes are more challenging to define and anno-
161   tate: It is quite obvious what a “dog” is, however calling an object “large” depends on the frame
      of reference. The Animals with Attributes dataset (Xian et al., 2019) for example has attributes


                                                          3
162
      annotated per class, which makes it unfavorable for testing per-image attribute classification. Bravo
163   et al. (2023) find weaknesses in attribute definition and annotation of existing benchmarks like VAW
164   (Pham et al., 2021) and provide the Open Vocabulary Attribute Detection (OVAD) benchmark, which
165   can be used for evaluating both attribute classification and attribute detection. The CUB dataset
166   proposed by Wah et al. (2011) provides images of 200 bird species, with 312 attributes densely an-
167   notated for each image. Since we propose a dataset built on entities and attributes derived from the
168   world of animals and plants, the CUB dataset is an obvious choice to test our model.

170   3       DATASET CREATION
171
172   We describe our dataset creation process, consisting of four main steps: entity extraction, attribute
173   generation, query building, and image search. In this work, our starting point for this process are
174   entities within our target domain of animals and plants. In general, this process is applicable to all
175   visual domains covered by the knowledge graph.
176
177   3.1     E NTITY EXTRACTION
178
179   We build our list of entities for both animal and plants from the Wikidata knowledge
180   graph (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014). We define an animal to be every entity that is a subclass
181
      or child taxon of the animal entity1 , either directly or via multiple intermediate entities. We exclude
      human individuals, mythical creatures, and other named individuals to avoid being too specific.
182
183   We define a plant analogously, but with the plant entity2 as root entity instead. For plants, we also
184   include their fruits, because they are typically not directly related to the plant entity via the taxon or
185   subclass hierarchy. We exclude cultivars, named individuals and all plants with a coordinate location
186   to avoid overly specific entities.
187   For every animal and plant, we download its identifier, name, description, number of Wikipedia
188   sitelinks, aliases, taxon common names, and taxon names. See Tab. 1 for example data. We use the
189   number of Wikipedia sitelinks as proxy for an entity’s popularity and order our final entity lists by it,
190   with popular entities coming first. Our final entity lists contain 204,918 animals and 84,612 plants.
191   We also select all nouns from WordNet (Fellbaum, 1998) that are a subclass of the “living thing”
192   node, excluding humans, named entities and entities that cannot be seen with the bare eye, e.g.,
193   microorganisms. Finally, we only consider leaf nodes and arrive at 6,983 entities, each with a
194   description and a total of 16,705 synonyms.
195
      To verify that our entity extraction procedure generalizes beyond animals and plants, we run it on a
196
      much broader visual domain in Appendix I.
197

      3.2     ATTRIBUTE GENERATION
199
200   We generate attributes for the top 500 Wikidata entities in both our animal and plant entity lists
201   using LLMs. We manually define 6 visual attribute categories for plants and 7 for animals, for
202   each of which we guide the LLM to predict between 1 and 10 attribute instances using constrained
203   decoding. We thereby obtain between 6 and 60 attributes per animal and between 7 and 70 attributes
204   per plant. The 6 attribute categories for plants are Color, Pattern and texture, Plant parts, Shape
205   and size, Habitat and environment, and Other. For animals, we switch Plant parts to Body parts and
206
      add another category called Behavior and movement. We specifically prompt the LLMs to generate
      visually observable attribute instances.
207

209   LLMs 3 and merge their results afterwards. The LLMs generate around 18.6 attributes per animal
210   or 19.7 per plant on average. After merging and deduplication, we end up with 44.4 attributes per
211   animal and 56.7 attributes per plant. Note that for each attribute within an attribute category we also

251   3.3   Q UERY BUILDING
252
253   Before building the search queries for our entities and attributes, we generate our entities’ natural
254   types. The natural type of an entity is the superclass that a human would most likely associate with
255
      it, e.g., bird for eagle, or tree for oak. It is neither too general nor too specific, and can be used to
      disambiguate the entity from other entities with the same name. We generate natural types for the
256
      first 5000 animals and plants in our entity lists with an LLM, and do this by asking the LLM to select
257
      a natural type entity from the entity’s parent hierarchy. To all other entities we assign the natural
258   type animal or plant. Incorporating natural types in the search queries improves the search results
259   by reducing ambiguities for entities with names that carry multiple meanings, and by reducing the
260   number of cartoons, illustrations, and other unwanted search results. For example, searching for
261   dove returns mostly pictures of the well known personal care brand, whereas searching for dove bird
262   returns pictures of the animal that we are actually interested in. We end up with 6 unique natural
263   types for animals: bird, mammal, insect, fish, reptilia and animal itself. We also get 6 unique natural
264   types for plants: tree, fruit tree, root vegetable, flowering plant, herb and plant itself.
265   We then generate three different types of search queries: Entity queries, entity-attribute queries and
266   type-attribute queries. As entity queries, we combine the name of the entity and its natural type. For
267   entity-attribute queries, we directly use the search queries generated by the LLMs (see Sec. 3.2).
268   For type-attributes queries we search for all unique combinations of an entity’s natural type with the
269   attributes the LLMs generated for it. For example, we search for flying bird if for at least one entity
      whose natural type is bird the LLMs generated the attribute flying.

      3.4       I MAGE SEARCH AND FILTERING
283
284   For all query sets, we search using both the Google Custom Search API and the Bing image search
285   API. The Wikidata animal and plant entity query sets are very large, so we limit them to the top 28k
286   entities each. For both entity-attribute queries and type-attribute queries, we use the full query sets
287   with 47k and 7k queries, respectively. We activate the SafeSearch filters of the search engines.
288
      Both search APIs also return the URL for the website an image is embedded in. We use that to
289   download the corresponding HTML and search for the image tag matching the returned image in
290   it. We then extract and store texts from attributes of the image tag as alt texts to use them later for
291   training.
292
      We thereby collect 4.7M / 15.6M images and 10.1M / 27.6M alt texts from the Google and Bing
293
      API, respectively. After downloading search results and alt texts, we postprocess the images and alt
294
      texts in the following way:
295
296   • Similar to Changpinyo et al. (2021), we use relaxed filtering heuristics. We do not use any multi-
297     modal filtering but instead rely on the search engines to provide image-text correspondences. We
298
        remove text that is longer than 500 chars or formatted in JSON. We also remove images with an
        aspect ratio of more than 4 or covering less than 4096 pixels.
299
      • We deduplicate all downloaded images using the Self-Supervised Descriptor for Image Copy
300
        Detection method (SSCD) (Pizzi et al., 2022). We keep the biggest image and collect all unique
301     alt texts from all duplicates.
302   • We detect duplicates between the images and all evaluation datasets using the same SSCD method.
303
304
      Our final dataset contains around 8.9M images and 11.8M alt texts, obtained from 125k queries,
      which is 71.2 images per query and 1.3 alt texts per image on average. We pay about $1,800 and
305
      $2,500 to download our Bing and Google subsets respectively. See Tab. 3 for an overview over the
306
      number of search queries, images and alt texts that make up our dataset and Appendix D for more
307   details about the number and format of the requests issued to both search APIs.

312   4.1       T RAINING
313
      We train all models with the standard CLIP loss (Radford et al., 2021), a batch size of 2048, and
314
      random resized crop augmentation. Similar to Li et al. (2023b), we reduce the context size of the
315   text encoder down from 77. We choose a length of 32 as a compromise to trade off speed and loss of
316   data: On CC12M, 75% of captions have 32 tokens or less and are not affected. We train all models
317   for 18 epochs using AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). Training on 9M images takes ∼30 hours
318   on 8 RTX 2080 Ti GPUs with 11GB VRAM per GPU.

      4.3   O BJECT CLASSIFICATION EVALUATION
356
357
      To test the VLMs on object classification we use the same procedure as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021).
358   Given an image I, class names C1 , ..., CN , image encoder f and text encoder g, we embed the image
359   using the image encoder v = f (I). To acquire a text embedding for class Cc , the CLIP authors start
360   by directly encoding the class names as wc = g(Cc ), e.g., “dog”. Alternatively, they create several
361   prompts P using templates, e.g., “graffiti of a dog.”, “a P
                                                                photo of the cool dog.”, etc., then encode each
362   prompt and compute the average embedding: wc = p∈P g(p)/|P |. They refer to this approach
363   as using “context prompts”. Finally, given the image and text embeddings, the prediction p is the
364   class which has the highest cosine similarity to the image. We evaluate all models on just encoding
365   the class name, as well as on using the average embedding of the 80 context prompts that the CLIP
366   author’s used for ImageNet, and report the higher top-1 accuracy.
367   Object datasets. We evaluate on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), a popular image classification
368   benchmark (Russakovsky et al., 2015). We use the ILSVRC2012 validation set, which contains
369   50,000 images from 1,000 classes. The classes include simple objects such as “broom”, but also
370   more fine-grained labels like 23 types of terrier dogs, e.g., “Staffordshire Bull Terrier”. We split the
371   classes into “living” (410 classes) and “other” (590 classes) using WordNet: Since ImageNet labels
372   are built on WordNet nouns, we simply select all labels that are children of the node “Living Things”
373   for the “living” set.
374   We also evaluate on two version of iNaturalist (Van Horn et al., 2018), a dataset for fine-grained
375   species classification. The 2019 version contains 3,030 images in the validation set, each annotated
376   with one of 1,010 Latin species. Similar to Parashar et al. (2023), we translate the species label to
377   English via a combination of Wikidata SPARQL queries and manually looking up the species, and
      test models on both english and latin. The more challenging iNaturalist 2021 version contains 100k


                                                         7
378
      images in the validation set and 10k different species. This version provides both Latin and English
379   labels for the species.
380
381   We test on the CUB dataset, which contains 5,794 images in the original author’s test set. Each
382
      image is annotated as one of 200 fine-grained species of birds, e.g., “grasshopper sparrow”.
383   Finally, we evaluate on the RareSpecies dataset (Stevens et al., 2024), which requires models to not
384   see the tested 400 species during training. We pretrain our model again and exclude all entities that
385   appear in RareSpecies. We use various english and latin prompts for the name of the species and
386   report the best result, for more details refer to Appendix G.

388   4.4   ATTRIBUTE CLASSIFICATION EVALUATION
389
390
      The considered attribute benchmarks are framed as binary classification problems, as described by
      Bravo et al. (2023). A fixed set of attributes A is annotated for each image as an attribute vector
391
      of length A with value 0 if the attribute is not present, 1 if the attribute is present, and −1 for
392
      undecidable situations (e.g., when the attribute is “red-colored wing”, but the photo only shows a
393   bird’s head). Models should assign high or low scores to positively or negatively labeled image-
394   attribute pairs, respectively. Predictions on undecidable attributes do not influence the final score.
395
396
      As metric, we use the established mean Average Precision (mAP) (Everingham et al., 2010); specif-
      ically, we use the implementation of Pedregosa et al. (2011). We compute AP per attribute and
397
      then average over all attributes. We only evaluate attribute classes which have at least one positive
398
      attribute, since otherwise AP cannot be computed for this attribute.
399
400   As in object classification, simply embedding only the attribute as text, e.g., “red”, may not be
401   the most efficient strategy. We consider using templates and synonyms to create a set of context
402
      prompts per attribute Pa . Then, we either create the average embedding as described in Sec. 4.3 or
      first evaluate the cosine similarities and report the prediction p as the maximum similarity over all
403
      context prompts: p = maxpa ∈Pa cossim(f (I), g(pa )). In both attribute benchmarks, we evaluate all
404
      models on the “average embedding” and “maximum similarity” strategy and on a variety of context
405   prompts depending on the dataset.
406
407   Attribute datasets. We evaluate on OVAD, which contains 80 object classes and 117 attribute
408
      classes. We consider the “oracle box” setting, where each input is one of 14,074 cropped bounding
      boxes annotated with one object class and an attribute vector of length 117. We group the dataset
409
      into animals, food, person, and other using the object label annotations. To create the prompt, we
410
      extend the prompts used by the original authors to include also class-specific prompts, instead of
411   only class-agnostic prompts, see Appendix B.3 for details.
412
413   Additionally we evaluate attribute classification on CUB. We convert the given annotations into an
414
      image-attribute matrix with each attribute being annotated either negative, positive or unknown for
      each image, see Appendix A.1 for details. Then, we create prompts similar to OVAD, e.g., “bird has
415
      a striped pattern on its wing”., see Appendix A.4. This way, we match the setup of OVAD and can
416
      evaluate in the same way.

526   6     C ONCLUSIONS
527
528   We have developed a compact high-quality dataset LivingThings consisting of 8.9M images from
529
      the domain of animals and plants, paired with 12.2M texts. Our method is generic, leveraging
      knowledge graphs (WordNet and Wikidata) and image search engines, and can be applied to any
530
      domain covered by the given knowledge graphs. We have demonstrated that we can train a CLIP
531
      model on our dataset with little compute, yet with a performance that is comparable to or even better
532   than that of much more expensive-to-train models. By adapting the CUB benchmark to VLM-style
533   evaluation, we also enable more comprehensive assessment in the field of open-vocabulary attribute
534   understanding.
535
      While previous studies have shown that alt texts offer better supervision than the search queries used
536
      to find images, in our work, we demonstrate that combining alt texts with search queries can indeed
537
      improve performance compared to using alt texts alone. This approach opens up new possibilities
538   for supervision in VLM training. Ultimately, we hope to provide researchers with a useful tool for
539   building custom datasets and facilitating affordable VLM pretraining experiments.

      A.1    C ONVERTING WORKER ANNOTATIONS TO ATTRIBUTE LABELS
759
760   Wah et al. (2011) design a vocabulary of 28 attribute groupings and 312 binary attributes, e.g., “wing
761   color” with 15 color choices. They create the attribute labels via crowd sourced workers such that
762   for each image-attribute combination, there is exactly one worker annotation with a binary label
763   whether the attribute is present, and a certainty score with values (1, not visible), (2, guessing), (3,
764   probably), (4, definitely). We now convert these ratings into image-attribute labels with values 0, 1
765   and -1 meaning attribute is not present, attribute is present, and attribute is unknown, respectively,
766
      same as in the OVAD dataset. The average certainty is 3.3. We consider all ratings with certainty (1,
      not visible) and (2, guessing) as unknown and otherwise use the annotated label.

891   B.3    OVAD PROMPTS
892
      When evaluating different prompts for attribute classification, we start with the original prompting
893
      strategies by Bravo et al. (2023). We create all permutations of the following choices. To create a
894
      single model prediction from several prompts, we evaluate both the “average embedding” (Sec. 4.3)
895   and “maximum similarity” (Sec. 4.4) strategies to create model the prediction. To write the prompt,
896   we use either “a”, “the”, or no article in the prompt and the word “object” or an empty string as the
897   noun. Additionally, we use the entity name directly (“a small sheep”) or the natural type (“a small
898   animal”). In total, we evaluate 48 prompt settings per model and report the best result. Models
899   are quite sensitive to the exact prompt setting. We show this in Supp. Fig. 4 and Supp. Fig. 5 by
900   plotting the distribution of the 48 different results for our model as well as two models trained on
901   DataComp-1B and CC12M, respectively.

       F    B IO CLIP DETAILS
1257
1258   Stevens et al. (2024) aim to create a general vision model for organismal biology and curate the
1259
       TreeOfLife-10M dataset, which is based on the sources outlined below. Encyclopedia of Life (EOL,
       2018) is a project aimed to aggregate biological knowledge in an online encyclopedia. Stevens et al.
1260
       (2024) download 6.6M images of 440K species from EOL. They also incorporate iNaturalist 2021
1261
       (Van Horn et al., 2018) described in Sec. 4.3 and add the 2.7M images of 10K species from the
1262   training set. Finally, they add BIOSCAN-1M (Gharaee et al., 2023) which contains 1M lab images
1263   from 494 different insect families. Their model BioCLIP is trained on a mix of common names (e.g.,
1264   “Rufous-crowned Sparrow”), scientific names (e.g., “Aimophila ruficeps”), as well as taxonomic
1265   names (the full taxonomy from kingdom to species, e.g., “Animalia Chordata Aves Passeriformes
1266   Passerellidae Aimophila ruficeps”). They finetune the OpenAI ViT-B-16 model for 100 epochs on
1267   16 A100 80GB GPUs.

       G    D ETAILED RESULTS FOR RARE SPECIES
1270
1271
       The “Rare Species” benchmark proposed by Stevens et al. (2024) consists of 400 species with 30
1272   images each and is used to test generalization to unseen taxa. The species are deliberately removed
1273   from TreeOfLife-10M, such that BioCLIP has not seen them during training. We replicate this
1274   process and find 290 of the 400 species as entities in our dataset using substring matching. We then
1275   remove these 290 entities from our LivingThings dataset and train our baseline model again.
1276
       As class names we evaluate all text types proposed by Stevens et al. (2024), i.e, various combinations
1277
       of the latin taxonomy and the english common name. Same as in Sec. 4.3 we evaluate on both the
1278   CLIP ImageNet prompt and no prompt, and report the better of both accuracies.
1279
1280   Our model significantly outperforms BioCLIP in classifying unseen rare species as shown in
1281
       Supp. Table 11. It has the highest accuracy when given mixed scientific and common class names,
       which is to be expected, since it has seen those types of text during training, but has not seen full tax-
1282
       onomic class names. BioCLIP performs best on a mix of taxonomic and common names, however
1283
       it does not match the performance of our model.

1335   I   E XTENSION TO ARBITRARY VISUAL DOMAINS COVERED BY KNOWLEDGE
1336       GRAPHS
1337
1338   One current limitation of this work is its domain restriction to the domain of animals and plants.
1339   To demonstrate that our approach is applicable more generally, we describe our current efforts of
1340   building a dataset covering the relevant aspects of the entire visual world.
1341
       Recall that the LivingThings dataset is built on Wikidata entities related to the root entities “animal”
1342
       and “plant” as well as the WordNet entities related to the “living thing” node. To build our world
1343
       dataset, we analyze which entities exist in Wikidata that are not animals or plants (e.g., coffee milk).
1344   Then, we find a fitting root entity (e.g., food) and add it to our list of root entities. Next, we find
1345   entities that are not animals, plants, or food, and repeat the process. This way, we define 45 root
1346   entities. We consider 21 of them as relevant to the visual world and harvest all their related entities,
1347   the result of which can be seen in Supp. Tab. 13. Additionally, we show examples of the resulting
1348   entities in Supp. Tab. 14 and the skipped root entities in Supp. Tab. 15. Note that entities can be
1349   related to multiple root entities, and we keep an entity as long as it is related to at least one of the 21
       visual root entities.


                                                           25
1350
       We also consider the WordNet knowledge graph and select all “physical objects” that are not a
1351   “living thing”. However, these entities are mainly professions (e.g., radiobiologist), other terms that
1352   characterize humans in some way (e.g., german) or specific people (e.g., Nelson Mandela). We
1353   therefore decide to not use WordNet for building our world dataset.
1354
       In total, we find around 91k entities with 236k unique terms. Assuming that we obtain around 71
1355
       unique images per search (8.9M unique images resulting from 125k search queries as described in
1356
       Tab. 3) and search for each term, this extends our dataset by around 17M unique images. Addi-
1357   tionally, we will generate attributes as described in Sec. 3.2 and download another 17M images for
1358   entity-attribute queries. Together with the existing 9M images of animals and plants, this would
1359   set our final dataset size at 43M images. This is still considerably smaller than billion-scale CLIP
1360   datasets, but with significantly higher quality, allowing for efficient training of world models.

1362   I.1   Q UERYING GENERIC ENTITIES WITH SPARQL
1363
1364   The SPARQL query used to harvest all entities for a root entity is displayed in Appendix I.1. It
1365   returns a list of entities from a specified target domain as defined by a root entity. This root entity can
1366   be determined manually by searching for appropriate entities on the Wikidata website. For example,
1367
       if we want to build a model specifically for cars, we set the root entity to motor car (Q1420), as done
       in Tab. 16.
1368
1369   This query uses the fact that entities within Wikidata are consistently modeled as ”instances” and
1370   ”classes/types”. Instances are specific entities of some type or class, e.g., ”Barack Obama” is an
1371   instance of a ”human”. On the other hand, classes or types are used to represent sets of entities
1372   having something in common and can have sub- or superclasses themselves. For example, the class
1373
       ”human” is a subclass of ”mammal”, which is a subclass of ”animal”. With SPARQL we can extract
       all subclasses and instances under one or more specified root entities, even across multiple hierarchy
1374
       levels.
