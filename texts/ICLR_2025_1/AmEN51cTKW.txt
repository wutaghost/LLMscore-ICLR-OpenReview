009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Satisfiability Modulo Counting (SMC) is a recently proposed general language to
012            reason about problems integrating statistical and symbolic artificial intelligence.
013            An SMC formula is an SAT formula in which the truth values of a few Boolean
014            predicates are determined by model counting, or equivalently, probabilistic infer-
015            ence. Existing solvers optimize surrogate objectives and hence provide no formal
016            guarantee. An exact solver is desperately needed. However, the direct integration
017            of Satisfiability and probabilistic inference solvers results in slow performance
018            because of many back-and-forth invocations of both solvers. We develop KOCO -
019
               SMC, a fast exact SMC solver, exploiting the fact that many similar probabilistic
               inferences are needed throughout SMC solving. We pre-compile the probabilis-
020
               tic inference part of SMC solving into probabilistic circuits, supporting efficient
021
               lower and upper-bound computation. Experiment results in several real-world ap-
022            plications demonstrate that our approach provides exact solutions, much better
023            than those from approximate solvers, while is more efficient than direct integra-
024            tion with the current exact solvers.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Symbolic and statistical Artificial Intelligence (AI) are two core paradigms with distinct strengths
030   and limitations: symbolic AI, exemplified by SATisfiability (SAT) and constraint programming,
031   excels in constraint satisfaction but cannot handle probability distributions. Statistical AI cap-
032   tures probabilistic uncertainty but does not guarantee to satisfy the symbolic constraints. Inte-
033   grating symbolic and statistical AI remains an open field and has gained much research attention
034   recently (Freuder & O’Sullivan, 2023; nes, 2023; neu, 2023).
035   As a motivating example, a manager needs to determine a set of supplier channels to ensure sufficient
036   raw materials for good production, taking into account stochastic events such as natural disasters or
037   policy risks. This robust supply chain design problem necessitates both symbolic reasoning to find
038   satisfiable supplier channels and statistical inference to ensure the suppliers are robust to stochastic
039   natural disasters. Slightly modified problem formulations can be found in many real-world appli-
040   cations including vehicle routing (Toth & Vigo, 2002), internet resilience (Israeli & Wood, 2002),
041   social influence maximization (Kempe et al., 2005), energy security (Almeida et al., 2019), etc.
042   The recently proposed Satisfiability Modulo Counting (SMC) (Fredrikson & Jha, 2014; Li et al.,
043   2024) provides a general language to reason about problems integrating statistical and symbolic AI,
044   including the aforementioned supply chain design problem. Specifically, an SMC formula is an SAT
045   formula in which the truth values of a few Boolean predicates are determined by model counting,
046   which calculates the number of distinct variable assignments so that the SAT formula evaluates to
047   true. The statistical reasoning is formulated as the model counting because inference over marginal
048   probability distribution can be cast as weighted model counting problems (Chavira & Darwiche,
049   2008). Solving SMC problems poses significant challenges since they are NPPP -complete (Park &
050
      Darwiche, 2004).
051   Several approximate SMC solvers have been proposed. Approximate solvers based on Sample Aver-
052   age Approximation (Kleywegt et al., 2002) were the most widely implemented, which used sample
053   mean to estimate the model counting. Another approximate solver, XOR-SMC (Li et al., 2024),
      offers a constant approximation guarantee, by using the XOR-sampling to estimate the counting.

070   Yet the solution quality of both methods lacks a formal guarantee, as the solution could still violate
071   a fraction of the constraints. Current exact SMC solvers directly combine an SAT solver for satisfia-
072
      bility with a model counting solver for statistical inference. Specifically, the SAT solver first gives a
      feasible variable assignment for the Satisfiability problem, which is then checked against statistical
073
      inference constraints by a model counting solver. If the assignment fails to meet all constraints,
074
      the process restarts with a new variable assignment. This method results in an excessive number of
075   back-and-forth invocations of SAT and model counter. Particularly for unsatisfiable problems, these
076   exact SMC solvers enumerate all possible solutions before confirming unsatisfiability and thus are
077   extremely slow.
078
      We introduce KOCO -SMC, an exact and efficient SMC solver, mitigating the extreme slowness typ-
079
      ically encountered in unsatisfiable SMC problems. KOCO -SMC saves time by detecting the conflict
080
      early and pruning the branch of the variable assignment tree. The core idea involves tracking the
081   upper and lower bounds of the probability inside probabilistic constraints during variable assign-
082   ments. If these bounds violate the satisfaction condition—such as when the upper bound of the
083   probability falls below the required minimum. These conflicts arising from probabilistic constraints
084   are recorded as ”learned Boolean clauses” and appended to the Boolean part, preventing the same
085   conflict from occurring in future iterations. Furthermore, integrating knowledge compilation from
086   discrete functions into probabilistic circuits allows for rapid, repetitive updates of bounds. Conse-
087   quently, our KOCO -SMC approach greatly improves the efficiency of SMC solving.
088   In experiments, we evaluate several approximate and exact solvers on 1350 SMC problems from the
089   UAI Competition benchmark. Figure 1 shows the comparison with state-of-the-art solvers. Com-
090   pared with exact solvers, KOCO -SMC scales the best. Our KOCO -SMC solves 85% of SMC prob-
091   lems in 3 hours while other exact solvers can only solve 45%, and KOCO -SMC needs 10 seconds
092   to solve those 45% of SMC instances. Compared with those approximate solvers, KOCO -SMC
093   reliably delivers higher quality solutions within the time limit. KOCO -SMC solves 40% more prob-
094   lems for hard SMC problems, whereas approximate solvers consistently produce infeasible solu-
095
      tions. We also demonstrate the process of formulating real-world problems, supply network design
      and package delivery, into the SMC formulation, and highlight the strong capability of our solver in
096
      addressing these problems.
097
098   To summarize, our main contributions are: (1) We propose KOCO -SMC, an efficient exact solver
099   for SMC problems, integrating probabilistic circuits for effective conflict detection. (2) Experiments
100   on synthetic datasets illustrate KOCO -SMC’s superior performance compared to state-of-the-art
101
      approximate and exact baselines in both solution quality and time efficiency. (3) The case study
      demonstrates the potential and effectiveness of applying KOCO -SMC to real-world problems.

      Probabilistic Inference and Model Counting. Probabilistic inference encompasses various tasks,
      such as calculating conditional probability, marginal probability, maximum a posteriori probability
119
      (MAP), and marginal MAP (MMAP). Each of them is essential in fields like machine learning, data
120
      analysis, and decision-making processes. Model counting calculates the number of satisfying as-
121   signments (models) for a given logical formula, and is closely related to probabilistic inference. In
122   many scenarios, especially in discrete probabilistic models, computing probabilities can be trans-
123   lated to model counting, by counting the number of ways certain events or configurations can occur.

126
      constraints that involve model counting. Recognizing the intrinsic connection between probabilis-
127   tic inference and model counting, SMC adaptively captures the satisfiability problem in scenarios
128   involving uncertainty.

152   variety of exact and efficient inferences (Darwiche, 2002; 1999; Poon & Domingos, 2011; Rahman
153   et al., 2014; Kisa et al., 2014; Dechter & Mateescu, 2007; Vergari et al., 2020; Peharz et al., 2020).

156   represents a probability distribution over certain random variables. Figure 2(c) gives an example
157   PC over four variables. A leaf node u encodes a tractable probability distribution Pu (xi ) over a
158   single random variable xi , such as Q  Gaussian or Bernoulli distributions. A product node u defines
159   a factorized distribution Pu (x) = v∈ch(u) Pv (x) where ch(u) denotes the children nodes of u.

161   the normalization weights of child node v. The root node r in the graph has no parent node. A
      probabilistic circuit is a model of its root node distribution.

242   3.2   M AIN P IPELINE OF KOCO -SMC
243
244   This section presents the proposed KOCO -SMC approach for solving SMC problems both ex-
245   actly and efficiently. KOCO -SMC follows the Conflict-Driven Clause Learning (CDCL) frame-
246   work (Silva & Sakallah, 1996; Eén & Sörensson, 2003) (Algorithm 1 in the Appendix), which
247   comprises four key components: variable assignment, propagation, conflict clause learning, and
248   backtracking. With the inclusion of probabilistic constraints in SMC problems, KOCO -SMC is
249   further tailored to efficiently address these challenges.
250   Pre-compilation. Before SMC problem solving, a knowledge compilation step compiles all dis-
251   tributions in probabilistic constraints into smooth, decomposable, and Q-deterministic probabilistic
252   circuits. An example is provided in Figure 2(c).
253
      Propagation. In the CDCL algorithm, unit propagation is used to propagate new variable assign-
254
      ments across clauses. This process can create additional variable assignments or detect conflicts. For
255
      example, if we have the clauses (x1 ∨ ¬x2 ) and (x2 ∨ x3 ), and we assign x1 = False, unit prop-
256   agation would force x2 = False, leading to further propagation x3 = True. This significantly
257   accelerates SAT solving. However, this procedure is specifically designed for Boolean clauses. How
258   to incorporate probabilistic constraints into the propagation process, extract useful information from
259   current variable assignments, and effectively detect conflicts remains an open problem. We propose
260   the Upper-Lower Watch (ULW) approach, an efficient propagation method for probabilistic con-
261   straints that leverages the power of probabilistic circuits. By utilizing modern knowledge compilers,
262   most common probability distributions can be compiled into tractable probabilistic circuits, making
263   our approach broadly applicable.
264   Conflicts Clause Learning. In the CDCL algorithm, once a conflict is detected within a Boolean
265   clause, there are existing techniques to add a learned clause to the original Boolean formula, pre-
266   venting the same conflict from occurring in the future. When a conflict arises in a probabilistic
267   constraint, KOCO -SMC generates a learned conflict clause by negating the current variable assign-
268   ments
      P       involved in the constraint and connecting them with logical OR. For example, a conflict in
269      y P (x 1 = True, x2 = False, y) > Q will produce the clause (¬x1 ∨ x2 ), preventing the
      assignment x1 = True, x2 = False from being used in future iterations.

272   The satisfaction or conflict of a probabilistic constraint is determined by the involved marginal prob-
273   ability. By maintaining a range of the marginal probability and refining it with each new variable
274   assignment, we can detect satisfiability or conflict early when the range significantly deviates from
275   the threshold. Determining the range of a marginal probability is solving the following problem:
276                 X                                    X

279   where xassigned denotes the assigned variables, xremain represents the unassigned variables, and y are
280   the marginalized-out latent variables. This problem is known as the Marginal MAP (MMAP) infer-
281   ence task. As discussed in the preliminaries, probabilistic circuits with specific structural properties
282   can efficiently compute MMAP queries. The maximum is referred to as the ”upper bound,” and the
283   minimum as the ”lower bound.” Our Upper-Lower Watch (ULW) algorithm monitors both values.
284   By leveraging the pre-compilation step, we can model probabilistic distributions using Q-
285   deterministic, decomposable, and smooth probabilistic circuits. The computation of upper and lower
286   bounds is then reduced to performing MMAP inference on these circuits. More specifically, each
287   node v in the probabilistic circuit represents a distribution Pv over a subset of variables. The ULW
288   algorithm associates each node with an upper bound U B(v) and a lower bound LB(v) for the
289   marginal probability of the sub-distribution Pv under the current assignment. Therefore, the U B
290   and LB at the root node provide the exact upper and lower bounds we seek.
291   Once a new variable is assigned, the bounds associated with each node can be updated in a bottom-up

      We have introduced KOCO -SMC, an innovative approach for solving Satisfiability Modulo Count-
533
      ing (SMC) problems exactly. Our method is distinct from existing approaches, which typically
534   combine SAT solvers with model counters. Instead, we introduce an early conflict detection mech-
535   anism by comparing the upper and lower bounds of probabilistic inferences. Through knowledge
536   compilation, our proposed Upper Lower Watch algorithm enables efficient tracking of both bounds.
537   Our experiments on synthetic benchmark problems demonstrate that our approach achieves supe-
538   rior solution quality compared to approximate solvers and significantly outperforms existing exact
539   solvers in terms of efficiency. The real-world application highlights the potential of solving practical
      problems due to the high generalizability of the SMC formulation.

708   Limitations Our KOCO -SMC requires all probability distributions to be compiled into Q-
709   deterministic, decomposable, and smooth probability circuits. However, due to the limitations of
710   knowledge compilers, compiling a complex distribution can (1) take too much time or space, (2)
711   introduce arithmetic errors, and (3) many knowledge compilers don’t support the structural re-
712   quirements. The knowledge compiler we used in experiments doesn’t guarantee to generate the
713
      Q-deterministic circuit. There is a gap between our theoretical analysis and the experimental results.
714   A relaxed version of ULW could be proposed , but it doesn’t guarantee tight upper and lower bounds.
715   As a result, the loose bounds could potentially slow down the detection of satisfaction or conflict,
716   representing a trade-off between fast-solving and structural requirements. We will explore it in the
717   future.
718   Broader Impact Satisfiability Modulo Counting (SMC) extends traditional Boolean satisfiability by
719   incorporating constraints that involve probability inference (model counting). This extension allows
720   for solving complex problems where both logical and probabilistic constraints must be satisfied.
721   SMC has significant applications in supply chain design, shelter allocation, scheduling problems,
722   and many others in Operation Research. For example, in scheduling problems, SMC can ensure
723   that selected schedules meet probabilistic events, while in shelter allocation, it can verify that the
724   accessibility under random disasters is above a specified threshold.
725
726   A     E XTENDED R ELATED W ORKS
727
728   Satisfiability Modulo Counting (SMC) was first introduced by Fredrikson & Jha (2014), aiming
729   to integrate logical satisfiability with probabilistic constraints to address complex decision-making
730   problems. Due to the novelty of this formulation, only a few specialized solvers have been proposed.
731   For instance, Li et al. (2024) introduced an approximate solver that employs XOR constraints to
732   relax SMC problems, enabling more tractable computations.
733   Other approaches often focus on specific subclasses of SMC problems. A representative example
734   is Marginal MAP inference, which maximizes a marginal probability over query variables. Its for-
735   mulation is identical to SMC problems with a single probabilistic constraint. Choi et al. (2022)
736   developed exact solvers for Marginal MAP by transforming probabilistic circuits. Marinescu et al.
737   (2014) uses AND-OR search approach to solve the MMAP problem.
738   Another related domain is Stochastic Satisfiability (SSAT), introduced by Papadimitriou (1985).
739   SSAT can further solve SMC problems with a Boolean constraint and a single probabilistic con-
740   straint by combining Boolean SAT with probabilistic quantifiers. Subsequent research has advanced
741   SSAT solvers (Lee et al., 2017; 2018; Fan & Jiang, 2023). However, despite their power, these
742   solvers cannot generalize to SMC problems with multiple probabilistic constraints.
743   The principle idea of our Upper-Lower Bound algorithm is to use the upper and lower bounds of
744   probabilities to avoid unnecessary search. Similar ideas can be found in branch-and-bound algo-
745   rithm Boyd & Mattingley (2007), which maintains upper and lower bounds of the objective function
746   to prune suboptimal branches of the search tree, and in alpha-beta pruning Knuth & Moore (1975),
747   which uses bounds on the evaluation of game states to eliminate branches in the game tree that
