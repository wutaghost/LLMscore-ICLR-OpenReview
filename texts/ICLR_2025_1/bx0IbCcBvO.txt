011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The efficiency of large vision-language models (LVLMs) is constrained by the
014            computational bottleneck of the attention mechanism during the prefill phase and
015
               the memory bottleneck of fetching the key-value (KV) cache in the decoding phase,
               particularly in scenarios involving high-resolution images or videos. Visual content
016
               often exhibits substantial redundancy, resulting in highly sparse attention maps
017
               within LVLMs. This sparsity can be leveraged to accelerate attention computation
018            or compress the KV cache through various approaches. However, most studies
019            focus on addressing only one of these bottlenecks and do not adequately support
020            dynamic adjustment of sparsity concerning distinct layers or tasks. In this paper, we
021            present ZipVL, an efficient inference framework designed for LVLMs that resolves
022            both computation and memory bottlenecks through a dynamic ratio of important
023            tokens. This ratio is adaptively determined based on the layer-specific distribution
024            of attention scores, rather than fixed hyper-parameters, thereby improving efficiency
025            for less complex tasks while maintaining high performance for more challenging
026
               ones. Then we select important tokens based on their normalized attention scores
               and perform attention mechanism solely on those important tokens to accelerate
027
               the prefill phase. To mitigate the memory bottleneck in the decoding phase, we
028
               employ mixed-precision quantization to the KV cache, where high-bit quantization
029            is used for caches of important tokens, while low-bit quantization is applied to
030            those of less importance. Our experiments demonstrate that ZipVL can accelerate
031            the prefill phase by 2.6× and reduce GPU memory usage by 50.0%, with a minimal
032            accuracy reduction of only 0.2% on Video-MME benchmark over LongVA-7B
033            model, effectively enhancing the generation efficiency of LVLMs.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   With the recent advancement of large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al.,
039
      2023; Vavekanand & Sam, 2024), many studies have extended their capabilities to comprehend and
      generate visual content. These models, commonly known as large vision-language models (LVLMs),
040
      have demonstrated remarkable performance in tasks such as image captioning and visual question
041
      answering (Ge et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024b; Team, 2024; Ge et al., 2024c; Lin et al., 2023).
042   Typically, to remain compatible with the next-token-prediction generation scheme of LLMs, images
043   or videos are encoded into visual tokens through a pre-trained visual encoder, and concatenated with
044   text tokens for input into the model. For instance, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) employs a pre-trained
045   CLIP-ViT-L-336px model (Radford et al., 2021), which encodes an image of size 336×336 pixels
046   to 576 visual tokens. However, for high-resolution images or videos, the visual encoder generates
047   excessive sequences of visual tokens, significantly limiting the generative efficiency of LVLMs.
048   Specifically, the prefill phase suffers from the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism,
049   resulting in computational bottleneck and prolonged time-to-first-token (TTFT). In the decoding
050
      phase, each new token interacts with all preceding tokens, requiring to fetch the full key-value (KV)
      cache from memory. This process slows down decoding due to memory bottleneck. Improving
051
      generative efficiency in both phases is essential for the practical deployment of LVLMs.
052
053   To address computational complexity in the prefill phase, sparse attention (Pagliardini et al., 2023;
      Jiang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024) has emerged as an effective strategy, particularly suitable for

087
      token dropping (Wan et al., 2024), token merging (Yang et al., 2024a), and quantization (Hooper
      et al., 2024; He et al., 2024b). However, these methods often rely on fixed compression ratios that
088
      are uniformly applied across all layers, failing to account for the distinct characteristics of attention
089
      maps in different layers. Moreover, despite the necessity of identifying important tokens for both
090   sparse attention and KV cache compression, a unified inference optimization framework has yet to be

093
      In this paper, we present ZipVL, an efficient inference framework tailored for LVLMs that jointly
      optimizes the prefill and decoding phases with a unified ratio of important tokens, as shown in Figure 2.
094
      To start with, we introduce a layer-wise adaptive ratio assignment scheme for important tokens. This
095
      ratio is adaptively determined based on the distribution of attention scores in each layer, rather than
096   relying on predefined hyper-parameters (Chen et al., 2024; Arif et al., 2024; He et al., 2024b; Zhang
097   et al., 2023). This adaptive approach allows the ratio to be adjusted according to task complexity,
098   enhancing efficiency for simpler tasks while preserving performance for more complex ones. After
099   determining the ratio, we then select important tokens with the highest normalized attention scores,
100   following prior work (He et al., 2024b; Ren & Zhu, 2024). To alleviate the computational bottleneck
101   in the prefill phase, sparse attention is performed at the token level by computing attention only
102   for the selected important tokens. Notably, this approach seamlessly integrates with existing fast
103   attention implementations without requiring custom GPU kernels. To tackle the memory bottleneck,
104
      the same set of important tokens is applied to compress the KV cache, where we employ high-bit
      quantization for caches of important tokens and low-bit quantization for those of less importance.
105
      Extensive experiments on multimodal benchmarks demonstrate that our method achieves nearly
106
      lossless performance while reducing prefill phase latency by 2.6× and GPU memory usage by 50%.

147   2.1             S PARSE ATTENTION FOR LLM S
148
149   Attention scores have been widely observed to exhibit high sparsity in both LLMs and LVLMs (Xiao
150   et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Zaheer et al., 2020; Beltagy et al., 2020). This
151   sparsity allows sparse attention to overcome the quadratic computational complexity of the standard
152   attention mechanism by restricting each token to focus on only a subset of tokens within the input
153
      sequence (Zhu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Pagliardini et al., 2023; Ribar et al., 2024). Depending
      on the granularity of sparsity, sparse attention can be categorized into unstructured, semi-structured,
154
      and structured schemes. The unstructured scheme (Lefaudeux et al., 2022; He et al., 2024a) employs
155
      sparse attention masks without a fixed structure, making it hardware-unfriendly and challenging to
156   achieve practical inference acceleration. The semi-structured sparse attention uses attention masks
157   with predefined sparse patterns (Jiang et al., 2024; Pagliardini et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024) or
158   introduces N:M sparsity to attention weights (Chen et al., 2023). However, it requires customized
159   computational kernels for each sparse pattern or specific hardware to achieve acceleration. Structured
160   sparse attention (Chen et al., 2024; Arif et al., 2024) directly prunes tokens before the attention
161   computation, enabling acceleration without the need for custom kernels. However, due to its coarse
      granularity, the pruning sparsity and the selection of tokens to prune significantly impact model


                                                              3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      performance. For instance, HiRED (Arif et al., 2024) selects patches with the highest responses
163   based on the feature maps of the visual encoder without considering the input text prompt, leading to
164   suboptimal performance. FastV (Chen et al., 2024) empirically retains all tokens in the first two layers
165   and prunes 50% of the visual tokens in all subsequent layers, resulting in performance degradation in
166   challenging tasks such as ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022). In contrast, our approach achieves superior
167   performance through an adaptive layer-wise ratio assignment scheme for important tokens.
168
169   2.2   KV C ACHE C OMPRESSION
170
171   KV cache prevents re-computation in the decoding phase by storing the key and value states of
172   previous tokens, but with a significant memory bottleneck in long-context scenarios. Previous efforts
173   to compress the KV cache can be broadly categorized into three types: token dropping-based (Ge et al.,
174   2024a; Ren & Zhu, 2024; Zhang et al., 2023), token merging-based (Wang et al., 2024; Wan et al.,
175
      2024; Liu et al., 2024d), and quantization-based approaches (Hooper et al., 2024; He et al., 2024b;
      Yang et al., 2024b; Kang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c). Both token dropping-based and merging-
176
      based methods aim to reduce the number of tokens stored in the KV cache by evicting or merging
177
      less important tokens. However, the information that is evicted or merged cannot be recovered,
178   potentially leading to risks such as contextual incoherency or hallucination (Yang et al., 2024b),
179   especially in multi-round dialogue scenarios. Conversely, quantization-based approaches retain all
180   tokens in the KV cache and apply quantization to the cached values. To preserve performance,
181   mixed-precision quantization further assigns higher bit-width to recent tokens (Liu et al., 2024c)
182   or important tokens (Yang et al., 2024b) in the KV cache. In this paper, we apply mixed-precision
183   quantization to compress the KV cache, leveraging the proposed layer-wise adaptive ratio assignment
184   scheme to achieve a higher compression ratio.

223   variations in the attention map patterns across different layers. Moreover, Figure 1(b) and (c) illustrate
224   that, even within the same layer, attention maps can differ depending on the task and input. In
225   scenarios involving complex tasks, a limited, static ratio for important tokens can impair model
226   performance. This raises the question:
227

526   6           C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   In this paper, we have proposed ZipVL, an efficient inference framework tailored for LVLMs. ZipVL
529   jointly optimizes both the prefill and decoding phases by assigning an adaptive ratio of important
530   tokens. This ratio is dynamically adjusted based on the distribution of attention scores across each
531
      layer, ensuring that the majority of attention scores are preserved. After identifying important
      tokens through normalized attention scores, less significant tokens are excluded from attention
532
      computation during the prefill phase to alleviate the computational bottleneck. Additionally, their
533
      KV cache is quantized to a lower bit-width, mitigating the memory bottleneck in the decoding phase.
534   Extensive experiments have demonstrated that ZipVL significantly enhances the generation efficiency
535   of LVLMs, achieving up to a 2.6× reduction in prefill phase latency and a 50% reduction in GPU
536   memory usage. However, a limitation of our approach is its focus on sparse attention during the
537   prefill phase only, while attention during the decoding phase and the multi-layer perceptron (MLP)
538   modules in both phases remain dense. Future efforts may explore extending sparse computations
539   to MLP modules or the attention mechanism in the decoding phase to further reduce computational
      complexity.
