009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Scaling the amount of compute used to train language models has dramatically
012            improved their capabilities. However, when it comes to inference, we often limit
013            the amount of compute to only one attempt per problem. Here, we explore in-
014            ference compute as another axis for scaling, using the simple technique of re-
015            peatedly sampling candidate solutions from a model. Across multiple tasks and
016            models, we observe that coverage – the fraction of problems that are solved by
017            any generated sample – scales with the number of samples over four orders of
018
               magnitude. Interestingly, the relationship between coverage and the number of
               samples is often log-linear and can be modelled with an exponentiated power law,
019
               suggesting the existence of inference-time scaling laws. In domains like coding
020
               and formal proofs, where answers can be automatically verified, these increases in
021            coverage directly translate into improved performance. When we apply repeated
022            sampling to SWE-bench Lite, the fraction of issues solved with DeepSeek-Coder-
023            V2-Instruct increases from 15.9% with one sample to 56% with 250 samples,
024            outperforming the single-sample state-of-the-art of 43%. In domains without au-
025            tomatic verifiers, we find that common methods for picking from a sample collec-
026            tion (majority voting and reward models) plateau beyond several hundred samples
027            and fail to fully scale with the sample budget.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
      The ability of large language models (LLMs) to solve coding, mathematics, and other reasoning
032
      tasks has improved dramatically over the past several years (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al.,
033
      2020b; OpenAI, 2024; Anthropic, 2024). Scaling the amount of training compute through bigger
034   models, longer pre-training runs, and larger datasets has been a consistent driver of these gains
035   (Hestness et al., 2017; Kaplan et al., 2020b; Hoffmann et al., 2022).
036
037
      In contrast, a comparatively limited investment has been made in scaling the amount of computation
      used during inference. Larger models do require more inference compute than smaller ones, and
038
      prompting techniques like chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2023) can increase answer quality at the
039
      cost of longer (and therefore more computationally expensive) outputs. However, when interacting
040   with LLMs, users and developers often restrict models to making only one attempt when solving a
041   problem.
042
043
      In this work, we explore repeated sampling (Figure 1) as a simple approach to scaling inference
      compute in order to improve reasoning performance. Existing work provides encouraging examples
044
      that repeated sampling can be beneficial in math, coding, and puzzle-solving settings (Wang et al.,
045
      2023; Rozière et al., 2023; Greenblatt, 2024). Notably, AlphaCode (Li et al., 2022), a state-of-the-art
046   system for competitive programming, finds that performance continues to improve with a million
047   samples per problem. Our goal is to systematically characterize these benefits across a range of
048   tasks, models, and sample budgets.
049
      The effectiveness of repeated sampling is determined by two key properties:
050
051
            1. Coverage: As the number of samples increases, what fraction of problems can we solve
052            using any sample that was generated?

069   Both properties are needed for achieving strong real-world performance. With unlimited samples,
070   any model that assigns a non-zero probability to every sequence will achieve perfect coverage. How-
071   ever, repeated sampling is only practical if we can improve coverage with a feasible budget. Simi-
072   larly, generating large sample collections is only useful if the correct samples in a collection can be
073   identified. The difficulty of the precision problem can vary by task. In some settings, existing tools
074   like proof checkers and unit tests can automatically verify every sample. In other cases, like when
075
      solving word problems, other methods for verification are needed.
076   Exploring coverage first, we find that sampling up to 10,000 times can significantly boost coverage
077   on math and coding tasks (Section 2). When solving CodeContests (Li et al., 2022) programming
078   problems using Gemma-2B (Gemma, 2024), we increase coverage by over 300x, from 0.02% with
079   one sample to 7.1% with 10,000 samples. Interestingly, the relationship between log(coverage) and
080   the number of samples often follows an approximate power law (Section 3). With Llama-3 (Meta,
081
      2024) and Gemma models, this leads to coverage growing nearly log-linearly with the number of
      samples over several orders of magnitude.
082
083   In settings with automatic verification tools, increases in coverage translate directly into improved
084   task performance. When applying repeated sampling to competitive programming and writing
085   Lean proofs, models like Llama-3-8B-Instruct can exceed the single-sample performance of much
086   stronger ones like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024). This ability to amplify weaker models extends to the
087
      challenging SWE-bench Lite dataset of real-life GitHub issues (Jimenez et al., 2024), where the cur-
      rent single-sample state-of-the-art (SOTA), achieved by a mixture of GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet,
088
      is 43% (Aide, 2024). When restricted to a single sample, DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct (DeepSeek-
089
      AI et al., 2024) solves only 15.9% of issues. By simply increasing the number of samples to 250,
090   we increase the fraction of solved issues to 56%, exceeding the state-of-the-art by 13%.
091
092   In addition to improving model quality, repeated sampling provides a new mechanism for minimiz-
093
      ing LLM inference costs (Section 2.3). When holding the total number of inference FLOPs constant,
      we find that on some datasets (e.g. MATH), coverage is maximized with a smaller model and more
094
      samples, while on others (e.g CodeContests) it is better to sample fewer times from a larger model.
095
      We also compare API prices between DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct, GPT-4o, and Claude Sonnet
096   3.5 in the context of solving SWE-bench Lite issues. When keeping the agent framework (Moatless
097   Tools (Örwall, 2024)) constant, sampling five times from the weaker and cheaper DeepSeek model
098   solves more issues than single samples from Claude or GPT while also being over 3x cheaper.
099

100
      sampling. As the number of samples increases, coverage improves through models generating cor-
101
      rect solutions to problems they have not previously solved. However, these increasingly rare correct
102   generations are only beneficial if verifiers can “find the needle in the haystack” and identify them
103   from collections of mostly-incorrect samples. In math word problem settings, we find that two com-

105   solving MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) problems with Llama-3-8B-Instruct, coverage increases
106   from 82.9% with 100 samples to 98.44% with 10,000 samples. However, when using majority vot-
107   ing or reward models to select final answers, the biggest performance increase is only from 40.50%
      to 41.41% over the same sample range. As the number of samples increases, the gap between cov-

      In summary, our primary observations are:
128
129
           1. We demonstrate that scaling inference compute through repeated sampling leads to large
130
              improvements in coverage across a variety of tasks and models. This makes it possible, and
131           sometimes cost-effective, to amplify weaker models with many samples and outperform
132           single samples from more capable models.
133
           2. We show that the relationship between coverage and the number of samples can often
134
              be modelled using an exponentiated power law, suggesting a form of scaling laws for
135           inference-time compute.
136
137
           3. In domains without automatic verifiers, we show that common approaches to verification
              plateau beyond approximately 100 samples. This leads to a growing gap between the per-
138
              formance achieved with these methods and the coverage upper bound.

141   2   S CALING R EPEATED S AMPLING
142
143   We focus on pass-fail tasks where a candidate solution can be scored as right or wrong. The primary
144   metric of interest for these tasks is the success rate: the fraction of problems that we are able to
145   solve. With repeated sampling, we consider a setup where a model can generate many candidate
146   solutions while attempting to solve a problem. The success rate is therefore influenced both by
147
      the ability to generate correct samples for many problems (i.e. coverage), as well as the ability to
      identify these correct samples (i.e. precision).
148
149   The difficulty of the precision problem depends on the availability of tools for sample verification.
150   When proving formal statements in Lean, proof checkers can quickly identify whether a candidate
151   solution is correct. Similarly, unit tests can be used to verify candidate solutions to coding tasks.
152   In these cases, precision is handled automatically, and improving coverage directly translates into
153
      higher success rates. In contrast, the tools available for verifying solutions to math word problems
      are limited, necessitating additional verification methods that decide on a single final answer from
154
      many (often conflicting) samples.
155

159

160        2. MATH: Another dataset of math word problems that are generally harder than those from
161           GSM8K (Chen et al., 2024a). Similarly, we evaluate on 128 random problems from this
              dataset’s test set.

204   generate N samples for each problem index i and calculate the number of correct samples Ci . We

249   2.2   R EPEATED S AMPLING IS E FFECTIVE ACROSS M ODEL S IZES AND FAMILIES
250
251   The results from Section 2.1 demonstrate that repeated sampling can improve coverage. However,
252   we only show this trend for three recent, instruction-tuned models with 8B or more parameters. We
253   now show that these trends hold across other model sizes, families, and levels of post-training. We
254   expand our evaluation to include a broader set of models:
255
256         • Llama 3: Llama-3-8B, Llama-3-8B-Instruct, Llama-3-70B-Instruct.
257
            • Gemma: Gemma-2B, Gemma-7B (Gemma, 2024).
258
259         • Pythia: Pythia-70M through Pythia-12B (eight models in total) (Biderman et al., 2023).
260
261   We restrict evaluation to the MATH and CodeContests datasets to minimize inference costs, report-
262   ing our results in Figure 3. Coverage increases across almost every model we test, with smaller
263   models showing some of the sharpest increases in coverage when repeated sampling is applied. On
264   CodeContests, the coverage of Gemma-2B increases by over 300x, from a pass@1 of 0.02% to a
265   pass@10k of 7.1%. Similarly, when solving MATH problems with Pythia-160M, coverage increases
266
      from a pass@1 of 0.27% to a pass@10k of 57.03%.
267   The exception to this pattern of increasing coverage across models is with the Pythia family evalu-
268   ated on CodeContests. All Pythia models achieve zero coverage on this dataset, even with a budget
269   of 10,000 samples. We speculate that this due to Pythia being trained on less coding-specific data
      than Llama and Gemma.

282   2.3    R EPEATED S AMPLING C AN H ELP BALANCE P ERFORMANCE AND C OST
283
      One takeaway from the results in Sections 2.1 and 2.2 is that repeated sampling makes it possible to
284   amplify a weaker model’s capabilities and outperform single samples from stronger models. Here,
285   we demonstrate that this amplification can be more cost-effective than using a stronger, more expen-
286   sive model, providing practitioners with a new degree of freedom when trying to jointly optimize
287   performance and costs.
288
      We first consider FLOPs as a cost metric, examining the Llama-3 results from Section 2.1. We
289
      re-plot our results from Figure 2, now visualizing coverage as a function of total inference FLOPs
290   instead of the sample budget. Since Llama-3 models are dense transformers where the majority of
291   parameters are used in matrix multiplications, we approximate inference FLOPs with the formula:
292
293
294             FLOPs per token ≈ 2 ∗ (num parameters + 2 ∗ num layers ∗ token dim ∗ context length)
295       total inference FLOPs ≈ num prompt tokens ∗ FLOPs per token
296
                                + num decoded tokens ∗ FLOPs per token ∗ num completions
297
298   We present our re-scaled results for MiniF2F, CodeContests, MATH, and GSM8K in Figure 4. Inter-
299   estingly, the model that maximizes coverage varies with the compute budget and task. On MiniF2F,
300   GSM8K and MATH, Llama-3-8B-Instruct obtains a higher coverage than the larger (and more ex-
301   pensive) 70B model when the FLOP budget is fixed. However for CodeContests, the 70B model is
302   almost always more cost effective. We note that examining FLOPs alone can be a crude cost metric
303   that ignores other aspects of system efficiency (Dehghani et al., 2022). In particular, repeated sam-
304   pling can make use of high batch sizes and specialized optimizations that improve system through-
305
      put relative to single-sample inference workloads (Juravsky et al., 2024; Athiwaratkun et al., 2024;
      Zheng et al., 2024). We discuss this in more detail in Section 7.
306
307   We also examine the dollar costs of repeated sampling when solving SWE-bench Lite issues using
308   current API pricing. Keeping the agent framework (Moatless Tools) constant, we consider drawing
309   a single sample per issue from Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o as well as repeatedly sampling from
310   DeepSeek-Coder-V2-Instruct. We report the average cost per issue and issue resolution rate with
311
      each approach in Table 1. While the DeepSeek model is weaker than the GPT and Claude models,
      it is also over 10x cheaper. In this case, repeated sampling provides a cheaper alternative to paying
312
      a premium for access to strong models while achieving a superior issue solve rate.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
449
450   Scaling Inference Compute: Methods that perform additional computation during inference have
451   been successful across many areas of deep learning. Across a variety of game environments, state-of-
452   the-art methods leverage inference-time search to examine many possible future game states before
453   deciding on a move (Campbell et al., 2002; Silver et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2020a). Similar tree-
454
      based methods can also be effective in combination with LLMs, allowing models to better plan and
      explore different approaches (Yao et al., 2023; Besta et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Trinh et al., 2024).
455
      Another axis for increasing LLM inference compute allows models to spend tokens deliberating on
456
      a problem before coming to a solution (Yao et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023; Zelikman et al., 2024).
457   Additionally, multiple models can be ensembled together at inference time to combine their strengths
458   (Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024b; Ong et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023). Yet
459   another approach involves using LLMs to critique and refine their own responses (Madaan et al.,
460   2023; Bai et al., 2022).
461
      Repeated Sampling: Previous work has demonstrated that repeated sampling can improve LLM
462
      capabilities in multiple domains. One of the most effective use cases is coding (Rozière et al., 2023;
463   Chen et al., 2021; Kulal et al., 2019), where performance continues to scale up to a million samples
464   and verification tools (e.g. unit tests) are often available to automatically score every candidate so-
465   lution. Recently, Greenblatt (2024) shows that repeated sampling is effective when solving puzzles
466   from the ARC challenge (Chollet, 2019), observing log-linear scaling as the number of samples
467   increases. In chat applications, repeated sampling combined with best-of-N ranking using a reward
468   model can outperform greedily sampling a single response (Irvine et al., 2023). In domains without
469   automatic verification tools, existing work shows that using majority voting (Wang et al., 2023) or
470   training a model-based verifier (Cobbe et al., 2021; Lightman et al., 2023; Hosseini et al., 2024;
471
      Wang et al., 2024c; Kang et al., 2024), to decide on a final answer can improve performance on rea-
      soning tasks relative to taking a single sample. Notably, recent work also treats the LLM itself as the
472
      source of verification either directly through prompting (Yuan et al., 2024; Davis et al., 2024), or by
473
      training a lightweight classifier on the model’s representations Li et al. (2024). Nguyen et al. (2024)
474   finds that performing majority voting over answers that exceed a threshold length can outperform
475   voting across all answers. Concurrent with our work, Song et al. (2024) finds that using the best
476   available sample improves LLM performance on chat, math, and code tasks, sweeping up to a max
477   of 128 samples. Additionally, Hassid et al. (2024) find that when solving coding tasks, it can be
478   more effective to draw more samples from a smaller model than draw fewer samples from a larger
479   one.
480   Scaling Laws: Characterizing how scaling affects model performance can lead to more informed
481   decisions on how to allocate resources. Scaling laws for LLM training find a power law relation-
482   ship between training loss and the amount of training compute, as well as provide estimates for
483   the optimal model and dataset size given a fixed compute budget (Hestness et al., 2017; Kaplan
484   et al., 2020a; Hoffmann et al., 2022). Jones (2021) finds scaling laws in the context of the board
485   game Hex, observing that performance scales predictably with model size and the difficulty of the
      problem. Interestingly, they also show that performance scales with the amount of inference-time


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      compute spent while performing tree search. Recently, Shao et al. (2024) observe scaling laws when
487   augmenting LLMs with external retrieval datasets, finding that performance on retrieval tasks scales
488   smoothly with the size of the retrieval corpus.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
492
493
      In this work, we explore repeated sampling as an axis for scaling compute at inference time in
      order to improve model performance. Across a range of models and tasks, repeated sampling can
494
      significantly improve the fraction of problems solved using any generated sample (i.e. coverage).
495
      When correct solutions can be identified (either with automatic verification tools or other verification
496   algorithms), repeated sampling can amplify model capabilities during inference. This amplification
497   can make the combination of a weaker model and many samples more performant and cost-effective
498   than drawing fewer samples from a stronger, more expensive model.
499
      Improving Repeated Sampling: In our experiments, we explore a simple version of repeated sam-
500
      pling where all samples are generated independently of one another using the exact same prompt
501
      and hyperparameters. We believe that this setup can be refined to improve performance, particularly
502   along the following directions:
503
504         1. Solution Diversity: We currently rely on a positive sampling temperature as the sole mech-
505            anism for creating diversity among samples. Combining this token-level sampling with
506            other, higher-level approaches may be able to further increase diversity. For example, Al-
507            phaCode conditions different samples on different metadata tags.
508         2. Multi-Turn Interactions: Despite automatic verification tools being available when solv-
509            ing CodeContests and MiniF2F problems, we use only a single-turn setup where models
510            generate a solution without any ability to iterate on it. Providing models with execution
511            feedback from these tools should improve solution quality. We are interested in the trade-
512            offs associated with multi-turn interactions, since each sample becomes more expensive,
513            but also may be more likely to succeed.
514         3. Learning From Previous Samples: Currently, our experiments fully isolate samples from
515            each other. Access to previous samples, particularly if verification tools can provide feed-
516            back on them, may be helpful for future generations.
517
518
      Repeated Sampling and Inference Systems: Repeated sampling is a distinct LLM inference work-
      load from serving chatbot requests. Production chatbot deployments place an emphasis on low
519
      response latencies, and adhering to latency targets can force a lower per-device batch size and re-
520
      duce hardware utilization. In contrast, when sampling many completions to a single prompt, a
521   larger emphasis can be placed on overall throughput and maximizing hardware utilization. Addi-
522   tionally, repeated sampling can benefit from specialized attention optimizations that exploit overlaps
523   in prompts across sequences (Juravsky et al., 2024; Athiwaratkun et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024).
524   Repeated sampling inference can therefore be accomplished at a lower cost than naively making
525   many parallel requests to a chatbot-oriented API. These cost savings can further motivate choosing
526   to sample many times from a cheaper model instead of fewer times from a more expensive one.
527   Verifiers: Our results from Section 4 highlight the importance of designing scalable sample verifi-
528   cation methods when tools for automatically doing so are unavailable. Equipping models with the
529   ability to reliably assess their own outputs will allow repeated sampling to be applied to far more
530   tasks. Of particular interest is applying repeated sampling to unstructured tasks like creative writing,
531   which can require a more subjective comparison between different samples than the pass-fail tasks
532   we consider. An alternative direction to developing model-based verifiers is to design converters that
533   can make an unstructured task verifiable, for example by formalizing an informal math statement
534   into a language like Lean so that proof checkers can be applied.
