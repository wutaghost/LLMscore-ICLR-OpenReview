009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In class-incremental learning (CIL), effective incremental learning strategies are
012            essential to mitigate task confusion and catastrophic forgetting, especially as the
013            number of tasks t increases. Current exemplar replay strategies impose O(t)
014            memory/compute complexities. We propose an autoencoder-based hybrid replay
015            (AHR) strategy that leverages our new hybrid autoencoder (HAE) to function as
016            a compressor to alleviate the requirement for large memory, achieving O(0.1t)
017            at the worst case with the computing complexity of O(t) while accomplishing
018
               state-of-the-art performance. The decoder later recovers the exemplar data stored
               in the latent space, rather than in raw format. Additionally, HAE is designed for
019
               both discriminative and generative modeling, enabling classification and replay
020
               capabilities, respectively. HAE adopts the charged particle system energy mini-
021            mization equations and repulsive force algorithm for the incremental embedding
022            and distribution of new class centroids in its latent space. Our results demonstrate
023            that AHR consistently outperforms recent baselines across multiple benchmarks
024            while operating with the same memory/compute budgets.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Incremental learning addresses the challenge of learning from an upcoming stream of data with
030   a changing distribution (Parisi et al., 2019; De Lange et al., 2021; Hadsell et al., 2020). To study
031   incremental learning, two common scenarios are often considered: task-incremental learning (TIL)
032   and class-incremental learning (CIL). CIL at the test phase requires jointly discriminating among all
033
      classes seen in all previous tasks without knowing task-IDs. Given task-IDs to the model during the
      test phase, CIL reduces to TIL (Ven & Tolias, 2019). The issue with CIL is that it not only suffers
034
      from catastrophic forgetting (CF) but also task confusion (TC), which happens when the model
035
      struggles to distinguish among different tasks observed so far while still being able to identify classes
036   within a given task (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Belouadah et al., 2021; Masana et al., 2020; Cormerais et al.,
037   2021).

084   We demonstrate that it is feasible to combine the strengths of both the exemplar (Rebuffi et al., 2017)
085   and generative replay (Shin et al., 2017; Ven et al., 2020) in a hybrid replay strategy and consistently
086   achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance while significantly reducing the memory footprint of
087   the exemplar replay strategy from O(t) to O(0.1t) at the worst case, using a new hybrid autoencoder
088   based on hybrid replay. Our main contributions are as follows:
089
090          • We propose a novel autoencoder named hybrid autoencoder (HAE): the term hybrid au-
091            toencoder’ indicates that HAE is capable of both discriminative and generative modeling
092            (Goodfellow et al., 2016), for classification and replay, respectively. Furthermore, HAE
093
               employs the charged particle system energy minimization (CPSEM) equations and repulsive
               force algorithm (RFA) (Nazmitdinov et al., 2017) for the incremental embedding and distri-
094
               bution of new classes in its latent space. HAE uses RFA (Nazmitdinov et al., 2017) in its
095
               latent space to repel samples of different classes away from each other such that in the test
096            phase the Euclidean distance can be used to measure the distance of a sample from centroids
097            of different classes in the latent space to know to which class the test sample belongs to (see
098            Figs. 1(a) and 1(b)).
099
             • We propose a new strategy called autoencoder-based hybrid replay (AHR): the term hybrid
100
               replay’ refers to the fact that AHR utilizes a combination of exemplar replay and generative
101
               replay. Specifically, AHR does not store the exemplars in the input space like exemplar
102            replay which would require a large memory O(t); it rather stores the data samples in the
103            latent space after they are encoded O(0.1t). Hence, AHR has characteristics that leverage
104            the advantages of both exemplar and generative replay. AHR can decode data samples
105            when they are needed for replay with negligible loss of fidelity because the decoder has
106            been designed to memorize the training data as opposed to being designed to generalize
107            and produce novel data samples. Fig. 1(c) shows how the data generation for replay is
               performed. As a result, AHR does not suffer from hazy pseudo-data during replay like

244   3    L ITERATURE REVIEW AND CONTEXTUALIZATION OF AHR
245
246
247   Task-based or task-free. Incremental learning literature features various learning scenarios that
248   present their own unique challenges, and accordingly, diverse strategies have been developed (Parisi
249
      et al., 2019; De Lange et al., 2021). In the first place, there are two learning scenarios of task-based
      (Ven & Tolias, 2019; Masana et al., 2020) and task-free (Ven et al., 2021; Aljundi et al., 2019b).
250
      In task-based, the model receives the data in the form of tasks: The task-based scenario is divided
251
      into two popular scenarios: task-incremental learning (TIL) and class-incremental learning (CIL).
252   Whereas in TIL the model receives the task-IDs during both training and inference, in CIL the
253   task-IDs are not given during inference and the model must infer them. The task-free scenario,
254   meanwhile, totally abandons the notion of tasks altogether (Ven et al., 2021; Aljundi et al., 2019b).
255   AHR makes no prohibitive assumptions and can operate in all the above scenarios. Nevertheless, for
256   comparability with the majority of works in IL, this paper examines the performance of AHR in the
257   CIL setting.
258   Offline or online. Incremental learning can be either offline (Masana et al., 2020) or online (Zajac˛
259   et al., 2023), where in the offline learning scenario, the data of each task can be fed to the model
260   multiple times before moving on to the next task. Conversely, in the online scenario, the model visits
261   the data only once as they arrive and cannot iterate on them. In the literature, there are more works
262   on the offline scenario (Parisi et al., 2019; De Lange et al., 2021; Masana et al., 2020). Therefore,
263   this paper examines the performance of AHR in the offline scenario.
264   Challenges and strategies. CIL faces many challenges such as CF, weight drift, activation drift,
265   task-recency bias, and TC (Masana et al., 2020; Cormerais et al., 2021). To tackle the weight drift
266   and activation drift challenges of CIL (Kao et al., 2020), the most popular category of strategies,
267   regularization, is often adopted (Zenke et al., 2017). Although regularization is not alone effective in
268   mitigating the TC challenge of CIL (Ven et al., 2021), it has been proven productive in tandem with
269   other strategies like exemplar strategies (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Farquhar & Gal, 2018a; Hsu et al., 2018;
      Castro et al., 2018; Dhar et al., 2019; Serra et al., 2018). Regularization strategies have two branches:


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Weight regularization and data regularization. Weight regularization mitigates weight drift of the
271   parameters optimized for the previous tasks by assigning an importance coefficient for each parameter
272   in the network (assuming the independence of weights) after learning each task (Kirkpatrick et al.,
273   2017; Zenke et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2018; Farquhar & Gal, 2018b; Aljundi et al., 2018; Chaudhry
274   et al., 2018). When learning new tasks, the importance coefficients help in minimizing weight drift.
275   EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) and SI (Zenke et al., 2017) are popular weight regularization strategies.
276   EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) relies on a diagonal approximation of the Fisher matrix to weigh the
277   contributions from different parameters. SI (Zenke et al., 2017) maintains and updates per-parameter
278   importance measures in an online manner.
279   Data regularization is the second regularization strategy, aimed at preventing activation drift through
280   knowledge distillation (Buciluundefined et al., 2006; Hinton et al., 2015), originally designed to
281   learn a more parameter-efficient student network from a larger teacher network. Differs from weight
282   regularization which imposes constraints on parameter updates, knowledge distillation focuses on
283   ensuring consistency in the responses of the new and old models. This distinctive feature provides a
284   broader solution space which is why distillation has become the dominant strategy used in tandem
285
      with rehearsal strategy (Li & Hoiem, 2017a; Jung et al., 2016; Dhar et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020;
      Lee et al., 2019). LwF (Li & Hoiem, 2017a), a popular data regularization strategy, uses a distillation
286
      loss to keep predictions consistent with the ones from an old model. Our AHR strategy as discussed
287
      in the previous section incorporates LwF into exemplar replay to overcome activation drift.
288
289   Bias-correction. Its aim is to address the challenge of task-recency bias, which refers to the tendency
290   of incrementally learned networks to be biased towards classes in the most recently learned task
291
      (Belouadah et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2019; Maltoni & Lomonaco, 2019; Lomonaco
      & Maltoni, 2017; Zeno et al., 2021; Belouadah & Popescu, 2020). Labels trick (LT) (Dhar et al.,
292
      2019) is a rehearsal-free bias-correcting algorithm that prevents negative bias on past tasks. LT
293
      often improves the performance when added on top of other strategies (Wu et al., 2019). However,
294   because AHR separates representation learning from classification similar to (Rebuffi et al., 2017),
295   which gives a dose of immunity to task-recency bias. Because AHR samples the tasks/classes for its
296   minibatch in a statistically representative manner during training inspired by (Castro et al., 2018),
297   incorporating an explicit bias-correction strategy such as LT proved unnecessary.
298
      Generative classifier. CIL strategies can be categorized into two types: discriminative- and
299
      generative-based classification. Strategies count as discriminative classifiers when eventually a
300   discriminator performs the classification. However, generative classifiers perform classification only
301   and directly using generative modeling (Ven et al., 2021; Pang et al., 2005; Zajac    ˛ et al., 2023). Gen-C
302   (Ven et al., 2021) is a novel rehearsal-free generative classifier strategy; the strategy does energy-based
303   generative modeling (Li et al., 2020) via VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013) and importance sampling
304   (Burda et al., 2016). SLDA (Hayes & Kanan, 2020) (popular in data mining (Kim et al., 2011; Pang
305   et al., 2005)) is thought to be another form of generative classifier (Ven et al., 2021); however, it
306   prevents representation learning.
307   Exemplar replay. The most popular exemplar replay strategy, iCaRL (Rebuffi et al., 2017), fuses
308   exemplar replay and LwF (Li & Hoiem, 2017a). To mitigate the task-recency bias, iCaRL separates
309   the classifier from the representation learning (implicit bias-correction). At inference, iCaRL classifies
310   via the closest centroid. EEIL (Castro et al., 2018) introduces balanced training, where at the
311   end of each training session an equal number of exemplars from all classes is used for a limited
312   number of iterations. Similar to iCaRL, EEIL incorporates data regularization (distillation loss)
313   into exemplar replay. As discussed in the previous section, our strategy, AHR, also separates the
314
      representation learning and classification. Furthermore, AHR employs balanced training (from EEIL)
      and distillation loss via LwF (similar to iCaRL).
315
316   MIR (Aljundi et al., 2019a) trains on the exemplar memory by selecting exemplars that have a larger
317   loss increase after each training step. GD (Lee et al., 2019) utilizes external data to distill knowledge
318   from previous tasks. BiC (Wu et al., 2019) learns to rectify the bias in predictions by adding an
319   additional layer while IL2M (Belouadah & Popescu, 2019) introduces saved certainty statistics
320
      for predictions of classes from previous tasks (explicit bias-correction). LUCIR (Hou et al., 2019)
      replaces the standard softmax layer with a cosine normalization layer. GDumb (Prabhu et al., 2020)
321
      ensures a balanced training set. ER (Chaudhry et al., 2019) analyzes the effectiveness of episodic
322
      memory.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Generative replay (pseudo-rehearsal). This strategy generates synthetic examples of previous tasks
325   via a generative model trained on previous tasks. DGR (Shin et al., 2017) generates synthetic samples
326   via an unconditional GAN where an auxiliary classifier classifies synthetic samples. MeRGAN (Wu
327   et al., 2018) improves DGR via a label-conditional GAN and replay alignment. DGM (Ostapenko
328   et al., 2019) combines the advantages of conditional GANs and synaptic plasticity using neural
329   masking leveraging dynamic network expansion mechanism to increase model capacity. Lifelong
330   GAN (Zhai et al., 2019) extends image generation without CF from label-conditional to image-
331   conditional GANs. Other strategies perform feature replay (Ven et al., 2020; Xiang et al., 2019;
332   Kemker & Kanan, 2017) which needs a fixed backbone network to provide good representations.
333   Hybrid replay (AHR). Technically, our AHR strategy lies somewhere between exemplar replay and
334   generative replay: AHR differs from exemplar replay in that it stores the samples in the latent space.
335   AHR also differs from generative replay in that it does not rely on synthetic data or pseudo-data;
336   instead, it relies on the memorization of the original data. By encoding and decoding the exemplars
337   into and out of the latent space, AHR mitigates the memory complexity of current exemplar replay
338   strategies significantly and can be readily applied to the exemplar replay strategies. In the literature,
339
      hybrid replay has been studied in several works (Zhou et al., 2022): Tong et al. (2022) utilizes a
      closed-loop encoding-decoding framework that stores only the means and covariances of features
340
      rather than the individual features themselves. The authors organize the latent space using a Linear
341
      Discriminative Representation, which partitions the latent space into a series of linear subspaces,
342   each corresponding to a distinct class of objects. Hayes et al. (2020b); Wang et al. (2021) do not
343   store raw exemplars but instead using compressed exemplars derived from mid-level CNN features (a
344   strategy that is increasingly recognized as a promising direction in incremental learning research). In
345   (Hayes et al., 2020b; Wang et al., 2021), classification occurs after the decoding process, employing
346   a cross-entropy loss function. In contrast, our method performs classification directly within the
347   latent space of the encoder, similar to (Tong et al., 2022). Pellegrini et al. (2020) stores ‘activations
348   volumes’ of intermediate layers rather than raw data.

353   Baselines. We consider five categories of baselines: vanilla strategies, generative classifier, generative
354   replay, exemplar replay, and hybrid replay. Vanilla strategies include the naive strategies that serve as
355   the lower or upper bound: Fine-Tuning (FT) does simple fine-tuning whenever a new task arrives,
356   FT-E incorporates exemplar replay into fine-tuning, and Joint trains on all tasks seen so far (Masana
357
      et al., 2020). For generative classifiers, we include SLDA (Hayes & Kanan, 2020), Gen-C (Ven
      et al., 2021), and PEC (Zajac
                                  ˛ et al., 2023). For generative replay, DGR (Shin et al., 2017), MeRGAN
358
      (Wu et al., 2018), and BI-R-SI (Ven et al., 2020) are considered. For exemplar replay, the most
359
      strong baseline strategy, we include GD (Lee et al., 2019), GDumb (Prabhu et al., 2020), iCaRL
360   (Rebuffi et al., 2017), EEIL (Castro et al., 2018), BiC (Wu et al., 2019), LUCIR (Hou et al., 2019),
361   and IL2M (Belouadah & Popescu, 2019). Finally, for hybrid replay, we include i-CTRL (Tong
362   et al., 2022), REMIND (Hayes et al., 2020b), and REMIND+ (Wang et al., 2021). Note that since
363   regularization and bias-correction strategies have been often applied to the aforementioned strategies
364   supplementarily (and they are not performant for CIL on their own), we do not provide results for
365   them independently.

367   et al., 2021; Zajac
                        ˛ et al., 2023), where the popular image classification datasets are split up such
368   that each task presents data pertaining to a subset of classes, in a non-overlapping manner. For
369   naming benchmarks, we follow (Masana et al., 2020), where dataset D is divided into T tasks with C

      of all tasks.

532   5                                    C ONCLUSION
533
534   Exemplar replay strategies rely on storing raw data, which can be highly memory-consuming,
535   especially since datasets usually require orders of magnitude more memory than models. Meanwhile,
536   generative replay, training a (generative) model for generating the pseudo-data of past tasks, requires
537   far less memory but tends to be less effective. We proposed AHR, a hybrid approach that combines
538   the strengths of both methods, utilizing HAE with RFA for the incremental embedding of new tasks
539   in the latent space. Instead of storing the raw data in the input space, AHR stores them in the latent
      space of HAE. Our experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of AHR.

      A    C OMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
757
758
      Generative replay. This strategy (Shin et al., 2017) utilizes a generative model denoted by G EN()
759   whose size is not growing with respect to the total number of tasks I. G EN() generates data of the
760   past tasks {1, . . . , Tℓ − 1} to be interleaved with the new task Tℓ to be fed into discriminative model
761   D IS() as well as G EN() lest they are forgotten. Since neither the size of G EN() nor D IS() is growing
762   with respect to the total number of tasks I, the memory complexity can be said to be O(cte). The
763   compute complexity, however, depends on the total amount of data to be fed to both G EN() and D IS()
764   which is a function of the number of tasks seen so far if they are not to be forgotten. Hence, the
765   compute complexity is O(t).
766   Generative classifier. This strategy (Ven et al., 2021) trains a brand new out-of-distribution detector
767   for each new class c (not task) denoted by OODc (). Hence, the number of the models grows as new
768   classes arrive indicating that the memory complexity is O(t). Because this strategy trains a brand new
769   model each time, it does not have to overwrite previous knowledge, and therefore, does not require
770   replaying of the old data. As a result, the amount of data to be fed into each OODc () whenever each
771   model is trained does not grow with the number of classes so far because only the data of class c is
772   fed into OODc (). Hence, the compute complexity is O(cte).
773   Exemplar replay. This strategy (Rebuffi et al., 2017) uses a memory denoted by M EM that stores
774   dozens of exemplars per class so that each time a new task arrives a representative minibatch of data
775   consisting of all previous tasks is fed into the discriminative model denoted by D IS(). In this strategy,
776   both the memory M EM has to grow in proportion to the number of tasks, and, the amount of data
777   each time the discriminative model D IS() must consume has to increase in proportion to the number
778   of tasks seen so far, therefore, memory and compute complexities are O(t).
779   Hybrid replay (ours). Although learning new tasks requires high-quality data, mitigating forgetting,
780   as it has been reported (Ven et al., 2020), can be effectively accomplished with tolerably lossy
781   data. Leveraging that, our hybrid replay strategy, a combination of generative and exemplar replay,
782   proposes to use an autoencoder consisting of an encoder denoted by E NC() and a decoder denoted by
783   D EC(). E NC() serves two goals: (i) it maps the input data to the latent space making it possible to use
784   Euclidean distance for classification, and (ii) it compresses down the input data such that they can be
785
      stored in the memory M EM efficiently. Meanwhile, D EC() decompresses the data in M EM each time
      learning a new task. Since E NC() compresses down the input data, 10 times at the very least in our
786
      experiments, the memory complexity becomes O(0.1t), whereas the compute complexity is O(t).
787
      Note that the introduced overhead by adding D EC() is negligible as discussed in the experimental
788   results section.
