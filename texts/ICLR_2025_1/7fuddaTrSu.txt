009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Climate models serve as critical tools for evaluating the effects of climate change
012            and projecting future climate scenarios. However, the reliance on numerical sim-
013            ulations of physical equations renders them computationally intensive and inef-
014            ficient. While deep learning methodologies have made significant progress in
015            weather forecasting, they are still unstable for climate emulation tasks. Here,
016            we propose PACE, a lightweight 684K parameter Physics Informed Uncertainty
017            Aware Climate Emulator. PACE emulates temperature and precipitation stably
018            for 86 years while only being trained on greenhouse gas emissions data. We in-
019
               corporate a fundamental physical law of advection-diffusion in PACE accounting
               for boundary conditions and empirically estimating the diffusion co-efficient and
020
               flow velocities from emissions data. PACE has been trained on 15 climate models
021
               provided by ClimateSet outperforming baselines across most of the climate mod-
022            els and advancing a new state of the art in a climate diagnostic task. Our code is
023            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PACE-6874/

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The past decade has seen superior performing data-driven weather forecasting models Kochkov
029   et al. (2024); Lam et al. (2023); Nguyen et al. (2023b) as compared to numerical weather prediction
030   models (ECMWF, 2023). However, the medium range forecasting ability makes them unstable for
031   climate modelling several years into the future (Chattopadhyay & Hassanzadeh, 2023).
032
      Climate models are governed by temporal partial differential equations (PDEs) to describe complex
033
      physical processes Gupta & Brandstetter (2022), enabling simulations of climate behavior under
034
      various forcing scenarios, such as fluctuating greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The computational
035   expense associated with solving these PDEs involves, executing these climate model simulations
036   typically for several months (Balaji et al., 2017).
037
038
      In order to faithfully emulate the reference climate model, a Machine Learning (ML) based climate
      emulator should follow the fundamental physical laws that govern the dynamics of the atmosphere
039
      (Watt-Meyer et al., 2023). Additionally, accurately capturing the influence of GHG and aerosols is
040
      essential for simulating realistic climate responses to different emission scenarios (Bloch-Johnson
041   et al., 2024).
042
043   The few existing climate emulators that incorporate GHG concentrations typically rely on auto-
044
      regressive training regimes. These models predict climate variables at future time steps based on past
      states, but often fail to account for the projected emissions at those future times. This limitation leads
045
      to significant inaccuracies in predicting future climate states, especially under varying anthropogenic
046
      emission scenarios, highlighting a critical gap in current climate modeling approaches.
047
048   To address this gap, we propose PACE, which treats climate emulation as a diagnostic-type predic-
049   tion and integrate emissions data directly into the model’s training framework, to predict climate
050
      variables from a given parallel time-series of climate forcer emission maps (GHG and aerosols) al-
      lowing for more accurate simulation of future climate states under varying concentration scenarios.
051
052   Furthermore, we focus on two key phenomenon observed by our climate system i.e. advection and
053   diffusion. In climate modeling, the advection-diffusion equation is fundamental for simulating the
      transport and dispersion of climate variables, such as temperature and moisture (Choi et al., 2023).

058   Our contributions are as follows:

097   2.1                         M ACHINE L EARNING (ML) AND P HYSICS BASED C LIMATE E MULATORS

099
      Recently, ML based and Physics Informed climate emulators have been successful in emulating
      several climate variables. Watt-Meyer et al. (2023) proposed ACE (AI2 Climate Emulator) based
100
      on Spherical Neural Operator (SFNO) architecture for effective physics informed emulation. Guan
101
      et al. (2024) proposed LUCIE, also based on SFNO to account for the computational complexity
102   of ACE. Choi et al. (2023) proposed climate modelling using Graph Neural Network (GNN) and
103   Neural ODE, but do not account for GHG emissions or show any long term stability. Additionally,
104   there are several climate emulators which are trained on only one climate model unknown for their
105   generalizability across different climate models (Scher, 2018; Mansfield et al., 2020; Beusch et al.,
106   2020; Cachay et al., 2021; Watson-Parris et al., 2022). Bassetti et al. (2024) use diffusion models
107   for climate emulation, however their primary goal is temporal downscaling. Nguyen et al. (2023a)
      accounts for multi-model training, however it is limited to medium range forecasting.

      2.2   M ODELLING P HYSICAL S YSTEMS USING N EURAL N ETWORKS
109
110   The neural ordinary differential equation(ODE) model proposed by Chen et al. (2018) has demon-
111   strated significant potential for solving partial differential equations (PDEs) that govern the complex
112   physical systems, opening up numerous new research avenues in the field (Mattheakis et al., 2022;
113   Dandekar et al., 2020; Finzi et al., 2020; Lutter et al., 2019). Further, physics-informed neural net-
114   works (PINNs) were used to to solve the advection-dispersion equation using discretization-free and
115   reduced-order methods (Vadyala et al., 2022; He & Tartakovsky, 2021). Neural Networks (NN) have
116
      also been used as surrogate models for obtaining PDE solutions in fluid dynamics and forecasting
      (Lu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Brandstetter et al., 2022; Sønderby et al., 2020; Keisler, 2022).

143   3.2   A DVECTION D IFFUSION P ROCESS
144
145   We model climate emulation as a continuous spatio-temporal process which captures two funda-
146   mental physical processes: advection and diffusion, which together dictate how substances are trans-
147   ported and spread out throughout the climate system. The general form of the advection-diffusion
148
      equation in a climate system is defined in equation 1.
149   To faithfully emulate the climate’s chaotic nature, it is essential to determine the path and rate
150   at which the physical quantities are transported given by v · ∇u where v is the velocity vector
151   of the fluid (e.g., wind velocity) and ∇u is the gradient of the quantity being transported (e.g.,
152   temperature or concentration). On the other hand, diffusion models the distribution of physical
153   quantities such as heat, moisture, and other properties within the atmosphere D∇2 u where D is the
154
      diffusion coefficient, indicating how the scalar field spreads out due to molecular diffusion.
155   We employ Neural ODE presented by Chen et al. (2018) to solve the 2D advection diffusion equa-
156   tion 3 by discretizing the spatial domain using Finite Difference Method (FDM) Fiadeiro & Veronis

158   gitude x latitude). FDM employ spatial discretization to approximate derivatives using the values at
159   grid points. We explain the spatail discretization and show it’s effect visually in section 4.

228   3.2.3    P ERIODIC B OUNDARY C ONDITIONS AND H ARMONICS S PATIO -T EMPORAL
229            E MBEDDINGS
230
      We implement periodic boundary condition (PBC) to simulate the entire planet. Considering Earth
231
      as roughly spherical, PBC ensure that the boundary at one edge of the domain connects seamlessly
232
      to the opposite edge, avoiding artificial edge effects and ensuring continuity. Mathematically, if
233   f (x, y) is the state variable, periodic conditions imply f (x, y) = f (x + Lx , y) = f (x, y + Ly )
234   where Lx and Ly are the domain lengths in the x and y directions, respectively.
235
236
      We implement harmonic embeddings to learn seasonal variations and cyclical changes in climate
      data. By employing a series of sine and cosine functions of varying frequencies, these embeddings
237
      introduce features that help the model learn and represent periodic behaviors in the data effectively.

349   4.5           S UPER E MULATOR
350
351   Here, the term super emulator is used to train a single ML model on all of 15 climate models.
352   This leads to the rich feature learning resulting in better generalization capabilities across different
353   climate models. We use the same multihead decoder proposed in ClimateSet to train all ML models
354
      including PACE. We train all ML models for 100 epochs to keep the training regime computationally
      efficient with 2 convolutional layers and and 32 units decoder head. For super-emulator experiments
355
      we use a batch size of 1 for all models due to computational constraints.
356
357   For super emulation PACE outperforms all ML models on 13 climate models while ConvLSTM per-
358   forms best for emulating EC-Earth3-Veg-LR and TaiESM1. The authors of ClimateSet Kaltenborn
359   et al. (2023) suggest that during super emulation, smaller models demonstrate superior learning
360
      efficiency compared to larger models. This is because smaller models converge faster, allowing the
      model to learn patterns and relationships in the data more rapidly. We believe PACE being phys-
361
      ically consistent and compute-efficient is able to learn complex climate features and outperform
362
      computationally intensive climate emulators.

      6   A BLATION S TUDIES
461
462
      To understand the importance of each component of PACE, we perform ablation studies across four
463
      climate models namely AWI-CM-1-1-MR, TaiESM1, EC-Earth3 and NorESM2-MM.
464
465   Advection-Only: For this study, we remove the empirical estimated diffusion term from PACE and
466   only model the advection process using Neural ODE. The resulting RMSE for surface air tempera-
467
      ture and precipitation increases deteriorating the model’s overall performance. The results show that
      missing approximation of diffusion has a greater effect on temperature as compared to precipita-
468
      tion, therefore determining that diffusion is critical in accurately simulating the transport of physical
469
      quantities like heat, moisture, and momentum.
470
471   Diffusion-Only: In order to understand the importance of advection process, we initialised the
472   model with constant velocities i.e. (vx = 1.0, vy = 1.0) rather than estimating their values from
473
      GHG emissions. This resulted in a much greater impact on emulating precipitation as compared to
      the previous study.
474
475   Neural ODE: For this study, we remove the advection diffusion process and only parameterize the
476   Convolution Attention Module using Neural ODE. Our results demonstrate that accurately capturing
477   advection-diffusion process is essential to simulate how energy and moisture are distributed, which
478   directly impacts predictions of temperature, precipitation, and long-term climate changes, highlight-
479
      ing the critical contribution of each element to optimizing the model’s emulating performance.

481   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
482
483   In this work, we present PACE, a physics and uncertainty aware climate emulator which accounts for
484   Earth’s atmospheric advection-diffusion phenomenon. We incorporate a key physical law in PACE
485   by solving a time-dependent partial different equation (PDE) using Neural ODE. Additionally, we
      encode periodic boundary conditions to avoid artificial edge effects that arise from rigid boundaries.

506
507   Our research aims to emulate temperature and precipitation for multiple climate models by solving
508   an atmospheric advection-diffusion equation using ML based approach while being computationally
509   efficient. The findings demonstrate that data-driven approaches can substantially enhance forecast
510   accuracy while utilizing computational resources more efficiently. The environmental impact of op-
511   timizing computational efficiency in forecasting is notable, as it reduces the carbon footprint asso-
512   ciated with large-scale computational processes, aligning with global initiatives to mitigate climate
513
      change. By combining machine learning (ML) techniques to both improve predictive accuracy and
      reduce computational overhead, we propose a sustainable and scalable solution for climate emula-
514
      tion that can better serve the global population.
