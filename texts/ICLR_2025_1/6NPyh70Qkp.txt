009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual learning (CL) requires models to sequentially learn multiple tasks, max-
012            imizing transfer and minimizing interference. However, current methods cannot
013            proactively detect all types of transfer and interference at the local optimization
014            level, limiting their effectiveness. To address this, we propose an adaptive contin-
015            ual learning strategy by proactively detecting transfer and interference. We derive
016
               the conditions for all types of transfer and interference from the perspective of pa-
               rameter sharing and optimization, based on the Fisher matrix and gradient update
017
               directions. Using this, we proposed a task transfer distance metric to help model
018
               modules detect transfer and interference. We propose a dynamic parameter up-
019            date mechanism and a dynamic expansion strategy, using inserted adapters in the
020            pre-trained model, to manage all types of transfer and interference. Experiment
021            results on seven benchmarks show that our method achieves the best accuracy with
022            limited parameters, maximizing transfer and minimizing interference.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Recently, artificial intelligence has made many significant breakthroughs across various fields. How-
028
      ever, the traditional approach of repeatedly training models on fixed datasets has resulted in high
      costs and delayed updates. To address this challenge, some researchers have proposed continual
029
      learning (Masana et al., 2023). Continual learning enables models to learn from data streams in
030
      open, dynamic environments without access to previously encountered data (Masana et al., 2023).
031   During this process, different types of transfer and interference can arise (Wang et al., 2024). Back-
032   ward interference occurs when the model’s performance declines on learned tasks during learning
033   new tasks, named catastrophic forgetting (Wang et al., 2024). Forward interference happens when
034   excessive protection of old knowledge prevents the model from effectively learning new ones (Zhou
035   et al., 2024c). On the other hand, forward transfer occurs when old knowledge helps accelerate learn-
036   ing on new tasks, while backward transfer happens when learning a new task improves performance
037   on earlier tasks (Wang et al., 2024; Masana et al., 2023). The primary goal of continual learning is
038   to maximize transfer between tasks while minimizing interference (De Lange et al., 2022).
039   Some studies suggest that transfer and interference in continual learning are linked to the shar-
040   ing and overlap of model parameters (Wang et al., 2024). Most current methods are designed to
041   maximize transfer while minimizing interference Rebuffi et al. (2017); Kirkpatrick et al. (2017).
042   Replay-based methods (Luo et al., 2024; Rebuffi et al., 2017) achieve it by storing, replaying, or
043   generating samples from previous tasks, simulating repeated training on fixed datasets. However, as
044   tasks increase, the required storage and computational resources increase uncontrollably, leading to
045
      issues like sample imbalance. Dynamic network-based methods (Bonato et al., 2024; Yoon et al.,
      2017; Wang et al., 2022a) promote forward transfer by reusing frozen old parameters while adding
046
      new ones to avoid interference with new tasks. However, freezing old parameters limits backward
047
      transfer, the network size grows uncontrollably with tasks added. optimization-based methods (Kao
048   et al., 2021; Saha et al., 2021; Saha & Roy, 2023; Zeng et al., 2019) reduce backward interference
049   and encourage forward transfer by preventing the overlap of important model parameters. These ap-
050   proaches hinder backward transfer and increase forward interference when new and old tasks share
051   too many important parameters. Recently, many studies have integrated fine-tuning of Pre-Trained
052   Models (PTMs) with these continual learning methods (Liang & Li, 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Qiao
053   et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024b; Luo et al., 2024), demonstrating superior performance (Zhou et al.,
      2024a). However, the methods mentioned above do not provide a detailed analysis of the specific

088   balance between accuracy and resource efficiency (see Section 4.3).

092   to detect transfer and interference while selecting strategies to maximize transfer and minimize
093   interference. Ablation studies further confirm the effectiveness of each component.

      4     M ETHODS
227
228
      This paper deals with class-incremental learning, where tasks with disjoint data label spaces and
229   task identities are only provided in training. We propose an adaptive continual learning approach
230   based on fine-tuning adapters added to a pre-trained ViT model. This approach can actively detect
231   transfer and interference and apply corresponding strategies. We first provide an overview of the
232   fine-tuning adapter scheme used. Next, based on the derived conditions for transfer and interference,
233   we introduce a task transfer distance metric. We then introduce how to use this metric to assess the
234   transfer distance between new and old tasks and apply different continual learning strategies. They
235   share the same adapter and apply dynamic gradient updates when tasks show high transfer or low
236   relevance. Otherwise, they introduce new adapters and activate the frozen old adapters to assist new
237
      task learning in cases of high interference. More details about methods are in Appendix A.3.
238
239   4.1   S PACE E XPANSION WITH A DAPTERS
240
      As shown in Fig. 2a, unlike previous methods that maintain an independent adapter for each task
241
      to support model expansion (Tan et al., 2024), we insert a set of adapters within each transformer
242   block and employ a Mixture of Experts (MoE) mechanism (Masoudnia & Ebrahimpour, 2014; Du
243   et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022b) for each task. We use LoRA (Gupta, 2021; Ding et al., 2023) as
244   our adapter. As shown in Fig. 2 (a), a dedicated router is used for each task to select the appropriate
245   adapters to activate. Since the task ID is not provided during inference, we learn a class center
246   for each task during training. Then, during inference, we calculate the closest class center to the
247   sample and use it to select the corresponding router. During the learning of task t, the activated
248   adapters are fine-tuned for the new task, while the pre-trained weights W and other adapters remain
249   frozen. Expanding the parameter space through adapter combinations creates a flexible framework
250
      that supports various strategies for both transfer and inference, improving the model’s adaptability.

      4.2   T RANSFER D ISTANCE E VALUATION
253
      According to Definition 1, the occurrence of transfer or interference between tasks depends on
254   whether the sensitive parameters of the new task overlap with those of previous tasks, as well as
255   the direction of the gradient updates on these overlapping parameters. Using Eq. 6, we identify a
256   task’s sensitive parameters through the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM). However, calculating the
257   FIM requires network activation based on task data, which can pose challenges if the network is not
258   well-trained. In contrast, we leverage pre-trained models with strong feature extraction capabilities
259   and high generalization during continual learning. Thus, we use the frozen pre-trained backbone
260   as the FIM estimator. Since we fine-tune only a small number of parameters while keeping most
261   frozen, the FIM derived from the pre-trained model is highly representative and can effectively guide
262
      adapter updates. As shown in Fig. 2 (a), we employ the frozen pre-trained backbone as a feature
      extractor and re-train a classifier for each block on the given task, which is typically efficient. After
263
      training, we compute the FIM for each block.
264

266   tries. To prevent noise when training with limited samples, instead of direct computation, we use

      6     CONCLUSION
528
      This paper addresses the issue that most continual learning methods do not actively detect trans-
529
      fer or interference during learning, which prevents them from maximizing transfer or minimizing
530
      interference. We conduct a theoretical analysis to identify the conditions under which transfer and
531   interference occur in continual learning. Based on this, we propose a method to measure task transfer
532   and interference using pre-trained models. Furthermore, we introduce different strategies to handle
533   transfer and interference. Our baseline experiments demonstrate that our algorithm can actively
534   detect these phenomena during continual learning and apply appropriate strategies to maximize
535   transfer and avoid interference.

      L2P (Wang et al., 2022d)applies visual prompt tuning to continual learning by learning a prompt
804   pool to select instance-specific prompts. DualPrompt (Wang et al., 2022c) introduces two types of
805   prompts, namely, general and expert prompts. CODA-Prompt (Smith et al., 2023) further improves
806   the prompt selection process by incorporating an attention mechanism. SimpleCIL (Zhou et al.,
807   2024a) freezes the pre-trained weights and extracts the center of each class by averaging the embed-
808   dings within the same class, resulting in the most representative pattern of that class. ADAM (Zhou
809   et al., 2024a) further advances this approach by comparing the performance of the prototype-based
      classifier with that of a fully fine-tuned model on new classes.
