009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Model robustness indicates a model’s capability to generalize well on unforeseen
012            distributional shifts, including data corruption, adversarial attacks, and domain
013            shifts. One of the most prevalent and effective ways to enhance the robustness
014            often involves data augmentations and label smoothing techniques. Despite the
015            great success of the related approaches in diverse practices, a unified theoretical
016            understanding of their efficacy in improving model robustness is lacking. We offer
017            a theoretical framework to clarify how augmentations, label smoothing, or their
018            combination enhance model robustness through the lens of loss surface flatness,
019            generalization bound, and adversarial robustness. Specifically, we first formally
020
               bridge the diversified data distribution via augmentations to the flatter minima on
               the parameter space, which directly links to the improved generalization capa-
021
               bility. Moreover, we further bridge augmentations with label smoothing, which
022
               softens the confidence of the target label, to the improved adversarial robustness.
023            We broadly confirm our theories through extensive simulations on the existing
024            common corruption and adversarial robustness benchmarks based on the CIFAR
025            and tinyImageNet datasets, as well as various domain generalization benchmarks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Model robustness, which is seen as a critical factor of deep models in applications requiring high
032   reliability such as autonomous vehicles and medical diagnosis, entails maintaining performance
033
      despite data distribution shifts. In the past decade, data augmentation has been widely used as
      a popular and pragmatic technique to enhance the model performance, as well as the robustness
034
      against data corruption, adversarial attacks, or even domain shifts (DeVries & Taylor, 2017; Zhang
035
      et al., 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2021a; Cubuk et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2023). The intuition of its efficacy
036   relies on the belief that augmentations enrich the training data distribution, which allows models to
037   easily extrapolate to unseen data distributions, which is the so-called generalization capability.
038
039
      On the other hand, label smoothing, which softens the confidence of target labels, has been studied
      for its robustness benefits, particularly against adversarial perturbations (Szegedy et al., 2016; Guo
040
      et al., 2017). When combining label smoothing with augmentations such as adversarial perturba-
041
      tions, also known as adversarial training, it has shown promise in strengthening model robustness
042   against adversarial attacks (Shafahi et al., 2019; Ren et al., 2022).
043
044   Despite the strong utility of augmentations, label smoothing, or their combinations, there is a lack of
045
      unified theories that formally clarify how they generally enhance model robustness. The prior for-
      mal analyses are quite limited on handling a few types of augmentations and addressing adversarial
046
      robustness. As one of the existing analyses, adversarial perturbation with label smoothing is proved
047
      to improve the smoothness of loss values on the input space, especially along the direction of adver-
048   sarial perturbation (Ren et al., 2022). However, the theory is still limited in the adversarial settings,
049   failing to elaborate the settings of the general form of augmentations and the generalization capa-
050   bility against unforeseen distributional shifts of data samples. As another analysis for other types
051   of augmentations, Mixup (Zhang et al., 2018) has been proved to enlarge the boundary thickness,
052   which is the marginal space between two differently labeled samples that is believed to improve the
053   adversarial robustness (Yang et al., 2020). Still, the analysis is restricted to mixup-based augmenta-
      tions and lacks a rigorous link to the improved performance on adversarial samples.

068   In this paper, we first provide theories for clarifying how augmentations combined with label
069   smoothing can bolster model robustness. Our analysis has two main logical sequences: i) First,
070   we formally bridge the general form of augmentations to the improved generalization bound. To
071   give a brief sketch of our thinking, we start to demonstrate the duality between the input space re-
072   gion covered by the augmented samples and the corresponding parameter space region with the same
073   loss values (formalized by Theorem 1 and Theorem 2). Based on the duality, we then claim that
074   the augmentations flatten the loss surface on the parameter space (Theorem 3), finally reaching to
075
      the improved generalization bound against distribution shifts via leveraging the flatten loss surface
      (Theorem 4). These findings still remain valid when combined with label smoothing. ii) Second, we
076
      switch the focus to the effect of label smoothing on adversarial robustness. As a rigorous link to the
077
      model performance against adversarial settings, we theoretically show that label smoothing lowers
078   the adversarial risk of the models trained with augmentations. Fig. 1 shows a full sketch.
079
080   To provide empirical evidence of the claims, we strictly demonstrate the flatness of loss surface and
081
      the enhanced generalization performance through augmentations1 In addition, we further demon-
      strate the improved adversarial robustness through label smoothing2 .

112
113   Label smoothing S(·) : Rc → Rc is a process of softening hard label y to obtain smoothed label

      3     H OW D O AUGMENTATIONS E NHANCE G ENERALIZATION ?
146
147
      In this section, we provide a rigorous link from the data augmentations to the improved general-
148   ization capability. Our main claims are twofold: i) Translation between the manifolds on input and
149   parameter spaces, ii) Association of flatter loss in the input space via augmentations with flatness in
150   the parameter space, leading to a reduced generalization gap.

      3.3.1     AUGMENTATION METHODS TO BE CONSIDERED
308   There exists a massive number of augmentation methods in the literature. To evaluate the practical
309   impact of well-known augmentation methods, we run experiments with a wide range of augmenta-
310   tion types and focus on advanced methods rather than a baseline. For the model-free augmentation
311   type, we pick CutOut (DeVries & Taylor (2017)), which tunes a single image by cutting out a
312   partial region of the image, and we also select AugMix (Hendrycks et al. (2021b)) and PixMix
313   (Hendrycks et al. (2022)), which are advanced forms of trying to mix multiple clean images. For the
314   model-based augmentation type, we pick StyleAug (Jackson et al. (2019)), which utilizes a gener-
315   ative model-based approach to diversify the style of clean images. Finally, among the policy-based
316
      augmentation type, we consider RandAugment (Cubuk et al. (2020)), which learns the policy for
      augmenting images.

      5.1   DATA AUGMENTATIONS AND L ABEL S MOOTHING
446
447
      Data Augmentation Strategies: Existing augmentation methods can be grouped into model-free,
448
      model-based, and policy-based algorithms, borrowing the taxonomy suggested by Xu et al. (2023).
449
450   Model-free augmentations can be divided into single-image and multiple-image methods. Single-
451   image augmentations not only include elementary image manipulations such as translation, rotation,
452
      and color jittering but also includes masking methods such as CutOut (DeVries & Taylor, 2017) and
      Hide-and-Seek (Kumar Singh & Jae Lee, 2017). Multi-image augmentations exploit two or more
453
      images to construct augmented images, including the mixing of the two images, such as Mixup
454
      Zhang et al. (2018) and CutMix (Yun et al., 2019). Recently, it is empirically observed that a few
455   augmentation methods including AugMix (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) and PixMix (Hendrycks et al.,
456   2022) exhibit their superiority over others in terms of model robustness.
457
458
      Model-based augmentations utilize pretrained models to generate augmented data. A number of
      methods that exploit generative models including CGAN (Mirza & Osindero, 2014), and their vari-
459
      ants (Douzas & Bacao, 2018; Mariani et al., 2018; Ali-Gombe & Elyan, 2019; Yang & Zhou, 2021)
460
      aim to aid data imbalance problems. Other augmentations, including DeepAugment (Hendrycks
461   et al., 2021a), ANT (Rusak et al., 2020), and StyleAug (Jackson et al., 2019), directly aim to en-
462   hance the classifier’s robustness against common corruption, adversarial attacks, or domain shifts.
463
464
      Policy-based augmentations focus on designing an automatic way to determine the optimal augmen-
      tation strategies by employing reinforcement learning or adversarial training. From a pioneering
465
      method called AutoAugment (Cubuk et al., 2019) that utilizes reinforcement learning for finding
466
      the best augmentation strategies, subsequent works such as Fast AA (Lim et al., 2019), Faster AA
467   (Hataya et al., 2020), and RandAugment (Cubuk et al., 2020) aim to enhance both the efficiency of
468   policy search and the model performance. Adversarial training-based augmentation strategies, in-
469   cluding AdaTransform (Tang et al., 2019), Adversarial AA (Zhang et al., 2020), and AugMax (Wang
470   et al., 2021) leverage adversarial perturbations which maximally disturbs samples to be misclassified
471   into other labels, finally leading to improve model robustness against unseen domains.
472
      Despite the effectiveness of augmentation methods in enhancing model performance, prior studies
473   have focused more on their practical use rather than on understanding their theoretical impact on
474   model robustness to data shifts. In our experiments, we carefully select representative methods from
475   each group: AugMix, PixMix, CutOut from model-free methods, StyleAug from model-based
476   method, RandAugment from policy-based methods.
477
      Label Smoothing: Label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) is a regularization strategy to enhance
478
      model robustness and generalization by softening training data targets. It reduces model overconfi-
479   dence, especially in high-dimensional spaces, to prevent overfitting.
480
481   Empirical evidence supports label smoothing’s benefits across multiple tasks and models. Müller
482
      et al. (2019) found that it improves deep neural networks’ accuracy and robustness by reducing
      noise overfitting. Vaswani et al. (2017) incorporated it into the Transformer model, achieving the
483
      top performance in machine translation, underscoring its value in sequence-to-sequence models.
484
485   Label smoothing also enhances model calibration, notably reducing Expected Calibration Error
      (ECE). Studies, such as those by Guo et al. (2017) and Thulasidasan et al. (2019), highlight its


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      role in improving calibration and adversarial robustness, making models more reliable in critical ap-
487   plications. Mukhoti et al. (2020) demonstrated its significance on deep neural network calibration.
488
489
      5.2   AUGMENTATIONS , L ABEL S MOOTHING AND M ODEL ROBUSTNESS
490
491   Augmentations and Model Robustness: A number of previous works have tried to reveal the
492   relationship between augmentation and model robustness. Zhao et al. (2020) and Volpi et al. (2018)
493   have theoretically found out that adversarial perturbations in the latent space can simulate worst-
494   case distributional shifts in the data. Rebuffi et al. (2021) have empirically found out that Mixup
495   and CutOut with model weight averaging is shown to improve adversarial robustness. Najafi et al.
496   (2019) and Alayrac et al. (2019) have theoretically shown that utilizing unlabeled data in training can
497
      improve adversarial robustness. Hendrycks et al. (2021a) have empirically found out that exploiting
      diverse augmentations together can improve model robustness against both adversarial and common
498
      data corruptions. Yin et al. (2019) interpret the augmentation-originated gains in model robustness
499
      by explaining that augmentations make deep models utilize both high and low frequency information
500   of images so as to enhance model robustness against data corruptions.
501
502   Nonetheless, most of the prior interpretations of how augmentation contributes to model robustness
503
      are either confined to specific augmentations, adversarial robustness, or empirical analysis. None of
      the prior works generally explain how augmentations theoretically improve model robustness across
504
      various, along with the extensive empirical testings across augmentations and benchmarks.
505
506   Label Smoothing and Model Robustness: Shafahi et al. (2019) have empirically discovered that
507   the benefits of adversarial training in boosting classifier robustness can also be achieved through sim-
508   pler regularization techniques like label smoothing and logit squeezing. Similarly, Fu et al. (2020)
509
      have found that label smoothing not only results in logit squeezing but also enhances adversarial
      robustness. However, Goibert & Dohmatob (2019) have shown that while label smoothing may im-
510
      prove a model’s defense against gradient-based adversarial attacks to some extent, this robustness
511
      is neither stable nor consistent. Additionally, Ren et al. (2022) have theoretically discovered that
512   label smoothing promotes logit squeezing and smoothens the loss surface in the input space, which
513   probably connects to the adversarial robustness.
514
515
      The related works consistently show a clue of the benefits of label smoothing against adversarial
      robustness but do not formally prove the reduced empirical risks against adversarial samples.
516
517   Augmentations with Label Smoothing and Model Robustness: While augmentations with label
518   smoothing have been empirically shown to potentially reduce calibration or adversarial error (Ren
519   et al., 2022; Shafahi et al., 2019), there’s a lack of research on their combined effect on model
520   robustness against distributional shifts, including adversarial and common corruptions.
521   We fill this gap, examining how augmentations and label smoothing together can improve robustness
522   against various distributional changes, highlighting the significance of our research in this area.
523
      Comparison with Fairness under Distribution Shift: While Jiang et al. (2024) focuses on main-
524
      taining fairness under distribution shifts by highlighting the existence of model weight perturbations,
525   our work provides a stricter formalization by demonstrating that input space augmentations with
526   bounded L2 norms can be transformed into equivalent parameter-space regions and vice versa. This
527   duality allows us to rigorously establish how augmentations and label smoothing enhance model
528   robustness against various distributional shifts.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper presents a theoretical framework that clarifies how data augmentations and label smooth-
533
      ing enhance model robustness through the lens of model flatness, generalization bounds, and adver-
534   sarial robustness. With the theoretical claims, we provide extensive simulations verifying each claim
535   via testing the various flatness metrics, corruption errors, domain generalization, and adversarial loss
536   with various augmentations. Our findings aim to establish a general foundation for understanding
537   the benefits of augmentations and label smoothing, which provide intuitions of future advancements
538   in creating robust deep models capable of handling real-world data variability.

       C.1.1    F LATNESS M ETRICS
1203
1204   Descriptions on Metrics. The maximum eigenvalue of Hessian matrix (λmax ) represents locally
1205   steep or sharp direction in the loss landscape, implying that small changes in the parameters in this
1206   direction may lead to large changes in the loss. The trace of the Hessian (trace(H)) provides an
1207   overall measure of the curvature in all directions. µPAC-Bayes represents simplified PAC-Bayesian
1208   bound introduced by Jiang et al. (2020). The simplified PAC-Bayesian bound, denoted as µPAC-Bayes ,
1209   is computed as 1/σ, where σ is the largest value such that |ED (θ + N (0, σ 2 I)) − ED (θ)| ≤ τ .
1210   Therefore, flatter local minima correspond to larger σ values and smaller µPAC-Bayes . The LPF-based
1211
       flatness measure evaluates the flatness of a local minimum θ∗ by computing the convolution of the
       loss
       R function    L with a Gaussian kernel K = N (0, σ 2 I). Formally, it is defined as (L ∗ K)(θ∗ ) =
1212
          L(θ − τ )K(τ )dτ . This measure effectively averages the loss over a neighborhood around θ∗ ,
              ∗
1213
       with the Gaussian kernel weighting nearby points more heavily, thereby quantifying how sharp the
1214   loss landscape is in that region. ϵ-sharpness (ϵsharp ) measures the sensitivity of a loss function near a
1215   local minimum by quantifying the largest perturbation of model parameters that leads to an increase
1216   in loss of more than ϵ.
1217
       Method Implementations. We have deployed power iteration (PI) method and Hutchinson’s
1218
       method to estimate λmax and trace(H) with 20 iterations and 10 samples, respectively. We have
1219
       adopted the default parameters for the µPAC-Bayes , LPF measure, and ϵsharp i.e. τ = 0.05, σ = 0.05,
1220   and ϵ = 0.1 respectively.

       C.1.2    ROBUSTNESS B ENCHMARK D ETAILS
1232
1233   Domain Generalization Benchmarks. We conducted comprehensive domain generalization ex-
1234   periments on the PACS, VLCS, and OfficeHome datasets to evaluate model robustness and gener-
1235   alizability using augmentations. These datasets are specifically designed to test a model’s ability to
1236   generalize across different domains, as they consist of images that share the same labels but differ
1237   in their domain representations. For example, the PACS dataset contains images from four distinct
1238
       domains—’art painting’, ’cartoon’, ’photo’, and ’sketch’—all featuring the same classes such as
       ’dog’, ’elephant’, and others. In all experiments, models were trained until convergence to ensure
1239
       fair and consistent comparisons across different settings.
1240
1241   Our baseline employed Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) with the best hyperparameters selected
       from DomainBed’s ERM training configuration (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021). To specifically


                                                          23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       assess the impact of augmentations on model performance, we excluded the heavy augmentation
1243   compositions used in the original DomainBed configuration.
1244
1245   For the baseline augmentations — including AugMix, PixMix, RandAug, StyleAug, and CutOut —
1246
       we systematically tuned the number of training epochs, exploring options of 1,000, 2,500, and 5,000
       epochs. The default setting was 5,000 epochs, aligning with the standard ERM training procedure.
1247
       Specifically for StyleAug, we performed additional tuning of its hyperparameter α alongside the
1248
       training epochs. Through manual experimentation, we found that setting α = 1.0 yielded the best
1249   results in terms of model robustness and generalization performance.
1250
1251   Common Corruption Benchmarks. We assessed the robustness of models trained with aug-
1252
       mentations against common corruptions using the CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and tinyImageNet
       datasets. Specifically, we utilized their corrupted counterparts — CIFAR-10-C, CIFAR-100-C, and
1253
       tinyImageNet-C — which are standard benchmarks generated from the original datasets by applying
1254
       15 distinct corruption types at 5 different severity levels. These corruption types include bright-
1255   ness changes, contrast alterations, defocus blur, elastic transformations, fog addition, frost addition,
1256   Gaussian blur, glass distortion, impulse noise, JPEG compression, motion blur, pixelation, shot
1257   noise, snow addition, and zoom blur. Models were trained and validated on the respective clean
1258   datasets, with robustness evaluations performed at test time only to simulate real-world scenarios
1259   where models encounter unforeseen corruptions. To quantify model performance across all corrup-
1260   tions and severity levels, we calculated the mean Corruption Error (mCE), which averages the error
1261   rates over the different conditions.
1262   For training, we adopted the configurations from Hendrycks et al. (2021b), with a modification to the
1263   maximum number of training epochs—extended from 100 to 400—to ensure thorough convergence.
1264   Specifically, we utilized stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with a momentum of 0.9 and a weight
1265   decay of 0.0005. A cosine learning rate decay schedule was applied over the 400 epochs to optimize
1266   learning efficiency.
1267   Regarding the augmentation techniques, default configuration parameters were used for all baseline
1268   augmentations unless specified otherwise. For StyleAug, the hyperparameter α was set to 0.95
1269   for the CIFAR experiments and adjusted to 1.0 for the TinyImageNet experiments to account for
1270   dataset-specific characteristics. In the CIFAR-10 experiment, CutOut was configured with a grid
1271   size of 4 to effectively augment the training data. All other augmentations — AugMix, PixMix, and
1272   RandAug — were applied using their default settings as described in their respective original works.
1273   Adversarial Robustness Benchmarks. To further evaluate the robustness of models in adversarial
1274   settings, we conducted tests using untargeted Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) attacks based on the
1275   L∞ norm. For clarity, we utilize the absolute value α to represent gradient ascent coefficient, rather
1276   than a relative step size with respect to the perturbation magnitude η (where η corresponds to the
1277   commonly used epsilon ϵ in adversarial literature, representing the maximum allowed perturbation).
1278
       For the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 experiments, we employed a PGD-7 attack with parameters
1279   (η, α) = (8/255, 2/255). In the tinyImageNet experiment, we used a PGD-3 attack with parameters
1280   (η, α) = (3/255, 1/255). Essentially, more intense attacks have been applied to simpler datasets,
1281   while milder attacks have been used for more complex datasets, with the CIFAR experiments’ attack
1282   configurations borrowed from the Yang et al. (2020). We then evaluate the adversarial robustness of
1283   models trained following the common corruption evaluation protocol detailed above.

1374
1375   D.1   W HAT A RE THE D ESIRED A SPECTS OF AUGMENTATIONS ?
1376
1377   Based on the theoretical foundations and the empirical investigations in our paper, we aim to figure
1378   out what are the desired aspects of augmentations. Revisiting Assumption 1, we found that the
1379   distribution of augmented samples, PA , should cover the surrounding area of each original sample.
1380   By doing so, models aim to suppress the loss values within the area, leading to the flat and minimized
1381   loss surface around each input, which ultimately leads to the flatter surface in the parameter space.
1382   However, in practice, augmentations do not have to densely encompass the surrounding region of
1383   each original sample. It is due to the fact that modern deep models with appropriate regularization
1384   methods show a strong interpolation performance on the input space. Therefore, we believe when
1385   augmentations sufficiently cover the wide range of surrounding region of each sample, deep models
1386   would show the flatter loss surface on the input space, thus on the parameter space.
1387   However, not much existing augmentations are designed to fulfil our key lesson. As a further work,
1388   it is convincing to propose augmentation methods that are tailored to cover the surrounding region
1389   of each sample, e.g., additive Gaussian within a sphere, and combine it with the existing strong
1390   augmentation practices, e.g., reinforcement learning or generative model-based augmentations or
1391   Mixup techniques, to boost up the generalization performance of augmentations.
1392
1393   D.2   H OW D O O UR C LAIMS F URTHER E XTEND TO L ARGE & D EEP M ODELS ?
1394
1395   As one of the key limitations of our work, the proposed theory is applied to linear models, which
1396   is quite shallow than modern architectures. Specifically, the part involving the translation between
1397   input and parameter space, i.e., Theorem 1 and 2, are for linear models. The reason of this condition
1398   is we can further achieve the closed-form or tighter bounds of the solutions, i.e., RγX , and RγΘ , by
1399   using the linear architecture of given models.
1400   However, in practice, we further investigate the proofs of our claims by using modern deep archi-
1401   tectures, such as the WideResNet architecture. Based on the experiments which consistently verify
1402   our claim, we strongly believe that the main claims of this work can be extended to large and deep
1403   model architectures. For a more precise description, formalizing the regions seems to be intractable
       for complicated large and deep models. However, as a further insight, when a complex model is

1410   might be unexplored that the key claims of this work can be further applied to other modalities, such

1412   vision cases, but we believe that it is an important step forward to understand theoretical importance
1413   of augmentations in machine learning. Furthermore, we want to emphasize that our main theoretical
