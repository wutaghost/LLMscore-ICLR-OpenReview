009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Latent linear interpolations are a powerful tool for navigating the representation
012            space of deep generative models. This aspect is particularly relevant in applied
013            settings, where meaningful latent traversals can be learnt to represent the evolution
014            of a system’s trajectory and mapped back to the often noisy and high-dimensional
015            data space. However, when real data lies on a manifold with non-trivial geome-
016
               try, linear interpolations of the representation space do not directly correspond to
               geodesic paths along the manifold unless enforced. An example of such a setting is
017
               scRNA-seq, where high-dimensional and discrete cellular data is assumed to lie on
018
               a negative binomial statistical manifold modelled by the decoder of a variational
019            autoencoder. We introduce FlatVI, a novel training framework enforcing Euclidean
020            geometry in the latent space of discrete-likelihood variational autoencoders mod-
021            elling count data. In our modelling setting, straight lines in the latent domain are
022            regularised to approximate geodesic interpolations in the decoded space, improving
023            the combination of our model with methods assuming Euclidean latent geometry.
024            Results on simulated data empirically support our claims, while experiments on
025            temporally resolved biological datasets show improvements in the reconstruction
026            of cellular trajectories and the learning of biologically meaningful velocity fields.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Generative models for representation learning like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) have influenced
031   computational sciences in multiple fields (Zhong & Meidani, 2023; Lopez et al., 2018; Griffiths
032   & Hernández-Lobato, 2020). One reason is that real-world experimental data often poses significant
033   modelling challenges as it is inherently noisy, high-dimensional, and complex (Sarker, 2021). As a
034   solution, learning a low-dimensional and dense latent representation of the data has gained traction in
035   applied machine learning. One example is reconstructing population dynamics, where the evolution
036   of individual particles in complex systems is learnt from disjoint samples of observations collected
037   at subsequent time points. In the presence of high-dimensional systems, modelling interpolations in a
038   well-behaved low-dimensional data embedding can offer valuable insight into the system’s evolution
039
      through time (Džeroski & Todorovski, 2003; Bunne et al., 2022). Furthermore, with the decoder
      parameterising a flexible likelihood model, VAEs present a promising avenue for representing both
040
      continuous and discrete data. The latter setting has demonstrated unprecedented potential in cellular
041
      data, particularly in gene expression, which is measured in counts that reflect the number of RNA
042   molecules produced by thousands of genes and is collected through single-cell RNA sequencing
043   (scRNA-seq). (Haque et al., 2017). Such a technique allows the measurement of thousands of genes
044   in parallel and the resulting discrete count vectors describe the state of a cell across diverse biological
045   settings (Regev et al., 2017).
046
      The representation learnt by VAEs is tightly connected to Riemanniann geometry, as one can see the
047
      latent space as a parametrisation of a low-dimensional manifold (Arvanitidis et al., 2020). When it
048   comes to modelling latent population dynamics, popular approaches still rely on assuming Euclidean
049   geometry in the representation space (Bunne et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2020; 2023). A notable example
050   is the standard formulation of dynamic Optimal Transport (OT) (Benamou & Brenier, 2000), which
051   exploits straight-line interpolations between observations for learning a time-dependent map across
052   population snapshots. However, building latent trajectories upon the Euclidean assumption is sub-
053   optimal when the data lies on a non-Euclidean manifold, as straight latent lines do not necessarily
      reflect geodesic paths on the manifold spanned by the decoder, see Fig. 1.

      To learn effective interpolations on real data manifolds, we establish the following desiderata: (i)
055   Approximate trajectories on intractable data manifolds via interpolations on a simpler latent manifold
056   with tractable geometry. (ii) Design a VAE decoding scheme that maps the shortest paths on the latent
057   manifold to geodesics in the decoded space. (iii) Formalise the geodesic matching framework in a way
058   that supports a flexible choice of the decoder’s likelihood. To achieve (i) and (ii), existing methods
059   regularise the latent representation of Gaussian AEs using Euclidean geometry (Chen et al., 2020;
060   Yonghyeon et al., 2021), but limit their application to continuous data by neglecting the decoder’s
061   general likelihood model support. Other works explore the connection between stochastic decoders’
062   geometry and the latent space manifold (Arvanitidis et al., 2021), even for discrete data, but do not
063
      address regularising the latent manifold to a simple, traversable geometry while preserving geodesic
      paths on the decoded manifold. To our knowledge, no VAE-based method integrates all desiderata.
064
065   In this work, we close this gap and introduce FlatVI, Flat Variational Inference, a theoretically
066   principled approach pushing straight paths in the latent space of a VAE to approximate geodesic paths
067   along the manifold of the decoded data. Our focus on statistical manifolds—those manifolds whose
068   points correspond to probability distributions of a pre-defined family, defined over the corresponding
069
      parameter space H—enables us to draw connections to the theory of VAEs and information geometry.
      When trained as a likelihood model, a VAE’s decoder image spans the manifold’s parameter space H.
070
      This formulation finds direct application in scRNA-seq, where individual gene counts are assumed
071
      to follow a negative binomial distribution, reflecting crucial data properties such as discreteness and
072   overdispersion (Zhou et al., 2011).
073
074   Crucially, FlatVI regularises the latent space through a flattening loss that pushes the pullback metric
075
      from a stochastic VAE decoder towards a spatially-uniform scaled identity matrix, thereby regularising
      towards latent Euclidean geometry (Fig. 1). In a controlled simulation setting, we demonstrate that
076
      our regularisation successfully constrains the latent manifold to exhibit an approximate Euclidean
077
      geometry, while enabling likelihood parameter reconstruction on par with standard VAEs. Our
078   method finds direct applications to single-cell representation learning and trajectory inference, which
079   we demonstrate across multiple biological settings by providing an improved data representation for
080   OT-based modelling of latent cellular population dynamics. In summary, we make the following
081   contributions:
082
          • We introduce a regularisation technique for discrete-likelihood VAEs to enforce straight latent
083
            interpolations to approximate geodesic paths on the statistical manifold spanned by the decoder.
084
          • We provide an explicit formulation of the flattening loss for the negative binomial case, which
085
            directly impacts modelling high-dimensional scRNA-seq data.
086
          • We empirically validate our model on simulated data and latent geodesic interpolations.
087
          • We show that our method offers a better representation space for existing OT-based trajectory
088         inference tools than existing approaches in real data settings.

179
      Deterministic Autoencoders. A common assumption is that continuous data lies near a low-
180   dimensional Riemannian manifold MX associated with a latent representation Z and with X = RG .
181   The decoder h of a deterministic AE model can be seen as an immersion h : Rd → RG of the latent
182   space Z = Rd into the embedded Riemannian manifold MX equipped with a metric tensor M.
183
      Definition 3.1. A Riemannian manifold is a smooth manifold MX endowed with a Riemannian
184   metric M(x) for x ∈ MX . M(x) is a positive-definite matrix that changes smoothly and defines
185   a local inner product on the tangent space Tx MX as ⟨u, v⟩MX = uT M(x)v, with v, u ∈ Tx MX
186   (Do Carmo & Flaherty Francis, 1992).
187
      From Definition 3.1, it derives that a Riemannian manifold in the decoded space has Euclidean
188
      geometry when M(x) = IG everywhere. In this setting, the geometry of the latent space is directly
189   linked to the geometry of observation space by the pullback metric M(z) (Arvanitidis et al., 2021):
190
191                                         M(z) = Jh (z)T M(x)Jh (z) ,                                   (4)
192   where x = h(z) and Jh (z) is the Jacobian matrix of h(z). Here, the decoder h is assumed to be
193   a diffeomorphism between the latent space and its image, such that Jh (z) is full rank for all z.
194   The existence of a metric M(z) identifies a latent Riemannian manifold MZ , whose properties are
195   defined based on the geometry of MX through Eq. (4). For example, Eq. (4) allows to define the
196   shortest curve γ(t) connecting pairs of latent codes z1 and z2 as the one minimising the distance

223   3.3   L EARNING POPULATION DYNAMICS WITH OPTIMAL TRANSPORT
224
225   The complexity of learning trajectories in high-dimensional data can be prevented by interpolating
226   latent representations and decoding intermediate results to the data space for inspection. Here, we deal
227   with learning population dynamics, which consists of modelling the temporal evolution of a dynamical
228   system from unpaired samples of observations through time. As such a task is naturally formulated
229
      as a distribution matching problem, dynamic OT has been a popular avenue for population dynamics.
230   Let the data be defined on a continuous space X = Rd . OT computes the most efficient mapping for
231   transporting mass from one measure ν, to another η, defined on X . Relevant to dynamical systems,
232   Benamou & Brenier (2000) introduced a continuous formulation of the OT problem. In this setting,
233   let pt be a time-varying density over Rd constrained by p0 = ν and p1 = η. Dynamic OT learns a
234   time-dependent marginal vector field u : [0, 1] × Rd → Rd , where ut (x) = u(t, x). Such a field is
235
      associated with an ordinary differential equation (ODE) dx = ut (x)dt whose solution matches the
      source with the target distribution. Therefore, one can use dynamic OT to learn a system’s dynamics
236
      from snapshots of data collected over time.
237
238   An efficient simulation-free formulation of dynamic OT comes from the OT Conditional Flow
239   Matching (OT-CFM) model by Tong et al. (2023), who demonstrated that the time-resolved marginal
240   vector field ut (x) has the same minimiser as the data-conditioned vector field ut (x|x0 , x1 ), where
241
      (x0 , x1 ) ∼ q(x0 , x1 ) = π(X0 , X1 ) are tuples of points sampled from the static OT coupling π
      between source and target batches, X0 and X1 . Assuming Gaussian marginals pt and x0 and x1 to
242
      be connected by Gaussian flows, both pt (x|x0 , x1 ) and ut (x | x0 , x1 ) become tractable:
243
244                                 pt (x | x0 , x1 ) = N (tx1 + (1 − t)x0 , σ 2 )                         (8)
245                                 ut (x | x0 , x1 ) = x1 − x0 ,                                          (9)
246
247   where the value of σ 2 is a small pre-defined constant. Accordingly, the OT-CFM loss is
248        LOT-CFM = Et,q(x0 ,x1 ),pt (x|x0 ,x1 ) ∥vξ (t, x) − ut (x|x0 , x1 )∥2 , with t ∼ U(0, 1).

                                                                                                         (10)
249
250   Given this formulation of dynamic OT, we highlight three aspects: (i) Dynamic OT only applies to
251   continuous spaces. (ii) OT-CFM benefits from low-dimensional representations since the OT-coupling
252   is optimised from distances in the state space. (iii) Based on Eq. (8), OT-CFM uses straight lines to
253   optimise the conditional vector field, thus assuming Euclidean geometry.
254
      In the presence of discrete data like scRNA-seq counts, one can tackle (i) and (ii) by learning dynamics
255
      in a low-dimensional representation of the state space–the latent space of a VAE with a discrete-
256   likelihood decoder. Note, however, that (iii) is still a shortcoming, since straight lines in the latent
257   space of a VAE do not reflect geodesic paths on the statistical data manifold unless enforced otherwise,
258   see Fig. 1. In this work, we tackle the latter problem through a VAE regularisation approach.

      4.1   L EARNING SINGLE - CELL POPULATION DYNAMICS
263
264   We are concerned with learning population dynamics along the statistical manifold spanned by the
265   decoder of a VAE—for example, a negative binomial manifold in scRNA-seq—, where observations
266   are collected in T unpaired distributions {νt }Tt=0 . Individual time points correspond to separate
267   snapshot datasets {Xt }Tt=0 , each with Nt observations. In latent trajectory modelling, each snapshot
268   is mapped to a collection {Zt }Tt=0 of latent representations Zt ∈ RNt ×d following the setting
269   described in Sec. 3.1. We wish to use optimal transport with OT-CFM to learn the dynamics of the
      system through a parameterised vector field vξ (t, z) in the latent space Z, such that trajectories in Z


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      respect the non-Euclidean geometry of the decoded manifold. To achieve such a correspondence, one
271   can either: (i) act on the OT-CFM algorithm to enforce matching distributions via statistical manifold
272   interpolations rather than straight paths or (ii) regularise the representation space, such that linear
273   latent paths correspond to geodesic paths.
274
      (i) The ideal approach would be to replace straight interpolations with geodesic curves in the definition
275
      of the OT-CFM objective, ensuring that trajectories in the latent space reflect the data geometry.
276
      Given a VAE decoder spanning a statistical manifold with metric tensor as in Eq. (7), computing
277   the optimal coupling π between Zt and Zt+1 to perform OT-CFM requires calculating the geodesic
278   distance in Eq. (5) between all pairs of observations from the consecutive snapshots. However,
279   estimating γ(t) for all pairs of observations in consecutive batches is unfeasible in large datasets like
280   in scRNA-seq, as it requires solving an optimisation problem to find the optimal γ ∗ (t) connecting all
281   pairs of observations in source and target batches.
282
      (ii) Alternatively, one could seek to regularise the latent space of a VAE in such a way that the latent
283   manifold MZ has Euclidean geometry, while the non-linear stochastic decoder reintroduces the
284   complex geometry of the statistical manifold MH of the decoded space, pushed by the minimisation
285   of the reconstruction loss. In other words, the goal is to set a correspondence between straight
286   paths in the latent space and geodesic interpolations along the statistical manifold. If this is verified,
287   decoded trajectories generated using Euclidean OT-CFM in the latent space of the VAE do not violate
288   the geometry of the data manifold. Motivated by applications to single-cell VAEs, we opt for (ii).
289

      4.2   F LATTENING LOSS
291
292
      To ensure that straight latent paths model geodesics along the decoded statistical manifold, we
293   introduce a regularisation to the standard VAE objective which enforces a constant Euclidean local
294   geometry in the latent manifold MZ . Recall from Sec. 3.2 that the latent space of a VAE has
295   local geometry denoted by the metric in Eq. (7) which is a function of the Fisher information of
296   the decoder’s likelihood given the decoded parameters ϕ ∈ H. From Eq. (5) we also know that if
297   M(z) = Id , then the geodesic distance between each pair of latent points is given by the straight line
298   between them. Therefore, regularising the product Jh (z)T M(ϕ)Jh (z) towards Id forces a VAE to
299   model Euclidean latent geometry. Crucially, the non-linear decoder is still trained to reconstruct the
300   original data space under the likelihood optimisation task in the ELBO (Eq. (2)), reinstating the local
301
      geometry of the decoded statistical manifold described by M(ϕ).
302   In summary, we implement a flattening loss Lflat to induce latent Euclidean geometry in VAEs with
303   the decoder modelling the data likelihood. Our regularisation is defined as follows:
304
                                                                          2
305                                      Lflat = Eqψ (z|x) M(z) − αId 2 .                                  (11)
306
307   Here, M(z) is calculated by Eq. (7), hence it depends on the Fisher information M(ϕ) of the
308   decoder’s likelihood and the Jacobian of the decoder Jh (z). Meanwhile, α is a trainable parameter
309   offering some flexibility on the scale of the diagonal constraint. In VAEs, the loss of FlatVI is
310   combined with the ELBO:
311
312
                                            LFlatVI = LELBO + λLflat ,                                     (12)
313
      where λ controls the strength of the flattening regularisation. On synthetic data, one can show
314
      that such an approach yields better manifold interpolations (more in App. I.2). In our real-world
315
      single-cell experiments on learning population dynamics, we first train a VAE with regularisation
316   as in Eq. (12), then we use the resulting Euclidean representation space for latent OT-CFM. The
317   procedure used to train FlatVI is summarised in Algorithm 1, whereas its combination with OT-CFM
318   is illustrated in Algorithm 2.
319   Remarks. Both the reconstruction loss in the ELBO and the flattening objective influence the
320   decoder parameters. This is because both the Fisher information and Jacobian depend on the decoded
321   likelihood parameters. Thus, training FlatVI involves updating the decoder to both reconstruct the
322   data and produce a scaled identity as a latent metric. Additionally, note that evaluating the flattening
323   loss is slower than the usual VAE loss, as it requires differentiating through the decoder to compute
      the FIM.

461   5.3      L ATENT VECTOR FIELD AND LINEAGE MAPPING
462
463   Task and dataset. We evaluate the capacity of continuous OT to identify a biologically meaningful
464
      cell velocity field using the representation spaces computed by FlatVI, the unregularised NB-VAE and
      the GAE model. We hypothesise that dynamic OT with Euclidean cost benefits from being applied to
465
      a flat representation space. As a dataset for the analysis, we employ the Pancreatic endocrinogenesis
466
      (Pancreas) by Bastidas-Ponce et al. (2019), which measures 16,206 cells and spans embryonic days
467   14.5 to 15.5, revealing multipotent cell differentiation into endocrine and non-endocrine lineages.
468   More specifically, we train the compared representation learning frameworks on the dataset and learn
469   separate vector fields for all models’ embeddings matching days 14.5 to 15.5 with OT-CFM. The learnt
470   vector field represents the directionality of the observations on the cellular development manifold.
471   Evaluation. Using the CellRank model (Lange et al., 2022; Weiler et al., 2023), we build random
472   walks on a cell graph based on the directionality of latent velocities learnt by OT-CFM in the different
473   representation spaces. Walks converge to macrostates representing the endpoints of the biological
474   process if the learnt velocity field points to biologically meaningful directions. We quantify the quality
475   of vector fields learnt by OT in different latent spaces based on (i) the number of macrostates identified
476   by random walks, (ii) the velocity consistency, measured as the correlation of the latent velocity
477   field of single datapoints with that of the neighbouring cells. Higher consistency indicates smoother
478   transitions in the vector field, suggesting that the representation space facilitates more coherent and
479
      biologically meaningful dynamics, making it a suitable space for learning trajectories (see App. F.1).
480   Results. Figure 3a summarises the number of terminal cell states identified by following the velocity
481   graph. From prior biological knowledge, it is known that the dataset contains six terminal states,
482   which are all identified on the representation computed by our FlatVI (λ = 1). In contrast, on the GAE
483   and NB-VAE’s representations, CellRank only captures four and five terminal states, respectively. In
484   Figure 3b, we further evaluate the velocity consistency within neighbourhoods of cells as a function
485
      of latent dimensionality. In line with previous results, OT on the Euclidean latent space yields a more
      consistent velocity field across latent dimensionalities.

      6   C ONCLUSION
527
      We addressed modelling temporal trajectories from unpaired cell distributions by using walks on flat
528
      NB manifolds. To achieve this, we introduced FlatVI, a VAE training strategy where the pullback
529
      metric of the stochastic decoder is regularised to approximate the identity matrix and regularise
530   towards local Euclidean geometry in the latent space. Consequently, straight latent paths correspond
531   to geodesic interpolations in the decoded space. Results on real and synthetic data demonstrate that
532   our flattening procedure holds and enhances the biological structure in the latent space. By combining
533   this approach with dynamic OT, we observed better prediction outcomes and more consistent vector
534   fields on cellular manifolds. These improvements benefit key tasks in cellular development, such as
535   fate mapping and trajectory analysis.
536   Limitations and future work. A limitation of applying FlatVI to real data is that excessive regulari-
537   sation may cause a trade-off between flattening and reconstruction likelihood. We aim to enhance
538   the model’s robustness to reconstruction loss and extend it to a wider range of statistical manifolds
539   and single-cell tasks, such as modelling Poisson-distributed chromatin accessibility, batch correction
      evaluation, and OT-mediated perturbation modelling.

      Definition B.1. A Riemannian manifold is a smooth manifold MX endowed with a Riemannian
815
      metric M(x) for x ∈ MX . M(x) changes smoothly and identifies an inner product on the tangent
816
      space Tx MX at a point x ∈ MX as ⟨u, v⟩MX = uT M(x)v, with v, u ∈ Tx MX .
817
818   For an embedded manifold MX with intrinsic dimension d, we can assume the existence of an
819   invertible global chart map ξ : MX → Rd mapping the manifold MX to its intrinsic coordinates. A
820   vector vx ∈ Tx MX on the tangent space of MX can be expressed as a pushforward vx = Jξ−1 (z)vz

824   coordinates via:
825                                   M(z) = Jξ−1 (z)T M(ξ −1 (z))Jξ−1 (z) .                     (23)

860   In machine learning, exploring latent spaces is crucial, especially in generative models like VAEs.
861   One challenge is defining meaningful distances in the latent space Z, which often depends on
862   the properties of stochastic decoders and their alignment with the observation space. Injecting
863   the geometry of the decoded space of a VAE into the latent space requires a different theoretical
      framework, where the data is assumed to lie near a statistical manifold.

      C.2   A DDITIONAL COMPARISON BETWEEN F LAT VI AND GAE
925
926   Although related in scope, FlatVI significantly differs from the Geodesic Autoencoder (GAE) pro-
927   posed by Huguet et al. (2022). Firstly, GAE is a deterministic autoencoder optimised for reconstruc-
928   tion based on a Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss. As such, the model is not tailored to simulate gene
929   counts. On the contrary, FlatVI optimises the decoder as an NB model, such that sampling from
930   it produces discrete counts. This aspect has two advantages. By focusing on learning continuous
931   parameters of a discrete likelihood, FlatVI explicitly models distributional properties of single-cell
932   transcriptomics data, such as overdispersion, sparsity and discreteness. On the contrary, a fully con-
933   nected Gaussian decoder produces dense and continuous cells, failing to preserve the characteristics of
934
      the data. Moreover, the GAE’s regularisation relies on the construction of a k-nearest-neighbourhood
      to approximate the geodesic distance between data points. This method requires the computation
935
      of pairwise Euclidean distances in the observation space. As suggested previously, gene expression
936
      is high dimensional and, therefore, deceiving due to the Curse of Dimensionality. On the contrary,
937   leveraging only the Jacobian and the output of the decoder to enforce latent space Euclideanicity,
938   FlatVI is more suitable for larger datasets and eludes computing distances in high dimensions.

943
944          • First, it is easier to reflect on the complexity of the computation from Eq. (7), M(z) =
945            Jh (z)T M(ϕ)Jh (z), which is the generalisation of Eq. (22).
946
             • We call G the number of genes and d the dimensionality of the latent space. We also
947            assume that G >> d. Since one mean parameter is decoded per gene dimension, ϕ is also
948            G-dimensional.
949
             • By the definition of decoder’s Jacobian, Jh (z) is a G × d matrix. The Fisher information
950
               matrix (M(ϕ)) is a G × G since it represents the second derivative of the likelihood of a
951            data point given the parameters. For a formal definition of the Fisher information metric,
952            please refer to Eq. (16) and Eq. (22).
953
             • Given this formulation, the complexity of Jh (z)T M(ϕ) is O(dG2 ), yielding a d × G matrix.
954
               Such matrix is multiplied by a G × d matrix (Jh (z)). This operation has complexity O(d2 G).
955
956
             • Since G >> d, the complexity is bounded by the first product, thus it is O(dG2 ).
957   Note that the same result is obtained by evaluating the sum of matrices derived by outer products in
958   Eq. (22) since the operation is equivalent. Finally, we only provided the computational complexity of
959   the product evaluation. Bear in mind that in practice one must factor in the complexity of evaluating
