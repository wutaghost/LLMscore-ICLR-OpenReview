009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformer has become fundamental to a vast series of pretrained large mod-
012            els that have achieved remarkable success across diverse applications. Machine
013            unlearning is an emerging field focused on efficiently removing the influence of
014            specific data from trained models, to comply with privacy regulations enforcing
015            the right to be forgotten. The sheer size of Transformer-based models poses a
016
               significant challenge to unlearning efficiency. Existing methods find it promising
               to restrict unlearning updates to a small portion of influence-critical parameters.
017
               However, their parameter-efficient unlearning methods are largely devised in a
018
               structure-oblivious manner, which tends to inaccurately identify these parameters
019            and leads to inferior unlearning performance for Transformers. In this paper, we
020            propose SPE-Unlearn, a structure-aware parameter-efficient machine unlearn-
021            ing approach tailored for the Transformer architecture. SPE-Unlearn intro-
022            duces a learnable pair of masks to respectively pinpoint influence-critical parame-
023            ters in the heads and filters of Transformers. The learning objective of these masks
024            is derived by jointly considering both desiderata of unlearning, i.e., sufficiency in
025            influence removal and efficiency, and optimized through an efficient algorithm fea-
026            tured by a greedy search with a warm start. Equipped with the identified key pa-
027
               rameters, SPE-Unlearn facilitates second-order unlearning, memory-free un-
               learning, and memory-aided unlearning scenarios. Extensive experiments on vari-
028
               ous Transformer models and datasets demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency
029
               of SPE-Unlearn for Transformer unlearning.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al. (2017)) has demonstrated superior performance in the field
035   of natural language processing. Its models, e.g., BERT (Devlin et al. (2018)) and GPT (Achiam et al.
036   (2023)), show impressive performance in a wide range of downstream tasks (Wei et al. (2021); Hao
037   et al. (2019)). In light of privacy regulations, such as General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)
038   (Hoofnagle et al. (2019)), users are granted the right to request the removal of specific training
039
      data from models. To fulfill this requirement, machine unlearning techniques have been extensively
      researched (Bourtoule et al. (2021); Yao et al. (2023)). However, when applying these techniques
040
      to Transformers, which commonly involves a large number of parameters, a significant challenge
041
      lies in achieving computational efficiency while ensuring effective unlearning and preserving model
042   fidelity (Warnecke et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2024a)).
043
044   Recent researches propose parameter-efficient unlearning techniques (Liu et al. (2024a); Pochinkov
045
      & Schoots (2024); Schoepf et al. (2024)), which identify the influence-critical parameters to govern
      the unlearning process. Specifically, these methods assess the importance of parameters through
046
      different evaluation strategies, allowing selective updates to reduce computational overhead and
047
      improve unlearning efficiency. However, applying parameter-efficient unlearning to address the
048   dilemma of the unlearning tasks in Transformers faces two major limitations. First, previous eval-
049   uation methods rely on heuristic or empirical strategies to identify parameters. For Transformer
050   models with an immense number of parameters, identifying those specifically relevant to unlearn-
051   ing becomes inefficient. Additionally, existing methods (Pochinkov & Schoots (2024); Liu et al.
052   (2023b); Shi et al. (2023)) assess importance of parameters by comparing performance (e.g., ac-
053   tivations) on forgetting dataset and remaining dataset may result in sub-optimal selection process
      for unlearning. Second, previous unlearning methods overlook the intricate interactions between

054
      structures in Transformers. Transformers utilize parallel attention heads and hierarchical filters to
055   perform computation and inference Vaswani et al. (2017). Consequently, attempting to identify criti-
056   cal parameters at a fine-grained level is often inaccurate, as this approach fails to capture the broader
057   contextual relationships inherent in Transformers.
058
      In this paper, we propose a Structure-aware Parameter-Efficient Unlearning (SPE-Unlearn)
059
      approach that targets influence-critical parameters at the structural level for Transformers. Specifi-
060
      cally, SPE-Unlearn formulates the unlearning objective through a pair of learnable masks applied
061   to heads and filters. The derivation for this formulation ensures the effective influence removal and
062   guides the identification of key structures. These masks are further refined by considering intra-layer
063   interactions, and a warm-start greedy search algorithm is employed to efficiently optimize the pro-
064   cess. Equipped with these structure-aware masks, we integrate SPE-Unlearn into second-order
065   unlearning updates. While second-order unlearning introduces an approximation error, sparse up-
066   dates using structure-aware masks can mitigate the errors, thereby preserving overall model perfor-
067   mance. In addition, we analyze that structure-aware masks can demonstrate significant advantages
068   in successive settings (Hu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023a)). In this context, we are the first to cat-
069
      egorize second-order successive setting into two types based on whether intermediate information
      from previous removal requests is retained: memory-free unlearning (Guo et al. (2020); Gu et al.
070
      (2024)) and memory-aided unlearning (Liu et al. (2023a)). Our approach demonstrates exceptional
071
      robustness by effectively containing errors within selected structures, especially in memory-free un-
072   learning scenarios. Our key contributions are summarized as follows:
073
074   • We introduce a new paradigm for identifying influence-critical parameters in Transformers,
075     SPE-Unlearn, which operates at the structural level. Our approach theoretically derives impor-
076     tance scores for selecting key structures using a pair of learnable masks. These structure-aware
077     masks can be seamlessly integrated into existing unlearning methods.
078   • We integrate SPE-Unlearn into second-order unlearning and analyze the gains with structure-
079     aware masks. Extensive experiments across diverse datasets using three models demonstrate pro-
080     posed method offers a superior trade-off among efficacy, fidelity, and efficiency.
081   • We categorize successive unlearning into two successive scenarios: memory-free unlearning and
082     memory-aided unlearning. Empirical studies show that unlearning with structure-aware masks can
083     handle a greater number of removal requests compared to standard unlearning before retraining
084     becomes necessary, especially in memory-free scenarios.

144   3     S TRUCTURE -AWARE PARAMETER -E FFICIENT M ACHINE U NLEARNING
145
146   Inspired by the lottery hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin (2018)), recent research suggests that localiz-
147   ing functional regions within neural networks can make the model more effective for specific tasks
148   (Zhang et al. (2024b)). Given the high dimension for large models, empirically identifying influence-
149   critical parameters from a too fine-grained perspective is both inefficient and often sub-optimal. To
150
      this end, we propose SPE-Unlearn, which derived a pair of masks to pinpoint influence-critical
      parameters within heads and filters in Section 3.1. By selectively targeting the most influence-critical
151
      parameters, SPE-Unlearn is integrated into second-order unlearning in 3.2, enabling more effi-
152
      cient and effective unlearning processes. At last, we extend SPE-Unlearn to support successive
153   unlearning, demonstrating its robustness in Section 3.3.

      By pinpointing influence-critical parameters within the heads and filters, SPE-Unlearn enables
242
      efficient integration with widely-adopted unlearning methods, e.g., fine-tuning (Golatkar et al.
243   (2020)) and gradient difference (Liu et al. (2022); Jia et al. (2024)). A key observation is that
244   both SPE-Unlearn and second-order unlearning share the computational need for gradients and
245   FIM. Therefore, we leverage second-order unlearning as a representative case study to showcase the
246   efficacy of our approach.
247

249   heads and filters:

255

267   receiving removal requests, we can compute the data information about forgetting dataset to obtain
268   the required data, i.e., the gradient of forgetting dataset and diagonal FIM of remaining dataset.
269   Alternatively, to reduce memory consumption, SPE-SO can retrieve only the necessary information

      3.3     S TRUCTURE - AWARE PARAMETER -E FFICIENT S UCCESSIVE U NLEARNING
271
272   Successive unlearning presents a practical scenario where data owners request the removal of data
273   points from the model at intervals, necessitating prompt deletion (e.g., machine learning as a ser-
274   vice (MLaaS) (Hu et al. (2023))). While prior work has proposed different approaches to succes-
275   sive unlearning, we introduce the classification to better differentiate how unlearning algorithm is
276   used. Specifically, we categorize second-order successive unlearning into two distinct types based
277   on whether or not the algorithm retains information from removed data: memory-free (Guo et al.
278
      (2020); Gu et al. (2024)) and memory-aided (Liu et al. (2023a)).
279   Memory-free unlearning iteratively update the latest model following each removal request without
280   retaining any information from the removed data. However, this method increases the unlearning
281   approximation error, as the updates are based solely on the latest model, which can be more severe
282   for Transformers. In contrast, memory-aided unlearning retains data information (i.e., gradients and
283   FIM) to efficient unlearn on the original model. In what follows, we apply structure-aware masks
284
      into these successive unlearning scenarios and discuss the advantages of these masks.
285

336   it does not fully account for the influence of the data points slated for deletion. Thus, we finally
337   rearrange the mask variables using Equation (9), which allows for a more targeted adjustment. In
338
      memory-aided scenario, unlearning is achieved through a single-step second-order update on the
      original model. Therefore, the key strength of structure-aware masks stems from the superiority of
339
      SPE-Unlearn in handling general second-order unlearning, which offers a tighter approximation
340
      error bound to facilitate more effective and accurate data removal.

      Models and Datasets. We conduct comprehensive experiments on three pretrained Transformer
347   models: BERT-base (Devlin et al. (2018)), DistilBERT (Sanh et al. (2019)), and RoBERTa-large
348   (Liu et al. (2019)). These models are accessed through the HuggingFace Transformers library (Wolf
349   et al. (2020)). Our evaluation spans four GLUE tasks (MNLI, QQP, SST-2, and STS-B) (Wang et al.
350   (2018)) and two SQuAD tasks (SQuAD v1.1 and SQuAD v2.0) (Rajpurkar (2016)). Consistent with
351   the configurations outlined in prior works Devlin et al. (2018); Sanh et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2019),
352   we fine-tune these models, treating them as the original models for our experiments.
353   Unlearning methods. Our experiments focus on comparing the proposed method SPE-SO with
354   several other unlearning methods. These methods include Fine-Tuning (FT), Gradient Difference
355   (GD) (Liu et al. (2022), Jia et al. (2024)), Sparsity-Aware unlearning (SA) (Liu et al. (2024a)). For
356   FT, we continue training the original model on the remaining dataset for 3 epochs. For GD, the
357   model is fine-tuned on entire dataset for 3 epochs, with the gradient direction reversed for the data
358   that needs to be forgotten. For SA, fine-tuning is performed on the remaining dataset with a sparsity
359   penalty (γ = 5e − 5) applied to the parameters for 3 epochs. Additionally, we also include the
360   standard Second-Order unlearning (SO) method, which excludes structure-aware masks, to evaluate
361
      the effectiveness of SPE-Unlearn. Meanwhile, Retraining from scratch (RT) serves as the gold
      standard, where the model is fine-tuned on the remaining dataset following the configurations from
362
      Devlin et al. (2018); Sanh et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2019). Detailed hyperparameters are presented in
363
      Appendix A.1.
364
365   Evaluation metrics. We analyze the unlearning methods from three aspects (Warnecke et al. (2021);
366   Gu et al. (2024)): 1) Efficacy in removing the targeted data. We evaluate this using unlearning
367
      accuracy and membership inference attacks (MIA) on Df . Unlearning accuracy directly reflects
      the effectiveness of the unlearning algorithm, while MIA assesses the vulnerability of the model
368
      to attacks after unlearning. In practice, we use a confidence-based MIA predictor to gauge the
369
      likelihood of a successful attack (Liu et al. (2024a); Song et al. (2019)); 2) Fidelity of model utility.
370   We measure this by examining both the remaining accuracy and the testing accuracy to assess the
371   preservation of model performance and its generalization ability after unlearning; 3) Efficiency of
372   executing the unlearning methods. We report the time required to perform unlearning as a measure
373   of speed and computational efficiency.

470

      results highlight the potential of structure-aware masks to enhance the robustness of unlearning.

474   5                         R ELATED W ORK
475
476   Transformer Unlearning. The concept of machine unlearning was first introduced by (Cao & Yang
477
      (2015)). Initially applied to simple model, machine unlearning has since been extended to Trans-
      former models (Jang et al. (2022); Eldan & Russinovich (2023); Yao et al. (2023; 2024); Chen et al.
478
      (2024); Jia et al. (2024); Gu et al. (2024)). (Jang et al. (2022)) proposed inverting the training objec-
479
      tive on forgetting sequences and utilize straightforward gradient ascent. As gradient ascent signifi-
480   cantly degrades performance, (Yao et al. (2024)) refined the objective function by employing gradi-
481   ent descent on in-distribution data to enhance robustness. Subsequently, (Jia et al. (2024)) provided
482   a comprehensive overview of unlearning objectives and developed a second-order optimization un-
483   learning approach. (Gu et al. (2024)) further investigated the effectiveness of second-order updates
484   in Transformers. However, these methods primarily focus on updating all model parameters, which
485   is computationally expensive and time-consuming. In our work, we study the parameter-efficient
      methods to achieve effective unlearning in Transformers.


                                                                                                                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Parameter-efficient Unlearning. Parameter-efficient unlearning methods focus on identifying
487   influence-critical parameters and updating only those to accelerate the unlearning process. Sev-
488   eral strategies (Ma et al. (2022); Pochinkov & Schoots (2024); Shi et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023b);
489   Wu & Harandi (2024); Foster et al. (2024); Schoepf et al. (2024)) have been proposed to assess pa-
490   rameter importance. Although these approaches may be applicable to Transformers, they are largely
491   heuristic or empirical, which can result in sub-optimal outcomes for unlearning tasks. Recently, (Liu
492   et al. (2024a)) highlighted that unlearning can be effective when performed on a pruned model with
493   a theoretical foundation. However, pruning primarily focuses on identifying parameters critical to
494   maintain model performance, which does not align with the desiderata of unlearning. Additionally,
495
      the focus on parameter ignore the complex intra-structural interactions within Transformers, which
      results in inaccurate identification of the parameters. Therefore, we specifically target at heads and
496
      filters within Transformers and derive an efficient strategy to identify influence-critical parameters.

499   6   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
500
501   In this work, we propose structure-aware parameter-efficient unlearning (SPE-Unlearn), a novel
502   approach tailored for Transformers. SPE-Unlearn derives an optimal masking strategy to iden-
503
      tify influence-critical parameters within heads and filters. By selectively targeting these key param-
      eters, SPE-Unlearn infuses into second-order unlearning update to demonstrate its efficacy and
504
      strengths. We further analyze the advantages of our method across both memory-free and memory-
505
      aided successive unlearning scenarios. Empirical study demonstrate that our method accommodates
506   more removal requests than standard second-order unlearning in memory-free unlearning scenarios.
507   Additionally, comprehensive experiments conducted on various Transformer models and datasets
508   reveal that our method with 90% sparsity outperforms existing approaches.
509
      For future work, we suggest extending to other existing unlearning methods to demonstrate the ef-
510
      fectiveness of SPE-Unlearn in Transformers. While our experiments focus on small-scale Trans-
511
      formers, we plan to explore larger-scale models (e.g, OPT-13b and LLaMA2-13b) to better under-
512   stand the behavior of structure-aware masks. Furthermore, our study concentrates on fine-tuned
513   models, which limits the ability to unlearn deeply ingrained undesired information from pre-trained
514   models. To address this, we aim to identify structure-aware masks directly in pre-trained models.

687   A.3   F IND THE APPROPRIATE SPARSITY
688
689   We aim to determine the level of sparsity that can ensure adequate model performance while pro-
690   viding sufficient unlearning guarantees. Therefore, we conducted a detailed sparsity analysis on
691
      additional datasets, as shown in Figure 5. Our results indicate that updating all parameters is not
      the most effective strategy for unlearning, as it can lead to excessive forgetting, causing a rapid de-
692
      cline in model performance. In contrast, we found that a sparsity of 50% offers the most efficient
693
      improvement in unlearning. Moreover, sparsity levels between 80% and 90% perform on par with,
694   and sometimes even surpass, the performance of other methods.

799   Machine unlearning typically relies on the specific unlearning objective to design optimization algo-
800   rithms. For instance, second-order unlearning is achieved by minimizing the loss on the remaining
801   dataset (i.e., Equation 2). To simplify the optimization, a Taylor expansion of the unlearning ob-
802   jective is performed on the original model. Following the optimization process, we identify the
803   influence-critical parameters using a structure-aware approach in Transformers, which then guides
804   the second-order unlearning update.

806   pattern of optimization based on the objective function. Gradient Ascent (GA) (Jang et al. (2022))
807   aims to maximize the loss for the forgetting dataset. Building on this, Gradient Difference (GD) (Liu
808   et al. (2022)) further strives to maintain performance on the remaining dataset. Direct Preference
809   Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al. (2024)) seeks to algin the model by replacing the original re-
      sponse on forgetting dataset with the alternative answers “I don’t know”. Inspired by DPO, Negative

      B.1     E XPERIMENTS
912
913   We evaluate unlearning methods on the Task of Fictitious Unlearning (TOFU) dataset (Maini et al.
914   (2024)) using LLama2-7b-chat model (Touvron et al. (2023)). The unlearning scenarios of TOFU
915   can be divided into three types: Forget01, Forget05, and Forget10, which represent forgetting dataset
916   proportions of 1%, 5%, and 10% of the total dataset, respectively. The baseline includes seven
917   methods: Retraining (RT), Fine-tuning (FT), Sparsity-Aware Unlearning (SA), GA, GD, DPO and
      NPO. We apply three structure-aware parameter-efficient unlearning methods into GA, GD, DPO


                                                            17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      and NPO for comparison. These methods include: 1) maximize the loss on the forgotten dataset
919   (MLF) as the unlearning objective, 2) minimize the loss of the remaining dataset (MLR) as the
920   forgetting objective (i.e. the original method in Section 3.1), and 3) use the norm of the gradients
921   associated with the structure to evaluate its importance (NORM).
922
      Experimental details. We use AdamW with a weight decay of 0.01 and a learning rate of 10−5 in
923
      RT, FT, SA, GA, GD, DPO and NPO. Besides, we set the learning rate for structure-aware parameter-
924
      efficient methods to 2 · 10−4 or 3 · 10−4 . In addition, the sparsity of structure-aware parameter-
925   efficient methods is 90%. All the experiments run for 5 epochs. We also use three main aspects
926   (i.e., efficacy, fidelity and efficiency) to evaluate the unlearning performance. We use Rouge scores,
927   normalized probabilities, and the True Ratios on the forgotten dataset to measure efficacy, and those
928   metrics on the real authors, world facts, and remaining dataset to measure fidelity. We still use
929   unlearning time to evaluate efficiency. Note that smaller values do not necessarily indicate better
930   forgetting performance. The goal for unlearning is to closely match that achieved through retraining.
931   Results. We find that performing FT and SA only on the remaining dataset does not meet the
932   unlearning requirements. Although the original GA, GD, and DPO methods can achieve unlearning,
933   they all exhibit severe catastrophic forgetting on the Forget10 dataset. In contrast, NPO is the most
934   efficient among these methods. Furthermore, our experiments indicate that sparse updates are better
935   suited for unlearning than full updates, as they offer a stronger guarantee of unlearning while more
936   effectively preserving performance, even on the Forget10 dataset. Additionally, the NORM-based
937   method significantly reduces computation time, but it is less effective than the MLF-based and MLR-
938   based methods. We observe that the MLR-based method offers a robust balanced trade-off among
939
      unlearning efficacy, model fidelity, and computational efficiency.
