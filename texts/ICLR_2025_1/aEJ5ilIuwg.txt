                                                 A BSTRACT
011
               Understanding the effectiveness of intrinsic geometry in enhancing a model’s
012
               generalization ability, we draw upon prior works that apply geometric principles to
013            optimization and present a novel approach to improve robustness and generalization
014            for constrained optimization problems. This work aims to strengthen the sharpness-
015            aware optimizers and proposes a novel Riemannian optimizer. We first present
016            a theoretical analysis that characterizes the relationship between the general loss
017            and the perturbation of the empirical loss in the context of Riemannian manifolds.
018            Motivated by the result obtained from this analysis, we introduce our algorithm
019            named Riemannian Jacobian Regularization (RJR), which explicitly regularizes the
020            Riemannian gradient norm and the projected Hessian. To demonstrate RJR’s ability
021
               to enhance generalization, we evaluate and contrast our algorithm on a broad set
               of problems, such as image classification and contrastive learning across different
022
               datasets with various architectures.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In deep learning and statistics, overfitting is a long-standing and challenging problem in which
028   the model fails to generalize to the whole population due to the training process getting stuck in
029   one of the local minima of the landscape of loss functions. This is attributed to high-dimensional
030   and non-convex loss functions, which have a complicated landscape with multiple local minima.
031   Regarding this issue, flat minimizers that seek regions with low sharpness have been known to be
032   among the most effective approaches (Keskar et al., 2016; Kaddour et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2022).
033
      Sharpness-aware minimization (SAM), as introduced by Foret et al. (2021b), stands out as a notable
      method by simultaneously minimizing the loss function and the worst-case loss within a neighborhood
034
      of the model’s parameters. SAM already has proven to be versatile across a diverse array of tasks
035
      such as meta-learning (Abbas et al., 2022), federated learning (Qu et al., 2022), vision models (Chen
036   et al., 2021), or language models (Bahri et al., 2022).
037
038   Another desired property of the model is robustness, which could be improved when encouraging
039
      the model’s parameters to satisfy strict conditions, i.e., SPD constraints (Gao et al., 2020), orthog-
      onality, and full rank (Xie et al., 2017; Roy et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020), etc. In those cases,
040
      the model’s parameters are restricted to certain Riemannian manifolds, such as Grassmann, SPD,
041
      etc. Consequently, it becomes more challenging to work with its loss landscape, thus requiring
042   novel optimization techniques that take into account the intrinsic geometry of the parameter spaces
043   (Bonnabel, 2013; Luenberger, 1972; Kasai et al., 2019; Sato et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2017).
044
045
      In this work, we address both problems by bridging the gap between the sharpness-aware and
      Jacobian-aware optimizers on Riemannian manifolds. In doing so, we first derive a comprehensive
046
      theoretical analysis showing that the general loss function can be bounded by the empirical loss,
047
      the Riemannian gradient, and the projected Hessian. Motivated by this analysis, we proposed
048   a Riemannian optimization technique named Riemannian Jacobian Regularization (RJR), which
049   explicitly regularizes the Riemannian gradient norm and the Jacobian. Our empirical study shows
050   that the RJR improves the model’s generalization ability across a range of different tasks, namely
051   supervised learning and self-supervised learning, for a diverse array of computer vision datasets
052   (CIFAR100, CIFAR10, STL10, FGVCAircraft (Maji et al., 2013)), as well as different model’s
053   architectures (ResNet34, ResNet50, EfficientNetV2-S, EfficientNetV2-L Tan & Le (2021), and
      PyramidNet-101 (Han et al., 2017)). RJR has made a notable improvement upon SAM, SupCon

      (Khosla et al., 2021), as well as other Riemannian optimizers, including Riemannian Stochastic
055   Gradient Descent (RSGD) (Bonnabel, 2013), and Riemannian-SAM (Yun & Yang, 2023). In the

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
068
069
      Optimization on Riemannian Manifolds. Imposing appropriate constraints on model parameters
      has been shown to obtain the desired effect on the model performance (Roy et al., 2019; Absil
070
      et al., 2008a). In those situations, studying the intrinsic geometry of the parameters manifold could
071
      lead to improved optimization methods. For example, in the domain of metric learning, Roy et al.
072   (2019) incorporated Stiefel manifolds to ensure that the learned parameters maintain orthogonality
073   constraints. When the model is a Gaussian mixture, Gao et al. (2020) proposed a strategy involving
074   learning on SPD manifolds to enforce SPD constraints. Furthermore, Grassmann manifolds have been
075   utilized in encompassing recommender systems (Dai et al., 2012; Boumal & Absil, 2015) or modeling
076   affine subspaces within document-specific language models (Hall & Hofmann, 2000). Since the
077   optimization is carried out on manifolds, the Riemannian gradient descent approach developed by
078   (Luenberger, 1972) is a tool to move on the manifold to look for minimums. Its stochastic version
079   introduced by Bonnabel (2013) reduces computational overhead, thus gaining widespread adoption.
080
081   Sharpness Aware Minimization and Jacobian Regularization. The Sharpness-Aware Minimiza-
082   tion (SAM) technique ( Foret et al. (2021a)) has gained prominence due to its effectiveness and
083
      scalability compared to previous methods. SAM’s versatility is evident across various tasks and
      domains, making it a powerful optimization approach. SAM has found applications in diverse areas
084
      such as meta-learning bi-level optimization (Abbas et al., 2022), federated learning (Qu et al., 2022),
085
      vision models (Chen et al., 2021), language models (Bahri et al., 2022), domain generalization (Cha
086   et al., 2021), and multi-task learning (Phan et al., 2022).
087
088   Recent works have further enhanced SAM’s capabilities by exploring its underlying geometry (Kwon
089
      et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022a), minimizing surrogate gaps (Zhuang et al., 2022), and speeding up
      training time (Du et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022). Additionally, Kaddour et al. (2022a) empirically
090
      studied SAM’s sharpness compared to Stochastic Weights Average (SWA) (Izmailov et al., 2018). In
091
      contrast, Möllenhoff & Khan (2023) demonstrated that SAM is an optimal Bayesian relaxation of
092   standard Bayesian inference with a normal posterior. Moreover, Nguyen et al. (2023b) developed
093   the sharpness concept for Bayesian Neural Networks. Nguyen et al. (2023a) generalized SAM by
094   leveraging optimal transport-based distributional robustness with sharpness-aware minimization.
095   Recently, Yun & Yang (2023) proposed Riemannian-SAM, which extends SAM to Riemannian
096   manifolds, and the technique has demonstrated its efficacy on a wide range of manifolds. Lee et al.
097   (2023) has extended SAM techniques to show that minimizing the Jacobian norm can affect the
098   sharpness and the model accuracy, thus proposing explicitly regularizing the Jacobian norm.

122

125   For each θ ∈ M, Tθ M is linear space, thus one can define an inner product ⟨·, ·⟩θ : Tθ M × Tθ M →

      5.2       O RTHOGONAL C ONVOLUTIONAL N EURAL N ETWORK
353
354   In the literature of deep learning, enforcing orthogonality on the convolutional filters has established
355   various significant benefits, such as alleviating gradient vanishing or exploding phenomenon (Xie
356   et al., 2017), decorrelating the filter banks so that they learn distinct features (Wang et al., 2020),
357   or stabilize the distribution of activations over layers within CNNs and make optimization more
358   efficient (Rodríguez et al., 2016; Desjardins et al., 2015). Let {Wi }D i=1 be the set of convolutional
359   kernels for the ℓ-th layer in which Wi ∈ RW HM . To impose orthogonality, Previous works
                                                                 D
360                                                           λX
      introduce orthogonal regularizers such as Lortho =            ∥Wi⊤ Wi − I∥22 (Xie et al., 2017), or a
361                                                           2 i=1
362   self-convolution regularization term of the kernels (Wang et al., 2020) to encourage orthogonality
363   between the convolutional kernels. In this section, we propose eliminating those regularizers and
364   strictly enforcing the kernels to be always orthogonal during training. To do so, we flatten the kernels
365   Wi into the column vectors of shape W × H × M . Let Kℓ be the matrix with the columns formed
366   by Wi′ s. With RJR, we can enforce Kℓ to always lie on the Stiefel manifold St(W × H × M, C)
367   during training. Therefore, throughout training, K⊤    ℓ Kℓ = Id always holds, therefore guarantees
368   orthonormality between the kernels on the layer ℓ. The next section will demonstrate that imposing
369   orthogonality onto a single convolutional layer in the middle of the architecture by training with RJR
370   can notably improve generalization ability.

526   8     C ONCLUSION
527
528   We have extended the flat minimizers to differential manifolds by introducing a novel Riemannian
529   optimizer. Theoretically, we presented a theorem that characterizes the generalization in terms of the
530   Riemannian gradient and Hessian. Motivated by this analysis, we propose RJR that considers the
531
      intrinsic geometry and simultaneously minimizes the loss function, the Riemannian gradient norm,
      and the Jacobian. Empirically, RJR has demonstrated its effectiveness on different tasks with various
532
      datasets and consistently outperforms the comparative methods by a notable margin.
533
534
      Limitations and Future works. Similar to other Riemannian optimizers such as Riemannian-SAM
535
      or RSGD, a limitation of RJR is that the method is only applicable to the class of Riemannian
536   manifolds where the operations such as retraction and projection are well-defined. Even though this
537   class of manifolds has demonstrated a wide range of applications in deep learning literature, further
538   studies are still needed for a broader class of manifolds, such as general transformer architecture.

      In the previous section, we demonstrated the efficiency of RJR compared to Riemannian-SAM. In this
730   section, we emphasize the importance of optimization on Riemannian manifolds in different scenarios
731   and the effectiveness of RJR in accomplishing this task. We designed a simple experiment on the
732   MNIST dataset to show a particular case where RJR is favorably robust compared to SAM, which did
733   not take into account the intrinsic geometry. Indeed, we train a simple PCA-style autoencoder that
734   aims to find an orthogonal matrix W which encodes each input x into lower-dimensional z = xW,
735   and then decodes as x̃ = zW⊤ . The encoded vector z is then used for the classification. Therefore,
736   the objective that we will minimize is the reconstruction loss, which is regularized with a classification
737   loss. Since W is constrained to be orthogonal, we want it to stay within a Stiefel manifold during
738   training. To enforce orthogonality with SAM, an orthogonal regulazrizer ∥W⊤ W − Id ∥22 is added,
739
      which gives the objective:
