009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               As large language models (LLMs) become increasingly integral to a wide array of
012
               applications, ensuring the factual accuracy of their outputs and mitigating halluci-
013
               nations is paramount. Current approaches, which primarily rely on self-consistency
014            checks or post-hoc fact-checking, often fall short by disregarding the nuanced struc-
015            ture of queries and the diverse forms of contextual knowledge required for accurate
016            response generation. To address these shortcomings, we introduce KnowHalu
017            (pronounced “No Halu”), the first multi-form knowledge-based hallucination de-
018            tection framework. We also introduce a new category of hallucinations, off-target
019            hallucinations, which occur when responses are factually accurate but irrelevant
020            or nonspecific to the query (e.g., answering ”What’s the primary language in
021            Barcelona?” with ”European language”). In particular, KnowHalu employs a
022
               rigorous two-phase process to detect hallucinations. In the first phase, it isolates
               off-target hallucinations by analyzing the semantic alignment between the response
023
               and the query. In the second phase, it conducts a novel multi-form knowledge-based
024
               fact-checking through a comprehensive pipeline of reasoning and query decomposi-
025            tion, knowledge retrieval, knowledge form optimization, judgment generation, and
026            judgment aggregation. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that KnowHalu signifi-
027            cantly surpasses state-of-the-art (SOTA) baselines across diverse tasks, achieving
028            over 15% improvement in question answering (QA) and 6% in summarization tasks
029            when applied to the same underlying LLM. These results underscore the effec-
030            tiveness and versatility of KnowHalu, setting a new benchmark for hallucination
031            detection and paving the way for safer and more reliable LLM applications.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Significant advancements have been achieved in the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP)
038   with the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs). While these models excel in generating coherent
039   and contextually relevant text, they are prone to ‘hallucinations’ — generating plausible but factually
040   incorrect or unspecific information (Bang et al., 2023). This poses a considerable challenge, especially
041
      in applications demanding high factual accuracy, such as medical records analysis (Singhal et al.,
      2023), finance (Wu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), and drug design (Vert, 2023; Savage, 2023).
042
043   To mitigate or detect hallucinations in LLMs, a series of approaches have been explored. For instance,
044   self-consistency-based approaches detect hallucinations by identifying contradictions in responses
045
      that are stochastically sampled from the LLMs in response to the same query (Wang et al., 2022;
      Manakul et al., 2023; Mündler et al., 2023). Other approaches detect hallucinations by probing
046
      LLMs’ hidden states (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023) or output probability distributions (Manakul et al.,
047
      2023). These methods do not incorporate external knowledge and are thus limited by LLMs’ internal
048   knowledge. Post-hoc fact-checking approaches have been recently shown to be effective even when
049   LLMs’ internal knowledge proves inadequate and achieved SOTA hallucination detection (Peng
050   et al., 2023; Semnani et al., 2023). However, due to the limitation of LLM reasoning capabilities,
051   even if the extracted knowledge is correct, the models may still struggle to perform factual checking
052   accurately, especially with complex queries or logic, such as multi-hop queries or those involving
053   multiple factual assertions. Thus, how to fully leverage the inherent reasoning capabilities of the
      model is important.

076   comprehensive “Factual Checking”, which consists of five steps: (a) “Step-wise Reasoning and Query” breaks
077
      down the original query into step-wise reasoning and sub-queries for detailed factual checking; (b) “Knowledge
      Retrieval” retrieves unstructured knowledge via RAG and structured knowledge in the form of triplets for each
078   sub-query; (c) “Knowledge Optimization” leverages LLMs to summarize and refine the retrieved knowledge
079   into different forms; (d) “Judgment Based on Multi-form Knowledge” employs LLMs to critically assesses the
080   answer to sub-queries, based on each form of knowledge; (e) “Aggregation” provides a further refined judgment
081   by aggregating predictions based on different forms of knowledge.
082   Recognizing this gap, our work proposes a novel multi-phase hallucination detection mechanism,
083   KnowHalu (pronounced as “No halu”), the overall framework of which is presented in Figure 1. In
084   particular, we first perform the off-target hallucination checking, where the answer indeed provides
085   a fact but it is not a helpful response to the question, such as the answer “ChatGPT can do lots of
086
      things” for the question “What can ChatGPT do?” This type of hallucination has been extensively
      discussed in various works on hallucination in LLMs (Huang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b;
087
      Li et al., 2023). Despite this, the step of identifying off-target hallucinations remains critically
088
      underrepresented in current detection methodologies. Existing approaches often fail to discern
089   answers that are factually correct but irrelevant to the posed questions. We then perform a step-wise
090   decomposition of queries, which enables targeted retrieval of external knowledge pertinent to each
091   logical step. For each decomposed logical step, we will perform the multi-form knowledge based
092   factual checking, leveraging both the unstructured knowledge (e.g., normal semantic sentences) and
093   structured knowledge (e.g., object-predicate-object triplets). This multi-form knowledge analysis
094   captures a comprehensive spectrum of factual information, enhancing the reasoning capability of
095   LLMs and ensuring a robust and thorough validation of each piece of retrieved knowledge. Finally,
096   we perform the reasoning step by composing the step-wise factual checking results together and
097
      guide the LLMs to make the final judgment by providing related demonstrations. Our multi-step and
      multi-form knowledge based factual checking not only improves the verification accuracy but also
098
      enhances the model’s ability to handle intricate and layered queries.
099
      Our extensive experimental evaluations reveal that KnowHalu significantly outperforms state-of-the-
100
      art (SOTA) baselines in detecting hallucinations. The experiments, conducted across diverse datasets
101   and tasks, demonstrate not only the high accuracy of our method in factual verification but also its
102   versatility in handling various types of queries.
103

105     • We introduce KnowHalu, a novel approach with two main phases (off-target hallucination
106       detection and multi-step factual checking) for detecting hallucinations in texts generated by
107       LLMs, leveraging multi-form knowledge for factual checking. In particular, we define the
          categories of off-target hallucinations for the first time.

137
      Hallucination of LLMs. Hallucination in the LLM literature generally refers to LLMs generating
138
      nonfactual, irrelevant, or unspecific outputs. Such phenomena have been observed in a variety of
139   tasks (Huang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Ji et al., 2023), such as translation (Lee et al., 2018),
140   dialogue (Balakrishnan et al., 2019), summarization (Durmus et al., 2020), and question answering
141   (Sellam et al., 2020). We summarize and describe the hallucination types for the QA task covered
142   in this paper in Table 1. Benchmarks have been proposed to evaluate the extent to which LLMs
143   hallucinate, such as Honovich et al. (2021), Huang et al. (2021), and Li et al. (2023).
144
      Hallucination detection and mitigation. Methods that attempt to detect and mitigate hallucination
145   without fact-checking include methods based on chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022;
146   Dhuliawala et al., 2023), methods based on self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022;
147   Mündler et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023), and methods that probe LLMs’ hidden states (Azaria &
148   Mitchell, 2023) or output probability distributions (Manakul et al., 2023). Since these methods do
149   not augment LLMs with external knowledge, they usually struggle when LLMs’ internal knowledge
150   is inadequate. On the other hand, fact-checking-based methods (Roller et al., 2020; Komeili et al.,
151   2021; Shuster et al., 2022a; 2021; 2022b; Izacard et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Semnani et al., 2023)
152   rely on retrieved knowledge to prevent hallucinations in LLMs. However, these methods are often
153
      limited by the ways they retrieve knowledge and utilize the retrieved knowledge. For instance, Li
      et al. (2023) employ only a single query with the knowledge for detecting hallucinations in inputs that
154
      inherently necessitate multi-hop reasoning, which would benefit from a step-by-step query process.
155
      Semnani et al. (2023) instead introduces a robust framework initially designed to deliver fact-checked
156   responses. This innovative method employs a comprehensive process that entails generating queries to
157   fetch information from Wikipedia, summarizing and filtering the retrieved content, and then crafting
158   a response informed by this vetted knowledge. However, when adapting WikiChat for hallucination
159   detection, although it effectively uses retrieved knowledge for fact-checking in parallel, it occasionally
160   neglects the coherence of the facts being verified, potentially leading to inaccuracies. Besides, during
161   the fact-checking phase, WikiChat directly retrieves the evidence with the claim, which may not be
      effective when the claim itself is the result of hallucination as shown in Appendix B. Our approach,

      in contrast, derives step-by-step queries with refined formulations and provides LLMs with either
163   structured or unstructured knowledge for consecutive fact-checking, leading to better retrieval and
164   higher knowledge utilization.

      3     K NOW H ALU
168
169
      KnowHalu provides a systematic hallucination detection framework based on multi-step query and
      reasoning. It starts with “Off-Target Hallucination Checking” to pinpoint non-specific hallucinated
170
      answers, followed by “Factual Checking”, verifying the correctness of the answer through a multi-step
171
      process based on different forms of knowledge.
172
173
      3.1   O FF -TARGET H ALLUCINATION C HECKING .
174
175   Current approaches in hallucination detection can mainly identify fabricated hallucinations, i.e.,
176   answers with mismatching facts (Manakul et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Semnani et al., 2023). Yet,
177   hallucinations also emerge in other types, as outlined in Table 1, which extend beyond simple factual
178   inaccuracies. A typical trait of these off-target hallucinations is their factual correctness while a lack
179   of direct relevance and helpfulness to the original query. For instance, given a question, “What is the
180   primary language in Barcelona?”, the hallucinated answer “European languages” is factually correct
181   but fails in providing specific answers, thus being off-target, which is important for the quality of
182
      real-world LLMs. Note that we mainly focus on the potential hallucinations of LLMs in practice, so
      if the model rejects to answer a question, it will not be viewed as hallucinations. For example, if the
183
      answer to the question “What is the capital of France?” is “France is in Europe,” it is categorized
184
      as an off-target hallucination due to a misinterpretation of the question. Conversely, responses like
185   “Sorry, the question is hard” or “Sorry, I don’t know” are not considered hallucinations in our work.
186   These replies correctly interpret the question and reject to provide an answer, and thus do not fall
187   under the “Misinterpretation of Question” category shown in Table 1. Instead, they acknowledge
188   the difficulty in providing a specific answer, thereby offering a useful response. In fact, we should
189   encourage models to refuse to answer questions that they do not know.
190
      To bridge this gap, in KnowHalu, we first introduce a “Off-Target Hallucination Checking” phase,
191   as depicted in the first row of Figure 1. This step aims to identify off-target hallucinations. A
192   straightforward approach might involve prompting the language model to identify such hallucina-
193   tions based on provided examples. However, this often results in high false positives, inaccurately
194   flagging correct answers as hallucinations. To counter this, we address this challenge by solving
195   an extraction task, which prompts the language model to extract specific entity or details requested
196   by the original question from the answer. If the model fails to extract such specifics, it returns
197   “NONE”. This extraction-based specificity check is designed to reduce false positives while ef-
198   fectively identifying off-target hallucinations. Responses yielding “NONE” are directly labeled
199
      as hallucinations, and the remaining generations will be sent to the next phase for further factual
      checking. Examples of instructions in the extraction task for each type of off-target hallucination are
200
      provided in Appendix A.1.
201

      3.2   FACTUAL C HECKING
203
204   The Factual Checking phase consists of five key steps: (a) Step-wise Reasoning and Query breaks
205   down the original query into sub-queries following the logical reasoning process and generates
206   different forms of sub-queries; (b) Knowledge Retrieval retrieves knowledge for each sub-query
207   based on existing knowledge database; (c) Knowledge Optimization summarizes and refines the
208   retrieved knowledge, and maps them to different forms, such as unstructured knowledge (object-
209   replicate-object triplet); (d) Judgment Based on Multi-form Knowledge assesses the answer for each
210   sub-query based on multi-form knowledge; and (e) Aggregation combines insights of judgments
211   based on different forms of knowledge and makes a further refined judgment.
212   a. Step-wise Reasoning and Query In this step, we aim to break down the original query into
213   local sub-queries following the reasoning logic, and we will retrieve knowledge for each sub-query
214   sequentially (details in steps b and c), which is similar with ReAct (Yao et al., 2023), to cumulatively
215   perform factual checking along the reasoning process. One main challenge here is “how do we
      craft precise and effective sub-queries, which can accurately retrieve the relevant knowledge at each


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      logical step?” To address this challenge, we identify two key factors that significantly enhance the
217   accuracy of knowledge retrieval for factual checking: (1) continuous and direct (one-hop) queries,
218   and (2) the formulation of queries. We will analyze these two key factors, which lead to our design
219   choice below.
220
      First, we observe that multi-hop queries (e.g., Example-1 in Figure 1) often struggle to retrieve
221
      specific and related knowledge due to their inherently complex and ambiguous context. On the other
222
      hand, the one-hop queries are effective to retrieve the most relevant and useful knowledge. Thus, we
223   decompose the original query into sequence of simpler and direct one-hop sub-queries following the
224   logical reasoning process, which significantly enhance the retrieval accuracy for factual checking.
225   Concretely, this iterative querying process starts by interpreting the original query as a series of
226   logical steps to form sub-queries accordingly, and then perform factual checking for each sub-query.
227   For instance, based on the example in Figure 1, the initial query first confirms whether “Star Wars” is
228   indeed the 1977 space-themed movie featuring Luke Skywalker. This step is crucial for connecting
229   the movie to its composer. Subsequent queries delve deeper, examining the accuracy of other specific
230   details provided in the answer, such as the composer’s identity. The queries are intricately connected,
231
      each building upon the knowledge obtained from the previous one. This iterative process continuous
      to generate subsequent queries based on newly acquired knowledge, until the logical reasoning
232
      process is completed.
233
234    Second, we observe that the formulation of queries also plays a critical role for the final factual
235    checking. In particular, queries with correct details will lead to high-quality knowledge retrieval;
236    while queries with incorrect or unrelated entities may lead to poor and irrelevant knowledge retrieval.
237
       As a result, we propose two query formulations: General Query and Specific Query. The General
       Query avoids mentioning specific, potentially hallucinated details (e.g., “Who composed the score for
238
      ’Star Wars’?”); the Specific Query is constructed based on the key entities mentioned in the answers
239
       (e.g., “Did Joy Williams compose the score for ’Star Wars’?”), as shown in Figure 1. More concrete
240    examples illustrating the impact of these two query formulations on the retrieval outcomes for both
241    correct and hallucinated details can be found in Appendix B.
242
       In our experiments shown in Section 5.2, we examine how different query formulations —general and
243    specific — affect knowledge retrieval and the accuracy of final hallucination detection, by leveraging
244    only one or both query formulations. More detailed prompts are provided in Appendix A.2.
245
       b. Knowledge Retrieval We perform knowledge retrieval for each sub-query generated from step
246    a. In particular, for QA tasks, we adopt the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework
247    developed based on WikiPedia knowledge base (Semnani et al., 2023), and the retrieval is based on
248    ColBERT v2 (Santhanam et al., 2022b) and PLAID (Santhanam et al., 2022a). We retrieve Top-K
249    relevant passages for each sub-query, each formatted as “Title: ..., Article: ...”. In addition, when
250    we perform knowledge retrieval for summarization tasks, we treat the source document itself as the
251    knowledge base for retrieval. In particular, we first segment the original documents into distinct text
252    chunks. We then embed the sub-queries and text chunks into dense vectors using a text encoder.
253    Similarly, we will retrieve the Top-K text chunks that exhibit the highest cosine similarity with the
254
       input sub-queries.
255    c. Knowledge Optimization The knowledge retrieved for each sub-query is usually a long and
256    verbose passage with distracting irrelevant details. Thus, this step aims to leverage another LLM
257    to distill useful information and optimize clear and concise knowledge, which could be in different
258
       forms. In particular, we propose two forms of knowledge, unstructured and structured knowledge.
       The unstructured knowledge represents the texts retrieved from given knowledge bases in a concise
259
       way, such as “‘Star Wars,’ released in 1977, is the space-themed movie in which the character Luke
260
       Skywalker first appeared.” Since the unstructured text may not be precise for logical reasoning, we
261    also retrieve structured knowledge as object-predicate-object triplets, such as (“Star Wars”, was,
262    1977 space-themed movie) and (Luke Skywalker, first appeared in, “Star Wars”) (examples of our
263    demonstrations are in Appendix A.3). Such multi-form knowledge will effectively assist LLMs to
264    perform logical reasoning and final factual checking. In addition, if a query retrieves no relevant
265    knowledge, the LLM is instructed to respond with “No specific information is available”.
266    d. Judgment Based on Multi-form Knowledge After obtaining the retrieved multi-form knowl-
267    edge for sub-queries, we gather #Query# and #Knowledge# and present them to another LLM for
268    hallucination judgment. The #Judgment# assesses the sub-query and its corresponding knowledge
269    sequentially to ascertain if there is any contradiction to verification each detail in the answer. If there

293   is any conflict between the answer the knowledge from a sub-query, the judgment is INCORRECT.
294   On the other hand, if all the details of the answer are verified by the knowledge of sub-queries, the
295   judgment is CORRECT. For some scenarios, where the knowledge is inadequate for a conclusive
296   judgment, the output will be INCONCLUSIVE. Prompts used to guide this judgment process are
297   shown in Appendix A.4.
298   e. Aggregation The judgment of hallucination above is based on each form of the retrieved
299   knowledge (e.g., structured and unstructured). To mitigate the prediction uncertainty, here we
300   aggregate these judgment based on the multi-form knowledge to make the final prediction.
301   The motivation for this aggregation mechanism is two-fold: 1) each knowledge form might uniquely
302   identify cases that the other cannot, particularly when one yields an INCONCLUSIVE judgment and
303   the other does not; 2) when the LLM makes a hallucinated judgment, it may lead to low confidence
304
      scores for the judgment of “CORRECT” or “INCORRECT.” Thus, we can rely on the alternative
      knowledge form if it demonstrates a higher confidence for the judgment.
305
306   Concretely, we treat the judgment based on one form of the knowledge—typically the form yielding
307   better average accuracy—as the base judgment and that of the other forms of knowledge as supplement
308
      judgment. If the confidence score for the base judgment falls below the a specific threshold δ1 and the
      supplement judgment maintains higher confidence above δ2 , we will take the supplement judgment
309
      as the final prediction. In all other cases, the base judgment will perform as the final prediction.
310
      The corresponding pseudo-code and more details for this aggregation mechanism are provided
311   in Appendix E.3.

449   5.1   I MPACT OF O FF -TARGET H ALLUCINATION C HECKING
450   Prompts provided by HaluEval (Li et al., 2023) not only cover cases of fabrication hallucination but
451   also off-target hallucinations as shown in Table 1. Thus, our pipeline separates the process into two
452   phases: (1) treating the detection of off-target hallucinations as an independent task; (2) if an off-target
453   hallucination is detected, no further checking is required; otherwise, we proceed to a second-phase
454   for factual checking. This approach raises two intriguing questions: (1) whether such decomposition
455   improves hallucination detection performance, and (2) what is the performance when factual checking
456   is conducted directly without any preliminary off-target hallucination demonstration. As shown
457   in Table 6, incorporating off-target checking consistently enhances the detection of hallucinated
458
      cases, with an approximately 15% improvement in TPR and 2% in FPR. Furthermore, even without
      off-target checking, our standalone factual checking phase still surpasses the baseline using the same
459
      knowledge source, demonstrating the effectiveness of our multi-query and reasoning process.

462   We investigate the impact of different query formulations used for knowledge retrieval on hallucination
463   detection. All experiments are conducted using Starling-7B with K = 2 for Wiki retrieval knowledge.
464   We evaluate the following three approaches: (1) using only specific queries, (2) using only general
465   queries, and (3) combining the Top-K results from both query types. The results are detailed in Table 4.
466   As we can see, the formulation of the query is crucial in knowledge retrieval. In particular, specific

      ones due to polluted context. Conversely, using general queries yields an inverse effect. Combining

469
      rate by over 5%, demonstrating that the combination of both query formulations indeed leads to more

      accurate and relevant knowledge retrieval. The results conducted with the off-the-shelf knowledge

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we have introduced KnowHalu, a novel framework for detecting hallucinations in text
532
      generated by Large Language Models (LLMs). Our approach stands out by employing a two-phase
533
      process: off-target Hallucination Checking and Factual Checking, which includes multi-form knowl-
534   edge retrieval and optimization, along with an aggregation method for the final judgment. Through
535   extensive experimentation on standard datasets, KnowHalu has demonstrated significant improve-
536   ments over state-of-the-art baselines in detecting hallucinations in both QA and text summarization
537   tasks. While the current implementation has limitations in handling extended dialogues and much
538   longer responses, specifically the lack of correlation consideration between sentences within a single
539   response, future extensions will focus on adapting the framework for dialogue systems and optimizing
      it for longer interactions.

949   E.3   AGGREGATION
950
951   In this section, we detail the selection of thresholds δ1 and δ2 , which is crucial for improving
952   the performance of aggregating judgments based on different forms of knowledge. Notably, the
953   confidence distribution for judgments, e.g., “INCORRECT” or “CORRECT,” varies due to differences
954   in both the prompts and the reasoning processes used for different forms of knowledge. Therefore, to
955   better accurately gauge the confidence levels, instead of testing different absolute values for exploring
956   the best setting, we leverage the quantile of the confidence distribution for each form of knowledge
957
      to identify these two values. Once they are identified, we will use the Algorithm 1 to make the
      aggregated judgment.
958
959   Therefore, we sample a small validation subset of 100 cases with both non-hallucinated and halluci-
960   nated answers for both the QA and text summarization tasks from HaluEval, separate from our test
961   dataset, to collect confidence distributions for the judgments based on each form. This enables the
962   determination of δ1 and δ2 by exploring various quantiles of these distributions; for instance, if the
963
      base judgment relies on “Structured Knowledge” and the supplementary judgment on “Unstructured
      Knowledge”, we will adjust δ1 across quantiles q1 from 0.05 to 0.95 with a step size of 0.05 of the
964
      confidence distribution collected for structured knowledge; and δ2 is set to a corresponding higher
965
      quantile q2 , starting with the value q1 (the relative confidence level for the supplementary judgment
966   must be higher) and also incrementing by steps of 0.05, up to 0.95. The final pair of quantiles q1
967   and q2 that leads to the best average accuracy will determine the corresponding δ1 and δ2 to be used
968   on our test set. The optimal δ1 and δ2 , with their corresponding quantiles q1 and q2 for each task
969   leading to the best performance, are summarized in Table 21. Notice that the judgments may undergo
970   different tokenizations. Specifically, ‘INCORRECT’ might be tokenized as ‘INC’, ‘OR’, ‘RECT’,
971   whereas ‘CORRECT’ can be tokenized as ‘COR’, ‘RECT’ when using the GPT-3.5. Thus, in our
      experiments, to maintain consistency and mitigate the impact of varying tokenization patterns on
