009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge comprehension capability is an important aspect of human intelli-
012            gence. As Large Language Models (LLMs) are being envisioned as superhuman
013            agents, it is crucial for them to be proficient at knowledge comprehension. How-
014            ever, existing benchmarking studies do not provide consistent, generalizable, and
015            formal guarantees on the knowledge comprehension capabilities of LLMs. In
016
               this work, we propose the first framework to certify knowledge comprehension in
               LLMs with formal probabilistic guarantees. Our certificates are quantitative —
017
               they consist of high-confidence, tight bounds on the probability that a target LLM
018
               gives the correct answer on any knowledge comprehension prompt sampled from
019            a distribution. We design and certify novel specifications that precisely represent
020            distributions of knowledge comprehension prompts leveraging knowledge graphs.
021            We certify SOTA LLMs for specifications over the Wikidata5m knowledge graph.
022            We find that knowledge comprehension improves with increasing model size.
023

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated human-level performance for several real-world
028
      tasks (Street et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023b; Bommasani et al., 2022; Harrison, 2024). An impor-
      tant use case for LLMs is knowledge comprehension (Lazaridou et al., 2022; Khattab et al., 2023),
029
      that is, they are often used to summarize long texts and webpages (Perplexity, 2023; Nakano et al.,
030
      2022), respond to user queries based on the context (Yang et al., 2018), and serve as adaptive task de-
031   composers for reasoning-based retrieval augmented generation tasks (Yao et al., 2023b). Knowledge
032   comprehension involves answering questions by extracting relevant information from large, unstruc-
033   tured texts and reasoning with it. Large context windows of millions of tokens in models like Gemini
034   v1.5 (Gemini Team, 2024) reduce reliance on large knowledge corpora of RAG systems and para-
035   metric knowledge held by LLMs. Users increasingly provide extensive references during inference
036   to guide responses. This makes analyzing the knowledge-comprehension and reasoning capabilities
037   of popular LLMs crucial. Moreover, knowledge comprehension is considered a basic evaluation
038   of language understanding in human learners, according to the Bloom’s taxonomy (Bloom, 1956).
039
      Students are tested on knowledge comprehension tasks at all levels of school education (National
      Center for Education Statistics, 2024). Standardized tests such as TOEFL (Educational Testing Ser-
040
      vice, 2024) and IELTS (IDP IELTS, 2024) contain knowledge comprehension as entire assessments.
041
      As LLMs are envisioned to become superhuman agents (Xi et al., 2023), it is imperative to formally
042   analyze them on tasks on which humans are extensively tested, like knowledge comprehension.
043
044   There are several benchmarking studies on the performance of LLMs for knowledge comprehen-
045
      sion (Liang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023a; Trivedi et al.,
      2022; Tang & Yang, 2024). Several of these studies use multi-hop question-answering datasets that
046
      consist of questions requiring several sequential reasoning steps to obtain the final correct answer.
047
      Thus, benchmarking knowledge comprehension often involves analyzing whether the target LLM
048   can combine multiple pieces of information in meaningful ways and reason its way to the correct
049   answer in the prompt, without deviating or hallucinating. However, the empirical nature of prior
050   studies results in inconsistency in their observations (Wei et al., 2023b; Olsson et al., 2022; Shi
051   et al., 2024). Moreover, while they can convey some high-level trends in the performance of popular
052   LLMs, the results are devoid of any formal guarantees on their applicability. Such guarantees are
053   crucial when deploying LLMs in large-scale knowledge-comprehension tasks in critical domains
      such as medicine or finance, as they give more confidence about the trustworthiness of LLMs before

054
      deployment. Our work aims to bridge this gap by introducing a novel formal certification method,
055   QuaCer-C1 , for obtaining certified bounds on the knowledge comprehension capability of LLMs.
056
057   Key challenges. We face the following challenges when developing a formal certification frame-
058
      work for knowledge comprehension in LLMs. (1) We need formal representations capturing the
      knowledge comprehension property, amenable to certification. Such representations should pre-
059
      cisely capture large, diverse sets of prompts (created by varying questions, supporting texts, etc.)
060
      for knowledge comprehension and their correct responses. (2) Failure examples where the desirable
061   property does not hold are fairly easy to construct for LLMs by appropriate prompt tuning (Xu et al.,
062   2024; Vega et al., 2023), making binary certificates that indicate whether an LLM satisfies a speci-
063   fication trivially false. (3) Giving provable guarantees on LLMs is a hard, open problem, due to the
064   high number of model parameters and nonlinearities (Zhang et al., 2024), for which the traditional
065   certifiers (Singh et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2020; Bonaert et al., 2021) would lose significant precision
066   leading to inconclusive analysis. The number of prompts over which we desire the target LLMs to
067   reason correctly is also large, making enumeration-based methods for formal guarantees infeasible.
068   Our approach. We formalize knowledge comprehension as a novel specification using knowledge
069   graphs. Instead of specifying correctness of LLM responses for all prompts in any given set, we
070   specify a quantitative property, which is the probability of correct response for any knowledge com-
071   prehension prompt sampled from a distribution, developed using a given knowledge graph. We pro-
072   pose a black-box quantitative certification approach, QuaCer-C, which circumvents the issues that
073   traditional approaches have with the number of parameters in LLMs and can even work for closed-
074   source API-access LLMs. QuaCer-C generates high-confidence bounds on the quantitative property
075
      using queries, leveraging binomial proportion confidence intervals (Clopper & Pearson, 1934). Es-
      timating with bounds is beneficial as they also account for the uncertainty in the estimation. While
076
      formal analysis has been conducted on individual generations of LLMs in prior work (Quach et al.,
077
      2024), there is no analysis for the average-case risk of LLMs in knowledge comprehension. Our
078   certificates contain provable bounds on the probability of getting correct responses for any random
079   knowledge comprehension task sampled from the distributions given in the specifications.
080
081
      Contributions. We make the following contributions:
082
               1. We specify the knowledge comprehension property desirable from the LLM responses as
083
                  a formal specification. Our specifications use popular knowledge graphs such as Wiki-
084               data5m (Wang et al., 2021) that are augmented with supporting information about each of
085               their entities. The specifications represent a large set of knowledge comprehension prompts
086               with their respective correct answers expected from any target LLM.
087
               2. We model certification in a target LLM as a probability estimation problem and lever-
088
                  age Clopper-Pearson confidence intervals to generate provable, high-confidence bounds

                  on the quantitative property of interest. Our implementation is provided at https:
090               //anonymous.4open.science/r/QuaCer_CAnon-4130.

092            3. We generate the proposed certificates for the popular LLMs: Llama-3, Mistral, Phi-3, GPT-

                  4o, and Gemini-1.5-Pro. We observe that as the number of model parameters increases, the

097   of LLMs, to ameliorate the caution needed when using LLMs (Shanahan, 2023) in a systematic way.
098   We anticipate it to go a long way in making LLMs trustworthy for deployment in critical domains.

      2.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
121
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are autoregressive causal language models that operate on a vo-
122   cabulary V, a set of tokens. LLMs takes a sequence of tokens x1 , ..., xn where xi ∈ V, n > 0
123   and outputs a probability distribution over V for the potential next token xn+1 . These models are
124   typically pretrained on vast corpora of text data (Liu et al., 2024) and have shown remarkable capa-
125   bilities (Touvron et al., 2023; Gemini Team, 2024; OpenAI, 2024). Numerous benchmarks (Yang
126   et al., 2018; Rein et al., 2023; Hendrycks et al., 2021) have been developed to evaluate the perfor-
127   mance of LLMs on tasks related to multi-step reasoning, knowledge comprehension and question
128   answering. However, there remains a gap in our theoretical understanding of LLMs’ capabilities.
129
130   2.3   I NFORMATION EXTRACTION AND REASONING
131
      Information extraction (IE) and reasoning are important research problems in natural language
132
      processing. IE involves “extracting structured information from unstructured or semi-structured
133
      data” (Chen et al., 2022) such as textual documents. Examples of IE are event extraction (Wadden
134   et al., 2019) and relationship extraction (Pawar et al., 2017). Reasoning is the ability of a model to
135   connect multiple facts using correct logical operations to arrive at a final answer (Huang & Chang,
136   2023). Typically, reasoning capabilities of LLMs are enhanced by using techniques such as Chain of
137   Thought reasoning and its variants (Wei et al., 2023a; Yao et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b), using
138   world models (Hao et al., 2023), etc. It is evaluated in several tasks such as planning (Wang et al.,
139   2024), mathematical reasoning (Imani et al., 2023), commonsense reasoning (Zhao et al., 2023), etc.
140
141   3     C ERTIFYING KNOWLEDGE COMPREHENSION
142
143   Knowledge comprehension is the ability of a model to accurately reason through a multi-hop ques-
144   tion (Yang et al., 2018) (combination of multiple simple information extraction questions that should
145   be answered sequentially to arrive at the final answer) and extract the answers to intermediate ques-
146   tions from information provided in its prompt to reach the correct final answer. Thus, knowledge
147   comprehension is a combination of reasoning and information extraction. Figure 2 gives an overview
148   of our certification framework, QuaCer-C. We formally define knowledge comprehension using a
149   knowledge graph G = (N , E) (Section 2.1) next. Our framework is agnostic to the internal structure
150   of the target model L which can be any text-to-text generating model.

      3.2   C ERTIFICATION METHOD
271
272   Our algorithm certifies the target LLM L by computing an interval [pl , pu ] containing the value
273   of the probability p (Algorithm 1, line 6) for a given pivot node v1 in G with high confi-
274   dence. We model p as the probability of setting the underlying boolean random variable R ≜
275   (any(L(P) == Π[−1]A )) to true (success). Thus, R ∼ Ber(p). Exactly determining p would
276   require enumerating over all possible P which can be developed from any path from a subgraph of
277   G with any random aliases, resulting in an infeasible number of possible prompts, as shown in Ap-
278   pendix B.6. Moreover, we want our method to generalize to closed-source LLMs as well, where the
279   internal structures of the models are unknown. Hence, we cannot use any symbolic methods (Mir-
280   man et al., 2020) to determine p. Thus, to scalably certify the black-box target LM L, we estimate
281
      p with provably high-confidence (low error) bounds. Confidence is defined as the probability of the
      true value p being within the bounds, i.e., P r[p ∈ [pl , pu ]]. To establish formal guarantees, we want
282
      our estimation procedure to be such that the actual confidence is at least the user-specified confi-
283
      dence level, 1 − δ (i.e., P r[pl ≤ p ≤ pu ] ≥ 1 − δ), where δ is a small positive constant. Hence
284   we use the conservative method of Clopper-Pearson confidence intervals (Clopper & Pearson, 1934;
285   Brown et al., 2001; Kurz et al., 2014), which is known to produce intervals that are guaranteed
286   to have high confidence. To compute high-confidence bounds on p, we make n independent and
287   identically distributed observations of R, in which we obtain k successes, k ∈ [0, n]. We generate
288   Clopper-Pearson confidence intervals with the n observations to bound p with 1 − δ confidence.

291   4     E XPERIMENTS
292
293   We certify the following open-source, instruction finetuned (Wei et al., 2022a) models — Llama-3-
294   instruct 8B model (Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral 7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023a), Phi-3 3B and
295   14B parameter models (Abdin et al., 2024). We also certify 4-bit and 8-bit quantized versions of the
296   open-source models to study the effects of quantization on a model’s knowledge comprehension ca-
297
      pabilities. Among the closed-source models with API access, we certify Gemini-1.5 (Gemini Team,
      2024) Flash-001 and Pro-002 models and GPT-4o-0827 (OpenAI, 2024).
298
299   We use Wikidata5m (Wang et al., 2021) as our knowledge graph after preprocessing (check Ap-
300   pendix B.1.1 for details). To generate challenging and diverse specifications, we sample 50 pivot
301   nodes from two populations: the top 2000 nodes by out-degree in the global graph, and nodes whose
302   subgraph within radius ρ contains at least 2000 vertices. This strategy ensures specifications rooted
303
      around any of the pivot nodes have a large number of paths, making enumerative certification (where
      all possibilities are tested for satisfaction of the specification) impractical. Note that QuaCer-C is
304
      not limited to such subgraphs, and owing to their challenges in terms of prohibitively large number
305
      of possibilities, we select them, only for illustration purposes. We set the maximum path length
306   parameter as ρ = 5, as we empirically observe that longer paths could result in queries that are very
307   unrelated to the head node of the path. As our certificates are over all paths with lengths at most ρ
308   in a given subgraph, we equally prioritize the different possible path lengths in [1, ρ], even though
309   paths with longer lengths can be fewer in number than those with shorter lengths. Hence, we define
310   our sampler from our distribution over paths (Algorithm 1, line 2) which first selects a path length
311   from the uniform distribution over the integers [1, ρ]. We then sample a path of the chosen length
312   from a uniform distribution over all paths of that length in the subgraph. This ensures that each
313   possible path length, and each path of a given length, has equal probability of selection, preventing
314

      the LLM’s ability to correctly identify the terminal entity (‘?’) given the starting entity (‘Chandler
      Bing’) and the specified relational path. Additional details and figures provided in Appendix B.2.2.

323   prompt includes a query and relevant context, presented as a multiple-choice question. This format
      allows for straightforward evaluation of LLM responses using string matching (Appendix B.7). Fur-

345   4.1   D IFFERENT KINDS OF SPECIFICATIONS
346
347    We study the certificates for 3 different kinds of specifications that arise from variations in the
348
      construction of context in the prompts to LLMs (Algorithm 1, line 4) — with context shuffling
      and distractor context (Shuffle Distractor), with context shuffling and without distractor context
349
      (Shuffle), and without context shuffling and without distractor context (Vanilla). These settings
350
      enable us to study the effects of these operations on the knowledge provided in LLM prompts. When
351   distractor context is provided, it is only for 1 distractor node, so as to fit the relevant context for the
352   nodes on the considered path within the context windows of the target LLMs. We hypothesize that
353   distractors to nodes later in the path, closer to the tail node which consists of the final answer, would
354   be more challenging for the LLM due to their proximity and similarity to the answer node. Our
355   distractor node sampler (Appendix B.3, Algorithm 4) thus employs a weighted sampling approach
356   to prioritize distractor nodes closer to the path’s tail. We provide an ablation study on the effects of
357   varying the distribution of distractor nodes in Appendix A.

513   5   R ELATED WORKS
514
515   In-context learning. As LLM context windows increase (Gemini Team, 2024; Chen et al., 2023;
516   Dubey et al., 2024), more information can be provided in the prompts like few-shot demonstra-
517   tions (Brown et al., 2020) and examples from related tasks (Qu et al., 2024). In-context learning is
518   the emergent behavior (Wei et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2024) in which LLMs become proficient at a
519   task with demonstrations in prompts. We use in-context learning and few-shot examples to enhance
520
      LLMs’ knowledge and reasoning capabilities.
521   Benchmarking LLM intelligence. Several benchmarks have been proposed to study the reason-
522   ing (Zhou et al., 2022; Huang & Chang, 2023; Plaat et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Zha et al., 2021),
523   arithmetic (Yuan et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a), planning (Pallagani et al.,
524   2023; Valmeekam et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022), and question-answering (Yang et al., 2018; Ho
525   et al., 2020; Welbl et al., 2018) capabilities of LLMs, which are integral components of human in-
526
      telligence. These benchmarks provide empirical insights and trends into the performance of LMs.
      However, these insights are generally for static datasets and are not guaranteed to generalize. On
527
      the other hand, certification methods provide guarantees on, for example, the scope (defined by
528
      specifications) and confidence of its claims, as we illustrate in this work.
529
530
531   6   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
532
533
      We present a novel framework to formally certify LLMs for knowledge comprehension. We develop
534   novel specifications that quantify the probability of correct responses over any random knowledge
535   comprehension prompts from distributions derived from knowledge graphs. Certificates consist of
536   high-confidence bounds on the probability of correct knowledge comprehension, thus providing a
537   method to compare different LLMs with formal guarantees. Our experiments show variations in
538   knowledge comprehension along the axes of model size, quantization, and task difficulty. Future
539   work can integrate our framework with knowledge graph construction methods (Ye et al., 2022), to
      specify and certify LLMs for comprehension and reasoning over less structured/proprietary inputs.

1089   This section details our experimental setup for generating multi-hop reasoning queries using the
1090   Wikidata5m knowledge graph. We describe the structure of the knowledge graph, the process of

1093   B.1     K NOWLEDGE G RAPH S TRUCTURE
1094
1095
       Our experiments are based on the Wikidata5m knowledge graph (KG). The KG has the following
       key characteristics:
1096
1097           • Nodes: Each node represents an entity and is associated with a text paragraph from Wiki-
1098             data5m.
1099
               • Edges: Edges represent relationships between entities.
1100
1101
               • Text Paragraphs: The text associated with each node may contain information relevant to
                 its connected edges.
1102
1103           • Node and Edge Aliases: Each node and each edge has a set of aliases associated with them
1104
                 which are just different names for them.
1105

       B.1.1    P REPROCESSING THE WIKIDATA 5 M KNOWLEDGE GRAPH
1108
1109    To ensure the generation of unambiguous queries and support the certification process, we prepro-
1110   cess the wikidata5m dataset.
1111
1112
             1. Relation Filtering: We remove relations such as ’instance of’, ’subclass of’, and ’part of’
                due to their inherent potential for ambiguity in query formulation.
1113
1114         2. Relevant Information Extraction for edges: To ensure the relevance of relationships in
1115
                the knowledge graph, we require textual evidence for each edge. When entity A is related to
                entity B, we identify specific sentences in the descriptive text of either entity that explicitly
1116
                mention any alias of the other entity. We assume these sentences support the relationship’s
1117
                existence. These sentences are then linked to the edge, providing context that can be used
1118            to answer queries about the relationship. This approach ensures that the knowledge graph
1119            contains valid relationships and the specific text that justifies them, enhancing the available
1120            context for further analysis. If we find no supporting text for an edge, we drop that edge
1121            from the knowledge graph.
1122         3. Unicode to ASCII:For consistency within our experiments, we convert all text containing
1123            Unicode characters into their respective ASCII approximations.

1131   B.2.1    R ANDOM PATH G ENERATION
1132
1133   We begin by selecting a pivot node v0 in the knowledge graph G. From this pivot, we construct
       a local subgraph G(v0 ) consisting of all paths Πv0 originating from v. This local subgraph serves

1134
       as the domain for our path generation process. As arbitrary long paths can lose their semantic
1135   meaningfulness, we use a constraint ρ to restrict the length of paths from the pivot node in the
1136   subgraph to be maximum ρ.
1137
1138
       Within G(v0 ), we generate a path Π using a randomized depth-first search algorithm. The length
       of this path, denoted as kchoice , is sampled randomly from the set 1, 2, ..., ρ according to a discrete
1139
       uniform distribution.
1140
1141   This randomized depth-first search traverses the neighbors of each node in G(v0 ) in a random order,
1142   which directly corresponds to the sampling process described in line 10 of Algorithm 1. Specifically,
1143   at each step, we sample the next node in the path from a discrete uniform distribution over the current
1144
       node’s neighbors within the local subgraph, expressed as ∼ (D([v ′ | (vi , v ′ ) ∈ E ∧ v ′ ∈ G(v0 )])),
       where vi is the current node in the path.
1145
1146   To ensure well-defined queries with unique answers, we introduce an additional constraint on path
1147   generation. This constraint requires that each generated path be unique in terms of its sequence
1148   of relationships. Specifically, traversing the path from the initial node using the specified relations
1149   must lead to a single, unambiguous answer node. This approach prevents queries with multiple
1150
       valid answers, which would complicate the evaluation of the language model’s performance. It’s
       important to note that this uniqueness constraint applies only to the specific path being generated.
1151
       Nodes within the path may still have multiple edges with the same relation type to other entities
1152
       not on the path. This allowance maintains the natural complexity of the knowledge graph structure,
1153   where entities can have multiple relationships of the same type with different entities.
1154

1171
       Once a valid path Π is generated, we convert it into a query string. This process aligns with line
1172
       11 in Algorithm 1. The query is constructed by sampling aliases for each node and relation in the
1173   path. For example, a path Π = [A, B, C] might be converted to a query ”sampled alias(A) →
1174   sampled alias((A, B)) → sampled alias(B) → sampled alias((B, C)) → ?”. Here the tuple of two
1175   nodes represents their edge. The aliases are sampled randomly from a discrete uniform distribution
1176   over the available aliases for a node or an edge.

1231   B.3.1    D ISTRACTOR S ELECTION
1232
1233
       Distractors are crucial in assessing an LLM’s comprehension and reasoning abilities. We hypoth-
       esize that distractors to nodes later in the path closer to the answer would provide more difficulty
1234
       for the LLM due to their proximity to the answer node. Our distractor selection process, imple-
1235
       mented in Algorithm 4, employs a weighted sampling approach to prioritize distractors associated
1236   with entities closer to the path’s end. The algorithm first identifies all potential distractors for each
1237   node in the path, then assigns weights inversely proportional to their distance from the final answer
1238   node. This weighting scheme favors distractors linked to nodes near the path’s end, but still allows
1239   for the selection of distractors related to any node in the path. This refers to the sampling procedure
1240   in the line 12 of the probabilistic progam specification 1. By performing weighted sampling from
1241   this pool, we ensure a balance between highly relevant distractors and a diverse selection across the
       entire path.

       B.4     C ONTEXT T RIMMING
1290
1291    To address the input length limitations of various LLMs, we implement a context trimming proce-
1292   dure. Including all text associated with each node in a reasoning path can result in excessively long
1293   contexts. Our procedure aims to preserve the most relevant information from the knowledge graph
1294   and supporting texts while respecting each model’s maximum input length. This involves identify-
1295   ing relevant sentences per edge in the graph and then trimming the context for each query based on
       this information.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       B.4.1   F INDING R ELEVANT S ENTENCES PER E DGE
1297
1298   Each node in the Wikidata5m knowledge graph has associated textual support for its relations. We
1299   utilize this textual information to provide query-relevant context. We need to determine the relevant
1300   information from the textual supports for each edge as this would help us trim the contexts accord-
1301   ingly. For each edge (u, v) in the knowledge graph used in the query or answer options generation,
1302
       we perform the following steps:
1303
            1. Collect Aliases and Text: We gather aliases and the associated text paragraphs for both
1304
               nodes u and v.
1305
1306        2. Split into Sentences: We split the text paragraphs of u and v into individual sentences
1307
               using NLTK.
1308        3. Identify Relevant Sentences: We identify sentences that explicitly link the two nodes. A
1309           sentence from u’s text is considered relevant if it contains an alias of v, and vice versa.
1310        4. Discard Edges without Relevant Sentences: If no relevant sentences are found for an
1311           edge, it is deemed unsupported and is discarded from the graph.
1312        5. Prepend First Sentence: To ensure the entity’s primary name or common alias is included,
1313           we prepend the first sentence of each node’s text to its list of relevant sentences.
1314
1315   B.4.2   T RIMMING TO F IT C ONTEXT L ENGTH
1316
1317   When constructing the final prompt for the LLM, we prioritize including the most relevant informa-
1318
       tion within the model’s context length limit. Therefore we need to trim the context according to the
       LLM’s context limit. We use the following procedure (detailed in Algorithm B.4.2):
1319
1320        1. Create Sentence Lists: We create three lists of sentences:
1321
                   • Sall : Contains all sentences from the text paragraphs of nodes involved in the query
1322
                     and answer options.
1323
                   • Squery : Contains all relevant sentences for the edges that constitute the query path.
1324
1325               • Soptions : Contains all relevant sentences for the edges used to generate the answer
1326
                     options.
1327        2. Construct the Final Context:
1328            (a) We prioritize including all sentences from Squery as they are directly related to the
1329                query.
1330            (b) Next, we add as many sentences from Soptions as possible, given the remaining con-
1331                text length limit.
1332            (c) Finally, we fill the remaining space with sentences from Sall that have not been in-
1333                cluded yet, ensuring no sentence is repeated.
