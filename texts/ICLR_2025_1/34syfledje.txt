                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               In machine learning, quantization is widely used to simplify data representation
012
               and facilitate algorithm deployment on hardware. Considering the fundamental
013            role of classification in machine learning, it is imperative to investigate the im-
014            pact of quantization on classification. Current research primarily revolves around
015            quantization errors, under the assumption that higher quantization errors generally
016            lead to lower classification performance. However, this assumption lacks a solid
017            theoretical foundation, and often contradicts empirical findings. For instance,
018            some extremely low bit-width quantization methods, such as {0, 1}-binary quanti-
019            zation and {0, ±1}-ternary quantization, can achieve comparable or even superior
020            classification accuracy compared to the original non-quantized data, despite ex-
021
               hibiting high quantization errors. To evaluate the classification performance more
               accurately, we propose to directly investigate the feature discrimination of quan-
022
               tized data, rather than analyze its quantization error. It is found that both binary
023
               and ternary quantization methods can surprisingly improve, rather than degrade,
024            the feature discrimination of original data. This remarkable performance is vali-
025            dated through classification experiments on diverse data types, including images,
026            speech and text.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Quantization has been widely applied in machine learning to simplify data storage and computation
032   complexities, while also catering to the requirements of algorithm deployment on digital hardware.
033   In general, this operation will lead to a decrease in classification accuracy (Baras & Dey, 1999;
034   Hoefler et al., 2021), due to reducing the precision of data or model parameters. To achieve a
035   balance between complexity and accuracy, it is crucial to delve into the impact of quantization
036   on classification. Currently, the impact is mainly evaluated through quantization errors, with the
037   assumption that larger quantization errors generally lead to decreased classification accuracy (Lin
038   et al., 2016a). However, this assumption lacks a solid theoretical basis (Lin et al., 2016a), as it
039
      merely adopts the quantization principle from signal processing (Gray & Neuhoff, 1998), which
      primarily focuses on data reconstruction fidelity rather than classification accuracy. In practice, it
040
      seems challenging to accurately assess the classification performance solely based on quantization
041
      errors.
042
043   For instance, it has been observed that some extremely low bit-width quantization methods, such
044   as the {0, 1}-binary quantization and {0, ±1}-ternary quantization, which have been successively
045
      applied large-scale retrieval (Charikar, 2002) and deep network quantization (Qin et al., 2020; Gho-
      lami et al., 2022), can achieve comparable or even superior classification performance than their
046
      full-precision counterparts (Courbariaux et al., 2015; Lin et al., 2016b; Lu et al., 2023), despite
047
      suffering from high quantization errors. Apparently, the remarkable classification improvement re-
048   sulting from quantization should not be attributed to the high quantization errors. This reveals the
049   inadequacy of quantization errors in assessing the actual classification performance. Due to the ab-
050   sence of a theoretical explanation, the classification improvement induced by quantization has often
051   been regarded as incidental and received little attention. Instead of quantization errors, in the paper
052   we demonstrate that this intriguing phenomenon can be reasonably explained by feature discrimi-
053   nation. Following the Fisher’s linear discriminant analysis (Fisher, 1936), we here refer to feature
      discrimination as the ratio between inter-class and intra-class scatters, and evaluate the classification

054
      performance based on the rule that the higher the feature discrimination, the easier the classification.
055   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study that exploits feature discrimination to analyze the
056   impact of quantization on classification, although it is more direct and reasonable than quantization
057   errors in evaluating classification performance. The scarcity of relevant research can be attributed to
058   the nonlinearity of the quantization operation, which substantially increases the analytical complex-
059   ity of feature discrimination functions.
060
      In the paper, it is demonstrated that the impact of the threshold-based binary and ternary quanti-
061   zation on feature discrimination can be analyzed, when the data are appropriately modeled using
062   a Gaussian mixture model, with each Gaussian element representing one class of data. The Gaus-
063   sian mixture model is chosen here based on two considerations. Firstly, the model has been well-
064   established for approximating the distributions of real-world data (Torralba & Oliva, 2003; Weiss &
065   Freeman, 2007) and their feature transformations (Wainwright & Simoncelli, 1999; Lam & Good-
066   man, 2000). Secondly, the closure property of Gaussian distributions under linear operations can
067   simplify the analysis of the feature discrimination function. By analyzing the discrimination across
068   varying quantization thresholds, it is found that there exist certain quantization thresholds that can
069
      improve the discrimination of original data, thereby yielding improved classification performance.
      This finding is extensively validated through classification experiments both on synthetic and real
070
      data.
071
072   The related works are discussed as follows. As mentioned earlier, our work should be the first to
073   take advantage of feature discrimination to investigate the impact of quantization on classification.
074   In the filed of signal processing, there have been a few works proposed to reduce the negative impact
075
      of quantization on signal detection or classification (Poor & Thomas, 1977; Oehler & Gray, 1995).
      However, these studies did not employ feature discrimination analysis, distinguishing them from
076
      our research in both methodology and outcomes. Specifically, in these studies the model design
077
      accounts for both reconstruction loss and classification loss. The classification loss is primarily
078   modeled in several ways, such as directly minimizing the classification error on quantized data
079   (Srinivasamurthy & Ortega, 2002), enlarging the inter-class distance between quantized data (Jana
080   & Moulin, 2000; 2003), reducing the difference between the distributions of quantized data and
081   original data (Baras & Dey, 1999), as well as minimizing the discrepancy in classification before
082   and after quantization (Dogahe & Murthi, 2011). Through analyses of these losses, the classification
083   performance of quantized data can only approach, rather than surpass, the performance of original
084   data (Baras & Dey, 1999).

      In this part, we conduct numerical analyses for two primary objectives. Firstly, we aim to prove the
      existence of the desired quantization threshold τ that holds Equations (8) and (9), namely making
245
      the left sides of the two inequalities larger than their right sides (with values equal to zero). For this
246
      purpose, we compute the values of the left sides of Equations (8) and (9), through assigning specific
247   values to τ , as well as to the two variables X and Y ’s distribution parameters µ and σ 2 . Note that we
248   here set σ 2 = 1 − µ2 , µ ∈ (0, 1), in accordance with Property 1. In Figure 1, we examine the case
249   that fixes µ = 0.8 and σ 2 = 0.36, while varying the value of τ with a step width 0.01. The results
250   for binary quantization and ternary quantization are provided in Figures 1 (a) and (c), respectively.
251   It can be seen that for the two quantization methods, their conditions shown in Equations (8) and
252   (9) will hold when respectively having τ ∈ [−0.2, 0.2] and τ ∈ [0, 0.5]. This proves the existence
253   of the desired quantization threshold τ that can improve feature discrimination. For limited space,
254   we here only discuss the case of µ = 0.8 (and σ 2 = 1 − µ2 ) in Figure 1. By examining different
255
      µ ∈ (0, 1) in the same way, we can find that the quantization threshold τ that holds Equations
      (8) and (9), is present when µ ∈ (0.76, 1) and µ ∈ (0.66, 1), respectively; see Figures 7 and 8
256
      for more evidences. This result implies two consequences. On one hand, ternary quantization has
257
      more chances to improve feature discrimination compared to binary quantization, as the former has
258   a broader range of µ. On the other hand, the improved discrimination tends to be achieved when µ is
259   sufficiently large, coupled with a correspondingly small σ, or when the discrimination between two
260   classes of data is sufficiently high. Empirically, as depicted in Figure 17, the two specific ranges of
261   µ values are attainable for the commonly-used features of real data.
262

274   4     E XPERIMENTS
275
276   Through previous theoretical and numerical analyses, we have demonstrated that binary and ternary
277   quantization can improve feature discrimination between two classes of data, when the data vectors
278   within each class exhibit Gaussian distributions across each coordinate point of their feature vec-
279   tors. Given that improved feature discrimination should yield better classification performance, this
280
      section aims to validate this improvement by assessing classification results.
281   Our experiments will mainly investigate binary classification using two fundamental linear classi-
282   fiers: the k-nearest neighbors (KNN) algorithm (with k = 5) (Peterson, 2009), employing both
283   Euclidean and cosine distances as similarity metrics, and the support vector machine (SVM) (Cortes
284   & Vapnik, 1995), equipped with a linear kernel. We choose linear classifiers for binary classifica-
285   tion based on two considerations. Firstly, linear binary classification can directly reflect the feature
286
      discrimination between two classes, unlike more complex nonlinear classifiers that often involve
      feature selection operations. Secondly, linear binary classification is a foundational concept in ma-
287
      chine learning. The insights gained from this analysis can be extended to multiclass and nonlinear
288
      classifier-based classifications, as evidenced in the subsequent experiments.
289
290   To assess the robustness and generalizability of our theoretical findings, we will conduct classi-
291   fication evaluations on both synthetic and real data. Synthetic data can conform perfectly to the
292
      distribution conditions outlined in our theoretical analysis, whereas real data usually cannot.
293

300   accordance with the data distributions specified in Property 1. Considering the fact that the features
301   of real-world data usually exhibit sparse structures (Weiss & Freeman, 2007; Kotz et al., 2012),

      not favorable for quantization to improve feature discrimination, as indicated by previous numerical

      the parameter λ.

      The datasets are briefly introduced as follows. YaleB contains face images of 38 persons, with about
400
      64 faces per person. CIFAR10 consists of 60,000 color images from 10 different classes, with each
401   class having 6,000 images. ImageNet1000 consists of 1000 object classes, with 1,281,167 training
402   images, 50,000 validation images, and 100,000 test images. For the above three image datasets,
403   we separately extract their features using Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT), ResNet18 (He et al.,
404   2016) and VGG16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014). For ease of simulation, the resulting feature
405   vectors are dimensionally reduced by integer multiples, leading to the sizes of 1200, 5018, and
406   5018 respectively. From TIMIT, as in (Mohamed et al., 2011; Hutchinson et al., 2012), we extract
407   39 classes of 429-dimensional phoneme features for classification, totally with 1,134,138 training
408   samples and 58,399 test samples. Newsgroup comprises 20 categories of texts, with 11,269 samples
409
      for training, and 7,505 samples for testing. The feature dimension is reduced to 5000 by selecting
      the top 5000 most frequent words in the bag of words, as done in (Larochelle et al., 2012).
410
411   For each dataset, we iterate through all possible class pairs to perform binary classification. The
412   samples for training and testing are selected according to the default settings of the datasets. For
413   YaleB without prior settings, we randomly assign half of the samples for training and the remaining
414   half for testing. In the simulation, we need to test the classification performance of quantized data
415
      across varying quantization threshold τ . The value of τ should correlate with the element scale of
      the feature vectors, in the pursuit of improving classification over orginal data. To address the scale
416
      varying of τ across different data, we here suppose that τ = γ · η, where η denotes the average
417
      magnitude of the feature elements (coordinates) in all the feature vectors used for classification, and
418   γ is a scaling parameter. By adjusting γ within a narrow range, as illustrated later, we can derive the
419   desired τ for various types of data.
420
421
      To verify the generalizability of our feature discrimination analysis between two classes, we not
      only evaluate binary classification using KNN and SVM, but also conduct multiclass classification,
422
      as well as nonlinear classification using multilayer perceptron (MLP) (Rumelhart et al., 1986) and
423
      decision trees (Quinlan, 1986). Due to space limitations, in the main body, we present the classifi-
424   cation results of YaleB, Newsgroup, and TIMIT using KNN with Euclidean distance and SVM, as
425   illustrated in Figures 4 to 6. The results for other datasets, such as CIFAR10 and ImageNet1000,
426   and other classifiers, including KNN with cosine distance, MLP and decision trees, are provided in
427   the appendix, but briefly discussed within the main text.

      5   C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In the paper, we have proposed utilizing feature discrimination to analyze the impact of quantiza-
531
      tion on classification. Unlike traditional analyses, which are primarily based on quantization errors,
      our feature discrimination-based approach offers a more direct and reasoned assessment of clas-
532
      sification performance. Through our analysis, we demonstrate that common binary and ternary
533
      quantization methods can improve the feature discrimination of original data, particularly when data
534   vectors within the same class follow Gaussian distributions at each coordinate. This improved dis-
535   crimination is validated through binary classification experiments on both synthetic and real data.
536   While our feature discrimination analysis primarily focuses on linear, binary classification issues,
537   our experiments indicate that the findings can be extended to multiclass and nonlinear classification
538   scenarios. This underscores the broad applicability of our theoretical results. Importantly, our study
539   challenges the traditional belief that larger quantization errors generally lead to lower classification
      performance, laying a theoretical foundation for developing better quantization methods.

1281   to improve binary classification can also be extended to multiclass classification, provided that the
