009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Throughout its lifecycle, a large language model (LLM) generates a substantially
012            larger carbon footprint during inference than training. LLM inference requests
013            vary in batch size, prompt length, and token generation number, while cloud
014            providers employ different GPU types and quantities to meet diverse service-
015            level objectives for accuracy and latency. It is crucial for both users and cloud
016            providers to have a tool that quickly and accurately estimates the carbon impact of
017            LLM inferences based on a combination of inference request and hardware con-
018
               figurations before execution. Estimating the carbon footprint of LLM inferences
               is more complex than training due to lower and highly variable model FLOPS
019
               utilization, rendering previous equation-based models inaccurate. Additionally,
020
               existing machine learning (ML) prediction methods either lack accuracy or de-
021            mand extensive training data, as they inadequately handle the distinct prefill and
022            decode phases, overlook hardware-specific features, and inefficiently sample un-
023            common inference configurations. We introduce LLMCO2 , a graph neural net-
024            work (GNN)-based model that greatly improves the accuracy of LLM inference
025            carbon footprint predictions compared to previous methods.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Large language models (LLMs) (BigScience, 2023; Meta, 2024; Mistral, 2024) have demonstrated
030   high efficacy across various generative Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks, such as code com-
031   pletion (Nam et al., 2024), question-answering (Shao et al., 2023), and text summarization (Pilault
032   et al., 2020). Their integration into daily activities (e.g., web browsing (Campello de Souza et al.,
033   2023)) highlights their increasing prevalence. However, this widespread adoption has led to signif-
034   icant carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2eq) emissions (Luccioni et al., 2024). For instance, training
035   the Google T5 LLM generates 40% more carbon emissions than a round-trip flight between San
036   Francisco and New York (Faiz et al., 2024).
037   Inferences for LLMs can produce an even larger carbon footprint than their initial training. Conser-
038   vative estimates suggest that OpenAI handles over 270 million daily inference requests (Chien et al.,
039   2023), with an average prompt length of 1.2K tokens per request (Patel et al., 2024). Training GPT-4
040   requires approximately 13 trillion tokens (OpenAI, 2024), with a single epoch requiring three times
041   the FLOPs of an inference (Faiz et al., 2024). Consequently, the carbon emissions from 121 days of
042   serving GPT-4 inferences equate to those of its training. As the volume of daily inference requests
043   rises, and with increased adoption of LLMs across various applications (OpenAI, 2024), the period
044
      required for inference emissions to match training emissions is rapidly decreasing.
045   Given the significant environmental impact, it is essential for both end users and cloud providers
046   to understand the carbon emission costs of different service-level objectives (SLOs) (Patel et al.,
047   2024) for LLM inference accuracy and latency. An accurate carbon footprint prediction tool is
048   crucial before initiating inference requests, enabling users to assess trade-offs between accuracy,
049   latency, and carbon emissions. This tool would also help cloud providers justify billing policies
050
      transparently, promoting low-carbon practices among users.
051   However, there is a lack of modeling tools for accurately estimating the carbon footprint of LLM in-
052   ferences. Users submit LLM inference requests with varying configurations (e.g., batch size, prompt
053   length, and token generation number) to cloud services, while cloud providers employ different GPU
      types and quantities to meet diverse SLOs for accuracy and latency. Prior studies (Nguyen et al.,

070   • Lack of autoregressive consideration. Existing tools treat CNN inference as a single task, failing
071     to account for the distinct autoregressive phases of LLMs: the compute-intensive prefill and the
072     memory-bound decode (Patel et al., 2024). This oversight reduces prediction accuracy or neces-

      • Neglect of hardware-specific features. Prior tools overlook critical hardware characteristics such

075
        dictions thus require exhaustive profiling across different GPU platforms (Pope et al., 2023), mak-
076     ing these methods inefficient and less reliable for new hardware.
077   • Disregard for prevalent configurations. Previous tools treat all inference configurations equally,
078     ignoring that most real-world LLM inference requests, such as those in Azure Cloud (Microsoft,
079     2024), feature small batch sizes, shorter prompts, and limited token generation. This approach
080     fails to improve prediction accuracy for typical usage scenarios.
081
      We introduce LLMCO2 , an accurate carbon footprint regression model for LLM inferences.
082   LLMCO2 employs a novel graph embedding technique that represents each transformer layer’s ker-
083   nels as a graph, with nodes indicating kernels and edges capturing data dependencies. Node fea-
084   tures for the prefill and decode phases are encoded separately, incorporating each node’s Roofline
085   performance as a hardware-specific feature. We also develop a focused data sampling algorithm
086   that emphasizes common configurations of inference requests, LLM architectures, and GPU setups.
087   LLMCO2 improves carbon footprint prediction accuracy by 51%-123% over existing ML-based
088   energy predictors for LLMs across diverse inference requests and GPU configurations.

090   2    BACKGROUND
091   Autoregressive LLM Inferences. As shown in Figure 1, during autoregressive inference (Pope
092   et al., 2023) of a decoder-only LLM, all input tokens are processed in parallel during the first it-
093   eration, generating the first token—this is the prefill phase (Patel et al., 2024). The context from
094   the LLM’s attention layers is stored in the key-value (KV) cache for future iterations. Subsequent
095   tokens are then generated using the latest token and the KV cache as inputs, forming the decode
096   phase (Patel et al., 2024).
097   Distinct Characteristics of Two Phases. In autoregressive LLM inference, the prefill phase com-
098   putes and stores context in the KV cache, while the decode phase primarily accesses this cache. The
099   prefill phase is compute-bound, relying on GPU cores, whereas the decode phase is memory-bound,
100   relying on GPU memory. Consequently, the phases differ in latency, energy consumption, and car-
101   bon footprint. As shown in Figure 2, during a Bloom-3b inference (BigScience, 2023) with a batch
102   size of 1 on an Nvidia L4 GPU, the prefill phase’s energy use is negligible for requests with many
103   generated tokens but dominates when fewer tokens are generated. Treating both phases as a single
104
      task without sufficient training data significantly reduces energy prediction accuracy.
105   Kernels in a transformer Layer. As illustrated in Figure 4, a transformer layer (Vaswani et al.,
106   2017) comprises a masked multi-head attention (MHA) layer and a feed-forward (FF) layer framed
107   by normalization layers. In the MHA layer, the attention mechanism is executed via Qproj , Kproj ,
      and Vproj kernels. Techniques like Grouped-Query Attention (Ainslie et al., 2023) and Flash Atten-

140   Roofline model. The Roofline model (Cardwell & Song, 2019) is a performance analysis tool that
141   estimates a kernel’s performance on a GPU, measured in operations per second (OPs/s). It considers
142   factors like peak GPU throughput, peak memory and network bandwidth, and the kernel’s memory-
143
      and network-specific arithmetic intensities. The memory and network Roofline models for an Nvidia
      V100 GPU (Nvidia, 2017) are shown in Figures 5(a) and 5(b), respectively. The X-axes represent
144
      arithmetic intensity, calculated as total kernel operations divided by total memory or network bytes
145
      transferred. A ridge point, or ‘balance’ point, indicates where compute and data movement perfor-
146   mance meet. The Nvidia V100 supports FP32, FP16, and INT8 operations with HBM or GDDR
147   memory, resulting in six ridge points in Figure 5(a). Two network interfaces (NVLink and PCIe)
148   yield two ridge points in Figure 5(b). Kernels with arithmetic intensities below the ridge point are
149   memory- or network-bound, while those above are compute-bound. For instance, in Figure 5(a), the
150   INT8 kernel 0 (K0 ) is compute-bound with HBM but memory-bound with GDDR.

      We compare LLMCO2 with prior work in Table 1. LLMCarbon (Faiz et al., 2024) employs an
179
      equation-based approach using FLOPs to estimate LLM inference carbon footprints, resulting in
180
      inaccuracies. Other models like nn-Meter (Zhang et al., 2021), DeepEn (Tu et al., 2023), and
181   NNLQP (Liu et al., 2023) use random forests or neural networks to predict latency or energy for
182   CNNs and transformers but treat predictions as a single task, overlooking the autoregressive nature
183   of LLM inferences and failing to distinguish between prefill and decode phases. These methods fo-
184   cus solely on architecture-specific features like input sizes and network structures, relying on brute-
185   force sampling across hardware platforms, yet they omit hardware-specific features such as GPU
186   peak throughput, memory bandwidth, and network bandwidth, resulting in reduced accuracy for
187   unseen hardware configurations. Furthermore, they do not address tensor parallelism across multi-
188   ple GPUs and uniformly sample inference configurations, neglecting common settings like medium
189
      prompt lengths and small batch sizes. In contrast, LLMCO2 separates prefill and decode phases,
      incorporates hardware-specific features, supports multi-GPU tensor parallelism, and prioritizes fre-
190
      quently occurring inference configurations, achieving more precise carbon footprint predictions.

      4.1   G RAPH E MBEDDING
231   We introduce a graph embedding technique for LLMCO2 to represent a transformer layer as a di-
232   rected acyclic graph (DAG) of kernels: G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes (kernels), and E
233   denotes the edges (data dependencies between kernels). Each edge’s source kernel supplies input
234
      data to the destination kernel. The key advantages of this technique are: (1) each node has two
      distinct feature sets—one for the prefill phase and the other for the decode phase, and (2) each node
235
      includes a hardware-specific feature, i.e., Roofline performance. Figure 8 provides an example of
236
      this graph embedding for a transformer layer.
237

239   estimate the carbon footprint of the two distinct phases in an LLM inference, each node is assigned
240

      concatenates the following elements:

      5.1 E XPERIMENTAL METHODOLOGY
349
350   Dataset construction. We developed an energy dataset to evaluate the performance of various
351
      energy prediction methods, selecting six LLM series: Bloom (Bloom-560m, Bloom-1b1, Bloom-
      1b7, Bloom-3b, Bloom-7b1) (BigScience, 2023), Gemma (Gemma-2b, Gemma-7b) (Team et al.,
352
      2024a), Gemma2 (Gemma2-2b, Gemma2-9b, Gemma2-27b) (Team et al., 2024b), Qwen2 (Qwen2-
353
      0.5b, Qwen2-1.5b, Qwen2-7b, Qwen2-72b) (Yang et al., 2024), Llama3.1 (Llama3.1-8b, Llama3.1-
354   70b) (Meta, 2024), and Mixtral (Mixtral-8×7b) (Jiang et al., 2024). The LLM architectural design
355   space was explored by varying parameters such as quantization bitwidth, hidden size, intermediate
356   size, head count, and layer count. Public Azure LLM serving traces (Microsoft, 2024) were used to
357   generate inference requests, encompassing two distinct traces: one for chat (19,336 entries) and the
358   other for code completion (8,199 entries), for LLMCO2 . We sampled inference configurations by
359   adjusting input prompt lengths and generated token numbers, ensuring batch sizes remained under
360   two. We adopted random sampling to generate inference requests with different input prompt lengths
361   and generated token numbers for our baseline schemes. LLM inferences were executed using the
362
      GPU configurations detailed in Table 2, with the number of GPUs per inference ranging from the
      minimum required to a maximum of four, regardless of each hardware configuration’s total GPU
363
      capacity. For the training dataset, we considered L4, A100, and H100 GPUs, while all four GPU
364
      configurations were included in the test dataset.
365
366   Measurement and implementation. We used the Nvidia Management Library (NVML) (NVIDIA,
367   2024) to measure the energy consumption of LLM inferences on the target GPUs. Each infer-
368
      ence was executed 5 times, and the average energy consumption was recorded as the ground truth.
      LLMCO2 consists of two graph convolution layers and two linear layers. LLMCO2 was developed
369
      using the PyG package (Team, 2024) and trained on a Tesla L4 GPU. The model training used the
370
      Adam optimizer, with a learning rate of 0.001 and a batch size of 512.
371
372   Evaluation metrics. We evaluated prediction accuracy using the Mean Absolute Percentage Error
373   (MAPE) and Error Bound Accuracy (EBA(δ)). MAPE quantifies the average absolute percentage
374
      deviation between predicted and actual energy values, with lower values indicating higher accuracy.
      EBA(δ) represents the percentage of predictions within a specified error bound δ of the ground truth,
375
      with higher values reflecting greater regression precision.
376
377   Baseline Schemes. To assess the performance of LLMCO2 , we compared it against three baseline
      schemes: LLMCarbon (Faiz et al., 2024), DeepEn (Tu et al., 2023), and NNLQP (Liu et al., 2023).

      We conducted ablation studies on EBA(10%) to evaluate the contribution of each component of

459   Carbon comparison between training and inference. LLM training requires numerous GPUs
460   operating at high throughput over extended periods. For example, Mixtral-8×7b training uses 512

463   onds. Figure 10 shows the normalized carbon footprint per GPU per second for Mixtral-8×7b train-
464   ing and inference, relative to the training baseline. The embodied carbon per GPU per second is
465   consistent across all configurations since A100 GPUs are used. The prefill phase of an inference
466   with a batch size of 4 and a 6K prompt length has a similar operational carbon footprint per GPU
467
      per second to training, while the decode phase with a batch size of 1 and a 1K prompt length has a
      much lower footprint. Unlike training, the embodied carbon overhead dominates the decode phase
468
      of Mixtral-8×7b inferences.

470   Inference on multiple GPUs. Additional GPUs can accelerate LLM inference (Pope et al., 2023),
471   but using more GPUs than necessary is often unsustainable, particularly for real-world cloud in-
472   ference configurations with small batch sizes and short prompts. Figure 10 shows the operational
473
      carbon footprint of Bloom-7b1 inferences across different GPU counts with varying prompt lengths
      and batch sizes. For example, although an inference with a batch size of 4 and a 1K-token prompt
474
      benefits from 2 or 4 GPUs, lowering the per-GPU carbon footprint, an inference with a batch size
475
      of 1 and a 64-token prompt incurs increased latency and higher per-GPU operational carbon due to
476   the communication overhead of all-reduce kernels when using more GPUs. As shown in Figure 11,
477   the total carbon overhead rises considerably with more GPUs for inferences with smaller batch sizes
478   and prompts.

480   7           C ONCLUSION
481   LLM inference produces a larger carbon footprint than training, necessitating accurate estimation
482   tools for both users and cloud providers. Existing models fall short due to their inability to capture
483   LLM autoregressive behaviors, hardware-specific features, and real-world configuration distribu-
484   tion. We presented LLMCO2 , a GNN-based model to address these challenges, offering improved
485   accuracy in predicting the carbon footprint of LLM inferences compared to prior methods.
