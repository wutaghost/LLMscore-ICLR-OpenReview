009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In long-form video understanding, selecting an optimal Temporal Receptive Field
012            (TRF) is crucial for Vision Transformer (ViT) models due to the dynamic nature
013            of diverse video motion contents, which varies in duration and velocity. A short
014            TRF can result in loss of critical information, while a long TRF may decrease
015            ViT’s performance and computational efficiency caused by the unrelated contents
016            in videos and the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism. To tackle this
017            issue, we introduce Adaptive Memory Mechanism (AMM) that enables ViT to
018
               adjust its TRF dynamically in response to the video’s dynamic contents. Instead
               of discarding Key-Value (KV) cache from the earliest inference when the settings
019
               limit is reached, our approach uses a Memory Bank (MB) to retain the most im-
020
               portant embeddings from the Key-Value cache that would otherwise be discarded
021            in memory-augmented methods. The selection is based on the attention score cal-
022            culated between the Class Token (CLS) in current iteration and the KV cache in
023            previous iterations. We demonstrate that Adaptive Memory Vision Transformer
024            (AMViT) outperforms existing methods across a diverse array of tasks (action
025            recognition, action anticipation, and action detection).

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The Vision Transformer (ViT) has recently been found effective for diverse computer vision tasks.
031   By leveraging the self-attention mechanism to capture long-range dependencies across image
032   patches, it can outperform convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and achieve the state-of-the-art
033
      performance in both image (Russakovsky et al., 2015) and video (Kay et al., 2017) (Carreira et al.,
      2018) (Carreira et al., 2022) domains. Notwithstanding its success, ViT’s application to video un-
034
      destanding is constrained by its inherent computational design, particularly when processing long-
035
      form videos. The quadratic complexity of the self-attention mechanism creates a computational
036   bottleneck, limiting the temporal receptive field (TRF) that can be supported and, consequently, the
037   model’s ability to integrate information over extended time periods.
038
039
      To mitigate the computational demands so as to extend the TRF without incurring prohibitive com-
      putation costs, memory-augmented methods such as MeMViT (Wu et al., 2022) have been intro-
040
      duced. These methods incorporate mechanisms like stop-gradient Key-Value (KV) cache that draw
041
      parallels to truncated backpropagation-through-time (BPTT) (Mikolov et al., 2010), enabling the
042   extension of the TRF without much increase in computation. Subsequent work (Balazevic et al.,
043   2024) has introduced compression methods that do not require training. Most memory-augmented
044   methods manages the KV cache using strategies such as First-In-First-Out (FIFO), which may lead
045   to the discarding of salient information when newer, but less relevant, KV pairs displace older, more
046   pertinent ones. It is common for a video to contain contents of different actions which may last for
047   different periods of time. For instance, as shown in Figure 1, actions like “wash pot” typically last
048   for a long period of time than actions like “take pot” in a kitchen scene. The former one needs a
049   longer TRF, while the latter needs only a short one.
050   To this end, drawing inspiration from some recent works (Oren et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024)
051   which showed that carefully selecting KV pairs for caching is effective for Large Language Models
052   (LLMs), we propose an Adaptive Memory Mechanism (AMM) to enable the ViT to adaptively pri-
053   oritize the retention of embeddings based on their attention score with the Class Token (CLS) in the
      current iteration. The retained embeddings will be stored in a Memory Bank (MB) which is then

092
      Vision Transformers The advent of Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017), initially de-
093
      signed for NLP tasks, revolutionized multiple domains due to their remarkable performance. In the
094
      realm of Computer Vision (CV), Vision Transformers (ViTs) have emerged as a powerful alternative
095   to CNNs. Pioneering this shift, Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) introduced a method to process images by
096   dividing them into non-overlapping patches and treating them as embeddings, analogous to tokens
097   in NLP. This approach has been extended to video tasks, where SpatioTemporal Attention (STA)
098   (Bertasius et al., 2021) is utilized to handle patches from consecutive frames, as seen in works such
099   as Tong et al. (2022) and Li et al. (2023).
100
101
      Memory-augmented Methods In NLP, Transformer-XL (Dai et al., 2019) introduced the con-
102   cept of Key-Value (KV) cache as Memory, enabling the model to retain and leverage information
103   from the KV cache. This method has shown significant improvements for tasks requiring the com-
104   prehension of extended textual sequences. Subsequent enhancements include the development of
105   Compressive Memory method (Rae et al., 2020), which aims to condense the Memory, and the use
106   of linear attention’s KV cache combined with the Deta Rule (Schlag et al., 2021) as more efficient
107   Memory from Munkhdalai et al. (2024). Transferring these NLP-inspired memory methods to CV,
      works like Wu et al. (2022) have adapted the Compressive Memory method for ViT, facilitating

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the understanding of long-form video. Despite these advancements, methods which could adapt the
109   temporal receptive field (TRF) based on the dynamic video content are still lacking.
110
111   Long-form Video Understanding The challenge of long-form video understanding has been ap-
112   proached by various methods trying to extend the TRF. Some methods (Wang et al., 2023) (Xu et al.,
113   2021) (Rodriguez-Opazo et al., 2023) scale the TRF to accommodate the entire video sequence in
114   one pass. For instance, Wang et al. (2023) employs a Gumbel softmax mechanism (Jang et al., 2017)
115   to identify and retain salient features, while Xu et al. (2021) proposes a two-stage encoder to dis-
116   till long video sequences into concise representations suitable for ViT processing. Rodriguez-Opazo
117   et al. (2023) suggests the use of I3D features as inputs, which allows for a more compact feature rep-
118   resentation and an expanded content capacity for ViT. Alternative methods, such as those proposed
119
      in Wu et al. (2022) and Balazevic et al. (2024), utilize past KV cache as Memory, enabling multiple
      passes over sequentially video segments and allowing the model to obtain information from previ-
120
      ous video segments. Our method builds upon the memory-augmented methods, employing novel
121
      Adaptive Memory Mechanisms to enhance the understanding of long-form videos.

217
218   Memory-augmented methods (Rae et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022) aim to extend the temporal receptive
219   field (TRF) of the ViT architecture by a factor of m. While these approaches have shown promising
220   results, the fixed TRF imposed by the first-in-first-out (FIFO) mechanism does not align well with
221   the dynamic nature of video content, as illustrated in Figure 1, and the memory-augmented Keys
222   and Values still face the redundancy issues mentioned in Wu et al. (2022). To address the limitations
223   from FIFO mechanism, we propose Adaptive Memory Mechanism (AMM) that utilize Memory
224   Bank (MB) to retain embeddings releted to the input in current iteration by Input-aware Selective
225
      Module (ISM). The AMM allows ViT to achieve a more flexible TRF that responds dynamically to
      the videos.

      4.1     DATASET
271
272   AVA dataset (Gu et al., 2018) was created to benchmark the ability of models to localize and identify
273   human actions in extended video sequences. The dataset comprises over 430 15-minute video clips
274   extracted from movies and television shows, resulting in a diverse representation of scenes and
275   scenarios. Each video in AVA is densely annotated with action labels at one-second intervals, with
276   an average of 1 to 12 labels per person per second, spanning 80 different action categories.
277   Epic-Kitchens dataset (Damen et al., 2018) is a first-person (egocentric) video dataset. It encom-
278   passes over 55 hours of video data, recorded by 32 participants across multiple kitchen environ-
279   ments. The dataset is annotated with a rich vocabulary of object interactions and actions, featuring
280   over 39,000 action segments and 3,806 unique action classes. Each action is defined by a verb-noun
281   pair, providing a detailed account of the interaction dynamics inherent to cooking activities.
282
283   4.2     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
284
285   Data Loading To implement the memory-augmented methods, both during the training and infer-
286   ence phases, we adopt an online processing approach for the video data, which involves sequential
287
      reading of consecutive segments. This aligns well with real-world applications such as robotic vision
      systems and live video streaming understanding, where data is inherently processed in a sequential
288
      manner. To facilitate this, following Wu et al. (2022), we concatenate the entirety of our video
289
      dataset and process it as a continuous stream. For the episode at the boundary of two consecutive
290   segments from different videos where there is context discontinuity, we adopt a masking strategy
291   that initializes the AMM to ensure that the memory from a previous video does not influence the
292   processing of a subsequent one.
293
294   Backbone Selection We adopt the pre-trained UMT’s ViT as the backbone in this paper, as under-
295   pinned by the findings of Amos et al. (2024) which suggests pre-training, even with a focus on short
296   TRF relationship, can be beneficial for understanding long TRF relationship in data. By utilizing
297   a pre-trained ViT, we could capitalize on the pre-existing learned representations and substantially
298   reduce the computational overhead that would be associated with training ViTs from scratch. The
299   backbones were pre-trained on the K710 dataset, a comprehensive fusion of K400 (Kay et al., 2017),
300
      K600 (Carreira et al., 2018), and K700 (Carreira et al., 2022) datasets, as described in Li et al. (2023).
301
302   Model Setting To ensure fair performance comparison with the state-of-the-art models including
303
      UMT (Li et al., 2023) and MeMViT Wu et al. (2022), we try to standardize the backbone architecture
      and the training hyperparameters for both models as well as the proposed AMViT. Thus, we change
304
      the MeMViT’s backbone from MViT to UMT (see Appendix A.2 for details).
305
306
      Positional Embedding Following UMT (Li et al., 2023), we utilize absolute positional embed-
307
      ding (Vaswani et al., 2017) so as to be compatible with the UMT’s pre-trained model and to benefit
308
      from the reduced memory requirement.

465   5   C ONCLUSION
466
467   The optimal temporal receptive field (TRF) is essential in achieving long-form video understand-
468   ing using Vision Transformer (ViT) models. Although memory-augmented methods could extend
469   TRF by caching the stop-gradient Key-Value (KV) pairs, they still have a fixed TRF and the first-
470   in-first-out caching strategy could lead to loss of salient information. To tackle the challenges posed
471   by varying duration and diverse dynamics in long-form videos, we propose the Adaptive Memory
472   Mechanism (AMM), which allows ViT models to adaptively adjust the TRF based on the input
473   video. AMM features an input-aware selective module that selects tokens from previous iterations
474
      that are relevant to the input in the subsequent iteration. By using a memory bank, AMM retains
      essential information that exceeds the memory length limit. Experimental results show that the
475
      proposed method outperforms the state-of-the-art methods in action detection, recognition, and an-
476
      ticipation tasks.
