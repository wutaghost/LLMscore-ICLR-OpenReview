009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Flow-based models have shown great success in generative modeling, making
012            them a promising candidate for solving inverse problems in physical sciences and
013            allow for sampling and likelihood evaluation with much lower inference times than
014            traditional methods. We propose to pretrain a neural network via flow matching and
015            include control signals based on a simulator as an additional input for finetuning via
016            a lightweight control network. Control signals can include gradients and a problem-
017            specific cost function if the simulator is differentiable, or they can be fully learned
018            from the simulator output. We motivate our design choices on several benchmark
019
               problems for simulation-based inference and evaluate flow matching with simulator
               feedback against classical MCMC methods for modeling strong gravitational
020
               lens systems, a challenging inverse problem in astronomy. We demonstrate that
021
               including simulator feedback improves the accuracy of reconstructed samples by
022            53%, making it competitive with traditional techniques while being up to 67x faster
023            for inference. Upon acceptance, we will make our code publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Acquiring posterior distributions given measurement data is of paramount scientific interest (Cranmer
029
      et al., 2020), with real-world applications ranging from particle physics (Baydin et al., 2019), over
030
      the inference of gravitational waves (Dax et al., 2021) to predictions of dynamical systems such as
031   weather forecasting (Gneiting & Raftery, 2005). In Bayesian modeling, given an observation xo and
032   model parameters θ, we are interested in the posterior p(θ|xo ). Traditional likelihood-based methods
033   can be expensive for high-dimensional data, when likelihood evaluations are costly or intractable
034   and priors are difficult to represent mathematically. Simulation-based inference (Cranmer et al.,
035   2020, SBI) addresses these challenges by including a learning-based component in the statistical
036   inference process. In this paper, we focus on neural posterior estimation (NPE), which represents the
037   posterior as a parametric function q(θ|xo ), which is a learnable conditional density estimator that
038   can be trained purely by simulations x ∼ p(x|θ) alone. By investing an upfront cost for training
039
      the density estimator, we can sample and compute likelihoods from q(θ|xo ) much faster than other
      methods, thereby amortizing the training cost over many observations. Traditionally, normalizing
040
      flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Dinh et al., 2017; Papamakarios et al., 2019) have been a popular
041
      class of density estimators used in many areas of science. To compute likelihoods and for sampling,
042   normalizing flows transform a noise distribution to the target distribution via a bijective mapping.
043   By conditioning the normalizing flow networks on the observation xo obtained from the simulator,
044   they can be trained as the conditional density estimator q(θ|xo ) for the posterior. The success of
045   diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2021) has demonstrated that
046   the mapping between sampling and posterior distribution can be specified by a corruption process
047   that transforms any data distribution to a normal Gaussian. Diffusion models and normalizing
048   flows can be linked via the probability flow ODE (Song et al., 2021), which has also influenced a
049   class of flow-based models that can be trained via flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023) on more
050
      general mappings between sampling and target distribution than considered by diffusion models. The
      resulting continuous-time models outperform discrete, classical normalizing flows in many areas, and
051
      training larger models is much more scalable (Wildberger et al., 2023).
052
053   Despite the widespread success of flow-based models for generative modeling and density estimation,
      there is no direct feedback from the simulator between the model, the observation xo and the sample

      We propose a simple strategy to reintroduce control signals using simulators into the flow network.
      We refine an existing pretrained flow-based model with a flexible control signal by aggregating the
078
      learned flow and control signals into a controlled flow, which requires only a minimal amount of
079
      additional parameters. To demonstrate how these refinements affect the accuracy of samples and
080   the posterior, we consider modeling strong gravitational lens systems (Hezaveh et al., 2017; Cunha
081   & Herdeiro, 2018; Legin et al., 2021), an inverse problem in astrophysics that is challenging and
082   requires precise posteriors for accurate modeling of observations. In galaxy-scale strong lenses, light
083   from a source galaxy is deflected by the gravitational potential of a galaxy between the source and
084   observer, causing multiple images of the source to be seen. Since these images and their distortions
085   are sensitive to the distribution of matter on small scales, this can act as a probe for different dark
086   matter models. With upcoming and current sky surveys (Laureijs et al., 2011) expected to release
087   large data catalogs in the near future, the number of known lenses will increase dramatically by
088
      several orders of magnitude. Traditional computational approaches require several minutes to many
      hours or days to model a single lens system. Therefore, there is an urgent need to reduce the compute
089
      and inference with learning-based methods. In this experiment, we demonstrate that using flow
090
      matching and our proposed control signals with feedback from a simulator, we obtain posterior
091   distributions for lens modeling that are competitive with the posteriors obtained by MCMC-based
092   methods but with much faster inference times.
093
094
      Additionally, we evaluate different related variants of flow matching such as using problem-specific
      priors, self-conditioning (Chen et al., 2023) or different loss formulations in the context of SBI
095
      using several benchmark problems. We then analyze our proposed control signals for the Lotka-
096
      Volterra model, a system of coupled ordinary differential equations (ODEs) descriping the population
097   evolution of predators and prey over time. Our analysis underscores the essential role of simulator
098   feedback for inference and that high accuracy is very challenging to achieve from scaling up datasets
099   and model sizes alone.
100
      To summarize, the main contributions of our work are:
101
102          • We propose a versatile strategy to improve pretrained flows with control signals based on
103            feedback from a simulator. Control signals can be based on gradients and a cost function,
104            if the simulator is differentiable, but they can also be learned directly from the simulator
105            output.
106          • We assess different variants of flow matching in the context of SBI and demonstrate with
107            the Lotka-Volterra model that performance gains due to simulator feedback are substantial
               and cannot be achieved by training on larger datasets alone.

116   Solving inverse problems under a diffusion prior Diffusion models have been proposed to solve
117   linear inverse problems (Kawar et al., 2021; 2022; Chung et al., 2022; Cardoso et al., 2023), as
118   well as general inverse problems (Holzschuh et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2023a;b),
119   via stochastic optimization (Graikos et al., 2022; Mardani et al., 2024) or through amortization by
120
      reinforcement learning (Black et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023). In most of these works, the diffusion
      model learns the prior distribution and sampling from the posterior is achieved through a modified
121
      inference procedure, which guides samples via a conditioning. The conditioning can be based on a
122
      class label, text input (Song et al., 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023)
123   or directly on a differentiable measurement operator (Chung et al., 2023a;b). In contrast to these
124   works, we finetune a pretrained flow and learn an optimal combination of the pretrained flow and
125   feedback from a simulator via control signals in the broader flow matching context.
126
127
      Flow matching Our work builds on top of prior work in flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023;
      Albergo et al., 2023a; Pooladian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023; Albergo et al., 2023b), particularly we
128
      adopt and evaluate conditional optimal transport paths (Lipman et al., 2023), test problem-specific
129
      priors and rectification of flows to produce straighter paths (Liu et al., 2023) for simulation-based
130   inference. Guiding flows has for example been explored by Zheng et al. (2023); Nisonoff et al. (2024).
131   We extend the existing literature by adding feedback from a simulator for scientific inverse problems.
132
133   Simulation-based inference Our work directly compares to neural posterior estimation approaches
134   for simulation-based inference (Cranmer et al., 2020; Lueckmann et al., 2021, SBI). Contrary to
135
      static architectures (Dinh et al., 2017; Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018; Papamakarios et al., 2017; Durkan
      et al., 2019), our approach extends the continuous-time paradigm (Chen et al., 2018; Grathwohl
136
      et al., 2019). Wildberger et al. (2023) have applied flow matching to neural posterior estimation
137
      and Sharrock et al. (2022) have used conditional diffusion models and Langevin dynamics during
138   sampling. In contrast to previous work, we include controls signals via problem-specific simulators
139   and cost functions during training to significantly improve the sampling quality.
140
141   Strong lensing and parameter estimation Machine learning has been successfully applied to
142
      estimate parameters of lens and source models (Hezaveh et al., 2017; Levasseur et al., 2017), however,
      previous methods are usually restricted to point estimates, use simple variational distributions,
143
      Bayesian Neural Networks (Schuldt et al., 2021; Legin et al., 2021; Poh et al., 2022) that are not well
144
      suited to represent more complicated high-dimensional data distributions. Legin et al. (2023) predict
145   point estimates for the lensing parameters, which are utilized by mixture density networks to model
146   their distribution in a likelihood-free inference framework. In this paper, we combine flow matching
147   with problem-specific simulators to obtain highly accurate samples via feedback from control signals.

200   4    C ONTROLS FOR I MPROVED ACCURACY
201
202   While flow-based models vϕ (t, θ) gradually transform samples from p0 to p1 in many steps during
203   inference via solving the ODE eq. 1, there is no direct feedback loop between the underlying
204   simulator, the current point on the trajectory θt , and the observation xo . A central goal of our work is
205
      to reintroduce this feedback loop into inference and training by incorporating a control signal.
206   Conditioning of flows Flows vϕ (t, θ) can be conditioned on an observation xo through an addi-
207   tional input vϕ (t, θ, xo ), therefore modeling the conditional densities pt (θ|xo ) (Song et al., 2021).
208   Models can be trained for both conditional and unconditional generation. This is achieved, for
209   example, in classifier free-guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022), by randomly dropping the conditioning
210   and setting it to 0 during training.
211
      A critical shortcoming here is that the conditioning xo is static, whereas we propose to have a
212   dynamic control mechanism that depends on the trajectory θt , the observation, and an underlying
213   control signal. The latter should relate θt and observation using a physics-based model represented
214   through a cost function C. As the accuracy of neural networks is inherently limited by the finite size
215   of their weights, and smaller networks are attractive from a computational perspective, physics-based
      control has the potential to yield high accuracy with lean and efficient neural network models.

225   models, sampling is based on the conditional score ∇θt log p(θt |xo ), which can be decomposed into

227   The first expression can be estimated using a pretrained diffusion model, whereas the latter is usually
228   intractable, but can be approximated using p(xo |θt ) ≈ pxo |θ0 (xo |θ̂(θt )), where the denoising
229   estimate θ̂(θt ) := Eq [θ0 |θt ] is usually obtained via Tweedie’s formula (Eq [θ0 |θt ] − θt )/tσ 2 . In
230   practice, the estimate θ̂(θt ) is very poor when θt is still noisy, impeding the inference in the early
231   stages. On the contrary, flows based on linear conditional transportation paths have empirically
232   been shown to have trajectories with less curvature (Lipman et al., 2023) compared to, for example,
233   diffusion models, thus enabling inference in fewer steps and providing better estimates for θ̂1 .
234   Controlled flow vϕC We pretrain the flow network vϕ (t, θ, xo ) without any control signals to make
235   sure that we can realize the best achievable performance possible based on learning alone. Then,
236   in a second training phase, we introduce the control network vϕC (t, v, c) with pretrained flow v and
237   control signal c as input. The control network is much smaller in size than the flow network, making
238   up ca. 10% of the weights ϕ in our large-scale experiments. We freeze the network weights of
239   vϕ and train with the conditional flow matching loss eq. 3 for a small number of additional steps.
240   This reduces training time and compute since we do not need to backpropagate gradients through
241   vϕ (t, θ, xo ). We did not observe that freezing the weights of vϕ affects the performance negatively.
242   We include algorithms for training in appendix A.

      4.2   A DDITIONAL C ONSIDERATIONS FOR S IMULATOR F EEDBACK
271
272   Stochastic simulators Many Bayesian inference problems have a stochastic simulator. For sim-
273
      plicity, we assume that all stochasticity within such a simulator can be controlled via a variable
      z ∼ N (0, I), which is an additional input. Motivated by the equivalence of exchanging expectation
274
      and gradient
275
276                    ∇θ̂1 Ez∼N (0,1) [C(Sz (θ̂1 ), xo )] = Ez∼N (0,1) [∇θ̂1 C(Sz (θ̂1 ), xo )],       (10)
277
278   when calling the simulator, we draw a random realization of z. During training, we randomly draw z
279
      for each sample and step while during inference we keep the value of z fixed for each trajectory.
280   Time-dependence If the estimate θ̂1 is bad and the corresponding cost C(θ̂1 , xo ) is high, gradients
281   and control signals can become unreliable. In appendix B, we empirically find that the estimates θ̂1
282   become more reliable for t ≥ 0.8. Therefore, we only train the control network vϕC in this range,
283   which allows for focusing on control signals containing the most useful information. For t < 0.8, we
284   directly output the pretrained flow vϕ (t, θ, xo ).
285
      Theoretical correctness Contrary to likelihood-based guidance, which uses an approximation
286
      for ∇θt log p(xo |θt ) as a guidance term during inference, the approximation θ̂1 only influences the
287
      control signal, which is an input to the controlled flow network vϕC . In the case of a deterministic
288
      simulator, this makes the control signal a function of θt . The controlled flow network is trained with
289   the same loss as vanilla flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023). Therefore all theoretical properties
290   remain preserved.

341   5.2     T RAINING VARIANTS
342
343
      There are several variants of training diffusion models that can be related to simulation feedback
      and which we consider promising in the context of SBI. Before we go on to evaluate the simulator
344
      feedback in section 5.3, we test if we can improve the performance using any of them. In particular,
345
      we assess the following modifications:
346
347   • Self-conditioning: conditioning a model on something that depends on its own output can be seen
348     as a form of self-conditioning. We evaluate an adapted version of self-conditioning (Chen et al.,
349     2023). Instead of providing θt to the flow network, the input is comprised of the concatenated
350     vector [θt ; Dropout(θ̂1 )], where θ̂1 is the 1-step prediction eq. 6. For computing θ̂1 , we require
351     one network evaluation with the input [θt ; 0] and stop the gradient backpropagation at θ̂1 . This
352     method is similar to our simulator feedback, as it introduces a feedback loop that conditions the
353
        model on its own output, but without any simulator.
354   • Task-specific priors: it is also possible to couple two non-Gaussian distributions by defining
355     the coupling as q(z) = p0 (θ0 )p1 (θ1 ) and setting the conditional probabilities to the linear paths
356     defined by pt (θ|(θ0 , θ1 )) = N (θ|tθ1 + (1 − t)θ0 , σI) and ut (θ|(θ0 , θ1 )) = θ1 − θ0 with
357
        bandwidth σ > 0. We can choose p0 as the prior distribution p(θ) which we know in the SBI
        setting. Obtaining information in the form of an observation changes our knowledge about θ from
358
        the prior distribution to the posterior, therefore resembling a transformation similar to the noise to
359
        data transformation in diffusion models. This also suggests that the prior distribution can be closer
360     to the posterior than a noise distribution.
361
      • x-prediction: the reliability of the control signal depends directly on the 1-step estimate θ̂. Instead
362
        of regressing the flow ut (θ), we can directly predict the denoised estimate θ̂ and obtain the velocity
363
        by rearranging eq. 6, giving vϕ (t, θt , xo ) = θ̂1 /(1 − t). We additionally weight the x-prediction

365     x-prediction potentially produces better estimates for θ̂, thus allowing for obtaining more reliable
366     feedback from control signals when t < 0.8.

368   Evaluation Figure 3 shows an evaluation of the different variants against vanilla flow matching
369   (Gaussian sampling distribution, no self-conditioning and velocity prediction). Using task-specific
370   priors produces outliers with better C2ST scores for SLCP but is consistently worse for LV and
371   SIR. We conclude that normal Gaussian distributions are more suited as sampling distributions for
372   most low-dimensional problems. Introducing self-conditioning does not show any improvements, so
373
      feedback loops without a simulator alone are not sufficient for better performance in this situation.
      Finally, the x-prediction loss consistently performs worse than the velocity prediction. Therefore, a
374
      potential improvement in the 1-step estimate is outweighed by a corresponding deterioration of the
375
      posterior correctness as indicated by the C2ST score.

      5.4   C OMPUTATIONAL EFFICIENCY
405
406   A critical issue in SBI is that calls to the simulator are potentially expensive. This imposes the
407   question of whether compute time is better spent on extending the training dataset or training with
408
      feedback from the simulator. We empirically verify that the latter is more efficient for the LV task
      in this setup by comparing our method to models with an increased training dataset from a larger
409
      simulator budget. Specifically, we train with dataset sizes of 106 and 107 . Training the gradient-based
410
      control signal took ca. 9 × 106 simulator calls. See fig. 5 for the evaluation. There is no improvement
411   in the C2ST for models trained without simulator feedback beyond 105 data points, and the final
412   train/validation loss for the 107 model indicates that there is no more overfitting. Nonetheless, the
413   model trained with controls clearly outperforms the model trained with more data, indicating that the
414   directed feedback of the simulator cannot be replaced by increased amounts of training data.

      6     S TRONG G RAVITATIONAL L ENSING
418   We present our results for modeling strong gravitational lens systems, a challenging and highly
419   relevant non-linear problem in astronomy. Strong gravitational lensing is a physical phenomenon
420   whereby the light rays by a distant object, such as a galaxy, are deflected by an intervening massive
421   object, such as another galaxy or a galaxy cluster. As a result, one observes multiple distorted images
422   of the background source. We aim to recover both the lens and source light distribution as well as the
423   lens mass density distribution with realistic simulated observations for which we know the ground
424   truths. We evaluate flow matching as an NPE method with gradient-based control signals from a
425
      differentiable simulator with two MCMC methods.
426   Lens modeling The lens equation relates coordinates on the source plane β and the observed image
427   plane Θ via the deflection angle α induced by the mass profile or gravitational potential of the lens
428   galaxy. We use a Singular Isothermal Ellipsoid (SIE) to describe this lens mass and Sérsic profiles for
429   both the source light and light emitted from the lens galaxy (full details are provided in appendix
430   C). There are 9 parameters for the lens mass and 7 parameters for each Sérsic profile, giving 23
431   parameters in total. The likelihood is measured by the χ2 -statistic, which is the modeled image plane
      Θ minus the observation xo divided by the noise. To solve the lensing equation, we make use of

519   6.2   L IMITATIONS
520
      While introducing additional control signals increases the quality of produced samples, it comes at the
521
      cost of slower inference and training times depending on the speed of the simulator. In general, using
522
      non-differentiable control signals is possible but removes the possibility of computing likelihoods via
523   the instantaneous change of variables formula (Chen et al., 2018). Compared to MCMC approaches,
524   inference with flow-based models requires a substantial upfront cost for training that needs to be
525   amortized across many problems. Additionally, priors are encoded in the learned flow networks, so
526   changing them would require retraining models with adjusted data sets.
527
528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   We presented a method for improving flow-based models with simulator feedback using control
531
      signals. This allows us to refine an existing flow with only a few additional weights and little
      training time. We thereby efficiently bridge the gap between purely learning-based methods for
532
      simulation-based inference and optimization with hand-crafted cost functions within the framework
533
      of flow matching. This improvement is critical for scientific applications where high accuracy
534   and trustworthiness in the methods are required. Purely learning-based methods face significant
535   difficulties in producing very accurate samples, as there is usually no feedback during inference of
536   how good samples are. In this paper, we demonstrated that we do not need large network sizes or
537   tremendous amounts of data to train accurate models that are competitive with established MCMC
538   methods if we include suitable control signals from simulators. We believe this work makes an
539   important step towards making posterior inference in science more accurate, understandable, and
      reliable.

950   The 1-step estimate θ̂1 becomes more accurate and closer to the end point of the trajectory θ1 as paths
951   become straighter. Rectified flows (Liu et al., 2023) have been proposed to learn a coupling between
952   two distributions by solving a nonlinear least squares optimization problem. Flows can be recursively
953   rectified, leading to increasingly straighter paths. We have trained the k-th rectified flow up to k = 3
954   following Algorithm 1 from Liu et al. (2023) for the Lotka Volterra SBI task. Networks, optimizers
955   and learning rates are the same as for the flow matching experiments. Results for the rectified flows
956   are show in table 4. The C2ST score gets worse for the 2- and 3-Rectified flow. We also finetune with
957
      gradient-based control signals. A difference compared to the finetuning experiments in section 5.3 is
      that we train the network starting at t ≥ 0, whereas we have used t ≥ 0.8 before. As flows become
958
      straighter, the 1-step estimates should become more reliable. This is why we consider finetuning
959
      with the control signal on the entire trajectory in this experiment, instead of only focusing on the last
960   part (t ≥ 0.8). When adding the finetuning, the C2ST score becomes better for the 1-Rectified flow
961   compared to the 2-Rectified flow, indicating that the 2-Rectified flow produces more reliable 1-step
962   estimates. However, the results for the rectified flows are not as good as the flow matching setup with
963   t ≥ 0.8 which we have used in section 5.3.

975
976           • For modeling the lens we use an SIE model with 6 parameters: the Einstein radius θE , the
977             ellipticities e1 and e2 and xcenter and ycenter . There is shear, for which we only consider γ1
978             and γ2 as free parameters.
979           • The source is modeled by a Sersic profile with free parameters being the amplitude, the
980             half-light radius, the Sersic index n, the ellipticities e1 and e2 as well as the positions xcenter
981             and ycenter .
982           • The lens light is modeled in the same way as the source, although when generating the
983             mock data, we fix the position as well as ellipticities to be the same as the lens mass model.
984             For training and inference, we infer positions for both lens mass and lens light model, so
985             the model could produce different values for them. The MCMC methods use the same
986             parameter for both lens light and lens mass position.

       We list all priors in table 5, table 6 and table 7. We do not have priors on the ellipticities e1 and e2
988    directly, but we obtain them from priors on the position angle and axis ratio. Also, we obtain the shear
989    parameters from γ1 and γ2 from ϕext and γext by converting them polar to cartesian coordinates. For
990    SBI, we also include the two parameters ra0 and dec0 for the shear, which are always set to 0 when

1014            2DConv layer with kernel size 3 and a single channel. We reshape the output and feed it
