011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit impressive capabilities across various
014            applications but encounter substantial challenges such as high inference latency,
015
               considerable training costs, and the generation of hallucinations. Collaborative
               decoding between large and small language models (SLMs) presents a promis-
016
               ing strategy to mitigate these issues through methods including speculative de-
017
               coding, contrastive decoding, and emulator or proxy fine-tuning. However, the
018            specifics of such collaborations, particularly from a unified perspective, remain
019            largely unexplored. Inspired by dual-process cognitive theory, we propose a uni-
020            fied framework in this paper, termed Fast and Slow Generating (FS-GEN). Within
021            this framework, LLMs (sometimes along with SLMs) are categorized as Sys-
022            tem 2 (slow and deliberate), while independent SLMs are designated as System
023            1 (fast and intuitive). We provide a comprehensive analysis of these collabora-
024            tive methodologies, elucidating their common properties and shedding light on
025            the differential knowledge capabilities of System 2 versus System 1 through the
026
               FS-GEN framework. Our findings indicate that only a small proportion of col-
               laborative interactions (approximately less than 20% in most instances) are nec-
027
               essary across various methods. These interactions between System 1 and System
028
               2 conform to a scaling law related to the parameter ratios, enabling predictable
029            collaboration. Furthermore, we explore the specific conditions under which col-
030            laboration proves most effective, particularly from an uncertainty perspective, of-
031            fering novel insights that may guide future optimization efforts. Our research
032            underscores that the fundamental distinction between System 1 and System 2
033            lies in the uncertainty of next token predictions, where interventions by System
034            2 are crucial to support System 1. We provide code for reproduction: https:
035            //anonymous.4open.science/r/ICLR2025_Anonymous-127D

085   community lacks a unified framework to analyze and understand the dynamics of their collaboration.
086   This gap in knowledge motivates our current study, which seeks to formalize the collaboration be-

088   to modeling human cognitive processes (Kahneman, 2011). As shown in Figure 1, within the human
089   brain, System 1 operates quickly and intuitively, automatically addressing everyday problems such

092   daily human activities achieves maximum efficiency and demonstrates robust intelligence. Notably,

095

097   small models exhibit the natural characteristics of slow and fast processing. Previous works (Kim
098   et al., 2024) on speculative decoding indicate that only a small percentage of generations require
099   intervention by LLMs, while SLMs can independently complete most of the remainder.
100

101

102   previous work has attempted to unify the collaborative decoding methods of SLMs and LLMs from
103   this perspectives. In this paper, we explore the fundamental questions of model collaboration under
104   the fast and slow generation framework: 1) What is the essential difference between Systems 2 and
105   1 from the perspective of decoding? 2) Why does System 1 under-perform compared to System 2?
106   3) How can we improve effiency of the large and small compound system? In this paper, we focus
107   exclusively on the high-efficiency features of System 1 and System 2 based on language models,
      deferring an exploration of their in-depth reasoning capabilities to future work.

139   2     R ELATION W ORKS
140
141   Collaborative Decoding Methods. Collaborative decoding has emerged as an effective strategy
142   to enhance large language models (LLMs) by leveraging both high-performing LLMs and cost-
143   efficient small language models (SLMs). Speculative decoding accelerates the generation process by
144   having SLMs draft candidate spans, which are then verified by LLMs in a single forward pass, with
145   verified tokens fed back for subsequent generations (Leviathan et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; Sun
146   et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024). This method focuses on drafting robust candidates (Xia et al., 2023;
147
      Liu et al., 2024b) and efficient verification strategies, such as lossless and approximate approaches
      for greedy decoding and nucleus sampling (Stern et al., 2018; Xia et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023b).
148
      Contrastive decoding, on the other hand, aims to improve factuality (Sennrich et al., 2023) and
149
      reasoning abilities (O’Brien & Lewis, 2023) by fusing logits from expert and amateur models (Li
150   et al., 2022), under the assumption that amateur models’ outputs are less reliable. This approach
151   enhances scrutiny on the tokens generated, reducing hallucinations and improving output quality
152   (Jin et al., 2024). Both methods primarily explore how to fuse logits without fully addressing the
153   extent and specific needs for collaboration. Additional techniques like emulator or proxy tuning
154   involve multiple models to improve training efficiency (Mitchell et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a) but
155   similarly lack deeper empirical analysis on collaboration dynamics.
156   Scaling Laws and Emergent Abilities. Scaling laws have been instrumental in the development
157   of LLMs, indicating that emergent abilities often manifest in larger models while smaller models
158   struggle with the same tasks (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022). Recent
159   studies suggest that these abilities are influenced by the continuity of evaluation metrics: continuous
160   metrics show minimal divergence between model sizes, whereas discrete metrics reveal significant
161   variations (Schaeffer et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024). Despite these insights, there remains a lack of
      empirical analysis into the essential differences between LLMs and SLMs from a decoding perspec-

163   providing new insights that significantly enhance the efficiency and quality of existing techniques.

168   3.1   T EXT G ENERATION
169
170   Text generation, also referred to as decoding (Welleck et al., 2024), is the process whereby a lan-
171   guage model M selects the next token in a sequence based on the context. Mathematically, this
172   involves estimating the conditional probability of a token given its preceding sequence. For a given
173   source context with N tokens and a target response with T tokens, noted as X = (x1 , x2 , · · · , xN )
174
      and Y = (y1 , y2 , · · · , yT ), the probability of the response sequence is expressed as:
175                                              T
                                                 Y
176                                 P (Y |X) =         p(yi |X, y1 , y2 , · · · , yi−1 )
177                                              i=1
178
179   During the decoding phase, at each step i, the model M computes the probability distribution
180   p(yi |X, y1 , y2 , · · · , yi−1 ) over the entire vocabulary, based on the input sequence provided so far.
181   The selection of the next token yi is then based on this distribution, typically employing methods
182   such as greedy search (Parsing, 2009), beam search (Vijayakumar et al., 2016; Freitag & Al-Onaizan,
183   2017), or various sampling strategies (Fan et al., 2018; Holtzman et al., 2020).

      3.2   D EFINITION OF FS-GEN M ETHODS
186
187   Beginning with the fundamental definition of text decoding, we extend this concept to encompass
188   collaborative decoding. In order to provide a unified definition of the collaborations employed in
189   various FS-GEN methods, we consider setups involving large language models, denoted as Ml , and
190   small models, denoted as Ms . The respective input prompts for Ml and Ms are given as Xl and Xs ,
191   which are identical in most settings. As illustrated in Figure 1, the logits for the next token generated
192   by LLMs and SLMs are denoted as pl and ps , respectively, with the reference golden logits noted
193   as pf . We categorize the outputs into three types: golden or collaborative outputs Og , which can
194
      also be denoted as Og , representing fused outputs., outputs Ol from Ml , and outputs Os from Ms .
      Note that the term golden logits pf and outputs Og refers to the results of collaboration, which may
195
      not always equate to the golden answers in some instances. These variables are assigned differently
196
      across different methods, varying according to the fusing strategy and underlying motivation:
197
198   Speculative Decoding. This approach leverages predictions from SLMs that are subsequently val-
199   idated or refined by LLMs. In this setting, the outputs from LLMs are considered golden outputs,
200
      denoted as pf = pl and Og = Ol , where Ml signifies the model chosen to select tokens, reflecting
      the trust in the outputs Os from Ms . Speculative decoding represents the most essential case.
201
202   Contrastive Decoding. This method employs differential strategies to reconcile the disparities be-
203   tween the predictions from large and small models, aiming to enhance accuracy. In the implemen-
204   tation (O’Brien & Lewis, 2023), the reference logits are calculated as pf = pl + β(pl − ps ), and Og
205   outperforms both Ol and Os , indicating that Og leverages the strengths of both models.
206   Proxy Tuning. This strategy involves tuning SLMs to emulate the behavior of LLMs, thus minimiz-
207   ing training resource usage without significantly compromising output quality. In this configuration,
208   SLMs are differentiated into base and chat versions, with their logits denoted respectively as ps for
209   the base version and p′s for the chat version. The fused logits are represented by pf = pl + (p′s − ps ),
210   suggesting that Og , incorporating insights from both Ol and the delta change between the tuned and
211   untuned states of SLMs, approaches the performance of the fully tuned LLMs.
212   As shown in Table 1, in speculative decoding, SLMs serve as the base models for fast genera-
213   tion, while LLMs manage the more deliberate and slow aspects, providing interventions to the fast
214   process. In contrast, there is difference in contrastive decoding and proxy tuning, with SLMs in-
215   troducing interventions into the LLM generation process. In addition to the three typical methods,
      further exploration of other FS-GEN methods is displayed in Appendix B.

      3.3   D EFINITION OF C OLLABORATION F REQUENCY
217
218   As shown in Figure 1 and as discussed previously, not all steps in the fast system’s generation require
219   collaborations or interventions from the slow system. We denote the number of collaborations in
220   FS-GEN methods as Nl and total length of generated content as Ntotal . We defined collaboration
                               Nl
221   frequency as CoF = Ntotal    , which is typically set to 1 in contrastive decoding and proxy tuning.
222   We can establish a lower bound of collaboration frequency, denoted as CoFlower , which has been
223   preliminarily proven in prior research (Kim et al., 2024) and is further discussed in § 5.1.1. The
224   term “lower bound” denotes the minimal collaboration rate required for the text produced by SLMs
225   (System 1) to exactly match the output generated solely by SLMs and LLMs (System 2). We provide
226   a running example for computing CoFlower in Appendix C for better understanding on our settings.

      4.2   E XPERIMENT D ESIGN
253
      Our empirical study investigates two key aspects of collaboration in FS-GEN: frequency and po-
254   sition. Collaboration frequency evaluates the minimum necessary interactions between large and
255   small language models, enabling predictions of interaction frequency across different model scales.
256   Collaboration position identifies specific interaction points, predicting when collaboration is needed.
257   For collaboration frequency, we first analyze lower bound of frequency across various model scales
258   and tasks to identify trends (§ 5.1.1). Next, we examine a scaling law in FS-GEN that links models
259   scale ratios with optimal interaction frequency for prediction (§ 5.1.2). For collaboration position,
260   we initially study mismatch points relative to generated content length (§ 5.2.1), followed by an
261   analysis based on token uncertainty in SLMs (§ 5.2.2).

         Finding 1: 20% Collaborations (2:8 Law)
299      The lower bound of collaboration frequency between models of different scales averages less
300      than 20% on various tasks and methods, reflecting the 2:8 law (the Pareto Principle).
301
302   As illustrated in Figure 2 and 3, the collaboration frequency varies with the difference between
303   the task and model combination. Notably, for the specific SLMs, the frequency of collaboration
304   increases as the size of LLMs increases, while models of similar sizes exhibit lower frequencies.
305   When the parameters of the models are more closely aligned, the CoF lower is reduced, generally
306   not exceeding 20% across various tasks. This observation suggests that the parameter gap between
307   models does not conform to traditional scaling laws, where task performance is typically emer-
308
      gent (Wei et al., 2022). It also indicates that scaling laws may be influenced by the continuity of
      metrics (Schaeffer et al., 2024), which are affected by token uncertainty. The primary distinction
309
      between large and small models lies in the selection of essential tokens. Small models can be signifi-
310
      cantly enhanced by a few key tokens from larger models, particularly those that are difficult for them
311   to select. This phenomenon is evident not only in oracle decoding, a form of speculative reasoning,
312   but also in other collaborative decoding methods such as contrastive and proxy decoding.

814   A.1   C OLLABORATIVE D ECODING
815
816
817   Speculative Decoding. Leveraging the high performance of LLMs and the low inference cost of
818   SLMs, speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024) has become
819   an effective method to accelerate the generation process in LLMs. This approach involves two main
820
      steps: drafting and verification (Xia et al., 2024). In the drafting step, SLMs generate a candidate
      span comprising several tokens. Subsequently, in the verification step, LLMs review all draft tokens
821
      in a single forward pass. The verified tokens are then fed back to the SLMs as input for subsequent
822
      generations. Current efforts in this area can be categorized into two primary streams: drafting and
823   verification. In drafting, researchers focus on developing robust drafters that aim to generate as many
824   tokens as possible that are likely to be verified successfully (Xia et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b). For
825   verification, methods such as lossless and approximate approaches (Stern et al., 2018; Xia et al.,
826   2023; Fu et al., 2023b) have been proposed for both greedy decoding and nucleus sampling. Nearly
827   all the aforementioned studies employ draft tokens of a fixed length, adjusted as hyper-parameters.
828   However, recent efforts have begun to determine the candidate length dynamically (Mamou et al.,
829   2024; Shen et al., 2024), with more in-depth empirical studies also currently under exploration.
830   Contrastive Decoding. Compared to the efficiency gains in speculative decoding, contrastive de-
831   coding (Li et al., 2022) focuses on improving factuality (Sennrich et al., 2023) and reasoning abili-
832   ties (O’Brien & Lewis, 2023) through collaborative decoding. In this approach, the next token logits
833   from expert and amateur models are fused comparatively. The underlying assumption is that tokens
834   generated by the amateur model are less reliable and should be less frequently utilized in expert
835   models, implying that greater scrutiny is required for tokens in expert models. Recent studies also
836   explore contrasting models with and without context, designated as expert and amateur (Jin et al.,
837
      2024), which helps reduce hallucinations. Similar to speculative decoding, these efforts primarily
      examine how to fuse the logits, without addressing the extent and specific needs for collaboration.
838
839   Emulator or Proxy Tuning and others. Unlike enhancements in inference time that involve two
840   models, emulator tuning (Mitchell et al., 2023) and proxy tuning (Liu et al., 2024a) employ three
841   models to improve training efficiency. In these methods, researchers fine-tune only SLMs which
842   generate logits from both base and chat model versions. By using the delta logits, the effectiveness
843
      of large chat models can be approximated through logits interpolation with large base models. Addi-
      tionally, Shen et al. (2024) propose learning to collaboratively decode with multiple LLMs, includ-
844
      ing both general and specific domain models, to achieve domain generalization. Zhang et al. (2024)
845
      suggest a collaborative generation framework leveraging large and small models under cloud-device
846   scenarios that focuses on secure context-aware instruction following.
847
848   Scaling Law and Emergent Ability. The experience of a “Scaling Law” has been successfully
849
      applied across various scenarios, including model size (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022),
      data size (Sorscher et al., 2023), and reward modeling (Gao et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024), serv-
850
      ing as a pivotal driver of LLMs development. Research indicates that emergent abilities (Wei et al.,
851
      2022) manifest in larger models as dictated by the scaling law, where certain capabilities appear
852   exclusively in large models, with smaller models failing to solve the same problems effectively. In
853   contrast, recent studies suggest that emergent abilities are significantly influenced by the continuity
854   of indicators (Schaeffer et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024). When continuous metrics such as token-level
855   match are employed, minimal divergence is observed between large and small models. However,
856   employing discrete metrics like accuracy in mathematical or reasoning tasks and pass rates on coding
857   assignments reveals pronounced variations across models of different scales. This paper delves into
858   the differences in abilities between large and small models from the decoding perspective, which can
859   be quantified using continuous metrics. Through this lens, we explore the key distinctions driven by
860
      token uncertainty.
861   Totally, deeper empirical analysis is still lacking, and the quest for further efficiency optimization
862   continues. This paper delves into the essential differences between LLMs and SLMs from the per-
863   spective of collaborative decoding. Our exploration provides new insights into various methods and
      significantly enhances the efficiency and quality of the aforementioned techniques.

927   D     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
928
929
930
      In speculative decoding, the number of tokens generated by draft small models is set as a hyperpa-
      rameter, denoted as K. In our empirical study, we define K = 1 as representing oracle decoding
931
      rather than speculative decoding. Although the experimental results are equivalent, implementing
932
      and analyzing K = 1 is simpler.
933
934   For each task across all methods, we adapt in-context few-shot to enhance generation performance
935   under base models, using 5-shots for MMLU, 10-shots for MBPP, and 8-shots for GSM8k. The
936
      few-shot examples are unified into a chain-of-thought format (Fu et al., 2023a).
937   Note that we regard the outputs from collaborative decoding as references; therefore, we do not
938   compute accuracy for each task. By default, results from collaborative decoding are generally bet-
939   ter than those from either smaller or larger models alone. Our primary focus is on exploring the
940   differences between larger and smaller models, rather than comparing them to the ground truth.
941   Additionally, our intent was to compare the top-1 token selected by the SLM and the collaborative
942   models under greedy decoding. While the final token is obtained using arg max logits, this approach
943   is effectively equivalent to using probabilities after applying softmax. Therefore, we use logits
944   throughout the main content, although probabilities could also be used for better understanding. For
945   contrastive decoding, we primarily follow the implementation in (O’Brien & Lewis, 2023) rather
946   than previous works (Li et al., 2022). This approach directly utilizes unnormalized scores (logits)
947   assigned by the amateur and expert models. In current work, we use greedy decoding to compute
948
      the matching rate of tokens between small and large language models. This choice aligns with our
      initial motivation of achieving collaborative decoding with minimal intervention in small models,
949
      treating the collaborative decoding results as golden tokens. For scenarios where exact matching is
950
      less critical and the focus shifts to performance-speed optimization, other sampling techniques can
951   be explored. These techniques might yield better performance with reduced collaboration frequency,
952   leading to more efficient collaborations. However, quantifying results becomes more challenging
953   due to the increased uncertainty introduced by sampling. We believe this is an exciting direction
954   for future research, as it opens up possibilities for balancing efficiency and performance through
955   alternative decoding strategies.
