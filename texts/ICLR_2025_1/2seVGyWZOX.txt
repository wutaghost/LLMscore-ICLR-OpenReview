                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent research in point cloud perception has achieved considerable progress in
012
               enhancing scene understanding by means of vision-language alignment through
013            large language models (LLMs). However, existing methods may still encounter
014            challenges in handling complex instructions that require accurate spatial reason-
015            ing, even if the 3D point cloud data has provided detailed spatial cues such as
016            size, position, and orientation for identifying the targets. To tackle this issue,
017            this study introduces a new 3D multi-modal LLM framework, Spatial Relation
018            Reasoning (SR2 ). This framework is designed to strengthen relational reasoning
019            capabilities in 3D environments. SR2 mimics human reasoning behavior by first
020            broadly identifying all relevant elements and then carefully examining them to
021
               determine the target. In addition, as current datasets may not comprehensively
               evaluate the complex spatial reasoning capabilities of various models, we pro-
022
               pose a new benchmark named 3D ReasonSeg that consists of 25,000 and 4,152
023
               high-quality samples for training and evaluation respectively. Both quantitative
024            and qualitative experiments demonstrate that SR2 and 3D ReasonSeg effectively
025            endow 3D point cloud perception with stronger spatial reasoning capabilities, and
026            we hope that the proposed SR2 and 3D ReasonSeg can serve as a new baseline and
027            benchmark for future work. The code and model will be made publicly available.

050   1    I NTRODUCTION
051
052   In recent years, multi-modal large language models (MLLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023;
053   Ye et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) have managed to connect
      language and vision, bringing in a new era of artificial intelligence. These models have shown


                                                                            1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      unprecedented abilities, enabling various fields to understand and create textual and visual content,
055   such as human-computer interaction, multimedia understanding, embodied intelligence, and more.
056
057   Based on the MLLMs, recent works (Chen et al., 2024a; Hong et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Huang
058
      et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2023) have attained outstanding 3D point cloud perception capabilities by
      offering deeper vision-language reasoning, i.e., 3D MLLMs. However, we observed that the exist-
059
      ing 3D MLLM-based methods might not be capable of handling instructions that demand intricate
060
      spatial reasoning. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the base model is unable to locate the target even when
061   the relational hints are directly provided. This prompts us to investigate why, compared to the 2D
062   images, the 3D point cloud can provide more accurate and detailed spatial statistics such as size,
063   location, orientation, and relationships with surrounding objects, but the models may still encounter
064   challenges in effectively utilizing this information. We believe that an explicit relational reasoning
065   pipeline is crucial to guide the model in acquiring such reasoning ability.
066
      To this end, we introduce the Spatial Relational Reasoning (SR2 ) pipeline to enhance the spatial
067   reasoning capabilities of the 3D MLLM through two key steps: 1) Reasoning Prior Learning and 2)
068   Prior-guided Refinement. In the first step, the model is trained to generate spatial priors that offer
069   pertinent clues for the target by identifying the target-relevant objects. Subsequently, in the second
070   step, hidden representations are refined based on these reasoning priors from the first step to aid
071   the spatial reasoning process in pinpointing the final target. In other words, the two steps of SR2
072   emulate human reasoning behavior, i.e., initially identifying all relevant elements broadly and then
073   scrutinizing them closely to pinpoint the target.
074   Moreover, we noticed that the queries requiring reasoning in the existing datasets tend to be overly
075   simplistic and ambiguous, as shown in Fig. 5. Consequently, we present a 3D reasoning segmenta-
076   tion dataset called 3D ReasonSeg, which covers object interactions, appearances, and relative spatial
077   relationships. This dataset is designed to improve and comprehensively assess the relational reason-
078   ing capabilities of 3D MLLM in 3D scene comprehension. 3D ReasonSeg comprises 25,000/4,152
079   training/validation samples, respectively, and employs rule-based filtering, including eliminating in-
080   correct spatial relations based on object coordinates, to ensure the data’s quality. When trained with
081
      the proposed SR2 framework and the 3D ReasonSeg dataset, notable performance enhancements
      can be observed across popular benchmarks including ScanRefer and ScanQA.
082
083   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
084
085       • We propose the Spatial Relational Reasoning (SR2 ) pipeline with two key steps (Reasoning
086         Prior Learning and Prior-guided Refinement) to enhance the spatial reasoning capabilities of
087         3D MLLM by emulating human reasoning behavior.
088       • We construct a 3D reasoning segmentation dataset called 3D ReasonSeg to improve and com-
089         prehensively assess the intricate relational reasoning capabilities of 3D MLLM in 3D scene
090         comprehension.
091
092       • Comprehensive experiments demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach. SR2 exhibits
093
            the potential to function as a versatile method for enhancing other dense perception frame-
            works.

      2.1    3D SCENE UNDERSTANDING
099
100
      In tasks related to 3D scene understanding, models need to accurately detect scene objects and com-
101
      prehend their characteristics (Chen et al., 2023; 2022b; Cai et al., 2021; Kolodiazhnyi et al., 2024;
102   Shi et al., 2019; Misra et al., 2021). Among these tasks, 3D segmentation is especially challenging
103   as it involves assigning a label to each point in a point cloud. On the other hand, language can
104   enhance model performance by facilitating their tasks, as demonstrated in 3D Question Answering
105   and 3D Referring tasks (Azuma et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023;
106   Huang et al., 2022). In these tasks, the model is required to respond to user inquiries or identify
107   objects based on descriptions, highlighting the importance of the model’s language comprehension
      and scenario reasoning abilities.

      Also, the integration of language has supported models in transitioning from closed-set to open-set
109   scenarios, such as in 3D Open Vocabulary Segmentation (Peng et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024; Chen
110   et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2023; Takmaz et al., 2023). This step is critical as it allows the model to
111   break free from being constrained by the finite set of categories in the training dataset. While most
112   task-oriented studies focus on specific objectives, recent advancements in 3D multi-modal large
113   language models have enabled addressing multiple 3D downstream tasks using these models (Chen
114   et al., 2024a; Huang et al., 2023b). Furthermore, the assistance of 3D multi-modal large language
115   models has empowered models to tackle more intricate object localization tasks Zhu et al. (2024);
116   Chen et al. (2024b).
117   However, these studies have not yet addressed the intricate spatial reasoning capabilities needed. In
118   this work, we introduce Spatial Relation Reasoning and 3D ReasonSeg to address complex spatial
119   reasoning challenges.

184   3.1   BASE M ODEL
185
186   Our base model comprises two parts: a multi-modal large language model (MLLM) and a Segmen-
187   tation Head. Drawing inspiration from the work of Lai et al. (2024), we have augmented the LLM’s
188
      vocabulary with a specialized [SEG] token which guides the segmentation head to yield the corre-
      sponding segmentation result. This enhancement empowers LLM with sophisticated segmentation
189
      capabilities, expanding its functional repertoire beyond language understanding.
190
191   Multi-modal Large Language Model. There are three key components in the adopted MLLM
192   for feature extraction: a visual encoder, a Q-Former, and a large language model (LLM). Initially,
193   the point cloud Xp is processed through the visual encoder to extract dense features. To optimize
194
      computational efficiency, we employ super-point Landrieu & Simonovsky (2018) within the visual
      backbone, aggregating the dense visual features into super-point features fp to reduce the following
195
      computational burden. Subsequently, the Q-Former Li et al. (2023) compresses the scene’s infor-
196
      mation into several latent queries ql based on the super-point features fp and text embedding w of
197   the instruction. After that, the latent queries q̂l will serve as the visual input, along with the text
198   embedding w, to be processed by the large language model FLLM . This process is formulated as:
199
200
                                       fp = Fenc (Xp ),   q̂l = FQ (ql , w, fp ).                          (1)
201
202
                                               ytxt = FLLM (q̂l , w).                                      (2)
203
204   Inside the Q-Former FQ , ql is concatenated with w to be the query in both the self-attention and
205   cross-attention layers of FQ , while fp serves as the key and value in the cross-attention:
206
207
                                    ql , w = SelfAttention(Concat(ql , w)),                                (3)
208                           ql , w = CrossAttention(Concat(ql , w), fp , fp ).                           (4)
209
210   Segmentation Head. If the output of LLM, i.e., ytxt , contains [SEG] tokens, it means that the
211   current query requires segmentation predictions. Then, the corresponding hidden features of the
212
      [SEG] token, i.e., hseg , are processed by the segmentation head Fdec that follows the decoder
      structure of Kirillov et al. (2023) to yield the requisite segmentation mask M , based on the reasoning
213
      between hseg and fp . The process can be formulated as:
214
215
                                               M = Fdec (hseg , fp ).                                      (5)

217   model’s output, allowing for the fusion of linguistic and visual information processing.

      3.2       S PATIAL R ELATION R EASONING
220
221   The base model can decently handle dense point cloud perception based on user queries. However,
222   in our observations, the direct instruction tuning performed in Lai et al. (2024) did not consider the
223   spatial relationships among objects in the scene, which hinders the ability to engage in complex
224   spatial reasoning among these objects, as illustrated in Fig. 9. Hence, to address this issue, we
225   suggest that the model initially identifies the objects related to the target, serving as the reasoning
226   prior to guide the target localization. This process is thus termed as Spatial Relation Reasoning
227   (SPR), and it contains two steps as follows.
228   Step 1: Reasoning Prior Learning. While the model may encounter challenges in understanding
229   intricate inter-object relationships, it can recognize objects relevant to the target. As depicted in
230   Fig. 2, despite the model’s limitation in accurately segmenting the chair beside the desk supporting
231   a monitor, it demonstrates the ability to detect desks and chairs close to desks. Given this capa-
232   bility, we may instruct the model to initially segment approximate question-related objects, thus
233   establishing the visual reasoning prior as the hint to help identify the final target.
234   In particular, following the process of the base model, we may first utilize an instruction structured
235   as: “Given the 3D scene, the question is [QUESTION]. Please segment
236   the question-related objects” 1 . The corresponding instruction feature w will be pro-
237   cessed by Eq. 1 and Eq. 2 to obtain the text output ytxt from which we can obtain the [SEG] tokens
238   representing these target-relevant objects. Subsequently, the segmentation head decodes the hidden
239   features hseg of [SEG] tokens into masks Mr for these target-relevant objects.
240   After that, we adopt mask pooling to aggregate the super-point features fp with masks Mr , yielding
241   the representations fr for the target-relevant objects. Both the masks Mr and features fr of the
242   target-relevant objects will serve as the reasoning priors in the subsequent step. The above process
243   can be formally written as:
244
245                                                Mr = Fdec (fp , hseg ),                       fr = MaskPooling(fp , Mr )         (6)

295   Training prior sampling. The proposed SPR utilizes the mask predictions of relevant objects as the
296   reasoning prior to steer the enhancement of the latent query and text embedding. Nonetheless, we
297
      have been observed that directly employing this approach could result in overfitting during model
      training. This arises because the relevant objects from the text ground truth remain constant during
298
      training, while in inference, the model struggles to identify all relevant objects, leading to a disparity
299
      between training and testing scenarios.
300
301   To alleviate this issue, we introduce a strategy during training where we randomly omit between zero
302   and half of the associated objects. This approach aims to inject diversity into the training dataset,
303
      simulating scenarios where the model may encounter incomplete reasoning priors during inference.

325   object based on a question that does not explicitly mention it. The model needs to understand the
326   visual characteristics, functions, and relationships of the objects described in the query and then

407   Implementation Details. We adopt the encoder of a pre-trained Oneformer3D as visual backbone
408   and the decoder of it as the mask decoder, and a pre-trained OPT-1.3B Zhang et al. (2022) as the lan-
409   guage model. To preserve the learned knowledge of the pre-trained language model, we completely
410
      freeze the parameter of the language model while fully training the parameters of all other modules.
      Note that, we found it is necessary to also fine-tune the visual backbone. We adopt the AdamW
411
      Loshchilov (2017) optimizer with a weight decay of 0.1 and a learning rate decaying from 1e-4 to
412
      1e-6 with a cosine annealing scheduler. We randomly sample 300k points from each 3D scene as
413   the 3D input. We conduct experiments on eight Nvidia A100 (80G) GPUs.

415   Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. In this study, we assess our methodologies on ScanQA Azuma
416
      et al. (2022), ScanRefer Chen et al. (2020), and our novel 3D ReasonSeg dataset. ScanQA is a
      dataset for the 3D question answering task, evaluating visual and spatial comprehension of 3D en-
417
      vironments. The evaluation metrics of ScanQA include BLEU-4, CIDEr, METEOR, and Rouge-L.
418
      ScanRefer evaluates the capability to localize 3D objects using natural language descriptionsthe.
419   Prediction accuracy is measured by the percentage of predictions with Intersection over Union (IoU)
420   above 0.25 and 0.5 compared to ground truth masks. For 3D ReasonSeg, the model is required to
421   focus on object attributes and relationships to reason, answering questions and localizing target ob-
422   jects. Following Lai et al. (2024), the general Intersection over Union (gIoU) metric is used, defined
423   as the average of all per-scene Intersection-over-Unions (IoUs). Detailed formulations of these tasks
424   can be found in the supplementary material.

      3D Question Answering tasks the model with responding to questions based on comprehensive
791   knowledge of a 3D scene. We utilize a structured format such as: ”USER: Given the 3D scene,
792   please answer the question: [QUESTION]. ASSISTANT: [ANSWER]”, where [QUESTION]
793   and [ANSWER] are sourced from the ScanQA dataset.

      Scene Description requires the model to describe the entire scene using natural language. We
795
      adopt a format like: ”USER: Describe this scene. ASSISTANT: [DESCRIPTION]”, where
796
      [DESCRIPTION] is derived from 3D-LLM.

798   3D Instance Segmentation task involves segmenting object categories within a scene. Our template
799   is: ”USER: Please segment the category in this scene. ASSISTANT: Sure, the segment results are
800
      [SEG]...[SEG]”, where the [CATEGORY] is from ScanNet200 and each [SEG] corresponds to an
      individual object.
801
802   3D Referring Segmentation requires the model to segment a target object based on a brief de-
803   scription. Our template is: ”USER: Please segment this object: [DESCRIPTION]. ASSISTANT:
804   Sure, the segment result is [SEG]”, where the [DESCRIPTION] is from ScanRefer and the [SEG]
805   represents the segmentation mask of the target object.
806   3D Reasoning Segmentation necessitates understanding the visual attributes, functions, and rela-
807   tionships of objects in the query, followed by using reasoning to segment the target object. Our
808   template is: ”USER: Given the 3D scene, please answer the question with segmentation mask:
809   [QUESTION]. ASSISTANT: [ANSWER]”, where [QUESTION] and [ANSWER] are sourced
      from the 3D ReasonSeg dataset.
