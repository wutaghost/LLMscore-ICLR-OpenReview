009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) have emerged as promising models for neu-
012            romorphic computing, but training them effectively remains challenging due to
013            their discrete and temporal nature Pfeiffer & Pfeil (2018). This paper introduces a
014            novel meta-learning framework for SNNs that dynamically adjusts synaptic plas-
015            ticity parameters, enabling the network to adapt its learning process over time. Our
016            approach integrates local spike-timing information with global task performance,
017            bridging the gap between biologically-inspired local learning rules and gradient-
018
               based optimization methods. We parameterize synaptic update rules and opti-
               mize these parameters using a two-loop meta-learning process inspired by Model-
019
               Agnostic Meta-Learning Finn et al. (2017). Extensive experiments on CIFAR-10,
020
               CIFAR-100, and DVS-CIFAR10 datasets demonstrate that our method outper-
021            forms existing SNN training approaches, achieving higher accuracy and faster
022            convergence. For instance, on CIFAR-10 with a Spiking ResNet-18 architec-
023            ture, our method achieves 91.5% accuracy, surpassing state-of-the-art methods
024            like STBP Wu et al. (2018) and EIHL Jiang et al. (2024) by 1.8% and 1.3%, re-
025            spectively. Furthermore, our approach shows improved generalization to unseen
026            tasks and robustness to input noise. Analysis of the learned plasticity parame-
027            ters reveals a heterogeneous distribution, aligning with biological observations of
028            meta-plasticity in neural systems Abraham (2008). This work contributes a signif-
029
               icant advancement in SNN training methodologies, offering insights into adaptive
               neural computation and opening new avenues for neuromorphic learning systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) have emerged as a promising class of neural networks that more
035   closely mimic biological neuronal processing by utilizing discrete spike signals for communication
036   and computation Maass (1997). Unlike traditional artificial neural networks (ANNs), which rely on
037   continuous activation functions, SNNs process information in both spatial and temporal domains,
038   enabling them to capture rich temporal dynamics inherent in biological systems.
039
      However, training SNNs effectively remains a significant challenge due to their non-differentiable
040
      nature and the complex temporal dynamics of spikes Pfeiffer & Pfeil (2018). The discrete and
041   event-driven characteristics of spikes hinder the direct application of gradient-based optimization
042   methods commonly used in ANNs. This limitation has led to the development of alternative training
043   strategies, broadly categorized into local and global learning methods.
044
      Local Learning Methods such as Spike-Timing-Dependent Plasticity (STDP) adjust synaptic
045
      weights based on the precise timing of pre- and post-synaptic spikes Song et al. (2000). While
046
      STDP is biologically plausible and computationally efficient, it struggles with scaling to deep net-
047   works and complex tasks due to its reliance on local information and lack of a global error signal.
048
049   Global Learning Methods, on the other hand, involve techniques like Spatio-Temporal Backprop-
050
      agation (STBP) that extend backpropagation to SNNs by approximating gradients through surrogate
      functions Wu et al. (2018). These methods achieve higher accuracy on complex tasks but at the cost
051
      of increased computational complexity and reduced biological plausibility.
052
053   Recently, hybrid approaches have been proposed to combine the strengths of local and global learn-
      ing. The Excitation-Inhibition Mechanism-assisted Hybrid Learning (EIHL) algorithm introduces a


                                                        1
054
      balance between excitatory and inhibitory synaptic interactions to integrate local STDP updates with
055   global error signals Jiang et al. (2024). EIHL dynamically adjusts network connectivity, switching
056   between local and global learning based on the network’s excitation state, thereby improving per-
057   formance while maintaining sparsity.
058
      Despite these advancements, there remains a gap in enabling SNNs to adaptively adjust their learn-
059
      ing rules in response to task demands and environmental changes. In biological neural systems,
060
      synaptic plasticity is not static; it is modulated by higher-order processes, allowing organisms to
061   learn how to learn Hosp & Luft (2018). This concept of meta-plasticity—the plasticity of synaptic
062   plasticity—plays a crucial role in cognitive functions such as learning and memory formation.
063
064
      In this work, we propose to fill this gap by introducing a meta-learning framework for SNNs that
      enables dynamic adjustment of synaptic plasticity parameters. Our hypothesis is that by optimizing
065
      the parameters governing synaptic updates through meta-learning, the network can learn to adapt its
066
      learning process over time, leading to improved adaptability and performance on complex tasks.
067
068   Specifically, we present a novel algorithm where the synaptic update rules are parameterized, and
069   these parameters are optimized using meta-learning techniques. The synaptic weight update ∆wij
070
      between pre-synaptic neuron i and post-synaptic neuron j is given by:
071
072                                          ∆wij = ηij · f (si , sj ),                                   (1)
073
      where ηij is the meta-learned learning rate for the synapse, and f (si , sj ) is a function representing
074
      the dependence on pre-synaptic spike si and post-synaptic spike sj timings. By allowing ηij to
075
      be adjusted through meta-learning, the network effectively learns how to modify its own synaptic
076   plasticity in response to task performance.
077
078   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
079
             • We introduce a meta-learning algorithm for SNNs that dynamically adjusts synaptic plas-
080            ticity parameters, integrating both local spike-timing information and global task perfor-
081            mance.
082
             • We demonstrate through extensive experiments on benchmark datasets that our proposed
083            method outperforms existing training approaches, achieving higher accuracy and faster
084            convergence.
085
             • We provide insights into the biological plausibility of our approach, drawing parallels with
086            meta-plasticity mechanisms observed in neuroscience.

113   learning algorithms.

142

143
      The Hybrid Plasticity (HP) model Wu et al. (2022) combines STDP and backpropagation by main-
144   taining separate synaptic weights for local and global updates, which are then combined:

153   introduces a dynamic balance between excitatory and inhibitory synapses to integrate local STDP

      2.3   M ETA -L EARNING IN N EURAL N ETWORKS
163
164   Meta-learning, or ”learning to learn,” has gained traction as a method for improving the adaptability
165   and generalization of neural networks Finn et al. (2017); Vanschoren (2018). In the context of
166   ANNs, Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) Finn et al. (2017) enables rapid adaptation to new
167   tasks by optimizing initial parameters that are sensitive to changes in the loss function.
168   Meta-Learning in SNNs is less explored due to the complexity of incorporating meta-learning al-
169   gorithms with spiking dynamics. Some studies have attempted to apply meta-learning for continual
170   learning in SNNs, but their approaches often simplify the spiking dynamics or do not fully leverage
171   the temporal aspects of SNNs.
172
173   2.4   B IOLOGICAL I NSPIRATION : M ETA -P LASTICITY
174
175   In neuroscience, meta-plasticity refers to the modulation of synaptic plasticity itself, influenced by
176   neuromodulators such as dopamine and serotonin Hosp & Luft (2018); Abraham (2008). These
177   neuromodulators adjust the learning rates and plasticity rules based on the organism’s experiences
178
      and environmental context.
179   For instance, dopamine signals are associated with reward prediction errors and can modulate the
180   strength and direction of synaptic changes Schultz et al. (1997). The synaptic updates can be influ-
181   enced by a global reward signal R(t), modifying the traditional Hebbian learning rule:
182
183                                            ∆wij = ηR(t)si sj ,                                         (6)
184
185   where η is the learning rate, and si , sj are the firing rates of pre- and post-synaptic neurons, respec-
186   tively. Such reward-modulated Hebbian learning rules have been shown to facilitate reinforcement
187   learning in neural networks Frémaux & Gerstner (2016).
188
189   2.5   G APS AND O UR C ONTRIBUTION

191   In contrast to previous studies, our method:

193
             • Integrates meta-learning directly into the synaptic update mechanism of SNNs without
               simplifying spiking dynamics.
194
195          • Combines the strengths of local and global learning in a unified framework, leveraging
196            meta-plasticity principles observed in biological systems.
197          • Demonstrates superior performance on benchmark datasets, validating the effectiveness of
198            dynamic synaptic plasticity through meta-learning.

      3.1   M ETHODOLOGY OVERVIEW
203
204   Our approach introduces meta-learning into the synaptic plasticity mechanism of SNNs by param-
205   eterizing the synaptic update rules and optimizing these parameters based on task performance.
206   Specifically, we aim to learn the learning rates ηij for each synapse, allowing the network to adapt
207   its plasticity during training.
208   The synaptic weight update between pre-synaptic neuron i and post-synaptic neuron j is defined as:
209
210
211                                           ∆wij = ηij · f (si , sj ),                                   (7)
212
      where f (si , sj ) is a function of the pre-synaptic spike si and post-synaptic spike sj . The learning
213
      rates ηij are not fixed but are meta-parameters that are optimized through a higher-level learning
214
      process.
215
      The meta-learning optimization follows a two-loop process:


                                                          4
216
             • Inner Loop: Updates synaptic weights wij using the current plasticity parameters ηij on a
217            batch of data.
218
219          • Outer Loop: Updates the plasticity parameters ηij based on the performance over multiple
220
               batches, optimizing for the task loss L.
221
222
      This framework allows the network to adjust its own learning dynamics, effectively learning how to
      learn.

527   5     C ONCLUSION
528
529   Our method aligns with biological observations of meta-plasticity, where synaptic plasticity itself is
530   subject to modulation based on experience Abraham (2008). The learned distribution of plasticity
531
      parameters ηij mirrors the heterogeneous plasticity observed in biological neural networks, where
      different synapses adapt at different rates. While our method shows significant improvements, it in-
532
      troduces additional computational overhead. The scalability to very large networks and datasets may
533
      be constrained by the increased memory and time requirements. Future work could explore more
534   efficient optimization techniques to mitigate these costs. The experimental results validate our hy-
535   pothesis that integrating meta-learning into synaptic plasticity enhances the learning capabilities of
536   SNNs. The dynamic adjustment of learning rates allows the network to focus on critical connections
537   and adapt more effectively to the task at hand. This approach bridges the gap between biological
538   plausibility and computational performance, offering a promising direction for future research in
539   adaptive neural computation.
