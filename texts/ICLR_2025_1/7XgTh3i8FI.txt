                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Existing training methods for Transformer-based large language models (LLMs)
012            rely on massive amounts of data training from scratch, which requires a high cost
013            in terms of compute and time. Recent studies have demonstrated the great poten-
014            tial of improving the LLM’s training efficiency by growing from small pre-trained
015            models to large ones—a technique known as model growth. There are two main
016            research problems associated with model growth: growth schedule and growth
017            operators. Existing research focuses on growth operators, detailing specific ma-
018            nipulations of potential dimensions to expand Transformer parameters. Few stud-
019            ies have investigated the optimal growth schedule, which involves integrating all
020
               possible growth operators to create an optimal multi-staged growth path. This
               work introduces SLOP, a Schedule Learning methodology via an Optimal Path for
021
               multi-stage growth of models with minimal experimental training. SLOP utilizes
022
               marginal utility as an appropriate measure for an optimal schedule that balances
023            training costs and model performance after multi-stage growth. With this mea-
024            surement, the objective of determining the optimal growth schedule is converted
025            into a dynamic programming problem, which is then solved mathematically in
026            polynomial time. Experimental results illustrate SLOP’s theoretical validity as
027            well as its efficiency, outperforming alternative schedules in a range of settings.
028
029
030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Transformer-based large language models (LLMs), such as GPT (Radford et al., 2019) and T5 (Raf-
033
      fel et al., 2020) have demonstrated impressive emergent abilities across various tasks. Existing LLM
      training methods, on the other hand, require enormous amounts of data training from scratch, which
034
      is both computationally and time costly. To save costs, there is a growing interest in effective pre-
035
      training paradigms. One promising research direction (Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Yao
036   et al., 2023) is growing from small pre-trained models to large ones, known as model growth. In
037   practice, Li et al. (2023) trained a large-scale 100B-parameter model from a 16B-parameter model
038   utilizing model growth methods.
039
      There are two main research problems associated with model growth: determining the optimal
040
      growth schedule, and designing efficient growth operators (Yao et al., 2023). Existing works (Gu
041
      et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2015) primarily focus on growth oper-
042   ators, detailing specific manipulations of potential dimensions (such as layers, hidden states, etc.) to
043   expand Transformer parameters. They also investigate ways to inherit knowledge from the smaller
044   model by developing initialization methods for the newly extended parameters and taking the entire
045   training state as input (e.g., the optimizer state, the learning rate schedule).
046
      Research on the growth schedule is limited. The methodology involves integrating all possible
047
      growth operators to create an optimal multi-staged growth path. At each stage, one dimension is ex-
048   panded to develop an intermediate structure until the entire target LLM structure is attained. Existing
049   works either adopt a single-stage growth without consideration for model schedules (Gong et al.,
050   2019; Gu et al., 2021), or focus on determining the optimal scheduling using empirical insights,
051   even though it may be theoretically suboptimal (Shen et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023). Ultimately, es-
052   tablishing an optimal schedule for multi-staged model growth necessitates consideration of several
053   fundamental challenges. 1) What is an appropriate measurement for an optimal schedule? 2) How
      are growth operators implemented sequentially, and what number of parameters are inserted at each


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      stage? 3) The exponential search space required for trial training makes it prohibitively expensive
055   to explore all possible growth paths (from the initial small model to the target large model) in order
056   to choose the most optimal one.
057
058
      To address the aforementioned issues, we present a Schedule Learning methodology via Optimal
      Path, abbreviated as SLOP, for multi-stage growth of models with limited experimental training ef-
059
      forts. For Transformer-based LLMs, SLOP considers all possible expansion dimensions, expanding
060
      one dimension per stage, but ignores more complex cases where several dimensions compound to
061   increase per stage, leading to a search space burst. It is worth noting that within our framework,
062   depth-only or width-only growth may be considered a specific case.
063
064
      Specifically, we formulate the problem of finding the optimal schedule for multi-stage model growth.
      The marginal utility (Samuelson, 1937) is used as an appropriate measure for an optimal schedule
065
      that balances training costs and model performance after multi-stage growth. With this measure-
066
      ment, we can consider the task of determining an optimal growth schedule as a dynamic program-
067   ming problem. Finally, we demonstrate that the dynamic programming problem enables the theoreti-
068   cal resolution of an optimal schedule in polynomial time, reducing the computational effort required
069   for trial training within the exponential search space.
070
      To validate the correctness of SLOP’s theoretical reasoning results, we conduct experiments by
071
      expanding various starting model sizes (e.g., 100M, 450M) to 1 billion decoder-only target LLMs.
072
      It also shows that SLOP outperforms alternative schedules in a variety of scenarios, resulting in a
073   reduction in computational usage. Further ablation studies are conducted to evaluate our approach
074   on various growth scenarios.

077   2     R ELATED W ORK
078
079   Efficient LLM training. Efficient pretraining of large language models aims to reduce FLOPs.
080   Recent research focuses on stagewise efficient pretraining (Panigrahi et al., 2024), progressive pre-
081   training, or model reusing (Chen et al., 2015; 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023). Specifically,
082
      model reusing involves maintaining the function of a pre-trained model as its size increases, result-
      ing in an initial state that performs well even when scaled to a larger model. Net2Net (Chen et al.,
083
      2015) is the first to introduce the concept of function-preserving transformations in model reusing,
084
      expanding the width dimension by splitting neurons and increasing the depth by adding identity
085   layers. Bert2BERT (Chen et al., 2021) applies function-preservation to the Transformer structure,
086   extending Net2Net’s concept. LiGO (Wang et al., 2023) recently utilizes a trainable linear opera-
087   tor to develop an efficient expansion strategy. ELLE (Qin et al., 2022) employs function-preserving
088   model expansion within specific domains, leveraging pre-trained domain prompts to efficiently adapt
089   to emerging data over time. Our method concentrates on the model growth schedule, an area that
090   previous studies have rarely addressed.
091   Model growth schedule. The formulation of a model growth schedule is an essential research
092   topic due to the rising prominence of model reuse and progressed pre-training. Gong et al. (2019)
093   and Gu et al. (2021) utilize heuristics rules that divide the training steps into distinct expansion
094   stages to determine the training schedule. Shen et al. (2022) identifies optimal growth schedules that
095   maximize compute savings by applying scaling laws to initiate a new stage when training efficiency
096   decreases. The most relevant work for us is MSG (Yao et al., 2023), which provides empirical
097   insights for constructing an efficient schedule considering all possible growth dimensions. However,
098   they implement an empirical optimal solution that demonstrates practical efficiency, despite the fact
099
      it may be theoretically suboptimal. Existing works have not systematically or theoretically explored
      methodologies for identifying optimal growth schedules by relying instead on empirical results,
100
      allowing more space for our method’s innovation in the field of optimal growth schedule learning.

174   Each feasible Mk structure possesses A44 schedules by enumerating all possible Φ =
175   {ϕlayer , ϕmha , ϕf f n , ϕhidden } combinations. Each operator is restricted to a single use at each
176   stage, thereby ignoring the occurrence of compound operator scenarios and reducing the schedule
177
      search space. Finding an optimal multi-staged growth path, which consists of multiple growth op-

178
      limited training time is not trivial.

180   Definition 1: Given a computing budget of C and the desired model parameter of N , an opti-
181   mal training schedule identifies the optimum sequence of growth operators and intermediate model
182   structures at each stage to use the least amount of computing power while maintaining target model
183
      performance.

185   3.3   M EASUREMENT OF OPTIMAL SCHEDULE USING M ARGINAL U TILITY
186
187
      To find an optimal schedule, we need to answer the first question: what is an appropriate measure-
      ment for an optimal schedule? We propose determining a good schedule measurement based on
188
      model performance and training costs. The model performance is evaluated using loss or perplex-
189
      ity (ppl) values, following most of the prior work for model optimization (Hoffmann et al., 2022).
190   Figure 1(b) shows the relationship between ppl and training time, which can reflect the training
191   efficiency of LLMs. It is non-trivial to find an appropriate objective function to optimize them
192   simultaneously.
193
      To address this problem, we borrow the concept of marginal utility in economics (Samuelson, 1937)
194
      and propose using the marginal utility of schedule (MUS) as the optimization objective. MUS
195
      evaluates the gains (reduction in ppl) that a model may obtain from an increase in cost (training
196   time). Formally, MUS represents the derivation of the reduction of ppl to training time, which is
197   calculated as:

533   5      C ONCLUSION
534
535   This study examines optimal model growth schedule learning problems, concentrating on determin-
536   ing a suitable sequence that integrates several operators to enhance performance for the target LLM.
537   We present a cost-effective optimal path learning method within the framework of a multi-stage
538   model growth scenario that could attain theoretically optimal results. Observe that we examine a
539   straightforward scenario in which each growth dimension occurs just once along the path. We will
      leave more complex scenarios for future work.

656
657   In this work, we propose a theoretical solution for finding the optimal growth schedules for multi-
658   stage growth involving all possible dimensions. However, we do not consider complex cases when
659
      multiple growth dimensions can combine at the same stage and execute more than once. Our work
      establishes a starting point for the development of self-adaptive growth schedules, vital to the ef-
660
      ficient pre-training of LLMs. Another limitation is that, due to limited computing capacity and
661
      budget, the largest models in our experiments have 1 billion parameters, which is a significant dif-
662   ference from existing LLMs. This constraint is present in the vast majority of research projects,
663   according to our knowledge.

      E    C ASES FOR THE GROWTH SCHEDULE PATHS
837
838
839
      Figure 7 illustrates that the expansion of the Transformer model is configured with four dimensions:
      hidden dim, ffn dim, head number, and layer number. We select one dimension for each expansion,
840
      resulting in a total of four expansions. The number of parameters in the model expands from a to b.
841
      For more complex scenarios in which each dimension can undergo multiple repetitions during the
842   expansion process, such as hidden dim, ffn dim, head number, hidden dim, and layer number, the
843   entire search space becomes significantly larger and more complicated. In this paper, we omit this
844   case, focusing only on expanding each dimension once along the expansion path.
845
      As an example depicted in Figure 7, let’s assume the initial model’s four-dimensional parameters
846
      are set to (384, 1024, 6, 6), and through four rounds of expansion, the target parameter for the target
847
      model is achieved at 1B. If the target model’s four-dimensional parameters are (768, 2048, 12, 12),
848   we can calculate the number of possible path choices for the expansion schedule by multiplying the
849   factorial of the number of steps in each dimension, which comes out to 4 ∗ 3 ∗ 2 ∗ 1 = 24. It is
850   essential to recognize that various configurations exist for the structure of the target model. Another
851   possible configuration is (1536, 4096, 32).
852
      It is obviously impractical to traverse each schedule and select the final optimal one. Therefore,
853
      our goal is to transform this into an optimization problem of model metrics. Through a series of
854   derivations, we can select the most suitable schedule before training to reduce the computational
855   cost of achieving the optimal model.
