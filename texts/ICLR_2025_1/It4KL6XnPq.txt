                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            A generalist agent should perform well on novel tasks in unfamiliar environ-
011            ments. While Foundation Policies (FPs) enable generalization across new tasks,
012            they lack mechanisms for handling novel dynamics. Conversely, agents equipped
013            with memory models can adapt to new dynamics, but struggle with unseen tasks.
014            In this work, we bridge this gap by integrating memory models into the FP
015            architecture, allowing policies to condition on both task and environment dy-
016            namics. We evaluate FPs enhanced with attention, state-space, and RNN-based
017            memory models on POPGym, a memory benchmark, and ExORL, an unsuper-
018
               vised RL benchmark. Our results show that GRUs achieve the best generaliza-
               tion to unseen tasks and dynamics for a given recurrent state size, approach-
019
               ing the performance of a supervised baseline that has access to task informa-
020
               tion during training and significantly outperforming memory-free FPs. Addi-
021            tionally, our approach improves FP performance on entirely new environments
022            not encountered during training. Our anonymized code is available at https:
023            //anonymous.4open.science/r/zero-shot-96A1, and our datasets
024            are open-sourced at REDACTED.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents [92] exhibit superhuman decision-making skill when tasked
033
      with a single objective in a single environment [89, 67, 90, 91]. A new line of work focuses on
      producing generalist agents that replicate such results across many tasks and environments [83, 53,
034
      105]. Foundation policies (FPs) [96, 97, 76, 45] are a promising approach for building generalist
035
      agents, providing a principled mechanism for generalising to any downstream task in an environment
036   after an offline reward-free pre-training phase. However, as yet, FPs are not equipped to deal with a
037   change in dynamics between pre-training and deployment.
038
039
      A concurrent line of work on in-context RL attempts to build generalist agents by using memory
      models to condition policies on reward-labelled trajectories [14, 43, 56, 54, 62, 23] or to reach arbi-
040
      trary goal states [31]. In principal, these models can perform dynamics generalisation by inferring
041
      changes between training and testing from the trajectory used to condition the policy. However, they
042   lack the task generalisation ability of FPs for two reasons. They are either 1) trained with reward
043   supervision and so cannot reliably generalise to new tasks with different reward functions, or are 2)
044   trained without reward supervision to reach any goal-state in an environment and so cannot reliably
045   generalise to new tasks not codified by a goal state.
046
      Here, we reconcile these lines of work and propose foundation policies with memory, an architecture
047
      that, like in-context RL agents, infers the current dynamics context using powerful memory models
048   and passes it to an FP for solving unseen tasks. We evaluate FPs with attention [98], state-space
049   [32, 33], and RNN-based [24, 17] memory models across a range of experiments testing their ability
050   to infer the dynamics context, and generalise to unseen tasks in unseen dynamics. We find that
051   GRUs achieve the best generalisation to unseen tasks and dynamics for a given recurrent state size,
052   approaching the performance of a supervised baseline that has access to task information during
053   training and significantly outperforming memory-free FPs (Figure 1). Finally, we find that FPs with
      memory improve FP performance on entirely new environments not seen during training.

126   Recall that our goal is to pre-train an adaptive policy π(a|h, z) that is conditioned on h, a hidden state

      4          E XPERIMENTS
198
199
      In this section we perform an empirical study to evaluate our proposed method. We seek answers
200
      to three questions: (Q1) Can our method encode trajectories into a Markov state for use in solving
201
      one task in an environment? (Q2) Can our method generalise to unseen tasks in an environment
202   with different dynamics to those seen in training? I.e. can our method perform zero-shot RL under
203   changed dynamics? And (Q3) Can our method generalise to unseen tasks in a completely different
204   environment to those seen in training? I.e. can our method perform zero-shot environment generali-
205   sation?

      Environments. We respond to Q1 using the POPGym benchmark [68], a set of tests that evaluate
210   an agent’s ability to infer Markov states from trajectories of observations and actions. We only eval-

212   Previous environments following [62]. For these experiments, we allow the agent to recondition
213   its policy on the previous L observation-action pairs every step so we can disentangle the memory
214   model’s ability to accurately model the Markov state from its ability to carry forward an accurate
215   hidden state. For all other experiments we do not allow such re-conditioning and require the policy
      to condition on only the previous hidden state, current observation-action pair, and task. Note for

      Baselines. We use FB [96] and HILP [76] as our FP baselines. FB is the most performant FP
239
      utilising successor measures, and HILP is the most performant FP utilising successor features. Both
240
      methods assume access to the Markov state for training as discussed in Section 2. So, instead of
241   conditioning their predictions on a single observation, we provide them a stack of the 4 most recent
242   observations i.e. st = (ot−3 , ot−2 , ot−1 , ot ), also known as frame-stacking [67]. Frame-stacking
243   is a naive method for inferring a Markov state from a short trajectory of observations, and is the
244   first solution one would use when faced with our problem. We also baseline against a single-task,
245   memory-based, supervised RL method. For this we use Offline TD3 [28] with a GRU memory
246   model [17], which we refer to as TD3-GRU. Offline TD3 is the most performant single-task method
247   on the ExORL benchmark [108]; TD3-GRU is the most performant method in [73], and TD3 with an
248   LSTM memory model was shown to be particularly performant in [66]. TD3-GRU should indicate
249
      how well an agent optimising for one task performs if provided reward supervision.
250   Datasets. Though FPs can be deployed online they require an exploration policy for data col-
251   lection. To disentangle test-time performance from an agent’s data collection ability, we collect
252   datasets on their behalf in advance using RND [11], an unsupervised RL algorithm. Agents trained
253   on datasets collected with RND exhibit better performance than comparable methods like APS [60],
254   APT [61], Proto [107] and DIAYN [25] in [108, 97, 76]. RND is run for 5 million learning steps
255
      in each of our environments and every transition is cached. For the supervised baseline TD3-GRU,
      transitions are relabelled with the appropriate rewards for a given task following [108]. All other
256
      methods are trained on these datasets reward-free.
257
258   Memory models. We test the performance of FPs equipped with three memory models. We use
259   the most performant versions from each of the categories discussed in Section 3: attention-based,
260   state-space based, and RNN-based. For our attention-based memory model we use a Transformer
261
      [98] with FlashAttention [19] for faster inference than a conventional Transformer. For our state-
      space-based memory model we use Diagonalized S4 [33], which uses a diagonal update matrix to
262
      perform faster training and inference than the popular S4 model [32]. For our RNN-based memory
263
      model we use a GRU [17] as it is the most performant RNN on the POPGym benchmark. Hereafter
264   we refer to the FB models we augment with these as FB-TF, FB-S4 and FB-GRU respectively.

269   perform their tensor products across the full input trajectory, and gives the RNN two 512-dimension

354   5     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
355
356   Context length. In Section 4 we train agents with a context length L = 64 timesteps, which is the
357   maximum context length we could afford with our computational budget3 . We see two limitations
358   with this. First, we have assumed the dynamics context and task for all of our experiments can be
359   inferred from this context, but it is not clear that this is the case. Indeed, TD3-GRU with reward
360   supervision and a maximally exploratory dataset does not match its performance with Markov states
361
      from [97]. Second, successful episodes run for a minimum of 200 timesteps (as in PendulumHard)
      and a maximum of 1000 timesteps (as in ExORL), meaning we never train the memory model
362
      over full episodes, nor can we reliably maintain an episode’s full trajectory in context at test-time.
363
      This introduces situations where the hidden state will be erroneously initialised mid-episode during
364   training, creating well-established theoretical issues for memory models in RL [68], though these
365   are yet to prove critical empirically [73, 66].
366
367
      The obvious solution to these problems, were it available to us, would be to increase L until it is the
      maximum episode length, and train for longer as in [31, 62, 68]. However, even if we were to do this,
368
      any real-world deployment may induce episodes longer than this assumed max length, or indeed we
369
      may wish to operate in the non-episodic, continual setting. The existing literature implicitly assumes
370   that if L is very large such issues will resolve themselves, but this is not clear to us. Exploring how
371   to deal with such situations is an important future research direction.
372
373
      Datasets. As outlined in Section 4.1, we train all methods on datasets pre-collected with RND
      [11] which is a highly exploratory algorithm designed for maximising data heterogeneity. However,
374
      deploying such an algorithm in any real setting may be costly, time-consuming or dangerous. As a
375
      result, our proposals are more likely to be trained on real-world datasets that are smaller and more
376

      6   R ELATED W ORK
386
387
      Generalist policy pre-training FPs build upon successor representations [20], universal value
388   function approximators [85], successor features [6] and successor measures [10]. The state-
389   of-the-art methods instantiate these ideas as either universal successor features (USFs) [11] or
390   forward-backward (FB) representations [96, 97], with recent work showing they can be trained
391   on low quality datasets [45], or used to perform a range of imitation learning techniques effi-
392   ciently [80]. A representation learning method is required to learn the features for USFs, with
393   past works using inverse curiosity modules [79], diversity methods [60, 38], Laplacian eigenfunc-
394   tions [101], or contrastive learning [16]. No works have yet explored the generalisation capac-
395   ity of FPs to unseen dynamics. Two concurrent lines of work on goal-conditioned RL and in-
396
      context RL also seek to build generalist policies. Goal-conditioned RL methods train policies to
      reach any goal state from any other goal state [75, 63, 104, 26, 99], but lack the ability to gen-
397
      eralise to tasks with “dense” reward functions, like those on the locomotion tasks in ExORL. In-
398
      context RL methods train policies using sequence models conditioned on reward-labelled histories
399   [14, 43, 58, 83, 110, 13, 30, 88, 103, 102, 31, 62, 94, 23], but, unlike FPs, do not have a robust
400   mechanism for training without access to rewards.
401
402
      Dynamics Generalisation Dynamics generalisation in RL is a well-established problem [50, 65,
      74]. Common remedies include: data augmentation [81, 5, 106, 37, 36, 55], domain randomisation
403
      [95, 21, 46, 47, 77], learning context-aware policies [87, 57, 9, 44], and meta-learning [15, 83, 27,
404
      71, 72]. Our work is most similar to those that tackle dynamics generalisation by conditioning
405   policies on dynamics inferred with a memory model [73, 18]. Where these methods are concerned
406   with generalising to one unseen task in unseen dynamics contexts, our method can generalise to
407   more than one unseen tasks in unseen dynamics contexts.
408
409
410
      7   C ONCLUSION
411
      In this paper, we explored augmenting Foundation Policies (FPs) with memory models to allow
412
      them to condition policies on a dynamics context inferred from a history of observations and ac-
413
      tions. We evaluated our proposals with attention, state-space, and RNN-based memory models on
414   POPGym, a memory benchmark, and ExORL, an unsupervised RL benchmark. Our results show
415   that GRUs achieve the best generalisation to unseen tasks and dynamics for a given recurrent state
416   size, approaching the performance of a supervised baseline that has access to task information dur-
417   ing training and significantly outperforming memory-free FPs. We believe our proposals represent
418   a further step toward the development of generalist, adaptive agents.

867   A.1   POPG YM
868
869   We consider 5 environments from the POPGym benchmark [68] which is built atop the OpenAI
870
      gym [12]. Each tests the agents ability to summarise a trajectory of observations and actions into
      a Markov state for use in solving one downstream task. Following [62] we only consider the hard
871
      variants, because the other variants are considered too straightforward.
872
873   Stateless CartPole Hard. The cartpole environment from [7], but with the angular and linear
874   positions removed from the observation. The agent must integrate to compute positions from ve-
875   locity and balance the pole atop the cart to receive reward. The hard variant requires the pole to be
876
      balanced for 600 timesteps (the easy and medium variants require the pole to be balanced for fewer
      timesteps).
877
878   Noisy Stateless CartPole Hard. The same as Stateless CartPole Hard but with Gaussian noise
879   added to observations. The hard variant sets the standard deviation of the noise σ = 0.3 (the easy
880   and medium set σ = 0.1 and σ = 0.2 respectively).
881   Stateless Pendulum Hard. The swing-up pendulum [22] with the angular position information
882   removed. The agent must integrate to compute positions from velocity and swing the pendulum up
883   to receive reward. The hard variant requires the pendulum to be balanced for 200 timesteps (the
884   easy and medium variants require the pole to be balanced for fewer timesteps).
885
      Noisy Stateless Pendulum Hard. The same as Stateless Pendulum Hard but with Gaussian noise
886   added to observations. The hard variant sets the standard deviation of the noise σ = 0.3 (the easy
887   and medium set σ = 0.1 and σ = 0.2 respectively).
888
889
      Repeat Previous Hard.        Observations contain one of four values. The agent is rewarded for
      outputting the observation from some constant k timesteps ago, i.e. observation ot−k at time t. The
890
      hard variant sets k = 64 (the easy and medium variants set k < 64).
891
892
893   A.2   E X ORL
894
895   We consider 4 environments (three locomotion and one goal-directed) from the ExORL benchmark
896   [108] which is built atop the DeepMind Control Suite [93]. We occlude their states by removing
897   all velocity components, similar to [73, 66]. Environments are visualised here: https://www.
898   youtube.com/watch?v=rAai4QzcYbs. The domains are summarised in Table 1.
899
      Walker-Occluded. A two-legged robot required to perform locomotion starting from bent-kneed
900   position. The observation and action spaces are 17 and 6-dimensional respectively (after occlusion),
901   consisting of joint torques and positions. ExORL provides 4 tasks stand, walk, run and
902   flip. The reward function for stand motivates straightened legs and an upright torso; walk and
903   run are supersets of stand including reward for small and large degrees of forward velocity; and
904   flip motivates angular velocity of the torso after standing. Rewards are dense.
905
      Quadruped-Occluded. A four-legged robot required to perform locomotion inside a 3D maze. The
906   observation and action spaces are 67 and 12-dimensional respectively (after occlusion), consisting of
907   joint torques and positions. We evaluate on 4 tasks stand, run, walk and jump. The reward
908   function for stand motivates a minimum torso height and straightened legs; walk and run are
909   supersets of stand including reward for small and large degrees of forward velocity; and jump
910   adds a term motivating vertical displacement to stand. Rewards are dense.
911
      Maze-Occluded. A 2D maze with four rooms where the task is to move a point-mass to one of
912   the rooms. The observation and action spaces are both 2-dimensional (after occlusion); the obser-
913   vation space consists of x, y positions of the mass, the action space is the x, y tilt angle. ExORL
914   provides four reaching tasks top left, top right, bottom left and bottom right
915   corresponding to each room. We add four other goals in each room following [97] to provide a total
916   of 20 goal reaching tasks. The mass is always initialised in the top left and the reward is proportional
917   to the distance from the goal, though is sparse i.e. it only registers once the agent is reasonably close
      to the goal.


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Cheetah-Occluded. A running two-legged robot. The observation and action spaces are 10 and
919   6-dimensional respectively (after occlusion), consisting of positions of robot joints. We evaluate on
920   4 tasks: walk, walk backward, run and run backward. Rewards are linearly propor-
921   tional either a forward or backward velocity–2 m/s for walk and 10 m/s for run.

      A.3   E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
924
925
      We evaluate the cumulative reward (hereafter called score) achieved by all methods across three
926   seeds in POPGym and 5 seeds in ExORL. We report task scores as per the best practice recom-
927   mendations of [1]. Concretely, we run each algorithm for 500k learning steps (1m for ExORL),
928   evaluating task scores at checkpoints of 20,000 steps. At each checkpoint, we perform 10 rollouts,
929   record the score of each, and find the interquartile mean (IQM). We average across seeds at each
930   checkpoint. We extract task scores from the learning step for which the all-task IQM is maximised
931   across seeds. Results are reported with their associated standard deviation. Aggregation across
932   tasks, domains and datasets is always performed by evaluating the IQM.
