011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            This work answers the question of whether it is feasible to create a compre-
014            hensive metric-semantic 3D virtual world using everyday devices equipped with
015            multi-view stereo. We propose an open-ended metric-semantic representation
016            learning framework based on 3D Gaussians, which distills open-set semantics
017            from 2D foundation models into a scalable and continuously evolving 3D Gaus-
018
               sian representation, optimized within a SLAM framework. The process is non-
               trivial. The scalability requirements make direct embedding of semantic informa-
019
               tion into Gaussians impractical, resulting in excessive memory usage and seman-
020
               tic inconsistencies. In response, we propose to learn semantics by aggregating
021            from a condensed, fixed-sized semantic pool rather than directly embedding high-
022            dimensional raw features, significantly reducing memory requirements compared
023            to the point-wise representation. Additionally, by enforcing pixel-to-pixel and
024            pixel-to-object semantic consistency through contrastive learning and stability-
025            guided optimization, our framework enhances coherence and stability in semantic
026            representations. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our framework presents
027            a precise open-ended metric-semantic field with superior rendering quality and
028            tracking accuracy. Besides, it accurately captures both closed-set object categories
029
               and open-set semantics, facilitating various applications, notably fine-grained, un-
               restricted 3D scene editing. These results mark an initial yet solid step towards
030
               efficient and expressive 3D virtual world modeling. Our code will be released.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   A 3D virtual world functions as a collective space where user avatars interact seamlessly within
037   a metric-semantic representation of 3D environments, encompassing both appearance and seman-
038   tics (Gupta et al., 2009; Dionisio et al., 2013). Recent technological strides, seen in platforms like
039
      VisionPro (Apple, 2023) and Metaverse (Meta, 2024), signify a transition towards richer content,
      vast expanses, and the ambitious goal of encompassing the entire Earth within this digital realm (Li
040
      et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023; Puig et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Cai et al., 2024). This progres-
041
      sion raises a fundamental query: is it possible to develop an open-ended metric-semantic 3D virtual
042   world using everyday devices equipped with multi-view stereo?
043
044   Such a system should not only capture the spatial layout but also incorporate semantic information
045
      crucial for meaningful interactions. It must possess three essential characteristics: i) scalability to
      adapt limitlessly to evolving environments; ii) open-ended semantic framework capable of accom-
046
      modating new concepts and free-form queries; iii) efficiency in rendering speed and memory usage to
047
      ensure portability as scene complexity increases. While recent breakthroughs like Neural Radiance
048   Fields (NeRFs) (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and Diffusion models (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Rombach
049   et al., 2022) have enabled photorealistic representations of intricate 3D scenes, yet their slow train-
050   ing and inference speeds impede their practicality (Barron et al., 2021; 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Sun
051   et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2022), particularly in expanding applications. On the other hand, recent
052   progress such as 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) has notably enhanced train-
053   ing and rendering speeds, holding promise for real-time novel view synthesis. Nonetheless, these
      advancements focus primarily on visual fidelity, still overlooking the intrinsic semantics within 3D

073   This work aims to bridge this gap by presenting an open-ended 3D metric-semantic representation
074   learning framework based on 3DGS. The core idea is to distill open-set semantics from 2D founda-
075   tion models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) into a scalable and con-
076
      tinuously evolving 3D Gaussian representation, optimized within a simultaneous localization and
      mapping (SLAM) framework. One straightforward approach involves embedding feature dimen-
077
      sions to each 3D Gaussian, alongside color information, to represent additional semantics. However,
078
      this process encounters two inherent challenges as environments evolve gradually. First, memory
079   and computation consumption can become prohibitively large, significantly reducing optimization
080   and rendering efficiency with respective to the number of Gaussian points and the dimension of the
081   learned feature. Existing solutions often restrict feature dimensions to a smaller scale (Qin et al.,
082   2024), i.e., 3 vs. 512 (the original feature dimension of CLIP), to mitigate this issue, albeit at the
083   cost of semantic expressiveness. Second, semantic inconsistency across viewpoints. As depicted
084   in Fig. 1, due to the 2D nature of off-the-shelf foundation models, ensuring semantic coherence for
085   identical objects across continuous view renderings cannot be guaranteed, thereby impeding effec-
086   tive learning of semantic fields.
087   Focusing on the aforementioned two intrinsic challenges, we first integrate the Semantic Feature
088   Aggregation mechanism into the framework. Instead of directly expanding the semantic feature
089   channel, this module associates each Gaussian with a low-dimensional key vector to access point-
090   specific semantics from a fixed-size learnable semantic feature pool. This pool holds a condensed
091   representation of open-set semantics across the entire scene, significantly smaller in scale compared
092   to the number of Gaussian points, i.e., 200 vs. 5 × 106 . This mechanism not only mitigates the sub-
093
      stantial memory requirements but also diminishes the redundancy of local semantic features. Given
      that local Gaussians representing the same object should exhibit similar semantics, this approach
094
      promotes more effective semantic field learning. Next, to improve semantic consistency during op-
095
      timization, the approach is intuitive: ensuring that the semantic representation of any pixel in a 2D
096   image aligns with neighboring pixels from the same object (intra-frame) and pixels at the same 3D
097   locations aligned with camera poses from previous frames (inter-frame). We employ contrastive
098   learning to enforce these correspondences and introduce the Intra-Inter Semantic Consistency Ob-
099   jective during semantic field learning. Finally, to further address semantic ambiguity, which arises
100   when different semantic labels are attributed to the same object, hindering field learning, we intro-
101   duce Semantic Stability Guidance. By measuring the inter-frame pixel-to-object semantic consis-
102   tency through cosine similarity, we leverage this metric to adjust the learning signal. Signals from
103   inconsistent regions are reduced, while those from consistent regions are amplified, enhancing the
104
      overall coherence and stability of semantic representations within the framework.

107   2024; Keetha et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c) SLAM methods on the Replica dataset (Straub et al.,
      2019) in §3.2. Experimental results demonstrate that our framework establishes a precise open-

      2.1        F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
142
143   Given an RGB-D stream, our framework aims to reconstruct a scalable Gaussian field enriched with
144   open-set semantic information. Since assigning a high-dimensional semantic feature to each Gaus-
145   sian is both computationally expensive and spatially redundant, we introduce the Semantic Feature
146   Aggregation, allocating semantic features only to Gaussians involved in rendering and optimiza-
147   tion. To further address inconsistencies of the open-set semantic features generated across frames,
148   we introduce the Intra-Inter Semantic Consistency Objective technique. Additionally, to reduce the
149
      impact of inaccurate new-income semantic features to the reconstructed semantic field, we develop
      a Semantic Stability Guidance. An overview of our framework is presented in Fig. 2.
150

177   Semantic Feature Aggregation. We enhance each Gaussian with an additional semantic feature.
178   Previous NeRF-based methods (Kerr et al., 2023) often directly integrate high-dimensional seman-
179   tic features into the scene. However, assigning such features to every Gaussian greatly increases
180
      memory demands, and many Gaussians are inactive during rendering, which reduces efficiency.
      Additionally, unlike color maps, objects of the same category share identical semantics, making re-
181
      dundant features unnecessary. To address this, we propose using a semantic feature pool to reduce
182
      memory and assign features efficiently to active Gaussians.
183
184   Specifically, we assign a key k̂ to each Gaussian, projecting keys of active Gaussians into high-
185   dimensional features. To facilitate this, we develop a learnable key pool K = {kl |l = 1, 2, · · · , M }

187   We calculate the similarity between each Gaussian’s key k̂ and the keys in the key pool, aggregating
188   the semantic feature fˆ from the most similar key:

200   2.3   S EMANTIC -E MBEDDED SLAM F RAMEWORK
201
      Our SLAM framework begins by processing RGB images to generate semantic feature frames with
202
      pre-trained models (supp. B.1). Given a Gaussian field constructed from frames 1 to t, along with
203   new RGB, depth, semantic feature frames at t + 1, the framework performs tracking and mapping.
204
205   Tracking. Camera tracking determines the current camera position using incoming data to estimate
206
      relative motion. During tracking, only camera parameters are optimized, with Gaussians parame-
      ters fixed. When initializing with the first frame, the camera tracking stage is bypassed. For each
207
      subsequent timestep, the camera pose is estimated by forward-projecting pose parameters from the
208
      camera center into quaternion space. Consequently, the camera parameters are initialized as:
209
                                          Et+1 = Et + (Et − Et−1 ).                                   (7)
210
211   Mapping. The mapping process generates an open-ended metric-semantic spatial representation
212   of the scene. Using the camera’s tracked position and depth frames, it refines the map by adding
213   Gaussians to underdeveloped areas. Unlike in the tracking phase, the poses remain fixed during
214   mapping, while Gaussian parameters are updated. We optimize these parameters through differen-
215   tiable rendering of RGB, depth, and semantic feature frames using gradient-based techniques. Large
      or low-opacity Gaussians are removed, as outlined in (Kerbl et al., 2023).

531
      We propose a novel method for generating a comprehensive metric-semantic 3D virtual world using
532
      multi-view stereo. By integrating 3D Gaussian representations with open-set semantics derived from
533
      2D foundation models, our approach enables the creation of scalable and evolving 3D representa-
534   tions within a SLAM framework. To address the significant memory and computational challenges,
535   we introduce Semantic Feature Aggregation. Furthermore, we incorporate the Intra-Inter Semantic
536   Consistency Objective and Semantic Stability Guidance to ensure that the semantic reconstruction
537   is both consistent and stable. Our experimental results demonstrate the potential of our open-ended
538   3D metric-semantic representation, opening up new possibilities for a wide range of downstream
539   applications. We believe that this work marks a significant step forward in detailed, semantically
      rich 3D environments and efficient, expressive virtual world modeling.

822
823
      3D Scene Representation. In recent years, NeRFs (Mildenhall et al., 2020) have emerged as a
824   pivotal advancement in the fields of 3D reconstruction and open-set semantic segmentation (Kerr
825   et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Engelmann et al., 2024), celebrated for their ability to
826   synthesize high-quality novel views. However, despite these advancements, NeRF-based method-
827   ologies are inherently constrained by certain limitations. Training times remain relatively slow,
828   and for open-set semantic segmentation, their implicit representations complicate precise 3D region
829   identification. In contrast, 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023), an explicit representation
830   technique, offers a more suitable framework for accurate region identification, while maintaining
831   reconstruction quality and significantly improving efficiency. Although several concurrent works
832
      (Shi et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2024; Zuo et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2024; Qu et al.,
      2024; Dou et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Liao et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) have advanced this field,
833
      these methods face challenges when applied to arbitrary scenarios, particularly in large-scale envi-
834
      ronments or tasks requiring continuous scene expansion. In response, our approach leverages SLAM
835   to achieve the reconstruction of any scene, aiming to achieve fast, efficient, and highly adaptable 3D
836   reconstruction capabilities, thereby meeting the demands of a wider range of practical applications.
837
838
      Dense Visual SLAM. A range of works (Sucar et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Rosinol et al., 2023;
      Yang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Johari et al., 2023; Sand-
839
      ström et al., 2023) have furthered the development of SLAM with NeRFs (Mildenhall et al., 2020)
840
      through innovations like hierarchical multi-feature grids, uncertainty estimation, and improved loss
841   functions. Nonetheless, these methods continue to grapple with the limitations posed by implicit
842   representations. The advent of 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) has spurred a variety
843   of works (Keetha et al., 2024; Matsuki et al., 2024; Yugay et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024; Huang
844   et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Sandström et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024; Ha et al.,
845   2024; Sun et al., 2024b; Peng et al., 2024) that have made substantial strides in SLAM, resulting
846   in enhanced reconstruction quality and processing speed. However, these works focus primarily on
847   improving the RGB map, while overlooking the fact that a map with semantic information is more
848   essential for expanding the applications of SLAM in various downstream tasks. To address this,
849
      both NeRF-based (Haghighi et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2024b) and 3DGS-based (Li
      et al., 2024c; Ji et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024a) approaches have attempted to integrate semantics into
850
      SLAM. However, most of these methods are trained in a highly supervised approach on closed-sets
851
      data. Although they enrich the scene information, they lack the flexibility to continuously adapt to
852   any scene and more downstream tasks. Our work endeavors to introduce precise open-set semantic
853   features into SLAM systems, thereby significantly enhancing the system’s ability to comprehend
854   arbitrary scenes and facilitating more effective downstream tasks.
855
      Contrastive Semantic Learning. To obtain open-set semantic features, we need to process each
856
      frame using a pre-trained model. Although this ensures the simplicity of using only RGB-D im-
857
      ages inputs, the pre-trained model cannot guarantee the consistency of semantic features of the
858   same object across different frames. Therefore, we introduce the idea of contrastive learning. In
859   2D self-supervised representation learning, instance discrimination (Dosovitskiy et al., 2014) has
860   achieved substantial progress as a pre-training task for visual representations. While notable trans-
861   fer learning performance has been demonstrated for image classification (Chen et al., 2020a;b; He
862   et al., 2020), instance discrimination treats entire images as holistic entities, overlooking the com-
863   plex internal structures of natural images. To address this, research has shifted towards pixel-level
      (Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021b) and object-level (Hénaff et al., 2021; 2022;


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Wei et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2021a) discrimination, leading to the enhancement
865   on the intrinsic structure of images and better transfer performance on dense prediction tasks. In
866   contrast to 2D, self-supervised representation learning in 3D is still emerging. Early works (Has-
867   sani & Haley, 2019; Sanghi, 2020; Sauder & Sievers, 2019; Wang & Solomon, 2019) focused on
868   object-centric point cloud data (Chang et al., 2015), but these methods do not facilitate 3D scene
869   understanding (Xie et al., 2020). More recent studies (Hou et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2021; Wu
870   et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023) have developed
871   3D self-supervised representation learning on scene-centric data (Dai et al., 2017), significantly im-
872   proving performance across a range of 3D scene perception tasks. Inspired by these advancements
873
      in contrastive learning, we introduce the idea of contrastive learning into semantic reconstruction.
      By applying 2D pixel-level semantic discrimination, we can reconstruct more accurate 3D semantic
874
      fields, thereby enhancing scene reconstruction quality and improving downstream task performance.

      B.3.2   P RECISE E DITING
952
953   Editing specific objects without unintentionally affecting surrounding areas, such as the background,
954   often involves using masks that restrict loss computation to the desired pixels. However, this ap-
955   proach faces significant challenges. First, updating masks during the editing process incurs sub-
956   stantial computational costs. Additionally, as the editing progresses, mask generation may become
957
      inaccurate, leading to cumulative errors. In contrast, static masks might not adequately support
      precise edits and could limit modifications that exceed the mask’s boundaries. Second, within the
958
      rendering pipeline, the impact is not confined solely to the NeRF regions or Gaussians covered by
959
      the mask; parts not visible in the current view, which, however, theoretically lie inside the mask
960   region, can still be inadvertently modified. This interaction complicates the management of error
961   propagation, particularly when utilizing 2D masks.
962
963
      To address these challenges, our methodology leverages the embedded semantic information in our
      Gaussian representation. This allows us to accurately identify the specific Gaussians that need mod-
964
      ification. Specifically, we employ the CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) to extract features from the
965
      input text, evaluate their similarity to the semantic features embedded in the Gaussians, and selec-
966   tively refine the relevant Gaussians for the editing task. This approach effectively improves editing
967   precision by directly interacting with the scene’s underlying representation.
968
969
      During the editing process, we randomly select a view obtained from §B.3.1 to render the original
      image Iori , and utilize InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) to generate the edited image Iedit . We
970
      then compute the perceptual loss (Johnson et al., 2016) between them:
971
                                    Lediting = Perceptual Loss(Iori , Iedit ).                         (20)

973    This iterative approach ensures a seamless editing outcome while preventing discrepancies across
974    views that could arise from a single editing pass.
