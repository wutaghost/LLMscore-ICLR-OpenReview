011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) has become a
014            cornerstone in multimodal intelligence. However, recent studies have identified
015
               that the information loss in the encoding process of CLIP is substantial. Such defi-
               ciency significantly limits the ability of a single CLIP model to handle images rich
016
               in visual detail. In this work, we propose a simple yet effective model-agnostic
017
               strategy, Diversified Multiplet Upcycling (DMU) for CLIP. It integrates multi-
018            ple CLIP models that capture diversified, complementary information into a Mix-
019            ture of Experts (MoE) architecture. Inspired by the recently proposed Multistage
020            Contrastive Learning (MCL), which constructs multiple CLIP models that share
021            the same structure while capturing different complementary information, Diver-
022            sified Multiplet Upcycling efficiently fine-tunes a series of CLIP models from a
023            dense pre-trained CLIP checkpoint to capture different feature distributions, shar-
024            ing parameters except for the Feed-Forward Network (FFN). These models are
025            then transformed into a CLIP-MoE with a larger model capacity but minimal
026
               computational overhead. Extensive experiments demonstrate the significant per-
               formance of CLIP-MoE across various zero-shot retrieval, zero-shot image clas-
027
               sification tasks, and downstream Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM)
028
               benchmarks by serving as a vision encoder. Furthermore, Diversified Multiplet
029            Upcycling enables the conversion of any dense CLIP model into CLIP-MoEs,
030            which can seamlessly replace CLIP in a plug-and-play manner without requiring
031            further adaptation in downstream frameworks. Through Diversified Multiplet Up-
032            cycling, we aim to provide valuable insights for future research on developing
033            more efficient and effective multimodal learning systems.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) is a strong vision-language
039
      foundation model that utilizes large-scale datasets to learn comprehensive visual representations by
      bridging vision and language via contrastive image-text pre-training. Beyond traditional tasks like
040
      classification, CLIP has been broadly applied in areas such as image (Wang et al., 2023; Zhang
041
      et al., 2023), audio (Guzhov et al., 2022), and video (Rasheed et al., 2023) understanding, cross-
042   modal retrieval (Ma et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2024), multimodal generation (Ramesh et al., 2022;
043   Xie et al., 2024), and data filtering (Schuhmann et al., 2022). Additionally, CLIP serves as the vision
044   encoder for various Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
045   2024b;c; Chen et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024c).
046
      However, existing CLIP models still face inherent limitations. Recent studies have highlighted that
047
      CLIP often encodes inputs in a very coarse-grained manner, overlooking much useful informa-
048   tion (Tang et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024b; Bleeker et al., 2022). As a result, CLIP frequently pro-
049   duces blind pairs (Tong et al., 2024b), where two semantically different images with similar visual
050   components are encoded into the same representation. This leaves downstream models with insuf-
051   ficient information, especially when CLIP serves as a vision encoder. Such substantial information
052   loss negatively impacts downstream tasks and can confuse downstream models, such as the base
053   LLMs in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). To address this issue and enhance CLIP’s
      ability to encode richer information, efforts have been made to improve the quality of training data


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and to scale up model size. However, these works typically involve retraining the CLIP model from
055   scratch (Li et al., 2024b; Ma et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023), which is both resource-intensive and
056   costly. Additionally, there are attempts to ensemble different types of vision encoders (Tong et al.,
057   2024b; Shi et al., 2024), which makes the entire model heterogeneous and total parameters grow
058   explosively.
059
      To address the above limitations, we propose a simple yet effective model-agnostic strategy, Diver-
060
      sified Multiplet Upcycling (DMU), for CLIP, which leverages the sparsely activated Mixture of
061   Experts (MoE) framework to extend model capacity while fully utilizing off-the-shelf pre-trained
062   dense checkpoints, avoiding the need for training from scratch. MoE has proven effective in scaling
063   large pre-trained models by using fixed activated parameters, enhancing both performance and ro-
064   bustness (Jiang et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). In Diversified Multiplet Upcycling,
065   we first fine-tune the base dense CLIP model to produce a series of multiplet CLIP models using the
066   recently proposed Multistage Contrastive Learning (MCL) (Zhang et al., 2024b). MCL generates
067   models that encode diversified information through a multistage clustering and fine-tuning process.
068   By multiplet, we refer to CLIP models that share all parameters except for the feed-forward network
069
      (FFN) layers during MCL fine-tuning. By diversified, we mean that these models yield a series of
      FFN experts, each capturing different aspects of the input information, which are then used to ini-
070
      tialize a CLIP-MoE model. Finally, through fine-tuning the router in CLIP-MoE, we ensure the full
071
      utilization of all experts, enabling CLIP-MoE to capture richer and more useful information than the
072   base model, while leveraging sparse activation to avoid the explosion of activated parameters.
073
074   We demonstrate that using a small high-quality image-caption dataset, our MCL-initialized CLIP-
075
      MoE significantly improves CLIP’s performance. Notably, on retrieval tasks, CLIP-MoE outper-
      forms the base OpenAI CLIP model by about 20%, while incurring minimal additional training
076
      overhead—less than 2% of the total computational cost of training the base CLIP model from
077
      scratch. When serving as a vision encoder for MLLMs, CLIP-MoE also shows substantial im-
078   provements in most benchmarks simply by replacing the original vision encoder. Our experiments
079   show that CLIP-MoE not only outperforms other fine-tuning baselines but also surpasses popular
080   MoE-construction methods like Sparse Upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al., 2022). To the best of our
081   knowledge, this work is the first to introduce sparsely activated MoE into CLIP foundation mod-
082   els, whereas previous methods have focused either on vision representation (Li et al., 2024a) or
083   model-wise ensembling (Ma et al., 2024).
084   In summary, the contributions of this work are as follows: First, we propose a novel method, Di-
085   versified Multiplet Upcycling for CLIP, which initializes a CLIP-MoE using FFN experts obtained
086   through multistage fine-tuning, offering a new pathway to effectively scale the CLIP foundation
087   model. Second, we demonstrate that our model-agnostic Diversified Multiplet Upcycling signifi-
088   cantly improves model performance by fully leveraging new high-quality data and pre-trained CLIP
089   checkpoints, while avoiding the high computational costs associated with training from scratch.
090   Third, we conduct extensive experiments, showing that our upcycled CLIP-MoE achieves signif-
091   icant performance improvements over the original CLIP and other baselines with lower computa-
092
      tional costs across various downstream tasks, including classification, retrieval, and serving as a
      vision encoder for MLLMs.

098   2.1   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
099
100   In contrastive learning, the core objective is to minimize the distance between positives and the
101   anchor while maximizing the distance between negatives and the anchor within the representation
102   space. This objective compels the model to effectively encode sufficient information of the inputs to
103   distinguish anchors from their negatives.
104   Contrastive learning has become a central technique in self-supervised learning, aiming to learn
105   representations by bringing semantically similar samples closer in the embedding space while push-
106   ing dissimilar samples apart (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020). This approach has been particu-
107   larly successful in multimodal settings, where models like Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training
      (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) have emerged as foundational tools. CLIP aligns visual and textual


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      representations by training on vast datasets of paired images and text, enabling the model to bridge
109   different modalities effectively.
110
111   Despite its success, CLIP is not without its limitations. One significant shortcoming is its tendency
112
      to encode only coarse-grained visual concepts, which can lead to the loss of fine-grained information
      that is crucial for certain downstream tasks (Tang et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024b). To address these
113
      limitations, recent works mainly focus on improving the quality of training data (Li et al., 2024b;
114
      Ma et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a). However, most of these approaches require
115   retraining the model from scratch, which is computationally expensive, time-consuming, and not
116   easily extendable when better data becomes available.
117
118   2.2   M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS
119
120   The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture could scale the model capacity without additional com-
121   putational cost (Fedus et al., 2022a). For each input token, only top-k best experts are selected to
122   obtain an aggregated representation (Shazeer et al., 2017). This sparsity allows MoE models to scale
123   to trillions of parameters while maintaining the computational efficiency (Lepikhin et al., 2020; Fe-
124   dus et al., 2022b). Due to the large model capacity, the performance could be improved by large
125
      margins (Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2024). Besides, specialized experts in MoE models are
      good at handling a wide range of tasks (Shen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024) with high
126
      robustness (Chen et al., 2024a).
127
128   However, one challenge in MoE training is expert initialization. Sparse Upcycling (Komatsuzaki
129   et al., 2022) has been proposed as a technique to initialize MoE models by copying Feed-Forward
130   Networks (FFN) from dense models as multiple experts. It selectively activates and fine-tunes only
131
      a sparse subset of parameters. This method significantly reduces the training cost.
132   In this work, we explore the integration of Multistage Contrastive Learning (MCL) with the MoE
133   architecture. By using MCL to initialize the experts, we aim to capture complementary information
134   across different CLIP experts, which can then be leveraged by the MoE structure to enhance overall
135   performance with minimal additional computational cost.

139   3.1   M ULTISTAGE C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING (MCL)
140
141   Multistage Contrastive Learning (MCL) (Zhang et al., 2024b) is designed to obtain a series of con-
142   trastive models, each capturing different and complementary information from the input data through
143   multiple cluster-and-contrastive processes. Specifically, at each stage, the learned representations
144
      are clustered. In the following stage, for each anchor, negative samples are drawn only from the
      same accumulated cluster from the previous stages. In this way, the model learns new information
145
      beyond what was captured in earlier stages. For example, consider a dataset containing objects with
146
      varying shapes, colors, and textures. In the first stage, the contrastive model might focus on learning
147   color information. After clustering, samples within the same cluster will share the same color. In the
148   second stage, since the anchor and its negative samples share the same color, the model is compelled
149   to learn other features, such as texture, to differentiate between them. After clustering in the second
150   stage, samples in the same accumulated cluster will now share both color and texture. Consequently,
151   in the third stage, the model must focus on other attributes, such as shape, to distinguish between
152   samples. After three stages, we obtain three contrastive models, each encoding distinct information:
153   color, texture, and shape.

      3.2   M IXTURE OF E XPERTS (M O E)
169
170   Mixture of Experts (MoE) is an efficient architecture designed to scale large models by dynamically
171   routing inputs through a subset of specialized sub-models, or “experts”. This structure allows the
172   model to maintain high overall capacity while only utilizing a fraction of its parameters for any given
173   input, thereby optimizing both computational efficiency and performance.
174
      In the context of Transformer, an MoE layer (Jiang et al., 2024) typically replaces the standard feed-
175
      forward network (FFN) with a set {Ei }N    i=1 of N experts, each of which is an independent FFN.
176   Given an input token representation x, it first passes through a gating network Wr to obtain the
177   logits corresponding to each expert, then the largest Top-K experts will be chosen, and finally, the
178   probabilities of these selected experts are normalized using Softmax. In this way, we can obtain the
179   probability R(x) of selected experts among all N experts. Notably, the probability of non-

      5.2   BASELINES
298
299   Direct Fine-tuning. As our experiments incorporate additional data, we use direct fine-tuning as a
300   basic baseline to evaluate the performance contributions from the additional data.
301
      Sparse Upcycling. Sparse Upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al., 2022) is a widely adopted method for
302
      initializing a Mixture of Experts (MoE) model using a pre-trained dense checkpoint. It is a simple
303   yet effective approach for scaling up a pre-trained model and is much more efficient than training an
304   MoE from scratch.
305
306
      Long-CLIP. Long-CLIP (Zhang et al., 2024a) introduces an efficient pipeline to enhance CLIP per-
      formance through fine-tuning on high-quality image-caption datasets with long captions. It aligns
307
      the long caption of an image with the encoded image features and the short caption with the primary
308
      components of the image features. While effective on the ShareGPT4V dataset, Long-CLIP is lim-
309   ited to datasets with a similar structure, where each image has both one short and one long caption.
310   Moreover, it requires significantly more computational resources compared to our approach.
311
312
      LLaVA-1.5. LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a) is an improved version of LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b),
      commonly used as a baseline for MLLMs. It bridges a pre-trained CLIP vision encoder with a pre-
313
      trained LLM using a simple MLP, enabling the LLM to gain visual understanding with minimal fine-
314
      tuning on image-text pairs. We evaluate the representation quality of our CLIP-MoE by replacing
315   the vision encoder in the original LLaVA-1.5 with our CLIP-MoE and fine-tuning it following the
316   same pipeline as LLaVA-1.5.

322   cluster the image features into 3 clusters and the text features into 3 clusters, resulting in 9 clusters

      inputs, we interpolate the positional embeddings following the approach in (Zhang et al., 2024a).

      6   C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
526
527
      In this paper, we proposed a novel Diversified Multiplet Upcycling for CLIP to enhance the model
528   with minimal computational overhead. Our method enables the extraction of diversified and comple-
529   mentary experts across multiple fine-tuning stages, which are then utilized within the MoE frame-
530   work to capture richer information from the inputs. This approach is straightforward to apply, model-
531   agnostic, and provides a new path to scale and improve CLIP foundation models. By leveraging
532   off-the-shelf CLIP checkpoints and newly constructed high-quality image-text datasets, our method
533   avoids the costly process of training CLIP models from scratch. We demonstrated the effectiveness
534   and efficiency of our approach through extensive experiments across various datasets and tasks.
535   For future work, our current experiments are limited to image and text modalities. We plan to ex-
536   tend our method to additional modalities, such as audio and video. Beyond the fine-tuning settings
537   explored in this paper, we aim to experiment with larger datasets and test large-scale continuous
538   training settings to further explore the scalability and performance boundaries of Diversified Multi-
539   plet Upcycling. Additionally, while we tested CLIP-MoE as a vision encoder for MLLMs, we will
      also investigate its potential as a text encoder in generative tasks, such as in stable diffusion.
