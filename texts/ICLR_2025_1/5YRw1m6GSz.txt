009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             Personalized Federated Learning (PFL) has gained attention for privacy-preserving
012             training on heterogeneous data. However, existing methods fail to capture the
013             unique inherent geometric properties across diverse datasets by assuming a unified
014             Euclidean space for all data distributions. Drawing on hyperbolic geometry’s
015             ability to fit complex data properties, we present FlatLand1 , a novel personalized
016
                Federated learning method that embeds different clients’ data in tailored Lorentz
                space. FlatLand is able to directly tackle the challenge of heterogeneity through the
017
                personalized curvatures of their respective Lorentz model of hyperbolic geometry,
018
                which is manifested by the time-like dimension. Leveraging the Lorentz model
019             properties, we further design a parameter decoupling strategy that enables direct
020             server aggregation of common client information, with reduced heterogeneity in-
021             terference and without the need for client-wise similarity estimation. To the best
022             of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to incorporate hyperbolic geometry into
023             personalized federated learning. Empirical results on various federated graph learn-
024             ing tasks demonstrate that FlatLand achieves superior performance, particularly in
025             low-dimensional settings.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Federated learning (FL) trains machine learning models across multiple clients while ensuring data
030   privacy. Traditional FL struggles with data heterogeneity, as one model cannot satisfy diverse
031   local requirements. Personalized federated learning (PFL) resolves this by sharing common model
032   knowledge and allowing for client-specific adaptations. PFL approaches mainly address heterogeneity
033   through three strategies during aggregation: (1) splitting models into shared and personalized
034   components (McMahan et al., 2017; Tan et al., 2023); (2) analyzing weights/gradients to evaluate
035   client similarities (Xie et al., 2021); or (3) incorporating additional modules to enable client-specific
036   customization (Baek et al., 2023). All these methods are conducted in Euclidean space.
037   Recent studies in various domains, including text (Tifrea et al., 2018; Dhingra et al., 2018), im-
038   ages (Atigh et al., 2022; Khrulkov et al., 2020), and graphs (Chami et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2023; Yang
039   et al., 2022b;a), have shown that real-world data exhibit non-Euclidean properties, such as scale-free
040   structures and implicit hierarchical relationships (Albert & Barabási, 2002; Khrulkov et al., 2020).
041   Euclidean space, being inherently “flat”, fails to adequately represent these characteristics, leading to
042   structural distortions and reduced performance (Chami et al., 2019). For example, the CiteSeer graph
043   dataset partitioned into 10 clients, shows varying degree distributions with long-tail characteristics
044
      which are poorly captured by Euclidean geometry, as illustrated in Figure 1(a). Besides, we calculate
      the Ricci curvature values of multiple real-world graph datasets after splitting them into 10 clients
045
      each and observe that they all exhibit negative Ricci curvature with significantly varying values, as
046
      shown in Figure 6. Higher absolute values indicate more pronounced non-Euclidean properties.
047
048   Moreover, embedding data from various clients into a fixed Euclidean space complicates inter-
049   pretability of model parameters. All parameters play the same role during training, obscuring which
050
      encapsulates client heterogeneity versus shared information. This makes it difficult to segment the
      model into meaningful components and assess client similarity. Additionally, incorporating extra
051
      modules to aid this process escalates complexity and reduces flexibility.

082

093
      particularly in low-dimensional representations.

097   Personalized Federated Learning With statistical heterogeneity (Kairouz et al., 2021), conven-
098   tional FL frameworks like FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) can hardly obtain a single global model
099   that generalizes well to every client (the basic framework is shown in Appendix A.4). Motivated by
100   this, researchers have proposed personalized FL (PFL) to train customized local models. Generally
101   speaking, existing PFL techniques can be categorized into the following three groups: (1) techniques
102
      that personalize client models via local fine-tuning (Fallah et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2019; Wang
      et al., 2019), (2) techniques that personalize client models via customized model aggregation (Huang
103
      et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021b; Luo & Wu, 2022; Sun et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023b; 2021b), and (3)
104
      techniques that personalize client models via creating localized models/layers (Arivazhagan et al.,
105   2019; Chen & Chao, 2022; Collins et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2020; Dinh et al., 2020; Hanzely &

109   operate in Euclidean spaces to encode data samples, which can hardly capture the scale-free property
110   and implicit hierarchical structure embedded within client data.

112   Personalized Federated Graph Learning When applied to graph data, personalized federated
113   graph learning (PFGL) can intuitively exhibit the problem mentioned above. For example, Xie
114   et al. (2021) clusters clients based on gradients to aggregate models with similar data distributions.
115   Another method (Tan et al., 2023) introduces additional personalized models to capture client-
116   specific knowledge of graph structure. Baek et al. (2023) calculates client-client similarities to
117   apply personalized model aggregation with local weight masking. All these methods learn node
118   representations in Euclidean spaces, which cannot model the power-law degree distributions that
119
      widely exist in real-world graph data (Albert & Barabási, 2002; Krioukov et al., 2010). Additionally,
      the client clustering procedure and additional model components introduce computational overhead
120
      that may not be feasible in real-world scenarios with strict privacy constraints or limited resources.
121
122
      Hyperbolic Federated Learning Very few research works have considered incorporating hy-
123   perbolic spaces into federated settings. An et al. (2024) leverages hyperbolic distances to distill
124   knowledge from the global model to the local model, to mitigate model inconsistency caused by data
125   heterogeneity. Liao et al. (2023) applies hyperbolic prototype learning to capture the hierarchical
126   structure among data samples. As the work most similar to our FlatLand, FedHGCN (Du et al.,
127   2024) is a simple combination of FedAvg and hyperbolic graph neural networks along with a node
128   selection process. Although these methods can benefit from the hyperbolic space to capture the
129   hierarchical structure in the data, they do not have the personalization capability to adaptively model
130   client data spaces with different curvatures. This may lead to suboptimal results when there is severe
131
      data heterogeneity. Therefore, our goal is to design a novel FL framework that can encode client data
      in hyperbolic spaces with adaptive curvatures using personalization techniques.

197       In "Flatland", a two-dimensional flat plane, the same shapes may represent the projections of
198
          various three-dimensional objects. For instance, a circle could be the projection of either a
          cylinder or a sphere from a higher dimension.
199
200
201   In the above case, "Flatland" captures the common feature of a cylinder or a sphere, while a higher
202
      dimension (the third dimension) highlights the differences between the objects. Analogous to our
      setting, informally speaking, by introducing an additional time-like dimension, we can imagine each
203
      client’s data residing in a unique Lorentz space (a curved world in a higher-dimensional space), where
204
      the curvature reflects the distinct distributions (objects). "Flatland", Rd (flat), serves as a metaphor
205   for a platform where common information (circle) is exchanged and integrated.

      A.2    F ORMAN -R ICCI C URVATURE
860
861   Curvature is a metric used in Riemannian geometry that expresses how far a curved line deviates
862   from a straight line, or how much a surface deviates from planarity. In this context, knowledge of the
863   local and global geometrical features depends on an understanding of sectional curvature and Ricci
      curvature, respectively Sun et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2019).


                                                              16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Sectional Curvature. This type of curvature is determined at any given point on a manifold by
865   examining all possible two-dimensional subspaces that intersect at that point. It provides a more
866   straightforward representation than the Riemann curvature tensor Lee (2018). Recent studies Chen
867   et al. (2021) often treat sectional curvature uniformly across the manifold, simplifying it to a singular
868   constant value.
869
      Ricci Curvature. Ricci curvature averages the sectional curvatures at a specific point. In graph
870
      theory, various discrete versions of Ricci curvature have been developed, such as Ollivier-Ricci
871   curvature Ollivier (2009) and Forman-Ricci curvature Forman (2003). The Ricci curvature on graphs
872   is intended to assess how the local structure around a graph edge deviates from that of a grid graph.
873   Notably, the Ollivier approach provides a rougher estimate of Ricci curvature, whereas the Forman
874   method is more combinatorial and computationally efficient.

899   A.3   L ORENTZ T RANSFORMATIONS
900
901   In special relativity, Lorentz transformations are a family of linear transformations that describe the
902   relationship between two coordinate frames in spacetime moving at a constant velocity relative to each
903   other. They can be decomposed into a combination of a Lorentz Boost and a Lorentz Rotation Moretti
904
      (2002). The Lorentz boost, given a velocity v ∈ Rn with ∥v∥ < 1, is represented by the matrix B,
      which encodes the relative motion with constant velocity without rotation of the spatial axes. The
905
      Lorentz rotation matrix R represents the rotation of spatial coordinates and is a special orthogonal
906
      matrix, i.e., R⊤ R = I and det(R) = 1.
907

909

      at a constant relative velocity, which involves a hyperbolic rotation in the space-time plane.

       B.6   T IME AND S PACE C OMPLEXITY C OMPARED WITH F EDAVG
1222
1223   We analyze the computational complexity of FlatLand compared to FedAvg, which gives insight for
1224   the scalability.
1225
1226   Local Update The additional operations in FlatLand’s local update phase compared with FedAvg -
1227   curvature estimation (Section 5.1), exponential map (line 4 in Algorithm 2, Equation 7). Notably,
1228   the curvature estimation can be pre-computed since each client’s data distribution corresponds to a
1229   constant curvature value. For exponential map, the transformation only requires a single non-linear
1230
       mapping operation based on the norm of input samples with the time complexity of O(1). These
       norms can also be pre-computed and cached. Therefore, while FlatLand introduces these additional
1231
       steps compared to FedAvg, their practical computational overhead is limited due to pre-computation
1232
       opportunities and constant-time operations.
1233
1234   Aggregation FlatLand and FedAvg have the same aggregation time complexity when the hidden
1235   embedding dimension is the same. Though FlatLand introduces extra time-like space parameters,
1236   it only aggregates shared parameters θs while maintaining personalized parameters. The overhead
1237   of the shared parameters is the same. Moreover, FlatLand can perform better in low dimensionality
1238   (Section 7.3), which potentially reduces practical communication costs.
1239
1240   Space Requirements and Storage FlatLand requires extra O(d + 1) storage per client compared
1241   to FedAvg due to the additional time-like dimension and curvature parameter, where d is the hid-
       den dimension. Since typically d is small, the increase in storage is small. Moreover, FlatLand
