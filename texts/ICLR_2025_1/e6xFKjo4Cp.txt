011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in machine learning, particularly in Natural Language Pro-
014            cessing (NLP), have led to the development of sophisticated models trained on ex-
015            tensive datasets, yet raising concerns about the potential leakage of sensitive infor-
016            mation. In response, regulatory measures such as the European Union’s General
017            Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) have driven increasing interest in Machine
018            Unlearning techniques, which enable models to selectively forget specific data
019            entries. Early approaches primarily relied on pre-processing methods, while more
020
               recent research has shifted towards training-based unlearning techniques. Despite
               their effectiveness, most existing methods require access to the original training
021
               data, which is often inaccessible. Additionally, directly applying unlearning tech-
022
               niques bear the cost of undermining the model’s expressive capabilities. To ad-
023            dress these challenges, we introduce the Iterative Contrastive Unlearning (ICU)
024            framework, which consists of three core components: A Knowledge Unlearning
025            Induction module designed to remove specific knowledge through an unlearning
026            loss; A Contrastive Learning Enhancement module to preserve the model’s ex-
027            pressive capabilities against the pure unlearning goal; And an Iterative Unlearn-
028            ing Refinement module that dynamically assess the unlearning extent on specific
029            data pieces and make iterative update. Experimental results demonstrate the effi-
030            cacy of our ICU method in unlearning sensitive information while maintaining the
031
               model’s overall performance, offering a promising solution for privacy-conscious
               machine learning applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   With the continuous evolution of machine learning, we have witnessed an unprecedented expansion
039
      in the size of models and the diversity of data used for their training. Particularly, the field of Natural
      Language Processing (NLP) has experienced remarkable progress, driven by the development of ad-
040
      vanced Generative Language Models (GLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude 3 (An-
041
      thropic, 2024), and Google Gemini (Team et al., 2023). Despite these advancements, concurrent
042   studies have highlighted a critical concern: the potential leakage of sensitive information (Carlini
043   et al., 2021), including phone numbers, email addresses, and other personal data embedded within
044   the training datasets (Brown et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022). In response to these privacy concerns,
045   regulations such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Voigt & Von dem Bussche,
046   2017) have codified the “Right To Be Forgotten” (RTBF) (Mantelero, 2013; Villaronga et al., 2018),
047   leading to the exploration of Machine Unlearning techniques. These techniques aim to make models
048   effectively “forget” specific data, treating them as if they were never part of the training dataset.
049   In the initial stages of research, various pre-processing methods were proposed to achieve unlearning
050   on certain data. For instance, Kandpal et al. (2022) found that the likelihood of Generative Language
051   Models regenerating training sequences is correlated with the frequency of those sequences in the
052   training set. They showed that deduplicating the training data makes GLMs significantly more
053   resilient against privacy attacks. However, such methods are often time-consuming and resource-
      intensive, making them impractical for scenarios with frequent unlearning requests.

071
072   More recently, researchers have shifted their focus to training-based machine unlearning approaches,
073   which modify the training process itself rather than solely manipulating the data. For example,
074
      SISA (Bourtoule et al., 2021) partitions the original dataset into several non-overlapping shards and
      then aggregates models trained on these separate shards. When handling data deletion requests, only
075
      the models trained on the affected shards need to be retrained. KGA (Wang et al., 2023) introduces
076
      an additional dataset, using it to fine-tune the original model alongside the original dataset. Nonethe-
077   less, both of these methods assume that the training data remains accessible during the unlearning
078   process. In practice, the training data for Generative Language Models may not be available after
079   model deployment, rendering such methods infeasible. A recent method, Knowledge Unlearning
080   for Mitigating Privacy Risks (KUMPR) (Jang et al., 2023), was designed to address this scenario.1
081   KUMPR reverses the training objective by maximizing the negative log-likelihood for target tokens
082   and uses metrics to determine whether the model has “forgotten” a target sequence.
083   However, directly reversing the training objective of GLMs can undermine their overall expressive
084   capabilities, beyond merely forgetting the intended information. As illustrated in Figure 1, given a
085   training sample with the prefix “Who is Harry Potter?” and its reference suffix, the goal is to make
086   the model “forget” specific knowledge, such as “J.K. Rowling” and “1997”. Although the existing
087   methods (e.g., KUMPR) successfully forget the key information contained in the reference suffix,
088   the resulting model loses its general expressive ability, often generating repetitive, nonsensical text
089   like “I have a Harry Potter”. To address this limitation, we propose an Iterative Contrastive Un-
090   learning (ICU) framework, which aims to achieve unlearning while preserving the model’s overall
091
      generalization ability.
092   More specifically, our ICU framework comprises three components: (1) a Knowledge Unlearning
093   Induction (KUI) module, which targets specific knowledge to be unlearned from the model; (2) a
094   Contrastive Learning Enhancement (CLE) module, which samples paired data from the analogous
095   documents of target knowledge. Subsequently, we further design two learning enhancement losses
096   to maintain the generalization ability against the unlearning process. (3) an Iterative Unlearning
097
      Refinement (IUR) module, which assess the unlearning extent on specific data pieces and updates
      the unlearning dataset dynamically. In brief, our contributions are as follows:
098
099   • We conduct an in-depth study on the unlearning techniques for Generative Language Models,
100     specifically focusing on maintaining the expression ability while effectively unlearning, an area
101     that has been largely overlooked in previous research.
102
      • We propose the ICU framework, which consists of three components: Knowledge Unlearning
103     Induction, Contrastive Learning Enhancement, and Iterative Unlearning Refinement.
104
      • We perform extensive experiments on three different backbone models of varying sizes,
105
        demonstrating the effectiveness of our proposed method.     Our code is available at
106
        https://anonymous.4open.science/r/unlearning-DA5C.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110
111   2.1   M ACHINE UNLEARNING
112
113   Machine unlearning, introduced by Cao & Yang (2015), aims to protect machine learning mod-
114   els from extraction attacks by removing specific data in such a way that the model behaves as if
115   the data was never part of the training set. Traditional approaches (Nguyen et al., 2022) that ex-
116   clude specific data from training datasets and retrain the model are highly time-consuming and
117
      resource-intensive, making them impractical for modern deep neural networks. Similar methods
      involving retraining (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Kumar et al., 2023) also struggle with scalability, par-
118
      ticularly when handling numerous deletion requests or when comprehensive datasets are not readily
119
      available. To address these challenges, researchers have explored approximate unlearning tech-
120   niques (Golatkar et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2019; Mehta et al., 2022). One such approach involves data
121   pre-processing, which efficiently identifies and removes sensitive information before model train-
122   ing. Kandpal et al. (2022) applied this method to structured private data, such as phone numbers
123   and medical records, and found it effective. However, challenges arise when dealing with unstruc-
124   tured data, as pre-processing may not fully remove all sensitive information (Brown et al., 2020) and
125   cannot comprehensively address ongoing deletion demands (Brown et al., 2022).
126   Recent studies (Jang et al., 2023; Kassem et al., 2023; Qu et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023; Eldan &
127   Russinovich, 2023) have focused on fine-tuning Generative Language Models to tackle machine
128   unlearning challenges. Jang et al. (2023) proposed a novel approach by reversing the traditional
129   training objective, aiming to maximize rather than minimize the negative log-likelihood of tokens
130   designated for forgetting. Despite effectiveness, this kind of methods cannot avoid undermining
131   models’ generalization ability. Other recent methods (Chen & Yang, 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Gu
132   et al., 2024; Kassem et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Maini et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023; Li et al.,
133   2024) use diverse techniques such as knowledge gap alignment and reinforcement learning. Despite
134
      unlearning effectively, these methods are often complex and computationally expensive, limiting
      their practicality. For instance, Gu et al. (2024) utilized second-order information (Hessian) to
135
      provide stronger guarantees for data removal while maintaining model utility, but this approach
136
      requires substantial computational resources for Hessian approximation, making it difficult to play a
137   role in real scenarios. Pawelczyk et al. (2023) applied in-context methods in unlearning approaches,
138   yet not effective for generation tasks.
139
140
141   2.2   G ENERATIVE LANGUAGE MODELS
142
143
      Generative Language Models are designed to understand, generate, and predict human lan-
144   guage (Zhao et al., 2023), which have gained considerable attention in recent years (Jang et al.,
145   2023; Kassem et al., 2023; Qu et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023).
146
147
      Traditional language models, such as rule-based approaches (Weizenbaum, 1966) and statistical
      models (Brown et al., 1992), generate outputs that resemble human language but do not perfectly
148
      reflect the training data. Early neural network models in NLP, including Recurrent Neural Networks
149
      (RNNs) (Werbos, 1990), faced limitations due to their sequential processing architecture, which
150   resulted in high computational demands and hindered scalability.
151
152   The introduction of the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) revolutionized NLP by en-
153
      abling the effective capture of contextual relationships through self-attention mechanisms. Subse-
      quent advancements have led to the development of three primary categories of transformer-based
154
      models: encoder-only models, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2018); decoder-only models, exem-
155
      plified by GPT (Radford et al., 2018); and encoder-decoder models, like T5 (Raffel et al., 2020).
156   Decoder-only models, including GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude 3 (Anthropic, 2024), and
157   others (Anil et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), have demonstrated exceptional
158   performance across a wide range of NLP tasks. However, this success has raised privacy concerns
159   due to the potential leakage of sensitive information from the training data. Additionally, increasing
160   model capacity has led to greater demands for training data and computational resources (Achiam
161   et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022), creating significant chal-
      lenges for researchers working on machine unlearning for advanced models.

      3.2      M ODEL OVERVIEW
189
190   We propose a novel Iterative Contrastive Unlearning (ICU) framework, illustrated in Figure 2. This
191   framework focuses on unlearning for decoder-only models, addressing the challenge of mitigating
192   the memorization of sensitive information while preserving language generation capabilities. In
193   addition to (a) Knowledge Unlearning Induction module, which trains the model to forget target se-
194   quences, we introduce two supplementary modules. (b) Contrastive Learning Enhancement module
195   utilizes specially selected data to maintain overall model performance during unlearning. Further-
196
      more, (c) Iterative Unlearning Refinement module updates the data to be forgotten in an iterative
      manner, preventing over-unlearning and mitigating performance degradation.

      4.1       DATASETS
282
      To evaluate ICU’s learning and unlearning capabilities, we selected two types of datasets: Target
283   Datasets, which assess the unlearning performance, and Downstream Dataset, which evaluates the
284   original capabilities of the models.
285
286   Target Dataset. The Pile corpus (825GB) is a large dataset constructed from 22 diverse high-
287   quality subsets, many of which derive from academic or professional sources (e.g. books, open
288   source code) (Gao et al., 2020). As the whole dataset is not available at present, we use a subset of
289   the Pile corpus, which is released as a benchmark for data extraction attacks.2 Designed to be easy-
290   to-extract, the subset contains 15,000 samples, randomly sampled from the Pile training dataset.
291   Most of them are in English, but there are also samples in Russian or Chinese. Each sample consists
292   of a 200-token sequence, among which are 100 pre-prefix tokens, 50 prefix tokens, and 50 suffix
293   tokens. Following Jang et al. (2023), we only use the prefix and suffix tokens thus t0 is set to 50.
294
295   Downstream Dataset. To assess the general performance of the LMs subsequent to the process
296
      of unlearning, a diverse array of downstream tasks is employed. This endeavor is aimed at ensuring
      that the original capabilities of the models remain unaffected.
297
298   This evaluation encompasses nine distinct classification tasks spanning three thematic domains.
299   Specifically, these domains include linguistic reasoning tasks such as Hellaswag (Zellers et al.,
300   2019) and Lambada (Paperno et al., 2016), as well as assessments of commonsense reasoning
301   through Winogrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021) and COPA (Gordon et al., 2012). Additionally, sci-
302
      entific reasoning abilities are evaluated through tasks such as ARC-Easy (Clark et al., 2018), ARC-
      Challenge (Clark et al., 2018), Piqa (Bisk et al., 2020), MathQA (Amini et al., 2019), and Pub-
303
      medQA (Jin et al., 2019).
304
305   Furthermore, four dialogue tasks, namely Wizard of Wikipedia (Dinan et al., 2018), Empathetic
306   Dialogues (Rashkin et al., 2018), Blended Skill Talk (Smith et al., 2020), and Wizard of Inter-
307   net (Komeili et al., 2021), are used to gauge the model’s proficiency in generating coherent re-
308
      sponses. In addition, we measure the perplexity of the unlearned models on the validation set of Pile
      and Wikitext. Following (Jang et al., 2023), we use the test set for Lambada and the validation set
309
      for the remaining tasks.

409         4.5      M AIN RESULTS
410
411         The results of all methods are summarized in Table 1. Overall, our method consistently achieves the
412         best or second-best performance across all metrics compared to the baselines. Although methods
413         like KUMPR, DPO, KL and LLMU may exhibit seemingly better unlearning ability measured by
414         EL, MA, and BERT, our method preserves the generation ability of the model. Compared to O PT,
415         our model unlearns the original model better. In terms of GPT Evaluation, our model achieves the
416         best score compared to all baselines. Additionally, we observe several interesting phenomena:
417         First, the impact of unlearning becomes more pronounced with larger models, indicating that larger
418         models have a higher tendency to memorize sensitive information, which our method effectively mit-
419         igates. Second, while KUMPR significantly forgets sensitive information, it also impairs the model’s
420         general performance (e.g., PPL). In contrast, our ICU approach preserves the model’s core linguistic
421         capabilities while erasing sensitive information, underscoring the importance of our method. Full
422         results for each sample are provided in Appendix G.

      4.7       PARAMETER SENSITIVITY
467
      To examine the influence of the loss hyperparameters α and β in Section 3.4, we conducted extensive
468
      parameter sensitivity experiments on GPT-N EO 125M. The results are summarized in Table 2.
469
470   In general, increasing α and β enhances learning ability while diminishing unlearning ability, with
471   the exception of memorization accuracy (MA). For MA, which assesses the model’s memory capac-
472
      ity, the model effectively memorizes corresponding tokens through paired data, thereby retaining its
      original generative capability. Meanwhile, we find that when the learning weight α is less than the
473
      unlearning weight of 1, variations in the regularization weight β significantly impact the model’s
474
      performance. Conversely, when α exceeds the forgetting weight of 1, changes in β do not signifi-
475   cantly affect performance. This indicates that both hyperparameters contribute to increased learning
476   ability, corroborating the findings presented in Figure 3. To balance the model’s learning and for-
477   getting abilities, we ultimately selected α = 0.5 and β = 1.0 as our reported parameters.

494   5   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
495
496   In this paper, we explored the direction of machine unlearning for Generative Language Models.
497   We analyzed the challenges inherent in this topic and proposed an Iterative Contrastive Unlearning
498   (ICU) framework. Specifically, we extended Knowledge Unlearning Induction with Contrastive
499
      Learning Enhancement, training the model using selected paired data. Additionally, we introduced
      Iterative Unlearning Refinement to prevent further unlearning of discarded information, thereby
500
      adaptively preserving the model’s capabilities. Extensive experiments on models of three different
501
      sizes demonstrated the effectiveness of our proposed method.
502
503   For future work, we plan to explore the application of our framework to multi-modal data, extending
504   unlearning capabilities to models that integrate text, images, and other data types. Additionally, we
505
      are very interested in studying the implications of unlearning algorithms in privacy-sensitive real-
      world applications, such as healthcare and personalized services.
