011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is gaining popularity as an alternative
014            to Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) for aligning Large Language Models
015            (LLMs). Recent research on aligning LLMs iteratively with synthetic or partially
016            synthetic data has shown promising outcomes, facilitating the scalability of DPO
017
               training in both academic settings and proprietary models such as Llama 3. De-
               spite its success, we observe that the issue of length exploitation in DPO becomes
018
               more pronounced during iterative preference optimization, with the severity es-
019
               calating progressively with each iteration. This observation prompts an in-depth
020            examination of iterative preference optimization with synthetic data. In this pa-
021            per, we present our findings and analyses in building our iterative preference opti-
022            mization pipeline. Specifically, we analyze the issue of length exploitation in this
023            iterative process and propose a novel training objective for iterative preference op-
024            timization, namely Agreement-aware Iterative Preference Optimization (AIPO).
025            To demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conduct extensive
026            experiments and show that it achieves state-of-the-art performance on MT-Bench,
027            AlpacaEval 2.0, and Arena-Hard.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al.,
032   2020a) has emerged as a crucial technique to align Large Language Models (LLMs) with human
033   preferences. Although RLHF is effective compared to Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Ouyang
034   et al., 2022a), it encounters scalability challenges due to its training inefficiency and multistage
035   process. Recently, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) has gained attention due to its scalabil-
036   ity to large-scale models and its superior performance compared to Proximal Policy Optimization
037   (PPO), thus serving as a good alternative to the conventional RLHF pipeline (Dubey et al., 2024).
038   The key success of DPO derives from re-parameterizing the reward model using optimal policy ob-
039
      tained from the reinforcement learning phase, enabling direct training of the language model via
      reward modeling. Consequently, DPO facilitates efficient scaling for training large-scale models to
040
      learn human feedback. Nonetheless, DPO still faces challenges due to the labor intensive labeling
041
      process required for preference data collection. Currently, advances in both proprietary and open-
042   source LLMs have demonstrated human-level performance across various tasks (Dubey et al., 2024;
043   Achiam et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024), indicating their potential to autonomously generate pref-
044   erence data. Based on this fact, replacing human annotation with LLM-generated data becomes a
045   popular solution to the aforementioned scalability challenge.
046
      Recent studies (Yuan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a; Tran et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) show
047
      that aligning LLMs with synthetic data in an iterative manner can effectively achieve continuous
048   improvement in performance and allow for a higher performance ceiling. However, despite their
049   success, the length exploitation issue that exists in the generic DPO setting (Park et al., 2024) has a
050   strong impact on performance, as observed in our research and noted in recent works (Yuan et al.,
051   2024; Tran et al., 2023). Current benchmarks for preference optimization exhibit a common bias
052   toward lengthy responses, which are less efficient for users to consume and require more hardware
053   resources to generate. Additionally, creating and training on synthetic data for lengthy responses
      consume more hardware resources. Therefore, we argue that high scores on existing benchmarks are

054
      insufficient to reflect alignment performance accurately, and the length exploitation issue needs more
055   attention. Moreover, due to the complexity of multistage iterative training, numerous combinations
056   of training procedures remain unexplored. These include the detailed steps for creating synthetic
057   preference pairs, the data amount to be trained on each iteration, the tuning of hyperparameters for
058   each training stage, data selection and cleaning, and the combination with other training methods,
059   etc. Despite the success of existing works, these questions remain unanswered. We thus argue
060   that research in the area of iterative preference optimization is still in its early stages and that the
061   fundamental building blocks for iterative preference optimization are still under explored.
062   In this work, we showcase our training recipe for aligning LLMs with purely synthetic data itera-
063   tively by carefully examining the design choices for each component in iterative preference opti-
064   mization, serving as a good starting point for investigating iterative preference optimization. During
065   this process, we reveal the severe length issue in iterative preference optimization, which we believe
066   significantly limits the potential application of this method. Through our analysis, we propose so-
067   lutions to overcome the length issue in training stages and introduce our own training objective for
068   iterative preference optimization. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
069   • Data: a Synthetic Data Curation Pipeline for Preference Optimization. We examine the
070     validity of preference optimization with synthetically generated data. The pipeline includes in-
071     struction creation, response generation, and preference ranking. We conclude that models trained
072     with synthetic data yield better performance and have the potential to scale up at a low cost. §3.1
073   • Finding: Length Exploitation Issue in Iterative Training Strategy. We define our iterative
074     preference optimization training strategy and perform ablations in different configurations. We
075     observe a more severe length exploitation issue during iterative training with synthetic data. §3.2
076   • AIPO: Optimized Training Objective for Iterative Preference Optimization. We dive deep
077     into the length exploitation issue and discover that one of the potential causes is related to the
078     DPO loss. To remedy this, we introduce a new optimized training objective, AIPO, which is more
079     suitable for iterative preference optimization scenarios. §4
080
      Altogether, we propose an effective training recipe for iterative preference optimization, including
081   the AIPO training objective for iterative training. By leveraging this new training recipe, we achieve
082   state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks including MT-Bench, AlpacaEval 2.0 and Area-Hard.

      2.2   P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION O BJECTIVES
109
110   Several preference optimization objectives have been developed in addition to DPO. One line of
111   research is to study preference optimization without relying on a reference model (Xu et al., 2023;
112   Hong et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024). Other methods try to add a margin between the chosen
113   and rejected responses (Gheshlaghi Azar et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023a). R-
114   DPO (Park et al., 2024) and SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) also explore length-controlled approaches
115   by leveraging length regularization and length normalization, respectively. RPO (Liu et al., 2024)
116
      proposes a training objective that incorporates a weighted SFT loss as the regularization term. It is
      worth noting that, despite the effectiveness of certain methods, the validation of the majority of these
117
      improved DPO losses has been primarily limited to non-iterative settings, with a lack of validation
118
      in iterative contexts.

      2.3   I TERATIVE A LIGNMENT M ETHODS
122
      Iterative alignment is gaining popularity for achieving continuous improvement through successive
123   training iterations. Self-Rewarding (Yuan et al., 2024) focuses on self-involvement of large language
124   models, utilizing the LLM-as-a-Judge mechanism (Zheng et al., 2023b) to score their own responses,
125   thus mitigating the performance bottlenecks that can arise from a frozen judge model. It leverages
126   iterative training to generate self-judgement using an up-to-date model. Based on Self-Rewarding,
127   Iterative RPO (Pang et al., 2024) aims to improve reasoning ability through iterative preference op-
128   timization by utilizing Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wu et al., 2023) reasoning. Meta-Rewarding (Wu
129   et al., 2024a) focuses on improving the self-judging ability in self-rewarding by adding a role of
130   meta-judge to judge the model’s own judgement. sDPO (Kim et al., 2024) suggests dividing the
131
      available preference datasets into multiple subsets and training on each subset iteratively. Snorkel-
      Mistral-PairRM-DPO (Tran et al., 2023) is trained iteratively, starting from an initial prompt pool
132
      sampled from UltraFeedback. The model is prompted with instructions from UltraFeedback to gen-
133
      erate several candidate responses and then uses PairRM (Jiang et al., 2023b) as reward model to
134   rank the responses. Finally, it trains on the top and bottom responses with DPO. SPPO (Wu et al.,
135   2024b) approximates the Nash equilibrium iteratively by pushing the chosen rewards to be close to
136   1/2 and the rejected rewards to be -1/2. Although all these methods involve iterative training, they do
137   not explain the differences between non-iterative and iterative preference optimization. In addition,
138   they lack a detailed analysis of the design choices and properties involved in the iterative preference
139   optimization.

      3     I TERATIVE P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION WITH S YNTHETIC DATA

143
      In this section, we detail the step-by-step development of a state-of-the-art training recipe for itera-
144
      tive preference optimization, examining the design choices for each component. We start with the
145
      non-iterative baseline, which is trained on the existing pairwise preference dataset, and then move
146   on to synthetic preference pairs and iterative training. Finally, we present our refined training recipe
147   for subsequent experiments and emphasizing the challenges posed by the iterative training approach.

154   lack of detailed comparison. To study the roles and effects of different components in the data

160   pairs, despite the presence of existing responses and rewards in preference datasets. To explore this

235
      total volume of training data while varying the data size per iteration. The results, as shown in
236   Fig. 1, reveal that increasing the number of iterations with smaller data sizes per iteration leads to
237   a higher performance ceiling, highlighting the importance of iterative training. We hypothesize that
238   iterative training improves preference optimization by updating both the data generation model and
239   the reference model for the training objective in each iteration, providing timely feedback. However,
240   using smaller data sizes per iteration requires frequent transitions between training phases, which
241   demands a more sophisticated implementation to maintain training efficiency. Moreover, we observe
242   that further reducing the number of training data per iteration yields only marginal improvements.

249   length exploitation is particularly evident in this scenario: increased length results in significantly

      longer training times, ultimately causing performance degradation due to excessive verbosity. To

531
      In this study, we investigate the transition from non-iterative DPO training to iterative alignment
532
      of LLMs using synthetic data. Our research addresses a significant gap in the existing literature
533
      by providing a comprehensive comparison and analysis of iterative preference optimization training
534   methodologies and presenting a robust framework for iterative preference optimization. Building
535   upon this foundation, we examine the phenomenon of length exploitation in iterative training, which
536   significantly degrades performance. We examine the characteristics of self-generated responses and
537   introduce AIPO, an approach designed to incorporate agreement-aware adjustments in the training
538   objective to mitigate the length issue and ensure stability in iterative preference optimization. By
539   integrating these techniques, we achieve state-of-the-art performance on MT-Bench, AlpacaEval
      2.0, and Arena-Hard without compromising on length.

680   a large-scale preference dataset containing approximately 64K prompts from various sources. The

      tokens and the maximum response length to 2048 tokens. For data curation ablations in Sec. 3.1,
690
      we train on 60K preference pairs sourced from either UltraFeedback or synthetic data, with a batch
691
      size of 128. For iterative training in Sec. 3.2 and 4, we set the batch size to 256. After performing
692   ablations for iterative training in Sec. 3.2, we chose to train on 20K preference pairs per iteration
693   by default for the iterative training in Sec. 4. For all experiments, we train for one epoch for each
694   training stage using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 5e−7. We apply a cosine learning

      Our training pipeline relies heavily on synthetic data, making it sensitive to the characteristics of the
      base model, such as response style, diversity, and capacity. For smaller-scale models, the synthetic
732
      data often contains more biases and unexpected responses due to their limited capacity. To address
733
      this, we implement some basic rule-based filtering and data cleaning strategies during the self-
734   instruct creation and candidate response generation stages to stabilize training and mitigate biases.
735   This includes removing self-asked instructions, filtering out URLs from responses, and eliminating
736   excessively long responses. Following the experimental settings in Sec. 3.1, we conduct experiments
737   to verify the effectiveness of filtering. As shown in Tab. 6, applying filtering slightly improves
738   overall performance. We thus apply filtering by default for all experiments in the main paper.
