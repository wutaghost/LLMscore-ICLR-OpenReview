009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The recent rise of large language models has been fueled by scale. More data, more
012            compute, and bigger models have consistently led to better performance. This
013            scaling paradigm has been applied most notably to the transformer architecture,
014            which is especially conducive to training parallelization and sequence modeling.
015            In this work, we ask what happens if we apply the power of scale to the simplest
016
               possible class of neural network: the multi-layer perceptron (MLP). Specifically, we
               train MLPs to perform next-token prediction on billions of tokens of text. Indeed,
017
               their performance consistently improves with scale, though vanilla MLPs are still
018
               clearly inferior to transformers for this task, especially because their parameter
019            count grows with the length of the input sequences. We then perform a mechanistic
020            analysis of the trained models, and identify a consistent emergent structure: most
021            neurons in the first hidden layer either perform arbitrary linear functions over a
022            small look-back window, or low-frequency functions over the entire context. These
023            neuron types recall n-gram and bag-of-words techniques from classical statistical
024            language modeling. Using the discrete cosine transform, we define a unified way
025            of reparameterizing these neuron types such that the number of parameters per
026            neuron does not depend on the sequence length.
027
028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   It has been almost a quarter-century since neural language models were introduced in the seminal work
031   of Bengio et al. (2000). The authors trained multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) to perform next-word
032   prediction on large text corpora, and found that the resulting models outperformed leading n-gram
033   based models, which had been state-of-the-art since Shannon first introduced language modeling a
034   half-century earlier (Shannon, 1951). MLPs, consisting of fully-connected layers of neurons, are the
035   prototypical basic deep learning architecture (Rumelhart et al., 1986). In their largest experiment,
036   Bengio et al. trained a width-120 MLP with a context length of 8 on a training set of 32 million
037   words.
038   In this work, we train simple MLPs on language modeling, just like Bengio et al. (2000), but at a much
039   larger scale—ReLU MLPs with up to 900 million parameters and 128 tokens of context, supervised
040   on 10 billion tokens. As observed in other contexts (Hestness et al., 2017; Kaplan et al., 2020;
041   Hoffmann et al., 2022; Bachmann et al., 2023), increases in scale lead to consistent improvements
042   in performance. See Figure 1 for empirical scaling results and comparisons to the transformer
043   architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017).
044   Recently, Bachmann et al. (2023) performed an extensive study of scaling MLPs in the vision domain,
045   and some of our motivations parallel theirs. Much theoretical research in deep learning studies MLPs
046   because of their simplicity, and we are interested in how much relevance this literature has for the
047   large-scale language modeling setting. We also see our MLP experiments as a test of the limits of
048   scaling: can an extremely generic architecture, given enough parameters and data, perform arbitrarily
049   well at language modeling? Of course, there are reasons vanilla MLPs are not actually a practical
050
      architecture for language modeling. Most importantly, a given MLP can only support a fixed context
      length, and scaling the context length requires increasing the number of parameters.
051
052   But training MLPs also provides an opportunity. Because the architecture is so generic, we can
053   think of it (informally) as a “blank slate” on which the learning process builds circuits for language
      modeling unconstrained by human-imposed inductive biases. If we are lucky, we might identify

      We thus perform a mechanistic analysis of the neurons in the first hidden layer of our models. The
072
      results are striking: most neurons can be assigned to one of two types (see Figure 4). “n-gram”
073
      neurons perform an arbitrary linear function over the most recent n − 1 tokens for some small value
074   of n, ignoring all of the preceding context. “Bag-of-words” neurons, meanwhile, apply a linear
075   map to the average of all the input token embeddings; in fact, this linear map typically picks out a
076   semantically coherent direction in token embedding space. This emergent structure echoes non-neural
077   language modeling techniques which combine n-gram and bag-of-words statistics (Wallach, 2006).
078   Although the proportion of neurons that fit neatly into these two categories increases during the
079   course of training, at the end of training there remain other “hybrid” neurons which can be expressed
080   as a linear combination of n-gram and bag-of-words neurons.
081   Directly inspired by this empirical finding, we introduce a new type of neuron, the DCT (discrete
082   cosine transform) neuron, which is rich enough to express n-gram neurons and bag-of-words neurons
083   as special cases, and has a number of parameters that does not depend on the sequence length.
084   Specifically, we leverage the fact that both types of neurons have weight matrices that are restricted
085   to some look-back window, and (for each embedding coordinate) are low-frequency functions (in
086   comparison to the total context length) over that window.
087   In summary: We begin by studying the scaling behavior of vanilla MLPs in a simple language
088   modeling setting in Section 2. We then perform an analysis of the neurons of the resulting model
089   in Section 4, in which we introduce the n-gram and bag-of-words neuron types. We conclude by
090   proposing a method of reparameterizing the MLP language model using the DCT, such that the
091   number of parameters does not depend on sequence length (Section 5). We conclude with an overview
092   of related work (Section 6 and a high-level discussion 7).

094    2           VANILLA MLP FOR SHORT- SEQUENCE LAST- TOKEN PREDICTION
095
096   While transformers have dominated the landscape of language modeling, we take a step back and
097   ask: With enough compute, can an MLP achieve comparable performance in language modeling to
098   architectures with more inductive bias? To study this, we simplify the language modeling task to a
099   setting in which it is feasible to train and scale MLP models, as a case study in how neural networks
100   model language when removing the impact of architectural biases.
101

106   sequences of length 16 where the network sees the first 15 tokens and is tasked with predicting the
107   last token; we count tokens by the number of supervised output tokens. An illustration of the MLP

142   3   S CALING LAWS
143
144   We study how MLP models scale with increasing compute as compared to the transformer architecture
145   on our language modeling task (Figure 1). Specifically, we follow Method 2 from Hoffmann et al.
146   (2022) to examine the trade-off between increasing parameters and increasing training tokens within
147
      a fixed compute budget. We train models over a range of 50M to 900M parameters (including all
      embedding parameters) for five compute budgets between 1e16 and 3e18. We measure compute
148
      budget in terms of training FLOPs using the approximation C = 6N D (Kaplan et al., 2020) for a
149
      compute budget C, number of parameters N and number of supervised training tokens D. We set the
150   cosine schedule to match the number of training steps for each run. We plot number of parameters
151   (on a log scale) versus loss and fit a quadratic curve through the data points of each compute budget.
152   We find that increasing depth does not make a large difference as compared to scaling width for
153   the MLP models, so for simplicity we fix the models to have three hidden layers and scale only the
154   width. We also fix depth for the transformer models, noting the finding from Kaplan et al. (2020) that
155   scaling trends are reasonably consistent for depths of at least 2. We note that the MLP curves display
156   a “bump”—a local maximum in the compute budget curves; this plausibly could be resolved with
157   optimal hyperparameter tuning, but due to resource constraints we leave this to future investigation.
158   The minima of the fitted parabolas correspond to estimates of the compute-optimal parameter count
159   Nopt for each compute budget. We use these estimates to plot training FLOPs vs compute-optimal
160   parameter count and training FLOPs vs compute-optimal token count Dopt (Figure 1), as well as
161   training FLOPs vs compute-optimal loss (Figure 3). While the transformers achieve consistently
      lower loss at the same compute level, the scaling trends are strikingly similar between architectures.

193    4                            A NALYSIS OF THE UNDERLYING ARCHITECTURE
194
195   We have shown that an MLP can be a non-trivial language model; now, we investigate what this
196   model has learned. Since the MLP architecture has minimal built-in inductive bias, we are interested
197   to see what structures emerge in the trained language model, as such structures can potentially inspire
198   some “natural” inductive bias. In other words, can what the MLP model learns inform how we build
199
      a more scalable architecture? In particular, we wish to find a parameterization or sub-architecture of
      the MLP model with a number of parameters that does not depend on sequence length.
200
201   In this section, we analyze what structures emerge at the neuron level, and introduce a unifying view
202   of these structures. In Section 4, we discuss a potential implementation of this unifying view such
203   that the resulting model does not depend on sequence length in its number of parameters.
204

289   4.2    A NOTE ON INTERPRETABILITY
290
291
      Given that the neurons in this first layer operate directly on the embeddings of the input sequence, we
      are interested in exploring whether we can interpret what these neurons are doing at a semantic level,
292
      as has been done by prior work such as Elhage et al. (2021) for simple transformer language models.
293
      For the bag-of-words neurons, nearly the same vector of weights is applied to each unit of the time
294   dimension. Given that this vector is of size Rd , we study whether this vector has an interpretable
295   meaning once unembedded. We take the average of the weights across time to obtain a single vector
296   of dimension d. We then look at the top-5 closest words to this vector in token embedding space to
297   understand the concepts associated with each bag-of-words neuron. We observe that the keywords
298   associated with a given bag-of-words vector are often related concepts.
299
      For the n-gram neurons, we take the weights of the most active row as measured by the ℓ2 -norm
300   and project it back into token space. We then run inference on our model with an input of all zeros
301   besides the last token (which is the projected column). We then observe how the model completes
302   this 2-gram. When unembedding the last column of the n-gram to get the 5-closest words as we do
303   in the bag-of-words case, we find that these keywords themselves tend to be less correlated from a
304   human-centered interpretability lens. However, these neurons often appear to be n-grams with n ¿ 1,
305   so it is possible that a single unit of the n-gram cannot be interpreted in isolation. An example of each
306   neuron type is given in Figure 4, and additional examples are listed in Appendix G. We encourage
307   future work in the interpretability of both the n-gram and bag-of-words neuron categories.

365   5    DCT-PARAMETERIZED L ONG -C ONTEXT MLP
366
367   In Section 2 we showed that MLPs can be trained for next-token prediction on short context lengths,
368   but are hard to train with long context windows. However, when we study the weights of the neurons
369   learned by the network, we observe that these neurons converge to particular low-complexity weights:
370   high-frequency local functions (n-gram neurons) or low-frequency global functions (bag-of-words
371   neurons). In this section we leverage these observations to construct a reparameterization of the MLP
372   that satisfies two properties: 1) the number of parameters does not grow with the sequence length and
373   2) this module can still express the neuron types discovered in the previous section. To achieve this,
374
      we will replace the linear layer of the MLP by a linear network, with some learned parameters and
      some fixed linear computations.
375

      6    R ELATED W ORK
438
439
      Scaling laws Scaling laws for transformers are well-studied and demonstrate that transformers
      exhibit a power law in which performance increases with increasing compute (Kaplan et al. (2020),
440
      Hoffmann et al. (2022)). Additionally, Tay et al. (2022) studies the impact of inductive bias on scaling
441
      behavior and finds scaling laws vary from model to model. Bachmann et al. (2023) introduces scaling
442   laws for MLPs on vision tasks. We extend this study to a simple language modeling setting.
443
444   MLP-inspired architectures Several past works study the question of how to best encode token-
445   mixing as an inductive bias, and in particular how self-attention can be replaced with simpler modules.
446   Tolstikhin et al. (2021) introduce MLP-Mixer and Touvron et al. (2023) introduce ResMLP, both
447   methods of applying alternating channel-mixing and token-mixing MLPs, primarily focusing on
448   the image domain. Melas-Kyriazi (2021) conducts a similar study. In addition, Liu et al. (2021)
449   introduces the gMLP architecture, which uses gating as an alternative to self-attention. All four works
450   indicate that self-attention is sufficient but not necessary when given enough compute. Our work
451   addresses a similar question, but we offer the novel approach of searching for this inductive bias by
452
      attempting to encode what the vanilla MLP learns.
453
      Fourier transform-based networks Several works study how to incorporate spectral information
454
      as an architectural bias. Rippel et al. (2015) and Chi et al. (2020) discuss Fourier-based methods for
455
      convolutional networks. Most similar to our architecture is FNet (Lee-Thorp et al., 2022), which
456   replaces self-attention with a standard Fourier transform and achieves competitive performance with
457   the transformer encoder. Rao et al. (2021) introduces GFNet, which similarly replaces self-attention
458   with spectral operations but focuses on the vision domain. Patro et al. (2023) propose Spectformer, a
459   vision transformer architecture that incorporates both spectral and attention layers. Other works use
460   Fourier analysis to understand what functions neural networks learn, noting a preference for functions
461   with low sensitivity (Rahaman et al. (2019), Xu et al. (2019), Xu (2020), Vasudeva et al. (2024)). Our
462   work provides another justification for applying operations in the frequency domain, as MLPs trained
463   on language modeling appear to develop a strong bias towards low-frequency computations.
464
465   Training models to find implicit bias Closely related to our work is Bachmann et al. (2023),
466
      which investigates how lack of inductive bias can be compensated with increased scale and compute.
      However, Bachmann et al. (2023) focuses on scaling laws for MLPs on vision tasks, whereas we
467
      scale MLPs as a basis for studying what structures emerge as natural biases for language modeling.
468
      Also similar to our work is Neyshabur (2020), which explores how to use a learning algorithm with
469   bias for sparse connectivity to replace the inductive bias of convolutions in vision tasks. Additionally,
470   Fernando et al. (2016) proposes an evolution-based algorithm to learn convolutions. Towards
471   understanding the impact of architectural bias, d'Ascoli et al. (2019) compare the bias of a CNN to a
472   fully connected network through the lens of training dynamics and show the benefits of the CNN
473   prior in achieving good generalization.

      7    D ISCUSSION
476
477
      The deep learning community has increasingly aimed to train versatile models at the largest possible
478   scale. We have pushed this effort to the extreme by training simple MLPs on large scale language
479   modeling datasets. We demonstrate that MLPs perform nontrivially as language models when
480   provided with enough data and compute. They learn to exploit structure in the data through local
481   “n-gram” neurons and global “bag-of-words” neurons. We find that the Discrete Cosine Transform
482   can be used to unify these neuron types, and leverage this finding to develop an architecture that is
483   not dependent on sequence length in its number of parameters. We leave this work as a first step in
484   understanding and leveraging emergent structures to develop scalable and efficient architectures for
485   language modeling.

      C     A DDITIONAL DETAILS ON DCT-PARAMETERIZATION
819
      C.1    N OTES ON THE DCT-N EURON
820
821   Observe that since the DCT operation is linear, the DCT Neuron is a linear function of the input, i.e.
822   this is just a re-parameterization of the “standard” neuron. Additionally, each neuron has k · d learned
823   parameters (in our experiments we choose k to be a small constant, e.g. k = 4), so the number of
824   parameters does not depend on the sequence length T . Finally, we note that the DCT Neuron can
825   express both the n-gram and the bag-of-words neurons: if we choose t = k, then DCT(k) operation
826   only changes the basis of the linear space, but the neuron can express any linear function over the last
827   k tokens; alternatively, if t = T , then the neuron can express a low-frequency function (with only k
828   frequencies) over the entire sequence (for k = 1 the neuron computes a linear function of the average
829   of sequence length). A linear combinations of such neurons (which still gives a linear function of
830
      the input) gives “hybrid” neurons, and choosing other values of t (e.g., t = T /2) allows different
      “trade-offs” between sequence length and complexity.
831
832
833
      C.2    DCT-PARAMETERIZED A RCHITECTURE D ESCRIPTION
834
      We propose a DCT-Parameterization architecture by replacing the causal attention heads of the
835   transformer with a causal DCT-head with hyperparameters k (number of frequencies) and t (look-
836   back window size). The input to the DCT-head is a sequence of vectors x1 , . . . , xT ∈ Rd . As
837   in the transformer attention head, for efficiency reasons we begin by projecting each vector to a
838   lower dimensional space, from dimension d to dimension dh , using the matrix Wproj ∈ Rd×dh .
839   Denote the projection of the i-th vector by x′i = Wproj  ⊤
                                                                   xi . Then, we define for every frequency
840   a linear map, denoted W1 , . . . , Wk ∈ Rdh ×dh . The output at position i of the DCT-head is given
841              Pk                 (t)                           (t)
      by: oi = j=1 Wj⊤ DCTj (x′i−t , . . . , x′i ), where DCTj denotes the j-th frequency in the DCT
842
      operator over a signal of length t. So, each head has two hyperparameters k, t and learned parameter
843   matrices Wproj , W1 , . . . , Wk . Note that the number of parameters does not grow with the sequence
844   length. As in the transformer architecture, we let dh = d/h, where h is the number of heads, and
845   concatenate the outputs of all heads back to dimension d, followed by a linear layer, normalization
846   and non-linearity.
847

851   of our MLP no longer depends on the context length, we train with a full context window size of
852
      2048. We train DCT-MLPs of depth 6, 12, 18, with 2 − 6 Billion Tokens, and report the results on
      the evaluation data. We use 16 heads, using k = 4 for each head, and varying t from 4 to 2048 (the

      maximal seqeunce length). We compare the performance to a GPT2-based transformer, with depth
