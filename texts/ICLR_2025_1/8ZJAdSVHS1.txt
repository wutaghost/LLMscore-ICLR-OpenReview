009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Flow-based generative models have recently shown impressive performance for
012            conditional generation tasks, such as text-to-image generation. However, current
013            methods transform a general noise distribution to a specific mode of the target
014            data distribution. As such, every point in the initial source distribution can be
015            mapped to every point in the target distribution, resulting in a long average path.
016            To this end, in this work, we tap into a non-utilized property of conditional flow-
017            based models: the ability to design a non-trivial prior distribution. Given an input
018
               condition, such as a text prompt, we first map it to a point lying in data space,
               representing an “average” data point of the minimal average distance to all data
019
               points of the same conditional mode (e.g., class). We then utilize the flow match-
020
               ing formulation to map samples from a Gaussian centered around this point to the
021            conditional target distribution. Experimentally, our method significantly improves
022            training times and generation quality (FID, KID and CLIP alignment scores) com-
023            pared to baselines, producing high quality samples using smaller number of sam-
024            pling steps.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Conditional generative models are of significant importance for many scientific and industrial ap-
030   plications. Of these, the class of flow-based models and score-based diffusion models has recently
031   shown a particularly impressive performance (Lipman et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024; Dhariwal &
032   Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2022). Although impressive, current methods suffer from long train-
033
      ing and sampling times. To this end, in this work, we tap into a non-utilized property of conditional
      flow-based models: the ability to design a non-trivial prior distribution for conditional flow mod-
034
      els based on the input condition. In particular, for class-conditional generation and text-to-image
035
      generation tasks, we propose a robust method for constructing a conditional flow-based genera-
036   tive model using an informative condition-specific prior distribution fitted to the conditional modes
037   (e.g., classes) of the target distribution. By better modeling the prior distribution, we aim to improve
038   the efficiency, both at training and at inference, of conditional generation via flow matching, thus
039   achieving superior results with fewer sampling steps.
040
      Given an input variable (e.g., a class or text prompt), current flow-based and score-based diffu-
041
      sion models combine the input condition with intermediate (possibly latent) representations in a
042   learnable manner. However, crucially, these models are still trained to transform a generic unimodal
043   noise distribution to the different modes of the target data distribution. In some formulations, such as
044   score-based diffusion (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), the use of the
045   source Gaussian density is intrinsically connected to the process of constructing the transformation.
046   In others, such as flow matching (Lipman et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden,
047   2022), a Gaussian prior is not required, but is often chosen as a default for convenience. Conse-
048   quently, in this setting, the prior distribution bears little or no resemblance to the target, and hence
049   every point in the initial source distribution can be mapped to every point in every mode in the target
050
      distribution, corresponding to a given condition. This means that the average distance between pairs
      of source-target points is fairly large.
051
052   In the unconditional setting, recent works (Pooladian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023), show that
053   starting from a source (noise) data point that is close to the target data sample, during training, results
      in straighter probability flows, fewer sampling steps at test time, and faster training time. This is in

094   2   R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Conditional Generation. Conditional generative models have been modeled through a spectrum
097   of generative architectures, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Mirza & Osindero,
098   2014) and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Sohn et al., 2015). Recently, innovations in diffusion
099   models have enabled unprecedented performance in conditional generation, including text-to-image
100   synthesis (Nichol et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2024), text-to-video translation
101   (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b), and text-to-speech conversion (Kong et al.,
102   2021; Kim et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2020; Popov et al., 2021). Traditionally, conditional information
103   is integrated into these networks via residual connections or cross-attention (Rombach et al., 2022).
104
      However, to the best of our knowledge, the literature lacks exploration of incorporating conditional
      information through the prior distribution of these models.
105
106   Flow-based Models.     Continuous Normalizing Flows (CNFs) (Chen et al., 2019) emerged as a
107   novel paradigm in generative modeling, offering a continuous-time extension to the discrete Nor-
      malizing Flows (NF) framework (Kobyzev et al., 2020; Papamakarios et al., 2021). Recently, Flow


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Matching (Lipman et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022) has been in-
109   troduced as a simulation-free alternative for training CNFs. In scenarios involving conditional
110   data (e.g., in text-to-image generation), conditioning is applied similarly to diffusion models, often
111   through cross attention between the input condition and latent features. Typically, the source dis-
112   tribution remains unimodal, like a standard Gaussian (Liu et al., 2024a). In contrast, our approach
113   derives a prior distribution that is dependent on the input condition.
114
      Informative Prior Design.        In the context of score-based models and flow matching, several
115   works designed informative priors. For score-based diffusion, Lee et al. (2021) has introduced an
116   approach of formulating the diffusion process using a non-standard Gaussian, where the Gaussian’s
117   statistics are determined by the conditional distribution statistics. However, this approach is con-
118   strained by the use of a Gaussian prior, which limits its overall flexibility. Recently, (Pooladian et al.,
119   2023; Tong et al., 2023) constructed a prior distribution by utilizing the dynamic optimal transport
120   (OT) formulation across mini-batches during training. Despite impressive capabilities such as effi-
121   cient sampling (minimizing trajectory intersections), they suffer from several drawbacks: (i). Highly
122   expensive training. Computing the optimal transport solution requires quadratic time and memory,
123
      which is not applicable to large mini-batches and high dimensional data. (ii). When dealing with
      high dimensional data, the effectiveness of this formulation decreases dramatically. An increase
124
      in performance requires an exponential increase in batch-size in relation to data dimension. Our
125
      approach avoids these limitations by leveraging the conditioning variable of the data distribution.

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we introduce a novel initialization for flow-based generative models using condition-
533
      specific priors, improving both training and inference efficiency. Our method allows for significantly
534   shorter flow paths, reducing the global truncation error. Our approach achieves state-of-the-art per-
535   formance on MS-COCO and ImageNet-64, surpassing baselines in FID, KID, and CLIP scores,
536   particularly at lower NFEs. The flexibility of our method opens avenues for further exploration of
537   other conditional initialization. While this work we assumed a GMM structure of the prior distribu-
538   tion, different structures can be explored. Furthermore, one could incorporate additional conditions
539   such as segmentation maps or depth maps.
