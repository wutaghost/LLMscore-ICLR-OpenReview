009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The design of controllers with correctness guarantees is a primary concern for
012            safety-critical control systems. A Control Barrier Certificate (CBC) is a real-
013            valued function over the state space of the system that provides an inductive proof
014            of the existence of a safe controller. Recently, neural networks have been suc-
015            cessfully deployed for data-driven learning of control barrier certificates. These
016            approaches encode the conditions for the existence of a CBC using a rectified
017
               linear unit (ReLU) loss function. The resulting encoding, while sound, tends to
               be conservative, which results in slower training and limits scalability to large,
018
               complex systems. Can altering the loss function alleviate some of the problems
019
               associated with ReLU loss and lead to faster learning?
020
               This paper proposes a novel encoding with a Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss
021
               function, which allows for more scalable and efficient training, while addressing
022            some of the theoretical limitations of previous methods. The proposed approach
023            derives a validity condition based on Lipschitz continuity to formally characterize
024            safety guarantees, eliminating the need for a post-hoc verification. The effective-
025            ness of the proposed loss functions is demonstrated through six case studies cu-
026            rated from the existing state of the art. Our results provide a compelling argument
027            for exploring alternative loss function choices as a novel approach to optimizing
028            the design of control barrier certificates.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
      Recent advances in deep learning have accelerated the integration of autonomous systems into var-
033
      ious safety-critical areas of everyday life, including self-driving cars, robotic manipulators, and
034
      personalized implantable medical devices. Consequently, even a minor fault in the control logic of
035   these systems can lead to catastrophic consequences, such as loss of human life, severe financial
036   losses, legal liabilities, and damage to infrastructure. In response to this grand challenge, the devel-
037   opment of formally certified control methods for autonomous systems has received a considerable
038   research interest in recent years (Xu et al., 2017; Salamati et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2023; Zhang
039   et al., 2024). Control Barrier Certificates (CBCs) (Ames et al., 2019; Prajna et al., 2007)—and
040   their neural network representations (Dawson et al., 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Anand & Zamani,
041   2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2021a; Edwards et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2021)—have emerged
042   as leading approach to design a safety controller along with an inductive proof of correctness. This
043
      paper focuses on the crucial role that the choice of loss functions plays in the scalable design of
      safety controllers.
044
045
      Neural Control Barrier Certificates. The key idea behind control barrier certificates (CBCs)
046
      is that: if one can learn a real-valued function of the state space of a dynamical system such that
047
      this function is negative in the initial states, positive in the unsafe states, and, for every state with a
048   non-positive value, there is a control signal choice that allows a transition to another state with a non-
049   positive value, then a feedback control exists that keeps the system safe indefinitely. Traditionally,
050   Sum-of-Squares (SOS) optimization has been used to synthesize such certificates and corresponding
051   controllers (Zhao et al., 2023; Schneeberger et al., 2023; Prajna et al., 2007); however, their appli-
052   cation requires human ingenuity in identifying an appropriate template and tends to scale poorly.
053   CBCs parameterized by neural networks—often referred to as Neural Control Barrier Certificates
      (NCBCs)—have recently gained traction, owing to their universal approximation capabilities, ease


                                                          1

054
      of automation, and the increasing availability of robust tool support (Dawson et al., 2022; Zhang
055   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Anand & Zamani, 2023). Due to their data-centric approach, NCBCs
056   only provide guarantees over the finite set of data points used during training. Consequently, the
057   resulting controller requires formal verification to provide rigorous guarantees about safety over the
058   entire continuous state space. This verification is typically achieved by framing the problem as a
059   constraint satisfaction task and solving it using Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) solvers, such
060   as Z3 (De Moura & Bjørner, 2008; 2011). The need for such post-hoc verification introduces another
061   weak link in terms of scalability.
062
063   Choice of Loss functions. The success of deep-learning based approximation depends on a well-
064   designed loss function to ensure that the model learns the correct objective, converges efficiently,
065
      and generalizes well to unseen data (Ma et al., 2021; Li et al., 2018). Following Zhao et al. (2020),
      the majority of work on NCBC (Anand & Zamani, 2023; Abate et al., 2020; Edwards et al., 2024;
066
      Zhao et al., 2021a; Žikelić et al., 2024) encodes the control barrier conditions using a ReLU function
067
      (x ∈ R 7→ max(x, 0)). The ReLU loss function is straightforward to encode and provides a natural
068
      termination condition for training, as training stops when the loss reaches zero. However, ReLU has
069   some fundamental disadvantages (Goodfellow et al., 2016), such as having a zero Hessian every-
070   where (which hampers interpretability (Torop et al., 2024)) and the instability of its derivative around
071   the global minimum (which affects convergence and robustness). Moreover, prior work (Anand &
072   Zamani, 2023) have demonstrated that using this loss function results in large Lipschitz constants
073   of the trained networks (and consequently the resulting controllers). In practice, small Lipschitz
074   constants for controllers are desirable to ensure more robust control (Chen, 2013). Moreover, bar-
075   rier certificates with small Lipschitz constants are preferable due to their robustness with respect to
076   small perturbations in the model of a dynamical system (resulting from mechanical wear and tear
077
      or changes in operating conditions), thereby improving the applicability and transferability of the
      resulting guarantees. Can altering the loss function alleviate some of the problems associated with
078
      ReLU loss and lead to faster learning?
079
080
      Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss. Mean Squared Error (MSE) is a popular choice (Goodfel-
081
      low et al., 2016) for loss functions in regression problems due to its strong convergence guaran-
082   tees (Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Cheridito et al., 2022). We investigate the suitability of MSE loss
083   functions for NCBCs by posing the following research questions:
084
085    RQ1 Can MSE effectively encode the conditions of neural control barrier certificates?
086    RQ2 Can an MSE-based loss function support intuitive termination checks?
087
       RQ3 Current methods typically fail to scale to more parameterized neural networks and high-
088
           dimensional systems. To what extent do MSE loss functions alleviate this drawback?
089
090    RQ4 Small Lipschitz constants are desirable for 1) interpretability, 2) robustness of training, 3)
091        robustness of the resulting controller, and 4) transferability of the resulting guarantees. How
092
           do MSE-based NCBCs compare to ReLU-based NCBCs in this regard?
093
      Contributions. Our contributions in addressing these research questions are summarized below.
094
095     RQ1 We reformulate, in Section 3, the traditional CBC conditions using MSE loss functions. By
096         leveraging MSE loss, we enable smoother gradients, thereby improving the stability and
097         convergence of neural network training.
098     RQ2 In Section 4, we leverage mild Lipschitz continuity assumptions on the system to establish
099         certain validity conditions (Theorem 8) for the resulting network, which, when satisfied,
100         allow us to terminate training and provide safety guarantees over the entire state space. This
101         approach eliminates the need for post-hoc verification, thereby improving the scalability of
102         the overall method.
103   RQ3-4 In Section 5, we experimentally address these questions by deploying our approach on six
104         case studies from state-of-the-art literature (Anand & Zamani, 2023; Edwards et al., 2024;
105         Zhang et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023). Our results show that, compared to existing work,
106         our approach offers greater scalability in terms of system dimensions and neural network
107         architecture, and is able to find formally correct NCBCs faster than current methods. Fur-
            thermore, our experiments demonstrate that our approach produces barrier certificates and

164   Neural networks, being universal approximators (Hornik et al., 1989), are able to represent any
165   Borel-measurable function based on input-output data. Consider a neural network F with k fully-
166   connected layers where each layer i is characterized with a weight matrix Wi and a bias vector bi
167   of appropriate size and is followed by an activation function. Such a network can be viewed as a
168   function F : Rni → Rno . Given y0 ∈ Rni , a network will computes its output yk ∈ Rno as:
169              y1 = σ(W1 y0 + b1 ), y2 = σ(W2 y1 + b2 ), , · · · , yk = σ(Wk yk−1 + bk ).
170
171
      We call yi−1 and yi , for i∈{1, . . ., k}, the input and output of the i-th layer, respectively, and σ is the
      activation function. One observes that neural networks with ReLU (σ(x)= max(0, x)) activations
172
      describe local Lipschitz continuous functions, with Lipschitz constant LF ∈ R≥0 , in the sense that
173
      for all x′1 , x′2 ∈ Rni , the following condition holds:
174
175                                     ∥F (x′1 ) − F (x′2 )∥ ≤ LF ∥x′1 − x′2 ∥.                               (6)
176   Moreover, an upper bound on the Lipschitz constant of a neural network with ReLU activations can
177   be obtained using the spectral norm (Combettes & Pesquet, 2020). While tighter Lipschitz upper
178   bounds for neural networks have been extensively studied (Fazlyab et al., 2019; Pauli et al., 2021;
179   Prach & Lampert, 2022; Meunier et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Araujo et al., 2023), we observed
180   that these methods are either too restrictive or introduce significant computational complexity dur-
181   ing the training process, as demonstrated in our experiments. The spectral norm approach strikes
182   a good balance: it provides a much tighter bound than the trivial upper bound while remaining
183
      computationally efficient.

203   In previous works, condition (5) is often replaced with B(f (x, k(x))) − B(x) ≤ −η, which re-
204   quires the barrier certificate to decrease as the system evolves. Although this is a more conservative
205   condition, it simplifies the verification process (Nejati & Zamani, 2023; Nejati et al., 2023; Anand
206   & Zamani, 2023). Additionally, it is typically assumed that this decreasing condition must hold
207

      still required to satisfy this condition. We tackle this by employing an implication-based approach.

      We propose an alternative approach by replacing the ReLU activation function with a MSE-based
226   formulation for constructing Neural Control Barrier Certificates. The motivation behind this substi-
227   tution is to exploit the smooth and continuous nature of MSE, which can lead to more efficient
228   gradient-based optimization and improve the overall performance and robustness of the system
229   equipped with the designed controller.
230
      We train B(x) and k(x) with the following loss function LMSE = L1 + L2 + L3 , where
231
232                     L1 := MSE(B(x), −η),            for all x ∈ Xd ∩ X0 ,                               (11)
233                       L2 := MSE(B(x), η),           for all x ∈ Xd ∩ Xu ,                               (12)
234
                 L3 := MSE(B(f (x, k(x))), −η),         for all x ∈ Xd \ Xu , such that B(x) ≤ γ,           (13)
235
236   for an η ∈ R>0 , which is a design parameter. Specifically, L1 , L2 and L3 encode conditions (3) to (5)
237   of control barrier certificate, respectively. Additionally, we train the network k(x) with L3 . Note
238   that this loss depends on both networks, hence, training both B(x) and k(x) requires dealing with
239
      the moving target problem (Mnih et al., 2015). To remedy this problem, we fix B for a predefined
      number of iterations for loss L3 .
240
241   To motivate the use of MSE theoretically, we present the following simple example. Consider the
242   scalar system S = (X , X0 , U, f ), where the dynamics are given by f (x) = x2 , with X = [−10, 10],
243   the initial set X0 = [3, 4], and the unsafe set Xu = [−10, 0). Since this system is positive, we have
244   x(t) ≥ 0 and thus the system is safe, for every t ∈ N. Consider a barrier certificate given by a linear
245
      neural network B(x) = M x with the Lipschitz constant LB = |M |. When using ReLU loss, any
      non-positive value of M leads to a loss of 0. However, with MSE loss, non-positive M with large
246
      absolute value—which corresponds to a larger Lipschitz constant for the barrier certificate—results
247
      in a larger loss. In this sense, MSE promotes barrier certificates with smaller Lipschitz constants,
248   leading to effectively smoother barrier functions.

373   5.1    D ISCUSSION
374
375   Zhang et al. (2024) assume that a candidate NCBC is already given, and after verification, they
376   synthesize an admissible controller. Therefore, their method performs well on shallow networks.
377   Moreover, they assume that they have access to exact model of the system (same as Edwards et al.
      (2024); Zhao et al. (2021a)). On the other hand, we train an NCBC from scratch and assume access


                                                                                7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      to a black-box representation. The only information that we need from the system is the Lips-
379   chitz constants Lx and Lu , as in Assumption 2. If those constants are unknown, one can leverage
380   sampling based methods to estimate those constants (Wood & Zhang, 1996; Strongin et al., 2019;
381   Calliess, 2017). As shown previously, even with milder assumptions, our method outperforms the
382   existing work and is able to scale to higher dimensional and more complex systems. Furthermore,
383   our method can employ over-parameterized networks to benefit from their representability. Other
384   than scalability, our synthesized controller also has a small Lipschitz constant compared to the rest.
385   This benefit stems from the fact that we have encoded conditions of CBC using MSE loss, which
386   is differentiable in its global minimum (as opposed to ReLU), and comes with convergence guaran-
387
      tees (Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Cheridito et al., 2022).
388   We acknowledge that other approaches in the literature provide tighter bounds on the Lipschitz con-
389   stant of neural networks compared to Combettes & Pesquet (2020), such as Fazlyab et al. (2019);
390   Pauli et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2024); Araujo et al. (2023). However, these methods are compu-
391   tationally expensive. In fact, our numerical experiments indicate that approximately 99% of the
392   training time is spent calculating the Lipschitz constant, with only1% dedicated to actual training.
393
      The spectral norm approach offers a good trade-off: it provides a much tighter bound than the trivial
      upper bound while remaining efficient to compute. We performed an a posteriori comparison be-
394
      tween the spectral norm approach and the method proposed by Fazlyab et al. (2019). Our results
395
      show that the spectral norm approach is an order of magnitude faster than Fazlyab et al. (2019),
396   while the upper bound it provides is only 40% to 50% larger than the upper bound obtained by Fa-
397   zlyab et al. (2019), which can be offset by ϵ and η. This finding aligns with the results reported
398   in Fazlyab et al. (2019), particularly in Figure 2a.

478   6   R ELATED W ORK
479
480   Barrier Certificates. Prajna & Jadbabaie (2004) first introduced the notion of barrier certificates,
481   whose level sets provide over-approximations of the reachable sets of systems. CBCs emerged as a
482   promising approach to synthesize safe controllers (Ames et al., 2019; Dai & Permenter, 2023; Xiao
483   & Belta, 2019; Clark, 2021; Jagtap et al., 2020). Traditionally, Sum-of-Squars (SOS) optimization
484   is deployed to synthesize such controllers (Zhao et al., 2023; Schneeberger et al., 2023; Prajna
485   et al., 2007). However, these methods require mathematical model of systems and are restricted to
      polynomial type dynamics only.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Neural Barrier Certificates. Zhao et al. (2020) first introduced a notion of neural barrier certifi-
487   cates. They consider a simple, one hidden layer neural network to represent a barrier certificate,
488   and employed Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) to verify its correctness. Later, Peruffo
489   et al. (2021) utilized SMT solvers to find counter examples to a candidate neural barrier certificate
490   and used those counter examples to train their neural networks. Neural barrier certificates have also
491   been employed for safety verification of hybrid (Zhao et al., 2021b) and stochastic (Mathiesen et al.,
492   2022) systems.
493   Neural Control Barrier Certificates. To tackle the controller synthesize problem, NCBCs have
494   been proposed recently (Dawson et al., 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Robey et al., 2020; Lindemann
495   et al., 2021; 2024). Existing work utilizes methods such as SMT solvers (Zhao et al., 2020; Ed-
496   wards et al., 2024; Abate et al., 2020), reachable set verification (Xiang et al., 2018), polynomial
497   approximation (Sha et al., 2021), Lipschitz continuity (Anand & Zamani, 2023), and ReLU networks
498   verification (Katz et al., 2017), to formally verify the correctness of NCBCs. More recently, Zhang
499   et al. (2024) proposed a novel algorithm for exact verification of NCBCs, by considering a given bar-
500   rier certificate, and synthesizing a controller if that barrier is correct. Almost all of aforementioned
501
      algorithms require exact model of a system (with the exception of Anand & Zamani (2023)), and
      utilize SMT solvers; These solvers cannot deal with deep neural networks efficiently, as the compu-
502
      tational complexity grows exponentially with respect to number of parameters, which restricts the
503
      architecture of neural networks.

      7   C ONCLUSION
508
509   This paper presents advancements in the synthesis and verification of NCBCs by addressing key
510   limitations in prior works. First, by reformulating traditional CBC conditions using MSE loss func-
511   tions, we introduced smoother gradients, resulting in more stable and efficient training of neural
512   networks. Second, leveraging Lipschitz continuity assumptions, we established training termi-
513   nation conditions that allow guaranteed safety across the entire state space, eliminating the need
514   for post-hoc verification and enhancing scalability. Finally, through experimental validation on six
515
      state-of-the-art case studies, we demonstrated that our method improves scalability in terms of sys-
      tem dimensions and network architecture. Additionally, our approach yields synthesized barrier
516
      certificates and controllers with smaller Lipschitz constants, simplifying the verification process,
517
      and improves robustness and transferability. Possible future direction is to encode conditions of
518   NCBCs using other losses, and investigate effects of MSE on other neural certificates such as Lya-
519   punov (Chang et al., 2019) and Closure certificates (Nadali et al., 2024), and alleviating the sample
520   complexity with properties of the system, such as monotonicity (Angeli & Sontag, 2003) and mixed-
521   monotonicity (Coogan & Arcak, 2015).
522

525
526
527   Although our method is capable of utilizing over-parameterized networks, it still suffers from ex-
528
      ponential sample complexity, which limits its applicability to higher-dimensional systems. Further-
      more, we use the spectral norm method (Combettes & Pesquet, 2020) to estimate the Lipschitz
529
      constant of neural networks. While this approach is more conservative compared to other meth-
530
      ods such as (Fazlyab et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2024), it offers the advantage of significantly lower
