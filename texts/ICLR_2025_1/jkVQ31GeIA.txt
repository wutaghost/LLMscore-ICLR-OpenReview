009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Iterative retrieval refers to the process in which the model continuously queries
012            the retriever during generation to enhance the relevance of the retrieved knowl-
013            edge, thereby improving the performance of Retrieval-Augmented Generation
014            (RAG). Existing work typically employs few-shot prompting or manually con-
015            structed rules to implement iterative retrieval. This introduces additional inference
016            overhead and overlooks the remarkable reasoning capabilities of Large Language
017            Models (LLMs). In this paper, we introduce Auto-RAG, an autonomous itera-
018
               tive retrieval model centered on the LLM’s powerful decision-making capabilities.
               Auto-RAG engages in multi-turn dialogues with the retriever, systematically plan-
019
               ning retrievals and refining queries to acquire valuable knowledge. This process
020
               continues until sufficient external information is gathered, at which point the re-
021            sults are presented to the user. To this end, we develop a method for autonomously
022            synthesizing reasoning-based decision-making instructions in iterative retrieval
023            and fine-tuned the latest open-source LLMs. The experimental results indicate
024            that Auto-RAG is capable of autonomous iterative interaction with the retriever,
025            effectively leveraging the remarkable reasoning and decision-making abilities of
026            LLMs, which lead to outstanding performance across six benchmarks. Further
027            analysis reveals that Auto-RAG can autonomously adjust the number of iterations
028            based on the difficulty of the questions and the utility of the retrieved knowl-
029
               edge, without requiring any human intervention. Moreover, Auto-RAG expresses
               the iterative retrieval process in natural language, enhancing interpretability while
030
               providing users with a more intuitive experience.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

      Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) for Large Language Models (LLMs) is widely employed
036   to tackle knowledge-intensive tasks (Asai et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Feng
037   et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024), which substantially improves output quality and effectively mitigates
038   hallucinations (Gao et al., 2024; Lewis et al., 2020). However, certain limitations persist, such as
039   noise in retrieved content (Yu et al., 2023) and the challenge of retrieving sufficient knowledge for
040   complex queries in a single attempt (Feng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). These issues ultimately
041   undermine the overall performance of RAG systems and impede their widespread adoption.
042   To address these limitations, iterative retrieval has been proposed, which consistently updates re-
043   trieval results to satisfy the dynamic information needs that arise during the generation process (Feng
044   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Asai et al., 2023). Existing work often relies on few-shot prompting
045   and manually crafted rules to implement iterative retrieval (Jiang et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023;
046   Wang et al., 2024a), which involves substantial human effort and additional computational over-
047   head during inference. Moreover, these methods overlook LLMs’ reasoning and decision-making
048   capabilities (Wei et al., 2023), wasting their potential on determining when and what to retrieve.
049   To this end, we introduce Auto-RAG, an autonomous iterative retrieval model centered on the
050   LLM’s powerful decision-making capabilities. As shown in Figure 1, Auto-RAG models the in-
051   teraction between the LLM and the retriever through multi-turn dialogue. During iterative retrieval,
052   Auto-RAG employs reasoning for retrieval planning, extracting valuable external knowledge, iden-
053   tifying information needs, rewriting queries, and continuously querying the retriever for new in-
      formation until it can adequately answer the user’s question. To empower LLMs with the ability

109   (Lewis et al., 2020; Chu et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024). The most common RAG approach follows
110   the Retrieve-Read framework (Gao et al., 2024), where retrieved documents are concatenated with
111   the user’s input to provide LLMs with external knowledge. However, retrievers are not without
112   flaws (Gao et al., 2024), and the retrieved content may contain noise, which has been shown to de-

      knowledge from a single retrieval attempt as the complexity of tasks increases (Jiang et al., 2023).
118
119   Iterative Retrieval Iterative retrieval was introduced to address the evolving knowledge require-
120   ments that arise when solving complex problems (Feng et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,
121   2023; Trivedi et al., 2023). The core principle of iterative retrieval is determining when and what
122   to retrieve (Jiang et al., 2023). For instance, ITER-RETGEN (Shao et al., 2023) concatenates the
123
      input question with the generated output from the previous iteration to form a new query for the
      next. While this method has achieved some success, it merely reflects existing knowledge without
124
      explicitly indicating the LLM’s information needs. To address this shortcoming, FLARE (Jiang
125
      et al., 2023) uses the next generated sentence as a query, refining the previous sentence based on the
126   retrieval results. Although this method more precisely identifies the LLM’s information needs, its ef-
127   ficacy heavily depends on meticulously crafted few-shot prompts (Brown et al., 2020) and requires
128   continuous retrieval and refinement, leading to substantial manual effort and increased inference
129   costs. Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023) trains LLMs to reflect on both retrieved and generated content.
130   However, Self-RAG only learns to mechanically predict reflection tokens during training, without
131   cultivating reasoning abilities, which further limits the effectiveness of this approach.
132   In contrast to the methods mentioned above, Auto-RAG fully releases the LLMs’ potential for
133   reasoning-based autonomous decision-making in the iterative retrieval process. Auto-RAG enables
134   LLMs to autonomously decide when to retrieve and what to retrieve through reasoning. Compared
135   to other iterative retrieval methods, Auto-RAG delivers superior performance and higher efficiency.

151   retrieval is required and what specific information to seek. Once sufficient information is acquired,

      3.1.1    R EASONING BASED P LANNING AND Q UERY R EFINEMENT
193
194   To optimize efficiency and ensure coherence during iterative processes, it is essential to develop
195   a well-designed reasoning paradigm. Specifically, mirroring the human cognitive process during
196   retrieval, we propose that iterative retrieval should incorporate three distinct types of reasoning: (1)
197   Retrieval Planning, (2) Information Extraction, and (3) Answer Inference.
198
      • (1) Retrieval Planning Upon receiving the user’s question, the LLM should explicitly identify the
199     knowledge necessary to address the query. Furthermore, upon receiving retrieved documents, the
200     LLM must evaluate whether further retrievals are needed and, if so, specify the precise information
201     to be sought next. Maintaining strategic planning throughout the retrieval process is crucial for
202     improving efficiency and mitigating the risk of losing direction midway (Wang et al., 2024a).
203   • (2) Information Extraction Upon receiving retrieved documents, the LLM should adeptly extract
204     relevant information essential for addressing the problem at hand. This human-like summarization
205     process bolsters the LLM’s capacity to filter out irrelevant information, thereby enhancing both its
206     efficiency and accuracy in processing external knowledge(Wei et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
207   • (3) Answer inference Once LLM has gathered all pertinent knowledge required to address the
208     question, it should employ reasoning to formulate the final answer. This process enhances LLM’s
209     ability to generate accurate responses based on available information, thereby mitigating the risk
210     of generating hallucinations (Wei et al., 2023).
211
      These three types of reasoning collectively constitute the Chain-of-Thought utilized during iterative
212   retrieval. To elicit such a reasoning process, we utilize few-shot prompting following Jiang et al.
213   (2023); Brown et al. (2020); Wei et al. (2023). It is noteworthy that steps (2) and (3) are typically

225   3.1.2    DATA F ILTERING AND F ORMATTING
226
227   Data filtering The preceding subsections have thoroughly elucidated the methodologies for eliciting
228   reasoning and query refinement in iterative retrieval. Nevertheless, there remains the possibility of
229   reasoning artifacts or suboptimal query quality. Following Yoran et al. (2023); Asai et al. (2023),
230   we undertake filtering for the generated reasoning and queries. In multi-hop question-answering
231
      datasets that encompass sub-answers, multiple queries are sampled at each retrieval iteration (Yoran
      et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2020). Each query is employed to perform the retrieval, and those queries for
232
      which the retrieved documents contain a sub-answer are retained. Moreover, to ensure the quality of
233
      the entire iterative retrieval process and the coherence of the output answers, data is retained if the
234   final answer A aligns with the reference answer Y provided in the dataset. For greater clarity, we
235   outline the framework of instruction synthesis and filtering in Algorithm 1.
236

260   3.3     I NFERENCE
261
      After training, Auto-RAG has acquired the ability to make reasoning-based autonomous decisions
262
      during iterative retrieval, effectively discerning both when and what to retrieve. During each it-
263
      eration, it suffices to provide Auto-RAG with input—whether user inquiries or retrieved docu-
264   ments—and to extract the planned actions designated by Auto-RAG for subsequent steps. Specif-
265   ically, in the 0-th iteration, Auto-RAG receives the user’s question as input and subsequently gen-
266   erates the reasoning and planning output yt . In the t-th iteration, if the output from the previous
267   iteration yt−1 includes a query q, this query is utilized for retrieval, and the retrieved documents dt
268   are then provided to Auto-RAG as input, resulting in the output for that iteration yt . Conversely,
269   if the output from the previous iteration yt−1 does not contain a query but instead presents a final
      answer, the iteration is concluded, and the final answer is returned to the user.

291   Utilization of parametric knowledge Due to the limitations of the retriever and the retrieval corpus,
292   Auto-RAG may fail to acquire the necessary knowledge to answer a question, resulting in perpet-

402   5.1                                                     S TRONG A DAPTABILITY TO Q UESTIONS AND R ETRIEVERS
403
      In practical applications, the complexity of questions and the length of retrieved documents can
404
      vary significantly, highlighting the importance of examining Auto-RAG’s adaptability to these ex-
405   ternal variations. We analyzed the proportion of iterations and performance for Auto-RAG when the
406   retriever provides different numbers of documents at each iteration across various datasets.
407
408
      First, as demonstrated in Figure 3, the proportion of terminations after a single iteration is slightly
      higher for NQ (Figure 3a) and TriviaQA (Figure 3b) compared to HotpotQA (Figure 3c). This differ-
409
      ence can be attributed to the fact that NQ and TriviaQA are single-hop QA tasks, whereas HotpotQA
410
      involves multiple hops. This observation suggests that Auto-RAG is capable of adaptively adjusting
411   the number of iterations in response to the complexity of the questions posed. Furthermore, as the
412   quantity of documents provided in each round increases, the proportion of terminations after one
413   iteration also rises. This indicates that Auto-RAG flexibly modulates the number of iterations based
414   on the sufficiency of available information. Additionally, as illustrated in Figure 4, providing vary-
415   ing quantities of documents at each iteration has a certain impact on the overall QA performance.
416   In these three tasks, offering three documents per iteration yields superior results, indicating that
417   supplying Auto-RAG with appropriately sized documents is beneficial. We also compared Auto-
418   RAG with the no-retrieval approach (Naive Gen) and Standard RAG. Auto-RAG consistently out-
419
      performed them across different document counts per iteration. Notably, Auto-RAG exhibited less
      performance fluctuation than Standard RAG, demonstrating its superior robustness to retrievers.

      To demonstrate the superior performance of Auto-RAG, we compare its results with those of FLARE
478

479
      manually constructed rules to retrieve and revise low-probability components of the generated con-

518              5.6                              C ASE S TUDY
519
520
                 We conducted a case study to compare Auto-RAG with Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023), as illustrated
                 in Figure 9. For each retrieved document, Self-RAG independently generates answers and reflects
521
                 on them by predicting a reflection token, ultimately selecting the highest-scoring answer as the re-
522
                 sponse. This method is not only time-consuming but also fails to account for the relevance among
523              documents. If the existing documents are all irrelevant, Self-RAG is unable to initiate new searches
524              to correct the erroneous answers. In contrast, Auto-RAG relies entirely on its autonomous decision-
525              making capabilities to determine when and what to retrieve. When confronted with irrelevant doc-
526              uments, Auto-RAG refrains from providing an answer and continues to retrieve information until
527              it acquires valuable knowledge, subsequently returning the answer to the user. Additionally, Auto-
528              RAG articulates its reasoning process in natural language rather than generating reflection tokens,
529              resulting in greater interpretability and a more intuitive user experience.
530
531               6                               C ONCLUSION
532
533
                 In this paper, we introduce Auto-RAG, an autonomous iterative retrieval model centered on the
534              LLM’s powerful decision-making capabilities. Auto-RAG interacts with the retriever through multi-
535              turn dialogues, systematically planning retrievals and refining queries to acquire valuable knowl-
536              edge until sufficient external information is obtained, at which point the results are presented to the
537              user. To this end, we develop a method for autonomously synthesizing reasoning-based decision-
538              making instructions in iterative retrieval and fine-tuned the latest open-source LLMs. Analysis re-
539              sults demonstrate that Auto-RAG not only achieves outstanding performance but also retains a high
                 degree of interpretability, offering users a more intuitive experience.

        generating more coherent yet fabricated knowledge that is convincing to LLMs.
