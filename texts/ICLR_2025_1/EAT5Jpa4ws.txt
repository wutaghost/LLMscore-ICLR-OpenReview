011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013                While generalizable 3D Gaussian Splatting enables efficient, high-quality render-
014                ing of unseen scenes, it heavily depends on precise camera poses for accurate ge-
015                ometry. In real-world scenarios, obtaining accurate poses is challenging, leading
016                to noisy pose estimates and geometric misalignments. To address this, we intro-
017
                   duce SHARE, a novel pose-free generalizable Gaussian Splatting framework that
                   overcomes these ambiguities. Our ray-guided multi-view fusion network consoli-
018
                   dates multi-view features into a unified pose-aware canonical volume, bridging 3D
019
                   reconstruction and ray-based pose estimation. In addition, we propose an anchor-
020                aligned Gaussian prediction strategy for fine-grained geometry estimation within
021                a canonical view. Extensive experiments on diverse real-world datasets show that
022                SHARE achieves state-of-the-art performance in pose-free generalizable Gaussian
023                splatting.

043   Recent years have witnessed unprecedented progress in Novel View Synthesis (NVS) and 3D scene
044   reconstruction, marked by the emergence of neural implicit representations (Sitzmann et al., 2019;
045   Park et al., 2019; Mildenhall et al., 2021) and explicit volumetric approaches such as 3D Gaussian
046   splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023). Central to this task is the utilization of precise camera poses, which
047   serve as a fundamental geometric prior coupling the spatial relationship between 3D space and their
048   corresponding 2D-pixel projections across multiple views.
049   However, the assumption of readily available accurate camera poses is often unsatisfiable in practi-
050   cal scenarios. While Structure-from-Motion (SfM) (Snavely et al., 2006) techniques have long been
051   the go-to solution for obtaining camera poses, they become increasingly unreliable as views become
052   sparser or camera baselines widen. This challenge is particularly acute in a generalizable setting
053   where the number of input views is often limited and test-time adaptation is not considered. A seem-
      ingly intuitive solution might be to employ learning-based camera pose prediction methods designed

054
      for sparse view inputs. Unfortunately, even slight inaccuracies in estimated camera poses can be am-
055   plified into large positional errors in 3D space, resulting in significant geometry misalignment across
056   input views, as shown in Figure 1.
057
058
      Pose-free 3D Gaussian splatting methods have been explored to overcome this problem. Previous
      works (Fu et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024) propose an iterative test-time adaptation approach that
059
      jointly rectifies pose and geometry for better alignment. GGRt (Li et al., 2024) proposes the gener-
060
      alizable reconstruction for video sequence inputs without pose information. Nevertheless, existing
061   methods require additional computation for each scene or only consider sequential frames as input,
062   compromising general applicability.
063
064
      In pursuit of novel view synthesis in general scenarios without poses, we propose SHARE, a novel
      approach for pose-free generalizable 3D Gaussian splatting. SHARE aims to learn a holistic repre-
065
      sentation of multi-view features in a unified canonical view space. The key to our multi-view fusion
066
      process lies in embedding relative poses as spatially defined Plücker rays, which allows easy in-
067   jection of pose information throughout the reconstruction pipeline. This acts as a multi-view prior
068   across the input views in the fusion process, resolving the misalignment issues and enhancing ge-
069   ometric consistency across views. Building on our pose-aware fusion, we introduce anchor-based
070   Gaussian prediction to reconstruct fine details in a unified space. We estimate pixel-aligned coarse
071   geometry from the unified representation as anchor points for the local 3D space for shape consis-
072   tency. From each anchor, we predict offsets to determine the splatting locations of Gaussians.
073   We evaluate our approach with scene level datasets such as DTU (Jensen et al., 2014) and
074   RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018) datasets. SHARE achieves robust reconstruction quality in pose-
075   free scenarios, showing superior performance to previous pose-free generalizable reconstruction
076   approaches (Jiang et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Smith et al., 2023), and even comparable to gener-
077   alizable 3DGS (Charatan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b) with ground-truth poses in DTU (Jensen
078   et al., 2014) datasets. Further analysis illustrates the effectiveness of our proposed multi-view fu-
079   sion process and synergetic improvement in geometry and pose estimation. Our contribution can be
080   summarized as below:
081
             • We propose SHARE, a novel pose-free generalizable 3D Gaussian Splatting framework that
082
               simultaneously estimates geometry and camera pose from sparse-view unposed images.
083
084          • Our ray-guided multi-view fusion strategy effectively constructs a holistic feature for 3D
085            shape representation, effectively mitigating the geometry misalignment while covering
086
               multi-view observations with fine details.
087          • SHARE shows superior performance on scene-level datasets with varying scales, including
088            DTU (Jensen et al., 2014) and RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018), outperforming exist-
089            ing pose-free generalizable reconstruction approaches and highlighting robustness under
090            scene-scale datasets.

095
      view synthesis methods have emerged to expand the applicability of novel view synthesis (NVS)
096   to a wider range of scenarios without test-time adaptations. Techniques based on Neural Radiance
097   Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) utilize neural features from input views to render images
098   by querying and accumulating features of sampled points along cast rays, leveraging known camera
099   poses (Yu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Sajjadi et al., 2022). As generalizable
100   NeRFs often face challenges in terms of rendering efficiency and processing speed due to neural
101   network inference on densely sampled points, recent advancements have introduced 3D Gaussian
102   Splatting for fast and efficient generalizable reconstruction (Zheng et al., 2024; Szymanowicz et al.,
103   2024b; Charatan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Wewer et al., 2024).

105   sians in three-dimensional space on the fly. PixelSplat (Charatan et al., 2024) learns probabilistic
106   depth distributions in ray space, effectively addressing the local support limitation of Gaussians
107   under rendering supervision. LatentSplat (Wewer et al., 2024) improves on this by encoding 3D
      variational Gaussians using a variational auto-encoder (VAE) and incorporating a discriminator to

130   approaches for reconstructing 3D shapes from sparse or noisy pose initializations (Hong et al.,
131   2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023).

      need for precise pose estimation by constraining a canonical camera space, thereby constraining the
      pose estimation space. While these works excel in generalizable 3D reconstruction without poses,
139
      the implicit nature of their NeRF backbone complicates the joint optimization of both scene repre-
140
      sentation and camera poses (Li et al., 2024).

142   Gaussian-based methods (Fu et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024) have improved optimization and ef-
143   ficiency for pose-free cases but continue to exhibit limitations in generalizability, requiring pro-
144
      gressive test-time optimizations. To address this problem, concurrent pose-free generalizable 3D
      Gaussian Splatting (g-3DGS) methods (Smart et al., 2024; Szymanowicz et al., 2024a) utilizes pre-
145
      trained models to leverage the power of geometric priors (Wang et al., 2024; Piccinelli et al., 2024).
146
      While promising, these approaches are often affected by scale ambiguities between the estimated
147   geometry and camera poses, requiring fine-tuning with ground-truth depth supervision. GGRt (Li
148   et al., 2024) also addresses pose-free 3D Gaussian Splatting by taking video frames as input, as-
149   suming sequential pose transformation between views. In contrast, our method is designed to handle
150   sparse-view inputs with diverse camera baseline configurations without scale ambiguity by offer-
151   ing improved robustness by jointly estimating cameras as rays and 3D Gaussian splats in a unified
152   canonical space.

154   3    OVERVIEW
155
156   Our approach addresses the critical challenge of geometric misalignment when extending 3D Gaus-
157   sian splatting to unposed settings. This misalignment arises from difficulties in aligning geometry
158   across different viewpoints and is particularly sensitive to errors in estimated relative poses (see Left
159   of Figure 2). To overcome this issue, we introduce a pose-aware feature fusion strategy that esti-
160   mates a unified, holistic representation from a chosen canonical view (see Right of Figure 2). This
161   representation aligns both geometry and appearance across all input views by integrating pose priors
      into the fusion process.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      A key insight of our method is that embedding estimated relative camera rays into multi-view fea-
163   tures provides effective guidance to mitigate geometric misalignments in the latent space. Previous
164   works (Zhou & Tulsiani, 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Tang et al., 2025) have used
165   ground-truth camera poses with ray-based representations to guide view-conditional generation or
166   3D reconstruction. However, our approach is distinct in that it operates in a pose-free setting, where
167   relative poses are estimated on the fly. Specifically, we jointly predict Plücker rays and 3D Gaus-
168   sians in a feed-forward manner directly from input images, eliminating the need for ground-truth
169   pose annotations.
170   Our ray guidance enables cost aggregation to refine multi-view cost volumes using ray embeddings
171   derived from the estimated poses and provide rich pose information for the reconstruction model.
172   These ray embeddings are injected throughout the refinement process, guiding the framework to
173   build a robust canonical representation from the multi-view cost volumes. Additionally, our an-
174   chor-based Gaussian prediction estimates fine scene details from arbitrary views based on the fused
175   canonical cost volume. This prediction strategy reduces misalignment by allowing multi-view fea-
176   tures to implicitly contribute to Gaussian estimation in a fixed canonical space, rather than estimating
177
      Gaussians per view. Further details of each method are provided in the following section.

      After constructing the cost volumes, we refine them through cost aggregation conditioned on
250   predicted Plücker rays, using patch-wise cross-attention to embed pose awareness into the vol-
251   umes (Hong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b). We argue that Plücker rays offer several advantages in
252   pose-free, generalizable pipelines. Being locally defined in the 2D spatial dimension, they integrate
253   seamlessly with spatial image features, and their over-parameterized nature introduces a geomet-
254   ric bias that global extrinsic camera matrices cannot capture (Schops et al., 2020). Furthermore,
255   their scale-invariant representation enables effective fusion across varying object scales and camera
256   positions (Chen et al., 2024a).
257   Specifically, we refine the cost volumes using a transformer-based 2D U-Net with cross-attention
258   layers, where the rays serve as key-value pairs and the cost volumes act as queries. This allows
259   the estimated ray embeddings to provide geometric guidance for constructing a unified canonical
260   volume, with the rays functioning as global positional embeddings across multi-view inputs.

343   Datasets. We train and evaluate our method on two distinct real-world datasets, DTU (Jensen et al.,
344
      2014) and RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018). These datasets were selected to assess our approach
      across varying scene scales and camera configurations.
345
346   DTU (Jensen et al., 2014) contains small-scale static scenes captured from 49 cameras with diverse
347   configurations, provided with camera parameters. We use 75 scenes for training and 15 for test-
348   ing (Na et al., 2024). This dataset validates SHARE on small-scale scenes with diverging camera
349   baselines.
350   RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018) contains 67,477 training and 7,289 testing scenes from YouTube
351   real estate videos, with camera parameters for each frame. We follow the train and test splits of
352   previous work (Chen et al., 2024b). This dataset, featuring typical camera movements in real-world
353   videos, evaluates SHARE on large-scale data.
354   Furthermore, we conducted cross-dataset experiments by evaluating SHARE on the ACID (Liu et al.,
355   2021) and BlendedMVS (Yao et al., 2020) datasets, each trained using the RealEstate10K (Zhou
356   et al., 2018) and DTU (Jensen et al., 2014) datasets. Detailed descriptions of the datasets and the
357   evaluation results are provided in Appendix A.4.
358
      Baselines. We compare our method with state-of-the-art generalizable 3D Gaussian splatting (g-

373   tion following (Hong et al., 2024).

401   5.2    N OVEL V IEW S YNTHESIS .
402
403   To evaluate its performance, we conduct experiments on the DTU (Jensen et al., 2014) and
404   RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018) datasets, which encompass a range of scenarios from object-
405   centric indoor scenes to large-scale environments.
406   On the DTU dataset (Table 1), where scenes are captured with large camera transformations around
407   a central object, our method exhibits superior performance across varying camera baselines. No-
408   tably, we outperform g-3DGS methods even with slightly noisy pose conditions. In particular, our
409   approach achieves LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) and SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) scores comparable to
410   those of g-3DGS with ground-truth poses, highlighting the robustness of our pose-aware multi-view
411   fusion approach in handling pose-free scenarios with diverse camera configurations.
412   Our key observation is that g-3DGS baselines, when using noisy poses, often produce significantly
413   distorted renderings. This emphasizes the sensitivity of g-3DGS methods to pose inaccuracies, espe-
414   cially in sparse-view settings where even minor pose noise (e.g., σ = 0.01, with less than 1◦ angular
415   error) leads to noticeable degradation in performance.
416   On the RealEstate10K dataset (Table 2), which features larger-scale scenes, our method consistently
417   outperforms both g-3DGS with estimated poses and pose-free generalizable NeRF methods (Hong
418   et al., 2024; Smith et al., 2023). These results further demonstrate the scalability and adaptability of
419   our approach across different scene complexities.
420
      Figure 5 provides qualitative visualizations of rendered results, showing superior fidelity and geo-
421
      metric consistency of our method compared to the baselines on both datasets. These visual results
422   further validate the effectiveness of our approach in challenging, pose-free scenarios.
423
424   Geometry Reconstruction. The strength of our method is particularly evident when visualizing
425
      the reconstructed geometry through rendered depth maps (see Figure 6). On both the DTU and
      RealEstate10K datasets, SHARE consistently predicts accurate geometry, while baselines often pro-
426
      duce incorrect and noisy geometry, even when their synthesized colors appear acceptable. Notably,
427
      CoPoNeRF (Hong et al., 2024), despite its high rendering performance, frequently fails in depth
428   prediction. This reflects SHARE’s ability to accurately capture appearance and geometry, thanks to
429   the ray-guided multi-view fusion pipeline.

512   6    C ONCLUSION
513
514   In this work, we present SHARE, the first pose-free generalizable 3D Gaussian splatting approach
515   validated across small and large-scale scene-level datasets with varying camera baselines. Our ray-
516   guided multi-view fusion strategy effectively addresses geometry ambiguity caused by incorrect
517   poses, capturing a unified feature representation from all input views into a single canonical esti-
518   mation space. Our two-stage Gaussian predictor aligns seamlessly with this strategy, successfully
519   capturing fine shape details visible in multi-view data, even within the constrained estimation space.
520
      Through comprehensive experiments, we demonstrated that our model can reconstruct high-fidelity
      3D structures across small and large scene scales, even in challenging scenarios involving large cam-
521
      era baselines. We believe that our approach introduces a novel approach for effectively addressing
522
      geometry misalignment with a pose-aware fusion pipeline.
523
524   Limitations and Discussions. While SHARE achieves strong pose-free generalization across di-
525   verse settings, it is not without its limitations. One potential drawback is its performance in scenes
526
      with significantly limited overlap between views. Thus, applying SHARE to challenging scenarios,
      such as 360-degree scenes with sparse image inputs, presents an area that could benefit from further
527
      exploration. Another consideration is the reliance on camera intrinsic parameters, which could re-
528
      strict the method’s usability in practical scenarios involving varied image sources, such as datasets
529   captured with different cameras or online images lacking metadata. Addressing these challenges and
530   broadening the scope of our approach present compelling directions for future work.

766   A.1   D ETAILED D ISCUSSION ON R AY G UIDANCE
767
      Most conventional methods in Multi-view Stereo (MVS) utilize cameras to establish geometrical
768
      relationships across the input views. However, the relationship becomes unreliable when given poses
769   are noisy. We argue that it is important for the image features to have an awareness of camera poses
770   to mitigate the influence of unreliable relationships during the 3D reconstruction. To this end, we
771   combine predicted Plücker rays with image features to construct the cost volume, leveraging the
772   advantages of using a generic camera representation. The intuition behind our design choice is to
773   inject awareness of camera pose in multi-view space to each image feature.
774
      Specifically, we project features from different viewpoints to compute correlation among input im-
775   ages by converting rays into camera poses and performing homography warping. While this allows
776   some pose error, which leads to misalignment in feature space, we rectify the cost volumes by pose-
777   aware cost aggregation process described in Section 4.2 of the main paper. As shown in Figure 9,
778   eliminating pose embedding leads to large discrepancies in geometry estimation, leading to blurry
779   images or introducing artifacts. This highlights the importance of pose embedding in our fusion
780   process.

      A.3   D ETAILS OF MODEL IMPLEMENTATION
865
866   In this section, we’ll discuss our framework in more detail. Given sparse-view unposed images, our
867   goal is to build comprehensive Gaussians in a canonical space. The output of the multi-view feature
868   extractor is V × C × H × W , where we set C as 128 in all experiments. Given these features, we
869   estimate the relative Plücker rays V × 6 × H × W with two additional transformer blocks following
870   the U-Net structure of (Zhang et al., 2024). Then, we embed ray with a lightweight MLP to latent
871   space and modulate multi-view features using AdaLN (Peebles & Xie, 2023), following LaRa (Chen
872   et al., 2024a). In the ray-guided multi-view fusion process, we first build the cost volumes from all
873
      input views, where the depth candidates D are all set to 128. We warp all the features to the reference
      views with the estimated pose (converted from Plücker rays). Then, we build the geometry volume
874
      Vg as in 3. The geometry volume is used to estimate the anchor points 3 × H4 × W   4 . Simultaneously,
875
      we build the feature volume Vf in a similar manner, but with the upscaled multi-view features, to
876   estimate the offset vectors and Gaussian parameters necessary for finer detail reconstruction.
877
878   We divide channels of Vf for displacement prediction of anchor points (32), and the remaining
879
      channels (96) encode texture-related Gaussian parameters. The geometry channels of Vf are passed
      through the offset prediction MLP head fo , which predicts the offset vectors ∆pk = fo (Vf ), for the
880
      Gaussian positions. We set K = 3 for all experiments. These offset vectors are then concatenated
881
      with the remaining channels of Vf Another MLP head, fp , processes the concatenated features to
882   estimate the remaining Gaussian parameters.
