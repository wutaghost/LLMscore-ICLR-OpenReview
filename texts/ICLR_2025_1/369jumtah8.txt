011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite the impressive capabilities of Multimodal Large Language Models
014            (MLLMs) in integrating text and image modalities, challenges remain in accu-
015            rately interpreting detailed visual elements. Fortunately, vision detection models
016            have shown superior performance in recognizing fine-grained image details, lead-
017            ing to their increased deployment by researchers to enhance the ability of MLLMs.
018
               Among the feasible strategies, infusing detection information in text format is easy
               to use and effective. However, most studies apply this method in a training-free
019
               manner. There is limited research on the effects of adaptive training, which has
020
               great potential for helping LLMs better comprehend the special input and dis-
021            card irrelevant information. In this paper, we address the key research question:
022            How does training influence MLLMs’ understanding of infused textual detection
023            information? We systematically conduct experiments with numerous represen-
024            tative models to explore the performance implications of training-free, retrain-
025            ing, and fine-tuning strategies when infusing textual detection information into
026            MLLMs. Additionally, we investigate the impact of training on the original abili-
027            ties of MLLMs, as well as the interchangeability of detection models. We find that
028            fine-tuning the pre-trained MLLM to adapt to textual detection information yields
029
               better results compared to the training-free strategy and the retraining strategy,
               with the fine-tuned MLLM outperforms the training-free MLLM by 6.71% across
030
               10 widely recognized benchmarks. Besides, we find that fine-tuning allows the
031
               MLLM to maintain performance improvements even after replacing the deployed
032            detection models, which means that it enables the MLLM to better understand the
033            specially formatted textual information. We release our codes to facilitate further
034            exploration into the fusion strategies of vision detection models and improving the
035            fine-grained multimodal capabilities of MLLMs.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      The advent of large language models (LLMs) has marked a transformative era in natural language
041
      processing (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023), paving the way for the development of Mul-
042   timodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) that blend linguistic and visual understanding. Pioneers
043   such as GPT-4V have demonstrated remarkable proficiency across numerous tasks (Yang et al.,
044   2023). However, a notable gap remains in these models’ ability to accurately discern and recognize
045   fine details within images (Fu et al., 2023). This limitation is particularly evident when MLLMs
046   generate coherent yet misaligned responses with the image content, a phenomenon often referred to
047   as “hallucination” (Li et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023).
048   Current advancements in object detection and optical character recognition (OCR) models have
049   established their effectiveness in identifying objects and text within images (Zou et al., 2023; Liu
050   et al., 2024b). Consequently, researchers have increasingly deployed vision detection models to
051   assist MLLMs in recognizing fine-grained visual elements. A popular approach involves converting
052   the outputs of vision detection models into textual descriptions, which are then supplied to the
053   backbone LLM, thereby enhancing the MLLM’s performance in visual tasks. This fusion strategy
      is both straightforward and effective.

      Nonetheless, the majority of existing research has primarily focused on training-free methods to
074   directly apply the textual detection information 1 . Little exploration has been conducted into adap-
075   tive training methods, which have great potential to enhance LLMs’ comprehension of specially
076   formatted textual content, enabling them to intentionally discard irrelevant information and generate
077   more pertinent responses (Zhang et al., 2024b; Cabessa et al., 2024). This highlights the need for
078   a systematic investigation, particularly concerning the core research question: Can adaptive train-
079   ing further enhance MLLMs’ performance beyond what is achievable through training-free
080   integration of textual detection information?
081   To provide insights into how training impacts the infusion of textual detection information into
082   MLLMs, we investigate training-free, retraining, and fine-tuning strategies for this fusion method.
083   Additionally, we examine how training influences the original image understanding capabilities of
084   MLLMs and the interchangeability of deployed detection models. Based on the experimental anal-
085   ysis encompassing representative advanced models, including LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a), DINO
086   (Zhang et al., 2022), PaddleOCRv2 (Du et al., 2021), and Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2023c),
087   alongside Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023) and YOLOv8 (Jocher et al., 2023) in the appendix, we sys-
088
      tematically uncover the following key insights:
089
      (1) The fine-tuning strategy yields better results than both the training-free and retraining
090
      strategies. Building on prior studies (Wu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Zhou
091
      et al., 2023), we convert the output of vision detection models into textual information and input it
092   into the LLM. We explore three distinct training strategies: the training-free strategy, where detec-
093   tion information is directly fed into the MLLM without additional training; the retraining strategy,
094   which involves retraining the MLLM from scratch and continuously infusing textual detection in-
095   formation; and the fine-tuning strategy, where additional fine-tuning is applied to a pre-trained
096   MLLM to help it comprehend the specially formatted information. Evaluating performance across
097   ten widely recognized benchmarks, we find that all three strategies enhance LLaVA-1.5’s perfor-
098   mance in fine-grained image recognition. Notably, the fine-tuning strategy achieves the most signif-
099   icant improvements, elevating performance by up to 6.71% compared to the training-free approach.
100
101   (2) Retraining with textual detection information impairs MLLMs’ original image compre-
102
      hension abilities. Most advanced MLLMs employ an image encoder to generate image features,
      and their ability to understand these features is crucial for effective multimodal understanding. Our
103
      experiments reveal that retraining the MLLM with textual detection information detrimentally af-
104
      fects its ability to interpret the features from its image encoder. In contrast, the fine-tuning strategy
105   does not run into this problem.

      (3) Fine-tuning allows the MLLM to retain performance improvements upon replacing the
109   deployed detection model. The characteristics and performance of the deployed detection models
110   significantly influence the enhanced MLLM’s effectiveness. Based on the fine-tuning strategy, we

113   user queries during inference. Additionally, we find that the fine-tuned MLLM maintains its training

115   To summarize, our work contributes comprehensive empirical evidence and practical insights into
116   the effects of various training strategies for infusing textual detection information into MLLMs. It
117   identifies a significant gap between the use of adaptive training and training-free methods, high-
118   lighting the potential of adaptive strategies and demonstrating their feasibility through systematic
119   investigation. Our code is publicly available at anonymous link to facilitate further research and
120   pave the way for systems that engage in more nuanced and accurate multimodal dialogue.

      2     BACKGROUND AND M OTIVATION
123
124
      2.1   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS (MLLM S )
125
126   Linking Text and Vision Information. Large Language Models (LLMs) are primarily designed for
127   text-based tasks (Zhao et al., 2023). To incorporate image processing capabilities, modality bridging
128   modules have been developed to reconcile the representation differences between text and images
129   (Yin et al., 2023). Generally, these methods can be categorized into three types:
130   (1) Learnable queries are used to distill information from image features. For instance, Flamingo
131   (Alayrac et al., 2022) employs a perceiver resampler, and IDEFICS (Hugo et al., 2023; Laurençon
132   et al., 2024) uses similar modules to extract features from Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy
133   et al., 2020). BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023c) utilizes learnable queries alongside a Q-Former module, while
134   Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023) compresses visual features into sequences of fixed length using cross-
135   attention layers. (2) Projection-based interfaces bridge modalities with straightforward techniques.
136   Notable examples include LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b;a; 2024a) and MGM (Li et al., 2023d), which
137   utilize simple linear layers to map image features into the text semantic space. (3) Parameter-
138
      efficient tuning modules are utilized to fine-tune MLLMs for image feature comprehension. For
      example, LLaMA-Adapter (Zhang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023) introduces self-attention layers
139
      with zero gating for fine-tuning, and LaVIN (Luo et al., 2023) employs modality-specific adapters.
140
141   Why Incorporating Detection Models into MLLMs? Existing MLLMs often struggle to accu-
142   rately detect fine-grained targets. For example, in Figure 1, LLaVA-1.5 miscounts a herd of sheep,
143   indicating a limitation in its object-counting capability. Additionally, it fails to detect a pedestrian
144
      who is partially obscured by a utility pole, highlighting a weakness in its object localization ability.
      In another scenario, LLaVA-1.5 incorrectly recognizes the license plate number “87025” as “547”,
145
      revealing a shortcoming in its text recognition ability. By contrast, SOTA object detection and OCR
146
      models demonstrate superior performance on detection and recognition tasks, which has led many
147   researchers to explore the application of detection models within the realm of MLLM research.
148
149
      2.2   E NHANCING D ETECTION C APABILITIES FOR MLLM S
150
151   Existing Methods for Detection Capabilities Enhancement. Various strategies have been ex-
152   plored to enable MLLMs aware of image details, generally classified into four types:
153
      (1) Expanding datasets with existing object detection or OCR data: InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023)
154
      utilizes data from 26 datasets across 11 tasks, including OCR data. ASM (Wang et al., 2023a) intro-
155
      duces 1 billion region-text pairs. LLaVA and SPHINX (Lin et al., 2023) compile hybrid instruction
156   fine-tuning datasets, incorporating object detection datasets like VG (Krishna et al., 2017) and the
157   OCR dataset OCRVQA (Mishra et al., 2019). PINK (Xuan et al., 2023) employs a bootstrapping
158   method to cover diverse referential comprehension datasets. MiniGPT4-v2 (Chen et al., 2023b),
159   VisionLLM (Wang et al., 2024b), and Shikra (Chen et al., 2023c) integrate object detection datasets,
160   such as RefCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014), PointQA (Mani et al., 2020), and Flickr30K (Plum-
161   mer et al., 2015), while introducing special detection tokens like “det” to guide downstream tasks
      (further details in Appendix D.7).


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (2) Restructuring the image encoder to extract fine-grained features: LION (Chen et al., 2023a)
163   introduces a Vision Aggregator module for feature aggregation, while Honeybee (Cha et al., 2023)
164   employs a deformable attention-based abstractor for capturing fine details. UReader (Ye et al., 2023)
165   utilizes a shape-adaptive cropping module to process local image features, and Vary (Wei et al.,
166   2023b) develops a dedicated image encoder for text recognition. Eagle (Shi et al., 2024) aligns
167   features from various visual experts, concatenating them as input for the MLLM. Mova (Zong et al.,
168   2024) introduces the MoV-Adapter, which extracts and fuses task-specific knowledge.
169   (3) Integrating pre-trained detection models into MLLMs’ output end to train MLLMs or perform
170   detection tasks: UNIFIED-IO (Lu et al., 2022; 2023) unifies image, text, and detection features into
171   discrete tokens and trains an end-to-end MLLM capable of detecting. ContextDET (Zang et al.,
172   2023) trains a visual decoder for bounding box prediction using contextual LLM tokens. Lenna
173   (Wei et al., 2023a), Lisa (Lai et al., 2023), and Next-chat (Zhang et al., 2024a) introduce additional
174   tokens to prompt detectors for target identification.
175   (4) Converting detection model outputs into text and using it as supplementary input for LLMs:
176   GLEE (Wu et al., 2024) builds on LISA (Lai et al., 2023) to generate SEG tokens for targeted
177   segmentation, enhancing performance by feeding textual object queries into the backbone LLM.
178   P2 G (Chen et al., 2024), Moai (Lee et al., 2024), and IVE (He et al., 2024) employ detection agents
179   to generate textual grounding clues for improved reasoning. Power-LLaVA (Wang et al., 2024a)
180   utilizes an object detector to produce textual class and location information to assist the MLLM
181   in generating high-quality outputs. VLPrompt (Zhou et al., 2023) leverages an object detector to
182   generate target names and infer relationships, thereby aiding MLLMs in reasoning tasks.
183   Why Adaptive Training with Textual Detection Information? Although methods in the second
184   and third categories can improve the detection capabilities of MLLMs, they typically require sub-
185   stantial datasets to train the restructured image encoders or achieve feature alignment. In contrast,
186   text-based methods are simpler, necessitate less extensive training data for the newly built detection
187   modules, and still deliver commendable results. Thus, the fourth type of method is likely to be more
188   frequently employed in practical applications.
189   While most research in this category has concentrated on training-free strategies for infusing tex-
190   tual detection information, we note relevant developments in pure-text LLMs. Zhang et al. (2024b)
191   propose leveraging Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG, Gao et al. (2024)) during fine-tuning to
192   help LLMs discard redundant information from augmented text. Additionally, Cabessa et al. (2024)
193   suggest that infusing well-crafted textual features during fine-tuning can enhance LLMs’ compre-
194   hension of specially formatted inputs. They primarily use a small amount of data to adaptively train
195
      LLMs for comprehending specially formatted text, yielding excellent results.
196   This leads us to an important question: Since the infusion of textual detection information already
197   performs well without training, could this fusion method achieve even better outcomes with appro-
198   priate training? Our work aims to address this question by utilizing the original training data of the
199   studied MLLMs, which is limited in quantity but high in quality, to conduct adaptive training for the
200   infusion of textual detection information into MLLMs.

246
247   Specifically, Figure 2 illustrates the overall architecture of the studied MLLM in different training
248   strategies, taking LLaVA-1.5 as an example. 2 Firstly, the CLIP-ViT-L-336px (Radford et al., 2021)
249   is used to extract image-level features and a two-layer MLP is employed to align these features with
250   text. Subsequently, we separately use DINO and PaddleOCRv2 for object detection and OCR. The
251   results are then converted into sentences using the aforementioned methods and transformed into
252   text features using the embedding layers of the backbone LLM. Next, we concatenate the image-
253
      level features and the detection features and input them into the backbone LLM. As a result, the
      MLLM can simultaneously obtain both the overall image information and the fine-grained image
254
      details during training and inference.

466   4.5     L ESSON 4: S UITABLE F INE -T UNING H ELPS MLLM S B ETTER U NDERSTAND S PECIALLY
467           F ORMATTED D ETECTION I NFORMATION
468
      In the previous experiments, we employ DINO to extract object detection information and success-
469
      fully facilitate performance improvement for the MLLM. However, it is essential to note that DINO
470
      is a closed-set object detection model, capable of detecting only 80 common object categories. Nev-
471   ertheless, images may contain uncommon objects or specific entities such as certain celebrities or
472   objects with attributive modifiers. In such scenarios, the closed-set models exhibit limitations.
473
474
      Fortunately, the studied MLLM architecture is modular, and the deployed detection models are
      independent of the MLLM. Hence, this architecture allows for flexible replacement of the deployed
475
      detection models. In this experiment, we will substitute the closed-set detector DINO with an open-
476
      set detector to observe whether, after the replacement, the finetuned MLLMs (FTBI) can still operate
477   effectively and acquire broader capability for detection.
478
479
      Constructing Detection Information with Grounding DINO. In this experiment, we substitute
480   the embedded closed-set detector DINO with an open-set object detector called Grounding DINO
481   (Liu et al., 2023c). Grounding DINO is designed to detect objects related to user-input. With
482   this model, the studied MLLM can locate targets by referring to the object names mentioned in
483   questions. To achieve this, we first extract target names from the input questions and combine them
484   to create prompts. Grounding DINO then follows the prompts to generate location information
485   for the targets. Finally, the outputs are converted into specially formatted detection information
      following the method in Section 3.1. Figure 3 shows an example of this process.

      Training Effect Inherited Following Replacement of Detection Model. In Table 4, we compare
487   the performance of TFI-7B and FTBI-7B after replacing the detection model DINO with Grounding
488   DINO. We use VQAv2 , GQA∗, POPE, MM-Vet, and SEED-Bench for evaluation as they contain
489   questions from which effective object names can be extracted. Due to the low detection accuracy
490   of Grounding DINO, some noise is introduced, which results in lower evaluation scores for both
491   models compared to LLaVA-1.5-7B. However, as FTBI-7B has been fine-tuned with DINO and it
492   can filter out some noise, the performance of FTBI-7B is superior to that of TFI-7B. These results
493   validate that the training effect remains after we replace the detection model.

521   6   C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we systematically conduct experiments to compare the effects of different training
524   strategies on the infusion of textual detection information into MLLMs. After thorough investiga-
525
      tion, we determine that fine-tuning the original MLLM for an additional epoch, along with the simul-
      taneous infusion of textual detection information, proves to be the most effective approach compared
526
      to the training-free strategy and the retraining strategy. Moreover, we replace the detection model
527
      deployed in the studied MLLM from a close-set detector to an open-set detector and observe that the
528   updated fine-tuned model retains the training effect and achieve better performance than the updated
529   training-free one. This indicates that the fine-tuned model, compared to the training-free model,
530   can better stay abreast of evolving object detection technologies and achieve sustained performance
531   enhancements.
532
      In a nutshell, we provide a series of progressive insights about the effective infusion of textual de-
533
      tection information into MLLMs. We aim to inform researchers that when attempting to convert
534   the outputs of vision detection models into textual information for assisting MLLMs, it can be ben-
535   eficial to use a small amount of general VQA data for additional fine-tuning (potentially using the
536   instruction-tuning data from the MLLM itself). This approach can yield models that perform better
537   than those not subjected to training, allowing the models to have a more comprehensive under-
538   standing and utilization of the detection information. With this work, we hope it can benefit future
539   MLLM research and development that approaches better understanding, interpreting and engaging
      with fine-grained multimodal content.

1034   category, we maintain brevity and clarity in our model’s inputs.

1056   B.2   LL AVA-1.5’ S I NSTRUCTION - FOLLOWING DATASET
1057
1058   The instruction-following dataset of LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a) is a combination of several
1059   datasets that relate to various tasks. Among them, the LLaVA dataset (Liu et al., 2023b) and
1060   ShareGPT dataset (Chiang et al., 2023) comprise high-quality GPT-4 conversation data. VQAv2
1061   (Goyal et al., 2017) and GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019) present samples that require one word or
1062   a short phrase to answer visual questions. OKVQA (Marino et al., 2019) and A-OKVQA (Schwenk
1063   et al., 2022) are VQA datasets designed to expand the knowledge base of multimodal models through
1064
       the incorporation of external prior knowledge. OCRVQA (Mishra et al., 2019) is expressly tailored
       to enhance the text recognition capabilities of multimodal models. TextCaps (Sidorov et al., 2020)
1065
       is an image captioning dataset, which presents samples in the form of concise one-sentence descrip-
1066
       tions corresponding to images. RefCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) and VG (Krishna et al., 2017)
1067   are object detection datasets designed to improve the object localization capabilities of multimodal
1068   models.
1069
1070
       This dataset enables our models to better harness the additional detection information through the
       newly trained MLP and LoRA modules, especially with its object detection and OCR data.
1071
1072   Nevertheless, this dataset comprises only approximately 467K image samples, with only 116K des-
1073   ignated for object detection and approximately 80K for text recognition, which is notably con-
1074   strained. Consequently, should one seek to augment the model’s proficiency in assimilating de-
1075   tection information effectively, the exploration of dataset expansion emerges as a viable and recom-
1076
       mended strategy.
1077   Regarding the pretraining dataset of LLaVA-1.5, it is pertinent to note that this dataset predomi-
1078   nantly consists of samples tailored for image captioning, thus inherently emphasizing image-level
1079   information. However, our detection information focuses more on fine-grained details, so we opt
       not to incorporate this dataset in our FTBI training strategy.

       B.3   I MAGE R ESOLUTION
1081
1082   The user-input images can be of any resolution and they are inputted into CLIP-ViT and detection
1083   modules respectively.
1084
1085         • For CLIP-ViT’s preprocessing, input images are processed to a size of 336x336 (requiring
1086           scaling and padding to form square images).
1087         • For DINO and Grounding DINO’s preprocessing, input images can have arbitrary aspect
1088           ratios. However, we need to limit the length of the shortest side to at least 224 and the
1089           length of the longest side to be within 2048. The setting for the shortest side length is
1090           to prevent insufficient multi-scale features extracted by DINO’s image encoder, ensuring
1091           an adequate number of anchor boxes. The setting for the longest side length is to reduce
1092
               additional memory usage, and this value can be set arbitrarily.
1093         • For PaddleOCRv2, we can input images of any resolution and let the model process them
1094           autonomously.

1100

1210   D.1   H OW LL AVA-1.5 R EPRESENTS OTHER MLLM S ?
1211
1212   On the main page of our paper, we exclusively select LLaVA-1.5 for experimentation, considering
1213   it representative of most advanced models. In this subsection, we will explain this choice from the
1214   following two aspects:
1215
1216   (1) The representativeness of LLaVA-1.5. We choose LLaVA-1.5 as we are in a highly dynamic
1217   field and LLaVA-1.5 is representative enough of most SOTA MLLMs. The advanced MLLMs typ-
1218   ically consist of three main modules: an image encoder, an input projector, and a LLM backbone.
1219   LLaVA-1.5 adheres to this structure.
1220
       The process begins by encoding images into image features with an image encoder and aligning
1221
       them with text features using an input projector. Most advanced MLLMs include a dedicated branch
1222   like this for processing images into analogous image token sequences. These image tokens are then
1223   combined with text tokens representing input sentences and inputted into the LLM.
1224
1225
       Following this structure, the tokens derived from textual detection information can be directly com-
       bined with image tokens and used during MLLM’s training and inference. In other words, as long
1226
       as the MLLM conforms to this structure, the additional textual detection information can be pro-
1227
       cessed similarly before being inputted into the LLM and serves a similar function during training
1228   and inference. Therefore, the results of experiments conducted on LLaVA-1.5 can be applied to
1229   other MLLMs with similar structures.
1230
1231
       Furthermore, LLaVA-1.5 has proven to be highly successful, spawning numerous outstanding
       works. We conduct our study based on LLaVA-1.5, enabling the application of our experimental
1232
       findings to the subsequent works of LLaVA-1.5.
1233
1234
1235   (2) The empirical support on Qwen-VL. To better illustrate the versatility of our work, we also
1236   conduct experiments on another MLLM, Qwen-VL. Qwen-VL uses a cross-attention layer to com-
1237   press visual features into a fixed-length sequence of 256, which differs from LLaVA-1.5’s MLP. And
1238
       the datasets for training are also different.
1239   Specifically, since the instruction-following dataset of Qwen-VL-Chat is not open-sourced, we con-
1240   duct visual instruction tuning on Qwen-VL (which has not undergone visual instruction tuning) with
1241   the instruction-following dataset of LLaVA-1.5. We compare three models: Qwen-VL-IT, Qwen-
       VL-IT-TFI, and Qwen-VL-IT-FTBI:

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

1464   D.7   F URTHER D ISCUSSION ON R ELATED W ORKS
1465
1466   (1) Why we conduct comparative experiments around adaptive training based on “deploying
1467   detection models to assist MLLMs”? Deploying independent detection models (or models for
1468   other downstream tasks) to generate auxiliary text for MLLMs is both straightforward and effective.
1469
       By simply incorporating external text descriptions into the MLLMs, it significantly improves their
       performance. Moreover, the deployed models are interchangeable, allowing for convenient updates
1470
       and the replacement with higher-performing models, thereby enhancing the overall performance of
1471
       the framework. Given its numerous advantages, an increasing number of researchers are investigat-
1472   ing this paradigm and working based on it.
1473
1474   Nevertheless, many researchers tend to adopt training-free strategies. The impact of adaptive train-
1475
       ing, however, remains an important area of investigation. Therefore, we conduct systematic exper-
       iments based on the training-free and adaptive training strategies in this paradigm, as there has not
1476
       been a comprehensive comparison between them.
1477
1478
1479
1480   (2) Distinctions from approaches involving the introduction of special tokens. In the academic
1481   community, there is a paradigm also focusing on detection information, which involves introducing
1482   special tokens to explicitly infuse detection information in both input and output, guiding MLLMs to
1483   leverage this information. Typical methods include MiniGPT4-v2 (Chen et al., 2023b), VisionLLM
1484   (Wang et al., 2024b), and Shikra (Chen et al., 2023c).
1485   Nevertheless, this paradigm differs significantly from the paradigm we focus on.
1486
1487         • First, the method of deploying detection models allows MLLMs to receive real-time de-
1488           tection information during both training and inference. This type of detection information
1489           encompasses the locations of all detectable objects in the image, containing rich details
1490           about the image. In contrast, the special token method, which does not deploy detection
1491           models, requires manual input of detection information at the input stage. Such detection
1492           information is typically limited to a single object or a small number of objects, serving
1493           primarily as task guidance. Thus, the role of detection information differs between these
1494
               approaches: in the former, it assists MLLMs for downstream tasks by providing useful de-
               tection details, while in the latter, it usually serves only as a signal, indicating that the task
1495
               involves detecting specific targets.
1496
1497         • Furthermore, the detection information introduced by MiniGPT4-v2 and VisionLLM is
1498           completely accurate, as it is derived from datasets. In contrast, deployed detection models
1499           may occasionally produce errors, introducing noise that affects the training-free model.
1500           This noise, however, also trains the MLLMs’ ability to denoise.
1501
1502
       Therefore, the focus of our paper is fundamentally different from them. The training strategies for
       deploying detection models to assist MLLMs have not been as extensively explored as methods
1503
       involving the special tokens. Our systematic study on this topic represents a new departure.
1504
1505
1506
1507   (3) Our study is a pioneering work, offering inspiration for further research. Our research
1508   investigates whether adaptive training can help MLLMs better identify noise in real-time detection
1509   information and more effectively leverage the outputs of additional detection models to enhance
1510   VQA performance. To the best of our knowledge, no previous work has systematically explored the
1511   impact of adaptive training on deploying detection models to assist MLLMs. To draw inspiration
       from it, we conduct a series of systematic experiments in this direction.


                                                         28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
       Our findings demonstrate that the adaptive training strategy indeed outperforms the training-free
1513   strategy. Additionally, we confirm that fine-tuning with only a small amount of high-quality VQA
1514   data can also lead to improved performance, and the performance gain is still preserved even after
1515   replacing the detection models. As a pioneering study in this area, we have uncovered many valuable
1516   insights, and we hope our findings can provide insights for researchers in the relevant field.

       E.1   M ODIFICATION ON THE GQA B ENCHMARK
1521
1522   In the original GQA benchmark, a response is considered correct only when it precisely matches
1523   the reference answer. However, due to the presence of numerous synonyms in the noun vocabulary,
1524   as well as variations in noun plurality, such evaluation criteria result in the omission of many correct
1525   responses. For example, if our model provides the response “ramp” instead of the expected answer
1526   “pavement”, or answers the question “what is the airplane flying above?” with “beach” instead of
1527   the expected answer “ocean”, it could lead to “inaccuracies”. Nonetheless, the model does not make
1528   mistakes.
1529   Thus, we make modifications to the GQA benchmark. We select only a subset of the evaluation
1530   dataset, including samples that only require yes or no answers, as well as those involving choices
1531   (questions containing “or”). For these samples, the answer can be chosen from a limited set of op-
1532   tions, eliminating the possibility of models providing correct but non-matching answers, which leads
1533   to more accurate evaluation outcomes. After filtering, the remaining number of samples is 5,677,
1534   approximately half of the original evaluation dataset. We name the modified evaluation benchmark
1535
       as GQA∗.

1651   In VALSE, a valid instance consists of an image, a caption, and a modified caption called a ‘foil’ that
1652   exemplifies a specific linguistic phenomenon. The tested model is required to distinguish between
1653   real captions and foils. VALSE employs four metrics to evaluate the model’s performance: overall
1654   accuracy (acc) on all classes (foil and correct); precision (pc ) measuring how well models identify
1655   the correct examples; foil precision(pf ) measuring how well foiled cases are identified; and pairwise
1656   ranking accuracy (accr ), which measures whether the image-sentence alignment score is greater for
1657   a correct image-text pair than for its foiled pair. accr is more permissive than acc as it consider the
1658
       model prediction correct if the score for a foil is lower than the score for a caption.
1659   Due to the inability of LLaVA-1.5 and our models to directly output “cross relationship score” as
1660   the image-sentence alignment score like models such as LXMERT, we modify the computation of
1661   accr , acc, pc and pf following the approach outlined in
1662   “lxmert valse eval.py” (https://github.com/Heidelberg-NLP/VALSE/
1663   blob/main/lxmert valse eval.py) as follows:
1664   (1) Let the model answer the following two questions and tally the number of ’yes’ and ’no’ re-
1665   sponses for each question.:
1666
1667             • Q1: “Does this image match the sentence ‘caption’? Use only ‘yes’ or ‘no’ to answer.”
1668             • Q2: “Does this image match the sentence ‘foil’? Use only ‘yes’ or ‘no’ to answer.”
1669
1670
       (2) When the answer to Question 1 is “yes”, increment the counters for foil accuracy and capt fits.
       When the answer to Question 2 is “no”, increment the counters for foil detected and foil accuracy.
