009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Gisting (Mu et al., 2023) is a simple method for training models to compress infor-
012            mation into fewer token representations using a modified attention mask, and can
013            serve as an economical approach to training Transformer-based hypernetworks.
014            We introduce HyperLlama, a set of Gisting-based hypernetworks built on Llama-2
015            models that generates task-specific soft prefixes based on few-shot inputs. In ex-
016
               periments across P3, Super-NaturalInstructions and Symbol Tuning datasets, we
               show that HyperLlama models can effectively compress information from few-
017
               shot examples into soft prefixes. However, they still underperform multi-task
018
               fine-tuned language models with full attention over few-shot in-context exam-
019            ples. We also show that HyperLlama-generated soft prefixes can serve as better
020            initializations for further prefix tuning. Overall, Gisting-based hypernetworks are
021            economical and easy to implement, but have mixed empirical performance.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   With the increasing capability and popularity of large language models (LLMs), much recent work
027
      on LLMs has focused on improving the efficiency of training and serving models, such as via
      more efficient attention implementations (Dao et al. 2022, Dao 2023), lower precision operations
028
      (Dettmers et al., 2023; Frantar et al., 2023) and speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023). De-
029
      spite great advances in parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods and implementation im-
030   provements, fine-tuning a large language model to adapt it to a given downstream application still
031   remains a computationally expensive task. In particular, parameter-efficient fine-tuning still gener-
032   ally requires backpropagating gradients through most of the model, despite only a small fraction of
033   parameters requiring updates.
034
      Given the strong in-context learning of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020), some recent work has explored
035
      using trained language models as optimizers–explicitly using their in-context learning capability to
036   learn patterns or encode knowledge into hidden states or parameters (von Oswald et al., 2023a;b).
037   Building on similar ideas, Phang et al. (2023) introduced HyperTuning: using specially trained large
038   language as hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2017) to generate parameters from in-context inputs, without
039   the need for backpropagation or gradient-descent-based training.
040
      In this work, we investigate Gisting (Mu et al., 2023) as an approach for efficiently training mod-
041
      els to compress few-shot examples into Gist token representations. Through large-scale multi-task
042   training, Gisting models can serve as hypernetworks that generate task-specific soft-prefixes. Using
043   LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023) models as the foundation, we train HyperLlama, a set of Gisting-
044   based hypernetworks that generates soft prefixes based on few-shot examples. In experiments across
045   P3 (Sanh et al., 2022), Super-NaturalInstructions (Wang et al., 2022) and Symbol Tuning datasets
046   (Wei et al., 2023), we show that while HyperLlama models can effectively compress information
047   from few-shot examples into a downstream model, their performance still generally pales in com-
048   parison to models with full attention over examples and trained for few-shot learning. We also
049   show that the performance of Gisting-based hypernetworks can be boosted by performing an addi-
050
      tion phase of pretraining to train the underlying model to perform Gisting, and jointly adapting the
      downstream model during the hypernetwork training phase. Consistent with prior work, we find
051
      that HyperLlama-generated soft prefixes can serve as better initializations for further prefix tuning,
052
      achieving better scores on held-out tasks.
053
      Our contributions are as follows:

065   2   R ELATED W ORK
066
067   Hypernetworks and LLMs Hypernetworks were initially introduced by Ha et al. (2017) in the
068   context of RNNs for sequence tasks. Recent work on hypernetworks have focused on using large
069   foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2022) to generate a subset of parameters of a larger frozen
070   model; this both significantly reduces the output space of the hypernetwork, and allows the resulting
071   parameters to take advantage of an already capable pretrained model. Karimi Mahabadi et al. (2021)
072
      and He et al. (2022) trained hypernetworks as part of a larger model to perform efficiently cross-trak
      transfer learning. Phang et al. (2023) introduced HyperTuning, using a T5-based hypernetwork to
073
      generate soft prefixes and LoRA parameters from few-shot examples for a frozen downstream T5
074
      model. Ivison et al. (2023) concurrently explored a T5-based hypernetwork with a similar setup, ex-
075   cept also allowing the downstream model to access the hypernetwork encoder representations. Deb
076   et al. (2022) similarly explored training BART models to generate parameters from task instructions.
077   Outside of language models, Ruiz et al. (2023) trained a hypernetwork to generate a reduced version
078   of LoRA parameters for personalizing generation of images of faces.
079
080   Meta Learning and LLMs as Optimizers Large language models have been shown to be able
081   to learn at inference time from in-context examples, through regular language modeling pretraining
082   (Brown et al., 2020) or being explicitly trained to do in-context learning (Min et al., 2022; Shi et al.,
083   2023). von Oswald et al. (2023a;b) showed that the attention mechanism can mimic gradient descent
084   while processing in-context tokens, providing another approach to using Transformers to generate
085
      parameters or parameter updates.
086
087   Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT) and Gisting To reduce memory and computation re-
088
      quirements for fine-tuning, many parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods have been proposed in
      recent years. We refer the reader to Lialin et al. (2023) for a comprehensive overview of PEFT
089
      methods. QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023), a method for fine-tuning LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) param-
090
      eters against 4-bit quantized language models, is heavily used in this work to reduce the memory
091   footprint of training runs. Gisting (Mu et al., 2023) involves training a decoder-only Transformer to
092   compress information from earlier tokens into Gist token representations via appropriate modifica-
093   tion of the attention mask. These Gist token representations are exactly equivalent to soft prefixes
094   (Li & Liang, 2021), as discussed in Section 3.1.

      3   H YPERT UNING WITH L ANGUAGE M ODELS

098
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning involves modifying a small number of parameters in a larger pre-
099
      trained model through standard gradient descent-based training. The goal of hypertuning is to use a
100
      large language model to generate the corresponding modified parameters (e.g. soft prefixes, LoRA
101   weights) in a single forward pass through a hypernetwork; those parameters can then be inserted in
102   the frozen downstream model in the same configuration as in parameter efficient fine-tuning. The
103   hypernetwork typically takes either few-shot examples and/or a task instruction as the input, and is
104   generally initialized from the same parameters as the downstream model.
105
      We briefly recap here the framework for hypertuning from Phang et al. (2023).

      3.1    H YPERT UNING WITH G ISTING
137   In Phang et al. (2023), the authors introduced HyperT5, a set of T5-based hypernetworks that gen-
138   erated soft prefixes and LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) parameters based on few-shot examples. One of the
139   major challenges identified in that work was the need to train parameter-generation heads, which
140   were themselves large neural network modules which required significant training, necessitating an
141   expensive hyperpretraining phase to initialize those modules before multi-task fine-tuning could be
142   performed.
143   As additional background: Prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) is a popular parameter-efficient fine-
144   tuning method that involves directly fine-tuning a set of soft prefixes—key and value hidden states
145   corresponding to a set of prefix tokens—to adapt a frozen language model to a downstream task.
146   Phang et al. also found that HyperT5 performed better when trained to generate soft prefixes com-
147   pared to generating LoRA parameters.
148
      Recently, Mu et al. (2023) introduced Gisting, a simple method for fine-tuning a decoder Trans-
149   former to generate representations for Gist tokens that condense information from prior tokens.
150   Referring to Figure 1a, by modifying the causal attention mask such that Gist tokens can attend to
151   prefix tokens and suffix tokens can only attend to Gist tokens, a Gisting model can be trained to
152   compress information relevant to language modeling on the suffix from the prefix within the Gist
153   token representations. We emphasize that Gist token representation can be reduced to just their key
154   and value hidden states, since these are the only hidden states that subsequent tokens interact with.
155   Correspondingly, prefix tuning involves tuning soft prefixes, the hidden representations of key and
156   value hidden states prepended to input text tokens. Hence, a Gisting model can be seen as a model
157
      that is trained to generate soft prefixes, with the gist representations being equivalent to soft prefixes
      in practice.3

187   3.2   H YPER L LAMA : A G ISTING - BASED H YPERNETWORK
188
189   We now introduce HyperLlama, a Gisting-based hypernetwork built on Llama-2 (Touvron et al.,
190   2023). We illustrate the architecture of HyperLlama in Figure 1b. HyperLlama consists of two mod-
191   els, a hypernetwork that takes as input few-shot examples and compresses the information into Gist
192   representations, and a downstream model that uses the Gist representations prepended to the input.
193   The Gist representations thus effectively serve as soft prefixes. We use Llama-2-7B as the foundation
194   for both the hypernetwork and downstream model, and use 16 Gist tokens for all experiments.
195   In practice, loading two large language models into memory is prohibitively expensive, especially
196   during training. To reduce the computational cost of training the hypernetwork (which requires
197   backpropagating gradients through both models), we apply QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023), quan-
198   tizing both the hypernetwork and downstream model to 4-bit precision and using LoRA to modify
199   the hypernetwork. During the forward pass through the hypernetwork, we activate the LoRA pa-
200   rameters for hypernetwork, and extract the Gist outputs. Then, we deactivate the LoRA parameters,
201
      restoring the untuned Llama-2 model, and feed both the Gist representations and the example inputs
      into the model. Aside from the LoRA parameters, we also introduce a set of additional embeddings
202
      for the Gist tokens. The LoRA parameters and the Gist embeddings are the only trained parameters
203
      of the hypernetwork.
204
205   We highlight that this approach differs from Mu et al. (2023), where they fine-tune all the model
206   weights and use a single model that processes the pre-Gist tokens, the Gist tokens, and the post-Gist
207
      tokens.

214   Gist tokens. The setup can be seen in Figure 2, and is based on the original hyperpretraining setup.

      A and D are concatenated and serve as inputs to be compressed into the Gist representations <G>.

341   Symbol tuning (Wei et al., 2023) is a variant of instruction tuning (Sanh et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
342   2022), where the natural language labels are swapped for random strings (random words, or strings
343   of letters or numbers). For instance, all instances of ‘Apple’ may be substituted for ‘True’ and
344   ‘7832’ substituted for ‘False’. Wei et al. showed that symbol tuning LLMs improves their in-
345
      context learning behavior and allows them to generalize better to tasks without instructions or natural
      language labels. The symbol tuning experimental setup is of interesting to us because it bypasses
346
      an issue with evaluating on the P3 and S-NI datasets above, where the task inputs cannot easily be
347
      separated from the instructions. In contrast, the symbol tuning training and evaluation sets include a
348   variant where task instructions are omitted and the labels are swapped for irrelevant strings, allowing
349   us to purely isolate the ability for either a hypernetwork or in-context learning to acquire knowledge
350   of a task.
351
      To evaluate the models on symbol tuned data, we use the same set of eleven held-out tasks with
352
      swapped labels and no instructions as in the original work. Because labels are randomly sampled
353
      even at evaluation time, we average over 10 random seeds for evaluating each model. All evaluation
354   is computed with accuracy, with predictions based on the relative probabities of output strings as
355   with the P3 experiments.
356
357
      We make two modifications compared to the original setup. First, whereas Wei et al. performed
      symbol tuning on instruction-tuned Flan-PaLM models, we perform symbol tuning on Llama-2 or
358
      HyperLlama models that have not been trained on instruction-formatted task data. Second, the orig-
359
      inal evaluation setup used a different distribution of random labels at evaluation time compared to
360   training time to evaluate the generalization capability of symbol tuning. We found that this mismatch
361   of distributions can greatly impact the performance of symbol-tuned models. We make a mild ad-
362   justment to the distribution of random labels in the evaluation distribution to make them more in
363   line with the training distribution, in a manner that does not detract from the ability for models to
364   generalize to unrelated label strings at inference time. More details on evaluation can be found in
365   Appendix D.
366   For the HyperLlama models, we follow the setup in the above sections, performing few-shot exam-
367   ples of the symbol-tuning training data to the hypernetwork and one input-output pair to the down-
368   stream model. We likewise include results with the HyperLlama variants with hyperpretraining and
369   downstream adaptation. We compare the performance of HyperLlama models to a 0-shot Llama-2
370   model, a few-shot prompted Llama-2 model, and a symbol-tuned Llama-2 model. We expect the
371   0-shot model to perform effectively at chance, as the output label string have no correspondence
372   with actual output classes. The symbol-tuned Llama-2 model should serve as an upper-bound of

      HyperLlama model is able to incorporate some information from the few-shot examples provided

433   shows that the process of compressing and transferring the information from the few-shot examples
434   is generally still far from perfect.
435
436
      From qualitative analysis of HyperLlama model mistakes, we found that the model particularly
      struggles on cases where the downstream model would benefit from being able to directly refer to or
437
      copy from the few-shot examples–for instance, tasks where there is a very specific output format that
438
      is only discoverable through few-shot examples. Such cases where there is a high sensitivity to exact
439   strings in the few-shot examples appear to be where the HyperLlama models perform worse, whereas
440   the Llama-2 models can directly attend to the few-shot examples. We find additional evidence for
441   this in our symbol tuning experiments, where we find that HyperLlama underperforms even a Llama-
442   2 without symbol tuning. In this case, even the few-shot learning capability inherent in the base
443   Llama-2 trumps HyperLlama, which struggles with compressing unintuitive input-output pairings.
444
      More generally, hypertuning also possesses several other weaknesses, such as only being able to take
445   as input as many examples as can fit into a language model’s limited context, needing to generate
446   the parameters in a single, forward pass with fixed amounts of computation and potentially high
447   sensitivity to the selection of few-shot input examples.
448
      However, HyperLlama possesses other advantages too. Compressing relevant task-specific informa-
449
      tion into a soft prefix is significantly more economical at inference time than needing to recompute
450
      or store the hidden states of the whole set of few-shot examples. A soft prefix generally consists of
451   such a small number of tokens worth of hidden states that the additional computation to attend to
452   them is negligible relative to the rest of the input, and the storage is also more convenient given the
453   small size. Moreover, we have shown that the HyperLlama-generated soft prefixes can be further
454   fine-tuned to achieve better performance, providing another avenue of computation savings. Lastly,
455   Gisting-based hypernetworks models can be efficiently trained and served since they only require
456   a modification to the attention mask and LoRA weights (for swapping between hypernetwork and
457   downstream model phases), which are common components in modern LLM infrastructure.
458   Overall, while we have found that Gisting-based hypernetworks such as HyperLlama face certain
459   limitations and underperform fine-tuned, full-context language models, we see them as a promising
460   and easy starting point for further investigations on LLM-based hypernetworks.

      B     H YPER L LAMA I MPLEMENTATION C OMPLEXITIES
618
619
      One complexity that arises with using Gist tokens is dealing with position encodings. Transformer-
620
      based language models such as Llama-2 tend to be highly sensitive to position encodings, and pro-
621
      viding an input with an incorrect encoded position can lead to very poor results. Specifically, the
622   issue is that for a given input to the downstream model, to prepend to Gist tokens as a soft prefix, we
623   would need the Gist tokens to be encoded to a position before the input. However, the Gist tokens
624   appear at the end of the Gisted input, meaning it is in a position very far from zero.
625
      Mu et al. (2023) addressed this in their implementation of Gisting by always jointly processing
626
      the whole sequence of the Gist input, the Gist tokens and the post-Gist input. An alternative and
627
      more flexible solution is to modify the implementation of Llama-2 to accept a ‘position offset’,
628   which pushes encoded positions of the post-Gist input down. While RoPE (?) is a relative position
629   encoding, implementation-wise it be treated as a position embedding scheme when precomputing
630   the rotary matrix, so this change is easy to implement. Hence, the output of the hypernetwork
631   actually consists of two elements: first the Gists (KV hidden states corresponding to the Gist tokens),
632   and secondly the offset corresponding to the position of the final Gist token, which is also how many
633   position the downstream input token needs to be ‘pushed down’.
634   This has also an additional benefit in allowing one to average over Gists of different sets of few-
635   shot examples, which otherwise would not be possible if the Gist tokens were encoded to different
636   positions. This can be implemented be providing an initial offset to the hypernetwork itself, with
637   the offsets computed such that the resulting Gist tokens have the same position ending.
638
      Lastly this implementation tweak is also helpful for Prefix Tuning, which similarly requires pushing
639
      the input tokens down by the number of prefix tokens.

      E    A DDITIONAL N OTES ON P REFIX T UNING
676
677
      As discussed in Li & Liang (2021), directly fine-tuning the soft prefixes in a language model gener-
678
      ally leads to very poor performance, or is highly sensitive to initializations and training hyperparam-
679   eters. To address this, the authors introduce a reparameterization trick, where instead of fine-tuning
680   the soft prefixes directly, the authors introduce an MLP that take a static input and outputs the soft
681   prefixes. For reasons not yet well explored, this significantly improved the performance and stability
682   of prefix tuning.
683
      In the context of this work, HyperLlama generates soft prefixes from the attention mechanism of
684
      the model. We then directly fine-tune the generated soft prefixes, with generally good results. To
685   fairly compare this to other forms of prefix tuning, we would need to find other baselines that also
686   fine-tune a soft prefix directly. In the shared baseline, we perform prefix tuning over the P3 dataset
687   with the reparameterization, then run the MLP and extract the resulting soft prefix–this soft prefix is
688   then an appropriate point of comparison to the HyperLlama-generated soft prefixes.
689
      On the flip side, we might consider using prefix tuning with reparameterization as a point of com-
690
      parison. This would be an unfair comparison, as it involves significantly more parameters than just
691   the soft prefixes. If we proceed with this knowledge, we have two experiments we can run. We show
692   the results in Figure 4 and in Table 6 We find that standard prefix tuning with reparameterization
693   outperforms directly fine-tuning the sof tprefix without reparameterization with any initialization
694   scheme, including share initialization and HyperLlama-generated initializations. However, we em-
695   phasize that directly fine-tuning the soft prefix requires much fewer parameters, and is thus much
696   less expensive than with the reparameterization.
