009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have shown great promise in tasks like node
012
               and graph classification, but they often struggle to generalize, particularly to
013
               unseen or out-of-distribution (OOD) data. These challenges are exacerbated when
014            training data is limited in size or diversity. To address these issues, we introduce a
015            theoretical framework using Rademacher complexity to compute a regret bound on
016            the generalization error and then characterize the effect of data augmentation. This
017            framework informs the design of GMM-GDA, an efficient graph data augmentation
018            (GDA) algorithm leveraging the capability of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs)
019            to approximate any distribution. Our approach not only outperforms existing
020            augmentation techniques in terms of generalization but also offers improved time
021            complexity, making it highly suitable for real-world applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027   Graphs are a fundamental and ubiquitous structure for modeling complex relationships and interac-
028
      tions. In biology, graphs are employed to represent complex networks of protein interactions and in
      drug discovery by modeling molecular relationships. Similarly, in social networks, graphs capture
029
      relationships and community interactions, offering insights into social structures and interactions
030
      (Zeng et al., 2022; Gaudelet et al., 2021; Newman et al., 2002). To address the unique challenges
031   posed by graph-structured data, GNNs have been developed as a specialized class of neural networks
032   designed to operate directly on graphs. Unlike traditional neural networks that are optimized for grid-
033   like data, such as images or sequences, GNNs are engineered to process and learn from the relational
034   information embedded in graph structures. GNNs have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance
035   across a range of graph representation learning tasks such as node and graph classification, proving
036   their effectiveness in various real-world applications (Vignac et al., 2022; Corso et al., 2022; Duval
037   et al., 2023; Castro-Correa et al., 2024; Chi et al., 2022).
038   Despite their impressive capabilities, GNNs face significant challenges related to generalization,
039   particularly when handling unseen or out-of-distribution (OOD) data (Guo et al., 2024; Li et al.,
040   2022). OOD graphs are those that differ significantly from the training data in terms of graph
041   structure, node features, or edge types, making it difficult for GNNs to adapt and perform well on
042   such data. This challenge is also faced when GNNs are trained on small datasets, where the limited
043   data diversity hampers the model’s ability to generalize effectively. To address these challenges, the
044   community has explored various strategies to improve the robustness and generalization ability of
045
      GNNs (Abbahaddou et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2022). One promising approach is data augmentation,
      which involves artificially expanding the training dataset by introducing variations of the original
046
      graph data. Data augmentation has shown its benefits across different types of data structures such as
047
      images (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and time series (Aboussalah et al., 2023). For graph data structures,
048   generating augmented versions of the original graphs, such as by adding or removing nodes and edges
049   or perturbing node features (Rong et al., 2019; You et al., 2020), allows for the creation of a more
050   varied training set. Inspired by the success of the Mixup technique in computer vision (Rebuffi et al.,
051   2021; Dabouei et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2021), additional methods such as G-Mixup and GEOMIX
052   have been developed to adapt the Mixup technique for graph data (Ling et al., 2023; Han et al.,
053   2022). These techniques combine different graphs to create new, synthetic training examples, further
      enriching the dataset and enhancing the GNN’s ability to generalize to new unseen graph structures.

      In this work, we introduce a novel graph augmentation technique based on Gaussian Mixture Models
055   (GMMs), which operates at the level of the final hidden representations. Specifically, guided by our
056   theoretical results, we apply the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to train a GMM on the
057   graph representations. We then use this GMM to generate new augmented graph representations
058   through sampling, enhancing the diversity of the training data.
059
      Contributions. The contributions of our work are as follows:
060
061          • Theoretical framework for generalization in GNNs: We introduce a theoretical framework
062            that rigorously analyzes how graph data augmentation impacts the generalization capabilities
063            of GNNs. This framework offers new insights into the underlying mechanisms that drive
064            performance improvements through augmentation.
065          • Efficient graph data augmentation via GMMs: We propose GMM-GDA, a fast and
066            efficient graph data augmentation technique, leveraging GMMs. This approach enhances
067            the diversity of training data while maintaining computational simplicity, making it scalable
068            for large graph datasets.
069          • Comprehensive theoretical analysis using influence functions: We perform an in-depth
070            theoretical analysis of our augmentation strategy through the lens of influence functions, pro-
071            viding a principled understanding of the approach’s impact on generalization performance.

076

077

078   N (v) = {u : (v, u) ∈ E}. The degree of vertex v is the number of neighbors it has, which is |N (v)|.
079   A graph is commonly represented by its adjacency matrix A ∈ RL×L , where the (i, j)-th element of
080   this matrix is equal to the weight of the edge between the i-th and j-th node of the graph and a weight

082   feature vectors. We denote these node features by X ∈ RL×D where D is the dimensionality of the
083   features.

105   Data Augmentation for Graphs. Graph data augmentation has become an essential aspect of
106   enhancing the performance and robustness of GNNs. Among the classical techniques, structural
107   modifications of the graph are widely used to generate augmented training graphs. Key methods in
      this category include DropEdge, DropNode, and Subgraph sampling techniques (Rong et al., 2019;


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      You et al., 2020). For example, the DropEdge technique randomly removes a subset of edges from the
109   graph during training, improving the model’s robustness to missing or noisy connections. Similarly,
110   DropNode removes certain nodes as well as their connections, assuming that the missing part of nodes
111   will not affect the semantic meaning, i.e., the structural and relational information of the original
112   graph. Another method is Subgraph, which samples a subgraph from the original graph using random
113   walk to use as a training graph. By training on these augmented graphs, GNNs can generalize to
114   unseen graph structures more efficiently.
115   Beyond classical methods, recent advancements have explored more sophisticated augmentation
116   techniques, focusing on manipulating graph embeddings and leveraging geometric properties of
117   graphs. Following the effectiveness of the Mixup technique in computer vision (Rebuffi et al., 2021;
118   Dabouei et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2021), several works describe variations of the Mixup for graphs.
119   For example, the Manifold-Mixup model conducts a Mixup operation for graph classification in the
120   embedding space. This technique interpolates between graph-level embeddings after the READOUT
121   function, blending different graphs in the embedding space (Wang et al., 2021). G-Mixup (Han et al.,
122   2022) uses graphons to model the topological structures of each graph class and then interpolates
123
      the graphons of different classes, subsequently generating synthetic graphs by sampling from mixed
      graphons across different classes. It is important to note that G-Mixup operates under a significant
124
      assumption: graphs belonging to the same class can be produced by a single graphon. The S-Mixup
125
      method, for a given pair of graphs, determines node-level correspondences between the nodes in
126   both graphs and subsequently interpolates the graphs (Ling et al., 2023). FGW-Mixup adopts the
127   Fused Gromov–Wasserstein barycenter as the mixup graphs, but suffers from heavy computation
128   (Ma et al., 2024). Finally, the GeoMix technique (Zeng et al., 2024) uses mixup graphs on the exact
129   Gromov-Wasserstein geodesics.

      3.3   T IME C OMPLEXITY
317
318   One advantage of our approach is its efficiency, as it generates new augmented graph representations
319   with minimal computational time. Unlike baseline methods, which apply augmentation strategies to
320   each individual training graph (or pair of graphs in Mixup-based approaches) separately, our method
321   learns the distribution of graph representations across the entire training dataset simultaneously using
322   the EM algorithm (Ng, 2000). If N = |Dtrain | is the number of training graphs in the dataset, d is
323   the dimension of graph hidden representations {hG , G ∈ Dtrain }, and K is the number of Gaussian
      Components in the GMM, then the complexity to fit a GMM on T iterations is O(N · K · T · d2 ) (Yang


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      et al., 2012). We compare the data augmentation times of our approach and the baselines in Table 7.
325   Due to our different training scheme, i.e., where we first train the message passing layers and then
326   train the pooling function after learning the GMM distribution, we measured the total backpropagation
327   time and compared it with the backpropagation time of the baseline methods. The training time
328   of baseline models varies depending on the augmentation strategy used, specifically, whether it
329   involves pairs of graphs or individual graphs. Even in cases where a graph augmentation has a low
330   computational cost for some baselines, training can still be time consuming as multiple augmented
331   graphs are required to achieve satisfactory test accuracy. In contrast, GMM-GDA generates only one
332   augmented graph per training graph, demonstrating effective generalization on the test set. Overall,
333
      our data augmentation approach is highly efficient during the sampling of augmented data, with
      minimal impact on the overall training time.

405

406
       GIN and GCN, both consisting of two layers with a hidden dimension of 32. The GNN was trained
407
       on graph classification tasks for 300 epochs with a learning rate of 10−2 using the Adam optimizer
408    Kingma & Ba (2014). To model the graph representations of each class, we fit a GMM using the
409    EM algorithm, running for 100 iterations or until the average lower bound gain dropped below 10−3 .
410    The number of Gaussians used in the GMM is provided in Table 9 of Appendix F. In 5 of Appendix
411    D, we also present the performance of using the Variational Bayesian estimation (VB) instead of
412    EM algorithm Tzikas et al. (2008). After generating new graph representations from each GMM, we
413    fine-tuned the post-readout function for 100 epochs, maintaining the same learning rate of 10−2 .
414    Computation of Influence Scores. Computing and inverting the Hessian matrix of the empirical risk
415    is computationally expensive, with a complexity of O(N × p2 + p3 ), where p = |θ| is the number of
416    parameters in the GNN. To mitigate the cost of explicitly calculating the Hessian matrix, we employ
417    implicit Hessian-vector products (iHVPs), following the approach outlined in Koh & Liang (2017).

      5     C ONCLUSION
499
500   In this paper, we introduced a novel approach for graph data augmentation that enhances both the
501
      generalization and robustness of GNNs. Our method uses Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) applied
      at the output level of the Readout function, an approach motivated by theoretical findings. Using the
502
      universal approximation property of GMMs, we can sample new graph representations to effectively
503
      control the upper bound of Rademacher Complexity, ensuring improved generalization of Graph
504   Neural Networks (GNNs), as shown in Theorem 3.1. Through extensive experiments on widely
505   used datasets, we demonstrated that our approach not only exhibits strong generalization ability but
506   also maintains robustness against structural perturbations. An additional advantage of our method is
507   its efficiency in terms of time complexity. Unlike baselines that generate augmented data for each
508   individual or pair of training graphs, our approach fits the GMM to the entire training dataset at once,
509   allowing for fast graph data augmentation without incurring significant additional backpropagation
510   time.

      In this section, we present a novel adaptation of Configuration Models as a graph data augmentation
835
      technique for GNN. Configuration Models Newman (2013) enable the generation of randomized
836   graphs that maintain the original degree distribution. We can, therefore, leverage this strategy to
837   improve the generalization of GNNs. Below, we present the steps involved in our approach to using
838   Configuration Models for Graph Data Augmentation:
839
840         1. Extract Edges: For each training graph Gn , we first extract the complete set of edges En .
841         2. Stub Creation: Using a Bernoulli distribution with parameter p ∈ [0, 1], we randomly
842            select a subset of candidate edges and break them to create stubs (half-edges).
843         3. Stub Pairing: We then randomly pair these stubs to form new edges, creating a randomized
844            graph structure with the same degree distribution.

       Let us consider the graph space (G, ∥·∥G ) and the feature space (X, ∥·∥X ), where ∥·∥G and ∥·∥X
       denote the norms applied to the graph structure and features, respectively. When considering only
984
       structural changes, with fixed node features, the distance between two graphs G λ , G is defined as
985
986                                          G λ − G = ∥A − Aλ ∥G ,                                     (8)
987
988    where Aλ , A are respectively the adjacency matrix of G λ , G, and the norm ∥·∥G can be for example

       extends to:
990
                                   G λ − G = α∥A − Aλ ∥G + β∥X − X λ ∥X ,                               (9)
991
992    where X λ , X are the node feature matrices of G λ , G respectively, and α, β are hyperparameters
993    controlling the contribution of structural and feature differences.
994    In most baselines graph augmentation techniques, such as for instance G-Mixup, SubMix, and
995    DropNode, the alignment between nodes in the original graph G and the augmented graph G λ is
996    known. However, in cases where the node alignment is unknown, we must take into account node
997    permutations. The distance between the two graphs is then defined as
998
                      G λ − G = min α∥A − P Aλ P T ∥G + β∥X − P X λ ∥X ,

                                                                                                (10)

1001

1002   over the set of permutation matrices, which corresponds to the optimal matching between nodes in
1003   the two graphs. This formulation represents the general case of graph distance, which has been used
1004   in the literature (Abbahaddou et al., 2024).
