009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            For federated learning in practical settings, a significant challenge is the consider-
012            able diversity of data across clients. To tackle this data heterogeneity issue, it has
013            been recognized that federated ensemble distillation is effective. Federated en-
014            semble distillation requires an unlabeled dataset on the server, which could either
015            be an extra dataset the server already possesses or a dataset generated by training
016
               a generator through a data-free approach. Then, it proceeds by generating pseudo-
               labels for the unlabeled data based on the predictions of client models and training
017
               the server model using this pseudo-labeled dataset. Consequently, the efficacy of
018
               ensemble distillation hinges on the quality of these pseudo-labels, which, in turn,
019            poses a challenge of appropriately assigning weights to client predictions for each
020            data point, particularly in scenarios with data heterogeneity. In this work, we
021            suggest a provably near-optimal weighting method for federated ensemble distil-
022            lation, inspired by theoretical results in generative adversarial networks (GANs).
023            Our weighting method utilizes client discriminators, trained at the clients based on
024            a generator distributed from the server and their own datasets. Our comprehensive
025            experiments on various image classification tasks illustrate that our method sig-
026            nificantly improves the performance over baselines, under various scenarios with
027
               and without extra server dataset. Furthermore, we provide an extensive analy-
               sis of additional communication cost, privacy leakage, and computational burden
028
               caused by our weighting method.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) has received substantial attention in both industry
034   and academia as a promising distributed learning approach. It enables numerous clients to collab-
035   oratively train a global model without sharing their private data. A major concern in deploying FL
036   in practice is the severe data heterogeneity across clients. In the real world, it’s probable that clients
037   possess non-IID (identical and independently distributed) data distributions. It is known that the data
038   heterogeneity results in unstable convergence and performance degradation (Li et al., 2019; Wang
039
      et al., 2020b; Li & Zhan, 2021; Kairouz et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023; Karimireddy et al., 2020).
040   To address the data heterogeneity issue, various approaches have been taken, including regularizing
041   the objectives of the client models (Karimireddy et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2019; Yao
042   et al., 2021; Mendieta et al., 2022), normalizing features or weights (Dong et al., 2022; Kim et al.,
043   2023), utilizing past round models (Yao et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b), sharing feature informa-
044   tion (Dai et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024), introducing personalized layers (Huang
045
      et al., 2023), and learning the average input-output relation of client models through ensemble dis-
      tillation (Chang et al., 2019; Gong et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2023; Sattler et al., 2020; Lin et al.,
046
      2020; Cho et al., 2022; Xing et al., 2022; Park et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2022;
047
      Zhang et al., 2022; 2023b). In particular, the last approach, federated ensemble distillation, has re-
048   cently gained significant attention for its effectiveness in mitigating data heterogeneity and for its
049   advantage of being effectively applicable to heterogeneous client models. It requires an unlabeled
050   dataset at the server, for which pseudo labels are created based on client predictions. By training on
051   this pseudo-labeled dataset at the server, the server distills the knowledge from the clients. This ad-
052   ditional dataset can be either public (Chang et al., 2019; Gong et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2023; Sattler
053   et al., 2020), held only by the server due to its exceptional data collection capability (Lin et al., 2020;
      Cho et al., 2022; Xing et al., 2022; Park et al., 2024), or obtained through a data-free approach (Wang

330   By leveraging the theoretical results in Section 3.1, we propose FedGO that constitutes an optimal
331   model ensemble and performs knowledge distillation. The main technical novelty of FedGO lies
332   in implementing the optimal weighting function wk∗ using client discriminators, which is a versatile
333   technique that can be integrated to both the following scenarios with/without extra server dataset.
334
335         (S1) The server holds an extra unlabeled dataset.
336         (S2) The server holds no unlabeled dataset, thus a data-free approach is needed.
337
338   For completeness, let us describe how the FedGO algorithm can be adapted depending on the cases
339   (S1) and (S2). FedGO largely consists of two stages: pre-FL and main-FL. In the pre-FL stage,
340   the server and the clients exchange the generator and the discriminators. First, the server obtains a
341   generator through one of the following three methods, and distributes the generator to the clients.
342
343
         (G1) Train a generator with an unlabeled dataset on the server, which is possible under (S1).
344      (G2) Load an off-the-shelf generator pretrained on a sufficiently rich dataset.
345
         (G3) Train a generator through an FL approach, e.g., using FedGAN (Rasouli et al., 2020).
346
347   After receiving the generator, each client trains its own discriminator based on its dataset and sends
348   the discriminator to the server.
349
      The main-FL stage operates according to Algorithm 1, except that the server assigns weights for
350
      pseudo-labeling according to (11) using the client discriminators. For the server unlabeled dataset
351
      U used for distillation, which we call distillation dataset, we consider the following cases:
352
353      (D1) Use the same dataset held by the server, which is possible under (S1).
354
355
         (D2) Produce a distillation dataset using the generator from (G2).
356      (D3) Produce a distillation dataset using the generator from (G3).
357
358   A comprehensive analysis of additional communication cost, privacy leakage, and computational
359   burden according to the methods for obtaining the generator and distillation set is provided in Ta-
360   ble 1, which shows the trade-off among the methods. In particular, an extra dataset at the server
361
      makes the communication cost and the client-side privacy and computational burden negligible, at
      the expense of server-side privacy leakage. In the absence of server dataset, the use of an off-the-
362
      shelf generator makes all the burdens negligible, but it can be challenging to secure an off-the-shelf
363
      generator whose generation distribution is similar to the client data distribution. Lastly, the data-free
364   approach (G3)+(D3) does not require an extra server dataset or an external generator, but it increases
365   the communication burden and the privacy and computational burden on the client side.

384
385   Datasets and FL Setup We employed datasets CIFAR-10/100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) (MIT
386   license) and downsampled ImageNet100 (ImageNet100 dataset; Chrabaszcz et al., 2017). Un-
387   less specified otherwise, the entire client dataset corresponds to half of the specified client dataset
388   (half for each class), and each client dataset is sampled from the entire client dataset according to
389   Dirichlet(α), akin to setups in Lin et al. (2020); Cho et al. (2022). α is set to 0.1 and 0.05 to rep-
390
      resent data-heterogeneous scenarios. The server dataset corresponds to half of the specified server
      dataset (half for each class) without labels. If not otherwise specified, the server dataset and the
391
      client datasets partition the same dataset disjointly. We considered 20 and 100 clients (20 clients if
392
      not specified otherwise), assuming that 40% of the clients participate in each communication round.
393
394   Models and Baselines For architecture, we employed ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) with batch
395   normalization layers (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015). For baselines, we considered the vanilla Fe-
396   dAVG (McMahan et al., 2017) and FedProx Li et al. (2020) that do not perform ensemble distil-
397   lation, FedDF (Lin et al., 2020), FedGKD+ (Yao et al., 2021) and DaFKD (Wang et al., 2023a) that
398   incorporate ensemble distillation. For comparison with other weighting methods, we considered the
399   variance-based weighting method of Cho et al. (2022), the entropy-based methods of Deng et al.
400   (2023) and Park et al. (2024), and the domain-aware method of Wang et al. (2023a), described in
401   Section 2. As an upper bound of the performance, we also compared with central training that trains
402
      the server model directly using the entire client dataset. FedGO and DaFKD require image genera-
      tors and discriminators. For the generator, we considered the three approaches (G1), (G2), and (G3)
403
      in Section 3.2. For (G1) and (G3), we adopted the model architecture and training method proposed
404
      in WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al., 2017). For (G2), we employed StyleGAN-XL (Sauer et al., 2022),
405   pretrained on ImageNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). Unless specified otherwise, we assume (G1). For
406   discriminators, we utilized a 4-layer CNN. More experimental details are provided in Appendix E.2.

1071   Hyperparameter Tuning We identified the best-performing hyperparameters on CIFAR-100 with
1072   Dirichlet α = 0.05 and used the same values for other settings. During the ensemble distillation
1073   process, we trained both clients and server with the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) at a
1074   learning rate of 0.001 with batch size 64, without weight decay. The (β1 , β2 ) parameters for Adam
1075   were set to (0.9, 0.999). Additionally, we applied cosine annealing (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016) to
1076
       decay the server learning rate until the final communication round T = 100 as in Lin et al. (2020),
       except for the results of F.2 and F.4.
1077
1078   For the client and server classifier training epochs, we performed a grid search to find the optimal
1079   number of training epochs. The initial grid was {5, 10, 30, 50}, and the experiments were conducted
       with 30 client epochs and 10 server epochs (Es = 10) for CIFAR-10/100. To leverage the increased


                                                                       20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       number of steps due to the additional number of data, experiments on ImageNet100 were conducted
1081   with 10 client classifier epochs and 3 server classifier epochs (Es = 3).
1082
1083   To train the generator utilized by our FedGO from scratch, we trained the WGAN-GP model
1084
       following the training method proposed in Gulrajani et al. (2017). The generator and discrimi-
       nator of WGAN-GP were trained using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0002 and
1085
       (β1 , β2 ) = (0, 0.9). The training was conducted with a batch size of 64 until the generator com-
1086
       pleted 100,000 gradient steps. The generator was updated every 5 steps of the discriminator, and a
1087   gradient penalty coefficient λ of 10 was used.
1088
1089   When training a generator in a data-free setting, i.e., the case (G3), we applied the same hyperpa-
1090
       rameters as in the scratch training, except for the gradient steps. For gradient steps, we used the
       same number of training epochs for the local generator as those used in classifier training.
1091
1092   For      the     client     discriminator,     we    adopted      the    hyperparameters     from
1093   https://github.com/Ksuryateja/DCGAN-MNIST-pytorch/blob/master/gan mnist.py and trained
1094   it with a batch size of 64 for 30 epochs for CIFAR-10/100, and for 10 epochs for ImageNet100. The
1095   optimizer Adam was used with a learning rate of 0.0002, and (β1 , β2 ) = (0.5, 0.999).
1096   FedProx (Li et al., 2020) introduces a proximal term to the client training loss, which helps to address
1097   heterogeneity by penalizing large deviations from the server model. The proximal term is multiplied
1098   by a coefficient µ and added to the primary objective loss. We performed a grid search to tune the
1099   value of µ from {0.1, 0.05, 0.01, 0.005, 0.001, 0.0005, 0.0001}, and chose the best value µ = 0.001.
1100   FedHKT (Deng et al., 2023) and FedDS (Park et al., 2024) introduce a temperature parameter τ > 0,
1101   which allows client weights to approach uniform weighting as τ increases. By following Deng et al.
1102   (2023), we set τ = 1.
1103
       FedGKD (Yao et al., 2021) introduces an additional buffer of length M on the server, where the
1104
       server model is stored after each round. The server then creates an additional model with averaged
1105
       parameters from the models stored in the buffer and sends this model to the clients each round. Each
1106   client uses a temperature parameter τ to compute the knowledge distillation loss on the received
1107   additional model, multiplies this loss by γ/2, and adds it to the primary objective loss. Consequently,
1108   it is necessary to tune three additional hyperparameters: M , τ , and γ. We conducted a grid search
1109   with M and τ in {1, 3, 5, 10} and γ in {0.1, 0.05, 0.01, 0.005, 0.001}. The best performing
1110   parameters were M = 5, τ = 3, and γ = 0.001.
1111
       Similar to our FedGO, DaFKD (Wang et al., 2023a) utilizes discriminators to implement client
1112   weighting function. However, unlike FedGO, DaFKD trains the generator and discriminators col-
1113   laboratively. To focus on the weighting method, the domain-aware weighting method in Figure 2 is
1114   implemented by only modifying the weighting step in our FedGO algorithm.

1298   In practice, the server may have no extra dataset. In this case, we first prepare a generator and
1299   then generate a distillation dataset using the generator. The generator can either be an off-the-shelf
1300   pretrained model or trained through an FL approach (Rasouli et al., 2020; Guerraoui et al., 2020; Li
1301   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023c; Fan & Liu, 2020; Behera et al., 2022; Hardy et al., 2019; Xiong
1302   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021; 2023a), corresponding to the 3rd and 4th scenarios in Table 1 in our
1303   main paper, respectively.
1304   Figures 9 and 10 present the results for the two data-free approaches with 100 clients on CIFAR-10
1305   dataset. We employed styleGAN (Karras et al., 2019) pretrained with ImageNet dataset for the off-
1306   the-shelf generator, and applied the FedGAN algorithm (Rasouli et al., 2020) for training a generator.
1307   For both the approaches, our FedGO shows performance gains in server test accuracy, ensemble test
1308   accuracy, and ensemble test loss compared to the uniform weighting of FedDF (Lin et al., 2020)
1309   and the domain-aware weighting of DaFKD (Wang et al., 2023a). In particular, the improvement of
1310
       FedGO over FedDF is much larger than that of DaFKD over FedDF. Note that the ensemble test loss
       of DaFKD becomes larger than that of FedDF after a certain round.
1311
1312   In both data-free approaches, we have ps = pg , under which our weighting method is optimal
1313   for ∀x ∈ ps = pg from Theorem 4 in our main paper. Note that the distance between p and
1314   pg = ps for the generator trained from FedGAN is smaller than that for the off-the-shelf generator.
1315   Consequently, despite using a simpler generator trained on a smaller dataset, we observe that the
1316
       performance of FedGO using the generator trained from FedGAN is slightly better than that using
       the off-the-shelf generator.
1317
1318   Finally, we can observe performance degradation compared to the case where the distillation is
1319   performed on a real dataset. This can be attributed to the naive reuse of generated images, which
1320   has been identified as a cause of performance degradation (Yoon et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024).
1321   An interesting future work would be on improving the performance of knowledge distillation using
1322
       generated images. Still, experimental results demonstrate that ensemble distillation is beneficial in
       improving performance even with generated images. Furthermore, by employing an optimal model
1323
       ensemble, our FedGO shows improvement over FedDF and DaFKD.

1482
1483   Let us provide a detailed explanation for Table 1. If the server dataset is available from the outset
1484   (first two rows in Table 1), we only need one-shot communication of generator (from the server to
1485   the clients) and discriminators (from the clients to the server). Hence, additional communication
1486   burden and client-side privacy leakage are negligible. In particular, for our experiments, the param-
1487   eters of the ResNet-18 classifier are approximately 90MB when stored as a PyTorch state dict. In
1488
       comparison, the generator and discriminator models are 4.61MB and 2.53MB, respectively. Over
       100 communication rounds, during which ResNet-18 is transmitted repeatedly, the additional com-
1489
       munication burden introduced by FedGO is nearly negligible. However, the server dataset is used for
1490
       distillation for each communication round, incurring non-negligible privacy leakage on the server
1491   side. If there is no server dataset (last two rows in Table 1), there is no additional privacy leakage
1492   on the server side. To train a generator through FL (last row), multiple rounds of GAN exchanges
1493   between the server and clients are required, leading to non-negligible increase in communication
1494   burden, client-side privacy leakage and computational burden. If we use a pretrained generator in-
1495   stead (third row), additional communication burden, client-side privacy leakage and computational
1496   burden become negligible, but it is challenging in general to secure an off-the-shelf generator which
1497   generates data with a distribution similar to the client data distribution.
1498   In the following, we provide a quantitative analysis of additional privacy leakage of FedGO com-
1499   pared to FedAVG, and an explicit comparison of computational cost for FedGO and baselines.

1503   For privacy measure, we consider local differential privacy (LDP) Dwork et al. (2006) which is
1504   widely accepted both in academia and industry. Note that when the data is provided n times by in-
1505   dependently applying an LDP mechanism with privacy budget ϵ for each provision, the total privacy
1506   budget becomes nϵ from the parallel composition result (Dwork et al., 2014).

1508   in Section 3.2, let T ′ denote the total number of communication rounds to train a GAN in the pre-
1509   FL stage. For simplicity, we assume that every client participates in FL for each communication
1510   round. Let ϵM , ϵD , and ϵG denote the privacy budgets of LDP mechanisms applied to the classifier,
1511   discriminator, generator sent from each client at each communication round, respectively. Let ϵ̂M
       and ϵ̂G denote the privacy budgets of LDP mechanisms applied to the classifier and the generator

1600
1601   Our study does not provide specific guidance on the selection of discriminator architectures, which
1602   may affect the overall performance of the federated learning system. Additionally, although our
1603   FedGO algorithm can be extended to model heterogeneous scenarios as in FedDF, we found it
1604   challenging to define an optimal model ensemble for multiple hypothesis classes. Consequently, it
1605   appears difficult to apply the results of Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 in such cases.
1606
1607   I     B ROADER I MPACTS
1608
1609   In this work, we proposed a federated learning algorithm that demonstrates strong performance in
1610   scenarios where client data is heterogeneous. This capability makes our approach highly effective
1611   for distributed learning in many practical situations, where data across different clients can vary
1612   significantly. By efficiently handling such data diversity, our algorithm holds the potential to enhance
1613   the applicability and robustness of federated learning systems in real-world applications.
