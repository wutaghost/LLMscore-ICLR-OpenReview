                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               The Fundamental Risk Quadrangle (FRQ) is a unified framework linking risk
012            management, statistical estimation, and optimization. Distributionally robust op-
013            timization (DRO) based on ϕ-divergence minimizes the maximal expected loss,
014            where the maximum is over a ϕ-divergence ambiguity set. This paper introduces
015            the extended ϕ-divergence and the extended ϕ-divergence quadrangle, which in-
016            tegrates DRO into the FRQ framework. We derive the primal and dual representa-
017            tions of the quadrangle elements (risk, deviation, regret, error, and statistic). The
018            dual representation provides an interpretation of classification, portfolio optimiza-
019            tion, and regression as robust optimization based on the extended ϕ-divergence.
020
               The primal representation offers tractable formulations of these robust optimiza-
               tions as convex optimization. We provide illustrative examples showing that many
021
               common problems, such as least-squares regression, quantile regression, support
022
               vector machines, and CVaR optimization, fall within this framework. Addition-
023            ally, we conduct a case study to visualize the optimal solution of the inner maxi-
024            mization in robust optimization.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Distributionally robust optimization with ϕ-divergence ambiguity set (Ben-Tal et al., 2013) mini-
030   mizes the worst-case expected loss over all probability measures within an ambiguity set defined by
031
      ϕ-divergence. Ahmadi-Javid (2012) shows that the inner maximization of DRO is a coherent risk
      measure (Artzner et al., 1999), which we refer to as the ϕ-divergence risk measure in this study.
032
      This result establishes a direct connection between DRO and risk-averse optimization.
033
034   The Fundamental Risk Quadrangle (Rockafellar and Uryasev, 2013) combines regular risk mea-
035   sures with regular measures of deviation, error and regret in a framework that links optimization
036   with statistics. In particular, regression is shown to be equivalent to deviation minimization. De-
037
      fined through axioms, FRQ clarifies the relationships between objective functions used in tasks such
      as classification, portfolio optimization, and regression. Through the dual representation of the
038
      quadrangle, these tasks can be interpreted as robust optimization.
039
040   It is natural to consider integrating DRO into FRQ by completing the quadrangle for ϕ-divergence
041   risk measure. The purpose of the integration is two-fold: FRQ provides insights into DRO by con-
042   necting objective functions in various tasks and interprets the tasks as DRO, while the ϕ-divergence
043
      risk measure inspires the construction of new risk quadrangles. However, ϕ-divergence risk mea-
      sure is coherent, which excludes risk measures widely used in machine learning and finance, such as
044
      the mean-standard deviation risk measure . These excluded measures are accommodated within the
045
      FRQ framework. By extending the divergence function to the entire real line, we introduce a family
046   of regular risk measures and associated quadrangles, which encompass many important examples.
047
048   Main Contributions. (i) Extension of ϕ-divergence: We define the extended ϕ-divergence and
049   its associated risk measure, allowing for negative values in the worst-case weight. The extension
050   recovers risk measures used as objective functions in various tasks. A notable example is the mean-
051   standard deviation risk measure associated with the extended χ2 -divergence. (ii) Completion of
052   Quadrangle: For the extended ϕ-divergence risk measure, we complete the risk quadrangle and de-
053   rive primal and dual representations for risk, deviation, regret and error. The primal representation
      facilitates convex optimization formulations. The dual representation provides a robust optimization

      (RO) interpretation of the measures associated with extended ϕ-divergence, and a DRO interpre-
055   tation of the measures associated with ϕ-divergence. Furthermore, the RO objective functions are
056   upper bounds for their DRO counterparts. A well-known example is that the mean-standard devia-

      2.1   ϕ-D IVERGENCE AND ϕ-D IVERGENCE R ISK M EASURE
081
082   Definition 2.1 (Divergence Function). A convex lower semi-continuous function ϕ : R → R is
083   a divergence function if (i)ϕ(1) = 0, (ii) dom(ϕ) = R, (iii)ϕ(x) = +∞ for x < 0, (iv)1 ∈
084   int({x : ϕ(x) < +∞}), (v)0 ∈ ∂ϕ(1), where the interior is denoted by int, the subgradient is
085   denoted by ∂.
086   Definition 2.2 (ϕ-Divergence (Csiszár, 1963; Morimoto, 1963)). Consider probability measures P
087   and P0 , where P is dominated by P
                                       R 0 . For a divergence function ϕ(x), the ϕ-divergence of P from
088   P0 is defined by Dϕ (P ||P0 ) := Ω ϕ (dP/dP0 ) dP0 . Let Q be the Radon–Nikodym derivative
089   dP/dP0 . We have Dϕ (P ||P0 ) = E[ϕ(Q)].
090
      Definition 2.3 (ϕ-divergence risk measure (Ahmadi-Javid, 2012; Dommel and Pichler, 2020)).
091   Consider a divergence function ϕ(x). The ϕ-divergence risk measure is defined by Rϕ,β (X) =
092   supP ∈Pϕ,β EP [X], where Pϕ,β = {P ∈ P(Σ) : Dϕ (P ||P0 ) ≤ β}.
093
094   For any divergence function of the form ϕ(x) + k(x − 1), where k ∈ R, the resulting ϕ-divergence
095   and ϕ-divergence risk measure remain unchanged. The condition (v) in Definition 2.1 ensures that
096   the other elements in ϕ-divergence quadrangle developed in subsequent sections satisfy the defining
097   axioms. The condition (iv) ensures that Rϕ,β (X) > E[X].

099   2.2   T HE F UNDAMENTAL R ISK Q UADRANGLE F RAMEWORK
100
101   FRQ framework studies closed and convex functionals of random variables. A functional ρ : L2 →
102   R is called  convex if ρ (µX + (1 − µ)Y ) ≤ µρ(X) + (1 − µ)ρ(Y ), ∀ X, Y ∈ L2 , µ ∈ [0, 1], and
103   closed if X ∈ L2 |ρ(X) ≤ c is a closed set ∀ c < ∞. A discussion on functional space can be
104
      found in Appendix B.
105   A risk measure aggregates the overall uncertain cost in X into a number, so that the inequality
106   R(X) < C models that X is adequately smaller than C. For a bet that loses a constant amount, the
107   risk equals the constant. Since a risk measures the undesired outcome, it is more conservative than
      the expectation. An example is the Markowitz risk E[X] + λσ(X), λ > 0.

      Definition 2.4 (Regular Risk Measure). A closed convex functional R : L2 → R is a regular
109   measure of risk if it satisfies: (1) R(C) = C, ∀ C = const, (2) R(X) > E[X], ∀ X 6= const.
110
111   A deviation measure quantifies nonconstancy as the uncertainty in X by measuring deviation from
112   the expectation. Intuitively, a bet that loses a fixed amount has risk but zero uncertainty. An example
113   is the (scaled) standard deviation λσ(X).
114                                                                                            +
      Definition 2.5 (Regular Deviation Measure). A closed convex functional D : L2 → R is a regular
115   measure of deviation if it satisfies: (1) D(C) = 0, ∀ C = const, (2) D(X) > 0, ∀ X 6= const.
116
117   Regret quantifies the displeasure associated with the mixture of potential positive, zero and negative
118   outcomes of a random variable. An example is E[X] + λ||X||2 .
119   Definition 2.6 (Regular Regret Measure). A closed convex functional V : L2 → R is a regular
120   measure of regret if it satisfies: (1) V(0) = 0, (2) V(X) > E[X], ∀ X 6= const.
121
122   An error quantifies nonzeroness. In regression, it measures how wrong an estimate is compared
123
      to the true value. It is therefore nonnegative. When the estimate is precise, the error is zero. An
      example is the scaled L2 norm λ||X||2 used in least squares regression.
124
                                                                                              +
125   Definition 2.7 (Regular Error Measure). A closed convex functional E : L2 → R is a regular
126   measure of error if it satisfies the following axioms: (1) E(0) = 0,, (2) E(X) > 0, ∀ X 6= const.
127
      The measures defined above are intrinsically connected. There is a one-to-one correspondence be-
128

129
      one-dimensional minimization problems involving regret and error, respectively. When reviewing
130   the axioms, it is helpful to use the provided examples as a guide: risk E[X] + λσ(X), deviation
131   λσ(X), regret E[X] + λ||X||2 , and error λ||X||2 .

      Next, consider the extended ϕ-divergence quadrangle. By extending the divergence function, Q can
217   take negative values. Note that the envelope with Q ≥ 0 is the necessary and sufficient condition
218   for monotonicity of the convex homogeneous functional associated with such envelope (Rockafellar
219   et al., 2006; Rockafellar and Uryasev, 2013). We therefore forgo the interpretation of Q as a Radon-
220   Nikodym derivative, and the monotonicity of the associated risk measure. Instead, Q can be viewed
221   as (potentially negative) weight on samples. In this case, the minimization of 3.1 can still be inter-
222   preted as a robust optimization, where the maximum is over a set of weights. Also, the covariance
223   between random variables X and Q is cov(X, Q) = E[(X − EX)(Q − EQ)]. Since EQ = 1 by
224   3.6, cov(X, Q) = E[X(Q − 1)]. The deviation 3.3 can be written as supQ∈Q1ϕ,β cov(X, Q). Thus
225   the worst-case Q∗ tracks X as closely as possible.

655

709   B    F UNCTIONAL S PACE S ETTING
710
711   This subsection discusses the logic behind choosing L2 as a working space. The choice of Lp :=
712   Lp (Ω, Σ, P0 ), p ∈ [1, ∞) seems to be reasonable, however, one still has to be careful since if
713   R : Lp → R is a proper convex risk measure, then either R(·) is finite valued and continuous on
714
      Lp or R(X) = +∞ on a dense set of points X ∈ Lp (cf. (Shapiro et al., 2014, Proposition 6.8)).
      Therefore, for some risk measures, it may be even impossible to find an appropriate space.
715
716   For ϕ-divergence risk measures, the natural choice of a functional space can be an Orlicz space
717   paired with a divergence function satisfying ϕ(0) < +∞, limx→+∞ ϕ(x)/x = +∞, suggested
718   by Dommel and Pichler (2020) and adopted by Fröhlich and Williamson (2022b).
719   However, this particular space excludes important divergence functions such as the total variation
720   distance (TVD). The TVD fits in the framework of Shapiro (2017), which uses Lp in general and
721   switches to L∞ for certain divergence functions. Of course, the simplest way would be to work with
722   finite Ω. Then every function X : Ω → R is measurable, and the space of all such functions can be
723   identified with the Euclidean space. Such an approach was taken by Bayraksan and Love (2015).
724   In light of everything mentioned above, we follow (Rockafellar and Uryasev, 2013) and take L2
725   as our working space assuming finiteness where needed. This choice also allows us to rely on the
726   extensive theory developed for the FRQ in this setting.
