017            applicable and admits an exact linear convergence rate with factor sin2 ηk .
018            Moreover, if f is convex and L-smooth, then αk⋆ ≥ 1/L.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The gradient descent (GD) algorithm, dated back to Cauchy in 1847, is arguably the most popular
036   iterative algorithm. It is often treated as the default optimizer for neural networks Rumelhart et al.
037   (1986); Ruder (2016); Goodfellow et al. (2016). GD’s procedure is remarkably simple: repeatedly
038   subtract the current iterate with its gradient. However, such a raw version suffers from a serious issue
039   — it almost always overshoots the minimum. To guarantee convergence, damping the gradient by a
040   stepsize α is necessary. How to properly choose such a stepsize is one of the most headache issues,
041   since a large choice would overshoot and a small one leads to slow convergence. In practice, the
042   stepsize (a.k.a. learning rate) is “often the single most important hyper-parameter” Bengio (2012).
043   To our best knowledge, in the current literature, a general convergence guarantee for GD only exists
044   in the convex case, and requires at least one strong assumption, the L-smoothness. Specifically, if
045   one can access the Lipschitz constant L, then any choice from α ∈ (0, 2/L) guarantees convergence,
046   with 1/L the default choice, see e.g. (Ryu & Yin, 2022, Sec. 2.4.3). Despite such a guarantee being
047   available, it is rarely used directly in large-scale problems, due to L is either not computable or simply
048   too expensive. There does exist some work that allow estimation of L, see e.g., Anil et al. (2019);
049   Fazlyab et al. (2019); Combettes & Pesquet (2020). However, their focus is often not regarding the
050
      stepsize selection issue, appears related to the complication of the estimation scheme and that the
      estimation error in L will propagate to the GD algorithm. In this manuscript, such an issue will be
051
      avoided, since our result does not rely on L.
052
053   One critique of the above classical theory is that the stepsize is fixed throughout all iterations of
      GD. This eliminates the possibility of some large feasible stepsize choices in the middle steps and

      consequently slows down the algorithm. A better strategy should be adaptively adjusting the stepsize
055   according to the current progress. Such an idea is old, at least traced back to Almeida et al. (1999).
056   The real issue is how to adjust the stepsize adaptively? In the literature, several outstanding heuristic
057   methods have been proposed, e.g., AdaGrad Duchi et al. (2011), RMSProp Tieleman & Hinton.
058   (2012), Adam Kingma & Ba (2015). However, an adaptive stepsize theory has not been established.
059   This manuscript will fill in this blank space. In the convex case, we show the feasible stepsize selection
060   range that guarantees convergence being (0, 2αk⋆ ), with αk⋆ the optimal k-th choice. Moreover, αk⋆ is
061   lower bounded by 1/L, implying the new range enlarges the aforementioned classical one (0, 2/L).
062   Also, our optimal stepsize yields an exact linear rate with factor sin2 ηk . Let us note that if sin ηk = 0,
063   then GD will converge instantly, see an example in Section 4.1.2.
064   Remarkably, our theory also applies to a non-convex function. A notable difference is that the optimal
065   choice αk⋆ can be negative now, and the feasible range becomes either (2αk⋆ , 0) or (0, 2αk⋆ ), depending
066   on the sign of αk⋆ . The negative sign is not too surprising, since if the function is locally concave, we
067   do need an ascent direction to pass the hill, otherwise stuck at the local minimum. This aspect shares
068   a similar flavour to the so-called ‘gradient descent ascent’ method for solving min-max problems, see
069   e.g. Lin et al. (2020); Zheng et al. (2024).
070   Despite our non-convex applicability, the situation is highly challenging. Unlike the convex case
071   where αk⋆ is lower bounded, here it can take an arbitrary value. The worst case is when αk⋆ = 0,
072   implying an empty selection range. This arises when x⋆ − xk ⊥ ∇f (xk ), and a stepsize that
073   can improve the current iterate xk does not exist. On the other hand, if one can exclude such an
074   orthogonal case, then convergence to the global optimal point is guaranteed, see Theorem 2.1.
075
      While our theory is powerful, it is not instantly useful in practice, due to quantity ⟨x⋆ −xk , −∇f (xk )⟩
076   is not a priori knowledge. Experts may instantly realize that, by Taylor expansion, it is an upper
077   bound for f (xk ) − f (x⋆ ) in the convex case, and the only concern is regarding f (x⋆ ). We show that,
078
079
      (i) when f (x⋆ ) = 0, the simplest tune-free stepsize f (xk )/∥∇f (xk )∥2 is applicable. It is at least
      1/(2L) large, see Proposition 3.2. In a special case, its two-times scaled version is optimal, see
080
      Section 3.1.3.
081
082   (ii) when f (x⋆ ) not known in advance, a parameter f¯0 is introduced as an initial guess for f (x⋆ ). It
083   will be updated if some criteria violated, see details in Algorithm 1. Moreover, such a guess can be
084   easily picked, for example, let f¯0 = 0.1 · f (x0 ), where f (x0 ) is the initial objective value.
085   An outstanding benefit of our scheme is regarding the ill-conditioning issue, which is a well-known
086   bottleneck for the GD algorithm. This aspect has been nicely illustrated in (Boyd & Vandenberghe,
087   2004, Sec. 9.3.2) through an R2 example, where an exact linear rate with factor (γ − 1)2 /(γ + 1)2 is
088   given, using an exact line search stepsize. A large γ (ill-conditioning) causes such a factor close to 1,
089   implying the error has almost no change as GD iterating. Ours yields a factor of (γ − 1)2 /(2γ 2 + 2),
090   which is strictly smaller than 1/2, i.e., the error is at least halved each iteration, see more details in
091
      Section 4.1.

093   bold, lowercase bold, and not bold letters are used for matrices, vectors, and scalars, respectively.

095   1.1   LITERATURE : ADAPTIVE STEPSIZE
096
097   Here, we briefly discuss some developments of the stepsize adaption technique in the machine
098   learning field. The most popular family includes AdaGrad Duchi et al. (2011), RMSProp Tieleman &
099
      Hinton. (2012), and Adam Kingma & Ba (2015). These approaches are strongly related to each other
      and are heuristic methods that typically require tuning multiple parameters. Recently, Baydin et al.
100
      (2018) propose to adaptively update the stepsize via a so-called ‘hyper-gradient’, which computes a
101
      derivative over the stepsize parameter. The good news is that doing so adds very limited cost owing
102   to an element-wise product. The bad news is that the ‘hyper-gradient’ introduces a ‘hyper-stepsize’
103   which still needs tuning (but tends to be easier). Also, a theoretical convergence guarantee is not yet
104   available. A follow-up work by Chandra et al. (2022) addresses the tuning issue by computing an
105   additional ‘hyper-gradient’ on the original ‘hyper-stepsize’. This would introduce another ‘hyper-
106   stepsize’, and they apply the same procedure again, and so on, ad infinitum. The good news is that
107   each additionally introduced ‘hyper-gradient’ reduces the stepsize sensitivity, and eventually they can
      easily pick an initial hyper-stepsize.

109   as ‘the global learning rate’ selection. This issue is avoided in our approach, since all of our choices,
110   including the initial one, are mathematically computed.

      5    C ONCLUSION
493
      In this work, we established a general theory on the adaptive stepsize selection issue, including
494
      feasible selection range, convergence rate, and optimal choice. Specifically, in the convex case, we
495
      show an adaptive range (0, 2αk⋆ ) that guarantees convergence, which enlarges the classical fixed
496
      one (0, 2/L). Its centre αk⋆ is the optimal choice, admitting an exact linear rate with factor sin2 ηk .
497
      Our theory also applies to a non-convex function, except the situation is much more challenging.
498   The optimal stepsize can now be negative, and the feasible range set could be empty when some
499   orthogonality arises. On the other hand, if a feasible stepsize choice always exists, then convergence
500   to the global optimal point is guaranteed.
501
      Despite the great power of our theory, it involves some optimal point information. To enable its
502
      practical use, we propose an approximation strategy. Such an approximation can be exact in a special
503
      practical scenario but in general sub-optimal. It also admits an exact linear convergence rate, and we
504   numerically test its power through several examples. Outstandingly, a tune-free version works nicely
505   for the non-convex MNIST problem via neural networks.
