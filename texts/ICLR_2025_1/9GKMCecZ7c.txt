009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we investigate the use of vision pre-trained models (PTMs) for
012            developing generalist robot manipulation policies. We study whether embodied
013            policies trained with representations from vision and language PTMs are capable
014            of multi-tasking and overcoming domain gaps. Evaluating a set of off-the-shelf
015            vision PTMs, our first finding is that the commonly used global features are
016            generally inadequate for building multi-task robot manipulation policies, while
017            keeping local features significantly improves in-domain performance and out-of-
018
               domain generalizibility. Experiment results show that DINOv2, a model trained
               on conventional vision datasets, outperforms models explicitly designed for robot
019
               learning. To bridge the domain gaps, we further experiment on the effect of
020
               augmentation methods on embodied robot policies and few-shot adaptation. On
021            the later case, we propose a novel objective by introducing self-distillation to the
022            objectives of few-shot adaptation. Experiment results show that our approach is
023            compatible with multiple PTMs, improving performance on novel domains when
024            the number of demonstration available is limited.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The design of robot manipulation policies has been transformed by advancements in large pre-trained
030   models (PTMs) in natural language processing and computer vision. The success of foundation
031   models has inspired the development of generalist embodied agents. These agents are designed to
032   understand instructions in natural language, perceive their environment through vision inputs, and
033
      take actions to interact with the physical world. In robot manipulation tasks, a generalist robot policy
      aims to perform a wide range of tasks using a unified model or framework. Additionally, a generalist
034
      policy also enables more flexible and efficient deployment to various environments and tasks.
035
036   As the most successful foundation models, the use of large language models (LLMs) in building
037   generalist robot manipulation policies has been extensively studied (Zitkovich et al., 2023; Szot
038   et al., 2024). The reasoning and planning capabilities of LLMs enable them to serve as high-level
039
      policies that plan macro actions in the language domain (Marza et al., 2024). For embodied agents,
      natural language provides a concise and environment-invariant description of tasks and surroundings.
040
      Therefore, policies built from LLMs often demonstrate decent performance when executing multiple
041
      tasks and adapting to unseen tasks in a single environment. For embodied agents that utilize both
042   vision and language inputs, vision plays a critical role in defining their perception of objects and
043   environments. However, the question of whether an embodied policy can generalize to environments
044   with unseen visual attributes remains a significant challenge. Thus, the effectiveness of pre-trained
045   visual representations in generalist agents requires extensive study. In this paper, we build robot
046   manipulation policies using frozen representations from vision PTMs and explore the following
047   questions: (1) If not relying on the predictive power of LLMs, are vision PTMs effective for building
048   a multi-task robot manipulation policy? (2) With “high-quality” features from vision PTMs, can
049   policies trained with pre-trained visual representations effectively generalize to unseen environments?
050
      (3) How can we effectively and efficiently bridge the domain gaps between training environments
      and unseen environments?
051
052   Most existing works on robot learning with vision PTMs focus on improving the quality of pre-trained
053   visual representations. These PTMs are benchmarked on whether their representations are effective
      and efficient for learning a high-performance policy for a single task. In this paper, we investigate


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      whether the representations from vision PTMs can be effectively scaled up to train a multi-task robot
055   policy. On the Metaworld (Yu et al., 2019) robot manipulation tasks, we found that commonly-used
056   aggregated visual representations (or global features) are often ineffective for training a multi-task
057   policy, as essential information, such as spatial structure, is lost during feature compression. We
058   demonstrate that using the full representations (or local features) from these models significantly
059   boosts the performance of the multi-task policy. Based on this discovery, we evaluate a set of
060   off-the-shelf vision PTMs within our problem setting. Surprisingly, we found that a PTM trained on
061   conventional image datasets (DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024)) outperforms the state-of-the-art PTM
062   (VC-1 (Majumdar et al., 2023)), which was pre-trained on explicitly selected datasets related to robot
063
      learning.
064   In building embodied agents, vision PTMs are also termed as artificial visual cortex (Majumdar et al.,
065   2023). As humans, we possess a structured and, in some sense, symbolic understanding of perceived
066   objects and environments, allowing us to easily generalize skills learned from tasks in one domain
067   to similar tasks in novel domains. For example, after learning to drive a white sedan in driving
068   school, we can naturally drive a red sedan on a highway or a road in a forest. We could probably
069
      also learn to drive a black SUV with just a few minutes of practice. Similarly, upon seeing a red
      object, we can immediately grasp the concept of the same object in green. This knowledge of visual
070
      representations in the human visual cortex enables us to generalize skills effectively without requiring
071
      extensive training on diverse experiences for a specific task. Huh et al. (2024) hypothesize that
072   vision and language PTMs trained on large-scale data converge to representations that are similarly
073   distributed, suggesting they gravitate towards a statistical model of the world. Given that PTMs are
074   trained on internet-scale datasets, could their inductive biases induce a similar pattern when training
075   embodied agents with their representations? In this work, we investigate whether robot manipulation
076   policies trained with PTM representations and demonstrations from a single domain can generically
077   generalize to multiple unseen domains.
078   When deploying embodied agents, the gap between training task domains and unseen task domains
079   still presents challenges, particularly if the diversity of training domains is limited. Observing that
080   policies trained with vision PTMs usually result in some level of performance degrade on unseen
081   domains, we further investigate possible approaches to bridge these domain gaps. Existing works
082   address domain gaps in robot learning through two main directions: data augmentation (Laskin et al.,
083   2020; Yu et al., 2023) and generation (Tobin et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2024), or few-shot adaptation
084   (Marza et al., 2024). In this work, we explore both directions within our problem setup. First, we
085   assess whether conventional augmentation methods effectively reduce the generalization gap, and
086
      we observe that each vision PTM is compatible with different types of augmentation. Next, in the
      few-shot adaptation setting, we propose a novel approach by introducing self-distillation into the
087
      fine-tuning objective.
088
089   Our contributions are summarized as follows: (1) We found that the commonly-used global features
090   from vision PTMs are generally ineffective for building multi-task robot manipulation policies, while
091   policies trained with local features from the PTMs achieve significantly better performance. (2) We
092
      evaluate a set of existing vision PTMs, comparing their in-domain multi-task performance and out-of-
      domain generalization, and conclude that policies trained with local features from DINOv2 perform
093
      the best on both metrics. (3) We conduct an extensive study on the effects of conventional data
094
      augmentation methods on robot policy training with pre-trained visual representations, summarizing
095   the compatibility of augmentation methods with different vision PTMs. (4) We propose a novel
096   objective for few-shot adaptation by introducing self-distillation on features from a trained policy,
097   which improves performance when the number of novel demonstrations is limited and generally
098   outperforms conventional fine-tuning methods when evaluated on unseen domains.

101   2   R ELATED W ORKS
102
103   Generalist Robot Policy Two primary research directions have emerged for building generalist robot
104   policies. The first direction leverages the power of large language models (LLMs). Myers et al. (2024)
105   decompose complex tasks into subtasks and use GPT4o to plan the sequence of subtasks for execution.
106   Szot et al. (2024) and Zitkovich et al. (2023) adapt LLMs into vision-language robot policies by
107   mapping visual representations and actions to the embeddings of a frozen LLM. The second direction
      focuses on creating more generalist robot policies, where vision and language pre-trained models


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (PTMs) serve only as feature extractors for visual and language inputs (Brohan et al., 2023). Octo
109   Model Team et al. (2024) trains a Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) policy on 800k episodes of
110   robot manipulation tasks, establishing a foundation model for robot manipulation policies. In this
111   paper, we explore the second direction and emphasize the role of vision PTMs in training generalist
112   robot manipulation policies.
113
      Pre-trained Visual Representations for Downstream Policy Training Utilizing pre-trained models
114
      for downstream tasks is already common in computer vision and natural language processing.
115   However, due to the large domain gap between standard vision benchmarks and control tasks, such
116   strategies have only recently been explored. Parisi et al. (2022) use outputs from multiple layers of a
117   frozen pre-trained MoCo ResNet (He et al., 2020) to train a single-task policy. Shridhar et al. (2021)
118   propose a two-stream framework that employs pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to guide the
119   training of a Transporter model for affordance prediction tasks. Khandelwal et al. (2022) utilize
120   local tokens from the pre-trained CLIP model to perform navigation tasks. Later studies found that
121   favoring egocentric view data in the pre-training distribution improves downstream single-task policy
122   performance. Specifically, R3M uses temporal-contrastive learning with video-text pairs from the
123
      Ego4D dataset (Grauman et al., 2022) to enhance single-task policy training. VC-1 (Majumdar et al.,
      2023) adopts a masked image modeling objective with a diverse dataset to provide unified visual
124
      representations for downstream policy training.
125
126   Domain Generalization and Adaptation A common approach to improve domain generalization
127   is to increase the amount of training data. Yu et al. (2023) use a text-to-image diffusion-based in-
128   painting model to randomly augment objects of interest, selected by an open-vocabulary segmentation
129
      model, to enhance domain generalization. Wang et al. (2024) and Yang et al. (2024) further explore
      generative modeling as a simulator to generate infinite examples. While the ability to produce
130
      numerous objects with varying attributes is beneficial, challenges arise due to computational overhead
131
      and inconsistencies in object generation across frames.
132
133   Marza et al. (2024) propose training a multitask embedding space that controls the output of a
134   pre-trained vision backbone using lightweight adapters. These adapters, along with the embedding
135
      space, enable rapid adaptation to new tasks with only a few demonstrations. Recent work (Myers
      et al., 2024) harnesses the generalization abilities of large vision-language models (such as GPT4o)
136
      to generate hierarchical language instructions for adapting to new long-horizon tasks. However, the
137
      language model tends to generalize low-level instructions (e.g., referring to both a potato and a turnip
138   toy as “purple thing”), and the objects remain unchanged between training and testing phases.

      A generalist policy should be capable of performing multiple tasks and generalizing to unseen
248   scenarios. Therefore, we benchmark the policies using the following two metrics:

252             Ttrain .
253          2. Success rate on out-of-domain tasks measures the policy’s ability to leverage knowledge
254             and skills learned from Ttrain to complete tasks in unseen domains. The policies are evaluated
255             on tasks from {Ttest         test
                                  1 , . . . T10 }.

257   In this paper, we consider two types of unseen domains: unseen object attributes and unseen
258   environments. Figure 2 presents examples of images from the training domain and these two unseen

261   global features v global . Details of the policy training procedure can be found in Appendix 5.1. Without

366
367   In this section, we introduce a sample-efficient method for adapting a trained policy to unseen
368   domains in a few-shot setting. Given a policy π train , trained on demonstrations from Dtrain , our goal
369   is to adapt this policy to unseen domains by learning from only a few demonstrations Dft . Existing
370   methods for few-shot adaptation typically imitate actions from the demonstrations using various
371   techniques. A common approach involves fine-tuning the policy or just the action head (Octo Model
372   Team et al., 2024). Marza et al. (2024) propose searching for a task embedding that controls the
373   intermediate features of the vision PTM. In this paper, we present a novel approach by introducing
374
      feature distillation into the fine-tuning objective. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to
      incorporate self-distillation techniques in domain adaptation for robot manipulation policies.
375
376   Self-distillation methods in computer vision typically align the features of two augmented views of
377   an image (Grill et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2022). Inspired by these approaches, our method aligns
      the features of two demonstrations that exhibit similar behavior. Given the policy π train , the training

448   5.1   E XPERIMENT R ESULTS ON F EW- SHOT A DAPTATION
449
      For benchmarking and fair comparison, we maintain the same configurations across all experiments
450
      in this paper. The detailed experimental setups are provided in Table 10 of Appendix A. Detailed
451
      experiment results are included in Appendix B.
452
453   The training task domain Ttrain consists of 10 tasks from the Metaworld benchmark, using datasets
454   selected by Yu et al. (2019) and Majumdar et al. (2023). These tasks are: assembly, bin-picking,
455
      button-press-topdown, door-open, drawer-open, hammer, pick-place, push, reach, and window-open.
      The training dataset Dtrain contains 500 expert demonstrations, with 50 demonstrations per task.
456
      During evaluation, an episode ends either when the goal condition G is reached (success) or when
457
      the maximum step limit is reached (failure).
458
459   The evaluation task domains consist of 5 domains with unseen object colors and 5 domains with
460   unseen environments. Each Ttest includes the same 10 tasks as Ttrain , but with randomized initial
461
      conditions. For each T ∈ Ttest , we evaluate the policy 10 times with 10 different random initial
      conditions and report the average success rates.
462
463   In the few-shot adaptation settings, we evaluate four representative PTMs: CLIP-ViT32, R3M, VC-1,
464   and DINOv2. We experiment with varying numbers of demonstrations {1, 2, 5} per task in the
465   test task domains. We benchmark our proposed self-distillation method against two baselines: (1)
466   Baseline: the success rate of π train , and (2) Fine-tuning: the success rate of π ft , fine-tuned with only
467
      components (i) and (ii) from Equation 1.
468   Figures 5, 6, 7, and 8 compare our approach with the baseline and conventional fine-tuning across
469   the four PTMs. When evaluated on unseen environments, our approach consistently improves the
470   performance of fine-tuned policies, especially when only 1 or 2 demonstrations are available per
471   task. We observe similar, though less pronounced, improvements when evaluated on unseen tasks
472   with novel object colors. The most significant performance gains are observed in policies trained
473
      with CLIP-ViT32. In other cases, our approach maintains performance comparable to conventional
      fine-tuning.
474
475   When 5 demonstrations are available for each task, the fine-tuning dataset Dft contains 500 samples,
476   which is sufficient to capture the domain gaps comprehensively. In these instances, adding the self-
477   distillation term does not yield further performance improvements. We conclude that our proposed
478   method effectively enhances performance when the number of demonstrations from unseen domains
479
      is limited.

481   6     C ONCLUSION
482
483   In this paper we investigate effective ways of building multi-tasks policies using vision PTMs. By
484   carefully evaluating in-domain and out-of-domain generalization ability of trained policy, we find
485   simply keeping local features from the last layers of PTMs can significantly improve the policy
      performance compared to the global feature counterpart that is widely used for policy training.
