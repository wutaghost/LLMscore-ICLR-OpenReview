009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Quantization-aware training (QAT) methods, generally produce the best model
012            performance, however it comes at the cost of excessive memory usage and runtime,
013            making it impractical when applying for LLMs. Inspired by parameter-efficient
014            fine-tuning (PEFT) literature, we propose Low-Rank QAT – a lightweight and
015            memory-efficient QAT algorithm for LLMs. LR-QAT employs several compo-
016            nents to save memory without sacrificing predictive performance: (a) low-rank
017            quantization-aware reparameterization; (b) downcasting operation using fixed-point
018
               or double-packing and (c) checkpointing. Unlike most related work, our method
               (i) is inference-efficient, leading to no additional overhead compared to traditional
019
               post-training quantization (PTQ); (ii) can be seen as a general extended pretraining
020
               framework, meaning that the resulting model can still be utilized for any down-
021            stream task afterwards; (iii) is orthogonal to most of recent PTQ methods and thus
022            can be seamlessly combined with them. We apply LR-QAT to LLaMA-1/2/3 and
023            Mistral model families and validate its effectiveness on several downstream tasks.
024            Our method outperforms most of recent LLM quantization approaches and reaches
025            the same model performance as full-model QAT at the fraction of its memory usage.
026            Specifically, we can train a 7B LLM on a single consumer grade GPU with 24GB
027            of memory.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have emerged as a powerful tool for a plethora of
033
      natural language processing tasks. As these models continue to grow in size and capability, addressing
      their ever increasing computational and memory demands becomes crucial for practical deployment,
034
      especially when considering resource-constrained edge devices.
035
036   One of the most effective methods to tackle this problem is neural network quantization, which uses
037   low-bit precision for weight and activation tensors. While recent post-training quantization (PTQ)
038   methods can help with decreasing the model size and improving the computational efficiency of
039
      LLMs, they typically lead to subpar performance, especially in the case of low-bit (≤ 4) quantiza-
      tion. Quantization-aware training (QAT), conversely, yields significantly better model performance
040
      compared to PTQ. However, due to extreme model sizes of modern LLMs, using traditional QAT
041
      is very computationally expensive and requires a prohibitively high GPU memory usage, making it
042   impractical.
043
044   Inspired by parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) and low-rank adaptation (LoRA) literature, we
045
      propose Low-Rank Quantization-Aware Training (LR-QAT) – a lightweight memory-efficient and
      inference-efficient QAT algorithm for LLMs. LR-QAT reduces the memory requirements of training
046
      a 7B LLM from >98GB of GPU memory to <21GB without degrading the predictive performance
047
      compared to traditional full-model QAT, making it possible to train such models on a single consumer
048   grade GPU. Unlike most related work that combines low-rank adaptation with quantization, our
049   method is also inference-efficient. After the training is complete, the auxiliary matrices are naturally
050   absorbed into the quantized weight tensor without loss of accuracy and no extra overhead at inference
051   time. LR-QAT is positioned as a general extended pretraining method, as opposed to being strictly a
052   fine-tuning method – the resulting model is a low-bit general pretrained LLM, that can still be utilized
053   for any task afterwards. If needed, our resulting low-bit pretrained LLM can be fine-tuned on specific
      downstream tasks or used with multiple LoRA adapters for rapid switching between various tasks.

      LR-QAT introduces and combines several innovations designed to reduce memory use without
      sacrificing model performance: (1) a form of QAT with low-rank reparameterization, in which we
070
      place the low-rank weights in the integer domain to ensure they align with the quantization grid of
071
      the pretrained weights. This allows for seamless fusion during inference into a single low-bit integer
072   matrix. (2) A downcasting operator that represents the frozen pretrained weights as low-bit INT-b
073   (b ≤ 4) double-packed into INT8 or as fixed-point values stored in INT8. (3) Finally, we combine the
074   proposed quantization formulation with gradient checkpointing to avoid aggressive memory spikes
075   from storing some of the intermediate results in memory for the backward pass.
076
      We apply LR-QAT to LLaMA-1/2/3 and Mistral model families and demonstrate its effectiveness
077
      on several general language modeling datasets and zero-shot evaluation on some of the common
078   reasoning downstream tasks. Our method outperforms recent LLM quantization approaches and
079   reaches the same predictive performance as full-model QAT at the fraction of its memory usage.
080   Finally, our method can be applied across a wide range of quantization settings, including per-channel
081   or per-block weight quantization, activation quantization, and can be combined with most of other
082   PTQ techniques.

      2   BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
085
086
      Neural network quantization is one of the most powerful ways to reduce model footprint, data
087   transfer and compute requirements. By quantizing a model, high bit-width floating point weights and
088   activations can be represented using low-bit numbers. On top of that, by using low-bit fixed-point
089   representations, such as INT8, one can further reduce energy consumption since the fixed-point
090   operations are more efficient than their floating-point counterparts. Quantizing to 8 bits or lower,
091   however, typically introduces quantization noise in the model, resulting in a potential drop in
092   accuracy/perplexity.
093   In this section, we provide a brief overview of uniform affine quantization and a summary of recent
094   methods for LLM quantization. We will discuss some of the trade-offs of those techniques. Finally,
095   we touch upon the challenges of LLM quantization and some of the limitations of current approaches.
096
097   Uniform affine quantization We use the following definition of the quantization function:

111   Post-training quantization methods Post-training quantization (PTQ) algorithms take a pretrained
112   high precision (FP32 / FP16 / BF16) network and convert it directly into a fixed-point network without
113   the need for the original training pipeline (Banner et al., 2018; Cai et al., 2020; Choukroun et al.,
114   2019; Hubara et al., 2020; Krishnamoorthi, 2018; Li et al., 2021; Meller et al., 2019; Nagel et al.,
115   2019; 2020; Zhao et al., 2019). These methods are either data-free or only require a small calibration
116   dataset and are generally quite easy to use. Having almost no hyperparameter tuning makes them
117   usable via a single API call as a black-box method to quantize a pretrained neural network in a
118   computationally efficient manner.
119   Post-training quantization of LLMs is a challenging task due to presence of numerical outliers
120   in weights and activations (Bondarenko et al., 2021; 2024; Kovaleva et al., 2021; Dettmers et al.,
121   2022; Sun et al., 2024). Existing LLM PTQ methods can be broadly categorized into weights-only
122   quantization and weight-activation quantization.
123   Weights-only quantization focuses on converting weights to low-bit values. For instance, GPTQ (Fran-
124   tar et al., 2022) employs second-order information to iteratively round grouped weights and correct
125   the quantization error in the remaining groups. SpQR (Dettmers et al., 2023), AWQ (Lin et al., 2023)
126   and OWQ (Lee et al., 2024) emphasize the importance of so-called “salient” weights that correspond
127   to high-magnitude activations. Other recent W-only methods include (Jeon et al., 2023; Lee et al.,
128   2023b; Luo et al., 2023; Chee et al., 2024).
129   Weight-activation quantization compresses both weights and activations. SmoothQuant (Xiao et al.,
130   2023), LLM.int8() (Dettmers et al., 2022) and Outlier Suppression (Wei et al., 2022) achieve
131   W8A8 quantization by managing activation outliers. LLM.int8() uses mixed-precision decompo-
132   sition, while the other two employ channel-wise scaling. OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) modulates
133   the extreme values of weights by optimizing the clipping threshold and shifts the challenge of quanti-
134   zation from activations to weights by employing the learnable equivalent transformation. Some of
135   the other recent W&A PTQ methods are (Lee et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2023; Yuan
136   et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024).
137
138   Quantization-aware training methods Quantization-aware training (QAT) methods (Bhalgat
139
      et al., 2020; Esser et al., 2020; Gupta et al., 2015; Jacob et al., 2018; Krishnamoorthi, 2018) simulate
      quantization during training, allowing the model to find more optimal solutions compared to PTQ
140
      approaches. However, better accuracy/perplexity comes at the cost of neural network training, i.e.,
141
      longer training times, increased memory usage, need for labeled data and hyperparameter search.
142
143   The excessive training cost and memory usage of traditional QAT methods make them unsuitable
144   for quantizing modern LLMs. A few works that apply QAT to LLMs include LLM-QAT (Liu
145
      et al., 2023b) and BitDistiller (Du et al., 2024), both of which explore the application of knowledge
      distillation within QAT setting. Additionally, EdgeQAT (Shen et al., 2024) investigates the application
146
      of QAT to tiny language models (those with fewer than 100 million parameters).
147
148

      3   M OTIVATION
185
      While generally fast and simple, PTQ suffers from limited performance in low-bit scenarios. Although
186
      QAT methods still perform well in low-bit regimes, their high training costs and memory usage make
187
      them impractical for LLMs (see Figure 1, right). Existing LoRA-based methods aim to address
188   memory efficiency during fine-tuning. However, in most cases they do not consider efficient inference.
189   Techniques such as QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024), which do not explicitly fuse the low-rank adapters
190   A and B, can incur up to 30% additional inference latency compared to the base model (Bhardwaj
191   et al., 2024). This is in line with our own simulation for LLaMA-7B model as shown in Table E1.
192
      Note that, after the model is trained with QLoRA-like method, it is not straightforward to fuse the
193
      high precision adapters A and B into the low-bit pretrained weights WZ . Naively fusing them leads
194   to a high quantization error, as demonstrated in Table E2. This issue persists even if the adapters A
195   and B are quantized, as the resulting quantization grid of the product AB differs from that of W0 .
196   As mentioned above, QA-LoRA is the only work we are aware of that attempts to fuse auxiliary LoRA
197   weights back into the frozen WZ , however it achieves so by imposing constraints on quantization
198   granularity.
199
      We are inspired by LoRA-based methods to make QAT more memory- and runtime-efficient. Our
200   goal is to design a method that is inference-efficient, i.e. where the low-rank adapters can be fused
201   back into a low-bit integer matrix WZ without any loss of accuracy or perplexity. This way, we will
202   not incur any additional inference overhead compared to PTQ, full-model QAT, or any other uniform
203   affine quantization approaches. We summarize different trade-offs for the discussed techniques in
204   Table 1.

293

294   have to compute a product AB in (5), and in the naïve implementation of our method, this product
295   together with the results of some intermediate computations (e.g., after rounding and clipping) will

297   prevent this, we employ gradient checkpointing (Chen et al., 2016) on (5). In other words, we
298   recompute the quantizer function in the backward pass, leading to a slight runtime overhead but

390   5.1      T HE IMPACT OF RANK r
391
392   We investigate the effect of different values of rank r of the auxiliary matrices A and B and present
393   results in Figure 2. Increasing the rank from 1 to 32 leads to progressively slightly better performance,
394   excluding one outlier. The fact that using r > 32 doesn’t lead to further improvement in perplexity is
395   likely because of the limited number of training steps we used for this experiment (103 ), and that
396   more steps might be needed for the procedure to fully converge. Interestingly, a rank r as small as 1
397
      already performs really well. We hypothesize that this is the case because of the following. Even
      though rank(AB) = 1, by applying a low-rank approximation inside the rounding and clipping
398
      operators in (8), this can overall lead to a high-rank perturbation to the original weights Φ0 (in the
399
      integer domain). Finally, for all ranks we observe only a small standard deviation between 0.005 and
400   0.008 ppl., indicating the robustness of LR-QAT to a random initialization of B. Going forward, we
401   use r = 32 in all our experiments2 .
402
403   5.2      T HE CHOICE OF THE DOWNCASTING OPERATOR φ(·) AND A, B INITIALIZATION
404
405   We study the effect of several choices of the downcasting operators discussed in Section 4 and
406   summarize results in Table 2. We can see that by going from FP32 to BF16, and finally to an
407   8-bit fixed-point representation of Φ0 , aside from memory savings we also maintain the same
408   WikiText-2 perplexity and even slightly improve zero-shot accuracy. The latter is likely due to a
409   slight regularization effect caused by the fact that we discard some of the information in the fractional
410
      part in W0 /s0 , some of which might be noise. One step further, however, while φ = INT-b still leads
      to a good model performance in the case of 4-bit weight quantization, it completely breaks for W3.
411
412   So far, we initialized matrices A and B following the procedure proposed in LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)
413   where B is initialized to zero, and A is initialized randomly as in (He et al., 2015). We refer to
414   this initialization scheme as ‘LoRA’. We hypothesize that a poor performance of φ = INT3 can
415   be explained by the fact that we lose all the information in the fractional part of W0 /s0 and that
416
      without that information it is difficult for low-rank approximation to learn. To address this, we adapt
      and experiment with a variant of SVD-based initialization proposed in LoftQ (Li et al., 2023). We
417
      see that using LoftQ initialization with T = 1 step recovers almost all the predictive performance
418
      compared to a fixed-point representation. Increasing number of LoftQ steps, or applying it to a 4-bit
419   case, however, did not help.
420
421   Finally, when using the fixed point representation for Φ0 , we still maintain the same model perfor-
422
      mance by switching the compute data type3 from FP32 to BF16, where the latter is what is commonly
      used for LLMs. Going forward, we use φ = Qb.(8 − b) with ‘LoRA’ initialization and BF16 compute
423
      data type.

528   6    C ONCLUSIONS
529
530   In this paper we propose LR-QAT, a lightweight and memory-efficient QAT algorithm for LLMs
531
      which enables training a 7B LLM on a single consumer grade GPU with 24GB of memory. Inspired
      by PEFT methods, we introduce a low-rank reparameterization that is aware of the quantization
532
      grid. We further reduce the memory requirements by introducing a downcasting operator involving
533
      fixed-point or double-packed integers, and applying checkpointing. In almost all cases, our method
534   outperforms common PTQ approaches and reaches the same model performance as full-model QAT
535   at the fraction of its memory usage.

785
      Quantization We experiment with both weight-only and weight-activation quantization. The
      default settings are INT4 / INT3 / INT2 per-channel (denoted ‘pc’) and group-wise weight quantization
786
      with a group size of 128 (denoted ‘g128’). We use symmetric quantization, except the INT2 case,
787
      where we use asymmetric quantization (1), for a fair comparison with related work. We quantize
788   all linear layers, except the classification head. In weight-activation quantization, defaults are
789   INT4 per-channel weight and per-token activation quantization (Dettmers et al., 2022). Following
790   OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023), we quantize all inputs to matmuls with exception of the softmax
791   output and additionally quantize the KV-cache as in LLM-QAT (Liu et al., 2023b).
792
793   Libraries We implement our method in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and use training and eval-
794   uation pipelines from HuggingFace libraries Gugger et al. (2022); Lhoest et al. (2021); Wolf et al.
795   (2020). For zero-shot evaluation, we use the LM Evaluation Harness framework (Gao et al., 2021).
796   Specifically, we use lm_eval v0.4.2 and report acc_norm for tasks where it’s available (PIQA,
797   ARC-e, ARC-c, HellaSwag) and otherwise acc (BoolQ and Winogrande).
798
799   Datasets and training To optimize the learnable parameters, we use AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov
800
      and Hutter, 2017) with weight decay set to zero, (β1 , β2 ) = (0.9, 0.95) and linear learning rate warm
      up over the first 10% steps, following by a linear decay to zero by the end of training. We use a
801
      separate maximum learning rate for quantization scales and for low-rank adapters, which are tuned
802
      depending on the experiment.
803
804   We apply our methods to all linear layers in the attention blocks (both in self-attention and in the feed-
805   forward network). We only train low-rank auxiliary matrices A, B and the quantization parameters s.
806
      Specifically, we freeze embedding layers, the final classification heads and RMSNorm parameters. In
      the case of asymmetric weight quantization, a zero offset z is set during range estimation phase and
807
      kept frozen throughout training.
808
809   We train on a small subset of SlimPajama (Soboleva et al., 2023), which is a close open-source
      replica of the dataset used for pre-training LLaMA models. We select hyperparameters based on the


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      perplexity of a small subset of Wikipedia validation set4 (512 sequences). For all weight-only and
811   weight-activation quantization results, including the comparison with full-model QAT in Section 5.3,
812   we train for 104 steps. For ablation studies in Sections 5.1 and 5.2 we use shorter training of 103
813   steps. Since the full-model QAT experiment requires more than 80GB of GPU memory, we apply
814   checkpointing on the quantization function W  c to be able to run the experiment on an Nvidia A100
815   80GB GPU. We have also experimented with CPU optimizer state offloading, but that turned out
816   to be significantly slower, see details in Table F1. The rest of the hyperparameters and their search
817   spaces are listed in Table A1.
818
819   PTQ initialization We compare with vanilla round-to-nearest quantization (RTN), where we
820   explore several choices of range setting and report the best one based on Wikipedia validation
821   set perplexity, and also use that as initialization for our method. Specifically, we experimented
822   with min-max range estimator and with Lp -norm range estimator with the following values for p:
823
      {2.0, 2.4, 3.0, 3.5, 4.0, 5.0}.

1160

1162
