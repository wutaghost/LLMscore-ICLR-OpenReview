009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Our goal is to enable embodied agents to learn inductive representations for
012            grounded spatial concepts, e.g., learning staircase as an inductive composition
013            of towers of increasing height. Given few human demonstrations, we seek a
014            learning architecture that infers a succinct inductive program representation that
015            explains the observed instances. The approach should generalize to learning of
016
               novel structures of different size or complexity expressed as a hierarchical compo-
               sition of previously learned concepts. Existing approaches that use code generation
017
               capabilities of pre-trained large (visual) language models as well as purely neural
018
               models show poor generalization to a-priori unseen complex concepts. Our key
019            insight is to factor inductive concept learning as: (i) Sketch: detecting and inferring
020            a coarse signature of a new concept (ii) Plan: performing MCTS search over
021            grounded action sequences (iii) Generalize: abstracting out grounded plans as
022            inductive programs. Our pipeline facilitates generalization and modular re-use
023            enabling continual concept learning. Our approach combines the benefits of code
024            generation ability of large language models (LLMs) along with grounded neural
025            representations, resulting in neuro-symbolic programs that show stronger inductive
026            generalization on the task of constructing complex structures vis-á-vis LLM-only
027
               and purely neural approaches. Further, we demonstrate reasoning and planning
               capabilities with learned concepts for embodied instruction following.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The ability to learn inductive representation for novel grounded concepts is one of the hallmarks
033   of human intelligence (Tenenbaum et al., 2011). Humans are highly data efficient – observing a
034   few instances of towers of a certain heights, we can generalize to constructing towers of any height.
035   Further, we interpret increasingly complex concepts as hierarchical composition over simpler ones,
036   e.g., a tower as a sequence of blocks placed on top of each other, or a staircase composed of towers of
037   increasing height. This paper considers the problem of learning a program representation, from a few
038   demonstrations, that models the inductive realization of grounded spatial concepts. Learning of such
039
      concepts is a challenging task due to an expansive space of programs and the need to reason about
      their physical plausibility. Further, the representation must support inductive generalization over
040
      learned concepts as well as express complex hierarchical concepts via modular re-use of concepts
041
      learnt previously.
042
043   Prior efforts such as Liang et al. (2023) uses a LLM (Large Language Model) to generate control
044   program for a given task specification but fail to generalize to complex spatial concepts which are
045
      difficult to tokenize. Extension of this to VLMs (Vision Language Models), Achiam et al. (2023),
      also fail to generalize when presented with linguistically novel concepts, due to over reliance on
046
      prior knowledge and their inability to effectively learn novel concepts from given demonstrations.
047
      On the other hand, neural approaches such as Liu et al. (2023), learn from given demonstrations but
048   generalize poorly due to their inability to (a) explicitly model symbolic concept of induction and
049   (b) modularize as well as re-use previously acquired concepts. Approaches such as Li et al. (2019)
050   train RL-based policies to attain spatial assembly by encoding an inductive spatial prior using GNNs
051   within the policy architecture. However, generalization is still limited and assumes an elicitation
052   of the goal as per object positions, resulting in lack of ability to take in goal description such as
053   “construct a tower of size three”. In essence, we attribute poor generalization of such approaches to an
      implicit entangling of the following objectives: (i) postulating a high-level program for new concepts,

079   This paper introduces an approach, termed SPG, that factorizes the concept learning task as: (a) Sketch:
080   Given a language-annotated demonstration of a novel concept, an LLM is used to postulate a function
081   signature. (b) Plan: Refinement of program sketches via MCTS search, rapidly evaluating actions
082
      sequences guided by a reward associated with constructing a concept. The search is accelerated by
      training a neural action predictor that uses the given demonstrations. (c) Generalize: Leveraging the
083
      code generation capability of an LLM to distill grounded plans into a program that is inductively
084
      generalize-able. This results in a continually evolving library of concepts which can be used
085   to hierarchically learn complex concepts in future. The modular architecture enables continual
086   learning by providing the ability to decide whether the new concept encountered either as a symbolic
087   composition of existing concepts, or, a neural embedding trained via gradient update. Our experiments
088   demonstrate accurate learning of simple and complex concepts from few demonstrations for a range
089   of spatial structures. Further, our approach shows inductive generalization in out-of-distribution
090   settings, significantly improving over the baselines. We also present deeper insights around the
091   efficiency gains obtained by combining symbolic MCTS with neural action predictor. Finally, we
092   show how learned concepts can be grounded in the visual input, enabling a robot to follow natural
093
      language instructions referring to a-priori unseen spatial configurations.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
096
097
098   Concept Learning: The problem of acquiring higher-order programmatic constructs is often modeled
099
      as Bayesian inference over a latent symbol space given observed instances. Seminal works have
      demonstrated efficient inference over latent generative programs to express hand-written digits (Lake
100
      et al., 2015), object arrangements (Ellis et al., 2018), motion plans (Mao et al., 2019), goal-directed
101
      policies (Silver et al., 2019) or compressed/refactored code (Grand et al., 2023; Ellis et al., 2021).
102   These works are focused on learning abstract programs without considering their grounding in the
103   3D world or the process of constructing them (e.g., via an embodied agent performing stacking).
104   In contrast, this paper focus on learning a representation of specific class of higher-order spatially-
105   grounded concepts, namely those possessing a notion of induction resulting in the construction of a
106   structure. While prior efforts have leveraged program synthesis/search methods in learning concepts,
107   we expose such a search to the assessing the physical construction plausibility thereby learning
      physically grounded concepts.

      Learning-to-plan Methods: Our work is complementary to efforts that learn symbolic constructs
109   for efficient planning. Works such as Silver et al. (2023; 2024); Liu et al. (2024), infer state-action
110   abstractions for planning by querying large pre-trained models or by optimizing a goal attainability
111   objective. This paper, instead, focuses on learning a representation for complex spatial assemblies
112   as inductive programs leading to the ability to infer complex goal specifications which can then be
113   combined with aforementioned works for synthesize efficient plans to realize complex assemblies.
114   Works such as Li et al. (2019) learn to construct structures by encoding relational knowledge via
115   graph neural network. However, this effort suffers from poor generalization to unseen examples, (e.g.,
116   tower of larger size) and do not possess a mechanism to re-use previously acquired concepts. Works
117
      such as Wang et al. (2023a;d) shows lifelong learning of skills by learning to plan high-level tasks
      through composition of simple skills for simulated agents. Others (Wan et al., 2023; Parakh et al.,
118
      2023) initiate new skill acquisition upon detecting task failure, building a library of skills over time.
119
      However, they do not model deep inductive use of learned concepts and initiate skill acquisition only
120   upon failure as opposed to learning continually even from goal-reaching demonstrations.
121
122   Robot Instruction Following: Instruction following involves grounding symbolic constructs ex-
123
      pressed in language with aspects of the state-action space such as object assemblies (Paul et al., 2018;
      Collins et al., 2024; Lachmy et al., 2022), spatial relations (Tellex et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2024),
124
      reward functions (Boularias et al., 2015), or motion constraints (Howard et al., 2014). These works
125
      assume the presence of grounded representation for symbolic concepts and only learn associations
126   between language and concepts. In contrast our work jointly learns higher-order concepts composed
127   of simpler concepts along with their grounding in the robot’s state and action space. Others (Singh
128   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b; Ahn et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023) leverage prior-knowledge em-
129   bodied in large vision-language models to directly translate high-level tasks to robot control programs.
130   Our experiments (reported subsequently) demonstrate their limitation in outputting programs for
131   structure assembly-type tasks that require long-range (inductive) spatial reasoning and consideration
132   of physical plausability of construction. Our approach addresses this problem by coupling abstract
133   task knowledge from pre-trained models with physical reasoning in the space of executable plans.

136   3    P RELIMINARIES AND P ROBLEM S ETTING
137
138
      We consider an embodied agent that uses a visual and depth sensor to observe its environment and can
139   grasp and release objects at specified poses. We represent the robot’s domain as a goal-conditioned
140   MDP < S, A, T , g, R, γ > where S is the state space, A is the action space, T is the transition
141   function, g is the goal, R is the reward model and γ is the discount factor. The agent’s objective is to
142   learn a policy that generates a sequence of actions from an initial state s0 to achieve the goal g in
143   response to an instruction Λ specifying the intended goal from a human. We assume that the agent
144   possesses a model of semantic relations (e.g., left(), right() etc.) as well as semantic actions such
145   as moving an object by grasping and releasing at a target location. Such modular and composable
146   notions can be acquired from demonstrations via approaches outlined in Kalithasan et al. (2023); Mao
147
      et al. (2019; 2022). Such notions populate a library of concepts L available as grounded executable
      function calls. Following recent efforts (Liang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022;
148
      Singh et al., 2022) in representing robot control directly as executable programs, we represent action
149
      sequence corresponding to a plan as a program consisting of function calls to executable actions and
150   grounded spatial reasoning.
151
152   Our goal is to enable a robot to interpret and learn the concepts in instructions such as “construct a
153
      tower with red blocks of height five". Specifically, we aim to learn spatial constructs like a tower that
      requires sequential actions that repeatedly place a block on top of a previously constructed assembly,
154
      a process akin to induction. Given a few demonstrations of constructing a spatial assembly, D, each
155
      consisting of natural language description Λ (“construct a tower of red blocks of size five") and a
156   sequence of key frame states {S1 , · · · , Sg } associated with the construction process, we seek to learn
157   a program that models the inductive nature of the concept of tower. This learned representation should
158   enable the agent to generalize inductively to new instructions, such as "construct a tower of blue
159   blocks of height ten." Moreover, the learned concepts should facilitate the learning of more complex
160   structures, which are challenging to represent using primitive actions alone. For example, the concept
161   of a "tower" should assist in learning a "staircase," which can be represented as a sequence of towers
      of increasing heights.

      5     L EARNING I NDUCTIVE C ONCEPTS FROM D EMONSTRATIONS
234
235   We address the problem of estimating a succinct generalized program, Eq. 2, modeling an inductive
236   spatial concept modeling structures whose construction is observed in a human demonstration. Direct
237   symbolic search in the space of programs is intractable (particularly due to looping constructs needed
238   for modeling induction) but can explicitly reason over previously acquired concepts. Alternatively,
239   neural methods attempting to predict action sequence to attain the assembly are challenged by
240   continual setting where concepts can increase over time building on previously learnt ones but are
241   resilient to noise. Our approach blends both approaches and factors the concept learning task as:
242
               • Sketch: From the natural language instruction (Λ), we extract a task sketch (HS∗ ) using an
243
                 LLM that provides the signature (concept name and instantiated arguments) of the concept
244
                 to be learned. When grounded in the initial scene of the demonstration, the task sketch
245              provides a particular instance of the concept demonstrated in the given demonstration.
246
               • Plan: MCTS-based search using the already learnt concepts that outputs the sequence of
247
                 grounded actions, best explaining the given demonstration.
248
249
               • Generalize: The grounded plan HP∗ and task sketch HS∗ are provided to an LLM to obtain a
                 general Python program whose execution on the given scene matches the searched plan.
250

      5.2   (P LAN ) P HYSICAL R EWARD G UIDED P LAN S EARCH
274
275   The plan search involves finding a generalizable plan that effectively explains the demonstration
276   S1 , · · · , Sg . Specifically, this involves determining the concepts, their respective grounded parameters,
277   and the order of composition as specified in the Equation 1.
278   Primitive Actions. Constructing complex structures involves two steps: (1) identifying or imag-
279   ining the placement location of an object/structure and (2) picking and placing the object at the
280   imagined location. The position posθ (.) for placement is determined using a head, which rep-
281   resents a cuboidal enclosure in 3D space. Conceptually, moving the head is akin to the robot’s
282   cognitive exploration of potential placements to achieve the desired spatial configuration. We
283   define a set of primitive functions, Ap , to guide the movement and placement of objects in
284   two stages: (1) move_head(direction): This primitive moves the abstract head to a de-
285   sired relative position and (2) keep_at_head(objects): This primitive places the target
286
      object from the list objects at the current location of the head. It is important to note that
      move_head(direction) is a neural operator, which takes the head’s current position and pre-
287
      dicts its new location based on the specified direction. This operator is trained on a corpus of
288
      pick-and-place instructions, such as "move the green object to the right of the red cube," similar to
289   the approach in Kalithasan et al. (2023).
290
291   MCTS Search. We use an object-centric state representation defined by bounding boxes (including
292
      the depth of the center) and visual attributes of all the objects that are present on the table. For each
      learned inductive concept <cpt>, we define a macro-action Make_<cpt>(size) that executes
293
      the corresponding program with the given size argument, resulting in the construction of the desired
294
      concept. Thus, the action space A is the union of primitive actions Ap and compound/macro-
295   actions Ac . Intersection over Union (IoU) between the attained state and the expected state in the
296   demonstration is provided as a reward for all macro actions and keep_at_head(objects); all
297   other actions yield zero reward. An MCTS procedure similar to Khandelwal et al. (2016) is performed
298   to find a plan that maximizes the reward. The node expansion process and reward calculation for the
299   MCTS procedure is detailed in Appendix A.3. The search outputs a sequence of grounded actions for
300   an instantiation of the given inductive concept by the task arguments.
301   Modularity and Scalability. MCTS that searches for a plan only in terms of primitive actions may
302   not be generalizable due to lack of modularity C.3. The use of macro-actions in the search ensures
303   that the plan Hp∗ for a given demonstration is concise, modular, and easily generalizable. This can be
304   seen as a form of regularization in terms of the length of concept description by making the prior
305   P (H) ∝ |H|−α (where α > 0) in equation (2)
306                            H ∗ = arg min [Loss({S1 ..Sn }, H(Λ)) + α log |H|]
307                                       H∈H
308   However, as the action space expands with the learning of more concepts, the search becomes
309   slower, necessitating the pruning of the search space. To avoid searching over the size parameter
310   in macro-actions, we greedily select the smallest size that achieves the maximum average reward
311   from the current state. Additionally, to prune primitive actions, we train a reactive policy πneural
312   which, given the current state s˜t and the next expected state st+1 (from the demonstration), outputs
313
      one of the primitive actions a∗t ∈ Ap . Consequently, the effective branching factor of the search is
      reduced from |Ac | + |Ap | to |Ac | + 1. Thus, our MCTS algorithm is modular through the hierarchical
314
      composition of learned concepts and efficient through action space pruning, and is referred to as
315
      MCTS+L+P. Further details regarding modifications in MCTS are given in the Appendix A.3.

369   search over the action space A: (i) MCTS+P+L: Our approach as described in section 5.2, that
370   uses the learnt concepts from L as macro actions in subsequent searches (e.g., Make_Tower(3,
371   objects) ∈ Ac ) (L). Further it performs pruning of Ap using πneural (P), (ii) MCTS-P+L: Our
372   approach without neural pruning and (iii) MCTS+P-L: No access to library of concepts during
373   continual learning and thereby lacks ability to use macro actions. This method greedily selects the
374
      action from Ap as given by πneural . We provide more details about the 3 methods in Appendix D.4.
375   Metrics. We adopt the following metrics to evaluate our models: (i) Program Accuracy: A binary
376   score obtained through human evaluation. 1 for constructing the structure fully, 0 otherwise. (ii)
377   Target Construction IoU: Intersection over Union (2D-IoU) between bounding boxes. (iii) Target
      Construction Loss: Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss over the bounding boxes + depth of the center.

527   8    C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper introduces a novel approach for learning inductive representation of grounded spatial
530   concepts as neuro-symbolic programs via language-guided demonstrations. Our approach factors pro-
531
      gram learning as: Sketch: generating the high-level program signature via an LLM, Plan: searching
      for a grounded plan that maximises the total discounted reward with the respect to the demonstration,
532
      and Generalize: abstracting the grounded plan into an inductively generalize-able abstract plan via
533
      an LLM. Continual learning is achieved via learning of modular programs by giving preference to
534   shorter programs through composition of learnt ones. Extensive evaluation demonstrates accurate
535   program learning and stronger generalization in relation to purely LLM based as well as purely
536   neural baselines. Grounding of learned concepts in visual data facilitates reasoning and planning for
537   embodied instruction following. Limitations include reliance on perfect demonstrations, assumption
538   of full observability of all objects and experiments confined to simulation. Incorporating noisy
539   demonstrations, reasoning with beliefs and interleaving planning and execution remains part of future
      work.

      In order to allow modular learning of programs, for every inductive concept already stored in
865   the library we define corresponding action instantiations which can be a potential candidate ac-
866   tions during our search. As an example, for the concept Tower we have one of the action in-
867   stantiation as Make_Tower(3, objects) which would be the action of constructing desired
868   tower. This can be visualized as a compound/macro-action which is composed of primitive ac-
869   tions keep_at_head(objects), move_head(‘top’). We define Ac as the space of such
870   compound actions, and Ap as the space of primitive action/function consisting reset_head(),
871   move_head(direction), keep_at_head(objects), store_head(). Realization
872   of equation (6) (increased preference of macro-actions over primitive ones) is done through discounted
873
      IoU rewards during our search (Make_Tower(3) would have a reward of 1+1+1, as compared to
      1 + γ 2 (1) + γ 4 (1) for a sequence of 3 (keep_at_head(objects), move_head(’top’)0)).

      We refer to MCTS using concept instantiations from L as macro actions in search as MCTS+L.
875
876   Scalablility (MCTS+P): as more concepts are added to the library L the action space of our search
877   A (specifically Ac , the space of compound concepts) increases, therefore we want to prune the
878   search space effectively. For this during the pre-training phase we train a reactive policy πneural
879
      which given the current state, s˜t and the next expected state st+1 (part of the demonstration) would
      output one of the primitive action, a∗t ∈ Ap where Ap is the primitive action space, i.e. a∗t =
880
      πneural (at |s˜t , st+1 ), at ∈ Ap Note that while expanding our search tree we only search among the
881
      space of compound actions Ac and the action a∗t , thereby reducing the branching factor of search
882   from |Ac ∪ Ap | to |Ac | + 1. We refer to MCTS using neural pruning as MCTS+P. Therefore our
883   MCTS algorithm is modular through hierarchical composition of learnt concepts and efficient through
884   pruning of action space and is referred to as MCTS+L+P.

915
      represented as a sequence of RGBD keyframes.

917   Output/Aim of the Model: The goal is to learn a representation of the unknown concept in the
      instruction, assuming there is only one unknown concept. If the unknown concept is inductive (e.g.,

          "tower"), the model learns a program definition def tower() and stores it in the program library. If the
919       unknown concept is a primitive concept, a concept embedding is learned through backpropagation.
920
921       Evaluation: The learned model is evaluated based on the correctness of the program representation
922
          for inductive concepts and the correctness of object placements, measured through the Intersection
          over Union (IoU) metric (see Metrics, line 262). d. Examples: Suppose the current library contains
923

924
          the process is as follows:
925
926       Parsing: The instruction is parsed into a sketch: Staircase(height=3, objects=filter(red, blocks)).
927       Grounding: The “objects” parameter is grounded using the visual grounder, which identifies the
928       indices of the red blocks, e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]. That is, filter(red, blocks) = [1,2,3,4,5,6].
929
          Planning: The planning step uses the demonstrations (sequence of keyframes) to identify the
930
          sequence of actions that best explains the demonstrations. In this case, the plan might be:
931
          Tower(height=1, objects=[1,2,3,4,5,6]), move_head(right), Tower(height=2, objects=[2,3,4,5,6]),

933

935
          into a program. The resulting program would be:
936   1 def staircase(height, objects):
937   2     for i in range(height):
938   3         tower(height=i, objects)
      4         move_head(right)

940
          Note: Whenever an object is placed, the objects list is modified in place, and the index of the placed
941
          object is removed. primitive actions: The movement of any object is achieved by first determining the
942
          placement position by moving the head (an imaginary bounding box) in specific directions and then
943       placing the object to be moved at the head. The head is implemented as a 3D bounding box defined
944       by coordinates (x1, y1, x2, y2, d), where x1, y1, x2, and y2 are the 2D corners of the bounding box,
945       and d is the depth at the center. The primitive action move_head(direction) shifts the bounding box in
946       the required direction. The action keep_at_head(object_list) picks the first object in the list and places
947       it at the center of the bounding box. Two other primitives, store_head and reset_head, are used to
948       save the current position of the head, allowing the search to return to useful positions later if needed.

       D.4   D ETAILS ON MCTS VARIANTS FOR P LAN S EARCH
1669
1670   Here we provide the details for 3 different plan search methods, that search over the space A =
1671   Ac ∪ Ap
1672
1673          • MCTS+L+P: This is the approach that we describe in section 4.2. For every concept
                say Tower ∈ L we have a corresponding set of macro action say Make_Tower(3,


                                                         31
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1674
                objects) (L). Further we use a neural pruner πneural that outputs a primitive action a∗p
1675
                (given current state and next expected state). We only consider the actions Ac ∪ {a∗p } during
1676            our search from the given state. This helps to reduce the effective branching factor and
1677            allows to search longer length plans within the same computational budget.
1678
1679
1680
1681          • MCTS+L-P: Here we do not use the reactive policy, therefore the branching factor for every
1682
                node becomes A = Ac ∪ Ap .
1683
1684
1685          • MCTS-L+P: We only search among the space of primitive actions i.e Ap . Given a state s̃t and
1686            corresponding next expected state st+1 we greedily pick the action a∗p = πneural (s̃t , st+1 ).
1687            This method is much more faster than the previous 2 methods as there is no explicit search.
1688            However the corresponding policy is trained only to output an action a ∈ Ap and lacks
1689            the ability to output modular plans composed of macro actions such as Make_Tower(3,

                network needs to be changed accordingly). As a result the plans found are not modular and
                difficult to generalize. Further, the reactive policy is not trained to output reset_head(),

                store_head() as additional annotated data is required in order to train a classifier over
1693
                them. This further decreases the space of grounded plans (and therefore corresponding

1749   Pre-conditions. We define the following 2 preconditions (and learn their grounding) in order to
1750   ensure that the generated plans are physically possible.
1751
1752        1. is-clear(blk, dir): We need to check whether a block blk has some free space in
1753           direction dir. For this we simply move the head in the direction dir with respect to the block
1754
               blk. If the resulting position of head has 0 overlap with bounding boxes of all the other
               objects the predicate is True otherwise False.
1755
            2. will-not-be-floating(pred_loc): We need to check whether the resultant/pre-
1756
               dicted location of an object on taking an action would be dynamically stable or not. The
1757           location would be stable if either it is on top of some already placed object or it is on the
1758           table surface. The former can be checked through the resultant scene graph itself (that is
1759           obtained by applying the algorithm 0), while for the later we train an on-table classifier.
1760           This would take as input a bounding box and predict whether the box is on the table or
1761           not. For training this classifier we use the dataset of pretraining phase. The blockwise
1762           positive and negative sample annotation can be done automatically by giving GPT-4 the
1763           corresponding scene graph and then querying which objects are on the table and which
1764           aren’t. 17 gives an example. (Though we have not taken this approach for the complete
1765
               dataset of 5k samples due to high cost).
1766
       Actions. We define the following two types actions
1767
1768        1. place-random(blk): To place a block at a random free position on the table. For this
1769           we train a generative model (VAE) which learns the underlying distribution of bounding
1770           boxes for all the blocks that lie on the table. Given a scene we would sample position
1771           (bounding box) from this until we get a position that is not overlapping with the existing
1772           blocks in the scene. For training the VAE we assume that for every demonstration in the
1773           pretraining data, the first scene has all the objects randomly placed on the table (we could
1774
               have also used the positive examples used for training on-table classifier).
            2. move(rel, blk1, blk2): This action corresponds to moving the blk1 in the di-
1775
               rection rel of blk2, resulting in the addition of a relation (rel, blk1, blk2) in the set
1776
               of spatial relations. This action is defined as a sequential composition of the actions
1777           assign_head(blk2), move_head(rel), keep_at_head(blk1) (blk1 is a
1778           one hot tensor for the corresponding block).
