010                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities
013            across a wide range of problem-solving tasks. Despite their success, LLMs
014            still face significant challenges in complex reasoning, particularly with advanced
015            mathematical problems. Existing data synthesis works either focus on the di-
016            versity of generated problems but ignore the quality of corresponding response
017            of hard problems, or generate high-quality response of existing hard problems
018            based on rejection sampling without synthesizing more diverse instructions. To
019
               address this gap, we introduce WISDOM, which draws inspiration from the cur-
               riculum learning and gradually synthesizes more diverse and difficult problems
020
               with high-quality responses from easy to hard based on response consistency.
021
               Based on the synthesized data, we further fine-tune and develop the WISDOM
022            series models, achieving significant improvements across multiple mathemati-
023            cal reasoning benchmarks. Notably, WISDOM-7B (DSMath) achieves a score
024            of 62.4% on MATH, matching GPT-4’s performance with 2/30 correct answers
025            on AIME2024. Furthermore, WISDOM-70B (Llama3) outperforms GPT-4 on
026            AIME2024 with 3/30 correct answers. More data and models will be available
027            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Wisdom-math-377B.

068   Inspired by the curriculum learning of human, where individuals tackle complex reasoning problems
069
      by breaking them down into smaller, solvable sub-problems, we propose WISDOM. Unlike traditional
      curriculum learning refers to the pre-defined difficulty of tasks, we redefine it based on the model’s
070
      performance. As shown in Figure 1, progressively synthesizing complex questions from simpler
071
      ones to enhance the performance of LLMs. Due to the lack of ground truth for newly synthesized
072   questions, we innovatively use response consistency to evaluate the quality of responses and diffi-
073   culty level of problems. The motivation comes from an intuitive hypothesis: simpler problems are
074   more likely to yield consistent results across various solutions. WISDOM gradually increases prob-
075   lem difficulty through the following steps: Weak Teacher Guiding, Critical Expert Teaching, and
076   Experts Consistency Voting, which fully leverage the inner consistency of weak models, consistency
077   between strong and weak models, and inner consistency of strong models. Instead of generating
078   all problems at once, we adopt a dynamic “funnel-like” filtering mechanism to evolve seed data
079   iteratively, which progressively synthesizes new and increasingly challenging problems.
080   Specifically, we use MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) as seed
081   data and build a synthetic dataset of 1.48 million size WISDOM through a cost-effective progres-
082   sive curriculum learning method from easy to hard, which encompasses problems and solutions at
083   various levels of difficulty. Experimental results demonstrate even small language models such as
084   Llama3-8b and DeepSeekMath-7B (DSMath-7B) can achieve competitive performance exceeding
085   Qwen2-72B and Llama3-70B. As illustrated in Figure 2, Llama3-8B with WISDOM achieves 59.7%
086
      on MATH, which is better than Llama3-70B-instruct and existing SOTA. Additionally, the perfor-

087

088
      are summarized as follows:
089
090   • We propose a novel framework for synthesizing mathematical reasoning data, which evolves the
091     difficulty and quality of questions in a progressive manner from easy to hard. In terms of response
092     generation, our approach is more cost-effective, and achieves remarkable SOTA on in-domain and
093     out-of-domain tasks based on same size model.
094   • Compared with Rejection Sampling and Problems Rewrite, our method leverages response con-
095     sistency to progressively enhance the difficulty of questions while improving response accuracy
096     in the absence of ground truth, which is more effective for generating high-quality responses to
097     newly synthetic questions.
098
      • We will open-source all our models and data to drive further advancements in the open-source
099     community for challenging mathematical reasoning tasks.

136
137   Previous data synthesis efforts (Yu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a;
138   Chan et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024b) focused on enhancing diversity in mathematical reasoning but
139   often overlooked question difficulty. Recent studies (Chen et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
140   2024a) show that using a subset of high-difficulty data for instruction tuning enhances performance
141
      compared to using the entire dataset. To automatically define problem difficulty, we use response
      consistency across LLMs, including inner consistency of weak models, consistency between weak
142
      and strong models, and inner consistency of strong models. To test the hypothesis that ”simpler
143
      problems can be solved by weak models with diverse solutions,” we analyzed the relationship be-
144   tween response consistency and difficulty on 5,000 MATH dataset problems (see Appendix B.4)
145   using the DeepSeek-V2.5 model. As shown in Table 9, results indicate a clear trend: higher dif-
146   ficulty correlates with lower response consistency. This enables a dynamic, “funnel-like” filtering
147   mechanism that progressively increases problem difficulty, evolving simple questions into more
148   challenging ones through iterative consistency evaluations.
149

203   2.3   COSTS AND RESPONSE EFFICIENCY BALANCING
204
205   For data synthesis, majority voting is an effective yet resource-intensive method to improve accu-
206   racy. Given that the data synthesis process may generate many trivial and vanilla problems (Tong
207
      et al., 2024), we apply majority voting specifically to evaluate and opt for the most difficult ques-
      tions. Therefore, we first employ a weak but cost-effective teacher to solve a large number of easy
208
      problems. Subsequently, a strong but more resource-intensive expert is used for medium-difficulty
209
      questions, thereby optimizing resource utilization. To ensure the quality of generated responses,
210   Rejection Sampling is an effective way. However, it relies heavily on the ground truth of seed data,
211   making it less applicable to newly synthesized problems. We enforce unsupervised consistency to
212   improve the quality of responses. With the weak teacher and inner consistency, we can filter out
213   many simple, easily solvable problems early on, saving costs and improving efficiency. Although
214   we also apply majority voting in the final stage to further increase response accuracy, by this point,
215   the dataset has been significantly reduced in size and the difficulty of the problem has been greatly
      enhanced.

      2.4     Q UESTION D IVERSITY E NHANCED VIA M ETA DATA G ENERATION
217
218   Since data diversity plays a crucial role in instruction tuning, following existing work (Tang et al.,
219   2024; Li et al., 2024a; Chan et al., 2024), we utilize the meta-information of questions to enhance
220   the diversity of synthesized data. In mathematics, meta-information often refers to extracted skills
221   or topics from questions, and synthesizing data can be generated based on this extra information.
222   While existing methods (Tang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024) rely solely on skill or topic for
223   question synthesis, we argue that focusing only on these aspects can be overly simplistic and limit
224
      the diversity of the synthesized questions. Instead, we extract a richer set of meta-information,
      including Skill, Topic, Knowledge Base, and Similar Problem, and combine multiple pieces of meta-
225
      information from various questions to maximize the diversity of the synthesized questions.
226
227   Specifically, we first extract meta-information from the questions (e.g., q3 ) and obtain embeddings
228   from the knowledge base in the meta-information using OpenAI’s text-embedding-ada-002. We then
229   apply k-means++ (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007) clustering to group these embeddings. Furthermore,
230
      we randomly combine all of the aforementioned meta-information across different clusters and mask
      the Knowledge Base and Similar Problem with a 40% probability to prompt GPT-4o-0513 to gen-
231
      erate new questions. This strategy not only prevents overfitting on the synthesized data but also
232
      significantly enhances the diversity of the generated questions.

250   2.5     K NOWLEDGE - BASED CURRICULUM LEARNING
251
252   To further enhance the quality of synthesized data and improve the model’s reasoning abilities, the
253   current mainstream approach is to adopt the Chain of Thought (CoT) method, which transforms
254
      simple seed data responses into structured, step-by-step reasoning processes and teaches the model
      to think before solving. Inspired by the principles of curriculum learning, it is advantageous to re-
255
      call key knowledge points relevant to the problem before answering a question. Thus, we integrate
256
      the knowledge base from the meta-information, which includes Key Definitions and Explanations,
257   Relevant Formulas and Equations, Common Pitfalls and Misconceptions, and Additional Theoret-
258   ical Insights, into the preamble of the CoT response. This step helps the model reinforce critical
259   knowledge points before proceeding to solve a given question. A simple illustrative example can be
260   found in Example 2.1, and more detailed examples can be found in Appendix D.

      the performance of Qwen2-Instruct. Remarkably, on the AIME 2024 dataset, our method performs

463   We conduct an in-depth analysis of the topics included in each stage of the problems, providing

465

466

467
      topics along with their frequencies. As shown in Figure 5, In the initial stage, the data predominantly
468   features simple and clear topics, such as arithmetic and kinematics. As the difficulty increases, more
469   complex subjects emerge, including linear algebra, optimization, and complex numbers. In the final
470   stage, financial-related problems appear, possibly due to the model’s difficulty in handling decimal
471   precision in financial interest calculations, where the reasoning path is correct, but the answers are
472   wrong. Therefore, future synthesis of mathematical reasoning problems should aim for more refined
473   generation and optimization, particularly targeting similar topics while balancing computational pre-
474   cision and cognitive complexity.

      4   R ELATED W ORK
502
503   Instruction Tuning. Instruction fine-tuning has been widely validated as an effective method for
504
      enhancing model capabilities. Previous work (Kang et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024); Xia et al.
      (2024); Li et al. (2024d)) has primarily focused on improving model performance through the careful
505
      selection of high-quality data. However, recent literature (Tang et al. (2024); Chung et al. (2022);
506
      Yue et al. (2024b); Tang et al. (2024)) suggests that increasing the volume of data can also contribute
507   to performance gains. Unlike efforts (Zhang et al. (2024); Muennighoff et al. (2023)) that aim to
508   broadly enhance general model capabilities, our instruction fine-tuning is specifically designed to
509   improve complex mathematical reasoning skills.
510
      Mathematical Reasoning. To enhance answer accuracy on challenging benchmarks, recent re-
511
      search (Gou et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024b)) has increasingly focused on
512
      leveraging external tools to improve large language models’ (LLMs) ability to solve mathematical
513   problems and achieve higher scores. Most approaches utilize Program of Thought (PoT) methods,
514   employing code interpreters to compute the final result (numerous methods cited here). Addition-
515   ally, some methods (Wang et al. (2023a); Aggarwal et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024a); Shao et al.
516   (2024)) adopt self-consistency techniques, ensembling multiple outputs to achieve better results. In
517   contrast, we concentrate on improving the model’s intrinsic reasoning capabilities, relying solely on
518   Chain of Thought (CoT) approaches to develop the model’s inherent mathematical reasoning skills.
519   Distillation. Training smaller student models using synthetic data generated by more powerful
520   teacher models has been widely validated as an effective approach (Xu et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024f);
521   Wang et al. (2024c); Li et al. (2024b)) . In the domain of mathematical reasoning, this effective-
522   ness has also been demonstrated (Yu et al. (2024); Yue et al. (2024a); Tang et al. (2024); Li et al.
523   (2024a); Azerbayev et al. (2024)). However, these methods still exhibit a significant gap in perfor-
524   mance when tackling challenging mathematical problems compared to closed-source models. Our
525   work addresses this gap by employing curriculum learning, which not only synthesizes diverse and
526   challenging responses but also gradually generates problems with greater diversity and complexity,
527
      thereby significantly narrowing the performance gap with closed-source models.

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we propose WISDOM as a data-synthesis framework to enhance the mathematical rea-
532
      soning abilities of LLMs. The key insight behind WISDOM is the use of progressive curriculum
533
      synthesis, which iteratively evolves the difficulty of questions, and generates high-quality responses
534   in unsupervised ways based on response consistency of weak and strong models without relying
535   on ground truths. Compared to traditional majority voting, WISDOM is 2.82x more cost-effective.
536   To validate the effectiveness of the synthesized datasets, we fine-tune a series of open-sourced
537   LLMs ranging from 7B to 72B parameters. Experimental results show that the fine-tuned LLMs
538   achieve significant improvements over the base models, highlighting the generalization capabilities
539   of WISDOM. Our empirical findings also offer valuable insights into tackling challenging problems,
      paving the way for future complex reasoning across various fields.
