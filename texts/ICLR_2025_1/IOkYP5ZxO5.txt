009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Tactile information plays a crucial role in human manipulation tasks and has re-
012            cently garnered increasing attention in robotic manipulation. However, existing
013            approaches struggle to effectively integrate visual and tactile information, result-
014            ing in sub-optimal performance. In this paper, we present ViTaS, a simple yet
015            effective framework that incorporates both visual and tactile information to guide
016            an agent’s behavior. We introduce Soft Fusion Contrastive Learning, an advanced
017
               version of conventional contrastive learning method, to enhance the fusion of these
               two modalities, and adopt a CVAE module to utilize complementary information
018
               within visuo-tactile representations. We conduct comprehensive experiments, in-
019
               cluding 9 tasks in simulation environment, across 5 different benchmarks, to com-
020            pare ViTaS with existing baselines. The results demonstrate that ViTaS achieves
021            state-of-the-art performance, with an average improvement of 51%. Furthermore,
022            our method signiﬁcantly enhances sample efﬁciency while maintaining minimal
023            parameters, underscoring the effectiveness of our approach. The code will be re-
024            leased upon acceptance.

043   Humans are adept at performing complex manipulation tasks, such as spinning a pen or lifting a
044   block. While vision plays a critical role in these activities, other modalities, particularly touch,
045   also provide rich information for object manipulation. Interestingly, visual and tactile information
046   often exhibit signiﬁcant relevance and complementarity (Apkarian-Stielau & Loomis, 1975). For
047   individuals with visual impairments, a clearer mental reconstruction of an original visual image can
048   be achieved by combining a blurred visual perception with tactile information (Kappers, 2011).
049   Most previous reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms have relied primarily on visual information
050   to address manipulation tasks (Yuan et al., 2022b; Xiao et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2022a; Li et al.,
051   2024b;c; Haarnoja et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Pitz et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023;
052   Qi et al., 2023b;a; Plappert et al., 2018b; Yuan et al., 2024b). Recently, several efforts have aimed
053   to incorporate tactile information to improve the performance of RL algorithms. However, these
      approaches generally exhibit limited fusion between the two modalities. For instance, Sferrazza et al.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (2023) directly concatenates visual and tactile inputs and feeds them into MAE, while Chen et al.
055   (2022) segments visual and tactile data into patches and uses a transformer to extract representations.
056   As a result, these methods often demonstrate limited performance in contact-rich manipulation tasks
057   that rely heavily on both visual and tactile inputs, such as in-hand rotation. Moreover, many previous
058   methods employ complex encoders like transformers and MAE, which involve intricate architectures
059   with numerous parameters, leading to prolonged training time. Due to the insufﬁcient utilization
060   of tactile information, these methods also diminish sample efﬁciency of RL algorithms in guiding
061   manipulation. Given these limitations, we pose the question: how can we more effectively fuse visual
062   and tactile information, to enhance the performance of RL algorithms for manipulation?
063   Drawing on prior research in human physiology regarding the processing of visuo-tactile informa-
064   tion, we propose Visual Tactile Soft Fusion Contrastive Learning (ViTaS), a novel visuo-tactile
065   representation learning framework for reinforcement learning. Generally ViTaS can be divided into
066   two parts. Firstly, given the inherent relevance between visual and tactile modalities, we utilize
067   contrastive learning to align the embeddings of visual data with their corresponding tactile informa-
068   tion in the latent space. Notably, we employ soft fusion contrastive learning inspired by Han et al.
069
      (2020) to fuse features in alternated modalities. Speciﬁcally, we extend the original RGB single-
      modality framework to incorporate both visual and tactile modalities, enabling the agent to leverage
070
      samples of different timesteps with similar tactile information as positive samples. Additionally,
071
      inspired by the ability of humans to reconstruct clear images from blurred visual inputs combined
072   with tactile information and complementarity of two modalities, we integrate conditional variational
073   autoencoder (CVAE) introduced by Sohn et al. (2015) to reconstruct the original image with the
074   embeddings of vision and touch, further improving the fusion of visual and tactile information.
075
      To evaluate the performance of our algorithm, we conduct experiments on 9 tasks across 5 en-
076
      vironments: Insertion (Sferrazza et al., 2023), Gymnasium (Towers et al., 2024), Robosuite (Zhu
077
      et al., 2020), Mobile Catch (Zhang et al., 2024) and Block Spin (Yuan et al., 2024a). Especially, the
078   original Mobile Catch and Block Spin environments lack tactile information, so we integrate tactile
079   sensors into the robotic hand, creating 2 challenging visuo-tactile tasks. Additionally, to demon-
080   strate the generalization capability of our system, we perform further experiments on 3 auxiliary
081   tasks, as well as several ablation studies. The overall experimental results illustrated in Figure 1 and
082   Figure 2 (a) show that ViTaS achieves state-of-the-art performance compared to other visuo-tactile
083   learning methods in all tasks, with an average success rate of 92% and average improvement of 51%,
084   while minimizing total trainable parameters in representation learning.
085   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
086
087          • We improve the traditional contrastive learning method and leverage it for the fusion of
088            visual and tactile modalities.
089          • We propose ViTaS, a simple yet effective representation learning paradigm that can inte-
090            grate visual and tactile inputs through soft fusion contrastive as well as CVAE, and utilize
091            it to guide the training of reinforcement learning and visuomotor control agent.
092          • We evaluate our algorithm on various simulation tasks, demonstrating state-of-the-art per-
093            formance with an average growth rate of 51%. ViTaS signiﬁcantly improves sample efﬁ-
094            ciency and also minimizes the number of parameters in the feature extractor.

098   Visuo-Tactile Representation Learning In recent years, numerous cross-modal representation
099   learning methods, particularly those focused on visuo-tactile integration, have emerged, as demon-
100   strated by Lee et al. (2020; 2019); Dave et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024); Polic et al. (2019); Xu
101   et al. (2024); Li et al. (2022a); Yang et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2023); Radford et al.
102   (2021); Li et al. (2024a; 2022b). Among them, Li et al. (2019) utilizes an adversarial loss to learn
103   representation in the latent space, while Chen et al. (2022) leverages a transformer architecture to
104
      integrate multiple modalities, introducing alignment and contact loss to enhance performance. Sfer-
      razza et al. (2023) proposes a jointly visuo-tactile training scheme using an MAE-based encoder
105
      trained through a reconstruction process, with the encoder co-trained for policy learning.
106
107   Despite the success of these approaches in speciﬁc tasks, they often fail to fully exploit the cor-
      respondence between visual and tactile modalities, leading to sub-optimal encoder training and re-

      duced success rates in tasks such as dexterous hand manipulation. In contrast, our method employs a
109   simpler yet highly effective CNN-based encoder to improve the alignment and fusion of modalities,
110   achieving superior performance across multiple benchmark tasks.
111
112   Contrastive Learning Extended into computer vision by the MoCo series (He et al., 2020; Chen
113   et al., 2021), and SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), contrastive learning has emerged as a prominent
114   technique for representation learning. We intend to extend the contrastive learning paradigm to
115   visuo-tactile framework for reinforcement learning. Related examples include Wang & Hu (2023a),
116   Dave et al. (2024), Yuan et al. (2021), Laskin et al. (2020), Wang & Hu (2023b), Han et al. (2020),
117   Yang et al. (2024), Wang & Hu (2023a), Yuan et al. (2024a) and Zhan et al. (2022). Among the
118   works most closely related to ours, Dave et al. (2024) proposes a visuo-tactile fusion approach
119
      based on contrastive pre-training. Zhan et al. (2022) employs contrastive loss within the visual
      modality to enhance policy learning. Yang et al. (2024) incorporates tactile, vision and text using
120
      contrastive learning to solve downstream tasks. Han et al. (2020) advances contrastive learning
121
      paradigm utilizing top K analogous samples in optical ﬂow as positive samples in RGB modality.
122
123   However, as Han et al. (2020) mentions, simply doing instance discrimination tends to neglect
124   some key information that two resembling samples may be negatives for each other due to dis-
125
      tinct timesteps. The phenomenon also pops up in the ﬁeld of cross-modal contrastive learning. We
      reﬁne the contrastive learning method to alleviate the issue, which is elaborated in Section 3.1.

157   visuo-tactile fusion framework tailored for reinforcement learning. We note that when two tactile

159   bear a strong resemblance and vice versa. This alignment makes the data particularly well-suited
160   for contrastive learning. Meanwhile, given the complementary information tactile and image offer,
161   our objective is to reconstruct the features extracted from two modalities, obtaining outputs with en-
      riched and more detailed information. CVAE is a good candidate for this process. Therefore, ViTaS

297
               and tactile is concatenated and passed through a uniﬁed encoder. Training process of pol-
298            icy and MAE encoder occurs concurrently.
299
             • VTT (Chen et al., 2022): A visuo-tactile fusion training method rooted in the transformer
300

301

302            latent representations are reconstructed into images and rewards using a VAE decoder. The

             • Concatenation (Lee et al., 2019): A relatively primitive multi-modal fusion method. Image
309
               and tactile data are passed through a CNN encoder independently, whose results are then

496   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
497
498
      In general, we introduce ViTaS, a succinct yet effective visuo-tactile fusion framework. Drawing
499   an analogy to human physiology, we extend the application of visual and tactile perception to the
500   domain of reinforcement learning, yielding remarkable results. We propose soft fusion contrastive
501   learning to extract key features from one modality according to another, and we adopt a CVAE mod-
502   ule to utilize complementary information from different modalities. Experiments, auxiliary tasks and
503   ablation studies are meticulously conducted to verify each component of ViTaS. The results show
504   the effectiveness of ViTaS and necessity of soft fusion contrastive and CVAE.
505   The main limitation of ViTaS is that addressing scalability to more complex manipulation and long-
506   horizon dexterous tasks still remains challenging. Moreover, despite the success in a wide range of
507   tasks in various simulated environments, the applicability in the real world is absent. However, it has
508   not escaped our notice that ViTaS is theoretically capable of being seamlessly deployed to physical
509   robotic systems, for information we use could be easily obtained in the real world. The results in
510   Figure 12 also show the robustness to noise, a main hindrance for real-world applicability, further
511   proving the capability of ViTaS. In the future, we will continually explore the potential of ViTaS for
512   more complex manipulation tasks and validate the transfer on real hardware.
