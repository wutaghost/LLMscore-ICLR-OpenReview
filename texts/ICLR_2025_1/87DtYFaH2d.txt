011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Role-Playing Agents (RPAs) have shown remarkable performance in various ap-
014            plications, yet they often struggle to recognize and appropriately respond to hard
015            queries that conflict with their role-play knowledge. To investigate RPAs’ per-
016            formance when faced with different types of conflicting requests, we develop
017            an evaluation benchmark that includes contextual knowledge conflicting requests,
018
               parametric knowledge conflicting requests, and non-conflicting requests to assess
               RPAs’ ability to identify conflicts and refuse to answer appropriately without over-
019
               refusing. Through extensive evaluation, we find that most RPAs behave significant
020
               performance gaps toward different conflict requests. To elucidate the reasons, we
021            conduct an in-depth representation-level analysis of RPAs under various conflict
022            scenarios. Our findings reveal the existence of rejection regions and direct re-
023            sponse regions within the model’s forwarding representation, and thus influence
024            the RPA’s final response behavior. Therefore, we introduce a lightweight represen-
025            tation editing approach that conveniently shifts conflicting requests to the rejection
026            region, thereby enhancing the model’s refusal accuracy. The experimental results
027            validate the effectiveness of our editing method, improving RPAs’ refusal ability
028            of conflicting requests while maintaining their general role-playing capabilities.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Role-Playing Agents(RPAs), ranging from non-player characters in video games(Wang et al., 2023a)
034   to virtual assistants(Tseng et al., 2024) and interactive educational tools(Wei et al., 2024), are rev-
035   olutionizing human-computer interaction(Chen et al., 2024b). The growing importance of RPAs in
036   AI applications underscores the need to improve their performance. Previous work in the field of
037   role-playing has primarily focused on enhancing the performance of RPAs through techniques such
038   as prompt-based methods and fine-tuning(Wang et al., 2023c; Zhou et al., 2023; Tu et al., 2023; Li
039
      et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024c). To assess these improvements, researchers have
      introduced several fine-grained evaluation dimensions(Wang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024b;d; Tu
040
      et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Sadeq et al., 2024), such as assess personality(Wang
041
      et al., 2023b) or hallucination(Ahn et al., 2024) of RPAs.
042
043   Although these efforts have effectively enhanced the performance of RPAs in terms of role consis-
044   tency and dialogue capabilities(Wang et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2023), RPAs often struggle when
045
      faced with queries that conflict with their role knowledge or capabilities. As a result, they tend to
      respond directly to queries instead of refusing to answer when faced with such conflicts(Ahn et al.,
046
      2024; Sadeq et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024). For instance, when interacting with an RPA playing the
047
      role of Gandalf, if a user queries, “Who murdered Harry Potter’s parents?”, an ideal response would
048   be, “I don’t know what you’re talking about. The story of Harry Potter is not part of my world
049   or knowledge.” Instead, the RPA might incorrectly reply, “Harry Potter’s parents, James and Lily
050   Potter, were murdered by...” Enhancing the refusal capability of RPAs is crucial for building reliable
051   AI systems. Although some studies have begun to address this issue(Ahn et al., 2024; Sadeq et al.,
052   2024), their scope remains limited, often focusing on specific scenarios such as temporal inconsis-
053   tencies. There is a lack of systematic research on diverse conflicting scenarios and little exploration
      of the reasons for RPAs’ performance gap across different types of conflicting queries.

085
086   In this work, we extend previous work(Ahn et al., 2024) to conduct an in-depth study of scenarios
087   where RPAs need to refuse queries that exceed their role knowledge and capabilities. Specifically,
088   we consider three research questions:
089
090             (RQ1) How do existing models perform when facing different types of conflicting queries?
091             (RQ2) Why is there a gap in RPAs’ abilities to handle different types of conflicting queries?
092
093
                (RQ3) How can we enhance RPAs’ ability to respond to conflicting queries without com-
                promising their general role-playing capabilities?
094
095
      To answer RQ1 and lay the groundwork for RQ2 and RQ3, we first categorized refusal scenarios
096   into two main categories: (1) conflicts with role contextual knowledge, and (2) conflicts with role
097   parametric knowledge(Xu et al., 2024b). These categories were further subdivided into four spe-
098   cific scenarios, as illustrated in Figure 1. The expected responses from RPAs in these scenarios can
099   range from direct refusal to acknowledging their inability to answer or providing disclaimers about
100   potential errors. To evaluate RPAs’ refusal capabilities, we constructed an evaluation benchmark
101   with queries designed to test various conflict scenarios. We also included non-conflicting queries to
102   assess whether RPAs would excessively refuse to answer. Our evaluation of state-of-the-art models,
103   including GPT-4 and Llama-3, revealed significant differences in their abilities to identify conflicts
104
      and refuse to answer across different scenarios. Notably, even advanced models showed unsatisfac-
      tory performance when dealing with queries conflicting with role parametric knowledge.
105
106   To understand these performance gap, we analyzed model representations under different conflict
107   scenarios(Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). This analysis revealed
      the existence of rejection regions and direct response regions within the model’s representation

      space. Queries near the direct response region tend to elicit direct answers, even when conflicting
109   with the model’s knowledge, while queries near the rejection region trigger refusal strategies.

      2.1   ROLE -P LAYING AGENTS
121
122   RPAs have garnered significant attention for their ability to simulate diverse personas, enhanc-
123   ing human-computer interaction in applications like virtual assistants and storytelling (Chen et al.,
124   2024b). Existing research on RPAs primarily addresses two key challenges: (1) improving the role-
125   playing capabilities of models; (2) evaluating the effectiveness of these role-playing performances.
126
      Enhancing Role-Playing Performance. Methods to improve RPAs are broadly categorized into
127
      prompt-based and fine-tuning-based approaches. Prompt-based methods provide models with de-
128   tailed character descriptions, outlining attributes such as age, personality, and abilities, to facilitate
129   accurate role-playing (Wang et al., 2023c; Zhou et al., 2023). Fine-tuning-based methods involve
130   training models on role-specific behaviors, often using data sourced from manual annotations (Zhou
131   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b), online resources(Zheng et al., 2019; Qian et al.,
132   2021; Song et al., 2020; Shao et al., 2023; Tu et al., 2024), or generated by LLMs (Wang et al.,
133   2023c; Li et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023a; Ahn et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). These methods aim to
134   instill role-consistent behaviors and dialogue patterns in the models.
135   Evaluating Role-Playing Capabilities. Evaluating role-playing performance is crucial for assess-
136   ing effectiveness and guiding improvements. Considering the complexity and comprehensiveness
137   of character personas, evaluation often encompasses multiple dimensions. Tu et al. (2024) propose
138   evaluating from 13 dimensions. Moreover, Yuan et al. (2024) propose the Motivation Recognition
139   Task to assess the model’s understanding and knowledge of characters through descriptions. Ahn
140   et al. (2024) and Sadeq et al. (2024) focus on evaluating hallucination issues in role-play models,
141   especially temporal hallucinations. Wang et al. (2023b) assess the personality of role-play mod-
142
      els through interviews. Chen et al. (2024a) systematically evaluate the sociality of RPAs at both
      individual and group levels.
143
144   Unlike previous work, we primarily focus on enhancing and evaluating the refusal capabilities of
145   RPAs. Also, to ensure that enhancing the refusal ability does not compromise their general role-
146   playing performance, we evaluate their general conversational skills and role-playing abilities.
147
148
      2.2   K NOWLEDGE B OUNDARIES AND R EFUSAL S TRATEGIES
149
150   Understanding and managing knowledge boundaries in RPAs is crucial for reliable and accurate
151   interactions. Prior work distinguishes between contextual knowledge, provided in the input context,
152   and parametric knowledge, inherent in the model’s parameters (Xu et al., 2024b).
153
      Parameteric Knowledge. Yang et al. (2023) and Cheng et al. (2024) explore teaching models to ex-
154
      press uncertainty using prompt-based, fine-tuning, and preference-aware optimization methods. Xu
155
      et al. (2024a) propose a reinforcement learning method based on knowledge feedback to dynamically
156   determine the model’s knowledge boundaries. Similarly, Zhang et al. (2024a) identifies knowledge
157   gaps between pre-trained parameters and instruction-tuning data, constructing refusal-aware data
158   by appending uncertainty expressions and improving the model’s ability to answer known ques-
159   tions while refusing unknown ones. Chen et al. (2024c) detect the knowledge boundaries of LLMs
160   through internal confidence and teach LLMs to recognize and express these boundaries. Zhao et al.
161   (2023b) propose a self-detection scheme to identify unknown knowledge by examining behavioral
      differences under varying formulations and the atypicality of input expressions. To address factual


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      errors and outdated knowledge in parameterized knowledge, mainstream methods convert parame-
163   terized knowledge into contextual knowledge.
164
165   Contextual Knowledge. Cao (2023) use an independent structured knowledge base to represent
166
      the knowledge scope of LLMs, making LLMs process input-output data without relying on internal
      knowledge, thereby avoiding misinformation. Prompting LLMs to refuse to answer difficult ques-
167
      tions improves system reliability. Deng et al. (2024) generate extensive unknown question-response
168
      data through class-aware self-augmentation and select qualified data via differential-driven self-
169   curation, fine-tuning LLMs to improve their response capabilities to various unknown questions,
170   enabling the model to refuse and explain why it cannot answer. Brahman et al. (2024) categorize
171   scenarios requiring refusal to answer, and explore different training strategies to teach models to say
172   “no.” Zhao et al. (2024) investigate decision boundaries in in-context learning by analyzing decision
173   boundaries in binary classification tasks.
174
      Although previous studies have explored the knowledge boundaries of models, there is still a lack
175   of in-depth research specifically on the knowledge boundaries of RPAs. To address this gap, we
176   systematically evaluated the ability of RPAs to recognize and refuse queries that conflict with their
177   role knowledge, thereby investigating their knowledge boundaries. Subsequently, we proposed a
178   representation editing approach that enhances their refusal capabilities without compromising their
179   general role-playing performance.

183   We first introduce the scenarios where RPAs should refuse to answer. Then, based on the scenarios
184   requiring refusal, we construct our dataset RoleRef (Role-playing agents Refuse to answer). Finally,
185   we propose an evaluation framework to comprehensively measure the role-playing capabilities of
186   RPAs, with a particular emphasis on how they refuse inappropriate or irrelevant questions.

188   3.1   S CENARIO D ESIGN
189
190   RPAs typically derive their knowledge from two main sources in responding to user queries. One
191
      source is the contextual knowledge provided by the role descriptions within the context, and the
      other is the parametric knowledge acquired during the model’s pre-training phase(Xu et al., 2024b).
192
193   Contextual Knowledge Conflicts. We devised two refusal scenarios involving conflicts with con-
194   textual knowledge:
195
             • Role Setting Conflict: The user’s query goes beyond the setting scope of role profile. For
196
               example, when interacting with an RPA that playing the role of Gandalf, the user queries:
197
               “Why was Aunt Petunia dyeing Dudley’s old clothes gray instead of buying new ones for
198            Harry Potter?”, where “Harry Potter” contradicts with the main setting “Gandalf”.
199
             • Role Profile Conflict: The user’s query is in accordance with the role profile, however, it
200
               violates specific content within the role profile. For instance, when interacting with an RPA
201
               whose role profile states “While Gandalf is powerful, he is not omnipotent.” the user asks:
202            “Gandalf, how can I become as omnipotent as you?”
203
204   Parametric Knowledge Conflicts. Similarly, we considered two refusal scenarios involving con-
205   flicts with parametric knowledge:
206
             • Role’s Factual Knowledge Conflict: The user’s query contains false information. For ex-
207
               ample, the user asks Gandalf: “Gandalf, how did you manage to evade the Black Riders
208            using invisibility spells during the journey to Weathertop?”. While in fact, the invisibility
209            spells were not actually used in the story.
210
             • Role’s Absent Knowledge Conflict: The character was not present when a specific event
211
               occurred. For example, when interacting with an RPA playing the role of Gandalf, the user
212            asks: “Were you there at the moment when Goldberry bid farewell to Frodo and his friends,
213            blessing their journey as they departed on their ponies?”.
214
215   Additionally, to verify the role-playing ability of RPAs in non-conflict scenarios, we designed non-
      conflict scenarios where the user’s query aligns with role’s knowledge.

      3.2   DATA C ONSTRUCTION
217
218   We created the RoleRef dataset, which expands upon the existing TIMECHARA (Ahn et al., 2024).
219   We generate queries based on reference content and then generate corresponding responses. Af-
220   terward, we use automated filtering methods to process the data. Finally, we randomly sample the
221   filtered data for manual verification.
222   Step 1: Generating Queries and Responses. For generating queries and their corresponding re-
223   sponses, we utilize GPT-4o for data synthesis.
224
225
      For generating queries in scenarios involving role profile conflicts, we utilize atomic knowledge
      derived from role profiles to create queries and responses(Sadeq et al., 2024). Initially, we used
226
      Wikipedia as a reference to generate role profiles. These role profiles are then broken down into
227
      multiple atomic pieces of knowledge. For each piece of atomic knowledge, we provide a seed(Sadeq
228   et al., 2024) to generate fake queries. Using the atomic knowledge and the seed, we prompt the
229   model to generate fake queries, refusal responses, and reference justifications.
230
231
      For queries involving role setting conflicts, we randomly sample from non-conflict queries of differ-
      ent series roles and prompt the model to generate corresponding refusal responses.
232
233   For scenarios involving conflicts with parameterized knowledge, we use the original novels related
234   to the roles as references to generate summaries at first. Based on these summaries, we then create
235   queries and responses (Yuan et al., 2024). Specifically, we first utilize the novels associated with
236   the roles as reference texts. Since the text length of novels often exceeds 128k, surpassing many
237
      LLMs’ context window limits, we divide the original novel content into multiple segments. For each
      segment, we prompt the model to generate a summary of that portion. To generate fake queries, we
238
      also provide a seed for creating these fake queries and their responses.
239
240   For generating non-conflict queries, we directly prompt the model to generate queries and responses
241   based on the summary content. Additionally, for each query, we require the model to provide the
242   corresponding reference information. The prompts we used are shown in Appendix B.

      attend in the original story).

323   knowledge conflicts. This discrepancy highlights the need to enhance models’ ability to recognize

      To understand why models perform differently in contextual and parametric knowledge conflict
343   scenarios, we conducted an in-depth analysis of the models’ internal representations using linear
344   probing and t-SNE visualization techniques.
345
346   5.1   A NALYSIS VIA L INEAR P ROBES
347
348   Previous work has shown that the internal states of LLMs can reveal the model’s knowledge about
349   query truthfulness (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023; Ji et al., 2024). Building on this, we used linear probes
350   to investigate whether models can distinguish between queries that should be refused and those that
351   should be answered. The detailed procedure of probe training is provided in Appendix C.2. The
352   results, shown in Figure 2, reveal following insight:
353   Models exhibit a keen awareness of contextual conflicts but struggle with parametric knowl-

      In this section, we aim to address RQ3: How can we enhance RPAs’ ability to respond to conflicting
      queries without compromising their general role-playing capabilities? Building on our findings
421
      from Section 5.2, which revealed distinct regions in the representation space for refusal and direct
422
      responses, we apply a representation-editing method to improve the model’s ability to identify and
423   refuse conflicting queries.
424
425
      6.1   R EPRESENTATION E DITING M ETHOD
426
427   The representation-editing approach is a lightweight method that enables a model to refuse to an-
428   swer without requiring additional model training. This method adopts an interpretability perspective
429   (Zou et al., 2023), where the refusal representation is activated when the model declines to answer,
430   thus aiding in the refusal process. By identifying the representations related to refusal within the
431   model and intervening in the model’s original representations using these refusal representations,
      the model’s ability to refuse can be enhanced. In this paper, we adopt the representation-editing


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      method proposed by Li et al. (2024) to intervene in the model’s representations. Specifically, this
433   method consists of three steps.See the Appendix C.3 for more detailed process of the representation
434   editing method.
435
436
      Step 1: Collecting activation. For each role, we first construct a batch of conflicting queries and
      non-conflicting queries. For conflicting queries, we use queries with conflicting roles. When the
437
      model responds to such conflicting queries, it can accurately identify the conflict and make corre-
438
      sponding rejections. When the model responds to non-conflicting queries, the rejection mechanism
439   is often not triggered. Therefore, in both cases, we collect the internal representation of the last
440   token position for each query, capturing both the model’s rejection and non-rejection states.
441
442
      Step 2: Identifying the rejection direction. Using the collected representations, we compute the
      difference between conflicting and non-conflicting query representations to isolate the rejection-
443
      related features. We then calculate the cluster center of these difference vectors, termed as “rejection
444
      direction.” To refine this direction, we compute the variance across the difference vectors and zero
445   out components with high variance, focusing on the most consistent rejection-related features.
446
447   Step 3: Steering activation. With the identified rejection direction, we intervene in the model’s
448
      processing of new queries. We compute the similarity between the query’s representation and the
      rejection direction. If the similarity exceeds a threshold, indicating a likely conflicting query, we
449
      adjust the query’s representation by adding a scaled version of the rejection direction. This steers
450
      the query’s representation towards the refusal region of the representation space, encouraging the
451   model to reject inappropriate queries.

536   dealing with parametric knowledge conflicts. Through analysis of model representations, we uncov-

      A    E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
703
704
      Inspired by Tu et al. (2024), we have expanded our evaluation framework beyond just assessing the
705
      refusal ability of RPAs. Our comprehensive framework evaluates three key capabilities of RPAs:
706
      general conversational ability, role-playing ability, and refusal ability.
707
708   Evaluation of General Conversation Ability. General conversation ability is the foundational
709   capability of RPAs. Assessing the general conversation ability of role-playing models is crucial
710
      because it directly impacts the user experience and satisfaction during interactions with the model.
      General conversation ability includes consistency, quality, and factuality, which collectively deter-
711
      mine the fluency, depth, and accuracy of the conversation (Mesgar et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021;
712
      Tu et al., 2024).
713
714         • Consistency of Response: The consistency of response refers to the model’s ability to pro-
715           vide replies that are coherent with the context and the query.
716
717         • Quality of Response: The quality of response involves the depth, richness, and creativity of
718           the replies. High-quality responses can enhance user experience and drive the conversation
719
              forward.
720         • Factuality of Response: Ensuring that the information provided in the replies is accurate
721           and truthful.
722
723   Evaluation of Role-Playing Ability. Role-playing ability directly influences the user experience
724   with RPAs. We aim for the model to maintain its role-playing ability even when refusing to answer.
725   We measure the role-playing ability of RPAs across four dimensions:
726
727         • Alignment with Role Background: This dimension assesses whether the content of the
728           replies is faithful to the character’s background and history. The background knowledge
729           defines the character’s basic behavior patterns and historical context, making it essential to
730
              ensure the consistency and credibility of the character’s actions and speech.
731         • Alignment with Role Style: This dimension evaluates whether the replies conform to the
732           character’s expression and behavior style. The role style reflects the character’s unique
733           traits, and maintaining a consistent style across different contexts helps preserve the char-
734           acter’s distinct appeal and recognizability.
735
            • Alignment with Role Personality: This dimension focuses on whether the content of the
736
              replies reflects the character’s personality traits. The character’s personality includes its
737           emotional responses and attitudes. Replies that exhibit the character’s personality can
738           highlight its unique behavior patterns, enhancing the realism and dimensionality of the
739           character.
740
741         • Alignment with Role Abilities: The final dimension examines whether the replies demon-
742
              strate the character’s abilities and skills. The character’s abilities determine its actions and
              approaches to problem-solving in specific contexts. Ensuring that the character can effec-
743
              tively handle various challenges makes its portrayal more credible and reliable.
744
745
      Evaluation of Refusal Ability. The expected model responses to different categories of refusal
746
      queries vary, ranging from directly refusing to answer to recognizing potential errors in the query.
747
      To better assess these different categories of refusal queries, we evaluate them from two aspects:
748
749
            • Refusal to Answer Judgment: Determining whether the model directly refuses to answer in
750           its replies.
751
752         • Awareness of False: Evaluating whether the model recognizes potential errors in the query
753           and takes appropriate response.
754
755   To assess RPAs’ performance across these dimensions, we use GPT-4o to score them. The specific
      scoring criteria for each dimension can be found in Appendix B
