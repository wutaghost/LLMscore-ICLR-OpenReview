009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training certifiably robust neural networks is an important but challenging task.
012            While many algorithms for (deterministic) certified training have been proposed,
013            they are often evaluated on different training schedules, certification methods, and
014            systematically under-tuned hyperparameters, making it difficult to compare their
015            performance. To address this challenge, we introduce CTB ENCH, a unified library
016            and a high-quality benchmark for certified training that evaluates all algorithms
017            under fair settings and systematically tuned hyperparameters. We show that (1)
018            almost all algorithms in CTB ENCH surpass the corresponding reported performance
019
               in literature in the magnitude of algorithmic improvements, thus establishing
               new state-of-the-art, and (2) the claimed advantage of recent algorithms drops
020
               significantly when we enhance the outdated baselines with a fair training schedule,
021
               a fair certification method and well-tuned hyperparameters. Based on CTB ENCH,
022            we provide new insights into the current state of certified training and suggest future
023            research directions. We are confident that CTB ENCH will serve as a benchmark
024            and testbed for future research in certified training.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      As a crucial component of trustworthy artificial intelligence, adversarial robustness (Szegedy et al.,
030   2014; Goodfellow et al., 2015), i.e., resilience to small input perturbations, has established itself
031   as an important research area. While initially the community focused on heuristic methods to craft
032   adversarial examples and defenses against these, it turned out that such defenses are often brittle
033   and can be evaded by adaptive adversaries (Athalye et al., 2018; Tramèr et al., 2020). Thus, neural
034   network certification has emerged as a method for providing provable guarantees on the robustness of
035   a given network (Gehr et al., 2018; Wong & Kolter, 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Singh et al., 2019).
036   Two families of neural network certification methods have been proposed: complete methods (Katz
037   et al., 2017; Tjeng et al., 2019) which compute the exact bounds but are extremely computationally
038   expensive, and convex-relaxation based methods (Zhang et al., 2018; Singh et al., 2019) which
039   provide approximate bounds but are more scalable. State-of-the-art (SOTA) verifiers (Xu et al., 2021;
040   Ferrari et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022) combine both approaches, by using convex relaxations to
041   speed up the solving of complete methods via Branch-and-Bound (Bunel et al., 2020).
042   However, the scalability of neural network certification is still a major challenge since the compu-
043   tational complexity of SOTA verifiers grows exponentially with network size. To tackle this issue,
044   certified training (Mirman et al., 2018; Gowal et al., 2018) was proposed in order to train neural
045   networks that are amenable to certification. Such methods are typically categorized into two groups:
046   (1) training with a sound upper bound of the robust loss (Zhang et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2021), and (2)
047   training with an unsound surrogate loss that aims to approximate the exact robust loss (Müller et al.,
048   2023; Mao et al., 2023; De Palma et al., 2024). The latter group has been shown to be more effective.
049   While certified training has made significant advances, there is currently no benchmark that can be
050   used to fairly evaluate the effectiveness of the different certified training methods. Specifically, the
051   literature often compares against previous methods using quoted numbers due to high computational
052   costs, although the verifier and certification budget differ. These unfair comparisons ultimately
053   hinder the community from drawing reasonable conclusions on the effectiveness of certified training
      methods. In addition, existing works systematically under-tune hyperparameters, in order to show

079   Benchmarking Certified Robustness Li et al. (2023) provides the first benchmark for certified
080   robustness, covering not only deterministic certified training but also randomized certified training
081   and certification methods. However, it is outdated and thus provides little insight into the current
082
      SOTA methods. For example, it reports 89% and 51% best certified accuracy for MNIST ϵ = 0.3
                           2
      and CIFAR-10 ϵ = 255   in its evaluation, respectively, while recent methods have achieved more than
083
      93% and 62% (Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023; De Palma et al., 2024).
084
085
      Certified Training D IFF AI (Mirman et al., 2018) and IBP (Gowal et al., 2018) apply box relaxation
086
      to upper bound the worst-case loss for training. Efforts have been made towards applying more
087
      precise approximations: Wong et al. (2018) and Balunovic & Vechev (2020) apply D EEP Z (Singh
088   et al., 2018), while Zhang et al. (2020) incorporate linear relaxations (Zhang et al., 2018; Singh

090   worse training results, attributed to non-smoothness (Lee et al., 2021), discontinuity and sensitivity
091   (Jovanović et al., 2022) of the loss surface. Some recent work (Balauca et al., 2024) aim to mitigate
092   these problems, however, the most effective training approximation is still the least precise box
093   relaxation. In this regard, the focus of the community has shifted towards improving IBP: Shi et al.
094   (2021) propose a new regularization and initialization paradigm to speed up IBP training; De Palma
095   et al. (2022) apply IBP regularization to make adversarial training certifiable; Müller et al. (2023),
096
      Mao et al. (2023) and De Palma et al. (2024) propose unsound but more effective IBP-based surrogate
      losses for training; Mao et al. (2024) propose to use wider models instead of deeper models for
097
      IBP-based methods. These methods achieve universal advantages and are thus the focus of our work.

104   3.1   T RAINING FOR ROBUSTNESS
105
106   We present the mathematical notations on adversarial and certified training here. We consider a neural
107   network classifier fθ (x) that estimates the log-probability of each class and predicts the class with
      the highest estimated log-probability.

      Adversarial Training A classifier fθ (x) is said to be adversarially robust with radius ϵ w.r.t.
      L∞ perturbation if fθ (x + δ) = y for all ∥δ∥∞ ≤ ϵ, where y is the ground truth label of x. For
121
      brevity, we will omit the perturbation type in the rest of the paper. Finding an adversarially robust
122
      classifier is formally defined to solve a min-max problem θ = arg minθ Ex,y max∥δ∥≤ϵ L(x + δ). In
123   this regard, adversarial training solves the inner maximization problem by generating adversarial
124   examples during training, and the outer minimization problem by optimizing the empirical loss of
125   adversarial examples.
126
127   Certified Training A classifier fθ (x) is said to be certifiably robust if it is adversarially robust
128   and there exists a sound verifier that certifies the robustness. A verifier typically computes an upper
129   bound on the margin fi (x + δ) − fy (x + δ) and certifies its robustness if the upper bound is negative
130   for all i ̸= y. Certified training thus replaces the inner maximization problem with an upper bound
131   and minimizes the upper bound during training instead.
132
133   Metrics The main metric for certified training is certified accuracy, defined to be the ratio of
134   certifiably robust samples in the dataset. The ratio of correctly classified samples in the dataset is
135
      thus called natural accuracy. For reference, we include adversarial accuracy as well, defined to
      be the ratio of adversarially robust samples in the dataset. We apply one of the most widely used
136
      SOTA certification methods, MN-BA B (Ferrari et al., 2022), as the verifier. To compute adversarial
137
      accuracy, we apply the strong AUTOATTACK (Croce & Hein, 2020) for adversarial training, and a
138   combination of PGD attack and branch-and-bound attack from MN-BA B for certified training.

      3.2   A LGORITHMS IN CTB ENCH
141
142   Here, we briefly introduce the core algorithms built into CTB ENCH. Concepts behind these algorithms
143   are visualized in Figure 2.
144
145   PGD and EDAC Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry et al., 2018) is the most widely
146   recognized adversarial training method. Starting from a random initialization, PGD solves the inner
147   maximization problem by iteratively taking a step towards the gradient direction and clipping the
148   result into the valid perturbation set. Then, it uses the generated adversarial input x′ to compute the
149   worst case loss as L(x′ ). Croce & Hein (2020) find that PGD remains effective against strong attacks,
150
      thus is popular as an integrated part of many certified training methods (Müller et al., 2023; Mao
      et al., 2023; De Palma et al., 2024). To further improve adversarial robustness, Zhang et al. (2023)
151
      improves adversarial generalization via an extra-gradient method called EDAC, which remains one of
152
      the SOTA methods in adversarial training. These methods achieve good but uncertifiable adversarial
153   robustness, hence we use them as adversarial robustness baselines in CTB ENCH.
154
155
      IBP Interval Bound Propagation (IBP) (Gowal et al., 2018) uses interval analysis to approximate
156   the output range of each layer. For example, for the toy network y = 2 − ReLU(x1 + x2 ) with
157   input bounds x1 , x2 ∈ [−1, 1], it first computes the output range of the first layer as x1 + x2 ∈
158   [−1, 1] + [−1, 1] ⊆ [−2, 2], the second layer as ReLU([−2, 2]) ⊆ [0, 2] and then final layer as
159   2 − [0, 2] ⊆ [0, 2], thus proving y ≥ 0 for all possible x1 , x2 ∈ [−1, 1]. Similarly, IBP computes the
160   layer-wise bounds and then derives the worst-case loss based on the output bounds of the final layer.
161   To stably train models with IBP, Shi et al. (2021) propose to rescale the parameter initialization
      to ensure constant growth of IBP bounds and a specialized regularization to control the activation


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      status of neurons. They also show that adding a batch norm (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) layer before
163   every ReLU can improve IBP training. These training tricks are adopted by every IBP-based method
164   introduced below. For brevity, we refer to this variant as IBP in the rest of the paper unless otherwise
165   stated, since it improves the original IBP universally with tricks that facilitate training.
166
167   CROWN-IBP CROWN-IBP (Zhang et al., 2020) tightens the imprecise interval analysis with
168   linear relaxations of ReLU layers based on IBP bounds and only solves the linear constraints for the
169   final layer output based on CROWN (Zhang et al., 2018), avoiding prohibitive costs during training.
170   To further reduce the cost of solving the bounds for each class, Xu et al. (2020) propose a loss fusion
171   trick to only solve for the final loss, thus reducing the asymptotic complexity by a factor equal to
172   the number of classes. For brevity, we refer to this variant as CROWN-IBP in the rest of the paper
173
      unless otherwise stated, since the original CROWN-IBP cannot scale to datasets with many classes.
174
175   SABR Since IBP is often criticized for the increasingly strong regularization w.r.t. input radius
176
      imposed on the neural network, SABR (Müller et al., 2023) proposes to use IBP only for a carefully
      chosen small box inside the original input box for IBP training. More specifically, it first conducts a
177
      PGD attack in the full input box to find an adversarial input, and then takes the surrounding small box
178
      with radius λϵ around the adversarial input as the input box for IBP training, where λ is a pre-defined
179   ratio. For exceptional cases (specifically CIFAR-10 ϵ = 255 2
                                                                      ), SABR further shrinks the output box
180   of every ReLU towards zero by a pre-defined constant to further reduce the regularization.
181
182   TAPS and STAPS Observing that IBP relaxation error grows exponentially w.r.t. model depth
183   (Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2024), TAPS (Mao et al., 2023) proposes to split the network into two
184   subparts, using IBP for the first subpart and PGD for the other. This way, the over-approximation
185   from IBP and the under-approximation from PGD partially cancel out, yielding a more precise
186   approximation of the worst-case loss. Further, TAPS uses a separate PGD attack to estimate the
187   bounds for every class to align better with the certification objective. STAPS (Mao et al., 2023)
188   combines TAPS with SABR by using the adversarial small box for TAPS training, thus further
189
      reducing regularization.
190
191
      MTL-IBP De Palma et al. (2024) formalizes a family of surrogate loss functions that interpolate
      between PGD and IBP training. We study MTL-IBP, one of the most effective algorithms in this
192
      family. MTL-IBP linearly interpolates between PGD loss and IBP loss, i.e., L = (1 − τ )LPGD +
193
      τ LIBP , where τ is the pre-defined IBP coefficient. To recover the re-weighing between PGD and
194   IBP as SABR does with box shrinking, MTL-IBP uses a larger input radius for a PGD attack in the
195                                              2
      same setting (specifically, CIFAR-10 ϵ = 255  ).

203   4.1    T HE CTB ENCH LIBRARY
204
205   We implement every algorithm described in Section 3.2 in a unified framework. The training loss
206   is composed of three components: the natural loss which measures performance on clean inputs,
207
      the robust loss which measures robust performance depending on the concrete algorithms and
      regularization losses which are used to stabilize training and improve generalization. Formally, the
208
      training loss is defined as L = (1 − wrob )Lnat + wrob Lrob + Lreg . We mainly use L1 regularization to
209
      reduce overfitting and the warmup regularization proposed by Shi et al. (2021) to improve certified
210   training methods. The IBP initialization (Shi et al., 2021) is applied for every certified training
211   method, while adversarial training is initialized with Kaiming uniform (He et al., 2015). Every
212   method has a warmup phase where ϵ is increased from 0 to the target value and a fine-tuning phase
213   where the model continues to train at the targeted ϵ to converge. The learning rate is held constant
214   during the warmup phase and decayed in the fine-tuning phase with a constant multiplier. We use
215   CNN7 as the model architecture, in agreement with recent literature (Shi et al., 2021; Müller et al.,
      2023; Mao et al., 2023; De Palma et al., 2024).

251   Due to the importance of batch norm in certified training, we consider it as a native part of CTB ENCH.
252   Specifically, the best practice so far is to set batch norm statistics based on the clean input and use
253   this for computing IBP bounds. However, we find several problematic implementations of batch
254   norm in the literature: (1) when gradient accumulation is involved, the batch norm statistics are not
255
      updated correctly, as sub-batch statistics are applied for training; (2) batch norm statistics change
      more than once before taking a gradient step, as typically running statistics is used for conducting a
256
      PGD attack and thus evaluating Lrob , while Lnat is evaluated with batch statistics. The first problem
257
      makes gradient accumulation ineffective since the quality of batch statistics depends highly on the
258   batch size, and the second problem prevents training with wrob ∈ (0, 1). To address the first problem,
259   we propose to use full batch statistics during gradient accumulation, which leads to slim overheads
260   but allows arbitrary gradient accumulation. To address the second problem, we conduct a PGD attack
261   with the batch statistics as well and evaluate everything with the current batch statistics. This way,
262   the batch norm statistics are set once per batch just like standard training, allowing training with the
263   combination of Lnat and Lrob . Further, Wu & Johnson (2021) find that running statistics lag behind
264   the population statistics and propose to use the population statistics for testing. We adopt this strategy
265   in CTB ENCH, since it only needs to compute Lnat and is much cheaper than the computation of Lrob .
266   We find that models trained with the hyperparameters reported in the literature frequently show strong
267   overfitting patterns. To remediate this, we conduct a magnitude search for L1 regularization until the
268   train and validation performance roughly match. To further aid generalization, we apply Stochastic
269   Weight Averaging (Izmailov et al., 2018) for methods that cannot provide metrics for model selection,
      e.g., MTL-IBP. A more detailed description of the implementation can be found in App. B.

      5.1   L OSS F RAGMENTATION
310
311
      ReLU networks are known to have a fragmented loss surface, due to the activation switch of neurons.
312   Fragmentation leads to a non-smooth loss surface and increases the difficulty of finding the worst-case
313   loss via gradient-based methods like PGD. Due to its connection to adversarial robustness, in this
314   section, we investigate the fragmentation of loss surfaces in certified models. Specifically, we answer:
315   (1) do certified models have less fragmentation, thus easing adversarial search, and (2) how does the
316   fragmentation change w.r.t. ϵ?
317
      Fragmentation is closely related to the number of unstable neurons, i.e., neurons that switch activation
318   status in the neighborhood, as all fragments are defined by a group of unstable neurons. Vice versa,
319   in most cases, a switching neuron introduces at least one fragmentation since every activation pattern
320   defines a local linear function. Therefore, we can quantify the fragmentation by the ratio of unstable
321   neurons. Since the exact ratio is NP-complete to compute, we use a heuristic but effective method
322   to estimate it: first, a group of inputs is sampled in the input box; second, these inputs are fed into
323   the model to get the corresponding activation pattern; finally, we count the ratio of unstable neurons
      observed in the sampled activations. This method always establishes a lower bound of the true ratio

414   5.4   R EGULARIZATION S TRENGTH
415
416   Previous work (Mao et al., 2024) has shown that IBP bounds are close to optimal bounds for
417
      IBP-based certified training, and this condition is established via strong constraints on the model
      parameters. They quantify this regularization effect by propagation tightness, defined to be the ratio
418
      between the optimal bound radius and the IBP bound radius, approximating the ReLU network locally
419
      with a linear replacement. We now extend the study of propagation tightness to more advanced
420   certified training methods and investigate how it interacts with certified accuracy. Specifically,
421   using propagation tightness as the representative of regularization strength, we answer: (1) do more
422   advanced certified training methods reduce the regularization strength, and (2) how does the input
423   radius ϵ affect the interaction?
424
      Figure 5 shows the interaction between certified accuracy and propagation tightness for certified
425
      models trained on MNIST and CIFAR-10. When ϵ is small (Figure 5a and Figure 5c), certified
426   accuracy has a negative correlation with propagation tightness, i.e., more advanced certified training
427   methods reduce the regularization strength. However, when ϵ is large (Figure 5b and Figure 5d), the
428   correlation is not clear, and the best model in certified accuracy does not necessarily have the lowest
429   propagation tightness. Instead, models with similar propagation tightness can have significantly
430   different certified accuracy. Therefore, we conclude that reducing regularization strength cleverly
431   is crucial for certified training, and the effect is more pronounced when ϵ is small, while improper
      reduction could hurt certified accuracy, especially when ϵ is large.

      5.5   O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION G ENERALIZATION
433
434   Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization is closely related to adversarial robustness (Gilmer et al.,
435   2019). However, the interaction between certified robustness and OOD generalization is not yet clear.
436   We thus investigate the OOD generalization of certified models and answer: (1) do certified models
437   generalize to OOD data, and (2) how does this compare to adversarial training?
438   We use MNIST-C (Mu & Gilmer, 2019) to evaluate OOD generalization, defined to be the ratio
439   between OOD accuracy and natural accuracy. MNIST-C includes 15 carefully chosen corruptions,
440   covering a broad range of corruptions that are not characterized by adversarial robustness while
441   preserving the semantics. We evaluate models trained with both adversarial training and certified
442   training under ϵ = 0.1 and ϵ = 0.3, and report the corresponding OOD accuracy of the model trained
443   via standard training. We note that none of the models has seen these corruptions during training.
444   Figure 6 depicts the result of OOD generalization for each model on all corruptions. We observe the
445   following: (1) certified training improves OOD generalization compared to standard training except
446   on the brightness corruption where both adversarial and certified training fails; (2) certified training
447   shows different OOD generalization patterns to adversarial training, e.g., certified training boost
448   generalization on the canny edges corruption while adversarial training wins on the stripe corruption.
449   In general, we find that certified training either greatly boosts the OOD generalization or significantly
450   downgrades the OOD generalization depending on the corruption, and the bad cases are usually those
451   in which adversarial training performs worse than or similarly to standard training. Therefore, we
452
      hypothesize that these corruptions are at odds with adversarial robustness. Further, different training
      ϵ does not significantly affect the OOD generalization except few cases, and ranking in certified
453
      accuracy does not show strong relations with the ranking in OOD generalization. Overall, these
454
      results suggest that certified training has the potential to improve OOD generalization to corruptions
455   that standard training struggles with, and the effect is exaggerated when adversarial training improves
456   over standard training. More results on CIFAR-10-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019) are included in
457   App. C as Figure 9.

      6     F UTURE D IRECTIONS
461
      We now summarize directions for future improvements of certified training and its potential appli-
462
      cations. As shown in Section 5.2, certified models make shared mistakes on some hard samples,
463   thus curriculum learning with some well-defined difficulty ranking could facilitate training, where
464   optimization has been known to be particularly hard (Jovanović et al., 2022). Moreover, in Section 5.3
465   we showed that even the most trainable method, IBP, struggles to keep necessary model utilization
466   on large ϵ. Therefore, future work is still required to improve the learning process of certified
467   training. Despite the challenges, in Section 5.5 we find that certified models can have surprising and
468   qualitatively different behavior on OOD generalization, which could be a promising application for
469   certified training beyond certified robustness.

705   A.1    D ECOMPOSITION OF I MPROVEMENTS
706
707   Decomposition of the universal modifications we made such as batch norm fixes and the hyperparam-
708   eter tuning is not always possible, as these modifications allow additional vectors of hyperparameter
709
      for tuning. For example, we fix batch norm statistics in one batch rather than reset it multiple times as
      done in some original implementations, allowing wrob to be tuned within [0, 1], while in the literature
710
      wrob has to be fixed to 1. Therefore, we cannot formally decompose the effects of hyperparameter
711
      tuning and batch norm behaviors, as they are closely dependent on each other.
712
713   The PreciseBN (Wu & Johnson, 2021) that we adopt, which is to set batch norm statistics based on
714   the entire training dataset at test time, does not change the training at all, since at every training step
715
      batch norm layers are set by batch statistics. Therefore, this only smooths the test time performance
      and potentially improves the final performance. While this is good for monitoring the learning
716
      curve, the final performance improvement is minimal in our experiments, and in most cases almost
717
      no improvement on the final model is observed. This is expected since batch norm statistics also
718   converge when the model converges.
719
720   The literature results are run with three different random seeds, and only the best results among
721
      them are reported. This prevents us from substituting our fine-tuned hyperparameter to the original
      implementation because merely using the same hyperparameters even based on the original imple-
722
      mentation hardly reproduces the same number as reported in the literature. In contrast, we run every
723
      experiment with the same fixed random seed to allow fair and faithful comparison. Nevertheless, we
724   can showcase the effect for one setting: IBP on MNIST ϵ = 0.3. The literature reports 93.1% certified
725   accuracy, while the same hyperparameter results in 93.18% in our implementation. Further tuning the
726   hyperparameters as in the CTBench benchmark gets 93.8%. While this proves the effectiveness of
727   both the implementation and our hyperparameter tuning, we would like to note that based on previous
728   arguments, this does not faithfully decompose the effect of hyperparameter tuning and batch norm
729   changes, and such decomposition efforts are doomed to fail.
730   In summary, while decomposition is beneficial, there are practical concerns preventing us from
731   formally decomposing the effects. However, since this work introduces a library and benchmark
732   rather than precisely decomposing the effect of each beneficial change, this does not undermine the
733   contribution of this work.
734
735
736   A.2    L IMITATIONS
737
738   The main limitation of CTB ENCH is that we only consider deterministic certified training, while
739   randomized certified robustness (Cohen et al., 2019) has also made substantial progress. Moreover,
740   we only consider the adversarial robustness, while other types of robustness, such as robustness
741
      against patch attacks (Salman et al., 2022) is also important. Finally, we only focus on L∞ robustness,
      and leave the discussion about other norms as future work.
742
743
744   A.3    B ROADER I MPACTS
745
746   This work focuses on certified defenses against adversarial attacks, which is a crucial component of
747   trustworthy artificial intelligence. The proposed benchmark CTB ENCH will facilitate future research
748   on certified training and its applications. The insights and tools provided by CTB ENCH will help
749   researchers to develop more robust and reliable machine learning models. The potential harm of this
750   work are as follows:
751
752   • Certified models can provide a fake security when the models are applied against non-adversarial
753     perturbations.
754
755   • Certification methods are computationally expensive, which will consume more energy and thus
        possibly harm the environment.

      to 64 × 64 after applying a 4 pixel zero padding at every margin for T INY I MAGE N ET. We train on

      B.2   M ODEL A RCHITECTURES
770
771   We follow Shi et al. (2021); Müller et al. (2023) and use a CNN7 with Batch Norm for our main
772   experiments. CNN7 is a convolutional network with 7 convolutional and linear layers. All but the last
773   linear layer are followed by a Batch Norm and ReLU layer. This architecture is found to achieve
774   uniformly better results across settings (Shi et al., 2021), and thus is adopted by the literature (Shi
775   et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023; De Palma et al., 2024). For T INY I MAGE N ET, the
776   stride of the last convolution is doubled to reduce the cost.
