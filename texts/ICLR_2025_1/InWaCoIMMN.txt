009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Despite the recent successes of large language models (LLMs), little is known
012
               regarding the representations of linguistic structure they learn during pretraining,
013
               which can lead to unexpected behaviors in response to prompt variation or dis-
014            tribution shift. To better understand these models and behaviors, we introduce
015            a general model analysis framework to study LLMs with respect to their repre-
016            sentation and use of human-interpretable linguistic properties. Our framework,
017            CALM (Competence-based Analysis of Language Models), is designed to inves-
018            tigate LLM competence in the context of specific tasks by intervening on models’
019            internal representations of different linguistic properties using causal probing, and
020            measuring models’ alignment under these interventions with a given ground-truth
021            causal model of the task. We also develop a new approach for performing causal
022
               probing interventions using gradient-based adversarial attacks, which can target a
               broader range of properties and representations than prior techniques. Finally, we
023
               carry out a case study of CALM using these interventions to analyze and compare
024
               LLM competence across a variety of lexical inference tasks, showing that CALM
025            can be used to explain and predict behaviors across these tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The rise of large, pretrained neural language models (LLMs) has led to rapid progress in a wide
032   variety of natural language processing tasks Brown et al. (2020); Chowdhery et al. (2022); Dubey
033   et al. (2024). However, these models can also be quite sensitive to minor changes in input prompts
034   Elazar et al. (2021a); Moradi & Samwald (2021); Mizrahi et al. (2024) and fail to generalize outside
035   their training or fine-tuning distribution Wang et al. (2023a); Yang et al. (2023). It is usually unclear
036   where these limitations come from, as LLM task performance is generally studied using only “black
037   box” behavioral analysis, in which case one can only detect limitations that are adequately repre-
038   sented by the benchmark, which cannot cover every possible limitation using a finite dataset Raji
039
      et al. (2021); Siska et al. (2024). Understanding the means by which these models can perform as
      well as they do while exhibiting such limitations is a key question in the science of LLM interpreta-
040
      tion and analysis Bereska & Gavves (2024), and is likely necessary in enabling robust, trustworthy,
041
      and socially-responsible LLM-enabled applications Shin (2021); Liao & Vaughan (2023); Zou et al.
042   (2023); Bereska & Gavves (2024).
043
044   We approach this question in terms of competence, drawing on the traditional competence-
045
      performance distinction in linguistic theory (see Section 2.1) to motivate the study of LLMs in
      terms of their underlying representation of language. We define LLM competence in the context a
046
      given linguistic task as the alignment between the ground-truth causal structure of the task and the
047
      LLM’s latent representation of the task’s structure, measured by intervening on the LLM’s represen-
048   tation of task-causal or non-causal properties and observing how its behavior changes in response.
049   While such representations are not directly observable, we take inspiration from causal probing,
050   which damages LLMs’ latent representations of linguistic properties using causal interventions to
051   study how these representations contribute to their behavior Elazar et al. (2021b); Lasri et al. (2022).
052   We introduce a general framework, CALM (for Competence-based Analysis of Language Models),
053   to study the competence of LLMs using causal probing and define the first quantitative measure of
      LLM competence.

      While CALM can be instantiated using a variety of existing causal probing interventions (e.g., Rav-
055   fogel et al., 2020; 2022b;a; Shao et al., 2022; Belrose et al., 2024), we develop a new intervention
056   methodology for damaging LLM representations using gradient-based adversarial attacks against
057   structural probes, extending causal probing to arbitrarily-encoded representations of relational prop-
058   erties and thereby enabling the investigation of new questions in language model interpretation. We
059   carry out a case study of CALM using two well-studied LLMs by implementing interventions as
060   GBIs in order to measure and compare these LLMs’ competence across 14 lexical inference tasks,
061   showing that CALM can indeed explain and predict important patterns in behavior across these
062   tasks by distinguishing between models’ use of causal and spurious properties.
063   Our primary contributions are as follows:
064

067         2. We provide a causal formulation of linguistic competence in the context of LLMs, using

071

080   2.1   L INGUISTIC C OMPETENCE
081
082
      Linguistic competence is generally understood as the ability to utilize one’s knowledge of a language
      in producing and understanding utterances in that language, and is typically defined in contrast
083
      with linguistic performance, which is speakers’ actual use of their language in practice, considered
084
      independently of the underlying knowledge that supports it Marconi (2020).1 Given a linguistic task,
085   we may understand competence in terms of the underlying linguistic knowledge that one draws upon
086   to perform the task. If fluent human speakers rely on (implicit or explicit) knowledge of the same
087   set of linguistic properties to perform a given task, then we may understand their performance of
088   this task as being causally determined by these properties, and invariant to other properties. For
089   example, if we consider the two utterances “the chicken crosses the road” and “the chickens cross
090   the road”, the grammatical number of the subject (i.e., singular and plural, respectively) determines
091   whether the verb “(to) cross” should be conjugated as “crosses” or “cross”. As English (root) verb
092   conjugation always depends on the grammatical number of the subject, grammatical number may
093
      be regarded as having a causal role in the task of English verb conjugation, so we may understand
      fluent English speakers’ (usually implicit) mental representation of verb tense as having a causal
094
      role in their behavior. In this work, we focus on lexicosemantic competence, the ability to utilize
095
      knowledge of word meaning relationships in performing tasks such as lexical inference Marconi
096   (1997; 2020).
097
098   While the study of human competence has a rich history in linguistics, there is currently no generally
099
      accepted framework for studying LLM competence Mahowald et al. (2023); Pavlick (2023). Our
      primary goal in this work is to define and test a general empirical analysis framework for interpreting
100
      and measuring LLM competence, as outlined in the following section.

107   However, the formalization of competence provided in this work is sufficiently general to incorporate most
      notions of competence, which may be flexibly specified by instantiating CALM in different ways.

124
125
      In order to make the study of competence tractable in the context of LLMs, we introduce the CALM
      (Competence-based Analysis of Language Models) framework, which describes an LLM’s compe-
126
      tence with respect to a given linguistic task in terms of its latent representation of the causal structure
127
      of the task.
128

130   predict y ∈ Y given x ∈ X , and a collection of latent properties Z = {Zj }m

132   process

141   For example, suppose a speaker wants to communicate that orangutans are a genus of primate. She
142   might say “orangutans are primates” or “orangutans, a genus of apes, are primates”. In both cases,

144   complemented by an appositive phrase like “a genus of apes”, and this phrase does not change the
145   grammatical number of the subject “orangutans”; so if we define TVC as English verb conjugation,

149   hypernymy relation, and ZNS ∈ Ze (e.g., the same answers will be correct if the question is instead
150   posed as “an orangutan is a y”). Thus, we expect competent English speakers to be invariant to
151   grammatical number when performing hypernym prediction (see Figure 2).
152
153   Internal Representation Our main concern is measuring how attributable an LLM M ’s behavior
154   in a given task T is to its representation of various properties Z = {Z1 , ..., Zm }, and how these
155   properties correspond to the causal structure of the task. If M respects the data-generating process
156   of T , then its behavior should be attributable only to causal properties Z ∈ Zc (and not to envi-
157   ronmental properties Z ∈ Ze ), in which case we say that M is competent with respect to T (see
158
      Figure 2). We study model M ’s use of each property Zj ∈ Z by performing causal interventions
      do(Zj ) on its representation of Zj in the course of performing task T , and measure the impact that
159
      these interventions have on its predictions.

165   alignment with a competence graph GT , which we define as a structural causal model (SCM) of T

168   properties Zc defined by GT is the set of all properties Zj ∈ Z such that there is an edge or path
169   from Zj to y.
170   To determine the extent to which M ’s behavior is correctly explained by the causal dependencies
171   (and lack thereof) in GT , we measure their consistency under interventions do(z), where setting z =

191   2.4   C AUSAL P ROBING
192
      A key technical challenge in implementing CALM (and causal probing more generally) is design-
193
      ing an algorithm to perform causal interventions do(Z) that maximally damage the representation
194
      of a property Z while otherwise minimally damaging representations of other properties Z ′ Rav-
195   fogel et al. (2022b). For example, amnesic probing Elazar et al. (2021b) uses the INLP algorithm
196   Ravfogel et al. (2020) to produce interventions gZ that remove all information that is linearly pre-
197   dictive of property Z from a pre-computed set of embedding representations H, showing that BERT
198   makes variable use of parts-of-speech, syntactic dependencies, and named-entity types in perform-
199   ing masked language modeling. However, Elazar et al. (2021b) also found that, when INLP is used
200   to remove BERT’s representation of these properties in early layers, it is often able to “recover” this
201   representation in later layers, which is likely due to BERT encoding these properties nonlinearly;
202   and later work has found that the same “recoverability” problem persists even when linear infor-
203
      mation removal methods like INLP are kernelized Ravfogel et al. (2022b). Thus, it is necessary to
      develop interventions that do not require restrictive assumptions about the structure of LLMs’ rep-
204
      resentations (e.g., linearity; see Vargas & Cotterell 2020), a problem which we aim to solve in the
205
      following section.

252   Benefits and Drawbacks The key advantage of gradient-based interventions (GBIs) as a causal
253   probing methodology is that they may be applied to any differentiable probe. For example, if we
254   are investigating the hypothesis that M ’s representation of Z is captured by a linear subspace of
255   representations in a given layer (see Vargas & Cotterell, 2020), then we may train a linear probe and
256   various nonlinear probes on representations and observe whether GBIs against the linear probe have
257   a comparable impact to those against the nonlinear probes. Alternatively, if we believe that a probe’s
258   architecture should mirror the architecture of the model it is probing (as argued by Pimentel et al.,
259
      2022), we may implement probes as such. Finally, where previous intervention methodologies for
      causal probing have focused on nullifying interventions that remove the representation of the target
260
      property Z Ravfogel et al. (2020; 2022b;a); Shao et al. (2022); Belrose et al. (2024), GBIs allow one
261
      to perform targeted interventions that set LLMs’ representations to counterfactual values do(Z =
262   z ′ ), effectively simulating the model’s behavior under counterfactual inputs, which may be useful
263   for predicting behaviors under various distribution shifts (see Appendix C.1). However, the benefits
264   associated with GBIs do come with some important limitations, as we discuss in Appendix A.

267   4   A PPLICATIONS OF CALM
268
269   Once we instantiate the general CALM framework with a specific probing technique such as the
      GBI introduced in the previous section, CALM would be “operational” and can be used in many

270
      novel ways to both facilitate understanding of representations learned in LLMs and predict behav-
271   iors of LLMs in many application contexts, which would otherwise be impossible without such a
272   framework. We briefly discuss some of them below for the purpose of demonstrating the generality
273   and great potential of CALM.
274
275   4.1   R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
276
277   The CALM framework, competence measure, and GBI methodology developed in Sections 2 and 3
278   are sufficiently general to be directly applied to analyze arbitrary LLMs on any language modeling
279   task whose causal structure is already well understood (or, for tasks where this is not the case,
280
      we may apply the causal graph discovery approach described in Section 4.4), allowing us to study
      the impact of various model architectures, pre-training regimes, and fine-tuning strategies on the
281
      representations LLMs learn and use for arbitrary tasks of interest. For example, just using the
282
      proposed competence measure as an additional dimension of evaluation as we have done in our
283   experiments should already enable obtaining additional insights about the behaviors of the models.
284   As the competence measure can be expected to have better correlation with the behavior of a model
285   than a regular task performance measure, using the competence measure or using it in addition to
286   regular performance measures can lead to better decisions in optimizing all kinds of decisions such
287   as model architecture and hyperparameters.
288
289   4.2   M ULTITASK L EARNING
290
291
      Are high competence scores on task T correlated with an LLMs’ robustness to meaning-preserving
      transformations (see, e.g., Elazar et al., 2021a) on tasks T ′ that share several causal properties Zc
292
      with task T ? Through the lens of causally invariant prediction (Peters et al., 2016; Arjovsky et al.,
293
      2019; Bühlmann, 2020), this hypothesis is likely true (however, see Rosenfeld et al. 2020 for ap-
294   propriate caveats) – if so, this would make it possible to use clusters of related tasks to predict
295   LLMs’ robustness (and other behavioral patterns, such as brittleness in the face of distribution shifts
296   introduced by spurious dependencies) between related tasks using CALM, given an appropriate
297   experimental model. Furthermore, the ability to characterize tasks based on mutual (learned) de-
298   pendency structures could be valuable in transfer learning applications such as guiding the selection
299   of auxiliary tasks in multi-task learning (Ruder, 2017) or predicting the impact of intermediate task
300   fine-tuning on downstream target tasks (Choshen et al., 2022).
301
302   4.3   TASK D EPENDENCIES
303
      Another possible application of CALM concerns causal invariance under multi-task applications.
304
      Existing approaches in invariant representation learning generally require task-specific training Zhao
305
      et al. (2022), as the notion of invariance is inherently task-centric (i.e., the properties which are in-
306   variant predictors of output values vary by task, and different tasks may have opposite notions of
307   which properties are causal versus environmental; see Section 2.2), so applying such approaches to
308   train models to be causally invariant with respect to a specific downstream task T is expected to
309   come at the cost of performance on other downstream tasks T ′ . Therefore, considering the recent
310   rise of open-ended, task-general LLMs Zhang et al. (2022); BigScience et al. (2022); Touvron et al.
311   (2023a;b); Groeneveld et al. (2024), it is important to find alternative approaches for studying mod-
312   els’ causal dependencies in a task-general setting to account for applications involving tasks with
313   different (and perhaps contradictory) causal structures, such as CALM.
314
315   4.4   C AUSAL C OMPETENCE G RAPH D ISCOVERY
316
      One of the key benefits of CALM is that, instead of simply measuring consistency with respect to a
317
      known, static task description GT , the competence metric in Equation (2) can also be used to discover
318   a competence graph G which most faithfully explains a model M ’s behavior in a given task or context
319   (see Section 2.3) by computing C(M |G) “in-the-loop” of existing causal graph discovery algorithms
320   like IGSP (Yang et al., 2018). Such algorithms can be used both to suggest likely competence
321   graphs based on interventional data collected by running CALM experiments, to recommend the
322   experiments that would yield the most useful interventional data for the graph discovery algorithm,
323   and to evaluate candidate graphs G using our competence metric, terminating the graph discovery
      algorithm once a competence graph G that offers sufficiently faithful explanations of M ’s behavior

      has been found. In this case, it is still necessary to define the set of properties Z being probed and
325   the scoring function S used to compare the predictions of M and G; but no knowledge of the causal

327   causal dependents Zj ; see Bongers et al. 2021) is required.

      These are only some of the possible applications enabled by the new CALM framework. One can

333   5         E XPERIMENTS
334
335   As the main contribution of our work is a theoretical framework, a general competence measure,
336   and a general perturbation method, our experiments are mainly to evaluate its feasibility in studying
337
      specific models on some specific tasks to measure and understand their competence, with the hope
      of generating quantitative measures of the competence of LLMs for the first time.
338
339   We begin by examining BERT Devlin et al. (2019) and RoBERTa Liu et al. (2019),4 two language
340   models which have been extensively studied in the context of probing Rogers et al. (2020); Ravfogel
341   et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Elazar et al. (2021b); Lasri et al. (2022). Our primary goal in the
342   following experiments is to develop and test an experimental implementation of CALM using GBIs
343
      in the context of comparatively small, well-studied models and tasks in order to validate whether
      CALM can explain behavioral findings of earlier work in this simplified environment. (We motivate
344
      this choice in greater detail in Appendix B.1.)
345
346
      5.1       TASKS
347
348   Masked language models like BERT and RoBERTa are trained to predict Pr(x[MASK] = w|x) for
349   text input (token sequence) x = (x1 , x2 , ..., x|x| ), mask token x[MASK] ∈ x, and token vocabulary
350   V = {w1 , w2 , ..., w|V | }. As such, it is common to study them by providing them with “fill-in-the-
351   blank” style masked prompts (e.g., “a cat is a type of [MASK]”) and evaluating their accuracy in
352   predicting the correct answer (e.g., “animal”, “pet”, etc.), a task known cloze prompting (Liu et al.,
353   2023).
354   We use the collection of 14 lexical inference tasks included in the ConceptNet Speer et al. (2017)
355   subset of LAMA Petroni et al. (2019), each of which are formulated as a collection cloze prompts.
356   For example, the LAMA “IsA” task contains ∼2K hypernym prompts corresponding to the “IsA”
357   ConceptNet relation (including, e.g., “A laser is a [MASK] which creates coherent light.”, where the
358   task is to predict that the [MASK] token should be replaced with “device”, a hypernym of “laser”),
359   with the remaining 13 LAMA ConceptNet tasks corresponding to other lexical relations such as
360   “PartOf”, “HasProperty”, and “CapableOf”. (See Appendix B.2 for additional details.)
361   Using these task datasets allow us to test how the representation of each relation is used across all
362   other tasks. In the context of a single task Tj , intervening on a model’s representation of the task-
363   causal relation Zj allows us to measure the extent to which its predictions are attributable to its
364   representation of the causal property Zc = {Zj } (where a large impact indicates competence). On
365   the other hand, intervening on the representations of the other 13 lexical relations Zk ∈ Ze allows
366   us (in the aggregate) to measure how much the model is performing task Tj by leveraging represen-
367   tations of general, non-causal lexical information (where a large impact indicates incompetence).5
368

450   6.1   A NALYSIS
451
452   Performance While their accuracies on individual tasks vary, BERT and RoBERTa have quite
453   similar aggregate performance: BERT outperforms RoBERTa on just over half (8/14) of the tasks,
454   achieving essentially equivalent performance when averaged across all tasks (0.3099 versus 0.3094).
455
456   Competence Given our experimental model E with m = 14 tasks, consider a random baseline
457
      language model R whose predictions always change in response to each intervention, making equal
                                                                                               1
      use of all properties in each task. R would yield a competence score of C(R|GT ) = m       ≈ 0.0714
458
      for each task. Both BERT and RoBERTa score above this threshold for all tasks, meaning that their
459
      competence is consistently greater than that of a model (R) that does not distinguish between causal
460   and environmental properties. However, RoBERTa is consistently less competent than BERT (on
461   12/14 tasks), and also has lower competence scores averaged across all tasks (0.381 vs. 0.334).
462
463
      We also observe that, for the two tasks (HasSubevent and MotivatedByGoal) where RoBERTa is
      more competent than BERT, it also achieves substantially higher performance. Specifically, relative
464
      performance and competence are correlated: the Spearman’s Rank correlation coefficient between
465
      the average difference in accuracy and average difference in performance is a moderately strong
466   positive correlation ρ = 0.508 with significance p = 0.064.
467
468
      6.2   D ISCUSSION
469
470   A priori, we might expect an LLM with nontrivial performance to also exhibit greater competence
471   than a random baseline like R; but this is not necessarily the case, given that it is common for deep
472   learning models to achieve remarkable performance by exploiting spurious correlations inherent
473   in a given task dataset McCoy et al. (2019); Geirhos et al. (2020); Feder et al. (2022). Thus, the
474   finding that BERT and RoBERTa’s performance on each task is supported by an intermediate level
475
      of competence on the part of both models is meaningful: for each task, their behavior is generally
      more attributable to their representations of causally-invariant properties than to spurious lexical
476
      associations, and this competence varies substantially between tasks.
477
478
      Explananda Prior work has shown that BERT and RoBERTa have widely varying performance
479
      in response to lexical inference tasks, depending on the specific manner in which they are prompted
480   (Hanna & Mareček, 2021; Ravichander et al., 2020; Ettinger, 2020; Elazar et al., 2021a; see Sec-
481   tion 7). One possible explanation for this phenomenon is that these models may not consistently
482   utilize a representation of the task-causal lexical relations (i.e., they are not highly competent for
483   these tasks), instead relying (at least in part) on spurious lexical associations learned from its train-
484   ing data. Previously, it has not been possible to empirically assess this hypothesis; but using CALM,
485   it is possible to provide direct evidence in its favor, as we find that both models possesses (only) an
      intermediate degree of competence for lexical inference prompt tasks.

      7    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Hypernym Prompting The performance of BERT-like models on lexical inference tasks such
489
      as hypernym prediction is known to be highly variable under small changes to prompts Hanna &
490   Mareček (2021); Ravichander et al. (2020); Ettinger (2020); Elazar et al. (2021a). Our findings offer
491   one possible explanation for such brittle performance: BERT and RoBERTa’s partial competence in
492   hypernym prediction indicates that it should be possible to prompt these models in a way that will
493   yield high performance, but that its reliance on spurious lexical associations may lead it to fail when
494   these correlations are broken – e.g., by substituting singular terms for plurals Ravichander et al.
495   (2020) or paraphrasing a prompt Elazar et al. (2021a).
496
497   Causal Probing Most related to our work is amnesic probing Elazar et al. (2021b), which we
498   discuss at length in Section 2.4. Lasri et al. (2022) applied amnesic probing to study the use of
499
      grammatical number representations in performing an English verb conjugation prompt task. As
      this experiment involves intervening on the representation of a property which is causal with respect
500
      to the prompt task, it may be understood as an informal instantiation of CALM (albeit without
501
      considering environmental properties or measuring competence).
502
503
      Gradient-based Interventions Tucker et al. (2021) developed a similar approach to our GBI
504   causal probing methodology (as outlined in Section 2.4) without explicit use of gradient-based ad-
505   versarial attacks. Their methodology is equivalent to performing a targeted, unconstrained attack
506   using standard gradient descent.6 In such attacks, it is standard practice to constrain the magnitude
507   of resulting perturbations Goodfellow et al. (2015); Madry et al. (2017); Kos et al. (2018), which we
508   do here in order to minimize the effect of “collateral damage” done by such attacks (see Section 5.4
509   and Appendix B.4); so failing to impose such constraints may result in indiscriminate damage to
510   representations.
511
512   Unsupervised Probing Instead of training supervised probes to predict a pre-determined property
513
      of interest (as we do here), an alternative approach is to train unsupervised probes such as Sparse
      Auto-Encoders (SAEs; Subramanian et al., 2018; Yun et al., 2021; Cunningham et al., 2023) to
514
      automatically learn an overcomplete basis of features that are useful for sparsely representing em-
515
      beddings, which can also be used to control models’ use of these learned features Bricken et al.
516   (2023); Templeton et al. (2024). However, as SAEs are unsupervised probes, they yield feature
517   vectors that are not inherently interpretable and must be retroactively interpreted, meaning that the
518   task of creating a supervised probe training dataset (as required for conventional causal probing) is
519   substituted for the task of interpreting learned features Davies & Khakzar (2024). However, given
520   features that can be reliably interpreted as representing task-causal or -environmental features, it is
521   also possible to implement CALM using unsupervised probes like SAEs.
522
523   8    C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we introduced CALM, a general analysis framework that enables the study of LLMs’
526   linguistic competence using causal probing, including the first quantitative measure of linguistic
527   competence. We developed the gradient-based intervention (GBI) methodology, a novel approach
528   to causal probing that can target a far greater range of representations than previous techniques,
529   expanding the scope of causal probing to new questions in LLM interpretability and analysis. We
530   discussed multiple new applications of CALM in analyzing and understanding the learned repre-
531   sentations of LLMs as well as predicting their behaviors. Finally, we carried out a preliminary case
532   study of CALM using GBIs, analyzing BERT and RoBERTa’s competence across a collection of
533
      lexical inference tasks, finding that even a simple experimental model is sufficient to explain and
      predict their behavior across a variety of lexical inference tasks. These results demonstrated the
534
      great potential of CALM in studying representations and behaviors of LLMs in novel ways that we
535
      could not do today.

851
852   Gradient-Based Interventions While GBIs are applicable to a more general range of model rep-
853   resentations than other interventions (see Section 3), this generality comes with a lack of constraints
854   on probes (gZ ); and as a result, GBIs cannot provide the strong theoretical constraints on collateral
855   damage as can methods like, e.g., INLP Ravfogel et al. (2020), which provably preserves distances
856   between embeddings as well as possible while completely removing the linear representation of the
857   target property. To minimize collateral damage to representations, the magnitude of perturbations
858   should be modulated via constraints on gradient attacks against gZ (see Section 5.4) and experimen-
859   tally validated to control the damage done to representations (see Appendix B.4). Thus, in cases
860
      where the structure of representations is believed to satisfy strong assumptions (e.g., being restricted
      to a linear subspace; Vargas & Cotterell, 2020) or strong upper bounds on collateral damage are
861
      required, CALM interventions can be implemented with methods like INLP rather than GBIs.7

875   As noted in Section 5, our primary goal in our experiments is to validate CALM by testing it in a
876   simplified experimental setting consisting of comparatively small, well-studied models and tasks. As
877   such, we need models that are just complex enough for CALM to be applicable (i.e., neural language
878   models that are capable of performing the tasks we consider at a nontrivial level of performance),
879   making BERT and RoBERTa ideal candidates; and in future work plan to scale CALM to more
880   complex contexts covering larger, more powerful models as they perform more difficult tasks (see
881   ??). This is a common setting in the context of substantial recent interpretability work: first, a
882
      theoretical framework is developed for interpreting an internal representation or mechanism and
      initially tested in the context of “toy” models or tasks Elhage et al. (2021); Olsson et al. (2022);
883
      Zhong et al. (2023); Geiger et al. (2023), and subsequent work scales these frameworks to the context
884
      of larger models “in the wild” Wang et al. (2023b); Conmy et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023). We
885   anticipate that all of our major contributions (the CALM framework, competence metric, and GBI
886   causal probing method) will in principle be scalable to much larger, more recent LLMs (e.g., Zhang
887   et al. 2022; BigScience et al. 2022; Touvron et al. 2023a;b; Groeneveld et al. 2024, etc.), and predict

       As noted in Section 2.3, the CT (M |GT ) metric defined in Equation (2) is an adaptation of the In-
       terchange Intervention Accuracy (IIA) metric (Geiger et al., 2022; 2023), which evaluates the faith-
998
       fulness of a causal abstraction like GT as a (potential) explanation of the behavior of a “black box”
999
       system like M . In our case, this is equivalent to evaluating the competence of M on task T , provided
1000   that GT is the appropriate SCM for T , as an LLM is competent only to the extent that its behavior is
1001   determined by a causally invariant representation of the task.10 IIA requires performing interchange
1002   interventions M (xi | do(zi )), where the part of M ’s intermediate representation of input xi hypoth-
1003   esized to encode latent variables Z (taking the values zi when provided input xi ) is replaced with
1004   that of xj (which, in the ideal case, causes M ’s representation to encode the values zj instead of zi ),
1005   and compute the accuracy of GT (xi | do(zj )) in predicting M ’s behavior under these interventions.
1006   Thus, given access to high-quality interchange interventions over M , IIA measures the extent to
1007   which GT correctly models M ’s behavior under counterfactuals, and thus its faithfulness as a causal
1008
       abstraction of M .
1009   To adapt IIA to the context of causal probing and define CT (M |GT ), we replace instance-level in-
1010   terchange interventions with concept-level interventions: instead of swapping M ’s representation of
1011   variables Z given input xi with that of xj , we intervene on representations at the level of arbitrary
1012   concept settings z that need not correspond to previously sampled x, allowing us to simulate the
1013   behavior of M under previously-unseen distribution shifts (i.e., settings z representing previously-
1014
       unseen combinations of property values) and therefore make broader predictions about M ’s consis-
       tency with a given causal model GT under such conditions. As one of the key desiderata in studying
1015
       LLM competence is to predict behavior under distribution shifts where spurious correlations are
1016
       broken, CT is more appropriate than IIA in this setting. However, it also introduces an additional
1017   challenge: where interchange interventions only require localizing candidate representations – as
1018   counterfactual representations are obtained merely by “plugging in” values from a different input –
1019   computing CT instead requires one to both localize representations and directly intervene on them
1020   to change the encoded value. Previous causal probing intervention strategies (e.g., Ravfogel et al.,
1021   2020; 2022b) have generally performed interventions by neutralizing concept representations, not
1022   modifying them to encode specific counterfactual values; so in order to carry out our study, it is also
