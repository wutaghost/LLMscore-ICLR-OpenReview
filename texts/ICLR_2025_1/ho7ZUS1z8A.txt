009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture has emerged as a powerful paradigm in the
012            development of Large Language Models (LLMs), offering superior scaling capabil-
013            ities and reduced computational costs. However, the increased parameter budgets
014            and memory overhead associated with MoE LLMs pose significant challenges to
015            their efficiency and widespread deployment. In this paper, we present MoE-SVD,
016
               the first decomposition-based compression framework tailored for MoE LLMs
               without any extra training. By harnessing the power of Singular Value Decompo-
017
               sition (SVD), MoE-SVD addresses the critical issues of decomposition collapse
018
               and matrix redundancy in MoE architectures. Specifically, we first decompose
019            experts into compact low-rank matrices, resulting in accelerated inference and
020            memory optimization. In particular, we propose selective decomposition strategy
021            by measuring sensitivity metrics based on weight singular values and activation
022            statistics to automatically identify decomposable expert layers. Then, we share
023            a single V-matrix across all experts and employ a top-k selection for U-matrices.
024            This low-rank matrix sharing and trimming scheme allows for significant parameter
025            reduction while preserving diversity among experts. Comprehensive experiments
026            conducted on Mixtral-8×7B|22B, Phi-3.5-MoE and DeepSeekMoE across multiple
027
               datasets reveal that MoE-SVD consistently outperforms existing compression meth-
               ods in terms of performance-efficiency tradeoffs. Notably, we achieve a remarkable
028
               60% compression ratio on Mixtral-7x8B and Phi-3.5-MoE, resulting in a 1.5×
029
               inference acceleration with minimal performance degradation. Codes are available
030            in the supplementary materials.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Mixture of Experts (MoE) (Cai et al., 2024b) have demonstrated promising advancements in the
036   realm of large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023). These architectures incorporate
037   multiple expert networks and employ a sparse gating mechanism, enabling efficient computation
038   and facilitating the scaling of LLMs within the constraints of limited computational resources(Dai
039
      et al., 2024). Numerous studies have shown that MoE models can achieve state-of-the-art results
      across various benchmarks while utilizing fewer resources compared to traditional dense models (Dai
040
      et al., 2024; Fedus et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024). Despite these advantages, MoE LLMs still face
041
      several challenges: (1) Immense Parameter Sizes: MoE models generally have a larger number of
042   parameters than dense models (Xue et al., 2024b), which can make them difficult to train and deploy,
043   especially in resource-constrained environments. (2) Memory Overhead: MoE models can suffer
044   from memory inefficiency due to the need to store and access multiple expert weights and biases,
045   potentially hindering their deployment on devices with limited memory (Song et al., 2023).
046
      Limitations of Traditional MoE Compressions: To address the challenges of large parameter
047
      size and memory overhead, some MoE-specific compression methods have been proposed to prune
048   unimportant experts or weights. For example, Lu et al. (2024) propose task-specific expert pruning
049   and dynamic skipping, while He et al. (2024) evaluate various types of sparse schemes across multiple
050   MoE components. Although these techniques show promise, they suffer from certain limitations:
051   (1) Performance Degradation, especially under high compression ratios, often necessitating costly
052   and time-consuming retraining. For instance, pruning 25% of experts in Mixtral-8×7B results in
053   a 23% performance drop (He et al., 2024). (2) Hardware Dependency: Some semi-structured
      sparse methods only gain speedup on NVIDIA Ampere and Hopper architecture GPUs, limiting their

089   Our Novel Framework: As this well-said quote goes, the sparse-activated MoE dynamic architecture
090   differs from common LLMs and deserves customized decomposition schemes based on the above
091   observations. To this end, we develop MoE-SVD, a novel compression framework specifically
092   designed for MoE LLMs. Our MoE-SVD leverages SVD to decompose expert layers in a structured
093
      manner, creating a naturally sparse expert structure that reduces computational costs while maintaining
      model expressiveness. The core innovation of MoE-SVD lies in: (1) Selective Decomposition
094
      Strategy: Unlike previous SVD approaches that apply uniform compression across different layers,
095
      our method introduces a sensitivity metric derived from matrix singular values and activation statistics,
096   facilitating adaptive decomposition. As illustrated in Figure 1 (middle), this metric accurately
097   identifies the sensitive expert layers, allowing for more targeted compression. (2) Low-rank Matrix
098   Sharing and Trimming: To further minimize parameter redundancy, we introduce V-matrix sharing,
099   where the most frequently sampled V-matrix is retained and shared across all experts. In addition,
100   we apply U-matrix trimming by selecting the top-k U-matrices based on sampling frequency, while
101   discarding the less frequently used matrices. This strategy significantly minimizes the number
102   of parameters, while ensuring the diversity for effective MoE functioning. With these innovative
103   schemes, our MoE-SVD offers substantial parameter reduction, creates a naturally sparse expert
104
      structure for faster inference, and can be deployed on standard computing infrastructure without
      requiring additional training phases. This flexible framework allows high compression ratios and
105
      strikes an optimal balance between computational efficiency and model performance.

135   Mixture of Experts. MoE is initially introduced for conditional computation (Jacobs et al., 1991;
136   Jordan & Jacobs, 1994; Eigen et al., 2013) and has evolved into a sparse activation framework
137   (Shazeer et al., 2017) that enables efficient training and inference in language (Fedus et al., 2022)
138   tasks. The architecture’s ability to achieve superior scaling laws at reduced costs (Clark et al.,
139   2022) has led to its widespread adoption in state-of-the-art Large Language Models (LLMs) (Jiang
140   et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024). Recent advancements in MoE focus on refining expert structures
141
      (Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2024), enhancing router designs (Zhou et al., 2022; Zoph et al.,
      2022), and developing innovative training strategies (Chen et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). However,
142
      MoE LLMs still face challenges, including increased parameter budgets due to expert replication
143
      (He et al., 2023), communication costs that enhance latency (Song et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024b),
144   and significant memory overhead issues (Li et al., 2024b), posing challenges to their efficiency.
145
146   MoE Compression. To address the above issues, researchers are developing MoE-specific com-
147
      pression methods (e.g., expert pruning (Lu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024)). For instance, Liu et al.
      (2024a) proposes search-based expert pruning and merging, while Zhang et al. (2024) investigates
148
      task-agnostic pruning methods that diversify expert knowledge. In contrast, our MoE-SVD develops
149
      a new decomposition route: (1) Unlike other methods that require training and drop inactive experts
150   without acceleration, MoE-SVD primarily exploits SVD to reduce the size of activated experts for
151   acceleration without extensive retraining. (2) MoE-SVD performs low-rank matrix sharing and
152   trimming, avoiding the direct dropping of entire experts like other methods, which prevents drastic
153   performance loss. (3) Other approaches include post-training quantization (Li et al., 2024a) and
154   system optimization (e.g., expert offloading (Xue et al., 2024a), parallelism (Cai et al., 2024a), and
155   switching (Liu et al., 2024b)). Our MoE-SVD focuses solely on expert compression and remains
156   orthogonal to these methods. (4) Our MoE-SVD enhances the SVD for large-scale MoEs without
157   expert merging and fine-tuning, in contrast to MC-SMoE (Li et al., 2024b), which only addresses
158
      small-scale MoE by first merging experts and then applying vanilla decomposition before fine-tuning.
159   Singular Value Decomposition. Recently, several SVD-based methods have been proposed for
160   compressing LLMs (Golub et al., 1987). For example, FWSVD (Hsu et al., 2022) introduces a
161   weighted low-rank factorization, while ASVD (Yuan et al., 2023) proposes an activation-aware
      SVD method that considers the activation patterns of the model’s layers to improve compression

      3.2       S ELECTIVE D ECOMPOSITION S TRATEGY
217
218   To determine the sensitivity of expert layers in the MoE to decomposition, we employ a selective
219   decomposition strategy. This approach is based on a carefully crafted sensitivity metric that considers
220   both the singular value decomposition of expert weight matrices and the activation patterns of these
221
      experts during inference. For a layer with N experts, we normalize these sensitivities using expert
      sampling frequency to obtain the layer-wise sensitivity metric SL :
222
                                                                      N
223                                                                   X
                                                             SL =             fi · ri · ai ,                                                   (4)
224
                                                                      i=1
225
226   where fi represents the sampling frequency of the i-th expert during router selection, ri denotes the
227
      principal rank (number of large value components) of singular vectors Σi = diag(σi,1 , σi,2 , ..., σi,d )
      obtained from the SVD of the i-th expert’s weight matrix, and ai measures the proportion of activation
228
      outliers of the i-th expert exceeding the mean absolute activation value (see more details in Appendix
229
      D.1 D). To apply selective decomposition, we set a threshold τ based on the desired compression
230   ratio. Expert layer with sensitivity Si ≥ τ are preserved without decomposition, while those below
231   the threshold undergo SVD decomposition. This process is repeated for each layer in the network.
232   This selective decomposition strategy allows for a nuanced approach to MoE compression, preserving
233   the most important experts while reducing the computational footprint of less critical components.

256   Motivation: For MoE models, different expert matrices contain some similarities and can be
257   merged Liu et al. (2024a). As shown in Figure 1 (right), decomposed V-matrices share certain
258
      similarities. Consider two expert matrices W1 and W2 with SVD decompositions W1 = U1 Σ1 V1T
      and W2 = U2 Σ2 V2T . The similarity in their output transformations can be quantified by the Frobenius
259
      inner product of their V-matrices ⟨V1 , V2 ⟩F = tr(V1T V2 ). For experts trained on similar tasks, this
260
      inner product is often close to high values, indicating high similarity in output transformations.
261   Consequently, we can perform a more fine-grained matrix selection and sharing, achieving a superior
262   trade-off between performance and the number of parameters.
263

      router sampling frequency f (Vi ) for each expert i is computed based on the routing decisions made

309
      original MoE model with N × m × n parameters. After applying our low-rank decomposition,
      each expert matrix Wi is decomposed into Ui Σi ∈ Rm×r , Σi ∈ Rr×r , and Vi ∈ Rr×n , resulting in
310
      N × (m × r + r × n) parameters per expert. With V-matrix sharing, we retain only one Vs ∈ Rr×n
311
      matrix shared across all experts, reducing the parameter count by a factor of N for the V-matrices.
312   Furthermore, with U-matrix selection, we typically select k = 2 U-matrices, reducing the parameter
313   count by a factor of N2 for the U-matrices. Thus, the total number of parameters in our compressed
314   MoE model is m × r × 2 + r × n = 2mr + rn. Comparing this to the original N × m × n parameters,
315   we achieve a substantial reduction in the number of parameters, especially when r ≪ min(m, n).
316   The parameter reduction ratio is approximately 2mr+rn

317   high input and output dimensions.
318   Experts Diversity: Despite the parameter reduction, our method still maintains diversity among
319   experts by leveraging the unique components of Ui Σi : the U matrices capturing expert-specific
320   input space transformations and the Σi matrices determining the importance of these transformations.
321   Through selecting distinct combinations of U matrices for each expert, individual transformations
322   of the input space are preserved, ensuring uniqueness across experts. This approach allows for a
323   balance between parameter efficiency and the preservation of expert diversity, crucial for the effective

523   5      C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this paper, we introduce MoE-SVD, a novel SVD-based compression framework tailored for MoE
526
      LLMs, effectively streamlining model parameters, computational expenses, and memory usage while
527
      upholding performance. To combat decomposition collapse stemming from matrix redundancy, we
528   propose innovative solutions, including the selective decomposition strategy and a low-rank matrix
529   sharing and trimming mechanism. The former utilizes a sensitivity metric for automated identification
530   of decomposable layers, while the latter harmonizes parameter efficiency and expert specialization
531   through V-matrix sharing and U-matrix trimming. Our extensive assessments on Mixtral-8×7B and
532   Phi-3.5-MoE models showcase the method’s superiority over existing compression techniques in
533   preserving model capabilities across diverse tasks. These promising results, encompassing preserved
534   performance, accelerated inference speed, and substantial memory reduction, position MoE-SVD
535   as a significant stride forward in making MoE LLMs more accessible and efficient for real-world
536
      applications, paving the way for widespread adoption and deployment of these powerful models.
537   Limitations: To avoid confusion, we do not show results of combining our MoE-SVD with pruning
538   method. In essence, our MoE-SVD is new technology and orthogonal to previous pruning-based
539   approaches. In future work, we will strive to extend MoE-SVD with weight sparsity and pruning
      methods to achieve more extreme compression.

      A    F URTHER D ISCUSSIONS
766
767   A.1: Ethics Statement
768   We focus solely on developing efficient techniques for Large Language Models (LLMs), utilizing
769   publicly available datasets and models. Our research is not designed to address human ethics
770   or privacy concerns directly. Instead, we concentrate on improving the computational efficiency
771   and deployment capabilities of existing MoE LLMs, which may indirectly contribute to broader
772   accessibility and utilization of these powerful models.
773   A.2: Reproducibility
774
775
      We affirm the solid reproducibility of our results and provide specific code implementations in the
      appendix. Our main experiments represent average outcomes from multiple repetitions, ensuring
776
      reliability. MoE LLMs, being very large models, exhibit relatively small variances in experimental
777
      results and evaluations. To further demonstrate the robustness and repeatability of our method, we
778   present detailed results for different initial seeds, showcasing consistent performance across various
779   conditions.
780
781
      A.3: Summary of Innovations
782   (1) We introduce MoE-SVD, the first SVD-based structured compression method specifically de-
783   signed for MoE LLMs, addressing unique challenges such as decomposition sensitivity and expert
784   redundancy. (2) Our selective decomposition strategy employs a novel sensitivity metric derived from
785   matrix singular values and activation statistics, enabling adaptive compression across expert layers.
786   (3) We develop low-rank matrix sharing and trimming techniques, including V-matrix sharing across
787
      experts and U-matrix trimming, significantly reducing parameters while maintaining expert diversity
      and model performance.
788
789   A.4: Performance Gains
790
      As the first SVD method developed for MoE LLMs, our approach demonstrates significant advantages
791   in both performance and efficiency. (1) Our performance gains compared to other SVD methods
792   are substantial, particularly at higher compression ratios. (2) Our main results are achieved without
793   additional training, with potential for further improvement through fine-tuning. (3) We offer notable
794   improvements in inference speed and memory optimization, crucial for practical deployment. (4) We
795   maintain good performance even at very high compression ratios, a feat difficult for other compression
796   methods to achieve.
797   A.5: Comparison to BERT-based Compression Methods
798
799   (1) While low-rank decomposition methods exist for BERT-based MoE models (Li et al., 2024b),
800
      these are not applicable to MoE LLMs due to significant differences in model scale and architecture.
      We consider compression of MoE LLMs a distinct field, separate from BERT-based MoE compression.
801
      (2) Other LLM compression methods (Sun et al., 2023) also do not consider previous BERT-based
802
      compression techniques as direct competitors, recognizing the unique challenges posed by large-scale
803   models.
804
805   A.6: Comparison to Pruning-based Compression Methods
806   Our MoE-SVD approach and pruning-based methods (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023)
807   are fully orthogonal, addressing different aspects of model compression. While pruning focuses on
808   removing less important components, our method restructures the model through decomposition and
809   sharing, offering complementary benefits. This orthogonality suggests potential for future research
      combining both approaches for even more efficient MoE LLM compression.

871   C     P SEUDOCODE
872
873   In our experimental implementation, we present a detailed algorithmic procedure for compressing
874
      MoE-based large language models using the proposed MoE-SVD method. Algorithm 4 outlines
      the main steps of this approach. The process begins by collecting scaling matrices through forward
875
      hooks during inference, as shown in Algorithm 1 (Step 1). This step is crucial for capturing activation
876
      patterns and computing the sensitivity metric for each expert. Subsequently, we perform singular value
877   decomposition (SVD) on the scaled weight matrices, followed by truncation for effective compression,
878   as detailed in Algorithm 2 (Step 2). Our method introduces a V-matrix sharing mechanism, where the
879   most frequently used V-matrix is selected and shared among all experts, as described in Algorithm 3
880   (Step 3). Additionally, we employ U-matrix trimming by retaining the top-k U-matrices based on
881   expert sampling frequencies to refine the expert functions (Step 4). To ensure numerical stability,
882   we apply the adjustment function provided in Algorithm 5, which modifies matrices to be positive
883   definite when necessary. This comprehensive approach enables significant model compression while
884   maintaining performance, effectively addressing the need for efficient large-scale language models.

       Following ASVD (Yuan et al., 2023) and SVD-LLM (Wang et al., 2024), our framework employs
       an activation-weighted SVD that enhances the vanilla SVD by incorporating activation statistics to
989
       improve decomposition accuracy. With activation matrix X and original weight Woriginal , we compute
990
       the activation-weighted matrix by scaling the original weight matrix based on the activation statistics:
991
992                                             Waw = Woriginal · S,                                       (11)
993    where Waw ∈ R    m×n
                           represents the activation-weighted matrix and matrix S is obtained through
994    cholesky decomposition of activation gram matrix XXT . We then perform SVD on Waw and final
995    compressed weight matrix is obtained by truncating the smallest singular values:
996
997
                                         Waw = U · Trunc(Σ) · V T · S −1 ,                                 (12)

       This activation-weighted approach effectively mitigates reconstruction loss from outliers during matrix
999
       decomposition while maintaining the essential characteristics of the original weight distribution.

1001   D.4: Model-Specific Configurations
1002   In our experimental realization, we develop tailored post-decomposition strategies for various large
1003   language models, each with unique architectures. For Mixtral-8×7B, which employs 8 experts per
1004   layer, we implement a novel approach of sharing V components (v1, v2, v3) from the most frequently
1005   activated expert while retaining U components (u1, u2, u3) from the top two most frequent experts.
1006   We extend this methodology to Phi-3.5-MoE, featuring 16 experts per layer, by broadening the
1007   retention scope. In this instance, we share V components from the most frequently selected expert
1008   and preserve U components from the top four most frequent experts. For the more complex Deepseek
1009   16B, which utilizes 64 experts per block, we innovate further by partitioning the experts into 8 distinct
1010
       groups. Within each group, we share the most frequent V component and strategically trim the 6
       lowest frequency U components. This carefully crafted selective retention and sharing approach
1011
       enables us to maintain model performance while achieving substantial reductions in both parameter
1012
       count and computational requirements.
1013
1014   D.5: Computational Efficiency
1015   We rigorously assess the computational efficiency of our LLM compression techniques, recognizing its
1016   paramount importance for practical deployment scenarios. Our MoE-SVD compression methodology
1017   comprises two distinct phases: activation data collection and SVD decomposition with expert
1018   trimming. Through extensive experimentation on the Phi 3.5 MoE model, we meticulously quantify
1019   time requirements for various layer counts. Our findings reveal that single-layer processing consumes
1020   266 seconds for activation collection and 107 seconds for SVD and trimming. These durations exhibit
1021   a non-linear increase, reaching 489 and 209 seconds for two layers, and 689 and 320 seconds for
1022
       three layers, respectively. In a comprehensive 20% layer compression test, we observe a total time
       requirement of 44 minutes, with 30 minutes allocated to data collection and 14 minutes to SVD and
1023
       trimming. These results provide crucial insights into the scalability and efficiency of our approach
1024
       across different model configurations.

       Our in-depth scalability analysis unveils intriguing patterns in the computational behavior of our
1027   compression technique. The activation collection phase demonstrates near-linear time growth with

1029   of data propagation. However, we emphasize that this collection process is a one-time operation
1030   per model, with the resulting data being storable and reusable for various compression ratios. In

1032   opportunities for parallelization. While our current implementation relies on sequential Python
1033   loops, we identify significant potential for efficiency improvements through parallel processing of
1034   experts across layers. This aligns seamlessly with the independent operation of experts in different
1035
       layers of MoE models, indicating promising scalability prospects for MoE-SVD, particularly in the
