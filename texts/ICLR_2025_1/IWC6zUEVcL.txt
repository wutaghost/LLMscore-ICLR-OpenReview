009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Evaluating generalist agents presents significant challenges due to their wide-
012            ranging abilities and the limitations of current benchmarks in assessing true gen-
013            eralization. We introduce the MineCraft Universe (MCU), a fully automated
014            benchmarking framework set within the open-world game Minecraft. MCU dy-
015            namically generates and evaluates a broad spectrum of tasks, offering three core
016
               components: 1) a task generation mechanism that provides high degrees of freedom
               and variability, 2) an ever-expanding set of over 3K composable atomic tasks, and
017
               3) a general evaluation framework that supports open-ended task assessment. By
018
               integrating large language models (LLMs), MCU dynamically creates diverse envi-
019            ronments for each evaluation, fostering agent generalization. The framework uses a
020            vision-language model (VLM) to automatically generate evaluation criteria, achiev-
021            ing over 90% agreement with human ratings across multi-dimensional assessments,
022            which demonstrates that MCU is a scalable and explainable solution for evaluating
023            generalist agents. Additionally, we show that while state-of-the-art foundational
024            models perform well on specific tasks, they often struggle with increased task
025            diversity and difficulty.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable progress in the field
030
      of AI (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023). The release of the GPT series (Brown et al.,
031   2020) has significantly reshaped AI research, moving the focus away from task-specific models
032   toward the development of foundation models. (Bubeck et al., 2023). These models excel across
033   a diverse set of tasks and are highly instructable, marking a substantial leap forward in versatility
034   and adaptability. The next step in this evolution is the development of Generalist Agents (Bubeck
035   et al., 2023). So, what is a Generalist Agent? From the perspective of users, the ideal generalist agent
036   should embody a multifaceted utility, seamlessly integrating a spectrum of complex services. For
037   instance, users typically prefer asking ChatGPT for a range of services like searching, translation,
038   writing, coding, etc., rather than relying on numerous specialized apps. This preference underscores
039
      the potential for a “single-brain” style generalist agent, which intriguingly aligns with neuroscience
      insights (Mountcastle, 1978; Zhu et al., 2020; Taylor, 2005), offering a two-way benefit. Beyond
040
      that, generalist agent extends its capabilities by being able to interact with its environment, directly
041
      influencing and adapting to the real world. This interaction capability bridges the gap between passive
042   task execution and active decision-making in complex, dynamic settings (Reed et al., 2022; Durante
043   et al., 2024; Oertel et al., 2020). Therefore, we think that generalists should have following two
044   characteristics: 1) possess the generalization capability to manage diverse tasks; and 2) exhibit robust
045   interactivity and adaptability in the real-world challenges.
046
      Creating a generalist agent presents significant challenges. Early efforts attempted to create a “one-fits-
047
      all” network (Schmidhuber, 2018) with life-long learning strategies but struggled with basic tasks due
048   to catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). Recent meta-reinforcement learning (meta-
049   RL) studies (Finn et al., 2017; Hospedales et al., 2021; Lake & Baroni, 2023) has shown potential
050   in endowing models with human-like abilities for systematic generalization, but challenges such as
051   scalability, sample inefficiency, and limited performance in complex environments persist (Parmar
052   et al., 2023; Hospedales et al., 2021). Recent efforts have shifted towards pretraining large foundation
053   models on extensive internet-scale datasets (Cai et al., 2023b; Baker et al., 2022), achieving significant
      advances in tackling more complex and diverse tasks in open-world environments. However, these

087   flexible testing. Furthermore, we propose a domain-general, vision-language model (VLM)-based
088   evaluation method capable of assessing open-ended tasks, even those without explicit end signals.

093   2       B ENCHMARK D ESIDERATA
094
095   Based on the aforementioned challenges, we argue that three keystones should be introduced to
096   benchmarking generalist agents.
097   First, diversity is the key. The emergence of human-like general intelligence is inextricably tied to
098   diverse environments (Taylor, 2005). Environmental diversity drives evolutionary pressures, fostering
099   the development of complex cognition, technological innovation, and adaptability (Elmqvist et al.,
100   2012; Zhu et al., 2020). Similarly, diverse challenges stimulate the capacities of agents, pushing
101   them to generalize and perform across a wide array of tasks and conditions. However, in reality,
102   their capabilities are vastly different. In our MCU benchmark, we incorporate two types of diversity:
103   1) intra-task diversity: Each task should embody a high degree of variability and randomness,
104
      providing freedom to truly test the agent’s adaptive skills. 2) inter-task diversity: The benchmark
      should encompass a broad spectrum of task categories, representing the diverse challenges agents are
105
      likely to encounter in real-world environments.
106
107   Second, task quality deserves attention. As the demand for automatic generation grows, some
      approaches (Cheng et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2022), rely heavily on large language models (LLMs)

189   3.2.1      ATOMIC TASK
190
191
      As demonstrated above, diversity is a crucial characteristic of effective benchmarks. Intuitively, this
      suggests that more tasks should be included. However, if tasks consistently overlap in skill assessment
192
      (e.g., mine stone with a wooden pickaxe, mine stone with a stone pickaxe, and mine stone with a
193
      golden pickaxe Fan et al. (2022)), they merely test the same fundamental skill with minor variations.
194   This leads to an artificial inflation of task quantity without contributing meaningfully to the evaluation
195   of generalization. In our work, we introduce the concept of an atomic task, which is characterized by
196   distinct challenges aimed at promoting genuine generalization. An atomic task is defined by two core
197   properties:
198
      Goal-oriented definition. An atomic task T is a basic unit defined exclusively by its goal g,
199
      independent of the methods, tools, or specific environmental conditions. During evaluation, the
200   atomic task is instantiated, which induces a task-specific initial state distribution P(s0 |g) (see 3.2.2).
201   For example, the atomic task “mine stone” is goal-centric, and across different evaluation batches, it
202   may be instantiated into different s0 states, such as “mine stone with a wooden pickaxe” or “mine
203   stone with a stone pickaxe on the rainy day.” However, all of these instances correspond to the same
204   atomic task, ensuring the independence between different atomic tasks .
205
      Composability.VAtomic tasksWcan be combined to form more complex tasks by using logical operators
206   such as “and” ( ) and “or” ( ), or by introducing constraints like “when,” “where,” and “how.” For
207   instance, an agent could be tasked to “[mine oak log] or [mine grass] bare-handed and then [craft
208   sticks],” where “[]” denotes individual atomic tasks. This compositional approach enables a vast task
209   space to be explored, leveraging the combinatorial complexity of atomic goals.
210
      The above two properties enable us to generate endless distinctive tasks. We collect over 3,000
211
      atomic tasks3 that represent unique functionalities in Minecraft. These tasks span a wide spectrum
212   in Minecraft domain and can compose almost all the feasible tasks for junior human players. The
213   annotated atomic tasks will also be released to community and researchers can DIY their open-ended
214   tasks using atomic tasks as building blocks freely.

237   3.2.2       LLM- POWERED SCENE GENERATION
238
      For a given atomic task T , we automatically generate a task-specific initial state distribution P(s0 |T )
239
      that meets two key criteria:
240
241             • The initial state distribution exhibits high diversity.
242
                • They need to be formulated into executable files within Minecraft.
243
244   To achieve above objectives, we propose a scalable task generation pipeline powered by LLMs,
245   coupled with an automatic verification process to ensure accuracy. LLMs provide scalability beyond
246   human-written programs, generating a broader spectrum of task scenarios by integrating broad
247   knowledge and creativity.
248   As illustrated in Figure 3, we input the atomic task and few-shot examples into GPT-4O, expecting
249   it to generate a specific task description (used as instructions for LLM-based agents (Lifshitz et al.,
250   2023) and for generating evaluation criteria 3.3), along with a set of executable “cheat commands”
251   that initialize the environment configuration. This initial configuration includes various attributes such
252   as the spawn point, inventory, equipment, items in the agent’s main hand and off-hand, nearby entities,
253   time of day, weather conditions, and more. For example, if the atomic task is to mine diamonds, the
254   initial environment would include nearby diamond ore and an iron pickaxe in the agent’s inventory.
255   To increase task diversity, we introduce random additional conditions related to the task (e.g., placing
256
      other ores nearby) and shuffle item arrangements to prevent predictable patterns.
257   To address common errors generated by LLMs, we integrate soft constraints into the prompts. LLMs
258   often struggle with numerical accuracy and game-specific rules. To mitigate this, we implement
259   constraints to guide the outputs. For example, when generating scenes for crafting, where exact
260   materials are required (e.g., three wool blocks and three wooden planks), we instruct LLMs to generate
261   a surplus to account for their insensitivity to quantities. Constraints also prevent the generation of
262
      inaccessible structures (e.g., via the /fill command), maintaining environmental integrity. Essential
      elements like crafting tables and furnaces are consistently reminded to ensure usability.
263
264
      3.2.3       AUTOMATIC VERIFICATION PIPELINE
265
266   To ensure the quality of generated task, Mineflayer (PrismarineJS, 2024) is employed as a super-agent
267   to conduct task verification. We validate generated scenes by executing tasks T within an initial
268   environment s0 for a maximum duration d = 60 seconds. Let V(g, s0 , d) represent the task execution
269   process. If the agent successfully completes the task within the time limit, the scene is validated. If
      V(g, s0 , d) results in failure (i.e., the task is not completed within d), an error signal ϵ is sent back


                                                                    5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      to the LLM. This feedback, denoted as F(ϵ), prompts the LLM to generate a revised scene s′0 . The
271   process ensure that the generated scenes meet benchmark standards for accuracy and usability.

      3.3   AUTOMATIC EVALUATION
274
275   In open-world scenarios, traditional benchmarks often fall short due to the diverse and open-ended
276   nature of tasks. In this section, We introduce an automated evaluation method designed to scale
277   task assessments beyond the limitations of human judgment. Our framework consists of two main
278   components. (1) Criteria generation: establishing clear, task-specific evaluation dimensions. (2)
279   Scoring based on criteria: using these predefined dimensions to infer “scoring points” from videos of
280   agent performance (see Figure 3).
281
282   Criteria generation We define six key dimensions for evaluating agent performance:
283
284          • Task progress: measures critical steps and factors required for task completion.
285          • Action control: evaluates the agent’s ability to avoid unrelated or unnecessary actions.
286          • Material usage: evaluates the ability in the selection and application of materials.
287
288
             • Task efficiency: focuses on minimizing unnecessary repetitions and optimizing strategies.
289          • Error recognition: assesses the agent’s capacity to identify and correct its own errors.
290          • Creative attempts: recognizes innovative approaches taken by the agent in task execution.
291
292   The LLMs can autonomously generate tailored criteria for each task. This dynamic approach
293   allows for efficient, task-specific evaluation standards across a wide variety of tasks. These six
294   metrics provide a comprehensive view of the agent’s capabilities, offering insights into both strategic
295   execution and adaptive problem-solving.
296
297   Scoring with criteria Given the task T and initial states P(s0 |g), an agent A will rollout the
298   trajectories based on its policy A : (s0 ; g) 7→ (a0 , s1 , a1 , · · · , at , st ), where {si }ti=0 are past and
299   current states, and {aj }tj=0 are past and current actions. We store the agent’s rollout trajectories
300
      in video format. In the evaluation phase, we leverage the VLM to analyze agent performance. To
      optimize resource utilization, we extract one frame from every n frames of the video. While this
301
      sampling approach may result in a certain degree of performance loss, it is possible to achieve a
302
      trade-off between resource conservation and evaluative efficacy hat aligns with researcher’s specific
303   conditions.
304
305   We input the sampled frames and task-specific criteria into VLM. To ensure rigor, VLM provides
306
      evidence and explanations before assigning a score (?). It evaluates each dimension by identifying
      supporting evidence from the video to justify the rating. We define the scoring intervals for each
307
      criterion as follows: very poor, poor, fair, good, and excellent. This structured scoring scale helps the
308
      VLM intuitively interpret performance levels, promoting consistent and detailed assessments that
309   lead to more instructive resuplts.

326   different agents combined with an Elo rating system. Individual rating provides a clear and intuitive

499   5      R ELATED W ORK
500
501   Minecraft as Test Bed Various test beds exist for multimodal generalist agents, such as Alf-
502   World (Shridhar et al., 2020) and BabyAI (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2018). However, Minecraft,
503   due to its openness and high degree of freedom, serves as a crucial platform for testing generalist
504
      agents on infinite tasks, leading to the emergence of specific benchmarks. MineDojo (Fan et al., 2022)
      introduced a suite of 1560 creative tasks defined by natural language instructions, but it suffers from
505
      significant redundancy and overly complex tasks that challenge practical evaluation (Lin et al., 2023).
506
      BEDD (Milani et al., 2023) presents five tasks that cover different Minecraft aspects, primarily aimed
507   at the MineRL BASALT competition (Shah et al., 2021). By decomposing the evaluation framework,
508   BEDD enables detailed assessments of agent performance across subgoals and characteristics like
509   human likeness.
510
511   Efforts to Generalist Many agents have been developed to interact with Minecraft environ-
512   ments (Baker et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023d;a; Cai et al., 2023b). Some focus on short-term
513   task execution; for instance, Baker et al. (2022) employs imitation learning from YouTube videos,
514   enhanced by reinforcement learning for specific tasks, but it is not a multi-task agent. Lifshitz et al.
515   (2023) utilizes pretrained VPT and the vision-language model MineCLIP (Fan et al., 2022) to follow
516   human instructions. These agents typically leverage pre-trained large language models (LLMs), like
517
      GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) or ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), to generate action plans and execute
      tasks via existing low-level controllers (Wang et al., 2023d; Zhu et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023c;a;
518
      Ding et al., 2023). However, current LLMs, especially open-source models like LLaMA (Touvron
519
      et al., 2023), often lack the necessary knowledge of the Minecraft environment, highlighting the
520   importance of enhancing their knowledge base for the development of generalist agents.
521
522
      LLM-as-Judge Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) have
523   been explored as cost-effective alternatives to human evaluation. While LLMs exhibit certain biases,
524   such as position bias and verbosity bias (Shi et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023), recent advancements
525   have mitigated these issues through techniques like providing few-shot examples to calibrate the
526   models’ scoring mechanisms (Kim et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). Recently, state-of-the-art models
527   have demonstrated high agreement rates with human evaluators (Liu et al., 2023), underscoring their
528   potential to replicate human judgment in complex scenarios. The scalability and cost-efficiency
529   offered by LLM-based evaluation address critical challenges in open-world domains (Stanley et al.,
530   2017; Standish, 2003), providing a promising direction for future research and application.
531
532   6      C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we present the MCU framework, an automated benchmarking methodology that inte-
535   grates task generation, verification, and evaluation. With evaluation results achieving an agreement
536   rate exceeding 90%, it becomes possible to conduct large-scale assessments of diverse tasks. More-
537   over, MCU reveals critical limitations in the generalization capabilities of current agents, highlighting
538   the urgent need for more comprehensive and rigorous benchmarks. We anticipate that MCU will
539   contribute to the advancement of more versatile and truly generalist agents, empowering the research
      community to expand the frontiers of agent generalization.

839   C.1   C OMPLEXITY
840
841   The environment in Minecraft is highly complex, encompassing various elements such as blocks,
842   creatures, terrain, vegetation, and more. This complexity poses diverse challenges for agents in this
843   environment, requiring them to learn to adapt and address a wide array of intricate tasks. The intricate
844   nature of the environment provides generalist agents with abundant learning opportunities, enabling
845   them to flexibly navigate through different scenarios.

847   C.2   O PEN - ENDEDNESS
848
849   Minecraft offers a vast open world where players can freely explore various regions. This openness
850   exposes agents to limitless potential environments, requiring them to possess exploration and naviga-
851   tion capabilities. In an open world, a generalist agent must be able to adapt to new terrains, scenes,
852   and situations. In this open-ended environment, it becomes more convenient to select tasks with
853   varying difficulty levels, each presenting unique dimensions of challenge. This allows us to evaluate
854   the agent’s performance in a targeted manner, assessing its proficiency across various abilities.

      C.3   DYNAMISM AND U NPREDICTABILITY
857
858   Compared to some static, text-based, or vision-language based test environments, the dynamism and
859   unpredictability of Minecraft provide unique advantages for the training and testing of intelligent
860   agents. The in-game environment is filled with dynamic changes and unknown factors, including
861   day-night cycles, the random appearance of creatures, diverse terrains, and more. This dynamism
862   and unpredictability necessitate that the agents adapt flexibly to various scenarios and possess the
863   ability to handle unexpected events, thereby better cultivating their generalization skills for complex
      environments.

      C.4   C REATIVITY AND I NNOVATION
865
866
      Minecraft allows for a high degree of creativity and innovation. The abundance of open-ended tasks,
867   such as construction and decoration, provides agents with ample space to unleash their creativity. By
868   exploring various ways to achieve goals, agents cultivate innovative abilities in addressing diverse
869   and complex challenges.

872   C.5   B ROAD CHALLENGE COVERAGE
873
874   Minecraft, with its outstanding freedom and depth of tasks in an open-world setting, is well-suited as
875   a training and testing platform for a generalist agent. In general, agents in the Minecraft environment
876
      may encounter the following four primary challenges.
877
878   Long-horizon Decision Making In the Minecraft environment, tasks can break into a sequence of
879   subtasks. For instance, to achieve the goal of mining diamonds, players need to complete various
880   subtasks including chopping the trees, crafting a crafting table, obtaining a pickaxe, and searching
881   for diamonds. The sequences of subtasks for a task are not necessarily the same; rather, they can
882   be entirely different. For example, to complete the task of ”obtaining wool,” the typical approach
883   is to kill sheep. However, if there are no sheep nearby, players might need to kill spiders to obtain
884   string, then use the string to craft wool, or even directly trade with villagers for wool. Different
885
      environments lead to different subtask sequences, placing a challenge on the agent’s ability for
      long-horizon decision-making.
886
887   In this context, agents must have the capability to predict and plan future environments and actions,
888   instead of mere reactions to the current state. Long-horizon decision-making in Minecraft requires
889   agents to understand the complex spatial and temporal relationships within the game environment. The
890   diverse and dynamic nature of tasks, combined with the multitude of possible approaches, demands
891
      that agents develop a comprehensive understanding of the environment to effectively navigate through
      various steps and reach their goals.
892
893
894   Precise Control As a well-known sandbox construction game, Minecraft allows players to engage
895   in intricate building and operations. Therefore, tasks related to building and crafting are important
896   components of the Minecraft task list. This task always involves precise movement, accurate block
897   placement, and destruction. For example, the task ”building a nether portal” requires the agent to
898   build at least a 2 × 3 rectangular frame with specific blocks. If the player placed the block in a wrong
899   position, they should mine that wrong block with the pickaxe and place it again. This demands
900   precise and accurate control. RL agents need to handle high-dimensional action spaces and achieve
901
      precise control in the environment to accomplish complex tasks. This presents a challenge for the
      stability and precision of the agent.
902
903
904   Out-of-distribution Generalization The Minecraft environment is dynamic, filled with various
905   possible scenarios and conditions. The terrain, ecology, organisms, and even the weather are ever-
906   changing, and it’s impossible for the agent to encompass all of them in the training data. On the
907   other hand, due to the fact that the vast majority of training data consists of reasonable behaviors, the
908   model’s free exploration or learning errors make it prone to encountering environments not present in
909   the training data, such as falling into the lava when mining the diamond. How to enable the model
910
      to generalize to out-of-distribution environments and adapt to the complexity of the ever-changing
      open-world is a notable challenge.
911
912
913   Compositional Generalization To adapt to the long-horizon and varied tasks in Minecraft, the
914   model needs to have the ability of compositional generalization. For example, when the training set
915   includes data crafting sticks from planks and crafting ladders from sticks, we hope the model can
916   generalize the ability to craft ladders from planks. The Minecraft environment offers nearly infinite
917   combinations of tasks, with the majority of them not appearing in the training set. Accomplishing
      these tasks poses a significant challenge to the model’s compositional generalization capability.

1676        The following case clarifies the impact of each metric on evaluating generalization performance.
1677        Metrics such as task progress and material selection assess basic task alignment, while action
1678        control and task efficiency provide insights into optimization strategies. Error correction and creative
1679        attempts, in contrast, measure higher-order generalization skills. These are critical for assessing
1680        agents in open-ended and complex scenarios, as they reveal resilience to failure and capacity for
1681        novel strategies.
1682        While Video B outperformed Video A across most metrics, the weaknesses in creativity and error
1683        correction indicate areas where even high-performing agents fall short. Incorporating tailored training
1684        modules and broader tasks emphasizing these dimensions will enhance the benchmark’s utility for
1685        developing and evaluating generalist agents.
