009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing alignment methods, such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), are
012            mainly tailored for pairwise preference data where rewards are implicitly defined
013            rather than explicitly given. In this paper, we introduce a general framework
014            for large language model alignment, leveraging a novel optimization objective
015            to bridge the gap in handling reward datasets with a list of responses explicitly an-
016            notated with scalar preferences scores. Our work comprise a novel algorithm, soft
017            preference optimization, SPO, which enables the direct extraction of an LM policy
018
               from reward data as well as preference data. The core of SPO is a novel listwise
               preference optimization objective with the exponential-logarithm function form
019
               and a adaptive loss coefficient that inject listwise preference signals into the large
020
               language model. We evaluate our methods in both reward and preference settings
021            with Mistral models in different sizes. Experiments suggest that our method sur-
022            passes various preference baselines when reward datasets are available. We also
023            find our method significantly outperforms DPO in complex reasoning tasks like
024            math and coding.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Aligning pretrained Language Models (LMs) with scalar rewards that reflect human intentions is
030   crucial for enhancing their ability to follow instructions (Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023). These
031   rewards can be given either explicitly or implicitly. Explicit rewards can be scalar ratings of hu-
032   man annotators or advanced models like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), while implicit rewards are usually
033
      preference labels assigned to pairwise responses. One effective approach for aligning LMs with
      preference data is Direct Preference Optimization (DPO, Rafailov et al. (2024b)). DPO applies a
034
      reward training loss but parameterizes the reward model as the response likelihood ratio between
035
      two LMs, allowing for training reward models and extracting LM policies simultaneously. This
036   approach is more streamlined and thus more favorable compared with traditional Reinforcement
037   Learning (RL) methods Ouyang et al. (2022), which typically require a two-stage training process:
038   first training reward models, then extracting LM policies.
039
      Despite its simplicity and effectiveness, DPO is only tailored for preference data (x → {yw > yl }
040
      with K = 2 responses per instruction x. When multiple responses are available, directly assigning
041
      a scalar reward to each response is usually more convenient and efficient than comparing them in
042   a pairwise manner. The resulting reward datasets (x → {(yi , ri )}1:K ), however, cannot be directly
043   leveraged for DPO training. Previous work Tunstall et al. (2023) usually prunes reward datasets
044   by selecting the best response and pairing it with a random remaining one. This is suboptimal as
045   all reward values and additional dispreferred responses are thrown away in its data-preprocessing
046   process. In addition, a well-observed problem with DPO is that the likelihood of the preferred
047   response tends to decrease throughout training Pal et al. (2024); Rafailov et al. (2024a). We find this
048   issue arises mainly from DPO’s focus on adjusting the relative likelihood across different responses
049   per instruction.
050   To address these issues, we present Soft Preference Optimization (SPO), an alignment method that
051   allows directly extracting LM policies from both reward datasets and preference datasets with arbi-
052   trary response numbers. Notably, SPO subsumes DPO loss as a special case under pairwise pref-
053   erence settings and can thus be seen as a natural extension of DPO. We show DPO is a binary
      classification loss while SPO is its multi-category version. However, unlike DPO which is built

      upon assumptions of Bradley-Terry models, SPO derives from Information Noise Contrastive Esti-
055   mation (InfoNCE, (Oord et al., 2018)) and knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015). We further
056   propose SPO-abs, an alternative alignment method to SPO, which adds a regularization term for the
057   absolute values to mitigate the decreasing reward issue. SPO-abs differs from SPO by only loss
058   definition and is also suitable for both preference and reward datasets.
059
      We evaluate our methods on the Mistral-7B models from two dimensions. When reward datasets
060
      (Cui et al.) are available, we show that directly applying our reward-based alignment offers clear
061   improvement compared with preference-based algorithms, achieving higher evaluation performance
062   in evaluations conducted by the GPT-4 model as a judge. We further validate this improvement
063   comes from SPO/SPO-abs’s ability to fully leverage the additional sub-optimal responses. When
064   only preference data is given (Yuan et al., 2024b), we compare the SPO-abs method against the
065   DPO loss. Our experimental results spanning various benchmarks show that SPO-abs outperforms
066   DPO in complex reasoning tasks such as math and coding.
067   Our main contributions are:
068
069             • We propose SPO, a preference optimization method based on the reward datasets. In addi-
070               tion, SPO-abs resolves the data likelihood decline issue of DPO. The two proposed methods
071               are uniquely suited for both reward and preference data, offering a general framework that
072               integrates preference-based algorithms.
073             • We conduct experiments on both reward datasets and preference datasets, demonstrating
074               that our methods can outperforms various preference methods by fully exploiting data in-
075               formation in reward datasets.

      Development of language models.         Recently, the development of language models has under-
080
      gone significant changes (Zhao et al., 2023), evolving from initial rule-based approaches to to-
081
      day’s data-driven deep learning models. A milestone advancement began in 2018 when Google
082   introduced the BERT model (Devlin et al., 2019) with a Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al.,
083   2017), which achieved remarkable results in various natural language processing tasks through un-
084   supervised training. Following this, even larger models like GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), ChatGPT
085   (Ouyang et al., 2022) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) further propelled this trend, not only reaching
086   unprecedented scales with hundreds of billions of parameters but also demonstrating versatility and
087   flexibility across a diverse range of NLP tasks. These advancements have spurred researchers to
088   explore applications of LLMs in areas such as machine translation, text summarization, dialogue
089   systems, and even multimodal understanding.
090   While it is important to develop large language models in the size of 100 billions, the even wider
091   applications rely on the relatively smaller sized models. These compact models, while perhaps not
092   matching the sheer breadth of knowledge or depth of understanding afforded by their gargantuan
093   counterparts (Zeng, 2023), offer significant advantages in terms of deployment flexibility, com-
094   putational efficiency, and accessibility across diverse platforms and devices, including those with
095   limited resources. Consequently, efforts aimed at refining smaller models to optimize both efficacy
096
      and practicality are critical for broadening the reach and utility of NLP solutions in everyday con-
      texts. Recently, the community has witnessed the releases of open-sourced models like LlaMA-2
097
      7B (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023). These models can run an edge device like
098
      laptop or cellphone, after being deployed under frameworks like Ollama1 .
099
100   Alignment of large language model. The alignment of large language models (LLMs) is critical
101   for ensuring they operate ethically, accurately, and responsibly Shen et al. (2023). Proper alignment
102
      helps prevent the generation of harmful or biased content, promotes factual correctness and reliabil-
      ity, and supports legal compliance and user trust Wang et al. (2023b). Additionally, it ensures that
103
      LLMs contribute positively to society by respecting social values, enhancing educational integrity,
104
      and maintaining accessibility and inclusivity for all users. As these models become more integrated
105   into daily life, their alignment with societal norms and expectations becomes increasingly important
106   to foster beneficial interactions and outcomes.

      Current approaches cater to either explicit reward data or preference data, often lacking the versa-
109   tility to address both concurrently. Reinforcement Learning (Schulman et al., 2017) is inherently
110   suitable for explicit reward scenarios. However, its on-policy nature necessitates learning a reward
111   model from data first, leading to an indirect two-stage optimization process (Christiano et al., 2017;
112   Ouyang et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2024). Recent developments in preference-based alignment tech-
113   niques (Rafailov et al., 2024b; Azar et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Hong
114   et al., 2024) have streamlined this process. They enable direct alignment of LMs through a singular
115   loss, but this comes at the expense of being confined to pairwise preference data. Other alignment
116   approaches (Yuan et al., 2024a; Song et al., 2024) are also not tailored for aligning with reward
117
      datasets. Recent work (Cai et al., 2023) attempts to extend DPO’s parameterization technique to
      explicit reward contexts. However, it only considers binary rewards. In comparison, our methods
118
      can handle both continuous rewards and preference data.

152   4.1   P REFERENCE DATA VERSUS REWARD DATA
153
154   Compared with constructing preference datasets, annotating each response with scalar rewards can
155   be more flexible and convenient. Preference methods are only suitable for pairwise data (x →
                                           2
156   {yw > yl }) and would require CK        evaluations for comparing K responses. In contrast, reward
157   datasets (x → {yi , ri }1:K ) allow an arbitrary number of responses per prompt with K evaluations.
158   Despite its simplicity in handling preference data, DPO is not tailored for reward datasets. We
159   introduce a new alignment method termed SPO to mitigate this gap. We show that reward alignment
160   can be solved by constructing a classification problem to identify the optimal response from multiple
161   candidates. We then demonstrate that SPO subsumes DPO as a special case and thus is a natural

      4.4    A BSOLUTE VALUE REGULARIZATION
237   A well-observed issue with DPO is that the likelihood of all responses continually decrease through-
238   out training (Pal et al., 2024; Rafailov et al., 2024a). We find SPO shares this trend due to their
239   inherent equivalence. Decreased data likelihood is unsatisfying because it directly contradicts the
240   maximum likelihood objective for supervised training and may eventually harm performance (Yuan
241   et al., 2024b).
242
      We hypothesize the main cause of this decreasing likelihood is that SPO methods only adjust relative
243   rewards among responses, rather than optimizing their absolute value. To address this problem, we
244   take inspiration from NCE (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2012), and propose SPO-abs, a variant to SPO.
245   SPO-abs can also guarantee convergence to the optimal LM policy by directly learning the absolute

      Reward datasets We employ the UltraFeedback (Cui et al.) dataset, an instruction-following
279   dataset annotated by GPT-4. UltraFeedback aims at advancing the alignment of large language
280   models (LLMs) with human preferences through the use of large-scale, high-quality, and diversi-
281   fied feedback datasets. Utilizing a vast dataset of approximately 64,000 prompts and 256,000 re-
282   sponses from various high-quality sources, UltraFeedback evaluates LLM responses on criteria such
283   as instruction-following, truthfulness, honesty, and helpfulness. GPT-4 rates each response with a
284   scalar reward on a scale of 0-10. Prior research indicates that these GPT-4 rewards closely align with
285   human annotations (Zheng et al., 2023), establishing them as an efficient, cost-effective alternative
286   to human feedback. This dataset is used to train models to better serve user needs while maintaining
287
      ethical standards.
288   Evaluation datasets We choose the well-acknowledged and widely used GPT4-based bench-
289   marks as follows for evaluating LLMs:
290
291          • MT-bench (Zheng et al., 2023). MT-Bench is a benchmark designed to evaluate the per-
292
               formance of chat assistants across multiple turns of conversation and various categories of
               tasks. It consists of 80 multi-turn questions covering common use cases that are challenging
293
               enough to differentiate among models. These questions are categorized into eight types of
294
               user prompts, including Writing, Roleplay, Extraction, Reasoning, Math, Coding, STEM,
295            and Humanities/Social Science. Each category includes 10 multi-turn questions crafted
296            carefully by experts. To assess the models’ performance on MT-Bench, strong language
297            models (LLMs) are used as judges, and the results indicate that these LLM judges, partic-
298            ularly GPT-4, can align well with both controlled and crowdsourced human preferences,
299            achieving over 80% agreement, which is comparable to the level of agreement between
300            humans. Thus, MT-Bench serves as a comprehensive tool to measure the effectiveness of
301            chat assistants, complementing traditional benchmarks.
302          • AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024) is an LLM-based automated evaluation metric consisting
303            of a set of 805 instructions that reflect typical user interactions on the Alpaca web demo.
304            Both a baseline model, typically GPT-4 turbo, and the model under evaluation generate
305            responses for each instruction, which are then compared side-by-side by another GPT-4
306            turbo-based evaluator that outputs the likelihood of preferring the evaluated model’s re-
307            sponse. The metric calculates a win rate, representing the expected probability that the
308
               evaluated model’s output is preferred over the baseline across these instructions. Initially
               designed for the Alpaca chatbot and AlpacaFarm simulator, AlpacaEval was intended to
309
               mitigate certain biases, such as presentation order, by randomizing sequences. However,
310
               other factors like length bias were not controlled for, leading to potential manipulation by
311            AI systems. To address this, Length-Controlled AlpacaEval was introduced, which em-
312            ploys a regression-based method to adjust for biases by controlling for length differences,
313            thereby improving the robustness and correlation with human evaluations.
314

318   On the AlpacaEval dataset, we compare the LLM against the reference responses from the Davinci-

463   5.3   F URTHER ANALYSIS
464
465
      Visualization of likelihood for the chosen and rejected To demonstrate the distinct optimization
466
      characteristics that cause performance differences between SPO-abs and DPO, we inspect how the
467
      data likelihood changes during training. The results are shown in Figure 1. As shown in Figure 1.
468   The likelihood of preferred responses interestingly decreases after DPO training and increases for
469   SPO-abs training. The decreasing chosen likelihood trend is concerning because it directly contra-
470   dicts the maximum-likelihood objective used during the SFT stage. This drawback is exacerbated
471   in reasoning tasks, where the preferred response is often the ground truth answer. Consequently,
472   we hypothesize that SPO-abs’ superior performance in reasoning tasks is due to its ability to avoid
473   decreasing chosen likelihood. Since DPO is essentially a specialization of SPO, their contrasting
474   likelihood trends can be explained theoretically. SPO-abs adjusts the absolute likelihood of data,
475   while DPO or SPO only considers relative likelihood across different responses. Thus, a declining
476
      chosen likelihood directly contradicts SPO-abs’s training objective but not DPO’s.
477   Effects of the number of responses K Figure 2 plots how different values of K affects the
478   LLM alignment performance. From Figure 2, we observe consistent performance improvements
479   when increasing the number of data responses from K = 2 to K = 4 for both SPO and SPO-
480   abs algorithms. In addition, we observe the SPO-abs outperforms SPO under different settings,
481   demonstrating the importance of maintaining the absolute values for the best response’s reward.
482   Figure 2 also conveys an important take-away. Previous practices always ensure selecting the highest
483   performing response when constructing preference data. The assumption behind this strategy is
484   that the dataset’s best-performing response determines the upper limit of alignment performance.
485   However, our experiments contradict this assumption. Results in Figure 2 indicate that extra sub-
      optimal responses can also be advantageous for policy training.

487
488
      In this work, we thoroughly investigate the language model alignment problem in the context of
489   explicit reward settings. By drawing inspirations from knowledge distillation, we introduce two
490   novel and practical algorithms: SPO and SPO-abs. Our proposed methods are uniquely suited for
491   both reward data and preference data, including DPO as a special case. Our experiments show that
492   our reward-based alignment methods, SPO/SPO-abs, outperform the preference baselines like DPO
493   and KTO by fully leveraging sub-optimal responses in reward datasets. In preference settings, the
494   pairwise version of SPO-abs outperforms DPO in complex reasoning tasks by effectively preventing
495   data likelihood from decreasing.
