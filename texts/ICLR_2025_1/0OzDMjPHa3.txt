009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            An implicit neural representation (INR) is a neural network that approximates a
012            function over space and possibly time. Memory-intensive visualization tasks, in-
013            cluding modern 4D CT scanning methods, represent data natively as INRs. While
014            such INRs are prized for being more memory-efficient than traditional data on a
015            lattice, discretization to a regular grid is still required for many visualization tasks.
016            We present an algorithm to store high-resolution voxel data only for regions with
017            significant detail, reducing memory requirements. To identify these high-detail
018
               areas, we use an interpolative decomposition pruning method on the weight ma-
               trices of the INR. The information from pruning is used to guide adaptive mesh
019
               refinement, allowing automatic mesh generation, tailored to the underlying reso-
020
               lution of the function. From a pre-trained INR with no access to its training data,
021            we produce a variable resolution visualization with significant memory savings.
022
023
024   1   I NTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION
025
026   Implicit neural representations (INRs) have gained traction in recent years for their ability to repre-
027   sent spatial and time-varying spatial data efficiently. While INRs are best known for their fast and ac-
028   curate visualization applications, these methods only apply to specific neural graphics primitives—
029   such as signed distance functions—and require training routines and data structures—such as hash-
030   ing techniques—to realize interactive visualization. For INRs encoding data not derived from graph-
031   ics primitives, the recourse for visual analysis is to discretize the INR to a uniform grid, thereby
032   enabling traditional techniques, but eliminating any computational savings afforded by the INR
033
      encoding. This presents an open challenge to communities using INRs in new contexts: given a
      pre-trained INR, how can the information encoded be visualized efficiently?
034
035   The need for efficient visualization of INR data is evidenced by emergent “dynamic micro-CT”
036   technology for additive manufacturing. Recently developed methodologies are capable of storing
037   time-varying volumetric data of materials undergoing physical changes as an INR with (x, y, z, t)
038   inputs. In one example, the size of an INR checkpoint file is on the order a few megabytes, but the
039
      potential resolution of the time-varying volume is 1024 × 1024 × 1024 × 700, roughly 3.6 terabytes
      worth of data in a uniform discretization, well beyond the capabilities of common visualization
040
      software. By visual inspection of time slices, many regions of the domain are of low variation while
041
      some regions require maximum resolution for subject matter expert evaluation. Hence, an approach
042   to adaptively sample the INR in a way that preserves fine-grained details of the function is of real
043   interest to practitioners with immediate benefits to dynamic micro-CT technology.
044
045
      In this paper, we present an algorithm that visualizes a pre-trained INR on an adaptive mesh, achiev-
      ing accuracy comparable to a uniform mesh while using less memory. The algorithm begins with
046
      a coarse uniform mesh of the domain and iteratively refines elements in which the INR is expected
047
      to encode finer-scale information. We assume knowledge of the INR architecture, as would be en-
048   coded in a standard checkpoint file, but we do not assume access to any training data; the algorithm
049   determines where to refine based solely on the weight matrices of the INR. The refinement decision
050   for a given element is based on a “pruning” method applied on the INR, restricted to the element’s
051   domain. Elements for which significant pruning is possible with small loss in accuracy are presumed
052   to have low-rank representations and therefore deemed sufficiently refined. Conversely, elements for
053   which significant pruning is not possible, or for which pruning causes significant information loss,
      are flagged for refinement.

      2.1   I MPLICIT N EURAL R EPRESENTATIONS
057
058   An implicit neural representation (INR) is a type of neural network that approximates a scalar- or
059   vector-valued field with inputs representing physical space or spacetime coordinates. The origi-
060   nal use of INRs in the context of visualization was to efficiently store an implicit representation of
061   an image (Sitzmann et al., 2020), but interest in the technique quickly grew to include volumet-
062   ric visualizations as well (Mildenhall et al., 2021). The output of the popular physically-informed
063   neural network (PINN) technique for approximating solutions to partial differential equations is a
064   coordinate-valued, multi-layer perceptron (typically), and hence could also be called an INR (Kar-
065
      niadakis et al., 2021).
066   The appeal of INRs over traditional discretization is, to quote Sitzmann et al. (2020), the network’s
067   “ability to model fine detail that is not limited by the grid resolution but by the capacity of the
068   underlying network architecture.” Only the weights and biases of the INR need to be stored in order
069   to recover the value of the field at the highest level of detail anywhere in the represented domain.
070   Accordingly, the INR data structure takes up orders of magnitude less storage than an equivalent
071
      standard representation. Still, the savings in data storage come with a tradeoff: evaluating the INR
      can only be done “pointwise”, meaning discretization and interpolation over a fixed grid of some
072
      type is required to employ standard visualization software for all but very speficic types of INR data.
073
074
075   2.2   V ISUALIZATION AND DISCRETIZATION
076
      While our work is related to both visualization using INRs and traditional data discretization meth-
077
      ods, neither of the associated research communities offers a solution to the problem we are address-
078   ing. Much of the visualization work on INRs focuses on methods to train INRs more efficiently,
079   such as ACORN (Martel et al., 2021), scene representation networks (Wurster et al., 2023), and
080   Instant-NGP (Wurster et al., 2023). None of these works, however, addresses the question of how
081   to process, analyze, or efficiently visualize a pre-trained INR. A separate body of work looks at
082   efficient management and visualization of data stored on adaptive meshes, such as multi-functional
083   approximation (Peterka et al., 2023), CPU ray tracing (Wang et al., 2020), and p4est (Burstedde
084   et al., 2011). These works presume data is provided on an adaptive mesh as input to their use cases,
085   rather than as a pre-trained INR.
086   We treat INRs as a native data format, akin to a compressed version of a much larger dataset. The
087   input to our method is a user-provided INR, with no access to the training data. As output, we
088   produce an adaptive mesh on which the INR has been sampled at vertices, allowing subsequent
089   visualization and analysis via established techniques. To the best of our knowledge, there is no prior
090   work considering this problem, other than sampling to a uniform grid.
091
092   2.3   P RUNING VIA INTERPOLATIVE DECOMPOSITION OF WEIGHT MATRICES
093
094   “Pruning” refers to the process of selectively removing weights and biases from a trained neural net-
095   work in a way that preserves its mapping from inputs to outputs; see, e.g. (Li et al., 2016; Lee et al.,
096
      2018; Liu et al., 2018; Liebenwein et al., 2019; Mussay et al., 2019). We use the pruning method
      of Chee et al. (2022), which merges neurons in each layer whose contributions to the output are close
097
      to a linear combination. The method for detection of such neurons employs a structured low-rank
098
      approximation called an “interpolative decomposition” (ID). We selected this pruning method due
099   to its theoretical guarantees, ease of implementation, and few number of hyperparameters.
100
101   We fix notation before describing the ID pruning method. In this work, we only consider INRs that
102
      consist of fully-connected linear layers. Hence, each layer takes as input x ∈ Rn , provides an output
      y ∈ Rm and has corresponding weight matrix W ∈ Rm×n and bias vector b ∈ Rm . We treat the
103
      inputs x and outputs y as row vectors and assume that the output of the layer is computed as
104
105                                             y = g(xW T + b),
106
107   where g is the activation function used for the layer. If a collection of ℓ inputs is provided, we still
      use x to denote the ℓ × n matrix of inputs yielding an output y ∈ Rℓ .


                                                         2
108
      An ID of W is a decomposition of the form W ≈ W:,I T , where I ⊆ {1, 2, ..., m}, |I| = k,
109
      and T ∈ Rk×n is called the “interpolation matrix.” For the ease of exposition, suppose the neural
110   network has a single hidden layer with output layer weight matrix U and output layer bias vector c.
111   Then, the output of the network with input x is N N (x) := Z(x)U T +c, where Z(x) = g(xW T +b)
112   is the output of the hidden layer.
113
      Let Z(x) ≈ Z:,I T be an ID of Z(x). We then have
114
115                                       Z(x) ≈ Z:,I T
116                                             = g(xW T + b):,I T
117
118
                                                = g(x(WI,: )T + bI )T.
119   Thus, the output of the full network with pruned hidden layer is
120
                                     N N (x) = g(x(WI,: )T + bI )T U T + c
121
122                                           = g(xW̄ T + b̄)Ū T + c,
123   where we define W̄ := WI,: , b̄ := bI , and Ū := U T T to be the new weights and biases of the
124   pruned network. Thus, pruning a layer not only affects the weights and bias of that layer, but also
125   the weights of the following layer. The result of pruning a given layer to rank k is that the resulting
126   pruned layer has k neurons. The following layer’s weights are updated to accept the new, smaller
127   number of inputs coming from the previous layer.
128
      Given ε > 0, the goal of pruning is to find I and T such that ∥W − W:,I T ∥2 ≤ ε∥W ∥2 , with |I|
129   as small as possible. We use the rank-revealing QR factorization approach from Chee et al. (2022)
130   to carry this out. For neural networks with more than one hidden layer, IDs for each layer’s weight
131   matrix can be computed in parallel, but the final weights of the pruned network must be determined
132   sequentially from the ID of the first layer forward.

      3   A LGORITHM
135
136
      The goal of our algorithm is to visualize an INR without computing and storing the complete fine-
137
      scale voxel data necessary to see high resolution details. By finding a suitable adaptive mesh for
138   visualization, we avoid expending compute time and memory evaluating regions of the INR domain
139   that are less detailed. We use the word detailed to describe a region of the domain where the function
140   has large variation, which would be harder to fit accurately with a neural network of few parameters.
141
      Since we only presume access to the weights and biases of the INR, we cannot easily determine
142
      regions of high variation. Instead, we rely on the hypothesis that the less detailed a function is on
143
      a region of the domain, the smaller an INR needs to be to accurately describe the function in that
144   region. If this hypothesis holds true, a less-detailed region of the INR should admit more pruning
145   with minimal loss in accuracy over that region . Furthermore, we observed that an INR evaluated
146   on small subsets of a domain can generally be pruned much more than for the whole domain. Thus,
147   if a region of the domain is not very prunable, then by splitting it into more, smaller regions, the
148   sub-regions are more likely to be prunable. This also makes sense because we are asking the INR
149   to describe less information if we restrict it to a smaller domain, so we expect to be able to use a
150   smaller network to do so. This is the motivation for our algorithm.
151   To decide which regions to check for prunability (i.e., the proportion of neurons that can be pruned
152   while maintaining an accuracy threshold), we start with an initial mesh on the INR’s domain and use
153   adaptive mesh refinement (AMR) to subdivide some elements into smaller ones. We keep refining
154   elements until the proportion of neurons left after pruning is below a threshold, which we denote
155   P , and the relative error of the pruned INR is less than a desired value, which we denote T . We
156   check both of these thresholds to ensure that a small network can accurately represent the INR on
157   that domain.

228   We consider two alternatives to Algorithm 1 for comparison: Uniform refinement and Basic
229   adaptive refinement. The Uniform method carries out refinement on every element until a maxi-
230   mum number of iterations are reached. The Basic method takes in an integer error samples and
231   a threshold τ . The inner loop of Algorithm 1 is replaced by drawing error samples random points
232   in E, computing the mean relative error of the INR with respect to the bilinear interpolant on E at
233   those points, and refining if the relative error is larger than τ . Note that the relative error computed
234   in Basic is distinct from the relative error of the pruned INR computed in Algorithm 1, hence, τ
235   should not be equated with T .

478   5   C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper we presented an algorithm for finding a variable-resolution visualization of pre-trained
481   implicit neural representations (INRs) with significant memory savings over existing methods. The
482   algorithm uses neural network pruning to determine which regions of the INR’s domain require
483   higher resolution, then uses adaptive mesh refinement to split up the domain into regions of higher
484   and lower resolution. We compared our algorithm to uniform resolution and a simpler variable
485   resolution algorithm; we demonstrated that our Pruning AMR algorithm achieves similar error
      tolerances to these other methods despite using many fewer degrees of freedom. However, we


                                                        9
486
      also observed that our algorithm is less beneficial for INRs that are detailed throughout their entire
487   domain, unless they are refined for many iterations. In the future, we wish to explore this direction
488   by testing our algorithm using GPUs so that we have the capacity to run for more iterations (and thus
489   DOFs). We also plan to test the algorithm on larger examples, such as INRs trained on full videos.
