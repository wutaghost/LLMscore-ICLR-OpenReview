011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Information Bottleneck (IB) theory provides a principled approach to analyze and
014            optimize how neural networks extract and learn latent representations from data,
015
               aiming to enhance network performance and generalization. The IB framework
               has been applied and validated across various domains in deep learning. How-
016
               ever, most studies employing IB require tuning of Lagrange multipliers to bal-
017
               ance compression and prediction during optimization. Finding the optimal La-
018            grange multiplier β to achieve the best balance between compression and pre-
019            diction is challenging, relying heavily on empirical tuning and potentially failing
020            to capture the complex trade-offs present within the IB paradigm. In this pa-
021            per, we redefine the IB problem as a multi-objective optimization problem with
022            respect to compression and prediction objectives. We employ a gradient-based
023            multi-objective optimization algorithm that adaptively determines the weights for
024            this optimization challenge. Our method is demonstrated to automatically find
025            Pareto-optimal solutions, achieving a balance between compression and predic-
026
               tion, and exploring more complex Pareto frontiers than linear weighting. We
               compare our approach with the Variational Information Bottleneck and its vari-
027
               ants across different datasets. Empirical results confirm that our method achieves
028
               a more stable and optimal trade-off compared to Information Bottleneck ap-
029            proaches with manually-tuned multipliers. The code is available in https:
030            //anonymous.4open.science/r/ASDGASDG.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Neural networks are powerful learning models that can adapt to complex patterns in data and excel
036   at various tasks Blazek & Lin (2021); Kriegeskorte & Golan (2019); Dai (2021); Sutskever et al.
037   (2014). How do highly parameterized neural networks exhibit good fit performance as well as
038   generalization while memorizing datasets? One possible answer is that neural networks can learn
039
      latent representations from datasets Dabagia et al. (2022); Kilinc & Uysal (2018); Yang et al. (2022).
      Latent representations are features that capture fundamental and hidden information about the data,
040
      such as shapes, colors, or textures in images, or themes, sentiments, or styles in text Ye & Shen
041
      (2020); Pati & Lerch (2021); Bau et al. (2017). By learning these latent representations, neural
042   networks can ignore irrelevant or noisy details and focus on what matters for the task Schneider
043   et al. (2023); Dabagia et al. (2022).
044
045
      A longstanding issue in deep learning is understanding the nature and quality of these latent rep-
      resentations and how neural networks learn them from data Hafner et al. (2019); Mo et al. (2020);
046
      Wieczorek et al. (2018). Good latent representations are those that capture the underlying features
047
      and structure of the data while discarding irrelevant and noisy details Zhang et al. (2017); Kim et al.
048   (2020). Learning such representations is crucial for achieving high performance and generalization
049   in various deep learning tasks, including image classification, natural language processing, and rein-
050   forcement learning Mo et al. (2020); Gelada et al. (2019); Ye & Bors (2020). However, it is not yet
051   clear how neural networks learn these representations from data and what factors influence the qual-
052   ity and efficiency of the learned representations. How can neural networks be guided to learn more
053   optimized representations? One possible answer is through the use of Information Bottleneck the-
      ory Harremoës & Tishby (2007); Tishby & Zaslavsky (2015), which provides a rigorous framework

      for examining the trade-off between the complexity and utility of latent representations in terms of
055   mutual information. The Information Bottleneck method aims to find a succinct representation of
056   the input random variable X that preserves relevant information about the target variable Y , given
057   their joint probability distribution p(X, Y ) Geiger & Kubin (2020); Li & Liu (2021).

063   (2022), self-supervised learning of graphs Wu et al. (2020); Alemi et al. (2017); Gu et al. (2022),

065   these studies benefit from the IB framework Razeghi et al. (2023); Zhai & Zhang (2022); Dai et al.

141   2.1    P ROBLEM R EFORMULATION
142
143   To comprehensively understand the trade-off between compression and prediction, we reformulate
144   the Information Bottleneck problem as a multi-objective optimization problem. Here, we recall the
145   classic Information Bottleneck objective function:
146                                        min I(X; Z) − βI(Y ; Z),                                    (1)
147                                       p(Z|X)
148
      where I(X; Z) represents the mutual information between input X and latent representation Z,
149
      measuring compression loss; I(Y ; Z) represents the mutual information between target Y and latent
150   representation Z, measuring predictive power. β is the trade-off parameter balancing these two
151   terms.
152
153
      Now, we reformulate Equation 1 as a multi-objective optimization problem. Given input variable
      X ∈ X , target variable Y ∈ Y, and latent representation Z ∈ Z, our goal is to find a set of Pareto-
154
      optimal solutions in the probability distribution space P(Z|X ) that simultaneously minimize the
155
      following two objective functions:
156
157                                 min            L(Z) = (I(X; Z), −I(Y ; Z))                         (2)
                               p(Z|X)∈P(Z|X )
158
159   Here, I(X; Z) corresponds to compression loss, i.e., the information loss from input X to latent
160   representation Z; −I(Y ; Z) corresponds to prediction loss, i.e., the negative mutual information
161   from latent representation Z to target Y . We multiply the second term by -1 to transform it into a
      minimization problem. Equation 2 explicitly describes the multi-objective nature of the IB problem,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      namely minimizing mutual information between input and latent representation while maximizing
163   mutual information between latent representation and target.
164
165   Unlike the original single-objective formulation (Equation 1), the multi-objective formulation
166
      (Equation 2) treats compression loss and prediction loss as two independent optimization objec-
      tives, rather than combining them into a single scalar through the trade-off parameter β. This al-
167
      lows us to directly search for and approximate the complete Pareto frontier describing the optimal
168
      compression-prediction trade-off, rather than obtaining only a single trade-off point corresponding
169   to a specific β value. In the next section, we will formally define the Pareto-optimal frontier for the
170   Information Bottleneck and discuss the limitations of the traditional Lagrangian relaxation method.
171
172   2.2    PARETO -O PTIMAL F RONTIER
173
174   Based on the above multi-objective optimization problem, we can formally define the Pareto-optimal
175   frontier for the Information Bottleneck. Let F ⊆ R2 represent the objective space, where each point
176   l = (I(X; Z), −I(Y ; Z)) ∈ F represents the loss values of a feasible solution on the two objectives.
177   We say that point l1 dominates point l2 if and only if I 1 (X; Z) ≤ I 2 (X; Z) and I 1 (Y ; Z) ≥
178
      I 2 (Y ; Z), with at least one inequality being strict. A point l∗ ∈ F is called Pareto-optimal if it is
      not dominated by any other point. The Pareto-optimal frontier F ∗ for the information bottleneck is
179
      the set of all Pareto-optimal points:
180
181                              F ∗ = {l∗ ∈ F|l ∈ F, such that l dominates l∗ }.                         (3)
182   The Pareto frontier delineates the optimal trade-off curve between compression and prediction
183   losses. It provides a global perspective for analyzing the IB problem, revealing the inherent conflicts
184   and dependencies between the two objectives. Moreover, each solution on the frontier corresponds
185   to specific task requirements or preferences, thus having practical significance in different applica-
186   tion scenarios. This aligns with the motivation of the Information Bottleneck, which is to maximize
187
      predictive performance under given compression constraints, or to minimize compression loss under
      given predictive performance requirements.
188
189
190
      2.3    L IMITATIONS OF THE T RADITIONAL A PPROACH
191   Here, we revisit the challenges faced by the Lagrangian relaxation method in finding the Pareto
192   frontier:
193
194       • Discrete Trade-offs. The Lagrangian relaxation simplifies the original problem to a single-
195         objective optimization but can only produce discrete solutions corresponding to specific β val-
196         ues. As β varies in the range [0, +∞), the generated solutions constitute a discrete subset of the
197
            Pareto frontier rather than a continuous curve. This may lead to suboptimal trade-off choices,
            especially when the best trade-off lies between discrete solutions.
198
199       • Approximation Incompleteness. Even with an exhaustive search over β, the set of solutions
200         Sβ = {lβ |β ∈ [0, +∞)} generated by the Lagrangian relaxation method may not well approx-
201
            imate the true Pareto frontier F ∗ . On the one hand, for many non-convex practical problems,
            the Lagrangian relaxation method may fail to find Pareto-optimal solutions located in concave
202
            regions. On the other hand, the mapping from β values to their induced trade-off points is often
203
            highly nonlinear and irreversible, making it difficult to control the distribution and coverage of
204         trade-off points by adjusting β.
205
206   To overcome these limitations, we propose a new multi-objective optimization method aimed at
207   directly searching for and generating optimal solutions on the Pareto frontier without relying on
208   preset trade-off parameters. This method can adapt to complex frontier shapes, providing decision-
209   makers with comprehensive and fine-grained trade-off choices.

304   4.1    E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
305
306   Datasets. We applied our method to FashionMNIST Xiao et al. (2017) and CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky
307
      et al. (2009), achieving consistent Pareto-optimal parameter adaptation across both datasets. Fash-
      ionMNIST is a 10-category grayscale image dataset, and CIFAR-10 is a diverse 10-class color image
308
      dataset with various objects, which are crucial for benchmarking image classification and analyzing
309
      performance trends.
310
311   Baselines and Adversarial Attacks. We select a variety of IB methods as baselines, which are
312   based on different theoretical ideas, including RES18, VIB, NIB, VIB-squared, and NIB-squared.
313
      For more detailed descriptions of these methods, please refer to Appendix D. In addition, to bet-
      ter measure the performance and robustness of different IB methods, we introduce a variety of
314
      attack methods. Depending on their complexity, these attacks can be classified as simple at-
315
      tacks, e.g., [FGSM] Goodfellow et al. (2014), [PGD] Madry   ˛    et al. (2017), and complex attacks,
316   e.g., [NIFGSM] Lin et al. (2019), [EOTPGD] Liu et al. (2018), [MIFGSM] Dong et al. (2018),
317   [UPGD] Madry ˛   et al. (2017), [Jitter] Schwinn et al. (2023). Here, we give a brief explanation of
318   these attacks:
319
320       • FGSM (Fast Gradient Sign Method): This is a quick method that perturbs images by adding
321         noise in the direction of the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image.
322       • PGD (Projected Gradient Descent): A more sophisticated method than FGSM, it iteratively
323         applies small perturbations and projects the perturbed image back to a valid range after each
            step.


                                                              6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
        • NIFGSM (Nesterov Iterative Fast Gradient Sign Method): An iterative method similar to FGSM
325       but includes Nesterov accelerated gradient, which can enhance the effectiveness of the attack.
326
327     • EOTPGD (Expectation Over Transformation Projected Gradient Descent): A variant of PGD
328       that considers transformations like rotation or translation of images during the attack process.
329     • MIFGSM (Momentum Iterative Fast Gradient Sign Method): Integrates momentum into the
330       iterative FGSM process to stabilize update directions and improve the attack’s success rate.
331
        • UPGD (Universal Perturbation Gradient Descent): Aims to find universal perturbations appli-
332
          cable to a wide range of inputs.
333
334     • Jitter: Involves adding random noise (jitter) to images to test the model’s sensitivity to small
335       variations.

337   Implementation. We evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed multi-objective optimization
338   method for information bottleneck by conducting comparisons with several established Lagrangian
339
      variants of IB. Optimal settings for the Lagrange multiplier method, as sourced from prior stud-

      predictive accuracy and data compression, eliminating the need for manual tuning of β. Model

      performance is gauged in terms of both generalization and robustness to adversarial attacks. We

413         Q1: Why is MIB a better choice for finding complex trade-offs than other IB methods?
414
415
      Figure 3 illustrates the advantages of our MIB over linear weighting in searching for the Pareto front
416
      of the Information Bottleneck. From left to right, the figures correspond to Pareto fronts of different
417   shapes, all of which are represented in Figure 1. When the shape of the Pareto front is convex,
418   corresponding to Figure 3(a) and the first subplot in 1, linear weighting can effectively search the
419   Pareto front (although this may rely on experience or extensive manual experimentation). However,
420   when the Pareto front has complex shapes or non-convex regions, as shown in Figure 3(b), linear
421   weighting fails to find certain solutions on the Pareto front because this method can only search for
422   points tangent to the front (blue dotted line). In fact, such complex scenarios are likely to occur in
423   the broad context of deep learning (as represented by the third subplot in Figure 1). Our method
424   MIB, through gradient projection and reconciliation, allows for a freer descent direction than linear
425
      weighting. The joint descent direction for both objectives enables us to find solutions on complex-
      shaped Pareto fronts, as shown in Figure 3(c). In Appendix A, we provide detailed proof that our
426
      method can converge to the Pareto front and search regions that linear weighting cannot reach.

506   5                   C HALLENGES AND F UTURE
507
508   The Information Bottleneck (IB) provides a profound perspective for deep learning. On the one
509   hand, incorporating the IB principle has led to significant improvements across numerous domains.
510   More importantly, IB offers a multitude of insights and considerations for deciphering the "black
511
      box" of neural networks. Nevertheless, the IB approach still faces several limitations and challenges,
      such as accurately estimating mutual information, delving deeper into the profound implications of
512
      IB, and providing more comprehensive explanations from an optimization standpoint. We attempt
513
      to re-evaluate the trade-off inherent in the IB from a multi-objective perspective. However, to pursue
514   deeper exploration in this direction, a more precise understanding of this series of issues is requisite.
515   In subsequent work, we will undertake a more comprehensive investigation of these matters.

883
884   Recent efforts in Information Bottleneck (IB) theory have addressed optimization challenges, with
885
      notable developments including variational approximations Tishby & Zaslavsky (2015), matrix-
      based entropy functionals Saxe et al. (2019), and invariant input transformations Achille & Soatto
886
      (2018). These methods seek to balance compression and prediction accuracy by determining an
887
      optimal representation Z.
888
889   A significant milestone was the Deep Variational Information Bottleneck (VIB) Alemi et al. (2017),
890   which simplifies mutual information estimation using variational techniques and Lagrangian multi-
891
      pliers. This innovation has broadened IB applications in disentangling multi-view data features Bao
      (2021), fine-tuning language models in low-resource settings Mahabadi et al. (2021), and improving
892
      graph neural networks Wu et al. (2020).
893
894   Attention has also turned towards the algorithmic determination of parameter β to modulate the
895   compression-prediction trade-off. The Elastic Information Bottleneck (EIB) Ni et al. (2022) and De-
896   terministic Information Bottleneck (DIB) Strouse & Schwab (2017) utilize assumptions about data
897
      distribution and structure, which can restrict their applicability. Despite the plethora of IB-related
      research, most studies aim to enhance performance in new domains or through precise information
898
      loss functions and mutual information estimation methods. This paper pioneers a multi-objective
899
      approach to deeply explore and understand the quintessential trade-off characteristic of IB, marking
900   a novel direction in IB research.

       F.1    M ULTI -O BJECTIVE O PTIMIZATION
1001
1002   In the context of multi-objective optimization (MOP), the goal is to optimize multiple conflicting
1003   objectives simultaneously. Formally, a MOP can be defined as follows:
1004
1005
1006                                          min{f1 (x), . . . , fm (x)}                                   (40)
                                              x∈X
1007
1008   where X ⊆ Rn represents the decision space, and fi : X → R for i = 1, . . . , m are m objective
1009   functions, each to be minimized. In such problems, it is typically impossible to find a single solution
1010   that optimizes all objectives simultaneously because the objectives often conflict with one another.
1011   Instead, the concept of Pareto optimality is used to describe solutions that strike a balance between
1012   the different objectives Miettinen (1999).
1013
       Definition 1 (Pareto Optimality). A solution x∗ ∈ X is called Pareto optimal if there does not exist
1014   another x ∈ X such that fi (x) ≤ fi (x∗ ) for all i = 1, . . . , m, and fj (x) < fj (x∗ ) for at least one
1015   j.
1016
1017   In other words, a solution is Pareto optimal if no other solution can improve one objective without
1018   worsening at least one other objective. The set of all Pareto optimal solutions is called the Pareto
1019   set, and its corresponding image in the objective space is referred to as the Pareto front. The goal in
1020   MOPs is often to approximate the Pareto front as well as possible.
1021

1025   proximating the Pareto front and handling the trade-offs between competing objectives. Below, we

1115         proposed in this paper builds on this idea but introduces a more sophisticated approach by finding
1116         the min-norm combination of gradients. Unlike MGDA, our method is specifically tailored for the
