                                                   A BSTRACT
009
010            Brain-computer interface (BCI) with functional magnetic resonance imaging
011            (fMRI) has enabled new communication interfaces for many real-world appli-
012            cations, e.g., fMRI to image or video. While useful for specific scenarios (e.g.,
013
               neurofeedback), the existing functions are limited in offering immersive user ex-
               perience as required by more complex applications (e.g., virtual reality). We thus
014
               propose Brain-to-4D, a more powerful yet challenging BCI function to construct
015
               4D visuals including both video and 3D directly from brain fMRI signals. In reality,
016            however, it is infeasible to acquire brain signals for multi-view 4D stimuli for
017            training data collection due to the instantaneity nature of brain activities. Typically,
018            brain fMRI data exhibit significantly large variation. To address both obstacles, we
019            introduce WSf4D, a novel Weakly Supervised decomposed fMRI-to-4D generation
020            approach, characterized by foreground-background decomposition for supervision
021            dividing and fMRI multifaceted vector quantization for noise suppression. To
022            explore the application of the new task Brain-to-4D and our solution WSf4D, we
023            conduct analysis and diagnosis on various brain regions by encoding distinct visual
024
               cortex groups. Extensive experiments show that WSf4D can accurately generate
               multi-view consistent 4D scenes semantically aligned with raw brain signals, in-
025
               dicating meaningful advancements over existing approaches on the potentials of
026
               neuroscience and diagnosis.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) (Saha et al., 2021; Rashid et al., 2020) have been increasingly
033   recognized for their capacity to enable new useful communication means directly through brain
034   activities, underpinning extensive applications in neuroscience (e.g., spatiotemporal functionalities
035   analysis (Yu et al., 2023a; You et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2020)), healthcare, diagnosis, assistive
036   technologies like virtual reality (see Section A.1 for more discussion on applications). As one
037   of the main non-invasive BCI approaches, functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) has
038   been extensively capitalized for implementing various BCI functions. Indeed, with recent advance
039
      of generative AI, latest fMRI decoding methods allow to decode a few visual formats such as
      images (Takagi & Nishimoto, 2023; Lin et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b), videos (Wang et al., 2022;
040
      Chen et al., 2024a) or 3D shapes (Gao et al., 2023) (see Figure 1 (a)). However, that is still largely
041
      limited for practical applications as mentioned above due not lacking of immersive communication
042   and interactions.
043
044   In this paper we propose for the first time a more powerful BCI function, Brain-to-4D, that decodes
045
      the brain fMRI signals to 4D visual format encapsulating both video and 3D components (Figure 1(b)).
      This opens new avenues for spatiomotion-related neuro-science and interactive brain health diagnosis
046
      (Figure 1(c)), providing more dynamic, responsive, and tailored virtual environments. Also, this task
047
      gives rise to even bigger challenges. The first challenge is no full supervision, as acquiring brain
048   signals for 4D stimuli is infeasible in practice (Zhang et al., 2021b) – brain response signals are
049   instantaneous, disabling simultaneous capturing of multi-view brain stimuli in reality. The second
050   challenge is with large variation of brain fMRI due to both intrinsic complexity of brain activities and
051   uncontrollable capturing factors. The interconnected nature of these challenges makes this problem
052   even more difficult. However, inspired by the human ability to continuously perceive dynamic scenes
053   across space and time from fleeting thoughts (Heft, 2010; Kiverstein & Rietveld, 2021; Wang &
      Spelke, 2002), we are determined to tackle the fMRI-to-4D problem.

070   To address the aforementioned challenges, we develop a novel Weakly Supervised decomposed fMRI-
071   to-4D generation approach, WSf4D, allowing to generate dynamic 3D scenes directly from brain
072
      fMRI signals. Our key idea is blending partial supervision in correspondence across two modalities –
      4D object targets (i.e., foreground) and 3D background in video format. This leads naturally to a scene
073
      decomposed architecture: first converting fMRI input into foreground and background representations
074
      for respective processing and optimization, then composing them back view by view to the desired 4D
075   visual format with a holistic integrated scene. Critically, this decomposition provides an opportunity
076   of incorporating 2D (partial) supervision available seamlessly. To suppress the signal variation, we
077   compress fMRI signals into discrete semantic vectors so that redundant and noisy information can be
078   filtered out, along with improved computational efficiency in lower dimension space. When applying
079   WSf4D to neuroscience (Figure 1(c)), we encode distinct visual cortex groups, such as full brain
080   regions and V1, to study the function of V1 region. Besides, we add noise to fMRI of V1 to imitate
081   disordered brain for diagnosis.
082   In summary, we make the following contributions: (i) To power BCI function with immersive
083   use experience, we introduce a novel, more challenging yet more powerful function, Brain-to-4D,
084   transforming brain fMRI signals to dynamic 3D scenes. (ii) We propose a novel weakly supervised
085   decomposed learning method, WSf4D, in a foreground and background decomposed architecture,
086   learnable at the absence of fully supervised fMRI-4D paired training data. (iii) For evaluation, we
087   create a new benchmark on top of a previous fMRI-video dataset (Wen et al., 2018) with extended
088   text annotations. We conduct extensive experiments to validate the superior performance of our
089
      model over previous alternative in generating dynamic 3D scenes with brain signals.

091   2    R ELATED WORK
092
093   Neural decoding for BCIs Existing BCI functions (Saha et al., 2021; Rashid et al., 2020) are
094   primarily confined to static 2D interactions (Lawhern et al., 2018; Guger et al., 2024; Abdulkader
095   et al., 2015). Previous neural decoding studies (Beliy et al., 2019; Buckner, 1998; Roelfsema et al.,
096   2018) are also limited to 2D images (Beliy et al., 2019; Takagi & Nishimoto, 2023; Chen et al.,
097
      2023b; Scotti et al., 2023), videos (Chen et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024) and 3D geometry (Gao et al.,
      2023), making them hard to support continuous, three-dimensional immersive user experience. We
098
      thus propose Brain-to-4D function for more seamless and intuitive interaction, providing a significant
099
      step forward for practical applications.
100
101
      Weakly supervised learning Previous weakly supervised learning approaches (Zhou, 2018; Maha-
102   jan et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2021) typically focus on incomplete (Settles, 2009; Zhu, 2005; Huang
103   et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2020), coarse (Dietterich et al., 1997; Foulds & Frank, 2010; Wei et al., 2016),
104   or inaccurate supervision (Frénay & Verleysen, 2013) assuming uni-modality labels are available. In
105   contrast, our fMRI-to-4D framework needs to tackle mismatched modality, with 2D video supervision
106   partially corresponding to 4D scene targets. By extracting and integrating information from 2D
107   videos into 4D scenes, our WSf4D expands the scope of weakly supervised learning due to its ability
      of bridging mismatched modalities.

      3D and 4D generation Recent advancements in text/image-based 3D generation (Poole et al.,
109   2023; Lin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023)
110   are predominantly based on strong 3D representations, including NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020),
111   DMTet (Shen et al., 2021) or Gaussian splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023), which leverage score distillation
112   sampling (Poole et al., 2023) (SDS) and extensive 3D datasets (Deitke et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023b;
113   Wu et al., 2023). With the emergence of 4D representations (Wu et al., 2024; Pumarola et al., 2020;
114   Cao & Johnson, 2023; Yang et al., 2024b; 2023), these techniques have also been extended to generate
115   dynamic 3D scenes (Jiang et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024). Our approach takes a step
116   further by integrating rich representations from brain signals as guidance to seamlessly bridge the gap
117
      between fMRI and 4D generation, highlighting its superiority in generating immersive and accurate
      3D/4D environments from neurological data. An extended discussion can be found in Section A.3 in
118
      the supplementary material.

      Deformable 3D Gaussian splatting 3D Gaussian splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) represents
125
      a 3D scene with a set of Gaussians. Each Gaussian is characterized by position mean µ ∈ R3 ,
126
      covariance matrix Σ ∈ R3×3 , color c ∈ R3 , and opacity α ∈ R. The color of each pixel results from
127   the 2D projection of these 3D Gaussians and depth pre-sorted volumetric rendering. In dynamic
128   setting, deformable 3DGS (Wu et al., 2024) uses an additional network Φ to predict the deformation
129   of S = {µ, Σ, α} given timestamp τ : S̃ = Φ(S, τ ), where S̃ denotes the deformed attributes of S.
130   With these deformed attributes, we can render images at different timestamp.

145   3.2   OVERALL FRAMEWORK OF WSf4D
146
147   We propose WSf4D, a pioneering Weakly Supervised decomposed fMRI-to-4D generation framework,
148   depicted in Figure 2. This framework is designed to tackle the challenge of mismatched modalities
149
      between 2D video supervision and 4D scene targets, circumventing the need for paired fMRI-4D
      data. Central to our approach is the decomposition of scenes into foreground and background,
150
      enabling tailored processing to blend partial supervision in correspondence across both foreground
151
      and background. Initially, fMRI signals X are encoded into multifaceted components, covering both
152   foreground representations ze,Fg , {Iτ }Tτ=1 and background representations ze,Bg , IBg , with
153
154                 {ze,Fg , ze,Bg , IBg } = {fFVE , fBVE , fBg }(fb (X)), {Iτ }Tτ=1 = fFg (X),            (2)
155

156   effectively learn rich and meaningful representations from the complex fMRI data with limited direct
157   supervision. Subsequently, these representations are then extended into the generation of 3DGS-based
158   4D scene (section 3.4) which is also decomposed with object foreground and scene background. This
159   decomposition strategy targets to separately exploit different multifaceted representations based on
160   their respective characteristics. The foreground involves generating a 4D object using deformable

192   3.3   V ECTOR QUANTIZED F MRI (VQ- F MRI) E NCODING
193
      In pursuit of robust fMRI extraction under sparse training samples, we propose the vector quantized
194
      fMRI (VQ-fMRI) encoders to map fMRI data X onto discrete latent space. Specifically, a backbone
195
      encoder fb , processes the fMRI data to produce an shared representation zb = fb (X). This is then
196   split into foreground and background VQ encoders (FVE and BVE):
197
198                                          ze,Fg = fFVE (zb ), ze,Bg = fBVE (zb ),                                                     (3)
199   resulting in quantized foreground and background latent space representations:
200
                                             gFg ∈ RKFg ×DFg , gBg ∈ RKBg ×DBg ,                                                         (4)
201
202   where KFg and KBg denote the size of latent vectors, and DFg and DBg represent their dimensionality.
203   Our designed vector quantization is performed as follows:
204                             zq,Fg = gk,Fg , where k = argminj ∥ze,Fg − gj,Fg ∥ ,                                                     (5)
205
206                           zq,Bg = gk,Bg , where k = argminj ∥ze,Bg − gj,Bg ∥ .                     (6)
207   The quantized foreground embedding, zq,Fg , provides semantic and geometric guidance for the
208   foreground reference video generated as {Iτ }Tτ=1 = fFg (X). The quantized background embedding
209   zq,Bg supports inpainting for the background reference image decoded as IBg = fBg (zb ). For further
210   implementation details, refer to section A.2.
211   One key advantage is its ability to bypass the curse of dimensionality. By constraining latent space
212   size K ≪ n, we significantly improve model regularization and avoid overfitting (Peng et al., 2023)
213   in high-dimensional feature spaces. Furthermore, our approach significantly reduces KL divergence
214   between empirical and ground truth distributions, as indicated by theorem 3.1. It shows that the
215   quantized latent space zq yields a much tighter approximation to the true distribution compared to the
      non-quantized embeddings ze , which is crucial for robust latent representations.

      3.4   F OREGROUND - BACKGROUND DECOMPOSING FOR 4D SCENE GENERATION
233
234   Modeling 4D scenes face two challenges: (1) Foreground and background present intrinsically
235   different characteristics (e.g., dynamic vs. static); (2) Camera perspectives in 4D scenes often blur out
236   nearby objects dynamically. To tackle these, we propose decoupling the foreground and background
237   elements of a scene.
238
239   Foreground generation The foreground is represented by deformable 3D Gaussians, optimized
240   in two stages: static and dynamic (Ren et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023), driven by foreground video
241
      {Iτ }Tτ=1 and the quantized embedding zq,Fg . In both stages, 3D Gaussians and its deformation are
      guided by object-level diffusion models under SDS. Along with mean squared error (MSE) loss under
242
      reference views with Iref ∈ {Iτ }Tτ=1 , the total loss Lf for foreground modeling can be expressed by:
243
244                       Lf = λimg LSDS,img + λtext LSDS,text + λref ∥Iˆref − Iref ∥22 ,                   (9)
245   where λ∗ are balancing weights, with img and text referring to AI (2023) and Shi et al. (2023)
246   guidance, respectively. Furthermore, at static stage we set the first frame I1 as reference image and
247   froze the deformation network Φ during training. In contrast, the dynamic stage utilizes all the frames,
248   allowing Φ to be trainable to accommodate temporal variations. Considering the unstable training of
249   Gaussians in the generative manner, we follow Pan et al. (2024a) to manually clip the gradient of
250   rendered image pixel-wisely. This operation significantly reduces the variance of gradients, avoiding
251   intricate densification parameter tuning and leading to improved shape and texture.
252
253
      Background generation The background is represented by 3D Gaussians around a sphere without
      deformation. A scene-level 3D-aware diffusion model serves as a 2D prior to extend the background
254
      image IBg into a complete 360◦ environment. The total loss Lb for background modeling is:
255
256                                  Lb = λBg LSDS,Bg + λref ∥IˆBg − IBg ∥22 ,                            (10)
257   where λ∗ denotes balancing weights and LSDS,Bg represents SDS under scene-level diffusion.
258

290   3.5     A PPLICATIONS : N EUROSCIENCE INTERPRETABILITY AND DIAGNOSIS
291
292   We apply WSf4D to two key applications: neuroscience interpretability and diagnosis (Figure 3). Our
293   design focuses on four specific groups within the visual cortex: primary (V1), associative (V2, V3,
294   V4), dynamic (MT, MST, LIP), and synthesis (TPOJ) visual cortex. For each group, we examine their
295
      role by encoding each region of interest (ROI) group separately. To simulate disorder diagnosis, we
      introduce perturbations to each group and analyze the resulting 4D scenes to evaluate their functional
296
      impact.

304
      Dataset Our research extends publicly available fMRI-video dataset (Wen et al., 2018). The fMRI
305   are acquired using a 3T MRI scanner at a repetition time (TR) of 2 seconds, comprising 18 segments
306   of 8-minute video clips, resulting in 4,320 training video-fMRI pairs, and 5 segments for 1,200
307   testing samples. For each video-fMRI pair, a single frame is randomly selected as the ground truth
308   image for background supervision. Besides, we annotated the video-fMRI samples with foreground
309   objects (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and background scenes (Bansal, 2019). Lacking 4D annotations, we
310   employ semantic embeddings of these labels as a codebook to supervise our VQ-fMRI encoders.
311
312
313   Metrics In line with (Chen et al., 2024a), we employ the Structural Similarity Index Measure
314   (SSIM) for pixel-level accuracy and classification-based score for semantic accuracy with respect to
315
      ground truth visual stimuli. The classification score compares the top-1 accuracy between the ground
      truth and rendered frames across selected N = 2 and N = 50 classes, with 100 repetition for an
316
      average success rate and standard deviation. Both image and video classifiers are used, designed
317
      as ICS-N and VCS-N , respectively. Additionally, following Yin et al. (2023); Pan et al. (2024b),
318   we incorporate CLIP-T as a 4D metric, which evaluates the temporal smoothness by computing the
319   CLIP similarity between adjacent frames in a rendered video. Except for reporting CLIP-T of videos
320   at specific views in Yin et al. (2023); Pan et al. (2024b), we also adopt a 360◦ video around the 4D
321   scene which represents the spatial geometry, resulting in CLIP-T-G. For 4D benchmark, we render a
322   4D model from the front view (reference view), side views and back view, with each view evaluated
323   separately across 100 cases. The SSIM is only applicable to the reference view because there is no
      ground truth for other views.

      4.5       C OMPREHENSIVE ROI ANALYSIS
501
502   ROI importance mapping We first analyze brain-related mechanisms by visualizing attention maps
503   in the video mapping and encoder weight distributions in VQ-fMRI encoder. As shown in Figure 9,
504   consistent with MinD-Video (Chen et al., 2024a), early video mapping layers prioritize structural
505   aspects of input data, highlighting a clear segmentation of brain regions. High-level visual cortex areas
506   (MT, MST and TPOJ) receive more attention than low-level visual cortex (V1, V2 and V3), reflecting
507   a focus on complex feature extraction. As processing deepens, attention becomes more dispersed,
508   shifting towards holistic and abstract visual features. In contrast, VQ-fMRI encoder demonstrates
509
      greater homogeneity among regions, indicating a more holistic visual features. Specifically, the
      foreground VQ-fMRI encoder identifies more high-value regions than the background encoder, which
510
      hints more brain areas are focused on forground object instead of background scenes. Most values
511
      in background VQ-fMRI encoder shows a small weight value, indicating their little contribution to
512   background encoding.
513
514   ROI interpretation The function of each specific ROI group is also analyzed separately (Fig-
515
      ure 3(b)). The V1 visual region maintains initial processing of edges, orientations, and spatial
      frequencies of the scene, confirming its essential role in basic visual feature detection. The associative
516
      (V2, V3, V4) cannot independently decode visuals, indicating their reliance on V1 for information
517
      processing. Meanwhile, the spatiomotion (MT, MST, LIP) regions could only generate motion and
518   flow, contributing little to complex patterns and shapes. The TPOJ region includes a cohesive visual
519   experience, illustrating its role in information integration. These findings align well with previous
520   research on region-of-interest (ROI) functionality in visual perception (Tong, 2003; Kim et al., 2020).
521
      ROI diagnosis These ROI functions points to the potential for ROI diagnisis. As depicted in
522
      Figure 3(c), the disorder in either primary (V1) visual regions or associative (V2, V3, V4) regions
523
      lead to impairments in overall visual comprehension, supporting the centrality of these regions in
524   foundational and complex visual processing. Disorders in the synthesis (TPOJ) region result in a
525   more comprehensive disruption of scene perception, suggesting its crucial role in integrating visual
526   inputs into a coherent whole. In contrast, the disorder in spatiomotion (MT, MST, LIP) produce only
527   marginal effects, showing their little impact on features and edges.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this study, we introduce WSf4D, a pioneering framework tailored for the newly proposed Brain-
532
      to-4D BCI function, enabling the generation of dynamic 3D scenes from brain fMRI signals for
533
      immersive user experience. Through meticulous design, the WSf4D framework overcomes the
534   challenges posed by the absence of fully supervised 4D brain training data and high variation with
535   brain fMRI signals. Our core idea is to adopt a weakly supervised learning approach that streamlines
536   weak, partial supervision from the pre-existing fMRI-video and single-view-to-3D in a background
537   and foreground decoupled architecture. Experimental results have demonstrated the capability
538   of WSf4D in decoding time-continuous and view-consistent 4D visuals closely aligned with the
539   underlying brain activity. We hope this work can open up and foster more advanced research and
      applications in BCI and neuroscience studies.

      A.2    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
865
866   Encoding In the VQ-fMRI encoder, the backbone fb first employs an MLP to map fMRI data
867   into a 4096-dimensional vector. This is followed by four MLPs with residual connections to further
868   extract fMRI features. The output is then transformed into 257 × 768-dimensional shared feature
869   representation zb . Both the foreground VQ encoder (FVE) and the background VQ encoder (BVE) use
870   two-layer MLPs to map this shared feature representation into the VQ-embedding space zq,obj , zq,env .
871   The codebook dimensions for foreground modeling are set to D = 77 × 1024, aligned with Shi
872   et al. (2023), while the background modeling follows Takagi & Nishimoto (2023) with dimensions
873
      of D = 77 × 768. Given the practical challenges in acquiring sufficient 4D stimuli for end-to-end
      optimization, these codebooks are crafted around specific categories of foreground objects Krizhevsky
874
      et al. (2009) and background scenes Bansal (2019).
875
876   For foreground modeling, a model in Chen et al. (2024a) is used to map fMRI data to a reference
877   video that guides appearance and dynamics. We then segment each frame of the video to extract the
878   foreground with total T frames, which are denoted by {Iτ }Tτ=1 . Typically, the video content includes
879
      cropped scenes or real people, which diverges from the distribution of existing 3D datasets. To
      bridge this gap, the VQ-fMRI encoder maps fMRI into an text embedding zq,obj for better semantic
880
      and geometric guidance. Background encoding starts with generating an background reference
881
      image from fMRI. An intuitive approach involves reusing segmented images from video branch in
882   foreground encoding, but this method faced two drawbacks: (1) these frames predominantly feature
883   foreground elements, restricting accessible background information and (2) the backgrounds are not
884   consistent across different frames. To overcome these challenges, we generate this background image
885   directly from the shared representation, and the image is optionally inpainted using scene-level text
886   embedding zq,env from VQ-fMRI encoder. Training all fMRI encoders is a one-time process that
887   takes approximately two days on one NVIDIA A6000 GPU. Once completed, the parameters are
888   fixed for subsequent 4D generation from any fMRI.
889
890   Generation In generation stage, we implement our pipeline based on the DreamGaussian4D (Ren
891   et al., 2023), a framework focusing on efficient 4D generation. Training involves 500 steps for static
892   foreground and background, 1,000 steps for dynamic foreground, and 50 steps for joint refinement.
893
      The Gaussians are initialized with 5,000 random points for foreground inside a sphere of and 200,000
      random points for background around a sphere of radius 5. Densification is performed every 50
894
      steps. For balancing weights, we set λimg = 1, λtext = 0.5, λref = 10, 000, λenv = 1. For diffusion
895
      guidance, we use pretrained models from Stable Zero123 (AI, 2023) and MVDream (Shi et al., 2023)
896   object-level 3D-aware diffusion, use adopt ZeroNVS (Sargent et al., 2023) as 2D prior in scene-level
897   3D-aware diffusion, and apply MinD-Video (Chen et al., 2024a) for Brain-tailored diffusion. The
898   whole generation pipeline takes about 30 minutes on one NVIDIA A6000 GPU. Following this, the
899   parameters for 4D Gaussian splatting are saved, enabling future inference processes. This setup
900   allows for an inference speed of 15 frames per second (FPS), supporting real-time interaction.

902   A.3    R ELATED WORK
903
904   Neural decoding for BCIs BCIs aim to establish communication links between the brain and
905   computers or other external devices (Saha et al., 2021; Rashid et al., 2020; Kawala-Sterniuk et al.,
906   2021; Wolpaw et al., 2002; Nicolas-Alonso & Gomez-Gil, 2012). However, BCI research is primarily
907   confined to static 2D interactions (Lawhern et al., 2018; Guger et al., 2024; Abdulkader et al., 2015;
908   Zander & Kothe, 2011) which do not support continuous, three-dimensional immersive experiences.
909
      Existing neural decoding studies have focused on extracting essential representations (Buckner, 1998;
      Roelfsema et al., 2018) of brain signals for tasks like visual content decoding (Naselaris et al., 2011;
910
      Kamitani & Tong, 2005; Haxby et al., 2001; Haynes & Rees, 2005; Thirion et al., 2006; Georgieva
911
      et al., 2009) and object recognition (Wen et al., 2018; Horikawa & Kamitani, 2017; Groen et al.,
912   2018). However, they often struggle to create detailed visuals directly from brain signals. These
913   investigations have also facilitated advancements in reconstructing images (Beliy et al., 2019; Li et al.,
914   2024), videos (Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024) and geometry (Gao et al., 2023;
915   Yang et al., 2024a; Gao et al., 2024) from fMRI data using techniques such as generative adversarial
916   networks (Schoenmakers et al., 2013; VanRullen & Reddy, 2019; Shen et al., 2019; Dado et al.,
917   2022; Seeliger et al., 2018; Gu et al., 2022; Ozcelik et al., 2022) and latent-space diffusion (Takagi &
      Nishimoto, 2023; Lin et al., 2022; Ozcelik & VanRullen, 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Scotti et al., 2023;


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Gao et al., 2023). Restricted by high cost of large-scale brain stimuli containing both multi views and
919   time continuity, all these reconstructions are limited to single view or static objects, which pose severe
920   limitation on immersive user experience under BCIs. WSf4D advances beyond these achievements
921   by offering more seamless and intuitive interaction that leverage both spatial and temporal dimension
922   interactions, providing a significant step forward in the practical application of BCIs.
923
924
      Weakly supervised learning Weakly supervised learning targets at situation with insufficient
925
      training dataset (Zhou, 2018; Mahajan et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2021). Previous approaches typically
926   focus on three key situations: incomplete supervision with mostly unlabelled data (Settles, 2009;
927   Zhu, 2005; Huang et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2020), inexact supervision with only coarse-grained
928   labels (Dietterich et al., 1997; Foulds & Frank, 2010; Wei et al., 2016), and inaccurate supervision
929   with partially incorrect labels (Frénay & Verleysen, 2013). These methods are effective in tasks like
930   object detection (Zhang et al., 2021a; 2018; Tang et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019; Nag et al., 2022),
931   localization (Choe & Shim, 2019; Jiang et al., 2019; Hou et al., 2018), and segmentation (Zhang et al.,
932   2020; Ahn et al., 2019), where similar modality labels are available. In comparison, our fMRI-to-4D
933   task face a novel challenge of mismatched modality supervision, where the available 2D video labels
934
      only partially correspond to the target 4D scenes. Our WSf4D fill this modality gap by squeezing
      available information from available 2D videos, and then distilling and integrating this information
935
      into 4D scenes. This pushes the boundaries of weakly supervised learning by advancing weakly
936
      supervision across mismatched modalities.
937
938
939   3D and 4D generation Recent advancements in 3D and 4D content generation have predominantly
940   utilized inputs such as text, images, and videos. The core of these innovations stems from techniques
941
      like score distillation sampling (Poole et al., 2023) (SDS) and the exploitation of extensive 3D
      datasets (Deitke et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2023). At the object-level, numerous
942
      works (Poole et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024;
943
      Yi et al., 2024) employ SDS to train fundamental 3D representations, including NeRF (Mildenhall
944   et al., 2020), DMTet (Shen et al., 2021) or Gaussian splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023). Following research
945   continues into training 3D-aware diffusion models for improved geometric consistency (Liu et al.,
946   2023; Shi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Voleti et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b). With the development
947   of fundamental 4D representations (Wu et al., 2024; Pumarola et al., 2020; Cao & Johnson, 2023;
948   Yang et al., 2024b; 2023), the extension for 4D generation fields have been explored. For example,
949   Consistent4D (Jiang et al., 2024) proposes video-to-4D task through a tailored dynamic NeRF
950   with SDS. DreamGaussian4D (Ren et al., 2023) extends the 4D function of DreamGaussian (Tang
951   et al., 2024) to further reduce optimization time with Gaussian splatting. However, these methods
952
      often struggle with in-the-wild scenes. DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2023) attempts to model the
      background using a small coordinate multi-layer perceptron (MLP) distilled by a text-to-image
953
      diffusion model, which leads to blurry results. Previous efforts (Yu et al., 2021; Jain et al., 2021) have
954
      aimed at single-image novel view synthesis but are confined to a limited range of camera viewpoints.
955   ZeroNVS (Sargent et al., 2023) employs a scene-level diffusion model for novel view synthesis. In
956   comparison, WSf4D not only leverages this prior but also innovates further by optimizing a Gaussian
957   sphere for background modeling. Moreover, WSf4D takes a step further by integrating brain signals
958   as inputs and designing an efficient fRMI encoder to seamlessly bridge the gap between brain and
959   various diffusion models, underscoring its superiority in generating immersive and accurate 3D/4D
960   environments from neurological data.
