011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Credit assignment has been utilized as a common technique for determining the
014            past key state-action pairs and assigning corresponding rewards that have strong
015            relevance with the final outputs in reinforcement learning, especially for environ-
016
               ments with delayed rewards. However, current reward function design methods rely
               heavily on domain knowledge and may not accurately reflect the actual reward that
017
               should be received, which will lead to noised reward assignments during the credit
018
               assignment process and deteriorate the performance of the agent. To address this
019            issue, in this paper, by leveraging the benefits of Preference-based Reinforcement
020            Learning (PbRL), we propose a novel trajectory preference-based credit assignment
021            method, where each trajectory is assigned to one of three different preferences
022            according to its related delayed reward and the entire trajectory space. Then, a
023            causal Transformer framework is introduced to predict the relevance between the
024            decisions at each timestep and the different trajectory preferences to guide the
025            credit assignment. Despite the unavoidable noised reward related to each trajectory,
026            we demonstrate that our method can still effectively guide agents to learn superior
027
               strategies. Experiments on the MuJoCo task and the treatment of sepsis under
               extremely delayed reward setting show that our method can mitigate the adverse
028
               effects resulting from the delayed noised rewards and provide effective guidelines
029
               for agents.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved impressive performances in many domains, such as video
035
      games (Mnih et al., 2013), the game of Go (Silver et al., 2017), InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022),
036   among others. While these remarkable applications demonstrate the significant potential and research
037   value of RL, its performances rely heavily on well-designed reward functions (Gangwani et al., 2020).
038   However, in real-world sequential decision-making problems, rewards are usually sparse and/or
039   delayed (Ren et al., 2022). For example, in the heparin dosing therapy for Intensive Care Unit (ICU)
040   patients, activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT), which is a key evaluation criterion and reward
041   signal of the effects of the treatment, can only be acquired after 4 to 6 hours of the intravenous
042   administration (Liang et al., 2023). In extreme cases, i.e., episodic reinforcement learning (Han
043   et al., 2022), the rewards are only given at the final state and are the sole evaluation metric for past
044
      decisions. This makes it difficult for RL agents to determine the relevance between final feedback
      and past decisions, leading to low learning efficiency (Chen & Tan, 2023).
045
046   To address this episodic reward issue, heuristic reward functions are designed for generating dense
047   rewards (Andrychowicz et al., 2017). However, even well-designed rewards may be inaccurate
048   and can mislead RL agents into undesirable behaviors. As an alternative, exploration and credit
049   assignment related techniques have been proposed and are seen as more effective solutions, where
050
      exploration can help agents to find better behaviors (Ménard et al., 2021), and credit assignment can
      assign rewards to the past relevant decisions (Zhang et al., 2024).
051
052   With the applications of reinforcement learning in more real-world problems, where the long-term
053   credit assignment problem and the sparse reward problem co-exist, it becomes harder for exploration
      techniques to be successfully used as in such domains, current exploration methods may fail to


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      discover the key actions that have the most influence on the final output due to the delayed reward
055   signals (Chen et al., 2023), leading to decreased performances and lower learning efficiency of the
056   RL agent. In theory, credit assignment approaches can mitigate this problem in a more favorable
057   way, and based on return decomposition, existing credit assignment methods (Zhang et al., 2024;
058   Ren et al., 2022; Gangwani et al., 2020) decompose episodic rewards by predicting rewards in the
059   trajectory and then assigning them to past decisions. However, as mentioned, the designed episodic
060   rewards are usually noisy and may not accurately reflect the actual reward that should be received.
061   With the noisy episodic reward, the decomposition of it to past decision steps will also be inaccurate,
062   and then the performances of the underlying agent will be affected.
063   In fact, rather than defining a reward function, it is easier to judge a trajectory as "good" or "bad"
064   and provide qualitative labels to it. By utilizing this idea, Preference-based Reinforcement Learning
065   (PbRL) (Novoseller et al., 2020) can learn from paired trajectory samples that contain such qualitative
066   labels, in which trajectories are manually labeled as preferences of "good" or "bad" in pairs, reflecting
067   the human’s relative evaluation of one trajectory compared to the other one. However, current PbRL
068   methods can only learn from manually labeled paired feedbacks, which makes these methods limited
069
      to specific offline datasets.
070   In this paper, by leveraging the benefits of PbRL, we propose a novel trajectory preference-based
071   credit assignment method. Specifically, we first extend the concept of preferences by defining three
072   preferences ("good", "neutral" or "bad") rather than paired preferences according to the trajectory’s
073   episodic reward and the entire trajectory space, and one of the three preferences will be assigned to
074   each trajectory as a label to describe its quality. Then, a causal transformer framework is introduced
075
      to model trajectory sequences. By utilizing the transformer’s self-attention mechanism, for each
      trajectory, the relevance between the decisions at each time step and its preference will be predicted
076
      and used as a guideline for assigning the trajectory’s episodic reward to past decision steps. Overall,
077
      the main contributions are as follows:
078
079         1. A global preference setting mechanism is proposed according to episodic rewards and the
080            entire trajectory space, which is used to describe the quality of each trajectory as "good",
081            "neutral" or "bad", and could mitigate the adverse effects of noised rewards.
082
            2. By introducing a causal transformer framework to model the temporal relationships among
083
               trajectory sequences, for each trajectory, the relevance between each decision step and its
084            preferences could be predicted to guide credit assignment for agents. What’s more, this
085            framework also allows our method to accept trajectories of varying lengths as inputs.
086
087
            3. With the aforementioned relevance as the guideline, even noisy episodic rewards can be
               assigned back to past decisions and the performance of the agent can still be guaranteed.
088
               Experimental results on the sepsis treatment offline dataset and the MuJoCo online envi-
089
               ronment PointMaze demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, which also show that
090            compared to traditional credit assignment methods, our method offers a more general form
091            of trajectory feedback that is robust to the noise of the episodic rewards.

094   2    R ELATED WORK
095
096   Credit assignment Credit assignment is one of the central challenges in reinforcement learning.
097   Arjona-Medina et al. introduced a return decomposition formula for credit assignment where episodic
098   rewards are decomposed into rewards for each time step by using an LSTM-based long-term return
099
      prediction model and a manually designed assignment rule (Holzleitner et al., 2021). In (Gangwani
      et al., 2020), based on return decomposition, the concept of uniform reward redistribution, namely
100
      IRCR, is proposed. In subsequent work, EMR (Chen et al., 2023) combines the intrinsic rewards
101
      of the exploration mechanism with IRCR to balance exploration and exploitation and avoid the
102   predictive model falling into local optima. In (Ren et al., 2022), an upper bound for the common
103   return-equivalent assumption is introduced to serve as a surrogate optimization objective, and return
104   decomposition can be combined with the uniform reward redistribution framework in IRCR.While
105   these methods have achieved good performances on many tasks, the defining of the episodic rewards
106   used often requires rich prior knowledge of the underlying systems, and the set rewards may also
107   contain noise, which will be cumulated during the credit assignment process and deteriorate the final
      performances of the agent.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Preference-based reinforcement learning In recent years, various methods have employed human
109   preferences to train RL agents without reward engineering (Ouyang et al., 2022; Christiano et al.,
110   2017; Ibarz et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2023). Christiano et al. (Ouyang et al., 2022) demonstrate that
111   preference-based RL can effectively solve complex control tasks using deep neural networks, and the
112   querying of human paired preferences is more efficient than querying from the demonstration data.
113   Another advantage of PbRL is that humans can provide preferences about uncertainties to promote
114   exploration efficiency (Liang et al., 2022). Despite these benefits, PbRL is ineffective in complex
115   environments, as paired preferences only provide relative information rather than a direct evaluation
116   of sample quality, and even if one sample is more preferred than the other, it does not necessarily
117
      mean that this sample is better (White et al., 2024). For PbRL, it is also challenging for humans to
      compare similar samples, which is time-consuming and may result in inaccurate preference labels.
118
      Unlike the relative comparison approach typically used in PbRL, in our approach, trajectories are
119
      evaluated from a global perspective according to the entire trajectory space and episodic rewards,
120   which allows a qualitative classification of trajectories and reduces the adverse effects of uncertainty
121   in preferences. Moreover, existing methods (Liu et al., 2022; White et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2022)
122   mainly focus on using preference information to evaluate the overall quality of trajectories, and the
123   problem of credit assignment within trajectories is not considered, which is validated to be effective
124   in our paper.
125
126   Transformer in reinforcement learning Chen et al. (Chen et al., 2021) introduced transformer
127   into offline RL, mainly taking advantage of the causal transformer sequence modeling to output
128   decision actions end-to-end. Although this approach avoids the disadvantage of the traditional RL
129   method of slow iteration through the Bellman equation in sparse reward scenarios, the framework is
130
      limited by the offline setting. Similar to our work is the Preference Transformer proposed by Kim
      et al. (Kim et al., 2023). This work uses the Causal Transformer to model human preferences and
131
      shows that the Causal Transformer can capture key events in trajectories and has the ability to assign
132
      long-term credits, but this work is limited to a specific human preference dataset. Compared with
133   setting a reward function, labeling every two trajectory data undoubtedly increases the manual cost,
134   while our work emphasizes learning from the manually set reward function by alleviating the impact
135   of reward noise. Although the work of Ni et al. (Ni et al., 2024) showed that the Transformer lacks
136   the ability to assign long-term credit, they only verified it on the ordinary Transformer, but not on the
137   Causal Transformer with restricted attention order.

142   reinforcement learning (Bellman, 1966), in which, at each time step t, the agent selects an action at

144   to the next state st+1 . The process can be defined as (S, A, P, R, ρ, γ), where S is the set of states,
145   A the set of possible actions, P : S × A × S → [0, 1] the state transition probability function that
146   represents the probability p(s′ | s, a) of reaching state s′ ∈ S after taking action a in state s, R the
147   reward function, ρ : S → [0, 1] specifies the initial state distribution, and γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount
148   factor, respectively. A trajectory τ = {(st , at )}Tt=1 will be generated by repeatedly executing a policy

194   4     M ETHOD
195
196   In this section, we introduce a preference-based credit assignment method for episodic reinforcement
197   learning. By introducing preferences for each trajectory, our method efficiently assigns credit-rewards
198   at each time step, providing a guideline for policy learning even when the episodic reward is noisy.
199   Specifically, our method evaluates trajectories qualitatively by introducing a global preference. For
200   each trajectory, the contribution of each decision step to the final outcome is estimated and used
201   to assign episodic rewards back to individual decisions. This approach achieves effective credit
202
      assignment and mitigates the adverse effects of noise in episodic rewards.
203

227   4.2   P REFERENCE PREDICTION
228
229
      As mentioned in Section 3, PbRL can be used to design the reward function in episodic reinforcement
      learning. However, the designed reward function only evaluates the quality of each trajectory, and the
230
      credit assignment within each trajectory is still not addressed. In our approach, we first show how
231
      to determine the relevance between each state-action transition and the trajectory preference. Then,
232   credit can be assigned to past decision steps according to such relevance and the episodic rewards.
233
234   In detail, a new preference prediction model P (py |st , at , τ1:t−1 ; φ) with the incorporation of his-
235
      torical information τ1:t−1 is firstly designed, where φ represents the optimization parameters, and
      py = [pgood , pneutral , pbad ] is a set of probabilities that denotes the relevance between each state-action
236
      transition and the "bad",   P"neutral", and "good" preferences, respectively. Each probability ranges
237
      from [0, 1] and satisfies py = 1. In this prediction model, by predicting the conditional probability
238   distribution of the preference labels for each state-action transition, the relevance between each
239   state-action transition and the trajectory preference is converted into a classification problem.
240
241
      To realize the prediction model Pφ , a causal Transformer framework (Radford et al., 2018) (namely
      the Transformer network with causal masked self-attention) is adopted as the primary framework.
242
      Causal Transformer consist of stacked "masked self-attention layers". Each layer receive input tokens,
243
      and outputs preserve the same dimensions. These layers allow the model to take into account the
244   information of all previous tokens in the sequence when processing each token. In our method, the
245   token output of st is ignored because the token output of at has already taken into account all previous
246   input tokens (including st ). Therefore, when processing the state-action pair (s, a), only the token
247   output of each at token needs to be retained to generate a prediction of the state-action. Besides,
248   at each time step, the output of the model is only related to historical information, establishing the
249   temporal relationships of the inputs, allowing the model to generate predictions for each transition
250   step that incorporates historical information.

      rewards while our proposed approach is more robust. Subsequently, to further validate our method’s

      5.1     A NAVIGATION MAZE BENCHMARK TASK
367
368   Setup One of MuJoCo’s benchmark tasks, namely PointMaze is first employed as the testbed,
369   where the task is to s control a 2-DoF sphere to reach a goal in a closed maze, and the episodic reward
370   is +1 once the task is successfully completed, while the failure of the task results in a negative reward
371   equaling to the negative distance between the end position and the goal. Such a reward setting, though
372   imprecise, can still reflect the trajectories’ quality. (More details of this environment are given in
373   Appendix C).
374
375   Baselines It is worth noting that we did not use the PbRL method as a baseline because our method
376   aims to solve credit assignment problem in delayed and noised reward environments, which differs in
377   focus from PbRL. More importantly, most PbRL methods are based on specific human preference
      datasets and cannot be applied in online environments or offline datasets with numerical annotations.

380   RL algorithm vanilla SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018), as well as the SAC algorithm using a discount
381   factor γ (γ = 0.99). To ensure a fair comparison, the same hyperparameters as in the SAC algorithm

      6   C ONCLUSION
515
516
      In this paper, to address the issue of existing credit assignment methods struggling to achieve effective
517
      assignment in noisy episodic rewards, we propose a trajectory preference-based credit assignment
518   method by leveraging the benefits of PbRL. Compared to existing credit assignment methods,
519   our method provides a new form of relative feedback by constructing global preference feedback
520   for trajectories, alleviating the assignment error caused by noisy episodic rewards. Additionally,
521   the designed prediction-based reward function provides dense rewards to agents during policy
522   learning, improving the learning efficiency. In experiments on the MuJoCo-based PointMaze online
523   environment and the sepsis treatment dataset, we verified the adverse effects of episodic reward
524   noise and demonstrated the effectiveness and applicability of our method. The results show that our
525   method has the capability of credit assignment even when rewards contain obvious noise. In the
526
      sepsis treatment, it is worth noting that our method can guide agents to learn better and more realistic
      behavioral strategies than other baselines, which may provide more effective treatment suggestions
527
      for clinicians.
528
529
      Limitations and future works From our method, we can also see that it still has some limitations
530
      when extends into practical applications, such as the need for adjusting numerous parameters and the
531   high time cost facing long trajectory lengths. We leave these issues as part of future work, hoping to
532   generate more interpretable guidance rewards using simple techniques with less parameters and more
533   effective models.

      A     S OLUTIONS OF DELAYED REWARDS
703
704
      In reinforcement learning settings, agents are usually assumed to receive dense real-time rewards as
705   feedbacks during interactions with the environment. However, in many real-world applications, for
706   example, medical decision-making and autonomous driving, such dense rewards are not available
707   (Efroni et al., 2021), and reward signals are delayed and sparse, where in extreme cases the rewards
708   can only be received at the end of a trajectory, i.e., episodic reinforcement learning. The delayed
709   and sparse rewards pose great challenges to the credit assignment of RL agents. In the literature,
710   potential-based reward shaping methods have been proposed to provide rapid credit assignment while
711   ensuring optimal strategies (Ng, 1999), but constructing a potential function for each state often
712   requires rich expert knowledge of the domain. While evolution-based algorithms (Zou et al., 2021)
713
      perform gradient-free optimization directly in the trajectory policy space, making them insensitive
      to delayed rewards, the ignoring of temporal information makes these methods less efficient than
714
      typical RL algorithms (Gangwani et al., 2020). Recent work has attempted to apply Transformers
715
      to sequential decision-making (Chen et al., 2021), which enables agents to be effective in tasks
716   with delayed and sparse rewards, but ample data is required in the offline pre-training stage of these
717   approaches to acquire reliable policy models.
718

1044   F    D ISCUSSION ON THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN OUR METHOD AND P B RL
1045
1046   According to the description of Wirth et al.(Wirth et al., 2017), the problem of learning a preference
1047   model C(a ≻ a′ |s) can be phrased as a classification problem trying to correctly predict all observed
1048   preferences. The pairwise preference predictions of the trained classifiers Cij may be combined via
1049   voting or weighted voting, where each prediction issues a vote for its preferred action. The resulting
1050
       count k(s, a) for each action a in state s correlates with ρ((s, ai ) ≻ (s, aj )|s) if the classifiers C
       approximate ρ well:
1051
1052                                         X                                           X
1053                    k(s, a) =                             C(ai ≻ aj |s) =                             Cij (s)
1054                                for all ai ∈A(s),aj ̸=a                     for all ai ∈A(s),aj ̸=a
1055
1056   Then, the optimal action can be defined as a∗ = argmaxa′ k(s, a′ ).
1057   Obviously, our method is similar to the above definition, and both output the probability of the corre-
1058   sponding preference for each state-action pair (s, a) (corresponding to the number k(s, a)), i.e., py .
1059   Under this premise, the optimal action can be described as a∗ours = argmaxa′ (py=good |s, a′ , τ1:st−1 ).
1060
       However, the focus of our work is on credit assignment, which requires measuring the impact of each
1061
       state-action pair (s, a) on the final result y ′ . Our approach is to use the probability (py=y′ |s, a, τ1:st−1 )
1062
       of the preference corresponding to each (s, a) and the final result y ′ as the basis for credit allocation.
1063
       In other words, our method uses the preference probability to obtain the correlation between each
1064   step and the final result to obtain dense rewards.
1065
1066   Therefore, it can be said that our method is preference-based, utilizing the concept of relevance in
1067
       PbRL to guide the long-term credit assignment task.
