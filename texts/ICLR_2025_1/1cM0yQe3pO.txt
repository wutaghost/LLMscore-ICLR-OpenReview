                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            We study Variational Rectified Flow Matching, a framework that enhances clas-
011
               sic rectified flow matching by modeling multi-modal velocity vector-fields. At
               inference time, classic rectified flow matching ‘moves’ samples from a source
012
               distribution to the target distribution by solving an ordinary differential equation
013
               via integration along a velocity vector-field. At training time, the velocity vector-
014            field is learnt by linearly interpolating between coupled samples one drawn from
015            the source and one drawn from the target distribution randomly. This leads to
016            “ground-truth” velocity vector-fields that point in different directions at the same
017            location, i.e., the velocity vector-fields are multi-modal/ambiguous. However, since
018            training uses a standard mean-squared-error loss, the learnt velocity vector-field
019            averages “ground-truth” directions and isn’t multi-modal. Further, averaging leads
020            to integration paths that are more curved while making it harder to fit the target
021            distribution. In contrast, the studied variational rectified flow matching is able to
022
               capture the ambiguity in flow directions. We show on synthetic data, MNIST, and
               CIFAR-10 that the proposed variational rectified flow matching leads to compelling
023
               results with fewer integration steps.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021a;b) and more generally flow matching (Liu
030   et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Albergo et al., 2023) have been
031   remarkably successful in recent years. These techniques have been applied across domains from
032   computer vision (Ho et al., 2020) and robotics (Kapelyukh et al., 2023) to computational biology (Guo
033
      et al., 2024) and medical imaging (Song et al., 2022).
034   Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023) can be
035   viewed as a continuous time generalization of classic diffusion models (Albergo et al., 2023; Ma
036   et al., 2024). Those in turn can be viewed as a variant of a hierarchical variational auto-encoder (Luo,
037   2022). At inference time, flow matching ‘moves’ a sample from a source distribution to the target
038   distribution by solving an ordinary differential equation via integration along a velocity vector-field.
039
      To learn this velocity vector-field, at training time, flow matching aims to regress/match a constructed
      vector-field/flow connecting any sample from the source distribution — think of the data-space
040
      positioned at time zero — to any sample from the target distribution attained at time one. Notably, in
041
      a ‘rectified flow,’ the samples from the source and target distribution are connected via a straight line
042   as shown in Fig. 1(a). Inevitably, this leads to ambiguity, i.e., flows pointing in different directions
043   at the same location in the data-space-time-space domain, as illustrated for a one-dimensional data-
044   space in Fig. 1(a). Since classic rectified flow matching employs a standard squared-norm loss to
045   compare the predicted velocity vector-field to the constructed velocity vector-field, it does not capture
046   this ambiguity. Hence, rectified flow matching aims to match the source and target distribution in
047   alternative ways, leading to flow trajectories that are more complex and usually more curved. This is
048   illustrated in Fig. 1(b).
049   To enable rectified flow matching to capture this ambiguity in the data-space-time-space domain, we
050   study variational rectified flow matching. Intuitively, variational rectified flow matching introduces a
051   latent variable that permits to disentangle ambiguous flow directions at each location in the data-space-
052   time-space domain. This approach follows the classic variational inference paradigm underlying
053   expectation maximization or variational auto-encoders. Indeed, as shown in Fig. 1(c), variational
      rectified flow matching permits to model flow trajectories that intersect. This results in trajectories

070   Note that flow matching, diffusion models, and hierarchical variational auto-encoders are all able to

080   using fewer integration steps.
081   In summary, our contribution is as follows: we study the properties of variational rectified flow
082   matching, and, along the way, offer an alternative way to interpret the flow matching procedure. We
083   think the proposed method naturally extends classic rectified flow matching.

085   2     P RELIMINARIES
086
087   Given a dataset D = {(x1 )} comprised of data samples x1 , e.g., an image, generative models learn
088   a distribution p(x1 ), often by maximizing the likelihood. In the following we discuss how this
089   distribution is learnt with variational auto-encoders and rectified flow matching, and how ambiguity
090   is captured in the data domain.
091
092   2.1   VARIATIONAL AUTO -E NCODERS (VAE S )
093
094   Variational inference generally and variational auto-encoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2014)
095   specifically have been shown to capture ambiguity. This is achieved by introducing a latent variable
096
      z. At inference time, a latent z is obtained by sampling from the prior distribution p(z), typically a
      zero mean unit covariance Gaussian. A decoder is then used to characterize a distribution p(x1 |z)
097
      over the output space, from which an output space sample x1 is obtained.
098
099   At training time, variational auto-encoders use an encoder to compute an approximate posterior
100   distribution qϕ (z|x1 ) over the latent space. As the approximate posterior distribution is only needed
101   at training time, the data x1 can be leveraged. Note, the approximate posterior distribution is often
102
      a Gaussian with parameterized mean and covariance. A sample from this approximate posterior
      distribution is then used as input in the distribution pθ (x1 |z) characterized by the decoder. The loss
103
      encourages a high probability of the output space samples while pushing the approximate posterior
104
      distribution qϕ (z|x1 , c) to not deviate much from the prior distribution p(z). To achieve this, formally,
105   VAEs maximize a lower-bound on the log-likelihood, i.e.,
106
                    Ex1 ∼D log p(x1 ) ≥ Ex1 ∼D Ez∼qϕ [log pθ (x1 |z)] − DKL (qϕ (·|x1 )|p(·) ,
107
      when training a variational auto-encoder.

      2.2   R ECTIFIED F LOW M ATCHING
109
110   For flow matching, at inference time, a source distribution p0 (x0 ) is queried to obtain a sample
111   x0 . This is akin to sampling of a latent variable from the prior in VAEs. Different from VAEs
112   which perform a single forward pass through the decoder, in flow matching, the source distribution
113   sample x0 is used as the boundary condition for an ordinary differential equation (ODE). This ODE
114   is ‘solved’ by pushing the sample x0 forward from time zero to time one via integration along a
115   trajectory specified via a learned velocity vector-field vθ (xt , t) defined at time t and location xt , and
116
      commonly parameterized by deep net weights θ. Note, the velocity vector-field is queried many times
      during integration. The likelihood of a data point x1 can be assessed via the instantaneous change of
117
      variables formula (Chen et al., 2018; Song et al., 2021b; Lipman et al., 2023),
118                                                              Z 0
119                                log p1 (x1 ) = log p0 (x0 ) +     div vθ (xt , t)dt,                         (1)
120                                                                1
121   which is commonly (Grathwohl et al., 2018) approximated via the Skilling-Hutchinson trace estima-
122   tor (Skilling, 1989; Hutchinson, 1990). Here, div denotes the divergence vector operator.
123   Intuitively, by pushing forward samples x0 , randomly drawn from the source distribution p0 (x0 ),
124   ambiguity in the data domain is captured as one expects from a generative model.
125
126
      At training time the parametric velocity vector-field vθ (xt , t) needs to be learnt. For this, coupled
      sample pairs (x0 , x1 ) are constructed by randomly drawing from the source and the target distribution,
127
      usually independently from each other. A coupled sample (x0 , x1 ) and a time t ∈ [0, 1] is then
128
      used to compute a time-dependent location xt at time t via a function ϕ(x0 , x1 , t) = xt . Recall,
129   rectified flow matching uses xt = ϕ(x0 , x1 , t) = (1 − t)x0 + tx1 . Interpreting xt as a location,
130   intuitively, the “ground-truth” velocity vector-field v(x0 , x1 , t) is readily available via v(x0 , x1 , t) =
131   ∂ϕ(x0 , x1 , t)/∂t, and can be used as the target to learn the parametric velocity vector-field vθ (xt , t).
132   Concretely, flow matching aims to learn the parametric velocity vector field vθ (xt , t) by matching
133   the target via an ℓ2 loss, i.e., by minimizing w.r.t. the trainable parameters θ the objective
                                        Et,x0 ,x1 ∥vθ (xt , t) − v(x0 , x1 , t)∥22 .

135
136   Consider two different couplings that lead to different “ground-truth” velocity vectors at the same
137   data-domain-time-domain (xt , t). The parametric velocity vector-field vθ (xt , t) is then asked to
138
      match/regress to a different target given the same input (xt , t). This leads to averaging and the
      optimal functional velocity vector-field v ∗ (xt , t) = E{(x0 ,x1 ,t):ϕ(x0 ,x1 ,t)=xt } [v(x0 , x1 , t)]. Hence,
139
      ambiguity in the data-domain-time-domain is not captured. In the following we discuss and study a
140
      method that is able to model this ambiguity.

143   3     VARIATIONAL R ECTIFIED F LOW M ATCHING
144
145
      Our goal is to capture the ambiguity inherent in “ground-truth” velocity vector-fields obtained from
      typically used couplings (x0 , x1 ) that connect source distribution samples x0 ∼ p0 with target data
146
      samples x1 ∈ D. Here, p0 is a known source distribution and D is a considered dataset. This
147
      differs from classic rectified flow matching which does not capture this ambiguity even for simple
148   distributions as shown in Fig. 1 and as discussed in Sec. 2. The struggle to capture ambiguity leads to
149   velocity vector fields that are more curved and consequently more difficult to integrate at inference
150   time. In turn, this leads to distributions that may not fit the data as well. We will show evidence for
151   both, more difficult integration and less accurately captured data distributions in Sec. 4.
152
      To achieve our goal we combine rectified flow matching and variational auto-encoders. In the
153
      following we first discuss the objective before detailing training and inference.
154

        3.2   T RAINING
244
245
       To optimize the objective given in Eq. (5), we follow the classic VAE setup. Specifi-
246
       cally, we let the prior p(z) = N (z; 0, I) and the approximate posterior qϕ (z|x0 , x1 , xt , t) =
247
       N (z; µϕ (x0 , x1 , xt , t), σϕ (x0 , x1 , xt , t)). This enables analytic computation of the KL-divergence in
248    Eq. (5). Note that the mean of the approximate posterior is obtained from the deep net µϕ (x0 , x1 , xt , t)
249    and the standard deviation is obtained from σϕ (x0 , x1 , xt , t). Further, we use the re-parameterization
250    trick to enable optimization of the objective w.r.t. the trainable parameters θ and ϕ. Moreover, we
251    use a single-sample estimate for the expectation over the unobserved variable z. We summarize the
252    training procedure in Algorithm 1. Note, it’s more effective to work with a mini-batch of samples
253    rather than a single data point, which was merely used for readability in Algorithm 1.
254     Note that variational rectified flow matching training differs from training of classic rectified flow
255     matching in only a single step: computation of a latent sample z in Line 5 of Algorithm 1. From a
256     computational point of view we add a deep net forward pass to obtain the mean µϕ and standard
257     deviation σϕ of the approximate posterior, and a backward pass to obtain the gradient w.r.t. ϕ. Also
258     note that the velocity vector-field architecture vθ (xt , t, z) might be more complex as the latent
259     variable z needs to be considered. The additional amount of computation is likely not prohibitive.
260    We provide implementation details for the deep nets vθ (xt , t, z), µϕ (x0 , x1 , xt , t), and
261    σϕ (x0 , x1 , xt , t) in Sec. 4, as their architecture depends on the data.

283   4                         E XPERIMENTS
284
285   We evaluate the efficacy of variational rectified flow matching and compare to the classic rectified
286   flow (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023) and the recent
287   consistency flow matching (Yang et al., 2024) using multiple datasets. We show the benefits of
288   our method in modeling the velocity ambiguity in the data-domain-time-domain, which leads to
289   compelling results with fewer integration steps using ODE solvers. Moreover, we demonstrate that
290
      explicitly modeling ambiguity through a conditional latent z enhances the interpretability of flow
      matching models, leading to controlability.

293   4.1                       S YNTHETIC 1D DATA
294
295   For our synthetic 1D experiments, the source distribution is a zero-mean, unit-variance Gaussian,
296   while the target distribution is bimodal, with modes centered at −1.0 and 1.0.
297
      For the rectified flow matching and the consistency flow matching baselines, we use a multi-layer
298
      MLP network vθ to model the velocity. The network operates on inputs xt and t and predicts the
299
      velocity through a series of MLP layers. We follow this structure in our variational rectified flow
300   matching, but add an encoding layer for the latent variable z. The posterior model qϕ follows a
301   similar design as vθ , outputting µϕ and σϕ . At inference time, qϕ isn’t used. Instead, we sample
302   directly from the prior distribution p(z) = N (z; 0, I). We set the the KL loss weight to 1.0. Further
303   implementation details are provided in Appendix A.
304

369
370   We further test efficacy using synthetic 2D data. Following Liu et al. (2023), we model the source
371   distribution as a mixture of Gaussian components positioned at six equidistant points along a circle

484   Generative modeling has advanced significantly in the last decade, thanks in part due to seminal
485   works like generative adversarial nets (Goodfellow et al., 2014), variational auto-encoders (Kingma
      & Welling, 2014), and normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015).

502   More recently, score matching (Song & Ermon, 2019) and diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) were
503   introduced. They can be viewed as augmenting variational auto-encoders hierarchically (Luo, 2022)
504   while restricting involved distributions to be Gaussian. Notably, and analogously to classic discrete
505   normalizing flows, the number of hierarchy levels, i.e., the number of time steps, remained discrete,
506   which introduced complications.
507   Flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023) was introduced recently as a compelling alternative to avoid
508   some of these complications. It formulates an ordinary differential equation (ODE) in continuous
509   time. This ODE connects a source distribution to a target distribution. Solving the ODE via forward
510   integration through time permits to obtain samples from the target distribution, essentially by ‘moving’
511   samples from the known source distribution to the target time along a learned velocity field.
512
      To learn the velocity field, various mechanisms to interpolate between the source distribution and
513   the target distribution have been considered (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Tong et al.,
514   2024). Rectified flow matching emerged as a compelling variant, which linearly interpolates between
515   samples from the two distributions. For instance, it was used to attain impressive results on large
516   scale datasets (Ma et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024). Compared to other interpolation techniques, linear
517   interpolation encourages somewhat straight flows, which simplifies numerical solving of the ODE.
518
      The importance of straight flows was further studied in ReFlow (Liu et al., 2023). Multiple ODEs are
519   formulated and multiple velocity fields are learned one after the other by sequentially adjusting the
520   interpolations and ‘re-training.’ Consistency models (Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
521   2024) strive for straight flows by modifying the loss to encourage self-consistency across timesteps.
522   More details are provided in Appendix K.
523
      While the aforementioned works aim to establish straight flows either via ‘re-training’ or ‘re-
524
      parameterizing’ of an already existing flow, differently, in this work we study the results of enabling
525   a rectified flow to capture the ambiguity inherent in the usually employed ground-truth flow fields.
526
527   Structurally similar to this idea is work by Preechakul et al. (2022). In a first stage, an autoencoder
528
      is trained to compress images into a latent space. The resulting latents then serve as a conditioning
      signal for diffusion model training in a second stage. Note, this two-stage approach doesn’t directly
529
      model ambiguity in the data-space-time-space domain. In similar spirit is work by Pandey et al.
530
      (2022). A VAE and a diffusion model are trained in two separate stages, with the goal to enable
531   controllability of diffusion models. Related is also work by Eijkelboom et al. (2024) which focuses
532   on flow matching only for categorical data, achieving compelling results on graph generation tasks.
533
534
535   6    C ONCLUSION
536
537   We study Variational Rectified Flow Matching, a framework which enables to model the multi-
538   modal velocity vector fields induced by the ground-truth linear interpolation between source and
539   target distribution samples. Encouraging results can be achieved on low-dimensional synthetic and
      high-dimensional image data.

      K        A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK D ISCUSSION
885
886
      Here, we discuss related work aimed at improving the sample efficiency of diffusion and flow
887
      matching models, either via consistency modeling or via distillation. We used work by Yang et al.
888   (2024) as the consistency model baseline because it improved upon earlier consistency modeling
889   work by Song et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023) and also distillation work by Nguyen et al. (2024).
890   Specifically, we used the publicly available baseline.2
891
      Consistency models. Consistency models, such as those by Song et al. (2023) and Yang et al. (2024),
892
      enforce self-consistency across timesteps, ensuring trajectories map back to the same initial point.
893
      Moreover, Kim et al. (2023) ensure consistent trajectories for probability flow ODEs. While consis-
894   tency models focus on improving performance via trajectory alignment if few function evaluations
895   are used, they don’t model the multimodal ground-truth distribution, which is our goal.
896
897
      To illustrate this, we trained the recently developed consistency flow
      matching model proposed by Yang et al. (2024) (which improves
898
      upon work by Song et al. (2023) and Kim et al. (2023); both are
899
      not flow matching based) on the data for which VRFM results are
900   presented in Figs. 3 and 9. We obtain the results illustrated in Fig. 11.
901   As expected, we observe that classic consistency modeling does not
902   capture the multimodal velocity distribution, unlike the proposed
903   VRFM.
904
      We also want to note that we think consistency models are orthog-
905
      onal to our proposed variational formulation. Hence, we think it
906   is exciting future research to study the combination of variational Figure 11: Velocity distri-
907   formulations and consistency models, which is beyond the scope of bution of consistency flow
908   this paper.                                                                 matching (Yang et al., 2024).
909
      Distillation. Nguyen et al. (2024) perform distillation by optimizing
910
      step sizes in pretrained flow-matching models to refine trajectories and improve training dynamics.
911
      Moreover, Yan et al. (2024) perform distillation by introduceing a piecewise rectified flow mechanism
912   to accelerate flow-based generative models . Note, both methods distill useful information from a
913   pretrained model, either by using dynamic programming to optimize the step size or by applying
914   reflow to straighten trajectories, i.e., they focus on distilling already learned models. In contrast, our
915   VRFM focuses on learning via single-stage training, directly from ground-truth data, and without use
916   of a pre-trained deep net, a flow-matching model, which captures a multimodal velocity distribution.
