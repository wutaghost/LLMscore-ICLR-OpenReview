011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Watermarking techniques have been used to safeguard AI-generated content. In
014
               this paper, we study publicly detectable watermarking schemes (Fairoze et al.) of
               LLM, and have several research findings.
015
016
               First, we observe that two important security properties, robustness and sound-
               ness, may conflict with each other. We then formally investigate these two prop-
017
               erties in the presence of an arguably more realistic adversary that we called
018
               editing-adversary, and we can prove an impossibility result that, the robustness
019            and soundness properties cannot be achieved via a publicly-detectable single wa-
020            termarking scheme. Second, we demonstrate our feasible result: we for the first
021            time introduce the new concept of publicly-detectable dual watermarking scheme,
022            for AI-generated content. We provide a novel construction by using two publicly-
023            detectable watermarking schemes; each of the two watermarking schemes can
024            achieve “half” of the two required properties: one can achieve robustness, and the
025            other can achieve soundness. Eventually, we can combine the two halves into a
026            whole, and achieve the robustness and soundness properties at the same time. Our
027
               construction has been implemented and evaluated based on OPT-2.7B , LLaMA-7B
               and Mistral.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Generative AI and robust watermarking. Generative AI technologies, especially advancements
033   in large language models (LLMs), exhibit a broad range of impressive capabilities. However, these
034   powerful tools also present risks, such as the potential for misuse in spreading fabricated or false
035   information. To address these cybersecurity concerns, watermarking schemes have been proposed to
036   safeguard AI-generated content Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Aaronson (2023); Kuditipudi et al. (2023).
037   These schemes embed a watermark into the output text during LLM generation, with the primary
038   goal of ensuring that the watermark remains detectable even if the text is modified by an adversary.
039
      Achieving both robustness and soundness properties, using watermarking. Two important security
040
      properties, robustness and soundness, have been formalized Christ et al. (2023); Fairoze et al. (2023).
041
      In Christ et al. (2023), the soundness property is formally defined. To achieve the soundness property,
042   a construction has been developed. Concretely, a “secret watermark” is embedded in the output of the
043   generative model, by using a secret key. When a text is received, we can check whether the text has
044   been watermarked or not by using the secret key. The downside of the above mentioned privately
045   detectable watermarking mechanism is obvious: the generative model and the detector must share the
046   same secret key, and a party is not allowed to detect LLM-generated content if he/she is not aware
047   of the secret information that has been embedded in the content. Very recently, publicly detectable
048   watermarking for AI-generated content is proposed in Fairoze et al. (2023). With this new primitive,
049   any party is allowed to detect if a content is watermarked or not.
050   A technical difficulty. Unfortunately, we observe that there is a technical difficulty in achieving
051   soundness and robustness properties at the same time. Intuitively, the robustness property requires
052   that even if a watermarked text has been modified, the embedded watermark should not be eliminated;
053   instead, it should still be able to be detected. We remark that, an adversary could simply remove the
      entire watermarked text with the goal of eliminating the embedded watermark. To avoid this trivial


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      attack, in the formalization for the robustness property in Fairoze et al. (2023), the adversary is not
055   allowed to remove the entire watermarked text; instead, the modified version from the adversary,
056   denoted as t′ , and the original version of the watermarked text, denoted as t, must have an overlapping
057   of at least a δ-length segment, where δ ∈ N. To better illustrate our ideas, here let’s use t′ ▷◁δ t to
058   denote the δ-length segment overlapping between text t′ and text t. On the other hand, the soundness
059   property requires that an adversary, after seeing multiple watermarked texts, say t1 , t2 , . . . , tq , should
060   not be able to generate a valid (i.e., detectable) but “different” watermarked text t′ . Here difference
061   means there is no overlapping of a k-length window between two texts t′ and t, we write as t′ ̸▷◁k t,
062   where k ∈ N. For all texts, it is required that (t′ ̸▷◁k t1 ) ∧ (t′ ̸▷◁k t2 ) ∧ · · · ∧ (t′ ̸▷◁k tq ).
063   We must note that, the conditions in the two properties are conflicting with each other. Robustness
064   requires that the modified text has a sufficient overlap (δ-length) with the original text, while soundness
065   requires that the generated text does not have a sufficient overlap (k-length) with the original text.
066   Let t ∈ {ti }1≤i≤q . These two properties will lead to the following dilemma.
067   For simplicity, Let ℓ be the actual length of the longest overlapping segment of t′ and t.
068
069         Case 1 (δ ≥ k): If ℓ ≥ δ, then the condition t′ ▷◁δ t is satisfied. However, since ℓ ≥ k, the
070         condition t′ ̸▷◁k t is not met. Conversely, if ℓ < δ, then t′ ▷◁δ t is not satisfied. Therefore, we
071         conclude that no modified text t′ can simultaneously satisfy both t′ ▷◁δ t and t′ ̸▷◁k t in Case 1.
072         Case 2 (δ < k): If ℓ < δ, then t′ ▷◁δ t is not satisfied. If ℓ ≥ k, then t′ ̸▷◁k t is not satisfied. If
073         δ ≤ ℓ < k and the robustness property holds, meaning the watermark can be detected from t′ ,
074         then the soundness property is violated.
075
076   More concretely, in Case 1, if the length of the overlapping segment between t′ and t is greater than
077
      or equal to δ (i.e., ℓ ≥ δ), then t′ overlaps with t by more than k. Consequently, t′ does not satisfy
      the assumption of the soundness property, which states that t′ ̸▷◁k t. Conversely, if the length of the
078
      overlapping segment is less than δ (i.e., ℓ < δ), then t′ does not meet the assumption of the robustness
079
      property, which requires t′ ▷◁δ t. In Case 2, the length of the overlapping segment between t′ and
080   t can satisfy the assumptions of both the robustness and soundness properties (i.e., δ ≤ ℓ < k).
081   However, if the watermark can be detected from t′ , the soundness property is violated; otherwise, the
082   robustness property is compromised.
083
      Our research question. Based on the above discussions, we have the following question:
084
085
                Is it possible to achieve the robustness and soundness properties at the same time,
086
                in a publicly detectable watermarking scheme for LLM-generated content?

090
091   We give an affirmative answer to the above research question. In this paper, we carry out a systematic
092   study on publicly detectable watermarking for LLM-generated content. We want to highlight that,
093
      we are the first to introduce the new concept of publicly detectable dual watermarking, for LLM-
      generated content. Concretely, we have the following results.

096   1.1.1    E DITING ADVERSARIES AND PUBLICLY DETECTABLE SINGLE WATERMARKING
097
098   New adversaries with edit distance. We first remark that in Fairoze et al. (2023), the differences
099
      between texts are measured using the length of overlapping substrings. This way of measuring
      differences is not strict enough, as an adversary could change small amounts of text at specific
100
      positions to avoid long consecutive substrings. In natural language, a more reasonable way to
101
      measure the differences of text is based on edit distance. Edit distance is the minimal steps that are
102   needed to modify a text to another one. We emphasize that it is non-trivial using edit distance to
103   describe texts embedding watermark because small edit distance cannot guarantee the integrity of
104   the watermark. We are the first to consider a restricted but arguably more realistic adversary, that we
105   called editing-adversary, with the goal of providing a better understanding of the security properties
106   when we study watermarking for LLM-generated content. Here, considering the text generated by
107   the adversary and the text generated by the generative model, if the difference is measured by edit
      distance, then the adversary is called an editing-adversary.

      A formal treatment for publicly detectable single watermarking. If in a watermarking scheme,
109   the watermark can be detected publicly, it is defined as publicly detectable watermarking in Fairoze
110   et al. (2023). If the watermark detector returns a unique boolean value to indicate if the watermark
111   is detected in the watermarking scheme, we observe that the robustness and soundness security
112   properties may conflict with each other. We define this type of watermarking scheme as publicly
113   detectable single watermarking.
114
      An impossibility result in the presence of editing adversaries. We redefine soundness and
115   robustness in the presence of editing-adversary. We now are able to formally investigate if the two
116   conflicting properties, soundness and robustness, can be achieved at the same time or not, for a
117   publicly detectable single watermarking. Indeed, we can formally establish an impossibility result for
118   achieving soundness and robustness at the same time in the presence of an editing-adversary, if we
119   use a single watermarking scheme.

121   1.1.2    P UBLICLY DETECTABLE DUAL WATERMARKING AGAINST EDITING ADVERSARIES
122
123
      A new concept: Publicly detectable dual watermarking. To bypass the impossibility result, we
      introduce a new primitive, publicly detectable dual watermarking, for LLM-generated content. We
124
      formally define the syntax and the required properties, including robustness and soundness, of the
125
      new primitive. We remark that, the impossibility result of achieving robustness and soundness at the
126   same time, does not hold for the dual watermarking scheme anymore.
127
128   A new construction of publicly detectable dual watermarking scheme. We then demonstrate our
129
      feasibility result by constructing a publicly detectable dual watermarking scheme. In our construction,
      we use two publicly detectable watermarking schemes as building blocks. Note that neither scheme
130
      can achieve soundness and robustness at the same time in the presence of an editing-adversary;
131
      however, the two watermarking schemes can achieve “half” of the two required properties, respec-
132   tively: one can achieve robustness, and the other can achieve soundness. Interestingly, we are able to
133   combine the two halves into a whole, and achieve the robustness and soundness properties at the
134   same time! In this way, we successfully reconcile the two properties in watermarking for LLMs.
135
      Implementation and evaluation. We implement our publicly detectable dual watermarking scheme
136
      based on OPT-2.7B Zhang et al. (2022) , LLaMA-7B Touvron et al. (2023) and Mistral Jiang et al.
137
      (2023). We then evaluate the probability that a watermark bit is embedded correctly; we also evaluate
138   the quality of the text which is affected by watermark embedding. Our experiments show that, with
139   a small tune factor the watermark can be embedded with very high probability. Our experiments
140   further show that in our dual watermarking scheme, the text quality is reduced marginally. Finally,
141   our experiments demonstrate that the parameter selection made in the theoretical parts of the paper is
142   achievable.

232   3.1   W HY USING EDIT DISTANCE ( INSTEAD OF OVERLAPPING SUBSTRING )
233
234   Using overlapping substrings to measure differences between two texts is equivalent to measuring
235   text similarity by the length of the longest common substring. Compared to the length of overlapping
236   substrings, edit distance has significant advantages in measuring text similarity. Unlike the length
237   of overlapping substrings, edit distance evaluates the minimum number of operations (insertions,
238
      deletions, and substitutions) needed to transform one text into another. This allows it to compre-
      hensively consider words change between two texts, whether these matching parts are successive
239
      or separated. Consequently, edit distance can more generally capture local similarities within texts,
240
      such as matching subsequences scattered in different positions, providing a more accurate similarity
241   assessment.
242

339   5    P UBLICLY-D ETECTABLE D UAL WATERMARKING : C ONSTRUCTION
340
341   In this section, we show how to bypass the impossibility result as we demonstrated in the previous
342   section. Due to space limitations, we provide only a brief description of the construction in the main
343
      text, with the complete version included in Appendix E. Our novel construction which is named as
      Publicly-Detectable Dual Watermarking Scheme (PD2WS) will utilize two different watermarking
344
      strategies, short-range watermarking and long-range watermarking, for generating text of a LLM.
345
      Short-range watermarking means that when a word in text t is modified, it only impacts a small
346   number of bits (at least 1 bit) in the extracted watermark. This ensures that even if certain words
347   are modified, the extracted watermark remains similar to the original. Short-range watermarking
348   provides the robustness property. On the other hand, long-range watermarking means that when a
349   word is modified, it will affect a lot of bits in the extracted watermark. This implies that when a
350   few words are modified, the extracted watermark is broken. Long-range watermarking provides the
351   soundness property.
352   To embed watermark information in tokens, it is essential to select suitable tokens to signify 0 and 1
353   individually. We utilize the least significant bit of the hash value of a token to indicate the respective
354   bit of the embedded watermark. The study in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023) has demonstrated that
355   employing a modified softmax function can enhance the likelihood of selecting appropriate tokens
356   with minimal effect on text quality. We use a similar method to generate a token. The algorithm
357   TGPB takes prompt ρ, previous output tokens t, a preferred bit b and tune factor τ as input. TGPB
358   first employ an auto-regressive model Model(·) to produce a vector of logits D of each word in the
359   vocabulary V. The procedure that the tokens are generated with dual watermarks is illustrated in

655   AI-generated content detection. AI-generated content detection means that the content does not
656
      introduce any extra information when it is generated. The content is detected passively.
657   Early approaches to detecting AI-generated text typically involve identifying special features present
658   in human-generated text. If these features are identified, it is considered to be generated by a human;
659   otherwise, it is attributed to AI. Examples of such features include relative entropy scoring Lavergne
660   et al. (2008), perplexity Beresneva (2016), and other statistical signals Gehrmann et al. (2019).
661   To automatically detect AI-generated text, researchers have proposed training-based classifiers. Deep
662   learning is utilized as a binary classifier for this purpose in Zellers et al. (2019); Mitchell et al. (2023);
663   Hendrik Kirchner et al. (2023). Another method involves fine-tuning pre-trained language models, as
664   discussed in Wu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2022). The issue with this approach is its reliance on the
665   assumption that AI-generated text cannot mimic human-generated text with similar features. While
666   this may hold for early AI models, as models improve, the distinct features of AI-generated text will
667   diminish. For instance, GPT-4 OpenAI (2023) and other state-of-the-art models closely resemble
668
      human writing. Research in Chakraborty et al. (2023) indicates that as AI-generated text approaches
      human quality, text distinguishers require longer text samples.
669
670   Furthermore, research has demonstrated the possibility of training models to alter text in a way that
671   deceives text distinguishers Krishna et al. (2023); Sadasivan et al. (2023).
672   Watermarking for LLM-generated content. Watermarking hides identifying information within
673   AI-generated text, enabling the detection of whether the text is AI-generated. Recent research has
674   explored the use of machine learning for watermarking, as evidenced by works such as Abdelnabi
675   & Fritz (2021); Qiang et al. (2023); Yoo et al. (2023); Munyer & Zhong (2023); Liu et al. (2023).
676   However, it is important to note that all schemes in this category are purely empirical and lack
677   of formal definition of security properties such as robustness, soundness, or distortion-freeness.
678   Recently, a series of research have advanced the rigorous definition and security proof of LLM
679   watermarking, and our work is also following this line of development. The main references are listed
680
      in the following.
681   In Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), it is demonstrated that a watermark can be inserted into the output of
682   LLM if the model entropy is high. A watermark can be planted by hashing previous tokens to embed
683   a watermark signal in the next token. Furthermore, this study quantifies the distortions introduced
684   by the watermark through the measurement of perplexity, which reflects the difference between
685   the distribution produced by the unaltered model and the distribution produced by the model with
686
      watermarking.
687   Another approach to LLM watermarking is the Gumbel softmax scheme introduced in Aaronson
688   (2023). This scheme utilizes exponential minimum sampling to draw samples from the model using
689   randomness derived from previous tokens (via hashing). Additionally, Kuditipudi et al. (2023) has
690   developed a family of watermarking schemes that are designed to maximize robustness.
691   The formal security properties such as soundness, completeness of LLM are defined in Christ et al.
692   (2023). The security properties are proved under the assumption that an contiguous substring of the
693   output remaining sufficiently high entropy. The watermark in Christ et al. (2023) is undetectable
694   without a secret key.
695
      In Fairoze et al. (2023), the concept of publicly detectable schemes is explored for the first time. The
696   scheme proposed in Fairoze et al. (2023) utilizes digital signatures to facilitate the public detection of
697   the watermark. The robustness and soundness of this scheme are demonstrated under the assumption
698   of substring overlapping. However, it is observed that the assumptions underlying these two properties
699   are contradictory and cannot be simultaneously satisfied, as we discussed in Section 3. To circumvent
700   the impossibility result, we introduce a novel watermarking approach termed “dual watermarking,”
701   detailed in Section 4. The concept of “dual watermarking” involves the use of two distinct watermarks
      to ensure robustness and soundness, respectively.

      The term ”dual watermarking” has also been employed in Zhu et al. (2024) (a work parallel to ours).
703   The objective of the method presented in Zhu et al. (2024) is to optimize the efficiency and quality of
704   watermarking by incorporating dual secret patterns into both the token probability distribution and
705   sampling strategies. It is important to note that the design and security aspects explored in Zhu et al.
706   (2024) are entirely distinct from those in our study.
707
      The watermarking mechanism for generative models is still in the early stages of research. In recent
708
      study Zhang et al. (2023), the impossibility of achieving strong watermarks for generative models is
709   proved. A strong watermarking scheme satisfies the property that a computationally bounded attacker
710   cannot erase the watermark without causing significant quality degradation. In their paper, the authors
711   demonstrated the attack on several existing watermarking schemes with minor quality degradation.
712   However, their attack requires extra computing resources to alter tokens of text. We remark, there
713   is no conflict between the impossibility result in Zhang et al. (2023), and our feasibility result (i.e.,
714   our dual watermarking in Section 5 and Section F): in our feasibility result, we assume that the edit
715   distance of text is bounded, and the attacker is not allowed to change the text a lot.
716   In a very recent paper Zhou et al. (2024), the authors noted that existing LLM watermarking schemes
717   cannot simultaneously achieve robustness and soundness, meaning they cannot resist both removal
718   attacks and spoofing attacks at the same time. In their paper, they proposed a scheme called Bilevel,
719   which uses two watermarking mechanisms to resist these two types of attacks separately. This
720   work aligns with the impossibility theorem presented in our paper, and the constructed scheme
721   also meets the definition of a Publicly-Detectable Dual Watermarking Scheme as provided in our
722   paper. However, the paper does not provide a rigorous definition of security, nor does it specify
723
      the conditions required to achieve both security features simultaneously. The construction of the
      Bilevel scheme also has shortcomings. For instance, its digital signature-based approach requires the
724
      signature to be embedded strictly correctly into the output text, which in some cases may necessitate
725
      choosing tokens that significantly degrade text quality.

791

792

793   into another (i.e., insertion, deletion, and substitution). We use edit distance to limit how a text t can
794   be modified by adversary.
795
796

797

798

799   define the substitution cost function S : V × V → ZS so that for a, b ∈ V, S(a, b) is the cost of
800   replacing the element a by the element b in a given string.

      We now describe the robustness and soundness properties as in Fairoze et al. (2023). Intuitively, the
865   robustness property requires that even if a watermarked text is modified, the embedded watermark
866   cannot be eliminated and can still be detected. However, an adversary could simply remove the entire
867   watermarked text so that the embedded watermark can be eliminated. To avoid this trivial attack, in
868   the formalization for the robustness property in Fairoze et al. (2023), the adversary is not allowed to
869   remove the entire watermarked text; instead, the modified version from the adversary, denoted as t′ ,
870   and the original version of the watermarked text, denoted as t, must share at least a δ-length segment,
871   where δ ∈ N.
872   On the other hand, the soundness property requires that an adversary, after seeing multiple water-
873   marked texts, say t1 , t2 , . . . , tq , should not be able to generate a valid (i.e., detectable) but “different”
874   watermarked text. We will introduce some notations, and formally define the difference between two
875   watermarked texts.

965

969
970   On the other hand, long-range watermarking means that when a word is modified, it will affect a lot
971   of bits in the extracted watermark. This implies that when a few words are modified, the extracted
      watermark is broken. Long-range watermarking provides the soundness property.

       F.1   A NALYSIS OF WATERMARK E RRORS
1210
1211   In Algorithm 4, a watermark bit b is probabilistically embedded in a token x by choosing x such that
1212   LSB (H(x)) = b. If a token x satisfies that LSB (H(x)) = b, we say x is good otherwise it is bad. A
1213   good token means a bit of the watermark is embedded correctly and a bad token implies that an error
1214   bit of the watermark is embedded. We use pgood to denote the probability that a token x is good and
1215   use pbad to denote the probability that a token x is bad.
1216                                       pgood = Pr[LSB(H(x)) = b],
                                                                                                        (4)
1217                                        pbad = Pr[LSB(H(x)) ̸= b].
1218   It is obvious that pgood + pbad = 1.
1219
       The probability pgood is adjusted by the factor τ using the softmax function. For a candidate
1220
       token x, if LSB(H(x)) = b, its probability of being chosen will increase according to the factor τ .
1221
       Otherwise, its probability of being chosen will decrease relatively. If we set τ = 0 in Algorithm 4,
1222   the probability of tokens being chosen will not be tuned. In this case, we have the probability that
1223   pgood = Pr[LSB(H(x)) = b] = 21 .
1224
1225
       The probability that a token is good is independent of the other tokens. For any consecutive n tokens
       that are generated in Algorithm 4, let α and β be the number of tokens which are good and bad
1226
       respectively. The expectation of α is E(α) = n · pgood and the expectation of β is E(β) = n · pbad .
