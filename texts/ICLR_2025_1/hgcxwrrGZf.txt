009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Sequential recommendation in e-commerce leverages users’ anonymous brows-
012            ing histories to offer personalized product suggestions without relying on per-
013            sonal information. While item ID-based sequential recommendations are com-
014            monly used, they often fail to fully capture the diverse factors influencing user
015            preferences, such as textual descriptions, visual content, and pricing. These fac-
016            tors represent distinct modalities in recommender systems. Existing multimodal
017            sequential recommendation models typically employ either early or late fusion of
018            different modalities, overlooking the alignment of corresponding positions in time
019
               of product sequences that represent users’ browsing preferences. To address these
               limitations, this paper proposes a unified framework for multimodal fusion in rec-
020
               ommender systems, introducing the Multimodal Time-aligned Shared Token Rec-
021
               ommender (MTSTRec). MTSTRec leverages a transformer-based architecture
022            that incorporates a single time-aligned shared token for each product, allowing for
023            efficient cross-modality fusion that also aligns in time. This approach not only
024            preserves the distinct contributions of each modality but also aligns them to bet-
025            ter capture user preferences. Additionally, the model extracts rich features from
026            text, images, and other product data, offering a more comprehensive representa-
027            tion of user decision-making in e-commerce. Extensive experiments demonstrate
028            that MTSTRec achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple sequential
029            recommendation benchmarks, significantly improving upon existing multimodal
030
               fusion strategies.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In e-commerce and online platforms, Sequential Recommendation Systems (SRS) have become
036   crucial for providing personalized product suggestions based on users’ browsing history. SRS has
037   evolved from simple Markov Chains (Shani et al., 2013) to more sophisticated neural networks.
038   Early approaches like GRU4Rec (Hidasi et al., 2016) employed recurrent neural networks (RNNs)
039
      to model user behavior sequences. While RNNs can capture short-term dependencies, they struggle
      with long-term dependencies due to vanishing gradients. Transformer-based models have been pro-
040
      posed to address the above issue, such as SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018) and BERT4Rec (Sun
041
      et al., 2019). They leverage self-attention mechanisms to capture dependencies across the entire
042   sequence, thereby improving both efficiency and scalability. However, these models still primarily
043   focus on single-modal data and overlook the rich multimodal information available in real-world
044   applications, which could provide deeper insights into user preferences.
045
      Multimodal recommendation systems combine diverse data types, such as images and texts. Image-
046
      based methods use pre-trained convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (O’Shea & Nash, 2015) like
047
      ResNet (He et al., 2015), to capture the visual features, while text-based methods use models like
048   BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) for product descriptions and reviews. CLIP aligns text and images
049   through a multimodal approach, enabling zero-shot transfer for various vision-language tasks (Rad-
050   ford et al., 2021). Recently, large language models (LLMs) have excelled in extracting hidden
051   textual information, enriching item representations (Geng et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2024). Rather than
052   full-scale LLM training, we focus on leveraging LLMs to enrich item descriptions by extracting
053   hidden textual features. Additionally, while many recommendation models rely on image classifi-
      cation or recognition to predict purchase intent, we argue that textual data alone is often sufficient


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      for identifying what a product is. Instead, images should capture product aesthetics and style, which
055   are especially important on platforms that sell the same product in various patterns or designs.
056
057   When it comes to multimodal fusion, designing a unified model that effectively integrates different
058
      modalities presents significant challenges. Different modalities and their specialized formats learn
      at different speeds and patterns. This makes it challenging to combine them effectively into a single
059
      system. In the context of recommendation systems, the common modalities are typically images
060
      and text, which have fundamentally different input representations. Early fusion techniques are
061   often used to unify these features, where modalities are combined before entering the recommen-
062   dation model (He & McAuley, 2015; Liu et al., 2019). However, this approach fails to account for
063   the significant differences in input representations and neural network architectures across modali-
064   ties. In contrast, late fusion methods process each modality separately, addressing modality-specific
065   challenges, but overlook the fact that at each time step, different modalities correspond to the same
066   product (Liang et al., 2023).
067   To address both the modality processing and fusion issues, we propose MTSTRec (Multimodal
068   Time-aligned Shared Token Recommender), a novel framework for multimodal feature integra-
069   tion and cross-modal interaction using time-aligned shared tokens. MTSTRec consists of two main
070   components: the Feature Extractor and the Multimodal Transformer. The feature extractor takes the
071   browsing history sequence, where each item includes a product ID, image, text, and price. We use
072   different extractors for each modality. For text, we enrich the data through LLMs with task-specific
073   prompts to extract implicit consumer preferences. For images, we focus on style rather than clas-
074   sification, using Gram metrics to capture visual patterns that influence purchasing behavior. These
075
      inputs are then projected into separate feature embeddings for each modality. In the proposed multi-
      modal transformer, a self-attention encoder is first applied to model the information for each feature.
076
      During fusion, we adopt a mid-fusion strategy, where modalities are processed independently and
077
      then combined in the intermediate stage. Our proposed Time-aligned Shared Token fusion module
078   (TST) learns cross-modal interactions by aligning features from different modalities at each time
079   step of the product interaction sequence. This ensurs efficient feature sharing across modalities
080   while maintaining the chronological order and consistency of product interactions.
081
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
082
083   • We propose a unified framework for the multimodal recommendation that seamlessly accommo-
084     dates various input data types, effectively integrating diverse information such as ID, text, image,
085     and other modalities. This flexible approach ensures that different types of data are processed and
086     combined in a cohesive manner, enhancing the system’s adaptability across different tasks.
087
      • We introduce a novel TST module that leverages shared tokens to learn cross-modal interactions
088
        at each time step of the sequence. This design ensures that information from different modalities
089
        is aligned and fused in a time-consistent manner. By maintaining the chronological structure of
090     the sequential data, the TST module effectively captures the evolving relationships between user
091     interactions and product features. This innovative module not only enhances the performance of
092     multimodal recommendation systems but also offers a flexible and scalable that can be applied
093     to various tasks beyond recommendation, making it suitable for a wide range of multimodal
094     applications.
095
096   • MTSTRec outperforms state-of-the-art methods on three real-world e-commerce datasets, setting
097     a new benchmark in multimodal recommendation systems. Our ablation studies not only highlight
098
        the contribution of individual features but also reveal deeper insights into the distinct roles of
        various modalities across diverse e-commerce environments, offering a valuable understanding
099
        for future advancements in the field.

104   2.1   S EQUENTIAL R ECOMMENDATION S YSTEMS
105
106   SRS aims to predict the next item a user will interact with based on their browsing history, provid-
107   ing personalized recommendations. Traditional Markov Chain model (Shani et al., 2013) employed
      simple probabilistic methods but struggled to capture complex user behavior patterns. GRU4Rec


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Hidasi et al., 2016) introduced RNNs to improve sequence modeling by capturing temporal depen-
109   dencies. Transformer-based models like SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018) enhance this approach
110   by using self-attention mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017) and causal masking, preserving tempo-
111   ral order and efficiently capturing short and long-term dependencies. BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019)
112   extends this further by adopting a bidirectional Transformer model and using a CLOZE task for
113   training. Unlike SASRec’s causal masking, BERT4Rec allows the model to attend to both past
114   and future items in the sequence, capturing richer contextual information. However, these models
115   remain focused primarily on single-modal data, overlooking the potential benefits of multimodal
116   information.
117
118
119   2.2   M ULTIMODAL R ECOMMENDATION S YSTEMS
120
121
      Multimodal recommendation systems are essential for integrating information from multiple modal-
122
      ities to create more accurate and comprehensive predictions. These systems generally operate
123
      through two main stages: raw feature extraction and feature fusion (Liu et al., 2024).
124
125   Different modalities require tailored methods to capture their unique attributes in the raw feature ex-
126   traction stage. For instance, image-based features are often extracted using CNNs (O’Shea & Nash,
127
      2015) or, more recently, Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), which excel at pro-
      cessing visual data. Textual features often rely on pre-trained language models (Devlin et al., 2019),
128
      capturing semantic meanings from product textual information. In addition, advances in LLMs
129
      (Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI et al., 2024) have significantly enhanced text-based feature extraction.
130   LLM-Rec (Lyu et al., 2024) utilizes LLM and prompts to generate richer contextual representations,
131   enhancing recommendation quality. Other LLM-based approaches have also emerged, further re-
132   fining text comprehension to optimize the use of textual features in recommendation systems (Zhao
133   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024).
134
      After raw feature extraction, the system proceeds to the feature fusion stage, where these multi-
135
      modal data are combined and processed, it can be categorized into three main approaches (Zhou
136   et al., 2023): (i) Early fusion involves merging the features from different modalities at the initial
137   stages of the model. For example, VBPR (He & McAuley, 2015) integrates visual features into a
138   matrix factorization approach. However, early fusion may miss important temporal relationships and
139   modality-specific behaviors by merging features too early.(ii) Mid-fusion delays modality combina-
140   tion for more refined processing, as seen in MM-Rec (Wu et al., 2022), which uses a cross-modal
141   attention mechanism to combine textual and visual information effectively. However, these methods
142   often fail to capture temporal relationships, which are crucial in sequential recommendations. (iii)
143   Late fusion keeps modalities separate until the final stage, as in MMMLP (Liang et al., 2023), the
144
      final outputs from three different modalities are concatenated before making the prediction. While
      preserving modality-specific features, it fails to capture early interactions between modalities and
145
      sequences, leading to suboptimal performance with complex sequential patterns.
146
147   To address these limitations, we aim to design a fusion method that can temporally align and inte-
148   grate different modalities, facilitating cross-modal interactions at each time step. This novel fusion
149   ensures that the chronological order of product interactions, ensuring temporal consistency while
150
      allowing for efficient multimodal feature sharing across the entire sequence.

192   3.1     P RELIMINARIES
193
194   Let the set of items be defined as I = {i1 , i2 , . . . , ik , . . . , i|I| }, where each item ik ∈ I consists
195   of four essential elements: bk , which represents the product ID; vk , which represents the image
196   of the item; tk , which contains the textual information; and ck , which represents the price of the
197   item. Therefore, each item ik can be expressed as a tuple ik = (bk , vk , tk , ck ), capturing the
198   multiple modalities that describe it. For each user, their browsing history is represented as S =
199   [s1 , s2 , . . . , sn ], where each si ∈ I corresponds to an item the user has interacted with, and n is the
200   length of the session. The goal of SRS is to predict the next item sn+1 that a user will interact with,
201   based on the user’s interaction history S.

214   to capture item relationships, improving the accuracy of recommendations by understanding item
215   identities within sequences. The processing method for the price extractor is similar; please refer to

      Dataset. Our experiments utilize three datasets: two proprietary datasets from AviviD Innovative
370
      Multimedia—Fresh-Food E-commerce and House-Hold E-commerce, which will be released after
371
      the study, and a public dataset from H&M. The proprietary datasets capture user interactions, includ-
372   ing pageviews and purchases, while the public dataset focuses on purchase records in the trousers
373   category from H&M. Detailed information is provided in Appendix A.7.
374   Dataset Splitting. Each session in the proprietary datasets consists of historical clicks and a final
375   purchase order. The data is split chronologically into 75% for training, 12.5% for validation, and
376   12.5% for testing based on the purchase orders. For the H&M (Trousers) dataset, which contains
377   only purchase actions, items are sorted by purchase time, and those bought on the last day are used
      as the answer set, ensuring consistency across all datasets (Meng et al., 2020). The model predicts

378
      items in purchase orders as a multi-label recommendation task, where each sequence may have mul-
379   tiple correct answers (purchases).
380   Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate all models using three popular top-k ranking metrics: Nor-
381   malized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@k), Hit Rate (HR@k), and Mean Reciprocal Rank
382   (MRR@k), with k set to 5 and 10. NDCG@k remains unchanged as it inherently supports multiple
383   selections. For HR@k and MRR@k, we adjust the calculations to account for the multi-answer
384   format, ensuring a fair evaluation. For more detail, please refer to Appendix A.8.
385   Benchmark Models. We compare our MTSTRec model with well-known SRS baselines, which are
386   grouped into two categories. The first category includes general models that rely solely on historical
387   interaction data, using item IDs as the primary feature, such as GRU4Rec (Hidasi et al., 2016;
388   Hidasi & Karatzoglou, 2017), SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018), and BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019).
389   The second category extends beyond item IDs by incorporating additional modalities, including
390   text, image, and price data. This group includes models like MMMLP (Liang et al., 2023), as
391   well as the enhanced versions of SASRec+ and BERT4Rec+ , which concatenate image and text
392   features as in our model. These modifications allow them to act as early fusion models, combining
393
      all features upfront to ensure a fair comparison of multimodal inputs. For more details on the

      4.4    A BLATION S TUDY OF F USION M ODULE
469
470   In this ablation study, we evaluated the impact of different fusion strategies on the Fresh-Food E-
471   commerce dataset, comparing TST (used in MTSTRec) with two alternative setups: one resembling
472   late fusion by removing the TST and fusion encoder, and another resembling early fusion by re-
473   moving the entire multimodal transformer. In the late fusion alternative, features are processed
474   independently and concatenated only at the final step, and in the early fusion alternative, all features
475   are concatenated immediately after feature extraction and passed through a single encoder.
476   As shown in Table 3, TST outperforms both alternatives. TST achieves an NDCG@5 of 0.8800,
477   compared to 0.8621 for early fusion. In the early fusion, all features are processed together from the
478   start, which can lead to the dilution of unique information from each modality, as the model is not
479   able to treat them distinctly. This reduces the model’s ability to fully leverage the strengths of each
480   feature. On the other hand, late fusion limits the interaction between different features until the end
481   of the sequence. This restriction hinders the model’s ability to capture cross-modal dependencies
482
      throughout the sequence, leading to a decrease in performance. Specifically, the NDCG@5 score
      dropped from 0.8800 to 0.8211, a decrease of 0.0589.
483
484   In conclusion, TST’s ability to facilitate cross-modal interaction at each time step leads to better
485   results, whereas early and late fusion approaches fall short due to inefficient handling of feature
      interactions.

526
527
528   This paper introduces MTSTRec, a novel Multimodal Time-aligned Shared Token Recommender to
529   fuse and transmit essential information across different modalities. Our approach allows for precise
530   integration of multimodal features such as product IDs, images, text, and prices while maintaining
531
      the unique contributions of each modality. Through extensive experimentation on real-world e-
      commerce datasets, we demonstrated that MTSTRec significantly outperforms state-of-the-art base-
532
      lines across various evaluation metrics. Our ablation studies revealed the critical role of each feature
533
      type in improving recommendation accuracy, particularly the importance of item identity and textual
534   descriptions in different e-commerce scenarios. Moreover, we showed that the proposed TST fusion
535   method consistently surpasses both early and late fusion strategies by enabling cross-modal inter-
536   action that aligns in time throughout the sequence. In summary, MTSTRec represents a significant
537   advancement in multimodal sequential recommendation, offering a flexible and efficient framework
538   that can be adapted to various e-commerce applications. Future work will focus on extending the
539   model to other domains and exploring additional multimodal features for even more personalized
      recommendations.

845   A.6   D ETAILED A RCHITECTURE AND P ROCESS OF THE S ELF -ATTENTION E NCODER
846
      Self-Attention Encoder architecture follows transformer encoder architecture proposed by Vaswani
847   et al. (2017). The input embedding from each modality Z are processed independently through
848   independent transformer encoder Encoder with multiple layers. Each layer consists of three key
849   components: Multi-Head Self-Attention (MSA), Layer Normalization (LN), and Multilayer Per-
850   ceptron (MLP), all connected via residual connections. The transformation at layer l is defined as
851   follows:
852
853
                                  Y l−1 = MSA(LN(Z l−1 )) + LN(Z l−1 ),                             (10)
854
855                                        Z l = MLP(LN(Y l−1 )).                                   (11)
856
857   In the MSA block, self-attention computes the attention scores between tokens, allowing each token
858   to dynamically attend to other tokens in the sequence. The attention mechanism is defined as:
859
860                            MSA(X) = Attention(WQ X, WK X, WV X),                                (12)
861
862   where WQ , WK , and WV are the weight matrices used to project the input tensor X into queries,
863   keys, and values, respectively. The attention scores are computed as the dot product of queries and
      keys, enabling the model to focus on the most relevant parts of the input.

896   our experiments: NDCG@k, HR@k, and MRR@k, where k is set to 5 and 10. Since our problem

       A.11    C ONGIGURATION D ETAILS OF THE S HARED T OKEN
986
987    Time-aligned Shared Tokens (TST) (1:1), used in our MTSTRec model, pairs each time step in
988    the sequence with a corresponding shared token in a 1:1 relationship. This means that each product
989    in the sequence is aligned with a single shared token, allowing features from different modalities of
990    the same product to interact and share information at that specific time step. This design ensures
991    that information sharing is precise and time-aligned, leading to more accurate feature fusion. TST
992    (1:2) & TST (1:4) include configurations where each time step is associated with multiple shared
993    tokens rather than just one. For example, in TST (1:2), each product in the sequence is paired with
994    two shared tokens, and in TST (1:4), each product is paired with four shared tokens. These variants
995
       allow for more extensive information sharing at each time step.
996    Fusion Bottlenecks (all:4+1) is based on a configuration from Google (Nagrani et al., 2021) orig-
997    inally designed for sequence fusion classification tasks involving image and speech data. In this
998    setting, shared tokens are not tied to a specific time step but attend to the entire sequence, enabling
999    broader information exchange across the sequence. For a fair comparison in our multimodal sequen-
1000   tial recommendation task, we adapted this method to a Fusion Bottlenecks (all:20+1) configuration,
1001
       matching the number of tokens used in our TST approach.
