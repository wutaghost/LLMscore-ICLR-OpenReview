009                                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                Recent self-supervised image segmentors have achieved promising zero-shot per-
012                formance. However, their pretraining schedule is multi-stage and alternates be-
013                tween offline pseudo-masks generation and parameters update, which leads to un-
014                stable training and sub-optimal solution. To solve this issue, we present Online
015                Agglomerative Pooling (OAP) that allows efficiently generating universal pseudo-
016                masks and updating parameters simultaneously at each training step. Specifically,
017                OAP contains a stack of instance pooling and semantic pooling layers. By us-
018
                   ing a layer-varied threshold, OAP can generate multi-hierarchy masks that can
                   provide more visual details for segmentation. Compared with MaskCut or Divide-
019
                   Conquer, each OAP layer can identify connected nodes in parallel, thus can gener-
020
                   ate universal pseudo-masks for a single image within tens of milliseconds. More-
021                over, to deploy OAP in online pretraining, we devise a teacher-student framework
022                with Query-wise Self-distillation, where the local view queries are each aligned
023                with the matched global view queries to learn the local-to-global correspondence.
024                Compared with other multi-stage offline pretraining methods, our framework can
025                effectively scale to larger datasets while ensuring quicker convergence. Exten-
026                sive experiments on the COCO, PASCAL VOC, Cityscapes, and UVO datasets
027                show that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on zero-shot instance
028                segmentation, semantic segmentation, and panoptic segmentation. Our code and
029
                   pretrained models shall be released upon acceptance of this work.

      terpretations of what constitutes an instance or how semantic categories are defined. These inherent

069   we aim to explore the design of a self-supervised pretraining model for image segmentation that

072   To reduce reliance on manual annotations and learn from visual data themselves, several self-
073   supervised zero-shot instance segmentors (Wang et al. (2023a; 2024); Niu et al. (2024); Arica et al.
074   (2024)) are proposed. They first use pretrained SSL features and graph-partitioning (Wang et al.

077

078
      ing is directly extracted from the last checkpoint, which makes the training unstable when switching
079   to another round. This may lead to sub-optimal solution.
080
081   The key solution for enabling online pretraining is to reduce the processing time of pseudo-mask
082
      generation algorithm. To achieve this goal, we propose the Online Agglomerative Pooling (OAP)
      algorithm to efficiently generate universal pseudo-masks of one image within tens of milliseconds.
083
      Our key insight is that semantically-similar patches are spatially close in group. We can identify
084
      these groups of strongly-connected nodes in parallel (Pearce (2005)). As shown in Table. 1, OAP
085   can generate comparable zero-shot instance segmentation performance to previous methods but is
086   100× faster than previous methods, which enables online large-scale pretraining for self-supervised
087   universal segmentation.
088
      Equipped with the much more efficient pseudo-masks generation algorithm, we propose to adopt
089
      a teacher-student framework with Query-wise Self-distillation as the pretext task to train the self-
090
      suerpervised segmentation model. Unlike previous self-supervised representation models that utilize
091   global pooling or the ViT[CLS] (Dosovitskiy (2020)) token, we condense each image into a set
092   of universal queries, and train each student query to predict the corresponding bipartite-matched
093   teacher query. We empirically find that this simple but effective approach benefits self-supervised
094   segmentation by enabling more fine-grained learning of individual segments instead of focusing just
095   on the global context.
096

106          • Extensive experiment results on COCO, PASCAL VOC, UVO, and Cityscapes validate

      Self-supervised Representation Learning. Self-supervised representation learning aims to learn
111
      universal (Bengio et al. (2013) features from large amounts of unlabeled instances without manual
112
      annotation. A pretext task is often pre-defined to train the model. According to types of pretext
113   task, they can be classified into contrastive learning methods and masked image modelling meth-
114   ods. Contrastive learning based methods include pretexts based on negative samples (Chen et al.
115   (2020a); He et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021)), clustering (Caron et al. (2020); Asano et al. (2019)),
116   self-distillation (Caron et al. (2021); Grill et al. (2020); Chen & He (2021)), feature decorrela-
117   tion (Zbontar et al. (2021); Bardes et al. (2021)). Masked image modelling methods include pretexts
118   tasks based on low-leve targets (He et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2020b); Wei et al. (2022)), high-level
119   targets (Bao et al. (2021); Dong et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024), self-distillation (Chen et al. (2022a);
120   Baevski et al. (2022)), and multi-modal teacher (Zhou et al. (2022); Peng et al. (2022)).
121   Self-supervised Instance and Semantic Segmentation. Recently studies (Caron et al. (2021);
122   Hamilton et al. (2022); Siméoni et al. (2021); Vo et al. (2020; 2021)) show pretrained SSL fea-
123   tures can capture pixel-to-pixel semantic similarity. Inspired by that, several works (Hamilton et al.
124   (2022); Wang et al. (2023a); Arica et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024); Seong et al. (2023); Kim et al.
125   (2024); Wang et al. (2023b); Van Gansbeke et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2024)) aim
126   to distill or self-train a segmentation model based on the pretrained SSL representation. These meth-
127
      ods can be classified into semantic segmentation methods, zero-shot instance segmentation methods,
      and universal segmentation methods. State-of-the-art unsupervised zero-shot instance segmentation
128
      methods (Wang et al. (2023a); Arica et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024) adopt a cut and learn pipeline,
129
      in the sense that they first generate pseudo-masks using pretrained SSL representation, and then learn
130   a model through multi-round self-training. Unsupervised semantic segmentation methods (Hamil-
131   ton et al. (2022); Seong et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024) adopts a distillation-based
132   objective, in the sense that the projected segmentation features should preserve the pixel-to-pixel
133   semantic correspondence in the original SSL representation space. Recently, U2Seg (Niu et al.
134   (2024)) proposes a self-supervised universal segmentation framework for semantic, instance, and
135   panoptic segmentation. They adopt the similar cut-and-learn pretraining pipeline. To make the
136   model semantic-aware, they cluster the masks to generate the semantic pseudo-labels for semantic
137   training.
138   In contrast, we propose Online Agglomerative Pooling, which is an efficient pseudo-mask generation
139   algorithm, that enables pretraining self-supervised segmentation models in an online manner and
140   effectively scaling to large datasets, without any offline generation or clustering. As far as we know,
141   our work is the first online method for self-supervised instance and universal segmentation.
142   Graph Pooling. As an esential component of Graph Neural Networks, graph pooling is important to
143   obtain a holistic graph-level representation. Graph pooling can be roughly divided into flat pooling
144   and hierarchical pooling according to its role in graph-level representation learning. Flat pool-
145   ing (Dwivedi et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2020); Noutahi et al. (2019)), also known as Graph Readout,
146   aims to obtain a global graph-level representation. Hierachical pooling aims to iteratively coarsen-
147   ing the graph into smaller size. It can be classified into node clustering pooling (Wu et al. (2019);
148   Liu et al. (2021)) and node drop pooling (Gao et al. (2019); Lee et al. (2021); Gao et al. (2021)).

      Unsupervised Universal Image Segmentation (U2Seg). Universal segmentation requires annota-
      tions of instance-level masks, semantic-level masks, and the class label for each mask. As the first
154
      self-supervised universal segmentor, U2Seg (Niu et al. (2024)) follows a similar cut-and-learn (Wang
155
      et al. (2023a)) pretraining pipeline. The ”cut” stage is offline and generates the pseudo universal an-
156   notations for the whole dataset. Specifically, based on the self-supervised feature maps (Caron et al.
157   (2021)), U2Seg uses the graph-partitioning algorithm MaskCut (Wang et al. (2023a)) to generate
158   a set of instance-level pseudo-masks for each image. K-means is then used to cluster these masks
159   and get their pseudo-labels. For semantic-level annotations, U2Seg uses a distillation-based model
160   STEGO (Hamilton et al. (2022)) to get the pixel-wise semantic pseudo-label. The ”learn” stage al-
161   ternates between training model using the current annotations and generating new pseudo universal
      annotations using the previous model checkpoint.

199   4.1   OVERVIEW A RCHITECTURE
200
201   As shown in Figure 2, given an image, we use multi-crop (Caron et al. (2020)) augmentation to get
202   a set of local views from the original view. The original view is input to the teacher branch, where
203   a multi-scale encoder (Chen et al. (2022b); Caron et al. (2021) extracts its features, and a series of
204   Online Agglomerative Pooling (OAP) layers are then employed to generate the instance-level and
205   semantic-level pseudo-masks along with their feature embeddings. Simultaneously, each local view
206
      is fed to the student encoder to first extract its features. A mask decoder (Cheng et al. (2022)) is
      then used to predict its universal masks and feature embeddings. We partition the object queries into
207
      two distinct parts—semantic queries, distinguished by the learnable token [SEM], which focus on
208
      capturing semantic-level information across the image, and instance queries, distinguished by the
209   learnable token [INS], which aim to identify individual instances within an image.
210
211   After both branches have generated their respective universal masks and feature embeddings, we
212
      treat teacher outputs as targets and perform bipartite matching to align the student outputs. Since the
      teacher processes the global view, we spatially crop the teacher’s pseudo-masks to correspond with
213
      the student’s local views before matching.
214
215   We also incorporate the pretext task, online clustering with self-distillation (Caron et al. (2020;
      2021)), to train the student branch. Unlike DINO or SwAV, which distills a single global image fea-


                                                              4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      ture, query-wise self-distillation uses multiple query features per image corresponding to different
217   segments. After matching, each student query learns from its teacher counterpart. This approach
218   benefits self-supervised segmentation by enabling fine-grained learning of individual segments.
219
220
      Unlike previous seminal works Wang et al. (2023a; 2024); Niu et al. (2024); Arica et al. (2024),
      our pretraining framework does not include any offline pseudo-masks generation or offline clus-
221
      tering, thereby enabling self-supervised segmentation models to scale effectively to larger datasets.
222
      Moreover, as shown in Section 5, continuously updating pseudo-annotations allows our model to
223   converge more rapidly and reliably, avoiding the loss fluctuations associated with previous alternat-
224   ing multi-stage frameworks.

348   4.3   M ODEL T RAINING
349
350   To deploy Online Agglomerative Pooling (OAP) for scalable online self-supervised universal seg-
351   mentation, we devise a teacher-student framework with momentum updating strategy. Moreoever,
352   we introduce a novel pretext task, Query-wise Self-Distillation, specifically designed to train models
353   for self-supervised segmentation.
354   Multi-scale Encoder and Zero-initialization. To utilize the important multi-scale information ( Lin
355   et al. (2017); Cheng et al. (2022)) for segmentation tasks, existing self-supervised models ( Wang
356   et al. (2023a; 2024); Niu et al. (2024)) utilize the DINO pretrained ResNet ( He et al. (2016)). To
357   further benefit from large-scale ViT( Dosovitskiy (2020)) pre-training, we devise Vit-Adapter( Chen
358   et al. (2022b)) as our multi-scale encoder. Given that OAP is a non-parametric module without prior
359   information, we zero-initialize the adapter parameters so that OAP can generate meaningful masks
360   during the early stages of training.
361   Level-specific Object Queries. To train a universal segmentation model, the mask decoder must
362   output both instance-level masks and semantic-level masks. Unlike U2Seg (Niu et al. (2024)) where
363   the decoder has two separate branches, we adopt a more streamlined approach by partitioning the
364   object queries in the mask decoder into the semantic queries and instance queries. To distinguish
365   between these two groups, we also introduce two special learnable tokens [INS] and [SEM], which
366   are added to the respective query features at each decoder layer. It is noteworthy that both groups of
367   queries share the same decoder parameters and projection head, enabling universal learning through
368
      parameter sharing across different levels.
369   Cropped Bipartite Matching. The teacher network processes the original global view of the im-
370   age, producing pseudo-masks that cover the entire image. In contrast, the student network processes
371   local views obtained from multi-crop augmentation. To resolve the mismatch, we crop the teacher’s
372   pseudo-masks to match the spatial regions of the student’s local views. Teacher embeddings and
373   pseudo-masks that does not overlap with the student local view are dropped. We employ the Dice
374
      similarity coefficient as the matching criterion. Moreover, the matching is conducted separately
      within each segmentation level, in the sense that the matching processes for the semantic and in-
375
      stance levels are independent and do not interfere with each other.
376

      after bipartite matching as Qt ∈ RL×d and Qs ∈ RL×d , where L is the query number. We propose

416   5.1      T RAINING S ETTINGS
417
418   Model Architecture. For the multi-scale encoder, we use the DINO pretrained ViT-base/8
419   and ViT-Adapter(Chen et al. (2022b)). For the mask decoder, we use the official setting of
420   Mask2Former(Cheng et al. (2022)). For the project head, we follow DINO(Caron et al. (2021))
421   using a 3-layer MLP with hidden dimension 2048 followed by L2 normalization and a linear layer
422   of K dimensions. The adapter parameters are zero-initialized for stable pretriaining. After training,
423
      the final teacher network with the mask decoder is used for inference. For unsupervised seman-
      tic segmentation, we follow (Niu et al. (2024); Hamilton et al. (2022)) to additionaly fine-tune
424
      our model using COCO’s training images. Parameter setting. We set σ = 0.07, {τt }Tt=1 =
425
      [0.8, 0.7.0.6, 0.5, 0.4], ϕ = 5, ωf = 0.6, ωs = 0.4, K = 512. The number of semantic queries
426   is set to 50 and the number of instance queries is set to 150. We use a local crop scale between
427   0.05 and 0.4 for multi-crop augmentation. The images are resized to 448 as input. Optimization
428   Setting. We train the model using adamw optimizer with a batch size of 16. The learning rate is
429   linearly ramped up for the first 10k iterations to 0.000625. A cosine schedule is used to decay the
430   learing rate to zero. It is noteworthy that our model is only trained for 160k iterations, while other
431   models are trained for another multi-stage self-training. We use a cosine momentum schedule from
      0.996 to 1 during training.

447
448   Self-supervised Instance Segmentation. As shown in Table.2 and Table.3, our framework signif-
449   icantly outperforms other state-of-the-art methods on COCO, PASCAL VOC, and UVO. For class-
450   agnostic unsupervised instance segmentation, our model achieves an increase of +5.9 in APbox        50 ,
451   which is 25.8% of increase compared to U2Seg (Niu et al. (2024)). Our online pretraining frame-
452   work can converge faster and achieve significant improvements over previous multi-stage alternating
453   methods. Most importantly, for our method, the performance of the class-aware instance segmenta-
454   tion is higher of that in class-agnostic instance segmentation. However, this is reversed for U2Seg.
455
      This is because U2Seg adopts the multi-stage alternating strategy, where the pseudo-labels are re-

      performance. This shows that the online clustering mechanism can better help model to capture the

      semantics information in the pretraining dataset.

      In this paper, we propose an efficient pseudo-mask generation algorithm, Online Agglomerative
      Pooling (OAP), to generate both the semantic-level and instance-level masks of one image within
505
      tens of milliseconds. Based on OAP, we propose the first online framework for self-supervised
506
      universal segmentation. A teacher-student framework is used, where we propose a simple but effec-
507   tive pretext task, Query-wise Self-distillation, specifically designed for self-supervised segmentation
508   models. Our pretrained model achives state-of-the-art performance on unsupervised zero-shot in-
509   stance segmentation, semantic segmentation, and panoptic segmentation tasks.
