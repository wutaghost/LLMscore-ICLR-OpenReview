009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Forecasting counterfactual outcome in the longitudinal setting can be critical for
012            many time-related applications. To solve this problem, the previous works propose
013            to apply different sequence models including long short-term memory (LSTM)
014            networks and transformers to model the relationship between the observed histories,
015            treatments and outcomes, and apply various approaches to remove treatment selec-
016            tion bias. However, these methods neglect the hidden heterogeneity of outcome
017            generation among samples induced by hidden factors which can bring hurdles
018            to counterfactual outcome forecast. To alleviate this problem, we capture the
019
               hidden heterogeneity by recovering the hidden factors and incorporate it into the
               outcome prediction process. Specifically, we propose a Time-shared Heterogeneity
020
               Learning from Time Series (THLTS) method which infers the shared part of hidden
021
               factors characterizing the heterogeneity across time steps with the architecture
022            of variational encoders (VAE). This method can be a flexible component and
023            combined with arbitrary counterfactual outcome forecast method. Experimental
024            results on (semi-)synthetic datasets demonstrate that combined with our method,
025            the mainstream models can improve their performance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Decision-making problems are widely prevalent in many applications, such as healthcare (Huang
031   & Ning, 2012) and marketing (Bottou et al., 2013). Therefore, it is of paramount importance to
032   forecast the counterfactual outcome for different choice of treatments to assist decision. The gold
033   standard for estimating the outcome of different treatments is conducting randomized controlled trials
034   (RCTs) (Booth & Tannock, 2014), which randomly assign treatments to the samples. However, the
035   high expense and time cost of RCTs (Kohavi & Longbotham, 2011) induce the people to instead
036   learning from large amounts of observational data to fulfill this purpose. A lot of previous literature
037   investigate the problem of counterfactual outcome forecast based on the observational dataset with
038   different approaches to address the treatment selection bias (Hassanpour & Greiner, 2019; Assaad
039
      et al., 2021), such as treatment invariant representation learning (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit
      et al., 2017; Tanimoto et al., 2021; Schwab et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2018; Zeng et al., 2020), sample
040
      re-weighting for adjusting distributions (Assaad et al., 2021; Hassanpour & Greiner, 2019; 2020;
041
      Johansson et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2020) and data imputation (Bica et al., 2020c; Yoon et al., 2018;
042   Qian et al., 2021).
043
044   In many scenarios, the decision-making problems can be more complex and may span a long period
045
      of time. It is thus required to forecast the counterfactual outcome at different time steps instead of
      a single time. Since the size of historical covariate information is varied among the time steps, the
046
      methods developed under the static setting can not be directly applied to this setting. To bridge this
047
      gap induced by the longitudinal property of the task, some methods apply the sequence models, such
048   as LSTM networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), to
049   characterize the time-dependency between the histories of varying length and outcomes. Generally, a
050   representation is extracted from the histories and play a substitute role of the raw confounder vectors
051   in the static setting. The outcome prediction module forecasts the counterfactual outcome with the
052   learned representation and the counterfactual treatment. Based on this design, the technologies for
053   removing treatment selection bias, can be integrated to achieve more accurate outcome forecast in the
      setting of time-series.

      The paradigm above attributes the heterogeneity of outcome generation among samples to the
072   observed variables of histories in dataset. This hypothesizes that the outcome-related factors are
073   all recorded in the histories. However, in many scenarios, this prerequisite does not hold. There
074   may exist extra factors unrecorded by the observed histories, and can also affect outcome. Thereby,
075   forecasting heterogeneous outcome conditional on solely the observed histories may neglect the
076   outcome variation among samples (i.e. heterogeneity of outcome generation) of the same observed
077   variables. Consequently, the neglect of this outcome variation can lead the prediction to be a coarse
078   approximation of individual outcome and deteriorate the forecast performance (Zou et al., 2023). We
079   call this problem as hidden heterogeneity and visually demonstrate it in Figure 1.
080   To resolve the problem, we try to address the hidden heterogeneity problem by uncovering the hidden
081   factors and capturing the extra outcome variation. Due to the limited supervision information (i.e.
082   outcome variable is of few-dimension) in this problem, the solution by aggressively learning latent
083   factors for all samples and time steps is excessively flexible and undergoes sub-optimal performance,
084   which is empirically presented in the experiments. To mitigate this circumstance, we propose a novel
085   Time-shared Heterogeneity Learning from Time Series (THLTS) method, which instead learn the
086
      shared part of latent factors across time steps for each sample. While sacrificing the flexibility in
      modeling time-varying dynamics of latent factors, this design is targeting the pursuit of forecast
087
      performance like a regularizer.
088
089   For practical implementations, we resort to variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling,
090   2014; Rezende et al., 2014) to model the joint distribution of the outcome and the time-shared latent
091   factors given the observed history and treatments. Due to the longitudinal property of the problem, we
092
      extend the model architecture to support the inference of latent factors with the varying prior, which
      are updated according to the successive observation over time. With the encoder component, we can
093
      easily infer the latent factors. By incorporating the learned factors into outcome prediction module,
094
      we can forecast the individual outcome more preciously compared to the same model-backbone
095   ignoring the hidden heterogeneity. Theoretical analysis validate the rationality of our proposed
096   strategy to learn time-shared latent factors. We conduct extensive experiments on the synthetic
097   datasets and semi-synthetic datasets, where the results reveal the effectiveness of our method.
098
      The main contribution of our paper can be summarized as following:
099
100
             • This paper investigate counterfactual outcome forecast with the existence of hidden hetero-
101
               geneity. To the best of our knowledge, this is the pioneer work tailored to improving the
102            off-the-shelf counterfactual forecast model by addressing the hidden heterogeneity problem.
103
104
             • We expose the insightful idea that learning the shared part of latent factors over time steps and
               propose a novel Time-shared Heterogeneity Learning from Time Series (THLTS) method.
105
106          • Extensive experimental results demonstrate that our method acts like a flexible component
107            and is beneficial for the mainstream counterfactual outcome forecast models when be
               integrated into them.

      2.1   C OUNTERFACTUAL O UTCOME P REDICTION IN THE S TATIC S ETTING
113
114   There have been a large amount of works devoted to counterfactual outcome forecast in the static
115   setting. The main challenge is the treatment selection bias manifested as the dependency of treatment
116   assignment on the observed confounders. To overcome this challenge, some papers (Johansson
117   et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017; Tanimoto et al., 2021; Schwab et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2018; Zeng
118   et al., 2020) borrow the idea of domain adaptation (Tzeng et al., 2014; Ganin & Lempitsky, 2015)
119   to learn the treatment invariant representation of confounders and predict counterfactual outcome
120   by taking the representation as input. Since over-forcing the independency may bring hurdles to
121
      prediction performance (Assaad et al., 2021), some other methods (Assaad et al., 2021; Hassanpour
      & Greiner, 2019; 2020; Johansson et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2020) re-weight samples to adjust the
122
      joint distribution of confounders and treatments, and train the countefactual predictive model on the
123
      re-weighted dataset. Moreover, there are also some works perform data augmentation to imputes the
124   counterfactual outcome for the observational samples (Bica et al., 2020c; Yoon et al., 2018; Qian
125   et al., 2021). When faced with unobserved confounders, the prediction result may suffer from severe
126   confounding bias. The previous literature resort to extra tools, such as instrumental variables (IVs)
127   and negative controls (Hartford et al., 2017; Heckman, 1997; Wu et al., 2022) to overcome it. Since
128   the assumptions on these tools are restricted, other methods (Louizos et al., 2017; Wang & Blei,
129   2019; Zou et al., 2023) try to recover the information of unobserved confounders according to the
130   knowledge of data generation process.
131
132   2.2   C OUNTERFACTUAL O UTCOME F ORECAST IN T IME S ERIES
133
134
      In many applications, it is required to forecast counterfactual outcome for a period of time. Under
      these scenarios, the outcome of a specific time is determined by not only the observations at the
135
      present time but also the whole histories. To handle the information of long history, Robins et al.
136
      (2000) conduct linear/logistic regression on the truncated history to predict the outcome. Due to
137   the potential complex dependency of outcome on covariates and treatments, previous works (Lim
138   et al., 2018; Bica et al., 2020b; Li et al., 2020; Melnychuk et al., 2022; Hatt & Feuerriegel, 2021;
139   Bica et al., 2020a; Kuzmanovic et al., 2021) propose to utilize sequence models (Chung et al., 2014;
140   Vaswani et al., 2017; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) to transform the histories of varying length
141   to representations of fixed size and forecast outcome based on it. Lim et al. (2018) utilize long
142   short-term memory (LSTM) networks to encode the histories and re-weights the samples by the
143   estimated propensity scores for removing selection bias. Alternatively, Bica et al. (2020b) resort to
144   impose treatment invariant representation regularizer on the represention learned by LSTM networks
145
      to remove treatment selection bias. Since the ability of LSTM networks to model complex and long
      dependencies in time is limited, Melnychuk et al. (2022) build the model based on transformers
146
      (Vaswani et al., 2017) and propose a novel counterfactual domain confusion (CDC) loss for training
147
      the models. Bouchattaoui et al. (2023) is a similar work to ours. However, this work designed a fixed
148   model architecture which can only deal with the problem setting of binary treatment. By contrast, our
149   proposed THLTS method can be viewed as a flexible component which is able to be combined with
150   various model backbones of counterfactual outcome forecast and deal with more complex treatment
151   scenarios. Moreover, this paper also consider the setting of time-varying latent factors and delivers
152   the insightful strategy of learning shared part of latent factors across time steps to deal with it.

      4     THLTS: T HE P ROPOSED M ETHOD
205
      In this section, we present the details of our proposed Time-shared Heterogeneity Learning from
206
      Time Series (THLTS) algorithm. It is a flexible component that can be combined with the different
207
      models and subsequently improve their performance.

209
      4.1    P RELIMINARY FOR C APTURING H IDDEN H ETEROGENEITY
210
211   The mainstream models predict counterfactual outcome based on the heterogeneity informed by the
212                                                                                               (i)
      representation encoding the histories. Formally, the prediction can be expressed by h(ϕ(Ht ), act ),
213   where ϕ(·) is the representation function built upon sequence models, and h is the predictive model.
214
215
      However, hidden heterogeneity of outcome generation beyond the histories can still exist among
                                                                                           (i)
      different samples and time steps, which is caused by hidden outcome-related factors et . It has


                                                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      been verified in previous literature (Zou et al., 2023) that by augmenting the prediction model input
217                                                      (i)    (i)
      with the extra hidden factors, that is g(ϕ(Ht ), et , act ), we can approximate the true individual
218
      outcome more closer. There have been large amount of works in reinforcement learning investigating
219
      the problem of partially observed markov decision process (POMDP) and trying to recover latent
220   states(Lee et al., 2020; Lei et al., 2022; Igl et al., 2018) across time steps. The supervision information
221   for time-varying latent variable inference (i.e. the high-dimensional observation) is usually sufficient
222   in POMDP. Conversely, the supervision information (i.e. outcome variable) is much limited in our
223   problem, since it is only one-dimensional. Therefore, the models built for capturing the time-varying
224   latent factors is excessively flexible and may result in sub-optimal performance. This will be presented
225   thoroughly in the section of experiments. To mitigate this circumstance, we give an subtle method
226   to learn the shared part of time-varying latent factors. This is designed for computational efficiency
227   while trading off the flexibility of models.

      6        C ONCLUSION
528
      In this paper, we studied the hidden heterogeneity problem of counterfactual outcome forecast
529
      in longitudinal setting. Neglecting the latent factors unobserved in the histories can degrade the
530
      prediction performance of counterfactual outcome forecast model. To tackle this problem, we propose
531   Time-shared Heterogeneity Learning from Time Series (THLTS) method to uncover the time-shared
532   part of latent factors and augment the forecast model with the learned latent representation. Owing
533   to the flexibility of our method, it can be combined with arbitrary model backbones. Extensive
534   experimental results indicate the effectiveness of our method on improving the performance of
535   mainstream counterfactual outcome forecast models.

906   F      BACKGROUND INFORMATION OF VAE
907
908   Generally, variational autoencoder is a framework that assume the observation o is generated from
909   latent factors z and conditions c (if exists) and learn a variational approximation qφ (z|o, c) to
910   substitute the true posterior distribution p(z|o, c). The encoder outputting this approximated posterior
911   is trained with decoder pρ (o|z, c) and conditional prior component pφ (z|c) to maximize a lower
912   bound of the log-likelihood of the observed data p(o|c). Formally, the evidence lower bound (ELBO)
913
      is defined as:
914   ED [log p(o|c)] ≥ L(φ, ρ)              = ED [Eqφ (z|o,c) [log pρ (o|z, c) + log pφ (z|c) − log qφ (z|o, c)]]
915
                                             = ED [Eqφ (z|o,c) [log pρ (o|z, c)] − DKL (log qφ (z|o, c)| log pφ (z|c))]
916
917   Usually, the outputted distributions of these components are defined as Gaussian distribution with
      parameterized expectation and variance. After training the models, we can use reparameterization
