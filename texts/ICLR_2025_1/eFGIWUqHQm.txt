011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Face Anti-Spoofing (FAS) plays a crucial role in safeguarding face recognition
014            systems from adversarial attacks. Current approaches leveraging Convolutional
015
               Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers encounter challenges in gen-
               eralizing across diverse attack behaviors and region-specific variations. These
016
               limitations stem from: (1) the heterogeneous characteristics of attacks across
017
               different facial regions, which arise due to varying color, texture, and material
018            properties, and (2) the expansive data space, complicating effective generaliza-
019            tion. To address these issues, we propose a novel approach using Chebyshev
020            Convolutional Graph Neural Networks (ChebConv GNN), which excels in cap-
021            turing spatial information within graph structures. Specifically, ChebConv effi-
022            ciently processes graphs constructed from image data, allowing for precise mod-
023            eling of visual features. We begin by processing regions around facial landmarks
024            through the initial layers of DenseNet to extract node features, capturing local-
025            ized and rich information from each region. Each facial region is assigned a node,
026
               forming a unified graph where corresponding nodes across faces represent the
               same regions. This design enables the network to adapt dynamically to region-
027
               specific features while modeling inter-regional relationships effectively, reducing
028
               the data space and improving generalization. To further enhance domain adap-
029            tation, we introduce a Domain-Adversarial Graph Network, which bolsters per-
030            formance across unseen domains. Additionally, we incorporate a self-supervised
031            auxiliary task to promote the learning of texture features in each region, strength-
032            ening the model’s ability to differentiate between genuine and spoofed faces. Ex-
033            perimental results demonstrate that our approach not only improves accuracy but
034            also significantly enhances generalization, surpassing the performance of existing
035            methods. The code for the model and the results can be found at the following
036            link: https://github.com/hassanyousefzade/RA-FAS.git.

      Face Anti-Spoofing (FAS) plays a crucial role in protecting facial recognition systems against var-
041
      ious presentation attacks, such as printed photos, replayed videos, and 3D masks Kunert (2023);
042   Greenberg (2017). Although existing methods for Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) George &
043   Marcel (2019); Liu et al. (2022a; 2023a; 2018; 2020); Yu et al. (2020a); Zhang et al. (2020a) per-

045   unseen domainsChen et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022a;e); Zhou et al. (2022; 2024). This issue arises
046   due to the large distributional discrepancies between different domains, which increases the security
047   challenges for facial recognition systems. Therefore, the development of robust FAS methods is
048   essential to enhance the security of facial recognition systems.
049   Spoof detection initially relied on handcrafted features like SIFT Patel et al. (2016), LBP Boulke-
050   nafet et al. (2015); de Freitas Pereira et al. (2013), and HOG Komulainen et al. (2013); Yang et al.
051   (2013b). With the advent of deep learning, researchers shifted their focus to deep neural networks
052   for feature extraction in spoof detection Yang et al. (2014); Feng et al. (2016b); Zhang et al. (2021);
053   Li et al. (2016). Despite these advancements, challenges related to performance in unseen domains
      and handling distribution shifts remain. To address these issues, Domain Generalization (DG) tech-

139
      of the Chebyshev Graph Convolutional Network (ChebConv). Second, we describe the process of
140
      extracting a graph representation from facial images. Third, we input this graph into a graph graph

150   2.1   C HEBYSHEV GRAPH C ONVOLUTIONAL N EURAL N ETWORKS
151
152
      In this subsection, Chebyshev Convolutional Neural Networks (ChebConv) are introduced as an
      effective method for processing graph data, particularly for graphs extracted from imagesDefferrard
153
      et al. (2016). The main idea behind ChebConv is to extend traditional convolution operations to
154
      irregular graph structures, enabling improved modeling of both local and spatial information. These
155   networks utilize local filters based on Chebyshev polynomials and graph Laplacians.
156
157   In our proposed method, each node corresponds to a specific region of the face, derived from facial
158
      key points, and a graph is constructed to connect these regions. By increasing the value of K, the
      filters can aggregate more information from neighboring nodes, which enhances the learning of both
159
      local and global features.
160
161   One of the main advantages of ChebConv over traditional Graph Convolutional Networks
      (GCNs) is the reduction in computational complexity. This reduction in complexity significantly

      improves the efficiency and speed of the model, especially in the context of large and complex
163   graphs.
164
165   This architecture is particularly effective for graphs extracted from images, allowing the model to
166
      effectively capture intricate spatial relationships between different facial regions.

      To increase the depth of the network and enhance its capacity, we utilize six Chebyshev Blocks
325   (Cheb Blocks) (Figure 3b). It is known that in the initial layers of the network, the node embeddings
326   are more local, while as we move closer to the end of the network, these embeddings become more
327   general and comprehensive.
328
      To leverage these embeddings at the graph level, we apply Global Average Pooling on the node
329
      features to achieve graph-level embeddings. In other words, the embeddings obtained from the
330
      initial, intermediate, and final layers are considered as outputs, allowing the classifier to utilize the
331   embeddings from all three levels (local, semi-global, and global) of the graph.
332
333   Additionally, we employ Skip Connections to mitigate the phenomenon of over smoothing, which
334
      is common in graph neural networks. This process enables the model to effectively capture both
      local and global node features in a multi-level manner, leading to improved generalization.

      2.5   A DVERSARIAL L EARNING FOR D OMAIN -I NVARIANT G RAPH R EPRESENTATION
368
      We assume that there are minor distributional differences across different domains based on the
369   following observations:
370
371         1. Considering samples from various domains, all graphs contain nodes corresponding to spe-
372            cific facial regions, and each node maintains a similar pattern in the same region. Therefore,
373            these graphs share a common semantic feature space.
374         2. Both real-world samples and spoofing attacks often exhibit similar physical characteristics,
375            such as shape and size.
376
377   Based on this, we employ adversarial learning to make the generated graphs indistinguishable across
      different domains.

      graph and the discriminator, as well as for the facial region classifier, is set to 128. The value of K
458
      is also set to 2. For optimization, the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e − 4 is used. The
459
      batch size is set to 64, and the dropNode probability is set to 0.3. In the feature generator graph,
460   the first and second blocks have a dropNode rate of zero, as applying dropNode in these blocks
461   harms the performance of the facial region classifier. Additionally, the input images are cropped
462   using MediaPipe and resized to 256x256. The experiments are conducted for a maximum of 500
463   epochs. The implementation of graph neural networks was done using the **PyTorch Geometric**
464   Contributors (2024) library and the **PyTorch** Paszke et al. (2017) framework. The computations
465   were carried out on an **RTX 3090** hardware.

823   In the early stages of research, handcrafted features were widely employed for detecting face spoof-
824
      ing attacks. These features include LBP Pereira et al. (2014), HOG Feng et al. (2016a), and SIFT
      Yang et al. (2013a). Concurrently, studies investigated predefined biometric traits and behaviors such
825
      as blinking Mostaani et al. (2020), lip movement Zhou et al. (2023b), head rotation, and changes
826
      in facial expressions Ganin & Lempitsky (2015a). With the emergence of deep neural networks
827   (DNNs), the detection capabilities of face anti-spoofing systems significantly improved Zhang et al.
828   (2020b); Lin et al. (2019); Yu et al. (2021). These improvements were further facilitated by incor-
829   porating diverse types of supervisory inputs such as depth maps Zhang et al. (2020b), reflection
830   maps Lin et al. (2019), and R-PPG signals Yu et al. (2021). Despite their success in intra-dataset
831   scenarios, the performance of these methods tends to degrade in unseen domains. To tackle this,
832   domain-generalization-based approachesShao et al. (2019a); Liu et al. (2021e) have been proposed
833   to learn domain-invariant features through adversarial training or meta-learning. These approaches
834   aim to align feature spaces across multiple domains. However, such direct alignment can neglect
835
      crucial discriminative information, especially when there is a large gap between domains. Addi-
      tionally, self-supervised learning Liu et al. (2022d; 2023c) has been explored to reduce reliance
836
      on labeled data. Most of these techniques rely on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for fea-
837
      ture extraction. Recently, transformer-based models George & Marcel (2021); Hong et al. (2023);
838   Huang et al. (2023) have shown remarkable success in face anti-spoofing. However, one of the major
839   limitations of these approaches is their inability to fully capture the spatial relationships and texture
840   variations across different regions of the face. To address this limitation, we propose a method based
841   on Chebyshev Graph Neural Networks (ChebConv GNNs)Defferrard et al. (2016). In our approach,
842   graph nodes correspond to specific facial regions, allowing each node to adapt its behavior according
843   to the position and texture of its respective region. This design not only provides each node with
844   a distinct feature pattern but also reduces unnecessary diversity (Diversity) in the features, thereby
845   significantly improving the generalization (Generalization) of the model.
