009                                                 Abstract
010
011              The human brain exhibits remarkable proficiency in dynamic learning and adap-
012              tation, seamlessly integrating prior knowledge with new information, thereby
013              enabling flexible memory retention and efficient transfer across multiple views.
014              In contrast, traditional multi-view learning methods are predominantly designed
015              for static and fixed-view datasets, leading to the notorious "view forgetting phe-
016
                 nomenon", where the introduction of new views leads to the erosion of prior
                 knowledge. This phenomenon starkly contrasts with the brain’s remarkable abil-
017
                 ity to continuously integrate and migrate past knowledge, ensuring both the re-
018
                 tention of old information and the assimilation of new insights. This oversight
019              presents a critical challenge: how to efficiently learn and integrate new views
020              while simultaneously preserving knowledge from previously acquired views and
021              enabling flexible knowledge transfer across diverse views. Inspired by underlying
022              neural processing mechanisms, we propose a view transfer learning framework
023              named Hebbian View Orthogonal Projection (HVOP), which realizes efficient
024              knowledge migration and sharing between multi-view data. HVOP constructs
025              a knowledge transfer space (KTS), where the KTS reduces the interference be-
026              tween the old and the new views through an orthogonal learning mechanism. By
027
                 further incorporating recursive lateral connections and Hebbian learning, the pro-
                 posed model endows the learning process with brain-like dynamic adaptability,
028
                 enhancing knowledge transfer and integration, and bringing the model closer to
029
                 human cognition. We extensively validate the proposed model on node classifi-
030              cation tasks and demonstrate its superior performance in knowledge retention and
031              transfer compared to traditional methods. Our results underscore the potential of
032              biologically inspired mechanisms in advancing multi-view learning and mitigat-
033              ing the view forgetting phenomenon.

036   1     Introduction
037
038   Brain-inspired computing seeks to mimic the human brain’s ability to process information through
039
      its complex network of neurons and synapses, enabling remarkable capabilities in learning and mem-
      ory retention. Neuroscientific research reveals that the brain can continually adapt and reconfigure
040
      its neural connections in response to new stimuli Deco et al. (2011); Hassabis et al. (2017). This
041
      dynamic capability underlies cognitive functions such as learning from multiple sensory inputs and
042   retaining memories over time Stein & Stanford (2008).
043
044   The need for such adaptability is particularly evident in modern computational environments where
045
      we increasingly deal with incremental views, such as in medical image analysis Konz & Mazurowski
      (2024); Weng et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2023), social network analysis Meng et al. (2024); Wen et al.
046
      (2023); Song et al. (2023), recommendation systems Li et al. (2024b); Paliwal et al. (2024); Prakash
047
      et al. (2023), and video surveillance Bao et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2020). In these applications, new
048   data sources, or "views", continuously emerge, challenging traditional multi-view learning methods
049   that were originally designed for static datasets. These traditional methods Zhao et al. (2017); Wu
050   et al. (2023) struggle with dynamic data integration, lacking the brain-like flexibility to adapt to
051   new views without forgetting previously acquired knowledge. This necessitates costly retraining to
052   integrate new views or risks discarding previously learned insights when trained directly on new
053   data. Such static approaches starkly contrast with the brain’s fluid and adaptive learning processes,
      underscoring a significant gap in existing methodologies.

083   (marked in blue) deteriorates because these models fail to effectively transfer the knowledge accu-
084   mulated from previous views to assist in learning the new view. Instead, they rely solely on the

086   pattern is heavily constrained by the specific performance of the current learned view, limiting the
087
      model’s overall knowledge integration and generalization capabilities. The gradient direction of the
      old view is forgotten during the weight updating process and is directly docked onto the new view

092   shown in Fig. 2(c)), require the model to possess strong knowledge integration capabilities to effi-
093   ciently assimilate the incremental information from the new view while maintaining the coherence
094   of knowledge from the old view.
095
      To address these challenges, it is instructive to consider how the human brain manages similar tasks.
096   The brain’s ability to handle information from various sensory inputs without losing prior knowl-
097   edge is underpinned by its unique structure and function Chiel & Beer (1997); Ito et al. (2022).
098   Notably, the hippocampus plays a crucial role in this process, aiding in the formation and storage
099   of new memories as well as in the retrieval of existing memories without interference from newly
100   incoming information. This capability is achieved through sophisticated mechanisms, such as the
101   creation of separate but interconnected neural pathways for new and old memories, allowing for the
102   simultaneous retention of stability and plasticity in neural representations Kumaran et al. (2016);
103   McClelland et al. (1995); Scoville & Milner (1957); Squire (1992). These insights into hippocam-
104
      pal function suggest a model of memory that is both dynamic and robust, essential characteristics
      for the development of effective multi-view learning algorithms.
105
106   To bridge the gap between human cognitive abilities and machine learning models, we propose a
107   novel framework that draws direct inspiration from neural mechanisms. Our model, termed Heb-
      bian View Orthogonal Projection (HVOP), integrates concepts from neuroscience to enhance the

132   outperforms both traditional and state-of-the-art multi-view learning methods.

135   2     Related Work
136
137
138   2.1   Multi-view Continual Learning
139
140   Multi-view learning Wang et al. (2021); Yao et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2025) involves integrating and
141   encoding information from multiple sources to derive a low-dimensional representation that cap-
142
      tures both consistency and complementarity across views. Current multi-view continual learning
      has two dominant classifications: task-incremental and class-incremental types Van de Ven et al.
143
      (2022). Multi-view task-incremental learning Li et al. (2017); Sun et al. (2018) establish connec-
144
      tions between tasks and extract consistent information across multiple views to complement each
145   task learning. Thus, the multi-view class-incremental learning Yang et al. (2022); Qian et al. (2023);
146   Li et al. (2024a) has been proposed, where new class data is seamlessly introduced without explicit
147   prompting to the model, requiring the model to autonomously discern the onset of a new task. These
148   methods focus regularizing the update of weights, thus alleviating the catastrophic forgetting. Catas-
149   trophic forgetting is the phenomenon in which a neural network forgets what it has learnt previously
150   when learning a new task Ramasesh et al. (2021); De Lange et al. (2021); Elsayed & Mahmood
151   (2024). Related research has focused on two strategies: knowledge retention and knowledge trans-
152   fer. Knowledge retention methods help models retain information from old tasks while training new
153
      tasks by introducing memory mechanisms or employing regularization techniques Babakniya et al.
      (2024); Chen et al. (2021a;b). On the other hand, knowledge transfer methods attempt to transfer
154
      knowledge from a previous task to a new task, e.g., by using the output of the old task as a guide
155
      in the new task through knowledge distillation techniques Kang et al. (2023); Kumari et al. (2022);
156   Zhou & Cao (2021). Despite ongoing research efforts to mitigate catastrophic forgetting, achieving
157   a balance between retaining old knowledge and acquiring new knowledge remains a challenge. Our
158   proposed view incremental learning is quite different from both task and class incremental learn-
159   ing settings. Notably, it operates without the need for prompts signaling new tasks, placing special
160   emphasis on monitoring the transferability of knowledge across different views. This approach un-
161   derscores the importance of capturing overall cognition, striving for equilibrium between preserving
      past knowledge and assimilating novel information.

      2.2     Transfer Learning
163
164   Transfer learning is a pivotal method in machine learning to enhance model’s efficiency by applying
165   knowledge from previously learned tasks to new, related ones Falk et al. (2023); Lin & Reimherr
166   (2024). It mirrors the way human brains utilize past experiences to learn new tasks faster. Tradi-
167   tionally, machine learning models are trained from scratch for each new task, which can be data-
168   intensive and time-consuming. Transfer learning mitigates these challenges by reusing pre-trained
169   models, thereby reducing the necessity for lengthy training periods. This technique has proven
170   effective in diverse applications such as natural language processing Zhao et al. (2024); Ge et al.
171   (2024), where models like BERT and GPT are adapted for tasks like sentiment analysis, and in com-
172
      puter vision Sohn et al. (2023); Jain et al. (2023), where convolutional neural networks pre-trained
      on large datasets are tailored for image recognition tasks. It also aids in adapting general speech
173
      recognition models to recognize specialized vocabulary or accents Kheddar et al. (2024); Lei et al.
174
      (2023). In multi-view learning, transfer learning is particularly crucial as it addresses the challenge
175   of effectively transferring and utilizing knowledge across different views that may have varying dis-
176   tributions or feature spaces. Traditional multi-view transfer learning methods, as discussed in Chen
177   et al. (2024); Tang et al. (2021), emphasize knowledge transfer across domains but often neglect the
178   heterogeneity between views, especially when the views are not only different but also dynamically
179   evolving. This oversight has spurred us to explore further and accomplish knowledge transfer among
180   diverse views. In multi-view learning, transfer learning acts as a bridge, our work aims to achieve
181   more effective knowledge transfer and integration within dynamically evolving view data.

      3.1   Cause of the View Forgetting
217
218   Firstly we introduce the basic notations used in our framework. Denote given multi-view feature
219   matrices as X = {Xv }Vv=1 , where Xv = [x1 , · · · , xn ] ∈ Rn×dV is the data from the vth view for any
220   v ∈ {1, · · · , V}. Here, n is the number of samples, V is the number of views, and dv denotes the
221   distinct dimensionality of the feature matrices for each view. Additionally, c represents the number
222   of classes. We examine the phenomenon of view forgetting using Fi, j , which denotes the testing
223   accuracy of task j after learning task i.
224
225
      Definition 3.1 View Forgetting. Given a sequence of view data X = {Xv }Vv=1 , after learning the v
      view data Xv , the performance of the learned view v is tested, represented as Fv,v . When the learning
226
      of the last view data XV is completed, the performance of the model significantly decreases in the
227
      previously tested views, shown as Fv,V < Fv,v .
228
229   Definition 3.2 View Transfer Learning. Given a sequence of view data X = {Xv }Vv=1 , where the
230   distribution of each view {Dv }Vv=1 differs significantly, but the task T remains the same. Transfer
231   learning is designed to help improve the learning of the target prediction function f (·), which enables
232   the model to achieve robust overall cognition despite the evolving data distributions.
233
234   View forgetting occurs when the model encounters new views. Although the model initially captures
235   the previous view, it struggles to retain this memory throughout the learning process. We attribute
236   the core cause of this phenomenon to the dynamic changes in neural network parameters during
237   training. We have conducted an in-depth analysis of the network’s specific updating mechanisms.
238
      Our findings reveal that the gradient update direction is influenced by the entire input vector space.
      The transformation of view information shifts this gradient direction, leading to the forgetting of
239
      previously learned view information. Furthermore, in graph networks, weight updates depend not
240
      only on the feature gradients of individual nodes but also on the gradients of neighboring nodes
241   and the structural gradients of the graph. As view data evolves, the graph’s structural information
242   changes, further complicating the retention of knowledge from earlier views. Notably, preventing
243   forgetting is a prerequisite for effective knowledge migration. It is due to the fact that shifts in the
244   direction of the gradient can impede this migration, making retention of learnt knowledge more
245   challenging.

267
268   For the initial complete first-view data, we employ graph convolutional neural networks and fully

521   5     Discussions and Conclusions
522
523   The traditional multi-view learning frameworks are often limited by their ability to handle static
524   multi-view data, making it difficult to adapt to the dynamic growth and changes in real-world view
525   data. In response, this study proposes a Hebbian-based View Orthogonal Projection framework,
526   called HVOP to overcome this challenge. Unlike continual learning, which focuses on adapting
527   to sequential tasks, HVOP emphasizes view incremental learning, aiming to maintain the stability
528   of the overall cognitive structure while focusing on the in-depth understanding of a single view in
529   each learning task. We have thoroughly analyzed the phenomenon of view forgetting, which refers
530
      to the issue of forgetting old view information when learning new views, and for the first time, as
      view transfer learning. This concept hypothesizes that by effectively mitigating view forgetting, it
531
      is possible to achieve smooth transfer and integration of knowledge across views. To realize this
532
      goal, the HVOP method cleverly integrates recursive lateral connection mechanisms with Hebbian
533   learning principles. This combination not only facilitates efficient knowledge extraction across views
534   but also ensures deep integration and continual optimization of both new and old view information
535   within the neural network. The experimental results exhibited that HVOP significantly enhances the
536   model’s adaptability and generalization ability in handling dynamic multi-view data.
