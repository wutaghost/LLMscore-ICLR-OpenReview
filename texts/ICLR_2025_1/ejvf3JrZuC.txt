009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly utilized in autonomous decision-
               making systems, where they sample options from an action space. However,
013
               the underlying heuristics guiding the sampling of LLMs remain under-explored.
014
               We examine LLMs’ response sampling and propose a theory that the sample of
015            an LLM is driven by a descriptive component (the notion of statistical average)
016            and a prescriptive component (notion of an ideal represented in the LLM). In a
017            controlled experimental setting, we demonstrate that LLMs’ outputs deviate from
018            the statistically probable outcome in the direction of a prescriptive component.
019            We further show that this deviation towards a prescriptive component consistently
020            appears across diverse real-world domains, including social, public health, and
021            scientific contexts. Using this theory, we demonstrate that concept prototypes in
022            LLMs are affected by prescriptive norms, similar to the concept of normality in
023
               humans. Through case studies, we illustrate that in real-world applications, the
               shift toward an ideal value in LLMs’ outputs can result in significantly biased
024
               decision-making, raising ethical and trustworthiness concerns.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
031   LLMs are often considered to be ‘System-1’(Daniel, 2017), characterized by their reliance on
032   heuristics, operating implicitly without deliberative reasoning (Dasgupta et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
033
      2023). Their performance in embodied decision making Li et al. (2024), expansive action spaces Wen
      et al., planning in action spaces Valmeekam et al. is attributed to the heuristics and mechanisms
034
      driving their operation. While LLMs are benchmarked as autonomous decision-making systems
035
      sampling options from an action space, the underlying heuristics guiding their response sampling
036   remain under-explored.
037
038   We study this heuristics and propose a theory that the sampling of an LLM is driven by a descriptive
039
      norm (the notion of statistical average) and a prescriptive norm (a notion of an ideal represented in the
      LLM)(Figure 1). We define response sampling as the process by which the model probabilistically
040
      selects outputs from a distribution of potential responses. A descriptive component represents what
041
      is observed or statistically likely within a given context, reflecting the occurrence or probability of
042   observations without implying any value judgment. A prescriptive component is an implicit standard
043   of what is considered ideal, desirable, or valued within on a concept, often encoded by grades/scores
044   that prioritizes outcomes deemed "better/optimal". The proposed theory implies, the sample of an
045   LLM not only reflects the statistical regularities of the data (descriptive norms) but also systematically
046   incorporates an idealized version of the concept (prescriptive norms).
047
      We design a critical experiment to validate the proposed theory. We show that the effect of this
048   heuristics appears consistently across diverse real-world domains. We perform extensive experiments
049   covering different LLMs, evaluated concepts, and ablations to show the robustness of observations.
050   We present a case study where an LLM is used to predict medical recovery time of patients to show a
051   practical implication of the LLM having a prescriptive component in sampling. To explain the theory,
052   we rely on its convergence with how humans consider options. Heuristics employed by humans is
053   driven by concept prototypicality which has a prescriptive component (e.g., a prototypical teacher is
      one that teaches well). In short, we make the following contributions:

      2    R ELATED W ORK
082
083   Understanding LLMs as ‘System-1’: Reasoning has been broadly characterized as a two-step
084   process involving quick ‘System-1’ thinking and a more deliberate ‘System-2’ reasoning (Daniel,
085   2017). Large Language Models (LLMs) have been conceptually likened to System-1 reasoning due
086   to their automatic and implicit nature (Yao et al., 2023). In fact, recent studies show overlaps in errors
087
      made by LLMs and humans in System-1 reasoning tasks, indicating that both might rely on heuristics
      for rapid decision-making (Dasgupta et al., 2022). We systematically study the heuristics that drive
088
      sampling in LLMs.
089
090   Understanding heuristics in response sampling: Simon (1996) uses the notion of heuristics to
091   explain the decision-making of ‘System-1’ mechanisms. These studies demonstrate the utility of
092   ‘mental shortcuts’ to navigate countless possibilities of the search problem (Newell et al., 1972).
093
      In the case of LLMs, exploring their heuristics can offer insights into how these models process
      information. However, previous research mainly uses sampling for tasks like action generation and
094
      decision-making rather than to explicitly understand the internal heuristics at work in LLMs (Hazra
095
      et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023; Suri et al., 2023). Our work aims to fill this gap by investigating the
096   heuristics driving LLM response sampling, which could provide a deeper understanding of their
097   decision-making processes.
098
099
      Earlier work that examined the mechanisms by which LLMs generate outputs highlights that LLMs
      may produce coherent text by probabilistically assembling language patterns without ‘genuine un-
100
      derstanding’ Bender et al. (2021). Later investigations have demonstrated that LLMs can develop
101
      internal, structured representations of the environment Li et al. (2022), and when trained on program-
102   ming languages exhibit an understanding of semantic structures, indicating a capacity for meaningful
103   text processing and generationJin & Rinard (2023). This slightly contradicting views on interpreting
104   LLM outputs shows the significance of further explorations. Recent work indicates that LLM agents
105   can understand probabilities, but they struggle with probability sampling Gu et al. (2024), hindering
106   their effectiveness in generating samples that align with expected probabilistic patterns. Our paper
107   provides a systematic framework that explains the sampling behaviour of LLMs. It enables precise
      exploration of LLM decision-making heuristics across diverse domains.

      3     T HEORY OF LLM SAMPLING
109
110
      Prominent theories explain decision-making in humans and animals as a search problem of countless
111   possibilities (Phillips et al., 2019; Phillips & Cushman, 2017; Mattar & Lengyel, 2022; Ross et al.,
112   2023). To navigate a huge search space of possibilities, Simon (1996) propose that humans (as well
113   as machines) must rely on heuristics to simplify the decision-making process (Newell et al., 1972).
114   Increasing evidence shows that humans use likelihood and value as heuristics (Bear et al., 2020;
115   Phillips et al., 2019; Bear & Knobe, 2017b). This dual nature of thought is hypothesized to originate
116   from humans being goal-driven agents and engaging in value maximization (Bear & Knobe, 2017b).
117   This human possibility sampling follows two stages: first, a fast but less accurate, heuristic driven
118   system generates a set of reasonable options (System-1), followed by a deliberate, but more precise,
119   system that selects the best choice (System-2) (Phillips et al., 2019). It is the heuristics of the first
120   stage that enable humans to make quick and effective decisions. LLMs are understood as System-1
121   machines that are driven by heuristics (Yao et al., 2023). In light of these studies, we examine the
122   sampling mechanisms of LLMs and observe that both LLMs and humans converge on the same
123   heuristics as the sampling is driven by the average and the ideal. Based on this, we propose a theory
124   for LLM sampling:
125
126              The sampling of an LLM is driven by a descriptive component (the notion
127              of statistical average) and a prescriptive component (a notion of an ideal).
128
129   Here, sampling is defined as the process by which the model probabilistically selects outputs from a
130   distribution of potential responses. In the following subsections, we describe the empirical evaluation
131   of this theory.
132
133   3.1   S AMPLING IN RELATION TO A NOVEL CONCEPT
134
135
      To empirically validate the proposed theory, we construct a setting by introducing a novel concept C.
      This approach eliminates potential confounding effects associated with using pre-existing concepts.
136
      We present the LLM with a task to sample values from a range of possibilities on this concept and
137
      evaluate the samples to uncover the effect of prescriptive and descriptive norms on sampling.
138
139   To establish a statistical baseline for the concept, we construct a distribution for concept C with a
140   mean Cµ . The LLM is provided with N samples from this distribution as values associated with C.
141
      We denote these options observed by the LLM as Co . In our experiments, we chose a sufficiently
      large N , such that the mean of observed options is almost equal to Cµ . To establish a prescriptive
142
      norm Cv on the concept C, we associate each option Co with a prescriptive component, represented
143
      by a grade. The grade associated with each observed sample gives a prescriptive norm to the concept;
144   we repeat the experiment with the following conditions: a higher value being ideal, a lower value
145   being ideal, and a control experiment. Based on these inputs (the observed N samples along with the
146   corresponding grades if any), we prompt the LLM to sample a value for the concept C. We denote
147   the value sampled by the LLM as Cs . By changing Cµ and Cv and keeping the rest of the prompt the
148   same, we show how the value of Cs changes with these two components.
149
      In independent contexts (i.e., prompts), we repeat this procedure M times to obtain a sample
150   distribution. We keep the value of M the same as N in all variants of the experiment. We evaluate
151   whether the distribution of samples Cs generated by the LLM is significantly different from the
152   distribution of input samples Co . If the sample is driven solely by the descriptive norm (statistics
153   of the observed samples), the distribution of samples Cs is expected to be statistically similar to
154   the observed distribution. However, the difference between observed samples and output samples
155   might occur due to the error in approximating the statistics of the observed samples. To exclude this
156   possibility, we instruct the LLM to report the average of the distribution. We denote the reported
157   average by Ca . Across all experiments, we observe that Cµ ≈ Ca , indicating that the LLM reliably
158
      approximates the statistics of the observed distribution. The control run also helps validate this.
159   We apply the Mann-Whitney U test to compare the distribution of samples, Cs , with the average
160   reported by the LLM, Ca , and the true mean of the observed samples, Cµ . We vary the direction of
161   Cv and demonstrate that the change in samples’ mean (mean of Cs ) corresponds to the change in Cv .
      For each concept, C, we calculate the Mann-Whitney U statistic and the corresponding p-value. If

183
184
      We investigate the validity of the theory outside the constructed setting using existing concepts in the
      LLM. We test the theory on multiple concepts learned during pre-training across different domains.
185
      Here, the distribution and the prescriptive norm are unknown. Therefore, we ask the LLM to report
186
      Ca (the average) and Ci (the ideal), and then to pick a sample, Cs . Note that Cv and Cµ are not

188   side of the average or the non-ideal side of the average. The latter can also be understood as the
189   sample falling on the average side of ideal. We classify each sample Cs as:
190
191                                                        Cs > Ca if Ci > Ca
                                 Ideal side of average :                                                   (1)
192                                                        Cs < Ca if Ci < Ca
193
194                                                        Cs < Ca if Ci ≥ Ca
                                 Average side of ideal :                                                   (2)
195                                                        Cs > Ca if Ci ≤ Ca

197   Samples of both concepts are shown in Figure 2. Consider the number of concepts for which sample
198
      falls on the ideal side of the average is n and the total number of concepts evaluated is ntotal . The
      binomial test is used to determine if n is significantly different from what would be expected by
199
      chance, assuming a null hypothesis where the probability p of a sample being on the ideal side is
200
      0.5. The p-value obtained from the binomial test is used to assess significance. p < 0.05 shows a
201   significant presence of prescriptive norm across concepts.
202
203   The setting described in Sections 3.2 and 3.1 is inspired by similar evaluation in humans (Bear et al.,
204
      2020; Phillips et al., 2019; Bear & Knobe, 2017b). We scale the experiments to show higher statistical
      significance and later replicate the exact setting to compare results with human studies.
205
206   Drift from the statistical norm: In most applications, one might expect the LLM sampling to be
207   driven by the statistical likelihood alone. We use a variable α to quantify the degree to which the
208   sample deviates away from the statistical norm. We define α such that, when the proposed theory
209   holds, the value of α is positive. That is, α is measured to be positive when Cs deviates from the Ca
210
      in the direction of Ci . We compute this direction as the positive direction of α (Figure 2). For each
      sample Cs of a concept C, α is computed as
211
212                                      α = (Ca − Cs ) × sign(Ca − Ci )                                  (3)
213   We also compute α̂: a normalized scale such that Ca is at the origin and Ci is at unit distance from the
214   origin. We compute α̂ as α/|Ca − Ci |. α̂ enables comparison across concepts with less dependency
215   on the scale of values. It also allows comparison with observations obtained in the experiments with
      human subjects.

      3.3   P RESCRIPTIVE COMPONENT IN CONCEPT PROTOTYPES
217
218   A prototype is the most typical or representative member of a concept, often viewed as the "average
219   example" based on shared features or frequency of occurrence (Murphy, 2004). But, it also serves
220   as a mental benchmark, embodying both statistical regularities and goal-oriented ideals within a
221   concept (Barsalou, 1985). For instance, a ‘Robin’ might be considered a prototype of the concept
222   ‘Bird’, as it shares many common features with most birds with high occurrence, and has the ability
223   to fly (expected of birds), making it a representative example of the ‘concept’ (Smith & Medin, 1981).
224   In this way, prototypicality can be used to understand the normality of a concept 1.15.
225   Our aim is to determine whether the LLM’s judgment of prototypes is influenced solely by statistical
226   regularities or whether prescriptive (goal-oriented) ideals also play a role. We provide a concept
227   C and corresponding exemplars of that concept. We ask the LLM to judge on three dimensions,
228   namely the average, ideal, and the prototypicality of the exemplar. As in the previous section 3.2, we
229   check whether the prototypicality rating falls on the ideal side of the average. To test significance, we
230   do a binomial test across concepts C to check if LLMs conception of prototypes has a perspective
231
      component. The evaluation is similar to the previous section.

233   4     E XPERIMENTS AND R ESULTS
234
235   In this section, we present three key experiments. First, we present a constrained setting to test the
236   validity of the proposed theorem. Second, we evaluate the presence of prescriptive and descriptive
237   components in sampling for concepts learned in training. Third, we show that concept prototypes
238   in LLMs are driven by prescriptive norms, similar to the concept of normality in humans. Our
239   results show significant evidence for the proposed theory. We test on the instruction-tuned models
240   of GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), GPT-3.5-Turbo (Brown et al., 2020), Claude (Anthropic, 2024),
241   Mixtral-8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), and both pretrained and instruction
242   tuned models from the family of Llama-2 and 3 models (Touvron et al., 2023). Unless mentioned
243
      otherwise, we report results for GPT-4 in the main text and the results for other models in the
      Appendix. Also, all prompts were appended with a pre-prompt to get floating-point numbers as
244
      outputs. The complete text used in the prompts for each experiment is given in the Appendix.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we set out to investigate the heuristics governing possibility sampling process of
      LLMs. We observe that both LLMs and humans converge on the same heuristics of having both
532
      descriptive and prescriptive components, however, the exact prescriptive component might not be
533
      aligned with humans. As LLMs continue to be integrated into real-world applications, understanding
534   their decision-making heuristics becomes increasingly important. Our results provide a foundational
535   framework for evaluating how LLMs balance statistically probable outcomes with norms of ideality,
536   raising interesting questions about their underlying mechanisms. This opens the door for further
537   exploration of how these prescriptive tendencies may influence performance across different domains.
538   As a final remark, we would like to emphasize that we do not intend to contribute to “humanizing”
539   AI/ML/LLMs in the way we use terminology or models. Our contribution is intended to draw parallels
      in behaviour and perform evaluations, as our findings can have an impact on downstream tasks.

      1.5   E XPERIMENT 1 HUMAN EXPERIMENT
811
812   A total of 1,200 participants were assigned across six conditions in a 2 × 3 pre-registered design. The
813   experiment manipulated the statistical distribution of new concept flubbing amounts (unimodal vs.
814   bimodal) and prescriptive value (high, low, or neutral ideal). Specifically, the flubbing amounts were
815   drawn from:
816
817          • Unimodal distribution: µ = 45, σ = 15
818          • Bimodal distribution: µ1 = 35, µ2 = 75, σ = 5
819
820   For the prescriptive value conditions:
821
822
             • High ideal: Flubbing amounts greater than 80 minutes were ideal (A+), while amounts less
               than 20 minutes received the lowest grade (D-).
823
824          • Low ideal: Amounts less than 20 minutes were ideal (A+), and those above 80 were
825            discouraged (D-).
826          • Intermediate ideal: The ideal amount of flubbing was set to 50 minutes, and grades were
827            linearly scaled based on deviation from 50.
828
829   After viewing 100 amounts of flubbing paired with health grades, participants were asked to report
830
      the first number of minutes of flubbing that came to mind. The results showed:
831
             • Participants’ sample judgments significantly differed from their estimates of the average
832
               flubbing amount. For the low ideal condition, the paired t-test yielded t(331) = 11.98, p <
833            .001. For the high ideal condition, the paired t-test was t(293) = 16.55, p < .001.
834
             • In the intermediate ideal condition, sample judgments and estimates of average flubbing
835
               did not significantly diverge, t(318) = 0.085, p = .93.
836
837   In analyzing the computational models, the softmax model provided the best fit across conditions
838   when compared to other models, such as the additive and multiplicative models. The softmax model
839   predicted participants’ sample judgments as a combination of statistical probability Ca (distribution
840   average) and prescriptive value Cv . The product of these factors explained the distribution of flubbing
841   amounts that came to mind.
842

846   The mean sample judgments is significantly influenced by the prescriptive values Cv , with deviations
847   from the true average Cµ . The differences between sample judgments and participants’ estimates
848   of average flubbing were highly significant in both the low ideal condition (p < .001) and the high
849   ideal condition (p < .001). No significant difference was found in the intermediate ideal condition

      1.6   ROBUSTNESS TO PROMPT
865
866   Our primary goal is identifying and measuring prescriptive norms in LLM rather than proposing
867   mitigation strategies which we leave to future work. To mitigate, we can draw inspiration from
868   human cognitive strategies where System-2 deliberation corrects or compensates for initial heuristic
869   judgments. Examples of System-2 inspired approaches include Tree of Thoughts Yao et al. (2023)
870   frameworks, which combine LLMs with symbolic reasoning or planning systems to enhance decision-
871   making. Also we can use explicitly debiasing prompts (Gallegos et al., 2024). We also use a critique
872
      model which could encourage deliberation if it’s able to detect prescriptive normativity. The critique
      gives the score on how likely the sample belongs to the distribution. We verify if this detection is
873
      correlated with the sampled value, else it wouldn’t be able to mitigate undesired prescriptive norms.

       1.15   M OTIVATION FOR EVALUATING PROTOTYPES
1446
1447   Barselou et al (Barsalou, 1985) state that ideals may determine a concept’s graded structure in one
1448   context, while central tendency may determine a different graded structure in another. In other
1449   words, when sampling, humans wouldn’t use both prescriptive and descriptive prototypical ratings
1450   in the same context. But, Bear et al (Bear & Knobe, 2017a) show that human concepts have both
1451   components in the same context in a unified representation, providing an insight into how humans
1452   think about concepts, and our notion of normality is in fact both prescriptive and descriptive. When
1453   we try to rate a normal teacher, we include both prescriptive and descriptive components in the same
1454
       context.
1455   Given the two different theories, we test this in LLMs. Previous experiments in this paper show that
1456   LLMs, when sampling from innumerable options, use both prescriptive and descriptive norms as
1457   a heuristic in the same context 1 akin to a unified representation. We show similar results of how
       prototypicality rating also has the same unified representation of both prescriptive and descriptive

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       norms in the same context. We consider this experiment as an initial foray into how representations
1459   of prototypes drive cognitive biases. More work needs to be done to understand where these
1460   representative prototypes which have prescriptive norms exhibit unfavorably biased decision making.
