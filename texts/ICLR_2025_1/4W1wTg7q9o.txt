009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Cities, as the essential environment of human life, encompass diverse physical
012            elements such as buildings, roads and vegetation, which continuously interact
013            with dynamic entities like people and vehicles. Crafting realistic, interactive 3D
014            urban environments is essential for nurturing AGI systems and constructing AI
015            agents capable of perceiving, decision-making, and acting like humans in real-
016            world environments. However, creating high-fidelity 3D urban environments usu-
017            ally entails extensive manual labor from designers, involving intricate detailing
018
               and representation of complex urban elements. Therefore, accomplishing this au-
               tomatically remains a longstanding challenge. Toward this problem, we propose
019
               UrbanWorld, the first generative urban world model that can automatically create
020
               a customized, realistic and interactive 3D urban world with flexible control con-
021            ditions. Specifically, we design a progressive diffusion-based rendering method
022            to produce 3D urban assets with high-quality textures. Moreover, we propose a
023            specialized urban multimodal large language model (Urban MLLM) trained on
024            realistic street-view image-text corpus to supervise and guide the generation pro-
025            cess. UrbanWorld incorporates four key stages in the generation pipeline: flexible
026            3D layout generation from OSM data or urban layout with semantic and height
027            maps, urban scene design with Urban MLLM, controllable urban asset rendering
028            via progressive 3D diffusion, and MLLM-assisted scene refinement. We conduct
029
               extensive quantitative analysis on five visual metrics, demonstrating that Urban-
               World achieves state-of-the-art generation realism. Next, we provide qualitative
030
               results about the controllable generation capabilities of UrbanWorld using both
031
               textual and image-based prompts. Lastly, we verify the interactive nature of these
032            environments by showcasing the agent perception and navigation within the cre-
033            ated environments. We contribute UrbanWorld as an open-source tool available at
034            https://github.com/Urban-World/UrbanWorld.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Cities are the most complex human-centric environments, characterized by their intricate spatial
040   structures, heterogeneous components such as buildings, infrastructure, and public spaces, and dy-
041   namic interactions between these components and human activities. Creating near-realistic 3D urban
042   world environments is a fundamental technique for broad research and real applications across var-
043   ious domains such as Artificial General Intelligence (Zhang et al., 2024), AI agents (Yang et al.,
044   2024), embodied AI (Wang et al., 2024a), urban simulation (Xu et al., 2023) and metaverse (Allam
045
      et al., 2022). Traditionally, achieving this involves expensive labor costs for human designers on de-
      tailed asset modeling, texture mapping, and scene composition. With the advancement of generative
046
      AI, there have emerged some automatic approaches for 3D scene generation based on volumetric
047
      rendering(Lin et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024) and diffusion models(Deng et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024).
048   These approaches have revolutionized the paradigm of 3D scene generation, alleviating the high
049   costs of manual design. However, the crafted 3D scenes are limited to the video format and unable
050   to provide embodied and interactive environments. Regarding this issue, a recent series of methods
051   known as world models have emerged, preliminarily focusing on autonomous driving scenes (Hu
052   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). These models are shown to possess the capability of understanding
053   the scene dynamics and predicting future states, uplifting the interactivity of 3D scene generation.
      However, there is still a large gap between the created urban environments and the real urban world

054
      in which humans live. To sum up, there is still a long way from the actual “urban world models”,
055   which we define as models able to create urban environments that are (1) realistic and interactive
056   (2) customizable and controllable (3) capable of supporting embodied agent learning. Table 1 pro-
057   vides a comprehensive review of existing 3D city generation methods from these key perspectives,
058   highlighting that no existing approach can fully satisfy these requirements.
059
      Urban world models are of great significance in developing embodied intelligence and Artificial
060
      General Intelligence (AGI). Firstly, it is promising to bridge the gap between virtual environments
061   and the real world, enabling embodied agents to interact with and learn from richly detailed, real-
062   istic urban environments. Secondly, by crafting synthetic 3D urban environments, researchers can
063   gain complete control over data generation, with full access to all generative parameters. Machine
064   perceptual systems can thus be trained on tasks that are not well suited to conduct in the real world
065   or require various environments. Finally, a sophisticated urban world model can simulate a wide
066   variety of environments, from bustling city centers to quiet residential neighborhoods, with realistic
067   visual appearances of physical infrastructures such as buildings, roads, and natural spaces. This
068   is crucial to avoid overfitting and creating agents with high generalization in diverse and dynamic
069
      environments. However, there are no specialized urban world models for automatically crafting
      interactive 3D urban environments, hindering the advancement of AI abilities toward general intel-
070
      ligence through embodied learning in complex open-world environments.
071
072   Toward this issue, we propose UrbanWorld, a generative urban world model that can automatically
073   create realistic, controllable and embodied 3D urban environments from user instructions and urban
074   layout data in various format such as OpenStreetMap1 (OSM) and layouts with semantic and depth
075
      maps (Deng et al., 2023; He & Aliaga, 2024). In detail, there are four key modules in the framework
      of UrbanWorld. Firstly, UrbanWorld automatically generates untextured 3D layouts with the above-
076
      mentioned input data and conducts detailed asset processing via Blender2 . Then, UrbanWorld adopts
077
      a fine-tuned urban-specific multimodal large language model (called Urban MLLM) to effectively
078   plan and design urban scenes following user instructions, generating detailed textual descriptions
079   of urban elements. Next, UrbanWorld integrates a 3D asset renderer based on texture diffusion
080   and refinement, flexibly controlled by textual and visual conditions. Finally, to further optimize
081   the visual appearance, it utilizes Urban MLLM to scrutinize the crafted 3D urban environment,
082   generating detailed suggestions for refinement and activate an additional iteration of rendering.
083
      Our framework is highly flexible to support generating two typical types of 3D urban environments.
084   On the one hand, UrbanWorld can generate a highly accurate replica of the real urban environment
085   with real street-view imagery as the generation condition, which holds significant potential for stud-
086   ies of urban planning and geographic information systems. On the other hand, it can also generate
087   fully customized urban environments with textual descriptions as the generation condition. This
088   capability is valuable for simulating and exploring hypothetical urban scenarios, especially in areas
089   such as virtual city design, gaming, and emergency response planning. In the experimental part, we
090   begin by conducting a comprehensive quantitative evaluation using five visual metrics, validating the
091   state-of-the-art realism of UrbanWorld’s generated environments. We then showcase diverse gen-
092
      eration results from various textual and image prompts, highlighting the superior controllability of
      UrbanWorld. Finally, we emphasize the interactive nature of the generated 3D urban environments
093
      by demonstrating agent perception and navigation within them. We contribute UrbanWorld as an
094
      open platform to support the creation and manipulation of more advanced 3D urban environments,
095   facilitating the advancement of broad AI communities.
096
097   The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
098
      • We present UrbanWorld, the first urban world model for automatically creating realistic, cus-
099
        tomized and interactive embodied 3D urban environments with flexible controls.
100
101   • UrbanWorld demonstrates its superior generative ability to craft high-fidelity 3D urban environ-
102     ments, greatly enhancing the authenticity of interactions in the environment.
103   • We provide UrbanWorld as an open-source platform to develop 3D urban environments, which can
104     support a wide range of research including embodied intelligence and AI agents, further laying a
105     foundation for the advancement of AGI.

      2.1      3D U RBAN S CENE G ENERATION
123
124   3D urban scene generation aims to create realistic 3D urban environments with sophisticated urban
125   planning and visual element design, usually requiring high human efforts such as complex asset
126   modeling, texture mapping, and scene composition. With the advancement of deep learning tech-
127   niques, recently there are three lines of work trying to achieve this in an automated way, including
128   neural rendering-based methods (Lin et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023), diffusion-based
129   methods (Deng et al., 2023; Inoue et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024b) and 3D modeling software-based
130   methods (Zhou et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a). An overview of the method com-
131   parison is shown in Table 1. Neural rendering-based methods implicitly represent the urban scene
132
      and perform the volumetric rendering for the neural fields. For example, CityDreamer (Xie et al.,
      2024) first separates the scene into buildings and backgrounds then introduces different types of
133
      neural fields for asset rendering. These methods can produce a high-quality visual appearance while
134
      potentially losing geometric fidelity. Diffusion-based methods utilize diffusion models to generate
135   city layouts or urban scenes. CityGen (Deng et al., 2023) provides an end-to-end pipeline to cre-
136   ate diverse 3D city layouts with Stable Diffusion. These methods are creative in generating scene
137   images or videos, but hard to obtain embodied 3D environments, limiting the practical usages. Re-
138   cently, some professional software script-based methods have been proposed, trying to develop an
139   automatic agentic workflow using LLMs to control the professional software for scene creation.
140   CityCraft (Deng et al., 2024) adopts LLMs in designing and organizing 3D urban environments
141   from off-the-shelf asset libraries. Such approaches are effective but only create urban environments
142   with existing 3D models, unable to flexibly create new assets when necessary. By comparison, our
143
      model can freely create new 3D urban assets in a highly controllable way, allowing for crafting
      diverse urban environments.

146   2.2      3D W ORLD S IMULATOR
147
148   A persistent objective in AI research has been to develop machine agents capable of engaging with
149   various environments in 3D space like humans. Toward this goal, researchers have been devoted
150   to building various interactive world simulators in the format of videos (Bruce et al., 2024) or em-
151   bodied environment (Shen et al., 2022). Existing world simulation environments and platforms are
152   mostly for indoor scenes (Puig et al., 2018; Xia et al., 2018; Kolve et al., 2017; Xia et al., 2020).
153
      Differently, Threedworld (Gan et al., 2020) paid attention to creating outdoor environments by re-
      trieving and compositing objects from an existing asset library. When it comes to urban scenes
154
      which is the most important open environment, existing works mostly focus on the generative world
155
      model for autonomous driving capable of learning scene dynamics and understanding the geometry
156   of the physical world (Hu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; 2024b). However, these models can only
157   generate new scenes in the format of videos, hard to provide an embodied and interactive urban envi-
158   ronment for real use. UGI (Xu et al., 2023) conceptualized some relevant ideas toward urban world
159   simulation, proposing to develop an embodied urban environment for agent development, but still
160   lacks practical implementation. More recently, MetaUrban Wu et al. (2024a) developed a simulation
161   platform for embodied agents in the urban environment, while the provided environment is limited
      to a fixed style without controllability. To address these challenges, we propose UrbanWorld, which

190   3      M ETHODOLOGY
191
192   There are three main challenges to solve for building a real “urban world model”: efficient interactive
193   environment construction, elaborate urban scene planning and high-quality texture rendering. To-
194   wards these objectives, UrbanWorld follows a novel “map-design-render-refine” generation pipeline,
195   accomplished by the collaboration of a specialized urban MLLM and an urban asset rendering mod-
196   ule. In detail, there are four key stages in UrbanWorld: (1) Flexible 3D urban layout generation,
197   achieving automatic 2.5D-to-3D mapping based on various urban layout metadata such as globally
198   open-accessible OSM data and urban layouts with semantic and height maps, which can address
199   the first challenge. (2) Urban MLLM-empowered scene design, which exploits the superior urban
200
      environment understanding ability of a fine-tuned urban MLLM to draft realistic urban scenes emu-
      lating human designers for addressing the second challenge. (3) Controllable diffusion-based urban
201
      asset texture renderer, achieving flexible urban asset rendering based on 3D diffusion supporting
202
      both textual and visual prompts. (4) MLLM-assisted urban scene refinement, exploiting the urban
203   MLLM to conduct reflection to refine the generated urban environment, mimicking the iterative re-
204   vision in the standard operation process of human designers. The last two components contribute to
205   high-fidelity textures of 3D assets, effectively tackling the third challenge. The overview framework
206   of UrbanWorld is illustrated in Figure 1.
207
208
      3.1     F LEXIBLE 3D U RBAN L AYOUT G ENERATION
209
210   UrbanWorld supports any type of 2.5D layout data as input to efficiently build the untextured 3D
211   urban environment, such as easily accessible OSM data and AI-generated urban layout data. These
212   2.5D layout data record the metadata of an urban area including the geographic locations and at-
213   tributes of diverse urban elements such as roads, buildings, vegetation, forests, and water. All urban
214   assets will be automatically separated as independent objects for subsequent element-wise render-
215   ing. In this step, UrbanWorld also records the object center location for further reorganization of
      assets, making it match the real urban layout.

240   3.2   U RBAN MLLM- EMPOWERED S CENE D ESIGN
241
242   Aiming to effectively craft customized urban environments, UrbanWorld integrates an advanced
243   MLLM fine-tuned on extensive real-world urban imagery data, named Urban MLLM. In detail,
244   we collected approximately 300K urban street-view images from Google Maps and used GPT-4
245   to generate associated textual descriptions. These descriptions were then manually reviewed and
246
      low-quality data were filtered out. Subsequently, we fine-tuned an advanced open-source MLLM,
      VILA-1.5 (Lin et al., 2024) with the curated dataset to improve its understanding of urban environ-
247
      ments. In the workflow of UrbanWorld, Urban MLLM is utilized to act as a human-like designer,
248
      which automatically drafts high-quality and detailed urban scene descriptions, ensuring the crafted
249   urban environment are visually coherent and instruction-following. Specifically, taking a simple
250   textual instruction (e.g., “a teaching area in the university”) from users as input, UrbanWorld calls
251   Urban MLLM with carefully designed prompts and returns diverse detailed descriptions about vi-
252   sual appearance and materials for each asset. The produced asset descriptions will be used to control
253   the condition of the later texture rendering process.
254

365   4.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
366
367   UrbanWorld incorporates three key techniques: Blender as the professional 3D modeling software,
368   diffusion-based rendering and Urban MLLM-empowered scene design and refinement. Specifi-
369
      cally, we use Blender-3.2.2 for Linux systems and the compatible Blosm addon to handle the OSM
      transformation. In terms of diffusion-based rendering, we utilize Stable Diffusion-1.5 (Rombach
370
      et al., 2022) as the fundamental diffusion backbone, combined with ControlNet-Depth (Zhang
371
      et al., 2023) when generating multiple views of 3D assets. We also introduce IP-Adapter (Ye
372   et al., 2023) to support taking reference images as the additional generation condition. We use
373   ControlNet-inpainting (Zhang et al., 2023) as the diffusion controller in the UV texture refinement
374   and ContrlNet-tile (Zhang et al., 2023) in the realness enhancement stage. For the hyper-parameter
375   settings in the rendering part, we set the number of camera views N = 4, which can basically satisfy
376   the rendering needs of most urban assets. The number of inference steps in all diffusion processes
377   is set as 30 by default. The UV maps of 3D assets are unwrapped in the “smart projection” mode
      operated in Blender. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA Tesla A100 GPU.

      A.1   D ETAILS OF THE METRICS FOR QUANTITATIVE EVALUATION
705
706   Frechét Inception Distance (FID) and Kernel Inception Distance (KID). Both metrics quantify
707   the similarity between the distribution of generated images and real images, where lower values in-
708   dicate better image quality of generated results. We compute FID and KID between frames sampled
709   from the generated scene and an evaluation set comprising 1000 real street-view images randomly
710   sampled from Google Maps. These metrics can effectively measure the realism of generated results.
711   Depth error (DE). Depth error is utilized to evaluate the 3D geometry accuracy, following the
712   implementation of EG3D (Chan et al., 2022) and CityDreamer (Xie et al., 2024). Specifically, we
713   use the pre-trained depth estimation model (Ranftl et al., 2020) to obtain the “ground truth” of depth
714   maps via density accumulation. DE is then calculated as the L2 distance between the normalized
715   predicted depth maps and the “ground truth”. The final result is averaged on the result from 100
716   captured frames of generated urban scenes.
717   Homogeneity index (HI). Realistic cities are featured by complex elements with diverse visual
718   appearances, indicating various functional uses of different urban areas. In order to capture this
719   key character, we propose to evaluate the homogeneity of generated scenes, mainly measuring the
720   variance of different urban scenes. To be specific, we first extract the visual feature of each generated
721   scene image with ResNet (He et al., 2016). The homogeneity index is then calculated as the averaged
722   cosine similarity of each pair of scenes in the feature space. The smaller value of the homogeneity
723
      index means a higher diversity of generated urban environments.
724   Preference score (PS). Another widely used approach to evaluate the generated result is utilizing
725   powerful LLMs as the evaluator Wang et al. (2023a); Peng et al. (2024), here we prompt GPT-4
726   to score the snapshots taken from the generated 3D environment, mainly considering the texture
727   sophistication and geometric completeness (ranging from 1 to 10). A higher score indicates stronger
728   preference for the generated result.
