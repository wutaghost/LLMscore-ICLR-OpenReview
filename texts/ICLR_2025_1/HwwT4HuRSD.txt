009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite recent efforts, federated learning (FL) still faces performance challenges
012            due to non-IID data distributions among clients. This distribution shift compli-
013            cates the addition of new clients and the transfer of federally learned models to
014            unseen data. Inspired by the adaptation ability of normalization layer parameters,
015            we first demonstrate the effectiveness of models trained using FedBN when be-
016
               ing adapted to so far unseen data. Specifically, we extend the adaptation method
               based on a visual analysis of the normalization layer feature vectors. We introduce
017
               Federated Personalized Client Embeddings (FedPCE), which utilizes local em-
018
               beddings to capture the underlying structure of the normalization feature vectors
019            and, by extension, the dataset. Our results show that FedPCE performs compara-
020            bly to other common FL algorithms during both training and adaptation. Notably,
021            FedPCE achieves this performance using only a fraction of the parameters during
022            fine-tuning (32 parameters in our experiments) compared to other methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
      The field of computer vision has progressed significantly in recent years, largely due to the emer-
027
      gence of deep learning. This rapid advancement of deep learning has been powered by the abundance
028
      of data available for training. Typically, the most effective setup in deep learning involves utilizing
029   a single model on a centralized system that can access the complete training dataset.
030
031   However, collecting sufficient data on a central system to support effective deep learning is not
032
      always feasible. This is particularly true when dealing with personal or medical data, where sharing
      is restricted due to data privacy regulations. Federated learning (FL) has emerged as a solution to
033
      utilize decentralized data McMahan et al. (2017).
034
035   This decentralization of data poses several challenges. One of the most significant is that the data
036   collected in this way is often not independently and identically distributed (non-IID) Li et al. (2022),
037   Rieke et al. (2020). For example, this issue can arise when healthcare centers use different imaging
038
      devices, or when variations in user practices exist across devices. In general, non-IID data hinders
      the performance of FL Zhao et al. (2018). Moreover, problems may arise when a model, already
039
      trained on certain data, is applied to newly acquired data with a distribution that differs from the
040
      original training set. Under such circumstances, the model’s performance is not guaranteed.
041
042   This scenario is common in the medical domain. Laws protecting patient privacy often make it
043   difficult to gather data centrally, and establishing agreements with new medical centers to use their
044
      data can be a lengthy process. Some centers may be unable or unwilling to participate in a FL
      process, meaning their data can only be used to adapt the model and evaluate its performance.
045
046   For both challenges, there are suggested solutions involving the adaptation of normalization layers
047   Li et al. (2021b), Li et al. (2016). We have observed that when using FedBN on artificially non-IID
048   data, some feature vectors of normalization layers tend to cluster together. As shown in Figure 1,
049   when clients with non-IID data are created using three different types of artificial degradation, the
050
      local normalization feature vectors of similar clients are closer to each other. We aimed to capture
      this similarity in a low-dimensional embedding space, leading to the introduction of FedPCE.
051
052   Our approach seeks to capture the underlying data structure through learnable embedding vectors.
053   We modify the model architecture by replacing normalization layer parameters with feature vec-
      tors generated from an embedding vector through a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). This allows us


                                                         1

054
      to focus on the low-dimensional embeddings during fine-tuning, making it possible to use fewer
055   parameters and training samples.
056
057   Our primary contribution is the introduction of a novel method in which all parameters of the normal-
058
      ization layers are generated from a local embedding vector. We further demonstrate that adapting
      these low-dimensional embedding vectors to new data is sufficient to achieve competitive perfor-
059
      mance, thereby reducing the dimensionality of the adaptation problem. This approach not only
060
      minimizes the number of parameters that need to be adjusted but also decreases the required data
061   for adaptation, making it highly efficient for scenarios with limited data.

064   2     R ELATED WORK
065
066   2.1   F EDERATED LEARNING
067
068   Federated learning (FL) was introduced by McMahan et al. (2017) by the development of Federated
069   Averaging (FedAvg), which laid the foundations for distributed learning in scenarios where data
070
      privacy and communication efficiency is critical. Since then, FL has attracted significant attention
      due to its ability to collaboratively train machine learning models across decentralized data sources
071
      without sharing raw data.
072
073   A primary challenge in FL is the heterogeneity of data across clients, commonly referred to as non-
074   IID (independently and identically distributed) data as this phenomenon makes the models trained
075   using FedAvg suboptimal Li et al. (2020b), Zhao et al. (2018), Hsieh et al. (2020). Several ap-
076
      proaches have been proposed to address this. FedProx Li et al. (2020a) extends FedAvg by in-
      troducing a proximal term to stabilize training on heterogeneous data. SCAFFOLD Karimireddy
077
      et al. (2020) mitigates the effects of client drift by correcting updates using control variates, FedMA
078
      Wang et al. (2020) uses matched averaging to better aggregate during global updates, MOON Li
079   et al. (2021a) introduces contrastive learning to align model representations between the client and
080   the server, further enhancing personalization, FedBS Idrissi et al. (2021) assigns weights to client
081   model updates based on the local loss and switches to FedProx after a while, FedDNA Duan et al.
082   (2021) weights the statistical parameters of the model differently and pFedLA Ma et al. (2022) uses
083   server-side hypernetworks to personalize model aggregation for each client.
084
085   2.2   D OMAIN ADAPTATION
086
087   Domain adaptation aims to transfer knowledge from a source domain with abundant data to a target
088   domain with limited data. Challenges arise because the data distribution in the target domain differs
089   from that in the source domain. Li et al. (2016) has shown that batch norm layers can play an
090   important role in adapting models to new domains. Lian et al. (2022) utilize scaling and shifting
091   features to adapt models to new data, and in FedIN Feng et al. (2023) this approach is applied to
092
      improve FL. The significance of scale and shift is also evident in style transfer Dumoulin et al.
      (2016); Huang & Belongie (2017). Additionally, Feature-wise Modulation Layers (FiLM), which
093
      scale and shift feature vectors, have been shown to support synergistic learning on partially labeled
094
      region-based segmentations, as demonstrated by Billot et al. (2024).

167
168   FedBN attempts to address the problem posed by data shift in FL by keeping the parameters of the
169   normalization layers local. In the case of the most common normalization layers (e.g., Batch Norm

171   refers to the scale and shift parameters. These were introduced by Ioffe & Szegedy (2015) to restore
172
      the representation power of the network after normalization. The action of these normalization
      layers is as follows for a feature vector x:

      3.4   D ECENTRALISED LEARNING WITH EMBEDDINGS
217
218   We now describe the usage of embeddings, which involves first training the model using the clients
219   available for FL—a process we refer to as collaborative training (Col. tr.)—and then adapting it to
220   unseen data, which we will call client training (Cl. tr.).
221
      Collaborative training is conducted using personalized FL, as outlined in Subsection 3.1. At each
222
      client Cn , only vn serves as a local parameter, i.e., Ωlocn   = {vn }, while the remaining weights
223   are global, denoted as Ωgl    . Specifically, the weights of the  MLPs mapping from the embedding
                                  n
224   to the normalization shifts and scales are global parameters. Thus, only an E dimensional vector
225   characterizes the personalization of each client. To initialize the embedding vector of each client for
226   collaborative training, we set vn = e(n) if n ≤ E and 0 otherwise. We experimented with different
227   initialization strategies but did not observe any empirical difference.
228
      The collaborative training enables the model to distill knowledge from the different local data dis-
229
      tributions and represent it by local vn . Thereby, encoding semantic information in the associated
230   embedding. Since the weights of the MLPs are global, the model collectively learns how to interpret
231   this condensed information.
232
233
      In the client training process, we utilize this encoded representation of the training data distribution
      to effectively personalize new clients to its associated data. To do so, we freeze all global param-
234
      eters of the model and only fine-tune the embedding vector vN +1 , as shown in the bottom right
235
      of Figure 2. Due to the low dimensionality of the embedding, only a small subset of the model’s
236   parameters have to be fine-tuned during client training, which reduces the number of labeled data
237   samples required to effectively adapt the model to unseen clients.

242   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL DESIGN AND DATASETS
243
244   We now describe the experiments that will demonstrate the effectiveness of local embeddings in
245   adapting a model to unseen data. The experiments simulate a scenario in which data centralization
246
      is not possible, and the data at some clients cannot be used for training; it can only be used to adapt
      the model for local usage. Therefore, we will generate a number of clients with artificially non-IID
247
      data distribution, use some of them for collaborative training, and then fine-tune the model on the
248
      remaining clients during client training.
249
250   To demonstrate the versatility of embeddings, we conduct experiments on three standard datasets:
251
252          • CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) are classification datasets, each con-
253
               sisting of 50,000 training and 10,000 validation images. Each of these RGB images is of
               size 32 × 32 and belongs to one of 10 or 100 classes, respectively.
254
255          • The third dataset, which we denote as Digits, is the union of four different public computer
256            vision datasets, all containing images of digits (0-9). These are the MNIST (LeCun, 1998),
257            USPS (Hull, 1994), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), and SYN (Roy et al., 2018) datasets. They
258            contain 60,000, 7,291, 73,257, and 10,000 training images, respectively, and 10,000, 2,007,
259            26,032, and 2,000 validation images, respectively. The SVHN and SYN datasets consist of
260
               colored images, while MNIST and USPS contain grayscale images. For training, all images
               were resized to 32 × 32 using bilinear interpolation. We convert all grayscale images to
261
               color images.

      For the Digits dataset, we set the total number of clients to be a multiple of 4, denoted as 4K. For

302   4.3   A RCHITECTURE
303
      We will conduct all of our experiments using ResNet-18 He et al. (2016). The models are imple-
304
      mented as described in He et al. (2016), with the following modifications. We use the ’CIFAR’ ver-
305
      sion of ResNet, meaning the first convolution layer has a kernel size of 3, a stride of 1, and padding of
306   1, instead of the usual kernel size of 7, stride of 2, and padding of 3. We use instance normalization
307   layers (Ulyanov et al., 2016) instead of the usual batch normalization Ioffe & Szegedy (2015), as we
308   observed that instance normalization improves the performance of FedPCE. Additionally, we insert
309   an extra normalization layer into the classification head, right before the final fully connected layer.
310   This allows the embeddings to more directly influence the class predictions through the shift and
311   scale vectors of the normalization layer, which is particularly helpful in the case of Class imbalance.
312   Because we only need to fine-tune the low-dimensional embedding vector in FedPCE during
313   client training, the fine-tuning process requires training significantly fewer parameters. Although
314   a ResNet-18 adjusted to FedPCE is 6.9% larger than ResNet-18 in terms of the number of pa-
315   rameters due to the additional MLPs (11.9M vs. 11.2M), fine-tuning it requires training only 32
316   parameters, compared to 10,624 for FedBN, representing a 332-fold decrease. To show that the per-
317   formance does not stem from the extra number of parameters, we also conduct our experiments on
318   a smaller version of ResNet and FedPCE, which we will denote by FedPCE(62). This is the same
319   architecture as a ResNet-18 adjusted to FedPCE but we replace the original channel dimensions of
320
      the ResNet blocks of (64, 128, 256, 512) by (62, 124, 248, 496). FedPCE(62) has only an extra
      600K parameters, a difference of 0.54% compared to ResNet-18.

354   5.1     T RAINING DETAILS AND BENCHMARK RESULTS
355
356   In the main experiments, we use a total of 30 clients for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and 40 clients
357
      for Digits. In all cases, 80% of the clients are used for collaborative training, and the remaining 20%
      for client training. Unless stated otherwise, we use MLPs with 2 layers and a hidden layer dimension
358
      of 64. Local training is conducted using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with an initial
359
      learning rate of 10−4 , betas of (0.5, 0.9), a weight decay of 10−4 , and cosine learning rate scheduling
360   with a minimum learning rate of 10−6 . For FedPCE, the same optimizer and scheduler are used,
361   except for the embeddings, where the initial learning rate is 0.1 with a minimum of 10−4 , and for
362   the MLP parameters, the initial learning rate is 10−2 . We use a batch size of 64 and train for 50
363   local iterations between each global model aggregation. The same hyperparameters are used during
364   client training, except for the starting learning rate for the embeddings which is 10−2 . Collaborative
365   training is run until the 1000th global aggregation, while client training is done for 200 global
366   aggregations. The training images are augmented by first padding with 4 pixels of value 0 on all
367   sides, randomly cropping the image to 32 × 32, flipping the image horizontally with a probability
368
      of 0.25, rotating it by a degree uniformly sampled from (−15◦ , 15◦ ) with a probability of 0.25, and
      randomly erasing a rectangle of size between 16 and 256 pixels with a probability of 0.5.

462   First, we demonstrate that, due to the low dimensionality of the embedding space, a model trained
463   with FedPCE can be fine-tuned effectively using a very small number of data points. In this ex-
464   periment, we compare FedBN and FedPCE models that were collaboratively trained on CIFAR-10.
465   During client training, we limit the number of available training images per client, ranging from
466
      as few as 2 images to the full dataset of 1600 images per client. The validation set remains con-
      sistent across all trials. The results are illustrated in Figure 4 and show that FedPCE consistently
467
      outperforms FedBN for a low number of available training samples. Its performance stabilizing af-
468
      ter around 100 training samples. Beyond this point, the additional fine-tuning parameters of FedBN
469   enable it to close the gap with FedPCE and eventually surpass it for more than 800 training samples.
470   This demonstrates FedPCE’s advantage in low-data regimes, while also highlighting the benefits of
471   FedBN’s more complex adaptation as the data volume increases.

499   6   C ONCLUSION
500
501   In this work, we proposed to distill the implicit representations encoded in the parameters of person-
502   alized normalization layers in FL using embeddings. These embeddings are learned from multiple
503   clients during collaborative training. Afterwards, the model can be easily extended to new clients
504   with different data distributions by just fine-tuning the client’s embedding vector. Extensive numer-
505
      ical experiments demonstrated that the proposed FedPCE approach generates comparable results to
      established personalization approaches, while drastically reducing the number of local parameters.
506
      This suggests that the adaptation problem is inherently low-dimensional. Moreover, the ability to
507
      fine-tune fewer parameters not only improves computational efficiency but also reduces the amount
508   of labeled data required for effective adaptation. This is particularly important in the field of health-
509   care, where labeled data is usually scarce and very costly.
