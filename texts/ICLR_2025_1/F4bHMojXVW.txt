009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-form video understanding has been a challenging task due to the high redun-
012            dancy in video data and the abundance of query-irrelevant information. To tackle
013            this challenge, we propose V IDEOT REE, a training-free framework which builds
014            a query-adaptive and hierarchical video representation for LLM reasoning over
015            long-form videos. First, V IDEOT REE extracts query-relevant information from
016            the input video through an iterative process, progressively refining the selection
017            of keyframes based on their relevance to the query. Furthermore, V IDEOT REE
018
               leverages the inherent hierarchical structure of long video data, which is often
               overlooked by existing LLM-based methods. Specifically, we incorporate multi-
019
               granularity information into a tree-based representation, allowing V IDEOT REE to
020
               extract query-relevant details from long videos in a coarse-to-fine manner. This
021            enables the model to effectively handle a wide range of video queries with varying
022            levels of detail. Finally, V IDEOT REE aggregates the hierarchical query-relevant
023            information within the tree structure and feeds it into an LLM reasoning model to
024            answer the query. Our experiments show that our training-free method improves
025            both reasoning accuracy and efficiency compared to existing methods. Specifically,
026            V IDEOT REE outperforms the existing training-free approaches on the popular
027            EgoSchema and NExT-QA benchmarks with less inference time, achieving 61.1%
028            and 75.6% accuracy on the test set without additional video-specific training.
029
               Moreover, on the long split of Video-MME benchmark (average 44 minutes), the
               training-free V IDEOT REE framework achieves better performance than the strong
030
               proprietary GPT-4V model and other MLLMs that were extensively trained on
031
               video data. Our code is provided in the supplementary and will be made public.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   With the surge in accessible long video content and the growing importance of applications such as
037   long-form human behavior analysis and movie analysis, developing models capable of reasoning over
038   and answering questions about long-form videos has become increasingly crucial. Recently, several
039   approaches (Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024g; Kahatapitiya et al., 2024) have emerged that
040   leverage the long-sequence reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) to tackle the
041   challenge in long-form video understanding in a training-free manner. . Typically, these approaches
042   leverage vision-language models (VLM) to caption densely sampled frames, thus representing the
043   video in text format. This text representation is then subsequently fed into an LLM, which reasons
044
      over the video and responds to the provided query. Although this strategy has demonstrated great
      potentials on long-form video understanding benchmarks, it still faces two major limitations:
045
046   1) Informational Overload: Long videos inherently contain high levels of information redundancy,
047   and current approaches (Zhang et al., 2023a; Chung & Yu, 2023) lack a principled method to
048   effectively address this challenge. A deluge of redundant and irrelevant information can overwhelm
049   the LLM, leading to mistakes in long-form video reasoning and reduced efficiency.
050   2) Inability to Capture the Coarse-to-Fine Video Structure: Existing approaches (Zhang et al.,
051   2023a; Wang et al., 2024c) often simplify video content into a list of captions without any structure,
052   failing to account for the hierarchical nature of video information. Especially in long videos, some
053   video regions are information-dense – requiring fine-grained temporal understanding – while others
      are irrelevant to the query, or information-sparse. Because of this, existing approaches not only suffer

079   These limitations underscore the pressing need for a new long-form video understanding method. To
080   this end, we introduce V IDEOT REE, a training-free framework for long-form video understanding.
081   V IDEOT REE dynamically extracts query-relevant keyframes from the video input in a coarse-to-fine
082   manner and organizes them within a tree structure, with child nodes representing more fine-grained
083   information. V IDEOT REE is adaptive, meaning that our method allocates more frames to relevant
084   video regions and fewer frames to irrelevant ones based on the given query. V IDEOT REE is also
085   hierarchical. Unlike existing approaches (Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024c), which treat video
086   as a list of frames, our method explores the inherent structure within the video data (e.g., events,
087
      scenes) to extract fine-grained information relevant to the video query.
088   V IDEOT REE relies on three crucial steps: adaptive breadth expansion (Fig. 1a), relevance-guided
089   depth expansion (Fig. 1b), and LLM-based reasoning (Fig. 1c). To address redundancy in long
090   videos, V IDEOT REE first leverages an adaptive breadth expansion module to extract query-relevant
091   information, forming the initial level of representation. We utilize an iterative process of visual
092   clustering, keyframe captioning, and relevance scoring until sufficient query-relevant information is
093
      gathered. Compared to existing approaches (Kahatapitiya et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a) that rely on
      dense frame captions, V IDEOT REE selects only sparse keyframes for captioning, which significantly
094
      improves inference efficiency and helps avoid irrelevant information that could interfere with accurate
095
      video reasoning. To capture more fine-grained information, we introduce a relevance-guided depth
096   expansion step that adds finer, query-specific details in a hierarchical structure, forming a tree-based
097   representation. Finally, we generate video descriptions from the structured representation using a
098   captioner and provide them, along with the query, to the LLM for long video reasoning.
099
      We demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of V IDEOT REE by evaluating it on two mainstream
100
      long video question answering (LVQA) datasets, EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024)and NExT-QA
101
      (Xiao et al., 2021). Compared existing training-free approaches, V IDEOT REE achieves 2.1% and
102   4.3% improvements on EgoSchema(subset) and NExT-QA validation set with less inference time or
103   LLM calls. To further validate V IDEOT REE effectiveness on very long videos, we test our method on
104   the long split of the recent Video-MME benchmark (Fu et al., 2024a) and V IDEOT REE achieves better
105   performance than the strong proprietary GPT-4V model. Our ablation studies show that V IDEOT REE
106   outperforms the the same category methods (VideoAgent (Wang et al., 2024c) and LLoVi (Zhang
107   et al., 2023a)) under all number of captions and observes better efficiency-effectiveness trade-off. We
      further provide addition results on open-source LLM, where V IDEOT REE shows strong generalization

114   Structural Video Representation. Video understanding (Lin et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023b; Li
115   et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2023d; Xu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023a; Ma et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
116   2023d;a; Wu et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022) has shown impressive
117
      advancement in both views of comprehension and efficiency. Recently, several video-language
      methods (Ashutosh et al., 2023; Li et al., 2020; Islam et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023e; Zhang et al.,
118
      2018; Zala et al., 2023; Qing et al., 2022; Sanders et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2022a;
119
      Lu et al., 2022) have further introduced a structured understanding of video frames to allow compact
120   and efficient recognition of scene contexts. For example, HierVL (Ashutosh et al., 2023) proposes a
121   bottom-up hierarchical video-language embedding that capture video representations across short
122   and long time periods. VideoReCap (Islam et al., 2024) introduces a progressive video captioning
123   approach that generates short clip-level captions and summarizes them into longer segments. These
124   methods process long videos by progressively building high-level knowledge from local temporal
125   information, i.e. in a bottom-up fashion that first captures all low-level details and then aggregates.
126   This results in significant computational and time overhead. In contrast, V IDEOT REE employs a
127   top-down approach with dynamic depth, enabling efficient and effective long video understanding by
128
      dynamically extracting query-relevant keyframes in a coarse-to-fine manner for LLM reasoning .
129   Video Understanding with LLMs. Inspired by the powerful reasoning capabilities of LLMs, recent
130   works have explored using LLMs to address complex video-related tasks. Since LLMs primarily
131   process text, various methods (Munasinghe et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023b; Korbar et al., 2024; Weng
132   et al., 2024; Maaz et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Tan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023a; Li et al.,
133   2024a; Jin et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024d; Wang et al., 2024h; Li et al., 2024c; Yu et al.,
134
      2024b) have been developed to efficiently train multimodal projectors to connect the visual encoder
      and LLMs or leverage caption-centric information. Past works (Wang et al., 2022a; Kahatapitiya
135
      et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c; Surís et al., 2023; Choudhury et al., 2023; Wang
136
      et al., 2023c; Ko et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024g) has investigated training-free combinations of
137   captioners and LLMs for video understanding. Specifically, LLoVi (Zhang et al., 2023a) proposes a
138   simple language-guided video understanding method. First, it extracts short-term video descriptions
139   with a captioning model, and then an LLM summarizes these dense captions and responds to the
140   given prompt. VideoAgent (Wang et al., 2024c) introduces a multi-round frame search strategy using
141   an LLM agent. Different from VideoAgent’s non-hierarchical keyframe searching, we propose to
142   extract the key information long videos in an adaptive and coarse-to-fine manner, which improves the
143   efficiency and generalize better to more frames and getting better performance.

      We present V IDEOT REE, a framework for constructing a query-adaptive, hierarchical video repre-
      sentation for efficient LLM reasoning over long videos. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the V IDEOT REE
150
      framework consists of three main steps: adaptive breadth expansion, relevance-guided depth expan-
151
      sion, and LLM video reasoning. Given the highly redundant nature of long videos, V IDEOT REE first
152   leverages an adaptive breadth expansion module to extract query-relevant information from the video,
153   forming the initial level of representation (Sec. 3.1). To capture finer-grained details, we propose a
154   relevance-guided depth expansion module that progressively adds finer-grained, query-specific details
155   to in a hierarchical manner, forming a tree-based representation (Sec. 3.2). Finally, we extract the
156   video description from the constructed representation using a captioner and feed it, along with the
157   query, into the LLM for long video reasoning (Sec. 3.3).

189   et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024d) select a fixed number of keyframes as the key information.
190   However, as discussed in Sec. 1, this fixed keyframe selection is sub-optimal for a general long
191   video-language understanding framework, since the information density varies across videos—some
192   contain numerous scene changes, while others remain largely static. To address this, we propose
193   an adaptive breadth expansion module that constructs the first level of the tree representation by
194   dynamically identifying keyframes that are relevant to the given query. Specifically, as shown in
195   the left of Fig. 2 (Step 1), given the video and a query about it, we build the first level of the tree
196   by iterating three operations: visual clustering, cluster captioning, and relevance scoring. These
197
      operations first group similar frames together, then generate captions for each cluster, and use the
      LLM to determine how relevant each cluster is to the query. V IDEOT REE iterate these operations
198
      until getting enough query-relevant information from long videos in an adaptive manner. In the
199
      following paragraphs, we motivate and introduce each operation in detail.
200
201   Visual Clustering. To reduce the redundancy, we first propose a visual clustering operation that
202   groups the video frames based on semantic similarity, allowing the model to focus on representative
203
      frames from each cluster while discarding repetitive or irrelevant content. Specifically, given a video
      sequence V = (F1 , F2 ..., Fn ), where Fi is the frame at the time step i and n is the length of the
204
      video, we extract visual features for each frame with the pre-trained visual encoder (Sun et al., 2024)
205
      E, such that fi = E(Fi ), where fi ∈ Rd is the visual features extracted by the frame Fi . These
206   features serve as a compact representation of each frame’s visual content, capturing diverse semantics
207   of each frame, such as scenes and objects. Then we use K-Means clustering (MacQueen et al., 1967),
208   to group frame features into k distinct clusters, which we denote as:
209

215   Cluster Captioning. To better extract the key semantics from each cluster, we leverage a captioner

217   ith cluster. We then feed the extracted keyframe into the VLM-based captioner Cap(·) (Zhao et al.,
218   2023; Liu et al., 2024) and obtain a text caption ti = Cap(Fi ) for each cluster. These text captions

      Relevance Scoring. To encourage the model to extract query-relevant information, after obtaining
221
      the cluster captions t, we leverage the reasoning capability of the LLM to assess whether the extracted
222
      information are sufficient for answering the given query. To this end, we first feed all cluster captions
223   {ti ∀i ∈ [1, . . . , k]} from the last operation and the video query Q into the LLM and output a set
224   of relevance scores {ri ∀i ∈ [1, . . . , k]} for each cluster, where ri is the relevance of the ith cluster.
225   Specifically, to obtain each ri , we prompt the LLM with the captions and the query, asking it to assign
226   a relevance score to each caption, with three levels: 1 (not relevant), 2 (somewhat relevant), and 3

228
      Then, we adaptively extract the query-relevant information within the video by iterating the clustering,
229   captioning, and relevance scoring operation. Specifically, given the list of relevance scores for each
230   cluster, we set a threshold of the number of highly relevant clusters rele_num_thresh to decide the
231   stop of the adaptive process. We also set a maximum value for the number of clusters (max_breadth)
232   to avoid infinite loops. If the number of highly relevant clusters is below the requirement, that
233   indicates the information extracted from the current cluster assignment is insufficient for the LLM
234   to answer the video query. In that case, we increase the number of clusters k by double the original
235   number and repeat the clustering, captioning, and relevance scoring operations. If the number of
236   high-relevance clusters meets the threshold rele_num_thresh or the number of clusters reaches
237
      max_breadth, we append the extracted clusters with their keyframes to the first layer of the tree and
      continue to the next step (see more details in lines 2-11 in Algorithm 1).

240   3.2   R ELEVANCE -G UIDED D EPTH E XPANSION
241
242   After obtaining the first-level clusters and their keyframes, V IDEOT REE captures high-level query-
243
      relevant information from the video input. However, some video regions are information-dense and
      critical for answering the query, requiring a more detailed selection of keyframes.
244
245   Existing approaches, such as SeViLA (Yu et al., 2024a) and VideoAgent (Wang et al., 2024c),
246   typically treat the selected frames as an unstructured list, overlooking the potential internal structure
247   within the video data. To address this, as shown in Step 2 of Fig. 2, we construct a hierarchical
248   video representation on top of the clusters from the previous breadth expansion step, allowing us to
249
      efficiently extract query-relevant details by leveraging the semantic relationships within the video
      data. Specifically, we expand the depth of the tree by sub-clustering the clusters with higher relevance
250
      scores from the first step. The intuition is that for high-relevance clusters, the LLM requires more
251
      detailed, granular information, while for low-relevance clusters, more information could actually lead
252   to irrelevant details and could overwhelm the LLM, leading to incorrect reasoning.
253
254   To build the hierarchical structure, we use the relevance of a top-level cluster to determine how many
255
      levels of more granular information will be extracted from it. Since the relevance score r falls into
      one of three levels, we handle each first-level cluster differently based on its assigned relevance level.
256
      For "somewhat relevant" clusters, we re-cluster the first-level cluster into w sub-clusters, where w
257
      represents the tree’s branch width, ensuring that more keyframes are allocated to these moderately
258   relevant clusters. For "highly relevant" clusters, we re-cluster into a two-level tree with a branch width
259   of w using hierarchical clustering. This coarse-to-fine exploration strategy allows for the detailed
260   extraction of relevant information, supporting comprehensive video analysis for complex queries. We
261   repeat this process for all first-level clusters and build the hierarchical structure of V IDEOT REE (lines
262   12-15 in Algorithm 1). After the breadth and depth expansion steps, we obtain the tree-based video
263   representation for LLM reasoning over the long video.

      into the captioner to obtain keyframe captions. We then sort these keyframe captions in temporal

      Tasks & Datasets. We test V IDEOT REE on three diverse long-form video question-answering
      benchmarks: (1) EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024), a long-range video question-answering
278
      benchmark consisting of 5K multiple choice question-answer pairs spanning 250 hours of video
279
      and covering a wide range of human activities. Our ablation studies are conducted on the official
280   validation set of EgoSchema which contains 500 questions (referred to as the EgoSchema Subset).
281   The videos are 180 seconds long on average. (2) NExT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021), a video question-
282   answering benchmark for causal and temporal reasoning. It contains 5440 videos with an average
283   length of 44s and approximately 52K questions. NExT-QA contains 3 different question types:
284   Temporal (Tem.), Causal (Cau.), and Descriptive (Des.). (3) Video-MME (Fu et al., 2024a) is a
285   recent-proposed comprehensive evaluation benchmark for video analysis. We test V IDEOT REE on
286   the “long-term videos” split of the dataset (long split), whose average video length is 44 minutes,
287   ranging from 30-60 minutes.

      6        C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we proposed V IDEOT REE, an adaptive and hierarchical framework for LLM reasoning
533
      over long-form videos. V IDEOT REE adaptively extracts query-relevant keyframes from the video
534   input in a coarse-to-fine manner and organizes them into a hierarchical representation, enabling the
535   LLM to effectively handle complex queries. V IDEOT REE resulted in strong performance on three
536   popular datasets (EgoSchema, NExT-QA, and Video-MME), while also improving efficiency by
537   reducing the inference time and LLM calls. We analyzed the role of the adaptive cluster selection
538   we implement in V IDEOT REE, finding that it is crucial to strong performance. In our qualitative
539   analysis, we showed that given a complex multi-scene video and its query, V IDEOT REE is capable of
      extracting key scenes and zooming into more detailed information that is highly related to the query.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      The intended use of V IDEOT REE is to answer questions about long-form videos. This does not have
543   any particular potential for misuse beyond the general potential for AI technology to be used in
544   harmful ways. Because it is based on VLM captioning and LLMs answering questions from captions,
545   V IDEOT REE has the potential to hallucinate (both in the captioning stage and the QA stage). Note
546   that potential is shared both with other caption-then-LLM approaches (Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang
547   et al., 2024c; Kahatapitiya et al., 2024) and end-to-end VLM-based approaches(Zhang et al., 2024b;
548   Lin et al., 2023c), which are also prone to hallucination (Ji et al., 2023). The fact that V IDEOT REE
549   answers questions based on coarse-to-fine captions that are tied to particular frames in a video makes
550   it more interpretable than black-box methods, mitigating the potential for hallucinations that mislead
551
      users, since users could potentially examine the sampled frames as we have done in Fig. 4.

      A     L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT
879
880
      Limitations. Like all LLM-based video-reasoning systems (including dense sampling) our method
881   is limited by the ability of the captioner to accurately capture the contents of sampled frames. However,
882   our method’s modular nature means that as captioners improve, we can easily include them into the
883   V IDEOT REE framework; similarly, we can use increasingly strong LLMs as the reasoning backbone
884   of V IDEOT REE. While V IDEOT REE is training-free, it includes a small number of hyperparameters.
885   In Sec. C, we ablate these hyperparameters, showing that V IDEOT REE outperforms the uniform-
886   sampling baseline regardless of the choice of max depth and branch width. Thus, while better
887   hyperparameters can benefit the method, even with sub-optimal settings V IDEOT REE outperforms
888   the uniform baseline.
889
890   Broader Impact. Our results indicate that we have the best of both worlds: improved accuracy
891   and improved efficiency. Given the importance of long video reasoning tasks, improving accuracy
892
      has obvious broader implications for building more usable video reasoning systems, which could
      contribute to a wide variety of positive applications. Efficiency improvements also contribute to the
893
      applicability of long video systems, as reducing latency and computational cost can speed up adoption.
894
      Furthermore, since both VLM captioners and LLM reasoners generally improve with increased scale,
895   reducing the number of calls to them will become increasingly important; we expect the efficiency
896   benefits coming from our method to play an even larger role in the future. Our work does not have
897   any particularly relevant potential for negative applications or misuse beyond the standard caveats
898   that apply to all machine learning systems.

1051       cluster rele_num_thresh, maximum number of clusters allowed max_breadth, branch width
1052       w, visual encoder E, LLM Fllm , captioner Fvlm , cluster information C, relevance score R,
1053       tree-based video representation T ree
        1: k ← k_init
1054
        2: while k ≤ max_breadth do                                         ▷ Adaptive breadth expansion
1055
        3:     C ← VisualClustering(E, V, k)
1056
        4:     Cap ← ClusterCaptioning(Fvlm , V, C)
1057    5:     R ← RelevanceScoring(Fllm , C, Q, Cap)
1058    6:     if count(r ∈ R | r = high) ≥ rele_num_thresh then
1059    7:         T ree ← T ree.append(C)                                     ▷ First level of V IDEOT REE
1060    8:         break
1061    9:     end if
1062   10:     k ←k∗2
1063   11: end while
1064   12: for Ci ∈ C do                                             ▷ Relevance-guided depth expansion
1065   13:     Ĉi ← DepthExpansion(E, Ci , Ri , w)
1066   14:     T ree ← T ree.append(Ĉi )                             ▷ Adding hierarchy of V IDEOT REE
1067   15: end for
1068   16: Cap ← GetCaptions(Fvlm , V, T ree)                                             ▷ LLM Reasoning
       17: pred_answer ← LLMReasoning(Fllm , Cap, Q)
1069
       18: return pred_answer
