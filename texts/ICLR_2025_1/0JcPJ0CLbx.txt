009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) presents an exciting opportunity to unlock the
012            potential of vast, untapped clinical datasets, for various downstream applications
013            that suffer from the scarcity of labeled data. While SSL has revolutionized fields
014            like natural language processing and computer vision, their adoption in 3D med-
015            ical image computing has been limited by three key pitfalls: Small pre-training
016            dataset sizes, architectures inadequate for 3D medical image analysis, and insuf-
017            ficient evaluation practices. We address these issues by i) leveraging a large-scale
018            dataset of 44k 3D brain MRI volumes and ii) using a Residual Encoder U-Net
019            architecture within the state-of-the-art nnU-Net framework. iii) A robust develop-
020
               ment framework, incorporating 5 development and 8 testing brain MRI segmenta-
               tion datasets, allowed performance-driven design decisions to optimize the simple
021
               concept of Masked Auto Encoders (MAEs) for 3D CNNs. The resulting model
022
               not only surpasses previous SSL methods but also outperforms the strong nnU-Net
023            baseline by an average of approximately 3 Dice points. Furthermore, our model
024            demonstrates exceptional stability, achieving the highest average rank of 2 out of
025            7 methods, compared to the second-best method’s mean rank of 3. Our code is
026            made available here.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   In recent years, the concept of Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) has emerged as a driving factor in
033
      data-rich domains, enabling large-scale pre-training that facilitates the learning of robust and trans-
      ferrable general-purpose representations (Assran et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2023; He et al., 2022).
034
      This paradigm shift has been instrumental in advancing various fields, particularly in domains with
035
      abundant labeled data like NLP or natural vision. In the domain of 3D medical image computing,
036   this trend has not caught on.
037
038   Currently, the domain is either focused on training from-scratch, mainly using the nnU-Net frame-
039
      work by Isensee et al. (2021), or using supervised pretraining, which is limited by the cost associated
      with annotated data (Wasserthal et al., 2023; Ulrich et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023). The usage of su-
040
      pervised pretraining implies the willingness of the domain to adopt pretraining and calls to question
041
      the currently established SSL methods in the domain. We believe this lack of widespread adoption
042   of previously established methods can be attributed to three major pitfalls of previous SSL research
043   in this field:
044
045
      P1 - Limited dataset size: Many SSL approaches have been developed on very few unlabeled vol-
      umes, often being trained on fewer than 10,000 images Zhou et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2024); Tang
046
      et al. (2024; 2022); Zhuang et al. (2023); Hatamizadeh et al. (2021), almost approaching scales of
047
      supervised dataset sizes. These datasets tend to be pooled from publicly available annotated datasets,
048   as larger datasets pose a greater hurdle to acquire. While many hospitals possess 3D medical images
049   in the millions, they are locked away from the public due to patient privacy concerns. While some
050   larger open-source datasets exist, e.g., the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Decline (ABCD) dataset of
051   the NIH (N=40k) or the UK Biobank (UK-BB) (N=120k), they restrict access pending an internal
052   review board’s approval, posing a hurdle for open science. More recently, the UK-BB stopped allow-
053   ing downloading data to local hardware, signaling a public decrease in the community’s willingness
      to share data.

054
      P2 - Outdated Backbones: Many studies develop their SSL method on non-state-of-the-art archi-
055   tectures, e.g., utilizing transformers (Wu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Chen
056   et al., 2023). While transformers are prevalent in the 2D natural imaging domain (Dosovitskiy,
057   2020), recent architectures leveraging attention (Vaswani, 2017) have so far not been able to reach
058   state-of-the-art performance in 3D medical segmentation. In fact, well-configured 3D U-Net (Çiçek
059   et al., 2016; Ronneberger et al., 2015) inspired CNNs dominate 3D medical image segmentation,
060   outperforming transformer-based models by a large margin Isensee et al. (2024). This underscores
061   the need for SSL methods that can be seamlessly integrated with CNNs to harness their full potential
062   in medical image analysis on downstream tasks.
063   P3 - Insufficient Evaluation: Existing methods often lack rigorous evaluation, masking the meth-
064   ods’ efficacy (or lack thereof). This is represented through: i) Evaluating on too few datasets to
065   show generalization of the pre-training ii) Stacking multiple contributions, e.g., novel architecture
066   with new pre-training, which does not allow one to draw conclusions if the pre-training is effective
067   without the architecture (Wang et al., 2023) iii) Comparing to bad from-scratch baselines, like a
068   badly configured, outdated Çiçek et al. (2016) model instead of a well-configured 3D nnU-Net CNN
069
      baseline (Isensee et al., 2021). iv) Evaluating their method on seen data they pre-trained on. We want
      to emphasize that we do not intend to point fingers, but to raise awareness that evaluation matters
070
      and insufficient evaluation can lead to a lack of clarity which methods are the best. This observation
071
      is similar to the recent study by Isensee et al. (2024), that shows that this is also prevalent in the field
072   of medical image segmentation when training from-scratch.
073
074   In this paper, we carefully avoided all these pitfalls while exploring the Masked Auto Encoder
075
      (MAE) paradigm for 3D CNNs, with the recent adaptations introduced by Tian et al. (2023); Woo
      et al. (2023), and highlight that MAEs are exceeding the current state-of-the-art SSL methods in 3D
076
      medical image segmentation given proper configuration. Our contributions can be summarized as
077
      follows:
078
079         1. We evade Pitfall 1 by leveraging a dataset collection of 44k 3D MRI volumes to develop
080            our self-supervised pretraining, exceeding the majority of 3D medical image segmentation
081            SSL methods in scale (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; 2022; Zhuang
082            et al., 2023; Hatamizadeh et al., 2021).
083         2. We evade Pitfall 2 by utilizing the state-of-the-art Residual Encoder U-Net CNN architec-
084            ture from Isensee et al. (2024) as the backbone. Moreover, we use this backbone for all
085            baseline SSL methods - allowing us to quantify the utility of the pre-training scheme for
086            pre-training CNNs.
087         3. We evade Pitfall 3 by employing 5 development and 7 testing datasets, spanning a diverse
088            set of downstream targets. This includes head and neck organs and pathologies, datasets
089            with novel image modalities not seen during pre-training, and datasets of the same pathol-
090            ogy acquired at different centers.
091
      By subsequently optimizing MAEs for CNNs based on performance on the development pool, we
092
      propose the novel sparse MAE inspired pre-training paradigm, unleashing the potential of huge
093   amounts of not annotated data in the medical domain. We show that the choice of the best fine-
094   tuning strategy is crucial. Moreover, we evaluate our method across a range of critical 3D medical
095   scenarios, including low-data regimes, accelerated fine-tuning schedules, and generalization across
096   unseen centers and modalities.

196   3   R EVISITING 3D MAE S
197
198   Masked autoencoders (MAEs) are a well-established pre-training paradigm in the natural imaging
199   domain and in the medical image segmentation domain for transformers. In this section, we in-
200   vestigate this paradigm and optimize it for 3D medical image segmentation using a ResEnc U-Net
201   architecture of Isensee et al. (2024).
202
203   Default parameters MAEs are trained by masking an input image to a certain degree and training
204
      the network to reconstruct the occluded regions, minimizing deviations betweem reconstruction and
      original image. In our experiments, we train the MAE with a L2-Loss in the z-score normalized
205
      voxel space and only calculate the reconstruction loss where regions were masked. Moreover, we
206
      do not remove skip-connections, following the general consensus of Woo et al. (2023), Tian et al.
207   (2023), and He et al. (2022). The default hyperparameters (as used by the model denoted in gray
208   in Table 1) are learning rate 1e-2, weight decay 3e-5, batch size 6, SGD optimizer with Nesterov
209   momentum 0.99, masking ratio of 75% trained with a PolyLR schedule for 250k steps (this represent
210   1000 epochs in the nnU-Net framework) and minor spatial augmentations of affine scaling, rotation
211   and mirroring.
212

214   in the same manner as transformers can. To address this, Tian et al. (2023) proposed to adapt the
215   CNN architectures to better fit the sparse inputs: (a) Sparse Convolutions and Normalization:
      Through the receptive field of convolutions masked-out regions are iteratively eroded from their

255   following evaluations are presented with these changes applied.

258   Masking strategy The masked region is determined by sampling randomly in the CNN’s bottle-
259   neck of shape [5x5x5] and up-sampling these regions to the input resolution, to ensure the masks
260   align in the bottleneck of the CNN architectures. This results in masking regions of [32x32x32]
261   voxels of non-overlapping regions in the input. As a sampling strategy, we follow random masking,
262   as previous work showed no benefit of structured masking for images nor videos (He et al., 2022;
263   Feichtenhofer et al., 2022). In the scope of the development phase we explore 5 static masking ratios
264   between 30 and 90%, and evaluate a dynamic masking ratio randomly masking between 60% and
265   90%.
266   Results are presented in Table Table 1b and highlight that the masking ratios of 60%, 75%, and the
267   dynamic masking ratio of 60% to 90% perform equally well. Due to the highly similar performance,
268   we choose to proceed with a dynamic masking ratio over the static masking ratio, due to expecting
269   this masking to be more difficult to learn and with the upcoming scaling experiment in mind. We
      refer to this model as Spark3D-Base (S3D-B= going forward.

314   Fine-tuning strategy Given a pre-trained model, a crucial question arises: Which weights to
315   transfer and how to schedule the fine-tuning? We investigated various different schedules. Regard-
316   ing weight transfer we investigated transferring (i) both the encoder and decoder , or (ii) only the
317   encoder       with a randomly initialized decoder. Regarding the fine-tuning schedule, we investigate
318   whether to use a learning rate warm-up of 12.5k steps, ramping the learning rate up to the maximum
319   LR. When only transferring the encoder, an additional warm-up of only the decoder is investigated
320   to adapt the randomly initialized decoder to the pre-trained encoder. In some configurations, this
321   results in two learning rate warm-ups of 12.5k steps each. Additionally, we investigate whether to
322   keep the encoder frozen      for the entire fine-tuning process, or to fine-tuning encoder weights as
323   well. Lastly, we investigate whether to decrease the peak learning rate to 1e − 3, 1e − 4 or to keep
      it at the default of 1e − 2.

346
      stages are essential: Not applying a warm-up step significantly reduces performance. Including a
      warm-up for both the encoder and decoder boosts accuracy by 0.6 to 1 DSC points. (ii) Learning
347

      SSL pre-training works Across all tested datasets, SSL pre-trained methods demonstrate im-

465   4.2   A BLATION EXPERIMENTS
466
      Low-Data Regime While previous experiments focused on comparing SSL pre-training against
467
      a from-scratch baseline with full-scale datasets available, many applications in the medical domain
468
      have access to only a very small amount of labeled images. To measure the benefits of pre-training
469   in such a low-data regime, we artificially reduced the total amount of data available for training to
470   10, 20, 30, or 40 labeled images.
471
472
      Results are presented in Table 4. It is evident that our pre-trained S3D-B model leads to better
      downstream performance compared to training from scratch in this setting. With just 40 trained
473
      images, the fine-tuned model nearly matches the performance of the from-scratch model trained on
474
      the full dataset. Future research could explore whether optimizing the training duration or learning
475   rate schedule could prevent the pre-trained network from overfitting during fine-tuning on such a
476   limited number of training images.
477
478
      Generalization Performance To assess the generalization capability of the proposed pre-training
479
      method, we tested two scenarios. First, we evaluated fine-tuning our method on an unseen modality
480   using the TOF Angiography Aneurysms dataset (D12). As shown in Table 7, without pretraining, the
481   fixed configuration suffers a performance drop of 20 Dice points. We attribute this to the significant
482   difference in median spacing for the downstream task ([0.50, 0.43, 0.43] mm), which has a higher
483   resolution than the fixed target spacing of [1, 1, 1] mm used in the pre-training experiments. This
484   lower resolution likely increases the difficulty of segmenting small aneurysms. Despite this decrease,
485   pre-training mitigates some of this degradation and proves highly beneficial compared to training the
      same configuration from scratch. Interestingly, ModelGenesis achieves the best results, potentially


                                                                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      due to its use of intensity augmentations during pre-training, which increases robustness against
487   brightness shifts, such as when generalizing to different MRI sequences. Second, we fine-tuned
488   on the D2 dataset using only the T1 contrast-enhanced (T1ce) sequence and applied these models
489   directly to D13 without any additional fine-tuning. While the dynamic configuration performed
490   best on the in-distribution validation cases of D2, the results on D13 indicate that MAE pre-training
491   improves generalization across different centers, with our S3D model yielding the best performance.
492
493   Pre-training Time Previous studies have demonstrated the positive impact of extended training
494   schedules on the quality of learned representations for downstream tasks (He et al., 2022; Feichten-
495   hofer et al., 2022). To explore the relevance of this factor in the 3D medical domain, we conducted
496   a similar experiment, evaluating training durations ranging from 62.5k to 1M steps. Our results
497   indicate that the benefits of longer training schedules begin to degrade after 250k steps, as evident
498
      in Table 5. This could explain why our scaled model did not achieve further performance improve-
      ments.
499
500
501
      Fine-tuning length Initializing from pre-trained weights has the potential to reduce the compu-
      tational resources needed for the network to adapt to new tasks. To assess this, we tested different
502
      pre-training durations on our development datasets. While maintaining 12.5k iterations for both
503
      the decoder and full network warm-up phases, we experimented with varying subsequent training
504   lengths. As shown in Table 8, adding just 12.5k additional iterations (37.5k total) already outper-
505   forms training from scratch. However, achieving optimal performance still requires completing the
506   full fine-tuning schedule.
507
508   Multi-Channel Input In many medical examinations, it is common to perform multiple scans, as
509   clinicians often require images with different characteristics for accurate decision-making. Conse-
510   quently, some datasets, like D2, D8, and D10, contain multiple input modalities. While pre-training
511   may involve all modalities, we only feed one modality at a time into the network since not all pa-
512   tients have scans in every modality. This raises the question of how to handle datasets with multiple
513   registered images. To address this, we conducted a 5-fold cross-validation on the D2 development
514
      dataset. We evaluated the replication of each input modality along with random initialization of the
      input stem weights. Additionally, we tested freezing the stem weights during the decoder warm-up
515
      phase. As shown in Table 6, the most stable and consistently effective approach was replicating the
516
      pre-trained stem and keeping it frozen during the decoder’s warm-up period.

519   5   C ONCLUSION
520
521   This work is the first to demonstrate the potential of properly configured MAEs in 3D medical im-
522   age segmentation. By overcoming key pitfalls in previous research, such as limited dataset sizes,
523   outdated architectures, and insufficient evaluation, we show a consistent performance improvement
524   over previous SSL methods. Notably, for the first time, we achieve consistent gains over the dy-
525
      namic, dataset-adaptive nnU-Net baseline, validated across a large and diverse set of development
      and testing datasets. While our findings are promising, several avenues remain open for future ex-
526
      ploration. Notably, increasing training time and batch size did not lead to performance gains, but
527
      the question remains of whether scaling the pre-training dataset size or the model parameters could
528   unlock new potential. Furthermore, the intensity shifts employed by ModelGenesis SSL task hint at
529   intriguing possibilities for improving generalization across unseen MRI modalities, which needs to
530   be further explored for MAEs. Lastly, a data-centric approach to curating the most relevant data for
531   SSL represents an exciting frontier for future research. Raw clinical datasets often contain images
532   not intended for diagnostic purposes, such as those used for scanner calibration, which can dilute
533   the effectiveness of pre-training. While we applied basic filtering to exclude low-quality data, more
534   sophisticated filtering techniques could significantly enhance the quality of the pre-training process.
535   This work follows the spirit of prior studies like nnU-Net Isensee et al. (2021), or XXX, showing
536   that a robust development strategy, informed model configuration, and rigorous validation lead to
537   true and sustainable performance gains, contrasting the current hype for employing and modifying
538   the latest network architecture. With our dynamic open-source framework, we hope to contribute
539   to a cultural shift in the community towards validation-driven development enabling true scientific
      progress.

      A     M ETHOD CONFIGURATION
703
704
      Across all baseline methods, we utilize a common set of hyperparameters. For all baseline methods
705
      we utilize the same pre-training dataset with the same image preprocessing. Moreover we use the
706   same amount of pre-training steps (250k) as for our S3D-B method and the same fine-tuning scheme,
707   as highlighted in Table 8. Aside from this, we employ the SGD optimizer with LR 1e-2 with a
708   PolyLR schedule, momentum 0.99 and weight decay 3e-5 across all pre-training experiments, as
709   they showed to be highly robust and reliable in the supervised medical image segmentation setting
710   using CNNs (Isensee et al., 2021). Moreover, we denote that all these methods have their backbones
711   replaced with a ResEnc U-Net to minimize confounding effects of different architectures.
712
713   A.1   M ODELS G ENESIS
714
715   Models genesis (Zhou et al., 2021) pre-text task is centered around restoring original patches from
716   transformed versions. The transformed version is achieved by applying four different transforma-
717   tions in various combinations, with the following transformations: a composition of four separate
718   pre-training schemes: (i) Non-linear intensity transformation: Alters the intensity distribution
719
      while preserving the anatomy, focusing on learning the appearance of organs. (ii) Out-painting:
      Removes part of the image and requires the model to extrapolate from the remaining image, forcing
720
      it to learn the global structure of the organs. (iii) In-painting: Masks a part of the image, and the
721
      model learns to restore the missing parts, focusing on local continuity and context. After having
722   transformed the original image through these augmentations, the model is trained to recover the
723   original image through a convolutional encoder-decoder architecture. This approach consolidates
724   different tasks (appearance, texture, and context learning) into one unified image restoration task,
725   making the model more robust and generalizable.

727   Model specific Hyperparameters: The entire set of hyperparameters of Models Genesis are con-

732   A.2   VOLUME F USION
733
734   Volume Fusion (Wang et al., 2023) is a pseudo-segmentation task using two sub-volumes from
735   different 3D scans, which are fused together based on random voxel-level fusion coefficients. The
736   fused image is treated as input, and the model predicts the fusion category of each voxel, mimicking
737   a segmentation task. Pretraining is optimized using a combination of Dice loss and cross-entropy
738
      loss.
739
740   Method specific parameters: Volume Fusion has unique parameters defining the size ranges of
741   the rectangles used for fusing together images. In our experiments we utilize a rectangle size range
742
      between [8, 100] sampled uniformly for each axis. This represent the 62.5% of our input patch size,
      and identical percentage as in the original paper. Moreover the amount of rectangles sampled is an
743
      important parameter. Like in the original paper we sample M Ũ(10, 40) different rectangles, itera-
744
      tively. Lastly, the number of categories was chosen to be 5, as in the original paper (this represent K
745   = 4).
