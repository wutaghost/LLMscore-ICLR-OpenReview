010
011            Event cameras, with a high dynamic range exceeding 120dB, significantly outper-
012            form traditional cameras, robustly recording detailed changing information under
013            various lighting conditions, including both low- and high-light situations. However,
014            recent research on utilizing event data has primarily focused on low-light image
015            enhancement, neglecting image enhancement and brightness adjustment across a
016
               broader range of lighting conditions, such as normal or high illumination. Based
               on this, we propose a novel research question: how to employ events to enhance
017
               and adjust the brightness of images captured under broader lighting conditions. To
018
               investigate this question, we first collected a new dataset, SEE-600K, consisting of
019            610,126 images and corresponding events across 202 scenarios, each featuring an
020            average of four lighting conditions with over a 1000-fold variation in illumination.
021            Subsequently, we propose a framework that effectively utilizes events to smoothly
022            adjust image brightness through the use of prompts. Our framework captures
023            color through sensor patterns, uses cross-attention to model events as a brightness
024            dictionary, and adjusts the image’s dynamic range to form a broader light-range
025            representation (BLR), which is then decoded at the pixel level based on the bright-
026            ness prompt. Experimental results demonstrate that our method not only performs
027
               well on the low-light enhancement dataset but also shows robust performance on
               broader light-range image enhancement using the SEE-600K dataset. Additionally,
028
               our approach enables pixel-level brightness adjustment, providing flexibility for
029
               post-processing and inspiring more imaging applications.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Every day, from daylight to nighttime, the illuminance varies from about 100,000 lux (bright sunlight)
035   to approximately 0.1 lux (starlight) (Koshel, 2012). Maintaining stable imaging under diverse natural
036   lighting conditions is a significant challenge. To achieve this, a series of influential works have
037   emerged, including automatic exposure (Bernacki, 2020), exposures correction (Yuan & Sun, 2012),
038   low-light enhancement (Li et al., 2021) and high dynamic range (HDR) imaging (McCann & Rizzi,
      2011). However, traditional cameras are limited by their imaging principle of synchronously capturing
039
      intensity values across the entire sensor, with a dynamic range of only 60 to 80 dB Hasinoff et al.
040
      (2016); Rebecq et al. (2019). Consequently, these traditional methods find it difficult to capture imaging
041
      information under a wide range of lighting conditions at the input (Gehrig & Scaramuzza, 2024; Gallego
042   et al., 2020). If the exposure is inaccurate - over and under exposures - traditional cameras lose the
043   potential to restore images under complex lighting conditions due to limited bits-width and noise.
044   Unlike traditional cameras, event cameras Gallego et al. (2020) asynchronously record pixel-level
045   changes in illumination, outputting the direction of intensity change (positive or negative) at each
046   pixel with extremely high dynamic range (120 dB), which far exceeds the capability of traditional
047   cameras in capturing various lighting intensity.
048   Research leveraging the events for image brightness enhancement can be divided into three categories.
049   (1) event-based image reconstruction, which aim to reconstruct images only from events. However,
050   these methods (Rebecq et al., 2019; Stoffregen et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024) rely solely on events, facing
051   uncertainties during reconstruction, and the events usually contain heavy noise, which leads to color
052   distortion and limited capabilities of generalization. (2) event-guided HDR imaging (Cui et al., 2024;
053   Yang et al., 2023; Messikommer et al., 2022) , which aims to employ events to extend the dynamic range
      of images or video to match human vision. However, synthesizing HDR images as ground truth

091   use events to enhance and adjust the brightness of images across a broader range of lighting
092   conditions becomes a more worthwhile research question.
093
      To address this novel research question, we first formulate the imaging model for brightness adjust-
094
      ment (Sec.3) and define the learning task. We aim to perceive lighting information from events,
095
      utilizing brightness prompts to convert this lighting information into images with a specific brightness.
096   In doing so, other image quality aspects (like sensor patterns, noise, color bias, and so on) are taken
097   into consideration.
098
099
      To realize our proposed task, we first collecte a new dataset by emulating each scene in different
      lighting conditions, covering a broader luminance range (Sec.4), as shown in Fig.1 (b) and (d). By
100
      capturing multiple lighting conditions per scene, we enable mappings across diverse illumination
101
      scenarios, providing rich data for model training. To tackle the challenges of spatio-temporal
102   alignment of video and event streams under various lighting conditions, we design a temporal
103   alignment strategy relying on programmable robotic arms and inertial measurement unit (IMU)
104   sensors. As a result, we obtain a temporal registration error up to one millisecond and a spatial
105   error at the sub-pixel level (∼ 0.3 pixel). Finally, we build a large-scale and well-aligned dataset
106   containing 202 scenes, each with 4 different lighting conditions, summing up to 610,126 images
107   and the corresponding event data. We term this dataset as SEE-600K, which supports learning the
      mappings among multiple lighting conditions.

      Building on the SEE-600K dataset, we propose a compact and efficient framework, SEE-Net, for the
109   proposed new tasks (Sec. 5). An event-aware cross-attention is used to enhance image brightness,
110   and the brightness-related prompt is introduced for controlling the overall brightness. This approach
111   effectively captures and adjusts lighting across a broader range of illumination conditions, providing
112   flexibility and precise control during inference. Despite of the advantage of performance, SEE-Net
113   still remains effective, compact, and lightweight with only 1.9 M parameters.
114
      Our method has been evaluated on two real-world datasets, SDE (Liang et al., 2024) and SEE-600K.
115   Quantitative results demonstrate that our framework fits well to a broader range of lighting conditions
116   (Sec. 6). Furthermore, our framework allows for smooth brightness adjustment, providing precise
117   exposure control. Therefore, this flexibility significantly improves post-processing capabilities and
118   enables potential applications in advanced imaging and processing tasks.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
122
123   Frame-based: These brightness enhancement methods aim to improve image quality under chal-
124
      lenging illumination conditions. Retinexformer (Cai et al., 2023) and other Retinex-based frame-
      works (Zhang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2023) decompose reflectance and illumination
125
      with complex training pipelines. Other approaches, e.g., structure-aware models (Xu et al., 2023b;
126
      Wang et al., 2023c), utilize edge detection or semantic-aware guidance to achieve sharper and more
127   realistic results. Exposure correction strategies (Afifi et al., 2021; Panetta et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2020)
128   target both overexposed and underexposed areas, leveraging multi-scale networks or perceptual
129   image enhancement frameworks to synthesize correctly exposed images. However, the reliance on
130   RGB frames with limited bit depth, limits the adaptability to dynamic lighting conditions, making
131   it difficult to handle a broader range of lighting scenarios. Event-based: These methods focus
132   on reconstructing images or videos exclusively from event data. For instance, Duwek et al. (2021)
133   introduced a two-phase neural network combining CNNs and SNNs, while Pan et al. (2019) proposed
134   the event-based double integral model to generate videos. Stoffregen et al. (2020) enhanced event-based
135
      video reconstruction by introducing the new dataset. Additionally, Liu & Dragotti (2023); Wang et al.
      (2024) developed a model-based deep network to improve reconstructed video quality. However, these
136
      event-based approaches face challenges due to event data noise, often leading to color distortion and
137
      limited generalization. Event-guided: These works are centered on enhancing images captured in
138   low-light conditions. E.g., Zhang et al. (2020) and Liu et al. (2024) recovered lost details in low-light
139   environments by reconstructing grayscale images. Similarly, Liang et al. (2023) and Liu et al. (2023)
140   improved low-light video enhancement by leveraging motion information from events to enhance
141   multi-frame videos and integrating spatiotemporal coherence. Furthermore, Jin et al. (2023) and Jiang
142   et al. (2023) utilized events to recover structural details and reconstruct clear images under near-dark
143   situations. Most notably, Liang et al. (2024) introduced the first large-scale event-guided low-light
144   enhancement dataset, which is significant for the development of this field. While these methods
145   use events for brightness changes and structural recovery in low-light conditions, they are limited to
146
      enhance low-light images with single mapping and cannot handle brightness adjustments across a
      broader range of lighting conditions, including normal- and high-light.

149   3    P RELIMINARIES AND N EW TASK D EFINITION
150
151   In this section, we formalize the physical model underlying our approach to enhance and adjust image
152   brightness across a broader range of lighting conditions using events. Imaging is fundamentally the
153   process of capturing the radiance of a scene, represented as a radiance field L(t) varying over a preset
154   slot t. The illuminates of light in daily life span a vast range, from 0.1 lux (starlight) to 1e6 lux (direct
155   sunlight). The goal of brightness adjustment is to recover or estimate L(t) and tone-map it into an
156   image that is visually suitable for human perception.
157   Traditional cameras record light signals through exposure (Mendis et al., 1997). This voltage is
158   influenced by the Gaussian noise N = N (µ, σ 2 ) (µ is the mean and σ 2 is the variance), and the
159   photon shot noise P = P(k), where k ∝ L(t) is the number of photons, proportional to light
160   intensity. In low-light conditions, Gaussian noise dominates, while in high-light conditions, photon
161   shot noise becomes more significant. These noises influence the final value in the RAW image, simply
      represented as Iraw ≈ Q(L(t) + P + N ), where Q is the quantization function that converts the

193   p ∈ {+1, −1} (polarity, indicating light intensity increase or decrease). Events are triggered when the

199   image that is optimally exposed for human observation. Our task setting can thus be formulated as

250   the source sequence S and target sequence T by finding the optimal bias b and matching length l

252   exhaustive search for the optimal bias is computationally infeasible. To address this, we introduce

254   Roumeliotis, 2008). Then, the average pooling is utilized to reduce the sequences to two additional
255
      levels, Level-1 (S1 , T1 ) and Level-2 (S2 , T2 ), as shown in Fig. 3 (a)-⃝

      complexity while preserving essential alignment features. The window size is chosen based on our
256

257
      bias b and matching length l at the lowest resolution (Level-2). The results from this level serve as
258   center points for finer searches at higher resolutions. Specifically, the bias and length identified at
259   each level guide local searches at the next level up, as displayed in Fig. 3 (a)-⃝      3⃝4⃝5 . At Level-1

      Encoding: In this stage, we aim to obtain the BLR by employing the event feature Fe to enhance the
325   image feature Fi , facilitating noise reduction and the acquisition of broader light range information.
326   Since Fe contains rich information about the lighting changes across different intensity levels, we
327   use it as the source for representing the broader light range. However, event data only records
328   changes in illumination, which differ fundamentally from the static RGB frame modality. This makes
329   directly utilizing event data for broader light representation challenging. To address this, we employ
330   a cross-attention (Liang et al., 2021) for feature fusion, producing the initial fused broad-spectrum
331   feature F1 , expressed as F1 = fc0 (Fe , Fi ), where fc0 is a cross-attention block. Then, inspired by
332   previous works (Wang et al., 2020), we utilize sparse learning to generate residuals for F1 from the
333
      event features Fe . These residuals are progressively generated from the loop that executes L times.
      Multiple iterations are used because they allow the model to iteratively refine the residuals, capturing
334
      finer details and enhancing the feature representations by progressively integrating information from
335
      the events. A single loop of this process can be expressed as, Fj+1 = fl (Fe , F1 , Fj ), where fl is a
336   loop function that contains two cross-attention blocks as shown in Fig. 4 (b), where Fj and Fj+1
337   are the input and output of one loop. After L iterations, the final feature FL represents the BLR, as
338   described by Eq. 4.
339
                         FL = fse (Fe , F1 ) = fl (Fe , F1 , fl (Fe , F1 , ...fl (Fe , F1 , F1 ))).        (4)
340
341   Decoding: The objective of this part is to decode the BLR into a brightness-adjustable image Io . In
342
      designing this decoder, we focus on two key insights: (1) The decoding process should be pixel-wise
      and efficient, allowing for greater flexibility during model deployment; (2) The embedding of the
343
      brightness information should be thorough and fully integrated. With these insights, we design
344
      the decoder with only a 5-layer MLP as shown in Fig 4 (c). Our decoder begins by encoding the
345   brightness prompt B ∈ (0, 1) into an embedding vector. To effectively encode the high-frequency
346   brightness prompt into features that are easier for the network to learn (Vaswani, 2017), we introduce
347   a learnable embedding, denoted as B = fpe (B) = fmlp (fcat (fmlp (B), B)), which consists of two
348   MLP layers. Through this embedding, the brightness prompt B is transformed into a vector B,
349   matching the dimensions of the BLR channels. We then integrate this embedding B into the decoder.
350   To ensure the brightness prompt is fully incorporated and prevent information loss through multiple
351   MLP layers, we employ a multi-step embedding approach, as displayed in Eq. 5, which guarantees
352   that the brightness is progressively embedded throughout the decoding process. During the training
353
      phase, the prompt B is derived from the reference image by applying fb to calculate the global
      average brightness. In contrast, during the testing phase, B can be set freely, with a typical example
354
      being a value of 0.5.
355
356                      Io = fd (FL , B) = fmlp (B + fmlp (B + ...fmlp (B + FL ))).                       (5)
357
      Loss Function: The purpose of our loss function is to supervise the prediction Io using the ground
358
      truth It , with the corresponding brightness B = fb (It ). The loss function consists of two main
359   components: image reconstruction loss Li and gradient loss Lg . The image reconstruction loss is
360   Charbonnier loss (Lai et al., 2018), which effectively handles both small and large errors. Additionally,
361   we employ gradient loss to improve the structural consistency of the output image. This is achieved
362   by enforcing L1 constraints on the gradients of both the output and ground truth images. Therefore,
363   the overall loss function is formulated as a weighted sum of the image loss and gradient loss, as
364   exhibited in Eq. 6. Here, ∇ denotes the gradient operator, and λ1 and λ2 are the weights that balance
365   the contributions of two loss terms.

531
      In this paper, we proposed a new research problem: how to use events to adjust the brightness of
532   images across a wide range of lighting conditions, from low light to high light. To address this
533   challenge, we made the following contributions. (1), we developed a physical model and formally
534   defined the problem of brightness adjustment using events, providing a solid theoretical foundation.
535   (2), we introduced a new spatiotemporal registration algorithm based on a robotic arm and collected
536   a large-scale dataset, SEE-600K, to overcome alignment issues and support our research. (3), we
537   presented SEE-Net, a novel and compact framework capable of accepting input images with a wide
538   range of illumination and producing output images with adjustable brightness. (4), we conducted
539   extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

785   A    M ORE D ETAILS A BOUT THE DVS346 S ENSOR
786
787   In our experiments, we employed the DVS346 event camera, a sensor capable of simultaneously
788   outputting asynchronous events and synchronous image frames (APS frames). Despite its widespread
789   use in the academic community, the DVS346 has inherent limitations that affect the quality of the
790   captured images, particularly due to various noise factors. Understanding these parameters is crucial
791   for contextualizing the performance of our proposed methods.
792   The specifications of the DVS346 sensor are detailed in Tables 4 and 5. Below, we explain the
793   significance of each parameter, emphasizing those related to noise, to illustrate the image quality
794   from this sensor.
795
      Events: Spatial resolution: refers to the number of pixels in the sensor array, which in this case
796
      is 346 × 260 pixels. Temporal resolution: of 1, µs indicates the sensor’s ability to detect rapid
797
      changes in brightness, allowing for precise temporal event detection. This high temporal resolution
798   is advantageous for capturing fast-moving scenes. Maximum throughput: of 12 MEPS means the
799   sensor can handle up to 12 million events per second, which is essential for recording scenes with a lot
800   of motion without losing data. Typical latency: of less than 1, ms ensures minimal delay between the
801   occurrence of an event and its registration by the sensor, which is important for real-time applications.
802   Dynamic range: of approximately 120, dB allows the event sensor to operate effectively under a
803   wide range of lighting conditions, from very dark to very bright environments. This high dynamic
804   range is a key advantage of event-based cameras. Contrast sensitivity: represents the minimum
805   percentage change in brightness required to generate an event. The sensor has a contrast sensitivity
806
      of 14.3% for ON events and 22.5% for OFF events. While higher contrast sensitivity reduces noise
      by preventing the sensor from triggering on minor fluctuations, it may also cause it to miss subtle
807
      changes in brightness.
808
809   Frame: Spatial resolution: for the APS frames is the same as the event output, limiting the detail in
      the captured images. Frame rate: of 40, FPS indicates that the sensor captures 40 frames per second.

       Imaging is the process of capturing light from a scene, which can be represented as a radiance field
