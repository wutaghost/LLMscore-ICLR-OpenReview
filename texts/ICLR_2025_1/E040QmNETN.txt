009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating music that aligns with the visual content of a video has been a chal-
012            lenging task, as it requires a deep understanding of visual semantics and involves
013            generating music whose melody, rhythm, and dynamics harmonize with the visual
014            narratives. This paper presents MuVi, a novel framework that effectively addresses
015            these challenges to enhance the cohesion and immersive experience of audio-visual
016            content. MuVi analyzes video content through a specially designed visual adaptor
017            to extract contextually and temporally relevant features. These features are used
018            to generate music that not only matches the video’s mood and theme but also
019
               its rhythm and pacing. We also introduce a contrastive music-visual pre-training
               scheme to ensure synchronization, based on the periodicity nature of music phrases.
020
               In addition, we demonstrate that our flow-matching-based music generator has in-
021
               context learning ability, allowing us to control the style and genre of the generated
022            music. Experimental results show that MuVi demonstrates superior performance
023            in both audio quality and temporal synchronization. The generated music video
024            samples are available at muvi-v2m.github.io.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The development of multimedia social platforms and AI-generated content (AIGC) in recent years
030   has fundamentally changed the way people engage with video content. Music, an essential element
031   of videos, has spurred considerable interest in video-to-music generation technology. The video-
032   to-music (V2M) task focuses on generating matching music based on the visual content of video,
033   offering immense potential in fields such as advertising and video content creation.
034
      The generated music for the video should exhibit two key qualities: semantic alignment and rhythmic
035
      synchronization. Semantic alignment ensures that the generated music captures the emotional and
036   thematic essence of the video content, while rhythmic synchronization ensures that the music’s tempo
037   and rhythm are in harmony with the visual dynamics. Achieving both is crucial for creating a cohesive
038   audio-visual experience.
039
      Previous V2M methods (Zhuo et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c) primarily focus on
040
      generating music that aligns with the global features (theme, emotion, style, etc.) of the entire
041
      video clip. Other methods focus only on semantic alignment (Tian et al., 2024) or on rhythmic
042   synchronization (Su et al., 2024). However, when video shifts from one theme or emotion into
043   another, the generated music should adapt accordingly in both terms of semantic and rhythm. The
044   most relevant research on generating synchronized music in accordance with video dynamics can be
045   found in studies that endeavor to create music based on human movement within dance videos(Zhu
046   et al., 2022; Era et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b). However, the topic is limited to dancing, whose visual
047   semantics are relatively simple to capture. Research on generating music that seamlessly adapts to
048   the content and style of videos in general are still absent.
049   In this paper we aim to tackle these long-standing challenges of video-to-music generation:
050
051          • Semantic alignment. It is anticipated that the style, melody, and emotions of the music will
052            evolve in harmony with the video content. Consider this scenario: Tom from Tom and Jerry
053            (Hanna & Barbera, 1940) is peacefully sleeping under a tree when suddenly an apple falls
               on his head. Initially, the music may have a soothing tone, but within just a few seconds,

054
               the mood of the music immediately becomes intense due to the sudden event of the apple
055            falling, and the music instruments may switch to the ones with more intense timbre.
056
057          • Rhythm synchronization. In common, music typically has a relatively stable rhythm, which
058            tends to remain constant in a musical section. However, in video soundtracks, the rhythm
059            often changes instantaneously in response to the dynamics of video content, especially to
060            the motion of the characters. The music’s beat is often synchronized with the movements of
061            characters. Consider the previous scenario, when Tom is sleeping, the music’s beat should
062            synchronize with the pace of snoring; and the moment the apple hits his head, a drumbeat
063            occurs and the rhythm suddenly becomes clipped and harsh. This requires precise frame-
064
               level synchronization between video and music. Relying only on global understanding of
               the video may lead to audio-visual asynchrony.
065
066
067
      It is also worth mentioning that, unlike regular music, video soundtracks often contain special sound
      effects mimicked by musical instruments. Still considering the previous scenario, the moment the
068
      apple hits Tom’s head might be accompanied by a loud crash cymbal or snare drum to simulate the
069
      impact sound. Moreover, the objects appearing in the video might not be directly related to generated
070   music or sound. For example, when Jerry hits Tom with a violin, it doesn’t necessarily imply violin
071   music. This renders some traditional video-sound or video-music datasets ineffective, as they are
072   constructed based on the pairing of the sound-producing object and the sound produced.
073
      To achieve precise music-visual synchronization, we need a high-frame-rate method for video
074
      semantic extraction that captures feature sequences with a small time span. Simultaneously, this video
075
      feature sequence should contain guiding information about the accurate positions of music beats. A
076   natural idea is to leverage contrastive learning to model synchronization, like Diff-Foley (Luo et al.,
077   2024). However, unlike contrastive audio-visual learning in Diff-Foley, music has a higher temporal
078   density and a more complex spectrum. Constructing negative samples by mismatched music-video
079   pairs might lead to inefficient learning, as the model could take shortcuts and overfit to more easily
080   identified features.
081
      This paper presents MuVi, a novel V2M method that generates music soundtracks for videos. The
082   main model features a simple non-autoregressive encoder-decoder architecture, where the encoder is
083   one of the open-source pre-trained visual encoders, and the decoder is adapted from a pre-trained flow-
084   matching based music generator (Lipman et al., 2022). We propose a visual adaptor to connect the
085   visual encoder and the music generator, which efficiently compresses high-frame-rate visual features
086   to provide sufficient temporal resolution for accurate synchronization. For rhythmic synchronization,
087   we apply contrastive pre-training on the adaptor at first to enhance its perception of rhythmic motion
088   and penalize asynchronous video-music pairings. In addition to using mismatched music and video
089   pairs, we employ two strategies to construct negative samples: 1) Temporal shifting, where we
090
      randomly shift the original music along the timeline to create asynchrony; 2) Random replacement,
      where we replace a randomly selected segment by other music segment or silence. It is worth
091
      mentioning that the pre-trained visual adaptor and audio encoder also serve as an evaluation metric
092
      for synchronization.
093
094   Experimental results show that the proposed method demonstrates superior performance over base-
095   lines in terms of semantic alignment and rhythmic synchronization. We investigate the impact of
096
      various visual compression strategies on the downstream music generation task. We also demonstrate
      the potential of in-context learning capability and scalability.
097
098   The main contributions of this work are:
099
100          • We propose a video-to-music framework that effectively generates music soundtracks with
101            both semantic alignment and rhythmic synchronization — a challenge that has not been
102            thoroughly explored before.
103
104          • We introduce a visual adaptor that efficiently compresses visual information and ensures
105            length alignment for the non-autoregressive music generator.
106
107          • We design a contrastive music-visual pre-training scheme that emphasizes the periodic
               nature of beats, forcing the model to focus on recognizing rhythmic asynchrony.

      2   BACKGROUND
109
110
      Video-to-music Generation Existing V2M methods can be categorized into several classes. 1) Ear-
111   lier methods (Koepke et al., 2020; Gan et al., 2020; Su et al., 2020a;b) mostly focus on reconstructing
112   the sound of instruments for silent instrumental performance videos, which is essentially more of
113   a video-to-audio task; 2) Some works (Yu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2022; Aggarwal & Parikh, 2021;
114   Lee et al., 2019) focus on generating music to match human motions (such as dance videos). These
115   methods are restricted to only a few kinds of body motions such as dancing and figure skating, which
116   are originally designed to match the tempo and style of the chosen music. They do not generalize well
117   to any human movements or any video; 3) Recent methods such as (Di et al., 2021; Zhuo et al., 2023;
118   Kang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c) focus on understanding general videos and generating symbolic
119
      music, where CMT (Di et al., 2021) generates chords and notes with rule-based video-music relation-
      ships, and SymMV (Zhuo et al., 2023) pays attention to semantic-level correspondence. However,
120
      symbolic music generation often requires costly annotated music score or MIDI data and thus cannot
121
      be scaled efficiently; 4) More recent works try to directly generate music waveforms from video
122   without the need for symbolic data – VidMuse (Tian et al., 2024) models the video-music correlation
123   using a long-short-term visual module, and V2Meow (Su et al., 2024) utilizes an autoregressive
124   Transformer (Vaswani, 2017) to generate acoustic music tokens. M2 UGen (Hussain et al., 2023)
125   leverages multiple multi-modal encoders and the power of large language models (LLM). However,
126   these methods primarily model global semantic and emotional features of the entire video clip and
127   lack the ability to effectively capture the sudden change in themes and emotions. To the best of
128   our knowledge, generating music video soundtracks with both semantic alignment and rhythmic
129   synchronization has not yet been thoroughly studied.
130
131
      Video-to-audio Generation Although we mainly focus on V2M, music generation and video-to-
      audio (V2A) methods share many similarities. One of the most important aspects that both V2M
132
      and V2A emphasize is the audio-visual synchronization. Some methods leverage GAN for audio
133
      generation, with specially designed hand-crafted visual and motion features to encode video content
134   (Chen et al., 2020; Ghose & Prevost, 2022; Iashin & Rahtu, 2021). Im2Wav (Sheffer & Adi, 2023)
135   adopts contrastively pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) visual encoder to extract semantic
136   content to condition the music generation, while Diff-foley (Luo et al., 2024) proposes a novel visual
137   feature obtained through contrastive audio-visual pre-training. Frieren (Wang et al., 2024) utilizes an
138   ODE-based generator and direct audio-visual fusion to improve sound quality and synchronization,
139   while FoleyCraft (Zhang et al., 2024) introduces sound event annotations and temporal supervision to
140   further enhance alignment. However, the current audio-visual contrastive learning based on a simple
141   mismatch-construction mechanism may not be suitable for music modality, where the complexity,
142
      both in the frequency and time domains, far exceeds that of audios studied in V2A tasks. Additionally,
      manual annotation of sound events may not be feasible for music.
143
144
      Music Generation Our work is essentially a conditional music generation method. There are plenty
145
      of methods (Dhariwal et al., 2020; Agostinelli et al., 2023; Schneider et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023;
146
      Forsgren & Martiros, 2022; Maina, 2023; Copet et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024; Li
147   et al., 2024a) focusing on generating acoustic music soundtracks, conditionally or unconditionally.
148   MusicLM (Agostinelli et al., 2023) and MusicGen (Copet et al., 2024) model the music representations
149   using language models (LM) with an autoregressive process. Music diffusion models (Huang et al.,
150   2023; Schneider et al., 2023; Maina, 2023) utilize denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al.,
151   2020) or latent diffusion strategy (Rombach et al., 2022) to improve audio quality and diversity, while
152   MelodyFlow (Lan et al., 2024) leverages the ODE-based flow-matching mechanism. Since our focus
153   is not on the music generator, we adopt a prevailing flow-matching-based architecture (Lipman et al.,
154   2022), which is used in many mainstream music generation frameworks.

193   3.1      V ISUAL R EPRESENTATION M ODELING
194
195
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), as the representative of the contrastive text-visual learning family,
      can generate image features rich in semantic space, and their visual transformer (ViT) (Alexey,
196
      2020) encoders come with a CLS token to capture global information. VideoMAE (Tong et al.,
197
      2022) and VideoMAE V2 Wang et al. (2023) focus on self-supervised pre-training for a single
198   video modality. As encoders, they are more unbiased compared to methods trained with semantic
199   supervision and maintain an understanding of the temporal dimension. There are also audio-visual
200   joint representations such as CAVP Luo et al. (2024), with lower frames per second (FPS).
201
      Since we use a non-autoregressive generator, we need to compress the video features to align with the
202
      frame length of the audio features. And it is of our interest to ensure that the compressed feature carry
203
      as much visual and semantic information to guide music generation as possible. Given a patchified
204   video representation sequence x ∈ RN ×L×C , where N , L, C are the number of frames, the number
205   of patches (where L = H × W ), and the channel dimension, the resulting feature, x̄ ∈ RN ×C , should
206   achieve a compression ratio of L times, representing each frame by a single vector. We compare
207   three major compression methods, which are illustrated on the left part of Figure 1.
208
209
      Gated Aggregation The gated aggregation method is essentially a dynamic weighting strategy.
210   We use a shallow projection as the gating layer g(·) (typically a single-layer MLP or CNN) to
211   transform x into a global weight map for each frame: wi = σ(g(xi )), where xi is the ith frame of
212   the video feature (i ∈ {1, ..., N }), wi ∈ RL×1 , and σ(·) is a non-linear function that ensures wi is
213   between 0 andP 1. If σ(·) is the Sigmoid function, then the aggregated feature can be computed as
214   x̄i = wi⊤ xi /( wi ). If σ(·) is Softmax, then x̄i = wi⊤ xi . Gated aggregation is useful for ViTs
215   without the CLS token, such as VideoMAE and VideoMAE V2, where the Softmax aggregation
      provides the capability to model global distributions.

      3.3      F LOW- MATCHING BASED M USIC G ENERATION
291
292   Flow-matching (Lipman et al., 2022) models the velocity field of transport probability path from
293   a noise distribution z0 ∼ π0 to a target data distribution z1 ∼ π1 , which is further modeled as
294   a time-dependent changing process of probability density (a.k.a. flow), determined by the ODE
295   dz = u(zt |c)dt, t ∈ [0, 1], where t is the time position, zt is a point on the trajectory at time t, c is
296   the condition, and u is the velocity (or the drift force). Our goal is to learn a velocity field v(zt |c; θ)
297   that approximates u. Following Rectified Flow-matching (RFM) (Liu, 2022; Liu et al., 2022), we
298   implement the target velocity field by linear interpolation between z0 and z1 , leading to the RFM
299
      objective:                                     Z         1

301                                                        0
302
      In our case, c is the visual representation, and z is the target music representation. Following
303
      the latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022) and diffusion Transformers (DiT) (Peebles &
304   Xie, 2023), we utilize a 1D variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013) to compress the music
305   Mel-spectrogram into a latent representation z ∈ RN ×C , reducing the computational burden of the
306   generator, which is a DiT composed of a feed-forward transformer (FFT) and some auxiliary layers.
307   The architecture of the DiT is shown in Figure 1.
308
      We utilize a pre-trained unconditional DiT generator to leverage its inherent generative capabilities.
309
      The unconditional DiT shares the exact architecture, except that a learnable embedding ∅ ∈ RC
310   is used to replace the visual condition c. We repeat ∅ for N times along the time axis to achieve
311   this replacement. During the conditional training, the condition c, the sampled point zt , and the
312   time t are transformed into the same dimension and summed element-wise. We believe that this
313   element-wise summation (or channel-wise fusion) is crucial, as it implies point-to-point precise
314   alignment. The fused representation is fed into the FFT blocks to estimate the velocity field. It is
315   worth mentioning that the unconditional condition ∅ is also used to implement the generation with
316   classifier-free guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022).

321
322   Data To train the unconditional music generator, the VAE, and the vocoder, we collect a music-only
323   dataset. We use about 50K tracks of the train split of MTG-Jamendo Dataset (Bogdanov et al.,
      2019), combined with 33.7K music tracks from the internet, resulting in a total of 5.3K hours of


                                                                   6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      music. We randomly segment the long tracks with a minimum and maximum duration of 4 and
325   30 seconds, resulting in around 1.4M music clips. We set aside about 1 hour each for test and
326   validation. For contrastive music-visual learning and video-to-music generation, we collect 1.6K
327   videos with semantically and rhythmically synchronized music soundtracks, resulting in around 280
328   hours after preprocessing. Instead of segmenting the videos, we randomly sample video segments
329   with a minimum and maximum duration of 4 and 30 seconds within each video file during the training
330   stage. We set aside 1 hour each for test and validation. The vocals of all the music tracks mentioned
331   above are removed using a music source separation tool (Anjok07 & aufr33, 2020). More details are
332   listed in Appendix D.
333   Implementation The sample rate of waveforms is 24 kHz, while the stride and number of frequency
334   bins of the Mel-spectrograms are 300 and 160. Therefore, the VAE encoder compresses a one-second
335   music clip into 10 frames with 8 channels. To provide a substantial amount of visual information, we
336   sample the video at a rate of 10 FPS (which is further discussed in Appendix F). The DiT consists of
337   12 layers, a hidden dimension of 1024, and 16 heads. RoPE positional encoding (Su et al., 2023) and
338   RMS normalization (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) are also implemented. The amount of parameters of
339   the DiT reaches 150M. We pre-train the DiT with the unconditional music dataset for 160K steps
340   with a batch size of 1.2M frames. We perform contrastive training with the video-music dataset for
341
      2M steps with a batch size of 160 samples, where the learnable temperature parameter is initialized
      as 0.07, following (Radford et al., 2021). The DiT is then trained for 60K steps with a batch size of
342
      32. All the training is conducted using 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs and the AdamW optimizer with a
343
      learning rate of 5e-5. More details are listed in Appendix A.
344
345   Evaluation For objective evaluation, we compute Frechet audio distance (FAD), Kullback–Leibler
346   divergence (KL), inception score (IS), Frechet distance (FD), following previous music generation
347   methods (Copet et al., 2024). To evaluate beat synchronization, we follow (Zhu et al., 2022) and
348
      compute Beats Coverage Score (BCS) and Beats Hit Score (BHS), where the former measures the
      ratio of overall generated beats to the total musical beats, and the latter measures the ratio of aligned
349
      beats to the total musical beats. We utilize a dynamic beat tracking algorithm (Ellis, 2007) to obtain a
350
      time-varying tempo sequence for evaluation, where the sliding window size is 8 seconds, the stride is
351   512 frames, and the BPM range is from 30 to 240. Also, for semantic synchronization, we use the
352   SIM measure derived from the contrastively pre-trained encoders as a reference-free metric, where the
353   visual encoder is selected as VideoMAE V2(base-patch16) combined with Softmax aggregation, and
354   this selection is discussed in details in Section 4.2. The results of BCS, BHS, and SIM are measured
355   on a percentage scale. We run inference on each test sample for 5 times and compute the average
356   results. For subjective evaluation, we conduct crowd-sourced human evaluations with 1-5 Likert
357   scales and report mean-opinion-scores (MOS-Q) audio quality and content alignment (MOS-A) with
358   95% confidence intervals. The raters are required to focus more on fine-grained aligment (such as
359
      emotion transition) in the MOS-A test. More details of the evaluation are listed in Appendix E.
360   Baselines We choose M2 UGen (Hussain et al., 2023) as a strong available baseline to compare.
361   M2 UGen leverages an LLM (Touvron et al., 2023b) to comprehend the input information extracted
362   from multiple multi-modal encoders (including video (Arnab et al., 2021)) and generate content-
363   correlated music soundtracks. VidMuse (Tian et al., 2024) and V2Meow (Su et al., 2024) are also
364   good baselines, but they have not open-sourced their training or inference code. Other works, such as
365   CMT (Di et al., 2021) and D2M-GAN (Zhu et al., 2022) are not considered for comparison because
366
      their scope of application differs from ours (symbolic music generation or dance-to-music), which
      could result in an unfair comparison. To provide a more comprehensive perspective, we construct
367
      an additional weak baseline for comparison: MuVi(beta), where we use CLIP-ViT(base-patch16)
368
      and the attention pooling adaptor as the visual encoder, and the whole model (adaptor and generator)
369   is trained from scratch (without any pre-training) with the video dataset. For the proposed model,
370   we use VideoMAE V2(base-patch16) and Softmax aggregation as the visual encoder for most of the
371   comparison (this selection is discussed in Section 4.2).

476   music-visual pre-training strategy by comparing different pre-training settings. We evaluate the
477   performances when dropping the contrastive pre-training phase, conducting only the basic contrastive

479   from which we can observe the performance decline when removing contrastive pre-training proce-
480   dures. Interestingly, introducing contrastive pre-training degrades the performance in terms of FAD.
481   However, there may be a reasonable explanation for this when viewed globally: the KL divergence is
482   relatively high when dropping the contrastive learning (or applying the basic one), indicating a lower
483
      diversity in generation. Lower diversity does not necessarily mean a higher FAD; sometimes, it may
      also cause the FAD to decrease. We believe that the absence of contrastive learning, or merely a basic
484
      version, might lead to a certain degree of overfitting, thereby reducing the diversity of the generated
485
      music. Introducing more types of negative samples implicitly creates an information bottleneck,
      causing the model to focus on more general features related to alignment and synchronization.

      5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduced MuVi, a novel V2M method that generates music soundtracks with
533
      both semantic alignment and rhythmic synchronization. We leveraged a simple non-autoregressive
534   ODE-based music generator, combined with an efficient visual adaptor that compressed visual
535   information and ensured length alignment. An innovative contrastive music-visual pre-training
536   scheme was constructed to emphasize temporal synchronization by addressing the periodic nature
537   of beats. Experimental results revealed that the proposed method achieved satisfactory results in
538   the V2M task, and we have investigated the effectiveness of different designs. For future work, we
539   will explore controllable V2M methods with textual prompts, which generate music with styles or
      emotions aligned with the textual descriptions. Further discussions are provided in the appendix.

771   A    A RCHITECTURE AND I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
772
773
      The main model, i.e., the velocity field estimator, is implemented as a multi-layer feed-forward
      Transformer. The detailed sizes and the parameter count are listed in Table 6. Following LLaMA
774
      (Touvron et al., 2023a), we adopt RoPE positional encoding (Su et al., 2023) and RMS Normalization
775
      layers (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) in the DiT. We utilize the Flash attention technique (Dao et al.,
776   2022) to save memory and accelerate computation. During the training stage, we apply the mixed
777   precision training strategy. Specifically, in all places with residual connections and normalization
778   calculations, we convert the data to fp32, while in other cases, we convert it to bf16.
779
      As for the 1D VAE, we adapt the VAE configuration from Rombach et al. (2022) and build a 1D
780
      version. Specifically, we use 1D convolution layers instead of 2D and drop the attention layers to
781
      support arbitrary-length input. The channel dimension is 256 with channel multipliers [1, 2, 4, 8],
782   while the latent dimension is set to 8. Therefore, the VAE model compresses 1-second audio into 10
783   latent frames. We train the VAE model with a batch size of 640K steps for 350K steps.
784
785
      As for the vocoder, we utilize a HiFi-GAN (Kong et al., 2020a) generator with 4 upsample layers,
      where the upsample rates are [5, 5, 4, 3], resulting in the stride of 300. The upsample kernel sizes
786
      are [16, 8, 8, 4], and the initial channel dimension for upsampling is 1200. We train the vocoder
787
      with a batch size of 102.4K frames for 520K steps. The multi-period discriminator (MPD) and the
788   multi-scale discriminator (MSD) are adopted to improve generation quality.
789
790   As for the visual adaptor, we use a single 2D convolution layer with a stride of 2×2 to downsample the
                                                                                               ′   ′
791   output features from visual encoders. Specifically, if the encoder output is z ∈ RN ×(H ×W )×C (will
                                                                                         ′       ′
792   be explained in Appendix C), the numbers of patches along the two directions, H and W , should be
793
      even numbered. For example, if we use VideoMAE as the visual encoder, then H ′ = 224/16 = 14.
                                                                                                 H′  W′
794   Therefore, the 2D convolution layer will further downsample the feature to z ∈ RN ×( 2 × 2 )×C .
795   After this downsampling, we then apply various compression strategies, followed by a linear layer for
796   output:
797          • Gated Aggregation. We utilize a single-layer convolution layer and a nonlinear function to
798            obtain the dynamic weights. The convolution layer has a kernel size of 3 and 4 filters, so the
799            output dimension is 4, and 4 groups of weights are generated. We then compute 4 weighted
800            averages on the downsampled features with the 4 groups of weights before computing the
801            average of groups. If the nonlinear function is Sigmoid, the average of groups will be further
802            divided by the average of weights to ensure that the sum of all weights within one group is 1.
803          • Attention Pooling. We utilize three linear layers to transform the downsampled features to
804            query, key, and value. When computing the attention weights, we apply multi-head attention
805            (Vaswani, 2017) by splitting the query and key vectors into 4 segments, and compute the
806            attention scores within each segmented group. The values are also segmented into heads,
807            which are eventually concatenated along the channel dimension before the output layer.
808          • Vanilla Pooling. For average pooling, we simply compute the average of the downsam-
809            pled visual features before the output layer. For CLS pooling, we directly feed the CLS
               embeddings into the output layer.

823
      Music Representation Given the Mel-spectrogram of a music clip, we utilize an 1-D VAE encoder
824
      to compress it into a latent representation z ∈ RN ×C , with a temporal compression ratio of 8 and
825   a channel compression ratio of 20. A KL penalty 0.01 is implemented (Rombach et al., 2022). A
826   HiFi-GAN vocoder (Kong et al., 2020a) is utilized to recover the decoded spectrograms to waveforms.
827
828   Video Representation We follow the corresponding pre-trained visual encoder to preprocess the
829   videos. We preserve the temporal dimension of the hidden states of encoder outputs to support
830   fine-grained alignment. More details of the inference procedure of the pre-trained encoders can be
831   found in Appendix C.
832
833   Training Procedure The training procedure of the main model is divided into two stages: 1) Given
834   the pre-trained visual encoder, we pre-train the visual adaptor with the auxiliary audio encoder with
835   the contrastive objective LT , where only the visual adaptor and the head of the audio encoder are
836   learnable; 2) Given the unconditionally pre-trained DiT with the flow-matching objective LRFM , we
837   further train it with the V2M task where the visual adaptor and the DiT are learnable. During training,
838
      we randomly replace the visual condition c with the unconditional condition ∅ with a probability of
      0.2 to enable CFG in inference.
839
840
      Inference Procedure The input video is preprocessed according to the selected visual encoder, and
841
      then compressed into the visual condition with the cascaded visual encoder and adaptor. We adopt
842
      the Euler sampler with a fixed step size to solve the ODE trajectory. If not otherwise stated, we use
843   25 sampling steps for generation. To enable CFG, a modified velocity field estimate is implemented:
844   vCFG (zt |c; θ) = γv(zt |c; θ)+(1−γ)v(zt |∅; θ), where γ is the guidance scale trading off the sample
845   diversity and generation quality. If not otherwise stated, γ is set to 4, which is further discussed in
846   Section 4.4.

      C    D ETAILS OF P RE - TRAINED E NCODERS
850
      Most video encoders (VideoMAE, VideoMAE V2, etc.) and audio encoders (AudioMAE, PANNs
851
      (Kong et al., 2020b), etc.) only support fixed-length signal input, as they are widely used in un-
852
      derstanding or classification tasks. However, since our method focuses on temporal alignment and
853   synchronization, and the input is of variable length, we need to modify the inference procedure of
854   these models to recover the temporal dimension of the features.
855
856   VideoMAE and VideoMAE V2 Given a batch of preprocessed videos x ∈ RB×N ×3×H×W during
857   training, where B is the batch size, N is the varying temporal length (number of frames), 3 indicates
858   three color spaces, H and W are the size of the image, we segment the frames along the temporal
859   dimensions with a segment length of L, where L is the only supported frame length of the visual
860   encoder. In this case, L = 16 for both VideoMAE and VideoMAE V2. It is quite likely that L cannot
861   evenly divide N , that is, ⌊N/L⌋ × L ̸= N . If so, we stack the first M segments together on the batch
862   size dimension to obtain ẋ ∈ R(B×M )×L×3×H×W , where M = ⌊N/L⌋, leaving out N − M × L
863   frames. Now, instead of padding the remaining frames with zeros to L frames, we fetch the last
      contiguous L frames of the video clip and append it to ẋ to obtain ẍ ∈ R(B×M +1)×L×3×H×W . Then


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864                                                                                  ′   ′    ′
      we run the visual encoders to have the last hidden states ẑ ∈ R(B×M +1)×(L ×H ×W )×C as outputs,
865   where L′ is the compressed temporal dimension, H ′ and W ′ are the number of patches along two
866   directions, and C is the channel dimension. In this case, L′ = L/2, H ′ = H ′ /16, and W ′ = W ′ /16,
867   since the compression ratio of the visual encoders is 2 × 16 × 16 (which also requires that N should
868   be an even number, leading to a compression of N ′ = N/2). Then we unpack B × M + 1 segments
869   and connect each other end-to-end to recover B batches of N frames. Note that we only recover the
                                                                                             ′    ′   ′
870   last N ′ − M × L′ frames of the last segment. The resulting representation z ∈ RB×N ×(H ×W )×C
871   is eventually fed to the subsequent modules.
872
873   AudioMAE We solve the varying-length problem of the audio encoder with the same strategy as
874   visual encoders. We use a fixed window size of 10 seconds (supported by AudioMAE) to segment
875   the input waveform, filling continuous values ahead for the last incomplete segment. We then recover
876
      the feature with varying temporal lengths and crop the last potential incomplete segment.

878   D     D ETAILS OF DATA
879
880   We crawl 1.6K videos with semantically and rhythmically synchronized music soundtracks from the
881   internet. These videos are mostly artistic creations like movies and TV series, which differ from other
882   music videos in that their music is also part of the creation. In other words, while other videos might
883   be created first and then set to music, the creative process for these videos often involves simultaneous
884
      creation of both music and video, or even music being composed first followed by video production
      based on the rhythm and melody. Therefore, these videos generally surpass ordinary music videos in
885
      terms of audio-visual synchronization, music quality, and video quality. We referenced a variety of
886
      data sources, such as Walt Disney (Disney, 1940), Tom and Jerry (Hanna & Barbera, 1940), Charlie
887   Chaplin comedies, and more. These masterpieces provided a high-quality data foundation for our
888   work.
889
890
      Specifically, we collect 1.6K videos, ranging in duration from less than 1 minute to hours. Instead of
      segmenting the videos beforehand, we adopt a continuous random sampling strategy during training.
891
      That is, for a batch of videos during training, we sample a random segment of each video on the
892
      fly. This strategy, compared to segmentation beforehand, allows more diversity in data distribution
893   and forms a data enhancement to some extent. However, because the duration of each video largely
894   differs, we apply a data sampler based on the video duration to balance the sampling frequency. It is
895   also worth mentioning that this strategy ensures that all the video clips within one batch come from
896   different videos, which further ensures the effectiveness of the negative samples in the contrastive
897   learning.

901   E.1   O BJECTIVE E VALUATION
902
903   We utilize the audio evaluation toolkit provided by Liu et al. (2023). The FAD scores are computed
904   from the embedding statistics of a VGGish classifier (Hershey et al., 2017), while the FD scores
905   are computed using PANNs (Kong et al., 2020b). For BHS, we compute the alignment between the
906   ground-truth beat sequences and the generated based on a 100ms tolerance. This means that if the
907
      time difference between a predicted beat and a real beat is less than 100ms, then the predicted beat is
      considered correct, and they will be paired (or aligned). These pairings are exclusive. We obtain the
908
      alignment between two sequences by constructing a prediction-reference pairing graph and solving
909
      the bipartite matching problem. We utilize a dynamic programming-based beat tracking algorithm
910   (Ellis, 2007) to compute time-varying tempo sequences from both ground truth and generation. The
911   highest accuracy reported for this method is 58.8% in their initial paper. We choose this method due
912   to its simplicity and efficiency, and the metrics obtained could be considered as a reference.
