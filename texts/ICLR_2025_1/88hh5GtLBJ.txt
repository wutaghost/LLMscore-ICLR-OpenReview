011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Few-shot class incremental learning (FSCIL) aims to enable models to learn new
014            tasks from few labeled samples while retaining knowledge of previously ones.
015            This scenario typically involves an offline base session with sufficient data for pre-
016
               training, followed by online incremental sessions where new classes are learned
               from limited samples. Existing methods either rely on a frozen feature extractor
017
               or meta-testing simulation to address overfitting issues in online sessions. How-
018
               ever, they primarily learn feature representations using only the base session data,
019            which significantly compromises the model’s plasticity in feature representations.
020            To enhance plasticity and reduce overfitting, we propose the MetaAdapter frame-
021            work, which makes use of meta-learning for expandable representation. During
022            the base session, we expand the network with pre-trained weights by inserting par-
023            allel adapters and employ meta-learning to encode generalizable knowledge into
024            these modules. Then, the backbone is further trained on abundant data from the
025            base classes to acquire fundamental classification ability. In each online session,
026            the adapters are first initialized with parameters from meta-training, and subse-
027
               quently tuned to adapt to the new classes. Leveraging meta-learning to produce
               initial adapters, MetaAdapter enables the feature extractor to effectively adapt to
028
               few-shot new classes, thus improving the generalization of the model. Experimen-
029
               tal results on the mini-ImageNet, CUB200, and CIFAR100 datasets demonstrate
030            that our proposed framework achieves the state-of-the-art performance.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Deep neural networks have excelled in various vision tasks (Ren et al., 2015; He et al., 2016; Huang
036   et al., 2017), but they usually depend on pre-collected datasets to achieve this success. However,
037   data often arrives as a stream with continuously emerging new classes in real-world applications.
038   An ideal model should recognize new categories while retaining the ability to distinguish previously
039   learned ones, a process known as class-incremental learning (CIL) (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Rebuffi et al.,
040   2017; Schwarz et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2021a). The primary challenge in CIL is
041   catastrophic forgetting (Goodfellow et al., 2015), where updating for new classes leads to forgetting
042   old ones. The trade-off between maintaining performance on old categories (stability) and adapting
043   to new ones (plasticity) is known as the stability-plasticity dilemma (Mermillod et al., 2013).
044   Traditional CIL methods typically assume abundant data for each new category, but this is often
045   impractical in real-world applications due to the high costs of data collection and labeling. This
046   challenge has driven the development of few-shot class incremental learning (FSCIL) (Tao et al.,
047   2020). In FSCIL, the model is first pretrained on abundant data during the base session, while it
048   must continually learn new classes from limited data in each incremental session. Similar to CIL,
049   FSCIL also suffers from the stability-plasticity dilemma. Moreover, the limited availability of new
050
      class instances often results in overfitting (Zou et al., 2022), which means the model performs well
      on the training data in incremental sessions but has poor generalization performance on unseen data,
051
      thus reducing the model’s generalization capability.
052
053   In the realm of few-shot learning (FSL), meta-learning enhances the learning effect of the current
      task by utilizing data from other related tasks (Rusu et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; Hospedales et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2021). Building on these advancements in FSL, many approaches in FSCIL use meta-learning to
055   reduce dependence on new data, which helps alleviate the risk of overfitting (Tian et al., 2024).
056   These FSCIL approaches that rely on meta-learning can generally be divided into two categories:
057   prototype-based and process-based methods. Prototype-based methods freeze the feature extractor
058   following the base session and subsequently use it to generate prototypes for new classes, which
059   either serve as classifier weights or align with the prototypes of base classes (Wang et al., 2024).
060   Process-based methods typically mimic the meta-testing scenario by sampling a sequence of incre-
061   mental tasks from base classes (Chi et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022b). Nonetheless, these two types
062   of methods rely primarily on base session data to learn feature representations, which restricts the
063
      model’s plasticity when adapting to new data (Zhang et al., 2023).
064   To reduce overfitting and improve model’s plasticity, we introduce the MetaAdapter framework, a
065   novel approach that integrates meta-initialized adapters to expand and enhance feature representa-
066   tions. Inspired by residual adapters (Rebuffi et al., 2018) used in domain adaptation (Zheng et al.,
067   2021), we encode the task-agnostic knowledge into lightweight adapters, which are embedded as
068   extensions of the backbone. The training process is divided into three phases, where the first two are
069
      conducted during the base session and the final phase focuses on few-shot adaptation in incremental
      sessions. During the first phase, we construct few-shot tasks by randomly sampling instances from
070
      each base class and train the adapters by meta-learning algorithms, such as Reptile (Nichol et al.,
071
      2018), to obtain generalizable initial parameters. During the second stage of backbone training, we
072   introduce the feature compactness loss (FCL) to bring feature representations closer together, which
073   prevents excessive dispersion in the embedding space, and thus reservs space for representation ex-
074   pansion. Additionaly, we search for flat local minima by adding gradient-based perturbations to the
075   parameters to enhance the model’s robustness against forgetting. For each incremental session in the
076   third phase, the backbone is kept frozen and serves as the teacher model for knowledge distillation,
077   while the adapters, initialized with parameters from the first phase, are tuned to expand the current
078   representations to encompass new class features. With the meta-initialized adapters, MetaAdapter
079   enables the model to adapt to new few-shot tasks efficiently without significantly increasing the ar-
080
      chitectural complexity. During the test stage, the backbone and adapters are fused through structural
      re-parameterization, ensuring that the model structure remains consistent during testing.
081
082   The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
083
             • We introduce a novel MetaAdapter framework, which incorporates meta-initialized
084            adapters to expand and refine feature representations with the goal of effectively mitigating
085            overfitting and improving the model’s plasticity.
086          • A unique loss for FSCIL, called feature compactness loss, is proposed to prevent the feature
087            space from becoming overly dispersed and leave more room for representation expansion.
088          • Extensive experiments on standard benchmarks CIFAR100, mini-ImageNet, and CUB200
089            show that our method outperforms baselines and achieves state-of-the-art results. Further-
090            more, we perform a thorough analysis to evaluate the importance of each component.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094
      Meta-learning. Meta-learning, often described as learning how to learn, involves extracting insights
095
      from multiple learning episodes and using this knowledge to improve learning efficiency in future
096   tasks (Hospedales et al., 2021). It is usually divided into two stages. During the meta-training stage,
097   the model is trained using multiple source tasks to obtain initial network parameters with strong
098   generalization ability. In the meta-testing stage, the model uses the parameters learned during meta-
099   training to quickly adapt unseen tasks with only a few samples. Due to its natural suitability for
100   FSL, meta-learning has been widely adopted in many studies (Triantafillou et al., 2018; Jamal & Qi,
101   2019; Elsken et al., 2020). In our study, we employ Reptile (Nichol et al., 2018), one of the most
102   popular meta-learning algorithms, for adapter initialization to mitigate overfitting.
103   Balancing Stability and Plasticity in Continual Learning. In continual learning, a core challenge
104   is the stability-plasticity dilemma, which involves balancing the model’s consistent performance on
105   learned classes (stability) and its adaptability to new classes (plasticity). Architecture-based methods
106   have been widely explored to enhance plasticity by allowing automatic adjustment of network archi-
107   tecture during runtime. A popular choice is to separate network components into task-sharing and
      task-specific components, with the latter often being expandable. These task-specific components


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      often include parallel branches, such as ACL (Ebrahimi et al., 2020) and ReduNet (Wu et al., 2021);
109   adaptive layers, including GVCL (Loo et al., 2020) and DyTox (Douillard et al., 2022); and low-rank
110   factorization techniques, like RCM (Kanakis et al., 2020) and IBP-WF (Mehta et al., 2021). An-
111   other direction involves leveraging parallel sub-networks or sub-modules to learn incremental tasks
112   without explicitly defining task-sharing or task-specific components. For instance, Progressive Neu-
113   ral Networks (Rusu et al., 2016) add identical sub-networks for each task, facilitating task-specific
114   learning while allowing knowledge transfer through adaptor connections. And methods like PathNet
115   (Fernando et al., 2017) and RPSNet (Jathushan et al., 2019) pre-allocate multiple parallel networks
116   to construct a few candidate paths and select the best path for each task. However, dynamic neural
117
      networks often suffer from increased architectural complexity, which compromises their efficiency.
      In our study, we propose using lightweight, meta-initialized adapters, which allow the model to
118
      efficiently adapt to new few-shot tasks without significantly increasing the model’s complexity.
119
120   Few-Shot Class Incremental Learning (FSCIL). As a variant of class-incremental learning (CIL),
121   FSCIL requires rapid adaptation to new classes with limited data in each incremental session (Tao
122   et al., 2020). Many FSCIL approaches build on advancements in FSL, particularly utilizing meta-
123
      learning to improve learning performance by leveraging data from related tasks (Rusu et al., 2019;
      Hospedales et al., 2021). The methods in FSCIL leveraging meta-learning can be broadly cate-
124
      gorized into two types: prototype-based and process-based approaches. Prototype-based methods
125
      typically freeze the feature extractor trained on base classes and use the prototypes of new classes
126   as the corresponding classifier weights (Zhang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021b). While the frozen
127   feature extractor helps alleviate overfitting problem, it often results in biased prototypes (Liu et al.,
128   2020). Existing prototype adjustment methods (Liu et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021b; Zhang et al.,
129   2021; Zhou et al., 2022a) aim to correct this bias but often involve complex pre-training algo-
130   rithms (e.g., contrastive learning, data mixup) (Zhou et al., 2022a; Peng et al., 2022; Song et al.,
131   2023). Process-based methods focus on meta-testing simulation by sampling sequences of incre-
132   mental tasks from base classes (Yoon et al., 2020; Chi et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022b). For example,
133   MetaFSCIL (Chi et al., 2022) adopts a meta-objective during the base phase to mimic the evaluation
134
      protocol through sequential task sampling. In contrast, our approach leverages meta-learning to pro-
      duce meta-initialized adapters, offering a generalizable starting point for enhancing and refining fea-
135
      ture representations. During the online incremental learning stage, MetaFSCIL uses Bi-directional
136
      Guided Modulation (BGM) to generate activation masks to mitigate forgetting. In comparison, our
137   MetaAdapter framework keeps the backbone frozen and utilize it as a teacher model for knowledge
138   distillation to guide the adaptation of lightweight adapters.

193   3.2   OVERVIEW OF M ETA A DAPTER F RAMEWORK
194
195
      Our MetaAdapter framework for FSCIL (see Figure 1) begins with a meta-training of the adapters
      in the first phase, with the aim of obtaining generalizable initial parameters for future few-shot
196
      tasks. In the subsequent second phase, referred to as backbone pretraining in Figure 1, we leverage
197
      feature compactness loss (FCL) to enhance the similarity of sample feature representations using
198   abundant data from D0 , which equips the model with fundamental classification ability and prevents
199   the feature space from becoming overly dispersed. The third phase, implemented in each subsequent
200   incremental session, uses meta-initialized adapters to rapidly adapt to new few-shot tasks while
201   preserving old knowledge retained in the backbone.
202
203   3.3    A DAPTER M ETA - TRAINING
204
205   In FSCIL, a model needs to adapt to new classes with limited instances and then evaluate over all
206   seen classes. As a result, a more generalizable feature will facilitate more effective learning of future
207   classes and improve overall performance. This indicates that during the offline training phase, the
208
      model needs to be trained on multiple source tasks to acquire initial parameters capable of strong
      generalization, which enables effective adaptation to new categories in incremental sessions. To this
209
      end, we construct pseudo support sets by randomly sampling instances from each base class and
210
      apply meta-learning on these sets to search for an effective initialization.
211

233

234

459   Hyper-Parameter Sensitivity. In Eq. (5), Eq. (11) and Eq. (12), three key hyper-parameters are
460   involved during training: wfcl , wkd and wa . We conducted a hyper-parameter search to determine
461   suitable values for wfcl and wkd by exploring their combinations. As observed from Figure 4(a),
462   our model achieves satisfactory performance on the mini-ImageNet dataset when using a relatively
463   larger value for wkd and a smaller value for wfcl . Moreover, the model’s performance remains robust
464   across a wide range of values for these two hyperparameters, with wkd ranging from 1.0 to 3.0 and
465   wfcl ranging from 0.5 to 2.0. This is because the model can prevent more knowledge from being
466   forgotten with the help of a larger knowledge distillation weight wkd . However, while the feature
467
      compactness loss can help to prevent feature representations from becoming overly dispersed, an
      excessively large coefficient for wfcl can negatively impact the classification performance. Addi-
468
      tionally, consistent experimental results are observed across the other two datasets. As a result, we
469
      set wfcl = 1.0 and wkd = 1.0 respectively throughout our experiments.
470
471   In addition, we analyze the final accuracy fluctuation under different adapter weights wa which con-
472   trols the strength of feature representation adjustments by the adapters in Eq. (12). The larger value
473
      of wa enhances the model’s attention to the knowledge brought by the newly expanded features. As
      depicted in Figure 4 (b), the accuracy improves initially as wa increases, which allows the model to
474
      better learn new class knowledge. However, as wa becomes excessively large, the expanded feature
475
      representations start to interfere with the performance of the original backbone, leading to a decline
476   in accuracy. The optimal wa for three datasets is 0.15, 0.20 and 0.25, respectively. For classes
477   from the fine-grained dataset CUB200 that share similar appearance, a smaller wa is required to
478   leverage more knowledge from the base stage. To the contrary, for classes from mini-ImageNet and
479   CIFAR100, which are less semantically related, a relatively larger wa is preferable.

514   5    C ONCLUSION
515
516
      In this paper, we present a novel framework that leverages meta-initialized adapters to expand and
517
      strengthen feature representations for FSCIL. By applying meta-learning during the base session,
518   we effectively train the adapters to capture generalizable knowledge, enabling the model to learn
519   efficiently from limited task samples. Furthermore, a novel loss function is used to drive features
520   closer together and prevent excessive dispersion in the embedding space. During incremental ses-
521   sions, we tune the adapters to refine feature representations, which allows the model to effectively
522   accommodate new knowledge. Through extensive experiments and comprehensive analysis, our
523   approach consistently surpasses previous methods and sets a new state-of-the-art.

757
758
759   Datasets and Evaluation. Following mainstream settings, our experiments are conducted on three
760   benchmark datasets: mini-ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015), CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al.,
761   2009), and CUB200 (Wah et al., 2011). mini-ImageNet is a variant of ImageNet, including 60,000
762   images with an image size of 84 × 84 from 100 chosen classes. CIFAR100 is composed of 60,000
763   tiny images of size 32 × 32 from 100 categories. CUB200 is a fine-grained classification dataset
764   for 200 bird species with similar appearance in a resolution of 224 × 224. For mini-ImageNet and
765   CIFAR100, 60 categories are selected as base classes (t = 0) while the remaining are split into 8
766   incremental sessions (1 ≤ t ≤ 8) with only 5 training examples per novel class (i.e., 5-way 5-shot).
767
      As for the CUB200 dataset, 100 categories are selected as the base training sets, while the rest forms
      10-way 5-shot tasks for 10 sessions in total.
768
769   Training Details. Previous studies commonly use ResNet-12, ResNet-18, and ResNet-20 (He
770   et al., 2016) for FSCIL experiments. For mini-ImageNet and CIFAR100, we use ResNet-12 follow-
771   ing (Hersche et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023), and we first pre-train the model on half of the base
772   classes to initialize the feature extractor. For CUB200, we use ResNet-18 (pre-trained on ImageNet)
773
      following other studies. In the base session, we first meta-train the adapters for 30 epochs across
      all datasets. We set α = 0.05, β = 0.01 for CIFAR100, α = 0.01, β = 0.1 for mini-ImageNet
774
      and α = 0.001, β = 0.001 for CUB200. Following the meta-training of adapters, the backbone is
775
      trained on base session data. For CIFAR100 and mini-ImageNet, the training is conducted with a
776   learning rate of 0.1, a batch size of 256, and for 1000 epochs. The cosine scheduler is used to adjust
777   the learning rate. For CUB200, the backbone is trained with a learning rate of 0.004, a batch size of
778   128, and epochs of 400. In each incremental session, we further tune the adapters for 1-5 iterations
779   using a learning rate of 0.001 on CUB200, 0.01 on mini-ImageNet, and 0.05 on CIFAR100. Be-
780   fore computing the cross-entropy loss, a commonly used temperature scalar is applied to adjust the
781   distribution of the output logits. For example, the original output logits of instance xj are denoted
                                                                                        O
782   as Oj ∈ RD . The logits used to compute cross-entropy loss are denoted as τoj . The τo is set to
783   64 for mini-ImageNet and CIFAR100 datasets and 32 for CUB200 dataset. The selection of other
784   hyper-parameters is provided in Section 4.2.

        To provide a clearer understanding of the semantic characteristics of the benchmark datasets: mini-
945     ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015), CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and CUB200 (Wah et al.,
946     2011), we describe the category information of the three datasets. The fine-grained classification
947     dataset CUB200 consists solely of bird categories with similar appearances, leading to strong se-
948     mantic correlations between the base and novel classes. It validates the empirical finding that more
949     knowledge from base classes (i.e., with a smaller value of the coefficient wa in Eq. (12) of the
950     main paper) should be transferred for facilitating the learning of novel classes in CUB200 due to
951     the strong semantic correlations between them. In contrast, images from the mini-ImageNet and
952     CIFAR100 classification datasets exhibit more diverse visual appearances, with lower semantic sim-
953
        ilarity between base and novel classes compared to CUB200. The first half of the base classes in
        mini-ImageNet (indices 1-35) are animal-related (i.e., ‘house finch’, ‘robin’, ‘green mamba’), while
954
        the remaining base classes (indices 36-60) and the novel classes (indices 61-100) consist largely of
955
        inorganic objects. For CIFAR100, the dataset contains 100 classes grouped into 20 superclasses,
956     covering a wide range of categories such as ‘aquatic mammals’, ‘large carnivores’ and ‘household
957     devices’. Similar to mini-ImageNet, the semantic differences between these categories are also sig-
958     nificant, offering diverse visual representation across various domains. Thus, for better handling
959     these classes, the model should pay more attention to the new few-shot classes by using a larger wa ,
960     which further confirms the results of Figure 4(b) in our main paper.
