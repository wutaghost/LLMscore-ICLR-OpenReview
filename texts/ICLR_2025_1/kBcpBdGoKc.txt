009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Stereo matching aims to estimate the disparity between matching pixels in a
012            stereo image pair, which is important to robotics, autonomous driving, and other
013            computer vision tasks. Despite the development of numerous impressive methods
014            in recent years, determining the most suitable architecture for practical application
015            remains challenging. To address this gap, our paper introduces a comprehensive
016            benchmark focusing on practical applicability rather than solely on individual
017            models for optimized performance. Specifically, we develop a flexible and efficient
018
               stereo matching codebase, called OpenStereo. OpenStereo includes training and
               inference codes of more than 10 network models, making it, to our knowledge,
019
               the most complete stereo matching toolbox available. Based on OpenStereo, we
020
               conducted experiments and have achieved or surpassed the performance metrics
021            reported in the original paper. Additionally, we conduct an exhaustive analysis and
022            deconstruction of recent developments in stereo matching through comprehensive
023            ablative experiments. These investigations inspired the creation of StereoBase, a
024            strong baseline model. Our StereoBase ranks 1st on SceneFlow, KITTI 2015, 2012
025            (Reflective) among published methods and performs best across all metrics. In
026            addition, StereoBase has strong cross-dataset generalization.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Stereo matching is a fundamental topic in the field of computer vision, aiming to compute the disparity
032   between a pair of rectified stereo images. It plays a crucial role in numerous applications such as
033
      robotics Zhang et al. (2015), autonomous driving Mur-Artal & Tardós (2017); Shamsafar et al. (2022),
      and augmented reality Yang et al. (2020), as it enables depth perception and 3D reconstruction of the
034
      observed scene.
035
036   Traditional stereo-matching algorithms typically match corresponding image regions between the left
037   and right views based on their similarity measures. Several techniques have been proposed in the
038   literature for stereo matching, including methods based on gray-level information Birchfield & Tomasi
039
      (1999); Li & Wu (2013); Yang et al. (2010), region-based approaches Zhang & Kosecka (2005);
      Pinggera et al. (2015), and energy optimization methods Scharstein & Szeliski (2002); Hirschmuller
040
      (2007). With the support of large synthetic datasets Mayer et al. (2016); Scharstein et al. (2014);
041
      Schops et al. (2017); Geiger et al. (2012); Menze & Geiger (2015); Yang et al. (2019b), CNN-based
042   stereo matching methods Kendall et al. (2017); Chang & Chen (2018); Guo et al. (2019); Xu et al.
043   (2023) has achieved impressive results. As shown in Figure 1, based on the network pipeline of stereo
044   matching, CNN-based stereo matching methods can be roughly grouped into two categories Wang
045   et al. (2021), including the encoder-decoder network with 2D convolution (ED-Conv2D) Mayer et al.
046   (2016); Poggi et al. (2019); Yang et al. (2019a); Saikia et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2021); Xu & Zhang
047   (2020); Tosi et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021); Lipson et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022); Weinzaepfel et al.
048   (2023); Li et al. (2024) and the cost volume matching with 3D convolution (CVM-Conv3D) Kendall
049   et al. (2017); Chang & Chen (2018); Zhang et al. (2019); Guo et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2020a);
050
      Duggal et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2020b); Gu et al. (2020); Badki et al. (2020); Cheng et al. (2020);

117   2.1   S TEREO M ATCHING
118
119   With the rapid development of CNNs, significant progress has been made in stereo matching. Based
120   on the network pipeline of stereo matching, stereo matching methods can be roughly grouped into
121   two categories Wang et al. (2021), including the encoder-decoder network with 2D convolution
122   (ED-Conv2D) and the cost volume matching with 3D convolution (CVM-Conv3D).
123   Stereo Matching with CVM-Conv3D The CVM-Conv3D methods are proposed to improve the
124   performance of depth estimation Kendall et al. (2017); Chang & Chen (2018); Yang et al. (2018);
125   Wang et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2019); Guo et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2020a); Duggal et al. (2019);
126   Zhang et al. (2020b); Gu et al. (2020); Badki et al. (2020); Cheng et al. (2020); Bangunharcana et al.
127   (2021); Shen et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2022; 2023; 2024). These methods learn disparities from a 4D
128   cost volume, mainly constructed by concatenating left feature maps with their corresponding right
129   counterparts across each disparity level Chang & Chen (2018). GCNet Kendall et al. (2017) firstly
130
      introduced a novel approach that combines 3D encoder-decoder architecture with a 2D convolutional
      network to obtain a dense feature representation, which is used to regularize a 4D concatenation
131
      volume. Following GCNet Kendall et al. (2017), PSMNet Chang & Chen (2018) proposes an approach
132
      for regularizing the concatenation volume by leveraging a stacked hourglass 3D convolutional neural
133   network in tandem with intermediate supervision. To enhance the expressiveness of the cost volume
134   and ultimately improve performance in ambiguous regions, GwcNet Zhang et al. (2019) proposes the
135   group-wise correlation volume and ACVNet Xu et al. (2022) proposes the attention concatenation
136   volume. CoEx Bangunharcana et al. (2021) proposes a novel approach called Guided Cost volume
137   Excitation (GCE), which leverages image guidance to construct a simple channel excitation of the cost
138   volume. IGEV-Stereo Xu et al. (2023) leverages an iterative geometry encoding volume to capture
139   local and non-local geometry information, outperforming existing methods on KITTI benchmarks
140   and achieving cross-dataset generalization and high inference efficiency.
141   However, these CVM-Conv3D methods still suffer from low time efficiency and high memory
142   requirements, which are far from real-time inference even on server GPUs. Therefore, it is essential
143   to address the accuracy and efficiency problems for real-world applications.
144
      Stereo Matching with ED-Conv2D The ED-Conv2D methods Mayer et al. (2016); Poggi et al.
145   (2019); Yang et al. (2019a); Saikia et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2020; 2021); Xu & Zhang (2020); Tosi
146   et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021); Lipson et al. (2021); Shamsafar et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022); Weinza-
147   epfel et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024), which adopt networks with 2D convolutions to
148   predict disparity, has been driven by the need for improved accuracy, computational efficiency, and
149   real-time performance. One of the early deep learning-based stereo matching methods, MC-CNN
150   (Matching Cost CNN) Zbontar et al. (2016), was proposed to learn a matching cost function for
151   improving performance in the cost aggregation and optimization stages. Subsequently, Mayer et
152   al Mayer et al. (2016) present end-to-end networks for the estimation of disparity, called DispNet,
153
      which is pure 2D CNN architectures. However, the model still faces challenges in capturing the
      matching features, resulting in poor estimation results. To overcome this challenge, the correlation
154
      layer is introduced in the end-to-end architecture Mayer et al. (2016); Dosovitskiy et al. (2015);
155
      Ilg et al. (2017; 2018) to better capture the relationship between the left and right images. By
156   incorporating this layer, the accuracy of the model is significantly improved. Furthermore, FAD-
157   Net++ Wang et al. (2021) proposes an innovative approach to efficient disparity refinement using
158   residual learning He et al. (2016) in a coarse-to-fine manner. AutoDispNet Saikia et al. (2019) applied
159   neural architecture search to automatically design stereo-matching network structures. More recently,
160   Croco-Stereo Weinzaepfel et al. (2023) shows that large-scale pre-training can be successful for stereo
161   matching through well-adapted pretext tasks. This method can achieve state-of-the-art performance
      without using task-specific designs, like correlation volume or iterative estimation.

      2.2      C ODEBASE
182
183   Numerous infrastructure code platforms have been developed in the deep learning research com-
184   munity, with the aim of facilitating research in specific fields. One such platform is OpenGait Fan
185   et al. (2023), a gait recognition library. OpenGait thoroughly examines the latest advancements in
186   gait recognition, providing novel perspectives for subsequent research in this domain. In object
187   detection, MMDetection Chen et al. (2019) and Detectron2 Wu et al. (2019) have emerged as an all-
188   encompassing resource for several favored detection techniques. In pose estimation, OpenPose Cao
189   et al. (2019) has developed the first open-source system that operates in real-time for detecting the
190
      2D pose of multiple individuals, including the detection of key points for the body, feet, hands, and
      face. In stereo matching, it is noteworthy that not all datasets are accompanied by official evaluation
191
      tools. For instance, the DrivingStereo Yang et al. (2019b) dataset does not have official evaluation
192
      codes, and there is a lack of unified tools for assessing the model generalization across different
193   domains. This absence of standardized evaluation resources contributes to the observed discrepancies
194   in cross-domain evaluations of the same model as reported in different studies. Therefore, it is time
195   to build a benchmark for stereo matching.

198   3      O PEN S TEREO
199
200   In recent years, there has been a proliferation of new frameworks and evaluation datasets for stereo-
201   matching. However, the lack of a unified and fair evaluation platform in this field is a significant
202
      issue that cannot be ignored. To address this challenge and promote academic research and practical
      application we have developed OpenStereo, a pyTorch-based Paszke et al. (2019) toolbox that
203
      provides a reliable and standardized evaluation framework for stereo matching.

361   4.3.2   F EATURE E XTRACTION
362
363   As shown in Table 2, pretraining the backbone is crucial for stereo matching as it enhances the model’s
364   ability to extract robust and informative features. Furthermore, the choice of backbone significantly
365   influences the model’s performance and computational efficiency. MobilenetV2 Sandler et al. (2018)
366   and EfficientNetV2 Tan & Le (2021) are lightweight CNNs that are particularly efficient in extracting
367   local features, which are crucial for stereo matching. Their designs allow them to perform well
368   with relatively low computational complexity. RepViT Wang et al. (2024a) is a Transformer-based
369
      architecture, which excels in capturing long-range dependencies and global context. While RepViT
      captures global features well, it might struggle with the fine-grained, pixel-level accuracy required
370
      for precise stereo matching. MPViT Lee et al. (2022) combines the strengths of both CNNs and
371
      Transformers. The CNN components effectively capture local features, while the Transformer
372   components excel in modeling global context. This hybrid approach allows MPViT to leverage the
373   advantages of both architectures, resulting in the lowest EPE. In summary, MobilenetV2 Sandler
374   et al. (2018) offers a good balance for applications with limited computational resources, while
375   more complex architectures like EfficientNetV2 and MPViT provide superior accuracy at the cost of
376   higher computational requirements. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to explore the
377   transformer-based feature extraction and the combination of CNN and transformer feature extraction
      for stereo matching.

      richer feature capture. The combined volume (G8-C16) offers a more optimal balance between

405   umes for disparity estimation. While deeper and combined volumes reduce the EPE, the gains might
406   be marginal compared to the significant increase in computational requirements, raising questions
407   about the practicality of these approaches in resource-constrained environments.
