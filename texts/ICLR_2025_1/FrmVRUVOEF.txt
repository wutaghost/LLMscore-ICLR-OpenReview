009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Dimension reduction algorithms aim to embed high-dimensional datasets into a
012            low-dimensional space in such a way that important structural properties, such as
013            clusters and manifolds, are preserved. Most such methods are designed for static
014            data, and naively applying them to time-dependent data can lead to unstable em-
015            beddings which do not meaningfully capture the temporal evolution of the data. In
016            this paper, we propose a new variant of the t-SNE algorithm for time-dependent
017            data, TC-tSNE (Temporally Coherent t-SNE) in which an extra term is added
018            to the cost function to promote temporal coherence: the notion that a data point
019
               which has a similar position in two time frames should be embedded to similar po-
               sitions at those times. Importantly, this notion captures temporal similarities over
020
               the entire time domain and can therefore capture long-range temporal patterns,
021
               not just local ones. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method for visualis-
022            ing dynamic network embedding, and we evaluate our method on six benchmark
023            datasets using a collection of metrics, which capture the structural quality and the
024            temporal coherence of the embeddings. We compare our method with existing
025            dynamic visualisation algorithms and find that it performs competitively.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Exploratory analysis of complex datasets is a fundamental task across science, industry and govern-
031   ment, and often starts with data visualisation. Good visualisation algorithms can reveal the important
032   structural properties of a dataset, such as clusters, manifolds and outliers, which may not be imme-
033   diately apparent from the raw data. In recent years, neighbor-embedding algorithms such t-SNE
034   (Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008) and UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018), and their many variants, have
035   proved enormously successful for this task, for example becoming a standard part of the scientific
036   practice in genomics (Kobak & Berens, 2019), neuroscience (Dimitriadis et al., 2018), molecular
037   biology (Li et al., 2017) and many other fields.
038   In many domains, datasets have a natural temporal component, and data points change and evolve
039   through time. Examples include collaborative document editing, neural imaging, dynamic networks
040   and video and sound recording. One might hope that a good visualisation algorithm could also
041   reveal the important temporal patterns in such datasets, such as trends, seasonalities, changepoints
042   and reversals to previous states.
043   While there is a highly-developed literature on data visualisation for static data, comparatively little
044   work has focused on data visualisation algorithms for time-dependent data. As well as represent-
045   ing the spatial properties of the data, such algorithms face the additional challenge of faithfully
046   representing its temporal evolution. We refer to this desideratum as temporal coherence.
047
      In a temporally coherent embedding, embedded positions should only move when the corresponding
048   data points move; clusters in the embedding space should merge and split as they do in the data; and
049   should a data point reverse to previous position, the corresponding embedded position should reverse
050   too. Embeddings which fail to achieve this goal may mislead a user into thinking that a change
051   has happened when it has not, or that two data points have switched places when they have not.
052   Simultaneously achieving high spatial quality, and maintaining temporal coherence is a challenging
053   task, and most existing algorithms seem to favour one goal or the other. In this paper, we present a
      strategy which achieves both.

      2    E XISTING STRATEGIES FOR TIME - DEPENDENT DATA VISUALISATION
055
056
      To see why this is such a challenging task, we present six existing strategies and describe their failure
057   modes. The first four are simple plug-in adjustments to existing base methods, while the final two
058   adjust the cost function by adding additional terms which aim to promote temporal coherence.
059
060   Independent. The simplest possible strategy for time-dependent data visualisation is to take a
061   static embedding algorithm and apply it to each time frame independently. Unfortunately, this tends
062   to lead to highly unstable embeddings. To see why this is, consider an embedding algorithm whose
063   objective function depends only on interpoint distances in the data and embedding spaces, such as
064   t-SNE or UMAP. In this case, small changes in the initialisation, data and any randomness into the
065   optimisation procedure can result in dramatic rotations in the embedding space. PCA suffers the
066   same fate due to the ambiguity of the signs of eigenvectors and the ordering of the dimensions by
067
      eigenvalue. One benefit of this strategy is that it tends to produce embeddings with the highest
      spatial quality.
068
069
      Global. On the other end of the spectrum is the global strategy, in which all timeframes and com-
070
      bined into a single dataset and embedded as one. By construction, this strategy will produce tem-
071
      porally coherent embeddings and it forms the basis of a variety of existing dynamic visualisation
072   algorithms (Hu et al., 2010; Fujiwara et al., 2018; 2020; Crnovrsanin et al., 2009). In the context
073   of dynamic network embedding, a recent line of works apply a global embedding strategy to obtain
074   temporally coherent variants of spectral and skip-gram-based graph embedding algorithms (Gal-
075   lagher et al., 2021; Modell et al., 2023; Davis et al., 2023). Despite this, positioning each data point
076   relative to all other data points at all time points can lead to poor spatial quality which can be par-
077   ticularly prevalent in the context of visualisation, where one has access to at most three dimensions.
078
079   Aligned. A simple strategy to alleviate the problems of the independent embedding strategy is
080   to apply a post-hoc transformation to the embeddings at each timeframe to align them with the
081   previous timeframes. This is typically achieved using sequential orthogonal Procrustes alignments
082
      (Schönemann, 1966). This strategy has been used in a number time-dependent embedding algo-
      rithms including visualisation (Crnovrsanin et al., 2009), temporal analysis using word embeddings
083
      (Kim et al., 2014; Yao et al., 2018; Szymanski, 2017; Kulkarni et al., 2015) and temporal network
084
      embeddings (Singer et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2019; Cape, 2021). The alignment strategy makes the
085   implicit assumption that the data evolves smoothly, and that temporally adjacency timeframes are
086   similar. Even if this holds, the strategy also suffers from drift in the long-term, and if it does not, it
087   can be highly unstable. For these reasons, long-range temporal relationships are not reliable.
088
089   Continuous. Another strategy which applies to optimisation-based methods is to sequentially op-
090   timise each timeframe, initialing the optimisation of the embedding for timeframe t with the embed-
091   ding computed for timeframe t − 1. We refer to this as the continuous strategy. This strategy has
092   been employed in the context of temporal word and network embeddings (Kim et al., 2014; Zhou
093   et al., 2019; Szymanski, 2017), however it unfortunately suffers from many of the same drawbacks
094
      as the alignment strategy.
095
096
      Velocity-penalised. This strategy involves simultaneously optimising the embeddings for each
      time frame subject to an additional penalty term in the cost function which penalises large move-
097
      ments in the latent space. We refer to this approach as the velocity penalised approach. Rauber et al.
098
      (2016) apply this approach to the t-SNE algorithm, and optimise the cost function

119   3     O UR STRATEGY
120
121   One of the major drawbacks of the aforementioned strategies for time-dependent data visualisation,
122   with the exception of the global and guided strategies, is that they only aim to promote temporal
123   coherence over short time-scales. However, many important exploratory analyses require temporal
124   coherence over long time-scales. In these contexts, these strategies are inappropriate and could lead
125   to misleading inferences. In this section, we will informally outline our strategy, which is funda-
126   mentally different from any of those outlined above. Our strategy can be applied to any neighbor-
127   embedding method, and we will outline informally in this section. In the next section, we will
128
      formally describe an variant of our strategy based on t-SNE, which we called TC-tSNE (Temporally
      Coherent t-SNE).

      3.1   S TRUCTURAL AND TEMPORAL COHERENCE
132   We will assume that our dataset is made up of n datapoints, each observed over T timeframes,
133   and we will denote the ith datapoint at the tth timeframe by xti . Similarly, we will let yit denote
134   the embedding of the ith datapoint at timeframe t. A neighbor-embedding objective function is
135   designed so that points which are nearby in the data space and positioned close to each other in the
136   embedding space. If an embedding achieves this goal for every timeframe, informally we will say
137   that it is spatially coherent. In symbols, an embedding is spatially coherent if and only if
138
                ∥xti − xtj ∥ is small ⇐⇒ ∥yit − yjt ∥ is small              for all i, j ∈ [n], t ∈ [T ].    (1)
139
140
      Analogously, we will define the (informal) notion of temporal coherence as follows: we say an
141   embedding is temporally coherence if every point that has similar positions in the data space at two
142   different timeframes has similar positions in the embedding space at those timeframes. In symbols,
143   we say an embedding is temporally coherent if and only if
144
145
               ∥xti − xsi ∥ is small ⇐⇒ ∥yit − yis ∥ is small               for all i ∈ [n], t, s ∈ [T ].    (2)
146   Importantly, our notion of temporal coherence covers all time ranges, both long and short.
147
      Many of the aforelisted strategies, such as the aligned, continuous and velocity-penalised strategies
148
      do not aim to satisfy this notion of temporal coherence. Instead, they only aim to satisfy what we
149
      will refer to as local temporal coherence, where s = t − 1.

487   we compute the metrics for a range of 20 values of k ranging from 1% to 20% of the data (except
488   Neighborhood hit, where to take values of k from 0.25% to 5% of the data), and record the average.
489   For a given metric and dataset, we report the average of these four metrics, each summed over all
490   data points and timeframes. We call the value the neighborhood metric. In addition, we consider
491   spatial Shepard diagram metrics, which measure a correlation coefficient of a scatterplot of inter-
492   point distances in the data space against interpoint distances in the embedding space. We do this
493   using Pearson correlation, Spearman rank and Kendall Tau coefficients, and report the average of
494   these three.
495   To measure local temporal coherence, Vernier et al. (2021) introduced a temporal version of the
496   Shepard diagram metric which measures the correlation of the scatter plot of interpoint distances
497   between individual datapoints at neighboring timeframes. We introduce a variant of this metric
498   which measures the correlation of the scatter plot of interpoint distances between individual data-
499   points across all timeframes. We will refer to this metric as the temporal Shepard diagram metric.

515   7   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we have presented a new strategy for temporally coherent visualisation of time-
518   dependent data. We have presented a specific variant of this strategy, TC-tSNE, which we have
519   shown to perform excellently at the task of visualising a dynamic network embedding. Our algo-
520   rithmic framework opens new possibilities for exploratory analysis of time-dependent datasets, and
521   we are excited to see how our method can be used in the applied sciences. In future work, we hope
522
      to apply our strategy to other neighbor-embedding methods such as UMAP, and to understand how
      our ideas can be applied for network and word embeddings.
