009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Trojan attacks can pose serious risks by injecting deep neural networks with hidden,
012
               adversarial functionality. Recent methods for detecting whether a model is trojaned
013            appear highly successful. However, a concerning and relatively unexplored possi-
014            bility is that trojaned networks could be made harder to detect. To better understand
015            the scope of this risk, we develop a general method for making trojans more evasive
016            based on several novel techniques and observations. In experiments, we find that
017            our evasive trojans reduce the efficacy of a wide range of detectors across numer-
018            ous evaluation settings while maintaining high attack success rates. Surprisingly,
019            we also find that our evasive trojans are substantially harder to reverse-engineer
020            despite not being explicitly designed with this attribute in mind. These findings
021
               underscore the importance of developing more robust monitoring mechanisms for
               hidden functionality and clarifying the offense-defense balance of trojan detection.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      A neural trojan attack occurs when adversaries corrupt the training data or model pipeline to implant
028
      hidden functionality in neural networks. The resulting networks exhibit a targeted behavior in
029
      response to trigger patterns known only to the adversary. For example, a trojaned traffic sign classifier
030   might behave normally until the trigger pattern appears on a sign, leading to a car crash. This presents
031   the threat that a user might suffer catastrophic losses by adopting a trojaned network that later does
032   something bad.
033
      A promising line of defense against trojan attacks is model-level trojan detection, which seeks to
034
      distinguish trojaned networks from clean networks. Successfully detecting trojans enables analyzing
035
      attacks and removing hidden functionality from networks (Wang et al., 2019). Further, the problem
036   of trojan detection is interesting in its own right. Being good at detecting trojans implies that one
037   must be able to distinguish subtle properties of networks by inspecting their weights and outputs,
038   and thus is relevant to interpretability research. More broadly, trojan detection could be viewed as
039   a microcosm for identifying deception and hidden intentions in future AI systems (Hendrycks &
040   Mazeika, 2022), highlighting the importance of developing robust trojan detectors.
041
      Recent work suggests that trojan detection is fairly easy. For example, Liu et al. (2019) and Zheng
042   et al. (2021) both propose model-level detectors that obtain over 90% AUROC on existing trojan
043   attacks. However, Goldwasser et al. (2022) show that at least for single-layer networks one can build
044   trojans that are practically impossible to detect. This is a worrying result for the offense-defense
045   balance of trojan detection, especially if such trojans could be designed for deep neural networks.
046   To date there has been no demonstration of trojan attacks in deep neural networks that evade a wide
047   range of detectors.
048   In this paper, we propose a method for making deep neural network trojans harder to detect. The
049   core of our method is a distribution matching loss inspired by the Wasserstein distance along with
050   specificity and randomization losses. Crucially, we consider a white-box threat model that allows
051   defenders full access to training sets of evasive trojans, which enables gauging whether our evasive
052   trojans are truly harder to detect. In experiments, we train over 6, 000 trojaned neural networks
053   and find that our evasive trojans considerably reduce the performance of a wide range of detection
      methods, in some cases reducing detection performance to chance levels.

084   2    R ELATED W ORK
085
086   Trojan Attacks on Neural Networks. Trojan attacks, or backdoor attacks, refer to the process
087   of implanting hidden functionalities into a system that affect its safety (Hendrycks et al., 2021).
088   Geigel (2013) devise a method to insert malicious triggers into a neural network. Since then, a wide
089   variety of neural trojan attacks have been proposed (Li et al., 2022). Gu et al. (2017) show how data
090
      poisoning can insert trojans into victim models. They introduce the BadNets attack, which causes
      targeted misclassification when a trigger pattern appears in test inputs. Chen et al. (2017) introduce a
091
      blended attack strategy, which uses triggers that are less conspicuous in the poisoned training set.
092
      More recent work develops attacks that are barely visible using adversarial perturbations (Liao et al.,
093   2020), learnable triggers (Doan et al., 2021b), and subtle warping of the input image (Nguyen & Tran,
094   2021). Others have considered making trojan attacks under fine-tuning threat models (Yao et al.,
095   2019), for textual domains (Zhang et al., 2021), and encompassing a diverse range of attack vectors
096   and goals (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020; Carlini & Terzis, 2021).
097
098
099
      Trojan Detection. An important part of defending against trojan attacks is detecting whether a
      given network is trojaned. Wang et al. (2019) propose Neural Cleanse, which reverse-engineers
100
      candidate triggers for each classification label. If a small trigger pattern is found, this indicates
101
      the presence of a deliberately inserted trojan. Several more recent methods build on this approach,
102   including K-Arm (Shen et al., 2021) and PixelBackdoor (Tao et al., 2022). Liu et al. (2019) analyze
103   inner neurons for suspicious behavior, then reverse-engineer candidate triggers to confirm whether a
104   neuron is compromised. Kolouri et al. (2020) and Xu et al. (2021) propose training a set of queries to
105   classify a training set of trojaned and clean networks. Remarkably, this generalizes well to unseen
106   trojaned networks. Other work uses conditional GANs to model trigger generation (Chen et al.,
107   2019b), adversarial perturbations (Wang et al., 2020), and persistent homology feature extraction
      (Zheng et al., 2021).

130      In this work, we consider model-level detectors such as those described above, which only require
131      a model as input. If a poisoned dataset or examples with trojan triggers are available, one can also
132      use dataset-level and input-level detectors such as activation clustering (Chen et al., 2019a), spectral
133      signatures (Tran et al., 2018), or online trojan detection (Gao et al., 2019; Chou et al., 2020; Kiourti
134      et al., 2021). This distinction is detailed by Xu et al. (2021), who point out that these levels of
135
         detection solve different problems and are not directly comparable.
136
137      Evasive Trojans. There has been considerable work on making trojan triggers evade dataset-level
138      and input-level detection (Liao et al., 2020; Nguyen & Tran, 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Nguyen & Tran,
139      2021; Doan et al., 2021b;a; Qi et al., 2022; Tan & Shokri, 2020). Understandably, these works focus
140      on this class of detectors and do not systematically evaluate model-level detection. In Appendix B.2,
141
         we show for the first time that methods for evading input-level and dataset-level detectors fail to
         evade common model-level detectors, illustrating the differences between these detection problems
142
         and how new methods are required to evade model-level detection. By comparison to this line of
143
         work, there has been relatively little work on evading model-level detection, which is the focus of this
144      paper.
145
146      Early work on neural trojans considered evasiveness to consist of maintaining high accuracy on
147
         clean inputs (Gu et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017). However, examining the clean accuracy is a very
         simple detection mechanism. Recently, several works have explored making trojans more evasive for
148
         sophisticated detectors. Xu et al. (2021) train trojans to fool a meta-network detector in a black-box
149
         setting, where the detector is not given full knowledge of the attack. Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov
150      (2021); Hong et al. (2021) train a trojaned network to fool the Neural Cleanse detector (Wang et al.,
151      2019), but their approach is not applicable to other detection methods. Goldwasser et al. (2022)
152      examine the problem from a cryptographic perspective and find that for one-layer networks it is
153      possible to construct trojans that are computationally infeasible to detect. Sahabandu et al. (2022)
154      train trojans and a meta-network detector in a min-max alternating fashion to be hard to distinguish
155      from clean networks, but only evaluate against one detector. Tang et al. (2021) propose a simple yet
156      effective technique called TaCT that increases evasiveness against two model-level detectors but is
157      only applicable for source-specific trojans.
158      We depart from prior work by developing a method for making trojans more evasive against a much
159      larger and more diverse array of detectors than was previously explored. Additionally, we are the
160      first to systematically evaluate reverse-engineering on a large scale, which allows us to make the
161      surprising discovery that trojans designed to evade detection are also harder for existing methods
         to reverse-engineer. While most prior works are not directly comparable to our own, we provide

184         Neural Trojans. A neural trojan is described by a trigger that can be applied to the inputs of a victim
185         network and a hidden behavior that the trigger should activate in the victim network. For simplicity,

208          Threat Model. We model trojan detection as an interaction between an attacker and defender. The
209          goal of the attacker is to insert a trojan into a victim network without being detected, and the goal of
210          the defender is to detect whether the network contains a trojan. The attacker randomly samples their
211          trigger and target label, and they may use any method for inserting the trojan.
212          Importantly, we assume that the defender has access to a dataset of clean and trojaned networks, where
213          the trojans are inserted using the same method as the attacker but with random triggers q ∼ Q and
214          target labels c ∈ {1, . . . , C}. In other words, the defender knows what the attacker’s distribution of tro-
215          jans looks like, but they do not know the specific trigger or target label used by the attacker. We make
             this assumption because we are interested in studying trojans that are fundamentally hard to detect.

219   We develop a general method for inserting evasive trojans that can be applied to a variety of underlying
220   trojan attacks, referred to as “standard trojans”. Starting with a standard trojan attack defined by an
221   attack specification (q, h, c), the form of our loss for training evasive trojans is Ltask +Ltrojan +Levasion ,
222
      where Ltask is the task loss that increases accuracy on clean examples, Ltrojan is the trojan loss that
      increases ASR, and Levasion is the evasion loss, which is designed to make trojans hard to detect.
223
      As with standard trojans, the task loss and trojan loss are implemented via cross-entropy on clean
224
      examples and examples with triggers inserted. The main modification for evasive trojans is the
225   evasion loss, which we describe below.

290   Specificity. Under our threat model, the defender has access to a training dataset of clean and tro-
291   janed models. In some cases, they may also have knowledge of the trigger distribution. If the attacker’s
292
      trojans have low specificity and respond to many unintended triggers, they can become trivial to detect
      by simply inserting random triggers into clean inputs and analyzing their effect on a given network f .
293
294   In experiments, we find that low specificity is a significant problem for trojan attacks on deep neural
295   networks. Thus, we add a loss encouraging high specificity. Let q ∈ Q be the trigger used for a
296   trojan. The general approach for a specificity loss involves inserting incorrect triggers q ′ ∈ Q \ {q}
297   into training examples and enforcing normal behavior on those “negative examples”. Prior works
298
      with specificity losses have used cross-entropy to the clean label on negative examples (Nguyen
      & Tran, 2021). However, we find that a more effective loss is to match posteriors between the
299
      trojaned network f and its clean initialization g on negative examples. Concretely, our specificity
300
      loss is Lspecificity = EX,Q [cross-entropy(f (h(X, Q)), g(h(X, Q)))], where h is the trigger insertion
301   function.
302
303
      Randomization. Empirically, we find that the distribution matching loss greatly increases
304   evasiveness against existing detectors. However, we identify a set of summary features of the
305   network parameters for which a simple logistic regression performs surprisingly well at detecting our
306   evasive trojans—even better than state-of-the-art detectors. We compare against this method, which
307   we call Param, in the main experiments. This suggests that the distribution matching loss induces
308   emergent coordination in the parameter space, such that independently trained evasive trojans acquire
309   similarities. To mitigate this problem, we propose a randomization loss Lrand = 1−cosine(θf′ −θg′ , r),
310   where θf′ , θg′ are the summary statistics for f and g, and r is a random direction fixed at the start
311   of training. This loss encourages the difference between θf and θg to not only be small, but to also
312   point in a random direction. Note that enforcing this loss directly in the parameter space is too strict,
313   so we use a feature space instead. For more details on our randomization loss, see Appendix A. In
314   Figure 4, we visualize the effect of the distribution matching and randomization losses.

318   To evaluate our approach for creating evasive trojans, we ask whether trojans with a fixed trigger
319   distribution can be made harder to detect for a variety of detectors, ranging from simple baselines to
320   state-of-the-art methods. We generate collections of clean networks, networks with standard trojans,

322   CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and GTSRB (Stallkamp et al., 2012) (downsampled to 32x32).
323   For each dataset, we generate 250 clean networks and 250 networks for each trojan attack, split evenly
      into training and test sets. The training sets of networks are used for developing trojan detectors.

329

330

334   more subtle attack, using image-wide random noise as the trigger, which is blended into the input
335   image at a low magnitude. Both attacks are implemented by poisoning a small percentage of the
336   training data. We poison 1% of the training set and refer to these attacks as Standard Trojans.

418   the trojan is or what causes it to activate. Reverse-engineering trojans is a nascent field with
419

420
      these reverse-engineering tasks as predicting the target label of a trojan attack and predicting the

422   we focus on reverse-engineering the trigger mask.

      6    C ONCLUSION
477
      We introduced a method for inserting evasive trojans in deep neural networks. Unlike standard trojan
478
      attacks, our evasive trojans are specifically designed to be hard to detect. To evaluate our method, we
479
      trained standard and evasive trojans on a large scale, creating training and test sets containing over
480   6, 000 neural networks. These networks were evaluated against a wide variety of trojan detectors,
481   including state-of-the-art detection algorithms and simple yet effective baselines. We found that our
482   evasive trojans are much harder to detect across a wide range of evaluation settings, in some cases
483   reducing detection performance to chance levels. Surprisingly, we also found that our evasive trojans
484   make reverse-engineering the target label and trigger of a trojan attack substantially harder. We hope
485   these results demonstrate the need for further research into robust mechanisms for monitoring and
      detecting hidden functionality in deep neural networks.

      Randomization Loss. The randomization loss minimizes the cosine distance between the network
651   parameters and a random direction. However, using the randomization loss in the raw parameter
652   space is far too restrictive and effectively amounts to adding noise to the parameters. Thus, we use a
653   space of differentiable summary features of the parameters instead. We concatenate the mean and
654   standard deviation of each parameter vector obtained via the PyTorch parameters enumerator, which
655   forms a single vector summarizing the network parameters.
656
      For MNIST networks, we found that even this loss was too restrictive and hard to satisfy, perhaps
657   due to the smaller size of the networks. Thus, we use an alternate randomization loss for MNIST
658   networks. Let θf′ be summary statistics of the trojaned model parameters, let θg′ be summary statistics
659   of the clean initialization, and let r be a binary random vector. The alternate randomization loss is
660
                      Lrand = ∥ min(θf′ − θg′ , 0) ⊙ r∥1 + ∥ min(θg′ − θf′ , 0) ⊙ (1 − r)∥1 ,
661
662   where the min function outputs the elementwise minimum. This loss allows each summary statistic
663   in the trojaned network to either be greater or smaller than the corresponding summary statistic in the
664   clean initialization, where the direction of allowed change is selected by the binary random vector.
665   This can be considered a softer, axis-aligned version of the original randomization loss.
666
667   Method Hyperparameters. Our evasive trojans have several hyperparameters. The two compo-
668
      nents of the distribution matching loss and the randomization loss each receive a separate weight.
      Let these weights be denoted by λ1 , λ2 , and λ3 , respectively. We use different loss weights for
669
      different experiments, and the weights are coarsely tuned primarily to ensure that ASR is high
670
      enough. Empirically, we find that increasing the weights for these losses smoothly decreases ASR and
671   eventually the task accuracy. This is because the evasion loss is in direct conflict with the trojan loss,
672   so there is a balancing act between satisfying the evasion loss and maintaining high ASR. Concretely,
673   we use the following settings of the loss weights:
674
675   • For blended attacks on CIFAR-10, λ1 = 0.02, λ2 = 0.02, λ3 = 0.00005
676   • For blended attacks on CIFAR-100, λ1 = 0.01, λ2 = 0.02, λ3 = 0.0001
677
      • For blended attacks on GTSRB, λ1 = 0.005, λ2 = 0.01, λ3 = 0.00001
678
      • For patch attacks on GTSRB, λ1 = 0.02, λ2 = 0.02, λ3 = 0.0001
679
680   • For patch attacks on CIFAR-10, λ1 = 0.02, λ2 = 0.02, λ3 = 0.00005
681   • For all other experimental settings, λ1 = 0.1, λ2 = 0.05, λ3 = 0.0001
682
683   We tune these weights by training small numbers of individual networks and monitoring the evasion
684   loss components and ASR. Importantly, we do not tune them specifically to obtain better results in
685
      the main experiments except in preliminary experiments meant to identify appropriate ranges for
      the losses, which we performed in a small number of settings. In a few experimental settings, we
686
      observed that there was a long tail of networks with low ASR. We hypothesized that this was due
687
      to the randomization loss picking a challenging direction. Thus, we retrained all networks below a
688   cutoff ASR using new random directions, which solved the problem. In general, we find that our
689   evasion loss is fairly robust to selections of loss weights and easy to use once the appropriate ranges
690   for the weights are identified. The specificity loss is implemented by inserting incorrect triggers into
691   16 examples for blended attacks and 10 examples for patch attacks. These numbers were selected
692   early during preliminary experiments.
693
694   Other Details. In preliminary experiments, we found that several implementation details were
695   important for increasing the evasiveness of our trojans. Namely, we train all evasive trojans without
696   dropout. Clean initializations are trained with dropout, but during the second stage of training we
697   turn dropout off. This is because dropout introduces uncorrelated randomness in the activations of the
698
      trojaned network and its clean initialization, which makes satisfying the logit matching component
      of Ldist challenging. For similar reasons, we also switch batch norm layers in clean initialization
699
      networks to eval mode throughout the second stage of training evasive trojans.
700
701   To improve performance on blended attacks, we found that it was important to process the inputs for
      the clean, trojan, and specificity losses together in a single forward pass. This is because networks

      that use batch norm are able to “cheat” by aggregating information across the batch. Empirically,
703   this issue arose most prominently with blended attacks. Concatenating the inputs together fixes the

709   Description of Detectors.
710
711
      • The accuracy-based detector (AB) simply uses the clean accuracy of a network as a score for
        detection. If a trojan insertion method consistently decreases clean accuracy, it can become trivial
712
        to detect, so this is an important baseline detector.
713
714   • The specificity-based detector (SB) assumes that the defender has access to a small set of k triggers
715     sampled from the same distribution of triggers that are used by the trojaned networks in question.
716
        This detector inserts each of the k triggers into images from the validation set and computes the
        entropy of the average posterior. The k entropy values are then averaged, the negative of which is
717
        used as the detection score. For trojans with low specificity, the entropy of the average posterior for
718
        triggered inputs will be lower than for clean networks, which enables detection.
719
720   • Neural Cleanse (NC) iterates over possible target labels for an attack and directly searches for
721
        candidate triggers using gradient-based optimization (Wang et al., 2019). We use a simplified
        version of Neural Cleanse that we found obtains stronger detection performance. Namely, in
722
        preliminary experiments we found that early stopping did not improve results, so we optimize
723
        for a fixed number of gradient steps. Additionally, the original Neural Cleanse method uses an
724     anomaly index based off of the ℓ1 norms of the optimized trigger masks for detection, which
725     enables selecting a principled threshold. However, we find that simply using the raw ℓ1 norms
726     results in significantly better detection, so we switch to this simpler score for Neural Cleanse. This
727     is enabled by our large-scale evaluations on datasets of clean and trojaned networks, which allows
728     using threshold-independent metrics and any real-valued detection score. For trigger synthesis, we
729     predict segmentation masks by thresholding the optimized masks with a threshold of 0.01, tuned
730     manually to maximize average IoU for both standard and evasive trojans.
731   • ABS monitors the behavior of neurons while perturbing the input to identify candidate compromised
732     neurons. These candidate neurons are used to generate a trojan trigger similarly to Neural Cleanse,
733     and the maximum attack success rate across candidates is used as a detection score (Liu et al.,
734     2019). We use the original PyTorch implementation provided by the authors for TrojAI Round 1.
735     For trigger synthesis, we predict segmentation masks with the threshold of 0.01 used in the original
736     code.
737   • K-Arm recasts trigger optimization as a multi-armed bandit problem, spending optimization steps
738     on the most promising potential target labels found so far while maintaining exploration of other
739     potential target labels. This identifies the most promising target labels in a more efficient manner
740     than the original Neural Cleanse scanning method (Shen et al., 2021). We use the original PyTorch
741     implementation provided by the authors.
742   • PixelBackdoor (Pixel) is a trigger optimization method like Neural Cleanse and K-Arm. It uses
743     a loss that is easier to optimize than the original Neural Cleanse loss (Tao et al., 2022). We use
744     the original PyTorch implementation provided by the authors. For trigger synthesis, we predict
745     segmentation masks by thresholding the optimized patterns with a threshold of 0.01, tuned manually
746     to maximize average IoU for both standard and evasive trojans.
747   • MNTD consists of a set of query inputs, which are passed through the network in question. The
748     outputs on these queries are then concatenated and passed to a shallow classifier, which outputs a
749     detection score. The queries and shallow classifier are optimized on the training set of clean and
750     trojaned networks (Xu et al., 2021). MNTD is an example of a broad class of techniques called
751     meta-networks: neural networks trained to interpret or monitor other neural networks.
752   • The Param detector is a logistic regressor with a single linear layer that takes summary statistic
753     features of the raw network parameters as its input. For summary statistics, we concatenate the
754     min, max, mean, median, and standard deviation of each parameter vector into a single feature
755     vector summarizing the raw parameters of the network. We develop this detector to highlight the
        emergent coordination issue described in Section 4, which motivates our randomization loss.

797   B.1   A BLATIONS AND A NALYSIS
798
799   Our evasive trojan training procedure has several distinct components. Here, we examine what
800   happens when certain components are removed or modified.
801
802   Randomization Loss. We include the randomization loss to mitigate emergent coordination across
803   independently trained evasive trojans. This coordination occurs when only using the distribution-
804   matching and specificity losses, and it enables strong detection performance with a simple detector
805   that performs a logistic regression on summary statistics of the parameters (Param).
806   In Table 9, we compare evasive trojans with and without the randomization loss. When the ran-
807   domization loss is removed, the Param and MNTD detectors become much stronger, while average
808   AUROC for the other detectors remains relatively unchanged. In several cases for trojans without
809   the randomization loss, the Param detector obtains 100% AUROC. Consequently, including the
      randomization loss substantially reduces the AUROC of the best detector from an average of 91.5%


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      to 84.5%. These results demonstrate that the randomization loss is an important component of our
811   method for training evasive trojans.
812
813
      Specificity Loss. We include the specificity loss to prevent the issue of low specificity, where
814   unintended triggers can activate the trojan. If a trojan has low specificity, then a defender with
815   knowledge of the distribution of triggers can easily detect the trojan by checking whether the known
816   triggers cause unusual behavior. Our specificity-based detector (Spec) is based on this intuition.
817   To validate the importance of the specificity loss, we retrain the CIFAR-10 blended evasive trojans
818   without the specificity loss. The specificity detector obtains 100% AUROC on these networks
819   compared to 67.2% AUROC when the specificity loss is used. This indicates that the specificity loss
820   has the desired effect and is an important component of our method for training evasive trojans.
821
822   Logit Matching Loss. The logit matching loss is one of the two components of our distribution
823   matching loss. To isolate the impact of this loss, we train retrain the CIFAR-10 patch evasive trojans
824   without the logit matching loss. The MNTD detector obtains 70.8% AUROC on these networks
825
      compared to 62.3% with the logit matching loss and 99.4% for standard trojans. This shows that the
      logit matching loss is an important component of our evasive trojans, but it only accounts for part of
826
      the increased evasiveness.
827
828
      Different Distance Metrics. Since the distance metric is an important component of our distribution-
829
      matching loss, an interesting question is what happens when the metric is changed. Here, we explore
830   adding an ℓ1 distance on the penultimate features to the distance metric. Concretely, we add
831   Lpenultimate = λp EX [∥fp (X) − gp (X)∥1 ], where gp and fp are functions that output the penultimate
832   features of the respective networks and λp is a scalar loss weight. We set λp to equal 0.1 and retrain
833   the MNIST evasive trojans using the modified distance metric. As before, we train 500 models,
834   split evenly into patch and blended triggers and divided into training and test sets. We evaluate
835   these trojaned models against baseline detectors and show the results in Table 10. We find that that
836   evasiveness against Neural Cleanse increases, but evasiveness against MNTD and Param decreases.
837   This demonstrates that the distance metric has a large effect on evasiveness, and designing good
838
      distance metrics that improve evasiveness across many diverse detectors is nontrivial.
839
840   Impact of Evasion Loss on Detector Performance. Here, we provide an expanded discussion of
841
      Figure 5. Two natural questions following our main results are (1) whether our evasion loss actually
      reduces the distance in parameter and logit space as intended and (2) whether this correlates with
842
      improved evasiveness. To more precisely evaluate the impact of our evasion loss, we retrain our
843
      evasive trojans with patch triggers on MNIST using different weights on the evasion loss. For each
844   training run, we multiply all components of the evasion loss by a fixed scalar ranging from 1 (original
845   evasion loss) to 0 (no evasion loss, but still initializing from a clean network). In particular, the loss
846   weights are 1, 0.01, 0.001, and 0. The corresponding distance values in parameter space are 0.7,
847   2.0, 6.5, and 8.8. In logit space, the distance values are 2.2, 2.5, 5.9, and 33.9, respectively. This
848   shows that our evasion loss is optimized successfully. To see whether this translates into changes in
849   detectability, we compute the percent AUROC for MNTD at each of these loss weights. In Figure 5,
850   we show the results of this experiment by plotting distance in parameter-space on the x-axis and
851   MNTD AUROC on the y-axis. There is a clear correlation: larger parameter distances result in higher
852
      detection performance. This suggests that evasiveness could be further improved by developing
      approaches that allow one to reduce our current distance metric even further.
853
854
855
      Effect of Summary Features in Param Detector. To compute the summary features used in
      the Param detector, we iterate through each parameter vector in the network and concatenate their
856
      standard deviation, min, max, mean, median, and skew statistics. This gives summary statistic vectors
857
      of length 580 for CIFAR networks, 330 for GTSRB networks, and 90 for MNIST networks. To
858   evaluate the robustness of our trojans to Param detectors using different summary statistics, we
859   repeated the experiments using random projections from the full parameter vectors down to the same
860   reduced dimensionality (580, 330, 90). The AUROC of this modified Param detector is 50.3% on
861   average, with a maximum of 54.5% across all experimental settings. By contrast, the average AUROC
862   of the Param detector using the original summary statistics is 75.4% on our evasive trojans. This
863   shows that the summary statistics we use in the paper are a strong baseline, and our evasive trojans
      are robust to other summary statistics.

      B.2   A DDITIONAL ATTACK C OMPARISONS
865
866   Here, we compare to other trojan attacks that are designed with evasiveness in mind. In each section,
867   we clarify how these prior attacks differ from our own.
868
869
      Blind Backdoors Neural Cleanse Evasion Method. Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov (2021) train
870
      trojans specifically to evade Neural Cleanse. Namely, a Neural Cleanse search process is carried out
871   simultaneously with model training in a two-phase update approach. We implement this method and
872   train a dataset of 500 MNIST models to evaluate its evasiveness. As with the standard and evasive
873   trojans, we evenly split these models into patch and blended triggers and divide them into training
874   and test sets.
875
      With MNTD, the AUROC for patch and blended trojans is 72.8% and 98.7%, respectively. With
876
      Neural Cleanse, the AUROC for patch and blended trojans is 77.3% and 98.9%, respectively. With
877   the Param detector, the AUROC for patch and blended trojans is 100.0% in both cases. Compared
878   to the performance of Neural Cleanse on standard trojans, this is slightly better in both cases. This
879   shows that their evasion method does work. However, MNTD and Param still have high performances
880   on their trojans (in the case of Param, this reaches perfect detection performance). This shows that
881   training trojans to be evasive for a specific detector may not generalize to all detectors. By contrast,
882   our evasive trojans do generalize to reducing the detection performance of a broad range of detectors
883   without specifically training against them.
884
885   Stealthy Trigger Attacks. As we note in the related work, there have been numerous prior works
886   exploring how to make trojan triggers more stealthy, which we distinguish from making trojans
887   themselves more evasive. These methods are specifically designed to evade dataset-level and input-
888   level detectors like Activation Clustering (Chen et al., 2019a), Spectral Signatures (Tran et al., 2018),
889   STRIP (Gao et al., 2019), and SentiNet (Chou et al., 2020). They do so by making inputs with
890   triggers appear more similar to inputs without triggers (either in the input-space or intermediate
891
      features). However, these methods are not designed to evade model-level detectors like MNTD
      or ABS and are rarely evaluated on these detectors. An interesting question is whether the strong
892
      evasiveness of this class of trojans on dataset-level and input-level detectors transfers to evasiveness
893
      on model-level detectors. To investigate this, we evaluate two representative attacks from this line
894   of work: WaNet (Nguyen & Tran, 2021) and LIRA (Doan et al., 2021b). WaNet uses subtle spatial
895   warping of the input as a trigger, which improves evasiveness against input-level detectors like STRIP.
896   LIRA uses a learned input-dependent perturbation function to generate trojan triggers, which allows
897   using imperceptible triggers with a very low perturbation magnitude.
898
      First, we evaluate model-level detectors against the WaNet attack. We train 250 trojaned models
899
      on CIFAR-10 using this attack, and we evaluate against several model-level detectors. The Neural
900   Cleanse, MNTD, and Param detectors obtain AUROC scores of 99.5%, 100.0%, and 99.98%,
901   respectively. Thus, WaNet is very easy to detect with model-level detectors. By contrast, we find that
902   input-level detection with STRIP (Gao et al., 2019) on five of the WaNet models only obtains 63.7%
903   AUROC for identifying trigger-embedded inputs in the test set. This illustrates how input-level and
904   model-level detection are entirely different problems. The result on Neural Cleanse runs counter to
905   Neural Cleanse experiments in the WaNet paper. We are not certain what the cause for this discrepancy
906   is. However, one possible explanation is that we use a custom PyTorch implementation of Neural
907   Cleanse that uses a different detection score due to our evaluations being threshold-independent.
908
      Our implementation of Neural Cleanse obtains very high AUROC on blended triggers, which is
      unexpected, since Neural Cleanse was not designed to work on whole-image blended triggers. This
909
      could partially explain why our Neural Cleanse implementation also works for whole-image warping
910
      triggers. We tried out different hyperparameters for the warping field to see if this would affect
911   evasiveness, but this did not change the results.
912
913   Next, we evaluate model-level detectors against the LIRA attack. We train 250 trojaned models on
914
      MNIST using this attack, and we evaluate against several model-level detectors. The PixelBackdoor,
      ABS, and MNTD detectors obtain AUROC scores of 100%, 98.3%, and 97.1%, respectively. Thus,
915
      LIRA is also very easy to detect with model-level detectors.
916
917   These results indicate that methods designed for evasiveness against input-level detectors do not
      necessarily generalize to being evasive for model-level detectors. We hope future work on designing


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      stealthy trigger attacks will take this into account and consider designing attacks to evade model-level
919   detectors as well.
920
921   Targeted Contamination Attack (TaCT). In our main experiments, we focus on one-to-all attacks.
922   However, one-to-one attacks, also known as source-specific attacks, are an important setting as well.
923   In these attacks, the hidden behavior is only trained to activate on one specific source class. The
924   target class is selected from among the other classes. Tang et al. (2021) find that in this source-
925   specific setting, one can greatly improve evasiveness against Neural Cleanse and ABS with a simple
926   modification to the standard data-poisoning attack. Instead of just inserting poisoned examples in
927   the source class, they also insert “cover examples”, which contain the trigger but are labeled with
928   their original clean label. These cover examples are inserted for all classes besides the source class,
929
      which can be considered a form of specificity loss for the source-specific setting. They name this
      method the Targeted Contamination Attack (TaCT). Note that TaCT is not applicable in our main
930
      experiments, which focus on all-to-one attacks.
931
932   TaCT is a method for training evasive trojans in the source-specific setting, and there is some evidence
933   in the original paper that it generalizes across various model-level detectors, as they evaluate it on
934   Neural Cleanse and ABS. To compare our evasive trojans to TaCT, we adapt our standard and evasive
935
      trojans for the source-specific setting. This involves only inserting triggers for examples from the
      source class. We reimplement TaCT, and we combine TaCT with our evasive trojans by adding cover
936
      examples to each training batch. Due to time constraints, we omit the K-Arm and Pixel detectors from
937
      the evaluation. We train 500 trojaned MNIST models for each setting and show results in Table 11.
938
939   Interestingly, we find that standard trojans are far harder to detect in the source-specific setting
940   than in the all-to-one setting. On top of this naturally more difficult detection setting, TaCT greatly
941
      improves evasiveness compared to the standard trojans. In fact, it is comparable to our evasive trojans.
      However, when we combine TaCT with our evasion loss, we obtain the best results. Averaging across
942
      all detectors and across patch and blended attacks, the percent AUROC values for standard trojans,
943
      TaCT, evasive trojans, and evasive trojans with TaCT are 66.9, 61.4, 59.9, and 57.2. This shows that
944   TaCT and our evasion loss are complimentary, and in settings where TaCT is applicable we strongly
945   recommend evaluating detectors against it.
