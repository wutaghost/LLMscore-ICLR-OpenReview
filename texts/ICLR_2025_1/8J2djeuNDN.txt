011                                               A BSTRACT
012
               In the era of big data, access to abundant data is crucial for driving research
013
               forward. However, such data is often inaccessible due to privacy concerns or
014
               high costs, particularly in healthcare domain. Generating synthetic (tabular) data
015            can address this, but existing models typically require substantial amounts of
016            data to train effectively, contradicting our objective to solve data scarcity. To
017            address this challenge, we propose a novel framework to generate synthetic tabular
018            data, powered by large language models (LLMs) that emulates the architecture
019            of a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN). By incorporating data generation
020            process as contextual information and utilizing LLM as the optimizer, our approach
021            significantly enhance the quality of synthetic data generation in common scenarios
022            with small sample sizes. Our experimental results on public and private datasets
023
               demonstrate that our model outperforms several state-of-art models regarding
               generating higher quality synthetic data for downstream tasks while keeping privacy
024
               of the real data.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028   Tabular data is the most common data format in high-stakes sectors like healthcare. There are
029   many fundamental problems in dealing with tabular data, such as data scarcity, missing values, and
030   irregularity. Among them, the data scarcity problem has been the main roadblock. Many datasets in
031   healthcare, such as clinical trial data, have small data sizes due to data collection costs and privacy
032   risks, and consequently, these data cannot afford modern machine learning (e.g., deep learning),
033   which generally has thousands of parameters, at minimum.
034   Recent advancements in generative models, particularly in text and image,(7; 28) have shown the
035   benefits of technology for generating synthetic data that resembles real data. Despite this potential,
036   generating tabular data has not been fully tapped into; it has evolved through traditional statistical
037   approaches, like Bayesian networks (38), to deep learning techniques, including autoencoders and
038   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (42). However, these methods require large amounts of
039   data for their training, contradicting our objective of solving data scarcity. Also, the sparsity and
040   heterogeneity in tabular data make GAN or other deep learning a suboptimal choice, as evidenced by
041
      the fact that the tree-based method (e.g., XGBoost) works better than deep learning model (11).
042   Recently, advancements in large language models (LLMs) have also enabled researchers to use their
043   general intelligence to synthesize tabular data.(6; 14) The premise is that prior knowledge encoded in
044   the parameters of LLMs can provide contextual knowledge for coherent semantics that is required
045   to learn the underlying data generation process. Several studies transformed tabular data to natural
046   language via serialization, and used pre-trained LLMs to generate text containing the synthetic tabular
047   data (6; 14; 21). While fine-tuning LLMs has led to the creation of more nuanced synthetic data,
048
      this process again requires a significant sample size, contradicting the objective of addressing data
      scarcity.
049
050   In contrast, in-context learning presents a promising alternative by allowing LLMs to customize
051   without compromising their general reasoning abilities (24) . Particularly, few-shot learning in in-
052   context learning is to provide a few “examples” of data to let LLM to learn the patterns and mimic the
053   examples (17). Our study aims to utilize this few-shot capability for synthetic tabular data generation.
      Currently, tabular generative model by in-context learning, such as (31) faces a critical shortcoming;

054
      it only accommodate too few “examples” (not an entire dataset available), thus discarding remaining
055   data that cannot manage to fit into limited context length (input token). For example, in our real-world
056   trial data ATACH2 with 37 variables, just ten samples consume 4,232 input tokens, whereas the
057   common input token size of LLMs, such as GPT3, is 2,048. This failure to utilize all available scarce
058   data can make LLMs perceive the underlying data-generating process merely based on “educated
059   guess” with its prior knowledge instead of the data itself. This, consequently, causes distribution
060   discrepancy between real data and synthetic data.
061
      Therefore, we aim to bridge this critical gap. Our key idea is to make the data generation process
062   explicit; the objective of our in-context learning is to generate a better data generation process, as
063   well as to generate individual data instances. Here, the data generation process is a prompt text that
064   consists of the context of data and any simple “model” that describes the relationship between data
065   variables. We chose to use a Bayesian network or causal structure due to its interpretability and
066   simplicity.
067
      However, another challenge is to identify the ground-truth data generation process. Motivated by
068   GAN’s adversarial training, we optimize the data generation process (“generator”) in adversarial
069   training with “discriminator” (Table 1). The discriminator’s role is to discriminate real data from the
070   generated data, and we use the accuracy of the discriminator as a “loss function” to be minimized to
071   optimize the generator. Unlike GAN, our generator is a text format, which doesn’t have derivatives.
072   We address it by Optimization by Prompting, which leverages an independent LLM as an optimizer
073   (43). After optimizing the data generation process, the LLM as generator uses it to finally generate
074   synthetic data.

145   LLM and causal structure discovery Causal structure discovery involves either data-driven or

148

149
      like healthcare or when data is scarce. Expert-driven approaches can be an alternative option, but

      these methods are time-consuming and require significant expert involvement. Recently, many studies
151   (18; 23) suggest that LLMs, which encode prior knowledge in their parameters, can support causal
152   discovery by complementing expert knowledge. In this paper, we leverage multiple LLMs with
153   different roles to mimic adversarial training in GAN and use the heuristic causal structure discovery
154   to guide the data generation process.

      on a training subset Dtrain of Dreal , generating synthetic dataset Dsyn .

      3.2 M ULTI - AGENT LLM AS GAN
163
164
      Overview. We propose to develop a multi-agent LLM as GAN (MALLM-GAN) that generates tabular
      data by mimicing adversarial optimization (Fig. 1). The objective is to optimize the data generation
165
      process θ, which is a natural language description of i) the problem description and ii) the simple data
166
      generation process or causal structures representing relationships between variables. In MALLM-GAN,
167   for each iteration i, an LLM agent Generator generates data Dsyn with θi and a batch in Dtrain ;
168   a supervised model Discriminator is accordingly optimized using [Dtrain , Dsyn ] and evaluates θi
169   using Dtest ; and another LLM agent Optimizer improves θi to decrease the discriminator’s accuracy
170   (Algorithm 1). We repeat the iterations until the discriminator’s accuracy converges or the iteration
171   reaches the maximum epoch.
172
      3.2.1 G ENERATOR
173
      Data generation process. The data generation process θ is described in natural language and prompts
174
      the generator LLM to create synthetic data. It includes: i) context of data collection, ii) data schema,
175
      iii) causal structure describing relationships between variables, and iv) task instruction. The context
176   provides external knowledge on data collection (e.g., “this dataset includes subject’s socioeconomic
177   factors...”). The data schema contains the meta-information of variables (e.g., name, description,
178   type, and categorical values). These elements remain constant during optimization. The causal
179   structure, represented as a DAG and converted into text format (x1 , x2 ), indicates x1 causes x2 .
180   Various serialization techniques were tested, but the original structured format proved most effective.
181   The initial causal structure is heuristically determined (e.g., Hill climbing (37)). The task instruction
182   guides the goal, such as “produce accurate and convincing synthetic data”. Through adversarial
183   optimization, the causal structure and instructions are refined to reduce the discriminator’s accuracy.
184
      Thus, for each iteration i, θi is:
185                        θi = [context][schema], [causal structure]i [task instruction]i .                 (1)
186
187
      Note that subscription for iteration i will be omitted for simplicity without loss of generalizability.
      Also, note that we used causal structure as a means to convey the relationship between variables
188
      within the prompt; thus, obtaining ground-truth causal structure is not our ultimate goal.
189
190   Few shot examples. The data generation process θ is supplemented with n examples to leverage
191   in-context few-shot learning. Structured data (x, y) is serialized into JSON format, e.g., {age: 53,
192   work class: self-emp, ...} (Supplement listing 2 Lines 25-28). Various natural language serializations
193
      were tested but had minimal impact on performance. The number n of examples is crucial; a large
      n allows learning from diverse examples but is constrained by context length, while a small n
194
      avoids overflow but underutilizes data. Our solution, “batches in a batch,” splits a batch into smaller
195
      pieces that fit the input token size, generates a set of synthetic data, and collates them into Dsyn (see
196   Algorithm 1 Line 6). This approach balances the trade-offs in in-context few-shot learning. The final
197   input to the generator LLM is:
198
199                      θi , [JSON ((x, y)1 )], [JSON ((x, y)2 )], ..., [JSON ((x, y)n )]                   (2)
200   for each optimization iteration. See Supplement listing 2 for a full example.

      LLM as generator. With the prompt in Eq. 2, the pre-trained, frozen LLM (e.g., GPT-3.5) generates
      synthetic data. The goal is to create similar but not identical text to the n samples, with the temperature
203
      parameter controlling variability. The temperature is set low enough to maintain the original data
204
      distribution but high enough to avoid copying. The generator LLM runs multiple times with smaller
205   examples in a batch, and the generated data is collated into Dsyn . Dsyn,i denotes the synthetic data
206   generated at iteration i. See Supplement listing 3 for an example.

      Conditional sampling. As MALLM-GAN generates synthetic data using LLM, it seamlessly inherits
      the benefits of LLM, such as conditional sampling. LLM predicts the next tokens given a user-
209
      provided context, even when the specified condition is rare or given as a range. To conditionally
210
      sample the synthetic data, we modify the task instruction to contain specific conditions (Supplement
211   Listing 4).

213   3.2.2 D ISCRIMINATOR
214   Based on the generated data, we evaluate and score the quality of θ by assessing how easy it is to
215   distinguish generated synthetic data from real data. Naturally, this is a supervised learning rather than
      a reasoning task with LLMs. We build a discriminator f such that f : X → c where x ∈ X and f (x)

      is the predicted label c, which is 1 if x ∈ Dtrain and 0 if x ∈ Dsyn . Specifically, at each iteration i,
217
      a new set of synthetic data Dsyn,i is generated. We form the combined dataset Dtrain ∪ Dsyn,i . We
218   assign labels to the combined dataset by
219
220                     Di = {(x, c) | x ∈ Dtrain , c = 1} ∪ {(x, c) | x ∈ Dsyn,i , c = 0} .
221   We update the discriminator fi incrementally based on fi−1 . We evaluate the accuracy of the
222   discriminator with Dtest and pass a pair of (θi , L(fi )) to the optimizer where L(f ) denotes the
223   discriminatory power of f (e.g., accuracy, likelihood). We prefer to use accuracy (rather than
224   likelihood) because this is a direct measurement we aim to increase and because our optimizer does
225   not require numerical derivatives.
226
      The discriminator obtains better discriminatory accuracy to distinguish real or synthetic data as the
227   discriminator accumulates the discriminatory power of past iterations 0, ..., i − 1 and is updated with
228   newly generated, more realistic synthetic data from the current iteration i. However, on the other
229   hand, as the Dsyn becomes more realistic over the iterations, it gets easier to fool the discriminator,
230   and the discriminator’s accuracy decreases. Therefore, our discriminator obtains better discriminatory
231   power during this adversarial optimization.
232
      3.2.3 O PTIMIZER
233
      The next task is to optimize θi based on its score L(fi ). Our parameter to optimize is θ, a text,
234
      which doesn’t have derivatives. So we use Optimization by Prompting, which leverages LLM as an
235
      optimizer (43). To make LLM acts as a optimizer, we provide a meta-prompt, which consists of the
236   causal structure and the optimization task descriptions such as “Your task is to optimize prompts for
237   generating high-quality synthetic data. Aim ... ” (see Example in Supplement listing 5 Line 3-6).
238
239
      To leverage LLM’s in-context few-shot learning in the optimizer (43), we provide a few “examples”
      of possible solutions (θ, L(f )). Note that the example here is different from data (x, y). We keep the
240
      top k solution pairs over the past iteration as the optimization trajectory to guide the optimization.
241
      We sort the score, so that the more desirable θ goes to the end of prompt. This will allow the LLM to
242   recognize patterns among the data generation process with better score. See example in Supplement
243   listing 5 Line 9-31.
244
245   A potential pitfall is that the L(f ) of past iteration 0, ..., i − 1 is not comparable to the L(f ) of current
246
      iteration i. The past discriminators f0 , ...fi−1 have much lower performance in discriminating real
      and fake, thus the score L(f0 ), ...L(fi−1 ) are not reliable to compare θ of past iterations with θ of
247
      current iteration. Thus we adjust the score L(f ) of past iterations with the latest discriminator fi , so
248
      that all the scores are directly comparable to select best θ.
249
250   In all, the optimizer LLM takes as input the meta prompt and a series of data generation process θ
251   and adjusted scores L(fi )). The optimizer outputs the revised data generation process, particularly
252
      focusing on causal structure and task instruction. We repeat the iterative optimization and generation
      until reaching to the maximum iteration.

277   3.3 C OMPARISON TO GAN AND CONVERGENCE
278   MALLM-GAN’s adversarial training is motivated by GAN, but it differs fundamentally from traditional
279   GANs in that it operates in a natural language optimization space, using LLMs for prompt-based
280   generation, which lacks formal mathematical guarantees. Unlike gradient-based optimization in
281   GANs, MALLM-GAN’s optimization relies on empirical refinement of prompts through adversarial
282   optimization with a discriminator. Theoretical convergence analysis is challenging due to the absence
283
      of numerical gradients. However, empirical convergence, demonstrated in our experiments in Section
      4 and prior work (43), shows stable convergence, where discriminator accuracy declines as prompts
284
      are refined. This practical convergence criterion serves as a reliable alternative to formal guarantees
285
      in real-world tasks.

372   DCR distributions. The DCR metric assesses the realism and diversity of synthetic data. It
373   determines whether synthetic data points are too similar to the real data points (potential pri-

375   as d(xsyn , Dreal ) = minxreal ∈Dreal l1 -norm(xsyn , xreal ). Low DCR indicates that synthetic data are very
376   close to real data points, implying a privacy leakage, as synthetic data too closely mimic the real
377   data. We chose to use l1 -norm distance to measure the distance between two data points (6). For
      the categorical variables, the distance is 1 if two categories are different; otherwise, 0. As a result,

418   A key advantage of MALLM-GAN is its transparent data generation process, described in natural
419   text, which allows us to observe the evolution trajectory of the data generation mechanism during
420   adversarial optimization. We present examples of optimization trajectories. We showed how the
421   causal structure evolves to ground truth (Fig. 3) over iteration. We used the Asia dataset because it has
422
      known ground-truth causal structures and reported graph edit distance (GED) between ground truth
      and identified causal structures. In this example, the heuristically initialized causal structure gradually
423
      converges to ground truth, thanks to the knowledge obtained from the pre-trained LLM. Different
424
      convergence patterns were observed with different initialization strategies (Table 9), supporting the
425   benefit of our heuristic initialization. We also investigated how the task instruction in the generator
426   prompt gets sophisticated and how the discriminator’s accuracy changes over iterations. In Table 4,
427   the task instructions evolved to include specific details, and the discriminator’s accuracy decreased,
428   implying that synthetic data gets indiscriminative to real data.

482   4.6 L IMITATIONS
483   The proposed framework has several shortcomings. Firstly, due to the limited context length of
484   the LLM, our model struggles with high-dimensional datasets with too many categorical variables,
485   which make the context information lengthy and reduce the success rate of data generation. Another
      limitation of synthetic data generation introduced by LLM is that the LLM struggles with random


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      number generation as pointed out in (15), which cast negative effects on our framework’s potential
487   when dealing with datasets of many continuous variables. Additionally, mimicking the traditional
488   GAN framework, it suffers from a theoretical convergence guarantee. While our model performs well
489   with small sample sizes, showing better results than other baselines, the improvement diminishes
490   with larger datasets. Moreover, the training and generation process is costly when dealing with large
491   data volumes.
492
493   5   C ONCLUSION
494
      We propose a novel framework to generate synthetic tabular data by leveraging multi-agent LLMs
495   to address the limited sample size issues that are prevalent in healthcare. Compared with other
496   LLM-based methods, we propose an in-context learning approach that does not require fine-tuning on
497   LLM but still leverages the whole data. We use causal structure to guide the data generation process
498   and mimic a GAN architecture to optimize the process. We demonstrate that our model can generate
499   high-quality synthetic data while preserving the privacy of real data. Moreover, compared with other
500   black box models, our proposed work enables transparent data generation that allows domain experts
501   to control the process.

      2.5 N UMBER OF EXAMPLES IN IN - CONTEXT FEW SHOT LEARNING
954
955
      Given the limited context length that the LLM can understand, we proposed “batches in a batch”
      method to leverage all training data in limited context length in the generator LLM (Section 3.2.1).
956
      We varied the number n of few-shot examples by n = 1, ...5 and measured the MLE (Fig. 5) and
957
      DCR distribution (Table 7, 8) to find the optimal number n. As a result, the increasing number n
958   of examples did not always increase the MLE of synthetic data (Fig. 5) but decreased the DCR
959   (Table 7), thus increasing privacy concerns. Instead, n = 1 achieved sufficiently high DCR without
960   compromising MLE. The MLE did not increase with more examples because the more examples will
961   increase the context length and the generator LLM overlook some key context information. On the
962   other hand, the DCR decreased with more examples because the generator LLM is more likely to
963   stick to copy the provided examples. Interesting, the increasing number of examples does not affect
964   the DCR of synthetic data generated from public dataset (Adult, Insurance).
