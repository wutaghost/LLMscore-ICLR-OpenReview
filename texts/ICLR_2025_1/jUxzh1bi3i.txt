009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Global seismic tomography, taking advantage of seismic waves from natural earth-
012            quakes, provides essential insights into the earth’s internal dynamics. Advanced
013            Full-Waveform Inversion (FWI) techniques, whose aim is to meticulously inter-
014            pret every detail in seismograms, confront formidable computational demands in
015            forward modeling and adjoint simulations on a global scale. Recent advance-
016            ments in Machine Learning (ML) offer a transformative potential for accelerat-
017            ing the computational efficiency of FWI and extending its applicability to larger
018
               scales. This work presents the first 3D global synthetic dataset tailored for seis-
               mic wavefield modeling and full-waveform tomography, referred to as the Global
019
               Tomography (GlobalTomo) dataset. This dataset is uniquely comprehensive,
020
               incorporating explicit wave physics and robust geophysical parameterization at
021            realistic global scales, generated through state-of-the-art forward simulations op-
022            timized for 3D global wavefield calculations. Through extensive analysis and the
023            establishment of ML baselines, we illustrate that ML approaches are particularly
024            suitable for global FWI, overcoming its limitations with rapid forward modeling
025            and flexible inversion strategies. This work represents a cross-disciplinary effort
026            to enhance our understanding of the earth’s interior through physics-ML model-
027            ing.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Global seismic tomography is a crucial yet intricate field within the earth sciences that facilitates
033
      understanding the earth’s internal structure and dynamics. This discipline has wide-ranging applica-
      tions, from the discovery of natural resources (Virieux and Operto, 2009) and the assessment of seis-
034
      mic hazards (Zang et al., 2021) to the exploration of our planet’s evolutionary history (Dziewonski
035
      and Romanowicz, 2015). It utilizes seismic signals–earthquake-induced ground vibrations–recorded
036   at surface stations to invert and map the earth’s interior. The fundamental challenge in this field lies
037   in the integration of forward modeling of seismic wave propagation with FWI, both of which are
038   essential for accurately correlating seismic data with velocity structures (Romanowicz, 2003; Ficht-
039   ner, 2010). However, accurately simulating seismic waves through the Earth’s complex structures
040   and inverting this data is challenging due to the heterogeneous nature of terrestrial media and the
041   inherently ill-posed characteristics of FWI (Tromp, 2020; Lyu et al., 2021).
042   Modern numerical simulation methods, such as finite difference methods (Kelly et al., 1976), finite
043   element methods (Zienkiewicz et al., 2005; Moczo et al., 2007), and spectral element methods (Ko-
044   matitsch and Tromp, 2002a;b; Krischer et al., 2021), are fundamental in the forward modeling of
045   seismic waves. Although these methods have proven effective, they impose significant computa-
046   tional demands, particularly when deploying higher-resolution models necessary for detailed geo-
047   physical interpretation. High-resolution imaging requires the simulation of high-frequency waves,
048   which in turn necessitates smaller time steps and finer spatial discretization. This is essential to pre-
049   vent numerical dispersion and maintain the stability of the simulations, as mandated by the Courant-
050
      Friedrichs-Lewy condition (De Moura and Kubrusly, 2013). Consequently, as the desired resolution
      increases, so does the computational burden, exponentially escalating the required computational
051
      resources and time, thereby introducing a substantial bottleneck (Li et al., 2022b).
052

      FWI aims to iteratively refine a model of the earth’s structure by minimizing the discrepancies

101   beyond trained scenarios, facilitating the exploration of previously uncharted Earth’s interior.
102   However, the efficacy of ML methods in geophysical applications is heavily contingent upon the
103   availability of extensive, high-quality training data. Currently, there is a significant lack of bench-
104   mark synthetic datasets specifically designed for global seismic tomography and the ML community.
105   Although datasets to support FWI (Deng et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023) have been introduced, they
106   are mostly limited to subsurface exploration scenarios and do not fully represent the Earth’s entire
107   scale, which is crucial for high-resolution wave propagation modeling. This deficiency highlights a
      critical gap in the resources needed to advance ML applications in this area.

      In response to these challenges, we introduce GlobalTomo, the first comprehensive 3D global
109   full-waveform dataset specifically crafted for the ML community. GlobalTomo encompasses
110   high-resolution seismic simulations that span from the earth’s surface to its core. It features three
111   distinct data tiers, integrating both acoustic and elastic wave equations to cater to a broad range of
112   seismic phenomena. The dataset’s scope varies from a local scale of a 1-km radius simulated at
113   20Hz to a global scale encompassing the entire earth’s radius of 6,371 km at a 30-second period.
114   We employ spherical harmonics to effectively parameterize velocity structures and account for vari-
115   able seismic sources. Extensive benchmark analyses demonstrate GlobalTomo’s capabilities in
116   fostering generalization, accelerating forward modeling, and addressing the inherent ill-posedness
117
      of seismic inversion tasks. By providing GlobalTomo, we aim to bridge the existing gap between
      geophysical sciences and the ML community, thereby enabling more precise and efficient seismic
118
      modeling and inversion processes through ML-driven innovations (Mousavi and Beroza, 2022).

121   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
122
      Full-Waveform Inversion FWI has been extensively applied across a variety of domains, includ-
123
      ing shallow crustal imaging (Tape et al., 2009; Górszczyk et al., 2017), continental tomography
124   (Chen et al., 2007; Zhu et al., 2012), medical imaging (Schreiman et al., 1984; Wiskin et al., 2019),
125   and nondestructive testing (Rao et al., 2016). In seismology, classical FWI employs numerical simu-
126   lations to derive seismograms from an initial earth model and iteratively optimizes this model using
127   the adjoint-state method (Tarantola, 1984; Tromp et al., 2005). While it has achieved significant
128   success in both local and global inversions (Fichtner et al., 2009; French and Romanowicz, 2015),
129   FWI still confronts substantial challenges such as high computational demands and susceptibility to
130   local minima (Tromp, 2020). Emerging ML approaches, bolstered by comprehensive global seismic
131   datasets, present promising avenues to address these challenges, potentially leading to more efficient
132
      and precise FWI solutions.
133
134   Neural Operator Learning Neural operator learning has garnered significant attention for solving
135
      parametric PDEs, as evidenced by a range of pioneering studies (Kovachki et al., 2023; Li et al.,
      2020b;a; Wang et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021). Among these, DeepONet stands out for its flexibility in
136
      modeling mesh-free spaces and producing continuous solutions (Lu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021).
137
      Conversely, the Fourier Operator Learning (FNO) operates with mesh-based inputs and outputs yet
138   efficiently models the frequency domain and demonstrates rapid convergence during training (Li
139   et al., 2020a; Lu et al., 2022). Once trained, neural operators can swiftly predict solutions for new
140   parameter sets with just a single forward pass. This capability is particularly advantageous in seismic
141   applications, enabling the instant modeling of wave propagation under various velocity structures
142   during inference. Such efficiency significantly streamlines the inversion process when compared to
143   PINN methods (Song and Alkhalifah, 2021; Rasht-Behesht et al., 2022), offering a more direct and
144   rapid approach to seismic data interpretation.

      2.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
149
150   The primary goal of forward modeling here is to solve the 3D acoustic and elastic wave equations
151   governed by interior PDEs and boundary conditions detailed in appendix B.1. The objective is
152   to predict the wavefield ϕc,s (p, t) at any spatial location p = (x, y, z) and time t across various
153   velocity structures c and source configurations s. This requires constructing and learning a nonlinear
154   operator G, which maps velocity and source information into a wavefield output function, G(c, s).
155   The velocity structure c is captured by sensor measurements at predetermined points, expressed as
156
      c = [c(p1 ), c(p2 ), ..., c(pm )]. The source s includes spatial locations and moment tensors. Given
      inputs p and t, the function G(c, s) computes the wavefield ϕc,s (p, t). This approach models wave
157
      propagation dynamics, aiding in accurate simulations and deeper geophysical understanding.
158
159   In particular, we monitor the displacements received by surface stations, known as seismograms, to
160   infer the earth’s underlying structures. The inversion problem can be formulated as:
161
                                   c∗ = arg min ∥d − Φ(c, s)∥2 + λF (c, s).                              (1)
                                             c

      Here, d represents the observed seismogram data. The function Φ(c, s) computes the seismograms
163   based on the velocity structure c and the source s. The term F (c, s) is a regularization function
164   that integrates prior knowledge and assumptions about the structure, while λ is a coefficient that
165   modulates the influence of the regularization term. The notation ∥ · ∥2 denotes the L2 norm, used
166   here to measure the misfit between the observed data and the predictions.
167
      Traditional FWI relies on numerical simulations to compute Φ(c, s) and uses adjoint methods for
168
      optimizing c. Due to the intensive computational demand of these simulations, the optimization
169   process is typically limited to a small number of iterations. We view ML methods as a potent
170   tool to expedite the forward simulation process and enable more flexible optimization strategies.
171   The solutions of modern ML approaches in addressing inversion problems are further discussed in
172   appendix F.1.

174   2.2   M ODEL C ONFIGURATION
175
176   For generating global wavefield and seismogram data for our dataset, we employ AxiSEM3D (Leng
177   et al., 2019), a forward simulator renowned for its efficiency in simulating global seismic wave prop-
178   agation. AxiSEM3D is exceptionally adaptable, allowing customization to various model complex-
179
      ities (Leng et al., 2016). This simulator effectively utilizes the axisymmetry of global wavefields in
      a source-centered frame and extends calculations into the azimuthal Fourier domain. Further details
180
      are discussed in appendix B.2.
181
182   Our dataset comprises three tiers of increasing complexity, each supporting different training scales
183   and applications. The first tier simulates acoustic wave propagation through a 1-km radius fluid
184   sphere using a 20Hz mesh, providing a foundation in basic wave physics. The second tier extends
185
      these simulations to elastic wave propagation through an isotropic solid medium of the same scale,
      with source variations. This scale is comparable to 600 km continental simulations at a 30-second
186
      period, allowing exploration of complex P/S converted interactions. The third tier targets real-world
187
      earth applications, integrating acoustic and elastic simulations from the earth’s surface to its core
188   over a 30-second period, addressing planetary-scale wave propagation for detailed geophysical anal-
189   yses.
190
191
      Each model’s 3D structure within these tiers is developed by introducing perturbations ranging from
      -10% to 10% to a 1D background model. These perturbations are meticulously designed to mirror
192
      the subtle yet significant 3D heterogeneity of the earth’s interior, as uncovered by global tomo-
193
      graphic studies (Dziewonski and Woodhouse, 1987; French and Romanowicz, 2014; Thrastarson
194   et al., 2024). Such adjustments are crucial for demonstrating the impact of tomographic features on
195   earth’s geochemical and geodynamical processes, including mantle convection (van der Hilst et al.,
196   1997), subduction of tectonic plates (Sun et al., 2014), and deep mantle heterogeneity such as the
197   large low-shear-velocity provinces (Cottaar and Lekic, 2016). Further details on the configurations
198   of each model are provided in appendix C.
199
200   Parameterization Perturbations within each defined layer are parameterized using real spherical
201   harmonics (Dahlen and Tromp, 2020) up to degree 8, with radial values interpolated linearly be-
202   tween layers. Spherical harmonics, employed as the basis for parameterization, are mathematical
203   functions that define a set of orthogonal functions on the sphere. These functions are particularly ad-
204
      vantageous in geophysical applications because they can naturally represent the spherical geometry
      of the earth. Although some current mantle tomographic models offer higher resolution (Ritsema
205
      et al., 2011), spectrum analysis indicates that the significant power predominantly resides at lower
206
      degrees (Meschede and Romanowicz, 2015; Ritsema and Lekić, 2020). Consequently, our choice
207   to limit structural parameterization to degree 8 in the published dataset effectively captures the pre-
208   dominant long-wavelength heterogeneity of the earth’s interior (Su and Dziewonski, 1991). This
209   approach reduces the number of parameters and enhances the solvability of this inverse problem.

      Our dataset comprises two types of output data. The first type is surface seismogram data, which

249
250   We experiment with several ML baselines as the general solution operator for the 3D acoustic and
251   elastic wave equations to accompany GlobalTomo. These baselines, trained on the Acoustic and
252   Elastic tiers, serve as practical workflows for forward modeling tasks, predicting wavefields and seis-
253   mograms based on given velocity structures and source parameters. Wavefield predictions include 7
254   timesteps from 0 to 3 seconds, with each timestep consisting of 16 slices and 3,648 points per slice.
255   Seismogram predictions cover a series of time series of length 150 for 1,369 stations. These forward
256
      modeling baselines are not only utilized to simulate data but also employed in inversion tasks to
      refine the velocity structures based on their predictions.

287   Highway Fourier Net (H-Fourier) Considering the tendency of neural networks to favor low-
288   frequency solutions (Rahaman et al., 2019), we explore the use of a H-Fourier. This model combines
289   the gating mechanisms of Highway Networks (Srivastava et al., 2015) with the frequency domain
290   learning capabilities of Fourier Neural Networks (Tancik et al., 2020). The H-Fourier is designed
291   to handle deep learning tasks requiring both complex layer-to-layer transformations and frequency-
292
      specific learning, making it potentially more adept at managing intricate patterns in seismic data.
293
294
      DeepONet Instead of directly mapping velocity structures to fixed output grids, this approach
      involves building a framework that models both wave propagation and seismogram generation on a
295
      continuous spatial-temporal domain. We utilize the DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021), where the trunk net
296
      processes inputs of positions and times, and the branch net handles velocity structures. The outputs
297   from both nets are combined through a dot product before passing through a linear output layer to
298   produce the final prediction. To improve training efficiency and reduce memory, we developed an
299   optimized method for parallel multiplication of the trunk and branch net outputs; see appendix E.2.
300
301   Physics-Informed DeepONet (PIDO) While DeepONet is trained using traditional supervised
302   learning, which might limit its generalization in high-resolution domains, we explore additional
303   capabilities with PIDO (Wang et al., 2021). This model is designed to enforce physically consis-
304   tent representations by incorporating interior and free-surface constraints during training. These
305   constraints are integrated by calculating the necessary derivatives within the PDEs using automatic
306   differentiation.
307
308   General Neural Operator Transformer (GNOT) The GNOT (Hao et al., 2023) leverages the
309
      strengths of both neural operators and transformer architectures to effectively capture intricate spa-
      tial and temporal dependencies in data. By utilizing a self-attention mechanism, GNOT can effi-
310
      ciently model long-range interactions and handle high-dimensional inputs, enabling accurate pre-
311
      dictions of dynamic systems like seismic wave propagation.

      Complexity analysis The complexity of the acoustic and elastic waves comes from the intrinsic
379   mechanical properties and mathematical formulation. For instance, the acoustic wavefield only has
380   compressional (P) waves, while the elastic wavefield contains complex P/S converted energy. The
381   performance of MM can serve as an indicator to reflect the dataset’s complexity and distribution
382   characteristics. Smaller error margins suggest more consistent patterns across velocity structures.
383   The Elastic tier, evidenced by the poorer performance of MM, exhibits greater complexity than the
384   Acoustic tier due to the intrinsic properties of the elastic wave equation and variations in source
385   dynamics. The trend towards increased complexity is also corroborated by the performance metrics
386   observed across different ML baselines.

      Forward modeling speed A single round of forward simulation using a numerical solver requires
      120 seconds with 24 CPU cores. In contrast, ML approaches achieve forward modeling speeds
389
      typically ranging from 1 to 3 milliseconds on a single GPU, making them about 60,000 times faster
390
      than traditional methods. Note that the acceleration rate may vary with the degree of parallelism.
391   While most computational costs for ML seismic modeling are incurred during the generation of
392   training data, once trained, the neural operator can predict all wavefields in a single inference step.
393   This capability underscores the potential of ML approaches for real-time seismic modeling and
394   extensive forward modeling applications. For a comparative visualization, see fig. 3b.

400   velocity structure using acoustic waves at timesteps 1, 3, 5, and 7, and subsequently evaluated on
401
      a denser sequence from timesteps 1 to 8, within the same temporal range. The findings, illustrated

      higher-resolution domain. To enhance its generalization capabilities, we incorporated physical con-

      straints (detailed in appendix B.1) into its training regimen. This integration of physical principles

499   3.2.3   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
500
      • Uniqueness of GlobalTomo. The universality and representativeness of the dataset are critical
501
        for ensuring robust performance when applying the trained network to real-world data. In the
502
        realm of earth science, we benefit from concentrating on a single, unique object–the Earth–from
503     its surface to its core. Unlike datasets from localized explorational or geological sites, which can
504     vary significantly, our dataset is crafted to encapsulate the global attributes of the earth’s interior.
505     This focus enhances the dataset’s applicability as all simulations and models are framed within
506     a consistent setup. Future work could expand the scope by straightforwardly incorporating more
507     degrees of spherical harmonics to explore the Earth’s interior with greater detail.
508
509   • Broader interest of GlobalTomo. Beyond application in earth, our dataset is also crucial for
510     advancing planetary sciences, especially for celestial bodies like Mars and the Moon. Seismic
511
        data for these planets are rare and costly to obtain. A pre-trained model that simulates seismic
        activity on these bodies can significantly improve our understanding of their interior.
512
513
      • Potential of ML. ML presents a swift and powerful approach to addressing long-standing scien-
514     tific challenges. Utilizing a finite set of simulated data that incorporates physics-inspired efficient
515     representations, ML methods can perform global forward modeling and inversion across various
516     structures and scales with minimal time investment. The accuracy and efficiency of these methods
517     could be further enhanced by increasing the dataset size and adopting more advanced models. Fur-
518     thermore, ML can be developed to reconcile differences between synthetic simulation outcomes
519     and real-world data.

522   4    C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this work, we introduce GlobalTomo, a comprehensive 3D global full waveform dataset for
525   physics-ML research in forward modeling and FWI. By synthesizing high-fidelity seismic data
526   across a range of scales and complexities with efficient spherical parameterization, GlobalTomo
527   facilitates the advanced training and evaluation of ML models in the realm of seismic tomography.
528   Our extensive evaluations confirm that ML-based approaches, supported by GlobalTomo, sub-
529   stantially enhance the efficiency and scalability of seismic wavefield modeling and FWI processes.
530   Moreover, this dataset offers new possibilities for integrating machine learning with physics-based
531   modeling. Through this cross-disciplinary endeavor, we anticipate that the convergence of advanced
532
      machine learning techniques and traditional geophysical methods will accelerate discoveries in earth
      science, promoting a deeper understanding of the Earth’s interior dynamics and supporting critical
533
      applications such as resource discovery and hazard assessment.

      C.2   T HREE DATASET T IERS
919
920   We introduce the details of the three tiers generated by AxiSEM3D. The simulator is licensed under
921   the MIT license at https://github.com/AxiSEMunity/AxiSEM3D.
922
      Our dataset includes three tiers of synthetic seismic data. The first tier models acoustic wave prop-
923
      agation through a fluid sphere with a 1-km radius using a 20 Hz mesh. The second tier models
924   elastic wave propagation through an isotropic solid medium of the same scale, with variations in
925   the source. The third tier is designed for real-world earth applications, integrating both acoustic and
926   elastic simulations over a 30-second period.
927
      We export the 3D velocity perturbations in Cartesian coordinates to facilitate rapid interpolation
928
      and retain the original spherical harmonics coefficients for use in network training. To ensure the
929
      adherence to physical constraints, we disable attenuation.
930
931   Acoustic Ball The acoustic ball model is designed on a 1000 m radius ball with pure fluid medium
932   and a mesh accurate to 20 Hz. This set of models has a pressure-free boundary condition. The wave
933   propagation in the model obeys the acoustic wave equation. The medium only has the P-wave
934   velocity (vp) attribute. The 1D background model has a uniform vp value of 1.0 km/s across the
935   ball. The 3D superimposed velocity perturbation is parameterized by spherical harmonics up to
936   degree 8, leading to 81 spherical parameters per layer, multiplied by 5 layers, resulting in 405 free
937   parameters in total.
938   The source is located at a fixed 200 m depth at the north pole with a fixed fluid pressure source.
939
940   Elastic Ball The elastic ball model is also designed on a 1000 m radius ball with pure elastic
941   medium and a mesh accurate to 20 Hz. This set of models has a stress-free boundary condition.
942   The wave propagation in the model obeys the elastic wave equation. The isotropic medium only has
943   three independent attributes: P-wave velocity (vp), S-wave velocity (vs), and density (rho). The 1D
944   background model has a uniform vp value of 1.5 km/s, vs value of 1.0 km/s, and rho of 1.0g/cm3
945   across the ball. The 3D superimposed velocity perturbation is parameterized by spherical harmonics
946
      up to degree 8, leading to 81 spherical parameters per layer, multiplied by 5 layers and 3 medium
      attributes, resulting in 1215 free parameters in total.
947
948   The source is located at a fixed 200 m depth at the north pole with a variable moment tensor source
949   mechanism. This introduces 6 extra free parameters in source terms.
950
951   Real Earth This set of models is designed for the ultimate real-earth applications. We utilize the
952   isotropic PREM (Dziewonski and Anderson, 1981) background model and a global mesh with a
953   period accurate to 30 seconds that encompasses various earth’s layers including the crust, mantle,
954
      outer core, and inner core, each characterized by their unique seismic attributes. The Real Earth
      tier has a stress-free boundary condition. The 3D velocity perturbations are imposed on the earth’s
955
      internal 23 discontinuities, resulting in a total of 5427 free parameters up to degree 8 across the
956
      whole earth.
957
958   The source is located at stochastic depths between 0 - 800 km at arbitrary geographical coordinates
959   with a variable moment tensor source mechanism. This introduces 9 extra free parameters in source
960
      terms.
961
      Surface Seismogram Output The seismogram data contains a one-dimensional time series
962
      recording ground displacement at each surface station. Each trace is low-pass filtered to 30 seconds
963
      to avoid numerical noise. We place such stations evenly along latitude and longitude to represent
964   a spatially abundant coverage. The actual station deployments limit the real seismic data coverage,
965   however, this issue can be considered by dumping part of the data during the application. The seis-
966   mogram data can be observed globally and be used in the inversion of the earth’s internal structure.
967
968   Seismic Wavefield Output The seismic wavefields are saved in discrete 15 or 20 time snapshots,
969   (total time = 3 s or 6,000 s), represented by 16 azimuthal slices with 3,648 or 16,470 (from surface
970   to the core) element points in each. Besides, we also saved the azimuthal Fourier coefficients of the
971   global wavefields for a continuous and compact representation in three dimensions. The wavefields
      and the seismograms are saved as HDF5 files for fast loading.

       F.1   C OMPARISON OF M ODERN ML- BASED I NVERSION
1095
1096   Here we discuss the difference between modern ML-based inversion approaches. The two most
1097   dominant schools are PINN and neural operator learning.
1098
       We first recall the formulation of the inversion problem.
1099
1100
1101                                  c∗ = arg min ∥d − Φ(c, s)∥2 + λF (c, s)                          (A24)
                                                 c
1102
1103   In comparing the inversion strategies of PINN and operator learning, we observe distinct paths of
1104   optimization. The PINN-based approach seeks to optimize the model parameters c in conjunction
1105   with the forward modeling function Φ (Rasht-Behesht et al., 2022). Thus, the c and Φ are closely
1106   coupled and the inversion of different structures requires training from scratch. This method limits
1107   the model’s ability to generalize across different velocity structures.
1108   In contrast, the operator learning approach addresses this limitation by initially learning a universal
1109   operator to model forward problems across all c (Yang et al., 2023). Subsequently, it fine-tunes the
1110   model parameters c for each specific case using the pre-trained Φ. Once trained, the neural operator
1111   can achieve inversion on any structures in inference time.
1112   Our study adopts the latter approach to achieve fast and flexible inversion. We study both the
1113   sampling-based and optimization-based methods when using the pre-trained neural operator to solve
1114   the inversion problem.
1115
1116   F.2   I S P REDICTING WAVEFIELD N ECESSARY FOR I NVERSION ?
1117
1118   As shown in section 3.2.2, direct inverse mapping demonstrates better inversion performance than
1119   the optimization-based method. We want to discuss whether predicting wavefield is still necessary
1120   from the aspects of complexity, interpretability, and flexibility.
1121
1122   Complexity Direct ML inversion methods offer an efficient alternative to traditional optimization-
1123
       based approaches by using neural networks to approximate the inverse mapping from data to subsur-
       face properties directly. This method significantly cuts down on computational time and resources,
1124
       simplifying the traditional iterative inversion process into a single step. In contrast, splitting the
1125
       FWI process into two stages might increase complexity regarding the implementation and integra-
1126   tion of different computational frameworks or software tools. Additionally, more stages could lead
1127   to greater cumulative errors.
1128
1129   Interpretability Predicting the wavefield explicitly as part of the inversion process can enhance
1130   the interpretability of the results. In FWI, the aim is to reconstruct the subsurface properties us-
1131   ing wave propagation data. Although direct inverse mapping can produce a result of the inferred
1132   structure, it doesn’t explicitly model the intermediate process and tends to lack transparency in its
1133   operations. By decomposing the inversion into two distinct phases—predicting the forward wave-
       field and then optimizing the subsurface model—researchers can trace the effects of specific inputs


                                                         A7
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       or changes in model parameters on the outcomes. This division aligns closely with traditional FWI
1135   methods where each step of the process can be examined and understood separately. Furthermore,
1136   explicitly modeling the wavefield allows for a more detailed examination of the physical accuracy
1137   and stability of the inversion process, enabling researchers to validate and refine the underlying
1138   physical models being used.
1139
1140
       Flexibility Predicting the wavefield explicitly provides opportunities to apply a broader range of
1141
       optimization strategies tailored to specific challenges or characteristics of the data. For example, if
1142   the predicted wavefield is treated as a separable component, researchers can implement advanced
1143   gradient methods such as adaptive step-sizing or preconditioning. These techniques can address
1144   specific dynamics of wave propagation in different geological settings, potentially enhancing the
1145   accuracy and efficiency of the inversion. Preconditioning, in particular, can help tackle issues like
1146   the ill-posed nature of the inversion problem or the presence of sharp local minima by altering the
1147   optimization landscape.

1149
       F.3   C ONNECTION WITH OTHER F IELDS
1150
1151   Similar to the challenges of interpreting the human mind in the field of intuitive physics, the task of
1152   inverting Earth’s structure involves comparable approaches and discussions.
1153
1154
       Both seismic inversion and intuitive physics deal with inverse problems—inferring causes from
       effects. Seismic inversion back-calculates to infer the Earth’s interior from surface measurements
1155
       (Tromp, 2020). Similarly, intuitive physics tries to deduce the cognitive rules or mental models
1156
       that people use to predict physical outcomes from their observed behavior in experimental settings
1157   (Kubricht et al., 2017).
1158
1159   Regarding methodologies, both fields heavily utilize computational models. In seismic studies, nu-
1160
       merical methods and statistical models predict how geological features influence seismic waveforms
       (Tarantola, 1984; Tromp et al., 2005). Similarly, in intuitive physics, physics engines simulate how
1161
       humans might predict physical interactions in their surroundings (Battaglia et al., 2013; Li et al.,
1162
       2023). Machine learning, particularly neural networks, plays a significant role in both areas. In
1163   seismic inversion, neural networks can be trained to recognize patterns in seismic data that correlate
1164   with specific subsurface structures (Yang et al., 2023). In intuitive physics, neural networks model
1165   human prediction and reasoning processes about physical laws, learning to mimic human judgment
1166   (Li et al., 2022a).
1167
       Uncertainty management is crucial in both domains. In seismic inversion, uncertainties arise from
1168
       limited sensor coverage and noise in data. In intuitive physics, uncertainties stem from the variabil-
1169   ity in human perception and cognitive biases. Both fields develop methodologies to handle these
1170   uncertainties, often through probabilistic models (Tromp, 2020; Battaglia et al., 2013).
