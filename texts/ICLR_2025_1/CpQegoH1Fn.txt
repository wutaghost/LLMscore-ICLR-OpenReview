009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This study proposes a method for infusing human knowledge into neural networks.
012            The primary objective of this study is to build a mechanism that allows neural net-
013            works to learn not only from data but also from humans. This motivation is trig-
014            gered by the fact that human knowledge, experience, personal preferences, and
015            other subjective characteristics are not necessarily easy to mathematically formu-
016
               late as structured data, hindering them from being learned by neural networks.
               This study is made possible by a neural network model with a two-dimensional
017
               topological hidden representation, Restricted Radial Basis Function (rRBF) net-
018
               work. In rRBF, the hidden layer’s low dimensionality allows humans to visualize
019            the internal representation of the neural network and thus intuitively understand
020            its characteristics. In this study, the topological layer is further utilized to allow
021            humans to organize it considering their subjective similarities criterion for the in-
022            puts. Hence, the infusion of human knowledge occurs during this process, which
023            initializes the rRBF. The subsequent learning process of rRBF ensures that the
024            infused knowledge is inherited during and after the learning process, thus gener-
025            ating a unique neural network that benefits from human knowledge. This study
026            contributes to the new field of human-in-the-loop (HITL) AI, which aims to allow
027
               humans to participate constructively in AI’s learning process or decision-making
               and define a new human-AI relationship.
028
029
030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In this study, we attempt to build a method for infusing human knowledge into neural networks.
033   Recently, various topics of human-in-the-loop machine learning (HITL-ML) (Mosqueira-Rey et al.,
034   2022; Xin et al., 2018) have been gaining much attention. The primary objective of HITL-ML is
035   to allow humans to participate in the various stages of machine learning, for example, to curate the
036   training data and arrange the training schedule, indicate a preferred learning direction, and partici-
037   pate in the decision-making process. Many attempts have been made to study how humans interact
038   with AI in HITL-ML (Bengio et al., 2009; Dudley & Kristensson, 2018; Cui et al., 2021). The
039
      adoption of HITL in Reinforcement Learning has also gained attention (Christiano et al., 2017; Ret-
      zlaff et al., 2024), many of them include robot learning (Fitzgerald et al., 2018), where the infusion
040
      of human common sense prior to the RL process has been demonstrated to be beneficial (Ogawa
041
      & Hartono, 2022; Oriyama et al., 2024). HITL has also been applied to non-engineering fields
042   (Chandler et al., 2022; Cohen et al., 2023).
043
044   In the past, human participation in machine learning was limited to curating training data, mon-
045
      itoring and changing the course of the training process, providing evaluation during the learning
      process, providing demonstrations, and taking active roles in decision-making. This study proposes
046
      a new way for human-AI interaction: intuitive infusion of human knowledge into neural networks.
047
      This is made possible by a layered neural network in this study, the Restricted Radial Basis Function
048   (rRBF) network (Hartono et al., 2015; Hartono, 2020a). The rRBF has a two-dimensional hidden
049   layer that is self-organized during the learning process to form label-relevant topological represen-
050   tations of high-dimensional inputs. The low dimensionality of the representations allows humans
051   to visually understand the neural network; thus, so far, rRBF has been studied in the context of
052   Explainable AI (XAI) (Hartono, 2020b; 2024). For this study, the two-dimensional hidden layer is
053   utilized for infusing human knowledge in the initialization process of the RBF. Here, the low di-
      mensionality of the hidden layer allows humans to hand-organize the high-dimensional input onto

067   2     H UMAN - IN - THE - LOOP LEARNING
068
069   The Human-in-the-loop (HITL) training in this study is made possible by using a hierarchical neural
070   network with a 2-dimensional hidden layer, the Restricted Radial Basis Function (rRBF) network,
071   introduced in (Hartono et al., 2015; Hartono, 2020a). The rRBF has a two-dimensional topological
072   hidden layer, similar to that of Self-Organizing Maps (SOM) (Kohonen, 1982; 2013). The low-
073
      dimensional topological layer allows humans to visualize and thus intuitively understand the neural
      network’s internal representation, which adds transparency to normally black-box hierarchical neu-
074
      ral networks.
075
076   In this study, the two-dimensional internal layer is utilized not for visualization but for knowledge
077   infusion. As this internal layer topologically represents the high-dimensional inputs, the distances
078   between the internal representations reflect their similarities in their original high-dimensional space.
079
      Here, this topological characteristic is utilized for infusing human knowledge to the neural network.
      The idea is to allow a person (called an initializer) to hand-organize the internal topological layer,
080
      by assigning a small subset of the high-dimensional inputs into the two-dimensional hidden layer.
081
      The organization should be based on the initializer’s subjective knowledge in which he/she assigns
082   similar inputs close to each other while distancing dissimilar inputs. This internal organization
083   becomes the initial state of the rRBF. The idea here is to allow humans to initialize the neural
084   network before the training process, and thus infusing the initializer’s knowledge or preferences
085   into the neural network. Here, it is expected that the rRBF subsequently inherits the initializer’s
086   knowledge during and after the learning process.
087
      We are aware that there are vast collections of dimensionality reduction methods that can be con-
088   sidered as the base for this study, for example, (van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008), (McInnes et al.,
089   2018), (Zhang et al., 2018). However, we chose the rRBF’s low-dimensionality due to its easy hu-
090   man interpretation. The chosen representation allows subjective and intuitive translation from high-
091   dimensional labeled input similarities into two-dimensional relative distances on the representation
092   space.
093
      The dynamics and the knowledge infusion are elaborated in the subsequent sessions.

      3   E XPERIMENTS
247
248
      In the experiment, cross-sectional brain MRI data for mild to moderate Alzheimer’s Disease (AD)
      detection provided by Open Access Series of Imaging Studies (Marcus et al., 2007) were used. Some
249
      samples are shown in Fig. 2. The task is a binary classification of normal or AD for each input.
250
251   From the data, 40 images were taken to be hand-organized by human initializers. Each initializer
252   was asked to organize the images according to his/her preferences on the topological layer of the
253   rRBF. Six initializers participated in this experiment; none were medical experts, so the images
254
      were organized based on their appearances and labels. Each initializer made an educated guess
      that similar images with identical labels should be assigned close to each other, and images with
255
      different appearances should be distanced. We understand that this assumption may differ from
256
      medical insight, but it is enough to infuse common-sensical knowledge into neural networks at this
257   stage. After the HITL initialization, the rRBF was trained with training data of 637 and then tested
258   against testing data of 159.
259
260
      Hereafter, human-initialized rRBF will be denoted as HITL-rRBF.
261   Figure 3 shows three initial organizations arranged by three different initializers. It can be observed
262   that they have distinctive topological organizations that reflect the difference in their common sense.
263   The difference in the heatmaps depicts the difference in each initializer’s common sense. It can
264   be observed that the resulting CRSOMs inherit the human-organized initial representations. This
265   demonstrates that it is possible to infuse human knowledge into neural networks.
266   The HITL process is not necessarily executed only at the start of the learning process but at any
267   stage. Here, after the termination of the learning process, humans can visualize the CRSOM and
268   find some regions where the HITL-rRBFs are likely to misclassify. This can be done by finding
269   tangled representations, such as adjacent neurons on the CRSOM with conflicting labels. The cor-

430   their environments, in the case of Reinforcement Learning, but not directly from human knowledge,
431   preferences, or experiences. The subjectivity of human knowledge and preferences is often difficult
      to formulate mathematically and express as structured data and, thus, difficult to be presented to neu-

489   It should be noted that the base neural network, rRBF, is essential for this objective. Here, it is im-
490
      portant for humans to understand the relations of high-dimensional inputs’ representation in the base
      neural network. In rRBF humans can subjectively or common-sensically project high-dimensional
491
      inputs onto the two-dimensional hidden layers, because the relative two-dimensional positions on
492
      the hidden layer can be subjectively translated into similarities of the high-dimensional inputs. This
493   translation from inputs to hidden representation cannot be done on most layered neural networks as
494   humans can’t understand the hidden representations of the neural networks.
495
496
      We are aware that this study is still in its preliminary stage. However, the experiments show promis-
      ing results in demonstrating that infusing human knowledge into neural networks is possible. For
497
      this research, the knowledge levels of the human initializers are indistinguishable. Hence, linking
498
      the resulting neural networks’ expertise level with that of their human initializers is impossible. This
499   will be our immediate future work.
500
501   The ability of neural networks from humans will form a new relationship between humans and AI.
502
      It will be possible to use personalized AI that can better emulate humans. It will also be possible
      to use AI as a kind of knowledge preservation mechanism that can subsequently be used for knowl-
503
      edge transfer: not only transfer from humans to machines, but also machines to humans, and most
504
      interestingly, human to human using AI as an intermediary.
