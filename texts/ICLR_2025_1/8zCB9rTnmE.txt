009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Medical image sequences, generated by both 2D video-based examinations and
012            3D imaging techniques, consist of sequential frames or slices that capture the
013            same anatomical entities (e.g., organs or lesions) from multiple perspectives. Ex-
014            isting segmentation studies typically process medical images using either 2D or
015            3D methods in isolation, often overlooking the inherent consistencies among these
016            images. Additionally, interactive segmentation, while highly beneficial in clinical
017            scenarios, faces the challenge of integrating text prompts effectively across multi-
018            modalities. To address these issues, we introduce an innovative task, Referring
019
               Medical Image Sequence Segmentation for the first time, which aims to seg-
               ment the referred anatomical entities corresponding to medical text prompts. We
020
               develop a strong baseline model, Text-Promptable Propagation (TPP), designed
021
               to exploit the intrinsic relationships among sequential images and their associated
022            textual descriptions. TPP supports the segmentation of arbitrary objects of inter-
023            est based on cross-modal prompt fusion. Carefully designed medical prompts are
024            fused and employed as queries to guide image sequence segmentation through
025            triple-propagation. We curate a large and comprehensive benchmark covering
026            4 modalities and 20 different organs and lesions. Experimental results consis-
027            tently demonstrate the superior performance of our approach compared to previ-
028            ous methods across these datasets. Code and data are available at TPP.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In the realm of medical imaging, both 2D video-based examinations, such as endoscopy and ultra-
      sound, and 3D imaging techniques like CT and MRI, produce sequential frames or slices that capture
034
      the same anatomical entities (e.g., organs and lesions). These image sequences are not merely col-
035
      lections of individual snapshots but are deeply interconnected, with each frame or slice providing
036   a unique view of the same object from different angles and in varying shapes. The consistencies
037   among these sequential images are crucial for comprehensive medical analysis and diagnosis.
038
039
      Current studies on medical image segmentation involve advanced machine learning tools designed
      to automatically identify and separate different organs, tissues, or pathological regions from medical
040
      images like CT scans, MRIs, and X-rays. These medical segmentation models are essential for tasks
041
      such as disease screening, organ segmentation, and anomaly detection. As depicted in Figure 1
042   (a-c), these models face two primary drawbacks: 1) Separate network architectures for 2D and 3D
043   medical images. Researchers often apply distinct methodologies for 2D and 3D medical images,
044   using 2D approaches for planar images or slices (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2021) and
045   3D techniques for volumetric data (Milletari et al., 2016; Zhao et al., 2023). These networks neglect
046   the consistency across temporal frames from 2D video-based examinations and sequential slices
047   from 3D volumes when images are interrelated or part of a sequence, leading to discrepancies and
048   sub-optimal outcomes. 2) Limitation to closed sets of categories and lack of human interactions.
049   In multi-class segmentation tasks, existing methods (Chen et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022) typically
050
      restrict results to predefined classes, reducing flexibility and preventing the specification of particular
      classes for referring segmentation. This rigidity limits the adaptation of segmentation processes to
051
      specific clinical needs or emerging requirements in complex medical scenarios.
052
053   To address these challenges, we introduce an innovative task, Referring Medical Image Sequence
      Segmentation, which aims to identify and segment anatomical entities corresponding to given text

148   a semantic integrated module and introduced a visual-linguistic contrastive loss to apply semantic
149   supervision on video-level object representations. Inspired by these works, we introduce a new task
150   termed Referring Medical Image Sequence Segmentation. We process both 2D and 3D medical
151   data into image sequences and perform an in-depth exploration of clip-level consistency within the

195   morphological aspects of the object.

367   4.2    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
368
369
      For all datasets, we convert videos into frames and 3D volumes into 2D slices. Images without
      a valid object are filtered out. In total, 3,644 sequences are used in training and 1,061 sequences
370
      for testing. Data augmentation techniques include random horizontal flip, random resize, random
371
      crop, and photometric distortion. All images are resized to a maximum length of 640 pixels. The
372   coefficients for the losses are set as λL1 = 5, λgiou = 2, λdice = 5, λf ocal = 2, and λcls = 2. We
373   adopt 4 encoder layers and 4 decoder layers in the Transformer, and the initial query number Nq is
374   set to 5. Both the hidden dimension of the Transformer and the channel dimension of text prompts
375   are C = 256. During training, 3 temporal images from a sequence are randomly sampled and fed
376   into the model at each iteration. Our model is trained on 2 RTX 3090 24GB GPUs with a batch
377   size of 1 per GPU, using AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate of 10−5 for 5 epochs. The
      learning rate decays by 0.1 at the 3rd epoch.

      5      C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce a new task, termed Referring Medical Image Sequence Segmentation, ac-
532
      companied by a large and comprehensive benchmark. The benchmark includes 20 different anatom-
533
      ical entities across 4 modalities from various regions of the body. We present an innovative text-
534   promptable approach that effectively leverages the inherent sequential relationships and textual cues
535   within medical image sequences to segment referred objects, serving as a strong baseline for this
536   task. By integrating both 2D and 3D medical images through a triple-propagation strategy, we
537   demonstrate significant improvements across a broad spectrum of medical datasets, emphasizing
538   the potential for rapid response in segmenting referred objects and enabling accurate diagnosis in
539   clinical practice. Future work should delve deeper into optimizing prompts and exploring additional
      modalities to further enhance the efficacy of medical image analysis.
