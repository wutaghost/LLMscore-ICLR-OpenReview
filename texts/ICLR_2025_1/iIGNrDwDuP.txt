                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Diffusion transformers (DiT) have already achieved appealing synthesis and scal-
010
               ing properties in content recreation, e.g., image and video generation. However,
011            scaling laws of DiT are less explored, which usually offer precise predictions re-
012            garding optimal model size and data requirements given a specific compute bud-
013            get. Therefore, experiments across a broad range of compute budgets, from 1e17
014            to 6e18 FLOPs are conducted to confirm the existence of scaling laws in DiT for
015            the first time. Concretely, the loss of pretraining DiT also follows a power-law
016            relationship with the involved compute. Based on the scaling law, we can not only
017            determine the optimal model size and required data but also accurately predict the
018            text-to-image generation loss given a model with 1B parameters and a compute
019
               budget of 1e21 FLOPs. Additionally, we also demonstrate that the trend of pre-
               training loss matches the generation performances (e.g., FID), even across various
020
               datasets, which complements the mapping from compute to synthesis quality and
021
               thus provides a predictable benchmark that assesses model performance and data
022            quality at a reduced cost.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Scaling laws in large language models (LLMs) (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hestness et al., 2017; Henighan
028   et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) have been widely observed and validated, suggesting that pre-
029   training performance follows a power-law relationship with the compute C. The actual compute
030   could be roughly calculated as C = 6N D, where N is the model size and D is the data quantity.
031   Therefore, determining the scaling law helps us make informed decisions about resource allocation
032   to maximize computational efficiency, namely, figure out the optimal balance between model size
033
      and training data (i.e., the optimal model and data scale) given a fixed compute budget. However,
      scaling laws in diffusion models remain less explored.
034
035   The scalability has already been demonstrated in diffusion models, especially for diffusion trans-
036   formers (DiT). Specifically, several prior works (Mei et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) reveal that larger
037   models always result in better visual quality and improved text-image alignment. However, the scal-
038   ing property of diffusion transformers is clearly observed but not accurately predicted. Besides, the
039
      absence of explicit scaling laws also hinders a comprehensive understanding of how training budget
      relate to model size, data quantity, and loss. As a result, we cannot determine accordingly the opti-
040
      mal model and data sizes for a given compute budget and accurately predict training loss. Instead,
041
      heuristic configuration searches of models and data are required, which are costly and challenging
042   to ensure optimal balance.
043
044   In this work, we characterize the scaling behavior of diffusion models for text-to-image synthesis,
045
      resulting in the explicit scaling laws of DiT for the first time. To investigate the explicit relationship
      between pretraining loss and compute, a wide range of compute budgets from 1e17 to 6e18 FLOPs
046
      are used. Models ranging from 1M to 1B are pretrained under given compute budgets. As shown in
047
      Fig. 1, for each compute budget, we can fit a parabola and extract an optimal point that corresponds
048   to the optimal model size and consumed data under that specific compute constraint. Using these
049   optimal configurations, we derive scaling laws by fitting a power-law relationship between compute
050   budgets, model size, consumed data, and training loss. To evaluate the derived scaling laws, we
051   extrapolate the compute budget to 1.5e21 FLOPs that results in the compute-optimal model size
052   (approximately 1B parameters) and the corresponding data size. Therefore, a 1B-parameter model is
053   trained under this budget and the final loss matches our prediction, demonstrating the effectiveness
      and accuracy of our scaling laws.

091   Diffusion Transformers Transformers have become the de facto model architecture in language
092   modeling (Radford, 2018; Radford et al., 2019; Devlin et al., 2019), and they have also achieved
093   significant success in computer vision tasks (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; He et al., 2021). Recently,
094   Transformers have been introduced into diffusion models (Peebles & Xie, 2023), where images are
095   divided into patches (tokens), and the diffusion process is learned on these tokens. Additional con-
096
      ditions, such as timestep and text, are incorporated into the network via cross-attention (Chen et al.,
      2023), Adaptive Normalization (Perez et al., 2017), or by concatenating them with image tokens
097
      (Bao et al., 2023). Zheng et al. (2023) proposed masked transformers to reduce training costs, while
098
      Lu et al. (2024) introduced techniques for unrestricted resolution generation. Diffusion Transformers
099   (DiTs) inherit the scalability, efficiency, and high capacity of Transformer architectures, positioning
100   them as a promising backbone for diffusion models. Motivated by this scalability, we investigate the
101   scaling laws governing these models in this work. To ensure robust and clear conclusions, we adopt
102   a vanilla Transformer design (Vaswani et al., 2023), using a concatenation of image, text, and time
103   tokens as input to the models.
104

106   performance of neural networks as they scale in size and data. This concept has been validated
107   across several large pretraining models (Dubey et al., 2024; Bi et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023).
      Kaplan et al. (2020); Henighan et al. (2020) were the first to formalize scaling laws in language

      3.1                       BASIC S ETTINGS
138
139   Unless otherwise stated, all experiments in this paper adhere to the same basic settings. While the
140   training techniques and strategies employed may not be optimal, they primarily affect the scaling
141   coefficients rather than the scaling trends. In this section, we outline the critical settings used in our
142   experiments. Additional details are provided in Appendix C.
143
144   Diffusion Formulation All experiments are conducted using the Rectified Flow (RF) formulation
145   (Liu et al., 2022; Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo et al., 2023) with v-prediction. For timestep sampling,
146   we adopt the Logit-Normal (LN) Sampling scheduler πln (t; m, s), as proposed in Esser et al. (2024).
147   A detailed ablation study of this choice can be found in Appendix F.1.
148
149   Models & Dataset As noted in Kaplan et al. (2020), the model design does not significantly impact
150   scaling trends unless the architecture is extremely shallow or slim. We employ a vanilla transformer
151
      architecture (Vaswani et al., 2023) with minimal modifications. The text, image, and timestep tokens
      are concatenated as input (in-context conditioning (Peebles & Xie, 2023)). A dataset of 108 million
152
      image-text pairs is randomly sampled from Laion-Aesthetic (Schuhmann et al., 2022). This subset
153
      is then re-captioned using LLAVA 1.5 (Liu et al., 2024). A validation set consisting of 1 million
154   pairs is created from this subset. Further details can be found in Appendix C.1 and C.2.
155
156   Scaling Metrics A natural question arises when investigating scaling laws during training: What
157   metrics should be selected to observe scaling behavior? In the context of Large Language Models
158   (LLMs), the standard approach is autoregressive training (Radford, 2018; Radford et al., 2019),
159   where the model is trained to predict the next token in a sequence, directly optimizing the likelihood
160   of the training data. This has proven to be a reliable method for measuring model performance as
161   the compute budget scales up. Inspired by this approach, we extend the concept of scaling laws to
      diffusion models, using loss and likelihood as our key metrics.

179   The training loss is estimated using a Monte Carlo method, which involves timesteps and noise
180   sampling. The stochasticity inherent in this process can cause significant fluctuations, which are
181   mitigated by employing a larger batch size of 1024 and applying Exponential Moving Average
182   (EMA) smoothing. In our experiments, we set αEMA = 0.9, which is found to produce stable results.

184   smoothing procedure helps reduce variance and provides clearer insights into training dynamics.
185   In addition to the training loss, validation loss is also computed on the COCO 2014 dataset
186   (Lin et al., 2015). To ensure consistency with the training loss, timesteps are sampled using the
187   LN timestep sampler πln (t; m, s), and evaluation is performed on 10,000 data points, with 1,000
188   timesteps sampled per point.
189
      Likelihood is our secondary metric. The likelihood over the dataset distribution PD given model
190
      parameters θ is represented as Ex∼PD [pθ (x)], which can be challenging to compute directly. In
191   this paper, we measure likelihood using two different methods. The first method is based on the
192   Variational Autoencoder (VAE) framework (Kingma et al., 2021; Song et al., 2021a; Vahdat et al.,
193   2021), which approximates the lower bound of log-likelihood using the Variational Lower Bound
194   (VLB). Since the VAE component in our experiments is fixed to Stable Diffusion 1.5, terms related
195   to the VAE remain constant and are ignored in our computation, as they do not affect the scaling
196   behavior. The second method uses Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) (Chen et al.,
197   2019; Grathwohl et al., 2018), enabling the computation of exact likelihood through reverse-time
198   sampling.
199
200   Variational Lower Bound (VLB) We estimate the VLB following the approach in Kingma et al.
201
      (2021), where it is treated as a reweighted version of the validation loss. The process starts by con-
      verting the estimated velocity into a corresponding estimate of x0 , after which the loss is computed
202
      based on the difference between the estimated x0 and the original sample. To obtain the VLB, this
203
      loss is further reweighted with the weighting coefficient being the derivative of signal-to-noise ratio
204                                                     ′
      of noisy samples with respect to time t, i.e., SNR (t). More details can be found in Appendix D. All
205
      models are evaluated on the COCO 2014 validation set using 10,000 data pairs. For each data point,
206   1,000 timesteps are sampled to ensure accurate estimations of the VLB.
207
208   Exact Likelihood The exact likelihood is computed using reverse-time sampling, where a clean
209   sample is transformed into Gaussian noise. The accumulated likelihood transition is calculated using

246   Scaling Laws In this section, we investigate the scaling laws governing diffusion transformers,
247   which describe the relationships between several key quantities: the objective function, model pa-
248   rameters, tokens, and compute. The objective measures discrepancy between the data and model’s
249
      predictions. The number of parameters N reflects model’s capacity, while tokens D denote the total
      amount of data (in tokens) processed during training. Compute C, typically measured in Floating
250
      Point Operations (FLOPs), quantifies the total computational resources consumed. In our exper-
251
      iments, the relationship between compute, tokens, and model size is formalized as C = 6N D,
252   directly linking the number of parameters and tokens to the overall compute budget.
253
254   Building on this, we hypothesize that power law equations can effectively capture the scaling re-
255
      lationships between these quantities. Specifically, we represent the optimal model size and token
      count as functions of the compute budget as follows:
256
257                                      Nopt ∝ C a    and    Dopt ∝ C b ,                                (4)
258
259   where Nopt and Dopt denote the optimal number of parameters and tokens for a given compute budget
260   C, with a and b as scaling exponents that describe how these quantities grow with compute.
261
      To empirically verify these scaling relationships, following Approach 2 in Hoffmann et al. (2022),
262
      we plot the isoFLOP figure to explore scaling laws. We select compute budgets [1e17, 3e17,
263
      6e17, 1e18, 3e18, 6e18]. We change the In-context Transformers from 2 layers to 15 layers.
264   For all experiments, we use AdamW (Kingma & Ba, 2017; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) as the default
265   optimizer. Except for the 1B parameter prediction experiments, we apply a constant learning rate of
266   1e-4. In the 1B parameter experiments, we train the model with a learning rate of 1e-4 for 80%
267   of the total iterations, then decay it to 3.16e-5 for the next 10%, and finally to 1e-5 for the last
268   10%. This learning rate decay is crucial for larger models, leading to more accurate predictions.
269   Following Wortsman et al. (2023); Team (2024); Molybog et al. (2023), we use a weight decay of
      0.01, an epsilon value of 1e-15, and betas [0.9, 0.95]. For all experiments, we employ a batch size

299   3.3   P REDICTING G ENERATION P ERFORMANCE
300
301   In generative tasks, relying solely on training loss to evaluate performance often falls short of cap-
302   turing the perceptual quality of generated images. Training loss does not fully reflect how closely
303   the generated images resemble real ones or how well they align with textual prompts in terms of
304
      content and style. To address this limitation, the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) is commonly
      used to evaluate image quality.
305
306
      Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) FID measures the distance between the distributions of gener-
307
      ated and real images in a feature space, with lower FID values indicating higher image quality. For
308   our experiments, we utilize compute-optimal models at each training budget, generating samples
309   using the Euler discretization method with 25 steps and a classifier-free guidance (CFG) scale of
310   10.0. Details on the impact of steps and CFG scale are provided in Appendix F.3. To compute FID,
311   we follow the approach of Sauer et al. (2021), using CLIP features instead of traditional Inception
312   features. Specifically, we employ ViT-L/14 Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) as the vision encoder to extract
313   features from both generated and dataset images and compare their statistics.
314
315   Scaling laws for Performance Predictions Our analysis reveals that the relationship between FID
316   and the training budget follows a clear power-law trend, as shown in Fig. 3 (left). The relationship
317
      is captured by the following equation:
318                                      FID = 2.2566 × 106 · C −0.234 ,                                   (8)
319   where C is the training budget. The purple dots in the figure represent the FID scores of compute-
320   optimal models at various budgets, and the orange line represents the fitted power-law curve. No-
321   tably, the prediction for FID at a large budget of 1.5e21 FLOPs (blue pentagon) is highly accurate,
322   confirming the reliability of scaling laws in forecasting model performance even at larger scales. As
323   the compute budget increases, FID values decrease consistently, demonstrating that scaling laws can
      effectively model and predict the quality of generated images as resources grow.

      4   S CALING LAWS AS A PREDICTABLE BENCHMARK
418
419
      Scaling laws offer a robust framework for evaluating the design quality of both models and datasets.
420
      Previous work such as Dubey et al. (2024); Bi et al. (2024) have all explored the scaling laws in data
421   mix and quality. By modifying either the model architecture or the data pipeline, isoFLOP curves
422   can be generated at smaller compute budgets to assess the impact of these changes. Specifically,
423   after making adjustments to the model or dataset, experiments can be conducted across a range of
424   smaller compute budgets, and the relationship between compute and metrics such as loss, parameter
425   count, or token count can be fitted. The effectiveness of these modifications can then be evaluated
426   by analyzing the exponents derived from the power-law fits.
427   Our evaluation follows three key principles:
428
429          • Model improvements: With a fixed dataset, a more efficient model will exhibit a lower
430            model scaling exponent and a higher data scaling exponent. This suggests that the model
431            can more effectively utilize the available data, allowing for a greater focus on increasing
               the dataset size with additional compute resources.

            • Data improvements: When the model is fixed, a higher-quality dataset will result in a
433           lower data scaling exponent and a higher model scaling exponent. This implies that a
434           better dataset enables the model to scale more efficiently, yielding superior results with
435           fewer resources.
436
437         • Loss improvements: Across both model and data modifications, an improved training
438
              pipeline is reflected in a smaller loss scaling exponent relative to compute. This indicates
              that the model achieves better performance with less compute, signaling overall gains in
439
              training efficiency.
440

442   are clearly illustrated in Fig. 5. The loss scaling curves demonstrate that as the compute budget

444   Transformer shows a steeper decline in loss compared to the In-Context Transformer, indicating that
445   it achieves better performance with the same amount of compute.

      5   D ISCUSSION
467
468   Limitations While our work demonstrates the presence of scaling laws in Diffusion Transform-
469   ers (DiT), several limitations must be acknowledged. First, we used fixed hyperparameters, such
470
      as learning rate and batch size, across all experiments. A more comprehensive investigation into
      how these parameters should be adjusted as budgets scale may lead to more precise predictions.
471
      Second, our study focuses solely on text-to-image generation, leaving the scalability of Diffusion
472
      Transformers with other data modalities, such as video, unexplored. Finally, we limited our analysis
473   to In-Context Transformers and Cross-Attention Transformers, leaving the evaluation of additional
474   model variants to future research.
475
476
477
      Conclusion In this work, we explored the scaling laws of Diffusion Transformers (DiT) across
      a broad range of compute budgets, from 1e17 to 6e18 FLOPs, and confirmed the existence of
478
      a power-law relationship between pretraining loss and compute. This relationship enables precise
479
      predictions of optimal model size, data requirements, and model performance, even for large-scale
480   budgets such as 1e21 FLOPs. Furthermore, we demonstrated the robustness of these scaling laws
481   across different datasets, illustrating their generalizability beyond specific data distributions. In
482   terms of generation performance, we showed that training budgets can be used to predict the visual
483   quality of generated images, as measured by metrics like FID. Additionally, by testing both In-
484   context Transformers and Cross-Attention Transformers, we validated the potential of scaling laws
485   to serve as a predictable benchmark for evaluating and optimizing both model and data design,
      providing valuable guidance for future developments in text-to-image generation using DiT.
