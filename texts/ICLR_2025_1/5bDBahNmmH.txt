009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We recast existing works on probabilistic dynamics forecasting through a unified
012            framework connecting turbulence and diffusion principles: Cohesion. Specifi-
013            cally, we relate the coherent part of nonlinear dynamics as a conditioning prior
014            in a denoising process, which can be efficiently estimated using reduced-order
015            models. This fast generation of long prior sequences allows us to reframe fore-
016
               casting as trajectory planning, a common task in RL. This reformulation is bene-
               ficial because we can perform a single conditional denoising pass for an entire
017
               sequence, rather than autoregressively over long lead time, gaining orders-of-
018
               magnitude speedups with little performance loss. Nonetheless, Cohesion supports
019            flexibility through temporal composition that allows iterations to be performed
020            over smaller subsequences, with autoregressive being a special case. To ensure
021            temporal consistency within and between subsequences, we incorporate a model-
022            free, small receptive window via temporal convolution that leverages large NFEs
023            during denoising. Finally, we perform our guidance in a classifier-free manner
024            to handle a broad range of conditioning scenarios for zero-shot forecasts. Our
025            experiments demonstrate that Cohesion outperforms state-of-the-art probabilistic
026            emulators for chaotic systems over long lead time, including in Kolmogorov Flow
027
               and Shallow Water Equation. Its low spectral divergence highlights Cohesion’s
               ability to resolve multi-scale physical structures, even in partially-observed cases,
028
               and are thus essential for long-range, high-fidelity, physically-realistic emulation.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Solving Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) with probabilistic emulators has gained significant
034   momentum relative to their deterministic counterpart Gao et al. (2024a); Rühling Cachay et al.
035   (2024); Gilpin (2024) due to their ability to generate ensemble forecasts that facilitate uncertainty
036   quantification useful for decision making processes Bhatnagar et al. (2019); Brandstetter et al.
037   (2022a;b); Guo et al. (2016); Li et al. (2020); Lu et al. (2021). In particular, diffusion, a power-
038   ful class of probabilistic model, has been widely used as emulators in an autoregressive manner to
039
      produce sequential forecasts over a target lead time, ∆t Li et al. (2024); Price et al. (2023); Lippe
      et al. (2024). However, this probabilistic forecasting approach poses several challenges. First, the
040
      conditional denoising process to estimate p(u | c), where u ∈ Rnu and c ∈ Rnu are the state and
041
      conditioning vector respectively, is generally tied to the condition-generating process. This is pri-
042   marily due to the use of (u, c) data pair during training, such that whenever the likelihood p(c | u)
043   changes, one would require further fine-tuning and re-training Stock et al. (2024); Zhao et al. (2024);
044   Gong et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2024a); Chen et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2024b); Hua et al. (2024); Li
045   et al. (2024). Second, a diffusion-based autoregressive approach is extremely costly as one needs to
046   perform multiple denoising passes such that the number of function evaluation (NFEs) grows pro-
047   portionately with the number of discretization of ∆t Price et al. (2023); Lippe et al. (2024). This is a
048   problem for many long-range forecasting applications, in weather and climate domains for example,
049   where previous gains in inference speed achieved by deterministic data-driven emulators are quickly
050
      offset.
051   As such, we introduce Cohesion (Figure 1), a diffusion-based forecasting framework that incorpo-
052   rates turbulence and reinforcement learning (RL) principles to achieve accurate and stable long roll-
053   outs with orders-of-magnitude inference speedups. First, by leveraging the idea of low-dimensional,
      coherent flow in turbulence as a conditioning factor within diffusion, we can efficiently generate long

901
902   We define a new coordinate system on the spherical domain x ∈ S 2 in terms of longitude φ ∈
903   [0, 2π] and colatitude θ ∈ [0, π] Bonev et al. (2023). The unit vector x can then be reparamterized
904   as (cos(φ) sin(θ), sin(φ) sin(θ), cos(θ))T . Given this coordinate transform, we define the set of
905   differential equations describing SWE:
906
907                                                                 ∂t φ + ∇ · (φu) = 0
                                                                                                                                                 (32)
908                                                                 ∂t (φu) + ∇ · T = S
909
910   with initial conditions φ = φ0 , u = u0 . The state vector (φ, φuT )T includes both the geopotential
911   layer depth φ (representing mass) and the tangential momentum vector φu (indicative of discharge).
912   Within curvilinear coordinates, the flux tensor T can be expressed using the outer product φu ⊗ u.
913   The right-hand side of the equation features flux-related terms, such as the Coriolis force.
914   Experimental setup. We use spectral method Giraldo (2001) to solve the PDE on an equiangular
915   grid with a spatial resolution of 120 × 240 and 60-second timesteps. Time-stepping is performed
916   using the third-order Adams-Bashford scheme and snapshots are taken every 5 hour for a total of 12
917   days, keeping the last 32 temporal sequences of vorticity outputs. The parameters of the PDE, such
      as gravity, radius of the sphere and angular velocity, are referenced to the Earth.
