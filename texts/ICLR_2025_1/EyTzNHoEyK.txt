011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine Learning (ML) has the potential to revolutionise the field of automotive
014            aerodynamics, enabling split-second flow predictions early in the design process.
015            However, the lack of open-source training data for realistic road cars, using high-
016
               fidelity CFD methods, represents a barrier to their development. To address this, a
               high-fidelity open-source (CC-BY-SA) public dataset for automotive aerodynamics
017
               has been generated, based on 500 parametrically morphed variants of the widely-
018
               used DrivAer notchback generic vehicle. Mesh generation and scale-resolving CFD
019            was executed using consistent and validated automatic workflows representative of
020            the industrial state-of-the-art. Geometries and rich aerodynamic data are published
021            in open-source formats. To our knowledge, this is the first large, public-domain
022            dataset for complex automotive configurations generated using high-fidelity CFD.
023

025   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   External aerodynamics plays an important role in the design of road vehicles (30). Aerodynamic drag
028   directly influences emissions from internal combustion engines, and the range of electric vehicles. For
029   high-performance cars, the magnitude and distribution of aerodynamic downforce are key factors in
030   cornering and handling (37). Pressure fluctuations from turbulent airflow can give rise to aeroacoustic
031   noise sources, with a negative impact on passenger comfort (53). Especially in the consumer vehicle
032
      segment, these factors are decisive differentiators influencing product competitiveness. Alongside
      aesthetic design, aerodynamics is therefore central in defining the external shape of the vehicle.
033
034   Engineering approaches to evaluate the external aerodynamics and aeroacoustics of road vehicles
035   began in the 1960s with wind tunnels, which remain an important tool to this day (64). Although
036   an approximation of true open-road conditions, wind tunnels have the advantage of measuring real
037   airflow over real vehicles (often at full scale) (31). Furthermore, variations of vehicle configurations
038
      (e.g. wheel and tyre options, ride height) and operating conditions (e.g. vehicle speed, yaw angle) can
      be assessed very efficiently. Alongside forces, modern techniques also allow measurement of surface
039
      pressure and off-body quantities at selected locations (61).
040
041   Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a more recent innovation, routinely used since the 1990s
042   (30). CFD can simulate real open-road conditions and provide insight into the entire flow field.
043   However, each configuration change requires a separate simulation, so CFD is typically used only
044
      for a subset of scenarios measured in the wind tunnel. Furthermore, computational cost necessitates
      modelling approximations, most notably regarding the onset and effects of turbulence.
045
046   Strategies for turbulence modelling in CFD (59) occupy different positions in the trade-off between
047   computational cost and accuracy. Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) approaches predict the
048   time-averaged flow field using statistical turbulence models. They are inexpensive and run within a
049   few hours, however the correlation to measurements is unreliable for complex flows (e.g. large-scale
050
      flow separation) (9; 4; 5; 8).
051   Greater accuracy is achieved by resolving some of the turbulent motion using “scale-resolving
052   simulation” (SRS) methods. Depending on the method, these time-accurate simulations can easily
053   consume an order of magnitude greater computational resources than RANS, due to the wide range
      of length scales inherent to turbulence (3). A road vehicle is metres long and multiple-second


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      time samples are required for robust statistics. Resolving all turbulent eddies via Direct Numerical
055   Simulation (DNS), or only the largest local scales via wall-resolved Large-Eddy Simulation (WRLES),
056   will remain unaffordable for decades to come, since the eddies are extremely small close to the vehicle
057   surface at full-scale Reynolds number.
058
      Bridging the inner boundary layer region using wall-modelled LES (WMLES) (39) delivers a
059
      significant cost reduction. However the eddies in the outer boundary layer still need to be resolved,
060
      and towards the front of the vehicle the boundary layers are thin (in the order of mm). Pioneering
061   WMLES have been demonstrated e.g. by participants of the Automotive CFD Prediction Workshop
062   series (33), but the approach is not yet routinely applied in industry.
063
064
      Hybrid RANS-LES (HRLES) methods (such as Delayed Detached-Eddy Simulation (DDES) (58))
      are the most mature and widely used category of SRS. These generally model attached boundary
065
      layers entirely with RANS, deploying the scale-resolving capability of LES only in separated flow
066
      where the eddies are larger. Typical grids for full scale vehicles have minimum cell spacings in mm,
067   solved with time steps in tenths of milliseconds (33). HRLES is currently applied routinely in the
068   automotive industry as a higher-fidelity option alongside RANS (4; 33). Simulation turnaround times
069   between several hours (overnight) and a few days are typical.

      M ACHINE L EARNING FOR AUTOMOTIVE A ERODYNAMICS
073
      Machine Learning (ML) has the potential to revolutionise automotive aerodynamics by offering faster
074
      and cheaper ways to predict fluid flow. For example, surrogate ML models, once trained, can be used
075   to predict the aerodynamic performance of a given geometry in seconds, rather than hours or days
076   required by CFD. This can be used to give vehicle designers near-instant aerodynamic feedback, as
077   well as to accelerate design optimisation studies (2). The development of ML for fluid dynamics
078   has seen significant progress in recent years, and a review is beyond the scope of this paper (see
079   e.g. the review of Lino et al. (45)). Generally, progress has been achieved in two key directions,
080   namely physics-driven and data-driven approaches. Physics-driven approaches often include the
081   partial differential equation (PDE) to the loss function in methods such as Physics-Informed Neural
082   Networks (PINNs) (57) and Physics-Informed Neural Operators (PINOs) (43).
083   In contrast, data-driven approaches do not explicitly learn a PDE but rather use simulation data to
084   learn the solutions or solution operator (16), e.g. supervised learning to minimise the difference
085   between the predicted and “true” solutions. Examples include message-passing graph neural networks
086   (GNNs) (10; 25), e.g. MeshGraphNets (56; 17; 38) and pure data-driven Neural Operators (40; 41;
087   27; 42)
088   The size and quality of training datasets are of paramount importance to the development of any ML
089   method (especially data-driven approaches). Canonical flows (e.g. boundary layers, vortices, wakes)
090   are already challenging to predict in isolation. In industrial aerodynamics, multiple such features
091   interact in highly non-linear ways, raising the challenge to a higher level. It is therefore doubtful
092   that an ML approach trained only using canonical flows can succeed in complex scenarios. For the
093   same reason, it is important to include complex cases when testing ML approaches intended for such
094   applications. For traditional CFD, the ability of a single model to predict a wide range of different
095
      flows (without the need for ad-hoc tuning) is a hallmark of a general-purpose approach.
096
097   R ELATED W ORK
098
099   The presented dataset, named “DrivAerML”, is not the first aimed at ML in automotive aerodynamics.
100   Jacobs et al. (34) demonstrated the potential for surrogate ML models to speed up automotive design
101   using a dataset of 1000 DrivAer variants but the data has never been made publicly available. The
102   recent “DrivAerNet” dataset of Elrefaie et al. (15), which was generated concurrently to DrivAerML
103   and is also open-source (CC-BY-NC), features an impressive 4000 DrivAer variants. Since the CFD
104
      data was generated with RANS on comparatively coarse meshes (8M to 16M cells), it is inherently in
      the low-fidelity category.
105
106   The DrivAerML dataset has been prepared in conjunction with two further high-fidelity CFD datasets
107   (i.e. deploying SRS methods), for the simpler Ahmed (“AhmedML”) (7) and Windsor (“WindsorML”)
      (6) car bodies. The consistency of file structure and naming conventions among these datasets is

      intended to facilitate the development and testing of ML approaches across multiple datasets, ensuring
109   better robustness and generalisability.

      O BJECTIVES AND M AIN C ONTRIBUTIONS
112
113   Progress in ML for aerodynamics is constrained by the scarcity and expense of high-quality data,
114   and it is the primary motivation of this work to address this. The paper describes the creation and
115   publication of the DrivAerML dataset, as well as the validation of the deployed CFD methods. The
116   DrivAerML dataset has the following specifications:
117
118           • A complex geometry relevant to the field, for which the “OCDA” variant (32) of the
119
                established DrivAer (29; 28) research model has been selected as baseline1 .
120           • A large dataset consisting of 500 variants of the baseline geometry, covering the main
121             features seen on this category of road vehicle.
122           • The use of hybrid RANS-LES, the highest-fidelity scale-resolving CFD approach routinely
123             deployed by the automotive industry, ensuring best possible correlation to experimental data.
124           • Consistency of the dataset, ensured by automated meshing and simulation workflows with
125             statistical quality control.
126
              • A rich dataset, comprising full flow-field data, surface data and application-relevant quanti-
127
                ties (e.g. force and moment coefficients).
128
129           • An open-source dataset, freely available, free of usage constraints and using open-source
130
                file formats.
131
      Based on input from automotive companies, we decided to focus only on geometry variants of
132
      the DrivAer vehicle rather than also changing the boundary conditions (e.g. Reynolds number or
133   incoming flow angle).
134
135   To the best of our knowledge, the DrivAerML dataset represents the first large, open-source ML
136
      training dataset comprising high-fidelity CFD data for complex automotive aerodynamics geometries.
      Although it primarily targets data-driven surrogate ML approaches, the dataset may also prove useful
137
      for other ML approaches, or even for purposes beyond the field of ML entirely.
138
139   The paper is organised as follows: The baseline case and experimental data is described in Sect. ,
140   and the generation of the high-fidelity CFD dataset is covered in Sect. , including descriptions of
141   the methods and their validation. The contents and structure of the dataset are then described in
142
      Sect. before discussing conclusions and limitations of the current work in Sect. . Extensive additional
      material is provided in the Supplementary Information; SI.

145   BASELINE GEOMETRY AND FLOW CONDITIONS
146
147   The DrivAer model was introduced (29; 28) as an open-source road vehicle for research into au-
148   tomotive aerodynamics, giving a more realistic stepping stone from simpler geometries such as
149   the Ahmed (1) and Windsor (62; 54) bodies. A variant including cooling flow was defined by
150   Ford (32). With three defined configurations—estate, fastback and notchback—it has been subject to
151
      numerous experimental studies (29; 65; 12; 62; 31) as well as a wide range of CFD investigations
      (28; 26; 65; 55; 5; 12; 35; 36; 44; 11; 14; 34; 33; 9).
152
153   For the baseline geometry of the DrivAerML dataset, we adopt the specific test case from the 2nd
154   to 4th Automotive CFD Prediction (“AutoCFD”) workshops2 , to benefit from and build upon this
155   extensive body of work (33). The geometry is the notchback variant of the Ford OCDA DrivAer (32)
156   with static wheels, sealed cooling inlets and a detailed underbody, Fig. 1a. Although wheel rotation
157
      effects are important, static wheels were chosen in the AutoCFD workshops to reduce complexity,
      since the focus was on comparing CFD approaches for the overall flow field.

189
190   PARAMETRISATION AND GENERATION OF GEOMETRY VARIANTS
191
      A set of morphing boxes was constructed around the baseline geometry using the ANSA software
192
      of BETA-CAE Systems3 , see Fig. 25a, allowing geometry variants to be created in a systematic
193   manner. Figs. 25b-25d show the 16 morphing parameters and their range constraints, designed to
194   avoid unrealistic shapes based on engineering judgement. The wide range of geometries is intended
195   to produce different flow topologies and to test the generalisability of ML approaches.
196
      A design of experiments (DoE) tool in ANSA was used to create the parametric values for 500
197
      experiments using a Modified Extensible Lattice Sequence algorithm, which fills the parameter
198
      space evenly, also for subsets of and extensions to the dataset. The DoE and morphing process
199   was automated through Python scripts to save each of the 500 variants along with metrics such as
200   wheelbase and frontal area. The choice of 500 geometries was based upon a mixture of computational
201   budget as well as expectations of industrial feasibility. The dataset could be expanded in future work,
202   based on user feedback.
203
204   C OMPUTATIONAL MESHES
205
206   The models were meshed in ANSA version 24.1.0 using the HeXtreme algorithm, which generates
207   hexa-dominant & polyhedral meshes. Approximately 160 million cells were generated in less than
208   an hour and satisfied OpenFOAM quality criteria. The boundary layer mesh was optimised for wall
209
      functions, with 7 layers, a first layer height of 0.75 mm, a total layer height of 12 mm and a variable
      growth rate between 1.2 and 1.4. The choice of high y + mesh vs. resolving to the wall was based
210
      on findings from the 2nd AutoCFD workshop, where direct comparisons by multiple participants
211
      found only a minor difference in results (33), with benefits of significantly faster run times. The mesh
212   in various sensitive areas, such as the wake, the underbody and the mirrors, was refined using size
213   fields that follow the geometry and extend downstream to capture these features, see Fig. 3. The
214   overall mesh resolution and topology was refined based on industrial feedback during the AutoCFD

255   CFD METHODS , WORKFLOW AND VALIDATION
256
257   The open-source software library OpenFOAM (v2212) was used to solve the incompressible Navier–
258   Stokes equations via the finite volume method (FVM) (63). The incompressible (constant density)
259   assumption is valid for such low Mach numbers. The high Reynolds number requires a turbulence
260   model (see Sect. ), for which a scale-resolving HRLES approach is chosen as the highest fidelity level
261
      routinely applied in the automotive industry. The model of Fuchs, Mockett et al. (47; 23; 22) is used,
      which a variant of the Delayed Detached-Eddy Simulation (DDES) approach of Spalart et al. (58).
262
      It uses the Spalart-Allmaras RANS model (60) in the near-wall region and an LES formulation
263
      equivalent to the σ model of Nicoud et al. (52) in regions of separated flow. The latter accelerates the
264   transition from RANS to LES after separation, a problem commonly referred to as the “grey area”
265   (49). The approach has been successfully applied for numerous applications and in different CFD
266   codes (20; 18; 21; 48; 19; 24). To ensure that RANS is active throughout the entire boundary layer,
267   the “enhanced protection” (EP) shielding formulation from Deck & Renard’s ZDES approach (13)
268   is additionally applied. This avoids the problem of modelled stress depletion (46) from unwanted
269   LES-mode activity inside attached boundary layers, which can induce premature flow separation
      where the near-wall grid is fine. See SI for a more detailed description of the simulation approach.

      CFD workflow: The fully-automated simulation workflow, is illustrated in Fig. 4. Taking the mesh
271   as input, the workflow executes domain decomposition, parallel flow simulation and post-processing.
272   Validated, application-specific best practices are automatically applied alongside computational
273   benchmarking data to optimise high-performance computing (HPC) efficiency. The automated
274   workflow ensures setup consistency between simulations.
275
      Achieving statistical consistency is challenging, since the simulation times needed to bridge the
276
      initial transient (memory of arbitrary initial conditions) and converge the mean vary widely and
277   unpredictably from case to case (50). Simulation runtime is dynamically controlled by interfacing
278   the solver with the time series analysis tool Meancalc, which detects initial transient and quantifies
279   statistical error (51). The simulation stopping criteria were:
280
281           1. Initial transient, detected using the drag, lift and side forces, is bridged;
282           2. Target statistical accuracy of ±1.5 drag counts4 is achieved.
283
284   This approach ensures that all data is free of initial transient and meets defined statistical accuracy
285   targets whilst optimising HPC cost. The flow field averaging sample is subsequently trimmed to the
286   optimal transient-free length using a custom OpenFOAM utility, ensuring statistical consistency with
287   the mean integral forces (see SI for more details).

413   ML EVALUATION
414
415   We conducted an example ML evaluation using a Graph Neural Network (GNN) approach (more
416   details in the SI) to demonstrate how this dataset could be used to train a ML model to predict unseen
417   cases. We find that using a 60/20/20 split of train, validation and test, it is possible to obtain a MAE
418   of less than 0.035 for the drag coefficient and MAE less than 0.0164 for the lift coefficient via the
419
      integration of predicted wall-shear stress and pressure quanities on the 8M node surface mesh of the
      vehicles (shown in Figure 7). We hope many more groups will use this data to improve upon this and
420
      go further to also predict the volume quanities using a range of different ML approaches.

423   C ONCLUSIONS
424
425   A new large scale, public dataset has been established to advance the state of the art for the devel-
426   opment of ML methods for the automotive external aerodynamics community. A specific emphasis
427
      has been placed on the high fidelity level of the CFD data, using scale-resolving methods, consistent
      meshing and simulation workflows, and computational grids closely aligned to current industrial best
428
      practices. ML models can therefore be trained and tested with greater confidence regarding their
429
      accuracy and applicability to complex industrial challenges.

      L IMITATIONS
451
452   Whilst the DrivAerML dataset goes beyond current public-domain datasets in several respects, a
453   number of remaining limitations could be addressed in future work. Parameter variations could
454   be expanded to help build more complete ML models, e.g. with variations of inflow conditions
455   (speed and yaw), the effect of moving ground and rotating wheels, or the inclusion of under-hood
456   cooling flow. Different baseline vehicle geometries could expand the dataset to include e.g. fastback,
457   estate, SUV categories. The inclusion of coarser (and potentially finer) mesh resolutions could
458
      facilitate the development of super-resolution and downsampling techniques, as well as addressing
      the challenges of training ML models on such large mesh counts. The dataset could be extended to
459
      include RANS results to investigate transfer learning between fidelity levels, which may lead to more
460
      computationally efficient ML approaches.
461   Whilst there are limitations to this work we believe this is one of the first examples of a large-scale
462   dataset, developed specifically for the automotive community using state-of-the-art CFD approaches.
463   We look forward to learning about other potential uses of the dataset and welcome feedback.

      A     N UMERICAL METHODOLOGY
757
758
      The open-source software library OpenFOAM (v2212) has been employed in this work for generating
759   the dataset. In this case, the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations for a Newtonian fluid are solved
760   to compute the unsteady fluid motion around the car, where the partial differential equations are
761   discretised in the software package via the finite volume method (FVM) (24). In addition, Reynolds-
762   averaging is applied to the equations to reduce the computational costs of solving. However, this
763   introduces additional modelling empiricism in the form of a turbulence model that is subsequently
764   required (see Sect. A.1). The equations can then be written in Einstein notation and Cartesian
765   coordinates as:
766
767                                                  ∂Ui
768                                                  ∂xi = 0 ,                                            (1)
769                                                          h                                i
                                 ∂(Uj Ui )        ∗                                    ∂Uj
770                        ∂Ui
                            ∂t +   ∂xj     = − ∂p     ∂
                                               ∂xi + ∂xj         (ν + νt )       ∂Ui
                                                                                 ∂xj + ∂xi        ,       (2)

      where Eq. 1 describes the conservation of mass and Eq. 2 the conservation of momentum. The
773   three-dimensional vector Ui contains the instantaneous velocity components in all three Cartesian
774   directions, p∗ = p/ρ is the kinematic pressure with the dimensions m2 /s2 and ν is the molecular
775   kinematic viscosity of the fluid (which is a constant fluid property). The quantity νt is referred to as
776   (turbulent) eddy viscosity and results from the Reynolds-averaging procedure and the Boussinesq
777   linear eddy viscosity assumption that is applied to the Navier-Stokes equations. An additional closure
778   model referred to as a turbulence model is required to compute the νt field, which is described in more
779   detail in Sect. A.1. The assumption of an incompressible fluid (i.e. ρ = const.) is commonly used
780   for automotive applications, since local Mach numbers Ma (i.e. ratio between velocity magnitude
781
      |Ui | and speed of sound c, Ma = |Ui |/c) are typically low. The Mach number can be interpreted as
      a measure for compressibility effects. As such a common threshold below which compressibility
782
      effects can be neglected is Ma = 0.3. In this study, the highest local velocities are around 70 m/s so
783
      that the maximum local Mach number is approximately 0.2, which indicates that the compressibility
784   assumption is justified here.
785
786   A fundamental scaling parameter in fluid dynamics is the dimensionless Reynolds number Re, which
787
      represents the ratio between inertial and viscous forces in a flow. It is defined by a characteristic
      velocity scale Uref , a characteristic length scale Lref and the kinematic viscosity ν:
788
789
790
                                                         Uref Lref
                                                  Re =                                                    (3)
791                                                         ν
792   The Reynolds number is often used in fluid dynamics to manage “scale effects”, e.g. between a
793   small-scale test model in a wind tunnel and a full scale production vehicle. In case the Reynolds
794   number is equivalent between both applications, the flow is assumed to be statistically equivalent
795   when all flow quantities are normalised accordingly. In addition, the Reynolds number delivers an
796   indication about the flow regime, where a low Reynolds number corresponds to a laminar flow state
797   (where viscous forces are dominant) and a high Reynolds number triggers a turbulent flow state that
798   is dominated by inertial forces. In external automotive applications, the flow is commonly considered
799
      to be turbulent in nature since Reynolds numbers are typically very high, so that three-dimensional
      turbulent eddies of different temporal and spatial sizes are generated in the flow field.
800
801
      In the following sub-sections, the turbulence modelling approach applied in this work is
802   detailed in Sect. A.1 before the solver and numerical infrastructure is described in Sect. A.2.

      A.2    F LOW SOLVER AND DISCRETISATION
919
920   The flow solver employed in the scale-resolving simulations (SRS) is a derivative of the
921   pimpleFoam solver of the central OpenFOAM v2212 release version, which was customised
922   by Upstream CFD. It is based on the transient SIMPLE algorithm (17) which performs multiple
923   sub-iterations per time step to achieve a sufficient convergence of the coupled non-linear equation
924   system that arises from the discretised Navier-Stokes equations. The custom solver features an
925   enhanced version (12; 15) of the original Rhie-Chow formulation (18) which offers a consistent
926
      pressure-velocity coupling and lower numerical dissipation in the LES regions. The hybrid blending
      scheme of Travin et al. (23) is used to discretise the convection term in the momentum equations,
927
      where a second-order central differencing scheme was employed in regions of resolved turbulence and
928
      a second-order upwind-biased scheme elsewhere. The latter scheme was also used to discretise the
929   convection term in the ν̃ transport equation of the turbulence model. Time integration was performed
930   via a second-order accurate implicit Euler scheme. For the boundary conditions of the turbulence
931   model at solid walls, a formulation based on Spalding’s law of the wall (22) is used that is valid for
932   arbitrary values of the non-dimensional near-wall spacing y + .

935
936   Due to the fact that the Navier-Stokes equations are solved in time, only snapshots of the flow are
937   computed at each physical time step, which can vary greatly due to the chaotic nature of turbulence.
938   To obtain meaningful flow statistics that can be further analysed for engineering purposes, averaging
939
      over multiple time steps has to be conducted. Assuming that a simulation consists of N snapshots /
      calculated time steps, the first and second order flow statistics are then computed via:

                        1            X                              1       X                 2

949   where φ is an arbitrary scalar quantity (e.g. pressure) and Φ is a vector quantity (e.g. velocity). The
950   overbar hereby denotes time-averaged values. In general, using all time steps N of a simulation
951   for computing the flow statisics is not possible, since a time interval at the start of the simulation
952   exists in which the flow field snapshots are still affected by the non-physical initial conditions. This
953   simulation phase is often referred to as “initial transient”. The time-averaging procedure is hence
954   only started at a certain point in time t = n, which has to be determined by the user based on suitable
955   criteria. A second issue concerns the total length of the simulation after the initial transient has been
956   bridged and time-averaging has been started, which determines both the computational expense of
957

959

960   transient simulation by simultaneously evaluating the expected initial transient interval and the
961   statistical accuracy of user-selected variables. An example plot of such an analysis is shown in Fig. 8,

      cases (see Sect. D.6). In the plots, the time axis (i.e. x-axis) is scaled both in physical simulation

967   The normalisation is achieved by multiplying the physical simulation time t with a characteristic
968   velocity and time scale, i.e. CTU = t × U0 /l0 . For this application, the characteristic velocity scale
969   is the freestream velocity U0 = U∞ and the characteristic length scale is set to be the wheelbase
970   of the car l0 = lwheelbase (see Fig. 10). This enables a better comparability between different car
971   geometries, since the time a fluid particle needs to completely pass over the body surface is usually a
      good measure for the physical scales of the flow.

1027            criterion, where the simulation is stopped when a target accuracy (i.e. 95% confidence

1037
       value, as we expect that statistical consistency is important in a machine learning training dataset.

1327   or experimental conditions. In this case, a large open-road domain, sketched in Fig. 11 has been
1328   chosen, which is representative of the types of setups commonly used in the automotive industry.
1329   To mimic the ground boundary layer present in the Pininfarina wind tunnel, the domain floor has
1330   been divided into two parts, an inviscid part upstream of the car (“Slip floor”) and a viscous wall
1331   starting at xBL = −2.339 m (“No-slip floor”), which is located 2.346 m upstream of the front axle.
1332   However, geometrical details of the wind tunnel, such as the upstream nozzle (with 11 m2 area) and
1333   downstream collector of the finite open test section, are not integrated into the CFD setup. The side
1334   and top patches of the CFD domain are prescribed as inviscid walls, and blockage ratios can be
1335
       considered negligible (≈ 0.25%) thanks to the large domain. This computational domain has been
       used for all dataset simulations.
1336
1337   An important further aspect of the CFD setup concerns the location of the reference pressure pref ,
1338   which is set at the outlet patch that located 80 m downstream of the start of the viscous wall patch
1339   (see Fig. 11). Due to the friction losses associated with the car wake and the ground boundary
1340   layer, the static pressure continuously decreases in the downstream direction, so that shifting the
1341
       reference pressure location in the x-direction would result in different values of pref . It is important
       to remember when processing the provided data that the absolute local kinematic pressure values
1342
       p∗ = p/ρ∞ contained in the dataset are influenced by the specific boundary conditions used in
1343
       this setup. This also concerns other quantities derived from the p∗ field such as the static and total
1344   pressure coefficients Cp and Cpt . This means that e.g. surface distributions of Cp provided in the
1345   database might not be strictly comparable to other cases in which different boundary conditions were
1346   used. In contrast, this issue is less relevant for the integral force coefficients, for which only the
1347   relative pressure distribution on the surface is relevant (a constant offset in pref is cancelled out in the
1348   integration).

       D.7     G EOMETRY VARIANTS
2215
2216   500 geometric variations of the DrivAer notchback were created to replicate as closely as possible
2217   the potential range of design studies that an automotive company would create for this category of
2218   vehicle. This differs from the AhmedML (2) and WindsorML (1) datasets, where more freedom was
2219   given to adapt the geometry more strongly given the academic nature of the geometry and to test the
2220   ability of a model to predict a wide range of geometries. This DrivAerML dataset is designed to
2221   test the ability of a ML model to predict subtle and small changes in the geometry, which is more
2222   representative of industry use of CFD for product design.
2223   To achieve this a set of morphing boxes was constructed around the baseline DrivAer Notchback
2224   using the ANSA software of BETA-CAE Systems15 , (see Fig. 25a), allowing geometry variants
2225   to be created in a systematic manner. The morphing box topology prevents undesired distortions
2226   (e.g. the wheels remain circular when the vehicle length is stretched). The parameters for morphing
2227   the baseline geometry are listed together with their ranges in Tab. 5, which were chosen to avoid
2228   unrealistic shapes based on engineering judgement. The range of geometries is intended to produce
2229
       different flow topologies and to test the generalisability of ML approaches, but within a typical
       engineering process.

2257
       Figures 27 & 28 show the variation of drag, lift, front-lift & rear-lift coefficients for each run, the
2258   former for constant Aref and Lref and the latter with these quantities varied for each geometry.
2259   Providing both outputs was intentional, since a constant reference area and length are sometimes
2260   used in the industry. Once multiplied by the area, it gives a sense of the actual force, e.g. a car with a
2261   larger frontal area will most likely produce in absolute terms a larger drag force. However, if one
2262   wants to focus on the aerodynamic efficiency of the vehicle (which is ultimately what the drag or

2811
2812   We have conducted preliminary analysis on our dataset using a modified version of one of the
2813   state-of-the-art scientific machine learning (SciML) methods, MeshGraphNet (5) on various tasks to
2814   illustrate the practicality of the dataset for ML evaluation. We utilize the encoder-processor-decoder
2815   architecture in MeshGraphNets and modify the method to enable it to make time-averaged predictions
2816   (2).
2817   The entire drivaer dataset is split into training (60%), validation (20%), and test (20%) sets. For each
2818   use case, the model is trained on the training set, and the checkpoint that had the best validation error
2819   was used to obtain the inference results on the test set.
2820
       For the DrivAer dataset, using the predicted surface pressure and wall-shear stress on the 8M node
2821   vtp surface mesh, we obtain predictions (shown in Figure 36) for the drag coefficient with a mean
2822   absolute percentage error (MAPE) of 0.032 and a mean absolute error (MAE): 0.009. For the lift
2823   coefficient the mean absolute error (MAE): 0.0164. The surface contours of the actual, predicted
2824   and error for the mean pressure and wall-shear stress are shown in Figure 37. Training time is
2825   approximately 108 hours on x8 NVidia L40s GPUs and the inference time is less than a minute on
2826   the same hardware. Please note that these runs are preliminary and further work to optimize the
2827   methodology and hyperparameters is on-going which will published in future papers.
