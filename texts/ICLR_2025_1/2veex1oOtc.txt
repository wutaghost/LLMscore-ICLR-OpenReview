011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recently, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have garnered widespread
014            attention due to their ability to perceive and understand multimodal signals. How-
015            ever, their large parameter sizes and substantial computational demands severely
016            hinder their practical deployment and application. While quantization is an effec-
017            tive way to reduce model size and inference latency, its application to MLLMs
018            remains underexplored. In this paper, we conduct an in-depth analysis of MLLMs
019
               quantization and identify several challenges: slow inference speed of the visual
               tokens, distributional differences across modalities, and visual outlier clipping
020
               degrades performance. To address these challenges, we propose MQuant, a quanti-
021
               zation framework tailored for MLLMs. Specifically, 1) we design Modality-specific
022            Quantization (MSQ) and Attention-Invariant Flexible Switching (AIFS) to support
023            per-tensor static quantization and facilitate efficient inference. 2) we introduce an
024            unified LayerNorm-to-RMSNorm transformation, achieving seamless integration
025            of the MLLM vision encoder with Hadamard rotation. 3) we propose Rotation
026            Magnitude Suppression (RMS) to mitigate outliers introduced by Hadamard rota-
027            tion. Experiments conducted on five mainstream MLLMs demonstrate the superior
028            performance and broad applicability of MQuant. For example, it maintains around
029            98% of the floating-point accuracy under the W4A8 setting. To the best of our
030
               knowledge, MQuant is the first quantization solution for MLLMs, paving the way
               for future advancements in their application.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Recently, large language models (LLMs) such as GPT (Brown et al., 2020) and the LLaMA se-
034
      ries (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024) have shown remarkable success across various
035
      domains, but they often struggle when handling non-textual data. Multimodal large language models
036   (MLLMs) (Reid et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023) overcome this by incorporating
037   various modalities such as images and video, enabling a comprehensive understanding of diverse data
038   types. However, the vast number of parameters and substantial computational demands due to the
039   processing of multimodal inputs, which severely hinders their practical application. This is especially
040   true in resource-constrained and privacy-sensitive environments.
041
      Quantization is an effective technique for compressing and accelerating neural networks by converting
042   high-precision values (e.g., FP32) to low-precision values (e.g., INT8), significantly reduces memory
043   usage and inference latency. While quantization has been widely applied to LLMs (Yuan et al.,
044   2023a; Shao et al., 2023), its application to MLLMs is essential but remains relatively under-explored.
045
      When quantizing MLLMs, it is essential to consider the differences in inference efficiency that arise
046
      from their multi-modal nature. A typical difference is the Time to First Token (TTFT) in MLLMs
047
      increases significantly with the resolution and aspect ratio of input images or videos. As shown
048   in Fig 1(a), in models such as Qwen2-VL (Wang et al., 2024), the number of prefill visual tokens
049   grows as image resolution increases (detailed figure in Fig. 7). This rapid expansion in token count
050   exacerbates the inference latency, particularly in per-token dynamic quantization, which requires
051   separate processing for each token, such as memory access and scale computation. As a result, the
052   TTFT in MLLMs increases drastically, severely impacting overall inference latency. Moreover, in
053   higher-demand scenarios such as video-based tasks and multi-image dialogues, the accumulation of
      visual tokens becomes even more pronounced, further exacerbating the increase in TTFT.

      in MLLM quantization:❶ Balancing efficiency and accuracy when adopting per-tensor static
073
      quantization for multi-modal tokens. In MLLMs, input multi-modal tokens include visual and
074   textual with distinct value distributions. Unlike per-token dynamic quantization, which can adaptively
075   compute token-wise scales in an online manner, static quantization applies few scale factors across the
076   mixed multi-modal tokens in an offline manner, leading to a difficult trade-off between efficiency and
077   accuracy. ❷ The data distribution of visual and textual tokens exhibits significant differences.
078   Unlike LLMs, which solely handle textual features, MLLMs face the challenge of processing
079   input with distinct distributions across multiple modalities. As shown in Fig 1 (b), the activation
080   distributions between visual and textual tokens reveal substantial numerical discrepancies. Joint
081   quantization of these tokens may cause the larger values of visual tokens to overshadow the smaller
082
      textual values, resulting in a loss of textual information. Therefore, MLLMs require modality-
      specific approaches to account for the heterogeneous nature of visual and textual token distributions.

092   To this end, we propose MQuant, an accurate and efficient post-training quantization (PTQ) solution
093   specifically designed for multimodal large language models (MLLMs). First, to reduce the TTFT
094   while maintaining accuracy, we introduce Modality-Specific Quantization (MSQ), which handles
095   the distinct distribution differences between visual and textual tokens. For further acceleration,
096   we design an Attention-Invariant Flexible Switching (AIFS) scheme. AIFS transforms mixed
097   multimodal tokens into unified, modality-decoupled tokens without the need for dynamic position
098   vectors, maintaining computational equivalence and memory efficiency by avoiding the overhead
099

100

101
      weight outliers caused by the online Hadamard rotations through theoretical analysis and propose
102   Rotation Magnitude Suppression (RMS) to mitigate them, further improving quantization accuracy.

119
      Multimodal Large Language Models. The rapid advancement of LLMs has spurred significant
120
      progress in MLLMs. Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) pioneered connecting pre-trained visual
121
      encoders to LLMs, demonstrating strong generalization across visual-language tasks. Following the
122   emergence of ChatGPT Achiam et al. (2023a), numerous open-source models based on pre-trained
123   LLMs like LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a) and its variants (Touvron et al., 2023b; Zheng et al., 2024)
124   have been proposed (Li et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). Subsequent
125   efforts like Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023b), InternVL (Chen et al., 2024b), and CogVLMV2 Hong et al.
126   (2024) enhanced MLLMs from perspectives such as high-resolution input and larger-scale training
127   data. However, the substantial parameters of MLLMs lead to high computational costs, limiting
128   broader application. Recently, smaller MLLMs like Mini-Gemini (Li et al., 2024), MobileVLM Chu
129   et al. (2023; 2024), and MiniCPM-V (Yao et al., 2024) have emerged. Despite these developments,
130
      dedicated quantization methods for MLLMs to further reduce memory usage and accelerate inference
      remain under-explored.
131
132   Post-Training Quantization for LLMs. Existing post-training quantization (PTQ) methods for
133   LLMs are categorized into weight-only and weight-activation quantization (Zhao et al., 2023; Yuan
134   et al., 2024). Weight-only methods like GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), QuIP (Chee et al., 2024), and
135   AWQ (Lin et al., 2023) achieve high compression rates but offer limited inference acceleration. In con-
136   trast, weight-activation quantization methods (Xiao et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023b;
137   Zhang et al., 2024) quantize both weights and activations, improving memory usage and latency. The
138
      main challenge is activation outliers causing quantization errors. Techniques like SmoothQuant (Xiao
      et al., 2022) shift quantization difficulty from activations to weights, while OmniQuant (Shao et al.,
139
      2023) optimizes performance by training quantization parameters. SliceGPT (Ashkboos et al., 2024a)
140
      reduces memory demands by designing a Pre-LN + Rotate Scheme for LLMs sparsification based on
141   computational invariance. They achieve this by adding a linear layer in the residual connection (see
142   Appendix A.14). Unlike SliceGPT, we further develop a Post-LN + Rotate scheme to accommodate
143   more vision encoder and extends its applicability to various MLLMs. This enhancement broadens the
144   the LayerNorm + Rotate approach, making it suitable for both Pre-LN and Post-LN configurations
145   across various MLLMs. Recently, Quarot (Ashkboos et al., 2024b) introduces rotations to eliminate
146   outliers; however, this solution is not applicable to MLLMs due to inherent modality differences.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528   In this paper, we propose MQuant, the first accurate and efficient post-training quantization solution
529   for multimodal large language models (MLLMs). Addressing their unique challenges, MQuant
530   reduces the time to first token (TTFT) with per-tensor static quantization and introduces modality-
531   specific quantization (MSQ) to handle distribution discrepancies between visual adn textual tokens.
532   Besides, we propose attention-invariant flexible switching (AIFS) to ensure consistent positioning of
533
      multimodal tokens and computational invariance, enhancing quantization efficiency. Furthermore,
      we propose a unified LayerNorm to RMSNorm transformation, enabling seamless integration of the
534
      MLLM vision encoder with Hadamard rotation. Finally, we reveal the weight outliers caused by the
535
      online Hadamard rotations and propose Rotation Magnitude Suppression (RMS) to mitigate them.
536   Extensive experiments on five mainstream MLLMs demonstrate that MQuant attains state-of-the-art
537   PTQ performance, with the W4A8 model matching FP model accuracy. These findings highlight
538   MQuant’s potential to advance MLLM quantization and enhance practical applicability.

       A.12    A DVANTAGE OF MSQ AND AIFS
1027
1028   In per-tensor static quantization, the quantization parameters (i.e., scale and zero-point) are precom-
1029   puted for an entire tensor (e.g., weights or activations) and remain fixed throughout inference. While
1030   efficient, this approach often leads to large and unacceptable accuracy loss in MLLMs due to their
1031   diverse activation distributions across varying inputs.
1032   In contrast, per-token dynamic quantization computes quantization parameters on-the-fly for each
1033   input token during inference. This approach incurs significantly higher computational overhead,
1034   as the quantization parameters must be recalculated for every input token, along with multiple
1035   additional memory traversals. Such requirements make per-token dynamic quantization unfriendly or
1036   impractical for edge devices and some AI accelerators, which struggle with fine-grained dynamic
1037   operations Tan et al. (2024). This issue is especially severe in MLLMs, where the token count
1038   increases significantly with higher image resolution or more video frames. The Modality-Specific
1039   Quantization (MSQ) in MQuant is a novel per-modality quantization approach specifically designed
1040
       to address the unique challenges of MLLMs quantization.
1041   Furthermore, MSQ can be naturally applied to the unified modality-decoupled tokens generated by
1042   AIFS. By integrating MSQ and AIFS, our designs yields three key advantages: (1) Computational
1043   Equivalence and Strong Compatibility: The unified causal mask and token index introduced by
1044   AIFS preserves the inherent causal relationships among tokens, ensuring numerical equivalence
1045   during attention computations. Moreover, since AIFS requires only a one-time rearrangement of the
1046
       input data (adjust causal mask and token index in offline), it does not alter the overall computation
       graph. This characteristic allows for seamless integration with other LLM inference acceleration
1047
       methods, such as FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022), ensuring both computational equivalence and
1048
       strong compatibility. As shown in Table 2, MQuant achieves SOTA quantization performance across
1049   5 mainstream MLLMs. (2) Reduced Inference Latency: MSQ not only addresses the substantial
1050   distributional differences between modalities but also mitigates the significant computational overhead
1051   and increased inference latency caused by the surge in token counts from higher image and video
1052   resolutions. As shown in Table 4, MSQ+AIFS significantly reduces latency from 2.057s to 1.1017s,
1053   closely matching the speed of the per-tensor static setting while maintaining near-lossless accuracy
1054   comparable to the original Float model. (3) Enhanced Memory and Computational Efficiency: By
1055   combining MSQ and AIFS, we convert mixed input tokens into unified, modality-decoupled tokens,
1056   eliminating the irregular memory operations (e.g., slice, concat, pad) introduced by directly applying
1057
       MSQ. This transformation reduces memory consumption and improves efficiency of GEMM kernel,
       which would otherwise be compromised by the interleaved and non-fixed positions of visual and
1058
       textual tokens. As shown in Table 10, MQuant can achieve up to 24.7% speedup and 152.9% memory
1059
       savings.
