037                                                  A BSTRACT
038
039            We introduce Image-GOal Representations (IGOR), aiming to learn a unified, se-
040            mantically consistent action space across human and various robots. Through this
041
               unified latent action space, IGOR enables knowledge transfer among large-scale
               robot and human activity data. We achieve this by compressing visual changes
042
               between an initial image and its goal state into latent actions. IGOR allows us
043
               to generate latent action labels for internet-scale video data. This unified latent
044            action space enables the training of foundation policy and world models across a
045            wide variety of tasks performed by both robots and humans. We demonstrate that:
046            (1) IGOR learns a semantically consistent action space for both human and robots,
047            characterizing various possible motions of objects representing the physical inter-
048            action knowledge; (2) IGOR can “migrate” the movements of the object in the one
049            video to other videos, even across human and robots, by jointly using the latent
050            action model and world model; (3) IGOR can learn to align latent actions with
051            natural language through the foundation policy model, and integrate latent actions
052
               with a low-level policy model to achieve effective robot control. We believe IGOR
               opens new possibilities for human-to-robot knowledge transfer and control. See
053
               video demonstrations on our anonymous webpage.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Learning foundation models for embodied AI has been notably constrained by a lack of interaction
057
      data. Unlike text or video data, which are abundantly available, interaction data is much scarcer.
058
      Research efforts have been devoted to creating large-scale interaction datasets, such as Open-X-
059   Embodiment (Collaboration et al., 2023) and DROID (Khazatsky et al., 2024). Based on multi-task
060   interaction data, a series of generalist agents (or foundation policy models) have been proposed,
061   such as RT-1 (Brohan et al., 2022), Robocat (Bousmalis et al., 2023), RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023),
062   Octo (Team et al., 2024), and OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024). However, the volume of interaction
063   data remains several orders of magnitude smaller than that of internet text or video data. Given that
064   the success of foundation models relies on scaling up datasets and extracting knowledge from such
065   large-scale datasets, it is essential to design methods for building embodied AI foundation models
066   that can effectively utilize internet-scale video data.
067   Internet-scale video data contains abundant sequential records of human activities and perfect
068   demonstrations of how human perform various tasks by interacting with the real world. When
069   the human brain extracts information from videos, instead of doing it frame by frame, it modular-
070   izes the differences between frames into a single word such as “move”, “open”, “close”. We refer
071   to these highly compressed, modularized actions as latent actions that are shared across different
072   tasks. The question to ask here is, is it possible to recover latent actions from video datasets with
073
      humans and robots performing various real embodied AI tasks? While recent works such as
      Genie (Bruce et al., 2024) and LAPO (Schmidt & Jiang, 2023) made attempts in recovering such
074
      latent actions from videos, they primarily focus on 2D platformer games where each latent action
075
      at corresponds to a specific control button. The action space is highly designed to fit a specific sce-
076   nario and incomparable to the complex human and robot action space in various embodied AI tasks.
077   To take a step further, the question would be, can we learn a unified, semantically consistent la-
078   tent action space, allowing the transfer of knowledge across different tasks, and embodiments
079   including human and various robots?
080
      In this paper, we propose Image-GOal Representations (IGOR), which learns a unified and seman-
081
      tically consistent latent action space shared across different tasks and embodiments, enabling the
082   knowledge transfer among internet-scale video data. We propose a latent action model designed
083   to capture robot and human actions across various embodied AI tasks. IGOR compresses the vi-
084   sual changes between an image and its goal state into latent actions, which are also embeddings of
085   sub-tasks defined by reaching the goal from the initial image. IGOR is trained by minimizing the
086   reconstruction loss of the goal state, which is predicted based on the image and the latent action.
087   The core insight behind IGOR is that if compressed sufficiently, the image-goal pairs with similar
088   visual changes will have similar embeddings.
089
090       We argue that, besides text embeddings for human instruction understanding and im-
091       age/video embeddings for state understanding, image-goal representations for latent action
092       learning and sub-task understanding are yet another crucial building blocks, which may
093       hold great potential for becoming the atomic control unit in embodied AI.
094
095
      With the latent action model, we can transform internet-scale human video data into interaction
096   data labeled with latent actions, which largely expands the data available to building embodied
097   AI foundation models. This unified latent action space allows us to train foundation policy and
098   world models on nearly arbitrary tasks performed by robots and humans. Specifically, we train a
099   foundation policy model on large-scale video data with text labels. This model uses text to describe
100   tasks and makes decisions, generating the next latent action to perform. Additionally, we train a
101   foundation world model on the same dataset, learning to simulate the outcome of executing the
102   foundation policy model. Image-Goal Representations can be viewed as atomic control units in
103   visual space. They function both as latent actions for a foundational policy model to predict in
104
      visual trajectory planning and as sub-tasks for a robot-specific low-level policy to execute.
105   We train our models on human video data and robot data with actions removed, with RT-1 dataset
106   held out for OOD evaluation. First, we evaluate the latent action model qualitatively, and find
107   that image-goal pairs with similar latent actions have similar visual changes, corresponding to se-
      mantically consistent movements, even on OOD scenarios. Then we evaluate the world model by

      2.1   L ATENT ACTION M ODEL
155
156   The primary objective of the latent action model is to label latent actions from unlabeled open-
157   domain videos in an unsupervised manner. Given a sequence of video frames o1:t+1 , the goal is
158   to derive the latent action at , which captures the key information describing only the changes that
159   occur at time step t, removing other redundant information. In contrast to prior works (Schmidt &
160   Jiang, 2023; Bruce et al., 2024), which primarily focus on 2D platformer games where each latent
161   action at corresponds to a specific control button, we aim to develop a more generalizable model.
      Our model is designed to handle the significantly greater complexity of open-world scenarios, where


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      latent actions may not correspond to any specific underlying actions. This presents several additional
163   challenges.
164
165   First, rather than focusing solely on absolute position of pixel changes, the latent action model must
166
      learn to capture semantic movements that remain consistent across varying scenarios. Moreover, due
      to the temporal redundancy, actions are often sparse given long contexts, which can lead the model
167
      to infer ot+1 directly from the history, bypassing the need for a more informative latent action at .
168
169   To address these issues, we propose a novel model architecture. Our latent action model consists of
170   a pair of Inverse Dynamics Model (IDM) and Forward Dynamics Model (FDM). IDM I is trained
171   to predict the latent action at based on the full sequence of observations o1:t+1 . Instead of using
172
      the raw observations, we first apply random cropping c1 to the inputs: at = I (c1 [o1:t+1 ]). For
      the architecture of I, we first extract features for each frame through Vision Transformer (ViT)
173
      (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) and then adopt a Spatio-Temporal transformer (ST-transformer) (Bruce
174
      et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2021) with a temporal causal mask as the backbone. Learnable readout tokens
175   are then used to extract and compress the visual changes into N tokens. To further compress the
176   information stored in latent action, we apply vector quantization to each token, restricting them to
177   a discrete codebook of size |C|. Finally, we derive the latent action at ∈ RN ×D where D is the
178   dimension of each code. We refer to at as the latent action embedding, or sub-task embedding, as
179   they describe the information that takes the observation ot to the next observation ot+1 .
180
      For the FDM F , we propose using a single-frame Vision Transformer to reconstruct ot+1 , in contrast
181   to previous works (Schmidt & Jiang, 2023; Bruce et al., 2024), which reconstruct the next frame
182   given the entire context o1:t . This approach mitigates the case where the model might predict the
183   next frame directly from the context, bypassing the latent action. By conditioning on a single frame,
184   it encourages more information to flow into the latent action at . For reconstruction, we apply another
185   random cropping c2 , and the next frame is predicted as ôt+1 = F (c2 [ot ], at ). By using different
186   croppings c1 and c2 , the model is encouraged to learn a more semantically invariant latent action
187   across different trajectories. The models are trained jointly with the reconstruction loss ∥c2 [ot+1 ] −
188   ôt+1 ∥2 and the commitment loss in vector quantization.
189
190   2.2   F OUNDATION W ORLD M ODEL
191
192
      Our foundation world model is a continuous-time Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2023b; Esser et al.,
      2024) that learns to predict the future frames ot+1:T conditioned on the history observation frame
193
      o1:t , and future latent actions at:T −1 . To achieve this goal, there are two key challenges: 1) Generat-
194
      ing the photo-realistic frame that describes the states precisely; 2) Controlling the generated frames
195   by the latent actions.
196
197   Accordingly, we start our foundation world model with the pre-trained Open-Sora (Zheng et al.,
198
      2024). It consists of two components: a 3D Variational AutoEncoder (VAE) that encodes the raw
      observation into latent space with 8 × 8 times downsampling in spatial dimension and 4× times
199
      downsampling in temporal dimension; a Spatial-Temporal Rectified Flow Transformer (ST-RFT)
200
      that generates the latent from the text conditions. To enable the control from the observation and
201   action, we make two modifications to the original Open-Sora: 1) We replace the original text input
202   of the pre-trained model with our latent actions a1:T obtained from LAM. Zero-padding is applied
203   for the last action. For each frame, we map the latent actions into a single token and feed it to the ST-
204   RFT via the cross-attention mechanism; 2) We also make the generation conditioned on the output
205   of FDM ôt+1:T , which provides a coarse-grained prediction according to the input latent action. For
206   the conditioning of ôt+1:T , we encode it to the latent space with the same 3D VAE and directly add
207   it to the noisy input element-wise.
208   Formally, Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2023b; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Esser et al., 2024)
209   aims at directly regressing a vector field that generates a probability path between noise distribution
210   and data distribution. For n ∈ [0, 1], we define the interpolation between the two distributions as:
211
212
                                              xn = (1 − n)x0 + nx1 ,                                        (1)
213   where x0 is the clean data, x1 is the sampled noise, and xn is the noisy data. During training, we
214   train a vector-valued neural network xθ with L2 loss:

225   2.3   F OUNDATION P OLICY M ODEL AND L OW- LEVEL P OLICY M ODEL
226
      The training of the policy model consists of two stages. In the first pretraining stage, taken as input
227   the raw observation frames o1:t and a textual description s for the task, the foundation policy model
228   predicts latent actions at = I([o1:t+1 ]) labeled by the IDM in the latent action model at each step.
229   The training dataset of this stage is the same as that used for the latent action model, i.e., with
230   large-scale and diverse sources of videos. In the second finetuning stage, we add an extra prediction
231   component on the foundation policy model to predict real continuous robot actions, with taking the
232   raw observations as well as the latent actions predicted by the first stage model as input. In this
233   stage, only the prediction component (i.e., the low-level policy model) is optimized on small-scale
234   and task-specific downstream datasets, while other components are frozen.
235   Specifically, similar to the latent action model, the backbone of foundation policy model is also a
236   ST-transformer equipped with a ViT image encoder, with a feed-forward layer as the final prediction
237   layer. The textual description s is encoded to a latent representation by a pre-trained text encoder,
238   which is concatenated with the observation representation encoded by the ViT encoder as the joint
239   input to the model. We use the L2 distance between the predicted hidden output and the latent action
240   as the loss function. Given a trajectory consists of t observations o1:t , the training objective can be
241
      written as:
                                          Lpolicy = ∥P ([s; o1:t ]) − at ∥2 ,                              (4)
242
243   where P (·) denotes the policy model.
244   In the second stage, we train the low-level policy model to predict the real continuous actions
245   within each latent action, where the image-goal latent actions can be seen as representations for
246   sub-tasks defined by reaching a goal from an initial image. The low-level policy model is also an
247   ST-transformer with a prediction layer. The input consists of the textual representation s, the ob-
248   servation o1:t and latent actions predicted by the foundation policy model P ([s; o1:t ]), which are
249   concatenated together at the patch level as one part. The latent action P ([s; o1:t ]) predicted by the
250   foundation policy model also serves as sub-task embedding for the low-level policy model. We de-
251
      note that each latent action corresponds to τ real robot actions, and the latent action at corresponds
      to real robot action u1:τ

      predictive of real robot actions including robot movements, rotations and gripper actions. It is
      also shown that the latent actions are more predictive of the robot movement than rotations and

      gripper actions, suggesting that the IGOR learned action space reflects more information in robot

      movements than robot arm rotations and gripping.

468   4     R ELATED W ORK
469
470   Foundation Agents for Robots Open-ended task-agnostic training and high-capacity neural net-
471
      work architectures have been recognized as key to the success of foundation models. In this context,
      a series of generalist agents have been proposed as the foundation policy models for robots (Brohan
472
      et al., 2022; Bousmalis et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024). RT-
473
      1 (Brohan et al., 2022) contributes a large-scale multi-task dataset and a robotic transformer architec-
474   ture,facilitating and assessing generalization across multiple tasks. RoboCat builds on Gato (Reed
475   et al., 2022), further enabling multi-embodiment generalization. RT-2 highlights the importance of
476   leveraging vision-language models trained on internet-scale data (Brohan et al., 2023). Octo (Team
477   et al., 2024) and OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024) can be seen as open versions of RoboCat and RT-2
478   respectively, with some additional technical contributions. IGOR is similar to RT-2 and OpenVLA
479   in the sense that we both leverage Internet-scale data. The difference lies in that we use video data of
480   human/robot performing embodied AI tasks, while they use text data and visual question answering
481   data for the training of vision language models. To the best of our knowledge, we present the first
482
      foundation policy model that performs decision making at the sub-task (i.e. latent action) level.
483   Image-Goal Visual Changes Tracking visual changes and establishing correspondence be-
484   tween an image and its goal state is crucial for dynamic visual understanding in embodied AI.
485   SiamMAE (Gupta et al., 2023) proposes to use a siamese encoder on the image and goal to learn vi-
      sual correspondence. Voltron (Karamcheti et al., 2023) introduces language-guided visual represen-


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      tation learning on image-goal pairs. Lin et al. (2024) and Ko et al. (2023) leverage optical flow be-
487   tween image and goal to capture visual changes and correspondence, while Video-LaVIT (Jin et al.,
488   2024) utilizes motion vectors. iVideoGPT (Wu et al., 2024) proposes using image-conditioned goal
489   representations as state representations to predict within a world model. VPT (Baker et al., 2022)
490   proposes to recover latent actions in videos using an inverse dynamics model trained on interaction
491   data to predict real actions. Perhaps the most similar approaches to our methods are LAPO (Schmidt
492   & Jiang, 2023) and Genie (Bruce et al., 2024). Both works primarily focus on 2D platformer games
493   where each latent action corresponds to a specific control button. By contrast, we aim to develop
494   a more generalizable model to handle the significantly greater complexity of open-world scenarios,
495
      where latent actions may not correspond to any specific underlying actions.
496   Video Generation for Embodied AI Video generation is another research topic closely related
497   to embodied AI. It has been proposed that video can be seen as the new language for real-world
498   decision making (Yang et al., 2024b). Many works on world models build on video generation
499   techniques (Bruce et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Xiang et al.,
500   2024). Some text-to-video works claim to be real-world simulators, such as Sora (Brooks et al.,
501   2024) and WorldDreamer (Wang et al., 2024). Unipi (Du et al., 2023) proposes to first predict
502
      the next goal state, then infer real robot actions with an inverse dynamics model. By contrast, our
      foundation policy model first predicts the latent action, which can specify the goal state, and then
503
      uses the latent action to enable sub-task level generalization. We argue that forward prediction in
504
      latent action space, rather than the original image space, offers several advantages. For example,
505   we can perform sub-task understanding for image-goal representations, and the compressed latent
506   action could be easier to predict than the entire image.
507
508   Pre-trained Visual Representations Pre-trained Visual Representations target on training repre-
509   sentations for images/videos in self-supervised learning manner (He et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2022;
510
      Radosavovic et al., 2022; Majumdar et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021; Nair et al., 2022; Ma et al.,
      2023; Oquab et al., 2023; Darcet et al., 2023; Kirillov et al., 2023; Assran et al., 2023; Bardes et al.,
511
      2024), and has been demonstrated to be very effective for state understanding in embodied AI. By
512
      contrast, IGOR learns image-goal representations for sub-task understanding, which we believe are
513   another crucial building blocks, that may significantly enhance generalization in embodied AI.
514
515   5   C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
516
517   In this paper, we propose IGOR, a novel training framework, taking the first step towards learning a
518
      unified action space for humans and robots in various embodied AI tasks.
519   Qualitatively, we demonstrate that:
520
             • IGOR learns similar representations for image pairs with similar visual changes.
521
522
             • The learned latent action has control over the next state given the current image.
523          • The foundation world model acquires knowledge about objects and their potential move-
524            ments.
525          • The foundation policy model learns to follow instructions across different states.
526
      Quantitatively, we show that:
527
528          • On the RT-1 dataset, image-goal pairs with similar latent actions have similar low-level
529            robot actions.
530          • The IGOR framework improves policy learning, potentially due to its capability to predict
531            the next sub-task by leveraging internet-scale data, thereby enabling sub-task level gener-
532            alization.
533
      The IGOR framework is limited in the following perspective: we cannot separate visual changes
534   caused by the agents, other agents (such as dogs), or the shakiness of the camera. To address this,
535   we mitigated camera shakiness and used only ego-centric videos without other agents in view. Just
536   like any other representation learning methods, scaling up the dataset and model size is always most
537   straightforward and effective. To facilitate the usage of more data, incorporating image processing
538   methods such as object segmentation with IGOR will be part of future works. For better applications
539   in embodied AI, the foundation world model can also be tuned to match real world scenarios, along
      with other improvements such as adapting the latent action model for multi-agent scenarios.

957
      Data Filtering We observed that video quality significantly affects the action model, particularly
958   for human activities video. Excessive shakiness in videos can introduce visual changes between
959   consecutive frames that are unrelated to the agent’s actions.
960
961
      We calculate the camera motion over the videos, and filter approximately 40% percent of open-world
      video data. For the remaining data, we further stabilize the videos. Although we retain only 60%
962
      percent of open-world video data, we find that the action model improves dramatically.
963
964
      Frame Interval A noticeable amount of visual changes is crucial for our latent action model. If
965
      we select two frames that are too close in time, the agent may barely move, resulting in visual
966   changes that are not significant enough for inferring meaningful actions. Conversely, if the frames
967   are too far apart, the changes might be too large to model accurately. To address this issue, we
968   tune the sampling interval. For robot data, we choose frames that are three intervals apart, using st
969   and st+3 as the image-goal pair. For real world videos, we control the sampling. For real world
970   data, we control the sample interval to be within [0.1s, 0.5s]. For the action and policy model, the
971   context frames follow the same interval, ensuring that each pair of consecutive frames maintains this
      consistent spacing.

1045   E    A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSION ON R ELATED W ORK
1046
1047   Any-Point Trajectory Modeling (ATM) (Wen et al., 2023) and Tract2Act (Bharadhwaj et al., 2024c)
1048   generates point tracking from the action-free videos for pretraining the policy to predict future tra-
1049   jectories of the tracked point, and learns a robot policy conditioned on generated trajectories. HOP-
1050   Man (Bharadhwaj et al., 2024b) generates future human plans as conditions for facilitating robot
1051   policy learning. Im2Flow2Act (Xu et al., 2024) conditions the robot policy on complete gener-
1052
       ated object flows, which captures movement information only for the object, improving its cross-
       embodiment transfer capability.
1053
1054   Same as ATM, HOPMan, Im2Flow2Act, and Tract2Act, IGOR also pretrain a foundation policy on
1055   action-free videos that generalize across embodiments. There are two major differences: (1) IGOR
1056   uses an unsupervised way to learn and compress the visual changes, while existing work needs an
1057   additional pretrained video model for point tracking; (2) IGOR uses a compact latent representation
1058
       for visual changes, while existing works uses an explicit representation for visual changes. The
       compact action representation enables IGOR to transfer human actions on robots directly, as shown
1059
       in Figure 2 in our paper.
1060
1061   In the world model aspect, Gen2Act (Bharadhwaj et al., 2024a) uses the VideoPoet model (Kon-
1062   dratyuk et al., 2023) for text-to-video generation, and the generated human videos are used for
1063   downstream policy learning. Compared to existing world model works, IGOR can learn general-
1064
       izable latent actions for fine-grained control on manipulation scenarios for both human and robots,
       while existing works use coarse grained text conditions for generating video predictions.
