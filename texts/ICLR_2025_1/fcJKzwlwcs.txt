009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have demonstrated their capabilities in modeling trajectories of
012            multi-tasks. However, existing multi-task planners or policies typically rely on
013            task-specific demonstrations via multi-task imitation, or require task-specific re-
014            ward labels to facilitate policy optimization via Reinforcement Learning (RL).
015            They heavily rely on task-specific labeled data, which can be difficult to acquire.
016
               To address these challenges, we aim to develop a versatile diffusion planner that
               can leverage large-scale inferior data that contains task-agnostic sub-optimal tra-
017
               jectories, with the ability to fast adapt to specific tasks. In this paper, we pro-
018
               pose SODP, a two-stage framework that leverages Sub-Optimal data to learn a
019            Diffusion Planner, which is generalizable for various downstream tasks. Specifi-
020            cally, in the pre-training stage, we train a foundation diffusion planner that extracts
021            general planning capabilities by modeling the versatile distribution of multi-task
022            trajectories, which can be sub-optimal and has wide data coverage. Then for
023            downstream tasks, we adopt RL-based fine-tuning with task-specific rewards to
024            quickly refine the diffusion planner, which aims to generate action sequences with
025            higher task-specific returns. Experimental results from multi-task domains includ-
026            ing Meta-World and Adroit demonstrate that SODP outperforms state-of-the-art
027
               methods with only a small amount of data for reward-guided fine-tuning.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   There has been a long-standing pursuit to develop agents capable of performing multiple tasks (Reed
032   et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022). Although traditional RL methods have made significant strides in
033   training agents to master individual tasks (Silver et al., 2016; OpenAI et al., 2019), expanding
034   this capability to handle diverse tasks remains a significant challenge due to the diversity of task
035   variants and optimization directions with different rewards. Multi-task RL aims to address this by
036   developing agents via task-conditioned optimization (Yu et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2022) or parameter-
037   compositional learning (Sun et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). However, the inherent diversity in task
038   trajectory distributions makes it challenging to model and accommodate modeling across different
039
      task structures. Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020), originally designed
      for generative tasks, provide a powerful framework to address these difficulties. Their capacity
040
      to capture complex, multi-modal distributions within high-dimensional data spaces (Podell et al.,
041
      2023; Ho et al., 2022; Jing et al., 2022) makes them well suited to represent the broad variability
042   encountered in multi-task environments.
043
044   Motivated by this, existing methods have employed diffusion models to mimic expert behaviors de-
045
      rived from human demonstrations on various tasks (Pearce et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Chi et al.,
      2023). However, acquiring task-specific demonstrations is often time-consuming and costly, espe-
046
      cially in environments requiring specialized domain expertise. Alternative approaches attempt to
047
      optimize diffusion models with return guidance (He et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2023) or conventional
048   RL paradigm (Wang et al., 2022b), which demands a large volume of data with reward labels for
049   each task. To address the above limitations, we wonder whether a generalized diffusion planner can
050   be learned from a large amount of low-quality trajectories without reward labels, with the ability to
051   adapt quickly to various downstream tasks. We only require the inferior data to comprise a mixture
052   of sub-optimal state-action pairs from various tasks, which can be easily obtained in the real world.
053   In training, the diffusion planner seeks to model the distribution of diverse trajectories with broad
      coverage, enabling it to acquire generalizable capabilities and allowing the planner to further con-

      In this paper, we propose a novel framework to utilize Sub-Optimal data to train a Diffusion Planner
071   (SODP) that can generalize across a wide range of downstream tasks. SODP consists of two stages:
072   pre-training and fine-tuning. By leveraging a set of trajectories of different tasks for pre-training,
073   we employ action-sequence prediction to capture shared knowledge across tasks. Since the state
074   space may vary between tasks, focusing on the common action space (e.g., end-effector poses of
075   a robot arm) facilitates task generalization. We frame the pre-training stage as a conditional gen-
076   erative problem that generates future actions based on historical states. Then, inspired by the re-
077   markable success of RL-based alignment for LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Glaese et al., 2022), we
078   adopt an RL-based fine-tuning approach to tailor the pre-trained diffusion planner to specific down-
079
      stream tasks. Specifically, we conduct online interaction based on the pre-trained planner to collect
      task-specific experiences with reward labels, and perform policy gradients to iteratively refine the
080
      predicted action-sequence distribution based on reward feedback of tasks. Through fine-tuning, the
081
      diffusion planner can gradually adapt toward generating actions with high task-specific rewards and
082   eventually become optimal for the given task.

139   We propose SODP, a two-stage framework that leverages large amounts of sub-optimal data to train
140   a diffusion planner that can generalize to downstream tasks. The process is depicted in Figure 3. In
141   the pre-training stage, we train a guidance-free diffusion model to predict future actions based on
142   historical states, using an mixture offline dataset cross tasks without reward labels. In the fine-tuning
143
      stage, we refine the pre-trained model using policy gradient to maximize the task-specific rewards,
      additionally incorporating a regularization term to prevent the model from losing acquired skills.

      How to select the target policy? Intuitively, an ideal target policy is the optimal policy that gener-
264   ates samples x∗ satisfying C(x∗ ) ≥ C(x) for all possible x, where C(x) represents a measure of the
265   performance or quality of the sample, such as the accumulated reward for action sequences. Since
266   µ is unknown during fine-tuning, we approximate it by sampling action sequences a that satisfy
267   C(a) ≈ C(a∗ ). In practice, we denote a∗ as the best actions from recent play experience, such as
268   those that yielded the top n highest rewards or successfully completed the given task. We then sam-
269   ple ak from these proficient action sequences obtained during online interaction, nearly equivalent
      to sampling from µ to regularize the fine-tuning process. We also remark that the BC regularizer

288   4   R ELATED W ORK
289
290   Diffusion Models in RL. Diffusion models are a leading class of generative models, achieving
291   state-of-the-art performance across a variety of tasks, such as image generation (Ramesh et al.,
292   2021), audio synthesis (Kong et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023), and drug design (Schneuing et al.,
293   2022; Guan et al., 2024). Recent studies have applied them in imitation learning to model human
294   demonstrations and predict future actions (Li et al., 2024; Reuss et al., 2023). Other approaches
295   have trained conditional diffusion models either as planners (Ajay et al., 2022; Brehmer et al., 2024)
296
      or policies (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2024). However, most of these efforts focus
      on single-task settings. While some recent works aim to extend diffusion models to multi-task
297
      scenarios, they often rely on additional conditions, such as prompts (He et al., 2024) or preference
298
      labels (Yu et al., 2024). These methods are limited by their dependence on expert data or explicit
299   task knowledge. In contrast, our method learns broad action-sequence distributions from inferior
300   data to enhance action priors, enabling effective generalization across a range of downstream tasks.
301
302
      Fine-tuning Diffusion Models. Despite the impressive success of diffusion models, they often face
      challenges in aligning with specific downstream objectives, such as image aesthetics (Schuhmann
303
      et al., 2022), fairness (Shen et al., 2023), or human preference (Xu et al., 2024), primarily due
304
      to their training on unsupervised data. Some methods have been proposed to address this issue
305   by directly fine-tuning models using downstream objectives (Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Clark et al.,
306   2023), but they rely on differentiable reward models, which are impractical in RL since accurately
307   modeling rewards with neural networks is quite costly (Kim et al., 2023). Other methods reformulate
308   the denoising process as an MDP and apply policy gradients for fine-tuning (Black et al., 2023; Fan
309   et al., 2024). However, they heavily depend on strong pre-trained models and have proven ineffective
310   in our case. Our goal is to fine-tune a less powerful model that has been trained on inferior data.
311   Concurrent with our work, DPPO (Ren et al., 2024) also explores reward fine-tuning for refining RL
312   diffusion planners. However, their approach focuses exclusively on single-task settings and allows
313   access to expert demonstrations. In contrast, we train our model on multi-task data without the need
314   for superior demonstrations. Additionally, we analyze the limitations of current regularization meth-
315   ods for versatile RL diffusion models and propose a new regularizer that improves the performance
316   of sub-optimal pre-trained models.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
325
326   We evaluate SODP in both state-based and image-based environments. We conduct experiments on
327   the Meta-World benchmark (Yu et al., 2019) for both state-based and image-based tasks. We also
328   perform image-based experiments on the Adroit benchmark (Rajeswaran et al., 2017).
329
      Meta-World. The Meta-World benchmark comprises 50 distinct manipulation tasks, each requiring
330
      a Sawyer robot to interact with various objects. These tasks are designed to assess the robot’s ability
331
      to handle different scenarios, such as grasping, pushing, pulling, and manipulating objects of varying
332   shapes, sizes, and complexities. While the state space and reward functions differ across tasks, the
333   action space remains consistent. Following recent studies (He et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), we
334   extend all tasks to a random-goal setting, referred to as MT50-rand.
335
      Adroit. The Adroit benchmark includes three dexterous manipulation tasks, requiring a 24-degree-
336
      of-freedom dexterous hand to solve complex challenges such as in-hand manipulation and tool use.
337
      The goals in this environment are also randomized. For Adroit, we use images as the observation to
338   assess whether our method can scale to high-dimensional input.
339
340   Datasets. Following previous work (He et al., 2024), for Meta-World, we use a sub-optimal dataset
341
      comprising the first 50% of experiences (50M transitions) obtained from the replay buffer of a
      SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) agent during training. To verify the applicability of our method to
342
      tasks of varying difficulty levels, we divide the entire dataset into four subsets based on the task
343
      categories presented in Seo et al. (2023). For Adroit, we train a VRL3 (Wang et al., 2022a) agent
344   for each task and use the initial 30% experiences (90K transitions) from the converged replay buffer.
345   For Meta-World, all baselines and our pre-training stage are trained on the same dataset. For Adroit,
346   the baselines are trained on expert demonstrations and ours is trained on sub-optimal transitions.
347
      Baselines. For Meta-World, we compare our proposed SODP with the following baselines: (1) MT-
348
      SAC. Extended SAC with one-hot task ID as additional input. (2) MTBC. Extended BC to multi-
349
      task learning through network scaling and a task-ID-conditioned actor. (3) MTIQL. Extended
350   IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2021) with multi-head critic networks and a task-ID-conditioned actor for
351   multi-task policy learning. (4) MTDQL. Extended Diffusion-QL (Wang et al., 2022b) which is sim-
352   ilar to MTIQL. (5) MTDT. Extended Decision Transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021a) to multitask
353   settings by incorporating task ID encoding and state inputs for task-specific learning. (6) Prompt-
354   DT (Xu et al., 2022). An extension of DT, which generates actions by utilizing trajectory prompts
355   and reward-to-go signals. (7) MTDIFF (He et al., 2024). A diffusion-based approach that inte-
356   grates Transformer architectures with prompt learning to facilitate generative planning in multitask
357   offline environments. We extend it with a visual extractor in image-based Meta-World experiments.
358
      (8) HarmoDT (Hu et al., 2024). A DT-based approach that leverages parameter sharing to exploit
      task similarities while mitigating the adverse effects of conflicting gradients simultaneously. The
359
      results for these baselines are directly replicated from those reported in HarmoDT (Hu et al., 2024).
360
361   The action space for different tasks in Adroit is different and is incompatible with MTDIFF and
362   HarmoDT. Therefore, we compare SODP with following baselines designed for complex environ-
363   ments: (1) BCRNN (Mandlekar et al., 2021). A variant of BC that employs a Recurrent Neural
364
      Network (RNN) as the policy network, predicting the sequence of actions based on the sequence of
      states as input. (2) IBC (Florence et al., 2022). Extended BC with energy-based models (EBM) to
365
      train implicit behavioral cloning policies. (3) Diffusion Policy (Chi et al., 2023). A diffusion-based
366
      approach that predicts future action sequences based on historical states. (4) DP3 (Ze et al., 2024).
367   A visual imitation learning algorithm that incorporates 3D visual representations into diffusion poli-
368   cies, using a point clouds encoder to process visual observations into visual features. The results for
369   these baselines are directly replicated from those reported in DP3 (Ze et al., 2024).

      5.3                                      E FFECTIVENESS OF BC R EGULARIZATION
436
437   To demonstrate the effectiveness of our BC regularization, we conduct an ablation study on fine-
438   tuning same pre-trained model with our BC regularization and other variants. We consider following
439   variants:
440
441   • SODP w/o regularization. This variant is similar to DDPO (Black et al., 2023) and DPPO (Ren
442
        et al., 2024), which fine-tunes the model directly using Eq. (12) without any regularization.
443   • SODP_kl. This variant is similar to DPOK (Fan et al., 2024), with the addition of a KL regular-
444     ization term to constrain the divergence between the fine-tuned model and the pre-trained model.
445   • SODP_pl. This variant is similar to DLPO (Chen et al., 2024), incorporating the original diffusion
446     pre-training loss (PL) into the fine-tuning objective to prevent the model from deviation.
447
448
      The details of these variants are presented in Appendix E and more ablation studies on different
      fine-tuning methods can be found in Appendix C.2. Figure 5 demonstrates the effectiveness of our
449
      regularization in achieving a higher success rate. We observe that directly fine-tuning the model
450
      without any regularization results in the worst performance, with a decline in success rate, as the
451   model may degrade the capabilities acquired from pre-training due to the lack of constraints. How-
452   ever, adding KL and PL is insufficient, as they cause oscillations near the pre-trained model. This
453   aligns with the original intent of these regularizers, which is to prevent excessive deviation. This is
454   reasonable for methods like DPOK and DLPO, which utilize pre-trained models such as Stable Dif-
455   fusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and WaveGrad2 (Chen et al., 2021b). These models already exhibit
456   strong generative capabilities without fine-tuning, and the goal is to make slight adjustments to align
457   them with more fine-grained attributes, such as aesthetic scores and human preferences.
458   In contrast, our model is pre-trained on sub-optimal data and lacks the ability to solve complex tasks.
459   We expect it to develop new skills for completing these tasks through fine-tuning. However, directly
460   applying KL regularization to the pre-trained model leads to conservative policies that heavily rely
461   on the existing capability, thereby confining the model to a sub-optimal region. While PL regular-
462   ization allows some slight exploration, it is uncontrolled and random. Consequently, we observe
463   that the KL regularization almost remains unchanged and the PL regularization slightly increases
464   the performance in basketball but decreases in other tasks.

516   knowledge is easily disrupted by a large number of ineffective trials.

      6                                              C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose SODP, a novel framework for training a versatile diffusion planner using sub-optimal
534   data. By effectively combining pre-training and fine-tuning, we capture broad behavioral patterns
535   drawn from large-scale multi-task transitions and then rapidly adapt them to achieve higher perfor-
536   mance in specific downstream tasks. During fine-tuning, we introduce a BC regularization method,
537   which preserves the pre-trained model’s capabilities while guiding effective exploration. Exper-
538   iments demonstrate that SODP achieves superior performance across a wide range of challenging
539   manipulation tasks. In future work, we aim to develop embodied versatile agents that can effectively
      learn to solve real-world tasks using inferior data.

      B     T HE D ETAILS OF SODP
838
      B.1   D IFFUSION P OLICY
839
840   We use diffusion policy (Chi et al., 2023) to generate future actions. For any given time step t, the
841   model uses the most recent To steps of states as input to generate the next Tp action steps. Then,
842   the first Ta steps of these generated actions are executed in the environment without re-planning. In
843   our experiments, we use Tp = 12, To = 2, Ta = 8 for Meta-World and Tp = 4, To = 2, Ta = 3 for
844   Adroit.
845   We employ a CNN-based diffusion policy as our noise model, utilizing a U-net architecture
846   that incorporates Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) (Perez et al., 2018) to condition on
847   historical states. The implementation is based on the code from https://github.com/
848   CleanDiffuserTeam/CleanDiffuser, and we use their default hyper-parameters. For
849   Adroit, we use a simplified backbone provided by Simple DP3 (https://github.com/
850   YanjieZe/3D-Diffusion-Policy), which removes some components in the U-net.
851
852   B.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
853
854   The pseudo-code of SODP is given in Alg. 1. We describe details of pre-training and fine-tuning as
855
      follows:
856
      • For pretraining, we use cosine schedule for βk (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) and set diffusion steps
857     K = 100. We pre-train the model for 5e5 steps in Meta-Wrold and 3e3 steps in Adroit.
858
      • For fine-tuning, we use DDIM (Song et al., 2020) with 10 sampling steps and η = 1. We fine-tune
859
        each task for 1e6 steps in Meta-World and 3e3 steps in Adroit. Following DPOK (Fan et al., 2024),
860
        we perform pstep ∈ {10, 30} gradient steps per episode. We set discount factor γ = 1 for all tasks.
861
862
      • We set Ninit ∈ {10, 20} for approximating target distribution and λ = 1.0 as the BC weight
        coefficient.
863
      • Batch size is set to 256 for both pre-training and fine-tuning.

1089   F    C OMPARISON TO DPPO
1090
1091   We summarize some similarities and differences between our work and the concurrent work
1092   DPPO (Ren et al., 2024) as follows:
1093
1094   • Both DPPO and our approach formulate the diffusion policy denoising process as an MDP and
1095     use policy gradients to fine-tune the model for higher environment rewards.
1096   • DPPO demonstrated that reward-based RL fine-tuning promotes effective exploration, which is
1097     consistent with our observations.
1098   • While DPPO requires task-specific expert demonstrations for pre-training, our method pre-trains
1099     a foundation model capable of capturing useful behavior patterns from multi-task inferior data.
1100
       • We show that directly fine-tuning the pre-trained planner without any regularization, as done in
1101     DPPO, fails in the multi-task setting. We further analyze the limitations of current regularization
1102     methods and propose a novel BC regularization term. By employing our regularizer, the pre-
1103     trained model achieves higher success rates after fine-tuning.
1104
       • Unlike DPPO, we don’t employ advantage estimator.
