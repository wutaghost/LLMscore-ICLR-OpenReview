009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Cooperative Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) has become a critical
012            tool for addressing complex real-world problems. However, scalability remains a
013            significant challenge due to the exponentially growing joint action space. In our
014            analysis, we highlight a critical but often overlooked issue: extrapolation error,
015            which arises when unseen state-action pairs are inaccurately assigned unrealistic
016
               values, severely affecting performance. We demonstrate that the success of value
               factorization methods can be largely attributed to their ability to mitigate this er-
017
               ror. Building on this insight, we introduce multi-step bootstrapping and ensemble
018
               techniques to further reduce extrapolation errors, showing that straightforward
019            modifications can lead to substantial performance improvements. Our findings un-
020            derscore the importance of recognizing extrapolation error in MARL and highlight
021            the potential of exploring simpler methods to advance the field.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Cooperative Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) has proven to be a powerful approach
027
      for addressing a wide range of complex real-world challenges, including autonomous driving (Zhou
      et al., 2020), traffic management (Singh et al., 2020), and robot swarm coordination (Hüttenrauch
028
      et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021a). The complexity inherent in these scenarios poses significant
029
      challenges, especially in terms of scalability, as the joint action space expands exponentially with the
030   number of agents. Furthermore, the necessity for decentralized decision-making, grounded in local
031   action-observation histories due to communication constraints, adds to the intricacies of MARL tasks.
032
033
      While various solutions have been proposed to address these challenges, we identify a critical and
      often overlooked issue: extrapolation error (Fujimoto et al., 2019b). This error arises when unseen
034
      or rarely encountered state-action pairs are inaccurately assigned unrealistic values. In the context
035
      MARL, where the joint action space is vast, extrapolation error becomes a key limiting factor,
036   especially during training when many actions remain unseen but are required for accurate Temporal
037   Difference (TD) target estimation. For example, in environments like the StarCraft Multi-Agent
038   Challenge (SMAC) (Samvelyan et al., 2019), joint action dimensions can exceed 105 , leading to
039   significant errors in estimating the value of these unseen actions.
040
      Although recent MARL approaches, particularly value factorization methods (Son et al., 2019; Sune-
041
      hag et al., 2017; Rashid et al., 2020b; Wang et al., 2020a), have demonstrated strong performance,
042   much of the focus has been on enhancing the expressive capacity of these models. Early approaches
043   like VDN (Sunehag et al., 2017) and QMIX (Rashid et al., 2020b) were critiqued for their limita-
044   tions in handling non-monotonic tasks (Mahajan et al., 2019), prompting efforts to improve their
045   representational power. However, despite these advancements, recent studies (Yu et al., 2022; Ellis
046   et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023) suggest that the performance is not entirely aligned with their expressive
047   capabilities. Notably, QMIX remains the most competitive algorithms across various domains.
048   In this paper, we propose a shift in focus, emphasizing extrapolation error as a key factor behind the
049   success of value factorization methods. By decomposing the joint Q-function into individual utilities,
050   value factorization significantly reduces extrapolation errors, particularly in environments with large
051   joint action spaces. Additionally, we demonstrate that monotonicity in value factorization plays a
052   crucial role by enabling self-correcting mechanisms in online RL, which help control the propagation
053   of errors. Our analysis reveals that the success of value factorization methods like QMIX is closely
      tied to their ability to manage extrapolation error.

      Building on this insight, we introduce two components: annealed multi-step bootstrapping and
055   ensembled TD targets. These components mitigate bias by incorporating temporally extended trajec-
056   tories and reduce variance by averaging target values. When applied to existing value factorization
057   methods, these modifications yield substantial performance improvements across tasks, including
058   those in SMAC, Google Research Football (GRF) (Kurach et al., 2020), and SMACv2 (Ellis et al.,
059   2023). Rather than presenting an entirely new algorithm, our goal is to highlight the critical role of
060   extrapolation errors in MARL and showcase how straightforward modifications can yield substantial
061   performance improvements.

134

135
      hinders convergence, resulting in a larger OD. To address this, prior research has often focused on
136   improving representation capacity to reduce TAE. Nevertheless, empirical findings from recent works
137   (Yu et al., 2022; Ellis et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023) suggest that improving representational capacity
138   alone does not always translate into performance gains in complex domains.
139

140

141

142   TAE pertains to Q(s, a). This distinction is crucial because a′ may not have been observed in the
143   past trajectories, leading to significant extrapolation errors when predicting its value.

147
      several representative MARL algorithms from the lens of extrapolation error, revealing its strong

      3.1    E XTRAPOLATION E RROR IN MARL
151
152   Extrapolation error arises in RL when the value function inadequately estimates the value of actions
153   that are unseen or rare (Fujimoto et al., 2019b). In single-agent setting, if the a state-action pair
154   (s, a) is absent from the dataset, the value Q(s, a; θ) becomes an uncertain prediction made by
155   the neural network. Consider the update rule Q(s, a; θt+1 ) ← r + maxa′ Q(s′ , a′ ; θt ), where
156   Q(s′ , a′ ; θt ) = Qπ (s′ , a′ ) + et (s′ , a′ ; θt ) is decomposed into the true value plus an error term et .
157   During training, while transition (s, a, r, s′ ) is sampled from the dataset, the next action a′ generated
158
      by the Q-function/policy may be unseen or rare, potentially leading to a significant et for (s′ , a′ ),
      propagating a poor estimate to subsequent values.
159
160   Extrapolation error is typically associated with offline RL (Fujimoto et al., 2019a; Levine et al.,
161   2020), where the agent operates with a fixed dataset and cannot interact further with the environment,
      making it likely that the policy samples actions not present in the dataset. In online RL, by contrast,

209
            As previously discussed, one critical way to mitigate extrapolation error is through increased execution
210         of unseen actions in the environment, which distinguishes online RL from offline RL. In online
211         RL, extrapolation error is often less severe because once a value is overestimated, the likelihood of
212         selecting the corresponding action increases, making it easier to collect additional samples. These
213         samples help reduce the error by providing more accurate updates. However, in value factorization
214         methods, overestimation of the joint Q-function does not directly lead to an increased probability of
215         choosing the corresponding actions for individual agents. To address this, we introduce an important
            constraint called Error Propagation Consistency (EPC):

254   3.3   E XTRAPOLATION E RRORS OF E XISTING A PPROACHES
255
      Extrapolation error is seldom addressed in existing online MARL approaches, yet it plays a critical
256
      role in shaping performance. Although conducting a unified analysis of existing works through the
257
      lens of extrapolation error is challenging—due to differences in learning schemes, structures, and
258   various factors contributing to performance—some general insights can be drawn. Specifically, we
259   observe that approaches that involve learning over the joint action space typically underperform in
260   evaluations. To illustrate this, we provide a brief analysis of existing approaches, and a detailed
261   analysis of QPLEX, which learns joint actions but still achieves relatively good performance.
262
      First, as extensions from single-agent RL, methods like MADDPG (Lowe et al., 2017) and COMA
263
      (Foerster et al., 2018) have become popular but tend to underperform in complex tasks. In comparison,
264   methods like MAA2C (Papoudakis et al., 2020) and MAPPO (Yu et al., 2022), which are also derived
265   from single-agent RL, demonstrate a clear performance gap. Moreover, FACMAC (Peng et al.,
266   2021), which applies QMIX’s factorization to MADDPG, also exhibits superior performance. A key
267   observation is that both MADDPG and COMA learn joint Q-functions, leading to significant TEE
268   as demonstrated in Fig. 1. By contrast, MAA2C and MAPPO rely on joint value functions, and
269   FACMAC employs factorized Q-functions, which avoid extrapolation over the joint action space. We
      provide further experimental evidence regarding MADDPG and FACMAC in Section 5.

      The proposition indicates that extrapolation error and its propagation are linked to λ. A larger λ
346   reduces error, aligning with the intuitive understanding that λ controls the balance between collected
347   returns and learned value functions for target estimation.
348
349
      However, adopting a larger λ is not always beneficial. With a fixed behavior policy µ, the PQL
      operator converges to the fixed point of λT µ + (1 − λ)T (Harutyunyan et al., 2016), implying
350
      convergence to a biased policy λµ + (1 − λ)π. Although PQL can eventually converge to the optimal
351
      policy as µ approaches π (Kozuno et al., 2021), this may not always happen in practice. The behavior
352   policy typically originates from the old policy stored in the replay buffer, which may not align closely
353   with the current policy before convergence. Moreover, behavior policies are often limited to ε-greedy
354   exploration in practical algorithms. Thus, a large λ may lead to a highly biased policy and suboptimal
355   performance. Experimental results (Fig. 7 in Appendix) across various λ confirm that while a larger λ
356   is more efficient during the initial stages of training, its performance may decline, eventually lagging
357   behind smaller λ values in later stages.
358   To address this issue, we propose a λ annealing strategy, which leverages the error-reducing properties
359   of PQL with a large initial λ while gradually annealing it during training to prevent policy bias. To
360   ensure stability, we update λk in sync with the fixed target network as follows:
361
362
                                         λk = λ∗ + (1 − λ∗ )/(1 + αk),                                     (8)
363   where α = 10/T , with T being the total environmental steps for training. This scheme is chosen
364   heuristically, but the algorithm is not very sensitive to how λ is annealed, as long as it converges to
365   λ∗ . While λ∗ would ideally be zero to achieve the optimal policy, practical training conditions may
366   necessitate stopping earlier. For instance, as seen in the 3s5z_vs_3s6z task (Fig. 5(c)), if the total
367   training steps are insufficient for convergence, maintaining a relatively large λ∗ proves beneficial.

532
533
      This paper has brought attention to the often-overlooked issue of extrapolation error in MARL. We
534   identified the critical role that value factorization methods play in mitigating this challenge and
535   proposed additional techniques, such as multi-step bootstrapping and ensemble methods, to further
536   reduce extrapolation error. Our experiments not only demonstrate the superior performance of our
537   proposed approach but also validate the significant impact of extrapolation error on MARL perfor-
538   mance. This success highlights the importance of addressing extrapolation error as a fundamental
539   factor in improving MARL methods. We believe that our findings open up new avenues for advancing
      value factorization methods and offer a fresh perspective for future research in MARL.

      A     R ELATED W ORKS
703
704
      Value factorization. In addition to the previously discussed methods, various approaches address the
705
      challenge of value factorization. For value-based methods, Qatten (Yang et al., 2020) employs an
706   attention mechanism to augment the expressive capacity of linear factorization. WQMIX (Rashid
707   et al., 2020a) improves QMIX’s expressive ability through the incorporation of a weighted operator
708   and a true value network. QTRAN++ (Son et al., 2020) refines the constraints of QTRAN to improve
709   efficiency. For policy-based methods, VMIX (Su et al., 2021) applies QMIX’s factorization to the
710   value function of A2C (Mnih et al., 2016). DOP (Wang et al., 2020b) utilizes linear factorization on
711   the Q-function of COMA (Foerster et al., 2018), while FOP (Zhang et al., 2021b) extend QPLEX’s
712   factorization to soft actor-critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018) framework. FACMAC (Peng et al., 2021)
713   combines QMIX’s factorization with MADDPG (Lowe et al., 2017). These approaches primarily
714
      concentrate on enhancing factorization itself, specifically addressing the TAE problem introduced
      in this paper, without delving into the underlying reasons for the success of value factorization.
715
      While theoretical papers such as (Wang et al., 2021) take steps to unveil the efficiency and credit
716
      assignment of value factorization, they lack substantial support for subsequent improvements. Recent
717   examinations of these methods (Yu et al., 2022; Ellis et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023), along with more
718   comprehensive experiments, highlight QMIX as the most popular and robust value-based MARL
719   algorithm. Therefore, distinct from previous approaches, our work approaches value factorization
720   from a novel perspective, introducing further enhancements to existing methods.
721
      Ensemble RL and MARL. Our analysis is similar to Averaged-DQN (Anschel et al., 2017), which
722
      ensembles the Q-functions from the past M steps. Despite proving effective in variance reduction,
723   Averaged-DQN relies on assumptions that may not always hold in practice. Other ensemble methods
724   (Lee et al., 2021) incorporating the standard deviation of Q-functions were not discussed here due
725   to limited observed improvements and their divergence from the main focus of this paper. REDQ
726   (Chen et al., 2021) employs in-target minimization across a random subset of Q-functions from the
727   ensemble. However, this approach proves unsuitable for value factorization, possibly due to the
728   presence of model bias (See Appendix E.3). In MARL, EMAX (Schäfer et al., 2023) applied a
729   similar ensemble method on VDN and QMIX with UCB and majority vote to improve exploration.
730   MMD-MIX (Xu et al., 2021) introduce REM (Agarwal et al., 2020) into a distributional view of
731
      QMIX to be more robust in randomness. These methods do not explicitly consider the extrapolation
      error.
732
733   Offline RL. The in-target average ensemble employed in our paper bears resemblance to the approach
734   used in offline RL (Agarwal et al., 2020; Fujimoto et al., 2019a; Levine et al., 2020). Additionally, the
735   out-of-distribution (OOD) action studied in offline RL aligns with the extrapolation error addressed
736   in this paper. As a result, similar methods may yield comparable effects. However, different from (An
737
      et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022), our paper does not require a more conservative/pessimistic estimation
      of the target, as the monotonic constraint enables self-correction in online RL. Moreover, we found
738
      that any degree of pessimism negatively impacts performance, as detailed in the Appendix E.3.
