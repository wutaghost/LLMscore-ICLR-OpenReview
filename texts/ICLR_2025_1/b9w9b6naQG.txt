                                                  A BSTRACT
009
010
               Neural Processes (NPs) are deep probabilistic models that represent stochastic
               processes by conditioning their prior distributions on a set of context points. De-
011
               spite their obvious advantages in uncertainty estimation for complex distributions,
012
               NPs enforce parameterization coupling between the conditional prior model and
013            the posterior model, thereby risking introducing a misspecified prior distribution.
014            We hereby revisit the NP objectives and propose Rényi Neural Processes (RNP)
015            to ameliorate the impacts of prior misspecification by optimizing an alternative
016            posterior that achieves better marginal likelihood. More specifically, by replacing
017            the standard KL divergence with the Rényi divergence between the model posterior
018            and the true posterior, we scale the density ratio pq by the power of (1-α) in the
019            divergence gradients with respect to the posterior. This hyper parameter α allows
020            us to dampen the effects of the misspecified prior for the posterior update, which
021            has been shown to effectively avoid oversmoothed predictions and improve the
022            expressiveness of the posterior model. Our extensive experiments show consistent
023
               log-likelihood improvements over state-of-the-art NP family models which adopt
               both the variational inference or maximum likelihood estimation objectives. We
024
               validate the effectiveness of our approach across multiple benchmarks including
025
               regression and image inpainting tasks, and show significant performance improve-
026            ments of RNPs in real-world regression problems where the underlying prior model
027            is misspecifed.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Neural processes (NPs) (Garnelo et al., 2018b) strive to represent stochastic processes via deep
033   neural networks with desirable properties in uncertainty estimation and flexible feature representation.
034   The vanilla NP (Garnelo et al., 2018b) predicts the distribution for unlabelled data given any set
035   of observational data as context. The main advantage of NPs is to learn a set-dependent prior
036   distribution, where the KL divergence is minimized between a posterior distribution conditioned
037   on a target set with new data and the prior distribution conditioned on the context set (Kim et al.,
038   2019; Jha et al., 2022; Bruinsma et al., 2023). However, as the parameters of such conditional
039
      prior are unknown, NP proposes a coupling scheme where the prior model that parameterizes the
      distribution is forced to share its parameters with an approximate posterior model. As the model
040
      parameter space induced by neural networks is usually large, enforcing coupling on such a space could
041
      lead to prior misspecification which could consequently produce a biased estimate of the posterior
042   variance and deteriorate predictive performance (Cannon et al., 2022; Knoblauch et al., 2019). Such
043   misspecification can be worsened under noisy context set (Jung et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Other
044   cases of prior misspecification encompass domain shifts (Xiao et al., 2021), out-of-distribution
045   predictions (Malinin & Gales, 2018) and adversarial samples (Stutz et al., 2019).
046
      To address the prior misspecification caused by parameterization coupling in vanilla NPs, several
047
      studies have been proposed to relax the constraint (Wang et al., 2023; Wang & Van Hoof, 2022;
048   Wu et al., 2018; Wicker et al., 2021). For instance, the prior and the posterior models can share
049   partial parameters instead of the entire network (Rybkin et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022); hierarchical
050   latent variable models are also utilized where both models share the same global latent variable and
051   induce prior or posterior-specific distribution parameterization (Shen et al., 2023; Requeima et al.,
052   2019; Kim et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2021). In this paper, we offer a new insight of handling prior
053   misspecification in NPs through the lens of robust divergence (Futami et al., 2018), which seeks to
      learn an alternative posterior without changing the parameters of interests. Instead of minimizing


                                                         1

054
      the standard KL divergence between the prior and posterior distributions, robust divergences are
055   theoretically guaranteed to produce better posterior estimates under prior misspecification (Verine
056   et al., 2024; Regli & Silva, 2018). The Rényi divergence (Li & Turner, 2016; Van Erven & Harremos,
057   2014b), for instance, introduces an additional parameter α to control how the prior distribution can
058   regularize the posterior during the posterior updates. By scaling the density ratio pq by the power
059   of (1-α) in the divergence gradients with respect to the posterior, this hyper parameter α allows
060   us to reduce the regularization effects of the misspecified prior (Knoblauch et al., 2019), thereby
061   mitigating the biased estimates of the posterior variance, avoiding oversmoothed predictions (Alemi
062   et al., 2018; Higgins et al., 2017), and achieving performance improvements. Due to the constraint of
063   parameterization coupling in NPs, such settings can give rise to a potentially critical case of prior
064   misspecification and the need for more robust divergences in NP learning.
065   In light of this, we propose Rényi Neural Processes (RNPs) that focus on improving neural processes
066   with a more robust objective. RNP minimizes the Rényi divergence between the posterior distribution
067   defined on the target set and the true posterior distribution given the context and target sets. We prove
068   that RNP connects the common variational inference and maximum likelihood estimation objectives
069   for training vanilla NPs via the hyperparameter α, through which RNP provides the flexibility to
070   dampen the effect of the misspecified prior and empower the posterior model for better expressiveness.
071
      Our main contributions are summarized as:
072   1. We introduce a new objective for neural processes that unifies the variational inference and
073   maximum likelihood estimation objectives via the Rényi divergence.
074
      2. We show that Rényi neural processes can be applied to several state-of-the-art neural process family
075   models in a simple yet effective manner without changing the model. We validate the effectiveness of
076   Rényi neural processes on comprehensive experiments including regression, image inpainting, and
077   prior misspecification.

136   In this section we describe our Rényi Neural Process (RNP) framework, a simple yet effective strategy
137   which provides a more robust way to learn neural processes without changing the model. We start
138   by analyzing the main limitation of the standard neural process objective and present a motivating
139   example. We illustrate a case where the prior is misspecified and describe our new objective with the
140   RD to mitigate this.

      We first consider a formal definition of prior misspecification.
145   Definition 3.1. (Prior misspecification (Huang et al., 2024)) Let η# Qφ be a pushforward of a
146   probability measure Qφ parameterized by φ under the map η: X × Y → Z. Then, {η# Qφ : φ ∈ Φ}
147
      defines a set of distributions on the space Z induced by the model, and η# P is the pushforward of the
      ground truth measure P. The prior model is misspecified if ∀φ ∈ Φ, η# Qφ ̸= η# P
148
149   The definition suggests that when the prior model η# Qφ is misspecified, there exists no optimal
150   parameter φ∗ that can represent the true prior η# P. This translate to NPs as the approximate
151   prior model qφ (z|XC , YC ) in Eq 3 can not recover the ground truth prior p(z|XC , YC ) for any
152   parameterization of φ . We now show how this definition can assist us to analyze how a misspecified
153   prior model can hinder neural processes learning.
154   Proposition 3.2. Due to the prior approximation in Eq 3, if the prior model is misspecified, meaning
155   that ∀φ ∈ Φ, η# Qφ (XC , YC ) ̸= η# P(XC , YC ), the resulting LV I is no longer a valid bound for
156   the marginal likelihood p(YT |XT , XC , YC ).

      Detailed proof can be found in Supp A.2. Proposition 3.2 challenges the common assumption
      p(z|XC , YC ) ≈ q(z|XC , YC ) that most NPs make. Such assumption, as we will show next, may
159
      result in a biased estimate of the posterior variance which hinders NP training.

329   predictions given a few observations as context. Both of them use deepset models (Zaheer et al.,
330
      2017) to map a finite number of data points to a high dimensional vector and their likelihood models
      assume independencies among data points. The main difference is whether estimating likelihood
331
      maximization directly or introducing the latent variable and adopting variational inference framework.
332
      Under the existing NP setting, more members were introduced with different inductive biases in the
333   model (Jha et al., 2022; Bruinsma et al., 2023; Dutordoir et al., 2023; Jung et al., 2024; Vadeboncoeur
334   et al., 2023). For instance, attentive neural processes (Kim et al., 2019) incorporated dependencies
335   between observations with attention neural works. Convolutional neural processes (Foong et al., 2020;
336   Huang et al., 2023) assume translation equivariance among data points. These two methods explicitly
337   defined the latent variable which requires density estimation. Recent works such as transformer neural
338   processes (Nguyen & Grover, 2022) and neural diffusion processes (Dutordoir et al., 2023) turn to
339   marginal likelihood maximization and do not have the latent distribution. More neural processes
340   which claim to provide exact (Markou et al., 2022) or tractable inference (Lee et al., 2023; Wang
341
      et al., 2023) are introduced. Stable neural processes (Liu et al., 2024) argued that NPs are prone
      to noisy context points and proposed a weighted likelihood model that focuses on subsets that are
342
      difficult to predict, but do not focus regularizing the posterior distribution. Compared to variational
343
      inference based methods, non-VI predictions can be less robust to noisy inputs in the data (Futami
344   et al., 2018). It is also challenging to incorporate prior knowledge (Zhang et al., 2018) into these
345   neural processes, which could be beneficial when no data is observed for the task.
346
347
      Robust divergences. Divergences in variational inference can be viewed as an regularization on
      the posterior distribution via the prior distribution. The commonly adopted KL divergence which
348
      minimizes the expected density ratio between the posterior is infamous for underestimating the true
349
      variance of the target distribution (Regli & Silva, 2018). Several other divergences have since been
350   proposed to focus on obtaining a robust posterior when the input and output features are noisy or the
351   existence of outliers. Examples of robust divergences include Rényi divergence (Lee & Shin, 2022),
352   beta and alpha divergences (Futami et al., 2018; Regli & Silva, 2018) and f-divergences (Cheng et al.,
353   2021). They provide the extra parameters or flexible functions to control the density ratio so that
354   the posterior can focus more on mass covering, mode seeking abilities or is robust against outliers.
355   Generalized variational inference (Knoblauch et al., 2019) suggested that any form of divergence can
356   be used to replace the KL objective when the model is misspecified. As neural processes facilitate
357   a posterior to approximate the prior distribution, we are able to control the density ratios of two
358
      posterior distributions with robust divergences for better predictions.

      Datasets and training details. We evaluate the proposed method on multiple regression tasks: 1D
364
      regression (Garnelo et al., 2018a; Gordon et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2019; Nguyen & Grover, 2022),
365
      image inpainting (Gordon et al., 2019; Nguyen & Grover, 2022). 1D regression includes three
366   Gaussian Process (GP) regression tasks with different kernels: RBF, Matern 5/2 and Periodic. Given
367   a function f sampled from a GP prior with varying scale and length and a context set generated by
368   such function, our goal is to predict the target distribution. The number of context points is randomly
369   sampled M ∼ U(3, 50), and the number of target points is N ∼ U(3, 50 − M ) (Nguyen & Grover,
370   2022). We choose 100,000 functions for training, and sample another 3,000 functions for testing. The
371   input features were normalized to [−2, 2]. Image inpainting involves 2D regression on three image
372   datasets: MNIST, SVHN and CelebA. Given some pixel coordinates x and intensities y as context,
373   the goal is to predict the pixel value for the rest of image. The number of context points for inpainting
374
      tasks is M ∼ U(3, 200) and the target point count is N ∼ U(3, 200 − M ). The input coordinates
      were normalized to [−1, 1] and pixel intensities were rescaled to [−0.5, 0.5]. All the models can be
375
      trained using a single GPU with 16GB memory.

531
      In this paper, we propose the Rényi Neural Process (RNP), a new NP framework designed to mitigate
      prior misspecification in neural processes. We bridge the commonly adopted variational inference
532
      and maximum likelihood estimation objectives in vanilla NPs through the use of Rényi divergence
533
      and demonstrate the superiority of our generalized objective in improving predictive performance
534   by selecting optimal α values. We apply the framework to multiple state-of-the-art NP models and
535   observe consistent log-likelihood improvements across benchmarks, including 1D regression, image
536   inpainting, and real-world regression tasks. Our framework can be further extended to improve the
537   robustness of contextual inferences, such as prompt design in large language models. Limitations of
538   our framework lie in drawing multiple samples with Monte-Carlo which scarifies the computational
539   efficiency in exchange for better performance and infeasibility to compute analytical solutions on the
      divergence.

714       Training:
715       for epoch=1 to max_epoch do

717         Obtain the posterior distribution with the encoding network: qφ (z|XT , YT , XC , YC )
718
            Obtain the approximated prior distribution with the encoding network: qφ (z|XC , YC )
            Sample z1 , ..., zK ∼ qφ (z|XT , YT , XC , YC )
719
            Construct the likelihood model with the decoding network: pθ (YT |XT , zk )

            Compute the objective LRN P using Eq 7 or Eq 12

725       Sample z1 , ..., zK ∼ qφ (z|XC , YC )
