009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In the last years, many neural network-based approaches have been proposed to
012            tackle combinatorial optimization problems such as routing problems. Many of
013            these approaches are based on graph neural networks (GNNs) or related trans-
014            formers, operating on the Euclidean coordinates representing the routing prob-
015            lems. However, GNNs are inherently not well suited to operate on dense graphs,
016            such as in routing problems. Furthermore, models operating on Euclidean coor-
017            dinates cannot be applied to non-Euclidean versions of routing problems that are
018            often found in real-world settings. To overcome these limitations, we propose
019
               a novel GNN-related edge-based neural model called Graph Edge Attention Net-
               work (GREAT). We evaluate the performance of GREAT in the edge-classification
020
               task to predict optimal edges in the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP). We can
021
               use such a trained GREAT model to produce sparse TSP graph instances, keep-
022            ing only the edges GREAT finds promising. Compared to other, non-learning-
023            based methods to sparsify TSP graphs, GREAT can produce very sparse graphs
024            while keeping most of the optimal edges. Furthermore, we build a reinforce-
025            ment learning-based GREAT framework which we apply to Euclidean and non-
026            Euclidean asymmetric TSP. This framework achieves state-of-the-art results.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool for learning on graph-structured
032   data such as molecules, social networks, or citation graphs (Wu et al., 2020). In recent years, GNNs
033   have also been applied in the setting of combinatorial optimization, especially routing problems
034   (Joshi et al., 2019; Hudson et al., 2021; Xin et al., 2021) since such problems can be interpreted
035   as graph problems. However, the graph representations of routing problems, which are typically
036   complete, dense graphs, are ill-suited for GNNs. This is because vanilla GNNs are not generally
037   suitable for learning on complete graphs. GNNs are related to the Weisfeiler Leman algorithm
038   which is known to exploit graph structure (Morris et al., 2019). Complete graphs feature no such
039
      structure, resulting in poor GNN performance. Moreover, over-smoothing is a well-known problem
      happening in (deep) GNNs which means that feature vectors computed for different nodes become
040
      more and more similar with every layer (Rusch et al., 2023). Naturally, in dense or even complete
041
      graphs this problem is even more present as all nodes share the same information leading to similar
042   encodings. Consequently, Lischka et al. (2024) showed that the performance of GNNs operating on
043   routing problems can be increased if graphs are made sparse in a preprocessing step. However, the
044   proposed sparsification methods of Lischka et al. (2024) rely on hand-crafted heuristics which goes
045   against the idea of data-driven, end-to-end machine learning frameworks.
046
      In this paper, we overcome the limitations of regular GNNs by introducing the Graph Edge Attention
047
      Network (GREAT). This results in the following contributions:
048
049         • Whereas traditional GNNs operate on a node-level by using node-based message pass-
050           ing operations, GREAT is edge-based, meaning information is passed along edges shar-
051           ing endpoints. This makes GREAT perfect for edge-level tasks such as routing problems
052           where the edges to travel along are selected. We note, however, that the idea of GREAT is
053           task-independent and it can potentially also be applied in other suitable settings, possibly
              chemistry or road networks.


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
             • We evaluate GREAT in the task of edge classification, training the architecture to predict
055            optimal edges in a Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) tour in a supervised setting. By
056            this, GREAT can be used as a learning-based and data-driven sparsification method for
057            routing graphs. The produced sparse graphs are less likely to delete optimal edges than
058            hand-crafted heuristics while being overall sparser.
059
060          • We build a reinforcement learning framework that can be trained end-to-end to predict
061            optimal TSP tours. As the inputs of GREAT are edge features (e.g., distances), GREAT
062            applies to all variants of TSP, including non-Euclidean variants such as the asymmetric TSP.
063
               The resulting trained framework achieves state-of-the-art performance for two asymmetric
               TSP distributions.

069   2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
070
071   Graph neural networks are a class of neural architectures that operate on graph-structured data. In
072   contrast to other neural architectures like MLPs where the connections of the neurons are fixed and
073   grid-shaped, the connections in a GNN reflect the structure of the input data.
074   In essence, GNNs are a neural version of the well-known Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) graph isomor-
075   phism heuristic (Morris et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019). In this heuristic, graph nodes are assigned
076   colors that are iteratively updated. Two nodes share the same color if they shared the same color in
077   the previous iteration and they had the same amount of neighbors of each color in the last iteration.
078   When the test is applied to two graphs and the graphs do not have the same amount of nodes of
079   some color in some iteration, they are non-isomorphic. WL is only a heuristic, however, as there are
080   certain non-isomorphic graphs it can not distinguish. Examples are regular graphs (graphs where
081
      all nodes have the same degree) (Kiefer, 2020). We note that complete graphs (that we encounter in
      routing problems) are regular graphs.
082
083   GNNs follow a similar principle as the WL heuristic, but instead of colors, vector representations
084   of the nodes are computed. GNNs iteratively compute these node feature vectors by aggregating
085   over the node feature vectors of adjacent nodes and mapping the old feature vector together with
086   the aggregation to a new node feature vector. Additionally, the feature vectors are multiplied with
087
      trainable weight matrices and non-linearities are applied to achieve actual learning. The node feature
      vectors of a neighborhood are typically scaled in some way (depending on the respective GNN
088
      architecture) and sometimes, edge feature vectors are also considered within the aggregations. An
089
      example can be found in fig. 1. Its mathematical formulation might look like this:

150   2.3   L EARNING TO ROUTE
151
152
      In recent years, many studies have tried to solve routing problems such as the Traveling Salesmen
      Problem or the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP).
153
154   Popular approaches for solving routing problems with the help of machine learning include rein-
155   forcement learning (RL) frameworks, where encodings for the problem instances are computed.
156   These encodings are then used to incrementally build solutions by selecting one node in the problem
157   instance at a time. Successful works in this category include Deudon et al. (2018); Nazari et al.
158
      (2018); Kool et al. (2019); Kwon et al. (2020); Jin et al. (2023b).
159   Another possibility to use machine learning for solving routing problems is to predict edge probabil-
160   ities or scores which are later used in search algorithms such as beam search or guided local search.
161   Examples for such works are Joshi et al. (2019); Fu et al. (2021); Xin et al. (2021); Hudson et al.
      (2021); Kool et al. (2019); Min et al. (2024).


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      A further possibility is iterative methods where a solution to a routing problem is improved over
163   and over until a stopping criterion (e.g., convergence) is met. Possibilities for such improvements
164   are optimizing subproblems or applying improvement operators such as k-opt. Examples for such
165   works are da Costa et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Cheng et al. (2023); Lu et al. (2019); Chen &
166   Tian (2019); Li et al. (2021).

168   2.3.1   N ON -E UCLIDEAN ROUTING P ROBLEMS
169
      Many of the mentioned works, especially in the first two categories, use GNNs or transformer mod-
170
      els (which are related to GNNs via GATs (Joshi, 2020)) to capture the structure of the routing
171
      problem in their neural architecture. This is done by interpreting the coordinates of Euclidean rout-
172   ing problem instances as node features. These node features are then processed in the GNN or
173   transformer architectures to produce encodings of the overall problem. However, this limits the
174   applicability of such works to Euclidean routing problems. This is unfortunate, as non-Euclidean
175   routing problems are highly relevant in reality. Consider, e.g., one-way streets which result in un-
176   equal travel distances between two points depending on the direction one is going. Another example
177   is variants of TSP that consider energy consumption as the objective to be minimized. If point A is
178   located at a higher altitude than point B, traveling from A to B might require less energy than the
179   other way around.
180   So far, only a few studies have also investigated non-Euclidean versions of routing problems, such as
181   the asymmetric TSP (ATSP). One such study is Gaile et al. (2022) where they solve synthetic ATSP
182   instances with unsupervised learning, reinforcement learning, and supervised learning approaches.
183   Another study is Wang et al. (2023) that uses online reinforcement learning to solve ATSP instances
184   of TSPLIB (Reinelt, 1991). Another successful work tackling ATSP is Kwon et al. (2021). There,
185   the Matrix Encoding Network (MatNet) is proposed, a neural model suitable to operate on matrix en-
186
      codings representing combinatorial optimization problems such as the distance matrices of (A)TSP.
      Their model is trained using RL.

189   3   G RAPH E DGE ATTENTION N ETWORK
190
191   Existing GNNs are based on node-level message-passing operations, making them perfect for node-
192   level tasks as is also underlined by their connection to the WL heuristic. In contrast, we propose
193   an edge-level-based GNN where information is passed along neighboring edges. This makes our
194
      model perfect for edge-level tasks such as edge classification (e.g., in the context of routing prob-
      lems, determining if edges are “promising” to be part of the optimal solution or not). Our model is
195
      attention-based, meaning the “focus” of an edge to another, adjacent edge in the update operation is
196
      determined using the attention mechanism. Consequently, similar to the Graph Attention Network
197   (GAT) we call our architecture Graph Edge Attention Network (GREAT). A simple visualization
198   of the idea of GREAT is shown in fig. 2. In this visualization, edge e14 attends to all other edges
199   it shares an endpoint with. While GREAT is a task-independent framework, it is suited perfectly
200   for routing problems: Consider TSP as an example. There, we do not have any node features, only
201   edge features given as distances between nodes. A normal GNN would not be suitable to process
202   such information well. Existing papers use coordinates of the nodes in the Euclidean space as node
203   features to overcome this limitation. However, this trick only works for Euclidean TSP and not other
204   symmetric or asymmetric TSP adaptations. GREAT, however, can be applied to all these variants.
205   Instead of purely focusing on edge features and ignoring node features, it would also be possible to
206   transform the graph in its line graph and apply a GNN operating only on node features on this line
207   graph. In the line graph, each edge ei,j of the original graph is a node ni,j and two nodes ni,j , nk,m
208   in the line graph are connected if the corresponding edges ei,j and ek,m in the original graph share
209   an endpoint. However, a TSP instance of n cities contains n2 many edges. Therefore, the line graph
210   of this instance would have O(n2 ) many nodes. Furthermore, as the original TSP graph is complete,
211   each of the endpoints {i, j} of an edge in the original graphs is part of n many edges. This means
212
      that in the line graph, each node has 2n many connections to other nodes. In other words, each of
      the O(n2 ) nodes has O(n) edges leading to O(n3 ) many edges in the line graph. This implies that
213
      the line graph has one order of magnitude more edges and nodes than the original graph.
214
215   We note that GREAT can be applied to extensions of the TSP such as the CVRP or TSP with time
      windows (TSPTW) as well: even though capacities and time windows are node-level features, we


                                                        4

      can easily transform them into edge features. Consider CVRP where a node j has a demand cj . We
217   can simply add demand cj to all edges ei,j in the graph. This is because we know that if we have an
218   edge ei,j in the tour, we will visit node j in the next step and therefore need a free capacity in our
219   vehicle big enough to serve the demand of node j which is cj . An analogous extension works for
220   TSPTW.
221
      We further note that even though GREAT has been developed in the context of routing problems, it
222
      generally is a task-oblivious architecture and it might be useful in completely different domains as
223   well such as chemistry, road, or flow networks.

      3.1    A RCHITECTURE
226
227   In the following, we provide the mathematical model defining the different layers of a GREAT
228   model. In particular, we propose two versions of GREAT.
229
      The first version is purely edge-focused and does not have any node features. Here, each edge
230
      exchanges information with all other edges it shares at least one endpoint with. The idea essentially
231
      corresponds to the visualization in fig. 2. In the following, we refer to this variant as “node-free”
232   GREAT.
233
234   The second version is also edge-focused but has intermediate, temporary node features. This essen-
235
      tially means that nodes are used to save all information on adjacent edges. Afterward, the features
      of an edge are computed by combining the temporary node features of their respective endpoints.
236
      These node features are then deleted and not passed on to the next layer, only the edge features are
237
      passed on. The idea of this GREAT variant is visualized in fig. 3 and fig. 4 In the remainder of this
238   study, we refer to this GREAT version as “node-based”.

345   4.1   L EARNING TO S PARSIFY
346
347   In this experiment, we demonstrate GREAT’s capability in edge-classification tasks. In particular,
348   we train the network to predict optimal TSP edges for Euclidean TSP of size 100. The predicted
349   edges obtained from this network could later on be used in beam searches to create valid TSP
350   solutions, or, alternately for TSP sparsification as done in Lischka et al. (2024). Therefore, we
351
      will evaluate the capability of the trained network for sparsifying TSP graphs and, while doing so,
      keeping optimal edges.
352
353   The hyperparameters in this setting are as follows. We train a node-based and a node-free version
354   of GREAT. For both models, we choose 10 hidden layers. The hidden dimension is 64 and each
355   attention layer has 4 attention heads. Training is performed for 200 epochs and there are 50,000
356   training instances in each epoch. Every 10 epochs, we change the dataset to a fresh set of 50,000
357
      instances (meaning 200 × 50, 000 = 10, 000, 000 instances in total). We used the Adam optimizer
      with a constant learning rate of 0.0001 and weighted cross-entropy as our loss function to account
358
      for the fact that there is an unequal number of optimal and non-optimal edges in a TSP graph. Targets
359
      for the optimal edges were generated using the LKH algorithm.
360
361   The evaluation of the trained networks is done on 100 instances. The performance of the network
362   is benchmarked against the results of the “classical” algorithms 1-Tree and k-nn used in the graph
363
      sparsification task of Lischka et al. (2024). The results are shown in table 1. For the “classical”
      sparsification methods, we can set a hyperparameter k specifying that the k most promising outgoing
364
      edges of each node in the graph are kept in the sparsified instance. This parameter k allows us to
365
      perfectly influence how many edges will be part of the sparse graph (k × n, where n is the TSP
366   size). We chose three different values of k, i.e., k = 3, 5, 10. For GREAT, there is no such a
367   hyperparameter. We can, instead, set different thresholds for the probability p that the network
368   predicts an edge to be part of the optimal solution. For this, we chose 0.00001, 0.5, and 0.999. We
369   can see that choosing p = 0.999 results in a similar number of edges as k = 3. In this setting, the
370   precision and recall of both GREAT versions are significantly better than the scores achieved by the
371   “classical” algorithms. Here, by precision, we quantify the performance of only keeping edges that
372   are indeed optimal. Recall refers to the ability of the approach to keep all optimal edges in the sparse
373   graph. The result indicates that GREAT can produce very sparse graphs while missing relatively few
374
      optimal edges. Overall, however, we can see that for the classical algorithms, it is easier to just make
      the graphs less sparse and by this prevent deleting optimal edges. For GREAT, this is not possible,
375
      as lowering p further to increase recall leads to prohibitively low precision.
376
377   Overall, we summarize that GREAT is a very powerful technique for creating extremely sparse
      TSP graphs while deleting only a small number of optimal edges. We hypothesize that creating such

379   where sparse graphs of the most promising edges are combined with dense graphs to prevent deleting

381   similar results in this experiment.

383   4.2   L EARNING TO S OLVE NON -E UCLIDEAN TSP
384
385   In this task, we train GREAT in a reinforcement learning framework to construct TSP solutions
386   incrementally by adding one node at a time to a partial solution. Our framework follows the encoder-
387   decoder approach (where GREAT serves as the encoder and a multi-pointer network as the decoder)
388
      and is trained using POMO (Kwon et al., 2020). We focus on three different TSP variants, and by
      this aim to demonstrate GREAT’s versatility to also apply to non-Euclidean TSP:
389
390         1. Euclidean TSP where the coordinates are distributed uniformly at random in the unit square.
391         2. Asymmetric TSP with triangle inequality (TMAT) as was used in Kwon et al. (2021). We
392            use the code of Kwon et al. (2021) to generate instances. However, we normalize the
393            distance matrices differently: Instead of a fixed scaling value, we normalize each instance
394            individually such that the biggest distance is exactly 1. By this, we ensure that the distances
395            use the full range of the interval (0,1) as well as possible.
396         3. Extremely asymmetric TSP (XASY) where all pairwise distances are sampled uniformly
397            at random from the interval (0,1). The same distribution was used in Gaile et al. (2022).
398            Here, the triangle inequality does generally not hold.
399
400
      The exact setting in this experiment is the following. For each distribution, we train three versions of
      GREAT. A node-based and a node-free network with hidden dimension 128 as well as a node-free
401
      network with hidden dimension 256. All networks have 5 hidden layers and 8 attention heads. Train-
402
      ing is done for 400 epochs and there are 25,000 instances in each epoch. Again, every 10 epochs,
403   we change the dataset to a fresh set of 25,000 instances (meaning 400 × 25, 000 = 10, 000, 000 in-
404   stances in total). We evaluate the model after each epoch and save the model with the best validation
405   loss during these 400 epochs for testing. Furthermore, while training, the distances of all instances
406   in the current data batch were multiplied by a factor in the range (0.5, 1.5) to ensure the models
407   learn from a more robust data distribution. This allows us to augment the dataset at inference by a
408   factor of ×8 like was done in Kwon et al. (2020). However, we want to note that while augment-
409   ing the data by this factor at inference time improves performance, using even bigger augmentation
410   factors like ×128 in Kwon et al. (2021) does not lead to much better results (especially considering
411
      the enormous blowup in runtime). We suppose that this is due to our augmentation implementation
      having a disadvantage. While the augmentation method in, e.g., Kwon et al. (2020) which works
412
      by rotating coordinates, does not alter the underlying distribution much, our method by multiplying
413
      distances does change the distribution considerably. We note that instances multiplied with values
414   close to 1 are favored in the end, compared to instances multiplied with values close to the borders
415   0.5 and 1.5.
416
417
      The overall framework to construct solutions, as well as the decoder to decode the encodings pro-
      vided by GREAT and the loss formulation, are adapted from Jin et al. (2023b). We note that in this
418
      setting of incrementally building TSP solutions with an encoder-decoder approach, we would like
419
      to have node encodings as input for the decoder and not edge encodings like they are produced by
420   GREAT. This is because we want to iteratively select the next node to visit, given a partial solution.
421   As GREAT is generally used to compute edge encodings, all GREAT architectures in this experi-
422   ment (node-free and node-based) have a final node-based layer where the results of the temporary
423   node features (compare fig. 3) are returned instead of processing them further to obtain edge embed-
424   dings again. By this, we can provide the decoder architecture with node encodings, despite having
425   operated on edge-level during the internal operations of GREAT. A visualization of the framework
426   can be found in fig. 6.

522   5   C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this work, we introduce GREAT, a novel GNN-related neural architecture for edge-based graph
525   problems. While for previous GNN architectures it was necessary to transform graphs into their
526   line graph representation to operate in purely edge-focused settings, GREAT can directly be ap-
527   plied in such contexts. We evaluate GREAT in an edge-classification task to predict optimal TSP
528   edges. In this task, GREAT is able to produce very sparse TSP graphs while deleting relatively
529   few optimal edges compared to heuristic methods. Furthermore, we develop a GREAT-based RL
530   framework to directly solve TSP. Compared to existing frameworks, GREAT offers the advantage
531
      of directly operating on the edge distances, overcoming the limitation of previous Transformer and
      GNN-based models that operate on node coordinates which essentially limits these architectures to
532
      Euclidean TSP. This limitation is rather disadvantageous in real-life settings, however, as distances
533
      (and especially other characteristics like time and energy consumption) are often asymmetric due
534   to topography (e.g., elevation) or traffic congestion. GREAT achieves promising performance on
535   several TSP variants (Euclidean, asymmetric with triangle inequality, and asymmetric without tri-
536   angle inequality). We postpone it to future work to adapt GREAT to other routing problems such as
537   CVRP (by translating node demands to edge demands). Furthermore, we aim to develop better data-
538   augmentation methods for GREAT, allowing us to increase optimality at inference time by solving
539   each instance multiple times. We further believe that GREAT could be useful in edge-regression
      tasks (e.g., in the setting of Hudson et al. (2021)) and, possibly, beyond routing problems.
