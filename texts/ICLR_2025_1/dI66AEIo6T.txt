011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) are traditionally trained on massive digitized text
014            corpora; however, alternative data sources exist that may help evaluate and im-
015            prove the alignment between language models and humans. We contribute to the
016
               assessment of the role of data sources in human-LLM alignment. Specifically, we
               present work aimed at understanding differences in the informational content of
017
               text, behavior (e.g., free associations), and brain (e.g., fMRI) data. Using repre-
018
               sentational similarity analysis, we show that word vectors derived from behavior
019            and brain data encode information that differs from their text-derived cousins. Fur-
020            thermore, using an interpretability method that we term representational content
021            analysis, we find that, in particular, behavior representations better encode certain
022            affective, agentic, and socio-moral dimensions. The findings highlight the poten-
023            tial of behavior data to evaluate and improve language models along dimensions
024            critical for human-LLM alignment.
025
026
027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) are trained to predict the occurrence of tokens given their context.
030   Research demonstrates that training larger models on more text leads to predictable improvements
031   on this objective and other benchmarks (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022).
032   However, optimizing for next-token prediction does not automatically produce models that align
033   well with people’s preferences, representations, or judgments. To remedy this (insofar as such align-
034   ment is desired), researchers are incorporating more explicit sources of human data into training and
035   evaluation pipelines.
036   For instance, in addition to the now-popular use of explicit feedback on language model outputs
037   (e.g., via reinforcement learning from human feedback or direct preference optimization Christiano
038   et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2022), researchers have also been leveraging semantic textual similarity
039   judgments (e.g., Cer et al., 2017, dataset), sentiment judgments (e.g., Socher et al., 2013, dataset),
040   sensorimotor judgments (Kennington, 2021), as well as brain imaging recordings (Toneva & Wehbe,
041   2019; Hollenstein et al., 2019, see also, github.com/brain-score/language). Not only do these efforts
042   demonstrate improvements in model helpfulness and accuracy, but they may also improve human-
043   model trust and communication (Sucholutsky & Griffiths, 2023; Bansal et al., 2019), as well as make
044
      for more predictive and plausible models of human psychology (Binz & Schulz, 2023; Hussain et al.,
      2024).
045
046   Ultimately, it is clear that human-generated data must play a crucial role, both in measuring and
047   increasing human-model alignment (henceforth, just human-model alignment). However, it remains
048   an open question which types of human data should be used, and what the promise of these prospec-
049   tive types may be.
050   Prospective data for human-model alignment can be grouped into three types (see also Roads &
051   Love, 2023): text, behavior, and brain. Although text has received considerable attention in language
052   modeling (i.e., for pretraining), behavior and brain data have attracted comparatively little. In light
053   of recent large-scale, high-resolution collection efforts (e.g., De Deyne et al., 2019; Jamali et al.,
      2024), these two data types might hold untapped potential for human-model alignment. Our study

054
      thus seeks to address two research questions: (a) do behavior and brain data encode systematically
055   different information than text, and (b) are these differences useful from the perspective of human-
056   model alignment?

058
      In what follows, we run a representational similarity analysis (RSA) to uncover systematic differ-
      ences between text, behavior, and brain data (Section 4.1). We then analyze the content of these
059
      differences via our representational content analysis (RCA, Sections 4.2, 4.3), and end with a dis-
060
      cussion of the merits and limitations of our work.

      2     O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
064
065
066   Our contributions are four-fold. First, we perform a comprehensive comparison of 10 text repre-
067   sentations, 10 behavior representations, and 6 brain representations, revealing robust differences
068
      between data types (Section 4.1).
069   Second, we collate the largest (to our knowledge) metabase of predominantly human-rated (behav-
070   ioral) word properties (word norms), Section 3.1), which we call psychNorms. The metabase is
071   publicly available at github.com/[ANONYM]/psychNorms (and in the supplementary materials),
072   and reflects over half a century of psycholinguistic research. We hope it will serve as a valuable
073   resource for researchers seeking to measure and interpret language representations along psycho-
074
      logically meaningful dimensions.
075   Third, leveraging psychNorms and linear probes (see, e.g., Belinkov, 2022), we demonstrate how
076   to build interpretable informational content profiles for abstract representations via a novel analysis
077   framework that we call representational content analysis (RCA, Section 3.3). By comparing the
078   profiles of different representations, we can provide crucial insight into the content of their differ-
079   ences. This could be especially useful for interpreting and navigating discrepancies between the
080
      plethora of otherwise opaque representational alignment metrics (Sucholutsky et al., 2023).
081   Fourth, and most importantly, we show that, despite being trained on orders of magnitudes less data,
082   the behavior representations encode psychological information of equivalent or even superior reach
083   and quality in comparison to their text-based cousins (Sections 4.2, 4.3). This indicates that behavior
084   contains a wealth of highly concentrated psychological information, and is a powerful complement
085   to text for measuring and improving human-LLM alignment.
086   We view our work as foundational with respect to the entitled goal of improving human-LLM align-
087   ment. By carrying out the necessary groundwork looking into the space of possible data sources
088   and the kinds of information they encode, we hope to pave the way for future researchers seeking to
089   measure and improve the human-likeness of the current state-of-the-art (SOTA).

254   3.2     R EPRESENTATIONAL SIMILARITY ANALYSIS
255
256
      We use representational similarity analysis (RSA) to compare the information encoded in the above
      representations. Developed within neuroscience (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008), RSA enables compar-
257
      isons of representations from otherwise-disparate modalities (e.g., fMRI, EEG, similarity ratings)
258
      by leveraging the fact that the different dimensions may nevertheless contain information that seeks
259   to distinguish a comparable set of mental states, stimuli, or other kinds of entities.
260
261   In our case, the entities being distinguished are words. Consequently, RSA measures the similar-
262
      ity between two matrices, M1 and M2 , where each row i represents a word, and each column j
      reflects a measurement unit (dimensions). For the brain representations, these units may be voxels
263
      (fMRI) or electrode readings (EEG), whereas for text and behavior models, the units are often latent
264
      dimensions. RSA addresses the challenge of correlating these different units by transforming M1
265   and M2 into a common space. This transformation is achieved by calculating the (dis)similarities
266   between the rows of M1 and M2 , forming what is known as a representational similarity matrix, S.
267   Following Lenci et al. (e.g., 2022)), we compute the cosine similarity matrices S1 and S2 , as:
268
269
                                      S1 = M̂1 · M̂1⊤    and   S2 = M̂2 · M̂2⊤ ,


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      where the hat notation M̂ indicates that the rows of the matrices have been L2 normalized. We then
271   compute the similarity between the two representations by taking the Spearman correlation between
272   the flattened upper triangles (excluding the diagonal) of S1 and S2 .

      3.3   R EPRESENTATIONAL CONTENT ANALYSIS
275
276   Representational content analysis (RCA) is an approach to interpretable informational content pro-
277   files for abstract numerical representations. Although it leverages the well-established technique
278   of probing from deep learning interpretability (see e.g., Belinkov, 2022), it differs from traditional
279   probing applications in its scope, employing tens or even hundreds (as in our case) of targets to more
280   holistically interpret the information encoded.
281   Our RCA implementation uses L2-regularized linear probing classifiers and regressors. We employ
282   L2-regularization to mitigate issues such as multicolinearity, underdetermination, and over-fitting
283   in high-dimensional settings. Following Hupkes et al. (2018), we use linear probes to avoid the
284   risk of more flexible estimators learning features that do not reflect what is present in the original
285   representations.
286
      For numerical norms, we use the Scikit-Learn API’s RidgeCV (Pedregosa et al., 2011). For bi-
287   nary and multi-class norms, we use the API’s LogisticRegressionCV. Both estimators per-
288   form automatic (hyperparameter) tuning of the L2 penalty. This parameter—alpha in the case of
289   RidgeCV, or C in the case of LogisticRegressionCV (equivalent to 1/apha)—is selected
290   from a grid of values ranging from 10−5 to 105 (in alpha terms) with even spacing in log (base-10)
291   space.
292
      Generalization performance is measured via 5-fold nested cross-validation (Pedregosa et al., 2011),
293   where the regression coefficients and L2 penalty parameter are fitted in an inner loop, and evaluated
294   on separate test sets in the outer loop (following, e.g., Varma & Simon, 2006).
295
296
      Finally, to ensure some minimum reliability for performance estimates, we do not probe in cases
      where the intersection of the representation and norm vocabularies results in a test set with fewer
297
      than 20 samples. This is important to keep in mind for Section 4.2, where, in a minority of cases,
298
      average performances are estimated from a reduced set of norms.

354

      This raises the question: What is the content of these differences? This is important from the per-
355
      spective of human-model alignment, where alignment on different dimensions will have varying
356
      implications for, for instance, a model’s helpfulness, accuracy, or psychological plausibility. To
357   address this question, we leverage our psychNorms metabase (Section 3.1) as targets in a represen-
358   tational content analysis (RCA, Section 3.3).

      5    D ISCUSSION
479
480
      This article began by asking whether behavior and brain data could help in measuring and increasing
481   human-LLM alignment (beyond text). We showed that behavior and brain representations encode
482   information that differs from that of the text representations (Section 4.1). Drawing on our psych-
483   Norms metabase and RCA, we probed these representations to reveal rich, interpretable psycholog-
484

      at which the median was taken (fold-level means for Wilcoxon versus norm-level means for Figure 4).

486
      ical profiles, with behavior outperforming text on several dimensions (e.g., Dominance, Arousal,
487   Section 4.2). Motivated by evidence suggesting psychologically important differences between text
488   and behavior, we carried out an ensemble RCA to reveal significant improvements from ensembling
489   behavior with text on affective, agentic, and Social/Moral dimensions.
490
      Our findings have important implications. The revealed differences in informational content can
491
      conceivably be exploited for human-LLM alignment. Consistent with the current practice of pre-
492
      training on text and fine-tuning on human behavior, our findings suggest that LLMs that are trained
493   on multiple sources of data—specifically, text and behavior—are well-equipped to cover a larger
494   number of dimensions relevant to human emotion, agency, and morality. Moreover, our RSA and
495   RCA findings can be used to better understand the contents of behavioral datasets already used in the
496   evaluation of language models—for instance, textual similarity judgements (e.g., Cer et al., 2017,
497   dataset), sentiment judgments (e.g., Socher et al., 2013, dataset), and, more prospectively, free as-
498   sociations (Thawani et al., 2019; Abramski et al., 2024). Our analyses provide insight into both the
499   content of these datasets, and how they relate to each other. We view this as crucial to improving
500   our understanding of what is being evaluated or optimized for in such cases (Burden, 2024).
501   Our work has several limitations. First, it is foundational with respect to the entitled goal of improv-
502   ing human-LLM alignment: Although we demonstrate that behavior could in principle complement
503   text in work seeking to measure or increase human-LLM alignment, we do not demonstrate this in
504   practice (i.e., with the latest, SOTA LLMs). Nevertheless, the work provides hints at how this may
505   be done—for instance, via RSA, RCA, or fine-tuning of the weights of SOTA models using behav-
506   ior data (provided those weights are open, see Wulff et al., 2024)—and methods for comparing and
507
      aligning data from different modalities are not in short supply (see, e.g., Sucholutsky & Griffiths,
      2023).
508
509   Second, our approach does not allow for perfectly controlled representational content comparisons.
510   As mentioned in Section 4.2, although better probing results may signal the encoding of more norm-
511   relevant information, they may also reflect larger probe-training set sizes. These issues can be al-
512   leviated by subsetting to the same vocabulary across comparison conditions (as we do in Section
513
      4.3). However, this will naturally reduce the probe’s sensitivity to norm-relevant signal due to the
      decrease in the training set size from subsetting.
514
515   One final limitation concerns our brain data, in which we detect scant evidence of psychological
516   information. Although this may simply be due to the brain representations’ small vocabularies, it
517   could also be that brain is poorly suited to word-level analyses such as ours. After all, the brain data
518   was collected during sentence-level tasks, meaning that word-level representations had to be ex-
519
      tracted via relatively crude heuristics (e.g., a four-second hemodynamic delay offset) and averaging
      across contexts (Hollenstein et al., 2019). We would thus caution against drawing strong conclusions
520
      against other brain data formats (e.g., github.com/brain-score/language) on these bases.

524   6   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this work, we investigated behavior and brain data as prospective complements to text for mea-
527
      suring and improving human-LLM alignment. We found that behavior, in particular, captures psy-
528   chological information to a breadth and depth rivalling that of text, and also captures unique psy-
529   chological variance on certain dimensions. Our work thus contributes to a growing body of research
530   (e.g., Bai et al., 2022; Kennington, 2021; Abramski et al., 2024) suggesting behavior as an important
531   complement to text in LLM-training and evaluation pipelines, with the potential to improve LLM
532   helpfulness, accuracy, and psychological plausibility.
