               Wikipedia serves as a rich repository of well-curated knowledge, making it a
               popular source for information retrieval through question answering (QA). Of-
013
               ten, these inquiries involve multiple entities, such as “How many Turing Award
014
               winners are Canadian?”, necessitating the consolidation of information from var-
015            ious Wikipedia pages. Multi-entity question answering typically comprises two
016            steps: multi-entity retrieval and subsequent reasoning using large language mod-
017            els (LLMs). The pre-defined connections within Wikipedia, known as the wiki-
018            graph, facilitate relatively straightforward multi-entity retrieval. However, tradi-
019            tional solutions leveraging retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) encounter lim-
020            itations, as LLMs often struggle to aggregate insights from multiple pages effec-
021            tively. In response, we propose a Structured QA (SQA) approach that first orga-
022            nizes extracted entities into a relational table (e.g., a table schema with columns
023             (name, nationality) for Turing Award winners) and then employs table-based
024            methods such as TableQA or NL2SQL for answering. Extensive experiments
025            demonstrate the superior effectiveness of SQA in addressing multi-entity QA chal-
026            lenges over Wikipedia, improves the overall accuracy 29.6% over the SOTA solu-
027
               tions, paving the way for more robust information retrieval from Wikipedia.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Wikipedia (Wikipedia web, 2004) serves as a crucial and meticulously curated repository of global
033   knowledge, offering users a comprehensive understanding of diverse topics. A common method for
034   extracting information from this extensive resource is through question answering (QA) (Chen et al.,
035   2017), often exemplified by inquiries like, ”What are the capitals of countries that border France?”
036   or ”How many Turing Award winners are Canadian?” These questions frequently involve multiple
037   entities, highlighting the intricate nature of information retrieval in today’s data-rich environment.
038   The significance of multi-entity QA lies in its ability to sift through vast datasets to uncover hidden
039   insights. Addressing questions that span multiple entities, not only expands the scope of retriev-
040   able information but also deepens users’ understanding of complex relationships between entities.
041   Furthermore, as the volume of available data skyrockets, multi-entity QA becomes increasingly
042   vital for enhancing user interactions with extensive knowledge bases, ultimately transforming the
043   way individuals access and utilize information. The primary challenges in multi-entity question
044
      answering (QA) involve the accurate parsing and understanding of complex queries to deliver pre-
      cise responses. This necessitates the integration of advanced Natural Language Processing (NLP)
045
      techniques to effectively address these issues. With the ongoing development of large language
046
      models (LLMs), LLM-based approaches have recently emerged, primarily focusing on question-
047   answering tasks using vanilla LLMs or incorporating Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Gao
048   et al., 2023).
049

053   et al., 2023), outdated information, etc. A prevalent one-step further method is to incorporate RAG,
      retrieving relevant Wikipedia pages prior to inputting the extracted content along with the query

082   Our Proposed Structured QA. To tackle the challenge of LLMs in aggregating information from
083   multiple sources, we propose the Structured Question Answering (SQA) approach, as illustrated in
084   Figure 1(c), to effectively address multi-entity QA. Specifically, we first infer a relational schema
085   R based on the user query Q. For example, we derive the schema (name, nationality) from the
086   query “How many Turing Award winners are Canadian?” Next, we perform a graph search over a
087   pre-defined Wikipedia graph to retrieve relevant entities. The information from these entities is then

089
      along with the query into an LLM, which is proven significantly enhances the ability in multi-entity
      QA tasks.

      2.1   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
111
112   Advanced Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems have evolved to incorporate pre-
113   retrieval, retrieval, and post-retrieval strategies aimed at mitigating the limitations of basic RAG
114   methods. In parallel, Modular RAG systems have introduced frameworks for iterative and dynamic
115   cycles of intertwined retrieval and generation processes (Gao et al., 2023). Community summaries
116   serve as a form of self-memory (Cheng et al., 2023), enhancing generation-augmented retrieval to
117   support future cycles of information generation. Additionally, the parallel generation of community
118
      answers from these summaries reflects an iterative (Shao et al., 2023) or federated (Wang et al.,
      2024b) retrieval-generation approach. Similar concepts have been amalgamated in other systems
119
      for tasks such as multi-document summarization (Su et al., 2020) and multi-hop question answer-
120
      ing (Feng et al., 2023). Furthermore, using a hierarchical index and summarization aligns with
121   alternative methods, such as generating a hierarchical index of text chunks through vector clustering
122   (Sarthi et al., 2024) or constructing a “tree of clarifications” to address multiple interpretations of
123   ambiguous questions (Kim et al., 2023).
124
125   2.2   G RAPH QA
126
127   A large body of research has emerged at the intersection of graph-based techniques and LLMs (Pan
128   et al., 2024). This exploration spans different aspects, from the design of generic graph models (Edge
129   et al., 2024) and multi-modal architectures (Yoon et al., 2023) to practical applications. Notable
130   applications include basic graph inference (Chai et al., 2023), node classification, graph classifica-
131
      tion/regression (Chen et al., 2024), and leveraging LLMs for knowledge graph-related tasks (Jiang
      et al., 2023). The domain of Graph Question Answering (Graph QA) is witnessing a notable shift
132
      with the introduction of emerging methodologies that apply Probabilistic Entity-centric Fine-tuning
133
      (PEFT) to graph-based Large Language Models (LLMs). This innovative approach is exemplified in
134   the creation of models such as GraphLLM (Chai et al., 2023) and GraphToken (Perozzi et al., 2024),
135   which are specifically designed to tackle basic graph reasoning tasks. Additionally, the development
136   of GNP (Graph Neural Prompting) (Tian et al., 2023) marks a pivotal enhancement for multi-option
137   QA on knowledge graphs.
138
139   2.3   B ENCHMARKS FOR G RAPH QA
140
141   Comprehensive benchmarks specifically tailored for graph modalities lack extensive research. In
142   contrast to existing benchmarks that focus on fundamental graph-based reasoning tasks such as node
143   degree, edge existence, and shortest paths (Wang et al., 2024a) (Fatemi et al., 2023), G-retriever’s
144
      benchmark (He et al., 2024) is applicable to complex and real-world graph applications, including
      commonsense reasoning, scene understanding, and knowledge graph reasoning. This fills a critical
145
      gap in evaluating the progress of models, but these models are not sufficient to solve various prob-
146
      lems in graph-based applications. Based on this, we propose comprehensive Multi- Entities Graph
147   QA benchmark (MEGraphQA) and provide a first-of-its-kind original model (SQA).

      4     SQA S YSTEM D ESIGN
198
199
      In our SQA system, we integrate several fundamental components, including graph search, table
      generation, and an executor, to provide users with a comprehensive platform for the exploration and
200
      analysis of graphs. It enables the transformation of entity properties to address specific queries and
201
      facilitates advanced semantic and relational operations, thereby allowing users to extract valuable
202   insights from the data effectively and efficiently.
203
204   The initial step involves conducting a graph search across the Wikipedia graph to identify relevant
205
      entities. Secondly, table generation begins with “guessing” a table schema based on the given
      query, followed by the extraction of information from the identified entities to populate the table.
206
      Finally, we implement an executor that processes the generated table to respond to the specified
207
      query. Next, we will elaborate on the details of each step.
208
209
      4.1   G RAPH S EARCH
210
211   Graph search in Wikipedia involves employing graph theory and algorithms to navigate the inter-
212   connected network of articles and links. We deploy GPT-4 as the Semantic Analysis Model to
213   identify entities and properties within user queries, as illustrated in Figure 1-a2. Subsequently, we
214   implement Named Entity Recognition (NER) to detect and classify named entities referenced in
215   the queries, alongside applying Relation Extraction (RE) techniques to extract relevant properties
      that serve as edges for each identified entity. For multi-hop queries, the Semantic Analysis process

216
      initially deconstructs the queries into sub-queries, allowing NER and RE to be applied sequentially
217   to each sub-query to identify named entities and extract properties until all sub-queries have been
218   processed.

220
      4.2   TABLE G ENERATION
221
222   The table generation consists of two steps: (1) generate the schema and (2) extract entity to fill the
223   table.
224
225   Schema Generation. We use GPT-4 to generate properties for the graph nodes which can be in-
226   terpreted as the table schema to construct the table, facilitating responses to queries. These gen-
227   erated properties may necessitate adjustments or refinements to align more closely with the in-
228   tent of the input query. For example, for the query “How many Nobel Prizes in Physics lau-
229
      reates have been awarded for discoveries in Particle Physics?”, the LLM produces a schema
230
       (name, YearAwarded, field) , there are two issues , first is that (field) is oversimplified, it should
231   be (field in Physics) . the second issue is the column YearAwarded are redundant. Therefore, we
232   indicatively prompt GPT-4 to review critically to reduce three issues as listed below, and the prompt
233   is in Appendix A.1.1.
234
        • Over-Simplification: Ensure the properties are not too simplistic for the complexity of the ques-
235
          tion it needs to support.
236
237
        • Missing Elements: Make sure no relevant component is overlooked. Every aspect of the ques-
          tion should be answerable with the properties.
238
239     • Redundancy: Avoid unnecessary properties (columns) or information that could complicate data
240
          analysis.
241
242   Entity Extraction. In our data processing workflow, we extract relevant information from the graph
243
      RAG data utilizing more cost-efficient LLMs like Mistral-7B. The extracted information is then
      used to populate a generated table, where each row corresponds to a unique entity from the graph
244
      RAG data, as illustrated in Figure 1-c3. Ultimately, we transform information in entities into struc-
245
      tured tables, facilitating subsequent access, manipulation, and interpretation for various analytical
246   purposes.
247
248
      4.3   E XECUTOR
249
250   Queries are received in natural language. Because of the ambiguity of natural language (Liu et al.,
251   2024b), the input query needs to be translated into a structured query language (e.g., SQL) that the
252   system can execute on the generated table. While LLM (like GPT-4) generates the table schema,
253   SQL is also generated. We leverage the SQLite3 library (SQLite Development Team, 2023) to
254   manage and interact with SQLite databases.
255
256   4.4   O PTIMIZATION
257
258   Three aspects of optimization are included in SQA system to enhance the overall performance:
259
260
      Data Filtering. One significant advantage of the process of extracting information from text and
      organizing it into a tabular format is the inherent ability of this process to filter out irrelevant data.
261
      During the extraction phase, LLMs are employed to identify and categorize the most pertinent pieces
262
      of information based on schema. This means that only data deemed relevant to the query is selected
263   for inclusion in the table. As a result, users are presented with a clean, concise table that contains
264   only the data necessary for their analysis or decision-making processes.
265
266   Model Selection. Model selection is straightforward yet highly effective for optimization (Liu et al.,
267   2024a). The SQA system comprises multiple tasks, necessitating the selection of the most suitable
268   model for different tasks. For basic tasks, more affordable and faster LLMs can suffice, while utiliza-
269   tion of the most advanced LLMs is essential in more complex tasks to ensure optimal performance.
      Specifically, SQA system employs powerful yet resource-intensive GPT-4 for tasks such as semantic


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      analysis or generation of table schemas and SQL queries. In contrast, for more basic information
271   extraction, we utilize open-source Mistral-7B, thereby achieving a balance between cost efficiency
272   and functional performance.
273
274   LLM Input/Output Control. SplitWise (Patel et al., 2023) shows that LLM inference time is
275   generally proportional to the size of input and output tokens. Since GPT models decide the cost
276   based on the input token, we try to minimize the input of large models. Meanwhile, we use the
277   instructive prompt to reduce the size of the outputs generated by LLM without changing the quality
278   of these outputs. The example of prompt is in Appendix A.1.2.

      5.1     D ESIGN O BJECTIVES AND S COPE

      Multi-entity questions extend the focus to consider the interactions and relationships between mul-
285   tiple entities simultaneously. We introduce MEGraphQA, covering three types of questions:
286
287       • Comparison questions are primarily concerned with examining differences or similarities be-
288         tween groups, entities, or time points, which involve comparing the performance of two different
289         groups under varying conditions or assessing changes in a particular variable over time.
290       • Statistical questions (Zhu et al., 2024) delve deeper into the data, seeking to understand the
291         distribution, trends, and patterns that emerge within a dataset. These questions often involve the
292         application of statistical methods to infer properties about the population from which the data
293         was sampled, such as mean, variance, and correlation coefficients.
294       • Relationship questions aim to uncover the connections and associations between different vari-
295         ables within the dataset, including exploring how one variable may influence another, identify-
296         ing causal relationships, or mapping out complex interactions among multiple variables.
297
      This categorization facilitates a more targeted approach to problem-solving, acknowledging that ap-
298
      plicable methodologies may vary considerably. Table 1 illustrates examples of multi-entity queries,
299   organized into three categories with eight further sub-types. A total of 4,780 questions have been
300   allocated across two datasets: the test set and the training set. The test set comprises approximately
301   30% of the total questions, amounting to around 1,374, while the training set contains the remaining
302   3,406 questions. Table 2 provides statistics of the MEGraphQA benchmark.

      5.2     AUTOMATED QA G ENERATION AND VALIDATION
305
306   We extract the introductory paragraph of textual content for each entity from Wikipedia, akin to an
307   abstract of the entity’s page, to derive relevant property values. The preprocessing of graph node
308   properties is conducted using GPT-4. GPT-4 is deployed to generate the essential properties of
309   key entities for each topic, and subsequently, property values are extracted from the respective web
310   pages of these entities. This process culminates in the formation of property tables. An illustrative
311   example of the topics and entities’ properties is provided in Appendix Table 4.
312   When questions or queries are posed, the SQA system efficiently navigates the graph by utilizing
313   both the connections (edges) and the nodes along with the associated property tables to retrieve
314   relevant information. The property tables, which contain attributes and values related to the entities
315   within the graph, serve as a comprehensive and structured data source that can be queried alongside
316   the graph structure. This dual approach facilitates thorough analysis, as it takes into account both
317   the relational context (the connections among entities) and the specific properties of the entities
318   involved. Moreover, such automated process benefits from low labor costs due to automation and
319   optimization within the graph database system, reducing the need for time-consuming and error-
320
      prone manual data processing and analysis.

416   tional and comparative query types by 11.6% and 17.1%, respectively, while achieving a remarkable
417   improvement of 46% for statistical query types.

454   Errors Analysis for SQA. We sample and analyze the output of SQA system, and the experimental
455   outcomes are in line with what was anticipated, which SQA achieves remarkable performance and
456   sets new SOTA. Just as we considered when designing our system model selection in Section 4.4,
457   it faces two challenges which are listed below. Also, we find a new challenge with synonyms,
458   revealing the potential for further improvement.
459
         • Relation semantic parsing. The semantic parsing model of SQA effectively recognizes entities
460
           but has limited capability in relationship identification. This limitation can result in difficulties
461
           during graph retrieval, where no entities are retrieved, negatively impacting the performance of
462        approaches with RAG including our SQA. For instance, in the query “How many US Presidents
463        have served more than one term in office?” the model incorrectly identifies the relationship as
464        “instance of” rather than the correct “position held”, leading to erroneous results.
465
466      • Insufficient information extraction. We also identified errors in SQA’s information extraction
467
           during the table-filling phase. An analysis of over 2,000 table-filling instances reveals that these
           errors primarily occur as omissions (albeit with a low probability of approximately 0.1%). A
468
           new challenge is the appearance of multi-word synonyms within the same column, like “US”
469
           and “America”, which negatively affects the accuracy of SQL execution such as “SELECT”.

      6.3        F UTURE W ORK
474
475   The challenges outlined in error analysis of Section 6.2 highlight the necessity for improved se-
476   mantic understanding of queries and the utilization of Large Language Models (LLMs) to enhance
477
      the accuracy of data extraction. To address multi-word synonyms, one potential solution involves
      the incorporation of semantic operators (Patel et al., 2024), which would empower users to execute
478
      advanced operations and queries on tabular data. The integration of these operators represents a
479
      substantial progression in user interaction with and querying of tabular datasets. Specifically, these
480   operators will be designed to grasp the intent and context of user queries, surpassing basic keyword
481   matching to capture the subtleties of natural language. By implementing semantic operators, users
482   will be able to conduct sophisticated operations, such as filtering by specific criteria, aggregating
483   data according to designated attributes, and performing complex comparative analyses, all through
484   user-friendly and intuitive queries. Addressing these challenges will be the primary focus of our
485   future efforts to refine our methodology and enhance the system’s ability to effectively process
      multi-entity queries.

      7   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In conclusion, our research presents a novel approach, Structured Question Answering (SQA), to
489   address the complexities involved in multi-entity question answering (QA) from Wikipedia. Exist-
490   ing methods, particularly those employing retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) alongside large
491   language models (LLMs), often fall short in effectively aggregating and reasoning over informa-
492   tion scattered across multiple Wikipedia pages. By leveraging the inherent structure of Wikipedia’s
493   wiki-graph for multi-entity retrieval and introducing a method to organize extracted entities into
494   a relational table format, the SQA approach significantly enhances the performance for answering
495   multi-entity questions. The exhaustive experiments conducted as part of this study underscore the
496   superior performance of SQA in overcoming the limitations of traditional LLM-based solutions.
497
      This research not only presents a more effective methodology for multi-entity QA but also sets the
      stage for future explorations into improving the accuracy and efficiency of information mining from
498
      large, unstructured knowledge bases.
