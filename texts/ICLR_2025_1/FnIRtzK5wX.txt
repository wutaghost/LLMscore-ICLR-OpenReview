009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
                Given a deep neural network (DNN) that has surpassed human beings in a task,
012             disentangling the explicit knowledge encoded by the DNN to obtain some new
013             insights into the task is a new promising-yet-challenging regime in explainable
014             AI. In this paper, we aim to disentangle the “QiGan1 ” encoded by the AI model
015             for the Go game, which has beat top human players. Specifically, we disentangle
016             primitive shape patterns of stones memorized by the value network, and these
017             shape patterns represent the “QiGan1 ” used to conduct a fast situation assessment
018             of the current board state. The universal-matching property of interactions ensures
019             that human players can learn accurate and verifiable shape patterns, rather than
020
                specious intuitive analysis. In experiments, our method explains lots of novel shape
                patterns beyond traditional shape patterns in human knowledge.

024   1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The explanation for AI models has gained increasing attention in recent years. However, in this paper,
027   we consider a new problem, i.e., if an AI model has achieved superior performance to human beings
028   in a task, how can we clearly disentangle exact inference patterns used by the AI model to help people
029   better understand new hidden rules for the task? Because AI models for the Go game have been
030   widely regarded to have surpassed human players (Granter et al., 2017; Fang et al., 2018; Intelligence,
031
      2016), in this paper, let us consider the Go game as a case study, and disentangle shape patterns2 used
      by AI models3 to play the Go game.
032
033   In fact, the disentanglement of shape patterns is of special values for the Go game. Because the Go
034   game is considered as the most difficult game with the vastest searching space of 10171 states, which
035   is far beyond the searching ability of human players or computers (Lee, 2004; Van Der Werf, 2004),
036   both people and neural network have to use “QiGan1 ” as a fast intuitive situation assessment. In
037
      comparison, other games (e.g.chess and poker) primarily rely on search capacities. In the AI model
      for the Go game, the value network encodes the “QiGan1 ,” and the policy network and the Monte
038
      Carlo Tree Search correspond to searching capabilities (Gelly & Silver, 2011; Buesing et al., 2020).
039
      “QiGan1 ” is also one of the core skills that human players need to learn.
040
041   Therefore, we aim to discover explicit new shape patterns2 of stones (i.e., QiGan1 ) used by the value
042   network to estimate the advantage score without a sophisticated search. The discovered patterns may
043
      probably be beyond current human understanding of the Go game. As the mathematical guarantee of
      the pattern discovery, Li & Zhang (2023) have discovered and Ren et al. (2024a) have proven that the
044
      output score of a DNN can usually be decomposed into effects of interactions encoded by a DNN. For
045
      example, given a board state x with n stones, N = {1, 2, ..., n}, the value network in Figure 1 outputs
046   the advantage score v(x) of white stones. Each interaction between stones in S ⊆ N represents
047   a shape pattern corresponding to an AND relationship between stones in S , which is equivalently
048   encoded by the value network. When all stones in S are present, the interaction S is activated and
049   contributes an effect I(S) to v(x). Removing any stone in S will remove the effect I(S) from v(x).
050

069   However, the theory of the previous interaction is no longer effective for the explanation of the Go
070   game due to the superior complexity of the Go game from the following three perspectives.
071
072
      • First, we prove that the previous interaction cannot well explain the OR relationship between stones
      encoded by the DNN. The presence of any stones in S activates an OR relationship and contributes
073
      an effect Ior (S). We prove that the OR pattern would be explained as 2|S| different AND interactions.
074
075   Therefore, we extend AND interactions to explain OR relationships between stones encoded by the
076   value network. More crucially, (1) Mathematical guarantee. We first prove the universal matching
077   property of AND-OR interactions to ensure that OR interactions faithfully explain the value network.
078
      I.e., given a board state x, the advantage score can be accurately matched by a small number of AND
      interactions and OR interactions, no matter how we randomly remove stones from the board (see
079
      Theorem 2.4, Figure 6). (2) Experimental verification. We verify that shape patterns extracted from
080
      one board state can also explain the network outputs on other board states.
081
082   • Second, we find that the advantage score estimated by the value network is usually shifted, when
083   white stones on the board are far less or far more than black stones on the board. This also hurts the
084
      faithfulness of explaining shifted advantage scores. Therefore, we develop a method to alleviate the
      shifting problem. The faithfulness of the explanation is experimentally verified in Section 2.6, so as
085
      to improve the faithfulness/fidelity of the explanation.
086
087   • Furthermore, we notice that some shape patterns for the Go game are usually quite complex, i.e.,
088   containing a large number of stones. Too complex shape patterns are usually considered as the unusual
089   shape patterns memorized by the value network, instead of a common shape pattern shared by different
090
      board states. This boosts the difficulty of understanding the Go game. Thus, we further identify some
      common combinations of stones that are shared by different interactions/shapes, namely coalitions.
091
      For example, in Figure 1, the interactions S1 = {1, 3, 4, 5, 8}, S2 = {1, 3, 8}, S3 = {1, 3, 8, 9} all
092
      contain the coalition T = {1, 3, 8}. We apply the attribution method (Xinhao Zheng, 2023) to estimate
093   the attribution of each coalition to help people understand the DNN’s logic.
094
095   We collaborate with professional human Go players to compare interactions/coalitions encoded
096
      by the value network with the human player’s QiGan1 of the Go game, so as to discover shape
      patterns beyond human understanding. We find that some discovered shape patterns well fit human
097
      understanding, but other discovered patterns conflict with traditional tactics of the Go game, which
098
      provide human players with new QiGan1 .
099
100   In sum, in this paper, we propose to solve distinctive challenges in explaining the Go game. We
101   extend the AND interaction to the OR interaction, penalize unreliable high-order interactions, and
102
      compute attributions of common coalitions. Expert human players claim that they have learned new
      QiGan1 (novel shape patterns beyond current human knowledge) encoded by the value network.
103
104   Value beyond the Go game. Discovering inference patterns encoded by a DNN is a generic AI
105   problem. We have also used similar techniques (1) to explain the Gobang game4 and (2) to explain
106   incorrect patterns used by a DNN for object detection4 . Please see Appendix M.3 and M.4 for details.

      In addition, the sparsity property of interactions has been observed by Li & Zhang (2023) and proven
149
      by Ren et al. (2024a), i.e., most DNNs only encode a small number of interactions6 between input

184   We aim to extract interaction primitives from the value network, and use them as shape patterns that
185   determine the winning probability. It is widely believed that the Go game applies much more complex
186   logic than other applications (Shin et al., 2021). Thus, the network for the Go game is supposed to
187   encode both AND relationships and OR relationships between stones.
188   However, we find that Theorem 2.2 shows that exclusively using AND interactions to explain an OR
189   relationship will significantly complicate the explanation, i.e., the OR relationship can be represented
190   as an exponential number of specific AND operations9 .

      How to understand OR interactions. Just like the AND interaction, an OR interaction S is measured
217   to reflect the strength of an OR relationship between stones in the set S encoded by the model vor . If
218   any stone in S appears on the board, then the OR interaction S makes an effect Ior (S) on the score
219   vor (x). Only when all stones in S are removed from the board, the effect Ior (S) is removed.

      2.3    H OW TO OBTAIN SIMPLE AND TRANSFERABLE SHAPE PATTERNS8
291
292   Can shape patterns extracted from a board be transferred to other boards? The transferability
293   of interaction primitives is crucial for the explanation of the Go game.
294
      First, Zhou et al. (2024) have found that high-order interactions are more likely to represent non-
295
      generalizable abnormal interactions, while low-order interactions are usually more generalizable,
296
      i.e., low-order interactions usually represent common patterns that frequently appear in different
297   games. For example, as Figure 3 (b) shows, 3-order interaction primitives S1 , S2 , S3 extracted from

451
452   An core issue is distinguishing whether extracted shape patterns that conflict with human understand-
453   ings represent novel shape patterns or incorrect explanations. Thus, we conduct three examinations
454
      to check if the interactions act as primitive inference patterns encoded by the value network.
455   First, let us examine the sparsity7 of interactions, i.e., checking whether the revised method
456   can still provide a few representative shape patterns, instead of many noisy patterns. We follow
457   experimental settings in Section 2.2 to generate 50 game states, and visualize the strength of all AND
458   interactions and all OR interactions of all these 50 game states in a descending order. Figure 6 (b)
459   shows that only a few interactions have salient effects, and more than 90% interactions have small
460
      effects, which verifies the sparsity7 of interactions extracted by the revised method.

462   AND-OR interactions extracted by our revised method still satisfy the universal matching property

672   B     R ELATED WORK
673
674   Many methods have been proposed to visualize the feature/patterns encoded by the DNN (Simonyan
675   et al., 2014; Dosovitskiy & Brox, 2016; Yosinski et al., 2015; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014), or to estimate the
676   attribution/attention on each input variable (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Selvaraju et al., 2017; Zhou et al.,
677   2016; Zintgraf et al., 2017). However, the complexity of the Go game presents a new requirement, i.e.,
678   accurately extracting exact shape patterns encoded by the DNN, instead of providing a vague heatmap
679
      of attention. Clarifying exact inference patterns is of significant values in knowledge discovery from
      DNNs towards various tasks. For example, our method can also be used to discover novel patterns
680
      for medical diagnosis.
681
682   However, explaining exact shape patterns encoded by a DNN proposes higher requirements for
683   the faithfulness/fidelity of the explanation. The faithfulness is even supposed to be theoretically
684   guaranteed and experimentally verified, beyond a specious understanding. To this end, (1) Ren
685
      et al. (2023) and Li & Zhang (2023) have found that a well-trained DNN usually encodes a small
      number of interactions, and the output score of the DNN on a certain input sample can always be
686
      well mimicked by numerical effects of a few salient interactions, no matter how the input sample
687
      is randomly masked6 . (2) Li & Zhang (2023) have further found the considerable transferability of
688   interactions over different samples and over different DNNs. (3) Interaction primitives (the AND
689   interaction) can explain the elementary mechanism of previous explanation metrics, e.g., the Shapley
690   value (Shapley, 2016), the Shapley interaction index (Grabisch & Roubens, 1999), and the Shapley
691   Taylor interaction index (Sundararajan et al., 2020).
692
      Despite of above findings, explaining the DNN for the Go game still proposes new challenges. To this
693
      end, we extend the AND interaction to the OR interaction, alleviate high-order interactions caused
694   by the value shift of the advantage score, and compute attributions of common coalitions shared by
695   different interactions, thereby obtaining concise and accurate explanation for shape patterns in the
696   value network.

699   C     P ROPERTIES FOR THE H ARSANYI DIVIDEND
700
701   In this paper, we follow Ren et al. (2023) to use the Harsanyi dividend (or Harsanyi interaction) to
      measure the numerical effect I(S) of the interaction primitive S. Ren et al. (2023) have proved that


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      the Harsanyi dividend satisfied the following properties, including the efficiency, linearity, dummy,
703   symmetry, anonymity, recursive, interaction distribution properties.
704
705   (1) Efficiency property: The inference score of a well-trained model v(x) can be disentangled
                                                                                          P         into
706
      the numerical effects of different interaction primitives I(S), S ⊆ N , i.e., v(x) = S⊆N I(S).
707   (2) Linearity property: If the inference score of the model w is computed as the sum of the inference
708   score of the model u and the inference score of the model v, i.e., ∀S ⊆ N, w(xS ) = u(xS ) + v(xS ),
709   then the interactive effect of S on the model w can be computed as the sum of the interaction effect
710   of S on the model u and that on the model v, i.e., ∀S ⊆ N, Iw (S) = Iu (S) + Iv (S).
711   (3) Dummy property: If the input variable i is a dummy variable, i.e., ∀S ⊆ N \ {i}, v(xS∪{i} ) =
712   v(xS ) + v(x{i} ), then the input variable i has no interaction with other input variables, i.e., ∀∅ ̸=
713   S ⊆ N \ {i}, I(S ∪ {i}) = 0.
714
715   (4) Symmetry property: If the input variable i ∈ N and the input variable j ∈ N cooperate with
716
      other input variables in S ⊆ N \ {i, j} in the same way, i.e., ∀S ⊆ N \ {i, j}, v(xS∪{i} ) =
717
      v(xS∪{j} ), then the input variable i and the input variable j have the same interactive effect, i.e.,
718
      ∀S ⊆ N \ {i, j}, I(S ∪ {i}) = I(S ∪ {j}).
719   (5) Anonymity property: If a random permutation π is added to N , then ∀S ⊆ N, Iv (S) = Iπv (πS)
720   is always guaranteed, where the new set of input variables πS is defined as πS = {π(i), i ∈ S}, the
721   new model πv is defined as (πv)(xπS ) = v(xS ). This suggests that permutation does not change
722   the interactive effects.
723   (6) Recursive property: The interactive effects can be calculated in a recursive manner. For ∀i ∈
724   N, S ⊆ N \{i}, the interactive effect of S ∪ {i} can be computed as the difference between the
725   interactive effect of S with the presence of the variable i and the interactive effect of S with the absence
726   of the variable i. I.e., ∀i ∈ N, S ⊆ NP    \{i}, I(S ∪ {i}) = I(S|i is consistently present) − I(S),
727   where I(S|i is consistently present) = L⊆S (−1)|S|−|L| v(xL∪{i} ).
728
      (7) Interaction distribution property: This property describes how an interaction function (Sundarara-
729
      jan et al., 2020) distributes interactions. An interaction function vT parameterized by a context T is
730
      defined as follows. ∀S ⊆ N , if T ⊆ S, then vT (xS ) = c; if not, vT (xS ) = 0. Then, the interactive
731   effects for an interaction function vT can be computed as, I(T ) = c, and ∀S ̸= T, I(S) = 0.

740
      samples, and when the input sample is less masked/occluded, the AI model will yield a higher

741
      samples does not significantly degrade.

1099   input variables in S ⊆ N \ {i, j} in the same way, i.e. ∀S ⊆ N \ {i, j}, v(S ∪ {i}) = v(S ∪ {j}),
1100   then the coalition formed by S ∪ {i} and the coalition formed by S ∪ {j} have the same attribution,
1101   i.e., ∀S ⊆ N \ {i, j}, φ(S ∪ {i}) = φ(S ∪ {j}).
1102
       • Additivity property: If the output score of the model v can be represented as the sum of the output
1103
       score of the model v1 and the output score of the model v2 , i.e. ∀S ⊆ N, v(S) = v1 (S) + v2 (S),
1104   then the attribution of any coalition S on the model v can also be represented as the sum of the
1105   attribution of S on the model v1 and that on the model v2 , i.e. ∀S ⊆ N, φv (S) = φv1 (S) + φv2 (S).
1106
1107

       M.4    E XTENSION OF OUR METHOD TO DEBUG REPRESENTATION FLAWS HIDDEN IN A DNN
1351
1352   The second application of our method is to debug representation flaws hidden in a DNN. Besides
1353   letting people learn new interactions/concepts encoded by the DNNs, another typical utility of
1354   explaining interactions in a DNN is to debug representation flaws hidden in a DNN.
1355
       Figure 12 shows the interactions extracted from a DNN for pedestrian detection. Given an input
1356   image, we manually label image regions with salient attributions as input variables, and compute
1357   interactions between image regions. The visualization of the interactions enables people to check
1358   the correctness of interactions encoded by the DNN manually. Let us consider the explanation on
1359   the first input image as an example. We can analyze the representation quality of the DNN from the
1360   following three perspectives. (1) The interactions Iand (S = {C, I}), Iand (S = {D, I}), Iand (S = {F, I})
1361   and Iand (S = {A, I}) between pedestrian patches and background patches may represent unreliable
1362   inference patterns. (2) High-order interactions, e.g., Ior (S = {A, C, D, E, F, G, H, I}) and Ior (S =
1363   {A, C, D, F, H, I, J}), usually represent too complex inference patterns. Complex interactions usually
1364
       have lower generalization power than simple interactions. (3) There are 29 positive interactions
       and 31 negative interactions extracted from an input image. The offsetting of positive and negative
1365
       interactions is another problem. Adversarially robust neural networks usually encode more positive
1366
       interactions and fewer negative interactions than normal neural networks.
1367
1368   In addition, the problematic interactions (e.g., interactions on background patches) reflect repre-
1369   sentation flaws of a DNN, because it is found by Li & Zhang (2023) that salient interactions are
1370
       usually transferable across different samples. In other words, problematic interactions may affect the
       inference of a large number of samples.
