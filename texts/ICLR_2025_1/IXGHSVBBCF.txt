009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive potential in helping with
012            numerous medical challenges. Deploying LLMs in high-stakes applications such
013            as medicine, however, brings in many concerns. One major area of concern relates
014            to biased behaviors of LLMs in medical applications, leading to unfair treatment
015            of individuals. To pave the way for the responsible and impactful deployment of
016            Med LLMs, rigorous evaluation is a key prerequisite. Due to the huge complexity
017            and variability of different medical scenarios, existing work in this domain has pri-
018
               marily relied on using manually crafted datasets for bias evaluation. In this study,
               we present a new method to scale up such bias evaluations by automatically gen-
019
               erating test cases based on rigorous medical evidence. We specifically target the
020
               challenges of domain-specificity of bias characterization, hallucinating while gen-
021            erating the test cases, and various dependencies between the health outcomes and
022            sensitive attributes. To that end, we offer new methods to address these challenges
023            integrated with our generative pipeline. Specifically, we use medical knowledge
024            graphs and medical ontologies; and customize general LLM evaluation frame-
025            works in our method. Through a series of extensive experiments, we show that the
026            test cases generated by our proposed method are reliable and can effectively reveal
027            bias patterns in LLMs. Additionally, we publish a large bias evaluation dataset,
028            which provides a comprehensive platform for testing and improving the fairness
029
               of medical LLMs. A live demo of our application for vignette generation is avail-
               able at https://vignette.streamlit.app. Our code is also available at
030
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/vignette_llm-2853.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Large language models (LLMs) have shown an impressive potential to assist in various forms of real-
036   world challenges, including finding long-sought solutions to some key medical challenges such as
037   provider burnout, processing of claims, and obtaining preauthorizations. The LLMs used in medical
038   applications (hereafter, Med LLMs) have been leveraged for clinical note summarization (Van Veen
039   et al., 2024), patient consultation (Ren et al., 2024), and generating differential diagnosis (Hirosawa
040   et al., 2023), among others.
041
      Despite the promising potential, using LLMs in sensitive assistive decision-making scenarios, such
042   as clinical decision support, brings about significant concerns. One of the most critical concerns
043   relates to the impact of LLM-based tools on bias and fairness. Specifically, deploying biased LLMs
044   in clinical settings can amplify existing healthcare inequities, for instance, by providing unfair care
045   recommendations or service denials based on patients’ demographics. A fairly large family of recent
046   studies has documented biased behaviors of Med LLMs across different (a) LLM types, (b) clinical
047   tasks, and (c) subpopulations (Poulain et al., 2024). Biased behavior in LLMs can arise from a
048   variety of sources, including the inherent biases in the training data, the limitations in capturing and
049   utilizing factual knowledge, and flawed training and inference strategies (Gallegos et al., 2024).
050   Prior to mitigating bias patterns in Med LLMs, it is critical to develop tools for comprehensive
051   evaluation of such patterns. Despite the growing number of methods for the evaluation of LLMs
052   (LLM Evals) (Liang et al., 2023), evaluating LLMs remains a challenging task due to reasons such
053   as open-endedness and stochasticity of the LLM outputs (Kiela et al., 2021). Evaluating Med LLMs
      generally involves creating benchmarking datasets with controlled clinical ‘vignettes’ (i.e., patient


                                                         1

054
      scenarios) that target sensitive cases within ‘red teaming’ frameworks (Singhal et al., 2022). De-
055   veloping such comprehensive benchmarks is, however, extremely resource-intensive and requires
056   manual design by clinical experts. Existing hand-crafted benchmarks for evaluating the fairness in
057   Med LLMs have a limited scope by design and cannot extend to various complex real-world clinical
058   scenarios (Singhal et al., 2023).
059
      To generate the evaluation scenarios at scale, one could follow the common practice of achieving
060
      scalability by using the LLMs (of the same or different type) as generators. In fact, this is the
061   practice that almost all prior studies have taken to generate clinical scenarios by prompting strong
062   LLMs and then vetting the generations by clinical experts before using those scenarios for Med
063   LLM evaluation. Removing the human experts (i.e., the main bottleneck in scalability) from this
064   generation loop is quite tricky. Medical scenarios are greatly contextualized and domain-specific,
065   with numerous exceptions and subtleties. For instance, treating individuals differently based on
066   race may be justified in the case of skin cancer (Gloster Jr & Neal, 2006) but not kidney disease
067   (Delgado et al., 2021). Furthermore, concerns about LLM hallucinations make relying solely on
068   LLMs to generate evaluation scenarios more challenging.
069   In this study, we present a greatly controlled process to generate evaluation scenarios by extract-
070   ing rigorous biomedical evidence guidelines documenting potential bias pitfalls in each domain.
071   We incorporate customized methods to contextually minimize hallucinations and actively check for
072   domain-specific medical intricacies related to the justified (vs. unjustified) associations between the
073   protected attributes and the targeted health outcomes. Specifically, the contributions of this study
074   are:
075
             • We present a new method for flexible vignette generation in the form of user-specified
076            scenarios by directly extracting documented bias pitfalls in biomedical knowledge bases
077            and then filtering unsupported scenarios.
078
             • We publish a new dataset generated by our method for assessing the fairness in Med LLMs
079            in the domain of obesity prevention and treatment.
080
             • Through a series of comprehensive experiments, using both analytical and human rating
081
               evaluations, we show our method can significantly outperform naive LLM-based methods
082            in generating reliable vignettes.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
086
087
      The advent of LLMs has made significant transformations across various fields, including healthcare
      and medicine. General-purpose LLMs, such as Claude (Bai et al., 2022) and Llama (Dubey
088
      et al., 2024), are trained on medical text (e.g., medical textbooks and blogs) and have shown an
089
      impressive potential to be used for various clinical decision support purposes, such as differential
090   diagnosis (McDuff et al., 2023) and treatment planning Liu et al. (2024). Beyond the general-
091   purpose models, there have also been specialized LLMs tailored for medical applications, such as
092   Google’s Med-Gemini (Saab et al., 2024), Palmyra-Med (Writer Engineering team, 2023), and
093   Meditron (Chen et al., 2023).
094
      Evaluating LLM behaviors is challenging and an active area of research. LLM outputs can be
095
      evaluated from various perspectives, making this a complex process to ensure their effectiveness and
096   reliability in various applications. LLM evaluation typically involves key metrics and techniques
097   to assess different aspects of LLM performance. Traditional evaluation metrics, such as BLEU
098   (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004), rely on n-gram overlap between model outputs and
099   reference texts to gauge the quality of outputs. However, these metrics have been criticized for their
100   weak correlation with human judgments (Sulem et al., 2018), as surface-level matching does not
101   reliably capture the quality of the generated text.
102   With the advancement of deep learning, model-based metrics like BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020)
103   and BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021) have been introduced and are increasingly used to evaluate
104   various dimensions of text quality. Although these metrics offer improvements over traditional ones,
105   they still do not deliver fully satisfactory performance and have a limited application scope (Kocmi
106   et al., 2021). Recent methods, such as G-EVAL (Liu et al., 2023) and GPTScore (Fu et al., 2024),
107   increasingly leverage LLMs themselves for evaluation. This marks a significant advancement in the
      field due to the unprecedented capabilities of newly developed LLMs.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Specifically, evaluating LLMs regarding bias patterns is crucial for their responsible deployment
109   in high-stakes applications like medicine. Existing studies primarily rely on ‘red-teaming’ designs
110   for fairness evaluation of Med LLMs . From a clinical point of view, the goal of red-teaming is to
111   ensure that clinical LLMs treat all patient groups equitably, providing unbiased support to healthcare
112   providers.
113
      Existing studies utilized red-teaming for fairness evaluation in Med LLMs including specialized
114
      datasets like Q-Pain (Logé et al., 2021) and comparative studies against human experts (Ito et al.,
115   2023; Omiye et al., 2023). To name a few examples, Pfohl et al. (2024) proposed a new framework
116   and dataset to assess LLMs’ bias and fairness against human ratings and evaluated Med-PaLM
117   on the proposed dataset. Zack et al. (2024) evaluated whether GPT-4 encodes racial and gender
118   biases and explored how these biases might affect medical education, diagnosis, treatment planning,
119   and patient assessment. Moreover, Chang et al. (2024) convened 80 multi-disciplinary experts to
120   evaluate the performance of popular LLMs across multiple real-world medical scenarios. Experts
121   were given a framework comprising four categories (i.e. safety, privacy, hallucinations, and bias) to
122   analyze for inappropriate LLMs-generated responses. Prior empirical findings highlight the potential
123
      for biased LLMs to perpetuate stereotypes and lead to inaccurate clinical reasoning (Poulain et al.,
      2024).
124
125   Previous work in this area has involved humans who manually generated prompts and clinical sce-
126   narios or evaluated the responses. These types of approaches, however, are costly, narrowly focused,
127   and not scalable.

129   3   P RELIMINARIES
130
131   We aim to address three key challenges in the automated generation of clinical vignette generation
132   for fairness evaluation, including domain-specificity of the fairness evaluation, outcome indepen-
133   dence, and hallucination.
134
135   Domain-specificity of the Fairness Evaluation: Medicine is a highly specialized field, and sim-
136   ilarly, fairness evaluation in clinical settings is highly domain-specific. Each domain generally
137   has certain ‘fairness pitfalls’ that are unique to that particular medical domain and are character-
138   ized through rigorous evidence-based epidemiological or mechanistic (biological) studies (Paulus &
139
      Kent, 2020; Anderson & Visweswaran, 2024). For instance, while focusing on gender disparities in
      heart attack diagnosis may be more meaningful in certain branches of cardiology, racial differences
140
      in cancer screening may be more relevant in some branches of oncology. Therefore, fairness (i.e.,
141
      a fundamentally sociotechnical concept) must be characterized within each specific clinical context
142   through a rigorous evidence-based process.
143
144   Outcome Independence: The common procedure for using red teaming strategies to evaluate
145   Med LLMs involves iterating over different values of a target sensitive attribute (e.g., male and
146   female in the case of gender). More formally, red-teaming approaches check for counterfactual
147   (parity-based) fairness defined as:
148
                                    P (ŶA←α |X = x) = P (ŶA←α′ |X = x),                                (1)
149
                                                                           ′
150   where A shows a sensitive attribute with two values of α and α , X shows the remaining input
151   variables and Ŷ shows the outcome. This formulation generally assumes that the sensitive attributes
152   are independent of the outcome (P (Y |A) = P (Y )).
153
      Such an approach cannot be naively applied to the generated clinical vignettes, as in many cases, the
154   sensitive attributes and health outcomes are correlated. Examples of such scenarios include race and
155   skin cancer, gender and pregnancy complications, place of birth, and exposure to tropical diseases.
156
157   Hallucination: Besides the two specific challenges listed so far, LLM-based generative methods
158   are also known to face the hallucination problem. Hallucination in LLMs can be categorized into
159   two main forms: lack of factuality or lack of faithfulness (Ji et al., 2023). Lack of factuality refers
160   to producing statements that are untrue or unsupported by real-world knowledge, while lack of
161   faithfulness means the generated content does not accurately represent the original context or input,
      deviating from the given prompts or context.

      Factuality hallucinations, in turn, have three main forms. First, knowledge recall failure (Singhal
176
      et al., 2023), where LLMs struggle to retrieve specific or less commonly known information (e.g.,
177
      symptoms of a rare disease) from their training data, potentially leading to inaccuracies in the content
178   of generated vignettes. Second, domain knowledge deficiency (Feng et al., 2023), where the LLMs
179   trained on diverse datasets (e.g., internet data) may lack deep, specialized knowledge in specific
180   medical fields. Third, LLMs can suffer from outdated factual knowledge (Mousavi et al., 2024) if
181   they are only trained on static without continuously updating their knowledge base with the latest
182   research or clinical findings.
183
      In the case of our study, faithfulness hallucinations refer to generating responses that deviate from
184   the input prompts and to generating irrelevant, fabricated, or contradictory details. Furthermore,
185   faithfulness refers to the degree to which the generated vignettes accurately reflect the content and
186   information presented in the retrieved evidence. This adherence ensures that the generated outputs
187   are not only relevant but also reliable in terms of the information they convey, maintaining the
188   integrity of the original data while creatively transforming it into a vignette-style output.

      We propose a vignette generation method to enable scalable and evidence-based evaluation of bias
193   patterns in Med LLMs while addressing the three discussed challenges. At a high level, in our
194   method, an information extractor (§4.1) retrieves relevant evidence from biomedical knowledge
195   bases related to user-determined scenarios. The extracted information aims to help contextualize
196   fairness and identify the bias pitfalls in each domain. Then, a vignette generator LLM uses the ex-
197   tracted evidence to generate “base” vignettes (§4.2). Next, we exclude the base vignettes with signs
198   of hallucination (§4.3). Then, outcome independence between the augmented sensitive attributes
199   and the health outcomes is confirmed (§4.4). After this step, to create the red-teaming vignettes,
200   targeted sensitive attributes are then augmented with each base vignette (§4.5).
201   More formally, given a user-specified set of inputs I, a generative LLM M, and a knowledge base
202   K, we propose a pipeline P that generates a set of distinct red-teaming vignettes V :
203
                                                P(I, K, M) → V,
204
205   where, the input I includes targeted health outcome, o; the desired number of vignettes, n; and the
206   sensitive attributes of interest S. Hence, the inputs are I = {o, n, S}. Figure 1 shows an overview
207   of the proposed method.

      in our prompt design, we describe a series of steps that the LLM needs to take in compiling the
252

257   or ‘no’ to all the questions. The LLM is also asked to explicitly identify and list the reference for

295

296

297
298   We use the UMLS ontology to identify the ancestors of a health outcome as the indicators of the sen-
299   sitive attributes that interact with the health outcome. More specifically, in our proposed approach,
300   we map the generated vignettes to the UMLS concepts using MetaMap (Aronson, 2001), which is
301
      a tool for recognizing UMLS concepts in the text. Next, we find the ancestors for every concept

302
      included. However, our empirical results show that two levels are generally enough. From this set
303
      of concepts’ ancestors, we especially extract a subset (SAnc ) that belongs to the specified sensitive

374   5.1   Q1: H OW MUCH THE GENERATED VIGNETTES ARE FAITHFUL TO THE PROVIDED
375         MEDICAL EVIDENCE ?
376
377   To comprehensively evaluate the faithfulness of the vignettes to the provided context, we use four
      different methods (BARTScore, Semantic Entropy, G-Eval, and RefChecker) to measure how

                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      closely the vignettes mirror the context and content of the provided references without introducing
379   irrelevant or incorrect details.
380
381   BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021) evaluates the likelihood of the generated text being faithful to the
382
      reference context based on the BART model (Lewis et al., 2019). By computing the log-likelihood
      of the candidate text given the reference, BARTScore assesses whether the generated vignette is
383
      plausible within the context of the reference text, focusing on how well the output aligns with the
384
      source information in terms of meaning and structure.
385
386   Semantic Entropy (SE) (Farquhar et al., 2024) is a metric used to measure the diversity and unpre-
387   dictability of the meanings within the generated text. High SE suggests that the vignette introduces
388
      varied or less predictable meanings, which can lead to deviations from the original context. Con-
      versely, lower SE is a sign that the content is more predictable and closely aligned with the provided
389
      reference, contributing to higher faithfulness.
390
391   We use the G-EVAL framework (Liu et al., 2023) to calculate a faithfulness score (characterized
392   within the prompt) and using the chain-of-though reasoning using a strong LLM (GPT-4).
393   We also use the RefChecker framework (Hu et al., 2024) to detect the faithfulness hallucinations.
394   Within this framework, a strong LLM first transforms each generated vignette into a statement,

431   vocabulary diversity. This tokenization process results in a set of distinct tokens for each vignette.

      The advent of LLMs has made significant transformations across various fields, including healthcare
534   and medicine. General-purpose LLMs, such as Claude (Bai et al., 2022) and Llama (Dubey
535   et al., 2024), are trained on medical text (e.g., medical textbooks and blogs) and have shown an
536   impressive potential to be used for various clinical decision support purposes, such as differential
537   diagnosis (McDuff et al., 2023) and treatment planning Liu et al. (2024). Beyond the general-
538   purpose models, there have also been specialized LLMs tailored for medical applications, such as
539   Google’s Med-Gemini (Saab et al., 2024), Palmyra-Med (Writer Engineering team, 2023), and
      Meditron (Chen et al., 2023).

540
      Evaluating LLM behaviors is challenging and an active area of research. LLM outputs can be
541   evaluated from various perspectives, making this a complex process to ensure their effectiveness and
542   reliability in various applications. LLM evaluation typically involves key metrics and techniques
543   to assess different aspects of LLM performance. Traditional evaluation metrics, such as BLEU
544   (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004), rely on n-gram overlap between model outputs and
545   reference texts to gauge the quality of outputs. However, these metrics have been criticized for their
546   weak correlation with human judgments (Sulem et al., 2018), as surface-level matching does not
547   reliably capture the quality of the generated text.
548   With the advancement of deep learning, model-based metrics like BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020)
549   and BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021) have been introduced and are increasingly used to evaluate
550   various dimensions of text quality. Although these metrics offer improvements over traditional ones,
551   they still do not deliver fully satisfactory performance and have a limited application scope (Kocmi
552   et al., 2021). Recent methods, such as G-EVAL (Liu et al., 2023) and GPTScore (Fu et al., 2024),
553   increasingly leverage LLMs themselves for evaluation. This marks a significant advancement in the
554   field due to the unprecedented capabilities of newly developed LLMs.
555   Specifically, evaluating LLMs regarding bias patterns is crucial for their responsible deployment
556   in high-stakes applications like medicine. Existing studies primarily rely on ‘red-teaming’ designs
557   for fairness evaluation of Med LLMs . From a clinical point of view, the goal of red-teaming is to
558   ensure that clinical LLMs treat all patient groups equitably, providing unbiased support to healthcare
559   providers.
560   Existing studies utilized red-teaming for fairness evaluation in Med LLMs including specialized
561   datasets like Q-Pain (Logé et al., 2021) and comparative studies against human experts (Ito et al.,
562   2023; Omiye et al., 2023). To name a few examples, Pfohl et al. (2024) proposed a new framework
563   and dataset to assess LLMs’ bias and fairness against human ratings and evaluated Med-PaLM
564   on the proposed dataset. Zack et al. (2024) evaluated whether GPT-4 encodes racial and gender
565   biases and explored how these biases might affect medical education, diagnosis, treatment planning,
566   and patient assessment. Moreover, Chang et al. (2024) convened 80 multi-disciplinary experts to
567
      evaluate the performance of popular LLMs across multiple real-world medical scenarios. Experts
      were given a framework comprising four categories (i.e. safety, privacy, hallucinations, and bias) to
568
      analyze for inappropriate LLMs-generated responses. Prior empirical findings highlight the potential
569
      for biased LLMs to perpetuate stereotypes and lead to inaccurate clinical reasoning (Poulain et al.,
570   2024).
571
572   Previous work in this area has involved humans who manually generated prompts and clinical sce-
573
      narios or evaluated the responses. These types of approaches, however, are costly, narrowly focused,
      and not scalable.
574

576   7   D ISCUSSION
577
578
      This study presents a new method for generating tailored clinical vignettes based on the facts ex-
579
      tracted from external biomedical knowledge bases to evaluate bias patterns in Med LLMs. Our
580
      procedure enables scalable fairness assessments of Med LLMs, supporting access to dynamic (user-
581   defined) and contextually relevant benchmarks.
582
583   Extraction of the most relevant biomedical information ensures that the vignettes generated reflect
584
      current guidelines based on the latest medical evidence. Our experiments indicate that connecting
      to external knowledge bases can significantly enhance the diversity and reduce the hallucinations of
585
      the generated clinical vignettes, as demonstrated by the performance across various approaches and
586
      experiments.
587
588   Several limitations of our work should be noted in particular. First, reliance on existing biomedical
589   literature and knowledge bases, such as PubMed, may inadvertently introduce new biases present in
590
      these sources or reinforce existing biases in LLMs. Additionally, while our method facilitates large-
      scale benchmark generation, the complexity of certain medical scenarios might be underrepresented
591
      due to constraints in the current knowledge extraction methods.
592
593   We also note that we view our pipeline as an assistive tool in ‘diagnosing’ undesired biased and
      unfair patterns in Med LLMs. Having humans (especially medical stakeholders such as providers

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


594
      and patients) is critical to ensure an effective human-in-the-loop process. As a diagnosis tool, our
595   generated scenarios may wrongly indicate biased patterns (false positive) or miss some of those
596   (false negative). Ultimately, the final users can decide how to adjust the model in trading off these
597   two cases.
598
      While our method represents a crucial step toward the fairness evaluation of Med LLMs, continued
599
      efforts to refine the proposed methods are essential. By continuously improving new methodologies
600
      for this purpose, we can ensure that Med LLMs can benefit all patient demographics equitably,
601   ultimately leading to improved health outcomes, reducing disparities in healthcare, and increasing
602   trust in health AI clinical decision support tools.

          Background: In the following, a series of “vignette” pairs are shown to you. A vignette de-
          scribes a short clinical scenario, simulating when a medical provider (a doctor) should make
955
          a clinical decision. It is often used for testing in medical education. The same questions can
956
          be asked from an LLM. Besides answering such questions correctly, it is critical that LLMs
957       answer the questions in an unbiased manner.
958       Our study aims to generate a series of synthetic vignettes to evaluate the medical LLMs in
959       terms of their fair performance. We aim to generate “red-teaming” vignettes, meaning vi-
960       gnettes that target tricky and challenging scenarios that can reveal biased behaviors (similar
961       to an adversarial learning framework).
962       To evaluate the performance of our vignette generation method, we show you two generated
963       vignettes in each question. One is generated by our method, and the other by a different
964       method.
965
          Each of these vignettes has a placeholder for some sensitive attributes (like [female
          male]). When using a vignette for red teaming, it can be asked once for each of those
966
          sensitive attributes (e.g., once for male and once for female). A non-identical answer to the
967
          questions can show the LLM’s unfair behavior.
968       Our Request: We ask you to check these two scenarios and tell us which one you think is
969       more “suitable” for evaluating the fair behavior of medical LLMs. “Suitable” here means
