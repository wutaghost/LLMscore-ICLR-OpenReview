009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As a fundamental task for indoor scene understanding, 3D object detection has
012            been extensively studied, and the accuracy on indoor point cloud data has been
013            substantially improved. However, existing researches have been conducted on
014            limited datasets, where the training and testing sets share the same distribution. In
015            this paper, we consider the task of adapting indoor 3D object detectors from one
016            dataset to another, presenting a first comprehensive benchmark with commonly
017            used ScanNet and SUN RGB-D datasets, as well as our newly proposed large-scale
018
               SimRoom and SimHouse datasets by a 3D simulator with far greater number of
               objects and more precise annotations. Since indoor point cloud datasets are col-
019
               lected and constructed in different ways, the object detectors are likely to overfit
020
               to specific factors within each dataset, such as point cloud quality, room layout
021            configuration, style and object size. We conduct experiments across datasets on
022            different adaptation scenarios, analyzing the impact of different domain gaps on
023            3D object detectors. We observe that through our evaluated domain gap factors,
024            synthetic-to-real adaptation is the most difficult adaptation hurdle to overcome. We
025            also introduce several domain adaptation approaches to improve adaptation perfor-
026            mances, providing a first baseline for domain adaptive indoor 3D object detection,
027            hoping that future works may propose detectors with stronger generalization ability
028            across domains.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      As a fundamental task for 3D perception and indoor scene understanding, indoor 3D object detection
      has been extensively studied. Most 3D object detectors for indoor scenes are designed for point
034
      cloud data due to its adaptability, precision, and richness of information. Indoor 3D detectors such
035
      as voting-based VoteNet by Qi et al. (2019) and transformer-based Pointformer by Pan et al. (2021)
036   and V-DETR by Shen et al. (2023) have achieved remarkable success in identifying and localizing
037   objects in point clouds.
038
039
      Though achieving great progress, existing indoor 3D object detection researches are mainly conducted
      on ScanNet dataset by Dai et al. (2017) and SUN RGB-D dataset by Song et al. (2015). Detector
040
      are trained and evaluated within each dataset where the training and testing set share the same data
041
      distribution. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), point clouds in ScanNet dataset are constructed by thorough
042   scans of RGB-D videos, thus with higher quality but relatively fewer scenes. Point clouds in SUN
043   RGB-D dataset are converted through single RGB-D images, exhibiting large areas of point omission.
044   In practical applications, detectors trained on data from a specific domain may need to generalize to
045   deployment environments with distributional shift, i.e. domain adaptation problem which has not yet
046   been explored in indoor 3D object detection field.
047
      Domain adaptation for point cloud data has been studied on object classification task Qin et al. (2019);
048   Shen et al. (2022); Cardace et al. (2023), outdoor LiDAR detection task Wang et al. (2020); Liu et al.
049   (2024); Chang et al. (2024) and indoor semantic segmentation task Ding et al. (2022). However,
050   object detection on indoor point clouds has the following challenges: (1) Indoor point clouds can be
051   constructed through multiple ways, creating more aspects of domain gap factors with larger domain
052   gap to be solved. (2) Indoor 3D object detection requires to distinguish multiple object categories (15
053   categories in our proposed benchmarks) with higher object diversity, resulting in richer semantics
      to be learned and adapted. (3) Localizing objects in indoor 3D scenes faces the challenges of close

079   To investigate different factors of domain gaps that impact the performance of indoor 3D object
080   detectors, we propose large-scale simulated indoor scene datasets SimRoom and SimHouse as a
081
      complement of existing indoor 3D object detection datasets, and re-arrange the label space of different
      datasets to construct a benchmark for domain adaptation. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), our proposed
082
      SimRoom contains scenes of single rooms which is closer to ScanNet and SUN RGB-D layouts,
083
      while SimHouse scenes are combinations of multiple rooms. Both datasets are constructed by directly
084   sampling points from 3D meshes created by a 3D simulator, thus contain an order of magnitude
085   more objects than ScanNet and SUN RGB-D datasets, and more precise bounding box and category
086   annotations. Furthermore, our proposed datasets do not incur costs for manual construction and
087   labeling, offering better scalability.
088
      Shown in Figure 1(a), comparing the four datasets, ScanNet has relatively high quality point clouds,
089
      SUN RGB-D exhibit obvious point omission, SimRoom has distinct synthetic style with single-room
090   layout and SimHouse has multi-room combination. After re-arranging the label space to select 15
091   common categories, to combine the four datasets to form a series of domain adaptation benchmarks
092   including: (1) ScanNet→SUN RGB-D: high quality to low quality point clouds. (2) SUN RGB-
093   D→SUN RGB-D: low quality to high quality point clouds. (3) SimRoom→ScanNet: synthetic style
094   to real rooms. (4) SimRoom→SimHouse: single-room to multi-room configuration.
095
      Under such benchmarks, we conduct experiments on the commonly used detectors Qi et al. (2019);
096   Pan et al. (2021); Shen et al. (2023). As shown in Figure 1(b), the VoteNet detector performs
097   well within each dataset (blue bars, trained and evaluated within target dataset), but cross-dataset
098   evaluation shows a significant performance drop (yellow bars, trained by source and evaluated on
099   target dataset). Within each adaptation scenario, we control the domain gap factors and further
100   analyze the challenge of adaptation hurdles including point cloud quality, room layout configuration,
101   style and object size. We observe that each factor causes a significant drop in detection performance,
102   and among all these domain gap factors, synthetic-to-real gap is the most challenging one. Even with
103   far greater number of training instances, synthetic-to-real setting shows the most severe performance
104
      degradation. We also implement several commonly used domain adaptation approaches in other tasks
      to improve adaptation performances. These approaches serve as a first baseline for domain adaptive
105
      indoor 3D object detection, hoping that future works may propose detectors or frameworks with
106
      stronger generalization ability across domains.

120   2.1   I NDOOR POINT CLOUD DATASETS
121
      As a universal 3D structure representation, point cloud data has been widely used in 3D indoor scene
122
      understanding tasks. Various 3D indoor scene datasets Armeni et al. (2016); Song et al. (2015);
123
      Dai et al. (2017) have been proposed by multiple construction ways. Song et al. (2015) propose
124   SUN RGB-D dataset by converting single RGB-D images into 3D point clouds. Dai et al. (2017)
125   propose ScanNet dataset by collecting RGB-D videos through large amount of indoor rooms with rich
126   semantic annotations, and has been the most widely used dataset in indoor 3D object detection and
127   semantic segmentation task. Simulated datasets like Structured3D Zheng et al. (2020) and 3D-Front
128   Fu et al. (2021) are proposed to accomplish large-scale training with relatively low cost. However,
129   these datasets are created by human designers and thus still have high construction cost. Deitke et al.
130   (2022) offers an indoor structure generation framework with a fully simulated dataset which mainly
131   focus on embodied AI tasks such as navigation and rearrangement.
132   Though helping downstream tasks to achieve great success, these datasets and benchmarks still have
133   limitations in terms of data scale, data quality, and label space. Moreover, these benchmarks all
134   assume that the training set and testing set share the same distribution, while in these paper we focus
135   on adapting the indoor 3D detector across domains. We propose SimRoom and SimHouse datasets
136   by the 3D synthetic framework proposed by Deitke et al. (2022), and combining with ScanNet and
137   SUN RGB-D datasets to propose the first domain adaptive indoor 3D object detection benchmark.
138
139   2.2   I NDOOR 3D OBJECT DETECTION ON P OINT C LOUDS
140
      Indoor 3D object detection is a fundamental task in indoor scene understanding, serving as the
141
      upstream task for 3D visual grounding, question answering and navigation. 3D-SIS by Hou et al.
142   (2019) back projects the 2D feature vector onto the associated voxel in the 3D grid to achieve 3D
143   detection and segmentation. Qi et al. (2018) frustum the PointNet framework by Qi et al. (2017) for
144   3D object detection. VoteNet by Qi et al. (2019) adopt deep hough voting strategy to train the model
145   to group points for detection proposal generation. In recent years, transformer architectures are also
146   introduced by Shen et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023).
147
      Though achieving great success, these studies all focus on performances where the training and testing
148   set share the same data distribution. The domain adaptation ability of indoor 3D object detectors
149   has not yet been explored. In this paper, we propose the first domain adaptation benchmark, and
150   mainly conduct experiments on VoteNet by Qi et al. (2019) due to its popular use among indoor scene
151   understanding tasks and its flexible and lightweight architecture.
152
153   2.3   D OMAIN ADAPTATION FOR POINT CLOUDS

159   feature reconstruction and self-training methods to improve domain adaptation performance for
160   3D classifiers. Ding et al. (2022) studies domain adaptation for indoor semantic segmentation
161   by synthetic-to-real data augmentation and mixing. However, domain adaptation approaches for
      classification and segmentation cannot be fully transferred to object detection task. We first construct

210   3.1     E XISTING INDOOR 3D OBJECT DETECTION DATASETS
211
212   SUN RGB-D SUN RGB-D dataset by Song et al. (2015) is constructed by 10,335 single RGB-D
213   images captured by 4 different sensors. It also provide tools to convert single RGB-D frames to point
214   clouds. The original split use 5,073 scenes as training set and 4,828 for testing. Instance-level object
215   oriented bounding box annotations with 620 fine-grained object categories are provided for 3D object
      detection and orientation estimation task.


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      ScanNet and ScanNet++ ScanNet dataset by Dai et al. (2017) is one of the most commonly used
217   dataset in indoor 3D scene understanding. By thoroughly scanning real-world indoor rooms, Dai et al.
218   (2017) collect 2.5M frames of RGB-D videos and reconstruct them into CAD models which can be
219   converted into point clouds. The original split use 1,201 scenes as training set and 312 scenes for
220   testing, containing 529 fine-grained object categories. ScanNet++ dataset by Yeshwanth et al. (2023)
221   has been recently proposed as a complement of ScanNet. It captures higher fidelity indoor scenes by
222   professional devices, containing 230 scenes for training and 50 for testing.
223   Due to the massive human labor of 3D data collection and calibration, the scale of indoor 3D datasets
224   is limited. Additionally, human annotated 3D datasets unavoidably contain label noise due to the
225   challenge of point cloud annotation, which has been evaluated by Yu et al. (2024). Moreover, the
226   real-world datasets lack extensibility, insufficient to investigate multiple aspects of domain gap that
227   may occur in practical scenarios.
228
229
      3.2     O UR PROPOSED S IM ROOM AND S IM H OUSE
230
231   To address the data scale issue, researches have tried to use simulators to create large-scale datasets
232   with precise annotations at a low cost such as Zheng et al. (2020); Fu et al. (2021), but they mainly
233   focus on room layout estimation, generation or embodied AI tasks. Moreover, the human designed
234   datasets still faces the high cost of construction and can hardly be scaled up.
235
      In this paper, to fill the void in synthetic indoor 3D object detection datasets, and to investigate
236   the domain gap of synthetic and real scenes, we use the 3D simulator proposed by Deitke et al.
237   (2022) to generate SimRoom and SimHouse datasets. Specifically, we randomly sample large-scale
238   diverse house layouts following the ProcTHOR procedure of Deitke et al. (2022), including room
239   spec generation, floorplan generation, adding structure materials, large object, wall object and finally
240   surface objects. In this procedure, the precise location and surface information of room structures
241   and objects can be obtained, without the need of human labor. We then export the layout information
242   into mesh format (e.g. .glb files) with object shapes and surface information, and uniformly sample
243   points from the mesh surface to construct point clouds.
244   The ProcTHOR framework is originally designed for embodied AI tasks such as navigation, thus
245   generates combination of rooms to form a house, while in indoor 3D object detection task, most
246   datasets are captured in single rooms. To control this factor and investigate the influence of room
247   configuration, we split single rooms from a part of the aforementioned house point clouds. For a
248   selected house scene, we use the room information in generation process to collect all the 3D assets
249   in each room, and convert these room sub-meshes into independent point clouds. We name the split
250
      single room point clouds as SimRoom, and the originally generated house point cloud as SimHouse.
      In practice, we totally generate 10,000 house scenes, in which 7,306 scenes are randomly selected
251
      to construct SimHouse dataset, and 2,000 houses are further split into 7,202 single rooms. In both
252
      SimRoom and SimHouse datasets, we randomly split 6,000 point cloud scenes as training set, and
253   the rest of the scenes as testing set. The scene number of both SimRoom and SimHouse is more than
254   5 times larger than ScanNet, and the number of bounding box annotations is an order of magnitude
255   larger than ScanNet and SUN RGB-D.
256
      Note that we choose to use a generative framework rather than manually designing simulated
257
      data scenarios, which also offers advantages in scalability and controllability. On one hand, the
258
      generative framework allows the generation of data at any scale, facilitating the study of the scale-up
259   characteristics of 3D models. On the other hand, using this framework enables flexible control over
260   the generated indoor 3D scene configurations, including object layout combinations and the semantics
261   of the objects used, allowing for more detailed control-variable research of the domain gap. Relevant
262   experiments will be discussed in the following sections.

      3.3.2    R E - ARRANGING LABEL SPACE
325
326   Conventional domain adaptation requires different domains to share the same label space, i.e. target
327   categories in object detection. (Partial domain adaptation or open vocabulary domain adaptation
328   is our of the scope of this work.) The original annotations in the aforementioned datasets contains
329   hundreds of fine-grained object categories with extreme long-tail distribution. Song et al. (2015)
330   evaluate the performance of 10 most common categories on SUN RGB-D, and ScanNet 3D object
331
      detection report the results on 18 categories started from Hou et al. (2019). The direct subset of these
      benchmarks is too limited to fully utilize the numerous instance annotations available in the datasets.
332
333   We cataloged the original fine-grained object categories and the number of objects in each category
334   within each dataset. After manually checking and eliminating typos in the category names, we
335   merged the fine-grained categories into broader categories. (For example, “dining table” and “office
336   table” are both merged into “table” category.) In the merged broader categories, we select ones that
337
      satisfy the following conditions: (1) The category is an object instead of room structure such as
      “wall” or “window”. (2) The category each contains at least 150 objects. In this way, we select 15
338
      object categories shown in the horizontal axis of Figure 3(b) as the unified label space in our domain
339
      adaptation benchmarks.

      3.3.3    D OMAIN ADAPTATION SCENARIOS
342
343   Based on the analysis of differences between datasets, we propose 4 domain adaptation settings based
344   on the practical requirements:
345
      High quality to low quality point clouds: Detectors trained on thoroughly scanned datasets should
346
      be able to adapt to environments where the point clouds are of lower quality. Under this setting, we
347
      use ScanNet as source domain and SUN RGB-D as target domain.
348
349   Low quality to high quality point clouds: In contrast with fully to partial scan, in some practical
350   scenarios where training data faces the challenge of point omission, the detector is required to adapt
351
      to normal scenes where the point clouds are of higher quality. Under this setting, we use SUN RGB-D
      as source domain and ScanNet as target domain.
352
353   Synthetic to real adaptation: Fully leveraging the large-scale, high-quality and precisely annotated
354   synthetic data, the detectors should be capable of adapting to real-world scenarios to meet practical
355   requirements. Under this setting, we use SimRoom as source domain and ScanNet as target domain.
356   Single-room to multi-room adaptation: Downstream tasks such as navigation requires the model to
357   understand complex scenes, i.e. detecting objects in the house scenarios. Adaptation from single-
358   room training data to multi-room environments is a challenging and unique task in indoor 3D object
359   detection. SimHouse dataset has room layout configuration gap between all other datasets of single
360   rooms. To control other domain gap factors, we focus on SimRoom→SimHouse adaptation in which
361   both datasets are constructed from synthetic meshes.
362   Note that the data scale of ScanNet++ dataset is far smaller that the other datasets. Through
363   experimental analysis on data scale factor, we do not include ScanNet++ dataset into our proposed
364   domain adaptation benchmarks, which will be elaborated in Section 4.2.

398   average precision) following the protocal of Song and Xiao (2016) with IoU threshold 0.25.

401   4.2   A NALYSIS OF DOMAIN GAPS
402
403   We present the basic experimental results on our proposed benchmarks in Table 2 and further domain
404   adaptation results in Table 3 to analysis the impact of multiple domain gap factors mentioned in
405   Section 3.3.1. We specially analyze the influence of data scale factor in domain adaptation in Table 5.
406
      Point cloud quality: ScanNet→SUN RGB-D and SUN RGB-D→ScanNet benchmarks exhibit the
407
      domain gap of point cloud quality. Illustrated in Table 2 “scn2sun”, training by ScanNet performs
408   20.96 mAP on SUN RGB-D evaluation set (line “src”), much lower than 37.52 mAP within target
409   domain (line “tgt”). Conversely, the mAP of training by SUN RGB-D is also much lower than
410   training by ScanNet when evaluated on ScanNet (Table 2 “sun2scn”), indicating the challenge of
411   adaptation from both high to low and low to high quality datasets.
412
      Room layout configuration: As shown in Table 2 “rm2hse”, SimRoom trained model performs
413
      33.69 mAP (line “src”) which is much lower than the result within target dataset (48.80 mAP in line
414   “tgt”), confirming the challenge of adapting single-room object detector to multi-room environments.
415
416   Style(synthetic to real): SimRoom→ScanNet exhibit synthetic to real style gap. As illustrated in
417
      Table 2 “rm2scn”, ScanNet trained detector performs well on its own evaluation set (44.09 mAP),
      but drastically decline to only 11.20 mAP (line “src”) when trained on SimRoom, showing a huge
418
      performance gap. Compared with “sun2scn” where ScanNet also serve as target domain, with more
419
      data, the detector trained by SimRoom performs far worse than trained by SUN RGB-D. In “rm2hse”
420   where SimRoom also serve as source domain, the “Souece only” performance is acceptable as 20.96
421   mAP. Moreover, as shown in Table 5, trained by only 230 scenes of ScanNet++ dataset performs
422   better than by 6,000 scenes of SimRoom. Qualitative visualization results in Figure 4(a) also shows
423   that model trained with SimRoom struggle to predict the right boxes in ScanNet validation set. In
424   conclusion, synthetic-to-real gap is the most challenging hurdle for domain adaptive indoor 3D object
425   detection among our proposed benchmarks.
426   In Table 4 we further analyze the detailed domain gap of synthetic and real data. Leveraging the
427   flexibility of the generative data framework, we integrated objects from the ScanNet dataset to
428   generate object placement layouts, with the objects being in an intermediate state derived from
429   real-world data. As shown in the table, incorporating real objects leads to a noticeable performance
430   improvement compared to fully simulated data, highlighting the semantic domain gap between
431   datasets. However, compared to the target domain Oracle, the performance gap remains significant,
      suggesting that differences in object placement layouts are a more critical domain gap factor.

510   4.3.2    U NSUPERVISED D OMAIN A DAPTATION
511
      We evaluate different domain adaptation techniques on our proposed benchmarks, including classical
512
      framework MT Tarvainen and Valpola (2017), VSS Ding et al. (2022) for segmentation task, PPFA
513   Zhang et al. (2024) and RV Fan et al. (2022) for classification task, and OHDA Yunsong et al. (2024)
514   which has not been officially published yet. Note that due to the difference on task setting, we
515   re-implement all the methods and make some adjustments to achieve better results on object detection
516   task.
517
      As shown in Table 3, mean teacher framework shows performance gains compared to source only
518
      results in each benchmark. In line “VSS”, this high-to-low quality augmentation method works
519   for ScanNet→SUN RGB-D and SimRoom→ScanNet benchmarks, for in these benchmarks higher
520   quality point clouds. But in other benchmarks, this low-quality simulation method shows counter-
521   productive. In line “PPFA”, prototype-based feature alignment method with adversarial training
522   and self-supervised learning techniques performs well in SimRoom→SimHouse benchmark, as the
523   object-level distribution of the two datasets are similar, sharing a better category prototype. In line
524   “RV” which is a unique method to improve classification consistency between different domains when
525   generating pseudo labels, it performs well in most cases except in SimRoom→SimHouse, which
526   indicates that RV can only improve the classification accuracy at the object level. However, the gap
527
      in SimRoom→SimHouse task is the object configuration and the number of rooms and the objects
      themselves are similar. This is why RV does not work well in this case.

530   5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      To facilitate domain adaptation for indoor 3D object detection on point clouds, we propose the
533
      first benchmark combining the commonly used ScanNet and SUN RGB-D datasets, as well as our
534   newly proposed large-scale synthetic dataset SimRoom and SimHouse. We propose 4 adaptation
535   scenarios, and conduct extensive experiments to investigate the domain gap factors including point
536   cloud quality, room layout configuration, style(synthetic to real) and object sizes. We observe that
537   the synthetic-to-real adaptation gap is the most challenging hurdle among our evaluated ones. We
538   also introduce domain adaptation approaches to improve the adaptation performances, proposing a
539   first baseline. We hope future works could effectively address the domain gaps and make indoor 3D
      object detectors generalize well.
