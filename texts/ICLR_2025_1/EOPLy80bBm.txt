                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Data pruning, the process of carefully selecting a small subset of training data, has
012            been shown to improve both training efficiency and performance. It typically in-
013            volves two steps: (1) obtaining a representation for each instance, and (2) applying
014            a selection algorithm using these representations. However, the distinct roles of
015            these two steps, as well as their interactions, remain unclear. To address this, we
016            conduct a systematic study of data pruning, focusing on NLP fine-tuning. Our
017            theoretical and empirical findings reveal that data representation often plays a more
018            fundamental role than the selection algorithm: gradients, despite being compu-
019            tationally expensive, provide stronger pruning signals than other representations,
020
               making gradient-based methods consistently outperform cheaper alternatives. We
               also demonstrate that different selection algorithms excel in specific scenarios but
021
               are heavily influenced by the chosen representation. These insights provide clear
022
               guidelines for future research and practical applications.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The remarkable success of modern deep learning is largely driven by vast training datasets (Kaplan
028   et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Sardana et al., 2024). However, scaling the size of datasets comes
029   with great computational costs. Fortunately, recent studies have shown that by carefully selecting a
030   small subset of the original large dataset, a process known as data pruning, it is possible to improve
031   training efficiency while also improving generalization performance (Paul et al., 2021; Sorscher et al.,
032   2022; Du et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). Moreover, effective pruning methods can offer insights into
033   learning algorithms and the roles of training data (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ilyas et al., 2022).
034   Most data pruning methods involve two steps: First, obtaining a representation for each instance in
035   the original training set (e.g., hidden states from a pretrained language model); second, selecting
036   instances based on these representations given a data budget (e.g., 30% of the training set), according
037   to a selection algorithm. Despite the success of data pruning, existing studies treat these two steps
038   as a single process, leaving fundamental questions about the roles of representations and selection
039   algorithms unanswered: Which data representations and selection algorithms are most effective, and
040
      what is their suitability for different tasks? How do different representations impact the data points
      selected by each selection algorithm? To address these questions, we conduct a thorough study of
041
      data pruning through both theoretical and empirical lenses, focusing on NLP fine-tuning tasks. Our
042
      contributions are as follows:
043
044         1. We offer a systematic overview of existing data pruning methods (§2). From this overview,
045            we identify three common ways to create representations: based on training dynamics, hid-
046            den states, and gradients. We also identify three common objectives of selection algorithms:
047            maximizing difficulty, maximizing diversity, and maximizing validation performance.
048         2. We both theoretically analyze the pruning signals contained in different representations
049            (§3), and empirically validate that the representations of stronger signals, despite being
050            computationally more expensive, are more effective (§4). Specifically, we find that data
051            pruning based on gradients often performs the best. In contrast, data pruning based on
052            hidden states, which is computationally cheaper, often does not perform better than random
053            selection, especially with low data budgets. Our experiments evaluate six representative
               methods across common representations and selection algorithms, covering a wide range of


                                                         1

054
               NLP tasks: hate speech detection (classification), commonsense reasoning (multiple choice),
055            and summarization (text generation).
056
057
            3. Our empirical experiments (§4) further show that maximizing difficulty requires a higher
               data budget to perform well, maximizing validation performance excels when train-test
058
               distributions differ, and that maximizing diversity in general does not work very well.
059
060         4. Through both interpretable experiments with synthetic data (§3) and common NLP tasks
061
               (§4), we show that with the same selection objective, different data representations can
               lead to drastically different selections of instances. For instance, when the aim is to select
062
               the most difficult instances, using hidden states results in selecting instances far from the
063
               decision boundary, while using gradients select instances close to it. Moreover, we show
064            that the sensitivity of selection algorithms towards the change of representations vary, and
065            that data representations often matter more than selection algorithms.
066
067   Our findings highlight the strengths and limitations of current data pruning methods, offering clear
068   guidelines for selecting appropriate representations and algorithms given specific tasks and constraints.
069   We recommend prioritizing the development of efficient gradient-based methods, due to their superior
070   performance and better interpretability (Pruthi et al., 2020; Park et al., 2023).

072   2     OVERVIEW OF DATA P RUNING M ETHODS
073
074   Various data pruning methods have been proposed. They typically follow two steps for data selection:
075   (1) obtaining a representation for each instance, and (2) using an algorithm to select instances based
076   on their representations. Regarding representations, most methods rely on one of the three types:
077   training dynamics, hidden states, and gradients. Our overview is structured accordingly. While recent
078   studies have explored external large language models, by prompting and distillation, particularly in
079   instruction tuning (Sachdeva et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024), we focus
080   on representations from the model that we are training. This allows us to analyze signals that directly
081   reflect its learning behavior.
082   Regarding selection algorithms, most methods are built on one or more of the following objectives:
083   retaining the most difficult data instances, maximizing the diversity of selected data, and retaining the
084   training instances that improve a model’s performance on held-out data the most.
085
086   2.1   T RAINING -DYNAMIC -BASED M ETHODS
087
088   Most data pruning methods based on training dynamics aim to maximize the ratio of difficult instances
089   being selected, where the difficulty is defined by heuristics like low prediction confidence and high
090
      training loss. For example, Toneva et al. (2019) determine the difficulty of examples by the earliest
      epoch after which that example is always correctly classified; Jiang et al. (2019) keep the examples
091
      with the highest training loss; Swayamdipta et al. (2020) and Du et al. (2023) use the standard
092
      deviation and mean of prediction probabilities of the correct class across different epochs; Paul et al.
093   (2021) keep the examples with the largest error norm; Baldock et al. (2021) select examples that need
094   to pass through more layers before being correctly classified; and Marion et al. (2023) and Kwok
095   et al. (2024) quantify difficulty by perplexity. Moreover, Maini et al. (2022) show that examples that
096   are forgotten slower when training on a held-out subset are more rare and thus worth keeping. In the
097   context of instruction tuning, Li et al. (2024) quantify the difficulty of a response by the ratio of its
098   losses between generations with and without its instruction.
099   Other selection objectives have also been explored. For instance, Mindermann et al. (2022) prioritize
100   training on learnable, worth-learning, and not-yet-learned examples, quantified by the loss difference
101   between the model itself, and a small reference model trained on a held-out dataset. Furthermore,
102   Yang et al. (2024) aim to diversify the training data by clustering instances based on their training
103   losses across different epochs and sampling evenly from each cluster.
104
105   2.2   H IDDEN -S TATE -BASED M ETHODS
106
107   Hidden-state-based methods often exploit similarities between data samples to realize various se-
      lection objectives. For example, Sorscher et al. (2022) first perform a k-means clustering of hidden


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      states, then select the ones that lie farther from their cluster centroids, because these instances are
109   less prototypical and therefore more likely to be difficult. Abbas et al. (2023) and Tirumala et al.
110   (2023) extend this approach by identifying and removing semantic duplication after clustering. This
111   de-duplication step helps balance data diversity and difficulty by discarding redundant instances.
112
113
      2.3   G RADIENT-BASED M ETHODS
114
115   Another line of methods uses gradient information of each training instance to estimate its importance.
116   Specifically, most gradient-based methods estimate the impact of removing instances by simulating
117   re-training scenarios. These approaches use gradient-based tools like influence functions (Koh &
118   Liang, 2017; Pruthi et al., 2020) and datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023).
119
      The most common objective is to maximize or maintain validation performance. For example, Xia
120
      et al. (2024) and Engstrom et al. (2024) discard training instances with low contribution towards
121   the validation performance of the target task. They approximate this influence by using the TracIn
122   influence function (Pruthi et al., 2020) and datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022). Moreover, Killamsetty
123   et al. (2021) and Yang et al. (2023) keep instances that likely result in similar models as when training
124   on the full dataset.
125
      Gradients are also often used to select difficult instances. For example, Feldman & Zhang (2020)
126
      demonstrate that data instances of high self-influence (i.e., training on itself contributes more to
127   its prediction) are more difficult and help generalization. Using the TracIn influence function, this
128   self-influence score can be estimated as the gradient norm. Similarly, Thakkar et al. (2023) prioritizes
129   data instances of different self-influences at different stages of pretraining, to limit the influence of
130   noisy data while focusing on higher-quality ones; and Bejan et al. (2023) uses automatic curriculum
131   learning to filter noisy data.

      3     A TALE OF DATA R EPRESENTATIONS AND S ELECTION A LGORITHMS
135
136   This section studies the distinct roles of data representations, selection algorithms, and their interac-
137
      tions. Specifically, we focus on six representative methods that span all three major types of data
      representations and selection objectives. First, we provide in-depth explanations of each method
138
      (§3.1). Next, we present a theoretical analysis of the signals each representation offers (§3.2). Finally,
139
      we conduct interpretable synthetic experiments to study how the data instances favored by each
140   selection algorithm are shaped by the chosen data representations (§3.3).
141
142
      Notation We now summarize the notation used in this paper. We denote the original training set
143
      with N instances as D = {(xi , yi )}N   i=1 . The selected subset of data is represented by S ⊂ D. We
144   use B to denote the data budget (e.g., B = |S| = 0.2N ). For a data point (xi , yi ) and a model
145   with parameters θ, we use fθ (xi ) to denote the model’s logits, ℓ(fθ (xi ), yi ) to denote the loss,
146   and pθ (yi |xi ) to denote the prediction probability of the correct class or token. Moreover, we use
147   hθ (xi ) to denote the last layer hidden state of input xi , and gθ (xi , yi ) = ∇θ ℓ(fθ (xi ), yi ) to represent
148   the gradient. Moreover, we train each model for T epochs, use pt (yi |xi ) to denote the prediction
149   probability of the correct class at epoch t ≤ T , and use ηt to denote the learning rate at epoch t.
150
151   3.1   P RELIMINARIES : D ETAILS OF DATA P RUNING M ETHODS
152
153   Data pruning typically involves two steps under a given data budget (e.g., 20% of the original dataset).
154   First, a reference model is used to obtain representations for each instance, such as hidden states from
155   a pretrained model. Second, a selection algorithm chooses a subset based on these representations,
156   e.g., the ones that are farthest from the clustering centroids, following a selection objective, e.g.,
157   selecting the most difficult instances. The selected instances are then used to a train new main model
158
      which is the final model of interest. We summarize the representations and selection objectives of
      these methods in Appendix B.
159
160   We first describe two training-dynamic-based methods, Hard-to-Learn and SmallToLarge (S2L).
161   These training dynamics (e.g., training losses) are collected from a reference training run, during
      which a reference model is trained on the original dataset.

162
      Hard-to-Learn The Hard-to-Learn method is based on a simple intuition: training instances that
163   are difficult for models to fit often contain fewer regularities, and these instances can improve model
164   generalization (Swayamdipta et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2021). Concretely, it represents each training
165   instance by a score computed from the training dynamics of a reference model. In classification tasks,
166   for a given instance (xi , yi ), its score is defined as the average prediction probability of the correct
                                                                    PT
167   label across different epochs, i.e., shard (xi , yi ) = T1 t=1 pt (yi |xi ).PInstances with the lowest
168   scores are selected for training the main model, i.e., S = arg min|S|=B (xi ,yi )∈S shard (xi , yi ). To
169   extend this concept to generation tasks, Bhatnagar et al. (2022) and İnce et al. (2023) replace shard
170   with the inverse perplexity. Additionally, Jiang et al. (2021) show that shard correlates well with the
171   expected accuracy of an instance when it is excluded from the training data. This correlation also
172   suggests that Hard-to-Learn instances contain fewer regularities, supporting the intuition.
173
174   SmallToLarge Also based on training dynamics, SmallToLarge (S2L) aims to select diverse
175   instances. Specifically, Yang et al. (2024) observe that training instances with different loss trajectories
176   across epochs likely require different knowledge. To make sure different skills are evenly represented
177   in the subset S, S2L performs three steps. Initially, it represents each training instance by its cross
178
      entropy loss trajectory. Then, it performs a k-means clustering on these trajectories, and sorts these
      clusters by size in an ascending order. Finally, it iteratively samples (B − |S|)/(K − k + 1) instances
179
      from each cluster1 , where K is the number of clusters, and k is the current cluster index. This sample
180
      size choice helps S2L prioritize smaller clusters to increase the diversity of the selected data.
181
182   Next, we discuss two hidden-state-based methods, Prototypicality and SemDeDup. Hidden states
183   are usually computed using a reference pre-trained model, and thus require no further training, making
184
      them computationally more efficient than other methods.
185
186
      Prototypicality The Prototypicality method (Sorscher et al., 2022) aims to select difficult instances
      by exploiting their similarities: it measures difficulty based on how prototypical an instance is in
187
      the dataset. Specifically, after representing instances by their hidden states, prototypicality applies
188
      k-means clustering and ranks instances based on their distances to their cluster centroids. Instances
189   with larger distances are considered less prototypical, more difficult, and selected for further training.
190
191   SemDeDup Building on Prototypicality, SemDeDup adds one more step to account for data
192   diversity besides difficulty (Abbas et al., 2023). Concretely, after the clustering step of Prototypicality,
193   SemDeDup identifies semantically duplicate pairs of instances within each cluster using cosine
194   similarities of their hidden states. For each identified duplicate pair, it retains the instance that lies
195   farther from the cluster centroid, thereby prioritizing diversity while maintaining difficulty.
196
      Finally, we discuss two gradient-based methods, LESS and Memorization. They require training a
197   reference model on the original dataset, and computing gradients using different checkpoints. This
198   makes them very costly, because they require performing back-propagation with batch sizes of 1 to
199   obtain the gradients, which have a high dimensionality, equal to the number of model parameters.
200
201   LESS Unlike the above methods that only rely on representations of the training data, LESS
202   assumes the availability of a validation set. It aims to select instances that maximize the validation
203   performance, measured by validation loss reduction (Xia et al., 2024). Naively, this estimation
204   requires retraining models on a large number of random training subsets, which is prohibitively
205   expensive for modern neural models. Therefore, LESS employs influence functions for approxima-
206
      tion (Pruthi et al., 2020). Specifically, given the t-th reference model checkpoint θt , LESS estimates
      the loss reduction of a training instance (xi , yi ) w.r.t. a validation instance (xval , yval ) by the dot
207
      product of their (normalized) gradients. For multiple checkpoints, LESS performs weighted averag-
208                                                         PT

209

210
      random projection (Park et al., 2023; Johnson & Lindenstrauss, 1984) for dimensionality reduction.
211   Additionally, LESS considers optimizer states when computing training gradients.
212
213   Memorization Feldman & Zhang (2020) define memorization in training as the loss increase of a
214   training instance before and after it is removed from the training set, i.e., self-influence. Following a

223   3.2      W HAT DO DIFFERENT REPRESENTATIONS REVEAL ?
224
225   Data representation is central to data pruning, yet it remains unclear how different representations
226   vary in the signals they retain. This section offers theoretical insights into this question. Specifically,
227   as described in §3.1, most data pruning methods rely on the similarity between instances based on
228   their representations (e.g., S2L, Prototypicality, and LESS). We therefore ask: How does the notion of
229   similarity between two instances change across different representation spaces? Our analyses build
230
      insights into the pruning signals from different representations, which we will further study in the
      following synthetic (§3.3) and NLP task (§4) experiments.
231
232   For clarity of analysis, we consider a binary classification task with labels y ∈ {−1, +1}, optimized
233   with binary cross-entropy loss. For the representations, we focus on the prediction probability of the
234   correct class, the hidden states before the classification layer (parameterized by w, i.e., the classifier
235   or language modeling head), and the gradients of this classification layer.2 Given a training instance
236
      (xi , yi ) and a model θ, let h(xi ; θ) be the hidden states. We can derive the prediction probability of the
      correct class pθ (yi |xi ) = σ(yi wT h(xi ; θ)), where σ is the sigmoid function. We can also compute
237
      the gradients as gw (xi , yi ) = ∇w ℓ(fθ (xi ), yi ) = (1 − σ(yi wT h(xi ; θ)))yi h(xi ; θ). Specifically, we
238
      use Euclidean distance as the similarity metric, and consider hidden states as the fundamental units,
239   given their involvement in both prediction probability and gradient expressions.
240
241   We first consider the correct class prediction probability difference. Formally, given two training
242
      instances (xi , yi ) and (xj , yj ), this is |pθ (yi |xi ) − pθ (yj |xj )| = |σ(yi wT h(xi )) − σ(yj wT h(xj ))|.
      Because the Sigmoid function is smooth and monotonically increasing, when this difference is small,
243
      wT |yi h(xi ) − yj h(xj )| should also be small. We can identify two cases: when the labels agree, i.e.,
244
      yi = yj , h(xi ) and h(xj ) should be close in both direction and length (i.e., L2 norm) after being
245   projected onto w; when labels do not agree, i.e., yi ̸= yj , h(xi ) and h(xj ) should be opposite but of
246   similar length. In other words, in contrast to distances between hidden states, i.e., ∥h(xi ) − h(xj )∥,
247   the difference in prediction probabilities also considers label agreement.
248

295   3.3          H OW DO REPRESENTATIONS AND SELECTION ALGORITHMS INTERACT ?
296
297   This section analyzes how different representations affect the outcomes of selection algorithms.
298   Specifically, we aim to answer two questions: does a given selection algorithm choose different subset
299   selections when using different representations? Which selection algorithms are more sensitive to
300   representation changes? For this purpose, we first conduct an interpretable analysis using a synthetic
301   dataset. Building on these insights, we will empirically study more complex scenarios in §4.
302   To compare selection objectives across different representations, we focus on three selection algo-
303   rithms that are representation-agnostic: prototypicality (prioritizing difficulty), S2L (prioritizing
304   diversity), and LESS (prioritizing influence on validation set performance). We also include Hard-
305   to-Learn for comparison. We generate 600 data points from a 2D Gaussian mixture model and use
306   each method to select 30% (180) of the data points (Figures 1 and 6). We train a logistic regression
307   classifier to serve as the reference model to obtain training dynamics and gradients, and the original
308
      data points are used as hidden states. Our observations are as follows.
309   First, even when data pruning methods have the same objective, the representations and selection
310   algorithms used can result in drastically different subset selections. For example, both Hard-to-Learn
311   and Prototypicality aim to select difficult instances. To achieve this, Hard-to-Learn selects instances
312   with low correct class probabilities, which favors the ones that are located near the decision boundary
313   (Figure 1a). In contrast, Prototypicality clusters data instances based on hidden states, and selects
314
      those that are far from the cluster centroids. As a result, it selects instances from the sparsely
      populated regions (Figure 1b).
315
316   Second, even the same selection algorithm can prefer different data points, when using different
317   representations. As discussed in §3.2, instances with similar hidden states but different labels are far
318   apart in gradient space (§3.2). For example, when using gradients, Prototypicality favors instances
319   near the decision boundary as they are far from centroids (Figure 1c). In contrast, with hidden states,
320
      it selects instances far from the decision boundary, as mentioned above (Figure 1b).
321   Third, selection algorithms vary in sensitivity towards representations. For example, S2L selects sim-
322   ilar instances with different representations (Figures 1d–f). Indeed, diversity-preserving algorithms
323   are less affected by the choice of representation because they sample evenly from different regions.

385   This section analyzes whether different pruning methods rank data points similarly. We use Spear-

387   correlation are more suitable for comparing scores of different ranges, and (2) it allows for evaluation

389   DialogSum are shown in Figures 2a and 2b, with additional results in Appendix C.2.

      4.4     A BLATION S TUDIES
461
462   This section presents ablation studies to explore the impact of key components, including fine-tuned
463   versus pretrained hidden states, the interaction between representations and selection algorithms (as
464   in §3.3 but focusing on task performance), and the ability of these methods to handle label noises.
465   We include our results in Figure 4 and Appendix C.4.
466
467   Fine-Tuned Hidden States Our previous results show that hidden-state-based methods perform
468   comparably to random selection. However, fine-tuning could potentially encode task-specific and
469   label information into hidden states, helping identify prototypical and diverse training instances.
470   We therefore study whether using fine-tuned hidden states can improve model performance. Specif-
471   ically, we experiment with two types of fine-tuned hidden states: early (fine-tuned for one epoch,
472   retaining more pretrained knowledge) and late (fine-tuned for 15 epochs, encoding more task-specific
473   and label information). The results of both prototypicality and SemDedup are shown in Figure 4a.
474   Fine-tuned hidden states can barely improve model performance: there is little difference between
475   using different hidden states, and they all perform comparably to random selection.
476
477   Representation-Selection Relation It remains unclear whether the effectiveness of different data
478   pruning methods stems primarily from the representations they use, the selection algorithms they use,
479   or a specific combination of both. Similar to §3.3, to disentangle the contributions from these different
480
      components, we conduct a systematic study that combines all three different data representations with
      the selection algorithms of S2L, Prototypicality, and LESS, which respectively prioritize difficulty,
481
      diversity, and validation performance. We show the results for DeBERTaV3Large on WinoGrande and
482
      DynaHate in Figure 4b and Figure 4c, and make two observations.
483
484      7

489   to selection algorithm, representation plays a more fundamental role.
490
      Second, the optimal selection algorithm is task-dependent and still plays a key role. For example,
491

492
      One possible explanation is that DynaHate is an OOD test set that presents a substantial distribution
493   shift from the training data (CAD), while the LESS selection algorithm compensates for this shift by

495   should choose algorithms based on goals: prioritizing difficulty for better robustness, and prioritizing

520   5    C ONCLUSION
521
522   Summary Despite the success of data pruning, the roles of its two key components – data represen-
523   tation and selection algorithm – and their interactions, are not well understood. In this work, we have
524   conducted both theoretical and empirical analyses on these two choices in fine-tuning tasks. Our
525   results highlight the importance of using rich data representations, showing that gradient-based meth-
526
      ods consistently outperform computationally cheaper alternatives. Additionally, the optimal selection
      algorithm varies depending on specific use cases, although the outcomes are heavily influenced by the
527
      chosen representation. Moving forward, our findings stress the need for the development of scalable
528
      supervised representations, i.e., representations that encode label-relevant information, as more
529   effective alternatives to the current unsupervised ones, e.g., pretrained hidden states.
530
531
      Limitations The most notable limitation of our work is its focus on task-specific fine-tuning that
532
      leaves multi-task instruction tuning unexplored. This is largely due to (1) the huge amount of
533   computation required to conduct rigorous controlled studies as ours, and (2) the challenges in scalable
534   and low-cost evaluation (Zheng et al., 2023). Future studies could explore instruction-tuning with
535   synthetic data, which has been recently shown to be effective for proof-of-concept studies (Allen-Zhu
536   & Li, 2024). Moreover, we focus on methods that do not require external models (e.g., prompting
537   language models to evaluate example quality). Future work can extend our analysis to them.

      A     E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
811
812
      Implementation Details All experiments were conducted using the AdamW optimizer. For most
813   models, we set the learning rate to 2e-5, except for DeBERTaV3Large , where we followed He et al.
814   (2023) and used 1e-5. Additionally, we warmup the learning rate for the first 10% of training steps.
815   For gradient-based pruning, the reference models were trained with LoRA, using a higher learning
816   rate of 1e-4, r = 64, and α = 16 following Ivison et al. (2023), and apply LoRA on all linear layers.
817   We train all models for 15 epochs, For batch size, we used 16 for both WinoGrande and DialogSum,
818   and 32 for CAD, to fit all experiments on a single NVIDIA A100-40GB GPU. We use maximum
819   sequence lengths of 300, 128, and 512 tokens. For all experiments we use the same reference models
820   as the main models for fair comparison.
821   For k-Means clustering in S2L, Prototypicality, and SemDedup, we use 100 clusters on CAD and
822   DialogSum, and 200 clusters on WinoGrande, following the suggestions from Tirumala et al. (2023)
823   to set the number of clusters to around the square root of the number of instances. Moreover,
824   we compute gradients using the first five checkpoints for all experiments, and project them into a
825   1024-dimensional space using Park et al. (2023) (details see hyperparameter search).
826
827   Evaluation Metrics We evaluated CAD and DynaHate using the macro F1 score, WinoGrande by
828   accuracy, and DialogSum by ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L (from HuggingFace Evaluate),
829
      following the original studies (Vidgen et al., 2021; Sakaguchi et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021).
830
      Infrastructure All experiments were run on a single NVIDIA A100-40GB GPU using three
831
      random seeds. We used PyTorch 2.3, Transformers 4.42, and vLLM 0.5 for training and inference.
832
      Moreover, we use bfloat16 on all experiments to improve efficiency.
833
834
      Hyperparameter Search We mainly searched for four hyperparameters: the number of training
835   epochs, the number of clusters for k-Means clustering, the dimensionality of the projected gradients,
836   and the checkpoints to use for gradient computation.
837
838
      For the number of training epochs, we first perform a search of 3, 5, 7, and 10 epochs on all datasets
      and models, using three random seeds. We observe that models of different sizes share similar
839
      performance trends over epochs, with improvements continuing as the number of epochs increased.
840
      We therefore use the smaller models, i.e., DeBERTaV3Base and OPT-125M, and extend this search
841   over 15, 20, and 25 epochs. Across all datasets, the best performance is achieved with 15 epochs.
842
843   For the number of clusters, we search over 2, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, and 200 clusters for each dataset and
844
      model, using three random seeds. The results are highly consistent across cluster numbers. Following
      Tirumala et al. (2023), we use the square root of the dataset size as a guideline, settling on 100
845
      clusters for CAD and DialogSum, and 200 for WinoGrande.
846
847   For gradients, we use smaller models (DeBERTaV3Base and OPT-125M) for hyper-parameter search,
848   and only one random seed (0) to avoid the high costs of computing and projecting gradients. We
849   compute the gradients for all 15 checkpoints, and project them into 1024, 2048, and 4096 dimensions.
850
      First, we observe that different dimensionality compute similar results, and thus choose 1024 for
      further experiments for efficiency. Second, we experimented with different strategies for selecting
851
      checkpoints, including the first three, the last three, the first five, the last five, and evenly spaced three
852
      and five checkpoints. Using the first checkpoints is the most consistent with using all checkpoints,
853   with the first five yielding a minimum Spearman’s rank correlation of 0.96. We therefore use the first
854   five checkpoints for all experiments.
