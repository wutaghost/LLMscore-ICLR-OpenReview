009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Electrocardiogram (ECG), a non-invasive and affordable tool for cardiac mon-
012            itoring, is highly sensitive in detecting acute heart attacks. However, due to the
013            lengthy nature of ECG recordings, numerous machine learning methods have been
014            developed for automated heart disease detection to reduce human workload. De-
015            spite these efforts, performance remains suboptimal. A key obstacle is the in-
016
               herent complexity of ECG data, which includes heterogeneity (e.g., varying sam-
               pling rates), high levels of noise, demographic-related pattern shifts, and intricate
017
               rhythm-event associations. To overcome these challenges, this paper introduces
018
               AnyECG, a foundational model designed to extract robust representations from
019            any real-world ECG data. Specifically, a tailored ECG Tokenizer encodes each
020            fixed-duration ECG fragment into a token and, guided by proxy tasks, converts
021            noisy, continuous ECG features into discrete, compact, and clinically meaningful
022            local rhythm codes. These codes encapsulate basic morphological, frequency,
023            and demographic information (e.g., sex), effectively mitigating signal noise. We
024            further pre-train the AnyECG to learn rhythmic pattern associations across ECG
025            tokens, enabling the capture of cardiac event semantics. By being jointly pre-
026            trained on diverse ECG data sources, AnyECG is capable of generalizing across
027
               a wide range of downstream tasks where ECG signals are recorded from vari-
               ous devices and scenarios. Experimental results in anomaly detection, arrhythmia
028
               detection, corrupted lead generation, and ultra-long ECG signal analysis demon-
029
               strate that AnyECG learns common ECG knowledge from data and significantly
030            outperforms cutting-edge methods in each respective task.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The electrocardiogram (ECG) is a widely used test that records the heart’s electrical activity, facili-
036   tating the monitoring and diagnosis of various cardiac conditions. Due to variations in ECG devices,
037   recording conditions, patient characteristics, the length of recorded ECG signals, the number of
038   leads, the sampling rates, as well as the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), can vary significantly. For ex-
039
      ample, in non-clinical settings, wearable devices typically collect long-term single-lead or dual-lead
      ECG signals at lower sampling rates, covering a variety of human activity scenarios, which often
040
      results in higher noise levels (Abbaspourazad et al., 2023; Ansari et al., 2023). In contrast, standard
041
      devices used in hospital outpatient clinics capture high-resolution eight-, twelve-, or eighteen-lead
042   ECG signals in a resting state for diagnostic purposes (Herman et al., 2024). Additionally, the noise
043   in ECG data can originate from device artifacts, baseline wander, muscle noise, as well as external
044   interference (Singh & Sharma, 2022). These heterogeneity and complexity present major challenges
045   in developing a unified model that can effectively handle ECG signals recorded across various de-
046   vices, scenarios, and clinical purposes.
047
      Sequence models, such as large language models, developed using large-scale data in the wild
048   have shown significant advantages in learning robust representations and demonstrated robustness in
049   downstream tasks. However, adapting sequence model pre-training approaches to ECG data in real-
050   world settings poses unique challenges: (1) Heterogeneity: real-world ECG signals vary in length,
051   sampling rate, and the number of channels due to differences in devices and scenarios. A unified
052   model (e.g., with fixed tokenizer settings) is needed to effectively manage this diversity while main-
053   taining temporal resolution and avoiding the introduction of artifacts. (2) Low SNR: ECG signals
      inherently have a low SNR, and pathological waveforms are often subtle, making it easy for noise

      to interfere with the understanding of critical features. (3) Demographic shift: ECG waveforms
055   can vary due to patient demographics (e.g., age, sex, ethnicity). For instance, pediatric ECGs differ
056   from adults in disease presentation and heart rate (Chen et al., 2024), and distinct ethnic groups
057   may exhibit unique ECG characteristics (Jain et al., 2010), which hinder models from generalizing
058   across diverse populations. (4) Implicit rhythm-event association: The systematic arrangement
059   of rhythm patterns may indicate some cardiac events. ECG analysis depends on identifying these
060   rhythm associations and event patterns. However, noisy real-world ECG data make it difficult for
061   models to capture sequential relationships, hindering the accurate cardiac event detection.
062   To overcome these challenges, we introduce AnyECGs, a family of ECG foundational models de-
063   signed for robust representation learning on ECG signals in diverse, real-world settings. The de-
064   velopment of AnyECG involves two main pre-training phases: the ECG Tokenizer pre-training and
065   the entire AnyECG foundation model pre-training. The first phase captures key local rhythmic pat-
066   terns from noisy ECG signals, while the second learns associations across the rhythmic patterns
067   that implies cardiac events. Specifically, we first pad any ECG signals to unified length, segment a
068   signal into a collection of fixed-duration fragments, project each fragment into a token orderly, and
069
      pad missing channels, standardizing the diversity in sampling rates, lengths, and channel numbers.
      We further design a new hierarchical modeling approach to tackle ultra-long ECG signal (solving
070
      challenge (1)). In the ECG Tokenizer pre-training phase, a Rhythm Codebook is established
071
      to capture the key local morphological and frequency features inherent in ECG signals. The ECG
072   Tokenizer extracts ECG features that are closely aligned with these Rhythm Codes, effectively
073   reducing noise by matching the input patches to those representative codes (solving challenge (2)).
074   Additionally, the extracted ECG features are also required to recover demographic information about
075   the patient (addressing challenge (3)). Then, we apply ‘masked modeling’ approach in the AnyECG
076   pretraining phase, where the model predicts Rhythm Code indices to fill in masked patches. This
077   approach encourages the recovery of masked ECG patches based on their relationship with un-
078   masked ECG patches, enabling the model to learn cardiac event semantics that are essential for
079   downstream tasks, thus addressing challenge (4). With these designs, AnyECG can facilitate knowl-
080
      edge transfer across various ECG sources in the wild, enabling to learn shared ECG and cardiac
      event knowledge that is applicable to downstream tasks. Our contributions are listed below.
081
082
             • ECG Foundational Model: We introduce AnyECG, a foundational ECG model that uni-
083            fies representation learning by capturing important local rhythm patterns in ECG signals
084            and their semantic relationships, providing a flexible framework adaptable to any ECG
085            signals for various downstream applications.
086
             • ECG Tokenizer: We present an ECG tokenizer that extracts compact, noise-resilient
087            Rhythm Codes utilizing the Multi-View Synergistic Decoder that reconstructs these
088            codes from Morphology, Frequency, and Demography perspectives to capture essential
089            diagnostic features and improve generalization across diverse populations.
090
             • Various Downstream Tasks Adaptability: By pretrained to learn the associations among
091            ECG tokens, our AnyECG is sensitive to potential cardiac events, demonstrating strong
092            generalization capabilities across various downstream tasks, including anomaly detection,
093            arrhythmia detection, corrupted lead generation, and ultra-long ECG signal analysis.

176   2.2.1    ECG T OKENIZER WITH R HYTHM C ODEBOOK

178   Motivation. ECG signals are inherently high-dimensional time-series data, often characterized by a
179
      low SNR due to sparse key information and contamination from various types of noise. To address
      these issues, we propose a vector-quantized rhythm codebook that transforms raw ECG signals into
180
      compact, discrete tokens, enabling robust and noise-resistant representation learning. The transfor-
181
      mation of rhythm codebook enhances low-SNR signals into a high-SNR representation, accurately
182   capturing true cardiac activity while minimizing the effects of noise.
183
184   Initially, each patch xj,k ∈ Rs represents a portion of the signal over w time steps in lead j. The
185   tokenizer processes these patches into feature representations, yielding embeddings hj,k ∈ Rd ,
186
      where d is the dimension of each embedding. To discretize these continuous embeddings into tokens
      suitable for subsequent processing, we employ a quantizer that maps each embedding hj,k to the
187
188
      nearest codeword in a predefined codebook V . The codebook V ∈ RK×d consists of K codes
      v1 , v2 , . . . , vK . The quantization process is defined as:

      to 1 second of ECG data. The maximum sequence length is fixed at 1,024 tokens, sufficient for most
345   ECG applications. During ECG Tokenizer training and AnyECG pre-training, sequences shorter
346   than this length are padded. To preserve data integrity, we mask the attention values associated with
347   these padding tokens.
348
349   Training Environment. The pre-training of AnyECG was conducted on a comprehensive dataset
350   compiled from seven different sources. For the downstream tasks, data splitting followed stan-
351   dard procedures, dividing the data into training and validation subsets using an 80/20 ratio. Binary
352   cross-entropy loss was employed for binary classification tasks, while cross-entropy loss was uti-
353   lized for multi-class classification tasks. Evaluation metrics for the downstream tasks are detailed
354
      in the Appendix 7.2. All experiments were executed on a computing cluster equipped with eight
      high-performance GPUs. We used the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-4 for all models
355
      training. Model selection was based on the best performance on validation sets, and final evalu-
356
      ations were conducted on separate test sets. To ensure the reliability of our results, performance
357   metrics—including averages and standard deviations—were reported across five random seeds.

      4    R ELATED W ORKS
475
476   Heterogeneous ECG Signal Analysis and Classification. The application of deep learning tech-
477
      niques has significantly advanced the analysis and classification of ECG signals. However, the
      heterogeneity of ECG data poses a major challenge for model generalization; models trained on one
478
      dataset often do not perform well on others. Consequently, researchers have focused on design-
479
      ing specialized models tailored to specific datasets, employing architectures such as convolutional
480   neural networks (CNNs) (Prathipati & Malyavantham, 2023; Kucukseymen et al., 2022), recurrent
481   neural networks (RNNs) (Kumar et al., 2023; Din et al., 2024), and transformer-based models (Shah
482   et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024). While these efforts have led to incremental performance improvements
483   (Srivastava et al., 2023; Jasvitha et al., 2024; Ribeiro et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021), the gains are
484   often not statistically significant due to the limited size and scope of the datasets used. The absence
485   of a unified model capable of handling the diverse nature of ECG data underscores the need for new
      approaches that can provide more substantial and broadly applicable performance improvements.


                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Self-supervised ECG Representation Learning. Self-supervised learning has emerged as a
487   promising approach for extracting representations from unlabeled ECG signals, enabling the use
488   of large amounts of raw data without manual annotations. Methods such as signal reconstruction,
489   contrastive learning, and masked signal modeling have been explored (Yun et al., 2024; Wu et al.,
490   2024; Li et al., 2024). However, existing self-supervised learning methods often struggle to general-
491   ize across heterogeneous ECG datasets, especially when faced with varying lead configurations and
492   noise levels. For example, contrastive methods (Kiyasseh et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023) encourage
493   similar representations for compatible signal segments but do not adequately account for variability
494   introduced by different lead setups. Moreover, the low SNR inherent in ECG data can cause models
495
      to focus on reconstructing noisy or redundant signal components due to high correlations among
      leads, rather than capturing critical physiological information. Models like contrastive predictive
496
      coding (CPC) (Mehari & Strodthoff, 2022) and masked autoencoders (Zhang et al., 2022; Na et al.,
497
      2024) often inadvertently emphasize less relevant features, diminishing their effectiveness in cap-
498   turing essential signal characteristics. This focus on less informative aspects can limit the models’
499   ability to extract meaningful representations that transfer effectively to unseen data or datasets with
500   different characteristics.
501
502
503   5   D ISCUSSION
504
505
506   Social Impacts. ECG is one of the most commonly used diagnostic tools in healthcare, with over
507
      100 million ECG reports obtained annually in the United States alone (Tison et al., 2019). Despite its
      widespread use, unlike other biomedical signals such as electroencephalograms (EEG) (Yang et al.,
508
      2024; Jiang et al., 2024), there is a scarcity of foundation models specifically designed for ECG data.
509
      This limitation hampers the potential for advanced analysis and interpretation of ECG signals on a
510   large scale. In this work, we propose AnyECG, the largest ECG foundation model family to date.
511   Compared to prior works (McKeen et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024), AnyECG adapts
512   to diverse downstream tasks and achieves significantly better performance. By providing a robust
513   and generalizable model for ECG data, AnyECG has the potential to greatly enhance diagnostic
514   accuracy, facilitate early detection of cardiovascular diseases, and improve patient outcomes on a
515   broad scale.
516
517
518
      Limitations. Although we pre-trained AnyECG using a large amount of data across seven
      datasets, there remains a significant gap between AnyECG and current foundation models like LLMs
519
      in the general domain. This gap is primarily due to the difficulty in obtaining extensive healthcare
520
      data. Additionally, the model size of AnyECG-XL (1.7B parameters) is considerably smaller than
521   that of foundation models in natural language processing and computer vision fields. Despite these
522   limitations, it is important to highlight that training a large-scale ECG foundation model with a two-
523   stage self-supervised learning approach and more data does yield appreciable performance gains
524   compared to existing methods developed for specific downstream tasks, even if it may be computa-
525   tionally costly. Exploring the trade-off between employing larger AnyECG models and enhancing
526   downstream task performance will be a focus of our future work.
527
528
529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we proposed AnyECG, a foundation model family that learns universal embeddings
532
      through a two-stage self-supervised pre-training on seven diverse ECG datasets. AnyECG effec-
533
      tively handles the heterogeneity of ECG data through the design of a novel ECG Tokenizer, which
534   includes a rhythm codebook and a multi-view synergistic decoder to learn representations from
535   different proxy tasks. Additionally, the masked modeling in the second-stage pre-training plays a
536   crucial role in enabling effective representation learning of both temporal and lead features of ECG
537   signals. We validated various sizes of AnyECG models on multiple downstream tasks, including
538   anomaly detection, arrhythmia detection, ECG lead generation, and ultra-long ECG signal recogni-
539   tion. Our experiments demonstrate that AnyECG outperforms all state-of-the-art methods in their
      respective fields, highlighting its effectiveness and versatility in ECG signal analysis.

      7.1     ECG DATASETS
743
744   To evaluate the performance of AnyECG and baseline models, we utilized a comprehensive set of
745   ECG datasets that cover a wide spectrum of cardiac conditions, patient demographics, and recording
746   scenarios, ensuring robust testing across diverse settings. The datasets include:
747
      CPSC and CPSC-Extra Databases (Liu et al., 2018): These consist of 12-lead ECG recordings
748
      ranging from 6 to 60 seconds in duration, sampled at 500 Hz, and include a balanced mix of male
749   and female subjects.
750
751   INCART Database (Tihonenko et al., 2008): This database provides 74 annotated recordings
752
      extracted from 32 Holter records, each 30 minutes long and sampled at 257 Hz, offering high-
      resolution data ideal for arrhythmia classification.
753
754   PTB (Bousseljot et al., 1995) and PTB-XL Databases (Wagner et al., 2020): The PTB Diagnostic
755   ECG Database includes 516 recordings sampled at 1000 Hz, while PTB-XL contains 21,837 12-lead
      ECGs sampled at 500 Hz, each 10 seconds long, encompassing a wide range of cardiac pathologies.

      Georgia 12-lead ECG Challenge (G12EC) Database: Comprising 10,344 recordings from the
757   Southeastern United States, sampled at 500 Hz, this dataset adds demographic diversity to our eval-

765   7.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
766
767   We conducted four distinct experiments, each utilizing a specific set of evaluation metrics tailored
768
      to the task:
769   1. Anomaly Detection: Evaluated using Accuracy, AUC-PR (Area Under the Precision-Recall
770   Curve), AUROC (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve), and Weighted F1
771   Score. These metrics assess the model’s ability to correctly identify anomalies and handle class
772   imbalances effectively.
773   2. Arrhythmia Detection: Assessed with Accuracy, AUC-PR, Weighted F1 Score, and Preci-
774   sion. This combination of metrics evaluates the model’s performance in detecting various types of
775   arrhythmias, emphasizing both overall accuracy and the precision of positive predictions.
776
      3. Corrupted Lead Generation: Measured using PSNR (Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio), SSIM
777
      (Structural Similarity Index), and MAE (Mean Absolute Error). These metrics quantify the quality
778   of the generated ECG signals by comparing the reconstructed signals to the original ones, focusing
779   on signal fidelity and structural similarity.
780
781
      4. Ultra-Long ECG Recognition: Evaluated with PSNR, SSIM, and MAE, similar to the cor-
      rupted lead generation task. These metrics ensure that the model maintains high-quality signal
782
      reconstruction and accurate recognition over extended ECG recordings.
783
784   The definitions of the evaluation metrics used across these experiments are as follows: Accuracy:
785   The proportion of correctly predicted instances out of all instances, indicating the overall effective-
786   ness of the model. Precision: The ratio of true positive predictions to the total number of posi-
787
      tive predictions, reflecting the model’s ability to avoid false positives. AUC-PR (Area Under the
      Precision-Recall Curve): Measures the trade-off between precision and recall for different threshold
788
      settings, particularly useful for imbalanced datasets. AUROC (Area Under the Receiver Operating
789
      Characteristic Curve): Represents the model’s ability to distinguish between classes across all clas-
790   sification thresholds. Weighted F1 Score: The harmonic mean of precision and recall, weighted
791   by the number of true instances for each class, providing a balanced evaluation of the model’s per-
792   formance. PSNR (Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio): Indicates the quality of signal reconstruction by
793   comparing the maximum possible signal power to the power of reconstruction noise, with higher
794   values signifying better quality. SSIM (Structural Similarity Index): Assesses the similarity be-
795   tween two signals in terms of luminance, contrast, and structure, with values closer to 1 indicating
796   higher similarity. MAE (Mean Absolute Error): Represents the average absolute difference between
797   predicted and actual values, serving as a measure of prediction accuracy. By employing these tai-
798
      lored metrics across different experiments, we ensure a comprehensive evaluation of our model’s
      performance in various aspects of ECG signal processing and classification.

841   7.4             L OSS F UNCTION A BLATION S TUDY
842
843   To assess the effectiveness of our loss function design, we conducted an ablation study by sys-
844
      tematically removing each component of the loss function. This allowed us to evaluate how each
      term contributes to the model’s ability to capture meaningful features from ECG signals. Table 7
845
      presents the results of this study. The ”Full Loss” configuration, which includes all components of
846
      our proposed loss function, serves as the baseline for optimal performance. When we individually
847   removed each loss component, we observed a decrease in performance across all evaluation met-
848   rics. Omitting the Morphology Loss resulted in a noticeable decline, indicating its significant role
849   in helping the model capture the morphological characteristics of ECG signals, which are crucial
850   for accurate anomaly detection. Excluding the Frequency Loss also led to reduced performance,
851   suggesting that capturing frequency domain information is important for understanding underly-
852   ing patterns in ECG signals. Removing the Demography Loss caused a performance drop as well,
853   though to a lesser extent compared to the Morphology and Frequency losses. This highlights that
854   incorporating demographic information refines the model’s predictions by accounting for variations
855
      in ECG patterns across different demographic groups. The most significant decrease in performance
      was observed when the Codebook Loss was removed. This component is essential for encouraging
856
      diversity and utilization of codebook entries in the vector quantization process, playing a critical
857
      role in the model’s ability to represent ECG signals effectively. Lastly, removing the Commitment
858   Loss also led to a decline in performance, though the impact was less severe than omitting the Code-
859   book Loss. The Commitment Loss ensures consistency in the representation of similar inputs by
860   encouraging the encoder to commit to specific codebook entries. The combination of Morphology,
861   Frequency, Demography, Codebook, and Commitment losses enables the model to capture compre-
862   hensive features of ECG signals, leading to improved anomaly detection capabilities. These results
863   validate the design of our loss function and underscore the importance of each term in capturing
      different aspects of the ECG data.
