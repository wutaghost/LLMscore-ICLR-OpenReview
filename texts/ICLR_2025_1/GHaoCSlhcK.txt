011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge distillation (KD) is a key technique for training lightweight deep
014            neural networks, particularly in resource-constrained environments. While
015            existing KD methods utilize intermediate features to improve student models, they
016
               often overlook the proper alignment between teacher-student layers and fail to
               select the most informative data for training each student layer. These limitations
017
               are especially pronounced in architecture-agnostic scenarios, where different
018
               network architectures complicate knowledge transfer.
019
               We propose PKD, a Progressive Knowledge Distillation framework that progres-
020
               sively aligns teacher and student layers through feature-based modularization.
021            Each student module is trained using the most representative features from its
022            corresponding teacher module, starting with the shallowest layers and progres-
023            sively moving to deeper ones. This training method enables efficient, architecture-
024            agnostic knowledge transfer across a variety of model architectures. Experiments
025            on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet-1K demonstrate that PKD outperforms baseline
026            models, achieving performance improvements of up to 4.54% and 6.46%, respec-
027            tively, thereby validating its effectiveness in diverse neural network settings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031   Deep neural networks (DNNs) have achieved unprecedented success across various domains, in-
032   cluding computer vision, natural language processing, and speech recognition. However, their
033   deployment in resource-constrained environments, such as mobile devices and embedded systems,
034   remains challenging due to their large computational and memory requirements. Knowledge Distil-
035   lation (KD), first introduced by (Hinton et al., 2015), offers a solution by enabling the transfer of
036   knowledge from a large, cumbersome teacher model to a smaller, more efficient student model,
037   without significantly compromising performance.
038   While most KD methods focus on transferring knowledge from the final output layer (Bucila et al.,
039   2006; Hinton et al., 2015), recent research has demonstrated that incorporating intermediate layer
040   representations can greatly improve the performance of the student model (Romero et al., 2015;
041   Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2017). However, two major limitations persist in existing methods: (1)
042   the misalignment between teacher and student layers, particularly in heterogeneous architectures,
043   and (2) the suboptimal selection of informative training data for specific student layers. These
044   limitations are particularly problematic in architecture-agnostic distillation, where knowledge
045
      must be transferred between models of different architectures (e.g., convolutional networks and
      transformers) Hao et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2021).
046
047   We propose Progressive Knowledge Distillation (PKD), a novel framework that addresses these
048   challenges by progressively aligning and training student and teacher models in a modular and
049   sequential manner.
050   PKD introduces the following key innovations:
051
052         1. Progressive Modular and Sequential Training: PKD leverages a progressive, modular,
053            and sequential training strategy. Instead of training the entire student model at once, PKD
               divides both the teacher and student networks into feature-based modules, which reflect


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
               the hierarchical structure of deep networks. Each module is responsible for learning specific
055            types of features, such as texture, shape, and high-level semantics. The training process in
056            PKD is both modular and sequential. It begins by training the shallowest student module
057            with the corresponding teacher module, ensuring that the most representative features
058            are transferred first. After the shallow layers are aligned, training proceeds progressively
059            to the deeper modules. This layer-wise training ensures optimal alignment at each stage,
060            mitigating the risks of parameter conflicts and knowledge loss that occur when training all
061            layers simultaneously.
062            This sequential mechanism is especially valuable in architecture-agnostic KD, where
063            student and teacher networks may have different architectures (e.g., CNNs vs. transformers).
064            By focusing on progressively aligning the modules of each network, PKD enables efficient
065            knowledge transfer across heterogeneous architectures, improving the performance of the
066
               student model in a structured manner.
067         2. Novel Application of PCA for Module-Specific Features: PKD also introduces a novel
068            application of principal component analysis (PCA) for feature selection during distillation.
069            While PCA is commonly used for dimensionality reduction (Wold et al., 1987; Jolliffe, 2002),
070
               we employ it to compute module-specific features. By extracting the most representative
               features from the teacher’s modules, PKD ensures that the student model learns the most
071
               relevant information at each stage of training, further enhancing the distillation process. This
072
               targeted feature selection is critical in architecture-agnostic settings, reducing redundancy
073            and ensuring that the student network captures only the most informative data from the
074            teacher (Kornblith et al., 2019a).
075
076   PKD Framework: By integrating these innovations, PKD provides a flexible, architecture-agnostic
077   framework for knowledge distillation. It offers substantial improvements over traditional KD methods
078   that focus on final output logits or train all layers simultaneously. PKD’s modular and progressive
079   training process ensures efficient knowledge transfer, even when the student and teacher networks
080   have fundamentally different architectures. Our experiments on standard benchmarks such as
081
      CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009) demonstrate that PKD
      significantly outperforms baseline methods, with improvements of up to 4.54% on CIFAR-100 and
082
      6.46% on ImageNet-1K, validating its effectiveness in real-world settings.

087
      In this section, we explore knowledge distillation, a crucial technique for model optimization, and
      Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA), a key tool used to measure the similarity between network
088
      modules within our framework.
089
090   Knowledge Distillation (KD) has emerged as a powerful technique for compressing large models
091   by training a lightweight student model to mimic the output logits of a pre-trained teacher model
092   (Tang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2019; Phuong & Lampert, 2019; Luan et al., 2019; Tung & Mori,
093
      2019; Zhu & Gong, 2018). The concept was introduced by (Bucila et al., 2006) and later refined
      by (Hinton et al., 2015). Subsequent research has improved logits-based KD with enhancements
094
      such as structural information (Guo et al., 2021; Park et al., 2019), model ensembles (Malinin et al.,
095
      2020), and contrastive learning (Tian et al., 2020). For instance, (Touvron et al., 2021) proposed a
096   logits distillation method for training Vision Transformer (ViT) students, while (Huang et al., 2018)
097   introduced relaxed KL divergence loss for teacher-student models with significant capacity gaps.
098
099
      Beyond logits, intermediate feature-based KD has gained traction. Hint-based distillation was first
      proposed by (Romero et al., 2015), with extensions such as attention map mimicry (Zagoruyko
100
      & Komodakis, 2017) and advanced feature distillation methods (Hao et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
101
      2020; Chen et al., 2021). ViTKD (Yang et al., 2022) explored feature-based KD for ViT models,
102   while VKD (Miles et al., 2024) introduced orthogonal projection and task-specific normalization for
103   transformers. KD-DETR (Wang et al., 2024), designed for DETR-based object detection, tackles
104   distillation point inconsistencies through shared and specialized queries, enabling effective distillation
105   for both homogeneous (DETR-to-DETR) and heterogeneous (DETR-to-CNN) setups. Similarly, (Liu
106   et al., 2022) proposed partially cross-attention and group-wise projectors to align features across
107   architectures, and ACMKD (Ni et al., 2024) leveraged multi-student mutual learning strategies for
      diverse inductive biases, further refining cross-architecture KD.

109   KD reduced photorealistic style transfer models by 20x while maintaining quality (Chiu & Gurari,

111   Recent approaches like RdimKD (Guo et al., 2023) employed dimensionality reduction to improve

136   3     M ETHOD
137   This section presents our Progressive Knowledge Distillation (PKD) method. The PKD framework
138   focuses on two core aspects: (1) network modularization and (2) progressive modular feature
139   alignment. The key contribution of our approach lies in progressively aligning student modules
140   with the corresponding teacher modules based on their learned representations and utilizing module-
141   specific features for effective knowledge transfer.
142
143   3.1   N OTATIONS AND P ROBLEM D EFINITION
144
      Knowledge distillation (KD) traditionally involves training a smaller student model to learn from a
145
      larger, pre-trained teacher model. The primary forms of knowledge transfer are logits and intermediate
146   feature representations. In our setting, logits represent the output probabilities across different classes,
147   while features are the internal learned representations across different layers. The objective is to align
148   these features across teacher and student models in a progressive manner. Our framework leverages
149   the following loss function:
150
                       LKT = λE(x, y) ∼ (X , Y)[DCE(ps , y) + (1 − λ)DKL(ps , pt )],                          (2)
151
152   where (X , Y) denotes the sample and class label distribution (Hinton et al., 2015; Park et al., 2019;
153   Tian et al., 2020; Hao et al., 2024). ps and pt represent the predictions from the student and
154   teacher models, respectively, and DCE denote the cross-entropy loss function, and DKL signifies the
155
      Kullback-Leibler divergence. λ is a hyperparameter that balances between one-hot label y and soft
      label pt .

      hinder the performance of the student model if the same features are transferred without modification.

179   3.3.1      N ETWORK M ODULARIZATION

181
      We introduce a network modularization technique that organizes network layers into distinct
      modules based on their feature similarity, quantified using Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)
182
      scores. Consecutive layers with high CKA scores (i.e., representing similar features) are grouped into
183
      the same module, while layers with low CKA scores are assigned to different modules. For any two
184   consecutive layers i and j, the representation distance d(i, j) is defined as d(i, j) = 1 − CKA(i, j).
185
186   Using the computed CKA scores for all layers, we distinguish two types of representation distances:
187
      (1) dSM , the distance between consecutive layers within the same module (i.e., layers that exhibit
      similar feature representations), and (2) dDM , the distance between layers assigned to different
188
      modules (i.e., layers that have distinct feature representations). Layers with small distances dSM are
189
      grouped into the same module, while layers with significantly larger distances dDM are assigned to
190   different modules. This process is illustrated in Figure 2.

212
      innovation is to align student modules with their corresponding teacher modules based on the teacher’s
      module-specific features and utilize these features for effective knowledge distillation, ensuring that
213
      the student’s learned representations closely match those of the teacher at the module level.
214

      Module Selection via Binary Vector We assume the student network (S) consists of s modules. To
310   manage the distillation process, we introduce an s-dimensional binary vector c ∈ C = {0, 1}s , where
311
      each element c(m) determines whether the m-th student module participate in distillation process or
312   remains inactive (frozen). For example, a binary vector c = [1, 1, 0, 0] means that student modules
313   1 and 2 are active in distillation while modules 3 and 4 remain frozen. For each teacher module i,
314   we compute its most specific representation M S[i] as detailed in algorithm 2. By leveraging this
315   representation, we pass the corresponding data through the forward phase of the teacher network. The
316   student module is then trained under various configuration of the binary vector c for a few epochs.
317   This allows us to explore different configurations and select the best corresponding student modules.

319   the representation distance between consecutive student modules. This is important to ensure

321   knowledge learned. The distance between two successive modules i and i + 1 is computed as

323   similarity between the feature representations of two modules. By maximizing the representation

380   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
381
      We conducted extensive experiments to evaluate the proposed PKD framework. This section provides
382   a concise summary of our experimental configurations; further details available in Appendix sections
383   D and E. All experiments were performed on NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs. In our experiments, we
384   followed the OFA experiments and included their baselines for consistency. For a fair comparison,
385   we focused on KD methods specifically designed for heterogeneous architectures. Although we
386   list self-distillation and homogeneous KD methods in our tables for comprehensiveness, we do not
387   compare directly with them, as they differ significantly from our approach. Additionally, ablation
388   studies are reported in the last section and Appendix section B.
389   Architectures We evaluated a variety of models with heterogeneous architectures. Specifically, our
390   experiments included ResNet (He et al., 2016), MobileNet v2 (Sandler et al., 2018), and ConvNeXt
391   (Liu et al., 2021) as CNN models, ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), DeiT (Touvron et al., 2021), and
392   Swin (Liu et al., 2021) as ViT models, and MLP-Mixer (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) and ResMLP (Touvron
393   et al., 2021)as MLP models. Notably, when Swin-T was used as the student model, it outperformed
394   several teachers when trained from scratch, such as surpassing Mixer-B/16 on the ImageNet-1K
395   dataset. To enable comparisons with these teacher-student pairings, we used two adapted versions of
396
      Swin-T: Swin-Nano and Swin-Pico, denoted as Swin-N and Swin-P, following (Hao et al., 2024).
397   Datasets For evaluation, we used the CIFAR-100 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), consisting of
398   50K training samples and 10K testing samples with a resolution of 32x32, and the ImageNet-1K
399   dataset (Deng et al., 2009), which contains 1.2 million training samples and 50,000 validation samples
400   with a resolution of 224x224. Since ViTs and MLPs require image patches as input, we upsampled
401   CIFAR-100 images to a resolution of 224x224 to facilitate the patch embedding process.
402   Baselines We employed both logits-based and hint-based KD techniques as our baselines. The
403   hint-based methods included FitNet (Romero et al., 2015), CC (Peng et al., 2019), RKD (Park et al.,
404   2019), and CRD (Tian et al., 2020), while the logits-based baselines included KD (Hinton et al.,
405   2015), DKD (Zhao et al., 2022), DIST (Huang et al., 2022), VKD (Miles et al., 2024), ViTKD (Yang
406   et al., 2022) and OFA (Hao et al., 2024).
407   Optimization In our implementation, all CNN students were trained using the SGD optimizer, while
408   those with ViT or MLP architectures were trained using the AdamW optimizer. For the CIFAR-100
409   dataset, all baseline models were trained for 300 epochs, whereas our PKD method required only
410   225 epochs. For the ImageNet-1K dataset, CNN-based baselines were trained for 100 epochs, and
411   ViT and MLP-based baselines were trained for 300 epochs. In contrast, we trained PKD for 75
412   epochs on CNN-based models and 240 epochs on ViT and MLP-based models. This demonstrates the
413   efficiency of our method, as it requires fewer training epochs, thereby offsetting the computational
414
      costs associated with PCA and modularization.
415   PCA Computation and Sensitivity Analysis In PKD, PCA is computed only for the teacher modules,
416   as a pre-processing step before training. Since the teacher network is pretrained, PCA is performed
417   once per module to identify the most informative masked indices, which are then used during student
418   training. Using just 25 randomly selected samples per class was sufficient for accurate index selection,
419   and with four modules, only four PCA computations were required, ensuring computational efficiency.
420
      The variance threshold (ϵth ) is set to 1 × 10−4 . To evaluate the robustness of our approach, we
      repeated the ImageNet and CIFAR experiments five times, respectively, using different random
421
      sample selections in each run. We report the average accuracy and standard deviation, demonstrating
422
      the stability of our method despite variations in feature selection.

      4.3     D ISTILLATION R ESULTS ON CIFAR-100
469
470   In addition to the ImageNet-1K dataset, we evaluated the proposed PKD method on the CIFAR-100
471   dataset. We conducted experiments with twelve different configurations of teacher and student
472   models, and the results are summarized in Table 2.
473
      On this smaller dataset, hint-based methods demonstrated weaker performance, especially when the
474   student model was based on ViT or MLP architectures, highlighting their limitations in handling
475   heterogeneous architectures. For example, FitNet achieved only 24.06% accuracy when paired with
476   a ConvNeXt-T teacher and a Swin-P student. In contrast, the PKD method consistently outperformed
477   the baseline methods, delivering performance improvements ranging from 1.50% to 4.54% over the
478   second-best approaches.
479
      Interestingly, while DIST ranked third on the ImageNet-1K dataset, DKD often achieved the third-
480   best results on CIFAR-100. This difference stems from the fact that DIST, which relaxes prediction
481   emulation via correlation mimicry, is more effective with a robust teacher model trained on ImageNet-
482   1K. On the other hand, DKD, which amplifies latent knowledge in teacher predictions, is more suited
483   for scenarios involving smaller teachers, such as those trained on CIFAR-100. Despite these variations,
484   our PKD method adaptively enhances target information, enabling the student model to consistently
485   achieve optimal performance. OFA (Hao et al., 2024) secured the second-best performance, with
      improvements over third-best baseline methods ranging from 0.28% to 8.00%.

488   4.4.1 PKD WITHOUT M ODULE - SPECIFIC F EATURE C OMPUTATION (PKD- PROGRESSIVE )
489   In this configuration, we progressively trained the student modules without using PCA-based module-
490   specific features, relying on all features instead. As shown in Table 3, while this approach outperforms
491   training from scratch, the performance gains are modest, highlighting the importance of module-
492   specific feature computation for improved knowledge transfer.
493
494   4.4.2 PKD WITHOUT M ODULARIZATION (PKD-PCA)
495   Here, we skipped modularization and computed PCA features over all data passing through the last
496   layerof the teacher network. These features were used for distillation without splitting the network
497   into modules. As reflected in Table 3, this method shows better performance than PKD-progressive
498   but remains inferior to the fully modularized approach, indicating the critical role of modularization.
499
500
      4.4.3 PKD WITH T WO M ODULES (PKD-2 MODULES )
501   We divided the network into two main modules—the last layer as one module and the rest of the
502   network as the other—and applied PKD. Table 3 shows that this setup performs better than both
503   PKD-progressive and PKD-PCA, but still falls short of the full PKD, underscoring the benefit of finer
504
      modularization.
505
      4.4.4 F ULL PKD
506
507   The fully modularized version of PKD achieves the best results across all configurations, as seen
508   in Table 3. This confirms that combining modularization with PCA-based feature computation
509
      significantly enhances the knowledge distillation process. For more experiments, please refer to
      Appendix, section B. In conclusion, the ablation results demonstrate that both modularization and
510
      module-specific feature computation are key to achieving optimal performance in PKD.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
      This paper presents a novel method for architecture-agnostic knowledge distillation, based on a
534   progressive modular representation framework. Our approach groups network layers with similar
535   feature representations into modules and aligns these representations between the teacher and student
536   networks to facilitate optimal knowledge transfer. By selecting the most representative data from
537   each teacher module for distillation, this method ensures that the student network learns the most
538   relevant information, regardless of differences in architecture. Extensive experiments on CIFAR-100
539   and ImageNet-1K demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, showing significant improvements in
      knowledge transfer and student network performance across a wide range of architectures.

       Although PKD introduces additional steps such as PCA and CKA-based modularization, several
988
       design choices address scalability:

990             • Subset-Based PCA: PCA is performed on a small subset (e.g., 25 samples per class),

995               tion of the entire network and reducing effective model size during training.

999             • Low-Rank Approximations for PCA: Techniques like randomized SVD can further reduce
1000
                  computational costs.
1001            • Approximation Methods for CKA: Nyström approximations or sketching techniques can
1002              compute Gram Matrices efficiently for deeper architectures.
1003            • Parallel and Distributed Training: PKD’s modular structure is inherently parallelizable,
1004              allowing multi-GPU setups.
1005            • Hierarchical Modularization: For very deep networks, hierarchical grouping of layers into
1006              coarse modules can further reduce complexity.

1030   G     R ELATED WORKS - A RCHITECTURES
1031
1032   In recent years, significant advancements have been made in the evolution of model architectures
1033   for computer vision tasks. This section offers a succinct overview of two notable architectures:
1034   Transformer and MLP.
1035
       Vision Transformer Vaswani and colleagues (Vaswani et al., 2017) initially introduced the trans-
1036
       former architecture for tasks in natural language processing (NLP). Due to the utilization of the
1037
       attention mechanism, this framework adeptly captures prolonged dependencies and attains remark-
1038   able performance. Motivated by its considerable success, endeavors have been made to devise
1039   transformer-based models for computer vision (CV) tasks. (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) partition an
1040   image into non-overlapping patches and map these patches into embedding tokens. Subsequently,
1041   these tokens undergo processing by the transformer model akin to NLP tasks. Their design achieves
1042   state-of-the-art performance and stimulates the creation of a sequence of subsequent architectures.
1043
       MLP For an extended period, MLP has exhibited inferior performance compared to CNN in the
1044   domain of computer vision. To explore the potential of MLP, (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) proposed
1045   MLP-Mixer, exclusively based on the MLP structure. MLP-Mixer takes embedding tokens of an
1046   image patch as input and interleaves channel and spatial information mixing at each layer. This
1047   architecture performs comparably to the leading CNN and ViT models. Touvron and colleagues
1048   (Touvron et al., 2021) proposed another MLP architecture termed ResMLP.
1049
       The most advanced CNN, Transformer, and MLP models achieve analogous performance. Nonethe-
1050   less, these architectures possess distinct inductive biases, leading to disparate preferences in rep-
1051   resentation learning. Generally, noticeable distinctions exist between features acquired by diverse
1052   architectures.
1053

1055   H     P RINCIPAL C OMPONENT A NALYSIS (PCA)
1056
1057   Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a statistical method aimed at reducing the dimensionality
1058   of high-dimensional data while preserving as much variance as possible. This is accomplished
1059   by identifying principal components, which are orthogonal vectors that indicate the directions of
1060   maximum variance (Shlens, 2014; Jolliffe, 2002).
1061
1062
1063
1064
1065
1066

1072   H.1   P ROCEDURE
1073
1074   This section details the steps involved in PCA to extract the most informative features.
1075   Data Standardization: Standardize the dataset features to have a mean of zero and a unit variance.
1076
       Covariance Matrix: Calculate the covariance matrix of the standardized data, which captures the
1077
       relationships between different features.
1078
1079   SVD of Covariance Matrix: Perform Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the covariance
       matrix. The SVD of the covariance matrix yields the principal components.


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Selecting Principal Components: Sort the singular values in descending order. The corresponding
1081   singular vectors are the principal components. Select the top k principal components to form a

1090   H.2    M ORE E XPLANATION R EGARDING SVD C OMPUTATION
1091
1092
       Consider an m × n matrix R, where m denotes the number of rows and n represents the number of
       columns. The primary objective of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is to decompose matrix
1093
       R into three distinct matrices: U , Σ, and V T (the transpose of matrix V ). This decomposition is
1094
       expressed as R = U ΣV T ∈ Rm×n , as illustrated in Figure 4.
1095
1096   - U : An m × m orthogonal matrix, whose columns are the left singular vectors of R. - Σ: An m × n
1097    diagonal matrix containing the singular values of R (non-negative and arranged in descending order).
1098   - V T : An n × n orthogonal matrix, with columns representing the right singular vectors of R.
1099
1100   H.3    E IGENVALUES AND E IGENVECTORS
1101
1102   Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are also fundamental to understanding matrix properties. An eigenvalue
1103   λ and its corresponding eigenvector v of a square matrix R satisfy the equation Rv = λv. Eigenvec-
1104   tors denote directions in the vector space that are scaled by the matrix R, while eigenvalues represent
1105
       the scaling factors for these eigenvectors.
1106
1107   H.4    SVD AND I TS R ELATIONSHIP TO E IGENVALUES AND E IGENVECTORS
1108
       SVD establishes a crucial relationship between eigenvalues and eigenvectors and the singular values
1109
       and singular vectors of a matrix. The singular values of R are the square roots of the eigenvalues of
1110   either RRT or RT R, and the left and right singular vectors are the eigenvectors of RRT and RT R,
1111   respectively.
1112
1113   H.5    R ANK AND M ATRIX A PPROXIMATION
1114
1115   The rank of a matrix R is determined by the number of non-zero singular values in Σ. By retaining
1116   only the largest singular values and their corresponding singular vectors, it is possible to approximate
1117   the original matrix R with a lower-rank approximation. This technique is valuable for tasks such as
1118   dimensionality reduction and noise reduction, and it is utilized in our approach.
1119
1120   H.6    P ROPERTIES OF SVD
1121
       - The singular values in Σ are non-negative and arranged in descending order. - The columns of U
1122
       and V are orthonormal, forming an orthogonal basis for their respective vector spaces. - The SVD
1123
       decomposition is unique, except for the sign of the singular values and the order of the singular
1124   vectors.
1125
1126   SVD is a powerful matrix factorization technique, offering a concise representation of a matrix while
1127
       preserving essential structural properties. Its applications span various fields, including data analysis,
       image processing, recommendation systems, and more (Deisenroth et al., 2020).
