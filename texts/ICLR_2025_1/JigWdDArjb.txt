009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Electro-optical (EO) images are essential to a wide range of remote sensing ap-
012            plications. With the advent of data-driven models, the efficiency of EO image
013            analysis has significantly improved, enabling faster and more effective outcomes
014            in these applications. However, EO images have inherent limitations—they can-
015            not penetrate cloud cover and are unable to capture imagery at night. To overcome
016
               these challenges, synthetic aperture radar (SAR) images are employed, as they can
               operate effectively regardless of weather conditions or time of day. Despite this
017
               advantage, SAR images come with their own difficulties: they are affected by
018
               speckle noise, complicating analysis, and existing algorithms developed for EO
019            imagery are not directly transferable to SAR data. To address these issues, we
020            introduce SAR2Earth, a benchmark dataset specifically designed for SAR-to-EO
021            translation. By translating SAR images into EO-like representations, SAR2Earth
022            allows the extensive range of algorithms developed for EO imagery to be applied
023            effectively to SAR data. The dataset consists of 18 spatially aligned pairs of SAR
024            and EO images, collected from 8 distinct regions encompassing both urban and
025            rural. We provide comprehensive evaluations, detailed model analyses, and exten-
026            sive experimental results. All codes and datasets will be made publicly available
027
               at https://sar2earth.github.io.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Remote sensing images provide the capability to observe the Earth on a large scale, making them
032   invaluable for analysis in various applications such as transportation (Ball et al., 2017), defense (Xu
033   et al., 2024), natural resource management (Kumar et al., 2015), disaster response (AlAli & Alabady,
034   2022), and environmental monitoring. However, the vast amount of data generated poses significant
035   challenges for manual analysis due to the time and expertise required. The advent of data-driven
036   models (Wang et al., 2021; Oh et al., 2023; Kuckreja et al., 2024) has enabled more efficient and
037   effective analysis of these images. Electro-optical (EO) imagery has been the primary modality for
038   remote sensing applications due to its intuitive representation of the Earth. However, EO imagery
039
      has significant limitations: it cannot penetrate cloud cover and is unable to capture images at night,
      restricting its utility in many scenarios (Seo et al., 2023; Low et al., 2023b). For instance, dur-
040
      ing natural disasters like floods—which are often accompanied by heavy cloud cover—EO imagery
041
      becomes ineffective for timely disaster assessment and response. To overcome these limitations,
042   synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imagery is employed. SAR sensors can operate independently of
043   daylight and weather conditions, providing consistent imaging capabilities. However, SAR im-
044   ages suffer from speckle noise due to the coherent nature of radar signal processing, which intro-
045   duces granular interference patterns. This speckle noise makes SAR images challenging to interpret
046   (Spigai et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2015), especially for non-experts, and complicates the applica-
047   tion of algorithms developed for EO imagery. To bridge this gap, SAR-to-EO translation methods
048   (Fuentes Reyes et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022a; Yang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023) have been pro-
049   posed, aiming to translate SAR images into EO-like images that are more accessible for analysis
050
      using existing EO-based algorithms.
051   Despite these efforts, there has been a lack of comprehensive analysis of these methods, and they
052   often remain isolated applications without standardized benchmarks. Existing SAR and EO multi-
053   modal datasets (Schmitt et al., 2018; Wang & Zhu, 2018; Shermeyer et al., 2020; Low et al., 2023a;
      2024) are limited in both quantity and diversity, often being captured within specific regions. This

086   2.1       A PPLICATIONS OF SAR I MAGERY
087
088   Numerous applications have been proposed to leverage SAR images across various domains. For
089
      instance, (Li et al., 2024) collected and labeled 100,000 SAR images to perform object detection
      directly on SAR data. Similarly, (Rambour et al., 2020) utilized spatially aligned SAR and EO
090
      images for multi-modal segmentation tasks, such as analyzing disasters like floods. Additionally,
091
      (Low et al., 2023b) focused on the classification of objects such as cars and buses within SAR im-
092   agery. Despite these efforts, SAR datasets face significant limitations. SAR data collection is costly
093   and technically complex due to advanced radar technology, making SAR sensors more expensive
094   and challenging than EO sensors. Processing SAR data is difficult due to speckle noise and other
095   artifacts, requiring specialized expertise. These challenges hinder researchers and limit the creation
096   of public datasets. Additionally, the lack of standardized datasets complicates widespread use, as
097   SAR data varies in format and resolution depending on the provider, unlike standardized EO images.
098
099
      2.2       C LOUD R EMOVAL USING SAR I MAGERY
100
101
      EO imagery cannot be used effectively when clouds are present, and to address this limitation, a
102   cloud removal task using SAR data has been proposed. (Schmitt et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2023; Xia
103   et al., 2024) introduced a benchmark dataset for cloud removal that uses multi-temporal EO images
104   along with SAR imagery to remove clouds from EO data. However, this approach still cannot be
105   used at night since EO imagery is unavailable during nighttime. Moreover, since SAR imagery is
106   used as a condition or reference from an EO perspective, dynamic objects cannot be restored due
107   to the time difference between SAR and EO acquisitions. To address these issues, the SAR-to-EO
      translation task is used, which aims to generate EO images using only the current SAR data.

      2.3   SAR- TO -EO T RANSLATIONS
128
129   To overcome the limitations of SAR datasets, SAR-to-EO translation techniques have been pro-
130   posed. For instance, (Low et al., 2023a) introduced a method to utilize SAR images by translat-
131   ing them into EO images. To enhance the performance of SAR-to-EO translation, models such
132   as Pix2Pix (Isola et al., 2017), Pix2PixHD (Wang et al., 2018), and CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017)
133   have been employed. In applications such as Amazon deforestation monitoring (Cha et al., 2023),
134   diffusion-based approaches (Rombach et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023) and generative adversarial net-
135
      works (Isola et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2018) have been widely used for SAR-to-EO translation.
      Despite the numerous SAR-to-EO translation methods proposed, there has not been a rigorous com-
136
      parison among paired methods, unpaired methods, and diffusion-based approaches. Furthermore,
137
      because the pre-processing and post-processing pipelines differ across studies, accurate analysis and
138   benchmarking have been lacking.
139
140
      2.4   R EMOTE SENSING APPLICATIONS
141
142
      Recent advancements in large foundation models and generalization models have brought signif-
143   icant benefits to satellite image analysis. GeoChat (Kuckreja et al., 2024) has demonstrated an
144   EO (Electro-Optical) image-based language model by efficiently fine-tuning large language mod-
145   els. Segment Anything (Kirillov et al., 2023) introduced a segmentation model that can be utilized
146   across any domain by training on billion-scale general vision datasets. These technologies have also
147   been applied in the remote sensing domain, being used in various tasks such as change detection (Oh
148   et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024) and building segmentation (Osco et al., 2023). However, as revealed
149   in the study (Yan et al., 2023), models based on Segment Anything and large language models like
150   GeoChat do not perform effectively on SAR images due to their training on EO images, which have
151
      significantly different characteristics. Consequently, in the context of SAR imagery, the benefits of
      advancements in large foundation models and generalization models have not been fully harnessed.

188   3.1           DATASET DESIGN
189
190   Data acquisition SAR imagery is sourced from the Capella Space Open Data Program, with a
191   resolution ranging from 0.3 to 0.6 meters per pixel. Its capability to capture detailed information
192   irrespective of weather, cloud cover, or lighting makes it reliable for continuous monitoring.
193   EO imagery is obtained from Google Earth, with resolutions between 0.15 and 0.6 meters per pixel.
194
195   SAR Pre-processing SAR images require significant pre-processing to address noise (such as
196   speckle), geometric distortions, and the wide dynamic range of pixel values. One of the critical
197   steps is translating the raw amplitude or intensity values into decibels (dB), which enhances inter-
198   pretability by compressing the dynamic range and providing a logarithmic representation suitable
199   for further analysis. The conversion to decibels is performed using the following equation:
200                                                                                                0
201
                                                                                                  σdB = 10 log10 (S · D2 )                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         (1)
202          0
      where σdB is the backscatter coefficient in decibels, S is a scaling factor specific to the sensor, and D
203   is the calibrated digital number (DN) values in geocoded format. Note that D is typically the square
204   root of the intensity value, as SAR data is often represented in amplitude.
205
      This conversion provides several benefits: it compresses the dynamic range for enhanced visualiza-
206
      tion, reduces the influence of extreme pixel values, and improves overall data interpretability, which
207   are crucial for subsequent analysis steps.
208
209
      Co-registration of SAR and EO A significant challenge in SAR-to-EO translation is achieving
210   precise co-registration between the two image modalities due to inherent differences in both spatial
211   resolution and coordinate systems. Accurate spatial alignment is essential to ensure that correspond-
212   ing features in both modalities are matched correctly. To address the georeferencing discrepancies,
213   both SAR and EO data are reprojected to a unified coordinate system, specifically the World Geode-
214   tic System 1984 (WGS84), which is the most widely adopted geodetic reference framework in re-
215   mote sensing and geospatial applications. This reprojection guarantees spatial consistency, enabling
      accurate overlay and comprehensive analysis across both data types.

234
235   To obtain detailed topological information, we utilized OpenStreetMap (OSM), classifying a total
236   of 25 distinct land cover classes across all regions. The entire dataset covers a combined area of
237   1444.91 km2 . The dataset comprises a total of 99,998 images, each sized 256x256, generated with
238   a stride of 128. For each region, the dataset is divided into training, validation, and test sets in a
239
      7:1:2 ratio, as detailed further in the Appendix A. The regions are classified as urban if residential

240
      as commercial, industrial, or retail spaces, occupy at least 5% of the total area, the region is also
241
      categorized as urban (Pesaresi et al., 2013; Esch et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2021).

244   urban and rural areas. Specifically, rural areas predominantly consist of natural landscapes, such
245
      as vegetation and bodies of water, while urban areas are marked by the presence of human-made

246
247   To assess the temporal diversity of our dataset, Figure 2-(b) illustrates the temporal differences

249   nificantly between regions, offering a wide range of temporal shifts. To the best of our knowledge,

      tions.

256
257   In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of our proposed SAR-to-EO pipeline. The SAR-
258   to-EO baseline consists of three main stages: first, a de-noising step to remove the speckle noise in-
259   herent in SAR images, as shown in Figure 3-(b); second, an image-to-image translation module that
260   translates SAR images into EO images, as illustrated in Figure 3-(c); and finally, a post-processing
261   structure that refines the generated images for enhanced quality, as demonstrated in Figure 3-(d).
262
263   4.1   D E - NOISING
264
265   SAR images inherently contain speckle noise due to the interference of radar signals interacting with
266
      multiple scatterers. This noise has a multiplicative nature and is closely linked to the signal itself.
      Since speckle noise strongly correlates with neighboring pixels, conventional methods that assume
267
      noise and signal independence are less effective in removing it.
268
269   To address this, we adopt a blind-spot method, which predicts the clean value of a pixel based
      on its surrounding pixels rather than the noisy pixel itself. Given the high correlation of speckle

270
      noise among neighboring pixels in SAR images, the blind-spot method is particularly effective at
271   distinguishing and removing noise. This de-noising process enhances image quality for SAR-to-EO
272   translation tasks.
273
274
      In our work, we compare two blind-spot-based de-noising methods: (Lehtinen et al., 2018) and
      (Zhang et al., 2023).

277   4.2     I MAGE TO IMAGE TRANSLATION
278
279   SAR-to-EO image translation poses a complex challenge, requiring the handling of both paired
280   and unpaired settings. Due to changes in ground conditions over time, achieving perfect temporal
281
      alignment between SAR and EO images is nearly impossible. For instance, while buildings and
      fixed structures remain relatively constant, elements like vegetation, moving objects, and lighting
282
      conditions vary, complicating precise registration.
283
284   Considering these factors, SAR-to-EO translation must effectively address both spatial alignment
285   and temporal misalignment. In this paper, we compare paired and unpaired image-to-image trans-
286   lation approaches. Additionally, we propose a partially-paired image-to-image translation method
287
      by incorporating objective functions, such as MSE or MAE loss, into the unpaired setting. Given a
      SAR image Isar and an EO image Ieo , the modified loss function is defined as:
288
289       Ltotal (G, Deo , Isar , Ieo ) = αLd (Deo , Ieo , G(Isar )) + βLg (G, Isar ) + γLmse (G(Isar ), Ieo ) (2)
290
291   Here, Ld is the discriminator loss, responsible for distinguishing real EO images Ieo from generated
292   EO images G(Isar ). The discriminator Deo learns this differentiation. Lg is the generator loss,
293   applied to various unpaired image-to-image translation models such as CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017)
294   and CUT (Park et al., 2020).
295   The term Lmse represents the MSE or MAE loss, which aims to minimize the reconstruction error
296   between G(Isar ) and Ieo . By leveraging partially-paired data, this loss encourages the generator to
297   produce EO images that closely resemble the real EO data, thereby reducing the differences between
298   the generated and real images.
299
      The terms α, β and γ are all hyperparameters, and in all of our experiments, we set α and β to 1,
300   and γ to 0.5.

347   refer to Appendix C. We strictly followed all the hyperparameters and settings from the original
348   code.
349
350   Evaluation metrics To evaluate the performance of the SAR-to-EO image translation task, we use
351   MAE (Mean Absolute Error), MSE (Mean Squared Error), PSNR (Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio), and
352   SSIM (Structural Similarity Index Measure) to measure pixel-level accuracy and structural similar-
353   ity. These metrics capture the absolute and squared differences between the generated and real EO
354   images, assess image quality in terms of noise (PSNR), and ensure structural consistency (SSIM),
355   which are crucial for maintaining fidelity in pixel values and structures in SAR-to-EO translation.
356   Additionally, we use FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) and LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch
357   Similarity) to evaluate the perceptual quality and realism of the generated EO images. FID assesses
358   the similarity in feature distributions between the generated and real EO images, while LPIPS fo-
359   cuses on perceptual differences based on deep feature representations, ensuring that the generated
360   images visually resemble real EO data.

      5.4   M ODEL G ENERALIZATION EVALUATION
413
414   The characteristics of SAR images vary significantly by region due to radar backscatter, making
415   it difficult to distinguish between surfaces with similar structures, like oceans and flat areas. As a
416   result, domain gaps in SAR data are often larger than in EO imagery. To evaluate this, we conduct
417   in-domain experiments by training and testing models within the same region.
418   Urban areas, with their complex structures, present larger domain gaps compared to rural areas,
419   which tend to have more uniform natural features. As shown in Table 4, rural regions generally
420   outperform urban areas in in-domain evaluations across all metrics. Notably, training on combined
421   urban regions often yields better results than training on a single region, likely due to increased
422   data diversity. However, for rural regions, training on individual regions produces better results,
423   suggesting that localized models perform better for natural features.
424   In cross-domain experiments (Urban → Rural and Rural → Urban), we observe significant perfor-
425   mance drops, emphasizing the large differences between these domains. Thus, for practical applica-
426   tions, collecting and training data tailored to specific regional characteristics is more beneficial than
427   simply expanding the dataset without considering regional uniqueness.

531
      In this paper, we present SAR2Earth, a novel public benchmark dataset for Synthetic Aperture Radar
532
      to Electro-Optical (SAR-to-EO) translation, aiming to support a wide range of remote sensing appli-
533
      cations. We systematically evaluate SAR2Earth by applying various state-of-the-art image-to-image
534   translation models and provide comprehensive benchmark results. Furthermore, we conduct exten-
535   sive ablation studies—from SAR data pre-processing to model architecture design—to offer valu-
536   able insights into the effective utilization of SAR data. Additionally, we validate the versatility of
537   SAR2Earth through experiments with GeoChat and SegmentAnything, demonstrating the potential
538   of SAR-to-EO translation in enhancing data accessibility and utility. Finally, we publicly release
539   our dataset and code to facilitate and encourage future research in this domain. We hope that our
      research will be widely utilized in tasks such as disaster response and AI for social good.

      EO translation tasks.

881   E.1   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORKS
882
883
      While SAR images may appear visually similar across regions, their actual representations can differ
      significantly due to variations in surface roughness. Therefore, regional characteristics play a critical
884
      role in SAR-to-EO translation. However, in this work, we do not leverage extra modalities, such as
885
      OSM-based label maps or metadata (longitude, latitude, ground sample distance, and date), which
886   can be used to better account for these regional differences.
887
888   The SAR2Earth dataset does provide metadata information for each image pixel level, allowing for
889
      future research to utilize this additional data. As part of our future work, we plan to incorporate extra
      modality-based regional features to enhance the performance of SAR-to-EO translation models by
890
      making them more sensitive to regional variations.
891
892
      E.2   B ORDER I MPACT
893
894   Our work holds significant implications for remote sensing and related fields. By providing a public
895   benchmark dataset, we aim to accelerate research in SAR-to-EO translation, facilitating advance-
896   ments in environmental monitoring, disaster response, and urban development. SAR2Earth is par-
897   ticularly valuable in disaster scenarios like floods, where heavy cloud cover renders traditional EO
898   imagery less effective. Since SAR can penetrate clouds and is unaffected by weather conditions or
899   daylight, translating SAR-to-EO images can provide critical information when it is most needed. By
900   translating SAR images into EO-like images, we facilitate the application of advanced AI models
901
      developed for EO imagery to SAR data, potentially maximizing the utility of established method-
      ologies.
