009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large pretrained models often struggle with underspecified tasks—situations
012            where the training data does not fully define the desired behavior. For exam-
013            ple, chatbots must handle diverse and often conflicting user preferences, requiring
014            adaptability to various user needs. We propose a novel framework to address the
015            general challenge of aligning models to test-time user intent, which is rarely fully
016            specified during training. Our approach involves training an efficient ensemble,
017            i.e., a single neural network with multiple prediction heads, each representing
018
               a different function consistent with the training data. Our main contribution is
               H Y R E, a simple adaptation technique that dynamically reweights ensemble mem-
019
               bers at test time using a small set of labeled examples from the target distribution,
020
               which can be labeled in advance or actively queried from a larger unlabeled pool.
021            By leveraging recent advances in scalable ensemble training, our method scales
022            to large pretrained models, with computational costs comparable to fine-tuning a
023            single model. We empirically validate H Y R E in several underspecified scenarios,
024            including personalization tasks and settings with distribution shifts. Addition-
025            ally, with just five preference pairs from each target distribution, the same ensem-
026            ble adapted via H Y R E outperforms the prior state-of-the-art 2B-parameter reward
027            model accuracy across 18 evaluation distributions.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Task specification—the process of communicating the desired behavior to a machine learning
032   model—is inherently iterative and rarely complete after a finite set of instructions or training exam-
033
      ples. Addressing task underspecification is a fundamental challenge in machine learning, especially
      as models are employed for increasingly complex and nuanced tasks. For example, personalizing a
034
      chatbot assistant is difficult because chatbots are typically trained to optimize an aggregate prefer-
035
      ence metric through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Siththaranjan et al.,
036   2023), using preference labels collected from a diverse set of users. This often leads to responses that
037   do not align with individual user needs since different users have conflicting preferences shaped by
038   individual backgrounds and experiences. The main challenge lies in adapting the model’s behavior
039   to suit each user based on minimal additional input. Underspecification can also arise due to other
040   factors, such as spurious correlations in the training data, insufficient training samples, label noise,
041   and limitations in supervisor ability. Our broader goal is to leverage the diverse latent capabilities
042   inside large pretrained models to facilitate adaptation with minimal additional supervision.
043   Existing methods for adapting models to previously underspecified tasks generally fall into two
044   categories: (1) optimizing zero-shot inputs, such as natural language prompts (Gao et al., 2020;
045   Khattab et al., 2023; Yuksekgonul et al., 2024), or (2) fine-tuning the model’s parameters (Houlsby
046   et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). While recent works have made
047   progress on both fronts, these approaches remain insufficient for real-time adaptation. Specifically,
048   these prior methods require substantial computational resources, involving at the very least multiple
049   passes through the model. Moreover, they are “passive” in nature, not allowing models to actively
050
      request additional information to resolve ambiguities. As a result, these approaches are impractical
      for on-the-fly adaptation at test time, where quick responses are critical.
051
052   To address these limitations, we build on recent advances in efficient ensemble architectures, which
053   enable a single neural network to represent a collection of diverse models with minimal computa-
      tional overhead (Osband et al., 2023). These architectures naturally model task ambiguity as a set

054
      of possible functions, enabling one network to capture several plausible interpretations of the train-
055   ing data. By dynamically switching between these functions based on how well each performs on
056   target inputs, the model can better align with the user’s intent. While previous work has used such
057   architectures to quantify uncertainty, we propose to use them to resolve ambiguity at test time. To
058   achieve this, we develop a method that efficiently reweights a given ensemble of models based on a
059   few labeled target examples.
060
      We propose Hypothesis Reweighting (H Y R E), a simple and computationally efficient method for
061   test-time task disambiguation. Our approach consists of two steps. First, we train a diverse en-
062   semble of models on training data, with each model initialized from the same pretrained backbone.
063   Then, at test time, we evaluate the performance of each ensemble member on a small set of examples
064   from the target distribution, which can be labeled in advance or actively queried from a larger un-
065   labeled pool. Based on their performance, we dynamically reweight the ensemble, assigning higher
066   weights to models that are more aligned with the target distribution. This reweighted ensemble is
067   our final model, which we use for making predictions on new, unseen data. H Y R E is an instance of
068   generalized Bayesian inference (Bissiri et al., 2016): starting from a maximum entropy prior (i.e.,
069
      a uniformly weighted ensemble), the procedure converges to the optimal weighting over ensemble
      members given sufficient i.i.d. examples from the target distribution. To our best knowledge, H Y R E
070
      is the first to apply this framework to adapting deep network ensembles using non-differentiable
071
      performance metrics such as 0-1 error.
072
073   We evaluate H Y R E using two ensemble architectures across over 20 target distributions, spanning
074   WILDS distribution shifts, preference personalization tasks, and benchmarks for safety and useful-
075
      ness in responses. Our findings show that H Y R E enables rapid test-time adaptation of large models
      with minimal targeted feedback, requiring as few as five labeled examples. Notably, in a preference
076
      personalization setting with 5 distinct evaluation personas, H Y R E achieves an average 20% accu-
077
      racy gain over the previous state-of-the-art model at 2B parameter scale. These results demonstrate
078   that H Y R E can effectively resolve task underspecification with minimal labeled data at test time.

087   only partially informs the model on performing under Peval . For instance, in a chatbot personaliza-
088   tion task, the training data from Ptrain may include a variety of user preferences regarding response
089   styles, while the test distribution Peval might involve a specific new user with unique preferences.
090

091

092

095   |Dadapt | = 16 and |Dtrain | > 300, 000, with the adaptation step occurring near-instantly after passing
096   Dadapt through the network once.

136   3       T EST-T IME E NSEMBLE R ECALIBRATION
137   In this section, we motivate and describe Hypothesis Reweighting (H Y R E), a simple and com-
138   putationally efficient method for few-shot adaptation to new tasks. H Y R E dynamically reweights
139   an ensemble of models, leveraging their diversity to prioritize functions most consistent with new
140   test-time data. This allows us to efficiently improve performance in scenarios where the task is un-
141   derspecified, and additional data can help resolve ambiguity. The adaptation happens entirely during
142   test time and makes no change to model parameters (as done for fine-tuning).
143
144   3.1      T HE E NSEMBLE AVERAGE IS S UBOPTIMAL IN U NDERSPECIFIED TASKS
145   Conventional wisdom suggests that an ensemble of independently trained models outperforms each
146   individual model by averaging out errors, a principle widely supported for improving performance
147   and uncertainty estimation (Krogh & Vedelsby, 1994; Hansen & Salamon, 1990; Dietterich, 2000;
148   Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Ovadia et al., 2019). The (uniform) ensemble average leverages the
149   diversity among models to reduce variance and mitigate individual model biases.
150   However, recent works have shown that in highly underspecified conditions—where the training
151   data does not sufficiently define the desired behavior on target inputs—the ensemble average can
152   be suboptimal (Teney et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). In such settings, ensemble members may
153   adopt different implicit assumptions to address gaps in the training data, resulting in a diverse set
154   of internally consistent functions. Uniformly averaging these functions effectively “blends” their
155   underlying assumptions, leading to outputs that may not align well with specific target behavior.
156   Instead, dynamically reweighting the ensemble based on alignment with the target task can better
157   capture the desired behavior and improve performance.
158   These earlier observations focused on synthetic tasks designed to highlight the shortcomings of point
159   estimates. We seek to evaluate whether these findings extend to large-scale real-world scenarios
160   where the underspecification is more subtle, such as in distribution shifts and personalization tasks.
161   Our results in Figure 1 confirm that the ensemble average is indeed suboptimal in these settings,
      achieving lower performance than a single model in the ensemble.

      This framework generalizes classical Bayesian inference by allowing arbitrary loss functions
      l(w, x), providing more flexibility than the traditional likelihood approach. Note that we recover
244
      standard Bayesian inference when l(w, x) = − log p(x|w) (i.e., the log likelihood). As shown
245
      in Bissiri et al. (2016), this flexibility is particularly useful when the underlying model is misspec-
246   ified, as is the case for underspecified tasks. Importantly, updates of this form are both consistent
247   and coherent, ensuring that this belief update is the only rational one based on the initial belief state
248   and observed data. Specifically, such updates converge to the true parameters as the amount of data
249   increases (consistency), and successive updates, whether from a single observation or accumulated
250   over multiple observations, lead to the same posterior (coherence).
251
      In classification tasks, using log-likelihood as l(w, x) often leads to belief updates being dominated
252   by outliers or extreme datapoints. By contrast, employing alternative loss functions such as the 0-1
253   error ensures consistent scaling of l(w, x) values and results in more stable updates to w. This is
254   in line with observations in Izmailov et al. (2021), which found that relying on log-likelihood can
255   cause issues on inputs outside the training distribution. Thus, generalized Bayesian inference allows
256   for more robust belief updates in real-world settings.

259   In Section 3, we introduced fast adaptation and argued that it is particularly effective when the
260   desired test-time behavior is underspecified. This section further explores this hypothesis through
261   illustrative examples. First, we demonstrate a simple method using PCA to analyze differences be-
262   tween ensemble members, revealing that the ensemble serves as a nonparametric representation of
263   task ambiguity. We then show that diverse ensembles can uncover distinct, sharp decision bound-
264   aries, which together explain aggregate behavior while capturing conflicting labeler preferences.
265   Finally, we explore the tradeoffs between fast adaptation and fine-tuning, demonstrating that fast
266
      adaptation is more advantageous when target data is limited.

302   This equivalance allows us to see the Bradley-Terry model as an aggregate of deterministic decision-

330

332   aside from performance benefits, reweighting is significantly more computationally efficient than
333   fine-tuning, making it especially suitable for large models or resource-constrained settings. The
334   computational cost of H Y R E is a single forward pass to compute predictions for each ensemble
335   member, and the cost of computing the ensemble weights (2) is negligible.

337   5     R ELATED W ORK
338   Ensembles and diversity. Many prior works have noted the benefits of ensembles in performance
339   and uncertainty representation, particularly when different ensemble members make different inde-
340   pendent mistakes (Krogh & Vedelsby, 1994; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Such findings moti-
341   vated Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models which use a gating mechanism to combine predictions from
342   different experts (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1994; Yuksel et al., 2012). Recent advance-
343   ments have incorporated MoE layers into large-scale models, offering computational benefits by
344   conditionally activating a subset of experts (Shazeer et al., 2017; Lepikhin et al., 2020; Fedus et al.,
345
      2022; Jiang et al., 2024). However, the focus of these works is different from ours, as we expect a
      small number of experts to outperform the others during evaluation. We leverage recent advances
346
      in efficient ensemble training methods (Osband et al., 2023) to train an ensemble of diverse models
347
      and propose a method for fast test-time adaptation by working in the space of ensemble weights.
348
349   Task (under-)specification and scalable alignment. Task specification is a fundamental aspect of
350   machine learning. While statistical learning theory suggests that expert labels can fully define super-
351
      vised learning tasks given infinite data (Vapnik, 1999), practical constraints such as limited data and
      out-of-distribution inputs often lead to task underspecification (Geirhos et al., 2020; D’Amour et al.,
352
      2022). Similarly, fully specifying a reward function in reinforcement learning is challenging outside
353
      of controlled environments such as games. Overoptimizing for poorly specified rewards can lead to
354   unintended consequences (Zhuang & Hadfield-Menell, 2020; Pan et al., 2022; Skalse et al., 2022;
355   Gao et al., 2023). Instead of specifying a reward function upfront, we can provide human demon-
356   strations, framing task specification as a cooperative game between humans and agents (Hadfield-
357   Menell et al., 2016). This paper introduces a new scalable mode of test-time task specification,
358   which leverages ensemble disagreements to proactively acquire information to resolve ambiguity.
359
      The standard reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) workflow for aligning
360   LLMs (Christiano et al., 2017; Wirth et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024) can
361   be understood as a cooperative game between a human and an agent, where the agent’s goal is to
362   learn the human’s preferences. Recent works have explored ways to improve the efficiency of RLHF
363   by leveraging the model’s uncertainty of human intent for active learning (Ji et al., 2024; Muldrew
364   et al., 2024) and exploration (Dwaracherla et al., 2024). Another line of work on personalization
365   methods (Jang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Poddar et al., 2024) show promise but require per-user
366   preference data, making it necessary to pre-identify user types and collect specific data accordingly.
367   We frame personalization as a special case of task underspecification, demonstrating that a diverse
368
      ensemble trained on aggregated data can capture ambiguity, which we can use to directly adapt to
      new users.

520   7       D ISCUSSION
521   This paper demonstrates that a diverse ensemble can rapidly adapt to new distributions, offering a
522   novel approach to test-time task specification. We think the design space of ensemble architectures
523   for test-time task specification, including mixture-of-experts architectures (Fedus et al., 2022), is
524   a promising direction for future work. Our results in reward modeling show that ensembles can
525   efficiently resolve ambiguities in preferences; future work can close the loop to produce behaviors
526   consistent with new preferences, for example by leveraging the parameterization of Rafailov et al.
527   (2024) which shows a 1-1 correspondence between a reward model and a language model.
528   Reproducibility Statement. This work uses publicly available pretrained models and datasets. We
529   describe experimental details in Section 6 and Appendix B. To facilitate reproducibility, we will
530   publicly release the data preprocessing pipeline, training code, and experiment scripts alongside
531   the final version of the paper. The public repository will also document all hyperparameters and
532   experimental configurations.

953   D     R EPULSION VS R ANDOM P RIORS FOR D IVERSITY
954
      A line of prior work use repulsion for enforcing diversity between ensemble members. The high-
955   level idea is to add a regularization term to the loss function that is minimized when the ensemble
956   members are sufficiently “different” according to some distance metric. For example, Teney et al.
957   (2022) uses a repulsion term that maximizes the cosine distance between the gradient of each en-
958   semble member, and Lee et al. (2023) maximizes the mutual information of ensemble predictions
959   on OOD inputs. While these techniqueshave seen success in certain settings, our early experiments
960   indicate that such explicit regularization often results in a suboptimal ensemble. The repulsion term
961   can overpower the learning signal in the training data, leading to ensemble members that are diverse
962   but inaccurate.
963   In contrast, diversification via random priors (Osband et al., 2023) provides a more balanced ap-
964   proach. The key idea is to initialize each ensemble member with a different random prior function
965   which is fixed throughout training. This introduces diversity from the start without explicitly opti-
966   mizing for it during training. This approach maintains diversity without sacrificing accuracy on the
967   training data, and the degree of diversification is easily controlled by scaling the prior functions.

1029   We highlight two known interpretations of PCA that have interesting implications for our goal of

1031   Maximum mutual information / variance after projection. PCA finds the linear projection
