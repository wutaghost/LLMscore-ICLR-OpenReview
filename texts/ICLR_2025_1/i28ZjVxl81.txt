009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            The open world assumption in model development means that a model may lack sufficient
012            information to effectively handle data that is completely different or out of distribution
013            (OOD). When a model encounters OOD data, its performance can significantly decrease.
014            Improving the model’s performance in dealing with OOD can be achieved through gener-
015            alization by adding noise, which can be easily done with deep learning. However, many
016            advanced machine learning models are resource-intensive and designed to work best with
017            specialized hardware (GPU), which may not always be available for common users with
018
               hardware limitations. To provide a deep understanding and solution on OOD for gen-
               eral user, this study explores detection, evaluation, and prediction tasks within the context
019
               of OOD on tabular datasets using common consumer hardware (CPU). It demonstrates
020
               how users can identify OOD data from available datasets and provide guidance on eval-
021            uating the OOD selection through simple experiments and visualizations. Furthermore,
022            the study introduces Tabular Contrast Learning (TCL), a technique specifically designed
023            for tabular prediction tasks. While achieving better results compared to heavier models,
024            TCL is more efficient even when trained without specialised hardware, making it useful
025            for general machine-learning users with computational limitations. This study includes
026            a comprehensive comparison with existing approaches within their best hardware setting
027            (GPU) compared with TCL on common hardware (CPU), focusing on both accuracy and
028            efficiency. The results show that TCL exceeds other models, including gradient boosting
029
               decision trees, contrastive learning, and other deep learning models, on the classification
               task.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The concept of open-world assumption in model development means that a model may not have enough
035
      information to effectively handle data that is completely different or out of distribution (OOD). When a
036   model meets OOD data, it may suffer a significant decrease in performance (Hsu et al., 2020; Hendrycks
037   and Gimpel, 2016). To handle this, model generalisation by introducing noise can be used, which can be
038   achieved easily with deep learning. However, advanced deep learning algorithms such as FT-Transformer
039   benefit from the advancement of specialised hardware such as GPU or TPU (Hwang, 2018), this type of
040   hardware is not always available to the general user (Ahmed and Wahed, 2020). These demand the user to
041   find the best way to deal with these challenges and emphasize the importance of our research.
042   While out-of-distribution (OOD) detection has been extensively studied (Lee et al., 2020a), the challenge of
043   prediction tasks for OOD data, particularly in tabular datasets, remains underexplored. Significant progress
044   has been made in OOD detection with algorithms like MCCD (Lee et al., 2020b), OpenMax (Bendale and
045   Boult, 2016), Monte Carlo Dropout (Gal and Ghahramani, 2016), and ODIN (Liang et al., 2017). However,
046   the study of prediction tasks on OOD for tabular data is limited. Tree-based classical models are known to

      4.1   TCL M AIN A RCHITECTURE
189
190   Augmented Data
191
      As a contrastive learning based algorithm, TCL works based on augmented data. TCL creates two data
192
      augmentations. Denotated as {x1 , x2 } = Aug(x). Noise was added to these augmented data.
193
194   Matrix Augmentation
195   In TCL, all original data (without slicing or splitting) is utilized as a representation. Unlike previous ap-
196   proaches such as simCLR (Chen et al., 2020) and SubTab (Ucar et al., 2021) that use slice, TCL utilizes
197   the entire original data matrix for representation. This allows for capturing more comprehensive feature
198   interactions in tabular data.
199
      Encoder-Decoder Structure
200
201   The contrastive learning architecture includes two main components: an encoder and a decoder. The encoder
202   transforms input data into a compressed representation called the latent space, denoted E : x; ω e → xe
203   where ω is parameter and e is encoder notation, while the decoder reconstructs the original data from this
204
      representation denoted P : xe ; ω p → xp where p is notation for decoder. During training, both components
      are used, but only the encoder is employed during inference, allowing efficient compression of new data into
205
      its learned latent representation without reconstruction.
206
207   Modified Contrastive Loss
208   Loss is calculated based on augmented data, not original data. TCL simplifies contrastive loss calculation
209   to enhance both performance and training speed. In contrastive learning, the loss is computed based on the
210   similarity or dissimilarity of the augmented noisy data. Since TCL deals with row-based tabular data and
211   it is a unsupervised learning, the method used is similarity. TCL aims to pull the noisy data points that
212   originated from the same data. This is achieved by minimizing the total loss Lc between representations.
213   During training, the total loss is calculated as follows:

231   5     TCL A LGORITHM
232
233   The TCL process involves several steps. First, a minibatch of N samples is sampled from the dataset. Then,
234   for each sample in the batch, two augmented views are created and added with noise. Although they came

                                                          5
235
      from the same data, both augmented data are different due to previous treatments. These augmented views
236
      are passed through an encoder network to obtain encoded representations. A decoder is then applied to
237   the encoded representations. The loss function then calculates the difference between two augmented data.
238   By minimizing this loss, noisy data is pulled together. Because TCL applies full matrix representation, the
239   process pulls noisy data row by row together. This results in generalized data for better inference. The
240   complete steps are presented on Algorithm 2 in the Appendix section.

451   8     C ONCLUSION
452
453   The choice of models for tabular datasets with out-of-distribution (OOD) data depends on the user’s needs
454   and available resources. TCL outperforms other heavier models for classification problems on OOD while
455   maintaining efficiency. RestNet and FT-Transformer perform well on many datasets, but these models re-
456
      quire more resources, which may not always be feasible. It is worth noting that TCL was trained on a CPU,
      and RestNet and FT-T were trained on a GPU. This makes TCL available for more users than other models
457
      that require more training resources. Both RestNet and TCL can be options for fine-tuning and serving as
458
      head-to-head comparison models.
459
460   Although TCL has shown promising results, there are opportunities for potential enhancement. A continual
461   learning can be proposed to improve performance. Further optimization of the contrastive learning process
462
      can be studied to achieve even greater efficiency. Additionally, there is a need to explore TCL’s perfor-
      mance on a wider range of domain-specific tabular datasets. Furthermore, it is crucial to investigate TCL’s
463
      interpretability, as this is important for many real-world applications.
