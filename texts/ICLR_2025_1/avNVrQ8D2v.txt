                                                           A BSTRACT
023
024                Spatiotemporal predictive learning methods generally fall into two categories:
025
                   recurrent-based approaches, which face challenges in parallelization and perfor-
                   mance, and recurrent-free methods, which employ convolutional neural networks
026
                   (CNNs) as encoder-decoder architectures. These methods benefit from strong in-
027
                   ductive biases but often at the expense of scalability and generalization. This
028                paper proposes PredFormer, a pure transformer-based framework for spatiotem-
029                poral predictive learning. Motivated by the Vision Transformers (ViT) design,
030                PredFormer leverages carefully designed Gated Transformer blocks, following a
031                comprehensive analysis of 3D attention mechanisms, including full-, factorized-,
032                and interleaved- spatial-temporal attention. With its recurrent-free, transformer-
033                based design, PredFormer is both simple and efficient, significantly outperform-
034                ing previous methods by large margins. Extensive experiments on synthetic and
035                real-world datasets demonstrate that PredFormer achieves state-of-the-art perfor-
036
                   mance. On Moving MNIST, PredFormer achieves a 51.3% reduction in MSE
                   relative to SimVP. For TaxiBJ, the model decreases MSE by 33.1% and boosts
037
                   FPS from 533 to 2364. Additionally, on WeatherBench, it reduces MSE by 11.1%
038
                   while enhancing FPS from 196 to 404. These performance gains in both accu-
039                racy and efficiency demonstrate PredFormer’s potential for real-world applica-
040                tions. The source code and trained models will be made available to the public.

043   1      I NTRODUCTION
044
045   Spatio-temporal predictive learning involves learning spatial and temporal patterns by predicting
046   future frames based on past observations. This capability is essential for various applications, in-
047   cluding weather forecasting (Rasp et al., 2020; Pathak et al., 2022; Bi et al., 2023), traffic flow
048   prediction (Fang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019), precipitation nowcasting (Shi et al., 2015; Gao
049   et al., 2022b) and human motion forecasting (Zhang et al., 2017b; Wang et al., 2018a).
050   Despite the success of various spatial-temporal prediction learning methods, they often struggle to
051   balance computation cost and performance. On the one hand, high-powered recurrent-based meth-
052   ods (Shi et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2017; 2019; Chang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2023;
053   2024) rely heavily on autoregressive RNN frameworks, which face significant limitations in paral-
      lelization and computational efficiency. On the other hand, efficient recurrent-free methods (Gao

079   In this work, we propose PredFormer, a pure transformer-based architecture for spatiotemporal pre-

081   integrating self-attention with gated linear units (Dauphin et al., 2017) to more effectively capture
082   complex spatiotemporal dynamics. In addition to retaining spatial-temporal full attention encoder
083   and factorized encoder strategies for both spatial-first and temporal-first configurations, we introduce
084   six novel interleaved spatiotemporal transformer architectures, resulting in nine configurations. This
085   exploration is motivated by the varying spatial and temporal resolutions and dependencies across dif-
086
      ferent tasks and datasets. This comprehensive investigation pushes the boundaries of current models
      and sets valuable benchmarks for spatial-temporal modeling.
087
088   Notably, PredFormer achieves state-of-the-art performance across three benchmark datasets, includ-
089   ing synthetic moving object prediction, traffic flow prediction, and weather forecasting, outperform-
090   ing previous methods by a substantial margin without relying on complex model architectures or
091   specialized loss functions. Moreover, our optimal model excels in performance and is efficient,
092
      offering fewer parameters, lower FLOPs, and faster inference speeds than previous models. This
      highlights its strong potential for real-world applications.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
115
116
117   Recurrent-based spatial-temporal predictive learning. Recent advancements in recurrent-based
118   spatiotemporal predictive models have integrated CNNs, ViTs, and Vision Mamba (Liu et al., 2024)
119
      into RNNs, employing various strategies to capture spatiotemporal relationships. ConvLSTM (Shi
      et al., 2015), evolving from FC-LSTM (Srivastava et al., 2015), innovatively integrates convolutional
120
      operations into the LSTM framework. PredNet (Lotter et al., 2017) leverages deep recurrent convo-
121
      lutional neural networks with bottom-up and top-down connections to predict future video frames.
122   PredRNN (Wang et al., 2017) introduces the Spatiotemporal LSTM (ST-LSTM) unit, which effec-
123   tively captures and memorizes spatial and temporal representations by propagating hidden states
124   horizontally and vertically. PredRNN++ (Wang et al., 2018b) incorporates a gradient highway unit
125   and Causal LSTM to address the vanishing gradient problem and adaptively capture temporal de-
126   pendencies. E3D-LSTM (Wang et al., 2018c) extends the memory capabilities of ST-LSTM by
127   integrating 3D convolutions. The MIM model (Wang et al., 2019) further refines the ST-LSTM
128   by reimagining the forget gate with dual recurrent units and utilizing differential information be-
129   tween hidden states. CrevNet (Yu et al., 2019) employs a CNN-based reversible architecture to
130
      decode complex spatiotemporal patterns efficiently. PredRNNv2 (Wang et al., 2022) enhances Pre-
      dRNN by introducing a memory decoupling loss and a curriculum learning strategy. MAU (Chang
131
      et al., 2021) adds a motion-aware unit specifically designed to capture dynamic motion information.
132
      SwinLSTM (Tang et al., 2023) advances spatiotemporal modeling by integrating the Swin Trans-
133   former (Liu et al., 2021) module into the LSTM architecture, while VMRNN (Tang et al., 2024)
134   extend this by incorporating the Vision Mamba module. Unlike these approaches, PredFormer is a
135   recurrent-free method offering superior efficiency.
136
      Recurrent-free spatial-temporal predictive learning. Recent recurrent-free models, e.g.,
137
      SimVP (Gao et al., 2022a), are developed based on a CNN-based encoder-decoder with a tem-
138
      poral translator. TAU (Tan et al., 2023a) builds upon this by separating temporal attention into
139   static intra-frame and dynamic inter-frame components, introducing a differential divergence loss
140   to supervise inter-frame variations. OpenSTL (Tan et al., 2023b) integrates a MetaFormer model
141   as the temporal translator. Additionally, PhyDNet (Guen & Thome, 2020) incorporates physical
142   principles into CNN architectures, while DMVFN (Hu et al., 2023) introduces a dynamic multi-
143   scale voxel flow network to enhance video prediction performance. EarthFormer (Gao et al., 2022b)
144   presents a 2D CNN encoder-decoder architecture with cuboid attention. WAST (Nie et al., 2024)
145   proposes a wavelet-based method, coupled with a wavelet-domain High-Frequency Focal Loss. In
146   contrast to prior methods, PredFormer advances spatiotemporal learning with its recurrent-free, pure
147
      transformer-based architecture, leveraging a global receptive field to achieve superior performance,
      outperforming prior models without relying on complex architecture designs or specialized loss.
148
149   Vision Transformer (ViT). ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) has demonstrated exceptional perfor-
150   mance across various vision tasks. In the field of video processing, TimeSformer (Bertasius et al.,
151   2021) investigates the factorization of spatial and temporal self-attention and proposes that di-
152   vided attention where temporal and spatial attention are applied separately yields the best accuracy.
153
      ViViT (Arnab et al., 2021) explores factorized encoders, self-attention, and dot-product mechanisms,
      concluding that a factorized encoder with spatial attention applied first performs better. On the other
154
      hand, TSViT (Tarasiou et al., 2023) finds that a factorized encoder prioritizing temporal attention
155
      achieves superior results. Latte (Ma et al., 2024) investigates factorized encoders and factorized self-
156   attention mechanisms, incorporating both spatial-first and spatial-temporal parallel designs, within
157   the context of latent diffusion transformers for video generation. Despite these advancements, most
158   existing models primarily focus on video classification, with limited research on applying ViTs to
159   spatiotemporal predictive learning. Moving beyond earlier methods that focus on factorizing self-
160   attention, PredFormer explores the decomposition of spatial and temporal transformers at a deeper
161   level by integrating self-attention with gated linear units and introducing innovative interleaved de-
      signs, allowing for a more robust capture of complex spatiotemporal dynamics.

      3.3        VARIANTS OF P RED F ORMER
259
260   Modeling spatiotemporal dependencies in predictive learning is challenging, as the balance between
261   spatial and temporal information differs across tasks and datasets. This requires flexible, adaptive
262   models that can accommodate varying dependencies and scales. To address these, we explore both
263   full-attention encoders and factorized encoders with spatial-first (Fac-S-T) and temporal-first (Fac-
264   T-S) configurations, as shown in Fig 4(b). In addition, we introduce six interleaved models based on
265   PredFormer layer, enabling dynamic interaction across multiple scales.
266   A PredFormer layer is a module capable of simultaneously processing spatial and temporal infor-
267   mation. Building on this design principle, we propose three interleaved spatiotemporal paradigms,
268   Binary, Triplet, and Quadrupled, which sequentially model the relation from spatial view and tem-
269   poral view as depicted in Fig 3(c). Ultimately, they yield six distinct architectural configurations. A
      detailed illustration of these nine variants is provided in Fig 4.

278   For full attention layers, given input X ∈ RB×T ×N ×D , attention is computed over the sequence of
279   length T × N . As illustrated in Fig 4 (a.1) and (b.1), we merge and flatten the spatial and temporal
280   tokens to compute attention through several stacked GTBst .
281   For Binary layers, each GTB block processes temporal or spatial sequence independently, where
282   we denote Binary-TS or Binary-ST layer, as illustrated in Fig 4 (a.2) and (c.2). The input is first
283   reshaped, and processed through GTB1t , where attention is applied over the temporal sequence. The
284   tensor is then reshaped back to restore the temporal order. Subsequently, spatial attention is applied
285   using another GTB2s , where the tensor is flattened along the temporal dimension and processed.
286
      For Triplet and Quadruplet layers, additional blocks are stacked on top of the Binary structure.
287   Triplet-TST captures more temporal dependencies, while Triplet-STS focuses more on spatial de-
288   pendencies, both using the same number of parameters. The Quadruplet layer combines two Binary
289   layers in different orders. We omit further detailed explanations.

      We present extensive evaluations of PredFormer and state-of-the-art models. We conduct experi-
295   ments across synthetic and real-world scenarios, including long-term prediction(moving object tra-
296   jectory prediction and weather forecasting), and short-term prediction(traffic flow prediction). The

      Implementation Details Our method is implemented in PyTorch, with experiments conducted on
300
      24GB NVIDIA RTX 3090 and 24GB A5000 GPUs, unless otherwise specified. All experiments are
301   run with a single GPU. PredFormer is optimized using the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019)
302   optimizer with an L2 loss, a weight decay of 1e-2, and a learning rate selected from {5e-4, 1e-3} for
303   best performance. OneCycle scheduler is used for Moving MNIST and TaxiBJ, while the Cosine
304   scheduler is applied for WeatherBench. Dropout (Hinton, 2012) and stochastic depth (Huang et al.,
305   2016) regularization prevent overfitting for TaxiBJ and WeatherBench. Further hyperparameters
306   details are provided in Appendix Sec A.2. For different PredFormer variants, we maintain a constant
307   number of GTB blocks to ensure comparable parameters. In cases where the Triplet model cannot
308   be evenly divided, we use the number of GTB blocks closest to the others.
309   Evaluation Metrics We assess model performance using a suite of metrics across three dimensions.
310   (1) Pixel-wise error is measured using Mean Squared Error (MSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE),
311   and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE). (2) Predicted frame quality is evaluated through similar-
312   ity metrics Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004). Lower values of MSE,
313   MAE, and RMSE, combined with higher SSIM, signify better predictions. (3) Computational ef-
314   ficiency is assessed by the number of parameters, floating-point operations (FLOPs), and inference
315
      speed in frames per second (FPS) on an NVIDIA A5000 GPU. This multi-faceted evaluation frame-
      work comprehensively evaluates the model’s accuracy, efficiency, and scalability.

502   Model Regularization. Pure transformer architectures like ViT generally require large datasets
503   for effective training, and overfitting can become challenging when applied to smaller datasets.
504   In our experiments, overfitting is noticeable on WeatherBench and TaxiBJ. We experiment with
505   different regularization techniques in Tab 7 and find that both dropout(DP) and stochastic depth
506   (SD) individually improve performance compared to no regularization. However, the combination
507   of the two provides the best results. Unlike conventional ViT practices, which use a linearly scaled
508
      drop path rate across different depths, a uniform drop path rate performs significantly better for our
      tasks. We adopt the exact regularization setting for all nine variants.

515   Discussion for PredFormer Recipe. Despite our in-depth analysis of the spatiotemporal decompo-
516   sition, the optimal model is not definite due to the different spatiotemporal dependent properties of
517   the datasets. Within this research, long-term prediction typically emphasizes temporal dependencies,
518   whereas short-term prediction relies more on spatial dependencies. We recommend starting with the
519   Quadruplet-TSST model for diverse spatiotemporal prediction tasks, which consistently performs
520
      well across datasets and configurations. Use M Quadruplet-TSST layers and experiment with mod-
      els having a total of 4M GTBs to identify the optimal configuration. Then, explore Triplet-TST and
521
      Triplet-TST with M layers to find spatial and temporal dependencies. Unlike SimVP framework,
522
      which adjusts hidden dimensions and block numbers separately for spatial encoder-decoder and
523   temporal translator, PredFormer uses fixed hyperparameters for spatial and temporal GTBs, lever-
524   aging the scalability of the Transformer architecture. By simply adjusting the number of PredFormer
525   layers, optimal results can be achieved with minimal tuning.

      5           C ONCLUSION
529
      In this paper, we introduce PredFormer, a recurrent-free and convolution-free model designed for
530
      spatiotemporal predictive learning. Our in-depth analysis extends the understanding of spatial-
531
      temporal transformer factorization, moving beyond existing video ViT frameworks. Through rigor-
532   ous experiments, PredFormer shows unparalleled performance and efficiency, surpassing previous
533   models by a large margin. Our results elucidate several critical insights: (1) Interleaved spatiotem-
534   poral transformer architectures establish new benchmarks, excelling across multiple datasets. (2)
535   Factorized temporal-first encoders significantly outperform both full spatial-temporal attention en-
536   coders and Factorized spatial-first configurations. (3) Implementing dropout and uniform stochastic
537   depth concurrently leads to superior performance enhancements on overfitting datasets. (4) Abso-
538   lute position encoding consistently outperforms learnable alternatives across all benchmarks. We
539   believe PredFormer will not only establish a robust baseline for real-world applications but also
      pave the way for future innovations in pure transformer-based spatiotemporal predictive models.

      A.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
705
706   Spatiotemporal predictive learning is to learn spatial and temporal patterns by predicting future
707   frames based on past observations. Given a sequence of frames X t:T = {xi }tt−T +1 , which en-
708   capsulates the last T frames leading up to time t, the goal is to forecast the following T ′ frames
             ′               ′
709   Y t+1:T = {xi }t+1+T
                       t+1     starting from time t + 1. The input and the output sequence are represented
                                                   ′      ′
710   as tensors X t:T ∈ RT ×C×H×W and Y t+1:T ∈ RT ×C×H×W , where C, H, and W denote channel,
711   height, and width of frames, respectively. The T and T ′ are the input and output frame numbers.
                                                               ′
712   For brevity, we use X and Y to denote X t:T and Y t+1:T in the following sections.
713
714
      Generally, we adopt a deep model equipped with learnable parameters FΘ for future frame predic-
      tion. The optimal set of parameters Θ∗ is obtained by solving the optimization problem:
715
716                                      Θ∗ = arg min L(FΘ (X ), Y)                                    (4)
                                                     Θ
717
718   where L is the loss function measuring the difference between the prediction and the ground truth.
