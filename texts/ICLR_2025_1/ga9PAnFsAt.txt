009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Embeddings, numerical representations of data like text and images, are funda-
012            mental to machine learning. However, the continuous emergence of new em-
013            bedding models poses a challenge: migrating to these potentially superior mod-
014            els often requires computationally expensive re-embedding of entire datasets even
015            without guarantees of improvement. This paper introduces Embedding-Converter,
016            a unified framework and a novel paradigm for efficiently converting embeddings
017            between different models, eliminating the need for costly re-embedding. In real-
018
               world scenarios, the proposed method yields O(100) times faster and cheaper
               computation of embeddings with new models. Our experiments demonstrate that
019
               Embedding-Converter not only facilitates seamless transitions to new models but
020
               can even surpass the source model’s performance, approaching that of the target
021            model. This enables efficient evaluation of new embedding models and promotes
022            wider adoption by reducing the overhead associated with model switching. More-
023            over, Embedding-Converter addresses latency constraints by enabling the use of
024            smaller models for online tasks while leveraging larger models for offline process-
025            ing. By encouraging users to release converters alongside new embedding models,
026            Embedding-Converter fosters a more dynamic and user-friendly paradigm for em-
027            bedding model development and deployment.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Embeddings are the cornerstone of many machine learning systems. They transform complex data,
033
      such as text and images, into a format readily processed by computers: numerical vectors. These
      vectorized representations serve as the foundation for a wide range of applications, including search,
034
      clustering, anomaly detection, classification, and information retrieval (Wang et al., 2016; Huang
035
      et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2019).
036
037   However, the landscape of embedding models is becoming increasingly complex. A multitude of
038   models are available, each with its own strengths and weaknesses (Wang et al., 2022; Li et al.,
039
      2023; Lee et al., 2024a). This diversity, while offering flexibility, presents a significant challenge:
      determining the optimal embedding model for a specific task often necessitates a computationally
040
      expensive and time-consuming evaluation process, especially when dealing with massive datasets.
041
      Consider the scenario of selecting the best embedding model for a billion text passages. Evaluating
042   each candidate model requires generating embeddings for all billion passages, a daunting compu-
043   tational undertaking (see Appendix. B for detailed computational complexities). This challenge is
044   further exacerbated by the continuous emergence of new and improved models, forcing a repetitive
045   cycle of re-embedding with no guarantee of substantial performance gains. Furthermore, the lack
046   of compatibility between different embedding models, even within the same family (e.g., Google’s
047   Gecko (Lee et al., 2024b) or OpenAI’s embeddings (Neelakantan et al., 2022)), poses a significant
048   obstacle. This incompatibility necessitates a complete recomputation of embeddings whenever a
049   user wishes to explore a new model or upgrade to a newer version, hindering efficient experimenta-
050
      tion and adoption of state-of-the-art techniques. This laborious process presents a major roadblock
      to leveraging the latest advancements in embedding models for real-world applications.
051
052   To address the aforementioned challenges, this paper introduces Embedding-Converter, a unified
053   framework designed to facilitate seamless transitions between different embedding models. Func-
      tioning as a universal translator for embedding spaces, Embedding-Converter empowers machine

      2.1     E MBEDDING MODELS
102
103   Embedding models have become indispensable tools for a wide range of applications, including
104   information retrieval, search, and various other downstream tasks. Driven by the pursuit of im-
105   proved performance, the field of embedding models is rapidly evolving, with industry leaders such
106   as OpenAI (Neelakantan et al., 2022) and Google (Lee et al., 2024b) continuously releasing new
107   and improved versions. This trend is further exemplified by the competitive landscape of the MTEB
      leaderboard (Muennighoff et al., 2022), where industrial models like NV-Embed (Lee et al., 2024a),

108
      SFR-Embedding (Rui Meng, 2024), and GTE-Qwen (Li et al., 2023) frequently update their ver-
109   sions to achieve top rankings. Furthermore, there are continual contributions from academia to this
110   vibrant ecosystem with models like General Text Embedding (GTE) (Li et al., 2023) and General-
111   izable T5-based dense Retrievers (GTR) (Ni et al., 2021), while multimodal embeddings are repre-
112   sented by models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and CoCA (Yu et al., 2022). However, this rapid
113   progress and diversity come at a cost – a lack of compatibility between different embedding models,
114   even across versions within the same family. As evidenced by the varying performance rankings
115   across datasets in the MTEB leaderboard, identifying the optimal embedding model for a specific
116   task or dataset often necessitates evaluating multiple models. This process can be computation-
117
      ally expensive and time-consuming, especially for large corpora, due to the need for re-embedding
      the entire dataset with each new model. This paper introduces a unified framework to address this
118
      challenge. We propose an efficient Embedding-Converter that enables seamless transitions between
119
      different embedding models without requiring recomputation of the entire embedding space. This
120   tool empowers machine learning practitioners to readily evaluate various models on their datasets
121   and facilitates effortless migration between models, fostering greater flexibility and efficiency in the
122   development and deployment of embedding-based applications.

125   2.2   V ECTOR SPACE TRANSFORMATION
126
127   The task of converting embeddings between different models can be framed as a vector space trans-
128   formation problem, where the goal is to map numerical vectors from one vector space to another.
129   This is a classic problem in linear algebra with various established approaches, including linear
130
      transformations (Marcus, 1971), change of basis (Shores et al., 2007), and kernel methods (Treves,
      2013). However, these techniques often assume that the target vector spaces are not predefined,
131
      which is not the case with pre-trained embedding models.
132
133   Existing research on cross-lingual embedding mapping, such as the work by (Artetxe et al., 2017)
134   and (Conneau et al., 2017), explores techniques for aligning word embedding spaces across different
135   languages. These methods, while relevant, primarily focus on word-level embeddings and might
136
      not be directly applicable to embeddings for longer text. Domain adaptation is another related
      area that investigates adapting embeddings from a source domain to a target domain. (Wang et al.,
137
      2021) and (Schopf et al., 2023) propose methods for domain adaptation in embedding spaces, while
138
      (Yoon et al., 2024) explore customizing pre-trained embeddings with labeled data. However, these
139   approaches are often tailored to specific domain adaptation scenarios. In contrast, the Embedding-
140   Converter proposed in this paper offers a more versatile solution, capable of converting any sentence
141   embedding from one model to another, regardless of the specific domain or task. This general-
142   purpose applicability distinguishes our approach from prior works and broadens its potential impact
143   across various embedding-based applications.
144
      While some research explores model compatibility in the image domain, these approaches differ
145   significantly from ours. Methods like Backward Compatible Training (BCT) Shen et al. (2020); Hu
146   et al. (2022) require modifying the training process of new models, which is infeasible in our set-
147   ting where both models are pre-trained and fixed. Forward Compatible Training (FCT) Ramanujan
148   et al. (2022), while employing a converter similar to ours, relies on unavailable ”side information”.
149   Jaeckle et al. (2023) removes this requirement but focuses on online backfilling with different data
150   requirements and objectives. Crucially, all these works primarily target the image domain, whereas
151   our method demonstrates broader applicability.

154   3     M ETHODS : E MBEDDING -C ONVERTER
155
156   This section introduces the proposed Embedding-Converter framework, designed to efficiently
157   ‘translate’ embeddings from one model to another. While we demonstrate it using text embedding
158   models, the framework is versatile and can handle various data types, including images and even
159   multimodal data, for conversion of embedding models for them. Importantly, Embedding-Converter
160   works with any embedding model, even those accessible only as prediction APIs with hidden inter-
161   nal details. This greatly expands its applicability, as many embedding models are provided solely
      via prediction-only APIs.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
163
164   The focus is to learn the transformation between two high dimensional spaces – specifically, we
165   aim to develop a method for converting text embeddings generated by a source embedding model,
166   denoted as f , into embeddings consistent with a target embedding model, denoted as g. Given a
167   text passage t ∈ T , where T represents the set of all text passages, we seek to construct a converter
168   function h such that h(f (t)) ' g(t). This function maps embeddings from the source space Rdf to
169   the target space Rdg , where df and dg represent the dimensions of the respective embedding spaces.
170   Our approach leverages unlabeled text data, denoted as D = {t1 , t2 , ..., tN }, comprising diverse text
171   passages. Notably, this method does not require labeled data depicting inter-passage relationships.
172   Any text corpus, such as the notable public ones like MSMarco (Bajaj et al., 2016) or Wikipedia cor-
173   pus, can be utilized. The learning objective is to identify the optimal converter h that maximizes the
174   similarity between the converted embedding and the corresponding target embedding for any given
175   text where the similarity measure can be defined using various criteria, including cosine similarity.
176   The proposed converter h is designed as a unified model capable of handling any text t ∈ T , irre-
177   spective of the dimensionality differences between the source and target embedding spaces. Conse-
178   quently, distinct converter functions would be required for different source-target embedding model
179   combinations. This contribution enables flexible utilization of various embedding models by facili-
180   tating seamless transitions between their respective embedding spaces.
181

      3.3   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
217
218   The proposed Embedding-Converter can be implemented using any architecture capable of map-
219   ping df -dimensional vectors to dg -dimensional vectors. In our experiments, we primarily employ
220   a 4-layer perceptron with SELU activations (Klambauer et al., 2017). As discussed in Sec. 5, a
221   Transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017) yields slightly worse performance. Model selection and
222   hyperparameter optimization are guided by the retrieval performance. That is, the model and hy-
223   perparameter configuration that maximizes retrieval effectiveness on a held-out validation set is
224
      selected. This criterion aligns with the practical objective of employing the converted embeddings
      in retrieval tasks. Hyper-parameters and additional training details can be found in Appendix. C.

227   4     E XPERIMENTS
228
229   This section presents empirical evaluations of the Embedding-Converter’s performance across var-
230   ious scenarios. We first demonstrate the effectiveness in converting embeddings between different
231
      versions of the same model. Subsequently, we assess the ability to bridge the embedding spaces of
      distinct models. While our primary focus lies in evaluating the Embedding-Converter’s impact on
232
      retrieval tasks, we also provide results on other embedding-dependent tasks, including text classi-
233
      fication and semantic text similarity (STS) (Yang et al., 2018), to showcase broader applicability.
234   A detailed comparison of the computational time and cost associated with traditional corpus re-
235   embedding versus our proposed Embedding-Converter approach is presented in Appendix B.
236
237   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
238
239   The Embedding-Converter is trained on a diverse set of text passages and queries drawn from 14
240   datasets in the BEIR benchmark (Thakur et al., 2021). We utilize a subset of the corpus data for
241   training: half of the corpus for datasets with fewer than 1 million passages, and 500,000 randomly
242   sampled passages for larger datasets (e.g., 10% of Fever, Climate-fever, and HotPotQA). To ensure
243
      adequate representation of query-side distributions, we include the entire query set from the MS-
      Marco dataset in the training data (∼ 500K queries). Consequently, MSMarco is excluded from
244
      the in-domain evaluation to avoid potential bias. We evaluate the effectiveness of the Embedding-
245
      Converter in two distinct settings: in-domain and out-of-domain. In-domain evaluation assesses per-
246   formance on the remaining 13 BEIR datasets using normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain at rank
247   10 (nDCG@10) as the retrieval metric (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2002). Out-of-domain generalization
248   is evaluated on 12 datasets from the CQADupStack benchmark (Hoogeveen et al., 2015), which are
249   entirely separate from the training data, using the same nDCG@10 metric. To further investigate the
250   versatility of the Embedding-Converter, we extend our analysis beyond retrieval encompassing other
251   embedding-dependent tasks, including text classification and STS. This evaluation provides insights
252   into the generalizability and transferability of the converted embeddings across diverse applications.
253   Dataset-specific details can be found in the Appendix D.
254

      4.6     L EVERAGING FOR L ATENCY R EDUCTION
412
413
      Thus far, we’ve focused on using the Embedding-Converter to transform corpus embeddings, a
414   particularly valuable application when dealing with large corpora. The Embedding-Converter also
415   offers significant advantages in scenarios where query latency is a critical concern. Often, deploying
416   large embedding models for online query processing is impractical due to their high latency. While
417   corpus embeddings can be pre-computed offline to mitigate latency concerns, query embeddings
418   must be generated in real-time, making latency a significant bottleneck. Consequently, developers
419   might resort to using smaller embedding models for both queries and corpora, even though larger
420   models would yield better retrieval performance for the corpus.
421   The proposed Embedding-Converter offers a solution to this challenge. By decoupling corpus and
422   query embedding models, we can leverage the superior performance of larger models for corpus
423   embedding extraction while maintaining low query latency. This is achieved by employing a smaller
424   model for initial query embedding generation and then utilizing the Embedding-Converter to map
425   these embeddings to the space of the larger corpus embedding model.
426   To demonstrate this use case, we evaluate the performance of the Embedding-Converter when ap-
427   plied to queries instead of the corpus. The results, presented in Table 4, show that query conversion
428   achieves comparable performance to corpus conversion in most cases (except the in-domain conver-
429   sion case from openai-3-small). This finding highlights the potential of query conversion to improve
430   retrieval performance in latency-constrained environments. By enabling the use of larger models for
431   corpus embedding without sacrificing query speed, the Embedding-Converter offers a valuable tool
      for optimizing the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency in real-world retrieval systems.

519   6   C ONCLUSIONS
520
521   This paper addresses the critical challenge of achieving seamless compatibility between different
522   embedding models. The lack of such compatibility hinders both machine learning practitioners, who
523   face difficulties in navigating model updates and selecting optimal models, and the overall robust-
524   ness of deployed systems. To overcome this limitation, we propose a unified Embedding-Converter,
525   capable of efficiently translating between different embedding models. In its design, we address the
526   unique challenges of learning how to convert embeddings efficiently via judicious chosen training
527   mechanisms and show that in many scenarios, the end-to-end performance with converted embed-
528
      dings can be largely preserved. This contribution can empower practitioners with the flexibility to ef-
      fortlessly transition between models, fostering greater experimentation and facilitating the adoption
529
      of improved model versions. Furthermore, Embedding-Converter can encourage a paradigm shift
530
      in model development practices. By emphasizing the importance of providing converters alongside
531   new model releases, a more user-centric approach is enabled for seamless migration from previous
532   versions. This fosters a dynamic and evolving ecosystem for embedding models, where innovation
533   and user experience are prioritized. This, in turn, contributes to a more robust and user-friendly
534   environment for developing and deploying embedding-based applications.

783   C       H YPER - PARAMETERS & TRAINING DETAILS
784
785   As the Embedding-Converter architecture, we employ a 4-layer multi-layer perceptron (MLP) model
786   with hidden state dimensions of (5x output-dimension, 5x output-dimension, 5x output-dimension,
787   output-dimension). Therefore, when converting between gecko003 and gecko004 embeddings, the
788   Embedding-converter comprises 35 million parameters. We utilize the SELU activation function and
789   apply L2 normalization to the output. Optimization is performed using the Adam optimizer with a
790   learning rate of 0.001. Training proceeds for 50,000 iterations, with batches sampled uniformly
791   from each of the 14 BEIR datasets to mitigate dataset bias and enhance coverage. This balanced
792
      sampling strategy involves selecting an equal number of batches from each dataset, with a batch
      size of 64. Validation performance is evaluated every 250 iterations, and the model yielding the best
793
      validation performance is selected. In all experiments, we utilized the Scifact dataset (comprising
794
      1109 queries, 1258 labels, and 5183 corpus passages) for validation data. The hyperparameters α
795   and β, which control the weighting of the global and local loss functions, are tuned within the range
796   of [0.01, 1.0]. In most cases, α = β = 0.1 consistently yielded strong results. For the local distance
797   loss, we consistently set the neighborhood size (k) to 100 across all experiments.
