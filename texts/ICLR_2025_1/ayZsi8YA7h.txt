                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011
               There is increasingly widespread use of reward model learning from human preferences to
012            align AI systems with human values, with applications including large language models,
013            recommendation systems, and robotic control. Nevertheless, a fundamental understand-
014            ing of our ability to successfully learn utility functions in this model remains limited. We
015            initiate this line of work by studying learnability of linear utility functions from pairwise
016            comparison queries. In particular, we consider two learning objectives. The first objec-
017            tive is to predict out-of-sample responses to pairwise comparisons, whereas the second
018            is to approximately recover the true parameters of the utility function. We show that in
019            the passive learning setting, linear utilities are efficiently learnable with respect to the first
020
               objective, both when query responses are uncorrupted by noise, and under Tsybakov noise
               when the distributions are sufficiently “nice”. In contrast, we show that utility parameters
021
               are not learnable for a large set of data distributions without strong modeling assumptions,
022
               even when query responses are noise-free. Next, we proceed to analyze the learning prob-
023            lem in an active learning setting. In this case, we show that even the second objective is
024            efficiently learnable, and present algorithms for both the noise-free and noisy query re-
025            sponse settings. This qualitative learnability gap between passive and active learning from
026            pairwise comparisons suggests that the tendency of conventional alignment practices to
027            simply annotate a fixed set of queries may fail to yield effective reward model estimates,
028            an issue that can be remedied through more deliberate query selection.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Guiding machines in accordance with human values is a fundamental principle in technology, commonly
      referred to as “alignment” in the field of Artificial Intelligence (AI). A common approach for achieving
034
      alignment involves learning a utility function (or reward model) from a large collection of human responses
035
      to pairwise comparison queries, that is, queries about their preferences between pairs of options. A notable
036   example of this is the collection of such data in training large language models (LLMs) to increase help-
037   fulness and reduce harm (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022) as part of a reinforcement learning from
038   human feedback (RLHF) framework, in which learned reward models are used as reward functions in a
039   reinforcement learning loop to fine-tune LLMs.
040
      Despite the centrality of utility function estimation from pairwise comparison data across a broad array
041
      of domains (from personalized recommendation systems (Kalloori et al., 2018; Qomariyah, 2018) to kid-
042   ney exchange platforms (Freedman et al., 2020) ), understanding of which utility functions are learnable
043   remains limited. Theoretical consideration of this problem has been largely in the context of random util-
044   ity model (RUM) learning based on complete preference information about a fixed set of candidates (out-
045   comes) (Marschak, 1974; Becker et al., 1963; Negahban et al., 2018; Noothigattu et al., 2018). However,
046   few have considered the issue of learning utility models over a vector space of outcomes, even when utility

                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      functions are linear, where the observed preference data constitutes a negligible fraction of possible pref-
048
      erence ranking data, and generalization beyond what is observed is key (a notable exception is Zhu et al.
049   (2023), which we discuss below). In the real world, we often face an infinite choice space that the traditional
050   framework fails to describe. As such settings constitute an important new horizon in social choice as it is
051   integrated into modern AI, a deeper understanding of the issue is needed.
052
      Our central goal is to initialize a comprehensive investigation of the problem of reward model learning that
053
      is central to value alignment, and particularly, the fundamental question under what conditions is sample-
054
      efficient learning from pairwise comparison data possible. We focus on linear utility models, but allow
055   arbitrary embeddings of the instance space so that the class of utility models we consider is much more
056   general (include the reward model used in LLM with neural network structure). Aside from a recent positive
057   result by Zhu et al. (2023), who showed that parameters of a linear utility function can be efficiently estimated
058   in the covariance norm when data follows the Bradley-Terry (BT) random utility model (Bradley & Terry,
059   1952), this question remains largely open. We extend their setting to the general random utility model of
060   which BT, and many other common noisy utility models, are a special case. Moreover, we consider two
061   natural measures of generalization error: 1) accuracy in predicting responses to unseen pairwise comparison
062   queries, and 2) efficacy (which we capture as ℓp distances) in estimating parameters of the utility function.
063   We first investigate the “passive” learning setting in which pairs of inputs (outcomes) are generated according
064   to an unknown distribution P, and responses to these comparison queries may be corrupted by noise that
065   comes from a given distribution Q. When there is no noise in query responses, efficient learnability of
066   learning to predict responses to unseen pairwise comparison queries follows directly from efficient learning
067   of halfspaces (Blumer et al., 1989). However, the traditional PAC model allows so much freedom of the
068   input distribution that the presence of any noise with its c.d.f. continuous at 0 can lead to an exponential
069
      lower bound on sample complexity. Nevertheless, we show that if we restrict the input distribution to be
      well-behaved, provided some mild conditions on the noise distribution, utilities are efficiently learnable in
070
      the PAC sense.
071
072   Turning next to the goal of estimating utility function parameters, we show that it is impossible to do so
073   effectively with polynomial sample complexity even when there is no query noise. This is in contrast to the
074   positive result by Zhu et al. (2023) which implies that we can efficiently learn linear utility functions when
075
      the smallest eigenvalue of the covariance matrix of the input distribution is bounded from below. A strong
      modeling assumption is thus necessary for learning in this sense.
076
077   While the “passive” setting is conventional in learning theory, the practice of reward model learning, partic-
078   ularly in the context of RLHF, allows one to carefully select the outcome pairs for which human preferences
079   are queried (for example, we can curate both the prompts, and alternative responses to these, before sending
080   the queries for annotation). We study this systematically by considering an active learning problem whereby
081
      we can select arbitrary pairs of inputs to query interactively in order to learn the parameters of a linear utility
      function. Our main results are that in the active learning model we can efficiently estimate parameters of
082
      the linear utility model, whether or not query responses are corrupted by noise. Our results thus suggest
083
      that conventional reward model learning approaches that construct the queries first, and subsequently have
084   these annotated with pairwise comparison preferences, may yield potentially misleading estimates. In con-
085   trast, dynamically generating queries (such as prompts and pairs of responses) in a way that is designed to
086   facilitate effective learning can lead to more reliable reward model estimation.

349   4     ACTIVE L EARNING
350
351   Thus far, we have noted that passive learning is typically quite challenging, especially when responses to
352   pairwise comparison queries are noisy and there is no precise noise model assumption. This has led to
353   more recent investigations into active learning in RLHF (Liu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Das et al.,
354   2024; Muldrew et al., 2024). While these models are motivated by the practice of large language models
355   (LLMs), they do not generalize to all random utility models (RUMs). In this section, we demonstrate that
356   the active learning approach in this setting differs qualitatively from passive learning, yielding stronger and
357   more efficient learnability results.
358   Our investigation falls under the umbrella of an interactive approach, which is considered a more efficient
359   framework than pool and stream-based sampling techniques (Ling & Du, 2008; Alabdulmohsin et al., 2015;
360   Wang & Singh, 2016; Chen et al., 2017). A common argument against query synthesis methods is that the
361   artificial queries we thereby generate are uninterpretable. However, there is evidence that generating highly
362   informative artificial training instances for tasks like text classification is feasible (Schumann & Rehbein,
363   2019; Piedboeuf & Langlais, 2022). Although more empirical tricks may need to be developed to apply
364
      active learning approaches practically, these can in general save considerable annotation labor. We structure
      our investigation in the same way as the passive learning case, considering first the problem of learning to
365
      predict responses to pairwise preference queries (the e1 error model), and subsequently dealing with the
366
      more challenging problem of estimating utility parameters.

374   the more challenging problem of estimating utility parameters in the active learning setting. We tackle this

      4.2   E STIMATING U TILITY PARAMETERS
377
378   Continuing from our discussion in Section 3.2, the goal in active learning is to ensure that in each step we
379   query the most informative comparison pair inside the current version space in order to reduce the size of the
380   version space as fast as possible. We first establish an efficient algorithm for this in the noise-free setting, and
381   subsequently in the case with query noise. Here, a key challenge is the ability to invert the embedding ϕ(x).
382   Since our focus is on query or sample complexity, our results hold whether or not computing an inverse
383   of ϕ (or, equivalently, a zero of ϕ(x) − v for a given v) is efficient (note that we do not need uniqueness);
384   however, this does, of course, impact computational complexity of the algorithms. In special cases, such as if
385
      ϕ(x) = x, or if ϕ(x) is affine (where an inverse can be computed using linear programming), computational
      complexity is polynomial as well, and more generally, we can in practice use gradient-based methods (such
386
      as Newton’s method) to approximately find a zero of ϕ(x) − v.
387
388   Specifically, we aim to enclose the true weight w∗ within a m − 1 dimensional hypercube with side length
      √ 2ε , such that the longest possible distance between the hypercube’s center and any point in the cube is
389    m−1               √
390   the half diagonal m−1 2

                                · √m−1   = ε. Then, by picking the center of the cube, we can ensure the error is
391   bounded as e2 ≤ ε. Thus, our algorithm runs like a binary search on each of the m − 1 dimensions. In order

393   difference ∆ϕ (x, x′ ) being (approximately) the normal vector of the hyperplane halving the original space
394   along that dimension. If there is no noise, our algorithm can bound the estimation error with probability 1.
395   If noise exists, we make each query more times, and take the majority vote as the true comparison label.

      5     R ELATED W ORK
415
      Learning Utility Models from Pairwise Comparisons: The fundamental framework that we build on is
416
      random utility model learning, in which utility information is provided indirectly through ranking (e.g., pair-
417
      wise) comparisons (Marschak, 1974; Bradley & Terry, 1952; Xia, 2019). Previously, “learning to rank” (Liu
418   et al., 2009) has been extensively explored for the application of information retrieval. These algorithms can
419   be grouped into three approaches: pointwise, pairwise, listwise. Our first learning goal is most similar to
420   their pairwise learning goal, as it involves producing pairwise comparisons of candidates, and our second
421   learning goal aligns with their pointwise learning goal, as it involves estimating the utility (or relevance de-
422   gree in the information retrieval’s context) of a single point. However, their learning inputs are usually not

                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


423
      pairwise comparisons. On the other hand, there is a branch of work on learning with pairwise comparisons
424
      (Xu et al., 2017; Zeng & Shen, 2022). Unlike our setting, they typically assume comparison data as addi-
425   tional information rather than the single data source. The limited work in the same setting all focuses on
426   a finite set of alternatives (Bong & Rinaldo, 2022; Li & Zhang, 2023; Shah et al., 2015; Negahban et al.,
427   2018; Jamieson & Nowak, 2011). Even though we can recast our model into the one studied in these paper
428   as sparse ”one-hot” features, they do not face the generalization challenge we address.
429
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback Unlike the traditional social choice community’s set-up,
430
      the recent practice of RLHF concerns itself with the generalization problem. However, their attention has
431   largely been limited to the Bradley-Terry model, which is just a special case within our broader consideration.
432   Indeed, in the passive learning set-up, the specific model has been considered by Zhu et al. (2023). As for
433   active learning, few empirical studies have demonstrated the advantage of online human feedback (Muldrew
434   et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). However, our work remains the first theoretical treatment of the subject. In
435   particular, we unify the framework of computational social choice with the RLHF practice.
436
      PAC Learning of Halfspaces With Noise: Common models of noise in random utility models have the
437   property that the closer the pair’s difference is to the halfspace defined by the linear utility function, the
438   higher the chance that this comparison is flipped. This kind of noise has been previously studied as boundary-
439   consistent noise in (Du & Cai, 2015; Zhang et al., 2021; Zhang & Li, 2021). The possibility of an arbitrarily
440   small difference between noise and 12 makes learning very challenging, even for learning halfspaces (Balcan
441   & Haghtalab, 2020). Consequently, proposals for tractable learning often rely on assumptions regarding
442   distributions of noise. The spectrum of such noise models starts from the easiest version called random
443   classification noise (RCN) (Angluin & Laird, 1988) to the other end of malicious noise (Kearns & Li, 1988)
444   and agnostic learning (Kearns et al., 1992; Klivans & Kothari, 2014; Daniely, 2016; Diakonikolas et al.,
445
      2020). Additionally, active learning has been proposed as a solution to reduce the number of samples
      the learner needs to query before approximately learning the concept class with high probability. While
446
      active learning requires exponentially fewer labeled samples than PAC-learning for simple classes such as
447
      thresholds in one dimension, it fails in general to provide asymptotic improvement for broad classes such as
448   halfspaces (Dasgupta, 2005).
449
450   Robust Parameter Estimation: Learning a linear classifier has also been a classical problem beyond learn-
451
      ing theory, with empirical risk minimisation the most popular paradigm (Vapnik, 1991), with a range of tools
      including Bayes classifiers, perceptron learning, and support vector machines (SVM). Most directly related
452
      is the connection between robustness (to input noise) and regularization in SVM (Xu et al., 2009). However,
453
      this robustness is with respect to input noise, whereas our consideration is noise in pairwise comparison
454   responses (outputs).

      6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
460
461
462
      We presented a theoretical investigation into the learnability of linear utility functions from pairwise compar-
      ison queries. Our results consider both the passive and active learning problems, as well as two objectives:
463
      the first involving prediction and the second concerned with estimation. Overall, we find that estimation is
464
      generally more challenging than prediction, and active learning enables qualitatively better sample complex-
465   ity in this case. There are several directions to extend our work. For example, one could aim to generalize
466   our results to list-wise comparisons as described in Zhao & Xia (2019). Specific to active learning, especially
467   related to LLM and RLHF, we acknowledge the challenges of query synthesis and the issue of inverting the
468   embedding ϕ(x). We believe that both theoretical and empirical follow-up work are crucial in AI alignment,
469   with active learning being a particularly important area of focus.
