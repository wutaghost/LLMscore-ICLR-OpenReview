009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel deep operator network (DeepONet) architecture for operator
012            learning, the ensemble DeepONet, that allows for enriching the trunk network of a
013
               single DeepONet with multiple distinct trunk networks. This trunk enrichment al-
               lows for greater expressivity and generalization capabilities over a range of operator
014
               learning problems. We also present a spatial mixture-of-experts (MoE) DeepONet
015
               trunk network architecture that utilizes a partition-of-unity (PoU) approximation
016            to promote spatial locality and model sparsity in the operator learning problem.
017            We first prove that both the ensemble and PoU-MoE DeepONets are universal
018            approximators. We then demonstrate that ensemble DeepONets containing a trunk
019            ensemble of a standard trunk, the PoU-MoE trunk, and/or a proper orthogonal
020            decomposition (POD) trunk can achieve 2-4x lower relative ℓ2 errors than standard
021            DeepONets and POD-DeepONets on both standard and challenging new operator
022            learning problems involving partial differential equations (PDEs) in two and three
023            dimensions. Our new PoU-MoE formulation provides a natural way to incorporate
024
               spatial locality and model sparsity into any neural network architecture, while our
               new ensemble DeepONet provides a powerful and general framework for incor-
025
               porating basis enrichment in scientific machine learning architectures for operator
026
               learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, machine learning (ML) has been applied with great success to problems in science
031   and engineering. Notably, ML architectures have been leveraged to learn operators, which are
032   function-to-function maps. In many of these applications, ML-based operators, often called neural
033
      operators, have been utilized to learn solution maps to partial differential equations (PDEs). This
      area of research, known as operator learning, has shown immense potential and practical applicability
034
      to a variety of real-world problems such as weather/climate modeling (Bora et al., 2023; Pathak
035
      et al., 2022), earthquake modeling (Haghighat et al., 2024), material science (Gupta & Brandstetter,
036   2022; Oommen et al., 2023), and shape optimization (Shukla et al., 2024). Some popular neural
037   operators that have emerged are deep operator networks (DeepONets) (Lu et al., 2021), Fourier neural
038   operators (FNOs) (Li et al., 2021), and graph neural operators (GNOs) (Li et al., 2020). DeepONets
039   have also been extended to incorporate discretization invariance (Zhang et al., 2023), more general
040   mappings (Jin et al., 2022), and multiscale modeling (Howard et al., 2023). In this work, we focus
041   on the DeepONet architecture due to its ability to separate the function spaces involved in operator
042   learning; for completeness, we discuss one possible extension to the FNO in Appendix B.
043   At a high level, operator learning consists of learning a map from an input function to an output
044   function. The DeepONet architecture is an inner product between a trunk network that is a function
045   of the output function domain, and a branch network that learns to combine elements of the trunk
046   using transformations of the input function. In fact, one can view the trunk as a set of learned,
047   nonlinear, data-dependent basis functions. This perspective was first leveraged to replace the trunk
048   with a set of basis functions learned from a proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) of the training
049   data corresponding to the output functions; the resulting POD-DeepONet achieved state-of-the-art
050
      accuracy on a variety of operator learning problems (Lu et al., 2022). More recently, this idea was
      further generalized by extracting a basis from the trunk as a postprocessing step (Lee & Shin, 2023);
051
      this approach proved to be highly successful in learning challenging operators (Peyvan et al., 2024).
052
053   In this work, we present the ensemble DeepONet, a DeepONet architecture that explicitly enables
      enriching a trunk network with multiple distinct trunk networks; however, this enriched/augmented

054
      trunk uses a single branch that learns how to combine multiple trunks in such a way as to minimize the
055   DeepONet loss function. The ensemble DeepONet essentially provides a natural framework for basis
056   function enrichment of a standard (vanilla) DeepONet trunk. We also introduce a novel partition-of-
057   unity (PoU) mixture-of-experts (MoE) trunk, the PoU-MoE trunk, that produces smooth blends of
058   spatially-localized, overlapping, distinct trunks. The use of compactly-supported blending functions
059   allows the PoU formulation to have a strong inductive bias towards spatial locality. Acknowledging
060   that such an inductive bias is not always appropriate for learning inherently global operators, we
061   simply introduce this PoU-MoE trunk into our ensemble DeepONet as an ensemble member alongside
062   other global bases such as the POD trunk.
063
      Our results show that the ensemble DeepONet, especially the POD-PoU ensemble, shows 2-4x
064   accuracy improvements over vanilla-DeepONets with single branches and up to 2x accuracy
065   improvements over the POD DeepONet (also with a single branch) in challenging 2D and 3D
066   problems where the output function space of the operator has functions with sharp spatial gradients. In
067   Section 4, we summarize the relative strengths of five different ensemble formulations, each carefully
068   selected to answer a specific scientific question about the effectiveness of ensemble DeepONets. We
069   conclude that the strength of ensemble DeepONets lie not merely in overparametrization but rather in
070   the ability to incorporate spatially local information into the basis functions.

072   1.1   R ELATED WORK
073   Basis enrichment has been widely used in the field of scientific computing in the extended finite
074   element method (XFEM) (McQuien et al., 2020; Belytschko & Black, 1999; Ballard et al., 2022),
075   modern radial basis function (RBF) methods (Flyer et al., 2016; Bayona et al., 2019; Shankar &
076   Fogelson, 2018; Shankar et al., 2021), and others (Cai et al., 2001). In operator learning, basis
077   enrichment (labeled “feature expansion”) with trigonometric functions was leveraged to enhance
078
      accuracy in DeepONets and FNOs (Lu et al., 2022). The ensemble DeepONet generalizes these
      prior results by providing a natural framework to bring data-dependent, locality-aware, basis function
079
      enrichment into operator learning. PoU approximation also has a rich history in scientific comput-
080
      ing (Melenk & Babuvska, 1996; Larsson et al., 2017; Shcherbakov & Larsson, 2016; Heryudono
081   et al., 2016; Safdari-Vaighani et al., 2015; Shankar & Wright, 2018), and has recently found use
082   in ML applications (Han et al., 2023; Cavoretto et al., 2021; Trask et al., 2022). In (Trask et al.,
083   2022), which targeted (probabilistic) regression applications, the authors used trainable partition
084   functions that were effectively black-box ML classifiers with polynomial approximation on each
085   partition. In Han et al. (2023) (which also targeted regression), the authors used compactly-supported
086   kernels as weight functions (like in this work), but used kernel-based regressors on each partition.
087   Our PoU-MoE formulation generalizes both these works by using neural networks on each partition
088   and further generalizes the technique to operator learning. In general, ensemble learning and MoE
089
      have a rich history, and we provide a more in-depth overview in Appendix A. The ensemble and
      PoU-MoE DeepONets introduced here extend this body of work to deterministic operator learning
090
      and PDE applications.
091
092   Broader Impacts: To the best of the authors’ knowledge, there are no negative societal impacts of
093   our work including potential malicious or unintended uses, environmental impact, security, or privacy
094   concerns.
095
096
      Limitations: Ensemble DeepONets, especially when using PoU-MoE trunks, contain 2-3x as many
      trainable trunk network parameters as a vanilla-DeepONet and consequently require more time
097
      to train (see Section 3.4 for runtime results and discussion); however, in future work, we plan to
098
      ameliorate this issue with a novel parallelization strategy for the PoU-MoE trunk. Further, due to
099   limited time, we used a single branch network that outputs to Rp for all our results (an unstacked
100   branch) rather than using p branch networks that each output to R (a stacked branch) from Lu
101   et al. (2022). This choice may result in lowered accuracy for all methods (not just ours), but certainly
102   resulted in fewer parameters. However, our results extend straightforwardly to stacked branches also.

120   2.1   O PERATOR LEARNING WITH D EEP ON ETS
121

      Let U Ωu ; Rdu and V Ωv ; Rdv be two separable Banach spaces of functions taking values in
122
      Ωu ⊂ Rdu and Ωv ⊂ Rdv , respectively. Further, let G : U → V be a general (nonlinear) operator.
123   The operator learning problem involves approximating G : U → V with a parametrized operator
124   Ĝ : U × Θ → V from a finite number of function pairs {(ui , vi )}, i = 1, . . . , N where ui ∈ U
125   are typically called input functions, and vi ∈ V are called output functions, i.e., vi = G(ui ). The
126   parameters Θ are chosen to minimize ∥G − Ĝ∥ in some norm.
127
128   In practice, the problem must be discretized. First, one puts samples the input and output functions
129   at a finite set of function sample locations X ∈ Ωu and Y ∈ Ωv , respectively; also let Nx =
130   |X| and Ny = |Y |. One then requires that ∥vi (y) − Ĝ(ui )(y)∥22 is minimized over (ui , vi ), i =
131   1, . . . , N , where ui are sampled at x ∈ X and vi at y ∈ Y . The vanilla-DeepONet is one particular
132   parametrization of Ĝ(u)(y) as Ĝ(u)(y) = ⟨τ (y), β(u)⟩ + b0 where ⟨, ⟩ is the p-dimensional inner
133   product, β : RNx × Θβ → Rp is the branch (neural) network, τ : Rdv × Θτ → Rp is the trunk
134
      network, and b0 is a trainable bias parameter; p is a hyperparameter that partly controls the expressivity
135   of Ĝ(u)(y). Θβ and Θτ are the trainable parameters in the branch and trunk, respectively.

254   3    R ESULTS
255   We present results of our comparison of the new ensemble DeepONet (with and without a PoU-MoE
256   trunk) against vanilla and POD DeepONets. We considered different ensemble combinations of the
257   vanilla, POD, and PoU-MoE trunks. Each of the following ensembles attempted to address a specific
258   scientific question:
259
            1. Vanilla-POD: Does adding POD modes to a vanilla trunk enhance expressivity over using
260            either trunk in isolation?
261         2. Vanilla-PoU: Does spatial locality introduced by the PoU-MoE trunk aid a DeepONet?
262         3. POD-PoU: Does having both POD global modes and PoU-MoE local expertise enhance
263            expressivity over simply using a vanilla trunk?
264         4. Vanilla-POD-PoU: If the answer above is affirmative, then does adding a vanilla trunk
265            (representing extra trainable parameters) to a POD-PoU ensemble help further enhance
266            expressivity?
267         5. (P + 1)-Vanilla: Is spatial localization truly important or is simple overparametrization
268            all that is needed? We use P + 1 vanilla trunks in this model, where P is the number
269            of PoU-MoE patches. This ensemble thus contains as many trunks as the vanilla-PoU or
               POD-PoU ensembles, but all basis functions are purely global in this setting.

468

      The ensemble DeepONet architectures all have more trainable parameters than the vanilla and POD

471

473   problem). The inference times showed a similar trend, although much less pronounced (only half
474   an order of magnitude slowdown in the 3D problem). These slowdowns are because our current
475   PoU-MoE implementation contains a serial loop over the patches in the forward pass, leading to
476   slower back-propagation over its parameters. In future work, we plan to address this with a novel
477   parallelization strategy; we believe this will speed up the ensemble architectures with the PoU-MoE
478   trunk considerably. It is also important to note that despite this increased cost, Table 1 shows that the
479   POD-PoU ensemble is more than 2x as accurate as the vanilla-DeepONet; the POD-DeepONet (and
480
      other ensembles) have errors that are two orders of magnitude worse!

      A     E NSEMBLE L EARNING AND M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS (M O E)
811
812   The key idea behind ensemble learning is to combine a diverse set of learnable features from individual
813   models into a single model (Polikar, 2012; Dong et al., 2020; Zhou, 2021; Dasarathy & Sheela,
814   1979). This technique has been used for both supervised and unsupervised feature selection in a
815
      variety of applications (Saeys et al., 2008; Li et al., 2012; Elghazel & Aussem, 2015; Abeel et al.,
      2010; Van Landeghem et al., 2010; Awada et al., 2012; Dittman et al., 2012; Piao et al., 2012). Guan
816
      et al. (2014) draws an important distinction between using ensemble learning for feature selection
817
      and using feature selection for ensemble learning (where the former category is known to overcome
818   the problem of local minima in machine learning). It is generally known that these methods are
819   more stable than single base learners (Tuv, 2006; Guan et al., 2014). While ensemble methods have
820   traditionally been studied with a statistical perspective (Krogh & Sollich, 1997; Chipman et al., 2006),
821   we focus more on its feature selection capability, i.e. our work falls in the category of ensemble
822   learning for feature selection. We use ensemble learning specifically to aggregate the global and local
823   spatial features learned by the vanilla, POD, and PoU-MoE trunks into a single ensemble trunk.
824   MoE, first introduced in (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1993), is a method in which an
825   “expert” model focuses on learning from a subset of the training dataset. These models can be
826   support vector machines (Lima et al., 2007; Lima et al., 2009; Collobert et al., 2001), Gaussian
827   Processes (Ng & Deisenroth, 2014; Yuan & Neubauer, 2008; Gadd et al., 2020), and neural networks.
828   Similar to ensemble learning, the MoE idea has also proven to be very successful in diverse ML
829   applications (Masoudnia & Ebrahimpour, 2014; Yuksel et al., 2012; Chen et al., 2022). Most recently,
830   MoE has also been used in physics-informed learning; Chalapathi et al. (2024) uses MoE across
831   the spatial domain with non-overlapping patches to decompose global physical hard constraints into
832
      multiple local constraints. Our PoU-MoE trunk uses a similar methodology where it has individual
      expert trunk networks on each patch in the domain, albeit with overlapping patches. However, instead
833
      of learning physical constraints, we let our experts learn spatially local basis functions (see Appendix
834
      G for further discussion on this spatial locality).
835

868   C    S UBOPTIMAL ENSEMBLE TRUNK ARCHITECTURES
869
      We document here our experience with other ensemble trunk architectures. We primarily made the
870
      following two other attempts:
871
872   A residual ensemble: Our first attempt was to combine the different trunk outputs using weighted
873   residual connections with trainable weights, then activate the resulting output, then pass that activated
874
      output to a dense layer. For instance, given two trunks τ 1 and τ 2 , this residual ensemble trunk would
      be given by
875
876                               τ̂ res = W σ (tanh(w1 )τ 1 + tanh(w2 )τ 2 ) + b,                         (17)
877   where σ was some nonlinear activation, W was some matrix of weights, and b a bias. We also
878   attempted using the sigmoid instead of the tanh. The major drawback of this architecture was that the
879   output dimensions of the individual trunks had to match, i.e., p1 = p2 to add the results (otherwise,
880
      some form of padding would be needed). We found that this architecture indeed outperformed the
      vanilla-DeepONet in some of our test cases, but required greater fine tuning of the output dimension
881
      p. In addition, we found that this residual ensemble failed to match the accuracy of our final ensemble
882
      architecture.
883   An activated ensemble: Our second attempt resembled our final architecture, but had an extra
884   activation function and weights and biases. This activated ensemble trunk would be given by
885
                                              τ̂ act = W σ ([τ1 , τ2 ]) + b.                               (18)
886
      This architecture allowed for different p dimensions (columns) in τ1 and τ2 . However, we found
887
      that this architecture did not perform well when the POD trunk was one of the constituents of
888
      the ensemble; this is likely because it is suboptimal to activate a POD trunk, which is already a
889   data-dependent basis. There would also be no point in moving the activation function onto the other
890   ensemble trunk constituents, since these are always activated if they are not POD trunks. Finally,
891   though W and b allowed for a trainable combination rather than simple stacking, they did not offer
892   greater expressivity over simply allowing a wider branch to combine these different trunks. We found
893   that this architecture also underperformed our final reported architecture.

       E.3 PARTITIONING
973
974
       The PoU-MoE trunk has certain hyperparameters that must be chosen. In our experiments, to
       maximize accuracy, we chose the patch size and the number of patches that produced the smallest
975
       possible patches and the smallest number of patches, while simultaneously seeking that the domain
976
       was covered and ensuring that the patches did not extend too far outside the domain boundary.
977    Coincidentally, this strategy coincided with placing individual patches over regions of high spatial
978    error in the vanilla-DeepONet solution (effectively, patches over "features of interest"). In the
979    reaction-diffusion examples, even though we used uniform patch radii, we ensured that the patches
980    did not overlap horizontally over line of the discontinuity. This choice combined with the use of
981    ReLU activation ensured that we resolved that discontinuity better than vanilla-DeepONet; we believe
982    this is one of the unique strengths of the PoU-MoE approach. Currently, we use the same trunk
983    architectures on each patch as in the vanilla-DeepONet. In future work, adaptive patch selection
984    strategies (such as making the patch centers and radii trainable or enforcing soft constraints on them
985
       as part of the loss function) can be used to automate determining patch placement and patch size.
       Furthermore, the patch shape can be changed depending on the problem domain; elongated/ellipsoidal
986
       patches can be used in narrower regions where spherical patches are not well suited.
