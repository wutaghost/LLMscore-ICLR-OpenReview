011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces MutualNeRF, a framework enhancing Neural Radiance Field
014            (NeRF) performance under limited samples using Mutual Information Theory.
015            While NeRF excels in 3D scene synthesis, challenges arise with limited data and
016
               existing methods that aim to introduce prior knowledge lack theoretical support in a
               unified framework. We introduce a simple but theoretically robust concept, Mutual
017
               Information, as a metric to uniformly measure the correlation between images, con-
018
               sidering both macro (semantic) and micro (pixel) levels. For sparse view sampling,
019            we strategically select additional viewpoints containing more non-overlapping
020            scene information by minimizing mutual information without knowing the ground
021            truth images beforehand. Our framework employs a greedy algorithm, offering a
022            near-optimal solution for this task. For few-shot view synthesis, we maximize the
023            mutual information between inferred images and ground truth, expecting inferred
024            images to gain more relevant information from known images. This is achieved
025            by incorporating efficient, plug-and-play regularization terms. Experiments under
026            limited samples show consistent improvement over state-of-the-art baselines in
027
               different settings, affirming the efficacy of our framework.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) (Neural Radiance Fields) is an advanced technique in computer
033
      graphics and computer vision that enables highly detailed and photorealistic 3D reconstructions of
      scenes from 2D images (Zhang et al., 2020; Park et al., 2021; Pumarola et al., 2021). It represents a
034
      scene as a 3D volume, where each point in the volume corresponds to a 3D location and is associated
035
      with a color and opacity. The key idea behind NeRF is to learn a deep neural network that can
036   implicitly represent this volumetric function, allowing the synthesis of novel views of the scene from
037   arbitrary viewpoints.
038
039
      Although NeRF can synthesize high-quality images, it often relies on a large amount of high-quality
      training data (Yu et al., 2021b). The performance of NeRF drastically decreases when the number
040
      of training data is reduced. To mitigate this, existing strategies include adding new samples to the
041
      dataset and integrating regularization terms to introduce prior knowledge. For adding new samples,
042   ActiveNeRF (Pan et al., 2022) aims to supplement the existing training set with newly captured
043   samples based on an active learning scheme. It incorporates uncertainty estimation into a NeRF model
044   and selects the samples that bring the most information gain. However, its reliance on the variance
045   shift between prior and posterior distributions as a metric for information gain is somewhat speculative
046   and can lead to unreliable outcomes. Regarding regularization, a plethora of studies (Niemeyer et al.,
047   2022; Yang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2021b; Jain et al., 2021) have explored the integration of prior or
048   domain-specific knowledge to facilitate high-quality novel view synthesis and enhance generalization
049   capabilities, even with limited training data. However, many of these methods lack theoretical support,
050
      hindering their explanation and optimization within a unified framework.
051   Confronting challenges in the few-shot scenarios, we introduce a theoretically robust and compu-
052   tationally efficient strategy addressing two pivotal tasks: sparse view sampling and few-shot view
053   synthesis. Sparse view sampling targets acquiring training images from a selection of candidate
      views without knowing their ground truth images. Our strategy intuitively emphasizes minimizing

086   We approach mutual information from a two-fold perspective: the macro perspective and the micro
087   perspective. The macro perspective focuses on the correlation in semantic features, particularly
088   employing the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) method for semantic space distance, while the micro
089   perspective in pixel space deals with the decomposition of relative information between images
090   based on ray differences. To ensure feasibility and computational efficiency, pixel space distance
091   is correlated with the Euclidean distance between camera positions and RGB color differences.
092
      Furthermore, we take into account multiple training set images for unknown scenes, introducing
      mutual information for multiple images.
093
094   Leveraging mutual information as the metric, our novel algorithmic framework can tackle board
095   challenges in sparse view sampling by introducing new samples with less mutual information, and
096   few-shot view synthesis by adding new regularization terms to increase the mutual information
097   between the inferred images and the ground truth.
098   In sparse view sampling, the task is to select a subset of images from a candidate view set with
099   unknown ground truth to supplement the training process. Ground truth is revealed only after
100   selection, following the active learning framework. Our strategy focuses on minimizing redundancy
101   in the selected views to maximize information gain. We introduce a computationally efficient greedy
102   algorithm with a look-ahead strategy, which functions as a near-optimal solution. This algorithm
103   iteratively selects images based on their contribution to unexplored information. The selection criteria
104
      combine semantic space distances, as derived from CLIP, with pixel space distance, calculated using
      the Euclidean distance between camera positions.
105
106   For few-shot view synthesis, the task is to directly train a NeRF model with limited and fixed training
107   samples. we aim to develop efficient, plug-and-play regularization terms for the training procedure.
      The objective is to maximize the mutual information between the training images and randomly

      rendered images, expecting inferred images to gain more relevant information from known images.
109   We assess semantic space distance by CLIP as the macro regularization term. As camera position
110   is invariant to the parameter of the NeRF, we utilize a computationally efficient metric dependent

      Finally, we have experimentally validated our conclusions. In sparse view sampling, following the
114

115   the information gain brought by our sampling strategy. The experiments demonstrate that our strategy
116   achieves the best performance with the introduction of the same number of new viewpoints. For
117   few-shot novel view synthesis, we compare our designed regularization terms with state-of-the-
118   art baselines, showing consistent improvements across three datasets. An ablation study further
119   analyzes the contribution of each term. Remarkably, the mutual information metric, intuitive and
120   straightforward yet theoretically robust, proves to efficiently guide the NeRF process at both input
121   and output stages with simple quantitative computation in our framework.

123   2    R ELATED W ORK
124
125   Mutual Information Mutual information is a basic concept in information theory and it has
126   many applications in machine learning. Oord et al. (2018) starts the research for unsupervised
127   representation learning train feature extractors by maximizing an estimate of the mutual information
128   (MI) between different views of the data. This work has been expanded in various directions, including
129   the explanation of this principle (Tschannen et al., 2019), the experiments improvement in more
130   datasets (Henaff, 2020), and the application of contrastive learning to the multiview setting (Tian
131
      et al., 2020). While the primary focus of their work is on unsupervised learning tasks, our research is
      centered on supervised learning with sparse samples. However, the concept of leveraging information
132
      from unlabeled data is also adopted in our approach.
133
134
      Active Learning Active learning (Settles, 2009) is a special case of machine learning in which a
135   learning algorithm can actively seek user (or another information source) input to label new data points
136   with desired outputs. It has been extensively explored across diverse computer vision tasks (Yi et al.,
137   2016; Sener & Savarese, 2017; Fu et al., 2018; Zolfaghari Bengar et al., 2019). ActiveNeRF (Pan
138   et al., 2022) is the first approach to incorporate an active learning scheme into the NeRF optimization
139   pipeline. We adopt this active learning pipeline for sparse view sampling. In contrast to ActiveNeRF,
140   which primarily focuses on modeling information gain through uncertainty reduction, our approach
141   explores mutual information from both macro and micro perspectives.
142
143   Few-shot Novel View Synthesis NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) has become one of the most
144   important methods for synthesizing new viewpoints in 3D scenes (Xiangli et al., 2021; Fridovich-Keil
145
      et al., 2022; Takikawa et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2021a; Tancik et al., 2022; Hedman et al., 2021).
      A growing number of recent works have studied few-shot novel view synthesis via NeRF (Wang
146
      et al., 2021; Martin-Brualla et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022;
147
      Wang et al., 2023). First, diffusion-model-based methods use generative inference as supplementary
148   information. SparseFusion (Zhou & Tulsiani, 2022) distills a 3D consistent scene representation
149   from a view-conditioned latent diffusion model. Second, some methods additionally extrapolate
150   the scene’s geometry and appearance to a new viewpoint. DietNeRF (Jain et al., 2021) introduces
151   semantic consistency loss between observed and unseen views. Third, some methods utilize regular-
152   ization terms to avoid overfitting and introduce prior knowledge. RegNeRF (Niemeyer et al., 2022)
153   regularizes the geometry and appearance of patches from unobserved viewpoints. FreeNeRF (Yang
154   et al., 2023) proposes to regularize the input frequency range. However, many methods lack a unified
155   theoretical foundation, making it challenging to provide a comprehensive explanation or optimize
156
      better. Our goal is to propose a generic framework with interpretable metrics to address this gap.

      In this section, we outline our principal framework for the algorithm’s design. As we need to
219   choose training images instead of rays, we denote R as the set of images in this section. Given
220   the limited number of training samples, it’s essential to select a sparse but information-rich subset,
221   Rs ⊂ R, to capture various details of scenes and generalization well in other views of the scenes or
222   object. Therefore, to establish a criterion for assessing the adequacy of an image in capturing scene
223   information, we draw upon principles from information theory to devise an appropriate metric.
224
      In the domain of information theory, mutual information quantifies the reduction in uncertainty of
225   one variable given the knowledge of another. This concept aligns with our objectives in the context
226   of NeRF. Specifically, we utilize the information from a known image, R, which includes a subset of
227   views, to infer properties about an unknown image, R.
228   Definition 1 (Mutual Information). Mutual information measures dependencies between random
229   variables. Given two random variables R and R, it can be understood as how much knowing R
230   reduces the uncertainty in R or vice versa. Formally, the mutual information between R and R is:
231
                               I(R, R) = H(R) − H(R|R) = H(R) − H(R|R).
232

      where H(R) represents the information of the random variables R, H(R|R) represents the relative
235
      uncertainty to infer R if we know R.
236   In the context of NeRF, H(R) represents the information of the image R, and H(R|R) represents the
237
      relative uncertainty to infer unknown R based on known image R. Our objective is to quantify the
238   mutual information I(R, R) and deduce information about one image from another to a certain degree.
239   Assuming symmetry among all images and an equal number of rays, we reasonably hypothesize that
240   the inherent information content of each image H(R) is equal. Consequently, we aim to maximize
241   the conditional information H(R|R) and H(R|R).

292   5     S PARSE V IEW S AMPLING
293
294   Sparse view sampling, proposed by ActiveNeRF (Pan et al., 2022), is an active learning scheme
295   designed to enhance the quality of NeRF by strategically selecting additional viewpoints. In this
296   setting, we begin with a limited number of training images, and a candidate set of viewpoints for
297   which we do not possess the corresponding ground truth images. It is only after a viewpoint is
298   selected that we acquire its ground truth image, subsequently transferring it from the candidate to
299
      the training set. By analyzing the shortcomings of initial images, we strategically select additional
      viewpoints and then get the corresponding images to improve the NeRF model’s synthesis quality.
300
      For instance, if constrained to capture only three images of the Eiffel Tower, we are presented with
301
      various potential viewpoints from the sky or ground. Sparse view sampling involves selecting the
302   most informative viewpoints based on the initial images.
303
304   Our framework selects an informative subset of views by minimizing mutual information without
305
      knowing the ground truth images beforehand. It stems from the observation that lower mutual
      information reflects reduced redundancy between views. For example, highly similar images exhibit
306
      high mutual information, indicating redundancy if both are selected. We aim to design an algorithm
307
      that intelligently chooses images based solely on the existing images and the candidate view positions.
308

310   need to choose the subset of images Rs . We represent Ri̸=j as all the images in R without the image
311
      Rj , then our goal can be formally described as minimizing the mutual information for Ri̸=j and Rj .

361   Setup Our greedy algorithm in Section 5.1 follows a ’train-render-evaluate-pick’ scheme similar to
362   that in Active Learning (Pan et al., 2022): 1) start by training a NeRF model with initial observations,
363   2) render images from candidate views and evaluate them to select valuable ones, 3) train the NeRF
364   model with the newly acquired ground-truth images corresponding to these selected views, then
365   repeat to step 2. Compared to ActiveNeRF, we modify the evaluation metric in step 2 as minimizing
366   mutual information, considering both semantic space distance and pixel space distance discussed in
367   Section 4.
368   Design By Assumption 1 and Lemma 1, we identify a viewpoint that exhibits both low semantic
369   similarity measured by CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) (large semantic space distance) and a considerable
370   distance in camera positions (large pixel space distance). If we consider only camera pose, furthest
371   view sampling (FVS) is optimal. However, incorporating semantic constraints necessitates balancing
372   these two criteria. We propose a sequential approach: first prioritize semantics to select a subset
373   from candidates, then evaluate based on camera pose (S→P), or vice versa (P→S). This strategy
374
      navigates the tradeoff without a tricky balance hyperparameter. The technical appendix provides
      more discussions.

      properties. PSNR assesses the absolute error between pixels, emphasizing pixel-wise comparison in
405
      a micro way. LPIPS quantifies perceptual similarity, capturing more global visual differences in a
406   macro way.
407
408   Results We demonstrate the performance of our sampling strategy on the Blender dataset compared
409
      to baseline approaches in Table 1 and Figure 3. Our strategy outperforms baselines in view synthesis
      quality. Our method, which considers both the semantic space distance between visible and invisible
410
      views and a tendency towards uniform sampling, provides better sampling guidance under a limited
411
      input budget. When prioritizing semantic space distance before pixel space distance (Ours (S→P)),
412   we observe lower LPIPS scores (-17.6%/-9.2%) and higher SSIM scores (+2.4%/+2.3%), aligning
413   more closely with human perception. Conversely, prioritizing pixel space distance first (Ours (P→S))
414   yields higher PSNR scores (+7.3%/+5.9%), reflecting differences in raw pixel values. In addition, as
415   shown in Table 2, our sequential method can get better results than simultaneous method.
416
      Ablation We conduct ablation studies using only semantic space distance or only pixel distance. As
417
      shown by NeRF + FVS(Pixel) and NeRF + Semantic in Table 1, considering either factor improves
418   performance compared to the naive method. However, combining both metrics yields even better
419   results, as seen in Ours (S→P) and Ours (P→S).

450   In our framework, maximizing the mutual information between images involves minimizing both
451   semantic space distance and pixel space distance. For the former, we can use CLIP (Radford et al.,
452   2021) as a macro regularization. However, camera position cannot be used to analyze pixel space
453   distance as in Section 5 because it is independent of NeRF parameters and cannot be optimized. Thus,
454   we need a new metric that depends on both camera position and network parameters to serve as the

      7    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
531
532   This paper presents a novel NeRF framework under limited samples using Mutual Information
533   Theory. We introduce mutual information from both macro (semantic space) and micro (pixel space)
534   perspectives in different settings. In sparse view sampling, we employ a greedy algorithm to minimize
535   mutual information. In few-shot view synthesis, we utilize plug-and-play regularization terms to
536
      maximize it. Experiments across different settings validate the robustness of our framework.
537   Our framework has some limitations, particularly in terms of comparisons with the diffusion-based
538   methods. We were unable to include these comparisons due to the lack of open-source code or
539   differing dataset settings, which are detailed in the appendix. Future work should aim to incorporate
      more baseline methods and explore additional variations within our framework.

      evaluation.

947   to study the pre-training or per-scene fine-tuning setting in a few-shot neural rendering scenario.
948
      Unlike FreeNeRF, we do not require pre-training. We follow (Niemeyer et al., 2022) to optimize

949
      55, 63, 82, 103, 110, and 114. In each scan, the images with the following IDs (counting from “0”)

951   26, 27, 29, 30, 31, 32, 33, 34, 35, 41, 42, 43, 45, 46, 47] serve as the novel views for evaluation.
952   According to the FreeNeRF, masks of the DTU dataset do not always help improve PSNR and SSIM
953   and sometimes the PSNR score in a specific scene drops a lot. For a fair comparison, we train one
954   model for one scene to produce the results in the object and full-image setting at the same time.

      LLFF Dataset: The LLFF dataset (Mildenhall et al., 2019) is a forward-facing dataset containing

      eight scenes. Adhering to (Mildenhall et al., 2020; Niemeyer et al., 2022), we use every 8th image as
957   the novel views for evaluation and evenly sample the input views from the remaining views.

1010   B.2.3      L IMITATIONS ON BASELINES
1011
       FreeNeRF is a strong baseline that achieves state-of-the-art performance compared to methods using
1012
       priors from diffusion models across many datasets. We get this conclusion from the experiment
1013   results of ReconFusion (Wu et al., 2023). Therefore, it is worthwhile to continue our comparison
1014   between our method and some diffusion-based methods like SparseFusion (Zhou & Tulsiani, 2022)
1015   or ReconFusion (Wu et al., 2023).
1016
       SparseFusion’s evaluation is currently limited to the CO3D dataset (Reizenstein et al., 2021), and
1017
       it lacks performance data on three popular and classical datasets which we have used to keep the
1018
       same as FreeNeRF: the Blender dataset, the DTU dataset and the LLFF dataset. Fair evaluations of
1019   SparseFusion on these datasets are absent, and addressing this gap would require significant additional
1020   time, which might divert from our primary research focus. Nonetheless, the datasets we employ are
1021   robust and widely accepted in NeRF research, providing sufficient support for our experiments with
1022   numerous baseline performances available for reference.
1023
       Additionally, the lack of open-source code for ReconFusion limits our ability to apply custom
1024
       regularization terms or conduct meaningful comparisons. Future work should aim to incorporate
1025   more new baseline methods and explore additional variations within our framework.
