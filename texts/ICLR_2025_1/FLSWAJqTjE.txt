009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011             Transformer-based Large Language Models (LLMs) traditionally rely on final-layer loss
012             for training and final-layer representations for predictions, potentially overlooking the pre-
013             dictive power embedded in intermediate layers. Surprisingly, we find that focusing training
014             efforts on these intermediate layers can yield training losses comparable to those of final
015             layers, with complementary test-time performance. We introduce a novel tuning frame-
016             work, Mixture-of-Depths (MoD), which trains late layers as ensembles contributing to the
017
                final logits through learned routing weights. With the auxiliary distillation loss and addi-
                tional normalization modules, we ensure that the outputs of the late layers adapt to lan-
018
                guage modeling. Our MoD framework, which can be integrated with any existing tuning
019
                method, shows consistent improvement on various language modelling tasks. Further-
020             more, by replacing traditional trainable modules with MoD, our approach achieves similar
021             performance with significantly fewer trainable parameters, demonstrating the potential of
022             leveraging predictive power from intermediate representations during training.

025   1       I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are predominantly Transformer-based, processing sequences of input
      tokens by representing them as vectors and transforming them through multiple layers of transformers
028
      (Vaswani et al., 2017). Prior research has demonstrated the intermediate hidden states can carry meaningful
029
      information (Li et al., 2024), and leveraging these hidden states during decoding can improve trustworthi-
030   ness (Chuang et al., 2024) and reasoning capabilities (O’Brien & Lewis, 2023). However, how to effectively
031   utilize these intermediate layers during training remains unexplored. While each layer transformation cre-
032   ates new token representations added to the residual stream, only the final layer representations are used to
033   obtain training loss. Consequently, loss minimization directly optimizes these final representations, leaving
034   hidden representations optimized only implicitly, thereby obscuring their potential predictive power.
035
      In this work, we investigate the predictive power of the late layers,1 which have proven to be task-aware
036   in early exiting language models (Schuster et al., 2022; Din et al., 2023). We begin by training models
037   on late layers by applying the pretrained language model heads to each layer’s output to calculate the loss.
038   Our initial observations indicate that the training loss curves for the later layers started at higher values but
039   eventually converged to similar levels, aided by simple distillation losses with respect to the output of the
040   last layer, even without incorporating the weights of the subsequent layers (Figure 1). Figure 2 demonstrates
041   that the trained “models” at these layers can even provide complementary evaluation results. These findings
042   suggest that the late layers possess significant predictive potential. Given the overparameterization typical
043   in large language models (Gao et al., 2023), the model can adapt effectively to downstream tasks even with
044
      fewer parameters.

046   literatures (Din et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2023).

082   2   M IXTURE - OF -D EPTHS
083
084   Recent language models consist of an embedding layer, n stacked transformer layers L, and an affine layer
085   ϕ(·) for predicting the next-word distribution, often referred to as the language model head (Geva et al.,
086   2022; Luo & Specia, 2024). We aim to identify a layer range k, where the last k layers carry higher-level
087   task-aware information and can map hidden states to meaningful predictive logits (Belrose et al., 2023).
088   For an LLM with n layers, we define the set of the last k layers as K = {Ln−k+1 , Ln−k+2 , . . . , Ln }. As
089   shown in Figure 1, late layers exhibit learning loss curves similar to the final layer, indicating their task
090   informativeness.
091   Additionally, metrics extracted from the inference process can dynamically determine this range. For exam-
092   ple, Chuang et al. (2024) use the Jensen-Shannon Divergence between early and final layers as a distance
093   measurement to decode in contrastively, ensuring that selected layers include more task-related knowledge.

      2.1   E ARLY-E XIT FOR L ATE L AYERS
115
116   The idea of applying language heads directly to the hidden states of the middle layers, known as early exit
117   (Teerapittayanon et al., 2016; Elbayad et al., 2020; Schuster et al., 2022), has proven effective even without
118   a special training process (Kao et al., 2020). The residual connections (He et al., 2016) in transformer
119   layers allow hidden representations to evolve gradually, enabling the formation of task-aware representations
120   without abrupt changes.
121   Given a sequence of tokens {x1 , x2 , . . . , xt−1 }, the embedding layer first converts the tokens into a sequence
122                      (0)         (0)               (0)
      of vectors H0 = {h1 , . . . , ht−1 }, where ht ∈ Rd and d is the hidden state dimension. This sequence H0
123   is then processed successively by each transformer layer, with the output of the j-th layer denoted as Hj .
124   The vocabulary head ϕ(·) then outputs the logits ℓt of the next token xt over the vocabulary set V:
125                                                                (N )
126                                      ℓ(xt | x<t ) = ϕ Np (ht )) xt , xt ∈ V.
127   Here, Np is the pre-trained normalization module before the vocabulary head. This method is often con-
128   sidered a form of logit lens (Nostalgebraist, 2020), which uses the vocabulary head to probe into inner
129   representations. However, the trainable predictive power of these representations remains unexplored. In
130   §2.2, we show how to combine the train-time predictive power of late layers with final layer logits.

      2.3   L ATE L AYERS A DAPTATION BY N ORMALIZATION AND D ISTILLATION
185
186   Directly combining the logits of late layers using the LM head can result in worse training loss at the start of
187   tuning (Figure 1). Previous works (Belrose et al., 2023) have attempted to learn an affine matrix Aℓ to map


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


188
      hidden states of layer ℓ to the input space of the LM head. We aim to investigate more efficient adaptation
189
      methods while minimizing interference with model predictions and avoiding excessive additional trainable
190   parameters.
191
192   Inspired by normalization studies in neural networks and the effectiveness of tuning the normalization mod-
193
      ule for domain adaptation (Zhao et al., 2023), we propose tuning an additional normalization module for
      each late layer as a simple yet powerful adaptation method. We set the additional normalization module Nk
194
      to match the architecture of the pretrained Np . For instance, in the LLaMA2 model (Touvron et al., 2023b),
195
      we follow the LayerNorm setting (Ba et al., 2016). The learnable parameters in the normalization, γk and
196   βk , are trained individually for each k-th late layer to ensure specific adaptation for each layer.
197
198   Following our assumption in §2.2, we treat each of the k − 1 late layers (excluding the final layer) as smaller
199
      models, with the final layer as the larger model with the most predictive power. We use the final layer as
      the teacher model to supervise the output of earlier layers for adaptation. We define a teacher-enforced
200
      distillation loss that measures the difference between the predictions of the intermediate models and the
201
      final layer’s predictions. The distillation loss is computed as the sum of the KL divergence between each
202   intermediate layer’s output distribution Pi and the final layer’s output distribution Pn :
203
                                                             k−2
204                                                          X
205
                                                Ldistill =         KL(Pi ∥ Pn ),
                                                             i=0
206
207   where Pi is the output distribution of layer i, and Pn is the output distribution of the final layer. The final
208   loss is then the sum of the task loss and the distillation loss:
209                                              LMoD = Ltask + λLdistill ,
210
211   where λ is a hyperparameter that controls the weight of the distillation loss. By tuning with the normalization
212   modules and distillation loss, we adapt the k − 1 layer representations to be more suitable for the language
213
      modeling task, ensuring their contributions are aligned with the original task loss.

345   4.1   L EARNED ROUTING PATTERN ACROSS T OKENS
346
347   In this section, we analyze the routing patterns learned with MoD for the K ensemble layers during training.
348   With a Gaussian-initialized routing network, we measure the sparsity of the weights across the training
349   tokens, i.e., how many weights are close to zero. We calculate the proportion of weights below a threshold,
350
      ϵ, which we set to 1 × 10−5 . A lower level of sparsity often implies that the model is selectively using
      current routes while ignoring others, leading to the discussion in §4.2. We also record the mean and variance
351
      to measure the tendency and dispersion for each k route, as detailed in Appendix C.1. We evaluate MoD
352
      trained on top of LoRA and MoD trained without k LoRA layers using the LLaMA 7B model on the ARC
353   easy subset. According to Figure 4, we notice an interesting learned pattern discrepancy between MoD
354   trained with or without LoRA layers. When trained without k LoRA layers, the sparsity score for the last
355   layer remains low, while the sparsity level of layer 30 is high initially and then decreases, and the sparsity
356   level of layer 29 increases through training. This suggests that the model generally learns to rely more on
357   the last two layers’ outputs, especially the last layer for the ensemble. However, when trained with k LoRA
358   layers in the ensemble, the sparsity level of the last layer is much higher, while the levels for the other
359   two layers remain low. This indicates that the additional trainable modules inside MoD help the late layers
360   contribute more to the ensembles and become more task-informative, aligning with our assumption in §2.3.
361
      Notably, both methods yield better performance than the baseline according to Table 3.2, suggesting that
      there is still significant predictive potential through different weight combinations for the ensembles.

438
439
440
      Early Exit in Transformer Layers Early exit strategies in language models are often explored to im-
      prove efficiency. Several works focus on enhancing inference efficiency by terminating computation at
441
      dynamically-decided earlier layer outputs (Xin et al., 2020; Schuster et al., 2022). A common approach for
442
      adapting intermediate layer output to language modeling involves training an affine transformation (Belrose
443   et al., 2023; Din et al., 2023). Early exit strategies have also been explored for interpretability, analyzing
444   the linearity properties of transformer components (Geva et al., 2023; Hernandez et al., 2023). However, the
445   utilization of intermediate layer output during training remains largely unexplored. A recent work (Elhoushi
446   et al., 2024) applies layer dropout and an early exit loss to increase the accuracy of early exits, but its primary
447   focus is still on inference efficiency. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to utilize early exit
448   logits together with the final layer logits to incorporate task-aware representations from intermediate layers
449   into the loss calculation.
450   Logit-Level Arithmetic Operations at the logit level have proven effective in steering the output of LLMs
451   (Luo & Specia, 2024). From a multi-model perspective, there has been a growing body of work focusing on
452   “mixturing” the abilities of different trained models in line with the Mixture-of-Experts framework (Shazeer
453   et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2024). Liu et al. (2021); Gera et al. (2023) have also shown the effectiveness of
454   ensembling logits from multiple LMs. From a single model perspective, contrasting logits from different
455   layers of a model (Chuang et al., 2024; Gera et al., 2023) has shown promising performance improvements
456   in the trustworthiness of generation and addressing the resource-intensive issues of larger models (Liu et al.,
457
      2024). Our work builds upon logit-level arithmetic and follows the line of ensembling logits, focusing not
      on a multi-model perspective but rather on utilizing the late layers’ outputs within a single model for tuning.
458
      This approach has been considered only during inference in previous work.

462   6    C ONCLUSION
463
464
      In this paper, we explored the predictive power of late layers in LLMs and introduced the Mixture-of-Depths
465   (MoD) tuning framework. By tuning LLMs using ensembled logits from MoD routing and adaptation com-
466   ponents, we demonstrated consistent improvements in reasoning tasks with minimal additional parameters.
467   Additionally, our approach shows the potential to replace traditional training modules with significantly
468   fewer parameters. Our findings highlight the effectiveness of leveraging intermediate layer representations
469   during training, offering a lightweight and complementary direction for optimizing LLMs.

      7   L IMITATIONS
471
472
      Future work could explore dynamic layer selection methods and refine the layer range of the MoD framework
473
      to maximize its potential, rather than relying on empirical selection. Additionally, more effective tuning of
474   other hyperparameters, such as λ, the weight of the distillation loss, should be investigated. Improving the
475   effectiveness of MoD on a broader range of tasks, such as instruction following, remains an open question,
476   as discussed in §3.3. Extending MoD to evaluate its performance on bidirectional LLMs, such as RoBERTa
477   (Liu et al., 2019), would help determine if it generalizes well across different transformer-based language
478   models. Due to hardware limitations, our experiments were restricted to LLMs at the 7B scale. Exploring
479   the impact of MoD on larger models is an important direction for future research.

      In this section, we analyze the routing patterns learned with MoD for the K ensemble layers during training.
762
      We measure the mean and variance of the weights across the training tokens. A higher mean suggests that
763   the model consistently chooses this route, while a higher variance indicates variability in the routes learned
764   for different tokens. We evaluate MoD trained on top of LoRA and MoD trained without k LoRA layers
765   using the LLaMA 7B model on the ARC easy subset.
766
      According to Figure 7, for the mean metric, we observe a reverse trend with respect to the sparsity score in
767
      Table 4. This aligns with our intuition that when the sparsity score of the current route is low, the routing
768
      value will be relatively larger than other routes. For the variance, we notice that when MoD is trained without
769   k LoRA layers, it maintains a high variance throughout tuning. This suggests that many tokens are trained to
770   select this route, but they are dynamically changing. When MoD is trained with LoRA, both the variance and
771   mean levels stay low, indicating that the other two layers primarily contribute to the final ensemble logits.
772   This suggests that the additional k trainable module within the MoD framework provides more predictive
773   power to the ensemble layers, aligning with our analysis in §4.1.
