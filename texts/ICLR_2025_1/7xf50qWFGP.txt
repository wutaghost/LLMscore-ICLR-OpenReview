009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Representation learning plays a crucial role in reinforcement learning, especially
012            in complex environments with high-dimensional and unstructured states. Effec-
013            tive representations can enhance the efficiency of learning algorithms by improv-
014            ing sample efficiency and generalization across tasks. This paper considers the
015            Laplacian-based framework for representation learning, where the eigenvectors of
016
               the Laplacian matrix of the underlying transition graph are leveraged to encode
               meaningful features from raw sensory observations of the states. Despite the
017
               promising algorithmic advances in this framework, it remains an open question
018
               whether the Laplacian-based representations can be learned online and with theo-
019            retical guarantees along with policy learning. To answer this question, we study
020            online Laplacian-based representation learning, where the graph-based representa-
021            tion is updated simultaneously while the policy is updated by the reinforcement
022            learning algorithm. We design an online optimization formulation by introducing
023            the Asymmetric Graph Drawing Objective (AGDO) and provide a theoretical anal-
024            ysis of the convergence of running online projected gradient descent on AGDO
025            under mild assumptions. Specifically, we show that if the policy learning algorithm
026            induces a bounded drift on the policy, running online projected gradient descent
027
               on AGDO exhibits ergodic convergence. Our extensive simulation studies empiri-
               cally validate the guarantees of convergence to the true Laplacian representation.
028
               Furthermore, we provide insights into the compatibility of different reinforcement
029
               learning algorithms with online representation learning.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Representation learning is an important part of machine learning that involves learning compact
035   and useful representations of data. The quality of these representations significantly impacts the
036   performance and efficiency of machine learning algorithms (Bengio et al., 2013). In reinforcement
037   learning (RL), agents often deal with complex environments characterized by high-dimensional and
038   unstructured states. This makes representation learning important for discovering and encoding
039
      meaningful features from raw sensory inputs. The main goal of RL is to learn an optimal strategy
      (policy) that maps each state to an action, aiming to maximize the expected reward based on the
040
      dynamics of the environment. Learning a good representation can improve the sample efficiency of
041
      value-function approximation algorithms (Farebrother et al., 2023), a major family of RL algorithms,
042   and enhance generalizations across different tasks (Yuan & Lu, 2022). In addition, representation
043   learning has found applications in reward shaping (Wu et al., 2018), learning options with larger
044   coverage (Machado et al., 2017a; Jinnai et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2024), and transfer learning
045   (Gimelfarb et al., 2021; Barreto et al., 2017).
046
      A graph representation is often used to learn a representation, i.e., a low-dimensional embedding,
047
      of the states (Mahadevan & Maggioni, 2007; Wu et al., 2018). States of an environment can be
048   viewed as nodes of a graph, and the transition probability between states under a given policy can
049   be viewed as weighted edges between these nodes. States that are closely connected in the graph
050   are expected to have similar representations in the embedding space. One representation that retains
051   this property is the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian. Formally, the d-eigenvectors of the graph
052   Laplacian corresponding to the d-smallest eigenvalues are used to construct an embedding function
053   that maps a state to a vector in Rd . We refer to those d-eigenvectors as the d-smallest eigenvectors
      for the remainder of this paper.

054
      Constructing the graph and performing eigendecomposition on the Laplacian is only feasible in the
055   tabular settings where the number of states is small. Therefore, Wu et al. (2018) proposed a scalable
056   method to compute the smallest eigenvectors by solving an unconstrained version of the graph drawing
057   objective (Koren, 2005) which is suitable for large and continuous state-spaces. However, the graph
058   drawing objective does not have a unique minimizer, rather the rotations of the smallest eigenvectors
059   are also its minimizers. To tackle this challenge, Wang et al. (2021) propose the generalized graph
060   drawing objective which breaks the symmetry and only has the smallest eigenvectors as a unique
061   minimizer. Gomez et al. (2023) show that under gradient descent dynamics, the unconstrained
062   version of the generalized graph drawing objective has permutations of the smallest eigenvectors as
063
      equilibrium points. They propose the augmented Lagrangian Laplacian objective (ALLO) which has
      the smallest eigenvectors and the corresponding eigenvalues as the stable equilibrium under stochastic
064
      gradient descent-ascent dynamics.
065
066   The Laplacian-based representation can be computed or learned for a given policy according to
067   its induced Markov chain. However, in RL the policy updates during the training phase as new
068   data comes in, which will in turn necessitate recomputation of the representation. To avoid this
069
      complexity, in practice, the Laplacian-based representation is learned for a uniformly random policy
      in a pretraining phase and then used throughout training. Nevertheless, that fixed representation
070
      may not be effective for the policies encountered during RL. Recently, Klissarov & Machado (2023)
071
      showed that learning the representation in an online manner while simultaneously updating the
072   policy can improve exploration and increase the total reward. In Figure 1, we illustrate an example,
073   comparing the representations of a uniform policy and a non-uniform policy, that further underscores
074   the need for adapting the representation. The non-uniform policy shows that some cells, despite
075   being far from the target in terms of Euclidean distance, are actually closer in the embedding space
076   than neighboring cells. This suggests that using the current representation to design rewards could
077   offer a better signal for improving the policy. Klissarov & Machado (2023) proposed online deep
078   Laplacian-based options for temporally extended exploration where a set of policies (also known as
079   options) are trained to select exploratory actions using an estimated Laplacian representation of the
080
      current overall policy. They provide an extensive empirical analysis of how learning options while
      updating the representation increases the received rewards; however, the theoretical analysis of online
081
      representation learning while updating the policy has remained an open question.

093   Motivated by that, we design an online optimization formulation by introducing the Asymmetric
094   Graph Drawing Objective (AGDO), a simplified version of ALLO that does not involve dual variables.
095   We prove that the only stable equilibrium for AGDO is the d-smallest eigenvectors under gradient
096   descent dynamics. Furthermore, we establish through theory and experiments that optimizing the
097   online version of AGDO converges to a stationary point under the assumption of bounded drift.

099   2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
100
101   In this section, we review existing studies and research directions in representation learning for
102   reinforcement learning, focusing on topics closely linked to this study.
103
104   Proto-Value Functions. Mahadevan (2005) introduced proto-value functions, a set of basis func-
105   tions that are independent of the reward function. These functions are defined as the eigenfunctions
106   of the normalized Laplacian of the graph generated by a random walk over the state space. This
107   representation has been demonstrated to reduce the number of samples required for training linear
      value function approximators (Mahadevan & Maggioni, 2007). The process of generating the graph


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      involves collecting samples from the environment and connecting neighboring states with edges.
109   However, this method does not adequately account for the stochastic nature of transitions and re-
110   quires a discrete state space. In continuous state settings, Mahadevan (2012) proposes using the
111   Nyström method to interpolate the values of eigenfunctions at unseen states based on visited states.
112   Additionally, Xu et al. (2014) suggests enhancing representative state selection by applying K-means
113   clustering to collected samples and constructing a graph from the resulting centroids.
114
115   Laplacian Representation Using the Graph Drawing Objective. Wu et al. (2018) formulated a
116   linear operator that represents the graph over the state-space generated by a fixed policy, capturing the
117   stochastic nature of transitions, and is applicable to continuous state spaces. They demonstrated that
118   obtaining the eigenfunctions of the graph Laplacian, typically solved via the graph drawing objective
119   (Koren, 2005), can be achieved through stochastic optimization using collected samples without ex-
120   plicitly constructing the graph. Additionally, they illustrated a method to recover these eigenfunctions
121
      up to orthonormal rotation by training a neural network. For precise eigenfunction recovery, Wang
      et al. (2021) introduced the generalized graph drawing objective, which breaks the symmetry inherent
122
      in the traditional graph drawing objective. Despite the constrained generalized objective ensuring the
123
      uniqueness of Laplacian eigenfunctions, Gomez et al. (2023) demonstrated that stochastic optimiza-
124   tion using the unconstrained objective—employed in neural network training—does not necessarily
125   converge to these eigenfunctions. Consequently, they proposed the augmented Lagrangian Laplacian
126   objective, which exhibits the eigenvectors of the Laplacian as the unique stable equilibrium. Other
127   equilibrium points correspond to permutations of the eigenvectors.
128
      Learning the Laplacian representation with any of these objectives is conducted under a fixed
129
      policy, typically a uniformly random policy in practice. Klissarov & Machado (2023) introduced
130   online deep covering eigenoptions, an online algorithm that concurrently learns the Laplacian
131   representation and options (Sutton, 1998), a well-established formulation of temporally extended
132   actions in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs). They demonstrated that the online version of DCEO
133   achieves performance comparable to a two-stage variant of the algorithm, where the representation is
134   learned under a fixed uniform policy.
135
136   Successor Features. The deep successor representation, introduced by Kulkarni et al. (2016) as
137   an extension of the successor representation (Dayan, 1993), decomposes the value function into
138   a successor feature function and a reward predictor function. The successor function encodes the
139   discounted expected value of representations of all future states within a given horizon. Leveraging
140   concepts from TD learning and Deep Q-networks (Mnih et al., 2015), both the representation and the
141   successor feature function can be learned simultaneously with neural networks. Successor features
142
      have found diverse applications, such as sub-goal states generation in sparse reward environments
      (Kulkarni et al., 2016), transfer learning (Barreto et al., 2017; Gimelfarb et al., 2021), and options
143
      discovery (Machado et al., 2017b; 2023). Notably, Machado et al. (2017b) demonstrated a connection
144
      between the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the successor representation matrix and the eigenvalues
145   and eigenvectors of the normalized Laplacian defined as proto-value functions.
146
147
      Contrastive Learning in Reinforcement Learning. Contrastive learning is a machine learning
148
      method used for learning representations that distinguish between similar and dissimilar pairs of data
149
      points using a contrastive loss function. Formally, an encoder is tasked with mapping data points to
150   a latent representation where similar points are closely positioned in the latent space. For instance,
151   Laskin et al. (2020) introduced the contrastive unsupervised representations for reinforcement learning
152   algorithm, where they train an encoder network using a contrastive loss with pairs of images randomly
153   augmented from the same source image. The learned representation is subsequently utilized to train
154   a deep reinforcement learning agent. Furthermore, augmented temporal contrast was developed by
155   Stooke et al. (2021), which involves selecting similar sample pairs from samples that are separated by
156   a short time distance. This approach is closely related to the Laplacian approach to representation
157   learning, as states that are connected in the graph have a higher probability of appearing in consecutive
158
      samples than disconnected states.
159   In this work, we focus on extending the Laplacian-based representation learing, which has been
160   shown in recent literature to be effective in learning options with high coverage Machado et al.
161   (2017a); Jinnai et al. (2019); Klissarov & Machado (2023); Chen et al. (2024), to the online setting.
      While empirical results, such as those by Klissarov & Machado (2023), have demonstrated that online


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      representation learning is effective and promising, a thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence
163   and accuracy of these learned representations in the online setting is still lacking. Therefore, our
164   work seeks to address this gap by developing a theoretical framework that ensures the stability and
165   accuracy of Laplacian representations in an online learning context.

348   4.3    C ONVERGENCE A NALYSIS OF O NLINE AGDO
349

473   Ablation Study In this study, we aim to analyze three points; (1) the importance of the drift bound
474   assumption, (2) the effect of the number of encoder update steps per sample collected, and (3) the
475   effect of noise caused by sampling from the replay buffer when the policy was different.
476
      To assert the importance of the bounded drift assumption, we compare running PPO with different
477
      initial clipping parameters, vanilla policy gradient (VPG) Sutton et al. (1999), and deep Q-network
478   (DQN) Mnih et al. (2015). First note that VPG is equivalent to PPO without clipping. We can see
479   in Figure 4a that the lower the clipping value is, i.e. the drift bound between policies is smaller,
480   the higher the accuracy for the learned representation is. However, a small drift might affect the
481   performance of the learned policy. In addition, for DQN the change in the policy distribution can
482   be drastic for an ϵ−greedy policy with a small ϵ whenever the Q-network changes the estimated
                                                                                                           ϵ
483   optimal action in a state. As for the new estimated optimal action, the probability will shift from |A|
484   to 1 − ϵ. This explains why the accuracy of the learned representation for DQN is much lower than
485   the on-policy methods. We conclude that the bounded drift assumption is necessary for learning an
      accurate representation.

      In figure 4b, we analyze the effect of increasing the number of steps. We vary the number of update
487   steps per sample between 1 and 20. While an increase in the number of steps is expected to enhance
488   accuracy, our findings indicate that this is not observed. We hypothesize that this discrepancy is due
489   to the presence of noise, caused by sampling from the replay buffer.
490
      To confirm the previous hypothesis, we test in Figure 4c the effect of varying the replay buffer size.
491
      Recall that estimating the AGDO loss in equation 7 is done through sampling steps from the replay
492
      buffer. In the online setting, the buffer would include steps from previous policies with different
493   stationary and transition distributions which would introduce bias to our loss estimate. However, a

495   as for a buffer that holds only one episode we see a worse performance than a buffer that holds 20
496   episodes. On the other hand, increasing the buffer size drastically also causes accuracy to drop as the
497   samples used have a different distribution which can be seen for buffers with sizes 50 and 400.

528   6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we studied online Laplacian-based representation learning and demonstrated that it
531   can be effectively integrated with reinforcement learning, enabling simultaneous updates of both
532   representation and policy. Our theoretical analysis, under mild assumptions, shows that running the
533   online projected gradient descent on the Asymmetric Graph Drawing Objective achieves ergodic
534   convergence, ensuring that the learned representations are aligned with the underlying dynamics.
535
      Additionally, our empirical studies reinforce these findings and give insight into the compatibility of
      reinforcement learning algorithms with online representation learning. Our work opens new avenues
536
      for enhancing representation learning in complex environments and lays out the assumptions needed
537
      for its success. Future research could explore the adaptability of the proposed framework to various
538   learning methods such as linear value function approximators and options learning.
