009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have exhibited strong mathematical reasoning
012
               and computational prowess, tackling tasks ranging from basic arithmetic to ad-
013
               vanced competition-level problems. However, frequently occurring subtle errors,
014            such as miscalculations or incorrect substitutions, limit the models’ full mathemat-
015            ical potential. Existing studies to improve mathematical ability typically involve
016            distilling reasoning skills from stronger LLMs or applying preference learning to
017            step-wise response pairs. Although these methods leverage samples of varying
018            granularity to mitigate reasoning errors, they overlook the frequently occurring
019            subtle errors. A major reason is that sampled preference pairs involve differences
020            unrelated to the errors, which may distract the model from focusing on subtle
021            errors. In this work, we propose a novel preference learning framework called
022
               eRror-Injected Self-Editing (RISE), which injects predefined subtle errors into
               partial tokens of correct solutions to construct hard pairs for error mitigation. In
023
               detail, RISE uses the model itself to edit a small number of tokens in the solution,
024
               injecting designed subtle errors. Then, pairs composed of self-edited solutions
025            and their corresponding correct ones, along with pairs of correct and incorrect so-
026            lutions obtained through sampling, are used together for subtle error-aware DPO
027            training. Compared with other preference learning methods, RISE further refines
028            the training objective to focus on predefined errors and their tokens, without re-
029            quiring fine-grained sampling or preference annotation. Extensive experiments
030            validate the effectiveness of RISE, with preference learning on Qwen2-7B-Instruct
031            yielding notable improvements of 3.0% on GSM8K and 7.9% on MATH.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated advanced logical reasoning and computational
038   capabilities, enabling them to tackle a diverse range of problems from elementary mathematics to
039
      complex, competition-level questions (Yue et al., 2024a; Gou et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024). Nev-
      ertheless, frequently occurring subtle errors remain one of the primary obstacles preventing these
040
      models from consistently realizing their full mathematical potential (Li et al., 2024). These errors,
041
      such as miscalculations, incorrect substitutions, and omission of calculation terms, account for ap-
042   proximately 75% of the total errors as shown in Figure 3.
043
044   Many existing works have attempted to improve mathematical ability by distilling reasoning skills
045
      from stronger LLMs using synthetic datasets (Shao et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024a; Azerbayev et al.,
      2024). While distilling helps the model acquire expanded reasoning patterns, it does not directly
046
      enable it to reduce the occurrence of errors (Hong et al., 2024). Recently, a growing body of research
047
      has shifted its focus toward preference optimization with fine-grained preference pairs to mitigate
048   mathematical errors (Lai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Setlur et al., 2024a). This method leverages
049   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to reduce the likelihood of generating the error step in a pair
050   of steps with the same context (Rafailov et al., 2023). However, learning with these fine-grained
051   preference pairs still struggles to focus on subtle errors, as the aforementioned pairs involve some
052   differences unrelated to the errors. Additionally, establishing the preference relationship within
053   fine-grained pairs requires extra LLM-based annotations or approximate estimations, which may
      introduce bias (Xie et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024).

      In our work, we argue that, in addition to sampling preference pairs and annotating their relation-
      ships, generating a correct solution and injecting errors into it can be an effective approach to con-
077
      struct pairs that specifically target subtle errors. First, error injection can introduce errors into a small
078
      number of tokens while preserving the overall structure of the solution. Such error-injected samples,
079   with limited differences from the correct solutions, can be regarded as hard negatives for preference
080   learning (Liu et al., 2024). Second, we can leverage the LLM itself to inject predefined errors using
081   appropriate prompts. Compared to randomly sampled pairs, pairs based on injected errors are more
082   controllable, allowing preference learning to focus on designed, targeted subtle errors.
083
      Based on the above motivation, we propose a novel preference learning framework called eRrror-
084
      Injected Self-Editing (RISE). The key idea is to prompt the LLM to inject errors into correct so-
085   lutions and construct hard pairs targeting predefined subtle errors for preference learning. To be
086   specific, we first apply an LLM to generate several multi-step solutions and construct a full-solution
087   pair composed of one correct solution and one incorrect solution. Then, we choose the correct one
088   and edit each step of the solution to inject subtle errors. The same model is used for error-injected
089   editing, as it may better recognize the tokens where it is most prone to making mistakes. We design
090   several types of subtle errors and modify a few tokens to introduce these errors. The edited steps
091   and the corresponding correct steps are constructed as self-edited pairs. Finally, we conduct subtle
092   error-aware DPO training on both self-edited pairs and full-solution pairs. Inspired by Pal et al.
093
      (2024), a negative log-likelihood loss is introduced to stabilize the training, as the self-edited pairs
      are highly similar, which can easily reduce the likelihood of the correct solutions.
094
095   We evaluate our framework on two LLM series: Qwen2 and Llama-3.1. Our method RISE-
096   Q WEN 2-7B achieves a 3.0% accuracy gain on GSM8K and 7.9% on MATH, and RISE-L LAMA -
097   3.1-8B achieves 3.9% and 2.7%, respectively. Detailed error analysis shows that RISE helps the
098   LLM further avoid predefined subtle errors. Moreover, our method successfully generalizes rea-
099
      soning preferences derived from mathematical tasks to other reasoning domains, such as logical
      reasoning and code generation.

137   2.1     DATASET C ONSTRUCTION VIA E RROR - INJECTED S ELF - EDITING
138
      To create hard preference pairs, we employ an editing approach to modify certain tokens in the
139
      correct solution to introduce predefined errors. We focus on the error injection of each step in one
140
      solution. Compared with previous step-wise preference learning (Lai et al., 2024), our approach
141   further refines the objective of preference learning by specifically targeting a few error tokens, en-
142   hancing the ability of LLMs to avoid subtle errors.
143
144
145
      Error Types. As we aim to optimize the model to avoid subtle errors, the main types of errors we
      concentrate on are as follows: (1) Numerical calculation errors; (2) Numerical or symbolic substitu-
146
      tion errors; (3) Omission of calculation terms. These errors frequently occur in most solutions and
147
      typically involve only a small amount of tokens. We also include two relatively less frequent errors:
148   (4) Errors in the calculation order and (5) Errors in the use of calculation symbols. We extract and
149   summarize these errors from the solutions generated by the models in our experiments.
150
151
152   Self-editing. We design appropriate prompts and utilize the model itself to edit the generated cor-
153
      rect step ŷi+ . As most of the steps in the correct solutions are accurate (Lu et al., 2024b), we use all
      the steps from the correct ones without applying any additional filtering. Since solutions to mathe-
154
      matical problems are highly error-sensitive, any modification of numerical values, symbols, or other
155
      mathematical elements disrupts the original correct procedure. Thus, even small language models
156   can be prompted to almost certainly inject errors. The error-injected step ŷi− is generated using an
157   edit prompt shown on the left of Figure 2. This edit prompt contains the problem x, the concate-
158   nation of previous steps ŷ<i , the correct step ŷi , and the edit type e. We mainly use three types of
159   editing operations: “REPLACE”, “SWAP”, and “DELETE” (Mallinson et al., 2022). Some error
160   injection examples are shown on the right of Figure 2. For each step, only around ten tokens will
161   be modified. We collect the error-injected steps and the correct steps to construct a paired edited

      3.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
219
220   Evaluation Datasets. We evaluate our framework on three in-domain datasets, GSM8k (Cobbe
221   et al., 2021), MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and AQuA (Ling et al., 2017), along with three out-
222   of-domain datasets, SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021), AIME24 (MAA, 2024), and Odyssey-MATH (Net-
223   mind.AI, 2024). These six datasets span a broad spectrum of mathematical problems, ranging from
224   basic arithmetic to advanced competition-level problems. The problems in these datasets, including
225   tabular, free-form, and multiple-choice formats, ensure a robust evaluation of the model’s mathe-
226   matical reasoning ability. The detail of all datasets is described in Table 6 in the Appendix A.
227
228   Baselines. Our framework is compared with several LLMs performing well in mathematical
229   reasoning. Two closed-source state-of-the-art LLMs: OpenAI’s GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and
230
      Anthropic’s Claude-3.5-Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024). Three open-sourced general instruction-tuned
      LLMs: Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023), Qwen2 series (Yang et al., 2024a), and Llama-
231
      3.1 series (Meta AI, 2024). Five mathematically enhanced LLMs: DeepSeekMath-RL (Shao et al.,
232
      2024), Llemma (Azerbayev et al., 2024), ToRA (Gou et al., 2024), MAmmoTH (Yue et al., 2024a),
233   and MathGenieLM (Lu et al., 2024a). Four additional LLMs under step-wise DPO optimization
234   are also included: Step-DPO series (Lai et al., 2024), SVPO (Chen et al., 2024), MCTS-DPO (Xie
235   et al., 2024), and SCDPO (Lu et al., 2024b). We display results with CoT prompts for most baseline
236   LLMs. ToRA and MAmmoTH are designed with built-in capabilities for tool usage, so we report
237   the best results with tools.
238
239   Training Details. We adopt mathematical problems used by Lai et al. (2024) for preference learn-
240   ing. The training dataset contains around 9K problems with corresponding correct step-by-step
241   solutions. We discard these solutions and use only the problems to construct our training set. The
242   problems are mainly from MetaMath (Yu et al., 2024) and AQuA Ling et al. (2017). Details are
243   presented in Table 7. We select two open-source LLM series, Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024a) and
244   Llama-3.1 (Meta AI, 2024) as our base LLMs. We apply the instruction-tuned version of these
245
      models to sample solutions and meanwhile edit each step of the solution. The number of sampling
      attempts is set to 5, and the number of self-edited pairs is set to 1 for the Qwen2 series and 3 for the
246
      Llama-3.1 series. Since some problems cannot yield correct and incorrect preference pairs within
247
      a limited number of sampling attempts, we ultimately use only about 4.5K problems to construct
248   preference pair combinations for training. Implementation details are presented in the Appendix B.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
502
503
      4.1   LLM FOR M ATHEMATICAL R EASONING
504
      Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable proficiency in mathematical reasoning, ex-
505
      celling in tasks ranging from basic arithmetic questions to complex mathematical Olympiad prob-
506   lems (Team et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024; Huang et al., 2024).
507   Furthermore, various approaches have been investigated to enhance the mathematical reasoning ca-
508   pabilities of LLMs. Methods such as Llemma Azerbayev et al. (2024), DeepseekMath Shao et al.
509   (2024), and Qwen2.5-Math Yang et al. (2024b) have focused on collecting vast amounts of math-
510   related data for continued pretraining. Recent efforts have also focused on designing more effi-
511   cient fine-tuning datasets to stimulate the mathematical capabilities of base models, such as MAm-
512   moTH (Yue et al., 2024b), MetaMath (Yu et al., 2024) and DART-Math (Tong et al., 2024). Addi-
513   tionally, some works attempted to enhance the LLM’s mathematical ability by incorporating external
514
      tools, particularly through the integration of code interpreter (Gou et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a;
      Liao et al., 2024).
515
516
517   4.2   S TEP - WISE P REFERENCE L EARNING
518
      In addition to pre-training and instruction fine-tuning, step-wise preference learning—particularly
519
      methods related to Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)—has been widely explored to enhance the
520
      mathematical capabilities of LLMs (Lightman et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024).
521   However, the final performance is highly dependent on the quality of the process-supervised reward
522   model (PRM) (Uesato et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b), and the training process of PPO or its
523   variants is notably complex. Recently, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023)
524   simplified this process by directly leveraging pair-wise data for preference learning. Many works
525   extend DPO, enabling it to perform step-wise preference learning to improve the model’s multi-step
526   mathematical problem-solving ability (Lu et al., 2024b; Xie et al., 2024; Setlur et al., 2024b; Lai
527   et al., 2024). To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to tackle subtle errors in mathematical
528   reasoning via step-wise preference learning especially on error tokens.
529
530
      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we propose a novel preference learning framework called eRror-Injected Self-Editing
533
      (RISE), which constructs hard pairs through self-editing to mitigate predefined subtle errors. Com-
534   pared to other fine-grained, step-wise preference learning methods, our framework further refines
535   the training objective to target subtle error tokens, without requiring LLM-based or estimation-based
536   preference annotations. To avoid optimization failure caused by overly similar preference pairs, we
537   additionally introduce a negative log-likelihood loss, controlled by the generation probability of the
538   correct samples. The effectiveness of our framework is demonstrated in two LLM series: Qwen2
539   and Llama-3.1. Results across multiple mathematical datasets demonstrate that our method can
      unlock the model’s potential in mathematical reasoning.
