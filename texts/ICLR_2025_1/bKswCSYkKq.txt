009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In contrast to the inherent ability of humans to continuously acquire new knowl-
012            edge, modern deep reinforcement learning (DRL) agents generally encounter a
013            significant challenge: the stability-plasticity dilemma, which refers to the trade-off
014            between retaining existing skills (stability) and learning new knowledge (plastic-
015            ity). In this study, we propose Neuron-level Balance between Stability and Plas-
016            ticity (NBSP) to tackle this challenge, by taking inspiration from the observation
017            that both stability and plasticity are integrally linked to the expressive capabili-
018            ties of networks, which are primarily determined by the behavior of individual
019
               neurons. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that addresses both
               stability and plasticity loss simultaneously in DRL at the level of neurons. Specif-
020
               ically, NBSP first (1) defines and identifies RL skill neurons that are crucial for
021
               knowledge retention through a goal-oriented method, and then (2) introduces a
022            stability-plasticity balancing mechanism by employing gradient masking and ex-
023            perience replay techniques targeting these neurons to preserve the encoded mem-
024            ory related to existing skills while enhancing the learning capabilities of other
025            neurons. Experimental results on the Meta-World and Atari benchmarks demon-
026            strate that NBSP significantly outperforms existing approaches in balancing sta-
027            bility and plasticity. Furthermore, our findings underscore the pivotal role of the
028            critic within this context, providing valuable insights for future research.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Deep reinforcement learning (DRL), which integrates the principles of both reinforcement learning
034   (RL) and deep learning (DL), has shown exceptional capabilities across a range of complex sce-
035   narios, such as gaming (Mnih et al., 2013), robotic manipulation (Andrychowicz et al., 2020), and
036   autonomous driving (Kiran et al., 2021). While most advancements in DRL focus on single-task
037   scenarios, real-world applications often require agents to handle tasks sequentially, which remains
038   under-explored in the research community. For example, a robot initially trained to open a window
039
      might subsequently require the ability to close it in response to rainy weather. Similarly, an au-
      tonomous vehicle designed to follow lanes under standard conditions must adapt to changing lanes
040
      to deal with obstacles. Naive solutions such as retraining an agent from scratch and developing a
041
      new agent for each task are either computationally expensive or impractical. To this end, a more
042   efficient approach presents training the agent continually on the new task based on the knowledge
043   acquired from the previous ones.
044
045
      Despite the promise of the sequential RL learning approach, the stability-plasticity dilemma re-
      mains a fundamental and under-explored problem. Ideally, the agent must maintain its performance
046
      on the previously learned task, a characteristic referred to as stability(McCloskey & Cohen, 1989),
047
      while simultaneously adapting to the new task, known as plasticity (Carpenter & Grossberg, 1987).
048   However, it has been revealed that emphasizing stability may hinder the ability of agents to learn
049   new knowledge (Nikishin et al., 2022a; Abbas et al., 2023), whereas excessive plasticity can lead to
050   catastrophic forgetting of previously acquired knowledge (Goodfellow et al., 2015; Atkinson et al.,
051   2021), a challenge known as the stability-plasticity dilemma (eMermillod et al., 2013). Our pre-
052   liminary experiments demonstrate that this dilemma persists in DRL when new tasks are learned
053   directly, necessitating the development of a mechanism that effectively balances these two compet-
      ing demands, which is the main focus of our work.

054
      Existing methods to strike a balance between stability and plasticity generally fall into three cate-
055   gories, i.e. (1) regularization-based methods (Kumar et al., 2023), which apply penalties to param-
056   eter changes to mitigate forgetting while acquiring new knowledge; (2) replay-based methods (Ahn
057   et al., 2024), which leverage past experiences to consolidate knowledge; and (3) modularity-based
058   methods (Anand & Precup, 2024), which seek to decouple stability and plasticity or isolate different
059   components for different tasks. Despite their contributions, these methods suffer from three key lim-
060   itations: (1) They primarily operate at the network level. However, stability and plasticity attribute to
061   the expressive capabilities, which are significantly influenced by the behavior of individual neurons.
062   To this end, identifying and leveraging these skill neurons is critical yet under-researched. (2) These
063
      studies are primarily conducted within the framework of continual learning, thus overlooking the
      unique characteristics intrinsic to DRL. (3) These approaches could sometimes unnecessarily inflate
064
      model parameters, thereby introducing unwarranted complexity (Bai et al., 2023a).
065
066   Motivated by the aforementioned observations, in this work, we tackle these problems from the per-
067   spective of RL skill neurons, and propose Neuron-level Balance between Stability and Plasticity
068   (NBSP), a novel DRL framework that operates at the level of individual neurons. In particular,
069
      (1) we first introduce RL skill neurons, which are essential for retaining knowledge. While skill
      neurons have been extensively investigated and successfully exploited in various domains, such as
070
      pretrained language models (Wang et al., 2022) and neural machine translation (Bau et al., 2018),
071
      skill neurons are still much less explored in DRL. We bridge this research gap by proposing a goal-
072   oriented strategy for identifying RL skill neurons. (2) We then apply gradient masking to these
073   neurons to preserve the encoded memory related to previous skills while allowing other neurons to
074   adapt to a new task. (3) Additionally, we incorporate experience replay to periodically revisit the
075   previous task, preventing excessive drift from the original knowledge. Integrally, NBSP offers three
076   key advantages compared with previous methods: (1) The neuron-level processing enables finer
077   control and greater flexibility, addressing the stability-plasticity trade-off at the most fundamental
078   level of the network. (2) The goal-oriented approach for identifying RL skill neurons is specifi-
079   cally tailored to DRL. (3) This framework is simple and parameter-free, avoiding complex network
080
      designs or additional parameters.
081   We conduct experiments on the Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020) and the Atari (Mnih et al., 2013)
082   benchmarks to evaluate the effectiveness of NBSP. Our results demonstrate that NBSP achieves
083   superior performance in balancing stability and plasticity compared with existing methods, which
084   not only enables effective learning of the new task but also preserves knowledge from the previous
085   task. Additionally, we analyze the DRL agents by dissecting the performance of the two critical
086
      modules, i.e., the actor and the critic, to assess their contributions in balancing stability and plasticity.
      Our findings reveal that (1) addressing both the actor and critic networks yields the best performance,
087
      and (2) the critic plays a more critical role in achieving this balance due to the differences in their
088
      inherent training mechanisms. In summary, our key contributions include:
089
090    • We are the first to introduce the concept of RL skill neurons which are essential for knowledge
091      retention, and propose a goal-oriented strategy specifically tailored to DRL for identification.
092    • We tackle the stability-plasticity dilemma in DRL from the perspective of RL skill neurons, by
093      employing gradient masking and experience replay on these neurons, eliminating requirements
094      of complex network designs or additional parameters.
095    • We conduct extensive experiments on the Meta-World and Atari benchmarks to demonstrate the
096      effectiveness of our method in balancing stability and plasticity. Furthermore, we highlight the
097      crucial role of the critic in achieving this balance, shedding insights on future research.

102   agents to learn new knowledge, whereas too much plasticity can result in catastrophic forgetting
103   of previously acquired knowledge (Jung et al., 2023). This issue, known as the plasticity-stability
104   dilemma, was initially outlined by Carpenter & Grossberg (1988), highlighting the trade-off be-
105   tween preserving performance on the previously learned task and adapting to the new ones. Recent
106   advancements have explored various strategies to address this dilemma. For instance, Cui et al.
107   (2023) integrate a hyperbolic metric learning module (Hyper-Metric) into a distillation-based frame-
      work to manage the trade-off between retaining old knowledge and acquiring new insights. LODE

      enhances balance by decoupling loss functions for new tasks, thereby effectively separating objec-
109   tives (Liang & Li, 2024). PromptFusion tackles the stability-plasticity dilemma through a Stabilizer
110   module to mitigate catastrophic forgetting and a Booster module to support concurrent learning of
111   new knowledge (Chen et al., 2023). Moreover, Bai et al. (2023b) split the learning process into
112   policy optimization and policy correction phases to strike an effective balance between stability and
113   plasticity. While these methods primarily operate at the network level and are conducted within the
114   framework of continual learning, our approach (1) delves into neuron-level research, which offers
115   finer control and operates at the most fundamental level of networks, and (2) focuses on DRL by
116   identifying RL skill neurons specifically tailored to DRL, beneficial for adapting to different tasks
117
      in DRL.
118   Neuron-level research. Recent studies indicate that not all neurons remain active across differ-
119   ent contexts, and this neuron sparsity often correlates positively with task-specific performance (Xu
120   et al., 2024). Concurrently, an increasing number of studies have focused on identifying skill neurons
121   to interpret network behavior or address challenges, achieving notable successes. For instance, skill
122   neurons in pre-trained Transformers, which exhibit high predictive value for task labels, have been
123
      leveraged to accelerate Transformers through network pruning and improve transferability (Wang
      et al., 2022). Sokar et al. (2023) explore dormant neurons in deep reinforcement learning, propos-
124
      ing a method to recycle these neurons throughout training. Dravid et al. (2023) identify Rosetta

      Neurons, which facilitate cross-class alignments, transformations without specialized training. Ad-
126   ditionally, language-specific neurons within large language models have been shown to steer the
127   output language by selectively activating or deactivating them (Tang et al., 2024). In the realm of
128   safety alignment, safety neurons have been identified and analyzed from the perspective of mech-
129   anistic interpretability (Chen et al., 2024). Thought skill neurons successfully exploited in various
130   domains, they are still much less explored in DRL. In contrast, we identify RL skill neurons that are
131   specifically tailored to DRL, successfully balancing stability and plasticity in DRL.

      3.2   I DENTIFYING RL SKILL NEURONS
184
185   In this study, we make a key observation that the stability and plasticity of a network are closely
186   related to its expressive capabilities, which are significantly influenced by the behavior of individual
187   neurons. As evidenced in Molchanov et al. (2022), neuron expression determines how information is
188   propagated and processed within the neural network, directly affecting the learning and knowledge
189   retention capabilities of the network. Therefore, understanding and controlling neuron behavior is at
190   the most fundamental level for striking a balance between stability and plasticity. On the one hand,
191
      when neuron expression is stable and generalized, the network tends to exhibit high stability. On the
      other hand, strong plasticity can be achieved given neuron expression is flexible and adaptable.
192
193   Existing research has demonstrated the multifaceted capabilities of neurons such as the storage of
194   factual knowledge (Dai et al., 2022), the association with specific languages (Tang et al., 2024), and
195   encoding of safety information (Chen et al., 2024). These specialized neurons, often referred to
196   as skill neurons, have been shown to significantly contribute to network performance (Wang et al.,
197
      2022). However, the potential of skill neurons in DRL remains largely under-explored. To bridge
      this research gap, we introduce RL skill neurons, which are essential for knowledge retention in
198
      DRL, and propose a goal-oriented method for the identification of these neurons. Unlike prior ap-
199
      proaches that focus on the inputs triggering neuron activations (Bau et al., 2020; Gurnee & Tegmark,
200   2023), our method emphasizes their impact on achieving the ultimate goal, i.e. succeeding in finish-
201   ing Meta-World tasks and attaining high scores in Atari games, by comparing the activation patterns
202   between the neurons of agents that exhibit varying performance levels. In Section 4.2, we empiri-
203   cally show that the proposed goal-oriented method can better identify neurons that are truly encoding
204   task-specific RL skills.
205

522   5   CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
523

525
      Conclusion. This work addresses the fundamental issue of the stability-plasticity dilemma in DRL.
      We begin by illustrating the challenges DRL agents face in retaining knowledge from a prior task
526
      while effectively learning a new one. To tackle this problem, we introduce the concept of RL skill
527
      neurons by identifying neurons that significantly contribute to knowledge retention, building upon
528   which we then propose the Neuron-level Balance between Stability and Plasticity framework, by
529   employing gradient masking and experience replay techniques on RL skill neurons. Experimental
530   results on the Meta-World and Atari benchmarks demonstrate that NBSP significantly outperforms
531   existing methods in managing the stability-plasticity trade-off. Furthermore, comparative studies
532   of NBSP with its variations reveal that the critic network plays a more crucial role in achieving
533   this balance than the actor network, which is consistent with their respective training mechanisms,
534   shedding valuable insights on future research in this field.
535   Future work. This work defines RL skill neurons, effectively balancing stability and plasticity
536   in DRL. Future research could explore model distillation by pruning neurons apart from RL skill
537   neurons for more compact models, and bias control by manipulating RL skill neuron activations
538   for targeted behaviors. The NBSP method could also extend to other learning paradigms, such as
539   supervised and unsupervised learning, to tackle similar challenges. We plan to investigate these
      directions further across diverse domains.

715   A     E XTENDED RELATED WORK
716
717   Plasticity. In deep reinforcement learning, plasticity refers to the agent’s ability to learn and adapt
718   to the new task. A common risk in this process is overfitting to previous experiences, which can
719   hinder subsequent learning, a phenomenon known as plasticity loss (Lyle et al., 2024). This issue
720   is not unique to reinforcement learning but is also prevalent in continual learning, prompting the
721   development of various strategies to preserve plasticity (Dohare et al., 2024). For instance, Nikishin
722   et al. (2022b) propose a simple yet broadly applicable mechanism that addresses plasticity loss by
723   periodically resetting a portion of the agent’s network. Similarly, Nikishin et al. (2024) introduce
724
      plasticity injection, a minimalist intervention that enhances network plasticity without increasing
      the number of trainable parameters or introducing bias into predictions. Another approach, Neuron-
725
      level Plasticity Control (NPC), seeks to preserve existing knowledge by adjusting the learning rates
726
      of individual neurons (Paik et al., 2019). While these methods effectively enhance plasticity, they
727   may still face the challenge of catastrophic forgetting when the agent attempts to learn a new task
728   based on previously acquired knowledge.
729
730
      Stability. Stability refers to the ability to retain previously acquired knowledge. Excessive plas-
      ticity can result in increased forgetting of earlier information, a phenomenon known as stability
731
      loss (Kemker et al., 2018). Current research primarily focuses on mitigating catastrophic forgetting
732
      (French, 1999) to preserve stability. For example, DR-Tune utilizes distribution regularization by
733   enforcing the downstream task head to reduce classification errors on the pre-trained feature distri-
734   bution, thereby preventing overfitting (Zhou et al., 2023). Similarly, the Adaptive Logit Regularizer
735   (ALI) enhances the model’s ability to learn new categories while maintaining knowledge of previ-
736   ous ones (Oh et al., 2022). Additionally, some approaches incorporate synaptic models designed to
737   mitigate catastrophic forgetting across multiple timescales (Kaplanis et al., 2018). Although these
738   methods are effective in preserving stability by constraining changes to the model, they often com-
739   promise plasticity. In contrast, our proposed method strikes a balance, maintaining stability while
740   still enabling effective learning of the new task.

745   B.1   M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS (MDP)
746
747
      A Markov Decision Process(MDP) is a framework used to describe a problem involving learning
      from actions to achieve a goal. Almost all reinforcement learning problems can be characterized as
748
      a Markov Decision Process. Each MDP is defined by a tuple < S, A, P, R, γ >, where S and A
749
      represent state and action spaces respectively. The transition dynamics of the MDP are defined by the
750   function P : S × A × S → [0, 1], which represents the probability of transitioning from a give state
751   s with action a to state s′ . The reward function is represented by R : S × A × S → R, and γ ∈ (0, 1)
752   is the discount factor. At each time step t, an agent observes the state of the environment, denoted
753   as st , and selects an action at according to a policy π(a|s). One time step later, the agent receives a
754   numerical reward rt+1 and transitions to a new state st+1 . In the simplest case, the return is the sum
755   of the rewards when the agent–environment interaction naturally breaks into subsequences, which
      we refer to episodes (Sutton, 2018).

      C.1   BASELINES
788
789   EWC: Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) addresses the challenge of catastrophic forgetting by
790   allowing neural networks to retain proficiency in previously learned tasks even after a long hiatus. It
791   achieves this by selectively slowing down learning for weights that are crucial for retaining knowl-
792   edge of these tasks. This approach has demonstrated excellent performance in sequentially solving
793   a series of classification tasks, such as those in the MNIST handwritten digit dataset, and in learning
794   several Atari 2600 games sequentially.
795   ANCL: Auxiliary Network Continual Learning (ANCL) is an innovative approach that incorpo-
796   rates an auxiliary network to enhance plasticity within a model that primarily emphasizes stability.
797   Specifically, this framework introduces a regularizer that effectively balances plasticity and stability,
798   achieving superior performance over strong baselines in both task-incremental and class-incremental
799   learning scenarios.
800   Importance-based NBSP: To assess the significance of individual neuron, Paik et al. (2019) em-
801   ploy a criterion based on Taylor expansion, a technique commonly used in network pruning. They
802   normalize this criterion and apply exponential moving averages (EMA) to retain the importance
803   derived from previous tasks. Then Important neurons are consolidated by applying lower learning
804   rates, thereby preserving knowledge from earlier tasks while continuing to learn new ones. We
805   replace the RL skill neurons with important neurons to create the Importance-based NBSP.

      The observation space has a consistent dimensionality of 39, although different dimensions corre-

1182
1183   Discussion. As shown in Section 3.1, the order in which tasks are learned significantly impacts
1184   the agent’s performance. Regardless of the sequence, stability loss or plasticity loss occurs. If the
1185   agent learns a more difficult task first, it struggles to retain memory after mastering the new task.
1186   Conversely, starting with an easier task may preserve some knowledge but hinder the ability to learn
1187   subsequent tasks. Thus, task order is crucial for balancing stability and plasticity, warranting further
       investigation.

       Future work. The neuron analysis introduced in this work offers a novel approach for identifying
       RL skill neurons, significantly enhancing the balance between stability and plasticity in DRL. The
1277
       identification of RL skill neurons opens up several promising directions for future research and ap-
1278
       plications, such as: (1) Model Distillation: by focusing on RL skill neurons, it becomes possible
1279   to distill models by pruning less relevant neurons, leading to more efficient and compact models
1280   with minimal performance degradation. (2) Bias Control and Model Manipulation: RL skill neu-
1281   rons could be leveraged to control biases and modify model behaviors by selectively adjusting their
1282   activations. This approach could be particularly valuable in scenarios requiring specific outputs or
1283   behaviors.
1284
       Regarding to the NBSP method, its applicable potential extends beyond DRL. It could also be
1285   adapted to other learning paradigms, such as supervised and unsupervised learning, to address sim-
1286   ilar stability-plasticity challenges. In future work, we plan to explore these extensions and verify
1287   their effectiveness across various domains.
