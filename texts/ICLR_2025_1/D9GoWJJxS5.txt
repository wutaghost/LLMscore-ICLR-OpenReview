011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In contrast to moderate-size neural network pruning, structural weight pruning
014            on the Large-Language Models (LLMs) imposes a novel challenge on the effi-
015            ciency of the pruning algorithms, due to the heavy computation/memory demands
016            of the LLMs. Recent efficient LLM pruning methods typically operate at the post-
017
               training phase without the expensive weight finetuning, however, their pruning
               criteria often rely on heuristically hand-crafted metrics, potentially leading to
018
               suboptimal performance. We instead propose a novel optimization-based struc-
019
               tural pruning that learns the pruning masks in a probabilistic space directly by
020            optimizing the loss of the pruned model. To preserve the efficiency, our method
021            eliminates the back-propagation through the LLM per se during the optimiza-
022            tion, requiring only the forward pass of the LLM. We achieve this by learning
023            an underlying Bernoulli distribution to sample binary pruning masks, where
024            we decouple the Bernoulli parameters from the LLM loss, thus facilitating an
025            efficient optimization via a policy gradient estimator without back-propagation.
026            As a result, our method is able to 1) operate at structural granularities of chan-
027            nels, heads, and layers, 2) support global and heterogeneous pruning (i.e., our
028
               method automatically determines different redundancy for different layers), and
               3) optionally initialize with a metric-based method (for our Bernoulli distri-
029
               butions). Extensive experiments on LLaMA, LLaMA-2, LLaMA-3, Vicuna, and
030
               Mistral using the C4 and WikiText2 datasets demonstrate that our method oper-
031            ates for 2.7 hours with around 35GB memory for the 13B models on a single A100
032            GPU, and our pruned models outperform the state-of-the-arts w.r.t. both perplexity
033            and the majority of various zero-shot tasks. Codes will be released.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   With the rapid development of Large Language Models (LLMs) Brown et al. (2020); Achiam et al.
039   (2023) and their expanding multitude of applications across various domains, the efficiency of LLMs
040   with vast parameters and complex architectures becomes crucial for practical deployment. In this
041   paper, we aim to compress the LLM through structural pruning, which removes certain structural
042   components such as channels or layers to reduce the model size with hardware-friendly acceleration.
043   Pioneering structural pruning in the pre-LLM era involves pruning channels or layers through op-
044   timization, which determines the structures to prune by back-propagating the task loss through the
045   networks Liu et al. (2018); Blalock et al. (2020); Zhu & Gupta (2017); Louizos et al. (2017); Gale
046   et al. (2019); Frankle & Carbin (2018). These methods operate at the in-training Huang & Wang
047   (2018); Evci et al. (2020); Zhao et al. (2019); He et al. (2018a) or post-training Molchanov et al.
048   (2019); Wang et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021) phase, where the latter exhibit better efficiency without
049   model weights update. We thus focus on post-training pruning in the following to ensure efficiency.
050   However, the heavy computational and memory demands of LLMs make existing optimization-
051   based pruning methods (even post-training ones) less appropriate for LLM pruning in terms of
052   efficiency. Metric-based pruning is introduced to alleviate this issue, which directly prunes spe-
053   cific network components based on carefully designed criteria, such as the importance score Sun
      et al. (2023); Das et al. (2023). Nonetheless, those criteria are often based on heuristics. As a re-

      In view of the above analysis, our proposed method is essentially a novel lightweight optimization-
      based method, where it 1) efficiently avoids the back-propagation through the heavy LLM, 2) nat-
092
      urally supports flexible pruning structural granularities such as channels, heads (of multi-head at-

096   ficient metric-based methods and requires the same memory overhead, such as Shao et al. (2023);

098   pruning of the entire LLM into a probabilistic space (that can be optionally initialized by an arbi-
099   trary metric-based approach), which eliminates the magnitude difference issue of most metric-based
100   methods, therefore directly facilitating global and heterogeneous pruning across the entire LLM.

104   probabilistic space of Bernoulli distributions to sample those binary masks. By decoupling the
105   Bernoulli parameters and the sampled masks, our method results in a disentanglement of the

      2       R ELATED W ORK
135   Pruning has proven effective in traditional deep neural networks Han et al. (2015); Frankle & Carbin
136   (2018); Kurtic et al. (2022); Gordon et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2019); He et al. (2018b); Zhong et al.
137   (2018), and extensive research has been conducted on this topic. Typically, post-pruning perfor-
138   mance is restored or even enhanced through full-parameter fine-tuning Liu et al. (2018); Blalock
139   et al. (2020). However, for large language models (LLMs) with their vast number of parameters,
140   full-parameter fine-tuning is computationally expensive and often impractical. To overcome this
141   challenge, various pruning strategies Ko et al. (2023); Xia et al. (2023a); Jaiswal et al. (2023);
142   Zhang et al. (2024a); Chen et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2024); Ashkboos et al. (2024); Dery et al.
143
      (2024); Men et al. (2024); Fang et al. (2024); Sreenivas et al. (2024); Shen et al. (2024); Song et al.
      (2024); Gromov et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024) have been developed for LLMs in recent years. These
144
      strategies can be categorized into metric-based pruning and optimization-based pruning.
145
146   Metric-based Pruning. Metric-based pruning methods focus on designing importance metrics for
147   model weights or modules. The most representative method is Wanda Sun et al. (2023). It intro-
148   duces a simple but effective pruning metric by considering both the magnitude of weights and acti-
149
      vations without updating model parameters. LLM-Pruner Ma et al. (2023) efficiently trims LLMs
      by pinpointing and eliminating non-essential coupled structures, assessing weight importance via
150
      loss change, and using Taylor expansion to preserve performance. SparseGPT Frantar & Alistarh
151
      (2023) propose an efficient technique for estimating the Hessian matrix to reconstruct the model.
152   These methods use pre-defined pruning metrics and often face challenges with high pruning rates.
153   Most recently, Dery et al. (2024) proposes a structured pruning method with only forward passes
154   with promising performance. However, it still relies on regressing the heuristically hand-crafted
155   criteria, e.g., the utility of the pruned sub-networks. Moreover, Dery et al. (2024) also leverages ad-
156   ditional assumptions that might not hold universally, e.g., the network’s utility as a linear summation
157   of building elements’ utilities, and the building element’s utility remaining the same/average-able
158   across different sub-networks.

194   3     M ETHODOLOGY
195
196   We introduce our optimization-based pruning for LLMs, which is efficient without back-propagation
197   through the LLM. We detail our probabilistic mask modeling in Sect. 3.1, and our optimization via
198   policy gradient estimator in Sect. 3.2. The overview of our method is illustrated in Fig. 2.
199
      3.1   P RUNING VIA P ROBABILISTIC M ASK M ODELING
200
201   We formulate the network pruning as seeking binary masks Srinivas et al. (2017) to determine
202   whether the corresponding structure should be pruned or retrained. Those binary masks are further
203   modeled by/sampled from underlying Bernoulli distributions stochastically. Such formulation
204   possesses several merits: 1) the mask formulation enables flexible pruning at channels, heads (of
205   Multi-Head Attention, MHA), and layers; 2) the probabilistic Bernoulli modeling facilitates
206
      global and heterogeneous pruning across the entire LLM; and 3) our stochastical sampling decou-
      ples Bernoulli parameters and the sampled masks from LLM loss thus empowers an efficient
207
      policy gradient optimization without back-propagate through the LLM (see Sect. 3.2).

      3.2   O PTIMIZATION BY P OLICY G RADIENT E STIMATOR
241
242   Conventional neural network training paradigm usually adopts back-propagation to estimate the
243   gradient of Eq. (3), e.g., through Gumbel-Softmax Maddison et al. (2016); Dupont et al. (2022)
244   which reparameterizes the mask m as a function of s, i.e., mi = ϕ(si ) or mi = ϕ(si , ϵ) with
245   ϵ ∼ N (0, 1). However, the back-propagation has the following intrinsic issues in LLM pruning.
246   Remark 2 Intrinsic issues of back-propagation in LLM pruning: 1) the back-propagation is com-
247   putationally expensive and costs a large amount of memory; 2) the computation of gradients can not
248   be satisfied by using the sparsity in m, i.e., ∂m
                                                     ∂si ̸= 0 even if mi = 0. In other words, one has to go

      6     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
516   Limitations and Future Works. Firstly, as an optimization-based pruning, our method requires
517   more (but affordable) training time for optimization (e.g., 2.7 hours for LLaMA-2-13B) compared to
518   the (heuristic) metric-based methods, while our method benefits from significantly improved perfor-
519   mance with a similar memory complexity (35GB for LLaMA-2-13B, as both only require forward).
520
      Secondly, there exist advanced policy gradient algorithms with potentially lower variance from the
521
      reinforcement learning community Schulman et al. (2017). As 1) the primary focus of this paper
522   is on the back-propagation-free formulation of the LLM pruning problem, and 2) our formulation
523   guarantees dense rewards at each step, we thus use a basic policy gradient algorithm similar to
524   REINFORCE Williams (1992) with simple variance reduction using a moving average baseline. We
525   leave exploiting more powerful policy gradient algorithms as our future work.
526
      Lastly, the performance of the proposed method on specific domains/tasks can rely heavily on the
527
      availability of domain-specific datasets. Though the cross-dataset evaluation is verified w.r.t. per-
528   plexity, using only the C4 dataset for pruning might introduce a negative influence on some particu-
529   lar cross-dataset zero-shot tasks such as WinoGrande Sakaguchi et al. (2021) and Hellaswag Zellers
530   et al. (2019), as shown in the Tables 1 and A5 of Appendix A.7.
531
      Conclusion. We propose an efficient optimization-based structural pruning method for LLMs,
532
      which 1) does not need back-propagation through the LLM per se, 2) enables global and hetero-
533
      geneous pruning, and 3) supports pruning granularities of channels, heads, and layers. Our method
534   can optionally take a metric-based pruning as initialization to achieve a further improved perfor-
535   mance. We implement our method by learning an underlying Bernoulli distribution of binary
536   pruning mask. As we decouple the Bernoulli parameter and the sampled masks from the LLM
537   loss, the Bernoulli distribution can thus be optimized by a policy gradient estimator without
538   back-propagation through the LLM. Our method operates for 2.7 hours with 35GB of memory on
539   a single A100 GPU to prune the LLaMA-2 13B model. Extensive experiments on various LLM
      models and sizes with detailed ablation analysis validate our promising performance.

      A.3   A R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING P ERSPECTIVE
906
907
908
      Our formulation can also be interpreted from the dense-reward model-free reinforcement learning
      perspective. Particularly, the heavy LLM can be viewed as the agnostic and fixed environment.
909
910   In terms of the Markov Decision Process (MDP) (action a, states s, state transition probabil-
911   ity p, reward r, discount factor γ), the environment takes the action a sampled from the cur-
912   rent Bernoulli policy π to insert the binary masks for pruning, produces the states s as the
913   masked/pruned network deterministically (i.e., the state transition probability p is constantly 1),
914
      and generate the stepwise dense reward r as the performance (e.g., the cross-entropy loss) of the
      pruned LLM. Since our problem exhibits dense rewards, therefore the discount factor γ is 1.
915
916   As a result, our policy to take actions, i.e., the Bernoulli distribution to sample the binary masks,
917   can be learned efficiently exploiting the policy gradient estimator (similar to REINFORCE), without
      back-propagating through the agnostic and fixed environment of the heavy LLM.

       As the initialization of our Bernoulli policy should be probabilistic values between 0 and 1,
       but the metrics calculated by the metric-based methods Sun et al. (2023); An et al. (2023); Ma
1166

1167
       initialization. We introduce two projection strategies from metric values m to probabilities s. The
1168   first is called Sigmoid-Norm strategy, which is applied in our main experiments:
1169
                                              s = sigmoid(Norm(x))                                   (A10)
1170
1171   where Norm(·) is used to linearly normalize the input to a Gaussian distribution with 0 mean and
1172   unit variance, then sigmoid(·) is used to transform the input to [0, 1].
1173
       An alternative second strategy is named Score-Const. It straightforwardly sets the mask 1 from
1174
       metric-based methods as a constant c, and mask 0 as 1 − c:
1175
1176
                                                      c,     if mi = 1,
                                               si =                                                    (A11)
1177                                                  1 − c, if mi = 0,
1178   The constant c is set to 0.8 in the following experiments, indicating that the initialized Bernoulli
1179   probability of the remaining modules is 0.8 and those to be pruned is 0.2.
1180   The results of different projection strategies on LLaMA-2-7B/13B are detailed in Table A10, which
1181   shows that the Sigmoid-Norm projection outperforms its Score-Const counterpart for most cases. It
1182   may be because the order-preserving projection strategy of Sigmoid-Norm preserves more informa-
