009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep extreme classification (XC) aims to train an encoder and label classifiers
012            to tag a data point with the most relevant subset of labels from a very large
013            universe of labels. XC applications in ranking, recommendation and tagging
014            routinely encounter tail labels, for which the amount of training data is exceedingly
015            small. One way to tackle the tail label problem is to use additional data - often
016
               structured as a graph associated with documents and labels - graph metadata.
               Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) present a convenient but computationally
017
               expensive way to leverage this graph metadata and enhance model accuracies in
018
               these settings. However, GCNs struggle to make predictions for a novel test point
019            when it has no edge in the graph. The paper notices that in these settings, it is much
020            more effective to use graph data to regularize encoder training than to implement a
021            GCN. Based on these insights, an alternative paradigm RAMEN is presented to
022            utilize graph metadata in XC settings that offers a significant performance boost
023            with zero increase in inference computational costs. RAMEN scales to datasets with
024            millions of labels and offers prediction accuracy up to 15% higher on benchmark
025            datasets than state of the art methods, including those that use graph metadata to
026            train GCNs. RAMEN also offers 10% higher accuracy over the best baseline on a
027
               proprietary recommendation dataset sourced from click logs of a popular search
               engine. Code for RAMEN will be released publicly upon acceptance.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Extreme classification (XC) refers to a supervised machine learning paradigm where multi-label
033   learning must be performed on extremely large label spaces. Thus, a data point must be annotated with
034   a subset of labels most relevant to it. The ability of XC to handle enormous label sets with millions of
035   labels makes it an attractive choice for applications such as product recommendation (Medini et al.,
036   2019; Dahiya et al., 2021b; Mittal et al., 2022; Kharbanda et al., 2022), document tagging (Babbar
037   & Schölkopf, 2017; You et al., 2019; Chang et al., 2020), search & advertisement (Prabhu et al.,
038   2018b; Dahiya et al., 2021b; Jain et al., 2016), and query recommendation (Jain et al., 2019; Chang
039
      et al., 2020). The key appeal of XC comes from the prospect of accurately tagging rare/tail labels
      relevant to a data point. Recommendations for rare but relevant objects can meaningfully improve
040
      user experience and the ability to associate rare tags with objects such as web documents can offer
041
      fine-grained object descriptions. A label is called tail if very few training data points are tagged
042   with that label. XC applications can exhibit extreme label skew and more than 75% of the labels
043   could appear in fewer than 10 training points (Jain et al., 2016; Dean, 2020). The tail problem is
044   further aggravated due to missing labels since tail labels are also at higher risk of going missing (Jain
045   et al., 2016). In solving the tail-data problem, XC approaches rely on metadata. Beyond textual label
046   descriptions (Mittal et al., 2021a; Dahiya et al., 2021a; 2023), the auxiliary metadata can augment
047   the meagre supervision available for tail labels and is typically available in the form of multi-modal
048   descriptions such as images (Mittal et al., 2022), or graphs (Mittal et al., 2021b; Saini et al., 2021).
049   In this paper, we focus on graph data which can be inferred in several applications, e.g., hyperlink
050
      graphs for document tagging and queries co-occurring in the same search session for ad placement.
051   Graph metadata in XC: Graph metadata has been used in XC to (a) enhance item representations,
052   and (b) handle missing labels. Examples of the former include OAK (Mohan et al., 2015), Graph-
053   Former (Yang et al., 2021), and PINA (Chien et al., 2023) which use textual descriptions of an item
      along with graph metadata to learn item embeddings via graph convolutional networks (GCN). These

076   Limitations of GCN Methods: Graphs can also be misleading in terms of linkages, and GCN’s im-
077   plementation posses limited applicability for real-world application. As an example of noisy linkages
078
      in graphs, consider the LF-WikiSeeAlsoTitles-320K hyperlink graph. Traversal over the graph can
      also lead to irrelevant labels such as “Vestigial organs” and extracting meaningful information from
079
      such noisy graphs is a challenge. Although the use of textual and graph metadata can offer enhanced
080
      model accuracy in XC and recommendation settings (Mittal et al., 2021b; Saini et al., 2021; Yang
081   et al., 2021; Chien et al., 2023), the use of GCN architectures makes both training and inference more
082   expensive (table 3). XC training is made challenging by the sheer size of training sets often containing
083   millions of data points and labels, necessitating some form of negative sampling (Mikolov et al.,
084   2013; Guo et al., 2019; Rawat et al., 2021; Reddi et al., 2018; Xiong et al., 2021). On the other hand,
085   most XC applications demand real-time inference i.e., the set of labels relevant to a test data point
086   must be identified within milliseconds. GCNs require the (bulky) graph to be preserved at inference
087   time to embed a test data point which increases inference time and makes deployment challenging.
088   This paper addresses the limitations of using graph metadata in XC. Our primary research question
089
      is: How do we leverage graph metadata to perform accurate prediction for rare labels with zero
      increase in inference time?

      1.1   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
094
095
096   To address the above question, we propose gRaph regulArized encoder training for extreME classifi-
097   catioN (RAMEN). RAMEN is a method to effectively utilize graph metadata at scale with minimal
098   overheads in training cost and zero overhead in model size or inference time (Table 3). RAMEN
099
      can be incorporated into existing XC systems in a modular manner with few alterations (Table 9).
      The key insights leading to RAMEN include a formal proof (cf. Theorm 1) that (a) in several use
100
      cases, GCN layers can be approximated by (much cheaper) non-GCN architectures and, (b) it is more
101
      effective to use graph data to regularize encoder training than it is to implement a GCN. RAMEN can
102   handle multiple graphs – graphs over data points, graphs over labels, or both – and offers increased
103   prediction accuracy, even when presented with noisy graphs (Section 4). While the RAMEN encoder
104   is trained using the metadata graph, during inference, unlike baseline GCNs, RAMEN does not
105   require graph traversal, significantly improving latency (cf. Figure 1). RAMEN scales to datasets
106   with up to 360M labels and can offer up to 15% higher prediction accuracies over state-of-the-art
107   methods including those that use graph metadata to train GCN. Code for RAMEN will be released
      publicly.

120   2    R ELATED WORK
121
122   Extreme classification (XC) is a key paradigm in several areas such as ranking and recommendation.
123   The literature on XC methods is vast (Medini et al., 2019; Dahiya et al., 2021b; Babbar & Schölkopf,
124   2017; You et al., 2019; Prabhu et al., 2018b; Jain et al., 2016; 2019; Guo et al., 2019; Mittal et al.,
125   2021a;b; Saini et al., 2021; Wydmuch et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2017; Jiang et al.,
126   2021; Chalkidis et al., 2019; Ye et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Mineiro & Karampatziakis, 2015;
127   Jasinska et al., 2016; Khandagale et al., 2020; Tagami, 2017; Yen et al., 2017; Wei et al., 2019;
128   Siblini et al., 2018; Barezi et al., 2019; Gupta et al., 2019; 2023). Early XC methods used fixed
129   (bag-of-words) (Babbar & Schölkopf, 2017; Prabhu et al., 2018b; Jain et al., 2016; Mineiro &
130
      Karampatziakis, 2015; Jasinska et al., 2016; Khandagale et al., 2020; Tagami, 2017; Yen et al.,
      2017; Wei et al., 2019; Siblini et al., 2018; Barezi et al., 2019) or pre-trained (Jain et al., 2019)
131
      features and focused on learning only a classifier architecture. Recent advances have demonstrated
132
      significant gains by using task-specific features obtained from a variety of deep encoders such as
133   bag-of-embeddings (Dahiya et al., 2021b; 2023), CNNs (Liu et al., 2017), LSTMs (You et al., 2019),
134   and transformers (Jiang et al., 2021; Chalkidis et al., 2019; Ye et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021).
135   Training is scaled to millions of labels and training points (Dahiya et al., 2021b) by performing
136   encoder pre-training followed by classifier training. A data point is trained only on its relevant labels
137   (that are usually few in number) and a select few irrelevant labels deemed most informative using
138   negative mining (Mikolov et al., 2013; Guo et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2021; Dahiya et al., 2021a;
139   2023; Faghri et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020a; Karpukhin et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2019;
140   Luan et al., 2020; Hofstätter et al., 2021; Qu et al., 2021).
141   Label Metadata in XC: Most XC methods use textual representation as label metadata since they
142   allow scalable training and inference and allow leveraging good-quality pre-trained deep encoders
143   such as RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019b), DistilBERT base (Sanh et al., 2019), etc. Examples in-
144   clude encoder-only models such as DEXML (Gupta et al., 2024), TwinBERT (Lu et al., 2020) and
145   ANCE (Xiong et al., 2021) and encoder+classifier architectures such as DECAF (Mittal et al., 2021a),
146   SiameseXML (Dahiya et al., 2021a), X-Transformer (Chang et al., 2019), XR-Transformer (Chang
147
      et al., 2020), LightXML (Jiang et al., 2021), ELIAS (Zhang et al., 2021) and others (Ye et al., 2020;
      Liu et al., 2019a; You et al., 2019; Chalkidis et al., 2019). There is far less literature on the use of
148
      other forms of label metadata. For instance, ECLARE (Mittal et al., 2021b) and GalaxC (Saini et al.,
149
      2021) use graph convolutional networks whereas MUFIN (Mittal et al., 2022) explores multi-modal
150   label metadata in the form of textual and visual descriptors for labels.
151
152   Graph Neural Networks in Related Areas: A sizeable body of work exists on using graph neural
153
      networks such as graph convolutional networks (GCN) for recommendation (Yang et al., 2021; Mohan
      et al., 2015; Hamilton et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2018; Chiang
154
      et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021; He et al., 2020b; Yang et al., 2022). Certain methods
155
      e.g., FastGCN (Chen et al., 2018), KGCL (Yang et al., 2022), LightGCN (He et al., 2020b) learn
156   item embeddings as (functions of) free vectors. This makes them unsuitable for making prediction
157   for a novel test point. Other GCN-based methods such as OAK (Mohan et al., 2015), PINA (Chien
158   et al., 2023), GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2018) and GraphFormers (Yang et al., 2021) learn node
159   representations as functions of node metadata e.g. textual descriptions. This allows the methods
160   to work in zero-shot settings but they still incur the high storage and computational cost of GCNs.
161   Moreover, diminishing returns are observed with increasing number of layers of the GCN (Mittal
      et al., 2021b; Chiang et al., 2019) with at least one model, namely LightGCN (He et al., 2020b)

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      foregoing all non-linearities in its network, effectively opting for a single-layer GCN. It must be
163   noted that GCN’s can be highly accurate if one can have an oracle to predict relevant nodes(table 2).
164   However, such oracle is never available online and the slightest error in first stage retrieval leads to
165   poor retrieval quality. (sec. 4).

      We now develop the RAMEN method that offers a far more scalable alternative to GCNs and other
167
      popular graph-based architectures in XC settings, significantly reducing the overheads of graph-based

      learning, yet offering sustained and significant performance boosts in prediction accuracies.

197   Intuition behind RAMEN: A popular way to incorporate graph information into XC and rec-
198   ommendation tasks is to take initial embeddings of a data point from an encoder and use a
199   graph convolution step to obtain augmented embeddings for the data point. For example, let
200   X ∈ RN ×D = [x1 , . . . , xN ]⊤ be the initial embeddings of the N data points over which a graph
201
      with adjacency matrix A ∈ [0, 1]N ×N is present. A typical layer in a GCN performs an operation
      of the form ϕ(AXW ) ∈ RN ×D where W ∈ RD×D is a transformation matrix and ϕ : R → R is
202
      some activation function applied coordinate-wise. Not only is this step expensive (Zeng et al., 2020;
203
      Hamilton et al., 2018), but also offers diminishing returns with increasing number of layers (Chiang
204   et al., 2019; Mittal et al., 2021b). Theorem 1 indicates that in cases where the adjacency matrix can
205   be well-predicted using a non-GCN network (say feedforward or transformer) over the initial features,
206   the convolutional layer can be well approximated by a non-GCN network as well. Note that edge
207   prediction is often possible with high accuracy since the metadata graph available is closely linked to
208   the prediction task at hand and Table 6 confirms this for the tasks considered in this paper. RAMEN
209   uses this result to infer that it may be less useful to perform graph convolutions on top of a reasonably
210   powerful encoder such as a transformer. Instead, utilizing the graph for regularization is cheaper yet
211   effective. Theorem 1 is specified and proved in Appendix E. Extensions of Theorem 1 to networks
212
      with multiple GCN layers are also discussed.
213   Theorem 1 (Informal). Let there exist a non-GCN (e.g. feedforward, transformer etc) network F :
214   X → S P −1 where S P −1 is the the unit sphere in RP , that effectively predicts edges in the metadata
215   graph for any i, j ∈ [N ], aij ≈ (1+F(xi )⊤ F(xj ))/2 where A = [aij ] is the adjacency matrix of the
      graph, then there exists another non-GCN network H such that ϕ(AXW ) ≈ [H(x1 ), . . . , H(xN )]⊤ .

      Negative Mining: The loss function and regularizers contain O (N L log L + (N + L)M log M )
271   terms where M = max {M1 , M2 } is the maximum number of anchors in any of the anchor sets. This
272   is because the number of relevant labels per data point is usually limited by |l : yil = +1| ≤ O (log L)
273   in XC applications (Jain et al., 2016) and we can construct the metadata graphs to have at most log M
274   relevant anchors per data point or label. Performing optimization with respect to all these terms is
275   expensive which is why RAMEN utilizes in-batch negative mining (Guo et al., 2019; Dahiya et al.,
276   2021a; 2023; Faghri et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020a). Specifically, a set of data
277   points is identified and for each data point, and a random relevant label and random related anchor
278   are chosen (from each anchor set if there are multiple anchor sets). For each of the chosen labels, a
279
      random related anchor is chosen from each anchor set. Then, hard negative labels for a data point are
      chosen only amongst those labels selected for that particular mini-batch. Similarly, hard negative
280
      anchors for a data point or label are chosen from only those anchors selected for that mini-batch.

      accuracy by 2-3% in P@1.

503   Ablations on the Design: Experiments were conducted to understand the impact of the design
504   choices made by RAMEN (ANCE) as well as the impact of metadata on the performance of RAMEN
505   (ANCE). Table 5 complies these experiments. In particular, experiment “No-Bandits” explored
506   the effects of different choices of giving weights to different sources of metadata on RAMEN’s
507   performance. A key finding was that when uniform weights were assigned to all graphs, there was a
508
      substantial 18% drop in P@1. In addition to that, similarly accuracy of RAMEN (ANCE) for different
      initialization of graph weights proved robustness of bandit learning. This highlights the importance
509
      of bandit learning, where each graph’s contribution is determined dynamically. RAMEN (ANCE)’s
510
      robust training strategy extends benefits beyond its own performance. NGAME, SiameseXML and
511   ECLARE, baseline methods, experienced improvements of 3% and 5% in P@1, respectively, when
512   leveraging RAMEN (ANCE)’s metadata regularizer. (Table 9).
513
514
      Ablations on the Graph: To understand the impact of noisy edges in metadata, experiment "No
      Pruning" disabled the trimming of noisy edges using cosine similarity filtering. A 4% loss in P@1
515
      was observed which underscores the necessity of pruning unhelpful edges during training. RAMEN
516
      (ANCE) uses multiple meta-data graphs for both document and label. To ascertain the contributions
517   of the anchor-doc and anchor-label metadata graphs, the ablations "No Doc. Graph" and "No Lbl.
518   Graph" were conducted. These experiments reveal that information from these graphs plays a
519   significant role, as disabling either leads to a 1.5–2% reduction in P@1. The information from these
520   graphs can be incorporated in baseline methods like ANCE. To understand its impact, experiment
521   “AugGT’ trains ANCE with augmented ground truth. The ground truth was expanded by using label
522   propagation wherein a label and a training point are linked by an edge if the label shares a neighbor
523   in the metadata graph of the said training point. RAMEN (ANCE) outperformed the “AugGT” setup
524   by 15%. This suggests that while leveraging graph information for ground truth enhancement is
525
      convenient, it may not be as effective due to noisy edges. RAMEN (ANCE) uses multiple sources of
      graph metadata, Table 10 in appendix shows the RAMEN (ANCE) benefits when all graph metadata
526
      is used but remains state of the art even if it uses only hyperlink metadata, similar to OAK. For more
527
      experimental details refer to Appendix A.

530   5    C ONCLUSION
531
532   This paper presented RAMEN, a novel approach for leveraging metadata to enhance the accuracy of
533   recommendation systems w.r.t. tail labels. A key takeaway from the study is that opting for graph-
534
      based regularization instead of the more prevalent GCN architectures, can yield gains of up to 15% in
      PSP@1/P@1 and up to 6% when compared with XC techniques tailored for recommendation systems.
535
      The bandit-style regularization technique adopted by RAMEN was found to offer performance boosts
536
      to baseline methods. Notably, RAMEN offers state-of-the-art performance without incurring any
537   computational overhead during inference.

      B    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
865
866
      Links obtained on the metadata graph from raw data suffer from missing links in much the same
867   way there are missing labels in the ground truth. To deal with this, RAMEN performs a random
868   walk with restart on each anchor node. The random walk was performed for 400 hops with a restart
869   probability of 0.8, thus ensuring that the walk did not wander too far from the starting node. This
870   random walk could also introduce noisy edges, leading to poor model performance. To deal with
871   such edges, in-batch pruning was performed and edges to only those anchors were retained which
872   had a cosine similarity of > 0 based on the embeddings given the encoder. To get the encoder,
873   RAMEN initialize the encoder with a pre-trained DistilBERT and fine-tuned it for 10 epochs(warmup
874   phase) using unpruned metadata graphs. Then the metadata graphs were pruned using the fine-tuned
875
      encoder. Encoder fine-tuning was then was continued for 5 epochs using the pruned graphs after
      which the graphs were re-pruned. These alternations of 5 epochs of encoder fine-tuning followed by
876
      re-pruning were repeated till convergence. The learning rate for each bandit was set to 0.01. Table 16
877
      in supplementary material summarizes all hyper-parameters for each dataset. It is notable that even
878   though RAMEN uses a graph at training time, inference does not require any such information,
879   making it highly suitable for long-tail queries.

1005   For Figure 4 and Table 8, labels were divided into 5 equi-voluminous quantiles. To each label l ∈ [L],
1006   a popularity score Vl = |i : yil = +2| was assigned by counting number of training datapoints tagged
                                                                                    def P
1007   with that label. The total volume of all labels was computed as Vtot =            l∈[L] Vl . Labels were
1008   arranged in decreasing order of their popularity score Vl . 5 label quantiles were then created so that
1009   the volume of labels in each bin is roughly ≈ Vtot /5. Thus, labels were collected in the first bin in
1010   decreasing order of popularity till the total volume of labels in that bin exceeded Vtot /5 at which point
1011   the first bin was complete and the second bin was created by selecting remaining labels in decreasing
1012   order or popularity till the total volume of labels in the second bin exceeded Vtot /5 and so on. For
1013
       example, for the LF-WikiTitles-500K dataset, the five bins were found to contain approximately
       1K, 9K, 30K, 84K, 375K labels respectively. Note that the first bin contains very few ≈ 1K labels
1014
       since these are head labels and a small number of them quickly racked up a total volume of ≈ Vtot /5
1015
       whereas the last quantile contains more than 100× more labels at around 375K labels since these are
1016   tail labels and so a lot more of them are needed to add up to a total volume of ≈ Vtot /5.
