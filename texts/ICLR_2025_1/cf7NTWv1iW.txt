011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The auto-regressive decoding of Large Language Models (LLMs) results in signifi-
014            cant overheads in their hardware performance. While recent research has investi-
015            gated various speculative decoding techniques for multi-token generation, these
016            efforts have primarily focused on improving processing speed such as throughput.
017
               Crucially, they often neglect other metrics essential for real-life deployments, such
               as memory consumption and training cost. To overcome these limitations, we
018
               propose a novel parallel prompt decoding that requires only 0.0002% trainable pa-
019
               rameters, enabling efficient training on a single A100-40GB GPU in just 16 hours.
020            Inspired by the human natural language generation process, PPD approximates
021            outputs generated at future timesteps in parallel by using multiple prompt tokens.
022            This approach partially recovers the missing conditional dependency information
023            necessary for multi-token generation, resulting in up to a 28% higher acceptance
024            rate for long-range predictions. Furthermore, we present a hardware-aware two-
025            stage tree pruning algorithm that adaptively optimizes this decoding scheme to
026            fully leverage the computational capacities on different GPUs. Through extensive
027            experiments across LLMs ranging from MobileLlama to Vicuna-13B on a wide
028
               range of benchmarks, our approach demonstrates up to 2.49× speedup and main-
               tains a minimal runtime memory overhead of just 0.0004%. More importantly, our
029
               parallel prompt decoding can serve as an orthogonal optimization for synergistic
030
               integration with existing speculative decoding, showing up to 1.22× further speed
031            improvement. Our code will be open-sourced upon acceptance of the paper.

123   2       BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
124
125   To enhance the inference speed of LLM, various approaches adopt an iterative guess-and-verify
126   strategy to enable multi-token generation. In the guessing phase, potential future tokens are proposed
127   at a faster speed than in traditional autoregressive implementations. Subsequently, a parallelized
128   verification process assesses which guessed tokens should be accepted. Depending on how tokens
129   are generated during the guess stage, these approaches can generally be categorized as i) speculative
130   decoding and ii) parallel decoding.
131
132   2.1       S PECULATIVE D ECODING
133
134   The guessing phase of speculative decoding adopts a lightweight draft model to generate multiple
135
      tokens at an increased speed (Kim et al., 2024). During the verification stage, the original LLM
      subsequently determines the acceptance of the guessed tokens. It is worth noting that both draft
136
      and original models still follow the auto-regressive inference scheme. The speedup comes from two
137
      factors: i) the draft model runs much faster than the original model and more tokens can be generated
138   within the same time unit; and ii) token verification is executed concurrently, either by batching or by
139   incorporating multiple candidates into a single input using customized sparse attention masks (Miao
140   et al., 2024). Therefore, the overall speedup depends on the acceptance rate and the inference latency
141   of draft models.
142
      Building on the speculative decoding scheme, various studies have been conducted to further optimize
143
      its inference speed. To improve the accuracy of the draft model, Eagle (Li et al., 2024a) incorporates
144   the hidden features into the draft model’s forward pass. Recently, Eagle-2 (Li et al., 2024b) enhances
145   their approach using a context-aware dynamic tree construction. However, both Eagle and Eagle-2
146   utilize a separate draft model for multi-token generation, diverging fundamentally from our prompt
147   decoding approach. Moreover, their dynamic tree construction scheme is an orthogonal technique
148   to our two-stage tree pruning method. SpecInfer (Miao et al., 2024) adopts a tree-based speculative
149   inference and verification scheme, improving the diversity of speculation candidates. Sequoia (Chen
150   et al., 2024) optimizes the sparse tree structure of speculative decoding by considering the capability
151   of the underlying hardware platforms. Our tree pruning algorithm differs from Sequoia by accounting
152
      for two types of tokens in the tree: prompt tokens and guess tokens, whereas Sequoia only considers
      guess tokens. Furthermore, their methods require the storage and maintenance of a separate draft
153
      model, and there is extra complexity in designing an efficient draft model.
154
155
      2.2       PARALLEL D ECODING
156
157   To overcome the inherent limitations of autoregressive inference and the memory overhead associated
158   with using a separate draft model, several attempts have been made to integrate both guessing and
159   verification using one unified model. Medusa1 (Cai et al., 2024) introduces language model (LM)
160   heads at the final layer of the original LLM, facilitating the generation of multiple tokens in a single
161

              We categorize Medusa as parallel decoding because it only adopts LM heads instead of separate models.

      forward pass. It also utilizes tree attention masks in its verification process to increase speed even
163   further. To enhance token drafting with retrieval-augmented generation (Karpukhin et al., 2020),
164   Rest (He et al., 2023) introduce retrieval-based decoding tailored for specific scenarios. Inspired
165   by Jacobi decoding (Santilli et al., 2023) that adopts multiple special tokens to accelerate machine
166   translation, Lookahead Decoding (Fu et al., 2024) improves upon this method by generating parallel
167   n-grams and employing a caching memory pool. To capture more information while using multiple
168   special tokens at distinct positions, PaSS (Monea et al., 2023) trains additional tokens with embedding
169   layers for parallel decoding. Hierarchical parallel decoding (Liu et al., 2024) introduces the use of
170   [F ork] and [Join] tokens, enabling parallel execution of multiple structural subroutines.
171   Our approach can be categorized as parallel decoding, with two novel features to distinguish it from
172   other approaches: 1) PPD trains the embeddings of parameterized ensemble prompt tokens, 2) it
173   utilizes a hardware-aware two-stage tree pruning algorithm for designing a sparse tree tailored to
174   each hardware platform.

      3       PARALLEL P ROMPT D ECODING (PPD)
177
178
      The primary advantage of PPD lies in training embeddings for prompt tokens rather than developing
179
      a separate model. Our method integrates three substeps into a single decoding step, following the
180
      guess-and-verify strategy: (1) candidate generation, where multiple candidate continuations2
181   are predicted by strategically inserting the prompt tokens into the input sequence. We adopt tree
182   attention (Miao et al., 2024) to merge the processing of multiple candidates into a single forward pass;
183   (2) candidate verification, where two verification schemes, exact matching (Fu et al., 2024) and
184   typical acceptance (Cai et al., 2024), are implemented; (3) candidate acceptance, where validated
185   candidates are integrated into the input and KV cache is updated accordingly. Figure 2 presents the
186   inference scheme of combining generation and verification steps in a single forward pass.
187
188   3.1       P ROMPT T OKENS
189
190   The prompt tokens are the key component of PPD to realize multi-token generation. Initially
191   introduced by Lester et al. (2021) to adapt LLMs for specific tasks, prompt tokens are typically
192   prepended to the input, with outputs generated in an autoregressive manner. In this work, we propose
193
      a novel approach of utilizing prompt tokens by strategically positioning them at locations where
      tokens are anticipated to be generated in parallel.
194
195   In the standard decoding process, the probability of predicting the next token is expressed as the
196   conditional probability p(yi+1 |x, y1:i ), where x is the input prompt, y1:i are the i tokens generated so
197   far, and yi+1 is the next token to be predicted. For conventional parallel decoding techniques (Stern
198   et al., 2018; Cai et al., 2024) that presume complete conditional independence among tokens decoded
199
      in a single step, the exact conditional probability is approximated by
200
201                                      p(yi+k+1 |x, y1:i+k ) = pθ (yi+k+1 |x, y1:i )
202
203   where k > 0 indicates the token distance.3 However, we observe that as k increases, the gap
204   between the actual probability and its approximation expands, primarily due to the absence of relevant
205   conditional dependencies. We argue that prompt tokens can bridge this gap by more accurately
206   modeling the conditional probability as
207
208                                 p(yi+k+1 |x, y1:i+k ) = pθ (yi+k+1 |x, y1:i , ti+1:i+k )
209
210   where ti is the prompt token with token distance i. Through this forward pass in the decoder
211   layers, these causally linked prompt tokens facilitate the flow of information along the sequence of
212   speculative tokens, thus restoring the conditional probability. We demonstrate the effectiveness of
213   this approach in Section 5.2.

      3.2   E NSEMBLE P ROMPT T OKENS
217
218   Inspired by prompt ensembling (Lester et al., 2021), which uses multiple prompts to generate diverse
219   responses and aggregates these to derive a single answer, we introduce the concept of ensemble
220   prompt token (EPT). This additional abstraction allows us to decouple each prompt token from the
221   fixed embedding dimension. For every prompt token, there exist multiple corresponding EPTs, each
222   with its distinct embedding. We modify the attention mask to ensure that each nth EPT only depends
223   on the corresponding nth EPTs from preceding prompt tokens. This selective visibility is maintained
224   for both training and inference, where the guess token for each prompt token is determined by
225   averaging the logits of its EPTs. The use of EPTs not only enables direct and flexible control over
226
      the trainable parameters, but also leads to an increase in prediction accuracy. The probability is
                           Pn                        j                j
227
      approximated as n1 j=1 pθ (yi+k+1 |x, y1:i , vi+1:i+k ), where vi+m  denotes the j th EPT at a token
228
      distance of m. Further details about EPTs can be found in Appendix D.

230   3.3   T RAINING
231
232   During training, only the embeddings of prompt tokens are changed, with the parameters of the
233   original LLM remaining frozen. We adopt the following two training techniques:
234
      Random Insertion of Prompt Tokens: Randomly inserting prompt tokens throughout the input
235   sequence reduces contextual bias from appending them only at the end. This approach broadens the
236   predictive capacity of prompt tokens beyond a limited vocabulary such as <eos> and punctuation.
237
238
      Knowledge Distillation: To align the predictive behavior of prompt tokens with the original LLM,
      we employ knowledge distillation. Instead of using hard labels, prompt tokens are trained against
239
      the logits produced by the original LLM. Following Medusa (Cai et al., 2024), the loss function is
240
      formulated as:

251   4.1   C USTOMIZED S PARSE T REE ATTENTION
252
253   Tree attention, introduced by SpecInfer (Miao et al., 2024), increases the expected acceptance rate by
254   considering the top-k candidates from a single decoding step. In their approach, the input is structured
255   as a tree, where each level of the tree corresponds to a specific output position. An attention mask
256   is applied to the tree-structured input, allowing the model to process multiple candidates efficiently
257
      without increasing the batch size.
258   To improve the efficiency and performance of LLM inference, this paper proposes a novel sparse
259   tree customized for PPD, which prioritizes candidates in the tree structure with higher prediction
260   accuracy. A key difference from previous works (Cai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) is the appending
261   of a sequence of prompt tokens to each guess token. The length of the prompt token sequence decides
262   the maximum depth of the speculative tree at the next decoding step. To further hide the latency
263
      introduced by the extra prompt tokens, we propose a novel tree pruning algorithm (Section 4.2) that
      optimizes the number of prompt tokens at each guess token.

284   4.2.1     S TATIC T REE P RUNING
285   The first stage, static tree pruning, is applied offline prior to runtime deployment. The goal is to
286   reduce the number of prompt tokens in the tree to achieve the desired tree size. As shown on the left
287   side of Figure 3, the tree pruning process consists of three key steps:
288
289           1. Candidate Trees Construction: Building trees using only candidate tokens at varying
290              depths, employing the algorithm from Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) and Sequoia (Chen et al.,
291              2024) to maximize f (Tk ).
292           2. Prompt Tokens Appending : Attaching the maximum allowable prompt tokens to each
293              candidate token from the first step.
294           3. Greedy Prompt Token Removal: Removing a prompt token greedily to maximize expected
295              amortized acceptance lengths, continuing until the desired prompt token budget is reached.
296
297
      Each guess token in the tree is appended with a sequence of prompt tokens, with each prompt
      token corresponding to a unique output position. The length of this sequence determines the tree’s
298
      maximum depth at the next decoding step. Thus, removing a prompt token at a guess token reduces
299
      the maximum tree depth at the next decoding step if this guess token is accepted in the current step.
300   Let pc represent the acceptance probability of guess token c, and fd denote the expected acceptance
301   length with d prompt tokens before removal. The decrease in expected acceptance length, ∆F , due
302   to removing a prompt token at c is given by ∆F = pc · (fd − fd−1 ). More details are discussed in
303   Appendix A.

         6                          C ONCLUSION
527
       We introduced PPD, a memory-efficient, cost-effective, and powerful parallel decoding method
528
       that incorporates a hardware-aware online tree optimization. Utilizing specially trained prompt
529
       tokens to predict long-range tokens accurately, PPD achieves a speedup of up to 2.49× in inference
530    while employing only 0.0002% additional trainable parameters without incorporating new models or
531    architectural components. We showcased that PPD offers a novel perspective on the capabilities of
532    parallel decoding. Importantly, it could be synergized with other speculative or parallel decoding
533    techniques to expedite inference even further. We hope that by open-sourcing the code base (upon
534    acceptance of the paper), PPD can help the community further advance the performance of real-world
535    deployment of the current and future decoder-based LLM models.

      A    D ETAILED T REE C ONSTRUCTION A LGORITHM
703
704   We follow the same optimal sparse tree construction approach as in Medusa (Cai et al., 2024).
705
      Definition A.1. Let m be the maximal number of prompt tokens per tree node. The sparse tree T can
706   exist in m states, each represented by Tk corresponding to state sk , where 1 ≤ k ≤ m. Let C(Tk )
707   denote the subtree of Tk composed solely of candidate tokens. The maximum depth of C(Tk ) is k.
708   Proposition A.1. For a sparse tree state Tk , where each candidate token v follows a path Path(v)
709   from the root, and the acceptance probability Ppk at each
                                                             Q path position k, the
                                                                                  Q expected number of
710   tokens f (Tk ) generated is given by f (Tk ) = v∈C(Tk ) i∈Path(v) pi , where i∈Path(v) pi represents
711   the contribution of a token v to the expected number of tokens.

      D.3   P REFIX T UNING + P ROMPT T OKEN
865
866   Prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), similar to prompt tuning, provides a parameter-efficient approach
867   to fine-tune a pre-trained model. Unlike prompt tuning, it modifies the KV cache of every attention
868   layer by prepending trained vectors. We hypothesize that the combination of prefix tuning and prompt
869   tokens can lead to greater learning capacity and higher prediction accuracy. This hypothesis is based
870   on the intuition that prompt tokens should see a different context than the input tokens when predicting
871   long-range tokens. For example, if the input sequence is ”Once upon a time”, then enhancing the
872
      input with a prompt template might provide more suitable semantic context for long-range prediction.
      An enhanced input like ”Predict the next-next token. Once upon a time” might empower the prompt
873
      token to predict the correct next-next token. Prefix tuning serves as the prompt template to enhance
874
      the hidden states visible to the prompt tokens.

959   D.5     ATTENTION M ASKING FOR EPT S
960
961   In this paper, we proposed a specialized attention mask for EPTs to achieve the effect of prompt
962   ensemble. However, there are alternative masking strategies available. Here, we describe and compare
963   three types of attention masks that we implemented and experimented with.
964
965
      D.5.1    E NSEMBLE ATTENTION M ASKING
966   The ensemble attention masking is the masking strategy we previously described. In this approach,
967   EPTs are divided into n disjoint groups, where n is the number of EPTs per prompt token. All k th
968   EPTs across prompt tokens are placed in the same group. An EPT v in group i can only attend to
969   EPTs that meet the following two criteria: 1) they must belong to group i, and 2) their position indices
970   must be smaller than the position index of v. Since this masking strategy effectively averages the
971   results of disjoint groups of EPTs, we refer to it as the ”ensemble attention masking”. Figure 13a
      provides an example of the ensemble attention masking.

1053   from the last k decoder layers are extracted and averaged to produce the final hidden state, which
1054   is then decoded by the decoding head into a guess token, as illustrated in Figure 14. This approach
1055

1120
1121         1. GPU compute resource constraint. Since PPD trades additional compute resources for
1122
                increased throughput, its effectiveness depends on the availability of idle GPU compute

1123
                are expected to decrease.
1124
1125         2. Extended input length. The improvement in acceptance length with PPD is not as signif-
1126
                icant as the gain in prediction accuracy compared to Medusa. This is because PPD must
                reserve a substantial portion of the input for prompt tokens, which limits the size of the
1127
