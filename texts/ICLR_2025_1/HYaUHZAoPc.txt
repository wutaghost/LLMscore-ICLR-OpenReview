010                                                          A BSTRACT
011
012              Reinforcement Learning (RL) applied in healthcare can lead to unsafe medical de-
013              cisions and treatment, such as excessive dosages or abrupt changes, often due to
014              agents overlooking common-sense constraints. Consequently, Constrained Rein-
015
                 forcement Learning (CRL) is a natural choice for safe decisions. However, spec-
                 ifying the exact cost function is inherently difficult in healthcare. Recent Inverse
016
                 Constrained Reinforcement Learning (ICRL) is a promising approach that infers
017
                 constraints from expert demonstrations. ICRL algorithms model Markovian deci-
018              sions in an interactive environment. These settings do not align with the practical
019              requirement of a decision-making system in healthcare, where decisions rely on
020              historical treatment recorded in an offline dataset. To tackle these issues, we pro-
021              pose the Constraint Transformer (CT). Specifically, 1) we utilize a causal attention
022              mechanism to incorporate historical decisions and observations into the constraint
023              modeling, while employing a Non-Markovian layer for weighted constraints to
024              capture critical states. 2) A generative world model is used to perform exploratory
025              data augmentation, enabling offline RL methods to simulate unsafe decision se-
026
                 quences. In multiple medical scenarios, empirical results demonstrate that CT
                 can capture unsafe states and achieve strategies that approximate lower mortality
027
                 rates, reducing the occurrence probability of unsafe behaviors.

054
      This paper aims to achieve safe healthcare policy learning to mitigate unsafe behaviors. The most
055   common method for learning safe policies is Constrained Reinforcement Learning (CRL) (Liu et al.,
056   2021; 2022), with the key to its success lying in the constraints representation. However, in health-
057   care, we can only design the cost function based on prior knowledge, which limits its application due
058   to a lack of personalization, universality, and reliance on prior knowledge. For more details about is-
059   sues, please refer to Appendix C. Inverse Constrained Reinforcement Learning (ICRL) (Malik et al.,
060   2021) emerges as a promising approach, as it can infer the constraints adhered to by experts from
061   their demonstrations. However, existing ICRL methods face the following challenges in healthcare:
062   1) The Markov decision 3 is not compatible with medical decisions. ICRL algorithms model
063   Markov decisions, where the next state depends only on the current state and not on the history
064   (Kijima, 2013; Zhang et al., 2023). However, in healthcare, the historical states of patients are
065   crucial for medical decision-making (Plaisant et al., 1996). Therefore, ICRL algorithms based on
066   Markov assumption can not capture patient history, and ignore individual patient differences, thereby
067   limiting effectiveness. 2) Interactive environment is not available for healthcare or medical
068   decisions. ICRL algorithms (Malik et al., 2021; Gaurav et al., 2022) follow an online learning
069
      paradigm, allowing agents to explore and learn from interactive environments. However, exploration
      in healthcare often entails unsafe behaviors that could breach constraints and result in substantial
070
      losses. Therefore, it is necessary to infer constraints using only offline datasets.
071
072   In this paper, we introduce offline Constraint Transformer (CT), a novel ICRL framework that in-
073   corporates patients’ historical information into constraint modeling and learns from offline data to
074   infer constraints in healthcare. Specifically,
075   1) Inspired by the recent success of sequence modeling (Zheng et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021; Kim
076   et al., 2023), we incorporate historical decisions and observations into constraint modeling using a
077   causal attention mechanism. To capture key events in trajectories, we introduce a Non-Markovian
078   transformer to generate constraints and cost weights, and then define constraints using weighted
079   sums. CT takes trajectories as input, allowing for the observation of patients’ historical information
080   and evaluation of key states.
081   2) To learn from an offline dataset, we introduce a model-based offline RL method that simultane-
082   ously learns a policy model and a generative world model via auto-regressive imitation of the actions
083   and observations in medical decisions. The policy model employs a stochastic policy with entropy
084   regularization to prevent it from overfitting and improve its robustness. Utilizing expert datasets,
085   the generative world model uses an auto-regressive exploration generation paradigm to effectively
086   discover a set of violating trajectories. Then, CT can infer constraints in healthcare through these
087
      unsafe trajectories and expert trajectories.
088   In the medical scenarios of sepsis and mechanical ventilation, we conduct experimental evaluations
089   of offline CT. Experimental evaluations demonstrate that offline CT can capture patients’ unsafe
090   states and assign higher penalties, thereby providing more interpretable constraints compared to
091   previous works (Huang et al., 2022; Raghu et al., 2017a; Peng et al., 2018). Compared to uncon-
092   strained, custom constraints and LLMs constraints (designed by Large Language Models (LLMs)),
093
      CT achieves strategies that closely approximate lower mortality rates with a higher probability (im-
      proving by 8.85% compared to DDPG). To investigate the avoidance of unsafe behaviors with offline
094
      CT, we evaluate the probabilities of “too high” and “sudden changes” occurring in the sepsis. The
095
      experimental results show that CRL with CT can reduce the probability of unsafe behaviors to zero.

100   Reinforcement Learning in Healthcare. RL has made great progress in the realm of healthcare,
101
      such as sepsis treatment (Huang et al., 2022; Raghu et al., 2017a; Peng et al., 2018; Do et al.,
      2020), mechanical ventilation (Kondrup et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2020), sedation
102
      (Eghbali et al., 2021) and anesthesia (Calvi et al., 2022; Schamberg et al., 2022). However, the
103
      works mentioned above have not addressed potential safety issues such as sudden changes or too
104   high medications. Therefore, the development of policies that are both safe and applicable across
105   various healthcare domains is crucial.

      Inverse Constrained Reinforcement Learning. Previous works inferred constraint functions by
109   determining the feasibility of actions under current states. In discrete state-action space, Chou et al.
110   (2020) and Park et al. (2020) learned constraint sets to differentiate constrained state-action pairs.
111   Scobee & Sastry (2019) proposed inferring constraint sets based on the principle of maximum en-
112   tropy, while some studies (McPherson et al., 2021; Baert et al., 2023) extended this approach to
113   stochastic environments using maximum causal entropy (Ziebart et al., 2010). However, discrete
114   approaches often face limitations when scaling to high-dimensional problems. As the state-action
115   space increases, the computational cost rises significantly. This makes inference in large, discrete
116   spaces challenging, requiring additional optimization techniques or assumptions. In continuous do-
117
      mains, Malik et al. (2021), Gaurav et al. (2022), and Qiao et al. (2024) used neural networks to
      approximate constraints. Some works (Liu et al., 2022; Chou et al., 2020) applied Bayesian Monte
118
      Carlo and variational inference to infer the posterior distribution of constraints in high-dimensional
119
      state space. Xu & Liu (2023) modeled uncertainty perception constraints for arbitrary and epistemic
120   uncertainties. However, these methods can only be applied online and lack historical dependency.
121
122   Transformers for Reinforcement Learning. Transformer has produced exciting progress on RL
123
      sequential decision problems (Zheng et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021; Janner et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
      2023). These works no longer explicitly learn Q-functions or policy gradients, but focus on action
124
      sequence prediction models driven by target rewards. Chen et al. (2021) and Janner et al. (2021)
125
      perform auto-regressive trajectories modeling to achieve offline policy learning. Furthermore, Zheng
126   et al. (2022) unify offline pretraining and online fine-tuning within the Transformer framework. Liu
127   et al. (2023) and Kim et al. (2023) integrate the transformer architecture into constraint learning
128   and preference learning. With its sequence modeling capability and independence from the Markov
129   assumption, the transformer architecture can capture temporal dependencies in medical decision-
130   making. Thus, it is well-suited for trajectory learning and personalized learning in medical settings.

174   Safe-Critical Decision Making with Constraint Inference in Healthcare. Our general goal for
175   our policy is to align the expert policy, which refers to the strategy under which the patient’s mor-
176   tality rate is minimized (achieving a zero mortality rate is often difficult since there are patients
177   who can not recover, regardless of all potential future treatment sequences (Fatemi et al., 2021)).
178
      Decision-making with constraints can formulate safer strategies by discovering and avoiding unsafe
      states, thereby aligning the expert policy.
179
180   However, most offline RL algorithms rely on online evaluation, where the agent is evaluated in
181   an interactive environment, whereas in medical scenarios, only offline policy evaluation (Luo et al.,
182   2024a) can be utilized. Besides, some works (Jia et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2022; Raghu et al., 2017b;
183   Komorowski et al., 2018) analyze by comparing the differences (DIFF) between the drug dosage
184
      recommended by the estimated policy π and the dosage administered by clinical physicians π̂, and
      the relationship of DIFF with mortality rates, through graphical analysis. In the graph depicting
185
      the relationship between the DIFF and mortality rate, at the point when DIFF is zero, the lower the
186
      mortality rate of patients, the better the performance of the policy (Raghu et al., 2017b).

334   structure is used to extract historical information for both the policy and the generative world models.
335   During training, the causal transformer is trained alongside the above models, with the goal of
336
      simultaneously minimizing Equations 7 and 8 until the convergence of the models.
337   Generating Violating Data. In RL, excessively high rewards, surpassing those provided by domain
338   experts, may incentivize agents to violate the constraints to maximize the total reward (Liu et al.,
339   2022; 2023). Therefore, we set a high initial target reward R̂1 to obtain violation data. We feed R̂1
340                      (i)                                                 (i)
      and initial state s1 into the model-based offline RL to generate τv in an auto-regressive manner,

342   target reward R̂ decreases incrementally and can be represented as R̂t+1 = R̂t − ret . Considering the
343   average error in trajectory prediction, we generate trajectories with the length K = 10. Repeating

525   6       C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we propose offline CT, a novel ICRL algorithm designed to address safety issues in
528   healthcare. This method utilizes a causal attention mechanism to observe patients’ historical infor-
529   mation, similar to the approach taken by actual doctors, and employs Non-Markovian importance
530
      weights to effectively capture critical states. To achieve offline learning, we introduce a model-
      based offline RL for exploratory data augmentation to discover unsafe decisions and train CT. Ex-
531
      periments in sepsis and mechanical ventilation demonstrate that our method avoids risky behaviors
532
      while achieving strategies that closely approximate the lowest mortality rates.
533
      Limitations. There are also several limitations of offline CT: 1) Lack of rigorous theoretical analy-
534   sis. We did not precisely define the types of constraint sets, thereby conducting rigorous theoretical
535   analysis on constraint sets remains challenging. 2) Need for more computational resources. Due
536   to the Transformer architecture, more computational resources are required. 3) Unrealistic assump-
537   tions of expert demonstrations. We assume that the policy capable of treating patients to survival is
538   the expert policy. However, in reality, this assumption may not always hold. Therefore, researching
539   a more effective approach to address the aforementioned issues holds promise for the field of secure
      medical reinforcement learning.

890   designed by Liu et al. in the Bullet safety gym environments (Liu et al., 2023). We define the cost

892   maximum dosage thresholds of the two medications, it incurs a cost due to constraint violation.
893
894                                c (s, a) = 1 (aIV > aIVmax ) + 1 (aVaso > aVasomax )                             (11)

896
      where, s and a represent the patient’s state and action, respectively. aIV max = 2000 indicates that

897
      Vaso dosage is 1µg/(kg · min).
898
899   We applied our custom constraint function in the CDT (Liu et al., 2023) method, and the results are
900   shown in Figure 10. Compared to the Vaso dosage recommended by doctors, our strategy exhibits
901   excessive suppression of the Vaso. The maximum dosage of Vaso is 0.0011µg/(kg · min), which is
902
      minimal and insufficient to provide the patient with effective therapeutic effects.
903   Therefore, Equation 11 is not suitable. The primary issues may include uniform constraint strength
904   for excessive drug dosages, for instance, the cost for IV exceeding 2000 mL and IV exceeding
905   3000 mL is the same at 1; lack of generalization, where the constraint cost does not vary with the
906   patient’s tolerance. If a patient has an intolerance to VASO, the maximum value for VASO maybe 0,
907   which cannot be captured by the self-imposed constraint function. Moreover, it lacks generalization,
908
      requiring redesign of the constraint function when addressing other unsafe medical issues; and it’s

1266
       Currently, some studies (Yoon et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2024b; Brophy et al., 2023) have proposed
1267
       simulation modeling approaches to address the challenges of directly testing RL-based dynamic
1268   treatment regimes in clinical environments. However, since the existing online testing systems (such
1269   as DTR-Bench (Luo et al., 2024b)) do not provide expert data in their simulation environment, and
1270   the offline method proposed in this paper requires expert datasets to train a safe policy, we are unable
1271   to use online testing systems for evaluation. In the future, we can establish an offline testing system
1272   to enable the testing of offline reinforcement learning strategies.
