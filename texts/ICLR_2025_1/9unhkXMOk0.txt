009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series representations learned from high-dimensional data, often referred
012            to as ”disentanglement” are generally expected to be more robust and better at
013            generalizing to new and potentially out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios. Yet, this
014            is not always the case, as variations in unseen data or prior assumptions may insuffi-
015            ciently constrain the posterior probability distribution, leading to an unstable model
016            and non-disentangled representations, which in turn lessens generalization and
017            prediction accuracy. While identifiability and disentangled representations for time
018
               series are often said to be beneficial for generalizing downstream tasks, the current
               empirical and theoretical understanding remains limited. In this work, we provide
019
               results on identifiability that guarantee complete disentangled representations via
020
               Contrastive Sparsity-inducing Learning, which improves generalization and inter-
021            pretability. Motivated by this result, we propose the TimeCSL framework to learn
022            a disentangled representation that generalizes and maintains compositionality. We
023            conduct a large-scale study on time series source separation, investigating whether
024            sufficiently disentangled representations enhance the ability to generalize to OOD
025            downstream tasks. Our results show that sufficient identifiability in time series rep-
026            resentations leads to improved performance under shifted distributions. Our code is
027            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TimeCSL-4320.

      Indeed, without identifiability, the representation of a model can be unstable and not consistent (Lo-
080   catello et al., 2019; Lenc & Vedaldi, 2015), in the sense that retraining the same model under small
081   perturbations of the data or hyperparameters may result in wildly different representations. More
082   formally, identifiability means that the parametrization of the model is injective (Roeder et al., 2021;
083   Khemakhem et al., 2020b). Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) methods, known for their flexibility
084   and efficiency (Liu et al., 2021), have improved supervised tasks via unsupervised learning, with
085   early nonlinear ICA work unintentionally using SSL (Hyvärinen & Morioka, 2016; Bai et al., 2021a;
086   Hyvärinen et al., 2023). However, as these methods are not always probabilistic, identifiability can be
087   uncertain, although uniqueness is defined more broadly.
088   In this work, we combine SSL with probabilistic modeling and sparsity to achieve identifiability for
089   time series representation up to affine transformations—essentially, disentangled representation for
090   time series via Contrastive Sparsity-inducing Learning (TimeCSL) (see Fig. 1, across 4 runs, the
091   latents are recovered, providing evidence of the latent space recovery up to the affine transformations).
092   Importantly, this can be achieved with commonly adopted weaker assumptions. Specifically, we
093   allow for statistically dependent latent factors, with empirical evidence indicating that relaxing
094
      independence improves OOD generalization (Roth et al., 2023; Oublal et al., 2024). Moreover, it
      requires no complete auxiliary data, handles nonlinear predictors and latent relationships for time
095
      series, and reduces reliance on labeled data via contrastive learning. Our contributions include:
096
097   [1] We rely on the sparsity assumption of time series representation, and provide theoretical in-
098       sight and empirical arguments on how, and under which condition, identifiability up to affine
099       transformation is preserved. We show that TimeCSL outperform an affine transformation
100       e.g., permutation and element-wise transformation.
101
      [2] Unlike many existing identifiability results, we allow for arbitrary dependencies without para-
102
          metric assumptions, achieving slot latent disentanglement through Partial Selective Pairing.
103
          This approach is particularly suitable for time series, where obtaining fully labeled data can be
104       challenging.

238   In this section, we begin with the intuition behind the proposed approach, which leverages sparsity
239   in the mixing process to achieve identifiability. Previous methods relying on independence or non-
240   Gaussian priors for identifiability often fail in nonlinear cases, as marginal transformations can
241   preserve independence without revealing true structure (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999; Hyvärinen
242   et al., 2019). We build on the insight that any alternative solution introducing indeterminacy, beyond
243   permutations or component-wise transformations, would result in a denser structure. Rather than
244   constraining functional forms (Taleb & Jutten, 1999; Ahuja et al., 2023) or relying on auxiliary
245
      variables (Khemakhem et al., 2020a), we assume Partial Contrastive Sparsity for time series. This
      enables learning identifiable and disentangled representations without requiring independence or
246
      parametric assumptions on p(z). In the following subsection, we present Partial contrastive Pairing.
247

251   general case, Partial Selective Pairing, which allows pairs to share only a subset of relevant
252
      factors, serving as a relaxation of Selective Pairing in SSL. Assuming the data process generat-
      ing Eq. (2.2), we define the shared support indices S of all sources that actively contribute to x as

254
      x′ is based on shared support I(x, x′ ) := S(x) ∩ S(x′ ).

      On the Possibility of Sufficient Partial Pairing In Thm. 4.2, we demonstrated how slot identifiability
358
      can be achieved on Z and OOD Zc under the compositionality condition in Eq. (4.6). A key insight
359
      is the sufficient partial pairing for contrastive learning (Asm 4.1). This assumption can be relaxed
360   to factor groups when the dataset is complex enough to discern varying features (e.g., in weather
361   time series). For such cases, grouping factors avoids assumption violations. We validated our results
362   on synthetic time series data (assumptions fully satisfied) and energy separation tasks, were used to
363   relax assumptions via grouping factors. Data was prepared in pairs (x, x′ ), with additional samples
364   generated as needed to cover all factors.
365
      Conditions on the Network. We proposed ResTimeCSL (see Fig. 3), an efficient architecture
366   for time series modeling that doesn’t violate Asm 2.1. It projects time series patches into high-
367   dimensional embeddings and processes them sequentially using a cross-patch linear sublayer and a
368   cross-channel two-layer MLP, similar to the Transformer’s FCN sublayer. Each sublayer includes
369   residual connections, two affine element-wise transformations, and uses ReLU or LeakyReLU
370   activations. For training, we leverage a VAE model with a mixture of Gaussians (Jiang et al., 2016)
371   for a fixed latent dimension by n and d, optimizing the objective LVAE . We sample i.i.d. pairs
372   (x, x′ ) ∈ X . Using a learnable encoder fˆϕ , x (resp. x′ ) is encoded into [µ̂ϕ k (x) , σ̂ϕ k (x)]⊤ (resp.
373   [µ̂ϕ k (x′ ) , σ̂ϕ k (x′ )]⊤ ) with reparameterization noise terms (Kingma & Welling, 2022). The inferred
374   latents are (ẑ, ẑ′ ). A learnable decoder ĝθ maps ẑ (resp. ẑ′ ) to single-source outputs ŷk = gθ k (ẑ)
375   (resp. ŷk′ = ĝθ k (ẑ′ )) for k = 1, · · · , n. Summing over these outputs reconstructs the mixed signals
376   x̂ (resp. x̂′ ). In practice, the sparsity of the ground truth variables z is unknown, so we instead
377   set a hyperparameters η for the sparsity constraint. Furthermore, for more stability, instead of

401   Datasets and Evaluation Setup. We conducted experiments for time series representation with
402   separation task on three public real datasets: UK-DALE (Kelly & Knottenbelt, 2015), REDD (Kolter
403   & Johnson, 2011), and REFIT (Murray et al., 2017) providing power measurements from multi-
404   ple homes. 60% of the data is used for training with additional 10% of data augmentation, while
405   the remaining 40% of real data is evenly divided between validation and testing. Inputs are zero-
406   mean normalized, we consider T = 256, C = 1 and number factors/sources n = 5: Fridge (FR),
407   Dishwasher (DW), Washing Machine (WM), Heater (HTR), and Lighting (LT). The mixed obser-
408
      vation may include unlabeled noise factors. Synthetic Dataset: we generate a nonlinear mixing
      observations with n = 3, from ground truth available signals of {FR, LT, HTR} from UK-DALE,
409
      REDD, and REFIT with adding some Gaussian noise. To generate OOD scenarios Tab. 2 i.e., strong
410
      correlation between factors, we adopt  the methodology outlined in (Träuble et al., 2021) where
411   p(y1 , y2 ) ∝ exp −||y1 − αy2 ||2 /2σ 2 and adjusting the parameter σ to control the correlation.
412
413   Metrics. To assess slot identifiability, we follow (Locatello et al., 2020) by fitting nonlinear
414   regressors to predict each ground-truth slot zk from inferred slots ẑj , evaluating the fit with the R2
415   score. Slot assignments are optimized via the Hungarian algorithm (Kuhn, 1955), and we report
416   the average R2 over matched slots. Additionally, we use the Mean Correlation Coefficient (MCC)
417   metric (Khemakhem et al., 2020a), reporting both strong MCC (before affine alignment) and weak
418
      MCC (after alignment). All MCCs are computed out-of-sample: the affine map Γ is fitted on one
      half of the data and evaluated on the other. RMIG (Robust Mutual Information GAP) (Do & Tran,
419
      2019), and DCI (Disentanglement, Completeness and Informativeness) (Eastwood & Williams, 2018)
420
      adapted for time series are used to evaluate the disentanglement of factors i.e., sources. We provide
421   in-depth details of metrics and their implementation in App. B.4.
422
423   Contrastive Partial Selective Pairing Pipeline. Four augmentations were sequentially applied to
424   all contrastive methods’ pipeline branches. The parameters from the random search are: 1) Crop and
425   delay: applied with a 0.5 probability and a minimum size of 50% of the initial sequence. 2) Cutout
426   or Masking: time cutout of 5 steps with a 0.8 probability. 3) Channel Masks powers: each time
427   series is randomly masked out with a 0.4 probability. 4) Gaussian noise: random Gaussian noise is
428
      added to window input x with a standard deviation form 0.1 to 0.3. Further details in App. B.3.

522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this work, we delved into the effectiveness of contrastive sparsity-inducing techniques in attaining
525
      both identifiability and generalization. We showcased that disentangled representations, comple-
      mented by sparse-inducing methods through contrastive learning, improve generalization, particularly
526
      when the downstream task can be tackled using only a portion of the underlying factors of variation.
527
      Looking ahead, future investigations could explore leveraging such meaningful representations for
528   downstream tasks, as evidenced by our primary experiments demonstrating performance enhancement.
529   Furthermore, we posit that such representations could prove efficient in scenarios characterized by
530   limited labeled data for time series representation. We have demonstrated generalization through com-
531   positional representations. We built on the literature in generative models and nonlinear ICA (Kivva
532   et al., 2022; Hyvarinen et al., 2019; Lachapelle et al., 2022) and made two key assumptions: i) partial
533   sufficiency holds, which enables sparsity through contrastive learning, and ii) the decoder gθ is
534   injective. Our results are a step toward identifiability and disentanglement in time series models.
535   Limitations & Future Work We acknowledge that our assumptions on time series representation and
536   source separation have room for extension. The piecewise injectivity assumption (Asm 2.1), though
537   potentially violated in practice, could be revised to incorporate structures like attention mechanisms
538   or instance normalization. The Sufficient Partial Pairing assumption (Asm 4.1) depends on having
539   sufficient data, and as noted in § 4.1, it can also be relaxed to group factors. Looking ahead, these
      extensions offer exciting opportunities for further improving the model’s robustness and flexibility.

946   Compositional contrast In recent work on compositionality (Assouel et al., 2022; Zhao et al.,
947   2022; Kurth-Nelson et al., 2022) and its importance in learning models that can generalize well
948   to novel situations, the concept of compositional contrast has emerged as a powerful tool for
949   evaluating how well a model separates information into independent, non-interacting components.
950   This concept is particularly relevant in the context of time series analysis or image generation,
951   where the model’s ability to decompose an input into distinct parts, or ”slots,” can significantly
952
      impact the quality of predictions and interpretability. Compositionality ensures that each slot, or
      latent variable, corresponds to a specific factor or component of the data. In highly compositional
953
      models, these components do not interact with each other—each one affects a distinct aspect of the
954
      output. In contrast, non-compositional models tend to mix these components, making it harder to
955   disentangle the factors and interpret the model’s output. Evaluating how well a model adheres to
956   compositionality principles can be challenging, as it requires quantifying how independent the slots
957   are in their contribution to the final output. To address this, Brady et al. (2023) introduced the notion
958   of compositional contrast, which measures the extent to which the model’s latent variables (slots)
959   interact when producing the final output. This measure is particularly useful in determining whether
960   a decoder is truly compositional—that is, whether each slot contributes independently of the others,
961   or if there are unwanted interactions between them. Before we introduce the formal definition of
962   compositional contrast, it is important to understand the underlying principle. The intuition behind
963
      the compositional contrast is that if a model is fully compositional, each slot should affect only a
      specific subset of the output (e.g., one region of an image or one time series variable) and have
964
      no influence on other components. Conversely, if the model is not compositional, changes in one
965
      slot will influence multiple components of the output simultaneously, indicating that the slots are
966   not independent. The compositional contrast function captures this idea by calculating how much
967   the gradients of each slot (with respect to the model’s output) overlap. If the gradients of different
968   slots with respect to the same output component are non-zero, this suggests interaction between the
969   slots, indicating a lack of compositionality. The function sums these interactions across all slots
970   and output components, providing a single value that quantifies the degree of interaction. A lower
971   compositional contrast value suggests higher compositionality, while a higher value indicates more
      interaction between slots. Formally, the compositional contrast is defined as follows:

1491
       In implementing the disentanglement metrics, we adhere to the methodology outlined in (Locatello
1492   et al., 2019), expanding it to accommodate time series data. For the computation of DCI metrics, we
1493   employ a gradient boosted tree from the scikit-learn package.
1494
1495   β-VAE Metric Disentanglement is then measured as the accuracy of a linear classifier that predicts
1496   the index of the fixed factor based on the coordinate-wise sum of absolute differences between the rep-
1497   resentation vectors in the two mini-batches. (Higgins et al., 2016) suggest fixing a random attributes
1498   of variation in the underlying generative model and sampling two mini-batches of observations x.
1499   We sample two batches of 256 points with a random factor fixed to a randomly sampled value across
1500   the two batches, and the others varying randomly. We compute the mean representations for these
1501
       points and take the absolute difference between pairs from the two batches. We then average these 64
       values to form the features of a training (or testing) point.
1502
1503
       FactorVAE Metric (Kim & Mnih, 2019) (Kim & Mnih, 2019) address several issues with this
1504
       metric by using a majority vote classifier that predicts the index of the fixed ground-truth attribute
1505
       based on the index of the representation vector with the least variance. First, we estimate the variance
1506   of each latent dimension by embedding 10k random samples from the data set, excluding collapsed
1507   dimensions with variance smaller than .05. Second, we generate the votes for the majority vote
1508   classifier by sampling a batch of 64 points, all with a factor fixed to the same random value. Third,
1509   we compute the variance of each dimension of their latent representation and divide it by the variance
1510   of that dimension computed on the data without interventions. The training point for the majority
1511   vote classifier consists of the index of the dimension with the smallest normalized variance. We train
       on 10k points and evaluate on 5k points.

1617   In this study, we evaluate the impact of different activation functions on the decoder’s behavior to
1618   satifies Asm 2.1. Specifically, we compare the use of ReLU (a piecewise affine activation) and GELU
1619   (a smooth, nonlinear activation) within an MLP decoder. The results suggest that the choice of
       activation function has a significant impact on the latent representation produced by the model.


                                                                                             30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
       ReLU Activation: The decoder becomes piecewise affine, meaning that it can be broken down into
1621   affine transformations over different regions of the input space. This causes the decoder to create
1622   latent representations that reflect distinct linear transformations in various regions of the input. As a
1623   result, the learned latent space is structured around these distinct affine regions, potentially making
1624   the model more sensitive to certain regions of the data space and leading to more discrete or sharply
1625   defined latent representations.
1626
1627
       LeakyReLU Activation: In contrast, the GELU activation is smooth and nonlinear across the
1628
       entire input space. This means that the decoder no longer operates piecewise affine, and the latent
1629   space learned by the model is more continuous and smooth. Since GELU smoothly transforms the
1630   input, it enables the decoder to create more nuanced, continuous latent representations. The absence
1631   of piecewise linear behavior allows for better modeling of complex, smooth relationships in the data,
1632   which may improve generalization to unseen data or tasks that require such smooth transformations.

       We evaluate TimeCSL on time-sequential data using preprocessed frames from the KITTI and
1775
       MOTSChallenge datasets. The original KITTI image resolutions are 1080 × 1920 or 480 × 640 for
1776   MOTSChallenge, and between 370–374 pixels tall by 1224–1242 pixels wide for KITTI MOTS. The
1777   video frame rates vary from 14 to 30 fps, as described in (Milan, 2016). To preprocess the data, we
1778   apply nearest-neighbor down-sampling to reduce each frame’s height to 64 pixels while maintaining
1779   the aspect ratio for the width. Using a horizontal sliding window, we extract six equally spaced
1780   windows of size 64 × 64 (with overlap) from each sequence in both datasets. This preprocessing
1781   produces a sequence of shape 64 × 64 × T , where T represents the number of time steps in the
       sequence. Our approach assumes reasonable invariance to horizontal translation and scale within
