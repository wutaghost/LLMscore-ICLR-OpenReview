009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving reasoning problems is an iterative multi-step computation, where a rea-
012            soning agent progresses through a sequence of steps, with each step logically
013            building upon the previous one to reach a desired conclusion. If the desired so-
014            lution is not attained, the agent must backtrack and try reasoning chains that are
015            quite different from previous attempts. Though prior work such as test-time search
016            against an outcome verifier can improve performance, most search is done in par-
017            allel via Best-of-N reranking, and independently for each attempt at a problem,
018            thus wasting a significant amount of computation in sampling multiple full solu-
019
               tions even beyond the point that is needed. Can we reduce the total amount of
               computation by sharing information and computation across multiple attempts to
020
               a given problem? In this paper, we build a novel approach combining process
021
               verifiers that predict likelihoods of success per step with preemptive backtracking
022            to maximize performance per generated token. To do this, the PRM can be used
023            to identify where a problematic step in a solution trace is by using the sensitivity
024            of the predictions of the learned verifier and allowing the model to do focused re-
025            sampling of the problematic portion of a solution. This approach can significantly
026            reduce the amount of computation by leveraging partial computation from previ-
027            ous revisions. To further enhance the computational efficiency of inference, we
028            introduce in-context process supervision, where the verifier is conditioned on the
029            history of revisions that are attempted, reducing uncertainty in the verification de-
030
               cisions and improving the verifier’s confidence with each round of backtracking.
               This framework for iterative backtracking, leveraging in-context process supervi-
031
               sion, enables an effective tradeoff between inference and model performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036   Solving reasoning problems in large language models (LLMs) involves drawing inferences, making
037   decisions with incomplete or ambiguous information, and solving problems in a structured, logical
038   manner. Reasoning problems are commonplace in many domains such as code development, where
039   reasoning is needed to understand complex logical structures, debug errors, and anticipate edge
040   cases, and mathematics, where it is vital for proving theorems and solving challenging problems.
041
      One promising direction to enhance response quality of responses in reasoning problems is the
042   strategic use of test-time computation, where a model is given an inference compute budget that it
043   could leverage to improve its solution quality. In fact, prior work such as Snell et al. (2024); Charniak
044   & Johnson (2005); Cobbe et al. (2021) explore different mechanisms for solution generation given a
045   fixed generation budget, exploring search algorithms such as BofN, Beam Search, and Look-Ahead
046   search. In scenarios where an LLM generates a complex sequence, small but consequential mistakes
047   made at intermediate steps can lead to a cascade of errors, rendering the entire sequence incorrect.
048   Traditional search methods, which operate in parallel and start from the beginning of the sequence,
049   fail to efficiently handle these errors, often wasting inference-time compute on parts of the solution
050
      that are already correct, thus failing to solve intermediate errors. Is there an approach that can more
      efficiently leverage inference-time compute for error recovery?
051
052   In this work, we propose backtracking-based iterative refinement for improving LLM solutions.
053   Instead of starting from the beginning with each new sample, our method identifies problematic
      steps in the solution trajectory and resamples only the parts of the sequence that need correction.

213   Let’s examine how a linear search algorithm like Best-of-N would perform in revising this incorrect
214   sequence. If we sequentially apply Best-of-N to revising an incorrect solution, and assume each
215   error is uncorrelated, the expected number of samples required to generate a fully correct sequence
      becomes exponential in the number of errors: E[N ] = 20150 . This requires an impractically


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      large number of samples to generate a fully correct sequence in expectation, rendering linear search
217   methods ineffective for this task.
218
219   Can we instead adaptively utilize our compute resources through non-linear search? A common
220
      failure mode for linear search is that the search can reach an unrecoverable state (e.g., a mistake in
      the last steps of a proof), leading to subsequent incorrect steps. By allowing the model to backtrack
221
      to a point in the solution before the irrecoverable state, we can give the model an opportunity to
222
      try again and correct its errors. Additionally, if multiple uncorrelated errors exist in a solution, this
223   process can be repeated until the problem is solved correctly or the sample budget is exhausted. This
224   adaptive approach prevents wasting computation on parts of the solution that are already correct.
225
226
      For intuition, let’s apply a sequential backtracking procedure to our didactic problem. For purposes
      of understanding, we start with the simpler scenario where we know the step where a mistake occurs
227
      from an oracle. We can backtrack to the step before the mistake and use the same linear search
228
      algorithm (Best-of-N) to correct this single mistake, which would require E[N ] = 20 samples in
229   expectation. By performing multiple iterations of revisions and retaining the best solution so far, we
230   can solve the problem in a much more tractable manner, with the total expected number of samples
231   being linear in the number of errors: E[N ] = 20 ∗ 150.
232
      This motivates the need for non-linear search approaches such as backtracking. While parallel algo-
233
      rithms like Best-of-N or Beam Search can perform well in some scenarios, they become computa-
234
      tionally expensive when revisions are required, as they necessitate generating a large, fixed number
235   of samples from the beginning of the solution. For challenging or long-horizon problems, this ap-
236   proach is computationally prohibitive, as significant computation could be wasted on generating
237   tokens for parts of the solution that are already correct or occur after an unrecoverable mistake has
238   been made. Next, we will construct a framework for effective backtracking.

240   3.2   D EFINING THE BACKTRACKING F RAMEWORK FOR S OLUTION R EVISION
241   In this work, we instantiate non-linear search through a novel sequential improvement and back-
242   tracking framework for solving reasoning problems. We build a setup to realize a practical imple-
243   mentation of this framework by answering the following key questions: (1) How can problematic
244   parts of a solution be identified and revised?, (2) Can we leverage the history of sequential revisions
245   to better guide search and identify problem parts of a solution, and (3) What are practical consider-
246   ations to build a robust framework for backtracking? We consider each of these key questions in the
247
      following discussion.
248   Localizing incorrect steps with PRMs. The first key component of the framework is identifying
249   where in the solution a mistake is made. One natural choice for this is the advantage function,
250   Aπ (s, a), defined as:
251
                                          Aπ (s, a) = Qπ (s, a) − V π (s)                                  (8)

256   icy. Intuitively, here the baseline can be viewed as a form of calibration/normalization, where the
257   advantage function not only considers the expected success rate (PRM, Qπ (s, a)) but the success
258   rate relative to the performance of the proposal distribution or policy.

260   may be a poor sample from the proposal distribution in expectation. Therefore, resampling may be

495
496
      Learning and leveraging process-level supervision for LLMs The idea of using process-level
      supervision was popularized in Uesato et al. (2022) and more recently in Lightman et al. (2023).
497
      Both of these works show the promise of using PRMs for MATH. Building upon this idea, papers
498
      such as Math-Shepherd (Wang et al., 2023), MiPS (Wang et al., 2024), and OmegaPRM (Luo et al.,
499   2024) present more efficient automated ways to gather data for process-level rewards.
500
501   Parallel sampling in test-time inference Test-time inference with search has been extensively stud-
502
      ied in works such as Feng et al. (2024); Yao et al. (2023); Hao et al. (2023). One key part of this
      equation is the way samples are selected (Welleck et al., 2024). A common paradigm involves gen-
503
      erating multiple trajectories in parallel, then employing some type of model or function to merge
504
      these trajectories. In particular, during test-time inference, the integration of a reward model with
505   a proposal distribution (LLM) can be employed to refine the output responses to a given prompt.
506   For instance, search algorithms such as best-of-N (Charniak & Johnson, 2005) and beam search
507   have been explored in works such as Snell et al. (2024), which leverage reward models to select the
508   most promising candidate samples in reasoning tasks. Another notable family of techniques include
509   self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023) and weighted majority voting (Uesato et al., 2022), which are
510   designed for factual queries with extractable answers. In self-consistency, the language model se-
511   lects the responses it generates with the highest frequency across multiple samples. This work can
512   be used in conjunction to our approach, without the use of a verifier. More recent methods such as
513
      universal self-consistency (Chen et al., 2023) and branch-solve-merge (Saha et al., 2024) explicitly
      prompt language models to merge the sampled trajectories.
514
515   Iterative revisions in test-time inference An alternative paradigm to parallel sampling in test-
516   time inference is iterative revisions of reasoning steps. Prior work such as RISE (Qu et al., 2024),
517   SCoRe (Kumar et al., 2024) and Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) parameterize a proposal distribu-
518   tion that can correct mistakes in an incorrect solution. This approach is complementary as our fixed
519
      proposal distribution can be substituted with the modified learned distribution from these works.
      Additionally, in statistics and Markov chain optimization/inference, Sitewise Resampling and
520
      similar approaches (Yang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2000; Gagnon et al., 2023)
521
      allow for the sequential reuse of generated samples.
522
523   6   D ISCUSSION , C ONCLUSION , AND L IMITATIONS
524
525   In this work, we present a framework for sequential response improvement for reasoning problems
526   with PRM based backtracking. Backtracking allows the model to localize where in the response
527
      an error has been made and make targeted revisions to efficiently resolve mistakes in a reasoning
      chain. We additionally introduce in-context process-supervision to allow the verifier to adapt its
528
      predictions throughout the search process conditioned on prior attempts at a solution, increasing
529
      confidence about a mistake it has made in the past and reinforcing behavior that has led to success.
530   Evaluating this framework with oracle and learned verifiers in the MATH domain, we achieve a
531   ≈ 15% improvement in test-time compute efficiency compared to linear search algorithms.
532
533
      There are still many open questions and limitations. While we used a fixed proposal distribution for
      responses, methods like RISE (Qu et al., 2024) leverage self-improvement to steer the distribution.
534
      Could these approaches be combined to enhance sequential corrections? Additionally, our analysis
535
      focused on a single reasoning domain (math). Can we develop a general verifier applicable across
536   domains such as code, legal reasoning, and robotic planning? How does scaling to multiple domains
537   affect performance, especially in areas where stepwise reasoning is less clearly defined, like code?
538   Finally, can we use sequential Monte-Carlo sampling approaches to use PRMs effectively?

819       threshold ω, Success Threshold pdes
820    2: Output: Revised solution trajectory τ ′
821    3: Initialize iteration count m ← 0
822    4: Initialize best trajectory τbest ← τ
       5: Compute initial advantage function A(si , ai ) for each step i in trajectory τ

       6: while m < M do
824
       7:     Identify step irevise ← arg mini A(si , ai )

       8:     Apply the smoothing criterion:

                                                j

830   10:    Update the trajectory τ ′ ← {s1 , . . . , sT −1 , s′T , . . . , s′N } using a linear search algorithm (e.g
831

      11:    if Improved Solution Found then
