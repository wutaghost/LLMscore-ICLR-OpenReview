011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this work, we present a novel method for music emotion recognition that lever-
014            ages Large Language Model (LLM) embeddings for label alignment across multi-
015            ple datasets and zero-shot prediction on novel categories. First, we compute LLM
016            embeddings for emotion labels and apply non-parametric clustering to group sim-
017            ilar labels, across multiple datasets containing disjoint labels. We use these cluster
018            centers to map music features (MERT) to the LLM embedding space. To further
019            enhance the model, we introduce alignment regularization that enables dissoci-
020
               ation of MERT embeddings from different clusters. This further enhances the
               model’s ability to better adaptation to unseen datasets. We demonstrate the ef-
021
               fectiveness of our approach by performing zero-shot inference on a new dataset,
022
               showcasing its ability to generalize to unseen labels without additional training.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The task of automatic music emotion recognition has been a long-standing challenge in the field of
029
      music information retrieval (Yang & Chen, 2012; Kim et al., 2010; Kang & Herremans, 2024). Ac-
030
      curately predicting the emotional impact of music has numerous valuable applications, ranging from
031   improving music streaming recommendations to providing more effective tools for music therapists.
032   By understanding the emotional responses evoked by music, we can improve the user experience
033   of music listening platforms, tailoring recommendations to individual preferences and emotional
034   needs. Furthermore, this knowledge can benefit music therapists, enabling them to select and apply
035   music more effectively in their treatments, ultimately leading to better outcomes for their patients
036   (Agres et al., 2021).
037   The predominant approach in this field has been to model emotions using Russell’s two dimensional
038   valence-arousal space (Russell, 1980). However, this representation fails to be interpreted by hu-
039   man (Eerola & Vuoskoski, 2011). to address this limitation, researchers have explored the use of
040   comprehensive categorical emotional models, such as the Geneva Emotional Music Scale (Aljanaki
041   et al., 2014), which encompasses a broad range of discrete emotional attributes. More recent datasets
042   (Bogdanov et al., 2019; Turnbull et al., 2007) introduce new label categories, sometimes in the form
043   of free tags, that aim to capture the multifaceted nature of human emotions. Nonetheless, a key
044   challenge in this area is the lack of a unified emotional taxonomy. Different datasets often employ
045
      disparate and often incompatible sets of emotional labels, posing a significant obstacle. The hetero-
      geneity of emotion label taxonomies across datasets hinders the ability to effectively compare and
046
      combine findings across studies. This impedes our comprehensive understanding of the nuanced
047
      emotional responses to music. The challenge of aligning these disparate emotion label taxonomies
048   limits our capacity to develop more comprehensive and robust music emotion recognition models.
049   Traditionally, the majority of studies have focused on training and testing with a single dataset.
050   However, recent advances in large language models have resulted in powerful general-purpose text
051   encoders (Reimers, 2019) that can be leveraged to effectively align emotions across diverse datasets.
052   The ability to align emotion labels across disparate datasets is a crucial step towards developing more
053   comprehensive and robust music emotion recognition models that can generalise to a wider range of
      emotional experiences.


                                                         1
054
      In this work, we present a novel methodology that leverages the powerful representational capabil-
055   ities of Large Language Model embeddings to enable effective cross-dataset label alignment and
056   facilitate zero-shot inference on new datasets with previously unseen emotion labels. The core of
057   our approach involves computing LLM embeddings for the emotion labels across multiple datasets,
058   and then applying non-parametric clustering to group semantically similar labels together. These
059   cluster centres serve as anchor points that allow us to map the MERT features (Li et al., 2023)
060   extracted from music wav files into a common label embedding space, effectively aligning the dis-
061   parate emotion taxonomies present across datasets. To further enhance the model’s performance and
062   ensure it captures the underlying semantic relationships between emotions beyond training data, we
063
      introduce an alignment regularization. This regularization encourages music features from differ
      clusters in the label embedding space to dissociate from each other. This, in turn help the model
064
      to generalize better to unseen data and labels. To evaluate the efficacy of our proposed approach,
065
      we conduct extensive experiments on three benchmark datasets for music emotion recognition. The
066   results demonstrate substantial improvements in the model’s ability to perform zero-shot prediction
067   on a new dataset containing previously unseen emotion labels, showcasing the strong generalization
068   capabilities of our method. This is a crucial step towards developing more robust and comprehen-
069   sive music emotion recognition models that can be applied to a wider range of emotional experiences
070   beyond the singular datasets used during training.
071
      To sum up, the key contributions of this work are:
072
073         • This work is the first to leverage the capabilities of Large Language Model embeddings
074           to enable robust cross-dataset label alignment for the domain of music emotion recogni-
075           tion. By computing LLM embeddings for emotion labels across datasets and applying
076           non-parametric clustering, we are able to establish a common embedding space that allows
077           us to align disparate emotion taxonomies.
078         • We introduce an alignment regularization that further enhances the model’s ability to dis-
079           sociate music features from distinct emotions.
080         • We demonstrate the ability of our approach to perform zero-shot inference on new datasets
081           with previously unseen emotion labels. This showcases the model’s strong generalization
082           capabilities and its potential to be applied to a wider range of emotional experiences.

092   The connection between music and emotions has long been a subject of study, dating back to
093
      (Leonard, 1956) and (Hevner, 1935), who explored how different musical elements evoke specific
      emotional responses. Over the decades, various frameworks have been proposed to represent emo-
094
      tions in music, ranging from categorical models (e.g., happy, sad, angry) to continuous dimensions
095
      like valence and arousal. Valence-arousal models (Russell, 1980) and more sophisticated systems
096   such as the Geneva Emotional Music Scale (Zentner et al., 2008) have become prominent in recent
097   studies of music emotion recognition (MER).
098
099
      Despite these advances, current models often struggle to achieve robust generalization, particularly
      across diverse datasets. Existing approaches have attempted to improve performance by incorporat-
100
      ing advanced encoders like MERT (Li et al., 2023), leveraging multi-modal data (e.g., lyrics, MIDI,
101
      or video), or introducing personalized models that adapt to listener-specific responses (Chua et al.,
102   2022; Koh et al., 2022; Sams & Zahra, 2023). However, these methods primarily explore single
103   dataset approach, i.e., training and testing done on a single dataset. Compared to other fields, such
104   as image recognition or natural language processing, datasets for MER are considerably smaller and
105   more fragmented, making it difficult to develop models that generalize across genres and emotional
106   taxonomies. Datasets such as MTG-Jamendo (Bogdanov et al., 2019), and smaller, domain-specific
107   collections like CAL500 (Turnbull et al., 2007) and Emotify (Aljanaki et al., 2016) all use different
      emotion representation models, focus on distinct musical genres, and are thus often incompatible.

      This discrepancy in representation and dataset size presents a major challenge to building robust
128   models that can generalize across various emotion taxonomies. Addressing this limitation requires
129   innovative solutions that can overcome the dataset heterogeneity. One promising direction is zero-
130   shot learning, which has shown success in fields such as computer vision (Xian et al., 2018) and
131   natural language processing (Brown, 2020). Zero-shot learning models generalize to novel classes
132   or tasks without requiring direct training data for every label. In the context of music emotion
133   recognition, zero-shot learning enables models to predict emotions in datasets with unseen labels or
134   emotion taxonomies, potentially overcoming the generalization bottleneck caused by small, disjoint
135   datasets. Our work builds on these ideas by introducing a novel approach for emotion prediction
136
      across music datasets with disjoint label sets. By leveraging large language models (LLMs) to align
      emotion labels through semantic embeddings and clustering, we bridge the gap between datasets,
137
      enabling cross-dataset generalization and zero-shot performance. The next section provides details

      of the building blocks of our approach.

141   3     P RESENT W ORK
142
143   Our approach leverages the power of Large Language Model (LLM) embeddings to align emotion
144   labels across multiple music emotion datasets and to enable zero-shot inference on previously unseen
145   emotion labels. An overview is shown in Figure 1. This section describes the key components of our
146   methodology, including the label embedding procedure, non-parametric clustering, and the mapping
147   of MERT features (i.e., encoded audio) (Li et al., 2023) to the label embedding space.
148
149   3.1   E MOTION L ABEL E MBEDDING
150
151   For each dataset, we obtain an embedding for each emotion label using a pre-trained LLM. Let
152   Ld = {l1 , l2 , . . . , lnd } represent the set of emotion labels in dataset d, where nd is the number
153   of labels in dataset d. We compute an embedding for each label li ∈ Ld using the LLM, which
154   produces a vector representation eli ∈ Rm , where m is the dimension of the LLM’s embedding
155   space:
156                                              eli = LLM(li )                                          (1)
157
158   This embedding process is performed independently for each dataset. The advantage of using
159   LLM embeddings is that the pre-trained model captures rich semantic relationships between words
160   (emotion labels), which allows for natural grouping of labels even when they differ slightly across
161   datasets. For example, the labels “happy” and “joyful” are likely to have similar embeddings despite
      being distinct.

165   is to group semantically similar labels. Instead of relying on a predefined number of clusters, we
166   utilize the Mean Shift clustering algorithm (Cheng, 1995), a non-parametric clustering technique
167   that automatically determines the number of clusters based on the density of points in the embedding
168   space.
169   Let E = {eli }N
                    i=1 represent the set of all emotion label embeddings, where N is the total number of
170   unique labels across all datasets. The Mean Shift algorithm identifies clusters by shifting each point
171   towards the mode (the densest region of points) iteratively.

309

310
      ging. It was constructed using music content hosted on the Jamendo platform, which is licensed
311   under Creative Commons, and incorporating tags provided by the content contributors. This dataset
312   encompasses a vast collection of over 55,000 full-length audio recordings annotated with 56 relevant
313   emotion tags.
314
      The Computer Audition Lab 500 (CAL500) dataset is a widely utilised dataset in music emotion
315
      recognition research. It consists of 500 popular Western songs, each annotated with a standard set
316
      of 17 emotion labels by at least 3 human annotators.
317
318   Lastly, the Emotify dataset is another prominent open dataset for music emotion recognition. It
319   contains 400 music excerpts annotated with 9 emotional categories of the Geneva Emotional Music
320
      Scale model, obtained through a crowdsourcing game.

323   none of the emotion score is greater than 3, we select the highest scored emotion. For the Emotify
      dataset, we fix the selection process by choosing the top 3 rated labels as the true labels. These

337   4.2    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
338
      For our experimental setup, we employ specific train-test-dev splits for each dataset to ensure a
339
      standardized evaluation process. For the MTG-Jamendo dataset, we utilize the official split-0 pro-
340
      vided by the dataset’s authors2 . For the CAL500 and Emotify datasets, no publicly available splits
341   exist since these datasets were initially designed for evaluation purposes. To address this, we de-
342   fine our own train-test-dev splits, which we plan to release in the future to facilitate reproducibil-
343   ity and benchmarking of our work. The details of these splits are provided in Table 1. For the
344   MERT feature extraction, we split the songs in 10 second segments and we utilise the widely-
345   used MERT-v1-95M model available on Hugging Face3 . For LLM embeddings, we select Sen-
346   tence Transformers (Reimers, 2019), given the multi-label nature of all three datasets. Specifically,
347   we use the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model4 , which has been fine-tuned on 1 billion sentence pairs from
348   a pre-trained MiniLM-L6-H384-uncased model. We leverage the Hugging Face implementation
349
      for this approach. The MERT features for each 10s fragment are averaged and fed into a shallow
      two-layered attention network to project these MERT features to the LLM embedding space. We
350
      implement Mean Shift Clustering (Fukunaga & Hostetler, 1975) using the scikit-learn library. The
351
      k parameter to obtain the actual labels ŷ is set to the average number of labels per instance in the
352   dataset: 2 for Jamendo MTG, 4 for CAL500, and 3 for Emotify. For all the training processes, we
353   ran for 100 epochs with a batch size of 256, using the AdamW optimiser. The base learning rate is
354   fixed to 1e−5 with a ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler monitoring the validation macro F1 score and a
355   minimum learning rate threshold of 1.6e−7 . The weight decay is set to 1e−4 . All models are trained
356   on 4 NVIDIA Tesla V100 DGXS 32 GB GPUs, and the training and evaluation code is implemented
357   using PyTorch and available online5 .

498   Discussion: Our findings show that combining the Jamendo-MTG dataset with others consistently
499   enhanced Jamendo-MTG’s performance in Baseline 2. However, this improvement was not ob-
500
      served across the other datasets. For instance, when integrating Emotify with CAL500, Emotify’s
      performance in Baseline 2 declined in comparison to Baseline 1 (0.442 vs 0.387). This can be at-
501
      tributed to CAL500 being a more extensive dataset with greater diversity in data and emotion labels,
502
      while Emotify is smaller and focuses on a specific kind of music. Incorporating CAL500 diluted
503   Emotify’s performance by introducing music of genres that was less representative of Emotify’s
504   concentrated content consisting of a specific genre. Conversely, adding Emotify to Jamendo-MTG
505   introduced focused data that enhanced Jamendo-MTG’s performance on that genre.
506
      When merging datasets, we should also be mindful of variations in music emotion datasets and the
507
      resulting challenges as outlined by Kang & Herremans (2024). For instance, the added dataset may
508
      contain noisier data, due to cloud annotation instead of expert annotation. It may contain different
509   genres of music or fragments of different length. There may also be many labels per instance, versus
510   only one. Our proposed framework can handle data of all these variations, however, the user should
511   be mindful to focus on high-quality data with similar distribution to the desired target music, in order
512   achieve a quality improvement in the model.
513
      Our results suggest that when aiming to improve a dataset’s performance, it is advantageous to
514
      augment it with another dataset that may be smaller but contains music of the same character present
515   in the original dataset. In such cases, Baseline 2 serves as an effective strategy to enhance the
516   performance of the larger, more diverse dataset.
517
518
      Furthermore, for tasks requiring zero-shot learning, alignment regularization proves to be more
      effective. However, when all data and labels are available, Baseline 2 should be preferred, as it tends
519
      to yield better results, particularly for larger datasets like Jamendo-MTG, as described earlier.

      In this paper, we introduce a novel approach to music emotion recognition by integrating Large
525   Language Model (LLM) embeddings to harmonize label spaces across diverse datasets, and enable
526   zero-shot learning for new emotion categories. Our method not only effectively clusters and aligns
527   emotion labels through LLM embeddings but also employs a novel alignment regularization, en-
528   hancing the semantic coherence between music features and emotion labels across disjoint datasets.
529   The experimental results show zero shot performance of 0.402, 0248, 0.262 in terms of macro F1-
530   score for Emotify, MTG-Jamendo, and CAL500 respectively, setting a new benchmark in the field
531   of music emotion recognition. This approach opens up possibilities for broader applications in af-
532   fective computing where emotional nuances can be universally understood and processed across
533
      contextual bounds.

758   A.4.1    MTG-JAMENDO AND CAL500 C LUSTERING
759
760   In the clustering results for MTG-Jamendo and CAL500, the following emotional labels are clus-

762
              • MTG-Jamendo: [’calm’, ’meditative’, ’relaxing’, ’soft’]

      These labels reflect overlapping interpretations of emotional states across MTG-Jamendo and
766
      CAL500 datasets. However, Emotify uses a more concise tag system, where a similar emotional
767
      concept is represented by a single label ’calmness’.
768
769   Similarly, for a more melancholic or somber emotional tone, MTG-Jamendo and CAL500 uses
770   [’melancholic’, ’sad’] while Emotify uses ’sadness’.
771
772   A.4.2    B RIDGING THE G AP
773   The challenge lies in reconciling these differences across datasets, where MTG-Jamendo and
774   CAL500 provide a richer set of nuanced labels, while Emotify uses broader, simplified terms. Our
775   proposed method bridges this gap through:
776
777         1. Semantic Alignment: By using embeddings for emotional labels (e.g., pre-trained lan-
778            guage models), our method captures the shared semantic meaning across different label
779            sets. For instance, ’calm, meditative, relaxing’ from MTG-Jamendo and ’calming, sooth-
780            ing’ from CAL500 are mapped closer to ’calmness’ in Emotify, ensuring consistency across
781
               datasets without losing interpretative richness.
782         2. Clustering-Based Grouping: Mean-shift clustering automatically groups related emo-
783            tional labels based on their feature density in the semantic space. This approach allows
784            us to unify nuanced labels like ’pleasant, comfortable’ with broader tags like ’calmness’,
785
               effectively bridging granularity differences.
786         3. Zero-Shot Prediction: Our model leverages shared semantics to generalize across
787            datasets. For example, even if a dataset does not explicitly include a term like ’calming’,
788            the model recognizes it as semantically aligned with ’calmness’ and makes predictions
789
               accordingly.
