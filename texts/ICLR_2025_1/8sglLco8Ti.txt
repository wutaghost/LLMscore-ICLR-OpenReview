011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
014            processing extensive contexts, but this ability comes with significant GPU mem-
015
               ory costs, particularly in the key-value (KV) cache. Although recent KV cache
               compression methods show strong performance, all use discrete tokens to main-
016
               tain the KV cache, leading to a loss of chunk semantic information. We introduce
017
               ChunkKV, a novel KV cache compression method that retains the most informa-
018            tive semantic chunks while discarding the less important ones. ChunkKV pre-
019            serves semantic information by grouping related tokens. Furthermore, ChunkKV
020            exhibits a higher similarity in the indices of the retained KV cache across dif-
021            ferent layers, so we also propose a layer-wise index reuse technique to further
022            reduce computational overhead. This technique not only improves compression
023            efficiency, but also provides insight into the similarities between layers within
024            LLMs. We evaluated ChunkKV on long-context benchmarks including Long-
025            Bench and Needle-In-A-HayStack, as well as the GSM8K in-context learning
026
               benchmark. Our experiments, conducted with models LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct,
               Mistral-7B-Instruct, and Qwen2-7B-Instruct, demonstrate that ChunkKV outper-
027
               forms other KV cache compression methods in performance, even surpassing the
028
               full KV cache under the same conditions. With a compression ratio of 10%,
029            ChunkKV achieves state-of-the-art performance on various tasks, indicating its
030            effectiveness in semantic preservation and model performance for long-context
031            and in-context LLM inference.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large Language Models (LLMs) have become essential for addressing various downstream tasks of
037   natural language processing (NLP), including summarization and question answering, which require
038   the interpretation of a wide context from sources such as books, reports, and documents, often
039
      encompassing tens of thousands of tokens (Raffel et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al.,
      2022; Tay et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a;b). Recent advances in long-context technology within
040
      the field of machine learning (ML) systems (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2024; Jacobs et al., 2023; Xiao
041
      et al., 2024) and model architecture design (Chen et al., 2023a; Xiong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b;
042   Peng et al., 2024) have significantly enhanced the ability of LLMs to process increasingly large input
043   context lengths (Liu et al., 2024b; Young et al., 2024), such as the Gemini-1.5-pro model, which can
044   manage documents up to 1,500 pages in length (Reid et al., 2024). However, this ability to handle
045   long contexts also presents significant challenges regarding the key-value (KV) cache for super-
046   long prompts. For instance, the KV cache for a single token in a 7 billion-parameter model requires
047   approximately 0.5 MB of GPU memory, resulting in a 10,000-token prompt consuming around 5 GB
048   of GPU memory, which constitutes nearly one fifth of the memory available on an RTX 4090 GPU.
049   Larger contexts will further increase GPU memory consumption during inference serving (AI21,
050
      2024; X.AI, 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024b; DeepSeek-AI, 2024). Consequently, KV
      cache compression methods have become crucial technologies for reducing GPU memory costs
051
      when deploying LLM services.

125   KV Cache Compression KV cache compression technology has developed rapidly in the era of
126   LLM, with methods mainly focused on evicting unimportant tokens. The compression process oc-
127   curs before the attention blocks, optimizing both the prefilling time and GPU memory. Xiao et al.
128   (2024) and Han et al. (2024) propose that initial and recent tokens consistently have high attention
129   scores between different layers and attention heads. As a result, retaining these tokens in the KV
130   cache is more likely to preserve important information. Furthermore, FastGen (Ge et al., 2023)
131   evicts tokens based on observed patterns. H2O (Zhang et al., 2023) and SnapKV (Li et al., 2024)
132
      employ dynamic KV cache compression methods, evaluating the importance of tokens based on at-
      tention scores and then evicting the less important ones. As inference scenarios become increasingly
133
      complex, dynamic KV cache compression methods demonstrate powerful performance. Recently,
134
      Yang et al. (2024b) and Zhang et al. (2024b) have closely examined the distributions of attention
135   scores during the pre-filling stage of the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) task, discovering
136   a pyramidal KV cache compression pattern in different transformer layers.
137
138
      Although these KV cache compression methods have explored efficient GPU memory management
      while maintaining original performance, our study focuses more on the semantic information of the
139
      prompt. We find that chunks of the original KV cache are more important than discrete tokens.
140
141
      Chunking Method The chunking methodology is widely used in the field of NLP due to its sim-
142
      plicity and effectiveness (Tjong Kim Sang & Veenstra, 1999). In the era of LLMs, chunking is
143
      primarily applied in data pre-processing. For example, Shi et al. suggest grouping related training
144   data into chunks to achieve better convergence curves to pre-train LLMs. Fei et al. (2024) apply
145   a topic-based chunking method to improve the semantic compression of prompts. Furthermore,
146   chunking plays an important role in the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) field (Yepes et al.,
147   2024; Smith & Troynikov, 2024; Anthropic, 2024a). It serves to divide documents into units of
148   information with semantic content suitable for embedding-based retrieval and processing by LLMs.
149
150   Layer-Wise Technique The layer-wise technique is widely used in the training and inference of
151   large language models (LLMs). LISA (Pan et al., 2024a) is a layer-wise sampling method based
152   on observations of the training dynamics of Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)(Hu et al., 2022) across
153   layers. LAMB(You et al., 2020) is a layer-wise adaptive learning rate method that speeds up LLM
154   training by stabilizing training convergence with large batch sizes. DoLa (Chuang et al., 2023)
155
      employs layer-wise contrasting to reduce hallucinations during LLM inference.

190   3.2.1    C HUNK KV
191
192   To address the limitations of existing KV cache compression methods, we propose ChunkKV, a
193   novel KV cache compression method that retains the most informative semantic chunks. The key
194
      idea behind ChunkKV is to group tokens in the KV cache into chunks that preserve more semantic
      information, such as a chunk containing a subject, verb and object. As illustrated in Figure 1,
195
      ChunkKV preserves the chunks of the KV cache that contain more semantic information. First, we
196
      define a chunk as a group of tokens that contain related semantic information. By retaining the most
197   informative chunks from the original KV cache, ChunkKV can effectively reduce the memory usage
198   of the KV cache while preserving essential information.
199

1046   A.2     H YPOTHETICAL S CENARIO
1047
1048   To provide a deeper understanding of ChunkKV’s effectiveness compared to discrete token-based
1049   methods, we present a detailed analysis using a hypothetical scenario. This analysis aims to illustrate
1050   the fundamental differences between these approaches and explain why ChunkKV is more effective
1051
       at preserving semantic information in long contexts.
1052   Consider a comprehensive document that contains detailed information on various animals, includ-
1053   ing their habitats, diets, and behaviors. A user asks the question ”What do pandas eat in the wild?”
1054
       Both ChunkKV and discrete token-based methods would use this question to calculate observation
1055   scores for the document. However, their approaches to selecting and retaining information differ
1056   significantly.
1057
1058   A.2.1    D ISCRETE T OKEN - BASED M ETHOD
1059
1060   A discrete token-based method might identify and retain individual tokens with high relevance
1061
       scores, such as:
1062           • “pandas”,“eat”, “bamboo”, “wild”, “diet”, “food”
1063
1064   Although these tokens are relevant, they lack context and coherence. The method might discard
1065   other essential tokens that provide crucial context or complete the information.
1066
1067   A.2.2    C HUNK KV M ETHOD
1068
       In contrast, ChunkKV would identify and retain semantically meaningful chunks, such as:
1069
1070           • “In the wild, pandas primarily eat bamboo shoots and leaves”
1071           • “Their diet consists of 99% bamboo, but they occasionally consume other vegetation”
1072
               • “Wild pandas may also eat small rodents or birds when available”
1073
1074   By preserving these chunks, ChunkKV maintains not only the relevant keywords but also their
1075   contextual relationships and additional pertinent information.
1076
1077   A.3     C OMPARATIVE A NALYSIS
1078
1079   The advantages of ChunkKV become evident when we consider how these retained pieces of infor-
       mation would be used in subsequent processing:

1080
             1. Contextual Understanding: Discrete tokens require the model to reconstruct meaning
1081            from isolated words, which could lead to ambiguity. ChunkKV provides complete phrases
1082            or sentences, allowing for immediate and accurate comprehension.
1083
             2. Semantic Coherence: ChunkKV preserves the semantic relationships within a chunk, cru-
1084
                cial to understanding nuances such as the difference between primary and occasional food
1085            sources for pandas.
1086
             3. Information Density: A single chunk can contain multiple relevant tokens in their proper
1087
                context, potentially retaining more useful information within the same compressed cache
1088
                size compared to discrete methods.
1089
1090         4. Reduced Ambiguity: Discrete methods might retain the token “eat” from various sen-
1091
                tences about different animals. ChunkKV ensures that “eat” is preserved specifically in the
                context of pandas in the wild.
1092
1093         5. Temporal and Logical Flow: ChunkKV can maintain the sequence of ideas present in
1094            the original text, preserving any temporal or logical progression that may be crucial for
1095
                understanding.

         KV cache sharing Recent work has explored various strategies for sharing KV caches across
1719
1720
         transformer layers. Layer-Condensed KV Cache (LCKV) (Wu & Tu, 2024) computes KVs only for
1721
         the top layer and pairs them with queries from all layers, while optionally retaining standard atten-
1722
         tion for a few top and bottom layers to mitigate performance degradation. Similarly, You Only Cache
1723
         Once (YOCO) (Sun et al., 2024) computes KVs exclusively for the top layer but pairs them with
1724     queries from only the top half of layers, employing efficient attention in the bottom layers to maintain
1725     a constant cache size. In contrast, Cross-Layer Attention (CLA) (Brandon et al., 2024) divides layers
1726     into groups, pairing queries from all layers in each group with KVs from that group’s bottom layer.
1727     MiniCache (Liu et al., 2024a) introduces a novel method that merges layer-wise KV caches while
         enabling recovery during compute-in-place operations, optimizing KV cache size. These methods

1743   Long-Context Benchmarks The landscape of long-context model benchmarks has evolved to
1744   encompass a wide range of tasks, with particular emphasis on retrieval and comprehension capa-
1745   bilities. Benchmarks for understanding have made significant strides, with ∞-Bench (Zhang et al.,
1746   2024a) pushing the boundaries by presenting challenges that involve more than 100,000 tokens.
1747   LongBench (Bai et al., 2024) has introduced bilingual evaluations, addressing tasks such as long-
1748
       document question answering, summarization, and code completion. Complementing these efforts,
       ZeroSCROLLS (Shaham et al., 2023) and L-Eval (An et al., 2023) have broadened the scope to
1749
       include a diverse array of practical natural language tasks, including query-driven summarization.
1750
1751   In parallel, retrieval benchmarks have largely relied on synthetic datasets, offering researchers pre-
1752   cise control over variables such as the length of input tokens. This approach minimizes the impact
1753   of disparate parametric knowledge resulting from varied training methodologies. A significant body
1754
       of recent work has concentrated on the development of synthetic tasks specifically for retrieval eval-
       uation (Kamradt, 2023; Mohtashami & Jaggi, 2023; Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024c; Hsieh et al.,
1755
       2024). In addition, researchers have explored the potential of extended contexts in facilitating vari-
1756
       ous forms of reasoning (Tay et al., 2021).
1757
1758   This dual focus on synthetic retrieval tasks and comprehensive understanding benchmarks reflects
1759   the field’s commitment to rigorously assessing the capabilities of long-context models across diverse
1760
       linguistic challenges.
1761
1762   Prompting Compression In the field of prompt compression, various designs effectively com-
1763
       bine semantic information to compress natural language. Wingate et al. (2022) utilize soft prompts
       to encode more information with fewer tokens. Chevalier et al. (2023) present AutoCompressor,
1764
       which uses soft prompts to compress the input sequence and extend the original length of the base
1765
       model. Both Zhou et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2023) recurrently apply LLMs to summarize input
1766   texts, maintaining long short-term memory for specific purposes such as story writing and dialogue
1767   generation. The LLMLingua series (Jiang et al., 2023b; 2024; Fei et al., 2024) explores the poten-
1768   tial of compressing LLM prompts in long-context, reasoning, and RAG scenarios. Fei et al. (2024)
1769   use pre-trained language models to chunk the long context and summarize semantic information,
1770   compressing the original context.

1833   The major limitation of the ChunkKV is that it uses fixed-size token groups for chunking. While
