009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have been achieving excellent performance for real-world image
012            super-resolution (Real-ISR) with considerable computational costs. Current ap-
013            proaches are trying to derive one-step diffusion models from multi-step counterparts
014            through knowledge distillation. However, these methods incur substantial training
015            costs and may constrain the performance of the student model by the teacher’s lim-
016
               itations. To tackle these issues, we propose DFOSD, a Distillation-Free One-Step
               Diffusion model. Specifically, we propose a noise-aware discriminator (NAD) to
017
               participate in adversarial training, further enhancing the authenticity of the gener-
018
               ated content. Additionally, we improve the perceptual loss with edge-aware DISTS
019            (EA-DISTS) to enhance the model’s ability to generate fine details. Our experi-
020            ments demonstrate that, compared with previous diffusion-based methods requiring
021            dozens or even hundreds of steps, our DFOSD attains comparable or even superior
022            results in both quantitative metrics and qualitative evaluations. Our DFOSD also
023            abtains higher performance and efficiency compared with other one-step diffusion
024            methods. We will release code and models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027   Real-world image super-resolution (Real-ISR) is a challenging task that aims to reconstruct high-
028   resolution (HR) images from their low-resolution (LR) counterparts in real-world settings (Wang
029   et al., 2020). Most image super-resolution (SR) methods (Kim et al., 2016; Johnson et al., 2016;
030   Ledig et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2022; 2023) use Bicubic downsampling of HR images to generate
031   LR samples for training and testing models. These methods achieve good results in reconstructing
032   simple degraded images. However, they struggle with the complex and unknown degradations
033
      widely existing in real-world scenarios. Moreover, these methods often amplify the noise in LR
      images during reconstruction. Previous research has predominantly employed generative adversarial
034
      networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020) architectures for image SR tasks (Wang et al., 2021a;
035
      Zhang et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2021). However, these approaches often struggle to train models that
036   accurately capture real-world data distributions, leading to suboptimal generated content. Diffusion
037   models (DMs), known for their strong denoising capabilities and ability to model data distributions,
038   have been widely adopted in the field of image generation in recent years. Recently, numerous
039   super-resolution (SR) methods based on pre-trained diffusion models have exhibited outstanding
040   performance by leveraging their powerful priors and generative capabilities.
041
      Specifically, recent real-world image super-resolution (Real-ISR) models have predominantly lever-
042   aged powerful pre-trained diffusion models, such as large-scale text-to-image (T2I) models like
043   Stable Diffusion (Wu et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). With training on billions of
044   image-text pairs and a strong capacity to model complex data distributions, these pre-trained T2I
045   models provide extensive priors and powerful generative abilities. Most diffusion model (DM)-based
046   methods generate high-resolution (HR) images by employing ControlNet models (Zhang et al., 2023),
047   conditioning on the low-resolution (LR) inputs. However, these methods typically require tens to
048   hundreds of diffusion steps to produce high-quality HR images. The introduction of ControlNet
049   not only increases the number of model parameters but also further exacerbates inference latency.
050
      Consequently, DM-based multi-step diffusion methods often incur delays of tens of seconds when
      processing a single image, which significantly limits their practical application in real-world scenarios
051
      for low-level image reconstruction tasks, such as Real-ISR.
052
053   To accelerate the generation process of diffusion models, recent research has introduced numerous one-
      step diffusion methods, known as diffusion distillation, which distill multi-step pre-trained diffusion

078   To overcome the aforementioned challenges, we propose DFOSD, a novel approach that generates
079   HR images from their corresponding LR inputs in a single sampling step. Unlike previous one-
080
      step diffusion SR models, we do not employ knowledge distillation to train our one-step diffusion
      generator. Our approach eliminates the need to leverage outputs or corresponding noise from multi-
081
      step diffusion models, allowing us to train solely on real-world datasets. This significantly reduces
082
      training overhead and overcomes the limitations imposed by teacher models. Furthermore, we do
083   not utilize models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to encode prompts as conditional inputs for the
084   diffusion model. Instead, we train a learnable text embedding. This further reduces the model’s
085   inference time without compromising performance. As shown in Fig. 1, DFOSD not only achieves
086   the best visual results but also attains the fastest inference speed.
087

088
      of the generated images, we propose a noise-aware discriminator (NAD) initialized with parameters
089   from the pre-trained stable diffusion (SD) UNet, which is trained adversarially alongside the generator.
090   Specifically, our NAD takes the forward diffusion results of the latent features at various time steps,
091   ensuring that its performance remains robust across different noise levels. NAD capitalizes on the
092   prior knowledge of the pre-trained diffusion model, enhancing the reconstruction quality (see Fig. 1).
093   Additionally, we propose edge-aware DISTS (EA-DISTS) loss to improve the authenticity of fine
094   details in the generated content. Our comprehensive experiments indicate that DFOSD achieves
095   superior performance and less inference time among one-step diffusion model (DM)-based Real-ISR
096   models. When compared with multi-step DM-based models, DFOSD obtains comparable or even
097
      better performance with over 7ˆ speedup in inference time (see Fig. 1).
098   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
099        • We propose DFOSD, a Distillation-Free One-Step Diffusion SR model training paradigm.
100           Our DFOSD significantly enhances the details and visual quality of generated images,
101           achieving remarkable results in both evaluation metrics and visual assessments.
102        • We propose a noise-aware discriminator (NAD), which capitalizes on the prior knowledge
103           from the pre-trained SD UNet and engages in adversarial training with the generator. Our
104           NAD effectively enhances the realism and details of the reconstructed images.
105
           • We improve the perceptual loss used in image SR model training by proposing the edge-
106           aware DISTS (EA-DISTS) loss. Our EA-DISTS leverages image edges to enhance the
107           model’s ability and improve the authenticity of reconstructed details.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   2.1 R EAL -W ORLD I MAGE S UPER -R ESOLUTION
111   Real-world image super-resolution (Real-ISR) aims to recover high-resolution (HR) images from
112   low-resolution (LR) observations in real-world scenarios. The complex and unknown degradation
113
      patterns in such scenarios make Real-ISR a challenging problem (Ignatov et al., 2017; Liu et al.,
      2022a; Ji et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2020). To address this problem, models continuously evolve. Early
114
      image super-resolution models (Kim et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2018c;b; Chen et al., 2022; 2023)
115
      typically rely on simple synthetic degradations like Bicubic downsampling for generating LR-HR
116   pairs, resulting in subpar performance on real-world datasets. Later, GAN-based methods such as
117   BSRGAN (Zhang et al., 2021), Real-ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2021a), and SwinIR-GAN (Liang et al.,
118   2021) introduce more complex degradation processes. These methods achieve promising perceptual
119   quality but encounter issues such as training instability. Additionally, they have limitations in
120   preserving fine natural details. Recently, Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022b) is considered
121   for addressing Real-ISR tasks due to its strong ability to capture complex data distributions and
122   provide robust generative priors. Approaches such as StableSR (Wang et al., 2024a), DiffBIR (Lin
123   et al., 2024), and SeeSR (Wu et al., 2024b) leverage pre-trained diffusion priors and ControlNet
124
      models (Zhang et al., 2023) to enhance HR image generation. While these methods significantly
      improve perceptual quality, the multi-step nature of diffusion models introduces latency issues,
125
      making them less practical for real-time applications in low-level image reconstruction tasks.
126
127   2.2 ACCELERATION OF D IFFUSION M ODELS
128   Acceleration of diffusion models can reduce computational costs and inference time. Therefore,
129   various strategies have been developed to enhance the efficiency of diffusion models in image
130   generation tasks. Fast diffusion samplers (Song et al., 2021; Karras et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022b;
131   Lu et al., 2022a;b; Zhao et al., 2024) have significantly reduced the number of sampling steps from
132   1,000 to 15„100 without requiring model retraining. However, further reducing the steps below 10
133   often leads to a performance drop. Under these circumstances, distillation techniques have made
134
      considerable progress in speeding up inference (Berthelot et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022c; Meng et al.,
      2023; Salimans & Ho, 2022; Song et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2023;
135
      Geng et al., 2024). For instance, Progressive Distillation (PD) methods (Meng et al., 2023; Salimans
136
      & Ho, 2022) have distilled pre-trained diffusion models to under 10 steps. Consistency models (Song
137   et al., 2023) have further reduced the steps to 2„4 with promising results. Instaflow (Liu et al., 2023)
138   further achieves one-step generation through reflow (Liu et al., 2022c) and distillation. Recent score
139   distillation-based methods, such as Distribution Matching Distillation (DMD) (Yin et al., 2024c;a)
140   and Variational Score Distillation (VSD) (Wang et al., 2024c; Nguyen & Tran, 2024), aim to achieve
141   one-step text-to-image generation. They minimize the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence between
142   the generated data distribution and the real data distribution. Although these approaches have made
143   notable progress, they still face challenges, like high training costs and dependence on teacher models.

      3     D ISTILLATION -F REE O NE -S TEP D IFFUSION (DFOSD)
146   In this section, we detail our Distillation-Free One-Step Diffusion (DFOSD) image super-resolution
147   (SR) model. First, in Section 3.1, we review the fundamentals of diffusion models and introduce
148   the principles underlying the DFOSD generator. Subsequently, we propose two key techniques for
149
      training our one-step diffusion SR model. In Section 3.2, we propose the noise-aware discriminator
      (NAD), which assesses image realism using the results of random forward diffusion applied to
150
      their latent representations. Then, in Section 3.3, we propose an improved perceptual loss function,
151
      edge-aware DISTS (EA-DISTS), designed to enhance the quality of image texture details. Finally,
152   in Section 3.4, we outline the complete training process of the model.
153

197   3.2 N OISE -AWARE D ISCRIMINATOR (NAD)
198   In an ideal scenario, we seek to achieve image restoration results that are almost indistinguishable
199   from real images. Yet, training the generator directly without distillation often falls short of this
200   goal. To improve the realism of generated images, we incorporate a discriminator. Training a
201   discriminator from scratch, however, may result in unstable training dynamics, and converting
202   the generator’s latent outputs to pixel space for evaluation introduces considerable computational
203   overhead. Stable Diffusion (SD), a robust pre-trained generative model with strong priors and a
204   UNet-based architecture, provides a promising solution to these challenges. This inspires us to
205   initialize the discriminator with pre-trained UNet parameters, perform operations directly in the
206
      latent space, and leverage the UNet bottleneck layer’s robust information filtering and semantic
      condensation capabilities to construct the discriminator.
207
208   Figure 3 illustrates the visualization results of the latent representations of both generated and real
209   images during the early stages of training. After undergoing forward diffusion at various random
210   time steps, the UNet middle block outputs are visualized using dimensionality reduction techniques
211   such as PCA, supervised UMAP, and LLE. The feature distributions of the generated images and
212
      real images exhibit distinct differences, thereby highlighting the Stable Diffusion (SD) UNet’s robust
      information filtering and semantic condensation capabilities.

247   3.3 E DGE -AWARE DISTS
248   To further enhance the quality of the generated images, we aim to incorporate perceptual loss. Most
249   image reconstruction methods utilize LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity) (Zhang
250   et al., 2018a) as the perceptual loss. However, to better preserve image texture details and alleviate
251   pseudo-textures in the reconstruction under higher noise levels, we need to focus on the textures on
252   HR images. DISTS (Deep Image Structure and Texture Similarity) (Ding et al., 2020) can compute
253   the structural and textural similarity of images, aligning with human subjective perception of image
254
      quality. Furthermore, regions with rich textures or details often exhibit strong edge information.
      Leveraging image edge information effectively enhances texture quality. Based on this, we propose a
255
      novel perceptual loss, termed Edge-Aware DISTS (EA-DISTS). This perceptual loss simultaneously
256
      evaluates the structure and texture similarity of the reconstructed and HR images and their edges,
257   thereby enhancing texture detail restoration.

297   3.4      D ISTILLATION -F REE T RAINING
298   Here, we summarize the whole distillation-free one-step diffusion model training process. As
299   described in Section 3.1, within the generator component, DFOSD obtains ẑH and the decoded
300   high-resolution image x̂H through one-step sampling. The generator then updates its parameters by
301   computing the spatial loss Lspatial in pixel space between the generated image and the ground truth, as
302   well as the adversarial loss LG derived from the discriminator in the latent space (Eq. 2). The loss
303
      function for updating the generator is defined as Lspatial ` λ1 LG . Specifically, we employ a weighted
      sum of Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss and perceptual loss to define the spatial loss:
304
305
                    Lspatial pGθ pxL q, xH q “ LMSE pGθ pxL q, xH q ` λ2 LEA-DISTS pGθ pxL q, xH q,       (8)
306   where λ1 and λ2 are hyperparameters used to balance the contributions of each loss component.
307   For discriminator training, we utilize paired training features, where each pair consists of a negative
308   sample feature ẑH (generated by the generator) and the corresponding real image’s latent repre-
309   sentation zH as a positive one. Using Eq. 3, we compute the adversarial loss LD to update the
310   discriminator’s parameters. Furthermore, the discriminator can be initialized with weights from more
311   powerful pre-trained models, such as SDXL (Podell et al., 2023), to achieve superior performance.
312   This distillation-free training approach allows our DFOSD to overcome the limitations imposed by
313   multi-step diffusion models, enhancing generator performance without increasing its parameter count
314   or compromising efficiency. Additionally, the integration of a robust discriminator initialized with
315   advanced pre-trained models ensures that the generator receives high-quality feedback, facilitating
316   the production of more realistic and detailed high-resolution images.

      Evaluation Metrics. To comprehensively assess the performance of each method, we employ four
      full-reference (FR) and four no-reference (NR) image quality metrics. The FR metrics consists of
393
      Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM), Learned Perceptual
394
      Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018a), and Deep Image Structure and Texture
395   Similarity (DISTS) (Ding et al., 2020). PSNR measures pixel-wise differences, while SSIM evaluates
396   structural similarity. Both PSNR and SSIM are computed on the Y channel in the YCbCr color
397   space. LPIPS assesses perceptual similarity using deep neural network features. DISTS combines
398   structural and textural comparisons. The NR metrics include Naturalness Image Quality Evaluator
399   (NIQE) (Zhang et al., 2015), Multi-scale Image Quality Transformer (MUSIQ) (Ke et al., 2021), Multi-
400   scale Attention-based Image Quality Assessment (ManIQA) (Yang et al., 2022), and ClipIQA (Wang
401   et al., 2023a). NIQE evaluates image quality based on statistical features. MUSIQ captures multi-
402   scale distortions using Transformers. ManIQA employs attention mechanisms to assess quality.
403
      ClipIQA leverages pre-trained models like CLIP to align quality assessments with human perception.

515   5 D IFFERENCES WITH OTHER O NE -S TEP D IFFUSION SR M ODELS
516   We further discuss the difference between our DFOSD and representative one-step diffusion image
517   SR methods, SinSR (Wang et al., 2024b) and OSEDiff (Wu et al., 2024a).
518   Difference with SinSR. First, SinSR requires performing multi-step deterministic sampling during
519   training to obtain noise-image pairs, which greatly increases the training time. DFOSD does not rely
520   on the results generated by the muti-step pre-trained diffusion models. Second, the involvement of
521   a teacher model during training further escalates memory consumption. In contrast, each training
522   iteration of DFOSD takes a lower latency than SinSR.
523   Difference with OSEDiff. First, OSEDiff leverages Variational Score Distillation (VSD) to optimize
524   generated images, which necessitates the participation of 3 SD UNets during training, resulting in
525   increased memory usage and prolonged training time. In comparison, our DFOSD requires only 1.5
526   SD UNets, reducing the training model size by at least 50%. Second, OSEDiff extracts prompts from
527
      LR images with DAPE, and encoding them into conditional input for SD UNet. DFOSD only uses
      learnable text embedding as the conditional input, which further reduce computational cost.

530
531
      In this work, we propose DFOSD, a Distillation-Free One-Step Diffusion model, for Real-ISR.
      Departing from the diffusion distillation strategies commonly employed in previous studies, our
532
      approach effectively reduces training overhead. Specifically, we design a noise-aware discriminator
533
      (NAD) that capitalizes on the aggregation capabilities of intermediate features from a pre-trained
534   SD UNet. NAD makes it hard for the generator to distinguish reconstruction from real images.
535   Additionally, we propose the edge-aware DISTS (EA-DISTS) perceptual loss, which significantly
536   enhances the texture realism and visual quality of the generated images. Our distillation-free strategy
537   enables DFOSD to outperform pre-trained multi-step diffusion models in terms of visual results.
538   Comprehensive experiments confirm that DFOSD achieves superior performance and substantially
539   improves the realism of the generated images. These advancements highlight the potential of our
      method for more efficient and effective image restoration tasks.
