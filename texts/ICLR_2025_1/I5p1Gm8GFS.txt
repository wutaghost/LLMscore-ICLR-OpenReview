011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) excel in general tasks but struggle in special-
014            ized domains like healthcare due to limited domain-specific knowledge. Super-
015
               vised Fine-Tuning (SFT) data construction for domain adaptation often relies on
               heuristic methods, such as GPT-4 annotation or manual data selection, with a data-
016
               centric focus on presumed diverse, high-quality datasets. However, these methods
017
               overlook the model’s inherent knowledge distribution, introducing noise, redun-
018            dancy, and irrelevant data, leading to a mismatch between the selected data and
019            the model’s learning task, resulting in suboptimal performance. To address this,
020            we propose a two-stage model-centric data selection framework, Decomposed
021            Difficulty Data Selection (3DS), which aligns data with the model’s knowledge
022            distribution for optimized adaptation. In Stage 1, we apply Prompt-Driven Data
023            Selection via Explicit Alignment, where the model filters irrelevant or redundant
024            data based on its internal knowledge. In Stage 2, we perform Decomposed Dif-
025            ficulty Data Selection, where data selection is guided by our defined difficulty
026
               decomposition, using three metrics: Instruction Understanding, Response Con-
               fidence, and Response Correctness. Additionally, an attention-based importance
027
               weighting mechanism captures token importance for more accurate difficulty cal-
028
               ibration. This two-stage approach ensures the selected data is not only aligned
029            with the model’s knowledge and preferences but also appropriately challenging
030            for the model to learn, leading to more effective and targeted domain adaptation
031            fine-tuning. In the case study of the medical domain, our extensive experiments
032            on real-world healthcare datasets demonstrate the superiority of 3DS over existing
033            methods in accuracy by over 5.29%. Our dataset and code will be open-sourced
034            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/3DS-E67F.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) have showcased significant potential
      in natural language understanding. Open-source models such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) and
040
      Qwen (Bai et al., 2023) have also rapidly advanced, delivering competitive performance. However,
041
      in specialized domains like healthcare, their effectiveness is often constrained by the lack of domain-
042   specific knowledge (Sanaei et al., 2023; Harris, 2023; Waisberg et al., 2023), essential for tasks like
043   diagnosis (Panagoulias et al., 2024; Ullah et al., 2024) and treatment recommendations (Wilhelm
044   et al., 2023; Nwachukwu et al., 2024). To address this, some works (Wang et al., 2023a; Zhang
045   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023a; Pal & Sankarasubbu, 2023) have adapted LLMs
046   to the medical domain by training on large-scale healthcare-specific datasets.
047
      A common approach for LLM domain adaptation is Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on domain
048   instruction tuning datasets. Unlike continued pre-training, where data quantity is crucial (Que et al.,
049   2024), existing works (Zhou et al., 2024) show that SFT requires only a small but high-quality
050   dataset to effectively trigger a model’s abilities in the desired direction. Expanding the dataset with-
051   out careful selection can introduce challenges that affect model performance (Wang et al., 2023d),
052   highlighting the need for additional factors in data selection to ensure effective fine-tuning. Yet, it
053   remains unclear how to define optimal data samples for instruction tuning and systematically iden-
      tify them. Efforts have largely relied on heuristic methods, such as GPT-4 annotation (Liu et al.,

054
      2023) or manual data selection (Ji et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024), taking a data-centric approach
055   that prioritizes what is assumed to be diverse and high-quality datasets. However, these datasets
056   may fail to align with the model’s actual needs, creating gaps between the selected data and the
057   model’s inherent knowledge, where the inherent knowledge refers to the broad, task-agnostic fac-
058   tual knowledge embedded in the model’s parameters during pre-training on large, diverse textual
059   corpora (Petroni et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2023; AlKhamissi et al., 2022). Training on data that fails
060   to align with this distribution can lead to suboptimal fine-tuning performance (Gekhman et al., 2024;
061   Ren et al., 2024). To bridge this gap, we hope to explore a model-centric approach that focuses on
062   effectively selecting data aligned with the model’s current knowledge distribution. We define this
063
      setting as model-centric instruction data selection:
064   Given a general LLM and a large domain-specific instruction dataset, how can we efficiently select
065   data based on the model’s knowledge distribution to best trigger its domain abilities?
066
      We address this problem by aligning training data with the model’s inherent knowledge distribution,
067   optimizing both its informativeness and complexity to drive effective learning. This alignment
068   ensures the model is exposed to data that is both engaging and appropriately challenging, allowing
069   it to build on existing knowledge while addressing gaps. As a result, two key challenges emerge:
070
      C1. How to filter low-quality and redundant data for efficient and effective domain adaptation?
071
      Domain-specific datasets, aggregated from diverse, large-scale sources, often contain noisy or re-
072
      dundant data—reintroducing knowledge the model has already internalized. Such data can disrupt
073   learning (Wang et al., 2024a), hinder the identification of knowledge gaps (Havrilla & Iyer, 2024),
074   waste resources, and increase the risk of overfitting (Budach et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b). In
075   domain adaptation, acquiring specialized knowledge makes a model-centric data selection strategy
076   necessary. The strategy must filter data based on the model’s internal knowledge, dynamically re-
077   moving redundancy and noise to focus on novel, challenging tasks.
078
      C2. How to balance data difficulty with the model’s learning capacity? Data difficulty refers to
079   the degree to which the model has mastered the data. The difficulty of domain-specific instruction
080   data plays a critical role in shaping the model’s learning. Overly simple data wastes resources, while
081   overly complex data can overwhelm the model and stall progress (Gekhman et al., 2024; Ren et al.,
082   2024; Kang et al., 2024). In domain adaptation, dynamically adjusting data selection to match the
083   model’s evolving knowledge is both essential and challenging. Accurately assessing the model’s
084   current state and determining how it handles increasing data complexity is difficult. The variability
085   in learning progress further complicates efforts to calibrate data difficulty, making it challenging to
086   avoid under- or overloading the model. Achieving this balance is key to ensuring steady learning
087
      and maximizing domain-specific knowledge acquisition.
088   To address these challenges, we propose Decomposed Difficulty Data Selection (3DS), a two-stage
089   model-centric data selection framework which aligns with the model’s knowledge distribution to
090   optimize domain adaptation. 1) For C1, we employ Prompt-Driven Data Selection via Explicit
091   Alignment, where the model scores the dataset to explicitly remove irrelevant information. This
092   ensures that only high-quality data aligned with the model’s internal knowledge and preferences
093
      (model’s judgment to decide what data is good) is retained, minimizing noise. 2) To address C2, we
      propose a novel Decomposed Difficulty Data Selection via Implicit Distribution Modeling. This ap-
094
      proach extends traditional perplexity (PPL) calculations by introducing three key difficulty metrics:
095
      Instruction Understanding Difficulty, Response Confidence Difficulty, and Response Correctness
096   Difficulty. Additionally, we apply an attention-based importance weighting mechanism to capture
097   the varying importance of tokens, ensuring more accurate difficulty evaluation. This method en-
098   sures that data complexity is dynamically aligned with the model’s learning capacity, optimizing the
099   fine-tuning process. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
100
101
      • We introduce 3DS, a two-stage model-centric data selection framework aligning training data with
        the model’s inherent knowledge distribution, optimizing effective domain adaptation.
102
      • We propose a novel difficulty decomposition strategy within 3DS, quantifying data difficulty
103
        through three metrics: Instruction Understanding, Response Confidence, and Response Correct-
104     ness, ensuring fine-grained data difficulty quantification in the domain adaptation process.
105   • Our extensive experiments on Chinese medical datasets demonstrate that 3DS outperforms exist-
106     ing methods, significantly boosting LLMs performance in the medical domain. 3DS has also been
107     successfully deployed in real-world medical applications (details omitted for anonymity).

114   2.1   DATA S ELECTION FOR LLM
115
116
      Data selection for LLM training has been explored through various approaches. Some works (Das
      & Khetan, 2023) utilize statistical clustering or core-set selection techniques to identify diverse and
117
      representative subsets, yet they neglect data quality and may incorporate noisy samples that hinder
118
      model training. To address quality concerns, some works leverage external models like proprietary
119   LLMs (Chen et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023; Wettig et al., 2024) or reward models (Du et al., 2023)
120   to evaluate and select high-quality training data. However, due to distribution differences and pref-
121   erence gaps between external models and the model to be trained, the selected data may not be
122   beneficial for the model to be trained, leading to limited performance gains. Another line of re-
123   search leverages information produced by the model to be trained, such as perplexity (Marion et al.,
124   2023), gradients(Xia et al., 2024) and derived metrics like data learnability (Zhou et al., 2023) and
125   instruction following difficulty (Li et al., 2024b;a). While these metrics provide more direct insights
126   into the model’s current understanding of data, they typically offer only coarse measures of data dif-
127
      ficulty, failing to capture different aspects of data complexity or account for the model’s generation
      behavior, leading to suboptimal selection. While these methods share similar challenges, insights
128
      and approaches with active learning methods Yoo & Kweon (2019); Karamcheti et al. (2021); Min-
129
      dermann et al. (2022),their application scenarios and workflows are distinct. In this work, we focus
130   exclusively on data selection tailored to the unique challenges of training LLMs. We note that exist-
131   ing data selection methods for LLMs are predominantly tailored for pre-training, general fine-tuning
132   (transforming a base model into a chat model), or targeted for specific downstream tasks. There re-
133   mains a significant absence in data selection for domain adaptation fine-tuning, where unique chal-
134   lenges lies in selecting data that effectively enhances the model’s diverse domain abilities. To bridge
135   this gap and overcome the limitations of current methods, our work introduces a novel data selection
136   framework for domain adaptation and provides a more fine-grained analysis of data difficulty.
137
138   2.2   DATA L EARNABILITY IN LLM SFT
139
140   LLMs encounter significant challenges when learning unfamiliar or complex knowledge during su-
141   pervised fine-tuning, particularly when the data was not encountered during pre-training, which can
142
      impede domain adaptation. Gekhman et al. (2024) found that models acquire new factual knowledge
      slowly during SFT, especially when the information diverges from their pre-existing understanding,
143
      leading to a higher risk of hallucinations. Ren et al. (2024) further show that when the knowledge in-
144
      troduced during Instruction Fine-tuning significantly differs from what was learned in pre-training,
145   the model struggles to integrate it, causing performance degradation. This highlights the difficulty
146   models face in using pre-training knowledge to understand new concepts. Kang et al. (2024) also
147   emphasize that unfamiliar examples during fine-tuning increase the likelihood of hallucinations,
148   suggesting that high-difficulty data can destabilize the model and negatively impact its ability to
149   adapt to new domains. Together, these findings underscore the risks associated with fine-tuning on
150   excessively difficult data, which can undermine model performance in domain-specific tasks.

186   3.2   P ROMPT-D RIVEN DATA S ELECTION VIA E XPLICIT A LIGNMENT
187
188   The first stage of our framework is to select high-quality data that closely aligns with the inherent
189   knowledge and preferences of the model to be trained. Unlike existing methods that rely on external
190   reward models or proprietary LLMs to score data quality, which often result in suboptimal outcomes
191   due to distributional mismatches and knowledge gaps, our approach directly uses the model itself
192   for data evaluation. As illustrated in Figure 1, we leverage a carefully crafted prompt, detailed in
193
      Appendix A, to instruct the model to explicitly rate data quality based on its understanding. After
      obtaining the model-generated scores, samples with scores exceeding a predefined threshold δ are
194
      retained for further selection. By utilizing this prompt-driven alignment approach based on explicit
195
      model generation, our framework effectively reduces the gap between the training data and the
196   model’s inherent preferences, filtering out possible noise from low-quality or misaligned data.
197
198
      3.3   D ECOMPOSED D IFFICULTY DATA S ELECTION VIA I MPLICIT D ISTRIBUTION M ODELING
199
200   The second stage of our framework is to analyze data difficulty via implicit distribution modeling
201   of the model to be trained, thereby selecting data with moderate difficulty that best aligns with the
202   model’s learning capacity, to facilitate efficient domain adaptation. To achieve this, our Decomposed
203   Difficulty Data Selection employs a fine-grained evaluation of data difficulty.
204   Inspired by the general problem-solving process Polya & Pólya (2014); OECD (2014)—understand-
205   ing the problem, assessing confidence in the solution, and finally providing the answer—we decom-
206   pose data difficulty into three key components that reflect the model’s understanding: (1) Instruc-
207   tion Understanding Difficulty measures whether the model comprehends the given instruction. (2)
208   Response Confidence Difficulty measures the model’s ability to provide a confident and determin-
209   istic response based on the instruction. (3) Response Correctness Difficulty measures whether
210   the model can generate a response that accurately matches the reference answer. In addition, we
211   incorporate an attention-based importance weighting mechanism that calibrates difficulty by ac-
212
      counting for the varying semantic significance of tokens in the output, to ensure a more precise
      evaluation of response-related difficulties. Next, we will delve into the quantification of the decom-
213
      posed difficulties and introduce the selection strategy.
214

      Selection Strategy based on Decomposed Difficulty Based on the decomposed data difficulties, the
292
      selection algorithm first identifies samples whose difficulty metrics fall within a predefined middle
293
      range, filtering out either trivially easy or overly complex data, focusing on moderately challenging
294   samples that matches model’s learning capabilities. Once this subset is identified, we apply K-Center
295   sampling based on instruction embeddings to enhance data diversity, reducing the risk of overfitting
296   on highly similar samples. Details about K-Center sampling process are introduced in Appendix C.

      samples for enhancing LLM’s domain abilities. Overall, the combination of these difficulty metrics
468
      contributes to a more accurate data difficulty measurement, ensuring that selected data matches the

      6        C ONCLUSION
509
510
      In this paper, we introduce a two-stage model-centric data selection framework for LLM domain
      adaptation fine-tuning. The first stage performs a prompt-driven selection strategy to explicitly align
511
      with the model’s preferences. The second stage selects data via data difficulty decomposition. By
512
      incorporating Instruction Understanding, Response Confidence, and Response Correctness difficul-
513   ties, alongside an attention-based importance weighting mechanism, our method effectively captures
514   the model’s implicit distribution and selects data that matches the its learning capacity. Experimen-
515   tal results across multiple medical tasks demonstrate significant performance gains, validating the
516   effectiveness of our selection framework. Our approach highlights the effectiveness of model-driven
517   data selection, offering a path toward more efficient LLM domain adaptation training. Future work
518   will explore extending this framework to other domains and refining the training procedure based
519   on difficulty metrics for broader LLM applications.
520
521   7        L IMITATIONS
522
523   Due to time and resource constraints, we have only validated our method in the medical domain.
524   While our data selection framework is domain-agnostic and adaptable to other fields, further exper-
525   iments in other domains are needed to fully verify its generalization. Since the selection process
526   requires the model to perform inference on the training data, it involves certain computational costs.
527   This additional inference step may increase computational overhead, especially when working with
528   very large datasets. Our framework performs data selection prior to LLM fine-tuning. Consider-
529   ing that the model’s evaluation of data difficulty may evolve during training, future research should
530
      explore dynamic selection that adapts to the model’s changing state. Additionally, data filtered out
      is currently discarded. Future work should consider integrating mechanisms such as human-in-the-
531
      loop validation or strategies to recover potentially relevant and valuable data from the discarded
532
      pool. Finally, considerations for social bias and fairness issues are discussed in Appendix J.

              DATASET
867
868   What is the primary purpose of creating this dataset?
869   This dataset was created to construct a large-scale medical domain instruction-following fine-tuning
870   dataset. The primary purpose is to support the adaptation of large language models (LLMs) to the
871   medical domain by providing diverse and comprehensive training instances. By integrating het-
872   erogeneous data sources, including doctor-patient dialogues, medical knowledge bases, and various
873   medical tasks formulated into the instruction-output format, the dataset aims to enhance the ability
874   of LLMs to perform effectively across a wide range of real-world medical scenarios. It is designed
875
      to address the unique challenges of the medical domain, such as specialized terminology, complex
      reasoning, and context-sensitive responses, thereby enabling LLMs to better meet the demands of
876
      healthcare applications.
877
878   What are the specific components of the dataset, and how were they constructed or sourced?
879   Our dataset integrates multiple open-sourced medical instruction fine-tuning datasets from di-
880   verse sources, along with doctor-patient dialogue data extracted from medical consultation web-
881   sites and a variety of medical tasks reformulated into the instruction-output format, as detailed in
882   Table 1. Medtalk singleround originates from open-sourced doctor-patient question-and-answer
883   datasets, including CMedQA2 (Zhang et al., 2018) and Health-Care-Magic1 . Medknowledge KG
884   is built from the Online Medical Knowledge-Based Data in Huatuo26M (Li et al., 2023), which
885   is derived from the extensive medical literature data provided by the Chinese Medical Associa-
886   tion. Medknowledge webqa includes knowledge-driven, open-ended question-and-answer pairs
887
      in the medical domain, sourced from (Wang et al., 2023b). Medtask promptcblue combines the
      promptCBLUE dataset (Zhu et al., 2023b) with additional data converted into the instruction-output
888
      format from the CBLUE benchmark (Zhang et al., 2022). QA website contains authentic doctor-
889
      patient dialogue data collected from the online platform of a collaborating hospital. Examples from
890   these datasets are shown in Table 5.
891
892   Are the data sources legal? How are privacy and ethical considerations addressed?
893   The dataset is derived from carefully selected sources, including publicly available datasets and data
894   crawled from the website of a collaborating hospital. Explicit permission was obtained from the
895   collaborating hospital for the use of the crawled data, and all data have been anonymized to ensure
896   that no personal information is exposed. Additionally, the hospital’s website provides open-access
897   data, complying with relevant legal and ethical standards. This ensures the legality and security of
898   the data while addressing privacy and ethical concerns.
899   What are the potential risks and limitations of this dataset?
900
      The dataset has certain inherent risks and limitations that should be acknowledged. First, as the data
901
      is collected from diverse sources, it may contain noise or inconsistencies, which could affect the
902
      quality and reliability of downstream applications. Additionally, since the dataset is derived from
903   Chinese text corpora, including medical advice and Q&A exchanges, its content may be culturally
904   and regionally specific, making it more suitable for East Asian populations. As a result, the med-
905   ical recommendations and insights in the dataset may not generalize well to other demographic or
906   cultural contexts.
907
      To address these issues, users should carefully evaluate the dataset’s suitability for their intended
908
      applications and, if necessary, consider adapting the data to align with broader use cases. Moreover,
909   noise reduction and validation techniques can be employed to improve data quality and reliability in
910   specific tasks.
911
912
      What is the usage case for this dataset?
913   This dataset is primarily intended for instruction fine-tuning of large language models (LLMs), as
914   already utilized in this study. Practitioners can use it to fine-tune LLMs to adapt to the medical
915   domain, as well as to enhance its medical abilities in general fine-tuning. Additionally, the dataset
916   may be useful for more specific tasks, such as fine-tuning for sub-tasks in the dataset.

930   C    K-C ENTER S AMPLING A LGORITHM
931
932   In our data selection framework, K-Center sampling is employed to ensure diversity within the
933   selected instruction fine-tuning data. After filtering based on difficulty levels, we obtain an interme-
934   diate set Smid , composed of data points within a moderate difficulty range. The K-Center sampling
935   is then applied on Smid . Specifically, the process works as follows:
936
      1. Embedding Generation: For each data sample, the instruction part is encoded into an embedding
937
      using the LLM. We extract the last hidden states of the LLM and compute the average across all
938   tokens in the sequence to form a fixed-size embedding vector. These embeddings represent the
939   semantic content of the instruction.
940
941
      2. K-Center Sampling: Using these embeddings, the K-Center sampling algorithm selects k data
      points in a greedy manner. The goal is to maximize the minimum distance between any pair of
942
      selected data points, ensuring that the sampled data points are as distinct as possible. This promotes
943
      diversity in the selected dataset and minimizes the risk of overfitting to similar data points.
944
945   The pseudo codes of this greedy K-Center sampling process are shown below:
946
947
      Algorithm 2: Greedy K-Center Sampling
948   Input: Intermediate set Smid = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sn }, model M , data budget k
949   Output: Final selected set S
950
      Step 1: Encode data in Smid using model M ;
      foreach si ∈ Smid do
951
           Encode s using M to obtain the embedding es ;
952
      end
953   Step 2: Run K-Center greedy algorithm;
954   Initialize S ← ∅ ;
955   Initialize min distances ← ∞ ;
956   for i = 1 to k do
957        if S = ∅ then
958            Select sj ∈ Smid randomly and add it to S ;
959        else
960            min distancesj = minsi ∈S ∥esj − esi ∥2 , ∀sj ∈ Smid \ S;
961            Select s∗ = arg maxsj ∈Smid \S min distancesj ;
962            Add s∗ to S;
963        end
964   end
965   return S

       Our proposed data selection framework is composed of two stages: 1. select high-quality data by
1135   prompting the model; 2. calculate decomposed data difficulties utilizing model perplexity. To eval-
1136   uate the contributions of each stage, we investigate the impact of removing each stage and conduct a
1137   series of ablation experiments. The experiments include (1) removing Stage 1, where 70,000 sam-
1138   ples are randomly sampled from the complete training dataset for subsequent difficulty calculation
1139   and filtering, and (2) removing Stage 2, where direct K-Center sampling is applied to the high-
1140   quality samples identified in stage 1’ without difficulty filtering. Additionally, to further validate
1141   the necessity of decomposed difficulty calculation based on model perplexity, we test (3) collaps-
1142   ing Stage 2 into Stage 1, where the model is prompted to verbalize its assessments of the three
1143
       data difficulties (Instruction Understanding Difficulty, Response Confidence Difficulty, Response
       Correctness Difficulty, with corresponding prompts shown below), bypassing the original difficulty
1144
       calculation.
1145
1146   The results highlighted in Table 7, Table 8 and Table 9 show a consistent pattern: each modification
1147   leads to a decrease in performance compared to the original method (3DS-Mean Attention),
1148   which consistently remains the best-performing approach across all models and all testing
1149
       benchmarks.
1150   Removing Stage 1 leads to significant performance degradation, demonstrating the importance of
1151   quality control. Removing Stage 2 also results in performance declines, further emphasizing the
1152   necessity of selecting appropriately difficult data for effective model fine-tuning. When Stage 2 is
1153   collapsed into Stage 1 via additional difficulty prompts, performance also degrades. During experi-
1154   ments, we observed that the model struggles to provide fine-grained assessments of data difficulty,
1155
       often generating coarse-grained scores such as 0.5, 0.8, and 1. This lack of granularity makes it chal-
       lenging to identify nuanced differences in data difficulty. Furthermore, without knowing the exact
1156
       capabilities of the model, we could not design in-context learning examples to guide finer-grained
1157
       difficulty judgments. Filtering based on model-prompted difficulties typically results in 20k-30k
1158   samples from an initial pool of 60k-70k, whereas the perplexity-based difficulty calculation reduces
1159   the selection to fewer than 10k samples. This smaller, more targeted dataset aligns better with the
1160   desired moderate difficulty range, leading to improved fine-tuning performance.
1161
       Although the performance differences between the proposed method and the ablation variants are
1162
       not very pronounced on CMB-Exam and MMCU-Medical, our method is notably the most consis-
1163
       tent across different models. Other variants, despite performing well on one model, tend to show
1164   degradation on another. For instance, collapsing Stage 2 into Stage 1 results in relatively good
1165   performance on Baichuan2-13B-Chat, but performs poorly on Baichuan2-7B-Chat. In
1166   contrast, our method maintains steady good performance across different models, underscoring its
1167   robustness and reliability.
1168
       Furthermore, domain-adaptation aims to enhance the model’s diverse abilities in the target domain,
1169
       where models need not only to answer questions accurately but also to analyze and present content
1170   effectively. The results on the medical analysis task CMB-Clin shown in Table 8 clearly demonstrate
1171   that our method significantly outperforms the ablation variants, exhibiting superior medical analysis
1172   capabilities. While the multiple-choice results did not conclusively indicate which stage is most
1173   important, the analysis performance reveals a clear trend: removing Stage 1 leads to the poorest
1174   performance, followed by removing Stage 2, and collapsing Stage 2 into Stage 1 achieves better
1175   results than both. This pattern highlights the crucial role of quality control in determining the
1176   model’s ability to provide coherent and high-quality answers. At the same time, difficulty filtering
1177   is also essential, as even the coarser-grained difficulty measurement by model verbalization yields
1178
       better results than ignoring difficulty at all. This progressive improvement reinforces the importance
       of the filtering metrics we consider, showing that both quality and difficulty are vital for selecting
1179
       beneficial data.

1181   We also conduct GPT-4 judgment to compare the analysis generated by the original method and

1183   When comparing 3DS-M eanAttention with ablation variants, the win-rate generally exceeds 60%,

1389
1390
1391   Fairness and bias are critical considerations, particularly in sensitive domains like healthcare. While
1392   our approach demonstrates promising results in fine-tuning LLMs for medical tasks, it is essential
1393   to acknowledge its limitations and potential implications concerning fairness and bias. Our method
1394   employs the LLM to evaluate data quality and calculate data difficulty. Although the evaluation
1395
       prompts and difficulty calculation metrics are designed to be neutral, the inherent biases in the base
       model may still influence the selection results. And the LoRA fine-tuning’s impact on LLM fairness
1396
       also needs further investigations Bui & Von Der Wense (2024). Another source of potential bias
1397
       arises from the composition of our training data, which predominantly consists of Chinese medical
1398   texts. While this dataset effectively reflects the health conditions and medical practices of East
1399   Asian populations, it may limit the generalizability to other regions or demographics. Current LLM
1400   data selection methods generally prioritize factors such as difficulty, quality, or diversity, without
1401   addressing fairness or examine what data is included or excluded. They focus on improving model
1402   performance on standard benchmarks, while the impact of these methods on fairness, safety, and
1403   truthfulness benchmarks, such as SafetyBench (Zhang et al., 2024) and TruthfulQA (Lin et al.,
       2022), remains underexplored. Therefore, we recognize that these issues are valuable directions for

       future research. Investigating how data selection and fine-tuning methods impact LLM fairness and
1405   safety will be essential for developing more equitable and reliable LLMs.
