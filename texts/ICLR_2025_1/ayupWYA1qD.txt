009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012            We introduce the Time Series Optimized Transformer for Observability (Toto),
013
               a foundation model designed for time series forecasting with a focus on observ-
               ability metrics. Toto features a novel proportional factorized attention mecha-
014
               nism and a Student-T mixture model head, enabling it to efficiently handle high-
015
               dimensional, sparse, and non-stationary data. Trained on one trillion time series
016            data points, including 75% proprietary observability data, Toto demonstrates state-
017            of-the-art zero-shot performance on standard benchmarks such as electricity and
018            weather forecasting. Furthermore, it significantly outperforms existing models in
019            observability-specific tasks, making it an ideal solution for real-time system mon-
020            itoring and anomaly detection. Toto’s architectural innovations make it a versatile
021            tool for both general-purpose forecasting and domain-specific applications, setting
022            a new benchmark for scalability and accuracy in time series analysis.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   We present Toto, a time series forecasting foundation model specifically designed to handle the
029   complexities of observability data. It leverages a novel transformer-based architecture to deliver
030   state-of-the-art accuracy and performance. Toto is trained on a massive dataset of diverse time
031   series data, enabling it to excel in zero-shot predictions. Our model is tailored to allow compute
032   and memory-efficient scalability to very large data volumes, thereby providing robust solutions for
033   high-frequency and high-dimensional data commonly encountered in observability metrics.
034   We detail the following key contributions:

      1.1   O BSERVABILITY DATA
067
      Observability data encompasses a comprehensive array of metrics collected to monitor and optimize
068   the performance and reliability of modern infrastructure and applications (Li et al., 2020). These
069   metrics are essential for providing insights into the health and performance of systems and include:
070
071          • Infrastructure metrics: Data related to hardware and system performance, such as mem-
072            ory usage, CPU load, disk I/O, and network throughput.
073          • Application performance indicators: Metrics that capture the performance and behavior
074            of applications, including hit counts, error rates, and latency.
075
076   Observability data is typically gathered from a variety of sources, including on-premise systems,
077   cloud services, and third-party tools. The integration of these diverse data sources enables a holistic
078   view of system performance, but also introduces several challenges for time series forecasting:
079
080         1. High temporal resolution: Observability data often requires high-resolution timestamps,
081            capturing data at intervals of seconds or minutes to detect rapid changes and anomalies.
082         2. Sparsity and zero-inflation: Many observability metrics are sparse, characterized by nu-
083            merous zero values due to the monitoring of infrequent events, such as system errors or
084            rare performance issues.
085         3. Extreme dynamic range and skewed distributions: Metrics can exhibit wide dynamic
086            ranges and heavy-tailed distributions, especially in latency measurements where occasional
087            extreme values occur.
088
            4. Dynamic and non-stationary nature: The monitored systems are dynamic, undergoing
089            frequent changes due to software updates, infrastructure scaling, feature toggles, and vary-
090            ing user behaviors, all of which contribute to non-stationary data patterns.
091
            5. High-cardinality multivariate data: Observability data often involves high-dimensional
092
               metrics, segmented by various attributes like service type, region, or instance. This results
093            in a large number of time series, each with potentially limited historical data.
094
095
            6. Historical anomalies: Historical data can contain anomalies and outliers resulting from
               past performance issues or incidents, complicating the forecasting process.
096
097
      Effectively forecasting observability data requires advanced time series models that can manage
098
      these complexities. Traditional forecasting methods often fall short due to their inability to scale
099   and adapt to the dynamic, high-dimensional nature of observability data. Therefore, there is a need
100   for innovative models that can capture intricate patterns and dependencies, ultimately enhancing the
101   ability to proactively detect and mitigate performance issues in real-time systems.
102
103   1.2   T RADITIONAL MODELS
104
105   Historically, time series forecasting has relied on classical models such as ARIMA, exponential
106   smoothing, and basic machine learning techniques (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos, 2021). While
107   foundational, these models necessitate individual training for each metric, presenting several lim-
      itations (Fildes et al., 1998). The need to develop and maintain separate models for each metric

108
      impedes scalability, especially given the extensive range of metrics in observability data. More-
109   over, these models often fail to generalize across different types of metrics, leading to suboptimal
110   performance on diverse datasets (Stevenson, 2007; Christodoulos et al., 2010).
111
112   1.3   R ECENT WORK
113
114   Neural models, particularly those based on transformer architectures, have shown promise for im-
115   proving the accuracy of time series forecasts. These models have demonstrated state-of-the-art
116   performance on benchmark datasets (Nie et al., 2023), frequently surpassing traditional models in
117   both accuracy and robustness. Their capacity to process high-dimensional data efficiently (Lin et al.,
118   2021) makes them ideal for applications involving numerous time series metrics with varying char-
119
      acteristics, such as observability. However, in the full-shot setting, continuous retraining and tuning
      to adapt to evolving data patterns create a significant operational burden for observability use cases.
120
      This scaling limitation has hindered the adoption of deep learning–based methods for time series
121
      analysis, even as they show promise in terms of accuracy (Salinas et al., 2020).
122
123   Even more recently, a number of time series “foundation models” have been released (Das et al.,
124   2024; Ansari et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024; Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023; Rasul et al.,
125
      2023; Gruver et al., 2023). By pre-training on extensive, multi-domain datasets, these large models
      achieve impressive zero-shot prediction capabilities, significantly reducing the need for constant
126
      retraining.
127
128
      1.4   ATTENTION MECHANISMS
129
130   To address the unique challenges of time series data, and particularly to adapt transformer architec-
131   tures for multivariate time-series forecasting, several works have implemented modifications to the
132   attention mechanism. These strategies have included:
133
134          • Concatenating variates along the time dimension and computing full self-attention between
135            every space/time location, as in the “any-variate attention” used by Woo et al. (2024).
136            This can capture every possible space and time interaction, but it is costly in terms of
137            computation and memory usage.
138          • Assuming channel independence, and computing attention only in the time dimension as
139            in Nie et al. (2023). This is efficient, but throws away all information about space-wise
140            interactions.
141          • Computing attention only in the space dimension, and using a feed-forward network in the
142            time dimension (Ilbert et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).
143          • Computing “factorized attention,” where each transformer block contains a separate space
144            and time attention computation (Zhang & Yan, 2023; Rao et al., 2021; Arnab et al., 2021).
145            This allows both space and time mixing, and is more efficient than full cross-attention.
146            However, it doubles the effective depth of the network.
147
148   In Section 2.4, we propose a novel approach that allows for both space and time interactions, while
149   reducing the computational cost and improving overall scalability.
150
151   1.5   P ROBABILISTIC OUTPUTS
152
      Practitioners who rely on time series forecasting typically prefer probabilistic predictions. A com-
153
      mon practice in neural time series models is to use an output layer where the model regresses the
154
      parameters of a probability distribution. This allows for prediction intervals to be computed using
155   Monte Carlo sampling (Salinas et al., 2020).
156
157   Common choices for an output layer are Normal (Salinas et al., 2020) and Student-T (Das et al.,
158
      2023; Rasul et al., 2023), which can improve robustness to outliers. Moirai (Woo et al., 2024)
      allows for more flexible residual distributions by proposing a novel mixture model incorporating a
159
      weighted combination of Gaussian, Student-T, Log-Normal, and Negative-Binomial outputs.
160
161   However, real-world time series can often have complex distributions that are challenging to fit, with
      outliers, heavy tails, extreme skew, and multimodality. In order to accommodate these scenarios, we

207   2.1   T RANSFORMER DESIGN
208
209   We build upon the ideas discussed above to define a novel architecture that efficiently models mul-
210   tivariate time series data.
211   Transformer models for time series forecasting have variously used encoder-decoder (Zhou et al.,
212   2020; Wu et al., 2021; Ansari et al., 2024), encoder-only (Nie et al., 2023; Woo et al., 2024; Liu
213   et al., 2024), and decoder-only architectures (Rasul et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024). For Toto, we
214   employ a decoder-only architecture (Fig. 2). Decoder architectures have been shown to scale well
215   (Radford & Narasimhan, 2018; Radford et al., 2019), and allow for arbitrary prediction horizons.
      The causal next-patch prediction task also simplifies the pre-training process.

247   2.2   I NPUT / OUTPUT SCALING
248
249   As in other time series models, we perform instance normalization on input data before passing it
250   through the patch embedding, in order to make the model generalize better to inputs of different
251   scales (Kim et al., 2022). We scale the inputs to have zero mean and unit standard deviation. The
252   output predictions are then rescaled back to the original units.
253
254   2.3   I NPUT EMBEDDING
255
256   Time series transformers in the literature have used various approaches for creating input embed-
257   dings. We use non-overlapping patch embeddings (Cordonnier et al., 2020; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021;
258   Nie et al., 2023) (Fig. 3) of size P = 32, to project input time-series containing L = 4096 points to
259   embeddings of size 128 × D per variate, where D = 512 is the embedding dimension.
260
261   2.4   ATTENTION MECHANISM
262
263   Observability metrics are often high-cardinality, multivariate time series. Therefore, we designed
264   our model to natively handle multivariate forecasting by analyzing relationships both in the time
265   dimension (“time-wise” interactions) and in the channel dimension (“space-wise” interactions).
266   In order to model both space and time-wise interactions, we adapt the traditional multi-head atten-
267   tion architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) from one to two dimensions. We follow the intuition that
268   for many time series, the time relationships are more important or predictive than the space relation-
269   ships. As evidence, we observe that even models that completely ignore space-wise relationships
      (such as PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) and TimesFM (Das et al., 2024)) can still achieve competitive


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      performance on multivariate datasets. However, other studies (e.g. Woo et al. (2024)) have shown
271   clear benefit to including space-wise attention in ablation studies.
272
273   We therefore propose a novel variant of factorized attention, which we call “Proportional Factorized
274
      Space-Time Attention.” We use a mixture of alternating space-wise and time-wise attention blocks.
      As a configurable hyperparameter, we can change the ratio of time-wise to space-wise blocks, thus
275
      allowing us to devote more or less compute budget to each type of attention. For our base model,
276
      we selected a configuration with one space-wise attention block for every two time-wise blocks.
277   This method allows for reduced computational complexity when compared to a traditional attention
278   scheme (see Section A.1).

345   3.1   O BSERVABILITY DATASET
346
347   An observability platform ingests more than a hundred trillion events per day. However, much of
348   this data is sparse, noisy, or too granular or high in cardinality to be useful in its raw form. To
349   curate a high-quality dataset for efficient model training, we sample queries based on quality and
350
      relevance signals from dashboards, monitor alerts, and notebooks. This provides a strong signal that
      the data resulting from these queries is of critical importance and sufficient quality for observability
351
      of real-world applications.
352
353   Observability metrics are accessed using a specialized query language supporting filters, group-bys,
354   time aggregation, and various transformations and postprocessing functions. We consider groups
355   returned from the same query to be related variates in a multivariate time series (Fig. A1). After we
356
      retrieve the query results, we discard the query strings and group identifiers, keeping only the raw
      numeric data.
357
358   Handling this vast amount of data requires several preprocessing steps to ensure consistency and
359   quality. We describe the details of preprocessing and data augmentation in Section A.3.1.
360
361   3.2   S YNTHETIC DATA
362
363   We use a synthetic data generation process similar to TimesFM (Das et al., 2024) to supplement our
364   training datasets, improving the diversity of the data and helping to teach the model basic structure.
365   The procedure used to generate synthetic data is detailed in Section A.3.2.

368   4     R ESULTS
369
370   To evaluate predictions, we sequentially divide a time series into context and forecast segments.
371   We input the context segment into Toto and autoregressively generate output patches by sampling
372   from the Student-T mixture model distribution. We forecast a number of steps equal to the nearest
373   multiple of the patch size, then truncate the predictions to the desired length. In order to keep
374
      inference time consistent, we vary the number of samples generated based on the cardinality and
      length of the dataset, with a minimum of 100 samples. We take the median sample at each time
375
      step as the final point prediction. This prediction is then compared against the ground-truth forecast
376
      segment for evaluation.
377
      We report experimental results for a pre-trained Toto model in Section 4.1 and Section 4.2.

396   4.1           LSF BENCHMARKS
397
398   To assess general-purpose time series forecasting performance, we use the Long Sequence Fore-
399
      casting (LSF) benchmark datasets (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1, ETTm2, Electricity, and Weather) (Wu
      et al., 2021). For Toto, we used a historical context window of 512 time steps and took the median of
400
      200 samples. Following standard practice, we report normalized Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and
401
      Mean Squared Error (MSE), fitted on a training split, in order to be able to compare performance
402   across different datasets. We compared Toto's performance with the reported results of other recent
403   zero-shot foundation models (Woo et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024), as well as full-shot time series
404   forecasting models (Liu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Zhang & Yan, 2023; Das
405   et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; LIU et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022). We evaluate with forecast lengths
406   of 96, 192, 336, and 720 time steps, in sliding windows with stride 512, and average the results. We
407   display these results in Table 1.
408   Toto demonstrates exceptional performance across a variety of benchmark datasets, excelling in
409   zero-shot scenarios. In the LSF datasets, Toto consistently outperforms other models in terms of
410   MAE and MSE. For example, on the ETTh1 dataset, Toto achieves an MAE of 0.389 and an MSE
411   of 0.363, outperforming all zero-shot models, including the previously reported Moirai series and
412   TimesFM. Macro-averaging across the six LSF datasets, Toto achieves an MAE of 0.312 and MSE of
413   0.265, again exceeding Moirai's reported zero-shot performance as well as the reported performance
414   of the full-shot models.
415   While Toto generally excels, there are areas where its performance is closely matched by other mod-
416   els. In full-shot scenarios, models like PatchTST, Crossformer, and FEDformer show competitive
417   results. For example, on the Electricity dataset, while Toto achieves a leading zero-shot MAE of
418   0.246 and MSE of 0.157, iTransformer and TimesNet also show strong performance, indicating that
419   these models can catch up when additional training data is available.

      To assess the prediction of other zero-shot models on the observability Benchmark, we follow sam-
446
      pling procedures delineated in their respective manuscripts. In short, for Chronos models, we gen-
447
      erate 20 samples and take the median prediction. For Moirai models, we take the median of 100
448   samples and set the patch size to “auto”. TimesFM only produces point predictions of the mean, so
449   we use those directly. Since TimesFM and Chronos only support univariate forecasting, we process
450   each variate independently. Moirai, on the other hand, like Toto, makes joint predictions for each
451   group of related variates. For Toto, we utilize the same evaluation procedure we used on the LSF
452   benchmarks.
453
      The relative proportion of these cases are displayed in Table A4. The evaluation results (Table 2)
454   demonstrate that Toto outperforms the other models.
455
456   Because observability data can have extreme variation in both magnitude and dispersion, we select
457
      symmetric mean absolute percentage error (sMAPE) as a scale-invariant performance metric (Arm-
      strong, 1985). We also report symmetric median absolute percentage error (sMdAPE), a robust
458
      version of sMAPE (Hyndman & Koehler, 2006) that minimizes the influence of the extreme outliers
459
      present in observability data. With the lowest sMAPE of 0.672 and sMdAPE of 0.318, Toto proves
460   to be the most accurate for forecasting observability time series data.
461
462   These results suggest that current open datasets may not provide sufficient information to extrapolate
463
      to the specific nuances of observability data, highlighting the importance of training on more relevant
      data as demonstrated by Toto's superior performance.

830
831   Initially, we apply padding and masking techniques to align the series lengths, making them divisible
832   by the patch stride. This involves adding necessary left-padding to both the time series data and the
833   ID mask, ensuring compatibility with the model's requirements.
834
      Various data augmentations are employed to enhance the dataset's robustness. We introduce random
835   time offsets to prevent memorization caused by having series always align the same way with the
836   patch grid. After concatenating the observability and LOTSA datasets for training, we also imple-
837   ment a variate shuffling strategy to maintain diversity and representation. Specifically, we randomly
838   combine variates from either observability , LOTSA, and/or synthetic data with a probability of
839   10%, thus creating new, diverse combinations of data points. To sample the indices when mixing in
840   this fashion, we employ a normal distribution with a standard deviation of 1000, favoring data points
841   that were closer together in the original datasets. This Gaussian sampling ensures that, while there is
842   a preference for adjacent data points, significant randomness is introduced to enhance the diversity
843
      of the training data. This approach improves the model's ability to generalize across different types
      of data effectively.
