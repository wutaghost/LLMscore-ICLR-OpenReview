                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Training large scale neural networks typically involves sharing the gradients be-
010
               tween all accelerators, which necessitates specialized high-speed interconnects.
011
               Taking cues from signal processing, we show that it is not necessary to share or
012            synchronize the full optimizer states and model parameters during training. By
013            decoupling the momentum and allowing divergence in the optimizer states across
014            accelerators, it is possible to even improve convergence compared to previous state
015            of the art optimizers. From this, we introduce Decoupled Momentum, a fused
016            optimizer and data parallel algorithm (DeMo) that reduces the communication re-
017            quirements by several orders of magnitude, potentially enabling future training of
018            large neural networks on slow internet bandwidths with heterogeneous network-
019            ing hardware. Furthermore, our method is agnostic to the network topology and
020
               neural network architecture, and supports scalable clock-synchronous distributed
               training with negligible compute and memory overhead. Empirically, we show
021
               that models trained with DeMo match or surpass the performance of equal models
022
               trained with AdamW, entirely bypassing the need for high-speed interconnects for
023            pre-training large scale foundation models.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large scale neural networks, particularly language models, are characterized by high parameter
029   counts. Indeed, it is not uncommon to talk about models with trillions of parameters. To train these
030   models, multiple accelerators (e.g. GPUs, TPUs) must be used to make training time tractable.
031   There are multiple strategies to split the training among these accelerators, such as Distributed Data
032   Parallelism (Li et al., 2020) and Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (Zhao et al., 2023). These techniques
033
      work by having the accelerators split the weights and synchronize the gradients (sometimes multiple
      times per step), the size of which is on the order of the size of the model itself.
034
035   Gradient synchronization between accelerators necessitates the use of specialized high-speed in-
036   terconnects (e.g. Infiniband). These interconnects are expensive localized networking topologies,
037   which requires that all accelerators be present in the same data center. However, if the amount of
038   data needed to be synchronized could be massively reduced, these requirements could be relaxed.
039   In this paper we show that the gradients and optimizer states during training of many large scale
040   neural networks are redundant and highly compressible. Armed with this knowledge, we develop
041   DeMo, an optimizer that leverages this compressibility to reduce the communication needs between
042   accelerators by several order of magnitudes. To evaluate DeMo, we train a standard LLM architec-
043   ture (decoder-only Transformer (Vaswani, 2017)) using the baseline optimizer (AdamW (Loshchilov
044   & Hutter, 2019)) on traditional high-speed interconnect as well as with DeMo under bandwidth con-
045
      strained scenarios, and show that the models trained with DeMo meet or exceed the performance of
      their vanilla counterparts.
046

048   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
049
050   To mitigate the communication overhead in distributed training, a variety of strategies have been
051   employed over the years. The most effective techniques for centralized and clock-synchronous
052   training can be sorted into three broad categories:
053

             • Quantization and sparsification of gradients.
055
056          • Low rank projection of gradients.
057          • Federated averaging (also known as Local-SGD).
058
059   It is important to note that this work will not consider asynchronous methods, decentralized methods
060   or any method that requires the use of specific network topologies or neural network architectures.
061   Such methods constitute a distinct class of techniques; they introduce considerable complexity in
062   analysis, are not generalizable to all use cases, and involve numerous factors that are beyond the
063   scope of this study. Rather, we will restrict our focus to the simplest and most generalizable scenario:
064   a centralized and clock-synchronous distributed optimizer.
065
066   2.1    Q UANTIZATION AND S PARSIFICATION
067
068   Previous work, such as (Wang et al., 2023), focuses mainly on the compressibility of the gradients
069   as-is, within the assumption that the gradient values are uncorrelated and tolerant to quantization
070
      and sparsification errors. Of note, the compression gain that can be obtained from quantization
      is bounded – a 16-bit gradient can only be at most compressed down to one bit. The gain from
071
      sparsification is unbounded, but sparsifiction can only achieve limited compression ratios without
072
      hampering training, and is therefore best suited for fine-tuning existing models.
073
074
075
      2.2    L OW R ANK P ROJECTION
076
      It was shown in (Zhao et al., 2024) that the gradients of LLMs are very low rank during training.
077   As such, a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) can be used to find a low rank projection matrix
078   that preserves the most significant directions of the gradient, and the model can be optimized using
079   only the projected gradients. This drastically reduces the size of the gradients and optimizer states
080   that have to be stored and transmitted to other accelerator nodes. A disadvantage of this approach
081   is that finding the SVD of the gradients of a very large model is expensive, and the relatively large
082   projection matrix must be shared or recomputed across all nodes. This overhead can be reduced
083   by not recomputing the SVD and projection matrices every single step, but it remains a significant
084   bottleneck that only gets worse as the model size increases. However, as this method achieves
085
      convergence1 parity with full rank optimizers when pre-training LLMs, it teaches us a very important
      and useful lesson: that compression using a low rank projection is better than sparsity and should be
086
      investigated further.
087
088
089   2.3    F EDERATED AVERAGING
090
      In (McMahan et al., 2017), federated averaging is used to reduce the amount of communication
091
      needed between nodes during a distributed training run. Essentially, each accelerator node trains
092
      independently for a fixed number of steps, then synchronizes the accelerator nodes by averaging
093   their weights, which means that the gradient and optimizer states do not need to be communicated
094   for every single step. However, at synchronization this method still requires sharing the full param-
095   eter weights across every node, which has the same order of bandwidth cost as unmodified training.
096   Furthermore, increasing the number of steps between synchronizations hurts convergence, exchang-
097   ing one training speed bottleneck with another. In one extreme, the training iterations run very fast
098   because there is little communication between the nodes, but convergence is very slow. In the other
099   extreme, we can have convergence parity with traditional optimizers, but the time spent synchroniz-
100   ing the parameters becomes significant, so on average one iteration will be prohibitively slow. The
101
      optimal point is somewhere in the middle. In practice, federated averaging slows down training by a
      non-negligible factor and does not scale well with respect to the number of accelerator nodes, which
102
      makes it difficult to find the optimal hyper-parameters2 for specific training runs.
103
104      1
           Rate of loss decrease per iteration, or per minibatch of data.
105      2
           A lot of extraneous variables unrelated to the optimizer itself will affect the optimal hyper-parameters,
106   such as (but not exclusively), the number of accelerator nodes, the network bandwidth, the neural network
107   architecture, the batch sizes, etc. In most cases, federated averaging only works on a case-by-case basis, and is
      not suitable as a drop-in optimizer replacement.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
109
110
      Rather than relying on ad-hoc modifications of existing optimization algorithms, we propose in
111   this work a novel and general decoupled momentum optimization algorithm that allows and utilizes
112   different optimizer states between accelerators.
113
114   3.1     A SSUMPTIONS
115
116   To formulate our method, we made three crucial assumptions that, while currently lacking theoretical
117   proof, show indications of validity based on empirical evidence.
118
119   Conjecture 3.1 Fast moving components of the momentum are highly spatially auto-correlated,
120
      most of the energy of the momentum is concentrated in a few principal components.
121
      Conjecture 3.2 Fast moving components of the momentum have low temporal variance and should
122
      be used to update the parameters immediately, whereas slow moving components of the momentum
123   have high temporal variance and should be smoothed out temporally and be used over a longer
124   period of time.
125
126   Conjecture 3.3 Slow moving components of the momentum are very important for long-term con-
127   vergence, they should not be filtered out or be discarded.
128
129   We will not formally prove any of these conjectures in this work, but the optimizer that we show
130   was made with all of these assumptions in mind. We hope that by proposing this novel method, it
131
      can help develop these ideas further in future research.
132
133   3.2     A LGORITHM
134
      Starting from the SGD with Momentum optimization algorithm, we first remove the all-reduce op-
135   eration on the gradients g̃k , decoupling the momentum m in each accelerator node. Then, after
136   updating the momentum, we extract and remove from it the fast components q, which can be syn-
137   chronized very efficiently with a minimal amounts of data being transmitted. The pseudo-code of
138   the algorithm is described in Algorithm 1.
139
140   Algorithm 1 Decoupled Momentum Optimization
141     Input: learning rate η, decay β ∈ (0, 1), parameters xt , momentum mt , hyperparameters s, k
142     g̃t ← LocalStochasticGradient(xt )                        ▷ Get local gradient g without all-reduce
143     mt ← βmt + g̃t                                            ▷ Accumulate gradient in momentum m
144     qt ← ExtractFastComponents(mt , s, k)                          ▷ Extract fast components q from m
145     mt+1 ← mt − qt                                                                  ▷ Remove q from m
146     Qt ← Synchronize(qt )                                       ▷ Synchronize q across all accelerators
147     xt+1 ← xt − ηQt                                                             ▷ Parameter update step
148
149
      3.2.1    E FFICIENT E XTRACTION OF FAST M OVING C OMPONENTS
150
151   In order for our method to work, we first have to decorrelate, separate and extract the principal
152   components from the momentum during training. Assuming 3.1 is true, one way would be to apply
153   a spatial Kosambi–Karhunen–Loève Transform (KLT) to the momentum in order to separate the
154   faster moving components from the slower ones. However, computing the KLT on the momentum
155   of a large neural network with billions or even trillions of parameters is prohibitively expensive.
156   Alternatively, taking cues from signal processing work, the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) can
157   act as an approximation of the KLT, if used for the purpose of energy compaction, as they are
158   both decorrelating transforms. It is shown that for highly spatially correlated signals, the DCT
159   approximation approaches the KLT (Roma & Sousa, 2011). While the DCT approximation is not
160   perfect, it has many advantages that cannot be overlooked. Firstly, the DCT is highly parallelizable
161   and is extremely fast to compute on modern GPUs. Furthermore, it being a separable transform
      ensures that its computational complexity scales linearly for 2D, 3D or even n-dimensional signals.


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Finally, the DCT has a fixed orthogonal basis, which means it is possible to perfectly decode a DCT-
163   encoded signal without any auxiliary information; the transform matrix and its inverse are known in
164   advance.
165
166
      If 3.1 is true, we can assume that the DCT would be a good approximation to the KLT for extracting
      the fast moving components from our momentum, as the momentum is assumed to be spatially
167
      auto-correlated. Empirically, we found that using the DCT alone is enough to extract a very good
168
      approximation of the principal components.
169
170   During training, we treat each momentum tensor as a d-dimensional auto-correlated signal, and
171   we chunk each momentum tensor of shape (n0 , n1 , ..., nd−1 ) into contiguous chunks with shape
172
      (s0 , s1 , ..., sd−1 ), where each si is a divisor of ni , and apply a separable d-dimensional decorre-
      lating DCT transform on all chunks. Next, we find the top-k DCT frequencies with the biggest
173
      amplitudes in each chunk and treat them as if they were the principal components of the momentum.
174
      Both (s0 , s1 , ..., sd−1 ) and k are hyperparameters and control the size of the effective ”rank” of the
175   frequencies that we extract.
176
177   After extracting the highest energy frequencies, we are left with two tensors of size
178
      ( ns00 , ns11 , ..., nsd−1
                             d−1
                                 , k), one tensor representing the discrete frequency bins as integer indices, the other
179   representing the amplitudes as a floating point number.
180   Effectively what we have done here is create a fast transform p that tries to maximize ”energy
181   compaction”. This way, most of the ”movement” described by the momentum can be compressed
182   down to fewer numbers without resorting to sparsity or quantization, which is a similar idea to but
183   is not exactly the same as a low rank projection. For ease of reference, we will define the transform
184   p here as follows, where s is the vector representing the chunk sizes for each dimension of mt :
185
                                             m̃t f req , m̃t ampl = p(mt , s, k)                                    (1)
186
187
      We can reverse this transform by first scattering both frequency and amplitude tensors onto a sparse
188
      tensor chunked the same way as before, then apply the inverse DCT transform, obtaining something
189
      close to the “fast moving components” q of the original momentum:
190
191                                            qt = p−1 (m̃t f req , m̃t ampl )                                     (2)
192
193
      The next step’s momentum is then set to be equal to the residual, which represents the ”slow moving
      components” of the original momentum:
194
195                                                  mt+1 = mt − qt                                                 (3)
196
197   Note that because the principal components are removed from the momentum at each step, the
198   momentum decay rate should be lowered in general. For example, β = 0.999 would be a more
199   reasonable value for pre-training a LLM, instead of the usual β = 0.9.
200
      Also, since the DCT is computed on relatively small static chunks of shape (s0 , s1 , ..., sd−1 ), the
201
      required transition matrices can be pre-computed in advance and reused at each iteration, which
202   makes the memory and computational overhead almost negligible if implemented correctly.
203
204
      3.2.2    L OW BANDWIDTH S YNCHRONIZATION
205
206   After extracting m̃t f req , m̃t ampl from the momentum mt , we can then perform an all-gather on the
207   last dimension of the extracted bins. This allows us to perform the same inverse DCT operation by
208   scattering both frequency and amplitude tensors the same way as before, but this time we average
209   the amplitude of any duplicate frequencies. If s and k are chosen appropriately, the two tensors
210   m̃t f req , m̃t ampl can be orders of magnitude smaller than the size of the model, which allows for a
211   communications efficient way of synchronizing the update step across all accelerators.
212   Given 3.2 and 3.3, here we are effectively averaging all of the fast moving components of the mo-
213   mentum at each step, while letting the slow moving components be decoupled from each-other. If
214   we assume that slow moving components in the gradient are high variance, they will be accumulated
215   over time in the momentum. As such, slow moving components can slowly overtake fast moving
      components in strength, which is then transmitted and removed from the momentum. From this,

216
      we can conclude that slow moving components are gradually transmitted alongside the immediate
217   transmission of fast components.
218
219   Finally, the gradient descent step is described here, where η is the learning rate and Qt the fast
220
      moving components of the momentum accumulated from all accelerators:
221
                                                     θt+1 = θt − ηQt                                               (4)

224   3.3     S IGNUM
225
226   In order to improve convergence when training LLMs, a signum (Bernstein et al., 2018) variant of
227
      DeMo can be used instead, where the gradient descent step is replaced by:
228
                                                θt+1 = θt − η sign(ηQt )                                           (5)
229
230
231
      Since the second moment is not computed here, this variant of DeMo uses less memory for optimizer
      states as compared to AdamW.

234   4       E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS
235
236
      We performed experiments on the signum variant of DeMo using OLMo (Groeneveld et al., 2024),
237
      a highly reproducible large language model pre-training framework. Adapting OLMo to use DeMo
238   consisted only of including the DeMo optimizer class in the training code. The only additional
239   change needed was disabling the gradient synchronization in PyTorch Distributed Data Paral-
240   lelism (Li et al., 2020). We have publicly released the modified OLMo code as well as the con-
241   figuration files for all experiments.
242
      Our experiments used the Dolma v1.53 dataset for pre-training. As a baseline we used the publicly
243
      released OLMo-1B4 , a standard decoder-only Transformer model consisting of 1.18 billion param-
244   eters using the AdamW optimizer (β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, weight decay = 0.1) as compared to using
245   the DeMo optimizer (β = 0.999).
246
247
      Due to constrained compute availability we trained models for 100 billion total tokens rather than
      the full 3 trillion tokens in Dolma. As a secondary baseline and for complete comparability we
248
      re-trained OLMo-1B with these same 100 billion tokens and the learning rate schedule adjusted
249
      accordingly. We also repeated the experiments on a smaller 300M model identical to the 1B except
250   halving the model’s hidden size. All experiments were performed on 64 H100 GPUs with a global
251   batch size of 2048 with a sequence length 2048 tokens, resulting in a per-GPU batch size of 8.
252

      training loss, per-GPU communication requirements, and downstream evaluation scores of the Hel-

259   5       C ONCLUSION
260
261   In conclusion, we have shown that our proposed DeMo optimization algorithm can act as a drop-in
262   replacement to AdamW when training LLMs, with no noticeable slowdown in convergence while
263   reducing communication requirements by several orders of magnitude. The signum variant of DeMo
264   is more memory efficient than AdamW and has negligible compute overhead if we use small pre-
265   computed DCT transition matrices. Finally, the LLMs pre-trained with DeMo have equivalent or
266   better scores on multiple standard benchmarks compared to their equivalents trained with AdamW.
