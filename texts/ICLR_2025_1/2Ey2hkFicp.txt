011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance
014            across various domains of biology, but their ability to reason about biological path-
015            ways remains underexplored. This includes reasoning about how perturbations in
016
               biological systems lead to various downstream effects through complex interme-
               diate processes. Such reasoning is crucial for explaining and predicting biological
017
               phenomena, as well as for formulating hypotheses and designing experiments.
018
019
               In this study, we investigate whether LLMs can effectively understand and reason
               about biological pathways by introducing BioMaze, a comprehensive benchmark
020
               focusing on reasoning about the effects and mechanisms of natural and synthetic
021
               interventions—such as mutations, infections, or treatments—on various down-
022            stream targets under different conditions through complex intermediate pathway
023            processes. BioMaze spans multiple biological domains and is categorized along
024            three reasoning dimensions, capturing various aspects of pathway reasoning.
025            We evaluate LLMs using the BioMaze benchmark with reasoning methods like
026            Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and pathway graph-augmented approaches. Results
027            show that while LLMs can understand mechanisms in natural organisms, they
028            struggle with predicting phenomena after perturbations, highlighting their lim-
029            itations in reasoning about biological pathways. To address these challenges,
030            we propose PATH S EEKER, a novel LLM agent that interactively reasons through
031            subgraph-based navigation within pathway graph. This approach enhances LLMs’
032            reasoning in biological pathways by leveraging pathway graph augmentation, par-
033
               ticularly in cases involving perturbations, potentially bridging the gap between
               LLMs’ current capabilities and the complexities of biological systems.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently shown impressive performance in science across var-
      ious domains, including mathematics (Yu et al., 2023), chemistry (Liu et al., 2023b), biology (Hayes
040
      et al., 2024; Madani et al., 2020), and materials science (Zheng et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024). In the
041
      biological domain specifically, recent studies have demonstrated the potential of LLMs in tackling
042   challenging tasks such as protein design (Valentini et al., 2023; Hosseini et al., 2024), drug discovery
043   (M. Bran et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023c), clinical trial analysis (Singhal et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023),
044   and experiment design (AI4Science & Quantum, 2023). Although LLMs are increasingly capable
045   of addressing more complex, real-world problems within the biological sciences, their fundamental
046   understanding, reasoning, and metacognitive abilities (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Kojima
047   et al., 2022) toward these scenarios—specifically in comprehending and reasoning through the in-
048   tricate, multi-step processes involved in biological systems—have yet to be thoroughly explored.
049   Biological systems are composed of complex networks called pathways, which function as intercon-
050   nected units involving various components, such as enzymes, substrates, and signaling molecules.
051   These components interact in a highly coordinated manner, enabling the integration of multiple sig-
052   nals and precise regulation of system responses. As a result, intervention in a single component of a
053   pathway—such as mutations, inhibitions, or pathogen infections—can influence other components
      within the organism via intricate, multi-step intermediate processes.

069   A wide range of biological phenomena can be explained and predicted by understanding and reason-
070   ing about biological pathways. In biological research, this understanding is essential for formulating
071   hypotheses, designing experiments, and predicting and interpreting results. For example, blocking
072
      muscarinic M3 receptors in taste cells triggers a sequence of events, including reduced calcium
      mobilization in Type II taste cells, a weakened role of CALMH1 in ATP release, and diminished
073
      taste-evoked responses in taste sensory fibers, as shown in Figure 1. These insights can be useful
074
      for toxicity analysis, designing experiment groups with induced taste suppression, and developing
075   treatments for decreased sense of taste.
076
077   Given the complexity of biological systems and the importance of understanding pathway interac-
078
      tions, the application of LLMs to analyze and predict their behavior presents both opportunities and
      challenges. In this paper, we first introduce BioMaze, a benchmark that serves as a crucial start-
079
      ing point for assessing LLMs’ ability to comprehend and reason about realistic biological pathway
080
      phenomena. BioMaze compiles biological pathway phenomena from literature and generating cor-
081   responding questions and answers. These questions span multiple biological domains, focusing on
082   predicting the effects and mechanisms of natural and synthetic interventions on various targets un-
083   der different conditions through complex intermediate processes. Targets may include individual
084   components, component interactions, their roles in biological processes, or larger-scale functions.
085
      We conducted extensive evaluations of LLMs using the BioMaze benchmark, incorporating reason-
086
      ing methods such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and pathway graph-augmented approaches (Li et al.,
087   2023a; Sun et al., 2023; He et al., 2024). The results show that while LLMs demonstrate an un-
088   derstanding of mechanisms within natural organisms, they struggle to predict phenomena and grasp
089   mechanisms when perturbations are introduced into the system—such as during interventions or
090   when organisms are in altered conditions. This reveals that LLMs’ causal reasoning abilities for
091   biological pathways are limited. To address these challenges, we then propose a novel approach,
092   PATH S EEKER, an LLM agent that interactively reasons through subgraph-based navigation while
093   exploring the pathway graph. This method enhances LLMs’ performance in complex biological
094   reasoning tasks by effectively leveraging pathway graph information as blueprints in reasoning, es-
095
      pecially in the case of interventions. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
096
               • We introduce BioMaze, a comprehensive benchmark designed to assess LLMs’ ability to
097              understand and reason about biological pathways. BioMaze focuses on evaluating the mod-
098              els’ capacity to predict the effects and elucidate the mechanisms of both natural and syn-
099              thetic interventions—such as mutations and infections—on various downstream targets un-
100              der diverse conditions through complex intermediate pathway processes. The benchmark
101              spans multiple biological domains and is structured along three dimensions: interventions,
102              conditions, and target types.
103            • We conduct extensive evaluations of LLMs using BioMaze, incorporating advanced rea-
104              soning methods such as CoT and pathway graph-augmented approaches. Our results reveal
105              that while LLMs demonstrate proficiency in understanding mechanisms within natural or-
106              ganisms, they encounter significant challenges when predicting phenomena and compre-
107              hending mechanisms in perturbed systems. These findings highlight critical limitations in
                 LLMs’ reasoning capabilities within the domain of biological pathways.

117   Biological Scientific Question Answering Previous studies have explored the potential of language
118   models in the biological scientific domain (Lu et al., 2022; Vilares & Gómez-Rodrı́guez, 2019; Jin
119   et al., 2021; Pal et al., 2022). MEDHOP (Welbl et al., 2018) and PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019) inves-
120
      tigated biological scientific QA in the form of reading comprehension. BioASQ-QA (Krithara et al.,
      2023) proposed a realistic question-answering benchmark for the information needs of biomedical
121
      experts. A few studies have examined language models’ ability to understand biological pathways.
122
      Chatpathway (Li et al., 2023b), Park et al. (2023), and Azam et al. (2024) specifically investigated
123   language models’ capacity for completing biological pathways. Different from previous tasks, this
124   work introduces a novel task for practical biological pathway phenomenon reasoning, addressing
125   the reasoning capacity of LLMs. See Appendix A.13 for a more detailed comparison.
126
      Graph-augmented Language Model Several studies have explored augmenting LLMs with graph
127
      data. In particular, some works enhance LLMs by encoding graph data as text (Ye et al., 2023;
128
      Wang et al., 2024; Fatemi et al., 2023), or tuning LLMs specifically for graph-based tasks (Liu et al.,
129   2023a; Tang et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; He & Hooi, 2024). Augmented LLMs
130   have been applied to knowledge-based QA (Sun et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Jin
131   et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024; Edge et al., 2024) and graph tasks like property prediction (Wang
132   et al., 2024; He et al., 2023). Some studies leverage graph structures for complex reasoning tasks
133   (Jiang et al., 2023; Besta et al., 2024). Most large graph databases use retrieval mechanisms (He
134   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a), while others employ LLMs as interactive agents for navigation (Sun
135   et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). This work introduces a more efficient agent-based
136   approach using subgraph navigation and reasoning to improve pathway database exploration.

      3.1   DATASET C REATION
142
      To gather realistic biological pathway phenomena, particularly those involving interventions, as il-
143
      lustrated in Figure 1, the data for BioMaze is sourced from over 6,000 biological pathway research
144   papers. These studies involve carefully designed intervention experiments supported by pathway
145   mechanisms to observe how biological systems respond. We extract detailed experimental observa-
146   tions and their contexts directly from the abstracts. Importantly, our focus is on the specific experi-
147   mental phenomena observed and reported, rather than the final conclusions drawn by the researchers.
148   This is essential for our goal of predicting detailed events in the benchmark. The dataset creation
149   involves prompting the large language model, and in this study, we choose LLaMA3.1-405B (Dubey
150   et al., 2024) and GPT-4 as the models for data creation.
151   After extracting the phenomena, we convert each one into either a True/False or open-ended ques-
152   tion, depending on its content. Each question is paired with corresponding labeled answers. We then
153   apply multiple data filters and human validation steps to ensure the correctness, quality, and rele-
154   vance to biological pathways. The correctness of each question is validated by checking whether
155   LLMs can answer it accurately using the original paper content. Question quality is ensured through
156   several filters, removing questions that are poorly defined, unpredictable (e.g., asking for specific
157   measurement values), query more than one fact, are trivial with answers revealed in the question’s
158
      context, or are unrelated to biological pathways. After all the filters, BioMaze contains 1.3k high-
      quality questions for biological pathways reasoning.
159
160   The questions of BioMaze cover a wide range of biological domains, including metabolism, ge-
161   netic information processing, environmental information processing, cellular processes, organismal
      systems, and human diseases. The biological domain distribution is illustrated in Figure 2 (left).

194   3.2     R EASONING T YPE C ATEGORIES
195
196   The questions in BioMaze are categorized across three dimensions: inquiry type, extra condition,
197   and investigation target, leading to varying types and difficulties of reasoning, as shown in Table 1.
198   More full question cases are in Appendix A.1. The distribution of the three dimensions’ questions
199
      is shown in Figure 2 (right). We introduce each category of the dimensions below:
200   Dimension 1: Inquiry Type
201
      Category 1: Normal Source This category involves predicting the effects of natural components
202
      in their normal state within a biological pathway. Tasks here focus on understanding the funda-
203   mental mechanisms of pathways in biological systems. The goal is to evaluate how well LLMs can
204   comprehend and explain typical biological pathway functions.
205
206
      Category 2: Perturbed Source This category deals with predicting the effects of external inter-
      ventions or treatments, such as mutations, infections, or experimentally introduced elements, on
207
      downstream targets within pathways. Tasks emphasize reasoning about how these interventions
208
      alter pathway functions. This mirrors real-world biological research, where the focus is often on
209   understanding how such interventions influence biological systems and their downstream targets.
210
211   Dimension 2: Extra Condition
212   Category 1: Natural Condition In this category, the task predicts the impact of the inquiry source
213   under an organism’s natural conditions, meaning no additional treatments are applied to the biolog-
214   ical system beyond the inquiry source. For example, in Table 1, the natural condition question asks
215   about the mechanism through which apelin affects TNF-alpha inhibition in brown adipogenesis,
      with no extra interventions present in the pathway.


                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Category 2: Intervened Condition This category assesses the inquiry source’s impact when com-
217   bined with other factors like mutations, infections, or interventions, focusing on how these condi-
218   tions alter the pathway. For example, the question in Table 1 examines BID’s role after MNNG
219   treatment, where the pathway differs from its natural state. Enhancing performance here is cru-
220   cial for modeling complex biological scenarios, such as predicting treatment outcomes and drug
221   interactions, as it shows how multiple factors interact within a system.
222
      Dimension 3: Investigation Target
223
224   Category 1: Single Component as Target This category focuses on investigating the effect of the
225   source on a specific component within the pathway, such as its expression, activation, or inhibition.
226   Category 2: Components Interaction as Target This category examines the effect of the source
227   on interactions between components within the pathway. It may involve understanding how down-
228   stream components interact with each other or their roles in regulating pathway processes. For
229   example, the question of this category in Table 1 queries influenza protein NS1’s effect on the
230   downstream process that viral ssRNA activates RIG-I.
231   Category 3: Function as Target This category evaluates the effect of the source on broader bio-
232   logical functions or macro-level phenomena within the organism. It addresses more comprehensive
233   system behaviors, helping to link pathway-level changes with organism-wide outcomes, which are
234   crucial for scenarios like understanding health and disease processes.

236   3.3   PATHWAY G RAPH AUGMENTATION
237
238   Text-only reasoning methods like CoT generate reasoning steps directly from LLMs based on a
239
      given question. However, reasoning about biological pathways poses challenges due to their graph-
      based nature, requiring LLMs to not only have an implicit understanding of these pathways but also
240
      effectively plan and execute complex reasoning steps.
241
242   This work addresses the question: Do large language models require pathway graph data augmenta-
243   tion to reason effectively about biological systems? Providing explicit pathway graphs could serve
244   as structural blueprints, enhancing reasoning from both knowledge and logic perspectives. We for-
245
      malize this problem as:
                                                   a = G(E, o),                                            (1)
246
      where G represents the language model, E denotes the task instruction (including the question), o
247
      refers to the observation from the augment pathway graph database, and a signifies the model output
248
      which could be the answer as well as the reasoning process.
249
250   Pathway Graph Database To augment LLMs with reasoning in biological pathways, we created a
251   pathway graph database based on KEGG (Kanehisa & Goto, 2000), a collection of pathway maps on
252
      metabolism and various cellular and organismal functions widely-used resource among biologists.
      We compiled all available pathway networks and maps from KEGG and integrated all of them into
253
      a single pathway graph database. The statistics for the pathways are in Appendix A.5. Each entry
254
      in the dataset is provided with a detailed description and function corpus. The graph is structured in
255   triples as [Head IDs, Tail IDs, (Relation Type, Biological Process IDs)].
256

304   augmentation methods’ performance is limited by their ineffective utilization of the pathway graph

352   We evaluate the reasoning performance of LLMs on BioMaze in both the unaugmented step-by-step
353   reasoning and the pathway graph-augmented methods. We adopt reasoning method without graph
354   augmentation Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022), and methods with
355   pathway graph augmentation: Chain-of-Knowledge (CoK) (Li et al., 2023a), Think-of-Graph (ToG)
356   (Sun et al., 2023), and G-Retriever (He et al., 2024). Details of baselines are in Appendix A.8.
357
      For True/False tasks, we compute accuracy averaged across the True and False labels to account for
358   label imbalance in the dataset. For open-ended tasks, the LLM is used to evaluate the accuracy of
359   generated answers by comparing them to the ground truth and determining whether they are correct
360   or incorrect. In this study, we use the LLaMA3.1-405B model as the evaluator, with five in-context
361   examples. The performance of the evaluator is further analyzed in Appendix A.9.

364
365   We evaluate PATH S EEKER and baseline methods on BioMaze, presenting results in Tables 2 and 3.
366   The comparison covers task dimensions including signal source, additional conditions, and target.
367   The results lead to the following conclusions:
368   LLMs can perform biological system reasoning tasks. Despite the extreme difficulty of the tasks
369   in BioMaze, LLMs still achieved strong results, especially in normal inquiry and natural condition
370   cases. The overall performance suggests that LLMs are capable of reasoning in many biological
371   research scenarios, effectively explaining and predicting phenomena within biological systems.
372   Question with perturbation query in BioMaze presents significant reasoning challenges for
373   LLMs. Specifically, LLMs struggle more with perturbed inquiry type settings than with normal
374   inquiry types in both True/False and open-ended formats. This suggests that reasoning about bio-
375   logical pathways becomes more complex in intervention scenarios, where the events are less likely
376   to align with common biological knowledge and require deductive reasoning to predict pathway
377   behavior. In contrast, questions set in normal scenarios are more likely to be answerable using
      established biological knowledge about how typical pathways work.

439   between task complexity and reasoning steps in BioMaze, we analyzed the reasoning steps for each
440   instance by prompting the LLaMA3.1-405B to explain their reasoning process based on the correct
441   answer and pathway information.

445   biological pathway reasoning are largely due to the complexity of the pathways involved.
446
      Notably, PATH S EEKER’s performance remains more consistent across different reasoning step
447
      counts. This suggests that augmenting LLMs with biological pathway information can mitigate the
448   challenges of pathway reasoning, particularly when dealing with intricate intermediate processes.

466

467
      missing the requested effects or other key elements. (3) Omission in Reasoning For errors where
468   critical pathway steps in the question’s biological process are left out, causing the final answer to
469   be incorrect. (4) Faulty in Reasoning When the reasoning path is correct, but there are significant

473

475   ical steps or branches of the pathway are overlooked. This highlights the challenges LLMs face in
476   reasoning about biological pathways, due to both knowledge gaps and difficulties in deductive rea-
477   soning. PATH S EEKER significantly reduces faulty reasoning by providing pathway graphs, enabling
478   more accurate reasoning about biological events. However, omissions remain a predominant issue,
479   often due to limitations in the pathway database and oversights during browsing. Additionally, with
480   the availability of pathways, LLMs are less likely to fail in providing definitive answers, becoming

533
534   In this study, we introduce BioMaze, a benchmark designed to evaluate LLMs’ ability to understand
535   and reason about biological pathways by predicting the effects of natural and synthetic interven-
536   tions, like mutations and infections, on downstream targets. Extensive evaluations using BioMaze,
537   incorporating advanced methods like CoT and pathway graph-augmented approaches, show that
538   LLMs struggle with understanding pathway mechanisms with intervention. We also propose PATH -
539   S EEKER, a novel LLM agent that uses interactive subgraph exploration to enhance reasoning in
      biological pathways by leveraging pathway graphs as structured blueprints.

920   We classify the error reason of biological pathway reasoning into four types: (1) Unresolved Con-
921   clusion For cases where the model fails to provide a definitive answer, indicating uncertainty or
922   belief that the answer is unknown. (2) Incomplete Answer When the response lacks essential de-
923   tails, such as missing the requested effects or other key elements. (3) Omission in Reasoning For
924   errors where critical pathway steps in the question’s biological process are left out, causing the final
925   answer to be incorrect. (4) Faulty in Reasoning When the reasoning path is correct, but there are
926   significant errors in deducing the events within that pathway.

1268   To ensure question quality, we employ a two-step process. First, we create and filter questions using
1269   an advanced language model ( LLaMa 3.1-405B) to assess their relevance and clarity. Subsequently,
1270   each question undergoes a final quality check by human experts.
1271
       The well-define filter removes questions that are poorly defined, unpredictable (e.g., asking for spe-
1272   cific measurement values), or require more than one prediction, and the nontrivial filter removes the
1273   data that answers revealed in the question’s context.
1274
1275
       To validate the answer quality, we require the LLM (LLaMa 3.1-405B) to answer the questions based
       on the original paper’s content. The model is explicitly instructed to respond with Undetermined if
1276
       it cannot confidently generate an answer. Each question is tested five times, and only questions
1277
       that are consistently answered correctly (i.e., aligned with the intended label) and not marked as
1278   Undetermined in any of the trials are retained. This process helps eliminate questions with incorrect
1279   labels, ambiguous phrasing, or poor structure.
1280
1281
       In the final stage, human experts perform an additional quality check to refine the questions further.
       Approximately 5% of the data is filtered out at this stage, primarily due to issues such as hint
1282
       leakage in the question, overly complex phrasing (e.g., asking for multiple facts), or poorly defined
1283
       structure. During this stage, human reviewers also verify label correctness, ensuring the dataset’s
1284   overall reliability and usability.

       A.8    BASELINE D ETAILS
1580
1581   Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022) prompting encourages language
1582   models to generate step-by-step reasoning to solve tasks. We adopt CoT as the unaugmented stan-
1583   dard method for LLM reasoning.
1584
       Chain-of-Knowledge (CoK) (Li et al., 2023a) enhances chain-of-thought reasoning through knowl-
1585
       edge verification, enabling language models to handle knowledge-intensive tasks. CoK first gener-
1586   ates reasoning using chain-of-thought processes, then employs knowledge triples to verify the ac-
1587   curacy of the reasoning. While the reasoning of CoK is primarily driven by the language model,
1588   graph-based information is used for fact verification.
1589

1651   A.12         I NTRODUCTION OF PATHWAY
1652
1653   Understanding biological systems is inherently complex due to the numerous interacting molecules,
1654   processes, and environmental factors involved. These systems operate with intricate interactions that
1655   result in non-linear, multi-layered, and dynamic behaviors. To address this complexity, biological
1656   researchers use pathway graphs as structured blueprints to simplify these systems into organized
1657   structures that consist of basic interactions. The linear reactions, cyclical relationships, or the local
1658
       network of pathways offer snapshots of how a system behaves under specific conditions and enable
       researchers to predict how changes in one molecule or interaction can affect the entire system. Path-
1659
       way graphs also provide a structured, static representation of dynamic processes, helping researchers
1660
       understand the sequence of events even as the system changes over time.

       Biological Scientific Question Answering Previous studies have explored the potential of language
       models in the biological scientific domain. MEDHOP (Welbl et al., 2018) and PubMedQA (Jin
1666
       et al., 2019) investigated biological scientific question answering in the form of reading comprehen-
1667
       sion. BioASQ-QA (Krithara et al., 2023) proposed a realistic question-answering benchmark for the
1668   actual information needs of biomedical experts. Beyond textual QA, several works have also studied
1669   multimodal scientific ability (Lu et al., 2022). Additionally, other studies have explored biomedical
1670   domain tasks (Vilares & Gómez-Rodrı́guez, 2019; Jin et al., 2021; Pal et al., 2022). Most exist-
1671   ing tasks in the biological sciences concentrate on knowledge probing, assessing how well models
1672   understand biological information. In contrast, our work, BioMaze, is the first to focus on mod-
1673   els’ reasoning abilities within the biological scientific domain, specifically targeting phenomena
       observed in experiments about biological pathways.

1691   Graph-augmented Language Model Several studies have explored augmenting large language
1692   models (LLMs) with graph data. In particular, some works enhance LLMs by encoding graph data
1693   as text (Ye et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Fatemi et al., 2023), or tuning LLMs specifically for
1694   graph-based tasks (Liu et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; He &
1695   Hooi, 2024). By augmenting LLMs with graph data, they have been applied to knowledge-based
1696   QA (Sun et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Jin et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024), and to
1697   graph-oriented tasks like graph property prediction (Wang et al., 2024; He et al., 2023). A few other
1698   studies leverage graph structures during LLM reasoning to tackle complex tasks (Jiang et al., 2023;
1699
       Besta et al., 2024).
1700   Unlike tasks in previous works, this study addresses whether reasoning in biological systems can be
1701   enhanced by pathway graphs, which act as a structured blueprint for reasoning about the system’s
1702   states. It is not sufficient to simply identify the correct paths in the pathway graph to find the answer.
1703   Instead, it is necessary to perform deductive reasoning about the events that occur when the system
1704   is intervened upon under specific conditions and to predict the resulting states and mechanisms of
1705
       the intervened system.
1706   For large graph databases, most works enable LLMs to access graph data through retrieval mecha-
1707   nisms (He et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a), while a few studies have explored using LLMs as interactive
1708   agents (Yao et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024) to navigate and explore vast graph
1709   databases (Sun et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024). In this work, we introduce an agent-based interactive
1710   graph exploration approach using subgraph navigation-based browsing, which is more efficient and
1711
       offers enhanced navigation capabilities for pathway database.
