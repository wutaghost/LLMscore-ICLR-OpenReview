010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            The rapid development of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) like GPT-
013            4V has marked a significant step towards artificial general intelligence. Existing
014            methods mainly focus on aligning vision encoders with LLMs through supervised
015            fine-tuning (SFT) to endow LLMs with multimodal abilities, making MLLMs’ in-
016
               herent ability to react to multiple languages progressively deteriorate as the training
               process evolves. We empirically find that the imbalanced SFT datasets, primarily
017
               composed of English-centric image-text pairs, lead to significantly reduced perfor-
018
               mance in non-English languages. This is due to the failure of aligning the vision
019            encoder and LLM with multilingual tokens during the SFT process. In this paper,
020            we introduce PARROT, a novel method that utilizes textual guidance to drive visual
021            token alignment at the language level. PARROT makes the visual tokens condition
022            on diverse language inputs and uses Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) to promote the
023            alignment of multilingual tokens. Specifically, to enhance non-English visual to-
024            kens alignment, we compute the cross-attention using the initial visual features and
025            textual embeddings, the result of which is then fed into the MoE router to select the
026            most relevant experts. The selected experts subsequently convert the initial visual
027
               tokens into language-specific visual tokens. Moreover, considering the current lack
               of benchmarks for evaluating multilingual capabilities within the field, we collect
028
               and make available a Massive Multilingual Multimodal Benchmark which includes
029
               6 languages, 15 categories, and 12,000 questions, named as MMMB. Our method
030            not only demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on multilingual MMBench and
031            MMMB, but also excels across a broad range of multimodal tasks.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The rapid development of Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Radford et al., 2018;
037   Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023a; 2024), has gained significant attention. However, LLMs are
038   limited to processing a single textual modality. The expansion into visual modalities has endowed
039   LLMs with multimodal capabilities (Ye et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023; Dai et al.,
040   2023; Li et al., 2022), thereby accelerating the development of Multimodal Large Language Models
041   (MLLMs) and further bringing us closer to the realization of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI).
042   Current MLLMs mainly rely on pre-trained LLMs and vision encoders, focusing on bridging the
043   modality gap by aligning visual features with language embedding tokens. Existing research predom-
044   inantly employs either a Q-Former (Li et al., 2023b; Bai et al., 2023b) or an MLP projector (Liu et al.,
045   2023b; Chen et al., 2023b) to align vision encoders with LLMs. The training data mainly consists
046   of English-centric data from image captions and multimodal conversations. During training, the
047   alignment component converts the visual features into language embedding tokens. By incorporating
048   encoded visual features, the LLM can integrate visual information to respond to multimodal inputs,
049   thereby enabling the model to possess visual question answering and reasoning capabilities.
050   Multilingual capability in MLLMs entails the ability to generate responses in the same language
051   as the input, catering to the linguistic diversity inherent in conversation. Therefore, it is critically
052   important for processing language-specific content and cultural differences, ensuring equitable access
053   to technological benefits for individuals across diverse regions and nations (Chen et al., 2022; Hu
      et al., 2023). Many LLMs possess multilingual capabilities (Touvron et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023a;

      The main reason for multilingual erosion is that the data used for multimodal alignment is highly

081   2021) is trained on the large-scale image-text pairs through contrastive learning, with the text corpus

088
      challenge arises: how to use English-centric multilingual image-text data to bridge the modality
089
      gap while enhancing the MLLM’s multilingual capabilities.

091   Due to the scarcity of non-English multimodal data (e.g., lack of large-scale, high-quality image-text

093

099   tion of each language expert. Subsequently, demanding the input language, the English-biased visual

101   ROT not only to enhance its multilingual capabilities but also to bridge the multimodal gap effectively.

      2     MMMB: A M ASSIVE M ULTILINGUAL M ULTIMODAL B ENCHMARK
134
135   In this section, we first discuss the limitations of existing benchmarks and then present the character-
136   istics that an ideal multilingual benchmark should possess. Furthermore, we design and construct a
137   new benchmark and provide its corresponding evaluation strategy.
138
139   2.1    L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING B ENCHMARKS
140
      There are several existing multilingual benchmarks (e.g., Multi30K (Elliott et al., 2016),
141   M3Exam (Zhang et al., 2024b), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c), and LLaVA-Bench (Liu et al., 2023b;
142   Hu et al., 2023)) for MLLMs, but they have some limitations: 1) Outdated Benchmarks. Multi30k
143   is designed for image-text retrieval tasks, and the performance has nearly reached the upper bound
144   due to the relatively easy problems. 2) Non-Standardized Evaluations. Other benchmarks, like
145   LLaVA-Bench, rely on evaluations using GPT-4. Dependence on GPT-4 as a de facto “Ground Truth”
146   may hinder reproducibility. Meanwhile, since LLaVA uses a deprecated version (GPT-4-0314), using
147   other different versions could result in unfair comparisons. On the other hand, because M3Exam
148   does not offer consistent test samples across different languages, it cannot ensure whether poor
149
      performance is due to the problem’s difficulty or the model’s lack of multilingual capabilities. 3)
      Limited Languages. MMBench and LLaVA-Bench are limited in English and Chinese, which can
150
      not measure the multilingual capabilities across a broad spectrum.
151
152
153
      2.2    C HARACTERISTICS OF AN E FFECTIVE M ULTILINGUAL B ENCHMARK
154
      To more suitably evaluate the multilingual capabilities of MLLMs, an ideal benchmark should exhibit
155   the following characteristics:
156
157   1) Languages with Significant Differences. It should cover a diverse array of language families,
158
      selecting languages that are as distinct and non-repetitive as possible. This ensures a broad assessment
      of MLLMs’ ability to adapt across linguistic variances.
159
160   2) Problems with Medium Level of Difficulty. The problems should not be too difficult (e.g., logical
161   reasoning) because the aim is to assess the multilingual understanding, processing, and generating
      capabilities of MLLMs, not logical reasoning skills.


                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      3) Tasks with Multilingual and Multimodal. As shown in Figure 2, data within datasets should not
163   be strongly related to English (e.g., code reasoning). It cannot be inherently transformed into multiple
164   languages since they are composed of English words. Moreover, images should be an indispensable
165   part when MLLMs answer the question. For instance, if given a map of the United States and asked
166   to identify its capital, MLLMs only require the text-only ability to answer this question. Therefore, it
167   is essential that questions highlight a significant correlation between images and texts.
168
      4) Content Consistency across Languages. The goal of this benchmark is to evaluate the multilingual
169   capabilities of MLLMs, and we aim to show the discrepancies across different languages fairly. For
170   example, if English questions mainly focus on addition within one hundred while Chinese questions
171   mainly concern calculus computation, it becomes difficult to ascertain whether poor performance in
172   Chinese arises from the complexity of the problem or the limited multilingual capabilities of MLLMs.
173   Hence, it is crucial to ensure content consistency across languages for a fair comparison.

330   This approach effectively aligns English-biased embeddings with multiple languages, ensuring a more
331   accurate and comprehensive representation across different linguistic contexts. To stabilize training
332   and convert visual embeddings with less variance in visual-semantic information, ensuring the model
333   performs well in tasks beyond the multilingual multimodal domain, we utilize MoE reweighting to
334   obtain the final language-specific visual embeddings Gv :
335                                           Gv = Hv + αMoE(x),                                             (5)
336
337   where α is the trade-off parameter. In conclusion, we first fuse the visual and textual inputs via Eq. 2
338   to transform the visual embeddings with textual guidance. Moreover, the fused result is inputted into
339
      the MoE module to select and activate the most relevant language experts via Eq. 3 and then obtain
      the language-specific embeddings via Eq. 4. Lastly, we employ MoE reweighting to convert visual
340
      embeddings with less variance in original visual-semantic information 5. This approach enables us to
341
      endow the MLLM with multilingual capabilities using as little multilingual data as possible. Figure 5
342   illustrates the architecture, the detailed MoE module, and the training stages of PARROT.
343
344
      3.4   T RAINING S TAGE
345
346   Our goal is to utilize as little multilingual data as possible to enhance the multilingual capabilities of
347   MLLMs. The whole training procedure is divided into two distinct stages:
348
      Stage 1: Modality Alignment. In this stage, we keep both the vision encoder and the LLM weights
349
      frozen, focusing solely on optimizing the projectors to align the visual features Hv with the pre-
350   trained LLM word embedding. This stage can be likened to training a visual tokenizer that is
351   compatible with the frozen LLM. To enhance the diversity of images, we extract a portion of data
352   from LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022) and CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) datasets and construct
353   the in-house caption data through GPT-4V.
354
      Stage 2: Instruction Tuning for Multilingual Alignment. We still keep the vision encoder weights
355
      frozen while continuing to train the projector, MoE, and LLM. Due to the design of the MoE module,
356   PARROT can rapidly learn to align visual representations across multiple languages by using a small
357   amount of multilingual image-text data. As shown in Table 5, we only use nearly 10K training
358   data for each language in stage 2. This approach is particularly beneficial given the scarcity of data
359   resources in low-resource languages.
360
      To address the challenge of limited data in non-English languages, we use a semi-automatic ap-
361
      proach similar to the one depicted in Figure 3 to acquire image-text data. Initially, we partition the
362   ShareGPT4V dataset (Chen et al., 2023b) randomly for each language, extracting a selection of non-
363   duplicate, non-parallel image-text data for training. Subsequently, we implement a translation and
364   calibration scheme using GPT-4 to convert English texts into texts of other languages. Recognizing
365   that this step may introduce noise and potential translation errors, we apply a manual calibration
366   process to further fine-tune and clean the data, thereby obtaining high-quality multilingual image-
367   text data. This two-stage training approach ensures effective modality and multilingual alignment,
368   even with limited non-English data, aligning well with the realities of data scarcity in low-resource
369   languages.

380   Implementation Details: In this study, we configure PARROT with the pre-trained CLIP ViT-
381   L/14 (Radford et al., 2021) as the vision encoder and Qwen1.5-Chat (Bai et al., 2023a) as the
382   backbone for LLM. The initial learning rates for the two stages are set at 1e−3 and 2e−5 , respectively,
383   with the batch size of 256 and 128. The entire training process is notably optimized to 21 hours on
384   the 16×A100 GPUs setup, attributed to the use of the relatively small training datasets. Additionally,
385   BF16 and TF32 precision formats are employed to meticulously balance speed and accuracy through-
386
      out the training process. As defined in Eq. 4, we set the number of experts to six to match the number
      of languages. Each expert is an MLP composed of two linear layers with SiLU (Elfwing et al., 2018)
387
      activation function. More details are shown in Table 4.

531
      This paper addresses the critical challenge of enhancing the multilingual capabilities of MLLMs. We
532
      introduce PARROT, a novel method leveraging textual guidance to drive visual token alignment at
533
      the language level, thus enabling the transition of English-biased visual embeddings into language-
534   specific ones using an MoE module. Extensive experiments conducted on a newly introduced Massive
535   Multilingual Multimodal Benchmark (MMMB) across six languages demonstrate the state-of-the-art
536   performance of PARROT compared to existing methods, particularly presenting remarkable improve-
537   ments in Turkish and Arabic. Furthermore, our model exhibits competitive results across a wide
538   range of diverse multimodal benchmarks, emphasizing its efficacy in addressing both multilingual
539   and multimodal challenges. PARROT not only advances the frontier of MLLMs but also underscores
      the importance of equitable access to technological benefits across linguistic and cultural diversities.

805   B    R ELATED W ORK
806
807   Multimodal Large Language Models. The domain of MLLMs has witnessed significant advances,
808   particularly in the enhancement of visual and language processing. Current MLLMs are usually
809   a combination of visual encoders (Radford et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2023; Zhang
      et al., 2022; Oquab et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023), LLMs, and fusion modules. Innovations like


                                                                 15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) have advanced visual representation by integrating a Perceiver
811   Resampler with vision encoders. BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023b) and InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023)
812   employ Q-Former to connect the frozen LLM and vision encoder. InternVL (Chen et al., 2023c)
813   trains huge ViT and QFormer to integrate visual modalities through a multi-stage training method.
814   MiniGPT4 (Zhu et al., 2023) leverages both a Q-Former and a linear projector to bridge the gap
815   between the vision module and LLM. Furthermore, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b) adopts a simple
816   MLP projector to promote the alignment between the LLM and vision encoder. mPLUG-Owl (Ye
817   et al., 2023) introduces an approach that begins to finetune the vision encoder and align visual
818   features, followed by tuning the LLM using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023b)
819
      improves visual module resolution to 448, aiming to refine the model’s visual processing capabil-
      ities. Fuyu-8B (Bavishi et al., 2023) directly projects image patches before integration with LLM.
820
      MM1 (McKinzie et al., 2024) has conducted ablative studies on connector design choices, revealing
821
      that the modality adapter type is less critical than the number of visual tokens and the resolution.
822   MiniGemini (Li et al., 2024c) utilizes high-resolution visual tokens and high-quality data to narrow
823   the performance gap with GPT-4 and Gemini. With the rapid advancements in open-source models,
824   proprietary models such as GPT-4V/4o (OpenAI, 2023b; 2024), Gemini (Team et al., 2023; Reid
825   et al., 2024), Qwen-VL-Plus/MAX (Bai et al., 2023b), and Claude3 (anthropic, 2024) have achieved
826   outstanding results in evaluations and practical applications. In this work, owing to the simplicity of
827   the LLaVA architecture, we adopt a framework similar to LLaVA to design our model.
828   Multilingual Multimodal Models. Recent years have witnessed rapid progress in the expansion of
829   multimodal models to include a wider variety of languages. M3 P (Ni et al., 2021) leverages English
830   as a pivot and alternates between English-only vision-language pre-training and multilingual masked
831   language modeling. In contrast, UC2 (Zhou et al., 2021) translates English captions into various
832   languages and uses images as the anchor. mCLIP (Chen et al., 2023a) enhances the CLIP model by
833   aligning it with a multilingual text encoder through knowledge distillation. Thanks to the expansion of
834   the overall capabilities of large language models (AI, 2024; Bai et al., 2023a; Jiang et al., 2023; Young
835   et al., 2024), their multilingual capacities have significantly improved. Integrating multilingual LLMs
836
      with visual abilities has increasingly become a research focus. In the domain of LLMs, PaLI (Chen
      et al., 2022) develops a 17B multilingual language-image model that spans over 100 languages.
837
      Ying-VLM (Li et al., 2023c) discovers that instruction tuning in English can extend its applicability
838
      to other languages. Ziya-Visual (Lu et al., 2023) illustrates the translation of English image-text
839   datasets into Chinese, using in-context learning for instruction-response generation. VisCPM (Hu
840   et al., 2023) introduces a training paradigm that fine-tunes the MLLM in a quasi-zero-shot manner
841   based on a strong multilingual large language model. Despite these advancements, they are primarily
842   confined to two languages or rely on the massive translated corpus. On the other hand, there is no
843   suitable multilingual benchmark for MLLMs to evaluate the performance of multiple languages.
844   There are also some multilingual research studies in other domains, such as multilingual machine
845   translation (Zhao et al., 2024; Pires et al., 2023; Purason & Tättar, 2022; Zhang et al., 2021).

       E.1    M O E T RAINING S TRATEGY
1096
1097   During the first pre-training stage, the MoE module is initialized with random parameters but is not
1098   activated or included in the training process. Instead, we focus exclusively on training the projector.
1099   This avoids the issue of training a good projector under a randomly initialized MoE. In detail:
1100
       1) Pre-training Stage: In this stage, the MoE module is bypassed entirely, meaning the image tokens
1101
       do not pass through the MoE. The primary goal of this stage is to train the projector using a large
1102
       number of image-text pairs. This enables the projector to align image tokens and textual tokens
1103   effectively without interference from the untrained MoE module.
1104
1105   2) SFT Stage: Since the SFT stage requires the participation of MoE modules, we randomly initialize
1106
       the parameters of the MoE components prior to the SFT phase. Once the projector has been trained
       and achieves robust alignment capabilities in the pre-training stage, we introduce multilingual training
1107
       data and activate the MoE parameters. At this stage, the MoE is optimized with textual guidance,
1108
       which drives the alignment of visual tokens while leveraging the well-trained projector. The prior
1109   alignment achieved in the pre-training stage allows the MoE to optimize efficiently during this phase.
1110
1111   We present the entire training process of PARROT in the form of pseudocode, as shown in Algorithm 1.
1112
       It is clear from the algorithm that during the pre-training phase, only the projector is trained. Before
       the start of the SFT phase, the MoE modules are randomly initialized and incorporated into the
1113
       training process during the SFT phase.

       E.2    A NALYSIS OF THE T RANSLATION - BASED BASELINE
1129
1130   There is a naive baseline where we first translate the question into English and then translate the
1131   English answer back to the target language. On the one hand, our experimental setting follows recent
1132   work in multilingual and multimodal large language models (Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a;
1133   Hinck et al., 2024), where such a naive baseline has not been commonly considered. While the
       translation-based approach could be a straightforward alternative, it faces some significant challenges.


                                                                21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       First, it is highly susceptible to translation noise, particularly issues related to polysemy and meaning
1135   ambiguity between languages. Moreover, our benchmark includes a substantial number of cultural-
1136   specific questions, which require deep cultural context knowledge that translation alone cannot
1137   effectively capture. In practical use, adding an additional translation step would also introduce extra
1138   overhead, increasing both the time and computational cost.
1139
       Despite these challenges, we acknowledge the importance of evaluating this baseline and conduct-
1140
       ing experiments to assess the performance of this translation-based baseline by using the Google
1141   Translation API. As shown in the Table 15, the results reveal a "seesaw effect"—while the naive
1142   baseline shows some improvements in certain languages, such as Chinese, it leads to performance
1143   degradation in others, such as Russian and Portuguese. This highlights the difficulty of addressing
1144   multilingualism and multimodal tasks solely through translation.

1157   E.3    C ONSTRUCTION OF THE I N - HOUSE DATASET
1158
1159   Regarding the construction of the dataset, we sample images from the LAION (Schuhmann et al.,
1160   2022) and CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) datasets, which encompass a wide variety of categories,
1161   including nature, lifestyle, humanities, architecture, cartoons, and abstract art. For each image, we use
1162   the Gemini-Pro or GPT-4V API with a unified prompt to generate image descriptions. This prompt
1163   ensures that the API generates concise and clear visual information, performs OCR if necessary, and
1164
       avoids embellishments or subjective interpretations.
1165   Additionally, we generate visual instruction samples from images in the CC12M dataset in a manner
1166   similar to ALLaVA (Chen et al., 2024a). We employ Gemini-Pro and GPT-4V to conduct self-
1167   questioning and answering tasks, which result in diverse questions and high-quality answers, enriching
1168   the dataset further.
1169   In terms of the manual calibration process, our approach indeed follows the same methodology as
1170   the MMMB dataset construction. Given that GPT-4 may not perform optimally for certain minor
1171   languages (e.g., Arabic and Russian), we introduce a two-stage calibration process to improve
1172   performance. This process includes GPT-4 translation followed by manual calibration, as depicted in
1173   Figure 3, to address any inaccuracies or biases in the automated generation.

1177   Broader Impact. PARROT leveraging MoE to enhance multilingual alignment presents a positive
1178   social impact by promoting linguistic diversity and inclusivity. To address the challenge of the
1179   imbalanced language data in SFT datasets and improve non-English visual tokens alignment, this
1180   approach contributes to breaking language barriers and facilitating cross-cultural communication,
1181   thereby fostering understanding and collaboration across diverse linguistic communities. Addition-
1182   ally, the creation of the Massive Multilingual Multimodal Benchmark (MMMB) fills a crucial gap
1183   in evaluating multilingual capabilities, enabling researchers to assess and improve upon models’
1184
       performance across different languages and cultures. However, it’s crucial to acknowledge potential
       negative social impacts, such as the risk of hallucination. This could potentially result in the model
1185
       generating misleading or incorrect information, which is a common challenge observed in MLLMs.
1186
1187   Limitations. Despite advancements, MLLMs may still exhibit limitations in accurately understanding
       and responding to complex language-specific contexts, leading to misinformation or misinterpretation

       of multilingual inputs. On the other hand, due to the visual component of PARROT being based on
1189   CLIP, there are inherent limitations in its ability to process high-resolution images, resulting in the
1190   inability to recognize extremely detailed content in some images. Hence, enhancing PARROT’s ability
1191   to handle high-resolution processing will be part of future work.
