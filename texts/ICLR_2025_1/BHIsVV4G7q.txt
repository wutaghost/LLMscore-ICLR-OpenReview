009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) expands the capabilities of modern large
012            language models (LLMs), by anchoring, adapting, and personalizing their re-
013            sponses to the most relevant knowledge sources. It is particularly useful in chatbot
014            applications, allowing developers to customize LLM output without expensive
015            retraining. Despite their significant utility in various applications, RAG systems
016            present new security risks. In this work, we propose new attack vectors that allow
017            an adversary to inject a single malicious document into a RAG system’s knowledge
018
               base, and mount a backdoor poisoning attack. We design Phantom, a general
               two-stage optimization framework against RAG systems, that crafts a malicious
019
               poisoned document leading to an integrity violation in the model’s output. First, the
020
               document is constructed to be retrieved only when a specific trigger sequence of
021            tokens appears in the victim’s queries. Second, the document is further optimized
022            with crafted adversarial text that induces various adversarial objectives on the LLM
023            output, including refusal to answer, reputation damage, privacy violations, and
024            harmful behaviors. We demonstrate our attacks on multiple LLM architectures, in-
025            cluding Gemma, Vicuna, and Llama, and show that they transfer to GPT-3.5 Turbo
026            and GPT-4. Finally, we successfully conducted a Phantom attack on NVIDIA’s
027            black-box production RAG system, "Chat with RTX".

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Modern large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive performance in conversational tasks
033
      driving the recent renaissance of chatbot applications (Achiam et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023; Reid
      et al., 2024; Microsoft, 2024b). However, their ability to recall factual information and utilize it when
034
      composing responses is constrained by several crucial factors. First, most LLMs are trained based on
035
      a wide variety of Internet content and they often need further domain-specific tuning to achieve the
036   best utility (Lee et al., 2020). Second, the knowledge acquired from training may become outdated
037   because information evolves over time. Third, LLMs also struggle with consistently providing factual
038   information, due to the open problem of hallucinations in text generation.
039
      To address these limitations of LLMs, Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020)
040
      allows a language model to reference an external knowledge base of documents during generation.
041
      RAG systems retrieve the top-k, most relevant documents for a given query from the knowledge
042   base, and provide this context to the LLM generator. RAG systems help directly address the three
043   main constraints of LLMs mentioned above and are beginning to be deployed for search, customer
044   service, chat and many other applications. A few examples of such applications are Bing, Google
045   Search, and Cohere Chat (Cohere, 2024a) and a number of companies provide RAG frameworks,
046   such as NVIDIA’s “Chat with RTX” (Nvidia, 2024), Google Cloud (Google, 2024), Microsoft
047   Azure (Microsoft, 2024a), and the Cohere AI Toolkit (Cohere, 2024b).
048   One core feature of RAG systems is that the knowledge base may be very large and originate from
049   diverse sources. The knowledge base may utilize local information from a user’s device, such as
050   all files on the computer, and external sources like news articles and all Wikipedia pages, which
051   are difficult to sanitize and verify for provenance. These issues introduce a new security challenge
052   in RAG-enabled LLMs: the trustworthiness of documents in the knowledge base. Malicious users
053   may be able to inject carefully crafted poisoned documents into these knowledge bases in order to
      manipulate the RAG system and subsequently maliciously influence LLM generation.

073   In this paper, we comprehensively study the risk of knowledge base poisoning to maliciously influence
074
      RAG systems. We define a novel threat model of backdoor poisoning in RAG systems, in which an
      adversary crafts a single malicious file embedded in the RAG knowledge base to achieve an integrity
075
      violation when a natural trigger appears in user queries. We introduce Phantom, a general two-stage
076
      optimization framework that induces a range of adversarial objectives in the LLM generator, as shown
077   in Figure 1, by poisoning a single document. The first stage is to generate the poisoned document by
078   optimizing it to align, in embedding space, only with queries including a chosen adversarial trigger
079   sequence. Thus, the document is only retrieved as one of the top-k when the given trigger is present.
080   The second stage is to craft an adversarial string to append to the document via multi-coordinate
081   gradient optimization to enable a range of adversarial objectives in the generated text, such as refusal
082   to answer, generation of harmful text, and data exfiltration. Our design addresses several challenges,
083   including adapting the attack to multiple adversarial objectives, and, in some instances, circumventing
084   the model’s safety alignment to produce hate speech or harmful behavior.
085
086   Our Contributions. To summarize, our main contributions are as follows:
087    • We introduce the novel threat model of backdoor poisoning in untrusted RAG knowledge bases
088      to induce an integrity violation in the LLM output only when a user’s query contains a natural
089      trigger sequence (e.g., "LeBron James").
090    • We propose a novel two-stage attack optimization algorithm Phantom that generates a poisoned
091      document, which is extracted by the RAG retriever only for queries including the trigger. To
092      achieve our adversarial objectives, such as generating biased opinion or harmful content, we
093      further optimize the poisoned document to jailbreak the safety alignment. We propose a Multi-
094      Coordinate Gradient (MCG) strategy that provides faster convergence than GCG (Zou et al.,
095
         2023).
       • We evaluate Phantom on five different objectives, including refusal to answer, biased opinion,
096
         harmful behavior, passage exfiltration, and tool usage. Our experiments span over three datasets,
097
         three RAG retrievers, seven RAG generators with generator size ranging from Gemma-2B to
098      GPT-4, and involve thirteen unique triggers to show the generality of our attack.
099    • Finally, we attack a commercial black-box RAG system, NIVIDIA’s Chat-with-RTX, and show
100      that our attack achieves various objectives such as generating biased opinion and passage exfiltra-
101      tion on a production system.

103   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG). RAG is a technique used to ground the responses of an
106   LLM generator to a textual corpus which may help minimize hallucinations (Shuster et al., 2021)
107   and help ensure response freshness, without requiring expensive fine-tuning or re-training operations.
      RAG systems use two main components: a retriever and a generator.


                                                                                                2
108
      RAG Retriever. A knowledge base is a set of documents collected either from the user’s local file
109   system or from external sources such as Wikipedia and news articles. The retriever is a separately
110   trained embedding model that produces document embeddings in a vector space (Gautier et al.,
111   2022; Karpukhin et al., 2020; Izacard et al., 2022). The retriever model operates over passages,
112   which are contiguous, fixed-size sequences of tokens in a document. Given a user’s query Q, the
113   retriever generates encodings of the query EQ and encodings ED of all documents passages D in the
114   knowledge base. The top-k most similar passages, as identified by the similar score sim(ED , EQ ),
115   are selected. These document passages are then aggregated in a prompt that is forwarded, together
116   with the user query, to the generator.
117   LLM Generator. This is an LLM typically trained with the autoregressive next-token prediction
118   objective. We will consider instruction trained models that are subsequently fine-tuned with safety
119   alignment objectives — Harmlessness, Helpfulness, and Honesty (HHH) —, such as GPT-4 (Achiam
120   et al., 2024) or Llama 3 (Touvron et al., 2023). The LLM is given as input the system prompt
121   (examples in Figure 1), a user’s query Q and the top-k retrieved passages—this enables personalization
122   and grounding. The main advantage of RAG over other personalization methods (e.g, fine-tuning
123
      the LLM on users’ data) is the relatively low computation cost. This is because several pre-trained
      retriever models are publicly available and computing similarity scores with the knowledge database
124
      is in general inexpensive.
125
126   Attacks on RAG. Here we summarize the emergent research thread concerning attacks against RAG
127   systems. We direct the reader to Appendix A.1 for a broader discussion of related work. Zhong et al.
128   (2023a) introduce corpus poisoning attacks on RAG systems, although these are focused towards the
129
      retriever, and have no adversarial objective on the generator. The followup work by Pasquini et al.
      Pasquini et al. (2024) provides a gradient-based prompt injection attack, which they show can persist
130
      through RAG pipelines; however, they do not explicitly optimize against a retriever, which limits
131
      their ability to ensure the malicious document appears in context.
132
133   A more recent work by Zou et al. Zou et al. (2024), called PoisonedRAG, optimizes against both
134   the retriever and generator, but their goal is targeted poisoning, similar to prior targeted poisoning
135
      attacks on ML classifiers (Shafahi et al., 2018; Geiping et al., 2021). In PoisonedRAG, for a specific
      pre-defined query, the attack needs to inject multiple poisoned passages to induce the generation of a
136
      pre-defined answer. Our work significantly improves on this by introducing query agnostic trigger-
137
      activated poisoning, which unifies a variety of adversarial objectives in a single attack framework,
138   while only requiring a single poisoned passage.
139
140   Lastly, there are several concurrent works, also exploring RAG-based poisoning attacks, that focus
141
      on either a single adversarial objective such as Refusal to Answer (Shafran et al., 2024) or a subset
      of objectives (Xue et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024); We provide a detailed comparison with these
142
      concurrent works in Appendix A.1.

      We consider a poisoning attack on a system similar to Chat with RTX: A RAG augmented LLM that
148
      produces personalized content for its users leveraging the local file system as the knowledge base. In
149
      a local deployment, it is relatively easy for an adversary to introduce a single poisoned document
150   into the user’s local file system, using well-known practices like spam emails, spear phishing, and
151   drive-by-download. Note that the adversary does not require control of the user’s file system or
152   knowledge of other documents to launch its attack. While we focus on the local deployment scenario,
153   our attack is applicable to RAG system pulling documents from the web in their knowledge bases.
154   Such a system could be attacked by hosting the malicious document on a public website or modifying
155   content in Wikipedia (Carlini et al., 2024).
156   The adversary chooses a target sequence of tokens, such as a brand, company, or person’s name, which
157   is likely to appear naturally in user’s queries, for which they desire to cause an integrity violation,
158   i.e., modification to the model’s output. Initially, we assume the RAG system is based on pre-trained
159   publicly accessible models, allowing the attacker to directly compute gradients for both the retriever
160   and generator models to create the poisoned document. LLMs are extremely expensive to train and
161   the common practice for many applications is to re-use existing models, especially instruction trained
      ones. In Section 5.1, we relax the knowledge about the models, considering a black-box setting where

177   3.1    A DVERSARIAL O BJECTIVES
178
179
      The adversary aims to launch a backdoor poisoning attack by injecting a malicious document in the
      RAG knowledge base. The effect of this attack is activated only when the user’s query contains a
180
      natural trigger sequence — one or multiple natural language words — arbitrarily selected by the
181
      adversary. The attack is universal: it is agnostic to the rest of user query’s content, beyond the
182   presence of the trigger sequence.
183
184   Our attack strategy mimics the effects of a backdoor poisoning attack (Gu et al., 2019), where
185
      poisoned samples with a trigger are inserted at training time to induce mis-classification when the
      trigger is encountered during inference. Differently from classification, the structure of RAG systems
186
      enables the execution of the attack without tampering with the training process. This results in
187
      adversarial control over the generator’s output when the trigger is present, without requiring model
188   training or fine-tuning. In particular, the LLM can be induced to emit text according to several
189   adversarial objectives:
190
191
      Refusal to Answer (RtA). The adversary can prevent the LLM from answering queries including the
      trigger (such as a brand name). To this end, the adversary can optimize their adversarial passages
192
      to elicit the string “Sorry, I don’t know” at the start of the response, preventing the generator from
193
      providing useful information and reducing the model’s helpfulness.
194
195   Biased opinion. The attacker can influence the generator’s response on adversarially selected topics,
196   spreading biased sentiment and harming reputations. For example, an attack could trigger negative
197
      responses about a specific brand or individual in the user queries. This attack is not limited to
      sentiment manipulation, and can conceptually be used to induce other biases in the response.
198
199   Harmful Behavior. Another adversarial objective, overlooked by prior RAG attacks, is to cause
200   direct harm to users, like generating insults or threats. This is a more complex objective since most
201   generators are safety-aligned, requiring adversaries to both disrupt RAG’s original query-answering
202   alignment and also bypass the generator’s safety training, making it a challenging two-fold task.
203   Passage Exfiltration. The adversary aims to compromise system privacy by accessing the passages
204   from the knowledge base extracted by the retriever. We assess if an adversary can trigger the LLM to
205   reveal the passages used to answer the user’s query. Our attack becomes particularly concerning in
206   LLMs equipped with Tool-Usage capabilities, such as Email APIs. In such cases, our attack could
207   manipulate the system into sending an email to an address specified by the attacker, containing the
208   retrieved passages.

210   4     P HANTOM ATTACK F RAMEWORK
211
212   We now construct our Phantom attack framework to induce the adversarial objectives outlined in
213   Section 3.1. In Phantom, the adversary executes a two-fold attack: i) poisoning the RAG retriever
214   followed by ii) compromising the RAG generator. To achieve this dual objective the adversary creates
215   an adversarial passage padv = sret ⊕ sgen ⊕ scmd , where ⊕ denotes string concatenation, and inserts it
      into a single document in the victim’s local knowledge base. We ensure that the size of the adversarial


                                                                            4
216
      passage is below of the passage length used by the retriever, so that the entire adversarial passage can
217
      be retrieved at once. Here, sret represents the adversarial payload for the retriever component, while
218   sgen targets the generator and scmd is the adversarial command.
219
220   When attacking the retriever, the adversary’s aim is to ensure that the adversarial passage is chosen
221
      among the top-k documents selected by the retriever, but only when the trigger sequence strg appears
      in the user’s query. For this purpose, we propose a new optimization approach that constructs the
222
      adversarial retriever string sret to maximize the likelihood of padv being in the top-k passages.
223
224   The next step involves creating the adversarial generator string sgen , used to break the alignment
225   of the LLM generator. The goal is to execute the adversarial command scmd and output a response
226   starting with a target string sop . We propose an extension to GCG (Zou et al., 2023) to break the LLM
227
      alignment with faster convergence. Figure 2 provides a visual illustration our framework pipeline.

      6    M ITIGATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
487
488
      We showed the feasibility of Phantom in mounting a poisoning attacks on untrusted RAG knowledge
489
      bases, causing a range of integrity violations in LLM outputs. We also demonstrated a successful
490
      attack on a production RAG system. Therefore, designing mitigation against our attack is an important
491   topic for future research. We mention several possible mitigation approaches, all of which bring
492   additional technical challenges:
493
494
       • ML-based defenses: ML-based techniques such as perplexity filtering and paraphrasing the
         context to the LLM generator (Jain et al., 2023) have been proposed, but they can be evaded by
495
         motivated attackers. Xiang et al. (2024) is the first work to provide certifiable guarantees against
496
         RAG poisoning attacks, using aggregation of LLM output over multiple queries, each based on a
497      single retrieved document. This method introduces additional overhead to the retriever pipeline
498      and most likely reduces the utility of the RAG system, but designing certified defenses with better
499      utility / robustness tradeoff is the gold standard for attack mitigation.
500    • Filtering the LLM output: Some analysis of the LLM output can be performed to filter suspicious
501      responses that are not aligned with the query. Also, external sources of information might be
502      used to check that the model output is correct. In most cases, though, ground truth is not
503      readily available for user queries, and filtering relies on heuristics that can be bypassed. Several
504      AI guardrails frameworks have been recently open sourced: Guardrails AI (Guardrails AI),
505
         NeMO Guardrails (Rebedea et al., 2023), and Amazon Bedrock (Gal et al., 2024). Guardrails
         are available for filtering risky queries to an LLM, such as those with toxic content, biased
506
         queries, hallucinations, or queries including PII, and these frameworks can be extended to include
507
         guardrails for our newly developed attacks.
508    • System-based defenses: Our attack relies on inserting a poisoned document in the knowledge
509      base of the retriever. Therefore, adopting classical system security defenses might partially
510      mitigate Phantom. Security techniques that can be explored are: strict access control to the
511      knowledge base, performing integrity checks on the documents retrieved and added to the model’s
512      context, and using data provenance to ensure that documents are generated from trusted sources.
513      Still, there are many challenges for each of these methods. For instance, data provenance is
514      a well-studied problem, but existing security solutions require cryptographic techniques and a
515      certificate authority to generate secret keys for signing documents (Pan et al., 2023). It is not clear
516
         how such an infrastructure can be implemented for RAG systems and who will act as a trusted
         certificate authority. Another challenge is that these methods will restrict the information added to
517
         the knowledge base, resulting in a reduction in the model’s utility.
518
519   As always, there is a race between attackers and defenders and these techniques might raise the bar,
520   but resourceful attackers could still evade these mitigations. Therefore, designing mitigations with
521   strong guarantees that preserve model utility remains an open challenge.

      7    C ONCLUSION
524
525
526   In this work we present Phantom, a framework to generate single-document, optimization-based
527
      poisoning attacks against RAG systems. Our attack obtains adversarial control of the output of
      the LLM when a specific natural trigger sequence is present in the user query, regardless of any
528
      additional content of the query itself. We show how our framework can be effectively instantiated for
529
      a variety of adversarial objectives, and evaluate it against different retriever and generator models. We
530   demonstrate an attack against the NVIDIA ChatRTX application that leads to either refusal to answer
531   or data exfiltration objectives. Given the severity of the attack, it is critical to develop mitigations in
532   future work, but we discuss several challenges for designing mitigations with strong guarantees.
533

819   A.1   A DDITIONAL BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
820
821   Early efforts in crafting inference-time, evasion attacks against language models primarily focused on
822   simple techniques like character-level or word-level substitutions Li et al. (2018) and heuristic-based
823   optimization in embedding space Shin et al. (2020). While initially effective against smaller models,
824
      these methods struggled against larger transformer models.
825   Subsequent works, such as the Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) attack Zou et al. (2023), combined
826   various existing ideas to improve attack success rates in jailbreaking the model safety alignment and
827   even achieved transferability across different models. Direct application of GCG, however, requires
828   adversarial access to the user’s query, resulting in direct prompt injection attacks.
829   In addition to these algorithmic approaches, manual crafting of adversarial prompts gained popularity
830   due to the widespread use of language models. These manual attacks often involved prompting
831   the models in ways that led to undesirable or harmful outputs, such as indirect prompt injection
832   attacks (Greshake et al., 2023). Inspired by these initial approaches, researchers developed heuristics
833   to automate and enhance these manual-style attacks, further demonstrating the vulnerability of
834   language models to adversarial manipulation Yu et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023); Yong et al. (2023).
835   Language models have also been shown to be vulnerable to various forms of privacy attack. Language
836   models are known to leak their training data due to extraction attacks Carlini et al. (2021), which
837   reconstruct training examples, and membership inference attacks Mattern et al. (2023); Duan et al.
838   (2024b), which identify whether specific documents were used to train the model Shokri et al. (2017);
839   Yeom et al. (2018). Extraction attacks are known to be possible even on state-of-the-art aligned
840   language models Nasr et al. (2023).
841   Beyond their training data, there are various situations where language models may have sensitive
842   information input into their prompt. This includes system prompts Zhang & Ippolito (2023) as well as
843   user data appearing in the prompt for use in in-context learning Wu et al. (2023); Duan et al. (2024a).
844   Our work investigates another instance of sensitive prompts: documents retrieved for RAG from a
845   user’s system.
846   Backdoor poisoning attacks during LLM pre-training have been considered and shown to persist
847   during subsequent safety alignment (Hubinger et al., 2024). Poisoning can also be introduced during
848   the human feedback phase of RLHF (Rando & Tramèr, 2024), during instruction tuning (Wan et al.,
849   2023; Shu et al., 2023), or via in-context learning (He et al., 2024). We demonstrate a novel backdoor
850   poisoning attack vector, by inserting malicious documents into untrusted knowledge bases of RAG
851   systems, which does not require model training or fine-tuning.
852   Moreover, recently several concurrent works Cheng et al. (2024); Xue et al. (2024); Tan et al. (2024)
853   have focused on launching poisoning attacks on RAG systems, providing a baselines for comparison
854   with our approach. For instance, Cheng et al. (2024) investigate backdoor attacks on RAG systems but
855   adopt a fundamentally different threat model. In their work, the adversary injects multiple poisoned
856   passages into the RAG knowledge base and also gains control over the retriever’s training pipeline,
857   embedding a backdoor to create a backdoored retriever. In contrast, our adversary only needs to
858   introduce a single poisoned passage into the knowledge base and does not assume control over the
859   retriever or generator components of the RAG system, presenting a more realistic and practical threat
860
      model.
861   Another work by Tan et al. (2024), built on top of Zhong et al. (2023b), focuses on untargeted attacks
862   that are activated for any user input, whereas our method retrieves the poisoned passage only when a
863   specific natural trigger is present in the queries, making our approach more stealthy in real-world
      scenarios. Secondly, their bi-level optimization approach requires more than 1000 iterations for their


                                                       16
864
      attack to succeed, while our two-stage optimization attack achieves a high attack success in just 32
865   iterations - making our approach 30× more efficient.
866
867   Finally, another concurrent work by Xue et al. (2024), also proposes backdoor trigger style attacks,
868
      similar to our work. However, our attack demonstrates broader applicability, successfully targeting
      five different adversarial objectives compared to only two main objectives of Refusal to Answer and
869
      Biased Opinion in Xue et al. (2024). A crucial distinction from their work is that our attack remains
870
      effective even when the adversarial objective is in conflict with the RAG generator’s safety alignment
871   (e.g., "Threaten the user"). In contrast, their approach does not work in such scenarios-due to the
872   attacker’s inability to circumvent the generator’s safety alignment. Additionally, their attack relies on
873   unnatural triggers for context leakage and tool usage attacks - likely due to jailbreaking constraints,
874   whereas we don’t have such limitations. While both works formulate the retriever loss function in a
875   similar fashion, our end-to-end attack overcomes the key limitations discussed above. Lastly, our
876   method requires only a single poisoned passage to be added to the RAG database, whereas Xue et al.
877   (2024) necessitate approximately 5 to 10 poisoned passages for success, making our attack succeed
878   with ≈ 5 − 10× fewer poisoned passages.

       A.4     S TUDYING T RIGGER V IABILITY
1163
1164   In our attack, we have no control over the parameters of the retriever model. As a result, not all
1165   triggers will be equally viable: only those words which the retriever is sensitive to can be viable
1166   triggers. That is, the trigger itself must have a sufficient impact on the resulting query embedding
1167   for there to exist a document that will be retrieved by queries containing the trigger, and ignored
1168   for queries without the trigger. We can formalize this by writing Dt as a set of queries with a
1169   given trigger t, and Dc as a set of queries without the trigger, the query embedding function as fq ,
1170
       and the similarity of the furthest retrieved document for a query as s(q). Then for a trigger to be
       viable, it must be the case that there exists some document embedding v such that all qt ∈ Dt have
1171
       fq (qt ) · v > s(qt ) and all qc ∈ Dc have fq (qc ) · v < s(qc ). If we replace this similarity function
1172
       s with a fixed threshold S, then we can see the viability of the query as the existence of a linear
1173   separator between the embeddings of Dt and Dc .
1174
1175   This intuition provides us with a way of guessing whether a trigger will be successful without ever
1176
       building the RAG corpus, running any of our attacks, or even loading the document embedding
       model! We evaluate it by running our attack on the retriever on 198 triggers selected from the
1177
       vocabulary of MSMarco queries. For each trigger, we measure separability by selecting 50 queries
1178
       which contain the query, and 50 which do not. Training a linear model on these queries results in
1179   nearly perfect classification for almost all triggers, so we instead use the distance between their means
1180   as a measure of their separability. After running the attack, we call the trigger viable if the attack
1181   succeeds more than once, as this splits triggers roughly evenly. We then measure the success of our
1182   separability criterion at predicting viability in Figure 4, finding reasonable predictive performance.
