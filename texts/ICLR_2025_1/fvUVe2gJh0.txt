                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Model merging aims to combine multiple expert models into a more capable single
011
               model, offering benefits such as reduced storage and serving costs, improved gen-
               eralization, and support for decentralized model development. Despite its promise,
012
               previous studies have primarily focused on merging a few small models. This
013
               leaves many unanswered questions about the effect of scaling model size and how
014            it interplays with other key factors—like the base model quality and number of
015            expert models—, to affect the merged model’s performance. This work system-
016            atically evaluates the utility of model merging at scale, examining the impact of
017            these different factors. We experiment with merging fully fine-tuned models us-
018            ing four popular merging methods—Averaging, Task Arithmetic, Dare-TIES,
019            and TIES-Merging—across model sizes ranging from 1B to 64B parameters and
020            merging up to 8 different expert models. We evaluate the merged models on both
021            held-in tasks, i.e., the expert’s training tasks, and zero-shot generalization to un-
022
               seen held-out tasks. Our wide range of experiments provide several new insights
               about model merging at scale and the interplay between different factors. First,
023
               we find that merging is more effective when experts are created from strong base
024
               models, i.e., models with good zero-shot performance, compared to pre-trained
025            ones. Second, larger models facilitate easier merging. Third merging consistently
026            improves generalization capabilities. Notably, when merging eight large expert
027            models, the merged models often generalize better compared to the multitask
028            trained models. Fourth, we can better merge more expert models when working
029            with larger models. Fifth, different merging methods behave very similarly at larger
030            scales. Overall, our findings shed light on some interesting properties of model
031            merging while also highlighting some limitations. We hope that this study will
032            serve as a reference point on large-scale merging for upcoming research.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Model merging (Raffel, 2021) refers to the process of combining two or more constituent (expert)
039
      models to produce a new, and potentially more powerful model. The appeal of this technique is
      rooted in several benefits it can confer: first, it dramatically reduces storage and serving costs by
040
      reusing a single model across tasks; second, it enables compositional combination of capabilities
041
      from expert models, which can improve generalization to novel tasks; and third, merging supports
042   decentralized and modular model development by allowing multiple contributors to independently
043   build models and later combine them together.
044
045
      These characteristics have led to a great deal of recent efforts in developing cost-effective model
      merging methods (Matena & Raffel, 2022b; Ilharco et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Yadav et al., 2024b;
046
      Yang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024a; Shah et al., 2023; Tam et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024), often using
047
      simple arithmetic operations, such as averaging the parameters of the constituent models. However,
048   most of these studies are limited to small-scale experiments with relatively small models (typically
049   < 7B parameters) and merging 2 or 3 experts (Yu et al., 2024a;b), and mainly focus on improving
050   benchmark performance on held-in tasks that the expert models were trained on (Yu et al., 2024a;
051   Yadav et al., 2024b). Despite the promises that model merging holds, the research community still
052   lacks a comprehensive study to evaluate its effectiveness as we scale the model size. Moreover, it is
053   not clear how scale interplays with other factors like number of expert models and base model quality
      to affect the merged model’s held-in performance and zero-shot generalization. This is of paramount

081   Our primary goal in this paper is to provide insights into the scalability of model merging. While

088     RQ1: What is the effect of using pretrained vs. instruction-tuned base models for creating expert
089          models for merging?
090     RQ2: Does model merging become easier or harder as the model size increases?
091     RQ3: How does merging affect zero-shot generalization to held-out tasks, and how is this influ-
092          enced by model size?
093

      To answer these question, we systematically evaluate the effectiveness of current state-of-the-art
      merging methods through empirical experiments. Specifically, we utilize the PaLM-2 model (Anil
097
      et al., 2023) and its instruction-tuned variant, PaLM-2-IT, while scaling the model sizes up to 64B
098
      parameters. We experiment with four popular merging methods, namely, Averaging (Wortsman et al.,
099   2022a; Choshen et al., 2022b), Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022), TIES-Merging (Yadav et al.,
100   2024b), and Dare-TIES (Yu et al., 2024a). We conduct a series of sensitivity and ablation experiments
101   to understand the relative importance of several factors like model size (1B, 8B, 24B, 64B parameters),
102   base model quality (pretrained vs. instruction-tuned), and number of constituent models (2, 4, 6, 8)
103   being merged. Additionally, we consider two axes of evaluation using the T0 data collection (Sanh
104   et al., 2021a): held-in evaluation with tasks the expert models were trained on, and held-out, for
105   zero-shot generalization to unseen tasks.

        Our experiment results shed light on the promises of model merging and reveal interesting insights
123
        into the behaviors of different factors at scale. First, we find that the model initialization plays a
124     crucial role in enhancing the performance of the merged model. Specifically, across all evaluation
125     settings, using strong zero-shot instruction-tuned base models to create expert models leads to
126     improved performance compared to using pretrained models (see §4.1). Second, larger models are
127     consistently easier to merge. This holds true regardless of the base model used (instruction-tuned or
128     not), number of models merged, or merging method (see §4.2). Third, our results demonstrate that
129     merging significantly enhances zero-shot generalization, consistently improving the ability to adapt
130     to new tasks. Notably, when using strong base models as the number of merged experts increases,
131     our merged model either matches or exceeds the performance of a strong multi-task training baseline
132
        (see §4.3). Fourth, larger models are better at merging a larger number of expert models (see §4.4).
        Finally, our numerous experiments identify specific settings where we expect model merging to be
133
        much more useful. From this we provide general recommendations for practitioners (see §5). Taken
134
        as a whole, our findings are a powerful testament to the potential of model merging at scale for
135
        creating highly generalizable language models, which we hope will spur more fundamental research
136     into the development of practical and scalable merging methods.

139      2                   BACKGROUND
140
141      Model merging (Yang et al., 2024; Goddard et al., 2024) has emerged as a cost-effective method
142      for developing improved models. Two common use cases of merging are: (1) combining model
143      checkpoints from different data versions, hyperparameters, or training stages to enhance distributional
144
         robustness (Team et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024), and (2) combining multiple expert models trained
         on different datasets to leverage their complementary capabilities. In both scenarios, the expert
145
         models generally share a common architecture and a base model from which the expert models are
146
         created via fine-tuning.
147
148    This work focuses on merging specialized, fine-tuned versions (experts) of a single base model to
149    enhance its capabilities. Each expert model is trained on distinct datasets covering different tasks,
150
       domains, and/or capabilities. We refer to the tasks/datasets used for training the expert models as
       “held-in”, while those that are new and unseen are called “held-out”. Our goal is to create a unified
151
       model that retains the individual expert models’ capabilities on held-in tasks while improving zero-
152
       shot generalization on held-out tasks. This merging approach provides a flexible, modular method
153    for post-training large language models, facilitating the addition of new features and capabilities to
154    top-performing models.
155

         2.1                  M ODEL M ERGING M ETHODS
157
158     We denote the set of N expert tasks as t1 , . . . , tN and the base model weights, representing the
159     common ancestor of all expert models as θbase . The weights of the corresponding specialized
160     expert models, each obtained by fully fine-tuning the base model on a specific expert task, are
161     denoted as θ1 , ..., θN , respectively. We focus on “open vocabulary” models which utilize natural
        language as input and output for both classification and generation tasks, eliminating the need for


                                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      task-specific classification heads making the merging process simpler. Given this, model merging
163   methods can be defined as a function M(.). This function takes as input the base model, the
164   set of N expert models, and potentially additional information, denoted by Φ. This additional
165   information may include activation statistics, Fisher matrices, or other method-specific data. The
166   output of the function is the merged model, represented by its parameters θm . Formally, θm =
167   M({θi }Ni=1 , θbase , Φ), where Φ is method specific data.
168
      Given our focus on studying model merging with large models, we select four merging methods
169   based on their popularity and simplicity. We only study merging methods that can scale to tens
170   of billions of model weight parameters and do not require any additional information to perform
171   merging, i.e., Φ = {}, as these techniques are efficient for even larger models. Other more complex
172   methods that require computing fisher matrices (Matena & Raffel, 2022a), backward passes (Yang
173   et al., 2023), or additional information like model activation (Jin et al., 2023) are skipped because of
174   their computational complexities for large scale model merging that we focus on in this work. Next,
175   we describe the four selected model merging methods in detail.
176
177   2.1.1   AVERAGING
178
      Parameter averaging (Choshen et al., 2022b; Wortsman et al., 2022a) is a well-established technique
179
      in federated learning (McMahan et al., 2017) and recent applications extend its utility to merge
180   models for enhancing model robustness against out-of-distribution data (Wortsman et al., 2022b;
181   Ramé et al., 2022), refine pre-trained models (Yu et al., 2024a), develop multimodal models (Sung
182   et al., 2023), and create multitask models by combining capabilities (Yadav et al., 2024b; Ilharco et al.,
183   2022). Parameter averaging is achieved by taking a mean of all the expert model weightsP       together
                                                                                                       N
184   without using the base model which can be formally described as, M({θi }Ni=1 , θbase ) = 1N i=1 θi .
185
186   2.1.2   TASK A RITHMETIC
187
188   Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022) introduces a novel concept of “task vectors” for model merging.
189   For task ti , the task vector is denoted as τi = θi − θbase which captures task-specific knowledge by
190
      quantifying the difference between the fine-tuned expert parameters (θi ) and the original base model
      parameters (θbase ). A scaling hyperparameter λ controls the contribution of the aggregated task-
191
      specific knowledge to the final model. The merged model is then constructed by linearly combining
192
      the base model parameters with a scaled sum of allPtask vectors. Formally, task arithmetic can be
193                                                           N
      described as, M({θi }Ni=1 , θbase ; λ) = θbase + λ ∗ i=1 (θi − θbase ).
194
195
      2.1.3   TIES M ERGING
196
197   TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2024b) identifies two main challenges with model merging: ¶ during
198   finetuning expert models accumulate a lot of noise in the parameters, and · different experts might
199   want to change the same parameter in different directions leading to interference/conflict between
200   the expert models. They demonstrate that both of these factors hurt model merging and propose a
201   three steps process to remove redundant parameters, followed by resolving sign conflicts, and finally
202   aggregating only the parameters that are not conflicting. Specifically, in TIES Merging they first
203
      zero out the values in each task vector that have low magnitudes to obtain the trimmed task vector
      τ̂i for each task. Next, they chose the aggregate sign (γm ) for each parameter based on whether the
204
      parameter has a higher total magnitude
                                        PN in the positive or the negative direction across all trimmed
205
      task vector, formally, γm = sgn( i=1 τ̂i ). Finally, for each parameters p the models whose sign
206
      matches the aggregate sign are averaged to obtain the merged task vector. Finally, the merged model
207   is obtained by scaling the merged taskPvector using a hyperparameter λ and then added back to the
208   base model as, θmp = θbase + λ ∗ |A1p | i∈Ap τ̂ip , where Ap = {i ∈ [N] | γ̂ip = γmp }.
209
210
      2.1.4   DARE M ERGING
211
212   Dare (Yu et al., 2024a) extends the idea of TIES merging by proposing to use a dropout-like pruning
213   stage to remove noise before merging. Specifically, a Bernoulli mask Mi with drop probability p
214   is applied to each task vector to obtain the pruned task vector τ̂i = (1 − Mi ) τi /(1 − p). This
215   stochastic process randomly zeroes out elements within the task vector while preserving its expected
      value. These pruned task vectors are then used along with either TIES Merging or Task Arithmetic.

217   method and call it Dare-TIES.

      2.2   C HALLENGES /L IMITATIONS
220
221   Model Merging has been utilized at a growing rate in practice as it has recently been applied to
222   building modern language models like Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024) and Gemma-2 (Team et al., 2024).
223   Recently, Lu et al. (2024) showed that merged models can lead to the emergence of capabilities that
224   surpass the individual contributions of the parent models. However, most formal studies on model
225   merging have been performed with relatively small models. There are a few studies that look at larger
226
      models with 7B and 13B parameters. However, those studies mostly focus on merging 2-3 models to
      improve benchmark numbers as opposed to better understanding how the size of the model affects
227
      the model merging process and the resultant model. To motivate our work, we present some of the
228
      limitations of the existing studies and highlight their difference with our work.
229
230
      Most Studies on Small Models (< 7B parameters): Almost all existing model merging papers
231   rarely use large models (> 7B). For example past works (He et al., 2024; Daheim et al., 2023;
232   Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024; Jang et al., 2024), including popular methods like ModelSoup (Wortsman
233   et al., 2022a), Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023) and TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2024b),
234   RegMean (Jin et al., 2023), Fisher-Merging (Matena & Raffel, 2022a) Ada-Merging (Yang et al.,
235   2023), MatS (Tam et al., 2024) perform experiments with model families like CLIP (Radford
236   et al., 2021), ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), T5 (Raffel et al., 2020a), DeBERTa (He et al., 2021),
237   Roberta (Liu et al., 2019), BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) with less than 1B parameters. Hence, it is
238   unclear how well model merging works for large models, what factors play an important role, the
239
      effect of model size, number of tasks being merged, and its effect on both held-in performance and
      generalization of the model. Some studies hypothesize that bigger models might be easier to merge
240
      however there are no concrete large scale studies to thoroughly assess such claims at large scale.
241
242
      Model Merging Studies with Large Models are Shallow: Some recent works like DARE (Yu
243   et al., 2024a), WIDEN (Yu et al., 2024b), Chat-Vector (Huang et al., 2024b) demonstrate merging
244   results for larger models with up to 13B parameters, however these studies have a few limitations: ¶
245   They primarily focus on using model merging to improve model quality and hence their experiments
246   do not provide concrete insights on how model size interplays with merging, · They only merge
247   a maximum of two or three models at once, ¸ They primarily focus on held-in tasks and do not
248   provide any insights on the effect of merging on a model’s generalization abilities. Other works like
249   RewardSoup (Rame et al., 2024), WARM (Rame et al.), WARP (Ramé et al., 2024), FuseLLM (Wan
250   et al., 2024a), FuseChat (Wan et al., 2024b) also work with ∼ 7B sized models and focus on specific
251
      applications of model merging without providing any deeper insight about how merging performance
      changes for large models.
252
253
      Varied Evaluation Setups: Most previous works rarely share their experimental setup where
254
      both the expert datasets and the objective vary. For example, RegMean (Jin et al., 2023), Task
255
      Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023), TIES (Yadav et al., 2024b), MaTS (Tam et al., 2024) uses GLUE
256   tasks (Wang et al., 2018), Vision tasks, T0 held-out, and T0 held-in (Sanh et al., 2021b) tasks
257   respectively. Moreover, different works evaluate for different use cases like intermediate task training
258   in Fisher merging (Matena & Raffel, 2022a), robustness in modelsoups (Wortsman et al., 2022a),
259   and held-in performance for Dare (Yu et al., 2024a), both held-in and held-out performance in TIES
260   Merging (Yadav et al., 2024b). Tang et al. (2024) attempts to unify these various problem setting
261   and different evaluation setup and compare many method on similar setups. Given our focus on
262   combining model capabilities in the post training phase, we focus on evaluating on both held-in tasks
263   and generalization to unseen held-out tasks.

265   3     L ARGE S CALE E VALUATION OF M ODEL M ERGING
266
267   In this work, we address the limitations mentioned above by systematically understanding the effect
268   of various factors like model size, base model quality, merging method, and the number of models
269   being merged on both the held-in and generalization performance of the final merged model. Next,
      we describe our experimental design.

      Data: Sanh et al. (2021a) found that explicit multitask training of T5 (Raffel et al., 2020b) on a
271   collection of prompted datasets produces a model with strong zero-shot performance on unseen tasks.
272   This has become a common experimental setting for benchmarking zero-shot generalization (e.g.
273   (Longpre et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024; Muqeeth et al., 2024).
274   Hence, we adopt the experimental setting from the T0 mixture (Sanh et al., 2021a) which contains
275   8 held-in and 4 held-out task categories. For each of these categories there are multiple datasets
276   in the T0 mixture (Sanh et al., 2021b) and hence to reduce evaluation costs, we select 2 datasets
277   from each category based on the popularity and the train dataset size. Specifically, the 8 held-in task
278   categories (with a total of 16 datasets) include Multiple-choice QA, Extractive Qa, Closed-Book
279
      QA, Sentiment Analysis, Topic Classification, Structure-to-text, Summarization, and Paraphrase
      Identification. Similary, the 4 held-out task categories (with a total of 7 datasets) are Sentence
280
      Completion, Natural Language Inference, Coreference Resolution, and Word Sense Disambiguation.
281
      For more details see Section B.

284   Expert Model Creation: Recognizing the significance of post-training for LLMs where models
285
      are typically fully fine-tuned, we perform full fine-tuning to create our expert models to better mimic
      the post-training setting. Moreover, in post-training phases it is common to first perform Instruction
286
      Tuning (IT) on the model before moving on to other steps. Hence, we examine the effect of using
287
      strong instruction-tuned base models on the process and outcome of model merging. Given this, we
288   utilize the PaLM-2 models (Anil et al., 2023) with sizes 1B, 8B, 24B, and 64B as our base models
289   (θbase ). To obtain the instruction tuned base model, we further fine-tuned the PaLM-2 models on the
290   FLAN-v2 dataset (Longpre et al., 2023) while excluding the T0-mixture tasks (Sanh et al., 2021a).
291   These instruction-tuned variants are denoted as PaLM-2-IT. For each of the 2 base model types
292   (non-IT vs IT) and 4 model sizes, we perform full fine-tuning on the 8 held-in task categories resulting
293   64 specialized experts models which are then used further in our experiments. Comprehensive details
294   regarding hyper parameters and computational requirements are provided in Appendix C.
295
296   Experimental Setting: Given our collection of expert models, for each merging experiment we
297   select a subset of expert models which we call the constituent models. We create a large merging
298   experiment grid with 2 base models (PaLM-2 and PaLM-2-IT), four model sizes (1B, 8B, 24B, 64B),
299   four Merging methods (Averaging, Task Arithmetic, Dare-TIES, and TIES), the number of constituent
300   models (2, 4, 6, 8), and 3 seeds to randomly select the constituent tasks for the experiment resulting
301   in a total of 384 merging experiments. These seeds are shared across different experimental settings
302
      to ensure the same tasks are selected across base models, model sizes and merging methods to ensure
      fair comparison. For example, in an experiment we merged 2 expert models, derived from the 64B
303
      PaLM-2 base model with the constituent models being MCQ and Summarization experts while the
304
      same experiment with a different seed resulted in Closed Book QA and Sentiment Analysis experts
305   as the constituent models.

      Evaluation: For each of the experiments above, we assess the merged model’s performance by
      evaluating it on both the held-in tasks – i.e., the training tasks of the constituent expert models – and
309
      all 4 held-out task categories. For example, if the constituent models are MCQ and Summarization
310
      experts, then for held-in tasks we evaluate on the MCQ datasets (DREAM and Cosmos QA) and
311   Summarization datasets (CNN Daily Mail and XSum) resulting a total of 4 held-in evaluation datasets.
312   Moreover, all merging experiments are also evaluated on the 4 held-out tasks categories consisting of
313   7 datasets listed in Appendix B. There we perform approximately ∼ 9000 model evaluations across
314   all of our experiments.

        In this section, we explore the interplay between model size and key factors such as base model quality,
346
        merging method, and the number of constituent (expert) model, along with their effect on both held-in
347     and zero-shot generalization (held-out) performance. Our findings are: ¶ Merging is more effective
348     when the constituent models are derived from instruction-tuned base models rather than pretrained
349     ones (see §4.1); · Larger models facilitate easier merging (§4.2); ¸ Merging significantly improves
350     zero-shot generalization, with instruction-tuned models benefiting from increased constituent models,
351     and larger model sizes allowing the merged model to match or exceed multi-task training (§4.3); ¹
352     We can merge more models effectively when using larger models (§4.4); and º Different merging

356      4.1                      I NSTRUCTION -T UNED M ODELS FACILITATE E ASIER M ERGING

358      Experimental Setup: Prior research suggests a connection between robust zero-shot models and
359
         effective model merging. Wortsman et al. (2022a) demonstrate that averaging strong zero-shot

360
         pretraining allows for weight disentanglement, and thus enhancing merging. Other studies (Yadav
361
         et al., 2024b; Ilharco et al., 2023) propose that strong base models could aid in model merging, though
362      this hypothesis remains largely untested.
363

365
        merging experiments with fully fine-tuned experts from PaLM-2 and PaLM-2-IT. We vary model

366
        is measured over three trials to minimize the impact of selected expert models and their data
367
        distributions. A consistent seed is used across different base models, model sizes, and merging
368     methods to ensure fair task comparisons. We evaluate four merging methods: averaging, task
369     arithmetic, TIES, and Dare-TIES, and also compare against the performance of task-specific expert
370     models.

377      disentangles model weights, facilitating effective model merging and improving the base model’s
         zero-shot performance.

513   5     C ONCLUSION , F UTURE W ORK AND TAKEAWAYS
514
515
      We summarize key insights from our study and provide practical recommendations for model
516
      merging practitioners. Overall, we find that: ¶ Creating expert models from the best available
517   base model is always beneficial. The quality of the base model can be gauged by its zero-shot
518   generalization capabilities. We hypothesize that better generalization leads to improved weight
519   disentanglement (Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024) and a flatter loss landscape, enhancing linear mode
520   connectivity and facilitating model merging; · Merged models often underperform compared to
521   task-specific expert models, indicating a potential loss in performance. Despite this, specialized
522   expert models generally outperform general-purpose multitask models (Liu et al., 2022; Roziere et al.,
523   2023; Luo et al., 2023), suggesting that the performance loss may not be significant when compared
524   to multitask models trained on specific tasks; and ¸ Our findings indicate that large-scale merging
525
      can accommodate more models and significantly improve generalization, outperforming multitask
      training when a powerful zero-shot base model is employed. ¹ Surprisingly, we find that when
526
      working with large instruction tuned models, different merging method perform very similary. This
527
      implies that using simple merging methods like averaging will result in models that are comparable
528   in quality with the models obtained from more advanced merging method.
529
530   Based on our findings, the following future directions are worth exploring. First, exploring strategies
531
      to mitigate held-in performance loss during merging. Some sort of iterative branched training and
      merging of expert models could be fruitful to mitigate performance held-in performance loss. Second,
532
      investigating weight disentanglement and its relationship with zero-shot capabilities could yield
533
      deeper insights for improving both held-in and held-out performance. Third, diving deeper into the
534   insight as to why different merging methods perform similarly at scale. It might be valuable to assess
535   the generality of this finding either theoretically or empirically across different models. Lastly, there
536   are some minor aberrations in our findings which we believe are impacted by the choice of the expert
537   data distributions. A thorough study analyzing the impact of data distribution on the merging process
538   would be very useful. Answering questions like which models are easier to merge and which ones are
539   harder can result in insights to build better merging methods. We hope our research inspires further
      fundamental studies on developing more practical and scalable merging methods.

      A.1    L OSS L ANDSCAPE AND W EIGHT I NTERPOLATION
902
903   While the loss function of a neural network is generally non-convex, recent work (Draxler et al.,
904   2018; Freeman & Bruna, 2016; Garipov et al., 2018; Jordan et al., 2023; Gueta et al., 2023) has
905   demonstrated that the parameter values from different training runs can sometimes be interpolated
906   without increasing the loss (i.e. they are mode-connected). Many methods (Kuditipudi et al., 2019;
907   Tatro et al., 2020; Benton et al., 2021) have explored finding these low-loss paths between models,
908   focusing on simple (not necessarily linear) interpolations. For example, Frankle et al. (2020) showed
909
      that if a part of the optimization trajectory is shared between two neural networks then they can be
      interpolated without lowering accuracy. On the other hand, Neyshabur et al. (2020) showed that
910
      naively interpolating two neural networks with completely disjoint optimization trajectories can result
911
      in a catastrophic drop in their accuracies. Entezari et al. (2021) hypothesized that if we account
912   for the permutation symmetry of neural networks, then all neural networks of a given architecture
913   trained on the same dataset are linear mode connected. This assumption of the existence of a low-loss
914   ”basin” in parameter space encompassing the models is critical for model merging (Ilharco et al.,
915   2023). Ainsworth et al. (2022); Singh & Jaggi (2020); Wang et al. (2020); Jordan et al. (2022); Peña
916   et al. (2023) therefore used techniques based on finding permutations (Wang et al., 2020; Ainsworth
917   et al., 2022) and optimal transport (Singh & Jaggi, 2020) to better align neural networks trained from
      scratch so that they can be merged or interpolated without increasing the loss.

      Section 2.1 discusses the merging methods that we use for our experiments, however, the popularity of
      model merging has led to a ever-growing number of methods and applications of model merging (He
922

924   considerations. Tangent Task Arithmetic (Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2024) fine-tune models in the tangent
925   space for better weight disentanglement when using Task Arithmetic. Akiba et al. (2024) explore
926   using evolutionary algorithms to choose which layers to merge. SLERP (Shoemake, 1985) and Model
927   Stock (Jang et al., 2024) consider the geometric properties in weight space where SLERP performs
928   spherical interpolation of model weights while Model Stock approximates a center-close weight based
929   on several FT models, utilizing their backbone as an anchor point. Tang et al. (2023) train a mask that
930   learns which parameters are important for the merged model. Ye et al. (2023) train a gating network to
931   predict a weight that is then used to compute a weighted average of examples during inference. Yadav
932
      et al. (2024a) provides a comprehensive survey of methods that train a router to route between the
      different models to merge. Moreover, other applications of model merging include intermediate-task
933
      training (Ramé et al., 2022; Choshen et al., 2022a;b), continual learning (Don-Yehiya et al., 2022),
934
      model alignment (Rame et al., 2024; Rame et al.; Ramé et al., 2024), merging pretrained models Yu
935   et al. (2024b), or merging models in different modalities (Sung et al., 2023).

959   In our research, we utilized two base models, namely PaLM-2 and PaLM-2-IT to create specialized
960   expert models. We train the PaLM-2model for an additional 60000 steps on the Flan-v2 dataset (Long-
961   pre et al., 2023) to obtain the PaLM-2-IT model. We removed the T0 tasks from the flan mixture in

963
      convergence. We used Sharded Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern, 2018) optimizer along with a cosine
      decay and a learning rate of 1e-4 for 1B, 24B, and 64B model sizes and 3e-5 for 8B model. We use a
964
      dropout value of 0.05. Following Chung et al. (2024), we used an input length of 2048 and output
965
      length of 512. To create expert models we perform full finetuning with the following hyperparameters.
966   For training the experts model, for all model size, we train by default for 2000 steps with a learning
967   rate of 3e-5 and dropout of 0.05. For some task we adjust the number of steps depending upon the
968   convergence. For the purpose of evaluating classification tasks (Raffel et al., 2020b), we perform rank
969   classification. In this method, the model’s log probabilities for all potential label strings are ranked.
970   The model’s prediction is deemed accurate if the choice ranked highest aligns with the correct answer.
971   It should be noted that rank classification evaluation can accommodate both classification tasks and
      multiple-choice tasks.
