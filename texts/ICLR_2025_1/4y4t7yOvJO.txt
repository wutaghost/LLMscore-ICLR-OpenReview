009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural Architecture Search (NAS) automates the design of neural network ar-
012            chitectures, minimising dependence on human expertise and iterative experimen-
013            tation. While NAS methods are often computationally intensive and dataset-
014            specific, employing auxiliary predictors to estimate architecture properties has
015            proven extremely beneficial. These predictors substantially reduce the number
016            of models requiring training, thereby decreasing overall search time. This strat-
017            egy is frequently utilised to generate architectures satisfying multiple compu-
018
               tational constraints. Recently, Transferable Neural Architecture Search (Trans-
               ferable NAS) has emerged, generalising the search process from being dataset-
019
               dependent to task-dependent. In this domain, DiffusionNAG stands as a state-
020
               of-the-art method. This diffusion-based method streamlines computation, gener-
021            ating architectures optimised for accuracy on unseen datasets without the need
022            for further adaptation. However, by concentrating exclusively on accuracy, Dif-
023            fusionNAG neglects other crucial objectives like model complexity, computa-
024            tional efficiency, and inference latency – factors essential for deploying models in
025            resource-constrained, real-world environments. This paper introduces the Pareto-
026            Optimal Many-Objective Neural Architecture Generator (POMONAG), extend-
027            ing DiffusionNAG through a many-objective diffusion process. POMONAG si-
028            multaneously considers accuracy, the number of parameters, multiply-accumulate
029
               operations (MACs), and inference latency. It integrates Performance Predictor
               models to estimate these secondary metrics and guide the diffusion gradients.
030
               POMONAG’s optimisation is enhanced by expanding its training Meta-Dataset,
031
               applying Pareto Front Filtering to generated architectures, and refining embed-
032            dings for conditional generation. These enhancements enable POMONAG to gen-
033            erate Pareto-optimal architectures that outperform the previous state-of-the-art in
034            both performance and efficiency. Results were validated on two distinct search
035            spaces – NASBench201 and MobileNetV3 – and evaluated across 15 image clas-
036            sification datasets.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   Deep learning has become indispensable in various domains by enabling models to learn intricate
042   patterns from large datasets. The architecture of neural networks plays a pivotal role in their perfor-
043   mance, traditionally requiring expert knowledge and extensive experimentation. Neural Architec-
044   ture Search (NAS) automates this design process, aiming to discover optimal architectures without
045
      human intervention. However, conventional NAS methods often involve significant computational
      costs and are typically tailored to specific datasets, limiting their scalability and general applica-
046
      bility. Transferable Neural Architecture Search (Transferable NAS) addresses these limitations by
047
      generalising the search process across different tasks. DiffusionNAG stands out in this domain, util-
048   ising diffusion processes to generate neural architectures optimised for accuracy on unseen datasets.
049   While effective in reducing computational overhead, DiffusionNAG focuses solely on maximising
050   accuracy, neglecting other crucial performance metrics such as model size, computational cost, and
051   inference latency. In practical applications, especially within resource-constrained environments
052   like mobile devices and embedded systems, it is essential to consider multiple objectives simultane-
053   ously. Existing Multi- and Many-objective NAS methods often face scalability challenges or require
      extensive computational resources, making them less practical for widespread adoption.

054
      This work introduces the Pareto-Optimal Many-Objective Neural Architecture Generator
055   (POMONAG), which extends the capabilities of DiffusionNAG by performing a many-objective
056   optimisation. POMONAG simultaneously considers accuracy, the number of parameters, multiply-
057   accumulate operations (MACs), and inference latency during the architecture generation process.
058   Auxiliary Performance Predictor models, trained to estimate these metrics, are integrated into the
059   diffusion process, guiding exploration towards regions of the search space offering optimal trade-
060   offs among these objectives. This approach facilitates the generation of architectures that are both
061   highly accurate and efficient in computational resources and speed. To enhance POMONAG’s per-
062   formance and adaptability, several key improvements have been implemented. The training Meta-
063
      Dataset has been significantly expanded, incorporating a diverse set of architectures and tasks to
      enhance the Performance Predictors’ capabilities across different domains. Pareto Front Filtering
064
      and Stretching techniques are applied to balance the multiple objectives effectively, ensuring that
065
      the generated architectures are Pareto-optimal. Additionally, the embeddings used for conditional
066   generation have been refined to facilitate more accurate and dataset-aware architecture synthesis.
067   Extensive experiments validate POMONAG’s effectiveness. Evaluations conducted on two promi-
068   nent search spaces—NASBench201 and MobileNetV3—and tested across 15 diverse image classifi-
069   cation datasets demonstrate that POMONAG outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods, includ-
070   ing DiffusionNAG. The generated architectures achieve superior accuracy while satisfying various
071   computational constraints and require significantly fewer trained models, highlighting the efficiency
072   of the proposed technique.
073   The key contributions of this work include:
074
075         • Diffusion-based Many-Objective Optimisation. POMONAG introduces a many-
076           objective diffusion approach within Transferable NAS, optimising neural architectures for
077           accuracy, number of parameters, MACs, and inference latency.
078         • Pareto-Optimal Architecture Generation. By computing Pareto Fronts, POMONAG
079           effectively navigates trade-offs among multiple objectives, generating architectures that
080           offer balanced compromises suitable for diverse deployment scenarios.
081         • Enhanced Meta-Datasets. New Meta-Datasets have been developed to improve the Per-
082           formance Predictors’ ability to predict architecture performance across various tasks and
083           datasets.
084         • Refined Performance Predictor Models. The Performance Predictor models have been
085           enhanced to increase prediction accuracy and effectiveness in guiding the architecture gen-
086           eration process.
087         • State-of-the-Art Transferable NAS Model. POMONAG establishes a new benchmark by
088           generating architectures that are high-performing and adaptable to various computational
089           constraints.

094   2   R ELATED W ORKS
095
096   Neural Architecture Search. Neural Architecture Search (NAS) seeks to automate neural archi-
097   tecture design, removing the need for manual trial-and-error processes. Early methods, such as
098
      reinforcement learning (Zoph & Le, 2016; Zoph et al., 2018), evolutionary algorithms (Real et al.,
      2019; Lu et al., 2019), and gradient-based techniques (Xie et al., 2018; Dong & Yang, 2019b), are
099
      computationally expensive as they require full training of numerous architectures.
100
101   One-shot NAS. To mitigate computational costs, one-shot NAS methods employ weight sharing
102   among candidate architectures. ENAS (Pham et al., 2018) uses an RNN controller to generate sub-
103   networks; DARTS (Liu et al., 2018) relaxes the search space into a continuous one; OFA (Cai et al.,
104
      2020) trains a single large network with many architectural choices, and OFAv2 (Sarti et al., 2023a)
      extends this approach with an enriched search space. While these methods reduce costs, they may
105
      face challenges in optimisation bias, training stability, or performance across diverse sub-networks.
106
107   Multi- and Many-Objective NAS. As application complexity grows, NAS methods increasingly
      consider multiple objectives. Approaches like MONAS (Hsu et al., 2018) and DPP-Net (Dong et al.,


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2018) balance criteria such as accuracy, latency, and energy consumption. NSGANetV2 (Lu et al.,
109
      2020) handles up to 12 objectives. Methods such as POPNASv3 (Falanti et al., 2023), NAT (Lu
110   et al., 2021), and NATv2 (Sarti et al., 2023b) offer Pareto-optimal solutions, addressing scalability
111   and computational challenges inherent in multi-objective optimisation.
112
113
      BO-based NAS. To further reduce computational costs, Bayesian Optimisation (BO) predictor-
      based methods (Luo et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2020) employ surrogate models to estimate architecture
114
      performance without full training. BANANAS (White et al., 2021) uses an ensemble of neural
115
      networks, while NASBOWL (Ru et al., 2021) combines graph kernels with Gaussian processes.
116   However, these methods often play a passive role, mainly filtering architectures generated by other
117   strategies.
118
119
      Transferable NAS. Transferable NAS methods leverage knowledge from previous tasks to accel-
      erate searches on new datasets. MetaD2A (Lee et al., 2021) employs meta-learning to generate
120
      dataset-specific architectures. TNAS (Shala et al., 2023) enhances predictor adaptability to unseen
121
      datasets using BO with a deep-kernel Gaussian process. While promising in reducing search times,
122   these methods may still face inefficiencies in exploring the architecture space.
123
124   Diffusion Models. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) have shown outstand-
125
      ing generative performance by learning to reverse the process of gradually adding noise to data. In
      graph generation, GDSS (Jo et al., 2022) applies diffusion models to undirected graphs. However,
126
      their application to neural architecture generation, involving directed acyclic graphs with specific
127
      constraints, remains unexplored.
128
129   DiffusionNAG. DiffusionNAG introduces a conditional Neural Architecture Generation framework
130   based on diffusion models (An et al., 2024), addressing inefficiencies in traditional NAS methods
131
      that require sampling and training numerous irrelevant architectures. It models neural architectures
      as directed graphs and employs a graph diffusion process for their generation. The forward diffusion
132
      process perturbs architectures with Gaussian noise, mapping the architecture distribution to a known
133
      prior. The reverse process then refines noise into valid architectures using a learned score function.
134   A key component is a specialised Score Network designed to capture dependencies between nodes,
135   reflecting the computational flow in directed acyclic graphs. This network utilises Transformer
136   blocks with an attention mask and incorporates positional embeddings to accurately represent the
137   topological ordering of layers, ensuring that generated architectures adhere to specific search space
138   rules. DiffusionNAG integrates a parameterised predictor into the reverse diffusion process, forming
139   a predictor-guided conditional generation scheme. This allows the model to generate task-optimal
140   architectures by sampling from regions more likely to satisfy desired properties.
141   In Transferable NAS scenarios, DiffusionNAG employs a meta-learned dataset-aware predictor con-
142   ditioned on image classification datasets. This predictor, trained over a distribution of tasks, enables
143   accurate predictions for unseen datasets without additional training. By integrating this predictor
144   into the conditional generative process, DiffusionNAG facilitates efficient architecture generation
145   for new tasks. By leveraging predictor-guided conditional architecture generation within the diffu-
146   sion framework, DiffusionNAG reduces computational overhead and enhances search efficiency in
147   NAS.

221   3.2    M ETA -DATASET
222
      The training of the Score Network, as well as the Performance Predictors, requires a Meta-Dataset containing
223
      the types of architecture structures to be generated, along with their characteristics and performance metrics.
224   These architectures must be sampled from a sufficiently large search space and subsequently trained to solve
225   specific tasks using a broad pool of datasets. This approach generalises the models and enables the Score
226   Network to perform dataset-aware generation.
227   In line with practices used in other models within the Transferable NAS family (Lee et al., 2021; Shala et al.,
228   2023; An et al., 2024), the datasets used for training the architectures – whose metadata will compose the
229   Meta-Dataset – are extracted from the ImageNet32 dataset (Chrabaszcz et al., 2017). For each iteration, ex-
230   traction is performed randomly by selecting 20 classes from ImageNet32. All corresponding samples of these
      classes form the dataset D. In parallel, an architecture A is selected from the designated search space to be
231
      associated with this classification task. The structure of this architecture is stored in encoded form. For more
232   information regarding the encoding of architectures, refer to Appendix A. Subsequently—and in contrast to
233   other approaches—the number of parameters, MACs, and the inference latency on a single 32×32 sample are
234   calculated for the architecture. Since latency is highly susceptible to noise, each measurement is repeated 100
235   times; measurements outside the 90% confidence interval are discarded, and the mean of the remaining values
      is computed.
236
237   Finally, the architecture is trained on the extracted dataset, split into training, validation, and test sets (80/10/10),
238   and its accuracy is calculated on the test split. This information is aggregated and stored as a new tuple in the
      Meta-Dataset, forming a triplet:
239
240           • Dataset: The dataset D of 20 classes extracted from ImageNet32.
241           • Architecture: The structure of the architecture A in encoded form, extracted from the designated
242             search space.
243           • Objectives: The test accuracy, parameters, MACs, and latency values of architecture A on dataset
244             D.
245
      The search spaces used in this work are NASBench201 (Dong & Yang, 2020) and MobileNetV3 (Howard et al.,
246
      2019), utilised independently to create two distinct Meta-Datasets. The choice of training and augmentation
247   techniques is as crucial as the size of the Meta-Datasets to obtain accurate Performance Predictors and generate
248   high-performing and efficient architectures. To this end, the Meta-Datasets created for training the models com-
249   posing POMONAG have undergone the following modifications compared to those used in the DiffusionNAG
250   model:
251           • Training Pipeline Optimisation. The training pipelines for the architectures included in the Meta-
252             Datasets have been optimised to maximise accuracy performance through hyperparameter tuning.
253             For each search space, five architectures were selected and five associated datasets were extracted, as
254             previously described. For each architecture, 50 optimisation steps were executed using Optuna (Ak-
                iba et al., 2019), incorporating a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator sampling method (Bergstra et al.,
255
                2011) alongside a Hyperband pruning mechanism (Li et al., 2018), aiming to maximise validation
256             accuracy. For more information on the search space and the obtained pipelines, refer to Appendix D.
257
              • Dataset Expansion. The size of the Meta-Datasets was increased by including a larger number of
258             architecture-dataset pairs. For the NASBench201 search space, the cardinality was expanded from
259             4,630 to 10,000 triplets. Since the cardinality of the MobileNetV3-based Meta-Dataset was not pro-
260             vided by An et al. (2024), and given the considerable size of the search space, the number of triplets
261             was set to 20,000.

271   remains consistent with that defined by An et al. (2024).

      Performance Predictors. The Performance Predictors are models designed to predict the characteristics of
273
      architectures during and after the generation phase. In the first instance, the objective is to calculate the regres-
274   sion error used as Reverse Diffusion Guidance; in the second, the aim is to estimate the performance of the
275   architecture once denoised, allowing for ranking without the need for any training and thereby identifying the
276   most promising architectures.
277   In DiffusionNAG, there are two Performance Predictors: one for estimating the accuracy of noisy architectures
278   during generation, and one for estimating the accuracy of denoised architectures. In POMONAG, there are five
279   Performance Predictors. Four are dedicated to the respective estimation of accuracy, parameters, MACs, and
280   inference latency of noisy architectures during the diffusion phase. The fifth estimates the accuracy of denoised
      architectures, given that the other metrics can be extracted with negligible overhead.
281
282   The Performance Predictors in POMONAG retain a similar architecture to that in DiffusionNAG. The sub-
283   stantial modifications pertain to the training procedure – which has been revised to maximise the Spearman
      correlation between the predicted values and the actual metrics – the size and content of the Meta-Dataset used
284
      during training, and the model employed as the Dataset Encoder. Specifically, while DiffusionNAG employs
285   a ResNet18 architecture, POMONAG utilises a Vision Transformer (ViT-B-16) model for dataset embedding.
286   From the conducted ablation studies, where the two Performance Predictors of DiffusionNAG are gradually
287   adapted to the version used in POMONAG, a gradual improvement in terms of Spearman correlation is observ-
288   able. Concretely, this amounts to improvements of +0.168 and +0.117 for the versions applied to noisy and
      denoised architectures, respectively. More details on the study are presented in Appendix C.

292   3.4    PARETO F RONT F ILTERING AND S TRETCHING
293
294   In DiffusionNAG, architectures are generated in batches of size 256. The scaling factor kϕ used to weight the
295
      contribution of the Performance Predictor during the generation process is set to 10000 and remains constant
      across all experiments. The generated architectures are then validated by filtering out those with inadequate
296   or incorrect structures. Subsequently, the Performance Predictor for denoised architectures estimates their
297   accuracy, obviating the need to train them. The architectures are then ranked based on this estimate, and the
298   top five are returned as the output of the generation.
299   Pareto Front Filtering. In POMONAG, an additional filtering step is introduced through the construction of
300   a Pareto Front, aimed at leveraging the secondary metrics. Specifically, after the many-objective generation of
301   architectures, the elimination of invalid configurations, and the estimation of accuracies via the Performance
302   Predictor, a Pareto Front is constructed for each of the three secondary metrics, and only the dominant archi-
303
      tectures are retained. This approach enables the selection of architectures that are Pareto-optimal, allowing
      for choices based on trade-offs between accuracy and secondary metrics. To this end, three configurations ex-
304   tractable from each Pareto Front are identified. The configuration POMONAGAcc represents the architecture
305   for which the highest accuracy is predicted; the configuration POMONAGBal represents the architecture for
306   which the ratio between predicted accuracy and the considered secondary metric is highest; finally, the config-
307   uration POMONAGEff represents the architecture with the lowest value of the secondary metric and therefore
308
      the most efficient.
309   Pareto Front Stratching. The Many-Objective Reverse Diffusion Guidance process introduced in POMONAG
310   utilises four different guides, one for each Performance Predictor, to model the distribution of the architecture
311
      space. To optimise the corresponding scaling factors, POMONAG employs a dynamic approach rather than
      a fixed value as in DiffusionNAG. This optimisation process aims to maximise the mean of the estimated
312   accuracies of the architectures in the Pareto Front by adjusting the scaling factors corresponding to each guide.
313
314
      This optimisation leverages Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019), incorporating a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator sam-
      pling method (Bergstra et al., 2011) alongside a Hyperband pruning mechanism (Li et al., 2018). The process
315   involves 100 evaluations on four datasets (CIFAR10, CIFAR100, Aircraft, Oxford III Pets). This procedure is
316   replicated for each search space.
317
      To fully exploit the secondary metrics, POMONAG introduces a novel technique termed Pareto Front Stretch-
318   ing. This entails generating architectures in two phases, each with batches of 128 elements. The first batch
319   favours efficient architectures with respect to the secondary metrics, assigning greater weight to the predictors
320   of parameters, MACs, and inference latency. The second batch aims to obtain highly accurate architectures
321   while still maintaining constraints on their complexity.
322   To implement these two generation processes, the optimisation of the scaling factors is executed twice with
323   different constraints. For efficient architectures, the scaling factor for the accuracy guide is searched within the
      interval [1000, 5000], while for the other guides it is within [100, 500]. For highly accurate architectures, the

      A     M ETA -DATASETS
649
650   This appendix describes the search spaces utilised in this work, the encoding and sampling strategies employed
651   for the creation of the Meta-Datasets, and introduces the Meta-Datasets themselves.
652
653   A.1    NASB ENCH 201 S EARCH S PACE
654
      NASBench201, introduced by Dong & Yang (2020), is a standardised search space for Neural Architecture
655
      Search (NAS) tasks within the domain of image classification. This search space is confined to the composition
656   of an optimal cell, which is used in series to construct the final architecture. A cell comprises four fixed
657   nodes, representing the summation operation of the feature maps received as input, and six possible connections
658   between the nodes. The connections are associated with operations for transforming and adapting the feature
659   maps. The available operations are Zeroise, Skip connection, 1x1 2D Convolution, 3x3 2D Convolution, and
      3x3 Average Pooling. Each convolution operation is followed by a Batch Normalisation layer and a ReLU
660
      activation. This leads to a total of 56 = 15, 625 unique architectures within the NASBench201 search space.
661   The evaluation of models in NASBench201 is conducted through full training on three datasets: CIFAR10,
662   CIFAR100, and ImageNet16. For each architecture, metrics such as accuracy, cross-entropy loss, training time,
663   number of parameters, MACs, and inference latency are provided.
664   Architecture Encoding and Sampling. The encoding of NASBench201 architectures used in this work in-
665   volves two complementary matrices: an operations matrix and an adjacency matrix, as proposed by Dong &
666   Yang (2020).
667   The operations matrix describes the transformations applied to the feature maps between the nodes of the graph.
668   This 8×7 matrix is structured such that each cell represents a possible edge of the directed acyclic graph. There
669   are eight rows—one for the input, six for the possible intermediate connections, and one for the output—and
670   seven columns, one for each possible operation, including placeholders for the input and output. The matrix
      is binary: a ’1’ indicates that the connection is assigned the corresponding operation, and a ’0’ otherwise.
671
      In this sense, each row has exactly one ’1’, since each connection must be assigned an operation. Parallelly,
672   the adjacency matrix defines the connection structure of the graph. Also of size 8×8, this binary matrix is
673   fixed for all architectures in NASBench201. The elements of the matrix are ’1’ to indicate the presence of
674   a connection between two nodes summing the feature maps, and ’0’ to indicate the absence of a connection.
675   Again, the first row and column represent the input, while the last row and column represent the output. The
      sampling of architectures is optimised by exploiting the knowledge of the evaluations present in NASBench201.
676
      Specifically, the top-250 most performant architectures are identified by calculating the average relative to
677   the three datasets used by the authors for their evaluation. Sampling is conducted such that there is a 95%
678   probability of sampling from this subset of high-performing architectures, and a 5% probability from the rest
679   of the search space.
680
681   A.2    M OBILE N ET V3 S EARCH S PACE
682
      The MobileNetV3 search space (Howard et al., 2019), implemented via Once-for-All (OFA) introduced by Cai
683   et al. (2020), is a flexible search space for NAS tasks in the domain of image classification on mobile devices.
684   This search space is defined by a pre-trained super-network that supports various architectural configurations,
685   allowing exploration of a wide range of sub-networks. The super-network comprises a sequence of inverted
686   bottleneck blocks, similar to those in MobileNetV2 (Sandler et al., 2018). The main dimensions of the search
687
      space are:
688          • Depth. Each stage of the network can have a variable number of blocks, typically chosen from 2, 3,
689            4.
690          • Expansion ratio. The number of channels in each layer can be adjusted using two width multipliers.
691          • Kernel size. For depthwise convolutions, the kernel size can be 3×3, 5×5, or 7×7.
692          • Input resolution. The input image can have variable dimensions, typically from 128×128 to
693            224×224.
694
695
      The input resolution is fixed at 224×224. Each of the five stages in the super-network, as provided by the
      original implementation, can include a Squeeze-and-Excitation module and use different activation functions
696   (ReLU or h-swish) based on its position within the architecture. This approach leads to a search space compris-
697   ing approximately 1019 unique architectures. The evaluation of models in this space is conducted through the
698   selection of subgraphs of the pre-trained super-network, eliminating the need to train each architecture from
699   scratch.

933   C     P ERFORMANCE P REDICTORS A BLATION S TUDY
934
      An ablation study was conducted to evaluate the improvements introduced in the Performance Predictors on the
935
      NASBench201 search space. The study involved a series of modifications to the predictors and their training
936   strategies, aiming to maximise the Spearman correlation between the predicted values and the actual metrics.
937
      Initially, a new predictor was implemented using the same training pipeline as An et al. (2024), which yielded
938
      slight improvements in correlation scores over the baseline DiffusionNAG predictor. Specifically, the Spearman
939   correlation for the accuracy of noisy architectures increased from 0.687 to 0.705, and for denoised architec-
940   tures from 0.767 to 0.772. Subsequently, the introduction of a novel training strategy significantly enhanced
941   performance for both noisy and denoised architecture accuracy estimates. This new training strategy involved
942   switching from the Adam optimiser to AdamW, incorporating a cosine annealing learning rate scheduler, and
      introducing a weight decay of 5 × 10−3 . These modifications improved convergence and generalisation of
943
      the Performance Predictors, leading to Spearman correlations of 0.822 for noisy architectures and 0.854 for
944   denoised architectures.
945
      Expanding the Meta-Dataset size from 4,630 to 10,000 architectures further improved the results, with corre-
946
      lations reaching 0.842 for noisy architectures and 0.884 for denoised ones. This demonstrates the benefit of a
947   larger and more diverse training set, providing the Performance Predictors with a richer array of examples to
948   learn from. Finally, replacing the ResNet18 Dataset Encoder with a Vision Transformer (ViT-B-16) feature ex-
949   tractor led to the best performance, achieving Spearman correlations of 0.855 and 0.884 for noisy and denoised
950   architectures, respectively. These results indicate that both the architectural choices and the training strategies
      for the Performance Predictors have a substantial impact on their effectiveness, directly influencing the quality
951
      of guidance provided during the diffusion process.
