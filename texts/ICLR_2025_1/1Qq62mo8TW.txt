009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011            As deep learning(DL) advances, effective feature extraction from big data remains critical for
012            enhancing DL model’s performance. This paper proposes a method for feature extraction in
013            the frequency domain, utilizing advantages such as concentrated signal energy and pronounced
014            data features. However, existing frequency component selection algorithms face challenges like
015            difficulty adapting to diverse tasks and achieving only locally optimal results with extended pro-
016            cessing times. To address these challenges, we introduce the Adaptive Fast Frequency Selection
017            (AFFS) algorithm, tailored for various subsequent tasks. AFFS incorporates a frequency com-
018
               ponent selection factor layer, integrating it with the subsequent DL model to select globally
               optimal frequency component combinations for the DL model. Additionally, we propose a fast
019
               selection algorithm to expedite the process, leveraging the experimental observation of rapid
020
               convergence of selection factor ranking. Experimental results demonstrate that AFFS achieves
021            superior performance across three datasets and three DL models. By using AFFS to select ap-
022            propriate frequency components, even though our input data size is only 10% of the original
023            frequency feature, the classification accuracy of the model is improved by about 1%. Further-
024            more, the early stopping mechanism can shorten the selection process by approximately 80%.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The rapid advancement of deep learning (DL) techniques has garnered increasing interest for handling data in
030   various applications, such as sentiment analysis (Ma et al., 2023), speech recognition (Li et al., 2022; Dhanjal
031   & Singh, 2024), object detection (Chan et al., 2023; Gui et al., 2024), and video recognition (Yan et al., 2022).
032   Typically, these studies tackle data handling in two steps: extracting features from the data and subsequently
033   inputting these features into DL models to achieve various objectives.
034   Feature extraction has become a crucial step in DL-based applications, directly impacting their performance. While
035   traditional methods usually encode data from the original high-dimensional space to low-dimensional spatial-
036   temporal feature embeddings, they often neglect the information in the frequency domain and struggle with issues
037   like noise and outliers.
038
      Recent studies (Kong et al., 2023; Patro et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022) advocate transforming data to the frequency
039
      domain for feature extraction. Some application examples include: Image Domain: JPEG (Hudson et al., 2017)
040   uses the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) to compress images by retaining significant frequency components
041   and discarding high-frequency parts. Speech Signal Domain: Techniques like the Short-Time Fourier Transform
042   (STFT) (Zhou et al., 2022; Kawamura et al., 2023; Kadiri et al., 2023) enable spectral analysis of speech signals
043   for applications such as coding, noise reduction, and recognition. Sensor Data Domain: Frequency-domain pre-
044   processing for sensor data, such as environmental and health monitoring, uses methods like the Fourier Transform
045   to enhance feature extraction and reduce noise (Xu et al., 2023).
046   These applications demonstrate that utilizing feature extraction in the frequency domain can enhance task perfor-
047   mance. The main advantages over direct processing in spatial or temporal domains can be summarized as follows:
048
049
      Energy Concentration: Frequency domain transformations often concentrate the signal’s energy into a few major
      frequency components, facilitating data compression by retaining these key components.
050
051   Enhanced Feature Extraction: Techniques like the Fourier Transform can separate different frequency compo-
052   nents, making certain features more prominent.
053   After frequency transformation, data typically consists of multiple frequency components. Some studies
054   (El Qacimy et al., 2014; Fu & Guimaraes, 2016) have found that DL models exhibit varying sensitivities to different
055   frequency components. Thus, selecting the most effective frequency components for tasks like image classifica-
056   tion (Maurı́cio et al., 2023; Bharadiya, 2023), speech recognition (Jeon et al., 2023a;b), and time series prediction
057   (Morid et al., 2023; Dudukcu et al., 2023; Ruan et al., 2023) is crucial.
058
      Consequently, various studies (El Qacimy et al., 2014; Qin et al., 2021; dos Santos et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020)
059   propose some methods to select the more important subsets of frequency components. These methods aim to


                                                        1

      extract important features and reduce redundancy by selecting various sets of frequency components. Among
061   these, most of them focus on the fixed frequency selection methods. For instance, DCT upper left corner (ULC)
062   coefficients (El Qacimy et al., 2014; Fu & Guimaraes, 2016; dos Santos et al., 2020), DCT zigzag coefficients.
063   However, they suffer from the following problems:
064
      Non-Adaptive: They often rely on fixed components that are not optimized for the specific subsequent task.
065
      Utilizing identical frequency components across different tasks can lead to suboptimal model performance.
066
067   Apart from fixed selection methods, some studies(Qin et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2020) propose learning-based methods
068   to adaptively select frequencies for different subsequent tasks. Although promising, they still have some problems:
069   Local Optima: They often struggle to determine the optimal combination of frequency components simultane-
070   ously. Typically, these methods employ a greedy algorithm that sequentially selects the optimal frequency com-
071   ponents (e.g., first selecting the component that yields the best performance for the subsequent tasks, then the
072   next best component, and so forth). However, this approach may result in a locally optimal solution rather than a
073   globally optimal one.
074   Slow Selection Speed: Their approach involves continuously evaluating the model performance achievable with
075   each selected frequency component, ultimately determining the optimal combination of frequency components
076   through a greedy method. This makes the frequency component selection process computationally expensive.
077   Therefore, designing a fast selection method is crucial to accelerate this process.

      1.1   T HE C ONTRIBUTIONS OF O UR M ETHOD
080
081   To address the aforementioned challenges, we propose the Adaptive Fast Frequency Selection (AFFS) algorithm.
082   This algorithm is designed to adaptively select the most critical frequency components for various subsequent
083   tasks. The main steps involve converting data into frequency components using the Discrete Cosine Transform
084   (DCT), quantifying the importance of each component using selection factors, and selecting components based on
085   their values. The key contributions of this paper are as follows:
086
      1) Task-Specific Frequency Component Selection: We design an easily implementable selection factor layer
087   that requires only minor modifications to existing DL models. Specifically, we add a selection factor layer after
088   frequency conversion and before the subsequent DL model. This layer enables adaptive adjustment based on
089   different subsequent tasks, dynamically selecting the optimal combination of frequency components. Importantly,
090   the selection factors are trained concurrently with the parameters of the subsequent DL model, allowing them to
091   automatically adjust to any subsequent task. Once training is complete, we can select the optimal combination of
092   frequency components at once, thereby avoiding the issue of local optima. Moreover, our FCS module is easy to
093   implement and has plug-and-play functionality for various subsequent tasks due to the minor modifications to the
094   subsequent DL model.
095   2) Fast Frequency Selection Algorithm: We propose a fast frequency selection algorithm to accelerate the selec-
096   tion of the most important frequency components. From extensive experiments, we discover that the ranking of the
097   most important selection factors is typically determined within the initial few iterations of model training, rather
098   than waiting for the model to converge. Moreover, we propose a metric called SFVA to effectively monitor the
099   convergence of the selection factor ranking, and further propose an early stopping mechanism to accelerate the se-
100   lection of frequency components, which significantly reduces training time while maintaining model performance.
101   3) Extensive Experimental Validation: We conduct extensive experiments on three datasets(e.g., CIFAR10,
102   ImageNet, NWPU-RESISW45) and three subsequent DL models(e.g., ResNet18, ResNet50, DenseNet121) to
103   verify the effectiveness of our AFFS algorithm. We have the following observations in experimental results:
104
      Accurate selection. Compared with other frequency component selection methods, our AFFS achieves higher
105   performance across different datasets and models, demonstrating its ability to adaptively select the optimal com-
106   bination of frequency components for various subsequent tasks. Fast selection. Our proposed early stopping
107   mechanism reduces training time by over 80%, enabling fast frequency component selection. Smaller size but
108   higher performance. After feature extraction using our algorithm, the size of the resulting feature data is reduced
109   by nearly 90% compared to the original frequency feature data. Moreover, even with the much smaller size of the
110   extracted feature data, the model performance improves by nearly 1% compared to using the original data.
111   Overall, the AFFS algorithm not only enhances the performance of subsequent DL model by selecting the opti-
112   mal frequency components but also significantly reduces computational requirements, making it able to achieve
113   efficient and effective feature extraction for various subsequent tasks.

      2.1   T HE F REQUENCY T RANSFORMATION M ETHOD
121
122
      There are various transformation methods available for frequency-domain data processing. The Fourier Transform
123   (FT)(Bracewell, 1989) is widely used for converting signals from the temporal domain to the frequency domain,
124   allowing for the analysis of different frequency components. However, the FT tends to distribute signal energy
125   across a wide range of frequencies, which can lead to inefficiencies in feature extraction. The Wavelet Transform
126   (WT) (Zhang & Zhang, 2019) offers a multi-resolution analysis, providing both temporal and frequency local-
127   ization. It is particularly useful for analyzing non-stationary signals and capturing transient features but involves
128   higher computational complexity and implementation challenges.
129   Different from FT and WT, the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) is a widely used frequency domain transforma-
130   tion method in signal processing, particularly for digital images and videos, due to its strong energy concentration
131   characteristics and high computational efficiency (Ahmed et al., 1974; Barbero et al., 1992). So we choose to use
132   the DCT over these methods.
133
      With the rise of deep learning, several studies (Ravı̀ et al., 2016; Gueguen et al., 2018; Rajesh et al., 2019) have
134
      integrated DCT into DL-based computer vision frameworks. For example, Gueguen et al. (2018) modify the
135   libjpeg library to generate DCT coefficients, applying these coefficients to train the ResNet-50 model for image
136   classification. This approach reduces image decoding time and achieves significant training acceleration by directly
137   using all DCT coefficients for network training. Building on this, Rajesh et al. (2019) propose a novel CNN model
138   called DCT-CompCNN, which can accept both quantized and non-quantized DCT coefficients as input. Their
139   results show that this method can produce performance similar to traditional CNN methods with faster model
140   training speed.
141   However, these methods use all frequency components for training and inference, without considering the varying
142   impacts of different frequency components on specific tasks. This results in a large amount of redundant informa-
143   tion, leading to unnecessary computational overhead. By evaluating the importance of frequency components and
144   selecting key frequency components, we can reduce input data redundancy and improve the performance of the
145   subsequent DL model.
146
147
148   2.2   F REQUENCY S ELECTION
149
150   After transforming the original data to the frequency domain using DCT, the data comprises multiple frequency
151   components. To extract efficient feature information, it is necessary to select specific frequency components. We
152
      categorize frequency component selection methods into the following two types:
153   Fixed Selection. This method primarily involves selecting fixed low-frequency components in the DCT coefficient
154   matrix. Fu & Guimaraes (2016) introduce a method combining DCT with truncation to accelerate neural network
155   training. They transform the original input into the DCT domain and, based on the energy compaction property
156   of DCT, truncate the upper-left portion of the DCT coefficient matrix, thereby reducing feature dimensions. dos
157   Santos et al. (2020) extend the ResNet-50 network improved by Gueguen et al. (2018) by incorporating a Fre-
158
      quency Band Selection (FBS) technique to select the most relevant DCT coefficients before inputting them into
      the network. The principle of FBS is that high-frequency information has a minimal visual impact on images, so
159
      only the lowest n frequency components from Y, Cr, and Cb (YCrCb color space) are selected. While effective,
160
      this method discards high-frequency information, resulting in a loss of image details and failing to consider the
161   different impacts of Y, Cr, and Cb on the model, which may reduce accuracy. These methods do not account for
162   the impact of other frequency components and are tailored for specific tasks, limiting their adaptability to various
163   subsequent tasks.
164
      Learning-Based Selection. Different from the fixed selection methods, Qin et al. (2021) apply DCT to channel at-
165
      tention and proposed a two-stage frequency selection method. They first evaluate the model performance resulting
166
      from different frequency components individually. Then, they select frequency components one by one that lead
167   to the current optimal performance, until a sufficient number of components have been chosen. This method aims
168   to find a local optimum at each step without considering the global performance, which always results in the local
169   optimal solutions. Moreover, after selecting each frequency component, it is necessary to reassess the impact of
170   all remaining frequency components on the model performance, which is high computation cost.
171
      Additionally, Xu et al. (2020) introduce a frequency channel selection gate module, which assigns two numbers
172
      to each frequency channel and decides whether to retain the channel by sampling from the Bernoulli distribution
173   Bern(p). Due to the non-differentiable nature of the Bernoulli sampling process, they employ the Gumbel Softmax
174   reparameterization method to update the gate module weights, allowing gradients to backpropagate through the
175   discrete sampling process. Although effective, the gate module’s training process has high computational cost too.
176
      To select the optimal combination of frequency components for different subsequent tasks, we propose the Adap-
177
      tive Fast Frequency Selection (AFFS) algorithm. This method addresses the loss of high-frequency information in
178
      the Fixed Selection approach and can adaptively select the optimal frequency combination for any subsequent task.
179   Additionally, we design an early stopping mechanism to accelerate the selection process of frequency components.

232   This section outlines the process of transforming image data to the frequency domain, which is crucial for feature
233   extraction. The DFDT module encompasses the following four steps:
234   Step 1: RGB to YCrCb. Convert the image from the RGB color space to the YCrCb color space, which separates
235   luminance (Y) from chrominance (Cr and Cb). Post conversion, the image XY CrCb ∈ RC×H×W is decomposed
236   into three parts: XY , XCr , and XCb .
237
      Step 2: 8 × 8 DCT. In this step, we transform the image data from the original domain to the frequency domain
238
      using DCT. Take XY as an example, we apply block-wise DCT to XY . The XY is divided into multiple non-
239   overlapping image blocks of size 8 × 8, following the JPEG method (Hudson et al., 2017). Among which, each

243   Step 3: Frequency Combination. After DCT, we obtain H ′ × W ′ DCT coefficient matrices Fm,n ∈ R8×8 , each

      3.3   E ARLY S TOPPING : G ET γ Q UICKLY
329
330   While the method described in Section 3.2 effectively identifies critical frequency components, it requires the
331   model to converge, resulting in significant time overhead. However, our selection process focuses on the relative
332   importance of each selection factor rather than their final values. By sorting the selection factors in descending
333   order and selecting the top K, we can determine the set Ω of indices corresponding to these factors. If we can
334   ascertain Ω before the model fully converges, we can expedite the frequency selection process, avoiding prolonged
335   training.
336   Rapid Convergence of Selection Factor Ranking. Our method updates γ each training epoch to evaluate the
337   relative importance of frequency components and select the K most significant ones. Fig. 3 illustrates the evolution
338   of all selection factors during training, with epochs on the horizontal axis and selection factor values on the vertical
339   axis.
340
      As shown in Fig. 3, the selection factors are categorized into two groups: unselected (brown in legend) and
341
      selected (other colors in legend). From the outset of training, the values of selected factors are markedly higher
342   than those of unselected factors. The unselected factors’ values decline rapidly early in training and remain near
343   zero, indicating the minimal contribution of their corresponding frequency components to subsequent tasks. Since
344   Ω is the index set of the top K selection factors, its elements stabilize early in training and remain unchanged. For
345   example, if K = 6, we finally select the 0-th, 1-th, 64-th, 65-th, 128-th, and 129-th frequency components, result
346   in Ω = {0, 1, 64, 65, 128, 129}. We can find that the Ω can be determined after 6 epochs and no longer changes
347   in the following epochs, due to the selection factors γ 0 , γ 1 , γ 64 , γ 65 , γ 128 , and γ 129 are significantly larger than
348   other selection factors.
349   Therefore, if we can detect this stabilization epoch, we can halt the training of γ at that point, to accelerate the
350   selection of frequency component.

390   consecutive N epochs, thereby triggering early stopping of training; otherwise, training continues.
391
      As shown in Fig. 5, without early stopping, the model is typically trained for a full 100 epochs. By implementing
392   the early stopping mechanism, we utilize the SFVA metric to automatically monitor the training progress of γ,
393   allowing the network to stop training at the appropriate epoch based on SFVA. The red line in the figure indicates
394   the early stopping epoch. With early stopping, we can limit training to approximately 20 epochs, significantly
395   reducing the training time for γ and enabling fast selection of frequency components.
396
      Discussion on N . The choice of N is crucial for the effectiveness of the early stopping mechanism. If N is too
397
      small, the SFVA metric may not capture sufficient stability in the selection factors, leading to premature stopping
398   and potentially selecting suboptimal frequency components. Conversely, if N is too large, the early stopping
399   mechanism may not trigger soon enough, thereby reducing the benefit of accelerated training.
400
401
      In our experiments, we empirically determine the optimal value of N by evaluating the trade-off between training
      time and the stability of Ω. We find that an N value in the range of 4 to 5 often provides a good balance, ensuring
402
      that Ω has stabilized while still achieving significant reductions in training time. This range allows the SFVA
403
      metric to effectively monitor the convergence of the selection factors and trigger early stopping at an appropriate
404   point.

418
      kernels is the same as the number of input channels required by the backbone model, followed by a BN layer and
419   a ReLU layer. This allows us to feed the extracted frequency features into the existing model.

585   To extract more effective data features for different subsequent DL models in the frequency domain, we propose

810   A.2   E XECUTION P ROCESS
811
812   As previously discussed, DL-based approaches primarily involve two stages: feature extraction and inputting the
813   extracted feature data into subsequent DL model. Our AFFS algorithm adheres to this two-step process, as depicted
814   in Algorithm 1.
815   In lines 4 − 14, we concentrate on the feature extraction stage. Initially, we transform the input data X into the
816   frequency domain using the DFDT module (line 4). Subsequently, the FCS module is employed to select the
817   optimal frequency components (denoted as F req S) for the subsequent DL model (lines 5 − 15). During this
818   phase, an early stopping mechanism is utilized to expedite the frequency component selection, as outlined in lines
819   7 − 13.
820   Upon selecting the frequency components, we obtain the extracted features F req S in the frequency domain. We
821   then move to the second stage: inputting the extracted features F req S into the subsequent DL model for fine-
822   tuning and final output (lines 15 − 17). To facilitate this, the ACM module is applied to adjust the dimensions of
823   F req S to meet the dimension requirements of the subsequent DL model.
824
      Although we use image classification as the task to illustrate our algorithm, AFFS is a general-purpose algorithm
825   that can efficiently and effectively select frequency components that are sensitive to various deep learning tasks.

930   To answer RQ1, we conduct the following experiments to understand the influence of the parameter K on classi-
931
      fication accuracy and early stopping.
932   Impact of K on Classification Accuracy. The parameter K denotes the number of frequency components selected
933   from F req. Using the selection method described in Section 3.2, we chose the K most relevant components out of
934   192 available frequency components for training and inference. Fig. 11 displays the results across three datasets,
935   revealing consistent patterns:
936
937         • Single Component (Freq-0): When only the first frequency component is used for training, reducing the
938           data dimension from 3 × 448 × 448 to 1 × 56 × 56, the model still performs well. This is due to the DCT’s
939           energy concentration property, which compresses most of the signal’s energy into the DC component at
940
              frequency 0, retaining significant information from the original image.
941         • Increasing Components: As the number of selected components increases, the input information be-
942           comes richer, leading to improved accuracy. Peak accuracy is observed at 24 components. Beyond this
943           point, additional components may introduce redundant data or noise, causing a slight decrease in accuracy.
944
            • Optimal Components: Using the top 24 components results in the best model accuracy, reducing the
945
              size of the data input into the subsequent DL model by nearly 90% compared to using all 192 frequency
946
              components. Despite the reduced input size, improvements of 0.96%, 1.91%, and 0.78% in classification
947           accuracy are observed. This demonstrates that appropriate frequency component selection can largely
948           reduce the original frequency feature size to 10% while enhancing model performance.
949
950   These results indicate that training neural networks based on features extracted and selected in the frequency do-
951   main effectively supports image classification tasks. An optimal number of frequency components yield better
952   performance than baseline models. A consistent pattern across the datasets shows that more frequency compo-
953   nents do not necessarily lead to better performance, highlighting the importance of selecting the right number of
954   components to maximize accuracy and achieve significant data compression.
