009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Non-line-of-sight (NLOS) imaging, recovering the hidden volume from indirect
012            reflections, has attracted increasing attention due to its potential applications. De-
013            spite promising results, existing NLOS reconstruction approaches are constrained
014            by the reliance on empirical physical priors, e.g., single fixed path compensation.
015            Moreover, these approaches still possess limited generalization ability, particu-
016            larly when dealing with scenes at a low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). To overcome
017            the above problems, we introduce a novel learning-based approach, comprising
018
               two key designs: Learnable Path Compensation (LPC) and Adaptive Phasor Field
               (APF). The LPC applies tailored path compensation coefficients to adapt to differ-
019
               ent objects in the scene, effectively reducing light wave attenuation, especially in
020
               distant regions. Meanwhile, the APF learns the precise Gaussian window of the
021            illumination function for the phasor field, dynamically selecting the relevant spec-
022            trum band of the transient measurement. Experimental validations demonstrate
023            that our proposed approach, only trained on synthetic data, exhibits the capability
024            to seamlessly generalize across various real-world datasets captured by different
025            imaging systems and characterized by low SNRs.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Non-line-of-sight (NLOS) imaging represents a groundbreaking advancement in visual perception,
031   enabling the visualization of hidden objects with significant implications in diverse fields, including
032   autonomous navigation, remote sensing, disaster recovery, and medical diagnostics (Bauer et al.,
033
      2015; Lindell et al., 2019a; Scheiner et al., 2020; Laurenzis et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2021; Maeda et al.,
      2019). By harnessing sophisticated time-of-flight (ToF) configuration, NLOS imaging systems can
034
      effectively capture light signals bounced off hidden objects, even when direct line-of-sight visibility
035
      is obstructed, as illustrated in Fig. 1(a). The core components of such systems typically include
036   pulse lasers, which emit short bursts of light, and time-resolved detection sensors like Single Photon
037   Avalanche Diode (SPAD) and Time-Correlated Single Photon Counting, which precisely capture the
038   flight of the time that takes for photons to travel from the light source to the hidden object and back
039   to the SPAD. The captured signals, known as transient measurement, undergo reconstruction using
040   various algorithms, including traditional approaches (Velten et al., 2012; Arellano et al., 2017; Liu
041   et al., 2019) and learning-based approaches (Chen et al., 2020; Grau Chopite et al., 2020; Mu et al.,
042   2022; Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; 2024).
043   For the traditional approaches, the back projection algorithms (Laurenzis & Velten, 2013; Velten
044   et al., 2012) and the light path transport algorithms (Heide et al., 2019; O’Toole et al., 2018) typi-
045   cally assume isotropically scattering, no inter-reflection, and no occlusions within the hidden scenes.
046   However, these approaches always yield noisy results and lack details. Conversely, the wave prop-
047   agation approaches (Lindell et al., 2019b; Liu et al., 2020) require no special assumptions and tend
048   to produce better results while being sensitive to scenes with large depth variations. Learning-based
049   approaches (Chen et al., 2020; Mu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023) leverage the powerful representation
050
      capabilities of neural networks, and push NLOS reconstruction to a higher level.
051   Despite promising results, current NLOS reconstruction algorithms are constrained by the reliance
052   on empirical physical priors and are still confronted with challenges. The primary challenge is
053   Radiometric Intensity Fall-off (RIF), i.e., the intensity of the reflected photons attenuates and the
      degree of attenuation is related to the surface material of the hidden object. To address this phe-

071   retro-reflective and diffuse surfaces, respectively (O’Toole et al., 2018), to compensate for intensity
072   attenuation. However, since various surface materials coexist within the same scene, applying path
073   compensation based on a single material type across the entire scene, as performed in physical pri-
074   ors or previous work, may not effectively counteract the effects of attenuation. Additionally, the
075   problem is exacerbated by the low quantum efficiency of the imaging system, particularly over long
076
      distances. As shown in Fig. 1(b), using a single coefficient to compensate for the entire scene can
      enhance the reconstruction of objects with corresponding material properties, but it will significantly
077
      reduce the quality of other objects in the same scene. Another challenge is the limited generaliza-
078
      tion ability mainly caused by various noises, which consist of the dark count of the SPAD and the
079   noise caused by ambient light (Hernandez et al., 2017). As the data acquisition time decreases,
080   the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) decreases, resulting in higher noise levels. The Poisson-distributed
081   noise photons degrade the quality of transient measurements especially at low SNR, manifesting
082   as high-frequency aliasing. This phenomenon poses grave challenges to existing approaches, with
083   traditional ones yielding a plethora of artifacts, and learning-based ones experiencing a breakdown
084   in their ability to generalize.
085   To address the above two challenges, we propose a novel learning-based approach by leveraging
086   the virtual wave phasor field (Liu et al., 2019). Our approach incorporates two key designs: the
087   Learnable Path Compensation (LPC) and the Adaptive Phasor Field (APF). Given that reflected
088   light with different degrees of RIF may be captured simultaneously, the LPC utilizes three physics-
089   based predefined compensation weights to initialize the features of transient measurements for path
090   compensation. Subsequently, a convolutional neural network is trained to implicitly learn and assign
091   optimized compensation coefficients to each scanning point in the transient measurements for the
092   entire scene. By utilizing these optimized coefficients, the LPC adaptively mitigates light wave
093
      attenuation in the same scene, as shown in Fig. 1(b), particularly for distant regions. Meanwhile, the
      APF learns an applicable standard deviation for the Gaussian window of the illumination function,
094
      allowing it to dynamically choose the relevant spectrum band for each transient measurement. The
095
      emphasis on the effective spectrum enables the discrimination of useful information from noise
096   under distinct SNR conditions.
097
098   To demonstrate the efficacy of our proposed approach, we train the approach on a synthetic dataset
099
      and subsequently test them on unseen data, including both synthetic and real-world datasets cap-
      tured from different imaging systems. The exceptional performance on unseen synthetic data and
100
      the diverse real-world data highlight the robust generalization capabilities of our approach. Even
101
      under challenging conditions, i.e., fast acquisition time and low SNR, our method consistently out-
102   performs competitors. To further increase the diversity of NLOS data, we provide three real-world
103   data captured by our own NLOS imaging system to conduct more comprehensive experiments.
104

122   2.1   T RADITIONAL A PPROACHES
123
      In the rapidly advancing field of NLOS imaging, significant progress has been made towards un-
124   veiling hidden objects. The groundwork was established by Kirmani et al. (2009), who pioneered
125   the use of time-resolved imaging to navigate photons around obstructions, despite facing compu-
126   tational challenges due to complex multi-path light transport. Efforts to streamline the complex
127   inverse problem have led to the development of back projection approaches, notable for their ability
128   to approximate the geometry of obscured objects through ultrafast time-of-flight information cap-
129   turing and light geometric relationship (Velten et al., 2012; Arellano et al., 2017). The Light-cone
130   Transform (LCT), marked by the introduction of simple assumptions for light propagation, further
131   facilitated the NLOS reconstruction with unprecedented detail by solving inverse problems in the
132
      linear space (O’Toole et al., 2018). The wave propagation approaches like frequency-wavenumber
      migration (FK) (Lindell et al., 2019b) and Rayleigh Sommerfeld Diffraction (RSD) (Liu et al., 2020;
133
      2019) provided enhanced accuracy for NLOS imaging by considering the interaction between the
134
      light wave and multiple hidden object surfaces. Despite considerable progress, traditional algorithms
135   are still limited with challenges in noise effects and complicated scenes.
136
137
      2.2   L EARNING - BASED A PPROACHES
138
139   Recently, learning-based approaches have been gradually introduced into NLOS imaging.
140   Grau Chopite et al. (2020) proposed the first end-to-end learnable network for NLOS reconstruc-
141   tion. The UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) based network regressed the depth from transient
142   measurements directly. However, it is an unstable solution that transforms the non-linear spatial-
143   temporal domain into the linear spatial domain solely by convolution layers. The instability is
144   particularly evident in real-world scenarios, resulting in poor reconstructions. To solve this prob-
145
      lem, Chen et al. (2020) developed the physics-based feature propagation module (LFE, Learned
      Feature Embeddings) to transform different domains, narrowing the domain gap between the syn-
146
      thetic and real-world data. Building on the insights from NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021), recent ap-
147
      proaches (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Mu et al., 2022) can render the albedo of hidden objects through
148   the radiance field in the unsupervised manner, which consumes large computation time for each
149   inference. Through analysis of transients histogram, Li et al. (2023) produced the first transformer-
150   based framework (NLOST) for capturing local and global correlations, while entailing a substantial
151   computational burden. Yu et al. (2023) introduced a learnable Inverse Kernel (I-K) with attention
152   mechanisms. However, I-K is actually tailored for the point spread function of the imaging system
153   rather than the transient measurements. While the above physics-based approaches (Chen et al.,
154   2020; Li et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023) consistently improve NLOS reconstruction performance, they
155   still encounter challenges when reconstructing real-world scenes with diverse object materials. Ad-
156
      ditionally, these approaches overlook the generalization of the real-world transient measurements
      with low SNRs. In this paper, we present specific solutions tailored to these two challenges.

214   To address the problems mentioned in Section 1, we integrate the proposed LPC and APF modules
215   into the LFE (Chen et al., 2020) framework, which comprises a feature extraction module, a wave
      propagation module, and a rendering module. An overview of the proposed network is shown in

      Fig. 2. Given transient measurements as input, the feature extraction module downsamples the
217   transient measurements in both spatial and temporal dimensions and extracts feature embeddings
218   FE . In addition to albedo, the feature embeddings also encode information such as normals and
219   shapes (Chen et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023)).
220
      Instead of directly applying the wave propagation module to convert features of the transient mea-
221
      surements to the spatial domain, we first employ the LPC to learn different compensation coef-
222
      ficients for each scanning position at the aperture. This allows us to compute the corresponding
223   feature compensation amplitudes, resulting in the compensated feature FC . Subsequently, the APF
224   module predicts the optimal frequency domain window width for the illumination function, which
225   illuminates FC and generates FA . Finally, the wave propagation module and rendering module
226   convert FA from the spatial-temporal domain to the spatial domain and render intensity and depth
227   images, respectively.

253   3.4   D ENOISING WITH A DAPTIVE P HASOR F IELD
254
255
      As described in the imaging formulation in Section 3.1, the transient measurement is illuminated by
      the virtual illumination function. In the frequency domain, the illumination phasor field PF (xp , Ω)
256
      acts as a Gaussian filter on the feature of transient measurements, modulated to the central frequency
257
      ΩC . It should be noted that not all frequency components contribute positively to the final scene
258   reconstruction, including frequency components associated with noise (Liu et al., 2020; Hernandez
259   et al., 2017). Applying an illumination function to the feature of transient measurements can be
260   understood as a process of selecting a certain effective frequency spectrum band △Ω. The bandwidth
261   of the Gaussian function for the illumination function is decided by the standard deviation, which can
262   be expressed as △Ω = 1/(2πσ). The △Ω is defined as the 3 dB bandwidth. Selecting an appropriate
263   standard deviation is crucial for obtaining clean measurements. However, past works (Liu et al.,
264   2019; 2020) have relied on an empirical standard deviation, which is not conducive to selecting the
265   correct frequency components for the reconstruction of complicated scenarios.
266   To address this problem, we devise the APF module to adaptively learn the standard deviation,
267   as illustrated in Fig. 4. Given the feature FC , the first step is to transform the feature into the
268   frequency domain along the temporal dimension. Subsequently, the Fourier features are successively
269   convolved across the spatial and spectrum parts to further enhance the features, which may help the
      module distinguish between useful information and noise more effectively in the frequency domain.

337   4.1   BASELINES AND DATASETS
338
339   Baseline selection. To assess the efficacy of our proposed approach, we undertake thorough valida-
340   tions by comparing it against several baseline approaches on the synthetic and real-world datasets.
341
      These baselines encompass three traditional approaches commonly used in the field: LCT (O’Toole
      et al., 2018), FK (Lindell et al., 2019b), and RSD (Liu et al., 2019), as well as three learning-based
342
      approaches: LFE (Chen et al., 2020), I-K (Yu et al., 2023), and NLOST (Li et al., 2023).
343
344   Public datasets. For the synthetic dataset, we utilize a publicly available dataset generated from
345   LFE (Chen et al., 2020). A total of 2704 samples are used for training and 297 samples for testing,
346   denoted as Seen test set. Each transient measurement possesses a resolution of 256×256×512, with
347
      a bin width of 33 ps and a scanning area of 2m×2m. To assess the generalization capabilities, we
      rendered 500 transient measurements from the objects not included in the Seen test set, denoted
348
      as Unseen test set. For qualitative validation, particularly in complicated scenarios, we employ
349
      publicly available real-world data from FK (Lindell et al., 2019b) and also the data from NLOST (Li
350   et al., 2023) with low SNR conditions. For example, instead of the commonly used measurements
351   with 180 minutes acquisition time, we utilize the measurements with 10 minutes acquisition time in
352   FK (Lindell et al., 2019b). We preprocess the real-world data for testing, and the real-world data has
353   a spatial resolution of 256×256 and a bin width of 32ps.
354
      Self-captured data. To further increase the diversity of NLOS data, we also captured additional
355
      real-world measurements using our own active confocal imaging system. The system utilizes a 532
356   nm VisUV-532 laser that generates pulses with an 85 picosecond width and a 20 MHz repetition
357   rate, delivering an average power output of 750 mW. The laser pulses are directed onto the relay
358   wall using a two-axis raster-scanning Galvo mirror (Thorlabs GVS212). Both the directly reflected
359   and diffusely scattered photons are then collected by another two-axis Galvo mirror, which funnels
360   them into a multimode optical fiber. This fiber channels the photons into a SPAD detector (PD-
361   100-CTE-FC) with approximately 45% detection efficiency. The motion of both Galvo mirrors is
362   synchronized and controlled via a National Instruments acquisition device (NI-DAQ USB-6343).
363   The TCSPC (Time Tagger Ultra) records the pixel trigger signals from the DAQ, synchronization
364
      signals from the laser, and photon detection signals from the SPAD. The overall system achieves a
      temporal resolution of around 95 ps. During data acquisition, the illuminated and sampling points
365
      remain aligned in the same direction but are intentionally offset to prevent interference from directly
366
      reflected photons. As such setting, we capture three transient measurements from customized scenes,
367   each containing different types of surface materials. All measurements were captured over a duration
368   of 10 minutes.

      In this paper, we propose a novel learning-based approach for NLOS reconstruction including two
534   elaborate designs: learnable path compensation and adaptive phasor field. Experimental results
535   demonstrate that our proposed approach effectively mitigates RIF and improves the generalization
536   capability. Additionally, we contribute three real-world scenes captured by our NLOS imaging
537   system. The future work of our study is twofold. We conduct experiments on the confocal imaging
538   system, with the extension to the non-confocal imaging system being one direction of our future
539   research. The modeling of the SPAD acquisition process still exhibits a certain gap from the real-
      world sensor, and considering additional factors remains a focus for future research.

816   Feature extraction module. Given transient measurements as input, the feature extraction module
817
      is responsible for downsampling and extracting feature vectors, as well as enhancing the transient
      measurements. As described in LFE (Chen et al., 2020), the module consists of two branches. The
818
      first branch contains a convolutional layer (kernel size = 3, stride = 1), while the second branch
819
      contains a ResNet block (He et al., 2016). Each ResNet block includes two convolutional layers
820   and one LeakyReLU layer. The output feature dimension of each branch is 1, and the spatial size of
821   the output is 4× smaller than the input data. The two branches are concatenated along the feature
822   dimension and then output.
823
      Spectrum convolution. Firstly, the features of the transient measurements are transformed into the
824
      frequency domain using a Fourier transform. Subsequently, a series of 3D convolutional layers with
825
      a stride of (1, 2, 2) is applied to reduce the spatial dimensions of the feature vectors, with each
826   convolutional layer followed by a ReLU activation layer. The extracted features are then processed
827   using a 1D convolutional layer, normalized with LayerNorm, and passed through a fully connected
828   layer to fuse the spectrum dimension into a scalar value. Finally, a sigmoid activation computes the
829   desired standard deviation.
830
      Wave propagation module. We utilize the physics-based approach RSD (Liu et al., 2019; 2020) as
831
      a wave propagation module. The module transforms the features from the spatial-temporal domain
832   into the linear spatial domain.
833
834   Rendering module. The rendering network consists of one convolutional layer and two custom
835
      convolutional blocks. Each convolutional block includes one convolutional layer and two ResNet
      blocks. The structure of the ResNet blocks is the same as in the feature extraction module. The
836
      network takes the output from the wave propagation module as input and applies the first convolu-
837
      tional block to obtain enhanced features. Next, the enhanced features are concatenated with the input
838   features along the spatial dimension and processed by the second convolutional block. To ensure
839   more stable training, we employ a residual structure, where the output of each convolutional block
840   is added to the input features, producing the final output of the rendering module (i.e., intensity and
841   depth images).
