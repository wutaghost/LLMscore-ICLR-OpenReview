009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As Large Language Models (LLMs) are being increasingly employed in critical
012            domains such as healthcare, it is essential to make these models trustworthy. In
013            this pursuit, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has emerged as a potential source
014            of transparency in LLMs. While CoT reasoning is appealing to humans, prior
015            studies have shown that these reasoning chains are not faithful i.e.; they do not
016
               accurately reflect the underlying LLM’s behavior. Ensuring the faithfulness of
               LLM-generated CoT reasoning is crucial for decision-makers, who rely on them to
017
               determine if, when, and to what extent, trust the recommendations made by these
018
               models. While several works proposed strategies to enhance accuracy and truth-
019            fulness in LLMs, there has been a lack of exploration on the effectiveness of these
020            common strategies to enhance the faithfulness of chain-of-thought (CoT) reason-
021            ing. Specifically, we explore the promise of in-context learning, fine-tuning, and
022            activation editing to improve the faithfulness of the CoT reasoning. Our empirical
023            analyses on benchmark tasks indicate that these strategies offer limited success in
024            improving the faithfulness of the CoT reasoning, with only slight performance en-
025            hancements in controlled scenarios. Activation editing demonstrated minimal suc-
026            cess, while fine-tuning and in-context learning achieved marginal improvements
027
               that failed to generalize across reasoning and truthful question-answering bench-
               marks. We subsequently analyse what makes faithful CoT reasoning challenging,
028
               and present findings to lay the groundwork for future research in trustworthy rea-
029
               soning from LLMs. In summary, our work underscores the inherent difficulty in
030            eliciting faithful CoT reasoning from LLMs, suggesting that the current array of
031            approaches may not be sufficient to address this challenge.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly being employed in diverse real-world applications
037   ranging from content generation and education to commerce and healthcare (Kaddour et al., 2023).
038   One of the primary reasons behind the widespread adoption of these models is their enhanced rea-
039
      soning capabilities, which enable them to generate responses that appeal to human end users (Brown
      et al., 2020b; Wei et al., 2022b). Furthermore, these models are also capable of explaining the ra-
040
      tionale behind the responses they generate, in a manner that is appealing to humans. Despite the
041
      aforementioned advantages, LLMs also suffer from some critical drawbacks. For instance, while
042   LLMs are adept at producing explanations that cater to human preferences, recent research (Lan-
043   ham et al., 2023; Turpin et al., 2023) has demonstrated that the explanations generated by these
044   models – e.g., Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning – do not faithfully capture their underlying be-
045   havior. The faithfulness of the generated explanations turns out to be an important desideratum in
046   high-stakes applications such as medical diagnostics and legal counseling. Ensuring the faithfulness
047   of LLM-generated CoT reasoning is crucial for decision-makers, such as doctors, who rely on them
048   to determine if, when, and how much to trust the recommendations made by these LLMs.
049   Despite the criticality of the faithfulness of LLM-generated reasoning, there is very little research on
050   measuring and enhancing this aspect of LLMs. Recently, Lanham et al. (2023) introduced a slew of
051   metrics for measuring the faithfulness of the CoT reasoning generated by LLMs. For instance, they
052   propose an early answering faithfulness metric, which considers a generated CoT to be faithful if
053   truncating that CoT causes the model to change its final response. While measuring the faithfulness
      of an LLM-generated CoT is one critical aspect, another piece of this puzzle is figuring out ways

054
      to improve the faithfulness of the CoT reasoning generated by LLMs. While prior works have
055   developed approaches to make CoT more aligned with human understanding or knowledge (Lyu
056   et al., 2023), there are no solutions that focus on improving the faithfulness of LLM-generated CoTs
057   in such a way that they accurately capture the behavior of the underlying model (please refer to
058   Appendix for a more detailed discussion on related work). Furthermore, it remains unclear how
059   difficult it is to improve the faithfulness of LLM-generated CoT reasoning.
060
      Present work. In this work, we address the challenge of generating faithful CoT reasoning by ex-
061   ploring the promise of three broad approaches—activation editing, fine-tuning, and in-context learn-
062   ing—to enhance the faithfulness of the CoT reasoning generated by LLMs. Activation editing (Li
063   et al., 2024) involves probing the internal structures of LLMs and strategically updating them to
064   improve certain properties while fine-tuning leverages curated datasets to learn the task. In-context
065   learning, on the other hand, involves providing a handful of samples to the model at inference time
066   to tweak its behavior. These three approaches have found success in modifying LLM outputs to im-
067   prove properties such as accuracy (Wei et al., 2022a), truthfulness (Li et al., 2024), tasks, reduction
068   of biases and hallucinations (Tonmoy et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), they have not been explored
069
      in the context of improving the faithfulness of LLM-generated CoT reasoning. This exploration is
      crucial and lays the groundwork for future research on improving the faithfulness of CoT reasoning
070
      in LLMs.
071
072   Here, we introduce novel activation editing, fine-tuning, and in-context learning strategies to im-
073   prove the faithfulness of LLM-generated CoT reasoning. Specifically, we introduce an activation
074   editing strategy that involves probing LLMs first to identify a vector/direction corresponding to
075
      faithfulness and then editing specific attention heads by translating along the identified faithfulness
      vector. Our fine-tuning and in-context learning strategies involve leveraging the metrics outlined
076
      in Lanham et al. (2023) to identify specific instances and their corresponding faithful CoT reason-
077
      ing, and providing these as inputs to the LLM during the fine-tuning or in-context learning phases,
078   respectively.
079
080   Despite the promise of these techniques, our findings reveal that none of them significantly en-
081
      hance the faithfulness of the CoT reasoning generated by LLMs. While activation editing approach
      demonstrates limited success in amplifying faithful behavior of CoT reasoning, the fine-tuning and
082
      ICL approaches slightly improved CoT faithfulness in controlled scenarios but did not generalize
083
      well across diverse datasets. Our results underscore the inherent difficulty in eliciting faithful rea-
084   soning from LLMs, suggesting that the current array of techniques available to us is insufficient
085   for addressing this complex challenge. Our research emphasizes the need for fundamentally new
086   methodologies that can delve into the inner workings of LLMs to enhance the faithfulness of LLM-
087   generated CoT reasoning, ensuring that LLMs are not only generating correct responses but also
088   doing so in a manner that faithfully reflects their internal reasoning processes.

095
      Chain-of-Thought (CoT). CoT reasoning in LLMs provides a structured response where the model
096   explicitly generates the step-by-step thought process leading to its final response. This technique is
097   particularly useful in complex reasoning tasks, such as solving math problems or logical question-
098   answering scenarios, and high-stakes decision-making, where transparency in decision-making is
099   crucial. By eliciting intermediate steps, CoT significantly improves the accuracy of LLMs on rea-
100   soning tasks and simultaneously leads to greater user trust and understanding. A relevant stakeholder
101   can now see how the LLM processes the input information and relies on it to generate the final out-
102   put response. See Fig. 1 for examples of CoT reasoning. This CoT reasoning can potentially make
103   the LLM’s reasoning process more transparent and easier to trust. Further, this also mimics human
104
      problem-solving approaches, allowing for easier debugging and refinement of model reasoning.

      Next, we describe three strategies to improve the faithfulness of CoT reasoning generated by LLMs,
174
      each focusing on a different aspect (data, weight, activations) of an LLM, i.e., in-context examples
175   (Sec. 3.1), fine-tuning weights (Sec. 3.2), and activation editing (Sec. 3.3) respectively.

      3.1   FAITHFUL R EASONING VIA I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
178
179   In contrast to traditional learning approaches that require explicit training or fine-tuning on task-
180   specific data, In-Context Learning (ICL) (Wei et al., 2022a) allows an LLM to generalize and adapt
181   its knowledge by learning patterns from a limited set of demonstrations added within the prompt
182   during inference. ICL is a computationally efficient technique that shows an LLM’s capability to
183   transfer knowledge to novel tasks without additional parameter updates and can be used for both
184   open- and closed-source LLMs.
185   In order to improve the faithfulness of CoT reasoning using ICL, we include demonstrations of
186   faithful CoT reasoning in-context before the question. The intuition behind using ICL is that each
187   ‘faithful’ CoT demonstration constitutes a set of ‘faithful’ reasoning blocks expressed in natural
188   language, and steering LLMs towards using these filtered faithful reasoning blocks to arrive at an
189   answer, can in turn makes the generated CoT reasoning more faithful.
190
      Formally, we consider N in-context examples, each represented as a triple (Qi , Ei , Ai ) for 1 ≤ i ≤
191   N , where Qi and Ai represents the question and answer associated with the i-th example, while
192   Ei denotes a ‘faithful’ CoT reasoning for the question Qi and answer Ai . Mathematically, we can
193   express the set of N in-context examples as {(Q1 , E1 , A1 ), (Q2 , E2 , A2 ), . . . , (QN , EN , AN )}.
194
      For a given question Q, a language model F and system prompt S to generate CoT reasoning Ae
195
      along with an answer A, the model F operates as follows, F : (Q + S) → (Ae + A), whereas
196
      in-context learning involves passing in the examples as:
197
198              F((Q1 , A1 , E1 ) + (Q2 , A2 , E2 ), . . . , +(QN , AN , EN ) + Q + S) = Ae + A,
199
200
      where N demonstrations chosen for ICL impact both the accuracy and faithfulness of answers and
      CoT reasoning. In order to systematically assess the influence of the specific ICL examples chosen,
201
      we propose the following sampling strategies.
202
203
            1) Deterministic Uniform (DU). Here, we query the LLM deterministically with tem-
204            perature τ = 0 to yield (Q, E, A) triplets over the full training set. We then uni-
205            formly sample N demonstrations for ICL. Mathematically, this can be expressed as
206            S(τ =0, nshot=N, mode=‘uniform’) (see Sec. 2).
207
            2) Deterministic Faithful (DF). As above, except we select the N most faithful CoT reason-
208
               ing across the (Q, E, A) triplets, expressed as S(τ =0, nshot=N, mode=‘faithful’).
209
210         3) Stochastic Uniform (SU). With this approach, we introduce diversity in eliciting CoT
211            reasoning by sampling at τ > 0, generating 10 samples per question and retaining only the
212
               most faithful sample. We then uniformly sample N demonstrations for ICL, expressed as
               S(τ > 0, nshot=N, mode=‘uniform’).
213
214         4) Stochastic Faithful (SF). Here, we combine stochastic sampling with most faithful se-
215            lection and select the N most faithful demonstrations for ICL, expressed as S(τ >
               0, nshot=N, mode=‘faithful’).

220   3.2   FAITHFUL R EASONING VIA F INE -T UNING
221
222   Recent progress in LLMs has led to a paradigm shift from the traditional development of models
223   from scratch to an adoption of shared pre-trained LLMs, e.g., BERT (Devlin et al., 2019),
224   GPT (Brown et al., 2020a), Llama (Dubey et al., 2024), that can readily be fine-tuned for specific
225   downstream applications. We utilize a combination of recent techniques like Parameter-Efficient
226   Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al.,
227
      2021) that allows efficient fine-tuning LLMs on smaller datasets and reduces the number of
      trainable parameters by learning low-rank adaptation matrices, making the fine-tuning process more
228
      memory and computationally efficient while retaining information that is important for downstream
229
      performance.
230
231   Our exploration of faithful CoT reasoning via fine-tuning is motivated by Liu et al. (2024a); Ding
232   et al. (2023) which argues that few-shot PEFT are more effective and cost-efficient as compared to
233
      ICL. Hence, we investigate the possible benefits of fine-tuning techniques to elicit more faithful CoT
      reasoning from LLMs. Our study explores a series of selection strategies aimed at enhancing the
234
      faithfulness of CoT reasoning. To this end, we curate a variety of datasets for fine-tuning state-of-
235
      the-art LLMs with the goal of fine-tuning LLMs with different question, answer, and CoT reasoning
236   examples and understanding their effects on the faithfulness of CoT reasoning generated by the
237   LLM for test samples during inference. In particular, the strategies we employ for the selection of
238   (Q, E, A) triplets used in finetuning are directly analogous to their ICL counterparts described in
239   Sec. 3.1:
240
241         1) Deterministic Uniform (DU). Selecting all examples (instead of N random examples) for
242            the finetuning dataset: S(τ =0, p=100%, mode=‘uniform’).
243         2) Deterministic Faithful (DF). Selecting a percentage of the most faithful examples (instead
244            of the top N ) for finetuning: S(τ =0, p < 100%, mode=‘faithful’).
245         3) Stochastic Uniform (SU). Selecting all examples (instead of N random examples) for the
246            finetuning dataset: S(τ > 0, p=100%, mode=‘uniform’).
247
            4) Stochastic Faithful (SF). Selecting a percentage of the most faithful examples (instead of
248            the top N ) for finetuning: S(τ > 0, p < 100%, mode=‘faithful’).
249
250   As in Sec. 3.1, the superscript c notation in the empirical analysis indicates that only (Q, E, A)
251   triplets with correct answers were used for fine-tuning.

253   3.3   FAITHFUL R EASONING VIA ACTIVATION E DITING
254
255   Seminal works in explainable artificial intelligence have shown that probing analysis (Alain & Ben-
256   gio, 2016) can find vectors in the activation space of deep neural networks that correlate to specific
257
      properties learned by the underlying model. Formally, editing activations to steer a LLM’s behavior
      involve two key steps - a probing analysis step to identify which components of the model to in-
258
      tervene on, and an editing step which manipulates the activations at run-time. These two steps are
259
      detailed below.
260
261   Step 1: Probing for Faithfulness. Analyzing a model’s internal structures, such as individual
262   neurons or specific mechanisms like convolution or attention, can offer insights into the inner
263
      workings of LLMs (Li et al., 2024). A standard tool to understand a model’s inner workings is a
      “probe” (Alain & Bengio, 2016). Probes are linear classifiers trained on a model’s intermediate
264
      activations to predict a property like factual correctness, harmful biases, etc. By assessing how well
265
      these probes perform, we can infer the extent to which certain types of (mis)information is encoded
266   at different layers or components of the model.
267
268   Specifically, we aim to identify attention heads that encode information for faithful reasoning. Using
269

522      5              C ONCLUSION
523
524     In this study, we make one of the first attempts at exploring the promise of various popular
525     paradigms, namely, activation editing, fine-tuning, and in-context learning, to improve the faith-
526     fulness of the CoT reasoning generated by LLMs. Our empirical results indicate that while these
527     methods provide marginal improvements, none were sufficient to consistently enhance the CoT
528     faithfulness across diverse datasets and LLMs. Generating faithful CoT reasoning from LLMs is
529     challenging because the training process does not optimize for faithfulness. While pre-training
530     focuses on next-token prediction using vast corpora of human-written text, SFT aims to improve
531
        performance on specific tasks like math problems and common sense reasoning. Neither of these
        training paradigms inherently encourages faithful reasoning. RLHF inadvertently discourages faith-
532
        ful reasoning by rewarding responses that appear convincing to human evaluators, regardless of their
533
        actual faithfulness to the model’s internal processes. The goal of faithful Chain of Thought (CoT)
534     reasoning involves fundamentally altering the model to generate reasoning that is more self-aware of
535     its internal decision-making processes. This represents an intrinsic change in the model’s behavior,
536     rather than simply learning a new task. In summary, our work underscores the inherent difficulty in
537     eliciting faithful CoT reasoning from LLMs, suggesting that the current array of approaches may not
538     be sufficient to address this challenge. This exploration is crucial to lay the groundwork for future
539     research on trustworthy reasoning from LLMs. Ethics and reproducibility statements are provided
        in Sec. A and Sec. B of the Appendix respectively.

761   Our work focuses on exploring whether we can improve the faithfulness of the CoT reasoning
762   generated by state-of-the-art LLMs. This has significant positive implications for societal benefit.
763
      For instance, if CoT reasoning output by LLMs faithfully captures the underlying model behavior,
      decision-makers and relevant stakeholders can leverage this to determine if, when, and how much to
764
      rely on the recommendations provided by LLMs. Therefore, our exploration itself is very valuable
765

766   niques commonly used to steer behavior in LLMs are not effective in enhancing the faithfulness of

768   in the right direction, informing us of the complexity of the problem and underscoring the need for
769   fundamentally different frameworks to address it. As far as we understand, our work does not have
770   any potential negative societal impacts, as it is mainly an exploration to improve the faithfulness of
771   LLM-generated CoT reasoning.

      C    R ELATED W ORK
785
786
      Chain-of-Thought Reasoning Large Language Models (LLMs) produce Chain-of-Thought
      (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al., 2022b; Agarwal et al., 2024) to help provide end users with a peak
787
      into the reasoning process leading up to their response. While the CoT reasoning generated by these
788
      models is often appealing to human end users (Wei et al., 2022b; Krishna et al., 2024), prior research
789   has argued that LLM-generated CoT reasoning does not faithfully capture the underlying behavior
790   of these models and that this is a critical challenge particularly in applications involving high-stakes
791   decision making (Agarwal et al., 2024). For instance, as discussed in Agarwal et al. (2024), a doctor
792   would benefit from seeing an explanation that faithfully captures why an LLM is recommending a
793   particular diagnosis for a patient, as opposed to seeing some plausible explanation that could lead
794   to the diagnosis at hand. In the former case, the doctor can actually use this faithful explanation to
795   determine if and how much to rely on the model’s recommendation.
796
797   Evaluating the Faithfulness of CoT Reasoning Despite the criticality of the faithfulness of
798   LLM-generated CoT reasoning, there is very little work on analyzing and measuring this aspect
799   of LLMs. Turpin et al. (2023) were the first to demonstrate that CoT explanations may not faithfully
800   capture the behavior of the underlying models. They showed that these explanations can be heavily
801
      influenced by biasing model inputs e.g., by reordering multiple-choice options in a few-shot prompt
      to always make the answer “(A)”—which these models systematically fail to mention in their ex-
802
      planations. Lanham et al. (2023) extended the above work and proposed novel metrics to measure
803
      the faithfulness of an LLM-generated CoT explanation. For instance, they propose an early answer-
804   ing metric, which considers a generated CoT to be faithful if truncating that CoT causes the model
805   to change its final response. Similarly, if adding mistakes in a generated CoT causes the model
806   to change its final response, then the original CoT can be considered faithful. Analogously, they
807   proposed other metrics to measure faithfulness based on paraphrasing the beginning portions of the
808   original CoT as well as replacing the CoT with filler tokens (e.g., ellipses). Using these metrics, they
809   demonstrated that the CoT reasoning produced by state-of-the-art LLMs does not faithfully capture
      the behavior of the underlying models. In our work, we use the early answering test proposed in


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Lanham et al. (2023) to measure faithfulness. Early answering faithfulness metric evaluates faith-
811   fulness by measuring the amount of post-hoc reasoning. The premise is that if reasoning is not
812   post-hoc, there are fewer ways for it to be unfaithful than there are for reasoning which is post-hoc.
813   While there are other possible faithfulness metrics, they have limitations as shown in Table 1.
814
815
      Enhancing the Quality of CoT Reasoning While there are some prior works that tackled the
816
      problem of improving the quality of CoT reasoning Lyu et al. (2023), their focus was on improving
817
      its quality vis-a-vis human knowledge or understanding. For example, Lyu et al. (2023) focused
818   on generating a reasoning chain that could then be put through a deterministic math solver, and the
819   resulting answer from this solver was compared to the answer produced by the LLM. The reasoning
820   chain was considered to be faithful if the answers of the solver and the LLM matched. Note that this
821   approach does not account for ensuring that the internal computations or the underlying behavior of
822   the LLM was captured in the reasoning chain, which is the focus of our work.
823
      In summary, our work makes one of the initial attempts at exploring the promise of various popular
824   paradigms, namely, activation editing, fine-tuning, and in-context learning, to improve the faithful-
825   ness of the CoT reasoning generated by LLMs.

887   D.3                           W HY CAN ’ T FINE - TUNING IMPROVE FAITHFULNESS ?
888
      An important aspect to note is that fine-tuning typically targets static datasets. Truthfulness or fac-
889   tual correctness is one example of this, where a fixed notion/ground truth is learned by the model.
890   Faithfulness, however, is a property intrinsic to the model itself, which poses significant challenges
891   when trying to finetune based on faithful CoT examples, since there is no guarantee that such ex-
892   amples remain faithful once model parameters are adjusted, i.e., finetuning for faithfulness poses
893   non-stationary/moving targets.
894
      We demonstrate this with the example in Fig. 13, where both models predict a final answer of op-
895   tion A. Despite the original and finetuned model yielding semantically similar CoT reasoning (and
896   identical Step 1), the finetuned model’s early answer probabilities are distinctly dissimilar (favoring
897   option C with low probability for A), and this reflects significantly on the faithfulness of the explana-
898   tion. The key takeaway is that faithfulness is not a fixed property w.r.t. CoT reasoning. Optimizing
899   for faithful CoT w.r.t. the original model holds unintended downstream effects on the finetuned
900   model’s internals that shift the goalposts of faithfulness, and hence finetuning on static datasets of
901   faithful CoT explanations is likely insufficient to solve a problem where dynamic optimization of
902   model parameters is required.
