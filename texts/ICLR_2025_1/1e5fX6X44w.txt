008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010             We propose a novel class of neural differential equation models called mean-field
011
                continuous sequence predictors (MFPs) for efficiently generating continuous se-
                quences with potentially infinite-order complexity. To address complex inductive
012
                biases in time-series data, we employ mean-field dynamics structured through
013
                carefully designed graphons. By reframing time-series prediction as mean-field
014             games, we utilize a fictitious play strategy integrated with gradient-descent tech-
015             niques. This approach exploits the stochastic maximum principle to determine the
016             Nash equilibrium of the system. Both empirical evidence and theoretical analysis
017             underscore the unique advantages of our MFPs, where a collective of continu-
018             ous predictors achieves highly accurate predictions and consistently outperforms
019             benchmark prior works.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Modeling spatiotemporal processes provides profound insights into and enhances the ability to
024   predict the behavior of complex systems that evolve across both temporal and spatial dimensions. In
025   recent studies, neural differential equation models (Chen et al., 2019; Tzen & Raginsky, 2019) have
026   demonstrated exceptional generalization capabilities and effectiveness in capturing continuous-time
027
      spatiotemporal dynamics, with applications ranging from generative modeling (Song et al., 2021) and
      quantitative finance (Cohen et al., 2023) to physically-informed neural networks (Iakovlev et al., 2024).
028
      Despite their notable performance, existing approaches fail to offer theoretical findings for a key
029
      question inherent to continuous time series: How does the model behave as time granularity becomes
030   finer, ultimately leading to infinite observations? To answer the question, a viable approach is to
031   directly model data dynamics over continuous intervals with infinite complexity. To this end, this work
032   focuses on employing mean-field games (Lasry & Lions, 2007), to develop an infinite-dimensional
033   predictive decision-making framework, generalizing existing differential equation models.
034
      The mean-field principle, a core philosophy in several scientific fields such as neuroscience (Faugeras
035
      et al., 2009), statistical physics (Negele, 1982), and economics (Carmona, 2020; Cardaliaguet &
036   Lehalle, 2018), serves as a powerful tool to model and analyze how large numbers of interacting
037   agents behave strategically in stochastic dynamics, decentralized environments. In the mean-field
038   regime, a continuum of infinitely many agents is expected to satisfy Nash equilibrium by individually
039   governing the dynamics of partially observed historical sequential data and collectively interacting
040   with each other to make optimal group decisions for forecasting future events. The central premise
041   of this game-theoretic interpretation of the predictive system can be encapsulated in the following
042   statement: We extend the continuous-time sequence prediction problem into the formal setting of
043   mean-field games. In relation to this statement, our contribution is twofold:
044
      • We extend existing differential equation models by proposing mean-field graphon SDEs as a novel
045
        framework for modeling sequence predictors. This framework effectively captures the stochastic
046
        spatiotemporal dynamics of an infinite continuum of agents, grounded in conjectures from time series
047     analysis (e.g., seasonality). To efficiently solve the mean-field games, we introduce gradient-based
048     FBSDEs, which significantly reduce the computational complexity associated with approximating
049     Nash equilibrium.
050
      • Building on the concentration of empirical measures and the propagation of chaos property, our
051     theoretical analysis clarifies the effect of leakage in past observations on the generalization perfor-
052     mance of the mean-field system. We demonstrate that, as the population of agents increases, the
053     coalition produces increasingly accurate and reliable predictions.

395   5     R ELATED W ORK
396
397   Neural Differential Equation Models. In recent years, neural differential equation models have
398   gained attention for their ability to capture the dynamics of complex continuous sequences. Latent
399   ODEs (Rubanova et al., 2019) extend standard RNNs to handle continuous signals by integrating
400   neural ODEs with them. Kidger et al. (2020) introduced differential equation models based on
401
      controlled differential equations (Neural CDE) to address a key limitation of neural ODEs, where
      solutions depend solely on initial conditions and not on subsequent observations. Recently, Contif-
402
      ormer (Chen et al., 2024) was developed, combining neural ODEs and Transformers into a single
403
      framework. Another line of research integrates stochasticity by utilizing SDEs, particularly for
404   time-series applications. Latent SDE (Li et al., 2020) encodes sequential data in the latent space
405   using neural SDEs, while MaSDE (Park et al., 2023) employs a concept of stochastic differential
406   games to analyze time series. Koshizuka & Sato (2023) proposed a regularized neural SDE based on
407   the Lagrangian Schrödinger bridge, and Oh et al. (2024) introduced three stable types (classes) of
408   neural SDEs: Langevin-type SDE, Linear Noise SDE, and Geometric SDE.
409
      Mean-field Principles in Generative Models. Recent works utilized the mean-field principle to
410   model the infinitely many random particles in high-dimensional data space, where they interact with
411   each other. In Liu et al. (2022), the Schrödinger bridge was incorporated to address mean-field games
412   in order to approximate data distributions for large populations. Park et al. (2024) introduced the
413   concept of propagation of chaos to generate data structures with exchangeable high cardinality such
414   as 3D point clouds.

477   7                 C ONCLUSION
478
479   This paper introduces mean-field continuous sequence predictors, a novel class of neural SDE model
480   for the efficient generation of continuous sequences, which can possess infinite-order complexity. To
481   capture the complex inductive biases in time-series data, we propose the mean-field dynamics using
482   meticulously designed graphons. We recast the time-series prediction problem as a mean-field game
483   and adopt a fictitious play approach, integrated with a gradient-descent-based method, to leverage the
484   stochastic maximum principle and identify the Nash equilibrium of the system. Both empirical and
485   theoretical results reveal the distinctive features of our MFPs, where the coalition of a continuum of
      predictors generates accurate predictions and consistently surpasses benchmark performance.

       This section provides the implementation of mean-field predictors in detail.
1471   Experimental Setup. Given that T := [0, T ] is the time span of each continuous sequence data
1472   instance, our prediction task is to read the first (historical) α% observations, [0, (αT /100)], and
1473   forecast the future (1 − α)% events, [(αT /100), T ]. We set T to 100 for the MIT Humanoid Robot,
1474   48 for MIMIC-II, and to 72 for the Beijing Air Quality dataset. The value of α is fixed at 80 across
1475   all datasets.
1476   Model Architecture. In each forward step of Xu (t) from t to t + ∆t, a neural network takes Xu (t), t,
1477   and u as inputs and outputs b(·, α), W (α), and w. In the first stage of the neural network, Xu (t) and
1478   t are concatenated into a single vector, which is then projected into a hidden vector via a multilayer
1479   perceptron (MLP). This hidden vector is subsequently passed through a computation block consisting
1480   of several MLP layers with skip connections. Finally, after the computation block, the hidden vector
1481   is projected into b(·, α), W (α), and w using respective MLPs. In our architecture, each MLP is
1482
       composed of two linear layers, with a Swish activation function positioned between them.
1483   To process the labeling information u in the neural network, we apply adaptive normalization (Peebles
1484   & Xie, 2023). Specifically, instead of using fixed scale and shift parameters in the normalization
1485   layers of α(.; θ), we regress these parameters based on u. The adaptive normalization layers are
1486   placed between MLP layers. We find that this conditioning mechanism effectively incorporates the
1487   labeling information, outperforming the approach of simply concatenating u into input vectors.
1488   After obtaining outputs from the neural networks, we evaluate bW (·, α) for forward evaluation of
1489   SDEs. To derive bW (·, α), we compute an exponential or cosine graphon W using u and v where
1490   v < t. Next we calculate the projection ProjS d−1 (x − y) := (x − y)/∥x − y∥ with x = Xu (t)
1491   and y = Xv<t (t). These values are then integrated into with W  P(α) using Defintion 2.2 and Eq (2)
1492   or Eq (4) into W α and ψ α , finally leading to bW (·, α) =        v<t ψ α (Xu (t), Xv (t))W α (u, v).
1493   After
       P      forward  evaluation, we utilize w to  aggregate  predictors by applying softmax. (i.e., Λt =
1494      v<t  Softmax(w(u,   v); {w(t, u)}u<t )X u (t) where Softmax(x    ∈ S; S) represents the value of x
       after applying the softmax operation to the entire set S which includes x.)

1496   Parallel Computation. Since the direct application of Alg. 1 is computationally intractable for large
1497   particle count N , we introduce novel parallel computing to efficiently sample proposed mean-field
1498   predictors, as described in Fig 6. At each step of forward evaluation, given all predictors Xαu , each
1499   predictor can be processed independently using Eq (1). In other words, no predictor needs to wait for
1500
       the others to complete their forward evaluation. By taking advantage of this property, at time t, we
       store all predictors with u ≤ t in the shared memory and forward predictors one step in parallel. This
1501
       parallel implementation significantly decreases empirical computation time by reducing the number
1502
       of iterations for forward evaluation from O(SN ) to O(S) where S is the number of steps for forward
1503   evaluation and N is the number of sampled observations.
