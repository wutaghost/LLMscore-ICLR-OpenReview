009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Autoregressive (AR) models have become central to modern foundation models
012
               like large language models (LLMs) and visual-language models (VLMs). Re-
013
               cently, AR-based approaches have extended into text-to-image generation. Al-
014            though these text-to-image AR models have been trained for visual-language to-
015            ken interaction, they often struggle when conditioned on visual inputs. Focus-
016            ing on this drawback, in this paper, we are curious about one question: how can
017            we inject vision information to a pre-trained AR model to ensure its output re-
018            flects visual conditions? We answer this question with a simple yet effective so-
019            lution termed InjectAR. Our key insight is that, while a pre-trained AR model
020            cannot handle visual inputs directly, its inherent capability for visual-language in-
021            teraction can indeed support visual feature extraction. Consequently, with only
022
               a few newly introduced parameters and minimal training, a pre-trained AR gen-
               eration model can successfully accommodate both text and image conditions and
023
               produce visually appealing results. To manage the relationship between textual
024
               and visual inputs, we reinforce InjectAR with a hierarchical attention mechanism,
025            which subdivides the attention scores for textual tokens into their corresponding
026            visual components, preventing either modality from dominating the output. As
027            the first AR model with this capability, extensive experiments show that InjectAR
028            achieves performance on par with, or even surpasses, state-of-the-art diffusion
029            models. Moreover, unlike diffusion models, once trained, our method has the po-
030            tential for flexible control over the positions of visual objects. Our codes will be
031            available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Text-to-image generation is a multimodal-involved task, which aims to generate corresponding high-
038   quality images based on the text descriptions provided by users. Recently, large-scale models, in-
039
      cluding models based on diffusion (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020b)
      and autoregression (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Ramesh et al., 2021; Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al.,
040
      2022; Li et al., 2024b; Sun et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024), have exhibited impressive capabilities
041
      in generating diverse and realistic images. In contrast with the continuous image representation in
042   diffusion models (Ramesh et al., 2021; 2022; Wei et al., 2023), autoregressive (AR) image genera-
043   tion typically treat images as discrete tokens, mimicking the process of text modeling via a dictio-
044   nary (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a; Anil et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023b). This kind
045   of “next-token prediction” exhibit a promising path towards unify the representing and generation
046   of vision and language.
047
      Although text-to-image AR models have been extensively trained to handle interactions between
048   visual and language tokens, they still encounter difficulties when tasked with generating outputs
049   conditioned on visual inputs. This is particularly evident in scenarios where the alignment between
050   the visual features and the corresponding linguistic descriptions is critical, such as in the customized
051   generation task Ruiz et al. (2023); Gal et al. (2022). Though Li et al. (2024c) has tried to intro-
052   duce pixel-level control in the “next-scale” based model, it ignores the inherent capability of unified
053   visual-language representation as well as interaction, and cannot preserve the main concepts in con-
      ditional image. Moreover, it can not be generally applied to the vanilla AR model.

      In AR image generation, utilizing Vector Quantization models (Esser et al., 2021; Van Den Oord
      et al., 2017), images are projected into discrete tokens. These image tokens together with textual
071
      tokens then are further aligned in the autoregressive pre-training mode. This can serve as an excellent
072
      prior when dealing with vision-injecting problems. However, simply injecting the ground truth
073   object tokens as the condition is not feasible, as shown in Figure 1. To inject this object-related
074   vision information into the AR model, we propose a model termed InjectAR. Firstly, we utilize the
075   BLIP to retrieve textual prompts, and the textual descriptions of the main subject and the background
076   in the image are intentionally separated. Since the pre-trained model already learns to unify visual
077   and textual embedding space, we straightly utilize this advantage and extract the needed feature
078   embeddings in the image through a mask and image compacting module. Moreover, a learnable K,
079   V mapper is attached to each attention layer, targeting at joining the visual embeddings with image
080   generation. Furthermore, we propose a hierarchy attention component which restricts the effective
081
      region of the image condition and prevents either modality from dominating the output. Dropout
      and classifier-free guidance are also used in the finetuning process.
082
083   Experimental results show that our model has better prompt-fidelity as well as maintaining more
084   object details compared to other customized models. Besides, our model has the potential for con-
085   trolling the position and size of target objects. These promising results might give more inspiration
086   and assist in building more unified autoregressive models.
087   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
088
089          • We are the first to introduce the discrete-type vision condition into image customized AR
090            generation and receive superior performance.
091          • We design an effective vision-condition introducing framework in AR model, which is
092            composed of text and image conditions retrieving, hierarchy attention component and the
093            classifier-free guidance from both conditions.
094          • Extensive experiments are conducted, which show that our InjectAR can faithfully recover
095            the target concept and the prompt-fidelity is fairly high. The ability of controlling object
096            position is discussed.

098   2     R ELATED WORK
099
100   In this section, we give a review on recent impressive advancements in image generation, which can
101   be divided into two categories: diffusion models and autoregressive models. Then we summarize
102   works related to image personalization.
103
104   2.1   D IFFUSION - BASED IMAGE GENERATION
105
106   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020b) regard the gen-
107   eration of an image as a process of gradually denoising from pure noise and are equipped with the
      ability by training to predict the noise applied to noisy images. Song et al. (2020a; 2023) explore


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      how to minimize the sampling steps. Rombach et al. (2022) models the denoising process on the
109   latent space instead of pixel space, which compresses the image and brings strong prior. To achieve
110   high-quality image generation and improve semantic understanding capability, large-scale diffusion
111   models are trained with billions of image-text pairs (Saharia et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022;
112   Nichol et al., 2021). Besides, there are also some other downstream models to provide additional
113   control on the image generation (Zhang et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2022; Ruiz et al.,
114   2023; Ye et al., 2023).
115
116
      2.2   AUTOREGRESSIVE IMAGE GENERATION
117
118   The unprecedented development and incredible capability of large language mod-
119   els (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a; Anil et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
120   2023b; Team et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023) exhibit a promising “next-token-prediction” path towards
121   artificial general intelligence (AGI). To unify understanding and generation of vision and text into
122   the same paradigm, many efforts (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Ramesh et al., 2021; Esser et al., 2021;
123   Yu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b) have been made in the field of autoregressive image generation.
124   In contrast with the continuous image representation in diffusion models, autoregressive image
125   generation typically treats images as discrete tokens, mimicking the process of text modeling via a
126
      dictionary.
127   Recently, Tian et al. (2024) treats image generation as a hierarchical multi-scale process. Li et al.
128   (2024b) uses a masked autoregressive method and models the per-token probability distribution via a
129   diffusion procedure. Differently, in order to exactly unify text and image modeling, LlamaGen (Sun
130   et al., 2024) adopts the same architecture as LLM and verifies the scalability in autoregressive image
131   generation. Our work is based on LlamaGen to verify the benefits of introducing discrete image
132
      conditions in image personalization, which eliminates the impact of different architecture design.
133
134   2.3   I MAGE PERSONALIZATION
135
136   Aside from generating images based on textual descriptions (Ramesh et al., 2021; 2022; Saharia
137   et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022), it is often expected to customize the appearance
138   within the images. However, such personalization needs are typically difficult to fully describe using
139   language. On this basis, many diffusion-based personalization approaches (Ruiz et al., 2023; Wei
140   et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2022; Kumari et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023) have been proposed to extract
141
      concepts from a few images and apply them to new scenarios. These methods can be divided into
      two parts: test-time optimization and training-based models. For test-time optimization (Ruiz et al.,
142
      2023; Gal et al., 2022; Kumari et al., 2023), the models need to be optimized each time when
143
      encountering a new concept, with the duration ranging from a few minutes to approximately one
144   hour. For example, DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) is designed to assign the concept to a unique
145   identifier and finetune the overall model, thus projecting image characteristics into language space.
146   Differently, Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2022) aims to learn a new pseudo-word (i.e., S*) bonded
147   to the target concept. Nevertheless, the optimization procedure can be time-consuming. In contrast,
148   some recent studies (Wei et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a) are conducted to design a
149   learnable visual encoder, which learns how to extract visual characteristics and encode these into
150   language embedding space during training. Thus, only one forward process is needed when faced
151   with a new image set.
152   However, these models are all based on diffusion models, which inherit the constraints of diffusion,
153   i.e., enduring high inference latency and distinct paradigms with LLMs. Besides, these training-
154   based models all require the incorporation of other pre-trained image processing model, for instance,
155   CLIP image encoder, to map the image into continuous features and then further project into textual
156   space. Consequently, the preservation of image characteristics are limited by the capabilities of
157   the image encoder. Moreover, the continuous image features are inconsistent with the paradigm of
158
      language modeling, thereby impose difficulties in unifying understanding and generation between
      vision and language.
159
160   In contrast, our method utilize the natural discrete image tokens which unify the representing of
161   image and text, thus being able to preserve the original image features. Furthermore, since the
      discrete visual and textual tokens are aligned in the autoregressive pre-training process, based on

179   3     M ETHODOLOGY
180
181   We focus on designing a customized image generation method utilizing discrete visual conditions
182   based autoregressive models. In this section, we describe the proposed InjectAR in detail. The
183   main architecture of the model is shown in Figure 3. The proposed InjectAR consist of three parts:
184   retrieving text and image conditions (designed prompts to split the main object and background &
185   image feature compacting module) (Sec. 3.1), the trainable hierarchy attention component which
186   embed the discrete vision condition into autoregressive image generation (Sec. 3.2). Besides, ran-
187
      dom dropout and regularization loss are utilized in this finetuning process (Sec. 3.3).
188
189
      3.1   R ETRIEVING TEXT AND IMAGE CONDITIONS
190
      Our goal is to adopt discrete visual tokens into text-to-image generation. Therefore, the first task
191   is to retrieve textual and vision conditions. However, extracting textual descriptions for customized
192   text-to-image generation is not trivial. To ensure the editability and the hierarchy control between
193   text and image conditions, we intentionally separate the textual descriptions of the main subject
194   and the background in the images, such as “A dog” and “is standing on the ground”, as shown in
195   Figure 2. This is done via BLIP (Li et al., 2022) and it helps the model distinguish the main object,
196   especially when there exist several entities. We obtain the textual features through:
197
                                                               t = MLP ◦ LM(c),                                                       (1)
198
199   where t ∈ RL×d , L is the number of textual tokens, and d is the dimension of textual embed-
200   ding. LM is the language model used for encoding textual conditions and MLP is the projection for
201   mapping textual conditions to visual generating space.
202   For the image conditions, different from previous diffusion-based customized models (Wei et al.,
203   2023; Ye et al., 2023), we no longer rely on introducing CLIP image encoder to extract the image
204   features. This eliminates the performance restrictions brought about by the CLIP model. In contrast,
205   we leverage the output of the Vector-Quantized model (VQ) (Sun et al., 2024) to generate discrete
206   visual tokens directly and use object masks to ease the impact of the redundant background. More-
207   over, in order to capture the most crucial information, we design an image compacting module, “IC”,
208
      which consists of a 3-layer MLP to get the image conditions e, and we adopt the bottleneck structure
      for it. The inner dimension is set to 384 in training. The process of retrieving image conditions is

      shown as follows:

238   3.2     H IERARCHY ATTENTION COMPONENT
239
      Usually, the visual autoregressive models utilize causal attention, which means that all the condi-
240
      tions as well as the tokens already generated will be attended to when generating the current token.
241
      Nevertheless, autoregressive models can become confused about how to leverage these provided
242   image conditions effectively. Namely, during generating process, the given image conditions may
243   exert a stronger impact in the generation of the main context. While when generating background-
244   related tokens, the given image conditions can be distracting since these tokens to be generated are
245   more closely tied to the textual descriptions regarding the background. In order to achieve better and
246   effective guidance, we employ hierarchy attention component when introducing image conditions,
247   as shown in Figure 3.
248   In the training process, we concat image conditions e, text conditions t and the image tokens gen-
249   erated from the VQ model as the input. In the hierarchy attention component, we design to split the
250   original causal attention module into two parts: one interacts with textual conditions and the other
251   with image conditions.
252

351   4.1.3          E VALUATION METRICS
352
353   For quantitative evaluation, we adopt three metrics: CLIP-T, CLIP-I, and DINO-I as in Ruiz et al.
354   (2023). The editing prompts set from Ruiz et al. (2023) are adopted, which contains 25 editing
355
      prompts for non-live objects and live objects separately. We randomly generated 5 images for each
      object-prompt pair, ultimately producing 2500 images in total. CLIP-T is defined as the cosine sim-
356
      ilarity of CLIP embeddings between text prompts and the generated images, which conveys prompt
357
      fidelity. While for CLIP-I, we calculate the average cosine similarity of CLIP visual embeddings
358   between the generated and concept images, which indicates the subject fidelity. DINO-I is the aver-
359   age cosine similarity between the ViT-S/16 DINO (Caron et al., 2021) embeddings of generated and
360   real images, and it concentrates more on structural details. More details about the inference set and
361   editing prompts can be found in the Suppl.

      4.4      E MPIRICAL STUDY
400
401   4.4.1       E FFECT OF HIERARCHY ATTENTION COMPONENT
402
403   We conducted an ablation study on the effect of the hierarchy attention component. As shown in
404   Figure 5, without this hierarchy attention component, the generated images are more prone to exhibit
405
      overlapping objects, which results from the mutual interference between the generation processes
      guided by visual and textual inputs. Our design successfully alleviates the incidence of this issue.
406
407
408
      4.4.2       I MPACT OF HIERARCHY ATTENTION SCALAR
409   As shown in Figure 6, when hierarchy attention scalar λ = 0, the generated image is barely in-
410   fluenced by the image condition in inference. With the increase of λ, the impact from the image
411   condition increases. When utilizing λ = 1, we arrive at a fairly satisfactory result. However, a much
412   larger λ can introduce unreasonable image information, which may destroy the whole image.
413
414   4.4.3       I MPACT OF VISUAL SCALE
415
416   Visual scale se can be adjusted in the inference stage, which controls the image generation direction
417
      between textual and visual conditions. We set st = 7.5 by default. From Table 2, we can see
      that with the increase of se , the text-fidelity decreases, while the image-fidelity metrics CLIP-I and
418
      DINO-I increase at first. When the visual scale se is too large, this damages the meaning and quality
419
      of the whole image, thus the image-fidelity metrics decrease.

      et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023). Moreover, the generated objects are also well-

476   6    C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this paper, a new and effective vision-condition introducing framework in AR model is proposed.
479
      In contrast with the continuous image features, our method utilizes the natural discrete image to-
480   kens which unify the representing of image and text, thus being able to preserve the original image
481   features. Our model consists of text and image conditions retrieving, hierarchy attention component
482   and the design of classifier-free guidance from both conditions. During training, only the weights of
483   the image compacting module and the trainable linear projection of image conditions are optimized.
484   Experiments show that our model achieves the best prompt-alignment performance, which demon-
485   strates its remarkable editability. The qualitative and quantitative comparisons with other models
      show its superior capability to retain the details and generate high-quality images.
