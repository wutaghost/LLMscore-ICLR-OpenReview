011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent theoretical works based on the neural tangent kernel (NTK) have shed
014            light on the optimization and generalization of over-parameterized neural networks,
015            and partially bridge the gap between their practical success and classical learning
016
               theory. However, the existing NTK-based analysis has a limitation that the scaling
               of the initial parameter should decrease with respect to the sample size which is
017
               contradictory to the practical initialization scheme. To address this issue, in this
018
               paper, we present the revised NTK analysis of optimization and generalization
019            of overparametrized neural networks, which successfully remove the dependency
020            on the sample size of the initialization. Based on our revised analysis, we further
021            extend our theory that allow for arbitrary initialization, not limited to Gaussian
022            initialization. Under our initialization-independent analysis, we propose NTK-
023            based regularizer that can improve the model generalization, thereby illustrating
024            the potential to bridge the theory and practice while also supporting our theory. Our
025            numerical simulations demonstrate that the revised theory indeed can achieve the
026            significantly lower generalization error bound compared to existing error bound.
027
               Also importantly, the proposed regularizer also corroborate our theory on the
               arbitrary initialization with fine-tuning scenario, which takes the first step for NTK
028
               theory to be promisingly applied to real-world applications.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Though neural networks (NNs) have achieved great success in practice, it remains a well-known
034   mystery that over-parameterized NNs generalize well and do not suffer from overfitting even with a
035   simple first-order optimization (Neyshabur et al., 2014; Livni et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2016; Arora
036   et al., 2018), seemingly contradicting the traditional learning theory. To theoretically explain this
037   phenomenon, extensive research has been conducted, and one of the main directions is based on the
038   neural tangent kernel (NTK). Given a NN fθ (·) parametrized by θ and n training inputs {xi }ni=1 , the
039   NTK is defined as a Gram matrix H ∈ Rn×n induced by the structure of target prediction function
040
      whose (i, j)-th entry is given by

041                                                ∂fθ (xi ) ∂fθ (xj )
042                                     Hij :=              ,            .                               (1)
                                                     ∂θ         ∂θ
043
044   The NTK was introduced in Jacot et al. (2018) to control the dynamics of learning NNs. In the
045   over-parameterized regime, the trained parameter of NN is close to its initialization, which also
046   makes the NTK almost unchanged throughout the training process. This stability of NTK allows
047   the learning dynamics of NN to be easily analyzed throughout the training process, thus making it
048   possible to derive training and generalization error bounds by using existing learning theory.
049   As a representative study using NTK, Arora et al. (2019a;b) showed the following important results
050   for over-parameterized NNs:
051
052   (a) (Training error bound) A training error bound reflecting a tighter characterization of training
053       speed than that of studies was proposed in Arora et al. (2019a). This bound implies that not only
          is a network able to represent any finite sample perfectly (as shown in Zhang et al. (2016)), but

054
          the speed at which a network learns training samples varies depending on a complexity measure
055       reflecting how well the data is ordered.
056
      (b) (Generalization error bound) A generalization error bound, referred to as complexity measure of
057
          data (CMD) was proposed in Arora et al. (2019a). CMD has no stringent conditions on certain
058       properties of the trained NN and the true model; it only depends on input x and label y of training
059       data and the initial parameter scale κ of NN, hence we can compute the bound before actually
060       training the network. CMD is considered as one of the most important achievements in the topic
061       of generalizability of over-parameterized NNs and has been the cornerstone of many follow-up
062       studies in recent years (Arora et al., 2019b; Su & Yang, 2019; Oymak et al., 2019; Xu et al.,
063       2019; Zhang et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2019; Du et al., 2018).
064
      The above results (a)∼(b) derived in Arora et al. (2019a;b) provide the upper bounds on the train-
065
      ing/generalization error that are uniformly available over all network scaling (e.g., initialization)
066
      parameter κ. Note that Arora et al. (2019a;b) focus only on the case where
067
                                           κ = o(1) with respect to n                                   (2)
068
069
      in order to have meaningful bounds that can converge to zero as n increases.
070   Surprisingly, however, we prove in this paper that, contrary to the theories of Arora et al. (2019a;b),
071   the training/generalization errors in (a)-(b) do not hold when κ decreases with respect to n as in
072   Eq. 2. The high-level reason for this is as follows. As trained parameter is known to be close to
073   its initial one in the over-parameterized regime (Jacot et al., 2018; Arora et al., 2019a;b; Du et al.,
074   2018; 2019; Ji & Telgarsky, 2019; Cao & Gu, 2019; Ma et al., 2019; Li & Liang, 2018; Hu et al.,
075
      2019; Oymak et al., 2019), the output value of trained NN |fθ (x)| is close to that of its initial NN
      |fθ(0) (x)|. Meanwhile, the output value of its initial NN deceases to zero as n increases if the scale κ
076
      of initialization decreases with respect to n. Thus, it cannot guarantee zero training error under the
077
      condition Eq. 2, as the output value of trained NN decreases to zero but the target label does not.
078
079   We further resolve the above issue and revise the analyses of Arora et al. (2019a;b) without major
080   modifications of the original statements. Hence, our revision makes it possible for results (a)–(b) to
081
      maintain their original meanings and implications without any issue on decreasing κ. Our revised
      analyses provide tighter results on training/generalization error bounds, and with these improvements,
082
      we can guarantee the bounds to converge to zero even when κ is a constant w.r.t. n.
083
084   Building on the proof technique in the revised theory, we further extend our analysis based on the
085   Gram matrix and network initial parameters drawn from a Gaussian distribution to allow for arbitrary
086   initialization. Grounded on our initialization-independent analysis, we propose the NTK regularizer
087
      that can boost the model generalization, which is unavailable in previous studies. Note that our
      initialization-independent analysis enables NTK theory to be applied to pre-trained networks, which
088
      is expected to provide the connections to various practical scenarios such as fine-tuning. Toward this,
089
      we empirically verify that our revised theory indeed achieves lower generalization error bounds than
090   the baseline theory and present the potential of the proposed NTK regularizer in fine-tuning scenario.

      5      C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this study, we revised the existing NTK-based theory of optimization and generalization for
531
      overparametrized neural networks. Our revised analysis successfully remove the unreasonable
      assumption on the initialization and provide tighter bound for generalization error. Going further, we
532
      extended our revised analysis that allow for arbitrary initialization and multi-dimensional outputs. By
533
      extending NTK theory to a network with arbitrary initialization, we were able to propose the concept
534   of NTK regularzer, which was previously unattainable, and validate its effectiveness. The most
535   promising aspect of this study is that it enables the application of NTK theory to pre-trained networks.
536   This extension of NTK theory is expected to be applicable to various practical scenarios that require
537   predicting the performance of pre-trained networks, such as fine-tuning, domain adaptation, out-of-
538   distribution detection, and more. We empirically validated that our revised analysis indeed achieve
539   significantly lower generalization error bound and also showed our NTK regularizer to be effective in
      fine-tuning, demonstrating that NTK theory provides a connection to real-world applications.
