009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Under Display Camera (UDC) is an advanced imaging system that places a dig-
012            ital camera lens underneath a display panel, effectively concealing the camera.
013            However, the display panel significantly degrades captured images or videos, in-
014            troducing low transmittance, blur, noise, and flare issues. Tackling such issues
015            is challenging because of the complex degradation of UDCs, including diverse
016
               flare patterns. Despite extensive research on UDC images and their restoration
               models, studies on videos have yet to be significantly explored. While two UDC
017
               video datasets exist, they primarily focus on unrealistic or synthetic UDC degra-
018
               dation rather than real-world UDC degradation. In this paper, we propose a real-
019            world UDC video dataset called UDC-VIX. Unlike existing datasets, only UDC-
020            VIX exclusively includes human motions that target facial recognition. We pro-
021            pose a video-capturing system to simultaneously acquire non-degraded and UDC-
022            degraded videos of the same scene. Then, we align a pair of captured videos
023            frame by frame, using discrete Fourier transform (DFT). We compare UDC-VIX
024            with seven representative UDC still image datasets and two existing UDC video
025            datasets. Using six deep-learning models, we compare UDC-VIX and an existing
026            synthetic UDC video dataset. The results indicate the ineffectiveness of models
027
               trained on earlier synthetic UDC video datasets, as they do not reflect the actual
               characteristics of UDC-degraded videos. We also demonstrate the importance
028
               of effective UDC restoration by evaluating face recognition accuracy concerning
029
               PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS scores. UDC-VIX enables further exploration in the
030            UDC video restoration and offers better insights into the challenge. UDC-VIX is
031            available at our project site.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   An under-display camera (UDC) is an imaging system where the camera is positioned beneath
037   the display (Hinton et al., 2006). Modern smartphones, including the Samsung Galaxy Z-Fold se-
038
      ries (Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd., 2021; 2022; 2023) and the ZTE Axon series (ZTE Corporation,
      2020; 2021; 2022) have adopted UDCs. The UDC area, depicted in Figure 1, serves as display space
039
      under normal circumstances and acts as the light’s passage to the camera when capturing pictures or
040
      videos. This design allows for a larger screen-to-body ratio, meeting the common consumer demand
041   for a full-screen display without a camera hole or notch. However, UDC introduces severe and com-
042   plex image degradations such as reduced transmittance, noise, blur, and flare in a single image or
043   video frame. Moreover, motion is also involved in UDC videos.
044
      The degradation in UDC arises from the diffraction of incoming light by the display pixels at a
045
      micrometer scale (Qin et al., 2016). Modern UDC smartphones have lower pixel density in the
046
      UDC area to minimize this diffraction, as described in Figure 1(c). Since a lower pixel density
047   prevents natural video viewing, improving the video quality captured by the UDC is essential.
048
049   Many studies have investigated UDC image datasets. These include synthetic datasets like T-
050
      OLED/P-OLED (Zhou et al., 2021) and SYNTH (Feng et al., 2021). Additionally, there exists a
      pseudo-real UDC dataset (Feng et al., 2023) and a real-world UDC dataset such as UDC-SIT (Ahn
051
      et al., 2024).
052
053   Ahn et al. (2024) demonstrate the importance of training DNN models using a real-world UDC
      dataset because the synthetic UDC datasets do not reflect the actual characteristics of UDC-degraded

065   Although several studies address the synthetic UDC video datasets (Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
066   2024), they have some limitations because they do not completely reflect the properties of actual
067   UDC videos. There are two main challenges in constructing a real-world UDC video dataset. One is

069   The other is to synchronize the time for all frames when capturing videos.
070   This paper proposes a new UDC video dataset called UDC-VIX (UDC’s VIdeo by X, where X
071   represents the anonymous creator). As far as we know, it is the first real-world UDC video dataset
072   to overcome the problems of the existing UDC video datasets.
073
      Using a non-polarizing cube beam splitter (Thorlabs, 2015), we create a video-capturing system
074   to minimize discrepancies between paired frames. We cut the UDC area of a smartphone display
075   (e.g., Samsung Galaxy Z-Fold 5 (Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd., 2023)) and attach it to the beam
076   splitter. Two Arducam Hawk-Eye (IMX686) camera modules (Arducam, 2022) are placed on both
077   sides of the beam splitter. These modules, operated by a Raspberry Pi 5 (Arducam, 2023), capture
078   synchronized video frame pairs using the Message Passing Interface (MPI) barrier.
079
      Figure 2 shows our UDC video capturing system. Despite the meticulous design, inevitable pixel-
080   position difference occurs. We correct this difference between the two matched frames for the same
081   scene by using the DFT (Brigham, 1988) following the previous work by Ahn et al. (2024).

100
      Existing UDC image datasets. There has been extensive research on UDC still image datasets.
101
      Zhou et al. (2021) propose the T-OLED/P-OLED datasets. Images are displayed on a monitor, and
102   paired images are captured with and without a T-OLED/P-OLED display in front of the camera.
103   However, due to the limited dynamic range of the monitor, flares are almost absent in their datasets.
104   Feng et al. (2021) propose the SYNTH dataset. They convolve the measured point spread function
105   (PSF) of ZTE Axon 20 (ZTE Corporation, 2020) with clean images (Haven, 2020), exhibiting flares.
106   However, it has limitations such as the absence of noise and spatially variant flares. Notably, UDC
107   distortion gradually increases from the center of the camera lens to outwards, leading to spatially
      distorted flares (Yoo et al., 2022). Feng et al. (2023) propose a pseudo-real dataset by capturing

108
      paired images of similar scenes using two cameras (e.g., ZTE Axon 20 UDC (ZTE Corporation,
109   2020) and iPhone 13 Pro camera (Apple Inc., 2021)). However, they use two cameras, leading to
110   geometric misalignment. They improve the geometric misalignment using AlignFormer (Feng et al.,
111   2023). Nonetheless, they encounter challenges with alignment accuracy. Ahn et al. (2024) propose
112   a real-world dataset called UDC-SIT and an image-capturing system. They attach Samsung Galaxy
113   Z-Fold 3 (Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd., 2021)’s UDC area to a lid. Paired images are acquired
114   by opening and closing the lid onto the Samsung Galaxy Note 10’s standard camera (Samsung
115   Electronics Co., Ltd., 2019). They use DFT to align the misalignment between the paired images
116   that occurs during the opening and closing of the lid. The images in the UDC-SIT dataset contain
117
      the actual UDC degradation (e.g., spatially variant flares). Finally, Wang et al. (2024) and Tan et al.
      (2023) propose still image datasets for face recognition. However, these datasets are synthesized
118
      using a GAN-based model trained on the T/P-OLED dataset (Zhou et al., 2021), which lacks realistic
119
      UDC degradation, particularly flares. Moreover, the datasets are not publicly available.
120
121   Existing UDC video datasets. Research has been conducted on synthetic UDC video datasets.
122   Chen et al. (2023) propose the PexelsUDC-T/P dataset. They train a GAN-based UDC video gen-
123   eration model using T-OLED/P-OLED datasets (Zhou et al., 2021), which do not show flares. They
124   generate UDC-degraded videos using clean videos (Pexels, 2014). Moreover, the datasets are not
125   publicly available. Liu et al. (2024) propose the VidUDC33K dataset. They convolve the measured
126   PSF on the clean video frames (Haven, 2020) to show flares. They simulate the dynamic change of
127   the PSF (Kwon et al., 2021) between consecutive frames following the previous work (Babbar &
128   Bajaj, 2022; Liu et al., 2022a; Ye et al., 2021). However, flares in their dataset are unrealistic.
129
130
      UDC image restoration. There has been active research on UDC image restoration. DISC-
      Net (Feng et al., 2021) incorporates the domain knowledge of the UDC image formation model.
131
      UDC-UNet, a second performer of MIPI challenge (Feng et al., 2022), introduces kernel branches
132
      to incorporate prior knowledge and condition branches for spatially variant manipulation.
133
134
      Video restoration. Many studies have focused on video restoration models for general tasks, such
135   as denoising (Tassano et al., 2020), deblurring (Wang et al., 2019; Zhong et al., 2020), and super-
136   resolution (Wang et al., 2019). Unlike image restoration, which only focuses on a spatial dimen-
137   sion, video restoration leverages temporal information. FastDVDNet (Tassano et al., 2020) uses a
138   two-step denoising process in a multi-scale architecture to leverage temporal information without
139   explicit motion estimation. EDVR (Wang et al., 2019) aligns features using deformable convolu-
140   tions (Dai et al., 2017) and applies both temporal and spatial attention to highlight essential features.
141   ESTRNN (Zhong et al., 2020) integrates residual dense blocks into RNN cells for spatial feature
142   extraction and employs a spatiotemporal attention module for feature fusion. However, studies on
143
      UDC video restoration are still rare. DDRNet (Liu et al., 2024), the pioneering work to address
      UDC video degradation, adopts a recurrent architecture that merges multi-scale feature learning and
144
      bi-directional propagation.

158   videos for the same scene, we propose a UDC video-capturing system that ensures precise camera
159   synchronization and accurate frame alignment.

196   Kinematic optical mount. Despite many studies using beam splitters for paired image dataset
197   collection (Hwang et al., 2015; Joze et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023; Rim et al., 2020), this paper is the
198   first to apply them in UDC research to the best of our knowledge. It presents challenges to align the
199   optical paths of the display panel’s UDC area, a beam splitter, and two camera modules. To ensure
200   alignment between the cameras’ optical axes and the beam splitter, we employ Thorlabs K6XS 6-
201   axis kinematic optical mounts (Thorlabs, 2013). Each camera module is mounted on a K6XS mount,
202   allowing for shifts, rotations, and tilts across the six axes to align their FOV.
203
204   The controller. We use a Raspberry Pi 5 (Arducam, 2023) that has two four-lane MIPI interface
205
      connections for high bandwidth to synchronize the two high-resolution cameras. It ensures stable
      high-resolution video recording. To synchronize the cameras, we use independent streamers man-
206
      aged by MPI barriers (Message Passing Interface Forum, 2023), achieving synchronization with an
207
      accuracy margin of up to 8 msec. Consistent frame rates for both cameras are ensured using the
208   uncompressed binary dump method (e.g., YUV420 format). Despite these settings resulting in less
209   than a 0.5 fps (8 msec) difference between paired frames, rapid movements may still cause the cam-
210   eras to capture different scenes. Thus, videos capturing fast-moving objects (e.g., speeding cars) are
211   excluded from the dataset.

      DFT alignment. Despite the careful design of our video-capturing system, unavoidable misalign-
232
      ments, such as shifts, rotations, and tilts, still occur between paired frames. Previous methods, such
233   as SIFT (Lowe, 2004), RANSAC (Fischler & Bolles, 1981), and deep learning approaches (Feng
234   et al., 2023), struggle to perform well in the existence of severe degradation introduced by the UDC.
235   Thus, we use DFT to align the paired frames, following Ahn et al. (2024)’s alignment technique to
236   achieve degradation-resilient alignment.
237
      The alignment process is summarized as shift, rotate, and crop paired frames using DFT. Captured
238
      videos have an original frame size of (1920, 1080, 3). The ground-truth frame is center-cropped
239   to (1900, 1060, 3), and the degraded frame undergoes a cropping around the center. To align the
240   cropped degraded frame D with the cropped ground-truth frame G, we iteratively shift the (x, y)
241   coordinates and rotate the frames to find the point of minimum loss. Our focus is on addressing
242   shifts and rotations while excluding tilts. Handling tilts is challenging because of the need for
243   perspective transforms optimized for objects in the same plane within a single image. Despite not
244   considering tilts, our video-capturing system minimizes all shifts, rotations, and tilts so that they do
245   not significantly affect alignment, as confirmed by our experiment (the PCK values in Table 2). The
246   loss function L for the alignment between D and G is defined as below:

288   noise in low-light conditions. In the UDC setting, where the sensor is beneath the display panel,
289   the transmittance decreases, leading to amplified noise. The camera sensors with QBC, used in
290   the Samsung Galaxy Z-Fold series (related to UDC-VIX) (Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd., 2021;

293   intensity scaling values in Equation 2 may not accurately depict real-world noise and transmittance

      reduction. For example, in the VidUDC33K dataset, the degraded frame’s noise level is somewhat

295

299   Flares. Conventional lens flares stem from intense light scattering or reflection within an optical
300   system (Dai et al., 2022; 2023). In contrast, UDC flares arise from light diffraction as it passes
301   through the display panel above the digital camera lens. Thus, it is crucial for each frame in the
302   UDC video dataset to precisely depict the its unique flare characteristics, including spatially variant
303   flares, light source variant flares, and temporally variant flares. The P-OLED dataset rarely exhibits
304   flares as it captures images displayed on a monitor in a controlled environment (Figure 5(a) and (d)).
305   Since UDC distortion increases outward from the camera lens center, spatially variant flares man-
306   ifest within an image (Yoo et al., 2022). Distorted PSFs must be convolved across different image
307   regions to depict this flare distortion accurately. However, VidUDC33K applies the same PSF con-
308   volution across all areas using Equation 2, failing to represent spatially variant flares, as illustrated in
309   Figure 5(b) and (e). Conversely, UDC-VIX effectively captures spatially variant flares (Figure 5(c)).

363   when capturing light sources. The motion results in changes in PSFs (Kwon et al., 2021). However,
364   in the VidUDC33K dataset, attempts to simulate PSF changes through inter-frame homography
365   matrix computations using the method proposed by the previous studies (Babbar & Bajaj, 2022;
366   Liu et al., 2022a; Ye et al., 2021) yield rare temporally variant flares, as shown in Figure 6(a).
367   Moreover, the shape of typical lens flares in ground-truth frames remains unchanged in degraded
368   frames, indicating the failure of PSF convolution to replicate natural sunlight flares. Conversely,
369
      UDC-VIX effectively captures temporally varying flares (Figure 6(b)).

411   Alignment quality. To assess the alignment quality of paired videos, we use LoFTR (Sun et al.,
412   2021) as a keypoint matcher, following the convention of the previous studies (Ahn et al., 2024;
413   Feng et al., 2023). We compare the Percentage of Correct Keypoints (PCK), representing the ratio
414   of correctly aligned keypoints to the total number. A keypoint pair is correctly aligned if d <
415   α × max(H, W ), where d is the positional difference between a pair of matched keypoints, α is the
416   threshold, and H and W are the frame or image dimensions. We set max(H, W ) = 1024 for fair
417   comparison across datasets with varying resolutions.
418   Table 2 compares alignment accuracy across datasets. The synthetic datasets (e.g., T-OLED/P-
419   OLED, SYNTH, and VidUDC33K) do not require an additional alignment process, leading to PCK
420   values near 100%. In contrast, the Pseudo-real dataset using AlignFormer (Feng et al., 2023), attains
421   a PCK value of 58.75% for α = 0.01. Unlike Pseudo-real, UDC-VIX maintains PCK values near
422   100%, demonstrating performance comparable to UDC-SIT, which previously led benchmarks.

515   6     L IMITATIONS
516
517   UDC-VIX has two limitations. One is that UDC degradations vary with display pixel design, affect-
518   ing diffraction patterns, PSF, and light propagation, leading to variation in degradation such as blur,
519   transmittance decrease, and especially flares (see Figure 5(b) and (c)). Models trained on UDC-VIX
520   may not work optimally on devices other than Samsung Galaxy Z-Fold 5 (Samsung Electronics Co.,
521   Ltd., 2023), such as the ZTE Axon series (ZTE Corporation, 2020; 2021; 2022) or other Samsung
522   Galaxy Z-Fold series (Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd., 2021; 2022). However, models trained on
523
      UDC-VIX can be fine-tuned for other devices. Please see Appendix B.4 for details. The other is
      that fast-moving objects like speeding cars are excluded from UDC-VIX. Despite the efforts to
524
      synchronize the two cameras to ensure a synchronization difference of less than 8 msec between
525
      paired frames (Section 3), rapid movements can still result in scene difference by the cameras.
526
527
528   7     C ONCLUSION
529
530   As far as we know, UDC-VIX is the first UDC video dataset that includes actual UDC degradation,
531
      such as low transmittance, blur, noise, and flare. We propose an efficient video-capturing system
      to acquire a matched pair of UDC-degraded and ground-truth videos with precise synchronization
532
      of two cameras. Furthermore, we align UDC-VIX frame by frame using DFT, showing the highest
533
      alignment accuracy, enough to train deep learning models. From the comparison experiments, we
534   demonstrate the effectiveness of UDC-VIX. Notably, UDC-VIX solely presents significant actual
535   UDC degradation (e.g., variant flares) and stands out from other datasets by featuring videos tailored
536   for face recognition. Through the thorough experiments, we figure out the models trained with the
537   synthetic UDC video dataset are impractical because they fail to capture UDC-degraded videos’
538   actual characteristics accurately. Moreover, restoring UDC degradation is significant in enhancing
539   face recognition accuracy. Based on the insights above, we expect that UDC-VIX will significantly
      contribute to the UDC video restoration studies.

       B.5   R EPRODUCIBILITY
1446
1447   This section provides detailed information on the deep-learning models used to compare the UDC-
1448   VIX dataset in the paper for reproducibility. The code can be found and downloaded at our project
1449   site.
1450
       The learnable restoration models used for evaluating the UDC-VIX dataset include DISCNet (Feng
1451   et al., 2021), UDC-UNet (Liu et al., 2023), FastDVDNet (Tassano et al., 2020), EDVR (Wang et al.,
1452   2019), ESTRNN (Zhong et al., 2020), and DDRNet (Liu et al., 2024). We use a single-node GPU
1453   cluster to train each benchmark model. Each node has eight AMD Instinct MI100 GPUs. While we
1454   mainly stick to the original authors’ code and training settings for the models, we introduce some
1455   modifications except ESTRNN.
1456
1457          • DISCNet. DISCNet is designed to restore UDC still images in high dynamic range (HDR)
                (e.g., SYNTH (Feng et al., 2021)). Accordingly, we modify the PyTorch DataLoader to use


                                                              27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
                normalization instead of Reinhard tone mapping (Reinhard et al., 2002). The DataLoader
1459            randomly selects one frame per video from the UDC-VIX dataset for each iteration during
1460            the training and validation phases.
1461
1462          • UDC-UNet. UDC-UNet is also designed to restore UDC still images in HDR. The original
1463
                authors do not conduct normalization or tone mapping in the DataLoader and employ a tone
                mapping L1 loss function. However, since the UDC-VIX dataset has a low dynamic range
1464
                (LDR), we modify the PyTorch DataLoader to use normalization. We clamp the model
1465
                output between 0 and 1 and then calculate the L1 loss. The DataLoader randomly selects
1466            one frame per video from the UDC-VIX dataset for each iteration.
1467
1468          • FastDVDNet. FastDVDNet is a video denoising model that utilizes NVIDIA’s Data Load-
1469            ing Library (DALI) (Nvidia, 2018), processing a noise map and multiple frames as inputs.
1470
                Instead of DALI, we employ the PyTorch DataLoader tailored to the UDC-VIX dataset in
                npy format. We set the noise level to zero. To accommodate FHD resolution and multiple
1471
                degradations in the UDC-VIX dataset, we increase the patch size from 64 to 256. Fur-
1472
                thermore, we extend the training duration of FastDVDNet to 400 epochs, compared to the
1473            original 95, to ensure the model reaches full saturation.
1474
1475          • EDVR. To address out-of-memory issues with EDVR, which boasts 23.6 M parameters,
1476
                we reduce the patch size from 256 to 192. Additionally, during inference on the test set, we
                divide it into two patches of size 3 × 1, 060 × 1, 060 each and merge them afterward.
1477
1478          • DDRNet. During the inference process, the authors of DDRNet partition each frame into
1479            patches of size 3 × 256 × 256 and input 50 frames simultaneously. However, patch-wise
1480            inference introduces the borderline between patches. To address this, we conduct inference
1481            at full resolution (3 × 1, 060 × 1, 900) with ten frames at a time.
