009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The problem of packing smaller objects within a larger one has long been of inter-
012            est. In this work, we employ an encoder-decoder architecture, parameterized by
013            neural networks, for circle packing. Our solution consists of an encoder that takes
014            the index of a circle as input and outputs a point, which is then transformed by a
015            constraint block into a valid center within the outer shape. A perturbation block
016            perturbs this center while ensuring it remains within the corresponding radius,
017            and the decoder estimates the circle’s index based on the perturbed center. The
018
               functionality of the perturbation block is akin to adding noise to the latent space
               variables in variational autoencoders (VAEs); however, it differs significantly in
019
               both the method and purpose of perturbation injection, as we inject perturbation
020
               to push the centers of the circles sufficiently apart. Additionally, unlike typical
021            VAEs, our architecture incorporates a constraint block to ensure that the circles do
022            not breach the boundary of the outer shape. We design the constraint block to pack
023            both congruent and non-congruent circles within arbitrary shapes, implementing
024            a scheduled injection of perturbation from a beta distribution in the perturbation
025            block to gradually push the centers apart. We compare our approach to established
026            methods, including disciplined convex-concave programming (DCCP) and other
027            packing techniques, demonstrating competitive performance in terms of packing
028            density—the fraction of the outer object’s area covered by the circles. Our method
029
               outperforms the DCCP-based solution in the non-congruent case and approaches
               the best-known packing densities. To our knowledge, this is the first work to
030
               present solutions for packing circles within arbitrary shapes.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Packing problems are commonly encountered in various domains of study. These problems involve
036   packing smaller objects into a larger one to achieve objectives such as maximizing packing density.
037   The circle packing problem, a specific instance of packing problems, focuses on packing circles
038   within a larger shape. This non-convex problem arises in various applications Zhang et al. (2013),
039   such as packing circular objects in a box Castillo et al. (2008), and in fields including nanotech-
040   nology, telecommunications Cover & Thomas (2006), the oil and automobile industries Wang et al.
041   (2002), forestry Hifi & M’Hallah (2009), and social distancing Bortolete et al. (2022). These prob-
042   lems are straightforward to state but difficult to solve, even approximately. For instance, Kepler’s
043
      conjecture, which concerns sphere packing in three-dimensional Euclidean space, was proved only
      recently Hales et al. (2015).
044
045   In this paper, we focus on developing a method to find sub-optimal solutions to packing problems.
046   The work in Jose et al. (2024) focused on packing circles of equal radii, i.e., congruent circles, within
047   a large circle using an autoencoder architecture. In this work, we extend the method to pack circles
048   of varying radii, i.e., non-congruent circles, into arbitrary shapes. Let the outer shape be centered
049
      at the origin of a Cartesian coordinate system, with the distance to its boundary at angle θ from the
      horizontal axis denoted by b(θ), for θ ∈ [0, 2π]. Within this outer shape, we aim to pack N ≥ 1
050
      circles, indexed by i ∈ N = {1, 2, . . . , N }, with radii ri by finding the centers ci for all i ∈ N .
051
      This packing must satisfy two constraints: (1) no circle should extend outside the outer shape, and

072   Our contribution is the development of a solution utilizing an encoder-decoder approach, akin to Jose
073   et al. (2024), but with significant modifications. This approach aims to determine the coordinates
074   of the centers of smaller circles within an outer shape while minimizing overlap. Unlike Jose et al.
075   (2024), we implement several architectural modifications and extend our focus to packing non-
076   congruent circles within various outer shapes. The outer shapes considered include circles, regular
077
      polygons (such as squares and regular pentagons), and arbitrary shapes.
078   The circle packing problem, defined by the no-overlap condition, has been extensively studied using
079   various techniques, including non-linear programming, stochastic search, heuristics, and neural gas
080   methods Jose et al. (2024). Non-linear programming approaches, such as the stochastic item descent
081   method He et al. (2020) and the formulation by Graham et al. (1998), focus on maximizing pairwise
082   distances between points within a unit circle. Stochastic search methods, proposed by Akiyama
083
      et al. (2003), involve initially scattering points and adjusting their positions until they settle without
      overlapping. This method has been further refined in Graham et al. (1998) and applied to packing
084
      circles in squares, as seen in Boll et al. (2004) and Szabó et al. (2007). Heuristic algorithms, which
085
      can be classified into construction and optimization methods, have also been explored Huang &
086   Ye (2011); fu Zhang & sheng Deng (2005); Grosso et al. (2010); Zeng et al. (2016). Construction
087   algorithms place circles incrementally, while optimization algorithms refine an initial solution. Ad-
088   ditionally, the neural gas method Pospı́chal (2015) uses clustering of random points to determine
089   circle centers.

      2     P ROPOSED E NCODER -D ECODER A PPROACH
093
094
      The architecture for packing equal-sized circles into a larger circle in Jose et al. (2024) consists
      of an encoder, normalization layer, perturbation layer, and decoder, where encoder and decoder
095
      layers are parameterized by neural networks. We adopt a similar architecture for packing circles
096
      within arbitrary shapes but introduce the following modifications: the encoder neural network is
097   significantly reduced in size, and batch normalization layers are added, along with Tanh activations
098   to ensure that the output points lie within a unit square. The normalization layer is replaced by a new
099   constraint block consisting of learnable parameters, which provides greater freedom for the circle
100   center to move within the outer shape while satisfying the constraint equation 1b. The perturbation
101   layer now uses a beta distribution to introduce noise, where we initially perturb the center by small
102   amounts and gradually increase the perturbation as the packing process progresses. Additionally,
103   the number of perturbations is increased. Finally, the decoder is expanded, and batch normalization
104   is added.

184   Perturbation Block We perturb the center ci by adding a random vector w, ensuring that ∥ci −
185   w∥2 ≤ ri for all i ∈ N . To generate w, we first sample two independent scalars from the uniform
186   distribution over [0, 1], stack them as a vector, and normalize it to obtain the unit vector ui . This
187   provides a random direction. Next, we sample a scalar ρ from the Beta distribution Beta(α, β), and
188   scale it by ri . Since the Beta distribution’s support is [0, 1], ρri ensures the perturbation magnitude
189   lies within [0, ri ]. Finally, we set w = ui ρri , ensuring that the perturbation remains within the
190   desired radius. The output of the perturbation block is ni = ci + w.
191   We do not use fixed values for α and β; instead, we change them according to a schedule as the
192   packing process progresses. This approach ensures that initially, we sample points closer to the
193   center, allowing the circles to gradually push away. In later stages, when the circles are no longer
194   in close proximity, we aim to sample points farther from the center. The rationale is that sampling
195   points from regions where circles intersect increases the difficulty for the decoder, making accurate
196   predictions harder. To counter this, the encoder pushes the circles apart, thereby reducing the loss
197
      and improving packing efficiency. Additionally, sampling more points per circle can accelerate the
      model’s learning process. This scheduling is explained in a later section when detailing the training
198
      process.
199
200   The functionality of this block is akin to adding noise to the latent space variables in variational
201   autoencoders; hence, we refer to this work as a variational autoencoder-like approach. However,
202   it differs in both the method and purpose of perturbation injection, as we inject perturbation to
203
      sufficiently separate the centers of the circles. Additionally, unlike typical VAEs, our architecture
      incorporates a constraint block to ensure that the circles do not breach the boundary of the outer
204
      shape.

412   4     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
413
414   In this work, we employed an encoder-decoder architecture for packing circles within arbitrary
415   shapes. The architecture consisted of an encoder that took the index of a circle as input and output a
416   point, which was then transformed by a constraint block into a valid center within the outer shape. A
417   perturbation block perturbed this center while ensuring it remained within the corresponding radius,
418   and the decoder estimated the circle’s index based on the perturbed center. The constraint block was
419   designed to accommodate both congruent and non-congruent circles, implementing a scheduled
420   injection of perturbation from a beta distribution to gradually push the centers apart.
421   Our approach demonstrated competitive performance compared to established methods, including
422   DCCP and best-known configurations, in terms of packing density, which we computed by scatter-
423   ing points within the outer shape and counting the fraction that lay within the area covered by the
424   smaller circles. We illustrated that the method successfully packed circles within a unit circle, ac-
425   curately classifying perturbed centers close to the boundary. Visual results showed that the packing
426   configurations were similar to the best-known configurations for various shapes, achieving packing
427   densities that approached the best-known. Notably, the proposed method outperformed DCCP in
428
      packing non-congruent circles, achieving higher packing densities. To our knowledge, this was the
      first work to present solutions for packing circles within arbitrary shapes.
429
430   Future work will focus on extending the method to pack any inner shape, which makes enforcing
431   constraints difficult. This will require modifications to the constraint and perturbation blocks. Ad-
      ditionally, the perturbation scheduler can be further optimized, and enhancements can be made to
