                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               The effectiveness of current machine learning models relies on their ability to
               grasp diverse concepts present in datasets. However, biased and noisy data can
011
               inadvertently cause these models to be biased toward certain concepts, under-
012
               mining their ability to generalize and provide utility. Consequently, modifying
013            a trained model to forget these concepts becomes imperative for their responsi-
014            ble deployment. We refer to this problem as concept forgetting. Our goal is to
015            develop techniques for forgetting specific undesired concepts from a pre-trained
016            classification model’s prediction. To achieve this goal, we present an algorithm
017            called Label ANnealing (LAN). This iterative algorithm employs a two-stage
018            method for each iteration. In the first stage, pseudo-labels are assigned to the
019            samples by annealing or redistributing the original labels based on the current
020            iteration’s model predictions of all samples in the dataset. During the second
021
               stage, the model is fine-tuned on the dataset with pseudo-labels. We illustrate
               the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms across various models and datasets.
022
               Our method reduces concept violation, a metric that measures how much the
023
               model forgets specific concepts, by about 85.35% on the MNIST dataset, 73.25%
024            on the CIFAR-10 dataset, and 69.46% on the CelebA dataset while maintaining
025            high model accuracy. Our implementation can be found at this following link:
026            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LAN-141B/

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The superior performance capability of deep learning systems is primarily attributed to their ability
032   to learn various concepts inherent in the dataset. For instance, advancements in face recognition
033   systems (LeCun et al., 1998; Krizhevsky et al., 2009; He et al., 2016) can be largely attributed to their
034   ability to discern and characterize different semantic features from facial images, such as age, gender,
035   and facial hair characteristics, etc. Similarly, in tasks involving image and text generation (Ramesh
036   et al., 2021; 2022; Rombach et al., 2022), the ability to learn varied concepts present in the dataset,
037   enables the generation of realistic and diverse outputs. Consequently, the efficacy of these models
038   relies upon the acquisition of learned concepts inherent within the dataset. Nevertheless, when the
039
      dataset is tainted with noisy samples or biased concepts (Tommasi et al., 2017), these models are
      susceptible to learning such undesired biased concepts. For example, suppose we are learning a
040
      model to predict whether a person should get a bank loan or not. Such a model should not depend
041
      on the gender or race of the person. However, it is possible that the machine learning model might
042   inadvertently use these features to make predictions, which is highly undesirable. As a result, there
043   emerges a pressing necessity to forget or unbias the undesired biased concept from these trained
044   models to ensure their reliable and accountable deployment. Apart from removing biases, forgetting
045   concepts can prove beneficial in topics such as domain generalization. For example, envision a
046   CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) image classifier that heavily relies on background color as a distinguishing
047   feature to classify different celebrities, limiting its ability to generalize effectively. Therefore, in such
048   scenarios, rapidly forgetting only undesired concepts from a pre-trained model, without affecting
049   the ability of the model to use other features, can improve the model’s fair decision-making and
050
      generalization capabilities.
051   To make a pre-trained model forget a concept, we start by asking the following question - what
052   is meant by forgetting? Our definition of forgetting is motivated by the fact that in the case of
053   humans, if one forgets a concept, the forgotten concept doesn’t affect one’s decision-making. Thus
      concept forgetting within the context of machine learning entails ensuring that a model’s predictions

054
      become entirely independent of the targeted forgetting concept. However, achieving this task presents
055   challenges, as the goal is to forget a specific undesired concept without adversely affecting the ability
056   of the model to use other concepts. This challenge is underscored by the phenomenon of catastrophic
057   forgetting observed in the literature (McCloskey & Cohen., 1989; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kirkpatrick
058   et al., 2017; Ginart et al., 2019) in similar contexts, where adapting a model for new tasks (in our
059   case task of concept forgetting), can significantly degrade performance. Thus, to explore the extent of
060   forgetting concepts in pre-trained models, we pose the following challenge:
061
062   Can we efficiently modify a pre-trained model to forget (unbias) an undesired (biased) concept while
063
                                          maintaining its performance?

097   Our contributions: Our contribution can be summarized as follows:
098   • We introduce the framework of concept forgetting from pre-trained classification models. Motivated
099   by works in fairness (Dwork et al., 2012; Hardt et al., 2016; Lowy et al., 2021), we measure the bias
100   or the dependence of a model on a concept, based on concept violation, which empirically quantifies
101   the extent to which the model remains neutral towards a concept for its predictions.
102   • We propose an algorithm called Label ANnealing (LAN). LAN employs an iterative approach, where
103   each iteration redistributes the class labels of data points containing forgetting concepts to the most
104   probable class labels, thus creating pseudo-labels. This realignment ensures that the distribution of
105   pseudo-labels for each concept value matches the class distribution predicted by the current iteration’s
106   model. The method draws an analogy to the term annealing frequently employed in material science.
107   It denotes the controlled redistribution of atoms within a solid material under specific temperature
      conditions to attain an equilibrium state, which inspired our method’s nomenclature. This strategy

      2.1   FAIRNESS
124
125   Fairness in machine learning systems is an important research area aimed at ensuring that system
126   predictions are both accurate and fair across different groups (based on their features) of data points.
127   Earlier works (Dwork et al., 2012) initially proposed the notion of demographic parity as a preliminary
128   definition of fairness. According to this concept, a machine learning algorithm satisfies demographic
129   parity if the predicted target is independent of sensitive attributes. However, promoting demographic
130   parity may lead to diminished performance, particularly if the true outcome is not independent of
131   sensitive attributes. To address this, subsequent works (Hardt et al., 2016) introduced a relaxed notion
132
      of fairness based on equalized odds and equal opportunity definition. Recent works (Kamishima
      et al., 2011; Feldman et al., 2015; Zafar et al., 2017; Donini et al., 2018; Mary et al., 2019; Cho et al.,
133
      2020a; Jiang et al., 2020; Rezaei et al., 2020; Lowy et al., 2021) have explored incorporating different
134
      fairness notions during the training process itself. These methods incorporate regularization-based
135   techniques based on different statistical distances between the distribution of the model’s prediction
136   and sensitive attributes.
137
138
      Drawing inspiration from fairness notions, especially demographic parity (Dwork et al., 2012; Lowy
      et al., 2021), we propose that forgetting a particular concept can also be interpreted as achieving
139
      independence between the model’s prediction and the undesired feature we aim to forget. However,
140
      methods focusing on enforcing fairness with respect to the forgetting concept require a large number
141   of epochs to converge and can be computationally inefficient. For instance, according to state-of-the-
142   art FERMI algorithm (Lowy et al., 2021), achieving ||∇ℓ(θ, x, y)|| ≤ ϵ where ℓ is the loss function
143   requires approximately O( ϵ14 ) iterations. Empirically, the convergence of the FERMI algorithm
144   varies depending on the dataset and application, typically ranging from as few as 50 to as many
145   as 2000 epochs (Lowy et al., 2021, Appendix E). Given our specific objective of forgetting only
146   certain concepts from a model’s parameters without affecting others, we aim to devise a more
147   computationally efficient approach for concept forgetting from pre-trained models.
148
149   2.2   M ACHINE U NLEARNING FOR TRAINING EXAMPLE REMOVAL
150
151   Machine unlearning, as described in the literature (Xu et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2022; Cao & Yang,
152   2015; Ginart et al., 2019; Golatkar et al., 2020a;b; 2021; Neel et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2020; Guo
153
      et al., 2020; Graves et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021), involves intentionally erasing the impact of
      particular subsets of training data from a pre-trained model, addressing user privacy concerns. Here,
154
      the objective is to craft a computationally efficient method that produces an unlearned model that
155
      mirrors the behavior of the model that is trained from scratch, on the training dataset devoid of the
156   sensitive data points. Although retraining serves as the optimal benchmark method for unlearning, this
157   method becomes computationally impractical for large models and iterative unlearning demands (Cao
158   & Yang, 2015; Ginart et al., 2019; Sekhari et al., 2021). Consequently, to address user privacy
159   concerns, more efficient data deletion methods (Cao & Yang, 2015; Ginart et al., 2019) were devised,
160   leading to the emergence of machine unlearning. The machine unlearning methods are broadly
161   categorized into two types: exact unlearning (Wu et al., 2020) and approximate unlearning (Neel
      et al., 2021; Sekhari et al., 2021). Exact unlearning aims to completely eliminate the influence of


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      unwanted data from the trained model, while approximate unlearning methods only partially mitigate
163   data influence, resulting in parameter distributions closely resembling the retrained model with minor
164   adjustments. More sophisticated methods (Guo et al., 2020; Graves et al., 2021) have suggested using
165   influence functions, but these are computationally demanding and limited to small convex models.
166   To extend unlearning techniques to non-convex models like deep neural networks, (Golatkar et al.,
167   2020a;b) introduced a scrubbing mechanism centered on the Fisher Information matrix.
168
      As we noted earlier, concept forgetting and machine unlearning have fundamental differences (
169   Figure 1 demonstrates machine unlearning cannot be applied for a general concept forgetting setup) in
170   their objectives. Machine unlearning seeks to forget specific data points while emulating the behavior
171   of a retrained model, whereas concept forgetting aims for the model’s predictive performance to
172   become independent of the forgotten concept.

356   5.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
357
      To evaluate the efficacy of any concept-forgetting algorithm we propose two different metrics as
358
      defined below:
359
360   • Test empirical concept violation: This metric denoted as V̂ (θC , C, D), is defined in equation 5,
361   quantifies the concept neutrality of the forgotten model θC . Observe that V̂ (θC , C, D) ∈ [0, 1], and a
362   smaller V̂ (θC , C, D) signifies that the model is conceptually neutral regarding the forgetting concept
363   C. In the rest of the section, we denote V̂ (θC , C, D) as V̂C .
364
      • Test accuracy: This metric evaluates the generalization performance of the forgotten model, denoted
365
      as AD . Any concept forgetting algorithm mustn’t render the initial model ineffective during the
366
      forgetting process. Therefore, maintaining accuracy close to that of the initial model θ∗ is desirable.

506   6       C ONCLUSION
507
508
      In the pursuit of safer and more responsible machine learning, the elimination of undesired concepts
509   from models is crucial. Our work focuses on efficiently removing these undesired concepts from
510   pre-trained classification models, a task that is challenging due to the risk of catastrophic forgetting,
511   which can render the model ineffective. To address this, we propose a computationally efficient
512   algorithm termed LAN (Label Annealing) to create a forgotten model while preserving its ability to
513   generalize. We define concept forgetting as the property of a model to disregard undesired concepts
514   during its decision-making process and introduce concept neutrality as a necessary attribute of a
515   forgotten model. To quantify the extent of concept neutrality in any model, we propose a novel metric
516   called concept violation. Our experimental results demonstrate that our method effectively reduces
517
      concept violation while maintaining the model’s performance across multiple concept-forgetting
      settings, various models, and datasets. Additionally, we acknowledge that our definition and method
518
      apply only to concept forgetting in classification models. Further research is needed to develop
519
      definitions and methods for concept forgetting that generalize to generative models as well.

724   B.1     DATASETS AND M ODELS
725
726   Here we have used four datasets as follows:
727
728
              • MNIST (LeCun et al., 1998): The MNIST dataset consist of 28 × 28 gray-scale represent-
                ing handwritten digits from 0 to 9. The MNIST dataset contains 6,000 images per digit class
729
                totaling 60,000 training samples and 1,000 images per digit class totalling 10,000 testing
730
                images.
731
732
              • CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009): The CIFAR-10 dataset consists of 60000 32x32 color
                images in 10 classes: airplane, automobile, bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship, truck with
733
                6000 images per class. There are 50000 training images and 10000 test images.
734
735
              • CelebA (Liu et al., 2015): The Celeb Faces Attributes Dataset (CelebA) is a large-scale
                facial attributes dataset comprising over 200,000 celebrity images, each annotated with 40
736
                attributes. This dataset features significant pose variations and background clutter. CelebA
737
                offers extensive diversity, a substantial quantity of images, and rich annotations.
738
739
              • miniImageNet (Vinyals et al., 2016): Here we have used a smaller subset of the ImageNet
                dataset consisting of 50,000 training images and 10,000 testing images, evenly distributed
740
                across 100 classes. Here we have used an image dimension of 224×224 same as the original
741
                ImageNet data dimension.
742
743   Models: Further to evaluate our method to different models we experimented with a variety of
744   models with different learnable parameter sizes such as 2-layer-MLP, Mobinet-v2 (Sandler et al.,
745   2018), Densenet-121 (Huang et al., 2017), Resnet-50 (He et al., 2016). The 2-layer-MLP net has
746   two hidden layers both having the size of 500. For Mobinet-v2, Densenet-121, and Resnet-50 we
747   have taken Pytorch default models with pre-trained weights. For all of these models, the last layer is
748
      changed to an appropriate size suitable for the classification tasks.
