009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large language models (LLMs) have shown amazing performance on tasks that
012
               require planning and reasoning. Motivated by this, we investigate the internal
013            mechanisms that underpin a network’s ability to perform complex logical reason-
014            ing. We first construct a synthetic propositional logic problem that serves as a
015            concrete test-bed for network training and evaluation. Crucially, this problem de-
016            mands nontrivial planning to solve. We perform our study on two fronts. First,
017            we pursue an understanding of precisely how a three-layer transformer, trained
018            from scratch and attains perfect test accuracy, solves this problem. We are able to
019            identify certain “planning” and “reasoning” circuits in the network that necessitate
020            cooperation between the attention blocks to implement the desired logic. Second,
021
               we study how a pretrained LLM, Mistral 7B, solves this problem. Using activa-
               tion patching, we characterize internal components that are critical in solving our
022
               logic problem. Overall, our work systemically uncovers novel aspects of small
023
               and large transformers, and continues the study of how they plan and reason.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Language models using the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) have shown remarkable
030   capabilities on many natural language tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019b). Trained
031   with causal language modeling wherein the goal is next-token prediction on huge amounts of text,
032   these models exhibit deep language understanding and generation skills. An essential milestone in
033   the pursuit of models which can achieve a human-like artificial intelligence, is the ability to perform
034
      human-like reasoning and planning in complex unseen scenarios. While some recent works using
      probing analyses have shown that the activations of the deeper layers of a transformer contain rich
035
      information about certain mathematical reasoning problems (Ye et al., 2024), the question of what
036
      mechanisms inside the model enables such abilities remains unclear.
037
038   While the study of how transformers reason in general remains a daunting task, in this work,
039   we aim to improve our mechanistic understanding of how a Transformer reason through simple
040
      propositional logic problems. For concreteness’ sake, consider the following problem:
041
042
                  Rules: A or B implies C. D implies E. Facts: A is true. B is false. D is true.
                                   Question: what is the truth value of C?
043
044   An answer with minimal proof is “A is true. A or B implies C; C is true.”
045
      The reasoning problem, while simple-looking on the surface, requires the model to perform several
046
      actions that are essential to more complex reasoning problems, all without chain of thought (CoT).
047
      Before writing down any token, the model has to first discern the rule which is being queried: in this
048   case, it is “A or B implies C”. Then, it needs to rely on the premise variables A and B to the locate
049   the relevant facts, and find “A is true” and “B is false”. Finally, it needs to decide that “A is true” is
050   the correct one to invoke in its answer due to the nature of disjunction. It follows that, to write down
051   the first token “A”, the model already has to form a “mental map” of the variable relations, value
052   assignments and query! Therefore, we believe that this is close to the minimal problem to examine
053   how a model internalizes and plans for solving a nontrivial mathematical reasoning problem where
      apparent ambiguities in the problem specification cannot be resolved trivially.

054
      In this work, we are primarily interested in understanding how transformers solve simple reasoning
055   problems of this form from the perspective of circuit analysis (Wang et al., 2023; Rauker et al.,
056   2023): we aim to understand how the transformer utilizes its internal components (attention heads,
057   MLPs, etc.) to execute the causal path of “QUERY→Answer”. We perform two flavors of ex-
058   periments. The first is on shallow transformers trained purely on the synthetic propositional logic
059   problems, where we mainly rely on linear probing and causal interventions to understand the model’s
060   reasoning strategy. The other set of experiments are on a pre-trained LLM (Mistral-7B), where we
061   primarily rely on causal intervention techniques and examining the attention statistics to discover
062   and verify the circuits for explaining the reasoning actions of the model.
063   At a high level, we make the following discoveries based on our two fronts of analysis:
064
065   1. (§3) We discover that small transformers, trained purely on the synthetic problem, utilize cer-
066      tain “routing embeddings” to significantly alter the information flow of the deeper layers when
067      solving different sub-categories of the reasoning problem. We also characterize the different
068      reasoning pathways: we find that problems querying for reasoning chains involving logical op-
069
         erators typically require greater involvement of all the layers in the model.
070   2. (§4) Of particular interest is our characterization of the circuit which the pretrained LLM
071      Mistral-7B-v0.1 employs to solve the minimal version of the reasoning problem. We find four
072      families of attention heads which have surprisingly specialized roles in processing different
073
         sections of the context: queried-rule locating heads, queried-rule mover heads, fact-processing
         heads, and decision heads. We find evidence suggesting that the model follows the natural rea-
074
         soning path of “QUERY→Relevant Rule→Relevant Fact(s)→Decision”. We present the circuit
075
         discovery process in §4.3.1, and circuit verification in §4.3.2.
076
077   In particular, our analysis of Mistral-7B in §4 is, to our knowledge, the first to characterize the
078   critical components of the circuit employed by an LLM in the wild for solving a nontrivial logical
079   reasoning problem (involving distracting clauses) that requires a correct execution of the reason-
080   ing path “Query→Relevant rule(s)→Relevant fact(s)→Decision” fully in context. Furthermore, we
081   make concrete progress towards general techniques of verifying the necessity and sufficiency of a
082   (general-purpose) pretrained LLM’s reasoning circuit.
083   Additionally, we define the scope of our analysis as follows. First, in the shallow transformer experi-
084   ments, we focus on the variant which only has self-attention layers in addition to layer normalization,
085   positional encoding, embedding and softmax parameters. While we could have also included MLP
086   layers, we choose not to because the no-MLP models already achieve 100% test accuracy on the
087   problem, and adding MLPs would unnecessarily complicate the analysis. As a second way to focus
088   the scope of paper, in the Mistral-7B experiments, we do not seek to uncover every model com-
089   ponent that participates in solving the reasoning problem. Instead, we focus on those which have
090
      the strongest causal influence on the model’s QUERY-sensitive reasoning actions. By doing so, we
      can fully justify the necessity of these key components for the model’s reasoning actions, without
091
      guessing about the roles of less-impactful sub-circuits (for handling low-level text processing, for
092
      instance).

      1.1   R ELATED WORKS
095
096   Mechanistic interpretability. Our work falls in the area of mechanistic interpretability, which aims
097   to understand the mechanisms that enable capabilities of the LLM; such studies involve uncovering
098   certain “circuits” in the network (Elhage et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022; Vig et al.,
099   2020; Feng & Steinhardt, 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Hanna et al., 2024; Merullo et al.,
100   2024; McGrath et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024). While the definition of a “circuit”
101   varies across different works, in this paper, our definition is similar to the one in Wang et al. (2023):
102   it is a collection of model components (attention heads, neurons, etc.) with the “edges” in the circuit
103   indicating the information flow between the components in the forward pass; the “excitation” of the
104
      circuit is the input tokens.
105   Evaluation of reasoning abilities of LLMs. Our work is also related to the line of work which
106   focus on empirically evaluating the reasoning abilities of LLMs across different types of tasks (Xue
107   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Patel et al., 2024; Morishita et al., 2023; Seals & Shalin, 2024; Zhang
      et al., 2023; Saparov & He, 2023; Saparov et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Tafjord

109   primarily benchmark their performance on sophisticated tasks, our work focuses on understanding
110   “how” transformers reason on logic problems accessible to fine-grained analysis.
111

      Analysis of how LLMs reason. There are far fewer studies that focus on providing fine-grained
      analysis of how LLMs reason. To the best of our knowledge, only a handful of works, such as Xue

      et al. (2024); Zečević et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2024), share similar goals of understanding how trans-

      2020; Meng et al., 2022; Hase et al., 2024; Heimersheim & Nanda, 2024; Zhang & Nanda, 2024),
120
      uses causal interventions for uncovering the internal mechanisms or “circuits” of LLMs that en-
121   able them to perform certain tasks. Typically, the LLM is run on pairs of “original” and “altered”
122   prompts, and we search for components inside the model that “alter” the model’s “original behavior”
123   by replacing parts of the model’s activation with “altered activations” when running on the original
124   prompts. The opposite “altered→original” intervention can also be adopted.

164
      In this section, we study how small GPT-2-like transformers, trained solely on the logic problem,
165   approach and solve it. While there are many parts of the answer of the transformer which can lead
166   to interesting observations, in this work, we primarily focus on the following questions:
167
168   1. How does the transformer mentally process the context and plan its answer before writing down
169      any token? In particular, how does it use its “mental notes” to predict the crucial first token?
170   2. How does the transformer determine the truth value of the query at the end?
171
172   We pay particular attention to the first question, because as noted in §2, the first answer token
173   reveals the most about how the transformer mentally processes all the context information without
174   any access to chain of thought (CoT). We delay the less interesting answer of question 2 to the
175   Appendix due to space limitations.

      3.2     M ECHANISM ANALYSIS
189
190   The model approximately follows the strategy below to predict the first answer token:
191
192   1. (Linear vs. LogOp chain) At the QUERY position, the layer-2 attention block sends out a special
193      “routing” signal to the layer-3 attention block, which informs the latter whether the chain being
194      queried is the linear one or not. The third layer then acts accordingly.
195   2. (Linear chain queried) If QUERY is for the linear chain, the third attention block focuses almost
196      100% of its attention weights on the QUERY position, that is, it serves a simple “message
197      passing” role: indeed, layer-2 residual stream at QUERY position already has the correct (and
198      linearly decodable) answer in this case.
199   3. (LogOp chain queried) The third attention block serves a more complex purpose when the Lo-
200      gOp chain is queried. In particular, the first two layers construct a partial answer, followed by
201      the third layer refining it to the correct one.
202
203   We illustrate the overall reasoning strategy and core evidence for it in Figure 8 in Appendix B.3.
204

206

207

208

209

215   2. The “sign” of the hroute signal determines the “mode” which layer-3 attention operates in at the
         ANSWER position. When a sufficiently “positive” hroute is present, layer-3 attention acts as if

330
331   In our Mistral-7B experiments, the input samples have the following properties:
332
333   1. We give the model 6 (randomly chosen) in-context examples before asking for the answer.

346   4.2   C AUSAL MEDIATION ANALYSIS
347
      We provide evidence in this part of the paper primarily relying on a popular technique in mechanistic
348
      interpretability: causal mediation analysis. Our methodology is roughly as follows:
349
350   1. Suppose we are interested in the role of the activations of certain components of the LLM in a
351      certain (sub-)task. For a running example, say we want to understand what role the attention
352      heads play in processing and passing QUERY information to the “:” position for inference. Let
353      us denote the activations as Aℓ,h;t (X), representing the activation of head h in layer ℓ, at token
354      position t.
355   2. Typically, the analysis begins by constructing two sets of prompts which differ in subtle ways. A
356      natural construction in our example is as follows: define sets of samples Dorig and Dalt , where
357      Xorig,n and Xalt,n have exactly the same context, except in Xorig,n , QUERY is for the LogOp
358      chain, while in Xalt,n , QUERY is for the linear chain. Moreover, denote the correct targets
359      yorig,n and yalt,n respectively.
360   3. We run the LLM on Dorig and Dalt , caching the attention-head activations. We also obtain the
361      logits of the model. We can compute the model’s logit differences
362                           ∆orig,n = logit(Xorig,n )[yorig,n ] − logit(Xorig,n )[yalt,n ],
363                                                                                                              (1)
                              ∆alt,n = logit(Xalt,n )[yalt,n ] − logit(Xalt,n )[yorig,n ].
364
365      For a high-accuracy model, ∆orig,n and ∆alt,n should be large and positive for most n’s, since
366      most of the time it must be able to clearly tell that on an Xorig,n , it is the LogOp chain which is
367      being queried, not the linear chain (similarly on an Xalt,n ).
368   4. We now perform intervention for all n, ℓ, h and t:
369      (a) Run the model on Xorig,n , however, replacing the original activation Aℓ,h;t (Xorig,n ) by
370          the altered Aℓ,h;t (Xalt,n ). Now let the rest of the run continue.2 Let us denote the logits
371          obtained in this intervened run as logit→alt;(ℓ,h,t) (Xorig,n ).
372      (b) Now compute the intervened logit difference

530   We studied the reasoning mechanisms of both small transformers and LLMs on a synthetic proposi-
531   tional logic problem. We analyzed a shallow decoder-only attention-only transformer trained purely
532   on this problem as well as a pretrained Mistral-7B LLM. We uncovered interesting mechanisms
533   the small and large transformers adopt to solve the problem. For the small models, we found
534   the existence of “routing” signals that significantly alter the model’s reasoning pathway depending
535   on the sub-category of the problem instance. For Mistral-7B, we characterized the circuit formed
536
      by four families of attention heads that implement the reasoning pathway of “QUERY→Relevant
      Rule→Relevant Facts→Decision”, and make concrete progress towards evaluating the necessity and
537
      sufficiency of the reasoning circuit through carefully designed causal interventions. These findings
538
      provide valuable insights into the inner workings of LLMs on mathematical reasoning problems.

822
823   As illustrated in Figure 1, the propositional logic problem always involve one logical-operator (Lo-
824   gOp) chain and one linear chain. In this paper, we study the length-3 case for the small-transformer
825   setting, and length-2 case for the Mistral-7B-v0.1 case.
826   The input context has the following form:
827
828   RULES_START K implies D. V implies E. D or E implies A.
829   P implies T. T implies S. RULES_END
830   FACTS_START K TRUE. V FALSE. P TRUE. FACTS_END
831   QUERY_START A. QUERY_END
832
      ANSWER
833
      and the answer is written as
834
835   K TRUE. K implies D; D TRUE. D or E implies A; A TRUE.
836
837   In terms the the English-to-token mapping, RULES_START, RULES_END, FACTS_START,
838   FACTS_END, QUERY_START, QUERY_END ANSWER, . and ; are all unique single tokens. The
839   logical operators and and or and the connective implies are unique single tokens. The proposi-
840   tion variables are also unique single tokens.
841   Remark. The rules and facts are presented in a random order in the respective sections of the context
842   in all of our experiments unless otherwise specified. This prevents the model from adopting position-
843   based shortcuts in solving the problem.
844   Additionally, for more clarity, it is entirely possible to run into the scenario where the LogOp chain
845   is queried, LogOp = OR and the two relevant facts both have FALSE truth values (or LogOp = AND
846   and both relevant facts are TRUE), in which case the answer is not unique. For instance, if in the
847   above example, both K and V are assigned FALSE, then both answers below are logically correct:
848
849   K FALSE V FALSE. K implies D; D UNDETERMINED. V implies E;

      and

857   sampled from a pool of 80 variables (tokens). The truth values in the fact section are also randomly
858   chosen. In the training set, the linear chain is queried 20% of the time; the LogOp chain is queried
859   80% of the time. We train every model on 2 million samples.

861   ants. We observe that the 3-layer 3-head variant is the smallest model which achieves 100% accu-
862   racy. We found that 3-layer 2-head models, trained of some random seeds, do converge and obtain

1502   the queried rule as consistently as it does at the QUERY position, however. We still chose to patch
1503   it at this position as we found that it tends to improve the altered logit difference, indicating that
1504   either the model relies on this head to pass certain additional information about the queried rule to
1505   the “:” position, or certain later parts of the circuit do rely on this head for queried-rule information.

1507   of Mistral-7B. We likely need to examine this head’s role in other reasoning problems to clearly
1508
       understand what its role is at different token positions, and whether there is deeper meaning behind
       the fact that, their apparently redundant actions at different token positions all seem to have causal
