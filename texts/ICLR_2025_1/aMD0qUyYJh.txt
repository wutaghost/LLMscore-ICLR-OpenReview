009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Classic reinforcement learning (RL) assumes that an agent can observe a reward
012            for each state-action pair. However, in practical applications, it is often difficult and
013            costly to collect a reward for each state-action pair. While there have been several
014            works considering RL with trajectory feedback, it is unclear if trajectory feedback
015            is inefficient for learning when trajectories are long. In this work, we consider a
016
               model named RL with segment feedback, which offers a general paradigm filling
               the gap between per-state-action feedback and trajectory feedback seemlessly.
017
               In this model, we consider an episodic Markov decision process (MDP), where
018
               each episode is equally divided into m segments, and the agent observes reward
019            feedback only at the end of each segment. Under this model, we study two popular
020            feedback settings: binary feedback and sum feedback, where the agent observes
021            a binary outcome and a reward sum according to the underlying reward function,
022            respectively. To investigate the impact of the number of segments m on learning
023            performance, we design efficient algorithms and establish regret upper and lower
024            bounds for both feedback settings. Our theoretical and empirical results show that:
025            under binary feedback, increasing the number of segments m decreases the regret
026            at an exponential rate; in contrast, surprisingly under sum feedback, increasing m
027
               does not reduce the regret significantly.
028
029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reinforcement learning (RL) is a class of sequential decision-making algorithms, where an agent
032   interacts with an unknown environment through time with the goal of maximizing the obtained
033   reward. RL is applied to extensive fields such as robotics, autonomous driving and game playing.
034
      In classic RL, when the agent takes an action in a state, the environment will provide a reward for
035
      this state-action pair. However, in real-world applications, it is often difficult and costly to collect a
036   reward for each state-action pair. For example, in robotics, when we instruct a robot to scramble eggs,
037   it is hard to specify a reward for each individual action. In autonomous driving, it is difficult and
038   onerous to evaluate each action, considering multiple criteria including safety, comfort and speed.
039
      Motivated by this fact, there have been several works that consider RL with trajectory feedback (Efroni
040
      et al., 2021; Chatterji et al., 2021). In these works, the agent observes a reward signal only at the
041
      end of each episode, instead of at each step, with the signal indicating the quality of the trajectory
042   generated during the episode. While these works mitigate the issue of impractical per-step reward
043   feedback in classic RL, the relationship between the frequency of feedback and the performance of
044   RL algorithms is unknown. In particular, if for example we get feedback twice in each trajectory,
045   does that significantly improve performance over once per trajectory feedback?
046
      To answer this question, we study a general model called RL with segment feedback, which bridges
047
      the gap between per-state-action feedback in classic RL (Sutton & Barto, 2018) and trajectory
048   feedback in recent works (Efroni et al., 2021; Chatterji et al., 2021). In this model, we consider an
049   episodic Markov decision process (MDP), where an episode is equally divided into m segments. In
050   each episode, at each step, the agent first observes the current state, and takes an action, and then
051   transitions to a next state according to the transition distribution. The agent observes a reward signal
052   at the end of each segment. Under this model, we consider two reward feedback settings: binary
053   feedback and sum feedback. In the binary feedback setting, the agent observes a binary outcome
      (e.g., thumbs up/down) generated by a sigmoid function of the reward on this segment. In the sum

054
      feedback setting, the agent observes the sum of the rewards over this segment. In our model, the
055   agent needs to learn the underlying reward function (i.e., the expected reward as a function of states
056   and actions) from binary or sum segment feedback, and maximize the expected reward achieved.
057   (Reviewer NUmy) While Tang et al. (2024) also studied this segment model before (they called it
058   bagged reward), their work is mostly an empirical work, and did not provide theoretical guarantees
059   for algorithms and rigorously reveal the influence of segments on learning.
060
      This model is applicable to many scenarios involving human queries. For instance, in autonomous
061   driving, a driving trajectory is often divided into several segments, and human annotators are asked to
062   provide feedback for each segment, e.g., thumbs up/down. Compared to state-action pairs or whole
063   trajectories, segments are easier and more efficient to evaluate, since human annotators can focus on
064   and rate behaviors in each segment, e.g., passing interactions, reversing the car and park.
065
      In this segment model, there is an interesting balance between the number of segments (queries to
066
      humans) and the collected observations, i.e., we desire more observations, but we also want to reduce
067   the number of queries. Therefore, in this problem, it is critical to investigate the trade-off between
068   the benefits brought by segments and the increase of queries, which essentially comes down to a
069   question: How does the number of segments m impact learning performance?
070
      To answer this question, we design efficient algorithms for binary and sum feedback settings in both
071
      known and unknown transition cases. Regret upper and lower bounds are provided to rigorously show
072
      the influence of the number of segments on learning performance. We also present experimental
073   results to validate our theoretical findings.
074
075   (Reviewer NUmy) (Reviewer R7pS) Note that studying RL with equal segments is an important
076
      start point and serves as a foundation for further investigation on more general models and analysis for
      RL with unequal segments. Even for the equal segment case, this problem is already very challenging:
077
      (i) This problem cannot be solved by applying prior trajectory feedback works, e.g., (Efroni et al.,
078
      2021), since they use the martingale property of subsequent trajectories in analysis, while subsequent
079   segments are not a martingale due to the dependence of segments within a trajectory. (ii) In prior
080   trajectory feedback works (Efroni et al., 2021; Chatterji et al., 2021), there exists a gap between upper
081   and lower bounds for sum feedback, and there is no lower bound for binary feedback. This fact poses
082   a great challenge for figuring out the essential influence of the number of segments m on learning
083   performance, since one cannot get too many hints from prior works.
084
      Our work resolves the above challenges and makes the following contributions:
085
086
            1. We study a general model called RL with segment feedback, which bridges the gap between
087            per-state-action feedback in classic RL and trajectory feedback seemlessly. Under this
088            model, we consider two feedback settings: binary feedback and sum feedback.
089
090         2. For binary feedback, we design computationally and sample efficient algorithms SegBiTS
091
               and SegBiTS-Tran for known and unknown transitions, respectively. We provide regret up-
092
               per and lower bounds which depend on exp( Hr2m

               and rmax is the universal upper bound of rewards. Our results exhibit that under binary

094
095         3. For sum feedback, we devise algorithms E-LinUCB and LinUCB-Tran for known and un-

      The algorithm design and theoretical analysis for classic RL were well studied in the literature (Sutton
109   & Barto, 2018; Jaksch et al., 2010; Azar et al., 2017; Jin et al., 2018; Zanette & Brunskill, 2019).
110   (Reviewer NUmy) Tang et al. (2024) proposed the RL with segment feedback problem (they called it
111   RL with bagged reward) and designed a transformer-based algorithm. However, their work is mostly
112   an empirical work and only considered the sum feedback type. They did not provide theoretical
113   results for their algorithm and rigorously quantified the influence of segments on learning. There are
114   two recent theoretical works (Efroni et al., 2021; Chatterji et al., 2021) studying RL with trajectory
115   feedback, which are most related to our work. Efroni et al. (2021) investigated RL with sum trajectory
116   feedback, and designed upper confidence bound (UCB)-type and Thompson sampling (TS)-type
117
      algorithms with regret guarantees. Chatterji et al. (2021) considered RL with binary trajectory
      feedback, and developed algorithms based on UCB value iteration. For binary trajectory feedback,
118
      Chatterji et al. (2021) provided regret upper bounds that exponentially depend on the scale of rewards.
119
120   Different from (Efroni et al., 2021; Chatterji et al., 2021), we study RL with segment feedback,
121   which allows feedback from multiple segments within a trajectory, with per-state-action feedback and
122   trajectory feedback as the two extremes.вҲҡ (Reviewer NUmy) Under sum feedback, we improve the
123   regret bound in (Efroni et al., 2021) by H using experimental design, and demonstrate the optimality
124   of our result by establishing a lower bound. Under binary feedback, we propose TS-style algorithms
125
      which are computationally efficient, and also build a lower bound to validate the inevitability of the
      exponential dependence in the regret bound, which is new to the literature.
126
127   Our work is also related to linear bandits (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2011) and logistic bandits (Filippi
128   et al., 2010; Faury et al., 2020; Russac et al., 2021), and uses analytical techniques from that literature.

      7                                        C ONCLUSION
474
475
476   In this work, we formulate a model named RL with segment feedback, which offers a general
477   paradigm for feedback, bridging the gap between per-state-action feedback in classic RL and trajectory
478   feedback. In the binary feedback setting, we deign efficient algorithms SegBiTS and SegBiTS-Tran,
479   and provide regret upper and lower bounds which show a dependence on exp( Hr2m    max
                                                                                            ). These results
480   reveal that under binary feedback, increasing the number of segment m greatly helps expedite learning.
481   In the sum feedback setting, we develop near-optimal algorithms E-LinUCB and LinUCB-Tran in
482
      terms of H and m, where the regret results do not depend on m when ignoring logarithmic factors.
      These results exhibit that under sum feedback, increasing m does not help accelerate learning much.
483
484   There are several interesting directions worth further investigation. One direction is to consider
485   segments of unequal lengths and study how to divide segments to optimize learning. Another
      direction is to generalize the results to the function approximation setting.
