                                                   A BSTRACT
009
010            Detection Transformers (DETR) are renowned object detection pipelines, however
011            computationally efficient multiscale detection using DETR is still challenging. In
012            this paper, we propose a Cross-Resolution Encoding-Decoding (CRED) mechanism
013            that allows DETR to achieve the accuracy of high-resolution detection while
014            having the speed of low-resolution detection. CRED is based on two modules;
015            Cross Resolution Attention Module (CRAM) and One Step Multiscale Attention
016            (OSMA). CRAM is designed to transfer the knowledge of low-resolution encoder
017
               output to a high-resolution feature. While OSMA is designed to fuse multiscale
               features in a single step and produce a feature map of a desired resolution enriched
018
               with multiscale information. When used in prominent DETR methods, CRED
019
               delivers accuracy similar to the high-resolution DETR counterpart in roughly 50%
020            fewer FLOPs. Specifically, state-of-the-art DN-DETR, when used with CRED
021            (calling CRED-DETR), becomes 76% faster, with ∼ 50% reduced FLOPs than its
022            high-resolution counterpart with 202G FLOPs on MS-COCO benchmark. We plan
023            to release pretrained CRED-DETRs for use by the community.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Detection Transformers (DETR) (Carion et al., 2020) are end-to-end object detection frameworks
029   without post-processing, such as anchor boxes, box matching, and non-maximal suppression (NMS)
030   that are required in ConvNet-based detectors (Ren et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2016). Since their first entry
031   (Carion et al., 2020), DETRs have evolved, mainly regarding query design (Gao et al., 2021b; Zhu
032
      et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022) to improve the
      detection performance, while recent DETR pipelines achieve that via salient points (Liu et al., 2023)
033
      or unsupervised pretraining (Chen et al., 2023).
034
035   State-of-the-art DETRs exploit high-resolution features (known as Dilated Convolution or DC variant)
036   (Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022) or dense multi-scale features (Zhang et al., 2023b) to push the
037   detection accuracy further. However, they suffer from high computation complexity w.r.t. their
038
      accuracy gains. For example, DAB DETR (Liu et al., 2022) with high-resolution features improved
      2.3AP, but it introduces a 114% rise in FLOPs.
039
040   The primary reason is the quadratic computational complexity (Dai et al., 2021) of the Transformer’s
041   attention mechanism (Zhang et al., 2023a) w.r.t. the spatial size, i.e., a Transformer is O(H 2W 2 )
042   complex in processing a feature map of a spatial size of H ×W . Hence, doubling the resolution of
043   the encoder input quadruples its computations while also affecting the decoder. Although deformable
044
      attention (Zhu et al., 2021) addresses this issue, it causes additional runtime overhead due to irregular
      memory accesses.
045
046   To address this issue, recent IMFA (Zhang et al., 2023a) proposes using a low-resolution feature map
047   and Top-K sparsely sampled higher-resolution features (Figure 1). IMFA exhibits improvements in
048   accuracy with lower FLOPs. However, sparse sampling incurs memory access costs due to irregular
049   memory access, which becomes prevalent with more samples. Recent (Li et al., 2023) exploits
050
      attention among only interleaved tokens for reducing computations in multiscale attention in the
      encoder. (Zhao et al., 2024b) works on reduced resolution images (640 × 640) instead of high
051
      resolution settings (800 × 1200) on standard DETR.
052
053   Our aim aligns with improving DETR speed and accuracy by using multiscale features at high-
      resolution settings. However, our key motivation is based on our finding that the encoder consists

066   of most computations relative to the decoder. Therefore, we propose a principal design change in
067   the DETR pipeline, i.e., feeding the encoder with low-resolution while feeding the decoder with a
068   high-resolution feature obtained from the backbone. By keeping the encoder input low-resolution,
069   we save computations, while by keeping the decoder input high-resolution, we provide the decoder
070   access to fine-grained details. Since the high-resolution features from the backbone lack large spatial
071   context (Carion et al., 2020), we develop a novel Cross Resolution Attention Module (CRAM). It
072
      utilizes encoder output that has a global context and transfers this information into the high-resolution
      feature map. When this feature map is fed to the decoder, the decoder has access to the fine-grained
073
      details and the global context, thus improving the accuracy and runtime.
074
075   Then, by exploiting this capability of CRAM to transfer the information from low-resolution encoder
076   output to high-resolution feature, we propose to reduce the resolution of an encoder further to save
077   computations. This behavior is intended to develop faster DETRs offering speed-accuracy tradeoffs.
078
      However, feeding the encoder naively with reduced resolution degrades its performance. Hence, we
      devise a novel module called One Step Multiscale Attention (OSMA), which attends to multiscale
079
      information in one step and can produce tokens or feature maps of the desired resolution enriched
080
      with multiscale information. When the encoder is fed the tokens produced by OSMA at aggressively
081   low resolution, accuracy is considerably improved while avoiding any runtime overhead relative to
082   the baseline which was fed with a low-resolution backbone output.
083
084
      We name our overall approach as Cross-Resolution Encoding-Decoding (CRED), shown in Figure 1.
      We demonstrate that our CRED-enhanced DETR can attain an AP (average precision) equivalent
085
      to the original DETR’s high-resolution counterpart in 50% fewer FLOPs at 76% improved runtime.
086
      For instance, DN-DETR (Li et al., 2022)+CRED reduces FLOPs from 202G to 103G (∼ 50%↓) and
087   improves FPS from 13FPS to 23FPS (∼ 76%↑) without losing accuracy. In addition, applying CRED
088   in DETR variants (Meng et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a) consistently improves
089   their accuracy and runtime compared with their DC variants. To improve runtime further, we half
090   the encoder resolution via OSMA. Interestingly, we only observe −1AP in this configuration while
091   the runtime is further improved by 84% compared with the vanilla DC variant. This signifies the
092   potential of Cross-Resolution Encoding-Decoding mechanism in DETRs.

094   2           P RELIMINARY
095
096   This section revisits the architectural design of vanilla DETR (Carion et al., 2020) and advanced
097   DETRs (Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021). DETR comprises a backbone, a Transformer
098   encoder, and a Transformer decoder. In a backbone (Li et al., 2022), it is common to keep five stages,
099   each operating at a resolution half of its previous stage. Thus, the final stage (stage-5) runs at a
100   stride of 32 at the original resolution. Once an image I ∈ R3×H×W is fed into a backbone (Figure 1),
101   the backbone output Fb ∈ RC×H0 ×W0 is fed to a Transformer encoder, producing encoded feature
102   embeddings or tokens Fe ∈ Rd×H0 ×W0 . These embeddings are fed to the Transformer decoder to
103   produce a fixed number of queries (Nq ), each representing an object detectable in the image. The
104
      queries are passed through two Feedforward neural Networks (FFN) to obtain the object class and its
      bounding box. During training, bipartite or Hungarian matching (Carion et al., 2020) is performed
105
      for one-to-one assignments of ground truth and predictions.
106
107   In DETR pipelines (Carion et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), the embeddings Fe are
      directly fed to the decoder where the Nq queries interact with H0 ×W0 features in Fe via cross attention.

      The encoder complexity is O(H02W02 ) in its self-attention, whereas for the decoder, it is O(Nq H0W0 ) in
125
      cross attention and O(Nq2 ) in its self-attention. In DETRs, Nq is relatively smaller, i.e., 300; however,
126   H0W0 is quite a large number depending on the image size, e.g., 1280 × 800 (Liu et al., 2022; Li
127   et al., 2022). Hence, most of the computations are concentrated in the encoder. When opting for
128   high-resolution detection, known as the DC variant (Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), the backbone
129   output stride is set lower than 32. For this purpose, stage-5 of the backbone is set to run at stride 16
130   (Meng et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022) w.r.t. the image, thereby doubling the resolution
131   of Fb . This leads to quadratic growth in the encoder computations due to the increased resolution
132   (Dai et al., 2021).
133   In this paper, we rethink the encoder-decoder information flow while leveraging multiscale features
134   in a computationally efficient manner. Summarily, we propose an approach that feeds the encoder
135   with a stride ≥ 32 while feeding the decoder with a stride ≤ 32 to harness the best of both worlds, i.e.,
136   the accuracy of high-resolution detection and speed of low-resolution detection. To our knowledge,
137   such a mechanism has not been demonstrated in DETRs.

139   3      M ETHOD
140
141   We propose to enhance the DETR design with the two modules. Firstly, we develop CRAM
142   which enables Cross-Resolution Encoding-Decoding. Secondly, we develop OSMA module, which
143   facilitates generating feature maps enriched with multiscale information in a computationally efficient
144   manner and further enhances the performance of CRED.
145
146   3.1       C ROSS R ESOLUTION ATTENTION M ODULE (CRAM)
147
148   As mentioned in Sec. 2, high-resolution detection improves DETR accuracy, but high-resolution
149   input to the encoder is compute-intensive due to its quadratic complexity (Dai et al., 2021). Hence,
150
      we feed the encoder with low resolution while feeding the decoder with high resolution, calling it
      cross-resolution encoding-decoding paradigm. However, the information flow between them no
151
      longer exists due to the different input sources to the encoder and decoder. Hence, we develop CRAM
152
      that acts as a bridge between the encoder and decoder in the proposed cross-resolution encoding
153   decoding paradigm. The overall design of our approach (CRAM) is shown in Figure 2.
154
155   Consider two feature maps X ∈ RC×h×w and Y ∈ RC×H×W . In our Cross-Resolution Encoding-
156
      Decoding approach, the low-resolution feature X is fed to the encoder layers, while the high-resolution
      feature Y is fed to the attention transfer module. To transfer the knowledge of the encoder embed-
157
      dings (Xe = Encoder(X), Xe ∈ RC×h×w ) to Y , we spatially upsample (X̂e = Upsample(Xe )|H,W X̂e ∈
158
      RC×H×W ) the encoder output to match the resolution of Y , and a concatenation operation is performed.
159
160   Now, we use a linear projection layer, which combines the features information of the high-
161   resolution features and the upsampled low-resolution feature from the encoder. The output
      (Z = Linear(Cat(Y, Xe ))) is normalized via LayerNorm (Ba et al., 2016) and passed through SiLU

188
      Why Could Cross Resolution Attention Transfer Improve Performance? In vanilla DETR
      design, the encoder embeddings are produced from stage-5 (low-resolution) and have global receptive
189
      filed via self-attention (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), which are fed to the decoder for the predictions.
190
      However, these embeddings do not have fine details of smaller objects. On the other hand, the
191   high-resolution input Y (earlier stages, e.g., stage-4) to CRAM has a small receptive field but has
192   details of smaller objects.
193
194
      In CRAM, the concatenation operation followed by a linear layer infuses the local and global context
      to produce fine-grained, high-resolution features, similar to what earlier semantic segmentation
195
      approaches (Zhao et al., 2017) used for improving performance. In the same way, with this operation,
196
      the high-resolution feature Y acquires a global receptive field when concatenated with the encoder
197   embedding or tokens Xe . After the layerwise refinement, it is fed to the decoder, which improves the
198   accuracy and speed; even the encoder still functions at a smaller resolution.

      3.2   O NE S TEP M ULTISCALE ATTENTION (OSMA)
201
202
      In single-scale operation of DETRs (Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), the backbone output Fb is fed
203   to the encoder. However, Fb does not have direct access to the multiscale information. Whereas
204   multiscale DETRs feed the encoder with either sampled (Zhu et al., 2021) or dense multiscale features
205   (Zhang et al., 2023b), which is computationally heavy.
206
      We propose OSMA, which produces Fo enriched with multiscale information, i.e., the best of
207
      both single-scale and multiscale methods without progressive fusion, i.e., fusing two scales at a
208
      time (yol)(Figure 1). In general, Fb has a stride 32 w.r.t. the input (He et al., 2016); however, using
209   multiscale features, OSMA can produce a feature map Fo (‘o’ refers to OSMA) of stride greater or
210   lesser than 32 which directly controls the encoder computations. With this functionality, OSMA
211   offers better features or tokens enriched with multiscale information to be utilized by the encoder.
212
      OSMA has three main steps: First, Local aggregation of multiscale features, Second, performing one-
213
      step attention on the aggregated features, and Third, broadcasting the output into desired resolution.
214

      obtained from the backbone, where i = 0, 1, ...n − 1 is the scale index (Figure 3). In this step, all the

      One Step Multiscale Attention. We perform information fusion by attending to all the multiscale
239
      features and their channels simultaneously, thus calling it a one-step multiscale attention. We describe
240   this process for a single grid or M j , also depicted in Figure 4.
241
242   In the attention process, M j undergoes a 1 × 1 convolution operation with weights W ∈ Rd×T ×1×1
243   which projects M j in a d dimensional latent space. The 1 × 1 layer combines information from all the
244   T multiscale features with a unit stride. In this way, the output of multiscale information becomes
245
      richer. Then, we perform a layer normalization over the columns of M j followed by SiLU activation
      (See Sec. 4.2).
246
247   This process is repeated for feature refinement, and the penultimate 1 × 1 layer produces P channels.
248   The final layer is a linear projection applied over the columns of M̂ j to refine the column information
249   because a column becomes a feature ∈ RC in the output feature map Fo . The output of this step
250   Q̂ ∈ RNg ×P×C is broadcasted into a feature based on the requirements, as discussed next. Output
251   Feature Broadcasting. This step broadcasts Q̂ into a feature map Fo of the desired resolution based
252   on the pair {g0 , P}. For example, for g0 = 1, if we aim to produce a feature Fo of size H0 ×W0 , the
253   value of P will be set to 1, or if a feature Fo of size 2H0 × 2W0 is required, P can be set to 4, or if a
254   feature Fo of size H0 /2 ×W0 /2 is needed, {g0 = 2, P = 1} can be used.
255   This flexibility of generating Fo of desired resolution allows controlling the encoder’s input resolution
256   and, hence, its computations. Meanwhile, multiscale information infusion helps improve DETR
257   accuracy with a slight computational overhead. We have studied various {g0 , P} combinations, and
258   our empirical results show that {g0 = 1, P = 1} is the best combination for keeping the resolution of
259   Fo equals to F0 whereas {g0 = 2, P = 1} is best for reducing the resolution. See Table 5.

286   Dataset and Evaluation Metric. Following (Carion et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), we
287   use MS-COCO 2017 benchmark (Lin et al., 2014) for evaluation, having 117k training images and
288   5k validation images. We use MS-COCO’s standard evaluation metric of Average Precision (AP) at
289
      different thresholds and different object scales.
290   Implementation Details. We plug the proposed CRED into state-of-the-art DN-DETR (Li et al.,
291   2022) for all our experimental evaluations, including ablations. However, to showcase generality, we
292   also adapt CRED into other prominent DETR methods, e.g., Conditional DETR (Meng et al., 2021),
293   DAB-DETR (Liu et al., 2022) etc.
294   We perform experiments in the 50-epoch setting, widely used for DETRs (Liu et al., 2022; Li et al.,
295   2022; Zhang et al., 2023a). We also show results for the 12-epoch or 1× (Li et al., 2022) setting
296   to demonstrate accelerated convergence due to the improved DETR design in this paper. The base
297   learning for the backbone is set to 1 × 10−5 while for the transformer, it is set to 1 × 10−4 . For the
298   12-epoch schedule, the learning rate is dropped by 0.1 at 11th epoch, whereas it is dropped at 40th
299   epoch for the 50-epoch schedule. We use 8× NVIDIA A40 with a batch size of 16 (2 per-GPU) for
300   training. All the ablations are performed at the 1× setting.

525   5   C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this work, we present a novel Cross-Resolution Encoding-Decoding (CRED) mechanism to
528   improve the accuracy and runtime of DETR methods. CRED is based on its two novel modules
529   Cross Resolution Attention Module (CRAM) and One Step Multiscale Attention (OSMA). CRAM
530   transfers the knowledge of low-resolution encoder output to a high-resolution feature. While OSMA
531   is designed to fuse multiscale features in a single step and produce a feature map of a desired
532   resolution. With the application of CRED into state-of-the-art DETR methods, FLOPs get reduced by
533   50%, and FPS increases by 76% than the high-resolution DETR at equivalent detection performance.
534   Future Scope & Limitations: CRED with its promising results shows huge potential in real-time and
535   affordable DETRs with high accuracy and high-resolution image processing. There is greater scope
536   for improvements, e.g., fusing CRAM and OSMA for even higher performance or adapting CRED
537   to sparse sampling-based DETRs because the current design can not fuse high-resolution features
538   with sparsely sampled encoder embeddings. In addition, CRED has huge scope in Transformer-
539   based semantic or instance segmentation by leveraging its attention transfer to improve runtime for
      processing high-resolution images because semantic segmentation produces high-resolution outputs.
