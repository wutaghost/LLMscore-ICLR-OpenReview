009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Information Retrieval (IR) methods aim to identify relevant documents in response
012            to a given query, which have gained remarkable attention due to their successful
013            application in various natural language tasks. However, existing approaches typi-
014            cally consider only the textual information within the documents, which overlooks
015            the fact that documents can contain multiple modalities, including texts, images,
016
               and tables. Further, they often segment each long document into multiple discrete
               passages for embedding, preventing them from capturing the overall document
017
               context and interactions between paragraphs. We argue that these two limitations
018
               lead to suboptimal document representations for retrieval. In this work, to address
019            them, we aim to produce more comprehensive and nuanced document representa-
020            tions by holistically embedding documents interleaved with different modalities.
021            Specifically, we achieve this by leveraging the capability of recent vision-language
022            models that enable the processing and integration of text, images, and tables into
023            a unified format and representation. Moreover, to mitigate the information loss
024            from segmenting documents into passages, instead of representing and retrieving
025            passages individually, we further merge the representations of segmented passages
026            into one single document representation, while we additionally introduce a rerank-
027
               ing strategy to decouple and identify the relevant passage within the document if
               necessary. Then, through extensive experiments on diverse information retrieval
028
               scenarios considering both the textual and multimodal queries, we show that our
029
               approach substantially outperforms relevant baselines, thanks to the consideration
030            of the multimodal information interleaved within the documents in a unified way.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Information Retrieval (IR) is the task of fetching relevant documents from a large corpus in response
036   to an input query, which becomes a fundamental process to various real-world applications including
037   web search engines and question-answering systems (Shah et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al.,
038   2020). Specifically, to retrieve documents for the query, traditional approaches have focused on their
039
      textual representations, utilizing either sparse retrieval methods such as TF-IDF and BM25 (Robert-
      son et al., 1994; Jones, 2004), which rely on exact term matching between the query and document,
040
      or dense retrieval methods such as DPR and ANCE (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Xiong et al., 2021),
041
      which leverage neural embeddings of the query and document text to capture semantic similari-
042   ties between them over a continuous vector space. Recently, dense retrieval methods have gained
043   more popularity over sparse methods due to their capability to capture semantic nuances and context
044   beyond simple keyword matching, leading to multiple successes with improved performance.
045
      Despite their huge successes, existing (dense) retrieval methods face a couple of severe challenges.
046
      First, they primarily rely on the textual data for document embedding and retrieval, overlooking the
047
      fact that modern documents often contain multimodal content, such as images and tables (beyond the
048   plain text), which can carry critical information that may be essential for accurately understanding
049   and retrieving the relevant documents. To be specific, a diagram within a medical article can more
050   effectively represent the structure of a molecule or the progression of a disease, offering more clarity
051   that would be difficult to achieve with text alone, and omitting such multimodal content can lead
052   to an incomplete understanding (and potentially inaccurate retrieval) of the documents. Also, the
053   segmentation of long documents into discrete passages, which is commonly employed by retrieval
      models to handle the length limitation for embeddings, may prevent models from capturing the full

078   In this work, we introduce a novel approach to holistic document embedding for IR, which addresses
079   the aforementioned challenges by representing and retrieving the documents interleaved with differ-
080
      ent modalities in a unified manner. Specifically, our method revolves around the recent advance of
      Vision-Language Models (VLMs), which enables the processing and integration of multimodal con-
081
      tent (such as text, images, and tables) directly into a single token sequence, thereby preserving the
082
      context and relationships between various parts of the document, unlike the previous approaches that
083   rely on the fragmented visual representations. Furthermore, in cases where the number of tokens in a
084   document is large and exceeds the capacity of a single context window of VLMs, we propose a strat-
085   egy to segment the document into manageable passages, each represented within the token limit, and
086   combine these passage representations into a unified document representation, which differs from
087   existing IR approaches that independently represent and retrieve at the passage level, potentially
088   losing the overall document context. Lastly, to accurately identify only the relevant sections within
089   the lengthy documents, we introduce a reranking mechanism that is trained to pinpoint the passage
090   most pertinent to the query (among all the other passages within the document), effectively allowing
091
      for both the coarse-grained document-level matching and the fine-grained passage-level retrieval.
      We provide the visual illustrations of the overall pipeline of IDentIfy against prior work in Figure 1.
092
      We refer to our overall method as Interleaved Document Information Retrieval System (IDentIfy).
093
094   We experimentally validate the effectiveness of IDentIfy on four different benchmark datasets, con-
095   sidering both the text-only and multimodal queries. On a battery of tests conducted, we then observe
096   that our approach substantially outperforms relevant baselines that consider only the uni-modality
097
      for document representations, thanks to the consideration of multimodal content. Further, we find
      that the strategy to represent the whole document with its single representation (by merging em-
098
      beddings of its splits if necessary) is superior to the approach of individually representing them
099
      for document retrieval, but also performing reranking over the sections of the retrieved document
100   is superior to the approach of directly retrieving those sections, which confirm the efficacy of the
101   proposed retrieval and reranking pipeline for document and passage retrieval, respectively.

      2       R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Information Retrieval Information Retrieval (IR) is the task of accurately finding documents rele-
106   vant to a given query from a large corpus, such as Wikipedia, which has been a crucial component for
107   a variety of applications, including search engines, question-answering systems, and conversational
      agents (Zhu et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Ram et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Jeong et al., 2024a).

108
      Specifically, to retrieve the relevant documents, earlier IR approaches measured similarity between
109   queries and documents based on their lexical term matching, such as BM25 and TF-IDF (Robert-
110   son et al., 1994; Jones, 2004). Yet, these methods often struggled to capture the semantic nuances
111   beyond surface-level term overlaps. To overcome this, along with advancements in language mod-
112   els (Devlin et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019). there has been dense retrieval approaches that embed both
113   the queries and documents into a shared dense vector space (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Xiong et al.,
114   2021), enabling the calculation of semantic similarity between them more effectively by capturing
115   the deeper contextual information. However, previous IR studies have mainly focused on enhanc-
116   ing the textual representations of queries and documents, while overlooking the fact that documents
117
      often consist of diverse modalities (such as images and tables) beyond text, which can potentially
      provide richer context and aid in more accurate retrieval (Liu et al., 2021; Jeong et al., 2024b).
118
119   Multimodal Information Retrieval Recent studies in IR have expanded the focus from purely
120   text-based retrieval models to those that consider other modalities, such as images (Radford et al.,
121   2021; Xiao et al., 2024), tables (Herzig et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024) and graphs (Baek et al.,
122   2023); however, the majority of these approaches (Zhou et al., 2024; Long et al., 2024; Lerner et al.,
123   2024; Nowak et al., 2024; Caffagni et al., 2024) have primarily explored how to process the mul-
124   timodal queries, meanwhile, they often overlook the equally important multimodal characteristics
125   of the documents being retrieved. Specifically, we argue that, while incorporating multimodal ele-
126
      ments in queries has expanded the range and diversity of query types that IR systems can handle,
      considering the multimodal nature of the documents can lead to a more holistic representation of
127
      retrieval targets, which can ultimately lead to enhancing the overall retrieval performance. In efforts
128
      to handle diverse multimodal elements within documents, there are concurrent studies that have pro-
129   posed to capture screenshots of documents, such as PDFs (Faysse et al., 2024) or Wikipedia web
130   pages (Ma et al., 2024a), and subsequently encoding them through vision models (Ding et al., 2024).
131   However, these methods are not only limited by factors, such as image resolution and computational
132   memory, constraining their application to documents longer than a single page1 , but also fall short
133   by treating the diverse modalities within a document as a single visual entity, leading to suboptimal
134   document representations that fail to effectively capture the nuanced interdependence between text
135   and images. Furthermore, they do not address the critical issue of splitting documents into smaller
136   fragments (e.g., sub-images), which may disrupt the holistic contextual view of the entire document.
137   Vision-Language Models Recent Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have emerged as a powerful
138   tool for jointly processing visual and textual data, combining the image understanding capabilities of
139   visual encoders (Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023) with the advanced reasoning abilities of lan-
140   guage models (OpenAI, 2022; 2023a). These models have achieved remarkable performance across
141   diverse vision-language (VL) tasks (such as image captioning and visual question answering) (Dai
142   et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023b), with the substantially limited attention on their applications to IR. We
143   note that the latest developments in this field have particularly focused on enabling VLMs to handle
144   interleaved, multimodal content, which involves a mixed sequence of images and text (Zhang et al.,
145
      2023; Li et al., 2024). In particular, LLaVA-NeXT-Interleave (Li et al., 2024) introduces a fine-
      tuning approach that specifically enhances the VLMs’ capacity to understand complex interleavings
146
      of multiple images and text within a single context. Drawing inspiration from these advances, in this
147
      work, we propose to harness the capabilities of VLMs to create unified embeddings for documents
148   interleaved with text and images (as well as tables) for IR, which is a big shift from even the recent
149   IR approach (Ma et al., 2024b) that still embeds the documents with the recent but text-based models
150   like Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), failing to fully capture the diverse multimodal content.
151

163   be represented as a sequence of tokens: q = [q1 , q2 , . . . , qn ] and d = [d1 , d2 , . . . , dm ] where [·]
164   indicates a concatenation operation in a specific order. We note that traditional IR approaches typi-

167   nature of many real-world documents. Then, this new extension raises important questions of how
168   can both the textual and visual content be represented within a unified token framework, and how
169   can these multimodal tokens be seamlessly integrated and encoded for document representations.
170   To answer those two questions, we harness the power of recent vision-language models below.

172   Vision-Language Models We now turn to describing Vision-Language Models (VLMs), which
173   are designed to jointly encode the textual and visual information in a unified token framework. We
174
      note that these models are generally comprised of two main components: a visual encoder and a
      language model, interconnected through a projection layer. Specifically, given an input document
175
      that may contain interleaved modalities (e.g., text and images), the visual encoder extracts high-level
176
      visual features from (multiple) images embedded within the document, mapping them into a latent
177   space. Then, these visual features are transformed into a sequence of visual tokens via the projection
178   layer, represented as follows: V ∈ RV×demb where V denotes the visual token length and demb is the
179   token dimension size. Similarly, for the textual content embedded within the document alongside
180   images, the language model uses a word embedding layer to convert the input text into a sequence
181   of text tokens, represented as follows: L ∈ RL×demb where L denotes the token length of text.
182
      In this work, we also propose to account for tables that are an integral modality for holistically rep-
183   resenting the full content of documents. However, in contrast to text and images that have dedicated
184   processing layers within the VLM architectures, tables do not have a specific representation layer.
185   Nevertheless, we argue that recent VLMs are pre-trained on diverse web data, and subsequently
186   they are implicitly learned to handle the table structures formatted in HTML. Consequently, we treat
187   HTML-format table data as a linearized sequence of HTML words, applying the same word embed-
188   ding layer as is used for plain text. To be formal, this process converts the table content into table
189   tokens, as follows: T ∈ RT×demb where T is the token length of the table. Lastly, once extracted, the
190   visual tokens, text tokens, and table tokens are concatenated (to form a unified token sequence) and
191
      then passed through the remaining layers of VLMs, to capture both uni- and cross-modal relation-
      ships across different modalities, enabling the comprehensive understanding of the input document.

194   3.2   R ETRIEVER
195   We now turn to explaining how we design a retriever specifically tailored for multimodal interleaved
196   document retrieval. In particular, to effectively retrieve documents that contain multiple modalities,
197   our approach leverages a VLM capable of processing text, images, and tables within a single doc-
198   ument. Further, following the standard practice of existing retrieval architectures (Karpukhin et al.,
199   2020; Xiong et al., 2021), we use a dual-encoder structure, which consists of a query encoder and a
200   section encoder, both are based on the VLM, which is illustrated in Figure 2 (a).
201   Specifically, thanks to the use of the VLM, our query encoder can take either purely textual queries
202   q = LQ or multimodal queries consisting of text and corresponding visual elements q = [VQ , LQ ].
203   Also, to obtain the final query representation, we introduce a learnable token called ‘End of Query’,
204   [EoQ]∈ Rdemb . This token is appended to the end of the sequence of query tokens q, and the final
205   concatenated tokens [q, [EoQ]] are then passed through the query encoder. Then, the model output
206   corresponding to [EoQ] is used as the final query representation, as follows: ZQ ∈ Rdemb .
207
      For documents, we first represent each document d as a sequence of sections d = [si ]Si=1 (with a total
208   of S sections), where each section si is derived by dividing the document according to the subtitles
209   in the document. si can contain a combination of text tokens LSi , visual tokens from embedded
210   images VSi , and table tokens TSi , denoted as follows: si = [VSi , LSi , TSi ]. Then, to obtain a
211   section-level representation, similar to the query representation, we introduce a learnable token,
212   called ‘End of Section’: [EoS] ∈ Rdemb , which is similarly appended at the end of each section.
213   We then forward the concatenated tokens [si , [EoS]] to the section encoder, and, after that, the
214   output corresponding to [EoS] is used to form the section representation, as follows: ZSi ∈ Rdemb .
215   Additionally, the overall document representation is obtained by averaging the representations of all

246   3.3   R ERANKER
247   To enable fine-grained retrieval within documents beyond the retrieval of documents themselves, we
248   introduce a section-level reranking mechanism that identifies the section most relevant to the input
249   query. In particular, once the document is retrieved, the objective of the reranker fR is to pinpoint
250   the specific sections within the document that best match the query. We also note that this reranker
251   is similarly operationalized with a single VLM along with a binary classifier on top of it, which
252   directly measures the relevance of each query-section pair, illustrated in in Figure 2 (b).
253   Formally, for a retrieved document, we take each of its sections si and concatenate it with the query q
254   and a learnable token for section embedding [EoS], forming the input sequence of [q, si , [EoS]].
255   The concatenated tokens are then processed through the reranker, and the model output correspond-
256   ing to [EoS] captures the relevant between the query and section, which is further subsequently
257   passed to a binary classifier consisting of a linear layer followed by a Sigmoid function. Through
258   this process, the classifier outputs a probability score indicating the likelihood of the section being
259   relevant to the query, i.e., a score close to one denotes a high relevance (positive section), meanwhile,
260
      a score near zero indicates irrelevance (negative section).
261   To train this reranker, we use the Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss, formalized as follows:

272   4.1   DATASETS
273
274
      To evaluate the effectivenss of IDentIfy, we focus on multimodal IR tasks that require understanding
      of both the textual and visual cues within queries and documents, which align well with our goal of
275
      enhancing retrieval of multimodal interleaved documents. The datasets considered are as follows:
276
277   Encyclopedic-VQA (Mensink et al., 2023) is a large-scale visual question-answering (VQA) bench-
278   mark dataset, widely used for measuring the performance of multimodal IR models. Each query is
279   linked to a specific section of a Wikipedia document (containing an answer for it) and is manually
280
      annotated by humans. Also, this dataset offers both text-only and multimodal queries. In addition
      to this, the queries are related to fine-grained properties of species and landmarks. Our experiments
281
      focus on the single-hop category where questions that can be answered in a single retrieval step.
282
283   InfoSeek (Chen et al., 2023) is a dataset designed for knowledge-intensive VQA, covering a wide
284   range of entities (such as landmarks, animals, and food). Questions are generated by filling human-
285   written templates with knowledge triples (subject, relation, object) available from Wikidata, which
286
      involve only the multimodal queries. As the test dataset is not available, we use the validation set as
      our test set, and split the training set into training and validation subsets with a 9:1 ratio.
287
288   ViQuAE (Lerner et al., 2022) is a dataset focused about human entities. It provides both text-based
289   and multimodal queries, with each query linked to a specific section of a Wikipedia document that
290   contains an answer (annotated by humans), which makes it an idea benchmark for section retrieval.
291   Open-WikiTable (Kweon et al., 2023) is an extension of WikiSQL (Zhong et al., 2017) and Wik-
292   iTableQuestions (Pasupat & Liang, 2015), designed for open-domain table question answering that
293   requires retrieval of the most relevant table from a broader corpus. For our experiments, we adapt
294   this dataset, aiming at identifying the document or document section containing the target table, and
295   correspondingly, utilize the WikiTableQuestions subset of Open-WikiTable that has labels for it.
296
297   4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
298   Model Training and Evaluation We use LLaVA-NeXT-Interleave (Li et al., 2024) of 0.5B pa-
299   rameters as the basis VLM, for both the retriever and reranker. To take the advantage of larger batch
300   sizes (while reducing GPU memory usage), we apply LoRA (Hu et al., 2022). Also, to further op-
301   timize the GPU usage, we combine four images into one, scaling each down to half of its original
302   height and width. During retriever and reranker training, we consider four sections per document in
303   representing documents and selecting negative samples. In contrast, during inference, we consider
304   all sections within each document. For section retrieval, the top 25 documents retrieved are split into
305   sections and passed to the rerankers. All experiments are conducted using a single H100 GPU.
306
      Baselines We compare our approach against a variety of IR baselines designed to capture dif-
307
      ferent document representations. We start with Entity and Summary baselines, which are trained to
308
      retrieve documents based on their titles and summary sections. Next, we consider the Text-document
309   retriever, which retrieves documents based on their textual content. Additionally, to consider a visual
310   component, we consider the Single-image baseline, incorporating the first document image.
311
312   Evaluation Metrics We evaluate the performance of the retriever and reranker with standard met-
313   rics: Recall@K (R@K) and Mean Reciprocal Rank@K (MRR@K). First, R@K measures whether
314   the relevant document or section is retrieved within the top-K results. MRR@K evaluates the rank-
315   ing quality by measuring the position of the first relevant item among the top-K retrieved results.
316

456     4.4        F URTHER A NALYSIS AND A BLATION
457
458     More sections enhance document retrieval performance but raise computational costs. In Ta-
459     ble 1 and Table 2, we observe that using the comprehensive multimodal content and enriched contex-
460     tual information significantly improves document retrieval performance. Accordingly, we anticipate
461
        further improvements as more sections are gathered to represent the document, during training. To
        validate this, we measure the document retrieval performance with varying the number of sections
462
        per document on the InfoSeek dataset for training. The results shown in Figure 3 then indicate that
463
        incorporating more sections raises the MRR@10 score from 7.5 to 15.7. However, this performance
464     boost comes with a clear trade-off; as the number of sections increases, the retriever must process
465     additional end-of-section tokens, leading to higher GPU memory consumption. To balance resource
466     limitations and performance gains, we select four sections per document for all experiments.
467
468     Sections from the same document act as effective negatives to enhance reranker performance.
469     We explore another method to improve IR effectiveness by leveraging the entire document. Specifi-
470     cally, we investigate the use of sections from the same document as negatives for reranker training,
471     namely In-document. We compare this approach with traditional methods, including Top-K, which
472     selects the top-K retrieved sections as negatives, and In-batch, which uses the positive sections for
473     other samples in the same batch as negatives. After training rerankers with each method, we evaluate
474
        section retrieval on the Encyclopedic-VQA dataset. The results shown in Table 6 demonstrate that
        our In-document approach achieves superior R@1 and MRR@10 scores. This suggests that the use
475
        of sections from the same document as negatives provides natural, cost-effective advantages thanks
476
        to their high similarity to the positive section. However, it does not consistently outperform the other
477     methods on the R@10 score. We hypothesize that this inconsistency may arise from the strengths of
478     each method: the In-document approach excels at distinguishing sections from the same document,
479     while Top-K and In-batch methods better differentiate sections from different documents.
480
481     BCE loss applied to each section produces the best reranker performance. In our reranker de-
482     sign, we apply BCE loss using the query concatenated with each document section (Section+BCE).
483     We also explore alternative training objectives to identify the most effective approach for section re-
484     trieval in interleaved documents. One such objective is contrastive loss (Contrastive). This approach
485     is similar to the retriever, but the retrieval unit is a section. Additionally, we also explore a variant of
        the BCE loss (Document+BCE), where, unlike Section+BCE, the query is concatenated with multi-

514   Rerankers require much larger datasets than retrievers. We analyze the effect of different
515   dataset sizes for training on retriever and reranker performance. To achieve this, we randomly prune

517   trained on these subsets. In Figure 4 (a), we observe that too many samples can degrade retrieval per-
518   formance. Also, retrieval of textual queries requires fewer samples to reach its optimal performance
519
      compared to multimodal retrieval. Similarly, in Figure 4 (b), section retrieval for multimodal queries
      requires 10% of the dataset to achieve 80% of the full-dataset performance, while section retrieval
520
      for textual queries needs only 5%. These observations suggest that additional modalities increase
521
      the need for more data. This accounts for the inferior performance of the interleaved format in the
522   ViQuAE experiments (Table 4 (a)). The ViQuAE dataset, at only 2.2% of the size of Encyclopedic-
523   VQA, may be small for the reranker to effectively learn multimodal query-section alignments. We
524   also observe that section retrieval is more challenging, with more samples improving the reranker’s
525   performance. This explains why the ViQuAE reranker has much lower section retrieval scores com-
526   pared to the one trained on the Encyclopedic-VQA (Table 4 (b)). Given the challenge of obtaining
527   large query-section pair samples, exploring more effective reranker training pipelines is necessary.

529   5        C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduced IDentIfy, a novel IR framework designed to address the limitations of
      conventional methods that rely on solely textual content of documents and their segmented passages.
532
      Specifically, our approach sits on top of recent VLMs, which enables integration and representation
533
      of diverse multimodal content (including text, images, and tables) into a unified document repre-
534   sentation. Also, unlike previous strategies that segment documents at the passage level, our method
535   merges these segments to maintain the document’s structural coherence, while further introducing a
536   reranking strategy for precise identification of relevant sections. Extensive experiments across vari-
537   ous IR datasets demonstrated that IDentIfy consistently outperforms existing baselines, confirming
538   that the interleaved multimodal representation significantly enhances the quality of the document re-
539   trieval. We believe IDentIfy represents a crucial step toward more comprehensive and contextually
      aware IR systems, capable of handling the increasing multimodality of modern information sources.

773   Dataset configuration Table 8 summarizes the key properties of the datasets used in our experi-
774   ment, including query modality, target item, entity domain, number of entities, and whether a section
775   ID is provided to indicate the section containing the answer. Additionally, we provide the number
776   of samples in the training, evaluation, and test splits, as well as the size of the corpus.
777
778   Dataset pre-processing In our study, we leverage interleaved multimodal content from Wikipedia
779   documents. However, the existing corpora associated with our IR datasets often lack this content,
780   typically only including the first few words of each document. Therefore, we augment the corpora
781
      by downloading the HTML file of each Wikipedia document.
782   If the dataset provides Wikipedia URLs for its corpus, we use them to download the HTML files.
783   Alternatively, if only entity names are provided, we generate Wikipedia URLs using those names.
784   If a Wikipedia URL is deprecated, we remove the corresponding document from the corpus along
785   with any associated queries. From the HTML files, we extract text, image URLs, and tables. We
786   then split the contents by subtitles in the document where each chunk corresponds to a section. For
787
      the images, we use the image URLs to download the corresponding images, removing any invalid
      URLs. This process produces a dictionary that organizes text, images, and tables by section.
788
789   Since downloading the complete contents for all documents across datasets is time- and memory-
790   intensive, we preprocess the subsets of each corpus, including documents relevant to queries in the
791   training, evaluation, and test splits, as well as unrelated entity documents.
792
793   B      L IMITATIONS
794
795   Due to the limitations of a single H100 GPU, we represent documents by selecting a limited number
796   of sections and averaging their corresponding embeddings. While this reduces the computational de-
797   mands, our findings suggest that capturing a broader document context leads to improved retrieval
798   performance. Hence, leveraging the long context window of LVLMs could further enhance docu-
799   ment retrieval by capturing more comprehensive information from the full document. Moreover, our
800   reranker design follows the conventional approach of concatenating the input query with individual
801
      sections. However, we believe that providing the reranker with all the sections together would allow
      the model to better leverage the contextual information from the entire interleaved document, po-
802
      tentially resulting in improved performance. In order to fully leverage the interleaved format in the
803
      IR system, addressing the issues by reducing the GPU load when processing interleaved documents
804   would greatly boost overall IR performance. We leave these explorations for future work.
