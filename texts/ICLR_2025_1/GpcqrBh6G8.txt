009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The most promising approach to achieving nuclear fusion is through tokamaks,
012            which confine plasma using magnetic fields. Understanding the current plasma
013            equilibrium state in tokamaks is critical for effective plasma control. Unlike pre-
014            vious studies, which reconstruct equilibrium from magnetic field information, our
015            work forecasts future equilibrium based on past equilibrium states. Specifically,
016            we formulate the plasma equilibrium prediction task as a video prediction task,
017            a well-explored problem in the machine learning community. This formulation
018            allows us to capture the spatio-temporal dynamics of plasma states and provides
019            a foundation for multimodal modeling of data streams from tokamak operations.
020
               Our methodology, incorporating a physics-inspired learning technique for physi-
               cally reliable predictions, achieved plausible results in forecasting future plasma
021
               equilibrium up to 200 ms ahead compared to baselines. This approach holds
022
               promise for predicting plasma instabilities and preventing disruptions, marking
023            a significant step towards developing stable fusion reactors.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   One way to categorize human history is by examining the main energy sources utilized in each era.
030   From the discovery of fire to the exploitation of fossil fuels, and more recently, to the adoption
031   of nuclear energy and solar power, humanity has continually evolved its primary energy sources.
032   To address climate change and global energy shortages facing humanity, and to enter a new era,
033
      developing new clean energy sources has become an urgent mission. Nuclear fusion technology
      emerges as the most promising candidate for next-generation clean energy and has been studied for
034
      decades (Sheffield, 1994; Harms et al., 2000; Freidberg, 2007). It offers the potential for nearly
035
      limitless, clean energy production by replicating the processes that power the sun.
036
037   Currently, the most viable approach for achieving nuclear fusion is through tokamak devices such as
038   the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor (ITER), the largest international collabora-
039
      tive scientific project in the world (Rebut, 1995). Tokamaks use powerful electromagnets to confine
      high-temperature plasma within a vacuum vessel to induce nuclear fusion reactions. However, nu-
040
      merous scientific and engineering challenges hinder the commercialization of nuclear fusion. In de-
041
      tail, one of the primary obstacles is plasma instability and the resulting plasma disruptions (Schuller,
042   1995). To predict and prevent these issues, it is essential to understand plasma equilibrium, a state
043   where the magnetic field’s confining force on plasma particles balances the force of the particles
044   attempting to diffuse outward. While plasma equilibrium information can be computed by analyz-
045   ing magnetic field data using an algorithm called equilibrium fitting (EFIT) (Lao et al., 1985), its
046   application is limited due to the difficulty of performing real-time calculations.
047
      To overcome these challenges, the nuclear fusion community has recently begun to actively intro-
048   duce machine learning (ML) and data-driven approaches (Seo et al., 2021; Degrave et al., 2022; Lao
049   et al., 2022; Char et al., 2023; Joung et al., 2023; Seo et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024). ML techniques
050   are being applied to tasks such as controlling tokamaks, predicting disruptions, and inferring plasma
051   equilibrium. Along with the aforementioned studies, this work aims to address challenges in the
052   nuclear fusion domain using data-driven methods, specifically by forecasting plasma equilibrium in
053   tokamaks. Unlike the previous study that predicted plasma equilibrium using magnetic field data,
      we propose predicting future plasma equilibrium based on past plasma equilibrium data. This ap-

      proach enables us to address the temporal dynamics of plasma, introducing a novel problem setting
055   in fusion research that goes beyond existing studies focusing on current-state reconstruction.
056
057   More precisely, we formulated the problem as a video prediction task by interpreting plasma equilib-

      to plasma data and introduced methods to incorporate plasma physics knowledge into the model

061   long-term prediction, as plasma physics typically deals with phenomena occurring on timescales of
062   a few to tens of milliseconds inside a tokamak.
063
064
      Our research introduces a novel data-driven approach to nuclear fusion, an under-explored field
      within the ML community. By tackling complex spatio-temporal plasma equilibrium data rather
065

066
      2023; Char et al., 2023), we demonstrate the potential of ML in fusion science. In this manner, our
067   work can serve as a foundation for developing multimodal models in fusion research, paving the
068   way for the simultaneous analysis of diverse fusion data types. Our findings, based on data from
069   Korea Superconducting Tokamak Advanced Research (KSTAR) (Lee et al., 2001), are applicable to
070   ITER, given the structural similarities between these tokamaks. Finally, current research efforts are
071   focused on developing a digital twin of KSTAR and conducting virtual fusion experiments (Kwon
072   et al., 2022). Our study has the potential to significantly advance the ultimate goal of comprehensive
073   virtual fusion experimentation.
074   The contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:
075
076             • We formulated the plasma equilibrium forecasting problem as a video prediction task, in-
077               troducing this under-explored problem to both nuclear fusion and ML communities.
078             • We introduced a physics-based loss function inspired by plasma equilibrium equations and
079               demonstrated its effectiveness.
080             • We evaluated our proposed model against widely-used baseline methods across various
081               domains and interpreted the results from the plasma physics perspective.

090   2.1      N UCLEAR FUSION AND T OKAMAK
091
092   Nuclear fusion is a physical process in which two light atomic nuclei combine into a single heavier
093   nucleus under high temperature and pressure, simultaneously releasing energy. This process results
094   in plasma, known as the fourth state of matter, where electrons are separated from atomic nuclei
095   and can move freely. Einstein’s famous equation E = mc2 can be used to calculate the amount of
096
      energy produced during nuclear fusion, showing that even a small amount of fuel can generate an
      enormous amount of energy. For example, to produce the same energy as 125 kg of deuterium and
097
      tritium fuel, approximately 2.7 million tons of coal are needed 1 .
098
099   In fact, nuclear fusion reactions occur in the sun, and its brightness is due to these reactions. While
100   nuclear fusion in the sun occurs at approximately 15 million Kelvin due to immense gravitational
101   pressure, achieving fusion on Earth requires temperatures of 100–150 million Kelvin because we
102
      cannot replicate the sun’s gravitational conditions. However, building a vessel or material that can
      directly contact plasma at such high temperatures is practically infeasible. Therefore, researchers
103
      have devised a method using magnetic fields to confine the plasma in a vacuum state, preventing
104
      it from contacting the vessel walls. This concept is implemented in devices known as tokamaks,
105   which are donut-shaped machines. The tokamak consists of various components, namely multiple
106   electromagnets for confining and controlling plasma, heating systems such as neutral beam injection
107

      (NBI) and electron cyclotron resonance (ECR) heating systems, and multiple types of sensors for
109   monitoring the tokamak’s operational status.
110
111   Representative tokamak devices, including KSTAR (Lee et al., 2001) in South Korea, DIII-
112
      D (Luxon, 2002) in the United States, EAST (the EAST et al., 2009) in China, and JET (Jacquinot

117   KSTAR; consequently, our findings have potential applications in the prediction and analysis of
118   long-duration tokamak operations.

120   2.2   P LASMA EQUILIBRIUM
121
122   The most crucial information for controlling and analyzing plasma inside a tokamak is plasma equi-
123   librium. Plasma equilibrium refers to the balance between the magnetic forces confining the plasma
124   and the outward pressure of the plasma itself. The Grad-Shafranov (GS) equation (Grad & Rubin,
125   1958; Shafranov, 1966), a partial differential equation describing this equilibrium state, illustrates
126   the spatial distribution of magnetic fields under conditions satisfying plasma equilibrium:
127
128                                                          dp(ψ)         dF (ψ)
                              ∆∗ ψ = −µ0 RJϕ = −µ0 R2              − F (ψ)        .                        (1)
129                                                           dψ            dψ
130
131   In this equation, ψ = ψ(R, Z) represents the poloidal magnetic flux function dependent on coordi-
132   nates R and Z, defined within the cylindrical coordinate system of KSTAR. The term µ0 denotes
133   the permeability of free space, while p(ψ) represents the plasma pressure and F (ψ) is related to
134   the toroidal magnetic field, both being functions of ψ. ∆∗ ψ is a two-dimensional non-linear partial
                                                       ∂2
135   derivative of ψ, defined as ∆∗ = R ∂R∂ 1 ∂
                                              R ∂R + ∂Z 2 .
136   Based on the R, Z, and Jϕ , which is the toroidal component of the plasma current density, we can
137   obtain the solution ψ (and ∆∗ ψ) of the GS equation. This information serves as an essential key to
138   understanding the state of the plasma for controlling and analyzing the tokamak. However, it is chal-
139   lenging to determine the solution of the GS equation, and the EFIT algorithm (Lao et al., 1985) was
140   developed to effectively find the solution. In brief, EFIT employs an iterative calculation process to
141   find the solution based on given initial conditions. Therefore, it has limitations in scenarios requiring
142   real-time application such as tokamak control due to its heavy computational time. Consequently,
143
      the real-time EFIT (rt-EFIT) algorithm (Ferron et al., 1998; Moret et al., 2015; Huang et al., 2017)
      has been proposed to reduce computational time by using the solutions at the previous time step as
144
      initial values, but this approach is also limited due to lower accuracy.
145
146   The reconstruction of plasma equilibrium through EFIT calculations provides abundant information
147   for both controlling and understanding plasma behavior. Primarily, the key output ψ is utilized
148   to determine the last closed flux surface (LCFS). The LCFS, which delineates the boundary of
149
      the confined plasma, is a crucial parameter in tokamak operations. By accurately identifying the
      LCFS, researchers can enhance plasma stability, optimize tokamak operational methods, and gain
150
      deeper insights into plasma dynamics. Furthermore, the results from the EFIT calculations can
151
      be combined with various experimental data collected from tokamak diagnostics. This allows for
152   the derivation of important plasma parameters, including temperature profiles, density distributions,
153   and magnetic pressure, which are crucial for optimizing plasma stability, confinement efficiency,
154   and fusion reaction rates.

156   2.3   DATA - DRIVEN APPROACHES IN FUSION DOMAIN
157
158   Due to the computational cost of the EFIT algorithm, data-driven approaches have been recently
159   proposed as alternatives to predict plasma equilibrium faster and with comparable accuracy to the
160   EFIT algorithm (Joung et al., 2019; Wai et al., 2022; Lao et al., 2022; Joung et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
161   2023). GS-DeepNet serves as a notable example (Joung et al., 2023). This model predicts the current
      plasma equilibrium in real time with the current magnetic field information given as input. Notably,

162
      GS-DeepNet introduced an unsupervised learning mechanism without requiring ground truth EFIT
163   data. Our method, in contrast, predicts the current or future equilibrium based on past equilibrium
164   information without magnetic field data, and can potentially collaborate with the aforementioned
165   methods by bootstrapping from the equilibria they reconstruct.
166
      Beyond the plasma equilibrium prediction, data-driven techniques are increasingly being applied
167
      to various challenges in the field of nuclear fusion. For example, Seo et al. (2021), Degrave et al.
168
      (2022), and Char et al. (2023) demonstrated the potential of ML in tokamak control; moreover, Seo
169   et al. (2024) and Kim et al. (2024) showed that ML-based control can help avoid plasma instabilities,
170   which are a major challenge in tokamak operation. While most studies have utilized a number of
171   simple and raw features from the tokamak as inputs, our method distinguishes itself by leveraging
172   spatio-temporal information, such as ψ and ∆∗ ψ from the EFIT calculation. This approach allows
173   us to capture complex plasma dynamics and their evolution over time, providing a framework that
174   can be extended to support diverse types of data with complex structures generated from tokamaks.
175   Our study, along with existing research, establishes a foundation for the development of multimodal
176   models capable of integrating and understanding the enormous volume of data from tokamaks.

      3.1   F ORMULATING PLASMA EQUILIBRIUM PREDICTION AS VIDEO PREDICTION
188
189   Video prediction is a task of predicting future frames based on the given past frames. Formally, given
190   T past frames Xt−T :t−1 = {xi }t−1                                                        ′
                                        t−T at time t, the objective of this task is to obtain T future frames
                             ′
191   Yt:t+T ′ −1 = {xi }tt+T −1 , where xi represents an image of dimensions C ×H ×W , with C channels
192   and spatial resolution of H × W . The EFIT data from fusion experiments have similar features to
193   video data, allowing us to approach fusion plasma prediction as a video prediction problem. In
194   this context, each EFIT frame xi can be considered as a two-dimensional image where channels
195
      correspond to ψ and ∆∗ ψ.
196   This video-based approach offers several advantages over the traditional pixel-level approach, which
197   treated ψ and ∆∗ ψ values at each point as separate data. By simultaneously utilizing ψ and ∆∗ ψ
198   information from multiple points, we can expect more accurate predictions. For instance, this com-
199   prehensive approach enables the model to identify complex spatial patterns and relationships that
200   traditional pixel-level methods could not capture. Furthermore, this framework has the potential to
201
      be extended to a multimodal model in the future by employing additional spatio-temporal data col-
      lected from tokamaks. Moreover, by processing spatial and temporal information concurrently, this
202
      method offers an opportunity to analyze plasma dynamics from a spatio-temporal perspective. This
203
      is particularly significant because the aforementioned GS equation, which is currently the primary
204   method for reconstructing plasma equilibrium, describes only spatial changes but not temporal evo-
205   lution. If the model based on video prediction approach provides us with significant results regarding
206   plasma’s temporal dynamics, it could lead to novel scientific insights into plasma behavior.
207
      This intersection of physics and ML is promising (Chen et al., 2022), as ML researchers’ expertise
208
      in video prediction can offer fresh perspectives unconstrained by traditional physical knowledge. To
209
      this end, we explore the application of SimVP (Gao et al., 2022), a widely adopted and versatile
210   video prediction model architecture. SimVP processes input frames by employing spatial down-
211   sampling, transforming spatio-temporal features through inception modules, and decoding them to
212   generate predictions at the original resolution. Despite the existence of various video prediction
213   models, we selected this one for its straightforward and versatile nature in video prediction tasks.
214   Specifically, SimVP does not make assumptions about the data type, making it an ideal candidate
215   for the plasma equilibrium prediction task. This flexibility is crucial for our future plans to develop
      multimodal models capable of integrating diverse data from tokamak operations.

      3.2       G RAD -S HAFRANOV CONSTRAINT LOSS
217
218   The ψ and ∆∗ ψ data used as input and output in our study are derived from the EFIT algorithm,
219   which is based on the GS equation (Equation 1 in Section 2.2). Essentially, these data inherently
220   satisfy the GS equation, implying that any accurate prediction from our model should also follow
221   this fundamental constraint. To ensure this, we introduce a GS-constraint loss to train the model in
222   addition to the mean absolute error (MAE) on ψ and ∆∗ ψ. The GS-constraint loss helps to maintain
223   the physical consistency of our predictions within the theoretical foundation of plasma equilibrium.
224                                                                                2
      In detail, we apply ∆∗ operator, defined as ∆∗ = R ∂R ∂ 1 ∂      ∂
                                                               R ∂R + ∂Z 2 , to the model output ψpred
225   to calculate ∆ ψest . Subsequently, we compare it with the model output ∆∗ ψpred to compute the
                     ∗
226   MAE, which serves as our GS-constraint loss as follows:
227
228
229                       LGS = E ∥∆∗ ψest − ∆∗ ψpred ∥1 = E ∥D∆∗ (ψpred ) − ∆∗ ψpred ∥1 .                      (2)
230
231   Here, D∆∗ (ψpred ) represents the application of the ∆∗ operator to ψpred using finite difference ap-
232   proximations. Since ψ and ∆∗ ψ are defined on a fixed grid over R and Z, and since the coordinates
233   R and Z are fixed in our data representation, we cannot use automatic differentiation to compute
234
      spatial derivatives in this case. Alternatively, following Ren et al. (2022), we approximated the ∆∗
      operator by employing a finite difference method, shifting ψ horizontally and vertically. Although
235
      this approximation technique is numerically less accurate than actual differentiation, it presents the
236
      most feasible solution for our problem. The approximation error (reported in Table 1 using † sym-
237   bol) in the GS-constraint loss term could lead to inaccurate predictions. Therefore, we scaled the
238   GS-constraint loss by a coefficient less than one and added it to the pixel MAE loss in order to
239   mitigate numerical errors: LT otal = LM AE + αLGS , where α = 0.01.
240
      Meanwhile, one might propose using the leftmost and rightmost terms of Equation 1 for constrain-
241
      ing the model predictions. This approach could allow for the natural calculation of derivative values
242
      through automatic differentiation. However, this approach faces practical limitations due to the am-
243   biguity in the GS equation, which does not provide information about the exact forms of p(ψ) and
244   F (ψ). Specifically, although we understand the physical meaning of p and F , we cannot precisely
245   determine their functional forms or characteristics as functions of ψ. In practice, the spatial distribu-
246   tion of current (F F ′ term) in the GS equation is typically approximated using low-order functions,
247   such as first to third-order polynomials. Consequently, we opted for the previously described loss
248   based on finite differences in a conservative manner. This conservative choice was made to ensure
249   both numerical stability during training and physical fidelity of the model, as the alternative method
250   could potentially lead to ambiguous physical interpretations and unstable training results. While
251
      this limitation could be addressed if additional conditions or data for p and F were available, such
      considerations remain a topic for future research 2 .

      Evaluation metric The basic evaluation was conducted using the pixel-level MAE, a common
318   metric in video prediction tasks. In our work, we computed MAE for specific regions of interest
319   in the ψ and ∆∗ ψ, rather than considering the entire region. The first area is the central region.
320   This area was prioritized because the prediction accuracy in the outer regions, where plasma does
321   not exist, is relatively less important. Second, we assessed the divertor region. The divertor is a
322   crucial component of the tokamak, responsible for extracting high-temperature exhaust and residues
323   from the plasma. Accurate prediction of plasma boundary in the divertor is essential for sustaining
      long-period operations and preventing damage to the tokamak. In detail, the plasma boundary al-

324
      lows us to predict and evaluate the accuracy of key features such as the X-point (the intersection of
325   magnetic flux surfaces) and the divertor strike points where plasma and residues make contact with
326   the divertor. However, predicting these features accurately requires centimeter to millimeter-scale
327   precision (Eldon et al., 2020), which presents a challenge for the spatial resolution of our current
328   EFIT data. In this context, we extended our evaluation to assess the accuracy of the LCFS deter-
329   mined from the predicted ψ. As a potential alternative evaluation metric, we experimented with
330   intersection over union (IoU) and mean IoU (mIoU) to compare the similarity between the ground
331   truth and predicted LCFS shapes. The detailed analysis of these metrics and their limitations are
332   discussed in Section B.

334   Baseline Since our proposed method differs from existing research in both approach and results,
335
      it is challenging in making direct and fair comparisons. To address this, we selected several base-
      line methods that are widely adopted across different domains. First, we employed a time-lagged
336
      baseline, widely recognized as a simple yet robust benchmark in time series forecasting problems.
337
      This baseline outputs ground truth values from our EFIT dataset, delayed by a single time step.
338   In detail, we applied a 50 ms time lag, which represents a significantly long period in the context
339   of plasma dynamics. For example, plasma dynamics is typically analyzed with a time resolution
340   of less than 10 ms, and tokamak control systems require a response time on the order of a few
341   ms. We also presented a constant prediction baseline that assumes that the most recently observed
342   plasma state persists unchanged. Next, we conducted experiments using ConvLSTM (Shi et al.,
343   2015), PredRNN (Wang et al., 2017), and PredRNN-v2 (Wang et al., 2023), which are fundamen-
344   tal video prediction models. Finally, we compared our results with dynamic mode decomposition
345   (DMD) (Schmid, 2010), a widely-used linear model for predicting spatio-temporal dynamics, and
346
      Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024), a general-purpose pretrained time series forecasting model.

348   4.3   T RAINING AND IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
349
      In our experiments, we used the implementation of model architectures from the OpenSTL li-
350
      brary (Tan et al., 2022; 2023), while developing training and evaluation code from scratch to fit
351   our problem setup. For hyperparameters, SimVP was configured with NS = 4 and NT = 8 layers,
352   with hidden dimensions of dS = 64 and dT = 256, respectively. The ConvLSTM model consisted
353   of four layers, each with a hidden dimension of 96. PredRNN and PredRNN-v2 were also config-
354   ured with four layers, but each utilized a hidden dimension of 128. To simplify our experiments, we
355   set the input and output frame lengths to be equal, in other words, T = T ′ . These sequences contain
356   one, four, or eight frames, representing time spans of 50 ms, 200 ms, and 400 ms, respectively.
357   The models shared the following training hyperparameters: We utilized the AdamW opti-
358   mizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with a learning rate of 10−4 , weight decay of 0.05, and gradient
359   clipping of 1. The dataset was sampled every four timesteps, and the batch size was set to 128.
360   The number of training epochs was 1, 500, and we applied an exponential moving average update
361   with β = 0.9999. To ensure statistical robustness, all experiments were repeated with three random
362   seeds, and we reported the average values. Each experiment was conducted on a single NVIDIA
363   H100 GPU and completed within 48 hours.
364   Predictions with DMD were generated autoregressively from the last observation after fitting it with
365   T past frames. Since DMD inherently operates on 1D vectors, we tested two approaches: (1) flat-
366   tening the 2D EFIT data into 1D vectors and (2) performing predictions on a per-pixel basis. For
367   the flattening approach, we considered both joint prediction of ψ and ∆∗ ψ (i.e., predicting a vector
368   of dimension 64 × 64 × 2 = 8, 192) and separate prediction (i.e., predicting two vectors, each of di-
369   mension 64 × 64 = 4, 096). All DMD experiments were conducted using the PyDMD library (Demo
370   et al., 2018). As for the Chronos model, we employed Chronos-t5-base, which has a com-

      parable number of parameters to SimVP, and assessed its zero-shot accuracy without finetuning.

418   Input configuration and Prediction length Prior to our experiments, we hypothesized that in-
419   corporating heating information, which partially represents control signals of the tokamak, would
420   enhance the prediction accuracy of the models. However, contrary to our expectations, Table 1 and
421   Figure 2 (a) demonstrate that the inclusion of heating data did not bring improvement, or led to
422
      a decrease in accuracy in some cases. These unexpected results suggest that developing a multi-
      modal model capable of integrating multiple data types from a tokamak requires more than simply
423
      adding extra input channels. It indicates a need for architectural improvements in the model design
424
      to properly leverage these additional data sources.

427   curate forecasting of ψ and ∆∗ ψ. In fact, heating in a tokamak is not limited to external devices
428
      such as ECR and NBI. There is also Ohmic heating, where the plasma is heated by its own electrical
      resistance. If Ohmic heating contributes more significantly to the future plasma equilibrium than the
429
      ECR and NBI, this could explain the current performance degradation when including heating data.

513   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this paper, we introduced a novel problem of forecasting future plasma equilibrium in tokamaks
516   based on past states. We formulated this as a video prediction problem and conducted experiments
517   across various methods and scenarios. Our results showed that the models can predict the poloidal
518   magnetic flux (ψ) up to 200 ms into the future more accurately than the baselines. In addition, we
519   found that introducing a loss function based on the GS equation improved the predictions of ψ and
520
      resulted in physically reliable forecasting. However, we observed an unexpected accuracy degrada-
      tion when integrating partial tokamak control signals, i.e., NBI and ECR heating data, suggesting the
521
      need for further investigation to effectively leverage additional data from the tokamak. We conclude
522
      by discussing the potential applications of our results in the fusion domain and future directions.
523
524
525
      Outlook In recent years, the nuclear fusion community has shown increasing interest in develop-
      ing digital twins of tokamaks (Kwon et al., 2022; Iglesias et al., 2017). Beyond simple 3D models,
526
      these digital twins serve as tools for virtual fusion experiments that integrate various experimental
527
      data and simulation results. This approach offers two significant advantages: it reduces the time and
528   energy costs required for each discharge, and it can minimize the risk of damage in the tokamak
529   that may occur during experiments. A notable example is the Virtual KSTAR (Kwon et al., 2022),
530   a digital twin of KSTAR. The techniques developed in this project are expected to be applied in the
531   future development of digital twins for ITER and K-DEMO (Cho et al., 2022).
532
      Typically, fusion experiments are conducted based on detailed control scenarios, and virtual exper-
533
      iments follow a similar approach; The system should be capable of generating simulated plasma
534   discharge results based on input scenarios describing the operational plan. To achieve this, a multi-
535   modal model is required to efficiently utilize not only current EFIT data but also various data from
536   the tokamak, including heating information and magnetic field data (i.e., control information). In
537   this context, our proposed method for predicting plasma equilibrium can be extended to serve as
538   a foundation for fusion multimodal models. We believe that our results can potentially contribute
539   to predicting plasma instabilities, preventing disruptions, and gaining novel insights into plasma
      dynamics, thereby taking a significant step towards realizing data-driven stable fusion reactors.

776    The nuclear fusion community has typically measured accuracy by comparing parametric informa-
777
       tion extractable from the LCFS, such as poloidal angle (Wan et al., 2023). However, these methods
       are limited to examining only partial aspects of the LCFS shape. This limitation highlights the need
778
       for quantitative evaluation metrics that can measure the similarity of the overall LCFS shape.
779
780    To address this issue, we experimented with the IoU metric, which is familiar to ML researchers, as a
781    potential method to compare the similarity between the ground truth LCFS and the predicted LCFS.
782    The process begins by drawing N contour lines using Matplotlib (Hunter, 2007). These lines are
783
       stored as lists of (R, Z) coordinates, allowing for the identification of closed surfaces by comparing
       the start and end coordinates of each contour line. The outermost closed surface is selected as the
784
       LCFS and converted into a Polygon object of Shapely library to calculate the IoU. We calculated
785
       the IoU for each timestep and then reported the mIoU across the entire test data.
786
787    While this method of estimating the LCFS and IoU is straightforward, it has limitations that hinder
788    its use as a primary evaluation metric. As illustrated in Figure 6, the IoU calculation is sensitive to
789
       the number of contour lines used. Based on this result, we concluded that IoU should be used only
       as a supplementary evaluation metric to MAE.
