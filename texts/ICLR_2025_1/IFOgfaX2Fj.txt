011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Hip arthroplasty is a surgical procedure that involves the replacement of a pa-
014            tient’s hip joint with a prosthetic implant. While these implants are initially effec-
015            tive, they may eventually fail and necessitate revision surgery. It is important to
016
               identify the 3 Charnley and 7 Gruen zones around the implant and then identify
               the zone-wise radiolucency which indicates loosening for effective pre and post-
017
               operative planning. Despite the importance of zones, there is a lack of automation
018
               attempts in this field. In this work, we have proposed a 3-stage algorithm that
019            detects the sanity of the image for diagnosis, segments into the zones, and then
020            identifies radiolucency within the zones. We have demonstrated a 94% accuracy
021            for Fit/Not Fit segregation, a 0.95 dice score for our zonal segmentation, and a
022            98% overall loosening accuracy. Obtaining an average dice score of 0.92 in the
023            segmentation of zones and 0.93 accuracy on loosening detection on a blind dataset
024            indicates the robustness of the proposed algorithm. This work will contribute to
025            the development of more efficient and accurate models to detect implant loosen-
026            ing.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Total joint replacement (TJR) is a surgical procedure in which parts of an arthritic or damaged
032   joints are removed and replaced with a metal, plastic, or ceramic device called a prosthesis. The
033
      prosthesis is designed to replicate the movement of a normal healthy joint. TJR has been around
      for more than five decades now. There have been significant improvements in materials, design, and
034
      technique making these surgical procedures more dependable and reliable. However, as the number
035
      of cases grew problems also emerged which needed these prostheses to be revised due to wear and
036   aseptic loosening, sepsis or infection, and periprosthetic fractures as well as dislocations. Aseptic
037   loosening refers to the prosthesis loosening out without any evidence of infection. This is a gradual
038   process, may take several years to manifest, and often be asymptomatic. It may occur as a result
039   of undersized or poor surgical technique or as a consequence of an inflammatory reaction to wear
040   particles generated from polyethylene liners of the joint. This is one of the leading causes of revision
041   and depends on careful analysis of serial x-rays of the implant coupled with clinical assessment of
042   symptoms such as pain or limp. Analysis of an anteroposterior radiograph of the hip can indicate
043   the presence of radiolucent areas around the implant as shown in Fig 1. Progressive radiolucencies
044
      or osteolysis suggest that the prosthesis is loose, and also the implant’s positional variations about
      the bone are symptomatic of early loosening.
045
046   The widely used clinical protocol for assessing the implant health of femoral prosthesis as described
047   by Gruen and Charnley is by dividing the femoral region into 7 Gruen zones, and the cup region into
048   3 Charnley zones. This is shown in Fig 2. These zones help in identifying the extent of radiolucency
049   and the severity of loosening.
050   Loose implants need to be revised. The accurate diagnosis and effective intervention in orthope-
051   dic surgery heavily relies on the expertise of experienced radiologists and orthopedic surgeons. In
052   regions with limited access to orthopedic surgeons, particularly in underdeveloped and developing
053   nations, the healthcare system faces significant strain. Identifying the areas of implant loosening
      and formulating a revision plan is a time-consuming task for experts when performed manually.

      2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
085
086
087   The challenge of identifying various anomalies in implants has been addressed by a few studies.
088
      There are few works reported on implant loosening detection. Rahman et al. created the aseptic hip
      loosening Kaggle dataset comprising 206 images for detecting hip arthroplasty loosening Rahman
089
      et al. (2022). They developed an object detection model using YOLO V5 to localize implants. They
090
      reported an accuracy of 94% for loosening detection using DenseNet. Alireza et al. conducted a
091   study focusing on hip loosening using DenseNet201 on a private dataset comprising 236 cementless
092   total hip replacement (THR) images from a cohort of 15277 THR patients. The model yielded an
093   area under the curve (AUC) of 84%, a specificity of 75.0%, and a sensitivity of 91.6% Alireza
094   et al. (2019). Lau et al. implemented the Xception neural network for 2-class classification in the
095   categories of ”loose” or ”control” and obtained an accuracy of 96.3% Lawrence et al. (2022). Kim
096   et al. Kim et al. (2023) investigated the effectiveness of using a deep convolutional neural network
097   (CNN) to identify the loosening of total knee arthroplasty (TKA) implants on plain radiographs us-
098   ing transfer learning. They utilized a pre-trained VGG and experimented with two techniques. In the
099
      first technique, they removed the fully connected layer and added a new one to create a new model.
      The convolutional layer was frozen without training, and only the fully connected layer was trained.
100
      In the second technique, a new model was created by adding a fully connected layer and adjusting
101
      the range of freezing for the convolutional layer. The second model demonstrated higher accuracy at
102   97.5%. Shah et al. Shah et al. (2020) aimed to assess the efficacy of a machine-learning algorithm
103   in diagnosing prosthetic loosening based on preoperative radiographs. The study gathered preop-
104   erative radiographs, as well as historical and comorbidity data from 697 patients who underwent
105   revision of total hip or total knee arthroplasty from 2012 to 2018. Convolutional neural network
106   (CNN) models were trained, demonstrating 70% accuracy in identifying prosthetic loosening from
107   radiographs alone. A detailed review paper on artificial intelligence for image analysis in total hip
      and total knee arthroplasty is given by Gurung et al. Gurung et al. (2022).

      There is not much work reported on implant zone segmentation. Asma et al. focussed on Gruen
109   zone segmentation using a multitask CNN for binary segmentation mapping of the implant and
110   detection of the implant tip point. The authors also utilized the statistical shape model to construct
111   a landmark-based shape model and fit this model to a new image using shape coefficients and pose

      Our research endeavors to provide a thorough health assessment of implants by segmenting them
114
      into the Charnley and Gruen zones, rather than simply classifying them as loose or control, as is
115   common in most research papers. The significance of our work lies in three key phases. Firstly,
116   we address the issue of poor image quality, including noise, blurriness, poor exposure, and artifacts,
117   which can lead to misdiagnosis and pose risks during surgical planning. To mitigate this, we have
118   developed a preliminary screening process for X-ray images, allowing surgeons to identify and
119   discard unsuitable images and request a rescan if necessary. Subsequently, we focus on segmenting
120   the implant into 11 zones (7 Gruen, 3 Charnley, and background). Finally, we utilize this zonal
121   information to identify zones with radiolucency, aiding surgeons in making well-informed decisions
122   regarding extent of loosening, affected zones, early signs of loosening, progress monitoring, and the
123
      necessity for revision surgery. To the best of our knowledge, we have not encountered any existing
      work that offers such a comprehensive analysis of the implant.

142   3.1   DATASET PREPARATION
143
144   Upon our thorough review, we have found that there is currently no open-source dataset containing
145   zonal regions (Gruen and Charnley) annotation and zone-wise loosening information available to the
146   best of our knowledge. Acquiring this information would significantly aid in accurately identifying
147
      radiolucency regions and improving the development of automated preplanning tools for implant
      revision surgery. Our study utilized the implant loosening detection dataset created by Rahman
148
      et al. Rahman et al. (2022), which consists of 206 single hip implant images, comprising 112 loose
149
      and 94 control images. These images were meticulously annotated by an orthopedic surgeon using
150   the ‘labelme’ annotation tool Russell et al. (2008), encompassing various zones, regions, landmark
151   points, and line-based annotations in both JSON and text formats. The annotations cover three
152   Charnley zones 1 to 3, seven Gruen zones 4 to 10, a femoral cup, and a stem, along with landmark
153   points such as the lesser trochanter, greater trochanter, ischium, medullary canal, pubis bone, neck
154   of the femoral stem, shoulder of the femoral stem, center of the femoral cup, acetabular component,
155   and calcar femoral. Furthermore, two lines were included: Line 1 for the lesser trochanter and Line
156   2 for the middle thirds. An illustrative example of the manual annotation is presented in Fig 3.
157   In conjunction with the JSON file, an Excel file has been created to indicate whether the image is
158   suitable for diagnosis, identify the presence of radiolucency, categorize the 10 zones (Gruen and
159   Charnley) as loose or control, determine any dislocation or missing cup, and pinpoint any break-
160   age or subsidence, specifying the affected zones and the total number of loose or control zones.
161   Some of these additional information has been utilized in our network for accurate segmentation
      and classification of zones.

200   check the fitness of the image for diagnosis. The block diagram for checking this fitness and zonal

      3.2.2    S TAGE 2 - I MPLANT ZONAL REGION SEGMENTATION :
210
211   Identification of 7 Gruen and 3 Charnley zones along with the loosening condition from each zone of
212   the preplanning x-ray image would enable the orthopedic surgeons to better understand the implant
213   condition and also help in planning the surgeries effectively. To achieve this, we converted the
214   annotated JSON file to an XML file and extracted the coordinates of each of the 10 zones. Our
215   primary aim was to identify any radiolucencies present in these specific regions, as radiolucency
      serves as a critical indicator of loosening and manifests exclusively within these 10 specific areas.

216
      We then created masks of these regions by marking the pixels of each class in a categorical template
217   (0 to background followed by 3 Charnley zones as 1 − 3 and then followed by 7 Gruen zones from
218   4 − 10). During the creation of these masks, we encountered the challenge of overlapping regions in
219   the annotations, which we carefully addressed to ensure separation and non-overlapping markings.
220
      The proposed segmentation network is structured as an encoder-decoder block as shown in Fig 4,
221
      wherein the encoder comprises 4 blocks, each consisting of 2D convolution, instance normalization,
222
      leaky ReLU activation, and dropout layers. It utilizes 64, 128, 512, and 1024 filters with a 3 × 3
223   kernel size and he norm as the kernel initializer. The decoder has the same 4 reverse blocks with
224   2D transpose convolution. The final layer of the network consists of an 11-class segmentation layer
225   with leaky ReLU activation and transpose convolution. This layer serves to segment the input image
226   into 10 distinct zones and a background. Moreover, skip connections are implemented between the
227   encoder and decoder, enhancing the overall performance of the network during the segmentation
228   process. The network employs the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 2−4 and a beta value of
229   0.5. A batch size of 8 is utilized, and the network is trained for 300 epochs.
230   The concept of semantic segmentation loss can be categorized into three primary groups: Pixel-
231   level, Region-level, and Boundary-level. The proposed segmentation network adopts a combination
232   approach that integrates elements from the pixel-level and region-level categories to optimize se-
233   mantic segmentation performance. This approach has significantly enhanced model performance,
234   particularly in the segmentation of 11 challenging classes, including 7 Gruen, 3 Charnley, and the
235   background region, each region with varying shapes and sizes across all images. The strategy of
236   incorporating multiple loss functions aims to strike a balance between pixel-wise precision, over-
237
      all object segmentation quality, and accuracy. In this approach, the cross-entropy loss (ce) ensures
      smooth gradients and precise segmentation at each pixel, while the dice loss (dc) prevents local min-
238
      ima and focuses on overall class segmentation by maximizing the alignment between the predicted
239
      segmentation and the ground truth mask. The combination of the losses is achieved by summing the
240   cross-entropy loss and the dice loss using an equal weighted contribution of each loss function. The
241   overall equation is defined as follows:

      5   C ONCLUSION
511
512
      In this study, a comprehensive analysis of implants has been proposed, encompassing the assessment
513
      of image quality, segmentation of the Gruen and Charnley zones, and the analysis of radiolucency
514   within these zones to determine the extent of implant loosening. This information is vital for both
515   planning implant revision surgeries and monitoring the overall implant health. Collaborating with
516   an orthopedic expert, we have developed an annotated dataset for zone segmentation and an accom-
517   panying Excel sheet containing zonal loosening information, as no such open-source dataset was
518   available. Our segmentation process achieved an average dice score of 95% for segmenting into the
519   standard zones, and an accuracy of 98% in identifying images indicative of loosening. Additionally,
520   we flagged images with artifacts, blurriness, or inadequate zone visibility. The proposed work also
521   assists the surgeons in determining cases that require immediate attention and also in identifying
522
      any early signs of loosening, and monitoring cases that require careful follow-up. Our future work
      aims to incorporate patients’ imaging history to identify any dislocations or implant sinking, along
523
      with detecting anomalies such as fractures, implant subsidence, and breakage. This comprehensive
524
      approach will facilitate the development of a complete implant health assessment suite.
