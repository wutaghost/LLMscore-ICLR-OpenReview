009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Vertical federated learning (VFL) is a type of federated learning where the col-
012            lection of different features is shared among multiple clients, and it is attracting
013            attention as a training method that takes into account the privacy and security of
014            training data. On the other hand, in federated learning, there is a threat of Byzan-
015            tine attacks, where some malicious clients disrupt the training of the model and
016            output an trained model that does not exhibit the behavior that should be obtained.
017            Thus far, numerous defense methods against Byzantine attacks on horizontal fed-
018            erated learning have been proposed, most of which focus on the similarity of the
019            models generated across clients having the similar features and mitigate the at-
020
               tacks by excluding outliers. However, in VFL, the feature sets assigned by each
               client are inherently different, making similar methods inapplicable, and there is
021
               little existing research in this area. In light of the above, this paper organizes
022
               and classifies feasible Byzantine attacks and proposes a new defense method CC-
023            VFed against these attack methods. Firstly, this paper organizes and classifies
024            attack methods that contaminate training data, demonstrating that sign-flipping
025            attacks pose a threat to VFL. Subsequently, in order to capture the differences in
026            client features, this paper proposes a method for detecting and neutralizing mali-
027            cious clients based on their contribution to output labels, demonstrating that it is
028            indeed possible to defend Byzantine attacks in VFL.

      1.1   BACKGROUND
034
035
      In recent years, artificial intelligence (AI) has been applied to solve various social issues. To enhance
036   the performance, it is necessary to train models using large amounts of data. However, when training
037   models using vast amounts of data, data collection and the lengthy training time pose challenges
038   in terms of computational cost. As the scale of models is expected to expand further, the issue
039   of computational cost is an unavoidable problem. Furthermore, privacy and security issues exist,
040   especially when handling personal information in vast amounts of data.
041
      Federated learning (McMahan et al., 2017) is a method that reduces the computational cost for each
042   participant (hereafter referred to as a client) by distributing model training among multiple clients
043   while considering the privacy and security of the training data. In federated learning, each client
044   trains a model using its dataset, and these models are integrated on a central server, resulting in a
045   large-scale model. Each participant can ensure privacy and security by training their models while
046   keeping their datasets confidential from the other participants. The primary federated learning meth-
047   ods are horizontal federated learning (HFL) (McMahan et al., 2017) and vertical federated learning
048   (VFL) (Vepakomma et al., 2018). These training methods are classified based on the structure of
049   each participant’s dataset. While HFL has been the main focus of traditional research, advancements
050
      in VFL have been reported recently.
051   HFL involves training models using datasets comprising the same features and is used for tasks such
052   as diagnosing diseases from X-ray images (Feki et al., 2021). Here, the datasets vary for clients.
053   Each client maintains a model with the same structure, and the models are integrated. HFL requires
      datasets with the same features.

054
      However, each client has its strengths and weaknesses in data collection, and there may be cases
055   in which the collection of features is divided. In such cases, HFL, which requires each client to
056   hold all the features, cannot be applied. VFL involves training models under the condition that each
057   client holds a partition of the features and is used for tasks such as determining creditworthiness
058   from transaction histories (He et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2023). In VFL, a single model is divided
059   among each client and the central server. This method is also referred to as split-federated learn-
060   ing. Only the central server knows the labels assigned to each data point. VFL involves exchanging
061   information between each client and the central server, updating their respective models based on
062   this information, and creating high-performance models. In the aforementioned example of credit-
063
      worthiness determination, the clients are banks and stores, and the central server is the institution
      determining creditworthiness. This study addresses VFL.
064
065   As threats to VFL, attacks targeting both the training phase (Chen et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2022;
066   He et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2022) and the inference phase (Duanyi et al., 2023;
067   Pang et al., 2022) have been proposed. If the inference phase is attacked, the generated model
068   itself remains legitimate. In contrast, if the training phase is attacked, the generated model becomes
069
      contaminated, necessitating the retraining of the model. The threat posed by attacks on the training
      phase is more significant, and this study focuses on discussing threats to the training phase.
070
071   The threats to VFL include attacks by malicious clients, label-inference attacks (Fu et al., 2022) and
072   poisoning attacks (Chen et al., 2024; He et al., 2023b) including Byzantine attacks (Xu et al., 2024;
073   Yuan et al., 2022). Label-inference attacks extract the labels of the training data held by the central
074   server. Poisson attacks degrade models by transmitting malicious data during training. This study
075
      focuses on poisoning attacks including Byzantine attacks.
076   Poisoning attacks are being studied actively both in terms of attack and defense methods. Poisoning
077   attacks are assaults in which the data provider to the client or the client itself can contaminate
078   a portion of the training data, causing the model to mispredict specific inputs without significantly
079   reducing its accuracy. Given the vast number of data points included in a dataset, prevention through
080   dataset verification is challenging, and various attacks (Cao & Gong, 2022; Chen et al., 2024; He
081
      et al., 2023b; Tolpegin et al., 2020) and defense methods (Cho et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2023; Lu
      et al., 2022; Rieger et al., 2022; Sagar et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023) have been proposed. Notably,
082
      several existing defense methods are proposed for HFL (Lu et al., 2022; Rieger et al., 2022). In
083
      HFL, the models generated by benign clients are similar because they use the same features for
084   training. Existing defense methods identify malicious clients by focusing on the similarity of benign
085   cases and identifying outliers among the models sent to the central server. Furthermore, because
086   the contaminated data are part of the training data, defense methods in VFL have been proposed
087   that involve relocating outliers from the values sent from the client to the central server (Chen et al.,
088   2024; Lai et al., 2023). This method combines the transmitted values from all clients and searches
089   for outliers within the combined values, leveraging the existence of several legitimate combined
090   values to discriminate a small number of adversarial combined values.
091   In VFL, the more potent Byzantine attack (Xu et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2022) poses a realistic threat.
092   Unlike poisoning attacks, Byzantine attacks significantly degrade the accuracy of the model by con-
093   taminating training data or tampering with the values sent to the central server. When the accuracy
094   of the model is significantly reduced by a Byzantine attack, services utilizing that model may be-
095   come dysfunctional, or substantial computational costs may be incurred for retraining the model. In
096   HFL, the model sent from a malicious client to the central server is expected to differ significantly
097   from those sent from other clients, making Byzantine attacks difficult to execute because of defense
098
      methods that detect outliers (Li et al., 2019; Murata et al., 2024). However, in VFL, outliers in the
      values transmitted from each client are meaningless because the features held by each client are
099
      inherently different. Notably, even in the absence of malicious clients, legitimate clients may be
100
      excluded. Furthermore, even if a malicious client contaminates all training data, it is challenging
101   to detect malicious clients through outlier detection. Therefore, security evaluation of Byzantine
102   attacks in VFL is of paramount importance.
103
104
      Security assessments of Byzantine attacks on VFL have been conducted with (Xu et al., 2024) and
      without communication (Yuan et al., 2022) between clients, respectively. This study focuses on the
105
      latter. In the security assessment by Yuan et al., attack experiments that tampered with the values
106
      sent to the central server were conducted, and defense methods against these attacks were proposed.
107

108
      Specifically, this study assumed a case in which l2 -regularized finite-sum minimization was solved
109   under multiple clients, and defense was conducted using the dual space.
110
111   However, Yuan et al. presented challenges in terms of both attacks and defenses. First, regarding the
112
      attack aspect, an attack that tampers with the values sent to the central server can be detected by at-
      taching a message authentication code (MAC) to the transmitted values. Specifically, if this process
113
      is executed in a trusted execution environment (TEE) within the client, there is no intervention by
114
      the attacker at the time of MAC attachment, making it virtually impossible to tamper with the values
115   sent to the central server. Therefore, to appropriately assess the threat of Byzantine attacks on VFL,
116   it is crucial to meticulously verify the attack methods under tamper detection conditions. Regarding
117   the defense aspect, there are concerns about whether similar defenses are possible with more com-
118   plex datasets and models, as the datasets and models considered are simple. The datasets they used
119   were simplistic, consisting of linear operations with added noise, making it unclear whether their
120   methods would be effective on real-world datasets. Moreover, Yuan et al. replaces l2 -regularized
121   finite-sum minimization with a dual problem; however, when using more complex models, the dual
122   problem may become complicated, increasing computational costs, or it may be difficult to compute
123
      the dual problem. Therefore, it is extremely important to consider simpler defense methods that do
      not rely on dual problems.
124
125   In this study, we improved the above two issues and conducted a more precise security assessment.

127   1.2   C ONTRIBUTIONS
128
129   In this study, we investigate the feasibility of Byzantine attacks and propose new defense methods
130   CC-VFed against these attack methods. First, in Section 3, we clarify the threat model of the attacker
131
      and classify and organize attack methods that can be executed only by polluting the training data.
      In particular, we qualitatively demonstrated that among the conceivable attacks, the sign-flipping
132
      attack is powerful, and we verified its threat experimentally. In Section 4, we propose a new defense
133
      method CC-VFed against Byzantine attacks using the contribution of each client to the output label.
134   CC-VFed leverages the fact that the output labels become illegitimate in the presence of malicious
135   clients. By removing clients that contribute significantly to the incorrectly outputted labels, it facil-
136   itates more legitimate training. In particular, unlike the prior research by Yuan et al., CC-VFed is
137   a simpler and more practical defense method against Byzantine attacks during the training phase,
138   which is applicable to diverse models and datasets. This study evaluates the effectiveness of CC-
139   VFed using real-world datasets such as BCW and CIFAR10. CC-VFed serves as a defense methods
140   against sign-flipping attacks without significantly reducing the accuracy of the original model.

147   2.1   V ERTICAL F EDERATED L EARNING (V EPAKOMMA ET AL ., 2018)
148
149   In this subsection, we discuss VFL. VFL distributes a single model among various clients and a
150   central server. Each client and central server train their own model, thereby efficiently realizing a
151   large-scale model.
152   First, we discuss the data collected by each client. In VFL, each client holds a proportion of the data
153   with the same ID. Furthermore, only the central server holds a label corresponding to the ID. Note
154   that each client and the central server keep the information they hold confidential from the other
155   participants. Under these conditions, they cooperate to train a large-scale model.
156   Here, we explain VFL using m pairs of training data and labels (X, Y ). Note that X =
157   [x1 , x2 , . . . , xm ] and Y = [y1 , y2 , . . . , ym ]. This dataset is trained by n clients and a central
158   server. Client j (1 ≤ j ≤ n) holds the model Fθj and a proportion of the divided training data
159   Xj = [x1,j , x2,j , . . . , xm,j ]. Here, θj is the model parameter. Additionally, by combining and
160   appropriately rearranging the vectors xi,j (1 ≤ j ≤ n), we obtain the original data xi . The cen-
161   tral server holds the model Fθ0 and the label set Y . In training and inference, communication is
      performed between the output layer of the client and the input layer of the central server, thereby

163   in the output layers of all the clients and the number of nodes in the input layer of the central server

      2.2   P REVIOUS B YZANTINE ATTACKS ON VFL (Y UAN ET AL ., 2022)
188
189   In this subsection, we discuss the existing Byzantine attacks on VFL conducted by Yuan et al. (Yuan
190   et al., 2022). Yuan et al. conducted three types of attacks: Gaussian, same-value, and sign-flipping
191   attacks based on the output vectors zi,j of the other benign clients. However, in practice, information
192   from other clients is not accessible. Therefore, in this study, instead of employing the attack methods
193   proposed by Yuan et al., we construct a Byzantine attack by referencing the methods proposed by
194   Ma et al. (Ma et al., 2022). Ma et al.’s attack target HFL and manipulate the local model before
195   sending it to the central server. By drawing on these methods, a Gaussian attack, a same-value
196   attack, and a sign-flipping attack are as follows. A Gaussian attack is an attack method in which a
197
      malicious client j sends a value to the central server that adds Gaussian noise to the average of the
      output values of the other clients. A same-value attack is an attack method in which a malicious
198
      client sends zi,j , all of which have the same value, to the central server. A sign-flipping attack is
199
      an attack method in which a malicious client j multiplies its output value zi,j by −c (c > 1) and
200   sends it to the central server. However, even if such an attack is carried out, the above attacks falsify
201   the output value zi,j , and become infeasible because of tampering detection. Specifically, to bypass
202   tampering detection, it is necessary to falsify the input data xi,j and not the output value zi,j .

      3     F EASIBLE B YZANTINE ATTACKS THAT TAMPER WITH T RAINING DATA
206
      In this section, we discuss the feasibility of Byzantine attacks that tamper with training data. In
207
      Section 3.1, we discuss the attack capabilities of malicious clients. In Section 3.2 we identify the
208   Byzantine attacks that a malicious client can execute. In particular, by comparing these attack
209   methods, we demonstrate that the sign-flipping attack is strong in the training data. Finally, in
210   Section 3.3, we demonstrate that the sign-flipping attack is indeed a powerful attack method.
211
212   3.1   T HE ATTACK C APABILITIES OF M ALICIOUS C LIENTS IN THIS PAPER
213
214   In this subsection, we discuss the attack capabilities of malicious clients, which are the premise of
215   this study. Before discussing the attack capabilities, we first describe the naive defense measures
      that the central server can use to prevent malicious behavior by each client. These defense measures


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      mainly comprise encryption, tampering detection, and input/output verification and are assumed to
217   be implemented by devices provided by the central server. In this study, we do not assume direct
218   attacks on the central server, and the central server is assumed to operate as usual. The naive defense
219   measures implemented by the central server are as follows:
220
221          • For each client, the model is placed within the TEE area or encrypted to prevent access to
222            its plaintext.
223          • The output values of the model sent from each client to the central server are encrypted
224            until they are received by the central server, where they are checked for tampering.
225          • The values sent from the central server to the client are encrypted.
226
             • When values are input into the model, it is checked whether they fall within a specified
227
               range.
228
229   Given the above, we assume that the malicious clients considered in this study cannot perform the
230   following:
231
232          • Obtaining training data label information (owing to vector and model encryption, label
233
               inference attacks (Fu et al., 2022) are also not possible)
234          • Manipulation of the model and values sent from the client to the central server
235          • Inputting data with out-of-range values
236
237   Based on the above, the malicious clients addressed in this paper can manipulate only the input to
238   the model, specifically, the training data, and all element values must fall within the specified range.
239   We considered strong Byzantine attacks by malicious clients with these attack capabilities.

241   3.2   C ONSIDERATION OF S TRONG B YZANTINE ATTACKS
242
      In this subsection, we first classify and organize feasible Byzantine attacks, as described in Sec-
243
      tion 3.1. During this process of organization, we demonstrate the high attack capability of the
244
      sign-flipping attack. The feasible attacks can be divided into two types: attacks that set the input
245   data xi,j randomly and attacks that transform it by an algorithm.
246
247   First, we consider methods that set the input data xi,j randomly. Because the training data comprises
248
      the data and labels, methods that manipulate either can be considered. An attack that randomly
      generates input data to meet the input/output conditions can be considered as a method to manipulate
249
      the data. To manipulate the labels, an attack that disrupts the correspondence between the data and
250
      labels by changing the order of the data to be input can be considered. In this type of attack, the
251   attacker does not know the labels corresponding to the training data; therefore, it is not possible to
252   bias the images of a specific label towards a specific class.
253
254
      Next, we consider attack methods that transform input data xi,j using an algorithm. We consider
      the possibility of reproducing the attack method of Yuan et al. (Yuan et al., 2022). The consideration
255
      of more effective attack methods, particularly the optimal attack, will be a topic for future work.
256
257   First, we consider a Gaussian attack and a same-value attack. These attack methods necessitate
258   manipulating the values sent from the client to the server; however, under the encryption of the
259   model, it is extremely difficult to reverse calculate the model to obtain the desired output. Therefore,
260
      the Gaussian attack and the same-value attack are not feasible.
261   Next, we consider a sign-flipping attack. The naive sign-flipping attack manipulating the output
262   value is not possible because of tamper detection. Here, the sign-flipping attack can be consid-
263   ered an input data tampering attack against the model of the central server alone. Therefore, if we
264   consider the target of the attack to be not only the central server but also all models, including the
265   clients, the sign-flipping attack can be established by the malicious client j multiplying the input data
266
      xi,j (1 ≤ i ≤ m) by −c (c > 0). However, if the value of c is extremely small or large, there is a
      risk that the distribution of values may become unnatural and be detected as anomalies. Therefore,
267
      the value of c needs to be set approximately close to one.
268
269   In the above discussion, we identified three types of feasible Byzantine attacks: an attack method that
      randomly generates training data (called “random attack”), an attack method that changes the order

      of training data (called “permutation attack”), and the sign-flipping attack. Below, we qualitatively
271   show that among these three types of attack methods, the sign-flipping attack has the highest attack
272   effect. In particular, the sign-flipping attack shows that the loss function is significantly different
273   from the usual function and that the gradient vector changes.
274
      First, the sign-flipping attack changes the loss function significantly. The model comprises linear
275
      transformations and activation functions. If the sign of xi,j is reversed, the effect of the activation
276
      function is reversed, resulting in a significantly different value for the loss function. Note that the
277   reversal of the effect of the activation function is more pronounced in parts that are far from the
278   threshold, that is, parts that have significant features that make up the input.
279
280
      Next, the sign-flipping attack changes the gradient vector significantly. We consider the following
      loss function L (Xi , θ), where the input Xi = [xi,1 , xi,2 , . . . , xi,n ] and all parameters θ of the

299   discussion, in the next subsection, we compare the random, the permutation, and the sign-flipping

      attack for verifying the above hypothesis.

390   First, we describe the proposed defense algorithm CC-VFed against Byzantine attacks. CC-VFed
391   leverages the fact that the output labels become illegitimate in the presence of malicious clients, and
392   images are shown in Appendix A. To identify such malicious clients as described above, methods
393   similar to Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) is utilized. The determination of malicious clients for
394
      one epoch was performed in the following three steps performed at the central server:
395
              1. Classification of raw inputs: For one batch, the central server accepts zi,j s from clients
396                                                                                                 ∂Li
                 and outputs the label yi∗ s with the highest score. Furthermore, we calculate ∂z        , the
397                                                                                                  i,j
                 gradient of the values each client sent to the central server, in order to send them to the

399

406              identifying malicious clients will be elaborated in Section 4.1.2.
407
              3. Updating each model: The values that malicious clients send to the central server are
408              replaced with random values, and the central server’s training is conducted while calcu-
409              lating the gradient for the input from each client. Then, the gradient calculated in Step 1
410              is then sent to the malicious clients, and the gradient calculated here is sent to the legit-
411              imate clients, updating each client’s model. The method for replacing the values sent by
412              malicious clients will be discussed in detail in Section 4.1.3.

428   4.1.2     I DENTIFYING MALICIOUS CLIENTS
429
430   Malicious clients are first detected for each input and then identified by aggregating these results
431   across the batch. In this paper, we propose two methods for determining malicious clients for each
      input and two methods for determining malicious clients in each batch, and we propose four defense


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      methods by integrating them, that is., 2 × 2 = 4 methods. Note that the experiments conducted in
433   this study assume a scenario in which the total number of clients is two or three and the number of
434   malicious clients is one, as a first step to verify the effect of contributions. At most one malicious
435   client was identified for each input and batch. However, if the model is further subdivided and the
436   total number of clients and number of malicious clients increase, the number of identified malicious
437   clients for each input and each batch can be set to half of the total number of clients, making the
438   proportion of malicious clients approximately equivalent. Therefore, it is believed that the defense
439   performance itself will also be approximately equivalent to that before subdivision. Below, we
440   describe the method for identifying malicious clients for each input and batch.
441   To determine the malicious clients at each input, we made a judgment based on the comparison
442   of the magnitude of each client’s contribution, as discussed earlier. For this, the first method of
443   judgment was a method that naively sorts the contributions of each client in ascending or descending
444   order and identifies a specified number of malicious clients. If the number of selected malicious
445   clients exceeded the specified number, the last added client was considered non-malicious because
446   the contributions have the same value. However, when the output label matched the label of the
447
      training data, clients with low contributions to the output label were determined to be malicious;
      however, when both contributions were high, there may not be any malicious clients. Therefore, the
448
      second method of judgment is a method that sets a threshold for the contribution of clients so that
449
      it can output that there are no malicious clients. Specifically, in addition to the judgment conditions
450   described earlier, after setting a threshold t, if the output label matched the label of the training
451   data, clients with contributions of t or more were considered to be non-malicious. In addition, if the
452   output label did not match the label of the training data, clients with contributions of t or less were
453   considered to be non-malicious. In particular, we set t = 0 and used these two methods of judgment.
454
      Based on the above discussion, we explain the method for determining malicious clients in each
455
      batch. The first method of judgment sorts the number of times each client has been judged to be
456   malicious in descending order and determines a specified number of clients as malicious clients in
457   that batch. If the number of selections exceeded the specified number for reasons such as equal
458   numbers of malicious judgments, the last added client is considered non-malicious. As the second
459   method of judgment, we determined the malicious clients in each batch based on the total number of
460   times they were judged to be malicious across all batches. However, when there were no malicious
461   clients in the relevant batch, we adhered to that judgment.
462
463   4.1.3    R EPLACING THE VALUES SENT BY MALICIOUS CLIENTS
464
465   From the experimental results in Section 3.3, it can be seen that the detection rate barely decreased
466   in the random attack. Therefore, we neutralized the effects of Byzantine attacks by replacing the
467   transmission values of clients judged as malicious with random values. The random values had
468   the same distributions as those used for each input dataset. Specifically, in BCW, each element is
469
      randomly generated according to a normal distribution with a mean of 0 and variance of 1; and in
      CIFAR10, each element is randomly generated according to a uniform distribution in the range [0, 1).
470
      Note that in the algorithm proposed in this paper, there may be cases where a client is mistakenly
471
      detected as malicious. Even in this case, it is believed that there will be no further attacks because
472   the situation would be approximately the same as a normal random attack if the defense method
473   proposed in this study is used.

780   B.2   CIFAR10
781
782   For CIFAR10, the experimental results are shown in Tables 10–13. Here, taking the average of
783   the top-1 accuracy after defense for the nine experimental conditions, we get 41.39% for Table 10,
784   74.72% for Table 11, 19.14% for Table 12, and 75.17% for Table 13. Therefore, for CIFAR10, the
785   defense is successful when threshold is not used. Below, we discuss the reasons why the defense is
786   successful for CIFAR10 when a threshold is not utilized.
787   Firstly, let’s focus on Table 10 and consider the reasons for the failure of the defense. In Table 10,
788   while the defense against the sign-flipping attack is successful, the defense against the random and
789   permutation attacks fails. CIFAR10, unlike BCW, takes time to train, and even for clean data without
790   data contamination, the top-1 accuracy at the start of the 6th epoch is 57.08%. Therefore, compared
791   to BCW, the probability of being judged as having an error in the output label is high. At this time, it
792   is believed that the random and permutation attacks have a clearly smaller contribution to the output
793   label, and legitimate clients are considered malicious. In this case, there will be no legitimate clients
794   left in the training process, and training will proceed with only the malicious client. Conversely, in
795
      the case of random and permutation attacks, the contribution value becomes random with respect to
      the output label, and even if the output label is judged as legitimate when a threshold is set, there are
796
      cases where the client performing these attacks is considered non-malicious. From the above, it is
797
      believed that the risk of only legitimate clients being considered malicious is high, and the situation
798   has become such that a model that judges legitimate clients as malicious is trained due to training
799   only by the malicious client. It should be noted that the aforementioned phenomenon may also be
800   attributed to the fixed threshold of zero for contributions. Therefore, setting an appropriate threshold
801   according to the depth of training remains a task for future research.
802
      It should be noted that the cause of failure in defending against sign-flipping attacks in Table 12 is
803
      believed to be almost the same. Sign-flipping attacks are considered to have a higher contribution
804   to the output label compared to random or permutation attacks, as they maintain a connection with
805   the original input. In Table 10, it is believed that the attack failed because a sufficient number of
806   successful attacks could not be achieved, and legitimate clients could not be misjudged as malicious,
807   resulting in the generation of a high-precision model. However, in Table 12, it is believed that even
808   after training has progressed, legitimate clients can be misjudged as malicious without using past
809   history, and in such cases, training progresses only with malicious clients, leading to a situation
      where a model that judges legitimate clients as malicious is trained.

872   C.1   E XPERIMENTAL C ONDITIONS
873
874   In this subsection, we conduct experiments in the case where there are three clients (referred to as
875   Client A, Client B, and Client C, respectively). The dataset handled in this experiment is the same as
876   in the previous section. Particularly, compared to Section 3 and 4, the data partitioning method and
877   the structure of the model have changed in the experiments in this section, while all other conditions
878   are equivalent.
879   Firstly, we will discuss the data partitioning method. In this experiment, for all datasets, the data is
880   divided into three parts, held by Client A, B, and C, respectively. Firstly, in BCW, the 28 numerical
881   data registered in the dataset are held in order, with Client A holding 9, Client B holding 9, and
882   Client C holding 10. In CIFAR10, for the 32×32 pixel images, Client A holds the first 10 columns
883   from the left, Client C holds the last 11 columns from the right, and Client B holds the remaining
884   11 columns in the middle. Based on this data partitioning method, the structure of the model was
885   determined according to each dataset. The model is as shown in Tables 17 and 18 of Appendix D. It
886
      should be noted that the client model for CIFAR10 remains as ResNet20.
887   The above are the experimental conditions for the experiments conducted in this subsection. Here,
888   when conducting experiments with the above partitioning method, it is expected that the contribu-
889   tions to training will vary greatly, and if a client who is supposed to have a high contribution is
890   malicious, the accuracy of the model is expected to decrease significantly. For example, in image
891   data, Clients A, B, and C are assigned the left, center, and right of the image, respectively. Since the
892
      object is likely to be in the center of the image, if Client B, who holds the center of the image, is
      malicious, the accuracy of the model is expected to decrease significantly, and even if other clients
893
      act maliciously, it is not expected to have much impact. Similarly, in the case of table data, it is ex-
894
      pected that the attack results will vary depending on the data used. Also, if a client who is supposed
895   to have a high contribution is malicious, it is expected that the defense will be less effective.
896
897   In this paper, to verify the above discussion, we trained model and calculated the top-1 accuracy
898
      for all patterns where the malicious client is Client A, Client B, or Client C. Furthermore, in this
      section, we utilize the defense methods CC-VFed that were shown to be highly effective for each
899
      dataset in Appendix B. Specifically, for BCW, we employ the defense method CC-VFed that uses
900
      both thresholds and historical data, while for CIFAR10, we conduct experiments under the defense
901   method that does not use either thresholds or historical data.
