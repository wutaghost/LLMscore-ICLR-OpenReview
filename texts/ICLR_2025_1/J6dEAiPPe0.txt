011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) like GPT-4o have shown promise in solving ev-
014            eryday tasks and addressing basic scientific challenges by utilizing extensive pre-
015            trained knowledge. In this work, we explore their potential to predict the effi-
016            ciency of various organic compounds for the inhibition of corrosion of the magne-
017            sium alloy ZE41, a material crucial for many industrial applications. Traditional
018            approaches, such as basic neural networks, rely on non-contextual data, often re-
019
               quiring large datasets and significant effort per sample to achieve accurate predic-
               tions. They struggle particularly with small datasets, limiting their effectiveness
020
               in discovering new corrosion inhibitors. LLMs can contextualize and interpret
021
               limited data points by drawing on their vast knowledge, including chemical prop-
022            erties of molecules and their influence on corrosion processes in other materials
023            like iron. By prompting the model with a small dataset, LLMs can provide mean-
024            ingful predictions without the need for extensive training. Our study demonstrates
025            that LLMs can predict corrosion inhibition outcomes, and reduce the amount of
026            data needed.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   In recent years, LLMs have gained significant attention in both public discourse and academic re-
033
      search, evolving from their original purpose in natural language processing to demonstrating re-
      markable versatility across various domains (Jablonka et al. (2023); Plaat et al. (2024)). Initially
034
      designed to handle tasks such as answering general questions, assisting in programming, and per-
035
      forming simple reasoning tasks, LLMs have since been applied to more complex challenges, includ-
036   ing regression, prediction, and classification of numerical datasets.
037
038   To enhance the quality and accuracy of LLM-generated responses, researchers have developed
039
      strategies like prompt engineering and advanced techniques such as chain-of-thought (CoT, Schul-
      hoff et al. (2024)), tree-of-thought (ToT, Yao et al. (2023)), and skeleton of thought (SoT, Ning et al.
040
      (2023)). These methods enable LLMs to produce structured and well-reasoned outputs by allowing
041
      them to engage in more reflective and self-guided problem-solving processes. This growing recog-
042   nition of LLMs as powerful tools has spurred their exploration in fields beyond traditional language
043   processing, including chemistry.
044
045
      The application of LLMs in chemistry, in particular, has emerged as a promising area of research,
      with efforts focused on how these models can effectively leverage chemical knowledge. Researchers
046
      have employed various approaches to enhance LLM performance in this domain. For example,
047
      Hatakeyama-Sato et al. (2023) examined GPT-4’s ability to process and apply chemical knowledge
048   through prompt-based interactions, identifying both its potential and its limitations in reasoning and
049   knowledge gaps. In addition, Jablonka et al. (2023) reviewed techniques like fine-tuning GPT-2 and
050   GPT-3.5 and connecting GPT-4 with external tools to improve its utility in chemistry-related tasks.
051   Advancing this work, Bran et al. (2024) developed ChemCrow, a tool that integrates simulations,
052   internet access, and chemical APIs to extend LLM capabilities in molecular transformations and
053   property predictions. Moreover, Jablonka et al. (2024) demonstrated the potential of fine-tuned
      GPT-3 models in tasks such as classification, regression, and molecule design, while Zhang et al.

054
      (2024) trained a domain-specific LLM, ChemLLM, on a large chemical database, showcasing the
055   value of specialized models in chemical research.
056
057   Magnesium (Mg) and its alloys are increasingly used in various novel industrial applications due to
058
      their abundance, affordability, and versatility. However, the high chemical reactivity of magnesium
      requires domain-specific adjustments of its degradation behavior. In transportation (Dziubińska et al.
059
      (2016); Joost & Krajewski (2017)), corrosion must be prevented to avoid critical material failure.
060
      In medical applications (e.g. temporary biodegradable stents or bone screws, Santos-Coquillat et al.
061   (2019); Witte et al. (2008)) the degradation rate must be precisely controlled to match varying treat-
062   ment or patient healing rates. For energy applications like Mg-air primary batteries (Höche et al.
063   (2018)) and secondary, a steady rate of Mg dissolution is essential to ensure constant energy output
064   (Ma et al. (2019)). Several strategies, including alloying and surface coatings, have been developed
065   to regulate the degradation of Mg-based materials (Gray & Luan (2002)). A promising approach
066   involves the use of small organic molecules which have shown significant potential in controlling
067   the dissolution properties of pure Mg and its alloys (Lamaka et al. (2017); Blawert et al. (2006)).
068   The vast chemical diversity of organic modulators is a major advantage, offering nearly limitless
069
      possibilities for tailored solutions. The number of available organic compounds is rapidly growing,
      with 120 million new compounds reported in the last decade alone. Estimates suggest that there
070
      could be as many as 1063 (Kirkpatrick & Ellis (2004)) organic compounds with useful properties,
071
      making the chemical space effectively infinite. Recent advancements in automation, robotics, and
072   combinatorial chemistry are enabling the synthesis of larger and more diverse chemical libraries and
073   the integration of computer-assisted synthesis methods further accelerates the growth of available
074   compounds.
075
      However, even with the most sophisticated high-throughput techniques available today (White et al.
076
      (2012); Muster et al. (2009); Meeusen et al. (2019)), researchers can only explore a tiny frac-
077
      tion of this space. Fortunately, data-driven computational methods can efficiently search larger
078   regions of chemical space with significantly less time and effort, rendering them invaluable for
079   short-listing molecules with desirable properties for specific applications (Winkler (2017); Fernan-
080   dez et al. (2016); Chen et al. (2016); Winkler et al. (2014); Segler et al. (2018); Coelho et al. (2022);
081   Würger et al. (2022)). Machine learning models that capture complex quantitative structure-property
082   relationships (QSPR) can predict the properties of yet-to-be-synthesized or tested compounds. How-
083   ever, these models require extensive, reliable, chemically diverse, and balanced training datasets to
084   achieve accurate and generalizable predictions. A synergistic approach combining experimental and
085   computational methods forms a robust foundation for data-driven discovery of dissolution modu-
086
      lators (a pool of chemicals that either accelerates or inhibits the corrosion onset and progression
      in metallic materials) by predicting their corrosion inhibition efficiencies (IEs) prior to testing. To
087
      ensure reliable performance, the training datasets must adequately represent the complexity of the
088
      relevant chemical environments, especially when predicting properties for molecules with under-
089   represented features in the data. However, the available training in the field of corrosion inhibitor
090   research is quite limited from a machine learning point of view as a large part of chemicals that are
091   labeled with an IE value are proprietary data and not available to the public domain. Fortunately,
092   LLMs show great potential to mitigate the lack of available training data by utilizing their extensive
093   base knowledge to understand contextual data and enhance model predictions. By drawing on a
094   vast repository of scientific information, LLMs can help bridge gaps in existing datasets, support-
095   ing more accurate and reliable predictions of inhibition efficiency without the immediate need for
096   additional experimental data.
097   In this study, GPT-4o is used to predict the efficiency of various compounds to inhibit corrosion of
098   magnesium. It is provided with exemplary data points and compared with baseline results of a neural
099   network from Schiessler et al. (2023). The large language model (LLM) is tasked to predict with two
100   different approaches. The first approach aims at incorporating additional contextual information in
101   combination with the pretrained knowledge of the LLM to increase the accuracy of the predictions.
102   The second approach focuses on evaluating the value of contextual information combined with the
103
      pretrained knowledge alone.

      2     M ETHODOLOGY

      2.1   OVERVIEW
111
112   This study aims to predict the corrosion inhibition efficiency of various compounds using an LLM by
113   integrating contextual information, including details of the experimental setup, molecular identities,
114   and structural representations. This methodology is intended to enhance predictive performance
115   beyond the neural network baseline reported in Schiessler et al. (2023), which was considered state-
116   of-the-art at the time of publication. With the emergence of advanced LLMs, particularly GPT-
117   4o, novel approaches have become feasible, offering the potential to outperform traditional neural
118   networks, especially when dealing with limited datasets. Given the small dataset of 75 samples used
119
      in this study, LLMs can leverage their extensive knowledge base to compensate for data scarcity
      and improve prediction accuracy. To ensure a fair and direct comparison, the same train-test split as
120
      utilized by the neural network model is employed. A naming convention employed in this paper and
121
      explained further in the following splits the input data given to the LLM in two parts. The first part,
122   referred to as ”data”, is the numerical information that is also used for training the neural network.
123   The second part, termed ”context”, contains all non-numerical information in the form of strings,
124   that can be supplied to an LLM, but not to a basic neural network.
125
      Two distinct prediction approaches are explored:
126
127
             • LLM with data and context: This approach provides the LLM with both the context
128            (molecule names, molecular structure, and experimental details) and the numerical data
129            the baseline neural network had access to during both training and testing. The aim is to
130            leverage the LLM’s inherent knowledge and reasoning capabilities to integrate these data
131            sources and outperform the neural network baseline.
132
             • LLM with context only: In this approach, the LLM is provided with only the context
133            information (molecule names, molecular structure, and experimental details), without the
134            numerical data. This setup tests the LLM’s ability to generate accurate predictions based
135            purely on its understanding of chemical properties and the provided experimental context.
136
137   By integrating this contextual data into the LLM’s prompting strategy, we hypothesize that the model
138   can better understand the underlying chemical processes, ultimately leading to more accurate pre-
139   dictions of inhibition efficiency than the neural network model, which lacks access to this qualitative
140   information. Furthermore, we hypothesize, that the context information together with the pretrained
141   knowledge of GPT-4o can replace the numerical descriptor data.
142
143   2.2   DATA AND BASELINE
144
145   The baseline results and input data are sourced from Schiessler et al. (2023). The dataset com-
146
      prises 75 chemical compounds along with their corresponding inhibition efficiencies, which serve
      as labels. Inhibition efficiency measures the extent to which a given compound alters the rate of
147
      corrosion compared to a control experiment with no compounds present. This value is expressed as
148
      a percentage, where 100% indicates a perfect inhibitor that completely halts corrosion. Conversely,
149   negative values suggest that the compound accelerates the corrosion process. In this study, the unit
150   of the inhibition efficiency is denoted as IE to clearly distinguish it from other percentage-based
151   metrics.
152
      The input dataset includes approximately 1,250 molecular descriptors for each compound. The
153
      descriptors were generated using the chemoinformatics software package alvaDesc and the quantum
154
      chemical software package TurboMole to encode the molecular and in part the electronic structure
155   of the 75 molecules that were used in the benchmark study.
156
157   To quantify the corrosion inhibition efficiencies of the used benchmark dataset, hydrogen evolution
158
      measurements have been performed following the experimental procedure described in the bench-
      mark study.
159
160   The predictive model utilized by Schiessler et al. (2023) is a neural network. The model receives
161   as input five molecular descriptors out of the available 1,250, selected through a recursive feature
      elimination algorithm. Among the various feature sets examined by Schiessler et al. (2023), this


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      paper focuses exclusively on the FS60 set. This set is selected using only the training data, ensuring
163   that it remains unbiased by excluding the test data from the feature selection process. The descriptors
164   in FS60 include P VSA MR 5, LUMO, E1p, CATS3 02 AP, and Mor04m. Consequently, only the
165   predictions generated using this feature set will be considered for comparison. Furthermore, to
166   maintain consistency, the same train-test split as in the original study will be used for prediction.

168   2.3     P ROMPTING S TRATEGY
169
170   2.3.1    T ECHNOLOGY
171
      The large language model (LLM) employed in this study is GPT-4o by OpenAI, accessed via Mi-
172
      crosoft Azure. The specific version used is ”2024-02-15-preview”, configured with a temperature of
173   0.7 and a top p value of 0.95. Detailed prompting strategies are included in the appendix.
174
175   2.3.2    C ONTEXT AND DATA
176
177   ”Context” comprises molecule names, SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System)
178   strings, and additional information about the experimental setup, while ”Data” refers to the molec-
179
      ular descriptors available to the neural network baseline. SMILES strings offer a concise and stan-
      dardized representation of molecular structures. These SMILES strings are sourced from the Pub-
180
      Chem database (Kim et al. (2022)). For the remaining context information, the LLM is provided
181
      with two prompts that define its role as a professional chemist, outline the importance of predicting
182   inhibition efficiency, describe the experimental settings, and detail the task at hand. These prompts
183   encourage the LLM to approach the problem systematically, working step-by-step to ensure a thor-
184   ough and accurate analysis.
185
      Upon providing GPT-4o with the dataset, the model undergoes an analytical process structured into
186
      four distinct prompts. In the first prompt, GPT-4o is instructed to generate a comprehensive list of
187
      functional groups and atomic structures for each molecule. The second prompt directs the model
188   to autonomously reduce the molecular descriptors from five to a more manageable set of 2-3 per
189   molecule, as the inclusion of all five descriptors often led to data overload, resulting in decreased
190   prediction accuracy. The third prompt combines the outputs from the first two steps into a single,
191   unified dataset. Finally, the fourth prompt guides GPT-4o to examine the consolidated data, iden-
192   tifying patterns and relationships between the input features and inhibition efficiency labels. This
193   refined analysis, coupled with the optimized dataset, serves as the foundation for subsequent predic-
194   tion prompts, enhancing the model’s overall reliability and precision.
195
196   2.3.3    P REDICTION
197
      The prediction process involves a guided, step-by-step prompt consisting of multiple steps. Key
198   steps include:
199
200           • Finding similar molecules: The LLM identifies molecules in the training set that are most
201             similar to the test molecule, using both original data and previously analyzed functional
202             group data.
203           • Ranking for similarity: The LLM assigns similarity scores to these molecules, facilitating
204             a weighted average calculation.
205           • Analyzing the test molecule: The LLM synthesizes and analyzes functional group and
206             atomic structure data to make an educated guess on the inhibition efficiency.
207           • Weighted average calculation: A weighted average of inhibition efficiencies from similar
208             molecules is computed, with a reevaluation of similar molecules beforehand.
209
              • Reevaluation and correction: If significant variability is found, the LLM employs a more
210
                sophisticated approach trying to analyze the corrosion mechanisms of similar molecules.
211
212   2.3.4    R EPETITIONS AND S TRATEGY
213
214   Due to the non-deterministic output of the LLM, the prediction process is repeated 20 times, with
215   the mean result used as the final output. Lowering the temperature setting to reduce randomness
      resulted in poorer predictions.

      4    D ISCUSSION
433
434
      The small size of the test set with 75 samples raises concerns about the generalizability of the find-
435
      ings. Nevertheless, the neural network exhibited significant difficulties in making accurate predic-
436   tions. These inaccuracies were mitigated through the use of GPT-4o for prediction. The substitution
437   of the input data with molecular names and SMILES strings, supplemented by contextual infor-
438   mation, underscores the utility of GPT-4o’s embedded knowledge, as evidenced by a substantial
439   reduction in error. The limited dataset size does not detract from this observation.
440
      For larger datasets, the proposed approach may encounter challenges, particularly in data analysis,
441
      which might need to be conducted in batches. Additionally, it remains unclear how well this ap-
442   proach scales with larger datasets and more extensive context windows, as maintaining an overview
443   of the data may become increasingly difficult.
444
445
      One potential method to reduce underestimation and overestimation errors involves employing a
      linear mapping, estimated using predictions on a small portion of the training set. Given the high
446
      correlation of the LLM results but significant discrepancies in the slopes of the linear fits (Figure 2),
447
      this technique could potentially decrease the prediction error.

450   5    C ONCLUSION
451
452   This study demonstrates the advantages of utilizing a large language model (LLM) like GPT-4o for
453   prediction tasks on small datasets. The predictions generated by GPT-4o, when supplemented with
454   additional contextual information, significantly outperform those from a traditional neural network.
455   Two experiments were conducted to compare the prediction capabilities of GPT-4o against a neural
456   network. In the first experiment, the LLM received the same data as the neural network, along with
457   a description of the problem setting and contextual information about the samples (SMILES strings
458   of the molecules and their names). The results of this experiment, when compared to the baseline
459   neural network, indicated a substantial reduction in error. The root mean squared error (RMSE)
460   decreased from 73 to 51.3, and the mean absolute error (MAE) decreased from 61.3 to 43.2 (Figure
461   1). These findings suggest that leveraging the knowledge embedded in GPT-4o, along with a detailed
462   problem description and contextual data, can significantly enhance prediction accuracy.
463   In the second experiment, the LLM was not provided with the data used by the neural network.
464   Instead, it was given only the problem setting and contextual information. This approach also led to
465   a reduction in prediction error. The RMSE decreased from 73 to 61.8, and the MAE decreased from
466   61.3 to 50.8 (Figure 1). These results imply that the combined knowledge of GPT-4o, along with a
467   problem description and contextual information, is more valuable for prediction than the molecular
468   descriptor data alone with a neural network.
469   The outcomes of these experiments illustrate the potential of LLMs to replace neural networks for
470   prediction tasks, particularly when dealing with small datasets. The extensive knowledge base of
471   LLMs makes this approach especially promising for cases where complex dependencies cannot be
472   captured solely by the data. Furthermore, in situations where parts of the data are challenging to
473   input into a neural network, such as strings and text, LLMs may enhance the quality of the results.
474   Another significant advantage is that, for small datasets, there is no need for extensive training. If the
475
      data can be provided through prompts, the setup of the prediction process becomes straightforward
      and does not require substantial computational resources, at least not at the users machine.
