                                                  A BSTRACT
011
               In the real world, sounds inherently vary in length, spanning a broad spectrum
012
               of durations. We particularly aim to address the challenge of generating variable-
013            length audio in text-to-audio (TTA) diffusion models. Extending audio length
014            beyond what was covered during training, also known as extrapolation, is specifi-
015            cally challenging for audio generative models. The existing TTA diffusion model
016            design notoriously suffers from the problem of generation with such flexibility.
017            Therein, the design of prior TTA diffusion models do not accommodate to the
018            change of positional information. In this work, we introduce a novel framework
019            based on relative position embeddings, which is specifically designed to support
020            the flexibility without fundamental changes to the current diffusion pipeline. Our
021
               proposed method allows tuning-free audio length extrapolation, thereby enhancing
               efficiency for generating audio with unseen lengths. Moreover, our approach en-
022
               ables a training strategy with shorter audio durations, enjoying reduced training
023
               costs while maintaining performance levels comparable to those achieved with
024            longer durations. Empirically, we demonstrate exceptional performance against
025            the existing state-of-the-arts on audio generation benchmarks with a significantly
026            lower model size compared to the counterparts. In variable audio length generation,
027            our approach consistently outperforms existing methods by a large margin. Our
028            demonstration page is available at https://flexoundit.github.io/.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Text-to-audio synthesis (TTA) is a generative task that aims to produce natural and accurate audio
034
      from text prompts (Liu et al., 2023a; Ghosal et al., 2023; Majumder et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023b;a;
      Comunità et al., 2024). The applicability of TTA is reflected in assisting the sound design in the
035
      movie and game industries, accelerating creators’ workflow (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a).
036
      Recently, diffusion models have shown a strong presence as an effective approach for text-to-audio
037   tasks. Text-to-audio diffusion models, e.g., Tango2 (Majumder et al., 2024), AudioLDM2 (Liu et al.,
038   2023b), and Make-An-Audio2 (Huang et al., 2023b), have demonstrated notable performance in
039   generating fixed-length audio, particularly sound effects.
040
      Despite the success of TTA in fixed-length audio generation, real-world scenarios often require
041
      generating audio of varying lengths. As mentioned in the applicability to games and movies, the
042
      sound comes along with diverse forms and conditions, thus it would be difficult to limit audio
043   generation only for a fixed duration. The length of each sound is influenced by the source and the
044   environment in which it occurs, reflecting the complex and dynamic nature of the auditory landscape
045   that surrounds us. This diversity in sound duration can range from brief, transient noises like a snap
046   of a twig or a drop of water hitting a surface, to longer, continuous sounds e.g., the roar of ocean
047   waves, the hum of a city, or the sustained white noise. Considering the importance, the ability to
048   generate audio of varying lengths with diffusion models remains underexplored.
049   To address the challenge of variable-length audio generation, we envision two possible scenarios:
050
051   • Train-Test-Consistent-Length (TTCL). In this setting, the model in train and test stages operates
052     under consistent audio lengths. In other words, all target durations in testing are covered during
053     training by means such as data augmentation. For example, if the the training data covers up to 30
        seconds, the model is capable to generate variable-lengths in a range up to 30 seconds in testing.

054
      • Train-Short-Test-Long (TSTL). This scenario presents shorter lengths in training but requires
055     the model to generate longer audio lengths without further tuning, also known as audio length
056     extrapolation. For instance, a model might be required to generate up to 30 seconds during testing
057     despite that the maximum length seen during training is only 10 seconds. In this scenario, the
058     model is encouraged to develop generalization capabilities due to the differences between the
059     training and testing stages.
060
061   While TTCL shows potential in generating variable-length audio, this approach requires the model
062
      to be trained under various audio lengths that can be costly in terms of memory and time efficiency.
      Specifically, to generate a 30-second audio, the model must learn from 30-second audio samples
063
      during training, meaning the data must be seen. If we aim for a longer lengths, we must supplement
064
      the shorter audio lengths with augmentation. The TTCL scenario has been investigated by prior
065   works (Huang et al., 2023a; Evans et al., 2024b) that limit audio generation in a certain range of
066   duration. In contrast, the TSTL scenario provides a more efficient way to work on audio generation
067   and allows testing on unseen audio lengths. TSTL allows shorter audio in training resulting a reduced
068   number of memory to be processed and no augmented audio. TSTL would be a preferred option for
069   training a TTA model with less computational resources. Later, we demonstrate in experiments that
070   prior diffusion-based TTA models (Liu et al., 2023b; Majumder et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023a)
071   unable to preserve performance in the TSTL scenario.
072   To enable variable-length TTA generation while optimizing quality, flexibility, and training cost in
073   diffusion models, we propose FleXible Sound Diffusion Transformer (FleXounDiT). Inspired by
074   the idea of extending the tokens out of the seen lengths in Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Su
075   et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; bloc97, 2023), FleXounDiT employs Diffusion
076   Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2022) by introducing a novel transformer block which essentially
077   combining the absolute position embedding and Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2021)
078   in the base model. Then, to address the problem in TSTL, we integrate RoPE and an improved
079
      version of Resonance YaRN (Wang et al., 2024) , allowing generation of flexible duration during
      the testing phase without additional finetuning. This consequently enables generating sounds of
080
      unseen durations. Even when trained with audio clips shorter than 10 seconds with limited memory
081
      consumption, our model guarantees high-quality sound of longer durations, which distinguishes our
082   model from prior arts that requires long-duration training data.
083
084   In summary, our contributions in this paper are:
085   1. Flexibility. FleXounDiT, a TTA model, generates sound events with consistently high quality
086      across variable durations. We showcase the “train-short-test-long” (TSTL) scenario, revealing that
087      to apply for unseen long durations our approach is finetuning-free.
088   2. Performance. FleXounDiT not only generates variable-length audios but also surpasses the
089      state-of-the-art (SOTA) on the standard 10-second benchmark, thanks to the novel architecture
090      featuring token modulation and RoPE.
091   3. Training cost. FleXounDiT outperforms SOTA models with a significantly small model size.
092      Moreover, the flexibility in the inference duration allows to train a TTA model with audio shorter
093      than the target duration, which eliminates memory overhead during training, benefiting ones
094      limited by low computational resources.

096   2   R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Diffusion-based Text-to-Audio (TTA) models. In TTA, a general framework is to use diffusion
099
      models pretrained on a large scale audio dataset. A seminal work is AudioLDM (Liu et al., 2023a;b)
      that introduces latent diffusion model framework adapted to audio data. The pipeline follows stable
100
      diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) in image domain. In AudioLDM, the basic model consists of
101
      Variational Auto Encoder (VAE) and Latent Diffusion model. The latent diffusion model architecture
102   is based on U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) with convolutional neural networks (CNNs) as the
103   main backbone. The extended version so-called AudioLDM2 (Liu et al., 2023b) allows to translate
104   human-understandable text and audio representation from AudioMAE (Huang et al., 2022) into a
105   single conditional framework. Another model is Tango (Ghosal et al., 2023) and Tango2 (Majumder
106   et al., 2024) that adopts a similar framework as in AudioLDM. However, Tango2 uses a filtered dataset
107   with better correspondences between text and audio. Thus, the representations from a text encoder
      (e.g., T5 (Raffel et al., 2020)) better aligns text embeddings with sound events. In adopting diffusion


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      models for TTA, Make-an-audio (Huang et al., 2023b) proposes a diffusion based model composed
109   of a 2D VAE and a convolutional U-Net denoiser equipped with pseudo prompt enhancement that
110   well-aligned with audio. Additionally, Make-an-Audio2 (Huang et al., 2023a) enhances the model by
111   incorporating a transformer architecture with improved text processing, structuring captions to define
112   the beginning, middle, and end of the audio.
113
114   Variable-length audio generation. The diffusion transformer model (Peebles & Xie, 2022; Zheng
115   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023a), while more advanced than convolution based U-Nets, struggles
116   to scale beyond the size covered during training. The limitation arises from reliance on positional
117   embeddings. The positional embeddings used in standard transformer is absolute. This limitation
118   prevents arbitrary modification of sizes while maintaining performance. In Make-an-Audio2 (Huang
119
      et al., 2023a), The model can generate shorter audio because it is trained on multi-length data with a
      maximum duration of 27-second samples. However, the learnable position embeddings in transformer
120
      are fixed during testing, preventing the model from handling longer positions than those seen in
121
      training. Unlike standard diffusion transformer, CNN based diffusion models (Liu et al., 2023b;
122   Majumder et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Ghosal et al., 2023) do not suffer from the limitation to scale
123   the audio length due to the limitation of absolute position embeddings, but we empirically show in
124   Sec. 5 that CNN models cannot preserve a consistent TTA performance for variable lengths.
125
126   TSTL scenarios using rotary position embedding extension. The absolute position embeddings
127   in transformer architectures reduce flexibility in generating longer contexts (Su et al., 2021; Peng
128   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023). To extend the context length without additional finetuning, positional
129   embeddings with relative distance is required. In natural language processing, Rotary Position
130   Embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2021) introduces a method to encode a position using a rotation
131
      matrix while simultaneously incorporating explicit relative position dependencies into the self-
      attention mechanism. RoPE acts as a base to achieve flexible length generation when integrated with
132
      interpolation techniques (emozilla, 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024) in the testing stage.
133
      Position interpolation (Chen et al., 2023), a RoPE feature scaling method, adjusts the new sequence
134   to fit within the original context window by scaling between the original and target context lengths.
135   This method applies the same scale to every dimension of key and query vectors in the attention
136   module. However, the same scale for every dimension is sub-optimal. Specifically, as described
137   by Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) theory (Tancik et al., 2020), high-frequency dimensions are more
138   challenging to learn than low-frequency dimensions in deep learning. As a result, NTK-aware
139   interpolation recommends adjusting dimensions by scaling high frequencies less and vice versa.
140   To further adjust the dimension regarding to benefit from position interpolation and NTK-aware
141   interpolation, YaRN (Peng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) proposes to use NTK-by-parts (emozilla,
142
      2023) to adjust the frequency for each dimension equipped with attention scaling. Our proposed
      method makes use of the RoPE feature scaling technique and adapt the techniques to audio domain.

204   Overview. In this section, we present a novel method for variable-length audio generation. Fol-
205   lowing the well-established framework of latent diffusion models for audio generation (Huang et al.,

208   waveforms via a pretrained vocoder. We introduce a novel DiT block for TSTL scenarios, using

210   Resonance YaRN (Wang et al., 2024) and frequency based attention scaling without test-time tuning.

288   Training and testing strategies for variable-length generation. To achieve variable-length gener-
289   ation, we need to address the problems of testing on the lengths uncovered during training (TSTL) and
290   the lengths covered during training (TTCL). To this end, we propose two strategies: 1) a novel RoPE
291   feature interpolation framework with a frequency-based scaling term, and 2) training on varying
292   lengths. Note that, we apply the RoPE feature interpolation only in the TSTL scenario where the
293
      target length in testing is longer L′ > L. We define our contribution in extending RoPE features
      applied to the audio domain below.

525   and 20-second audio. In the inpainting task, we mask the inputs for 15 seconds in the middle with
526   2.5 second for each end. In the outpainting task, we mask the inputs for 17.5 seconds starting from
527   the timestamp 2.5 seconds. To test the capability on these tasks, we employ the AudioCaps test set.
528   A pretrained FleXounDiT performs on this task without further finetuning. Table 5 shows that our
529   method can preserve the FAD and KL scores low against the upperbound scores which are obtained

532   6      C ONCLUSIONS
533
      In conclusion, our proposed text-to-audio (TTA) diffusion model so-called FleXounDiT appears to
534   address the challenge of generating variable-length audio, a limitation in existing TTA diffusion
535   models. By using a novel framework based on relative positional embeddings, our proposed approach
536   enables the generation of audio at arbitrary lengths without requiring additional training or input
537   conditions, thereby improving computational efficiency. This training-free approach not only reduces
538   resource demands but also delivers high performance for various TTA tasks, demonstrated by
539   surpassing state-of-the-art methods in both standard audio generation benchmarks and variable-length
      audio generation, while utilizing a significantly smaller model size.

      A    M ODEL A RCHITECTURE D ETAILS
703
704   Our architecture and design details. We set the depth of transformer blocks to 12 with a token
705   hidden size set to 768. The number of attention head is set to 16. The latent input for DiT is in the
706   size of 8 × 256 × 16. For patchification, we use a 2D convolution layer with a kernel size of 2 (i.e.,
707   patch size p = 2) and transform the embedding size to 768. We use FLAN-T5 (Chung et al., 2024) to
708   encode text and use all embeddings out of this model to pair with audio embeddings from our DiT in
709   cross-attention modules. To encode the text for shift and scale modules, we use a simple average
710   pooling operation to aggregate the text embeddings.
711
712   VAE and vocoder options. We developed FleXounDiT upon the VAE and vocoder (i.e., the
713
      reconstruction pipeline) used in AudioLDM series (Liu et al., 2023a;b) for two reasons. (1) Model
      size. The reconstruction pipeline in Make-an-Audio2 (Huang et al., 2023a) has a similar performance
714
      to the AudioLDM pipeline in terms of FAD score (around 1.0 on AudioCaps) (Huang et al., 2023a).
715
      In terms of the model size, the reconstruction pipeline of Make-an-Audio2 has a larger size of ∼215M
716   parameters for VAE and ∼100M for the BigVGAN vocoder (Lee et al., 2022), while the AudioLDM
717   VAE only comprises of ∼115M parameters, with a HiFiGAN vocoder of ∼15M parameters. It is also
718   observed that the light-weight HiFiGAN is not the bottleneck for the Mel-spectrogram reconstruction
719   pipeline (Comunità et al., 2024). (2) Wide adoption. The AudioLDM reconstruction pipeline has been
720   widely adopted in recent TTA research (Ghosal et al., 2023; Majumder et al., 2024; Saito et al., 2024).
721   By following the identical reconstruction pipeline, we concentrate on the design of the diffusion
722   denoiser that is flexible with variable lengths.
723
724   On the adoption of RoPE in SOTA transformer models. The transformer module is adopted
725   in Make-an-audio2 (Huang et al., 2023a). However, the module only consists of cross-attention
726
      to the input conditions. This design prohibits the use of RoPE which is implemented for self-
      attention modules. RoPE has been adopted for sound source separation (Lu et al., 2024), and
727
      recently introduced to audio generation tasks (Liu et al., 2024; Evans et al., 2024a;b) for the sake
728
      of performance. Nevertheless, none of the aforementioned transformer architectures equipped with
729   RoPE is designed to work on generating longer audio lengths beyond what are covered during training
730   (TSTL), which differentiates FleXounDiT from prior arts.
731
732   RoPE extension. In our proposed approach, we make use of RoPE extension derived from Reso-
733   nance YaRN (Peng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). We need to set the hyperparameters to define
734   the ramp up function Eq. (10) as described in the main paper. These hyperparameters are to define
735   interpolation to RoPE features. Recall in Eq. (10), all hidden dimensions i where ri < α are linearly
736   interpolated by a scale s, and the dimensions where ri > β are not interpolated. In our implementa-
737   tion, we set α = 1 and β = 32. As the number of processing tokens for 10-second sound event in our
738
      model is 1024, we calculate the scale s based on this number.

742   Implementation and experiment details. To complement the implementation and experiment
743   details of our main page, we provide additional information below. The first stage in latent diffusion
744   process is to train the VAE model. In this case, we make use off-the-shelf VAE model as well as the
745
      HiFiGAN vocoder provided by AudioLDM series (Liu et al., 2023a;b). As we target to generate
      audio from text description, we could use any text encoder (e.g., CLAP (Wu et al., 2023) or T5 (Raffel
746
      et al., 2020)). However, the CLAP model (Wu et al., 2023), which computes global-level conditions,
747
      has been observed to struggle with capturing temporal information in text data. To address this, we
748   employ a different pretrained text encoder to capture the semantic details of the textual input, which
749   may include important temporal sequences. Specifically, we utilize FLAN-T5 (Chung et al., 2024),
750   an enhanced version of the text-to-text transfer transformer model (Raffel et al., 2020), which has
751   been finetuned on a variety of tasks. Unlike some prior arts (Liu et al., 2023a; Comunità et al., 2024)
752   that can train using audio only, our proposed method needs to be trained using text-audio pairs on the
753   datasets. In addition, for analysis on memory usage, we use NVIDIA RTX A6000 with 32 batch size.

875   C    L IMITATIONS AND BROADER IMPACTS
876
877   Limitations. Our proposed method have some limitations when it comes to accurately matching
878   audio generation with the intended semantic meanings. In certain cases, the model may generate
879   sound effects for only a subset of the sounds mentioned in the text, even though the text may
880   describe multiple different sounds. This results in incomplete outputs that do not fully capture the
881
      range of elements present in the input text description. Addressing this limitation requires further
      refinement in the text description and structure to map textual cues to corresponding audio components
882
      comprehensively.
883
884
      Broader impacts. From an ethical perspective, generative models operate probabilistically when
885
      producing sounds. This means the generated audio might sometimes be undesirable, particularly if the
886   model is trained on inappropriate or unwanted data points.Thus. model deployment might also raise
887   concerns, particularly regarding the potential for generating misleading contents and propagating
888   biases that present in training data. This phenomenon might yield bad perception from humans to the
889   text-to-audio models.
