009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are the mainstream method to learn pervasive
012
               graph data and are widely deployed in industry, making their intellectual property
013
               valuable. However, protecting GNNs from unauthorized use remains a challenge.
014            Watermarking, which embeds ownership information into a model, is a poten-
015            tial solution. However, existing watermarking methods have two key limitations:
016            First, almost all of them focus on non-graph data, with watermarking GNNs for
017            complex graph data largely unexplored. Second, the de facto backdoor-based wa-
018            termarking methods pollute training data and induce ownership ambiguity through
019            intentional misclassification. Our explanation-based watermarking inherits the
020            strengths of backdoor-based methods (e.g., robust to watermark removal attacks),
021            but avoids data pollution and eliminates intentional misclassification. In particu-
022
               lar, our method learns to embed the watermark in GNN explanations such that this
               unique watermark is statistically distinct from other potential solutions, and own-
023
               ership claims must show statistical significance to be verified. We theoretically
024
               prove that, even with full knowledge of our method, locating the watermark is an
025            NP-hard problem. Empirically, our method manifests robustness to removal at-
026            tacks like fine-tuning and pruning. By addressing these challenges, our approach
027            marks a significant advancement in protecting GNN intellectual property.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al., 2008; Kipf & Welling, 2017; Hamilton et al.,
034   2018; Veličković et al., 2018) are widely used for tasks involving pervasive graph-structured data,
035   such as social network analysis, bioinformatics, and recommendation systems (Zhang et al., 2021;
036   Zhou et al., 2020). Various giant companies have integrated GNNs into their systems or open-
037   sourced their GNN frameworks: Amazon uses GNNs to analyze user behavior patterns for product
038   recommendation (Virinchi, 2022); Google develops TensorflowGNN (Sibon Li et al., 2021) for real-
039
      time traffic prediction in Google Maps (Oliver Lange, 2020); Meta uses GNNs to improve friend and
      content recommendations on Facebook and Instagram (MetaAI, 2023); and Alibaba open-sources
040
      the AliGraph (Yang, 2019) platform and uses GNNs for fraud detection (Liu et al., 2021b) and risk
041
      prediction (Li, 2019). Given these companies’ huge investment in labor, time, and resources to
042   develop and deploy GNNs, it is crucial for them to be able to verify the ownership of their own
043   models to protect against illegal copying, model theft, and malicious distribution.
044
045
      Watermarking, an ownership verification technique, embeds a secret pattern into a model (Uchida
      et al., 2017) so that if it is stolen or misused, ownership can still be proven through the retained
046
      watermark. Among the watermarking strategies (see Section 2), backdoor-based watermarking is the
047
      de facto approach, especially for non-graph data (Adi et al., 2018; Bansal et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2023;
048   Yan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2022; Lansari et al., 2023). In these methods, a backdoor
049   trigger (e.g., a logo) is inserted as the watermark pattern into some clean samples (e.g., images)
050   with a target label different from the true label, and the model is trained on both the watermarked
051   and clean samples. During verification, ownership is proven by demonstrating that samples with
052   the backdoor trigger consistently produce the target label. Backdoor-based watermarking methods
053   have several merits: they are robust to removal attacks such as model pruning and fine-tuning, and
      ownership verification only requires black-box access to the target model.

054
      However, recent works (Yan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) show backdoor-based watermarking meth-
055   ods – which are primarily developed for non-graph data – have a fundamental limitation: they in-
056   duce ownership ambiguity, as attackers could falsely claim misclassified data as ownership evidence.
057   Note that this security issue also exists in the few backdoor-based watermarking methods designed
058   for graph data (Xu et al., 2023). Further, they purposely manipulate the normal model training with
059   polluted data samples, which could cause security issues like data poisoning attacks.
060
      Recognizing these limitations, researchers have explored alternate spaces for embedding water-
061   marks. For example, (Shao et al., 2024) embed watermarks into explanations of DNN predictions,
062   avoiding tampering with model predictions or parameters. While (Shao et al., 2024) offers com-
063   pelling benefits, such as eliminating data pollution risks, their approach assumes a ground-truth
064   watermark is known. This requirement introduces challenges, such as reliance on trusted third par-
065   ties and potential disputes over the true watermark. Moreover, (Shao et al., 2024) do not address the
066   unique complexities of graph data, including structural dependencies and multi-hop relationships.
067   Motivated by these insights, we extend explanation-based watermarking to GNNs, addressing the
068   challenges specific to graph data while avoiding reliance on ground-truth watermark verification.
069   Our approach aligns explanations of selected subgraphs with a predefined watermark, ensuring ro-
070   bustness to removal attacks and preserving the advantages of explanation-based methods. In doing
071   so, we present the first explanation-based watermarking method tailored to GNNs.
072
      Our approach: We develop a novel watermarking strategy for protecting GNN model ownership
073   that both inherits the merits from and mitigates the drawbacks of backdoor-based watermarking.
074   Like backdoor-based methods, our approach only needs black-box model access. However, in con-
075   trast to using predictions on the polluted watermarked samples, we leverage the explanations of
076   GNN predictions on clean samples and align them with a predefined watermark for ownership veri-
077   fication. Designing this explanation-based watermarking presents several challenges: First, how do
078   we optimize the GNN training such that these explanations effectively align with the watermark?
079   Second, how do we guarantee that this alignment provides unique proof of ownership (to eliminate
080   ownership ambiguity)? Third, is the embedded watermark pattern robust to removal attacks? And
081
      fourth, is the ownership evidence undetectable to adversaries?
082   Addressing these challenges requires careful design. Prior to training, the owner selects a secret set
083   of watermarked subgraphs (private) and defines a watermark pattern (possibly private).1 The GNN
084   is trained with a dual-objective loss function that minimizes (1) standard classification loss, and (2)
085   distance between the watermark and the explanation of each watermarked subgraph. Our method,
086   like GraphLIME (Huang et al., 2023), uses Gaussian kernel matrices to approximate the influence
087
      of node features on GNN predictions. However, instead of GraphLIME’s iterative approach, we
      employ ridge regression to compute feature attribution vectors in a single step, providing a more
088
      efficient, closed-form solution.
089
090   Our approach is (i) Effective: We observe that explanations of watermarked subgraphs exhibit high
091   similarity to the watermark after training. (ii) Unique: This similarity across explanations is sta-
092   tistically unlikely to be seen in the absence of watermarking, and hence serves as our ownership
093
      evidence. (iii) Undetectable: We prove that, even with full knowledge of our watermarking method,
      it is computationally intractable (NP-hard) for adversaries to find the private watermarked subgraphs.
094
      (iv) Robust: Through empirical evaluations on multiple benchmark graph datasets and GNN mod-
095
      els, our method shows robustness to fine-tuning and pruning-based watermark removal attacks. We
096   summarize our contributions as follows:
097
098
      • We introduce the first known method for watermarking GNNs via their explanations, avoiding data
        pollution and ownership ambiguity pitfalls in state-of-the-art black-box watermarking schemes.
099
100   • We prove that it is NP-hard for the worst-case adversary to identify our watermarking mechanism.
101   • We show our method is robust to watermark removal attacks like fine-tuning and pruning.

105
106   Watermarking techniques can be generally grouped into white-box and black-box methods.
107
          1 Ownership verification does not use the watermark itself, and will work regardless of whether it is known.

108
      White-Box Watermarking. This type of watermarking technique (Darvish Rouhani et al., 2019;
109   Uchida et al., 2017; Wang & Kerschbaum, 2020; Shafieinejad et al., 2021) directly embeds water-
110   marks into the model parameters or features during training. For example, Uchida et al. (2017) pro-
111   pose embedding a watermark in the target model via a regularization term, while Darvish Rouhani
112   et al. (2019) proposed embedding the watermark into the activation/feature maps. Although these
113   methods are robust in theory (Chen et al., 2022), they require full access to the model parame-
114   ters during verification, which may not be feasible in real-world scenarios, especially for deployed
115   models operating in black-box environments (e.g., APIs).
116   Black-Box Watermarking. Black-box approaches verify model ownership using only model pre-
117   dictions (Adi et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Szyller et al., 2021; Le Merrer et al., 2019). They often
118   use backdoor-based methods, training models to output specific predictions for “trigger” inputs; this
119   owner-specified output can serve as ownership evidence (Adi et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018). These
120   methods have significant downsides. First, purposeful data pollution and model manipulation can
121   cause security issues like data poisoning attacks (Steinhardt et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019). Fur-
122   ther, backdoor-based methods suffer from ambiguity — since they rely on misclassification, attack-
123
      ers may claim naturally-misclassified samples as their own “watermark”(Yan et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
      2024). Noting these issues with backdoor-based methods, Shao et al. (2024) proposed using expla-
124
      nations as the embedding space for DNN watermarks. This avoids modifying model predictions or
125
      parameters, eliminates data pollution risks, and retains compatibility with black-box querying.
126
127   Watermarking GNNs. There are unique challenges to watermark GNNs—graphs vary widely in
128   size and structure, making it difficult to embed a watermark that can be applied uniformly across
129
      different graphs. Moreover, the multi-hop message-passing mechanisms in GNNs are more sensitive
      to changes in data than other neural networks that process more uniform data, such as images or
130
      text (Wang & Gong, 2019; Zügner et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2023). The only existing black-box
131
      method for watermarking GNNs (Xu et al., 2023) is backdoor-based, and suffers from the same
132   data pollution and ownership ambiguity issues as backdoor watermarking of non-graph models (Liu
133   et al., 2024)2 . These issues, coupled with the complexity of graphs, make existing watermarking
134   techniques unsuitable for GNNs. This highlights the need for novel watermarking approaches.

195   3.3   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
196
      We design an explanation-based watermarking method to protect GNN ownership. This involves
197
      defining a watermark pattern (a vector w) and selecting a set of watermarked subgraphs from 𝐺.
198
      Our approach trains a GNN 𝑓 to embed the relationship between w and the watermarked subgraphs,
199   enabling the explanations of these subgraphs to serve as verifiable model ownership evidence.
200
201   Threat Model: There are three parties: the model owner, the adversary, and the third-party model
202
      ownership verifier. Obviously, the model owner has white-box access to the target GNN model.
203   • Adversary: We investigate an adversary who dishonestly claims ownership of the GNN model 𝑓 .
204     We primarily assume the adversary does not have direct knowledge of the watermarked subgraphs
205     in 𝐺. To evaluate the robustness of our method, we allow that the adversary might know other
206
        details, such as the shape and number of watermarked subgraphs, or the watermark itself. The
        adversary seeks to undermine the watermarking scheme by (1) attempting to find the watermarked
207
        subgraphs (or similarly-convincing alternatives), or (2) implementing a watermark removal attack.
208
209
      • Model Ownership Verifier: Following existing backdoor-based watermarking, we use black-box
        ownership verification, where the verifier does not need full access to the protected model.
210
211   Objectives: Our explanation-based watermarking method aims to achieve the below objectives:
212
           1. Effectiveness. Training must embed the watermark in the explanations of our selected
213
              subgraphs: their feature attribution vectors must be sufficiently3 aligned with vector w.

240   Our watermarking method occurs in three stages: (1) design, (2) embedding, and (3) ownership ver-
241   ification. Since design relies on embedding and ownership verification requirements, we introduce
242
      stages (2) and (3) beforehand. Training 𝑓 involves a dual-objective loss function balancing node
      classification and watermark embedding. Minimizing watermark loss reduces the misalignment be-
243
      tween w and the explanations of 𝑓 ’s predictions on the watermarked subgraphs, embedding the wa-
244
      termark. During ownership verification, explanations are tested for statistically-significant similarity
245   due to their common alignment with w. Lastly, we detail watermark design principles, which ensure
246   the similarity observed across our explanations is statistically-significant, unambiguous ownership
247   evidence. Figure 1 gives an overview of our explanation-based watermarking method.

      5.2.2   ROBUSTNESS
457   A good watermark will be robust to removal attacks. We explore two types of attacks. The first is
458   pruning for model compression (Li et al., 2016), as used to assess watermark robustness by Liu et al.
459   (2021a), Tekgul et al. (2021), and others. The second is fine-tuning Pan & Yang (2010), as used for
460   robustness assessment by Adi et al. (2018), Wang et al. (2020), and more.
461
      Pruning: Pruning is a model compression strategy that sets a portion of weights to zero (Li et al.,
462
      2016). The particular approach we explore, structured pruning, targets rows and columns of para-
463   mater tensors — such as node embeddings and edge features — based on their importance scores,
464   or 𝐿 𝑛 -norms (Paszke et al., 2019). An attacker hopes that by pruning the model, they may remove
465   the watermark while still maintaining high classification accuracy.
466
      Fine-Tuning: Fine-tuning is a technique that continues training on previously trained models to
467
      adapt to a new task (Pan & Yang, 2010). An attacker may use fine-tuning to get the GNN to “forget”
468
      the watermark. To test our model’s robustness to this type of attack, we continue training the model
469   on the validation dataset, 𝐺 𝑣𝑎𝑙 ,at 0.1 times the original learning rate for 49 epochs. (See Appendix
470   Section A.5 for results with other learning rates and GNN architectures.)
471
472
      Figure 2 shows the impact of pruning and fine-tuning attacks. The left shows the impact of pruning
      rates 0.0 (no GNN parameters pruned) to 1.0 (all pruned). In all datasets, the MI 𝑝-value only rises
473
      as classification accuracy drops, meaning the owner would notice before the pruning affects the
474
      watermark. The right shows classification accuracy and MI 𝑝-value in a fine-tuning attack. CS has a
475   near-zero MI 𝑝-value for about 25 epochs, whereas Photo and PubMed have low MI 𝑝-values for the
476   full duration. This demonstrates the watermark’s robustness for extended periods during fine-tuning.

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we introduce the first-known method for watermarking GNNs via their explanations.
536   This avoids common pitfalls of backdoor-based methods: our watermark is designed with a statis-
537   tical guarantee of unambiguity, and since it does not reside within the training data space, it is not
538   vulnerable to attacks on the data itself. We demonstrate the robustness of our method to removal
539   attacks, while also highlighting the statistical infeasibility of locating the watermarked subgraphs.
      This presents a significant step forward in securing GNNs against intellectual property theft.

1099   Enhancing Robustness.
1100
1101   An important future direction is to safeguard our method against model extraction attacks Shen
1102
       et al. (2022), which threaten to steal a model’s functionality without preserving the watermark. One
       form of model extraction attack is knowledge distillation attack Gou et al. (2020).
1103
1104   Knowledge distillation has two models: the original “teacher” model, and an untrained “student”
1105   model. During each epoch, the student model is trained on two objectives: (1) correctly classify
1106   the provided input, and (2) mimic the teacher model by mapping inputs to the teachers’ predic-
1107   tions. The student therefore learns to map inputs to the teacher’s “soft label” outputs (probability
1108
       distributions) alongside the original hard labels; this guided learning process leverages the richer
       information in the teacher’s soft label outputs, which capture nuanced relationships between classes
1109
       that hard labels cannot provide. By focusing on these relationships, the student model can generalize
1110
       more efficiently and achieve comparable performance to the teacher with a smaller model and fewer
1111   parameters, thus reducing complexity.
1112
1113   We find that in the absence of a strategically-designed defense, the knowledge distillation attack
1114
       successfully removes our watermark (𝑝 > 0.05). This is unsurprising, since model distillation maps
       inputs to outputs but ignores mechanisms that lead to auxiliary tasks like watermarking.
1115
1116   To counter this, we outline a defense framework that would incorporate watermark robustness to
1117   knowledge distillation directly into the training process. Specifically, during training and watermark
1118   embedding, an additional loss term would penalize reductions in watermark performance. At peri-
1119   odic intervals (e.g., after every x epochs), the current model would be distilled into a new model,
1120
       and the watermark performance on this distilled model would be evaluated. If the watermark per-
       formance (measured by the number of matching indices) on the distilled model is lower than the
1121
       watermark performance on the main model, a penalty would be added to the loss term. This would
1122
       ensure that the trained model retains robust watermarking capabilities even against knowledge dis-
1123   tillation attacks.
