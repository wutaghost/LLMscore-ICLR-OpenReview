009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) are a powerful method of learning representa-
012            tions of graph-structured data. While they excel at learning class-discriminative
013            representations of nodes in homophilous graphs, where connecting nodes tend to
014            belong to the same class, many GNNs struggle with heterophilous graphs whose
015            inter-class connections can muddy the message passing. Inspired by this finding,
016
               we propose a topological filtration scheme, treating graphs as 1-dimensional sim-
               plicial complexes N with a filter function based on estimated edge heterophily, and
017
               introduce two methodologies that use a backbone GNN to learn from the resulting
018
               graph filtration. The first trains a GNN on each graph in the filtration sequence
019            consecutively for a portion of the total training time, using embeddings from pre-
020            vious graphs to initialize node embeddings in subsequent graphs. The second ap-
021            proach uses a novel message passing scheme to pass messages jointly within each
022            and between graph levels in the filtration sequence with common nodes. Both
023            methods enhance the influence of early birth adjacent nodes in homophilous sub-
024            graphs yet allow for the model to learn from the full range of heterophilous and
025            homophilous connections in the graph. We further extend our approach to learn
026            a graph filtration sequence of graphs through a learnable node filter function. In
027
               this work, we provide a framework for multiscale learning of graph data struc-
               tures for any backbone GNN, along with an approach for learning a geometrically
028
               significant sequence of nested subgraphs obtained through a leaned topological
029
               filtration of graph data structures. Experiments show that our heterophily-filtered
030            GNNs achieve superior node classification accuracy on both heterophilous and
031            homophilous networks.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Graphical representations offer an accessible and actionable means to express and expand our under-
037   standing of the world, from observed phenomena to abstract notions, by providing explanations or
038   interpretations based on relationships and connections between elements, circumstances, and com-
039
      plex systems. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful tool for learning from
      graph structured data by generalizing the convolution operator to unstructured domains by leverag-
040
      ing message passing or neighborhood aggregation schemes to harness structural information. Re-
041
      cently, in graph machine learning, the classical task of node classification has demonstrated strong
042   results when employing GNNs.
043
044   Despite their potential, GNNs face several known challenges limiting their effectiveness, one in par-
045
      ticular being graphs with high heterophily, whose edges predominantly adjoin nodes of disparate
      classes. In such cases, it is not clear which parts of the graph structure are most helpful for guid-
046
      ing the message passing and expressivity; of these obstacles, message passing networks schemes
047
      probably can not distinguish specific topologies, such as two triangles from a 6-cycle, and a leading
048   known impediment to GNNs are graphs with high heterophily, namely, graphs whose edges pre-
049   dominantly adjoin nodes of disparate classes, which leads to oversmoothing Murphy et al. (2019);
050   Sato et al. (2021); Li et al. (2018). This paper introduces a graph learning methodology that ad-
051   dresses the limitations of conventional GNNs by offering a novel approach employing hierarchical
052   GNNs-leveraging topological insights from persistent homology, namely persistence filtration, to
053   reveal meaningful structure in graphs informed by class connectivity and allowing for a hierarchy of
      multilevel graph resolutions.

054
      To filter our graphs, we first learn class relationships between nodes by estimating edge-level het-
055   erophily labels using the features of their adjacent nodes. We then leverage the probability of
056   homophily assigned to each edge with persistent homology through a persistence filtration func-
057   tion defined over edge values to obtain a multilevel sequence of graphs well suited for hierarchical
058   GNNs. By formulating the learning problem over graph edges, integrating topological summaries,
059   and learning multilevel subgraph hierarchies based on persistent filtration dependent on class con-
060   nectivity, our approach offers an ordered sequence of graphs that captures both local and global
061   structural information while also addressing critical issues such as over smoothing, reducing com-
062   putational complexity, and a paradigm for GNNs more resilient to heterophily We argue that by
063
      providing a more nuanced understanding of graph topology and leveraging hierarchical learning,
      our proposed approach can lead to more resilient, computationally efficient, and expressive GNNs.
064
      Using either of two hierarchical GNN training strategies we propose, a message-passing GNN can
065
      place greater emphasis on edges that are more likely homophilous. At the same time, we retain the
066   ability to learn from heterophilous edges too. Empirically, our approach to topological filtration and
067   hierarchical graph learning compares favorably to previous learning techniques, including standard
068   learning models, standard GNNs, and heterophily-specific models. We also demonstrate competi-
069   tive results over established benchmark datasets for evaluating GNN performance in low and high
070   heterophily scenarios.
071
      Our contributions are as follows:
072
073          • We propose a homophily-based topological filtration method for graphs, where het-
074            erophilous edges may be used for learning but given different emphasis than homophilous
075            edges. Our filtration preserves topological structure compared to approaches that drop het-
076            erophilous edges.
077          • We introduce two different methods for improving backbone GNN models, especially on
078            graphs with heterophily, by allowing them to learn from a multi-scale sequence of topolog-
079            ical graph filtrations based on class connectivity.
080          • We introduce an approach for learning a topological filtration of graphs, minimizing the
081            obstacle of heterophily, to obtain a nested sequence of graphs for training backbone GNNs.
082
             • We experimentally demonstrate that our methods lead to improved node classification ac-
083            curacy relative to previous models.

100   Filter Functions Let E be the domain of simplices and K the set of possible simplicial complexes

128

129
      In the topological filtration of graphs, we can think of filtration as a growth process of a weighted
130   graph. As the graph grows, new edges and nodes are introduced, introducing new connected com-
131   ponents and neighborhood structures.

175   3.3     G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS WITH T OPOLOGICAL F ILTRATION
176
177   We consider two methods for learning node representations for classification using GNNs, which
178   exploit the topological information from the nested sequence of graphs obtained through topological
179   filtration, referred to as the graph hierarchy, in the context of training GNNs. The first method
180
      modifies the training of GNN over graph hierarchies, while the second modifies the architecture to
      pass messages jointly between all hierarchical graph levels.

186   For our first method, we use a GraphSAGE architecture which learns node features via message
187   passing in the spatial domain Hamilton et al. (2017).
188
      Instead of training a GNN for N epochs on the finest graph GP , we train it for NP epochs each on
189
      graphs G1 , . . . , GP , using the embeddings from Gi−1 to initialize the embeddings of corresponding
190   nodes in Gi ∀ i ∈ [1 . . . , P]. The MLPs and node embedding weight matrices for target and neighbor-

398   5     R ELATED W ORK
399
400
401   Here, we review related works on graph neural networks, whether using similar techniques based on
402   filtration learning, or focused on the problem we consider of learning from graphs with heterophily.
403
404
405
      5.1    G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS FOR H ETEROPHILOUS G RAPHS
406
407
408   For a recent overview of graph neural networks, see Zhu et al. (2023); Luan et al. (2024). Meth-
409   ods designed to handle graphs with heterophily usually focus on one of two challenges posed by
410   heterophily. The first is that in heterophilous graphs, nodes may not have relevant neighbors (e.g.
411
      neighbors of the same class) to attend to, and one solution is to expand the receptive field of graph
      neural networks to allow them to learn non-local dependencies. Such approaches include higher-
412
      order neighborhood aggregation, proposed by Zhu et al. (2020), rewiring the graph based on other
413
      forms of node similarity Pei et al. (2020), or introducing non-local attention Zhu et al. (2020). Such
414   approaches may incur a higher computational cost.
415
416   Our work falls into a second category of approaches, which recognize that heterophily also may
417
      muddy the message passing of graph neural networks by causing messages to be aggregated from ir-
      relevant neighbors. Such approaches modify the message passing to better suit heterophilous graphs,
418
      for example by performing it over signed Yan et al. (2022) or directed ? edges, or by learning a class
419
      compatibility matrix that may be used to control message passing ?. Other approaches include
420   rewiring the graph to be better suited to heterophily, by connecting nodes based on some other
421   measure of similarity or distance Pei et al. (2020); Bi et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023).
422
423
      Most related to our work, this rewiring has been performed with a edge classifier that predicts if an
      edge is heterophilous or homophilous Huang et al. (2022); Xue et al. (2024). A backbone graph
424
      neural network is trained on a graph that has been preprocessed using the classifier to improve its ho-
425
      mophily properties. The edge classifier may be used to filter the graph only by pruning heterophilous
426   edges Huang et al. (2022), or new edges may also be added that are predicted to be homophilous Xue
427   et al. (2024). Both methods result in a single output graph which loses any information that the het-
428   erophilous edges may contain. In contrast, our approach gives a model access to the input graph at
429   a variety of filtration levels, giving us the best of both worlds: an ability to use all the information in
430   the original graph, while also being able to emphasize the high-homophily connections in a more fil-
431   tered graph. Moreover, by using persistent homology to perform our filtration rather than arbitrarily
      thresholding edges, we maintain more topological structure in the filtered graphs.

432
      5.2   F ILTRATION L EARNING FOR G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
433
434   Graph filtration learning using persistent homology has been used for graph-level representation
435   learning Hofer et al. (2020). For node classification, persistent homology has also been used to guide
436   message passing in graph neural networks Zhao et al. (2020). More related to our work, Leventhal
437   et al. (2023) has used graph neural networks to classify topological objects in image data, learning
438   successively or jointly from hierarchical levels of a topological filtration. Here, we show how to
439   derive such a hierarchical filtration for graphs specifically based on homophily, and we develop a
440
      node-level filtration that allows us to attend to different levels of filtered graphs.
441
442   6     C ONCLUSION
443
444   We propose graph neural network methods that learn from hierarchies of graphs representing a se-
445   quence of nested graphs obtained from topological filtration. Treating graphs from the perspective
446   of 1-D simplicial complexes, our filtration is defined as being over 1-cells (graph edges). We obtain
447   a sequence of simplicial complexes, where each complex in the series is subset to the subsequent,
448
      from a sorted ordering of 1-cells, thresholded by filtration values prescribed to simplexes through
      a filter function defined as a learning model trained to infer if edges are homophilous, akin to a
449
      class similarity measure between 0-cells (graph nodes). The proposed approach is generalizable
450
      to any edge based classification task or graph with weighted edges that afford an ordering. We
451   then present two methods for hierarchical graph learning. The former, achievable for any standard
452   GNN model, performs message passing across subsequent graphs in the graph hierarchy, aggregat-
453   ing node embeddings sequentially moving up the graph filtration sequence. the ladder introduces
454   a novel multi-scale message passing scheme with aggregation performed jointly across graphs in
455   the filtration sequence, with learned attention based aggregation or learned topological filtration of
456   subset graph nodes, effectively learning the degree of contribution graphs within the hierarchy con-
457   tribute to learned aggregated node embeddings. Our results demonstrate promise for increased node
458   classification accuracy on heterophilous networks compared to conventional GNNs.
