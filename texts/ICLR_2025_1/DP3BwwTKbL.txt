009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Understanding the mechanisms of brain function is greatly advanced by predictive
012            models. Recent advancements in machine learning further underscore the potency
013            of prediction for learning optimal representation. However, there remains a gap
014            in creating a biologically plausible model that explains how the neural system
015            achieves prediction. In this paper, we introduce a framework employing an energy-
016            based model (EBM) to capture the nuanced processes of predicting observation
017            after action within the neural system, encompassing prediction, learning, and
018            inference. We implement the EBM with a hierarchical structure and integrate
019            a continuous attractor neural network for memory, constructing a biologically
020
               plausible model. In experimental evaluations, our model demonstrates efficacy
               across diverse scenarios. The range of actions includes eye movement, motion in
021
               environments, head turning, and static observation while the environment changes.
022
               Our model not only makes accurate predictions for environments it was trained
023            on, but also provides reasonable predictions for unseen environments, matching
024            the performances of machine learning methods in multiple tasks. We hope that
025            this study contributes to a deep understanding of how the neural system performs
026            prediction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   To survive, humans need to interact with environment through actions, requiring an understanding
033
      of how these actions impact the surroundings. This involves building an internal model in the brain
      to represent the outside world Knill & Pouget (2004); Friston & Price (2001). The success of large
034
      language models (LLMs) in understanding token-based worlds also indicates that predicting the
035
      next observation is a good objective in learning representations Radford et al. (2017). However, as
036   humans living in the physical world, our received observations are high-dimensional and diverse.
037   This presents a challenge in understanding how the brain predicts the next observation.
038
039
      The world model Schmidhuber (1990); LeCun (2022) has laid out a basic framework for prediction.
      Recently, the machine learning society has made significant progress in predicting high-dimensional
040
      observations through planning in latent spaces Ha & Schmidhuber (2018); Hafner et al. (2019a;
041
      2023); Nguyen et al. (2021). However, these models, not designed for explaining the neural system,
042   lack consideration for biological realism Chung et al. (2015), and they typically employ biologically
043   implausible training algorithms such as backpropagation (BP) or backpropagation through time
044   (BPTT). In the neuroscience society, there are ongoing efforts to model the hippocampal-entorhinal
045   system as sequential generative models Whittington et al. (2018); George et al. (2023). However,
046   these approaches either employed a variational method, leading to still requiring BPTT, or assumed
047   access to the underlying state of the world, which is not realistic to the neural system.
048   Energy-based models (EBMs) Ackley et al. (1985) provide a framework for inference with sampling
049   methods and learning with Hebb’s rule. The variability of neuronal responses in the brain has been
050   explained as Monte Carlo sampling Hoyer & Hyvärinen (2002), which naturally accounts for the
051   regular firing and other response properties of biological neurons Haefner et al. (2016); Orbán et al.
052   (2016); Echeveste et al. (2020). Hebb’s rule is a widely observed local learning rule in the neural
053   system. A recent work Dong & Wu (2023) has shown that hierarchical EBMs are capable of learning
      complex probability distributions, suggesting their potential widespread applications in the brain.

054
      In this paper, we propose a sequential generative model based on hierarchical EBMs to capture
055   how the brain predicts the next observation after an action (Section 3). In our model (Section 4), a
056   Markov chain of latent variables is employed, whose conditional probabilities following Gaussian
057   distributions. This choice helps us bypass the computation of the partition function, leading to
058   accelerated model convergence. Furthermore, the introduction of error neurons ensures that the
059   learning process is localized. We also utilized a continuous attractor neural network (CANN) Amari
060   (1977); Ben-Yishai et al. (1995); Wu et al. (2008) to memorize past events to improve prediction. In
061   the brain, sensory information undergoes hierarchical processing through the cortex before entering
062   higher brain regions (such as the IT region and hippocampus) DiCarlo et al. (2012), while CANNs
063
      have been widely used as canonical models for elucidating the memory process in these higher brain
      regions Wills et al. (2005). In the experiments (Section 5), we considered visual observations and
064
      constructed various actions to assess model performances. The actions include eye movement, motion
065
      in a virtual environment, motion and head-turning in a real environment, and static observation while
066   the external world varies. Our model demonstrates effective predictions for the environments it was
067   trained on, and the model also generates reasonable predictions for unseen environments. In several
068   tasks, our biologically plausible model has achieved performances on par with machine learning
069   methods. Key contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
070
071          • Energy-based Recurrent State Space Model (RSSM) We introduce a novel framework
072
               for RSSM grounded in energy-based principles. This approach diverges from the conven-
               tional variational RSSM Chung et al. (2015); Hafner et al. (2019b) by offering distinct
073
               methodologies for inference, learning, and prediction within the energy-based paradigm.
074
075
             • Biologically Inspired RSSM Implementation Our implementation leverages hierarchical
               EBMs and CANNs to realize the RSSM. The learning mechanism is characterized by its
076
               local properties, both spatially and temporally, without relying on BP or BPTT. Algorithms
077
               for inference and prediction can be implemented through neural dynamics.
078
079
             • Establishment of a Prediction Error Upper Bound Setting our approach apart from
               previous methodologies that employ free energy or the evidence lower bound (ELBO) as
080
               the loss function, we adopt the prediction probability distribution within the latent space for
081
               sampling. This provides a novel perspective on model optimization.

084   2    R ELATED WORK
085
086   The world model Schmidhuber (1990); LeCun (2022) laid out a framework for predicting observa-
087
      tions following an agent’s action. Recently, RSSM compresses observations through a variational
      autoencoder (VAE) , then performs predictions in the compressed temporal space using temporal
088
      prediction models like RNNs Chung et al. (2015); Hafner et al. (2019b), Transformers Chen et al.
089
      (2022), S4 models Samsami et al. (2024) or continuous Hopfield networks Whittington et al. (2018).
090   Our model adopts this RSSM framework but innovates by incorporating EBMs instead of VAEs and
091   utilizing CANNs for temporal predictions. Our model aligns with biological plausibility, departing
092   from less biologically realistic architectures and training methods.
093
      Active inference Friston et al. (2017); Smith et al. (2022) is another framework which can predict the
094
      observation after an action. These works are unified under the free energy principle framework Friston
095
      (2010), modeling the prediction process as a hidden Markov model (HMM, a special case of the
096   RSSM), and using the variational message passing algorithm for inference Da Costa et al. (2020);
097   Parr et al. (2019). We model the entire process as an RSSM with a temporal model (CANN) that can
098   compress all previous states rather than reliance on the current state alone. Also our model employs
099   a sampling algorithm, enabling online inference and learning without the need to know the entire
100   sequence.
101
      Predictive coding networks (PCNs) Rao & Ballard (1999) can be viewed as an implementation of
102   EBMs, with most current PCN works deal with static inputs Salvatori et al. (2021; 2023); Millidge
103   et al. (2022), do not involve temporal prediction of the next observation after actions. A recent study,
104   ActPC Ororbia & Mali (2023), does introduce actions within the Markov process (a special case
105   of HMM); however, it lacks an encoder-decoder structure, assuming an identity matrix mapping
106   between observations and latent states. Our model integrates an EBM as the encoder-decoder part.
107   Furthermore, while their approach utilizes a buffer to store observations directly, our model employs
      a CANN to efficiently compress all previous states.

      3    E NERGY- BASED RECURRENT STATE SPACE MODEL
109
110   To build an internal model capturing the change of the environment during interaction, the brain
111   needs to learn to predict the next observation after an action. We consider that the brain employs
112   an energy-based RSSM as the intrinsic generative model for generating predictions. This section
113   outlines the model framework encompassing the predicting, learning, and inference processes, with
114   the detailed neural implementation presented in Section 4.
115
      Problem setup. Consider an action at taken at time t. The brain anticipates the observation ot
116   the sensory neurons will receive after this action. According to the laws of physics, the world is
117   Markovian, i.e., the next moment is solely determined by the previous moment. However, our
118   observation ot and action at reflect only a subset of the world, and we do not have the full knowledge
119   of the world. To enhance the prediction, the brain can rely on past experiences. Denote Kt =
120   {o<t , a≤t } the observation-action sequence we have experienced, upon which the brain can build a
121   memory trace mt to facilitate the next prediction of ot .
122   Generative model. We consider that the brain utilizes the marginal distribution of the intrinsic
123   generative model (Figure 1a) to approximate the distribution of the true observation. The joint
124   distribution at time t is expressed as,
125
                                          pθ (ot , st |mt ) = pθ (ot |st )p(st |mt ),                               (1)
126
127   where st are latent variables represented by neuronal responses. We employ the sampling-based
128
      probabilistic representation Hennequin et al. (2014); Dong et al. (2022), assuming that the neural
      activity at time t is a sample of the random variable st . pθ (ot |st ) is the likelihood function and
129
      p(st |mt ) can be regarded as the prior of the latent variable before receiving the observation given
130
      the memory state mt . At the next time step t + 1, the memory is updated following a transition
131   probability mt+1 ∼ p(mt+1 |mt , st , at+1 ), which contains the information of the past experiences

133   generative model essentially a recurrent state-space model Hafner et al. (2019b).

312   We evaluate our model by selecting four types of action in different environments, including eye
313   movement, motion in a virtual environment, motion and head-turning in a real environment, and
314
      static observation while the external world varies. To simulate the high-dimensional inputs received
      by the brain, all observations in our study are exclusively chosen to be visual inputs. We refer to the

      appendix for hyper parameters (Appendix E).

      parietal cortex encode stimuli that will be seen after planned eye movements Cui & Andersen (2011);
      Kuang et al. (2016). Additionally, experiments Seung (1996) suggest that neurons in the medial
321
      vestibular nucleus form a CANN to record eye direction.

455   6     D ISCUSSION
456
457   We consider that the brain employs an EBM as an intrinsic generative model to predict the next
458   observation after action. We utilize a hierarchical neural network to implement this process and
459   incorporate a CANN as memory to compress past experiences. As a biologically plausible neural
460   network, our model succeeds in various environments with different actions. This provides insight
461   into how the brain builds an internal model capturing the dynamics of the environment. Moreover, our
462   model achieves performances on par with machine learning methods, indicating that our framework
463   has the potential for further development.
464   Unlike previous machine learning works Hafner et al. (2020) or predictive coding network approaches
465   Tang et al. (2023), our framework differs in that we first perform learning and then inference (see
466   the order in Algorithm 1). In contrast to previous approaches that conduct learning after inference,
467   this order in our framework leads to a distinct objective function. Our objective is expressed as
468   Ep(s|m) log p(o|s) (see Eq.(3)), whereas previous works often use Eppost log p(o|s). We experimented
469   with the latter objective as well, but it resulted in poorer and less robust performance in our model. In
470
      fact, our approach of learning before inference is closer to the autoregressive models used in LLMs.
471   In the current model, when computing the gradient of the objective function with respect to the model
472   parameters, we ignore the influence of parameters on memory (see Eq.(5)). This can be understood
473   as a form of Truncated BPTT. Nevertheless, in experiments related to movement, we found that this
474   does not affect our long-distance predictions.
475   Future work. Due to the Markovian nature of our generative model, our framework can seamlessly
476   integrate with reinforcement learning (RL). By simply defining additional rewards for specific tasks,
477   we can achieve model-based RL, creating a biologically plausible world model. In our current model,
478   though direct access to the underlying state is not available, the utilization of CANNs effectively
479   establishes a prior structure for the underlying state. We have not yet thoroughly investigated the
480   impact of the environment dynamics on the CANN structure. The two for-loops in Algorithm 2
481   are executed sequentially. In neural systems, however, all neurons compute simultaneously. It has
482
      been demonstrated that by introducing a modulation function for error neurons, both for-loops can
      be unrolled to achieve parallel computation Song et al. (2020). Nevertheless, when using a real
483
      continuously changing environment, we still need to carefully adjust the model’s time constants to
484
      ensure it can maintain synchronized interaction with the real environment. We will explore these
485   aspects in our future work.
