011            Segment Anything Model (SAM), a prompt-driven foundation model for natural
012            image segmentation, demonstrated impressive zero-shot performance. However,
013            SAM does not work when directly applied to medical image segmentation tasks,
014            since SAM lacks the functionality to predict semantic labels for predicted masks
015            and needs to provide extra prompts, such as points or boxes, to segment target
016
               regions. Meanwhile, there is a significant gap between 2D natural images and
               3D medical images, so the performance of SAM is imperfect for medical image
017
               segmentation tasks. Following the above issues, we propose MaskSAM, a novel
018
               mask classification prompt-free SAM adaptation framework for medical image
019            segmentation. We design a prompt generator combined with the image encoder
020            in SAM to generate a set of auxiliary classifier tokens, auxiliary binary masks,
021            and auxiliary bounding boxes. Each pair of auxiliary mask and box prompts,
022            which addresses the requirements of extra prompts, is associated with class label
023            predictions by the sum of the auxiliary classifier token and the learnable global
024            classifier tokens in the mask decoder of SAM to solve the predictions of semantic
025            labels. Meanwhile, we design a 3D depth-convolution adapter for image embed-
026            dings and a 3D depth-MLP adapter for prompt embeddings. We inject one of
027
               them into each transformer block in the image encoder and mask decoder to en-
               able pre-trained 2D SAM models to extract 3D information and adapt to 3D med-
028
               ical images. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on AMOS2022 Ji
029
               et al. (2022), 90.52% Dice, which improved by 2.7% compared to nnUNet. Our
030            method surpasses nnUNet by 1.7% on ACDC Bernard et al. (2018) and 1.0% on
031            Synapse Landman et al. (2015) datasets.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Foundation models Devlin et al. (2018); He et al. (2022), trained on vast and diverse datasets, have
037   shown impressive capabilities in various tasks OpenAI (2023b); Radford et al. (2021) and are revo-
038   lutionizing artificial intelligence. The extraordinary zero-shot and few-shot generalization abilities
039
      of foundation models derive a wide range of downstream tasks and achieve numerous and remark-
      able progress. In contrast to the traditional methods of training task-specific models from scratch,
040
      the “pre-training then finetuning” paradigm has proven pivotal, particularly in the realm of computer
041
      vision. Segment Anything Model (SAM) Kirillov et al. (2023), pre-trained over 1 billion masks on
042   11 million natural images, was recently proposed as a visual foundation model for prompt-driven
043   image segmentation and has gained significant attention. SAM can generate precise object binary
044   masks based on its impressive zero-shot capabilities. As a crucial branch of image segmentation,
045   medical image segmentation was dominated by deep learning medical segmentation methods Ron-
046   neberger et al. (2015); Akkus et al. (2017); Avendi et al. (2016) for the past few years. The existing
047   deep learning models are often tailored for specific tasks and achieve remarkable progress due to
048   the consumption of a strong inductive bias. This raises an intriguing question: Can SAM still have
049   the ability to revolutionize the field of medical image segmentation? Or can SAM still achieve high-
050
      performance results in medical image segmentation by properly fine-tuning based on SAM’s strong
      zero-shot capabilities in natural image segmentation?
051
052   Since the publication of SAM, numerous studies have attempted to adapt it for medical image seg-
053   mentation; however, few SAM-based models have effectively addressed the medical challenges,
      such as the AMOS22 challenges Ji et al. (2022), a key benchmark for validating medical image

      Inability to predict semantic labels: SAM generates a single binary mask per prompt without associ-

      essential semantic information. Existing SAM-based methods typically produce binary (one-class)

077   et al. (2023), and 3DSAM-Adapter Gong et al. (2023), sacrificing SAM’s zero-shot capabilities and
078   restricting their functionality to one-class datasets.
079   Inadequate handling of prompt requirements: SAM demands precise user input prompts to seg-
080   ment target regions, a requirement not addressed by many models. Typically, these models rely
081   on ground truth (GT) data to generate prompts during inference, as seen in Med-SA Zhang et al.
082   (2024), 3DSAM-Adapter Gong et al. (2023), and SAM-U Deng et al. (2023a). However, these
083   methods cannot participate in challenges where labels for prompts are unavailable.
084   Subpar performance: Even when using appropriate prompts, SAM often underperforms in med-
085   ical image segmentation tasks. Although some models, like SAMed Zhang & Liu (2023) and
086   SAM3D Bui et al. (2024), attempt to address semantic label generation and prompt requirements,
087   their performance remains inferior. Consequently, refining the fine-tuning process for adapting SAM
088   from natural image segmentation to medical image segmentation is crucial, contributing to the lim-
089   ited participation of SAM-based models in medical challenges. Additional related work is presented
090   in the Appendix A.
091   Our proposed SAM-based MaskSAM effectively addresses the aforementioned challenges and
092   achieves state-of-the-art performance in the AMOS22 challenge. To handle the extra prompt re-
093   quirements, we designed a prompt-free architecture for SAM. We observed that the image encoder
094   utilizes the Vision Transformer (ViT)Dosovitskiy et al. (2020), pre-trained with a masked auto-
095   encoderHe et al. (2022), as its backbone. Leveraging ViT’s robust representation capabilities, the
096   image encoder extracts essential features through a series of transformer blocks. To eliminate the
097   dependency on manual prompts, we introduce a prompt generator that employs multiple levels of
098
      feature maps from the image encoder, generating a set of auxiliary binary masks and bounding boxes
      as prompts, thus resolving the need for additional prompts.

102   tions, we were inspired by MaskFormer Cheng et al. (2021) to introduce global learnable classifier
103   tokens. These tokens, combined with the auxiliary classifier tokens, associate each predicted binary
104
      mask with its corresponding class in the mask decoder.

107   lengths of binary masks in the ground truth data. Drawing inspiration from DETR Carion et al.
      (2020) and MaskFormer Cheng et al. (2021), our prompt generator creates a sufficient number of

131   Several studies Ma & Wang (2023); Wu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Gong et al.

139   approach enables us to retain SAM’s zero-shot capabilities while effectively adapting it to medical
140   image segmentation tasks. In this paper, we employ the lightweight adapter for efficient fine-tuning.
141
      However, the above works usually abandon the prompt encoder or mask decoder to avoid the re-
      quirements of additional prompts provided, which would destroy the consistent system of SAM and
142
      abandon the robust prompt encoder and mask decoder, which are trained via large-scale datasets and
143
      lots of resources. Therefore, the primary challenge lies in modifying the structure to preserve the
144   inherent capabilities of SAM. Therefore, we keep all structures, freeze all weights, and only insert
145   designed blocks into SAM to adapt. In this way, we retain the zero-shot capabilities of SAM and

      2.1                         SAM P RELIMINARIES
207
208
      SAM is a prompt-driven foundation model for natural image segmentation, trained on the extensive
209
      SA-1B dataset containing 1 billion masks and 11 million images. Its architecture comprises three
210   main components: an image encoder that employs a Vision Transformer as the backbone, a prompt
211   encoder that embeds various types of prompts (e.g., points, boxes, or text), and a lightweight mask
212   decoder that generates masks based on image embeddings, prompt embeddings, image positional
213   embeddings, and output tokens. For segmenting a provided 2D image, SAM requires prompts such
214   as points or boxes, subsequently generating a single binary mask per prompt without any associ-
215   ated semantic labels. However, medical segmentation tasks typically involve multiple objects with
      distinct semantic labels within a single image, making this approach insufficient.

      2.2   OVERVIEW OF THE P ROPOSED M ASK SAM
217
218   In this section and the ones that follow, we introduce the complete pipeline and individual compo-
219   nents of MaskSAM, as illustrated in Figure 1 and Figure 4. MaskSAM retains the full structure
220   of SAM but introduces specifically designed blocks to adapt the model from 2D natural images to
221   3D medical images. The architecture of MaskSAM includes a modified image encoder, a custom
222   prompt generator, the original prompt encoder, and a modified mask decoder. Additionally, we have
223   developed a dataset mapping process that converts multi-class labels into binary masks for each class
224   with semantic labels, ensuring that each predicted binary mask corresponds to a single class.
225
226   2.3   P ROPOSED DATASET M APPING
227
228   SAM generates a single binary mask without an associated semantic label for each prompt, whereas
229   the typical ground truth for medical images comprises multiple classes. Each input patch image may
230   contain several different classes, and the challenge lies in handling varying lengths of binary masks
231
      in the ground truth. Inspired by DETR Carion et al. (2020) and MaskFormer Cheng et al. (2021),
      our model generates a sufficient number of binary masks, with each mask dedicated to predicting a
232
      single class. We employ bipartite matching to align the predicted masks with ground truth segments
233
      accurately. As shown at the bottom of of Figure 1, our dataset mapping pipeline converts a multi-
234   class mask into a set of binary masks with semantic labels per class.
235

      2.4   P ROPOSED P ROMPT G ENERATOR
238
      To address the need for additional prompts, we introduce a prompt generator, illustrated in Fig-
239
      ure 4(b), that automatically generates a set of auxiliary binary masks and bounding boxes instead of
240   relying on manual prompts. We utilize both box and mask prompts, as point prompts tend to intro-
241   duce instabilities detrimental to medical segmentation tasks. Leveraging the strong representation
242   capabilities of the Vision Transformer (ViT), we extract multiple levels of feature maps from the
243   image encoder as input to our prompt generator. The final output of the image encoder is connected
244   to convolution layers, upsampled, and concatenated with feature maps from lower levels, resulting
245   in feature maps with the same size as the ground truth.
246   A convolutional layer is then applied to adjust the channel size to a fixed number, N , which exceeds
247   the maximum number of object-level binary masks in the dataset. Additionally, we extract outputs
248   from the last convolutional layer at each level, apply adaptive average pooling to adjust the spatial
249   dimensions to (2, 2) for box queries, and concatenate all box queries. An MLP layer then adjusts
250   the channel to N , resulting in N learnable binary masks and bounding boxes.
251
      There are several combinations of learnable binary masks and boxes, as illustrated in Figure 2(a)-
252   (d). Figure 2(a) depicts a prompt generator that produces only learnable binary masks, using these
253   masks to calculate their corresponding bounding boxes. Figure 2(b) shows a prompt generator that
254   creates only boxes as prompts. Figure 2(c) combines both learnable binary masks as mask prompts
255   and learnable boxes as box prompts. In Figure 2(d), the prompt generator produces learnable binary
256   masks as mask prompts and learnable boxes, with the final box prompts obtained by averaging the
257   bounding boxes derived from the binary masks and the learnable boxes. Based on extensive experi-
258   mentation, we found that the approach shown in Figure 2(d) is the most effective, as it incorporates
259   more information and offers greater robustness.
260   To address the challenge of predicting semantic labels, the prompt generator simultaneously gener-
261   ates a set of auxiliary classifier tokens in a similar manner to the generation of auxiliary box prompts,
262   with the exception of using adaptive average pooling layers to adjust the spatial dimension to (1, 1)
263   for classifier tokens. These auxiliary classifier tokens are then combined with our designed learnable
264   global classifier tokens within the mask decoder.

525   4    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we introduce MaskSAM, a mask classification prompt-free SAM adaptation frame-
528
      work for medical image segmentation that adapts pre-trained SAM models from 2D natural images
      to 3D medical images without requiring any prompts. We achieve this by designing a prompt gen-
529
      erator integrated with the SAM image encoder to generate auxiliary classifier tokens, binary masks,
530
      and bounding boxes. Each pair of auxiliary mask and box prompts addresses the need for additional
531   prompts and is linked to class label predictions through the sum of auxiliary classifier tokens and
532   learnable global classifier tokens within SAM’s mask decoder, enabling semantic label predictions.
533   We further enhance our model by incorporating a 3D depth-convolution adapter (DConvAdapter)
534   for image embeddings and a 3D depth-MLP adapter (DMLPAdapter) for prompt embeddings into
535   each transformer block of the image encoder and mask decoder. This enables pre-trained 2D SAM
536   models to effectively extract 3D information and adapt to 3D medical images. Our method achieves
537   state-of-the-art performance, with a Dice score of 90.52% on the AMOS2022 Ji et al. (2022) dataset,
538   representing a 2.7% improvement over nnUNet. Additionally, MaskSAM outperforms nnUNet by
539
      1.7% on the ACDC Bernard et al. (2018) dataset and by 1.0% on the Synapse Landman et al. (2015)
      dataset.

729   A.1   R ELATED W ORK
730
731   Deep Learning Methods for Medical Image Segmentation. Deep learning methodologies have
732   dominated medical image segmentation recently. The predominant approaches can be categorized
733   into convolution-based models Ronneberger et al. (2015); Zhou et al. (2018); Milletari et al. (2016);
734   Çiçek et al. (2016); Isensee et al. (2019), transformer-based models Zhou et al. (2021), and hybrid
735
      models Chen et al. (2021); Cao et al. (2021) that integrate convolutional and transformer architec-
      tures. The adoption of encoder-decoder networks, particularly U-shaped networks, has dominated
736
      the prevailing trend. These methods stand out by explicitly tailoring architectures for medical image
737
      segmentation and training from scratch. However, the models exhibit high inductive bias, which
738   also limits their adaptability and overall capacity.
739
740   Foundation Models and Parameter-efficient Finetuning. Foundation models Brown et al. (2020);
741
      OpenAI (2023a) are dedicated to the development of large-scale, general-purpose language and
      vision models. These models derive a wide range of downstream applications, achieving remarkable
742
      success following the pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm He et al. (2019); Hu et al. (2021). The
743
      goal of parameter-efficient finetuning Houlsby et al. (2019); Pan et al. (2022); Hu et al. (2021);
744   Yang et al. (2023) is to decrease the number of trainable parameters and reduce the computation cost
745   while achieving or surpassing the performance of full finetuning. Recently, the Segment Anything
746   Model (SAM) Kirillov et al. (2023), pre-trained over 1 billion masks on 11 million natural images,
747   was proposed as a visual foundation model for image segmentation and has gained a lot of attention.
748   In this paper, we adopt the strategy of parameter-efficient finetuning to adapt SAM from 2D natural
749   image segmentation to 3D medical image segmentation.
750   SAM-based Medical Image Segmentation. SAM-based medical image segmentation research can
751   be broadly divided into two primary streams. The first stream of studies Deng et al. (2023b); Hu
752   & Li (2023); Zhou et al. (2023); Mohapatra et al. (2023); Roy et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023);
753   He et al. (2023) focuses on evaluating SAM’s performance across various medical image segmen-
754   tation tasks and modalities using manually provided prompts. These evaluations, conducted across
755   different datasets, reveal that SAM performs well with certain objects and modalities compared to
      state-of-the-art methods. However, SAM’s performance is often suboptimal in scenarios with weak


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      boundaries, low contrast, or small and irregular shapes, limiting its effectiveness for many medical
757   imaging tasks. The second stream of research Ma & Wang (2023); Wu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023);
758   Gong et al. (2023) aims to adapt SAM more effectively for medical image segmentation tasks. The
759   challenge lies in addressing the need for prompts. While some studies attempt to bypass this re-
760   quirement by modifying or removing the prompt encoder or mask decoder, such approaches risk
761   disrupting SAM’s cohesive structure and diminishing the strengths of these components.
762
      To overcome these limitations, we propose a prompt-free SAM framework that generates auxiliary
763   masks and bounding boxes directly through the image encoder using a specially designed prompt
764   generator. This approach not only retains SAM’s zero-shot capabilities but also eliminates the de-
765   pendence on additional prompts, providing a more comprehensive and efficient solution for medical
766   image segmentation.
