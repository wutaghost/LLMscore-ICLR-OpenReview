009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Test-Time Adaptation has recently emerged as a promising strategy that allows
012            the adaptation of pre-trained models to changing data distributions at deployment
013            time, without access to any labels. To address the error accumulation problem,
014            various approaches have used the teacher-student framework. In this work, we
015            challenge the common strategy of setting the teacher weights to be an exponential
016            moving average of the student by showing that error accumulation still occurs,
017            but only on longer sequences compared to those commonly utilized. We analyze
018
               the stability-plasticity trade-off within the teacher-student framework and propose
               to use an intransigent teacher instead. We show the surprising result that not
019
               changing any of the weights of the teacher model within existing test-time adapta-
020
               tion methods allows them to significantly improve their performance on multiple
021            datasets with longer scenarios. Finally, we show that the proposed changes are
022            applicable to different architectures and experimental setups, and are more robust
023            to changes in hyper-parameters.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Machine learning models typically assume that training and testing data originate from a similar
029   distribution. However, in real-world applications, distribution shifts between training (source) and
030   testing (target) data domains are common and can lead to performance issues throughout infer-
031   ence (Geirhos et al., 2019; Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019; Koh et al., 2021). Test-Time Adaptation
032   (TTA) (Wang et al., 2021) is an emerging paradigm that allows for an online adaptation of a pre-
033
      trained model to the changing data distributions during testing, where there is a lack of access to any
      labels. While many methods have been developed in recent years (Gong et al., 2022; Goyal et al.,
034
      2022; Marsden et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2022; 2023; Rusak et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
035
      2022; Yuan et al., 2023b), important challenges remain within TTA, such as adaptation over very
036   long scenarios (Press et al., 2023), robustness to noisy data (Gong et al., 2023), and sensitivity to
037   hyper-parameter change (Boudiaf et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Cygert et al., 2024).
038
039
      The teacher-student paradigm is a popular TTA framework (Chen et al., 2022; Döbler et al., 2022;
      Sójka et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2024), where the teacher
040
      weights are set to the exponential moving average (EMA) of the student weights. This strategy,
041
      follows pioneering works in semi-supervised learning (Laine & Aila, 2017; Tarvainen & Valpola,
042   2017), representation learning (Grill et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Oquab et al., 2024) and learning
043   under label noise (Liu et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2020). The averaged model provides more accurate
044   and consistent predictions which the student uses for training. However, this strategy does not
045   necessarily prevent error accumulation, which may result in model collapse (i.e., falling below the
046   accuracy of the source model). In this work, we present experimental evidence indicating that state-
047   of-the-art TTA methods that utilize teacher-student framework (Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022;
048   Yuan et al., 2023b) are prone to significant accuracy degradation on extended test sequences.
049   We observe that using a simple technique of making the teacher more intransigent (not updating
050   the teacher’s weights) prevents model collapse over very long testing sequences and, interestingly,
051   can teach students to fulfill the age-old cliche of surpassing their teacher (see Fig. 1). Based on
052   that finding, we take a closer look at the stability-plasticity trade-off (Mermillod et al., 2013) within
053   teacher-student frameworks and how it affects the final model performance. We show that while in-
      creased teacher plasticity can lead to better performance in the short run, using a more stable teacher

087   2               R ELATED WORK
088
089
      Test-time adaptation. TTA (Wang et al., 2021) aims to adapt a pre-trained model to shifting data
090   distributions during testing, without any labels. Different unsupervised objectives can be used, ex-
091   amples of which include entropy minimization (Niu et al., 2022; 2023; Wang et al., 2021), cross-
092   entropy variants (Döbler et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019; 2022) or self-supervised objectives (Sun
093   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022). Since the objective is unsupervised, optimizing it over multiple
094   iterations might result in error accumulation (Chen et al., 2019), which is a great challenge in
095   test-time adaptation. Therefore, numerous strategies have been developed to circumvent that is-
096   sue, amongst of which is the teacher-student framework (Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Yuan
097   et al., 2023b; Sójka et al., 2023). The teacher-student framework was introduced to TTA by the
098
      CoTTA method (Wang et al., 2022), where they proposed to use an exponential moving average
      (EMA) of weights. However, since the teacher is an EMA of student weights, nothing prevents error
099
      accumulation in the long run, and therefore we analyze the teacher-student framework in this work.
100
101   Other works have explored strategies that keep the fixed source model. ROID (Marsden et al., 2024)
102   continually ensembles weights of a fixed source model with those of a backpropagated model to
103   stabilize the backpropagated model update. Both GROTTA (Li et al., 2023) and TRIBE (Su et al.,
104
      2024) incorporate a third, fixed source model for additional regularization with an extra loss term.
      While these methods employ complex designs, we explore how a simple intransigent teacher tech-
105
      nique can address the performance degradation problem in TTA, especially for longer sequences.
106
107   Parallel work (Zhou et al., 2024), also proposes to evaluate existing TTA methods over very long
      adaptation scenarios. Our works are complementary, since they propose an adaptive method to work


                                                                             2
108
      in such conditions (which requires parameter tuning), while we focus on evaluating existing ap-
109   proaches on a more extensive experimental setup, and observe that a simpler modification improves
110   the reliability in such conditions.
111
112
      Plasticity-stability trade-off. EMA ensemble of weights is parametrized by a β parameter , which
      determines the balance between retaining old averaged weights of the teacher and incorporating
113
      newly updated student ones, commonly set in TTA to 0.999 (Wang et al., 2022) (following tempo-
114
      ral ensembling works (Laine & Aila, 2017)), where β = 1 means full stability (frozen model), and
115   β = 0 means maximal plasticity. The trade-off has been widely analyzed in continual learning (Mer-
116   millod et al., 2013; Chaudhry et al., 2018; Masana et al., 2022), where the learner needs to balance
117   learning of new tasks with the risk of forgetting the previously acquired knowledge. While a vari-
118   ety of strategies have been developed in continual learning, the most successful ones are those that
119   promote stability. Many recent works freeze the feature extractor and learn only the classification
120   part (Goswami et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023; Panos et al., 2023). Note that TTA can be considered
121   more challenging due to unavailability of labels. Our work is inspired by those studies and aims to
122   analyze the plasticity-stability trade-off within TTA.
123   Teacher-student framework. In knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015), a usually larger model
124   (teacher) guides the optimization of the target model (student) by providing informative training
125   signals (teachers’ outputs). Such a strategy is also commonly used in continual learning to prevent
126   forgetting of previous tasks when learning new ones (Buzzega et al., 2020; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017).
127   Self-distillation is a special case in which the teacher and student have the same architecture. It
128   has been shown that in such a scenario, the student can outperform its teacher (Furlanello et al.,
129
      2018). In their work, the teacher is updated at the end of every training epoch, by copying students’
      weights. They show improvement gains until such a procedure is repeated three times. Note that
130
      in TTA, there is no access to labeled data, and therefore, updating the teacher might result in even
131
      more significant error accumulation.

      3   I NTRANSIGENT TEACHER

136
137
      The teacher-student framework is widely studied in TTA, with great results being obtained by pop-
      ular methods such as AdaContrast (Chen et al., 2022) and CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022). Although
138
      these methods also rely on other components (e.g., memory queue or weight restoration), they share
139
      a common trend of incorporating a self-supervision loss. We dissect their self-supervised losses (di-
140   rectly related to learning), which allows for disentangling the impact on the teacher-student frame-
141   work by the component that is most related to the stability-plasticity trade-off.
142
143
      We probe standard EMA teachers (ET) as proposed in their original works (both using β = 0.999)
      and compare them with the presented intransigent teacher (IT) technique (β = 1). Intransigent
144
      teachers have all trainable parameters frozen. Their batch normalization statistics are calculated on
145
      a per-batch basis (Schneider et al., 2020), as commonly done in TTA (Niu et al., 2022; 2023; Wang
146   et al., 2021; 2022), and the final predictions are taken from the student model. As a motivation
147   example, we evaluate on the popular ImageNet-C corruption benchmark (Hendrycks & Dietterich,
148   2019) and on the recently introduced CCC benchmark (Press et al., 2023), to observe the adaptation
149   performance over long sequences. Furthermore, we introduce the setting with ImageNet-C repeated
150   20 times (ImageNet-C (L)), in order to have another very long sequence similar to the classic TTA
151   scenarios. Table 1 shows numerical results and Fig. 2 shows accuracy over time, where the evaluated
152   objectives are described as:
153
154         • Consistency: CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022) minimizes the cross-entropy consistency between
155           predictions from the teacher and the student. The input of the teacher is transformed via
156
              additional augmentation following the original implementation.
157
            • Contrastive: AdaContrast (Chen et al., 2022) uses a MoCo-inspired (He et al., 2020) con-
158           trastive task in which features from different views of the same image (positive pairs) are
159           pulled closer, while features from different images (negative pairs) are pushed away by
160           pseudo-labels. Input from both teacher and student is augmented by randomly drawing
161           two strong augmentations.

193   The results from the above experiment lead to the following observations:
194   Observation 1: Self-supervised objectives combined with intransigent teachers provide great re-
195   liability on their own. This is a very positive result, as recent work (Press et al., 2023) observed

197   interesting in the case of AdaContrast, where the contrastive loss changes only the backbone of the
198   model without changing the classification layer, mostly relying on feature alignment.
199   Observation 2: An exponential moving average on its own does not prevent error accumulation.
200   As clearly shown in Fig. 2, the problems when using EMA take some time to become apparent
201   and are only visible over long sequences. In both consistency and contrastive cases, performance
202   degradation starts rather early, after the 2nd and 3rd loops, respectively.
203
      Observation 3: When using the EMA, there is a small gap between the teacher and student accura-
204
      cies. When an intransigent teacher with consistency loss is used, the student performance is reliable
205   and comparable to the teacher, while in the case of using the contrastive loss, the student is able to
206   outperform their teacher significantly.

209   4               M ETHODOLOGY
210
211   As presented in the previous section, using an intransigent teacher with self-supervised objectives
212   provides great model consistency throughout longer scenarios without encountering catastrophic er-
213   ror accumulation. In this section, we aim to better understand the plasticity-stability trade-off within
214   existing TTA teacher-student frameworks, this time when considering all proposed components.
215   First, we describe how to extend existing TTA methods with an intransigent teacher strategy. After,
      we evaluate and discuss the effects of low-to-none plasticity within the proposed adapted methods.


                                                             4
216
      4.1    A PPOINTING AN INTRANSIGENT TEACHER
217
218   We extend popular TTA methods, AdaContrast (Chen et al., 2022), CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022),
219   RoTTA (Yuan et al., 2023a), and PETAL (Brahma & Rai, 2023), with our proposed intransigent
220   teacher. We only modify the value of the β parameter used in an EMA ensemble of weights by
221   setting it to 1, and take the final output predictions from the student model, regardless of which
222   prediction was used in the original method. We do not alter the usage or adaptation of batch normal-
223   ization statistics. Below, we briefly describe the strategies extended with IT.
224   AdaContrast conducts weight updates using a three-part loss function: cross-entropy loss , diversity
225   regularization, and contrastive loss. Pseudo-labels are refined by keeping a buffer of previous image
226   features and their predictions. Refined predictions are based on the nearest neighbors of the current
227   feature within the buffer. Statistics in batch normalization layers are updated with EMA.
228
      CoTTA updates the student model by minimizing the cross-entropy consistency between the teacher
229   and the student predictions. Depending on the prediction confidence, the pseudo-labels are the result
230   of averaging predictions on multiple, differently augmented images. At each iteration, there is a
231   small probability for each of the student’s weights to be reset to the source pre-trained value. It
232   calculates batch normalization statistics on a per-batch basis.
233
      RoTTA keeps the class-balanced memory buffer of images and uses it to perform the optimization
234
      in constant intervals. The loss is weighted based on how long the sample has been stored. Cross-
235   entropy-based consistency between the student and teacher models is utilized for a loss function.
236   Batch normalization statistics are updated via EMA.
237
238
      PETAL is similar to CoTTA but it enhances the learning objective by the regularizer term based
      on a posterior distribution over a source model weights and a data-dependent prior. Moreover, it
239
      improves CoTTA’s stochastic model reset scheme with the Fisher Information Matrix.
240

      5                  E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
289
290
291
      Benchmarks. We focus on the continual test-time adaptation setting introduced in (Wang et al.,
      2022), where methods are continually evaluated on a stream of unlabelled test data, without utiliz-
292
      ing any knowledge about domain changes. We evaluate the methods on a wide variety of bench-
293
      marks with domain shifts. Our experiments include popular corruption benchmarks (Hendrycks &
294   Dietterich, 2019) - CIFAR10-C and ImageNet-C. They involve training the source model on clean
295   CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2009)/ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) images and testing the adaptation on
296   corrupted images. There are 15 types of corruptions with 5 levels of severity. We follow the setup
297   from (Wang et al., 2022; Niu et al., 2022; Döbler et al., 2022) and use the standard corruption se-
298   quence with the highest severity level. The adaptation on natural domain shifts is tested utilizing
299   DomainNet-126 (Peng et al., 2019) and ImageNet-R (Hendrycks et al., 2021) datasets. DomainNet-
300   126 includes data from 4 distinct domains: real, clipart, painting, and sketch. We pre-train the model
301   on a real domain and experiment on the remaining ones. ImageNet-R is composed of 30,000 images
302
      portraying different renditions of 200 ImageNet classes.
303   Our goal is to focus on very long adaptation sequences to evaluate the methods in terms of stability
304   during long-time operation. For that reason, we repeat the standard test sequences of benchmarks
305   described above and loop them 20 times. We call this setup, a Long (L) scenario. Our longest
306   sequence of 3,000,000 images is generated using CIFAR10-C benchmark. Simply repeating the
307   benchmarks 20 times provides limited data variability. Nevertheless, we argue that if existing meth-
308
      ods do not cope well in such settings (as we show), they especially would not work well in more
      complex real-world settings.
309
310   Additionally, we take advantage of CCC (Press et al., 2023) benchmark. It was created by applying
311   2 corruptions from the corruption benchmarks to images from the ImageNet (Russakovsky et al.,
312   2015) dataset. It greatly fits our experiments, since one of the assumptions of this benchmark was
313   to make it very long. We use a CCC-Medium sequence with a 1k transition speed, which consists
314
      of 7,500,000 images.
315   Batch size. We utilized two batch sizes: a standard value of 64, as in multiple previous works (Mars-
316   den et al., 2024; Press et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021), and a lower one (equal to
317   10). Smaller batch sizes are more challenging in TTA because they result in an increased number of
318   model updates and due to difficulties in batch statistics calculation. In some papers, even the batch
319   size of 1 is used (Niu et al., 2023); in our experimental setup, we choose the less extreme value of
320
      10, which is also commonly used in online continual learning (Mai et al., 2022). This means that
      for the longest sequence (CCC) each model is adapted 750,000 times.
321
322   Methods. We apply our modification to 4 teacher-student state-of-the-art frameworks: AdaCon-
323   trast (Chen et al., 2022), CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022), RoTTA (Yuan et al., 2023a), PETAL (Brahma
      & Rai, 2023). Moreover, we report the performance with IT using (I-) prefix. Additionally, we com-

347

      Following the insights from the motivation experiment in Sec. 3, we evaluate the intransigent teacher
349
      modification over longer scenarios and compare the results with various state-of-the-art methods.
350   Since this strategy extends existing methods, we refer to our proposed intransigent teacher with the
351   prefix (I-) in front of the method being extended. All the proposed longer sequences than the usually
352   reported ones in TTA are denoted with an (L) and consist of 20 loops over the commonly estab-
353   lished adaptation scenarios. Further, the efficacy of the proposed approach is verified on numerous
354   model architectures, including transformer-based ones. Finally, we present the evaluation of the
355   hyperparameter selection robustness of our strategy.

      6.1   O N ADAPTATION OVER LONG SCENARIOS
358
359
      The vulnerability of EMA teachers is revealed on extended test sequences. In long scenarios (see
360   Table 3), methods based on teacher-student framework often achieve lower performance compared
361   to baselines or even cause the model to collapse, which is especially apparent on both ImageNet-C
362   and CCC benchmarks. Those shortcomings are amplified in the lower batch size setting, potentially
363   caused by a more significant error accumulation due to difficulties in estimating batch statistics and
364   a larger number of adaptation steps. While the goal of using EMA is to provide a more stable
365   adjustment and more accurate pseudo-labels for adaptation, we find this to not hold true for the
366   longer settings. This is in contrast to the evaluation on common sequence length (see Supplemental
367   Material), where the EMA teacher performs well and the original methods don’t cause model col-
368
      lapses in most cases, except for the more challenging lower batch size setting. Results show that the

445
446   Intransigent teachers work across different architectures. To verify if our findings hold for
447   various model architectures, we present results on CIFAR10-C (L) and ImageNet-C (L) in Table 4.
448   The learning rate is not tuned specifically for any of the models, and thus default values are used.
449   The results with adjusted learning rates can be found in the supplementary (Table A.2). The IT is
450   able to improve the TTA accuracy on long sequences for all of the compared models, including the
451   ones without batch normalization layers. Therefore, rendering itself a highly universal approach.
452   On robustness to learning rate selection. In Table 5, we verify learning rate sensitivity by multi-
453   plying its value by 10 and 50. Default AdaContrast shows robustness when 10x learning rate is used,
454   but collapses with a 50x learning rate on ImageNet-C. ITs allow increased robustness in all settings
455   and achieve a non-collapsed solution, even for the highest learning rate. CoTTA lacks learning rate
456   robustness, which is mitigated by the IT extension. Our strategy improves RoTTA’s performance
457   on CIFAR10-C. However, its low performance on ImageNet-C rendered our approach almost inef-
458
      fective for this setup. Overall, ITs do not fully prevent the collapse when significantly altering the
      learning rates, although they seem to promote a better-performing parameter space.
459
460   Effects varying temporal correlation. Figure 5 illustrates the impact of varying degrees of class
461   temporal correlation (Gong et al., 2022) on methods enhanced with IT. The analysis reveals that as
462   temporal correlation increases, IT-enhanced methods consistently outperform their original versions,
463   demonstrating superior robustness in these scenarios. This is the case even when the original method
464
      achieved better results on uniform class distribution (RoTTA). However, it should be noted that
      while IT-enhanced methods show improved performance, they are also negatively impacted by the
465
      effects of correlation. The degree of improvement varies significantly and is largely dependent on
466
      the underlying base technique employed.
467
468   Are students better than intransigent teachers? Figure 6 illustrates the accuracy difference be-
469   tween student and teacher models across various methods and datasets, allowing us to assess the
470
      student’s learning capacity in relation to the intransigent teacher. In most cases, the student outper-
      forms the teacher, with only two exceptions: I-AdaContrast on ImageNet-R (L) and I-CoTTA on
471
      CIFAR10-C (L). In these instances, the stability introduced by IT was insufficient to prevent degra-
472
      dation, only limiting further performance decline. Notably, after adjusting the learning rate (Figure 6
473   (right)), we observe no such cases of student underperformance.

523     7                                             C ONCLUSIONS
524
525
        In this work, we explore the well-known strategy of using an exponential moving average teacher for
526
        test-time adaptation. We identify that this strategy, while being very effective at a common length
527
        of test sequences, has some shortcomings in longer ones, leading to model collapse on many bench-
528     marks. After analyzing the plasticity-stability trade-off within existing teacher-student frameworks,
529     we present a simple, effective strategy with an intransigent teacher that can be adapted to existing
530     state-of-the-art methods. We show the efficacy of the presented modification by achieving significant
531     improvements across many sequences, often preventing model collapse and increasing robustness
532     to hyper-parameter changes. Most importantly, the benefits of this strategy are visible without any
533     parameter tuning, it even removes the need to tune the β parameter used in EMA teachers.
534     Limitations. It is unclear if the capabilities of the intransigent teacher become limited when there is
535     some knowledge or prediction over the expected domain shift. However, the motivation behind the
536     presented strategy is to provide consistent and reliable improvements when no assumptions about
537     the distribution shift are available. Furthermore, we note that by adding the intransigent teacher
538     to existing methods, we inherit their limitations. That is even though we improve the performance
539     on many scenarios, if the baseline performs poorly, then using an intransigent teacher might not
        improve over the source (non-adapted model).

771   A.2   BASELINES IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
772
773
      The experiments were conducted using the code repository of the previous test-time adaptation
774   works (Marsden et al., 2024; Döbler et al., 2022). It provides the implementation of every tested
775   state-of-the-art method. In terms of hyperparameters, we followed the implementations for tests on
776   the typical batch size of 64.
777
      TENT (Wang et al., 2021), EATA (Niu et al., 2022), SAR (Niu et al., 2023), and RDUMB (Press
778
      et al., 2023) use Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 for CIFAR10-C and SGD optimizer
779
      with a learning rate of 0.00025 for other benchmarks. AdaContrast (Chen et al., 2022) utilizes an
780   SGD optimizer with a learning rate set to 0.0002 for all of the benchmarks. CoTTA (Wang et al.,
781   2022) uses Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.001 for CIFAR10-C and SGD with a learning
782   rate of 0.01 for the rest of the benchmarks. Adam optimizer with a learning rate set to 0.001 is
783   used by RoTTA (Yuan et al., 2023a) for all of the tested datasets. MEMO (Zhang et al., 2022)
784   uses an SGD optimizer with a learning rate of 0.005 for CIFAR10-C and 0.00025 for other datasets.
785   PETAL (Brahma & Rai, 2023) in the original paper uses Adam optimizer with a learning rate of
786   0.001 for CIFAR10-C and SGD with a learning rate of 0.01 for other datasets. However, since we
787   often experienced poor performance using these values on long scenarios, we utilized 10 times lower
788
      learning rates.
789   The learning rate used in experiments with batch size set to 10 was adjusted accordingly by scaling
790   it linearly.
791
      CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022) and PETAL (Brahma & Rai, 2023) methods update the student net-
792   work using a consistency loss between the student and teacher. If the prediction confidence of the
793   source model is below a certain threshold, the teacher’s predictions are averaged over 32 different
794   augmentations of the image which adds 31 additional forward operations of the neural network for
795   each batch. It creates a significant computation overhead and causes the methods to be significantly
796   slower, compared to other state-of-the-art methods. It is especially problematic for long adaptation
797   sequence scenarios, which were the main part of our experiments. Our tests indicate that using a
798   single augmentation does not alter the results notably. Therefore, for the ease of experimentation,
799   we reduce the number of augmentations to 1.
800   The learning rate selection process for Figure 6 (right) was conducted using the Oracle method.

1001   across datasets, with model performance dependent on this parameter. When following a realistic
