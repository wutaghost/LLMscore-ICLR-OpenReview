009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In medical image registration, where targets exhibit piecewise smooth structures, a
012            carefully designed low-resolution data structure can effectively approximate full-
013            resolution deformation fields with minimal accuracy loss. Although this physical
014            prior has proven effective in traditional registration algorithms, it remains underex-
015            plored in current learning-based registration literature. In this paper, we propose
016            AdaWarp, a novel neural network module that leverages this prior for efficient and
017            accurate medical image registration. AdaWarp comprises an encoder, a guidance
018
               map generator, and a differentiable bilateral grid, enabling an edge-preserving
               low-frequency approximation of the deformation field. This design reduces compu-
019
               tational complexity with low-resolution feature maps while increasing the effective
020
               receptive field, achieving a balanced trade-off between registration accuracy and
021            efficiency. Experiments on two registration datasets covering different modalities
022            and input constraints demonstrate that AdaWarp outperforms existing methods in
023            accuracy-efficiency and accuracy-smoothness tradeoffs.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Image registration is a fundamental step in various medical imaging tasks, such as population
029   modeling and statistical atlas construction [1, 2]. Traditional methods [3, 4, 5] minimize an energy
030   function via gradient descent or discrete optimization, often requiring many iterations and extensive
031   hyperparameter tuning. Since they optimize each input pair independently, these methods cannot
032   perform amortized optimization, making it challenging to integrate label supervision from a cohort and
033
      leading to slow processing times. Recently, learning-based approaches have accelerated registration
      by pre-training neural networks on image pair cohorts using amortized optimization. VoxelMorph [6],
034
      a seminal model in this domain, leverages a convolutional neural network (ConvNet) [7] to predict
035
      deformation fields, achieving fast and accurate image registration.
036
037   Several follow-up works have explored different strategies to improve registration accuracy [8, 9, 10,
038   11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18]. Many of these approaches leverage advanced network architectures,
039
      such as transformers [19, 8, 10] and large convolutional kernels [11, 20], achieving modest accuracy
      gains at a disproportionately higher computational cost. Moreover, to handle datasets with large
040
      deformations, cascaded and pyramid structures such as VTN [16] and LapIRN [9] have been employed
041
      to improve registration accuracy; however, they often compromise the balance between registration
042   accuracy and deformation smoothness. While several methods [15, 12, 18, 14], such as DeepFLASH
043   [15] and FourierNet [12], have specifically targeted improving the accuracy-efficiency tradeoff,
044   achieving an optimal balance between the two remains a challenge.
045
      Previous studies have shown that incorporating prior knowledge improves the accuracy-efficiency
046
      trade-off in image segmentation [21, 22, 23, 24] and image registration [15, 12, 25], motivating
047
      the design of our proposed architecture. Combining this insight with our observations from daily
048   MRI and CT scans in cardiac and abdominal regions, we note two consistent patterns: (1) intensity
049   variations within anatomical regions are generally smooth, and (2) distinct boundaries often exist
050   between organs and the background or neighboring organs. For instance, in cardiac imaging, intensity
051   within regions like the right ventricle and myocardium is relatively homogeneous, while clear and
052   well-defined boundaries are formed by intensity differences between these regions (see Fig. 1,
053   columns 1&2). These consistent intra-region smoothness and inter-region boundaries indicate that
      certain medical images exhibit piece-wise smooth structures, serving as a valuable physical prior for

075   Piece-wise Smooth (P-S) Assumption. In medical image registration tasks where the targets of
076   interest exhibit piece-wise smooth structures, a carefully designed low-resolution data structure can
077   effectively approximate full-resolution deformation fields.
078
      Several studies [12, 15] have shown that displacement fields exhibit limited high-frequency content in
079
      the Fourier domain. This insight allows neural networks to operate in a band-limited space, reducing
080   computational complexity without sacrificing accuracy, particularly in brain MR image registration.
081   Interestingly, follow-up research by Jia et al. [13] demonstrates that FourierNet, leveraging band-
082   limited approximation for cardiac registration, not only reduces computations but also improves
083   accuracy by enlarging the effective receptive field (ERF) [26]. However, reliance on global smoothness
084   constraints makes these methods less effective for datasets with large deformations and complex
085   motions. For instance, as shown in Fig. 1, cardiac imaging involves the heart moving within the rigid
086   thoracic cavity or displaying complex localized motion between ventricles and myocardium, resulting
087   in local discontinuities. In such cases, imposing a globally smooth deformation field becomes too
088
      restrictive. To address these discontinuities, some works have employed bilateral filters [27, 28],
      which preserve edges and improve registration performance in the presence of local discontinuities
089
      [29, 30]. However, the non-trainable nature of these filters limits their broader adaptability in
090
      learning-based registration frameworks.
091
092   Here, we identify a gap in the literature: existing learning-based registration frameworks lack an
093   end-to-end learnable approach to incorporate the physical prior, i.e., the P-S Assumption, into
094
      neural networks, leading to suboptimal registration performance. In this paper, we address this gap
      by introducing AdaWarp, a neural network module that integrates the P-S assumption, enforcing
095
      global smoothness while respecting local discontinuities. AdaWarp employs learnable adaptive
096
      filtering to register medical scans, achieving better accuracy-efficiency and accuracy-smoothness
097   trade-offs. AdaWarp comprises an encoder, a guidance mapper, and a differentiable bilateral grid. The
098   encoder, based on ConvNets [32, 33] or Vision Transformers (ViTs) [34], generates low-resolution
099   representations, while the guidance mapper, a multi-layer perceptron (MLP), produces a guidance map
100   representing the range dimension and capturing local intensity differences. The core of AdaWarp is
101   the differentiable bilateral grid [35, 36], which inherently incorporates the P-S prior. The grid begins
102   with a differentiable splatting module that maps the 3D image into a 4D bilateral grid, spanning
103   the 3D spatial domain and a 1D range domain. This grid undergoes learnable blurring for adaptive
104   filtering, followed by a slicing module to produce a piece-wise smooth output. The main contributions
105
      of this paper are as follows:
106
107          • We propose AdaWarp, a novel neural network module that integrates the P-S prior in an end-
               to-end learnable manner, filling the gap of existing learning-based registration frameworks.

113   2   R ELATED W ORK
114
115   Bilateral Grid and High-Dimensional Filtering: The bilateral filter [27, 28] enhances image quality
116   by replacing each pixel with a weighted average of its neighbors, using weights based on spatial
117   proximity (spatial domain S) and intensity similarity (range domain R). While effective for edge-
118   preserving image manipulation, its native implementation is slow. Accelerated techniques like the
119   bilateral grid [35, 36], Gaussian KD-Trees [37], permutohedral lattice [38], and adaptive manifolds
120
      [39] project signals into compact high-dimensional spaces for real-time performance. These methods
      have been integrated into neural networks for tasks like scene-dependent image transformation [40]
121
      and stereo matching [41], though their use in dense medical image registration remains limited.
122
      Recent innovations such as bilateral neural networks [42] and the fast bilateral solver [43] extend
123   these ideas but have yet to find broad applicability in medical imaging.
124
125   Learning with Differentiable Transformations: The bilateral grid requires both grid-push and
126
      grid-pull operations to manage splatting and slicing. While prior studies [44, 41] have represented the
      range domain using deep bilateral grids and channel shuffling [45], by directly reshaping the encoded
127
      tensor U ∈ RC×H×W with C = CΓ × R into a bilateral grid Γ ∈ RCΓ ×H×W ×R , this method does
128
      not fully capture the range domain. Though conceptually similar, grid-push and grid-pull are adjoint
129   operations. Grid-push handles transformations involving summation, such as Hough transform [46,
130   47] and splatting in the bilateral grid [35, 36]. Conversely, grid-pull deals with transformations
131   involving spatial sampling, such as image warping using a deformation field [6]. In our work, we use
132   existing differentiable grid-push and grid-pull techniques [48, 49] to build the differentiable grid.
133
      Learning-based Image Registration: Image registration aims to align a moving image Im with
134
      a paired fixed image If by estimating a deformation field ϕ. The transformation at each voxel is
135
      defined as ϕ(x) = x + u(x), where x is a spatial location in the domain Ω ⊆ RH×W ×D , and u(x)
136   is the displacement vector at x. This deformation field ϕ establishes a voxel-wise mapping from each
137   location in If to its corresponding location in the warped moving image Im ◦ ϕ. Learning-based
138   methods, such as VoxelMorph [6], employ unsupervised learning to optimize the expected loss
139   function and derive neural network weights θ from a cohort of image pairs D, formulated as:
140
141                  θ̂ = arg min{E(If ,Im )∼D [f (If , Im ◦ gθ (If , Im )) + λr(gθ (If , Im ))]}.       (1)
                             θ
142
143   Here, f and r represent the dissimilarity and regularization functions, respectively. The function gθ ,
144   once trained, predicts the deformation field ϕ directly from the input, i.e., ϕ = gθ (If , Im ). Recent
145   advancements in learning-based image registration extend beyond VoxelMorph [6] by incorporating
146   Vision Transformers [19, 50] into frameworks like TransMorph [8] and XMorpher [10]. Innovations
147   such as symmetric networks [51, 52], multi-channel architectures [31], large-kernel convolutions
148   [11, 53], and cascaded frameworks [54, 9, 17] have further advanced the field. More recent methods
149   like RDP [55] and CorrMLP [56] combine image pyramids [57] or multi-scale strategies [17] with
150
      advanced modules like MLPs and vision transformers [58], achieving competitive performance.
      However, as noted by Jena et al. [59] and Hansen et al. [60], amortized optimization with the same
151
      voxel-wise dissimilarity as iterative instance optimization methods offers no clear advantages in
152
      unsupervised settings. AdaWarp addresses this gap by integrating the P-S prior, naturally modeling
153   pairwise voxel relationships and enhancing flow signal propagation within a learned cost volume in
154   an edge-aware manner.

253   3.2.2    ACHIEVING A DAPTIVE F ILTERING
254
      For edge-preserving filtering in the bilateral grid, it is essential to track the pixel count or weight per
255
      grid cell. During splatting, a tensor the same spatial size as U, filled with ones, is concatenated with
256   U across channels. The grid is then divided into a value tensor and a weight tensor post-splatting,
257   with the former normalized by the latter before further processing.
258
259
      Filtering in the original image maintains translation-equivariance in the spatial domain S but can
      propagate information across nearby objects, causing issues in image registration with local dis-
260
      continuities. In contrast, filtering in the bilateral grid preserves locality in both spatial and range
261
      domains S × R, enabling edge-preserving filtering. As the same filter is applied to each cell in the
262   grid, originally adjacent cells in the spatial domain may become farther apart in the range domain.
263   When projected back to image space, each pixel is effectively filtered with a unique kernel, adapting
264   to local intensity variations, akin to the adaptability of self-attention mechanisms. See the appendix
265   for the derivation of connections between adaptive filtering and self-attention.

310   setting, masks were used only during training.

      4.1.2     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
338
339   For all methods, including AdaWarp, we follow Balakrishnan et al. [6] and use scaling and squaring
340   [69] with 7 integration steps for diffeomorphic transformation. It is worth noting that the guidance
341   map is sourced from the fixed image only, as for each voxel location in the target image, the
342   deformation field samples a value from moving image. Dataset-specific implementation details are
343   provided below.
344   Ada-Cost. The instantiation of AdaWarp for deformable image registration is Ada-Cost, which
345   follows ConvexAdam’s discrete optimization strategy [62, 61] while being end-to-end trainable like
346   other learning-based methods. It uses two 3D conv-norm-act blocks as encoder to extract moving and
347   fixed feature maps, followed by trilinear downsampling to form a 3-level image pyramid. At each
348   pyramid level, a cost volume with one neighbor is computed, followed by two 4D conv-norm-act
349   blocks for adaptive filtering, and finalized with a 3D conv-norm-act block and a convolution to extract
350   the deformation field. Unlike prior multi-scale methods [70, 71, 17], Ada-Cost processes cost volumes
351   with shared network weights across pyramid levels, reducing parameters while maintaining optimal
352
      performance. For the cardiac dataset, raw images are processed through an additional conv-norm-act
      block before being input to the encoder. For the abdomen dataset, feature maps extracted by a
353
      pretrained universal segmentation network [72] (pre-softmax) serve as input to the encoder.

506   5     D ISCUSSIONS
507
508   5.1   E FFECTS OF S AMPLING R ATE sr & ss .
509
510   The sampling rate sr affects the range domain (R). At sr = 1, the range dimension reduces to
511   1, turning the process into a gated attention process, where the guidance map gates the linearly
512   upsampled encoder output. Decreasing sr increases range dimensionality, enhancing edge distinction
513   but also raising computational costs. Experiments show that reducing sr from 1 to 1/64 improves
514
      accuracy in both cardiac and abdomen registration, peaking at sr = 1/8 and sr = 1/32, respectively,
      before declining. The smaller optimal sr for abdomen reflects its higher need for handling local
515
      discontinuities. The sampling rate ss influences the spatial domain (S). At ss = 1, spatial dimensions
516
      match the original image, and increasing ss smooths the results while reducing computation. Raising
517   ss from 1 to 32 improves accuracy, peaking at ss = 8 for both tasks before decreasing.
518
519
      5.2   L IMITATIONS .
520
521   The development of AdaWarp revealed two key limitations. First, while AdaWarp effectively
522   respects object boundaries by projecting image signals to a higher-dimensional space, we only
523   explored intensity differences for edge awareness. As discussed in §B of the appendix, AdaWarp
524   represents a special case of a more generalized adaptive filtering framework. Future work could
525   investigate contextual differences beyond intensity differences through high-dimensional adaptive
526   filtering. Second, projecting to high-dimensional space results in many zero-valued cells, with
527
      non-zero cells forming a submanifold [76]. Restricting grid manipulations, such as convolutional
      filtering, to this submanifold could further reduce computational overhead.
528
529
530   6     C ONCLUSIONS
531
532   Our paper introduces AdaWarp, a novel neural network module for medical image registration that
533   improves both accuracy-efficiency and accuracy-smoothness trade-offs by leveraging the piece-wise
534   smooth prior. This prior is implemented through a learnable bilateral grid with guidance mapping,
535
      enabling accurate low-frequency approximations while preserving boundary details. Its success in
      two medical registration tasks highlights its broader applicability to similar problems. Moreover,
536
      AdaWarp transforms deformable registration into a keypoint detection task, with potential applications
537
      in segmentation tasks as briefly discussed in the appendix.

902   be projected onto a bilateral grid as Γ = D(u, G; K). Here, G includes mesh grids and Gr , and K is
903   a linear sampling kernel. The task then is to minimize the equation arg minΓ̃ ||grad(Γ̃)||2 , subject

963   concentrate in the low-frequency range, while smaller masks spread their frequency components. For
964   vessel masks, frequency components, except for the 0 component, are distributed more uniformly.
965   This suggests that bilinear upsampling may suffice for segmenting large masks, but smaller masks
966   require more careful handling.
967   We use skin lesion segmentation to validate our hypothesis, leveraging the ISIC2018 challenge dataset
968   [88, 89] with 2,594 training images, 100 for validation, and 1,000 for testing. The model’s ability
969   to segment piece-wise constant masks aligns with its core design. During training, raw images
970   are input with corresponding masks as output, without architectural modifications. We evaluate
971   segmentation accuracy using Dice and HD95 (in pixels). For this task, we use Swin Transformer
      [50] as the encoder, with one model performing direct upsampling ("Swin Encoder Upsample")
