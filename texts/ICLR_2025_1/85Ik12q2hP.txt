009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The reasoning abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) remain a topic of
012            debate, which are critically tested in sequential decision-making problems. ReAct,
013            a recently popular method has gained popularity for claiming to enhance LLM
014            reasoning abilities while directly prompting them by “interleaving reasoning trace
015            with action execution" in text-based planning domains such as AlfWorld and
016            WebShop. However, given the different components of ReAct-style prompting, it
017            remains unclear what the source of improvement in LLM performance is. In this
018
               paper, we critically examine the claims of ReAct-style prompting for sequential
               decision-making problems. By introducing systematic variations to the input
019
               prompt, we perform a sensitivity analysis along the original claims of ReAct.
020
               Contrary to these claims and common use-cases that utilize ReAct-style prompting,
021            we find that the performance is minimally influenced by the interleaved reasoning
022            trace or by the content of these generated reasoning traces. Instead, the performance
023            of LLMs is primarily driven by the unreasonably high degree of similarity between
024            input example tasks and queries, implicitly forcing the prompt designer to provide
025            instance-specific examples which significantly increases the cognitive burden on
026            the human. Our empirical results, on the same suite of domains as ReAct, show that
027            the perceived reasoning abilities of LLMs stem from the exemplar-query similarity
028            and approximate retrieval rather than any inherent reasoning abilities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have seen rapid advancements specifically in Natural Language
      Processing and Understanding (NLP & NLU). LLMs have unparalleled capabilities in text generation,
034
      summarization, translation, question answering to name a few. (Bubeck et al., 2023). Motivated by
035
      these capabilities of LLMs, there has also been a rush to look for other emergent abilities–especially
036   for reasoning and planning. A popular way of enhancing LLM performance on reasoning/planning
037   tasks has been in-context prompting or prompt-engineering (Sahoo et al., 2024) to include instructions
038   (Giray, 2023), syntax structure (Marvin et al., 2023), criticism and plan guidance with verification
039   (Kambhampati et al., 2024) etc. Among these approaches, ReAct (Yao et al., 2022b), presented at
040   ICLR 2023, stands out which claims to improve LLM planning abilities through the use of reasoning
041   traces interleaved with action execution given as plan guidance. While their original study includes
042   multiple domains tasks such as question answering on HotPotQA and FEVER datasets (Yang et al.,
043   2018; Thorne et al., 2018), in this paper we are particularly interested in planning tasks where ReAct
044
      claims superior performance.
045   In our initial experiments with ReAct for planning, we found that the system is overly dependent on
046   a high degree of syntactic similarity between the example prompt and the query, and is extremely
047   brittle to minor perturbations to the input prompt. For example, when provided with an explicit set of
048   examples of pick-and-place-object task and asked to plan for a pick-and-place-two-objects task, it
049   should be trivial to generalize the solution of the examples to the queried task. Unfortunately, even
050
      such a minor variation to the original ReAct agent setup disrupts its performance.
051   Given the seemingly widespread adoption of ReAct methodology (as of this writing, it has 1,408
052   citations), the brittleness we witnessed calls for a systematic study of the factors contributing to the
053   performance of ReAct-based LLM Agents. Moreover, recent studies have highlighted similar case
      studies on the original Chain-of-Thought idea (Stechly et al., 2024a; Sprague et al., 2024). Based on


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the claims of (Yao et al., 2022b), we isolate three possible reasons for the claimed performance of
055   ReAct framework: 1) the utility of interleaving reasoning trace during action execution, 2) the utility
056   of providing plan guidance, and, 3) the significance of example prompt provided to the the LLM.
057
058
      The brittleness of ReAct becomes apparent when considering the variability in prompt designs.
      Depending on the domain and task, prompt designers may provide abstract guidance, task-specific
059
      instructions within the same domain, analogical examples, or global reasoning traces. ReAct’s
060
      inability to robustly adapt to such variations underscores its limitations in handling diverse input
061   prompts, thereby necessitating a closer examination of its design and implementation. In this work,
062   we systematically evaluate the brittleness of ReAct by studying which potential factors contribute
063   to its performance. This analysis is conducted through variations in input prompts to understand
064   how a ReAct LLM Agent responds to (1) where the guidance is provided, (2) the different types
065   and structure of this guidance, and finally, (3) on varying the resemblance of example prompt to the
066   queried problem. We investigate the research questions :
067   RQ1: Does the agent performance depend on interleaving reasoning trace with action execution?
068   RQ2: How does the nature of the reasoning trace or guidance information affect the performance of
069   LLM Agents?
070   RQ3: How does the similarity between the example 〈problem, solution〉and the query 〈problem, ? 〉,
071   which are present in the prompt, affect LLM Agent performance?
072
      We conduct extensive experiments on the AlfWorld and WebShop domain using various LLM Models,
073   including GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-3.5-instruct, GPT-4-0314(Old Variant), GPT-4-0613 (Latest Variant),
074   GPT-4o, Claude-Opus and Llama 3.1-8b. Through our comprehensive empirical study, we answer
075   each of the research questions above. (RQ1) We find that LLM performance in-fact improves when
076   the reasoning trace is not interleaved with action execution. (RQ2) Moreover, providing weaker
077   guidance or placebo-guidance (where the text provides no information about the task) has comparable
078   performance to strong reasoning trace-based guidance. Answers to RQ1 and RQ2 suggest that the
079   source of ReAct LLM agent performance is not the interleaving aspect or the content of the reasoning
080   trace. Finally, in (RQ3), we see that variations to the example prompt such that it belongs to different
081
      task within the same domain, or has a different goal or plan attributes than the queried problem;
      causes the performance of ReAct-LLM Agent to plummet.
082
083   Our findings highlight that the benefits of ReAct-style prompting are present when prompt engineers
084   can curate highly instance-specific examples. This may not scale for domains with a large number of
085   problem instance classes, and it places an undue burden on prompt engineers to provide instance spe-
086   cific examples. Finally, our experiments call into question claims of enhanced “emergent reasoning"
087
      of LLMs with prompt engineering efforts; corroborating contemporary research (Verma et al., 2024;
      Valmeekam et al., 2024; Stechly et al., 2024b; Ullman, 2023; Schaeffer et al., 2023; McCoy et al.,
088
      2023; Stechly et al., 2024a; Sprague et al., 2024) questioning reasoning abilities of LLMs.
089
090   For the rest of the paper, we begin with discuss the relevant literature discussing LLM prompting for
091   planning problems in Section 2. Next, we provide the preliminaries in Section 3, and discuss our
092   investigations of ReAct-style prompting in detail in Section 4. We present our empirical results with
093
      a detailed discussion drawing insights from our results in Section 5. Finally, we conclude the work
      in Section 6. An appendix with additional experiment details, results, and prompts, has also been
094
      attached, and the code will be released on acceptance.

      WebShop: (Yao et al., 2022a) is an online shopping website environment with 1.18M real-world

      products and 12K human instructions. The agent is provided with an initial human instruction (for
151   example, “I am looking for a nightstand with drawers. It should have a nickel finish, and priced lower
152   than $140"). The agent’s task is to crawl the shopping environment using actions such as search
153   ‘nightstand drawers’, choose ‘white buttons’, back to search, etc. For
154   this work, we randomly sample 500 test instructions from the environment and evaluate the success
155   rate of the agent’s task completion.

      4     A C RITICAL E VALUATION OF R E ACT-S TYLE P ROMPTING
187
188
      We examine the claims of ReAct to understand the performance of ReAct-based LLM agents. It is
189
      crucial to assess whether ReAct’s fundamental claims hold, particularly in planning. As outlined in
190   Section 3, ReAct comprises three main components: interleaving the think tag with actions, plan
191   guidance after the think tag, and the selection of exemplar problems for LLM prompts. We perform
192   a sensitivity analysis by proposing alternatives along these three dimensions. The subsequent sections
193   explore the design of exemplar prompt variations to investigate our research questions concerning
194   the claims of ReAct. Each variation modifies the base ReAct prompt, and we use the AlfWorld
195   domain as the running example for discussing these variations in the following sub-sections. We
196   curate variations for WebShop domain in a similar manner. Readers can find the variations in the
197   attached supplementary materials.
198

200

201

      will not be possible.

256   4.2   RQ2 : P LAN G UIDANCE FOLLOWING think TAG
257
258   How does the nature of the reasoning trace or guidance information affect the performance of LLM?
259   ReAct claims to use reasoning trace as the guidance information following the think tag. For instance,
260   in ReAct () thoughts are to (1) decompose the goal (2) track subgoal completion (3) determine
261   the next subgoal and (4) reason via common-sense where to find and object and what to do with
262   it. It is, however, unclear what is the motivation to use these as the reasoning trace. The potential
263   anthropomorphization of large language models (LLMs) may suggest that their thought processes
264   are similar to the abstract plans humans make, and that they must be prompted in the same manner.
265   However, it is unclear why this assumption should hold true. Alternatives can be, we can prompt the
266
      LLM to reflect on past failures and provide possible explanations (hindsight-guidance) or We can
      substitute task-relevant guidance with placebo-guidance by using "magic incantations".
267
268   Variation 1: Failure From the example prompts used in ReAct, we note that none of the examples
269   for any task consist of invalid actions. We inject two invalid actions in the execution trace : the first
      that attempts to execute the action pertinent to the task (such as put spraybottle 2 in/on


                                                                 5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      toilet) when not possible and, second, executes some other invalid action. We include the expected
271   simulator response, Nothing happens., when invalid actions are taken. Intuition: Reasoning
272   trace can be about what to do such as subgoals of the future, or what not to do such as mistakes in
273   hindsight. This should be weaker guidance than in base ReAct as the exemplars do not point out what
274   to do next.
275
      Variation 2: Failure + Explanation We place think actions after invalid actions injected in Failure
276
      Variation which consist of explanations for the failure (see RQ2 b in Figure 2). Intuition: We
277   can augment pointing out mistakes in hindsight with explanations to avoid similar failures. This is
278   stronger guidance signal than Failure, however, the exemplars still not provide information on what
279   to do next.
280
      Variation 3: Guidance Ordering LLMs are known to be susceptible to minor syntactic perturbations
281
      to inputs. We test whether it is true for guidance information given as prompt as well (see RQ2 c
282
      in Figure 2). We identify chain of subtasks in a reasoning trace S1 → S2 · · · Sn and reverse it to be
283   Sn → Sn→1 · · · S1 . Intuition: LLM agent should be invariant to the syntax of reasoning trace if the
284   semantic information is preserved. This does not change the reasoning trace from the perspective of
285   information content.
286
      Variation 4: Placebo Guidance It is unclear to what extent LLM agent uses the supposed helpful
287
      thoughts for the decision making task. In this variation we replace think tag guidance with a placebo
288
      thought that does not contain any task relevant information (see RQ2 c in Figure 2), but has been
289   widely used as prompt engineering trick (Kojima et al., 2022). Intuition: According to claims of
290   ReAct, we expect the performance to get worse when the guidance does not have any information
291   useful for task success.

      4.3   RQ3 : S IMILARITY BETWEEN EXAMPLE S AND QUERY
294
295   How does the similarity between the example 〈problem, solution〉and the query 〈problem, ? 〉, which
296   are present in the prompt, affect LLM Agent performance?
297
      RQ3 investigates the role of example similarity to the query in LLM agent’s performance. Establishing
298
      problem similarity can be challenging, especially where minor variations to the problem can have
299
      varied interpretations (such as an analogy to a different task altogether). Our work explores this
300   challenge in a systematic way. During example prompt construction, prompt designers may use
301   synonyms to refer to objects (Domain), come up with examples where the agent task is the same
302   as query but the goals are different (Instance), or provide optimal solutions as the examples
303   (Optimal) preventing LLM to obtain information regarding exploration strategy. Furthermore,
304   given that the domain has the same underlying action dynamics and that the tasks reuse several
305   actions, prompt designers may choose to provide query specific example prompts (as in base ReAct),
306   provide one of a different task and one of the same task (One), provide both examples to be of a
307   different task (Both), or take an exhaustive approach and provide one example of all tasks (All).
308   Variation 1: Synonyms - (Domain) For this variation, we replace the object and location names in
309   the example prompts with their synonyms. For example, spraybottle → aerosolbottle,
310   cabinet → cupboard, and, microwave → oven. We make 36 such changes to object and
311   location names across all the examples. Note that the object names / location are unchanged for the
312   problem query and subsequent interaction with the simulator. Intuition: Exemplar guidance maybe
313   specified with alternate synonymous object and location names. Reasoning agents should be invariant
314
      to variable name substitution for closed world dynamics such as PDDL based AlfWorld.
315   Variation 2: Problem Instance-level - Instance We inject instance-level changes to the examples
316   provided in the prompts. Recall that we are updating the base ReAct’s prompts, where the exemplar
317   tasks are same as the query. We change the goal location in exemplar problem to ensure that it does
318   not match with any of the goal locations in query problem. Moreover, we add repetitive yet futile
319   actions in the exemplar execution trace which does not effect the solution. Intuition: Ensuring
320
      a different goal location in exemplar from the queried problem is a natural usecase. Moreover,
      exemplars may contain arbitrary exploration strategies such as action repetition (Sharma et al., 2017).
321
      By ReAct’s claims, LLM agent performance should not be affected.
322

388   5.1   U TILITY OF INTERLEAVING REASONING TRACE WITH ACTION EXECUTION
389
390   Given the claims of ReAct, a practitioner’s expectation is that all the rows would be red as the
391   performance would drop by changing the location of the think tag. From Table 1(RQ1) note that
392   the exemplar CoT and the anonymized exemplar CoT performs significantly better than base ReAct
393   for all GPT-X family of models. Moreover, the performance dips slightly for Claude-Opus along
394   these variations. From Fig. 3 (larger area represents worse performance), we observe that base ReAct
395
      consistently performs worse in most of the tasks. This refutes ReAct’s first claim on the importance
      of interleaving reasoning trace generation with action execution. Even in the case of the Claude
396
      where there is a slight dip in performance, the models seems to be performing at reasonably high
397
      success rate which questions the importance of interleaved reasoning and action execution. We
398   omit LLAMA-3.1-8B and GPT-4o (See B.4) for AlfWorld as they achieve zero performance over
399   baselines and all the variations. From Table 2, we find a similar pattern: CoT and Anon. CoT variants
400   perform closely or better than the baseline ReAct. A surprising result consistent in both the domains
401   is the performance of Act baseline (where think tags are absent and actions are generated directly).
402   Act baseline is weaker only for two models GPT-3.5-Instruct, Claude-Opus for both the
403   domains, which further questions the utility of using ReAct style paradigm in the first place.
404
405   5.2   U TILITY OF G UIDANCE I NFORMATION FOLLOWING think TAG
406
407   Recall that reasoning trace guidance pertains to the prospective actions or behaviors an agent should
408   execute (foresight guidance). This type of guidance is more informative compared to other variations,
409
      such as hindsight guidance, which focuses on past errors without providing future solution steps, and
      placebo guidance, which is entirely unrelated to the task. ReAct claims that reasoning trace is crucial
410
      for LLM agent performance, which would predict a decline in performance with hindsight guidance
411
      and a collapse with placebo guidance. Therefore, a practitioner would expect all the rows to be a
412   dark shade of red. In contrast, our findings in Table 1 indicate that hindsight guidance (Failure,
413   Explanation) actually improves the performance of the GPT family of models. The Claude-Opus
414   model’s performance remains stable with hindsight (Failure) guidance and declines with placebo
415   guidance. Figure 4 illustrates these models’ performance across six AlfWorld tasks and variations,
416   showing that the performance of LLMs either improved or remained consistent when provided with
417   weaker or irrelevant guidance information. This refutes ReAct’s claim that task-specific reasoning
418   trace is the source of LLM agent performance. Our argument that LLM agent’s performance is only
419   slightly affected by the reasoning trace explains the indifference to ordering perturbation as well.
420
      If the LLM is not utilizing the reasoning trace for decision making, change in ordering would not
      affect the agent’s performance. Our arguments hold for the Webshop domain as well, where all of the
421
      variants perform closely or better than the baseline ReAct. Finally, contrary to the general perception
422
      that better GPT models would improve over reasoning, we find that GPT-4-(Old)’s performance is the
423   worst among GPT-X family further highlighting the brittleness of claims of ReAct. GPT-4-(Latest)
424   performs similarly to GPT-4-(Old), except for the Act baseline which again shows the futility of
425   ReAct prompting. In all our experiment settings, we note that LLMs replicate the exact steps as
426   shown for the examples in the prompts. Hence, they do not output what ReAct claims as think tags if
427   those tags are not present in the original prompt.

431   Intuitively, the similarity of Domain examples is closest with base ReAct, followed by Instance
      and Optimal variations. Finally, All contains an overload of information followed by One and

      Both which has the same action space but uses different tasks as exemplars. Recall that AlfWorld
433   being a PDDL domain has a shared environment dynamics across all tasks with upto 80% of actions
434   shared across execution traces. While ReAct does not investigate impact of varied exemplars, given
435   the popular usage one expects LLMs to be robust to such changes especially in a common-sense
436   household domain. Table 3 shows the severe brittleness of ReAct based LLM agent to even minor
437   variations (such as Domain, Instance). Specifically, performance of GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-4
438   plumments for Domain. Claude-Opus which was more robust in RQ1, RQ2, is also impacted
439   severely by Domain, Instance variations. Furthermore, when we do not expose the exploration
440   strategy and only provide Optimal exemplars, the performance of LLM agents further drops (except
441
      in GPT4).
442   Overloading the LLMs with more exemplars All does not impact its performance. We posit, this is
443   because the query-task exemplar is still part of the large input prompt. Among the two exemplars, as
444   provided in ReAct, when one of them is of a different task (One) then the performance significantly
445   reduces for LLMs. When both of the exemplars are of a different task then the performance collapses
446   to single digit success rates for all the models. This is a key result of this work highlighting the severe
447
      dependence of LLMs on the similarity of the exemplars to the query task. Through sensitivity analysis
      using our RQ3 variations we could find parts of the input (the task similarity of the exemplar with
448
      query) which is the source of ReAct performance. Essentially, the LLM is mimicking / performing
449
      approximate retrieval from the context presented to it. Moreover, our results corroborate the line of
450   research that questions the inability of LLMs to reason or plan (Verma et al., 2024; Valmeekam et al.,
451   2024; Stechly et al., 2024b; Ullman, 2023; Schaeffer et al., 2023; McCoy et al., 2023; Stechly et al.,
452   2024a; Sprague et al., 2024).
453
      The reported success-rate from the ReAct paper Yao et al. (2022b) on the WebShop domain is 40%.
454
      Due to the absence of the exact queries used in the paper, we randomly sampled queries from the
455
      WebShop dataset comprising 12K records. This approach possibly resulted in the decoupling of
456   any relationship between the exemplars and the queries. Referring to Table 2, it is evident that the
457   performance of the WebShop ReAct agent significantly declined, reaching single digit percentages
458   (as well as other variants). This mirrors the trends observed in the Both variant of the Alfworld in
459   Table 3, further supporting our arguments.
460
      Unrolling and Subtask Similarity We perform additional experiments where the query task is to
461
      essentially repeat the task in the exemplar (Unrolling). For instance, in AlfWorld, the exemplar
462   is Put and the query is PutTwo to put two objects at given location. In this case, the LLM has
463   to unroll the given advice and repeat exemplar task execution to solve the query. The success rate
464   of GPT-3.5-Instruct (the best performing GPT model in our experiments) drops down from 52% to
465   9%. Similarly, we experiment with a Subtask Similarity variation where the exemplar task
466   subsumes execution of the query task. For instance, the Heat task requires the agent to pick and
467   place object in the microwave (which is an instantiation of Put task). One would expect that Heat
468   is a good exemplar for Put, however, the performance of GPT-3.5-instruct model goes from 18% to
469   0% in this case. These results further underscore the brittleness and the need for instance-specific
470
      exemplars in ReAct.
471   Thought operationalization ability of LLMs Given the free form nature of thought generation
472   and arbitrary nature of thought (about subtask, common-sense next steps etc.), checking whether
473   the generated thoughts are in-fact reasonable is a challenging problem. For completeness, we find
474   that 40% of the times after generation of a think tag, subsequent environment action taken by the
475   LLM was invalid (for GPT-3.5-instruct) in AlfWorld. It is much higher ( 80% for GPT-3.5-Turbo,
476
      90% for Claude-Haiku) for weaker LLM models. This further highlights the inability of LLMs to
      operationalize its generated thought as also seen in (Roy et al., 2024). From manual inspection we
477
      find that the typical thoughts would enlist all possible locations as next locations to visit for most
478
      of the tasks. As demonstrated in Section 5.2, the performance of LLMs actually decreases when
479   provided with foresight guidance, as seen with the base ReAct model. A detailed investigation into
480   the validity of the generated reasoning traces is beyond the scope of this work and is suggested as
481   future research.

      5.4   D ISCUSSION
487
488   In this sub-section, we aim to draw insights from our experiments across the three RQs which can be
489   further extended to understanding the limitations of LLMs for planning problems. Specifically, we
490   discuss a) the pitfalls of using ReAct-style prompting for planning domains which could further be ex-
491   acerbated by approaches that build on top of ReAct framework, and b) scalability and generalizability
492   issues as observed across multiple LLMs.
493
494   Pitfalls of ReAct-Style Prompting: Recall, that ReAct claims an improved performance for
495   text-based planning domains, namely - AlfWorld and WebShop, where the presence of a think
496   tag acts as guidance for the LLM to generate the next set of actions during the LLM-environment
497   interaction. Through our sensitivity analysis, we dissect each component of ReAct-style prompting
498   in a critical effort to understand the factor that leads to the observed success rates in these domains.
499   With variations on the placement (RQ1) and content (RQ2) of the think tag, we eliminate it as the
500   primary cause of any improvement. Furthermore, slight variations in exemplar tasks (RQ3) lead to a
501   stark decline in success rate, clearly indicating the dependence of performance on the highly curated
502
      instance-specific examples by domain experts. While newer research in the art of prompting has
      pointed out the impact of well-curated examples, our work specifically highlights exemplar-query
503
      similarity as the cause of ReAct’s performance and rejects contemporary belief that the heavy-lifting
504
      of LLM reasoning & planning is done through the think tag.
505
506
507   Relevance of ReAct to newer LLMs: ReAct uses the Act baseline in their work to showcase
508
      improvements due to the presence of the proposed think tag. For AlfWorld, ReAct reports 45%
      success rate for Act baseline and 71% success rate for ReAct prompting using the PaLM model.
509
      For WebShop, ReAct reports 30.1% success rate for Act baseline and 40% success rate for ReAct
510
      prompting on PaLM. However, we note from our results on both domains that the Act baseline
511   performs much better than ReAct for several LLMs, which questions on the compatibility of ReAct
512   to newer-age LLMs. ReAct performs worse with newer models as compared to the results they report
513   on PaLM, which is currently decommissioned. This observation also questions the contemporary
514   belief that such prompting strategies are generalizable throughout different LLM families, including
515   newer models.
516
      We re-iterate our key result, given any LLM model, the success rates plummet with our RQ3
517   variations showing a consistent pattern of dependence on the provided examples irrespective of the
518   LLM. Moreover, the performance of all the LLMs remain quite high (if not better) when we vary the
519   location and content of the think tags. This highlights the need for higher rigor in agentic LLM
520   experimentation and in-depth evaluation seeking source of improvements. Finally, we highlight our
521   previous discussion on unrolling, subtask-similarity (discussing the brittleness of perceived reasoning
522   abilities of LLMs) and the inability of LLMs to perform reliable thought operationalization as key
523   limitations which exist despite ReAct style prompting.

      6     C ONCLUSION
527
528   ReAct based prompt engineering methods have been claimed to improve planning abilities of Large
529   Language Models. In this study, we critically examine ReAct along three dimensions, informed by its
530   claims and our hypotheses regarding its performance sources. Contrary to ReAct’s claims, our findings
531
      reveal that its performance is neither due to interleaving reasoning trace and guidance information
      generation with action execution, nor due to the specific nature of the guidance information. Instead,
532
      we identify that the true source of LLM performance in sequential decision-making tasks, such as
533
      AlfWorld, is the high degree of similarity between exemplar problems (few-shot) and the query
534   task. We also showed that ReAct is susceptible to trivial variations in exemplar prompts (such as
535   with the use of synonyms, or Unrolling and Subtask Similarity cases). Our findings
536   caution against an uncritical adoption of ReAct-style frameworks for their putative abilities to
537   enhance performance in domains requiring planning. To conclude, we believe that it will be helpful
538   for practitioners and future works to take these results into account, particularly when designing
539   prompts for text-based decision-making problems, and benefit from avoiding putting any efforts into
      constructing reasoning traces but rather selecting the right examples for subsequent problems.
