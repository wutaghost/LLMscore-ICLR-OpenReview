                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            We consider a multi-player multi-armed bandit problem (MP-MAB) where N
010            players compete for K arms in T rounds. The reward distribution is heterogeneous
011            where each player has a different expected reward for the same arm. When multiple
012            players select the same arm, they collide and obtain zero reward. In this paper, we
013            aim to find the max-min fairness matching that maximizes the reward of the player
014            who receives the lowest reward. This paper improves the existing regret upper
015            bound result of O(log T log log T ) to achieve max-min fairness. More specifically,
016            our decentralized fair elimination algorithm (DFE) deals with heterogeneity and
017
               collision carefully and attains a regret upper bounded of O((N 2 + K) log T /∆),
               where ∆ is the minimum reward gap between max-min value and sub-optimal
018
               arms. We assume N ≤ K to guarantee all players can select their arms without
019
               collisions. In addition, we also provide an Ω(max{N 2 , K} log T /∆) regret lower
020            bound for this problem. This lower bound indicates that our algorithm is optimal
021            with respect to key parameters, which significantly improves the performance of
022            algorithms in previous work. Numerical experiments again verify the efficiency
023            and improvement of our algorithms.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Multi-player multi-armed bandit (MP-MAB) problem has been widely studied in recent years (Wang
      et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2019; 2022; Yang et al., 2023). In such a problem, N
029
      players simultaneously play K arms. In each round, each player selects an arm and observes the
030
      reward generated from a fixed distribution. In typical MP-MAB problems, players try to maximize the
031   summation of their cumulative expected reward throughout T rounds. Equivalently, they minimize
032   the total regret, defined as the difference of cumulative reward between their decision and the optimal
033   strategy. MP-MAB problems can be divided into the homogeneous setting and the heterogeneous
034   setting by whether the arm’s reward varies among players. Besides, collision is also considered in
035   many applications: when different players select the same arm in one round, they collide and all
036   receive a 0 reward rather than the original reward drawn from the fixed distribution.
037   When considering the heterogeneous setting and collisions, it often raises concerns about unfairness.
038   In order to maximize the total rewards and avoid collisions meanwhile, some players have to sacrifice
039   for the global objective by selecting arms with lower reward (Hossain et al., 2021; Bistritz et al.,
040   2020; Leshem, 2023). Therefore, the primitive objective which only cares about maximizing total
041   rewards, is unfair for those players and makes them have a willingness to not follow the algorithm.
042   Thus researchers hope to search for a better objective to avoid this unfairness. One reasonable
043   objective is to maximize the expected reward of the player who receives the lowest reward, which
044   is called max-min fairness (Bistritz et al., 2020; Leshem, 2023). This ensures that every player is
045
      considered equally and nobody should sacrifice. For instance, in wireless networks, when more
      than one users transfer information using one channel, they will interfere one another and fail, and
046
      this is modeled as collisions in our MP-MAB problem. If we only pursue the maximal Quality of
047
      Service (QoS) globally, some users will be annoyed as they can only use channels with low quality to
048   avoid collisions of others. Therefore, fairness guarantee indeed matters. Besides, these applications
049   also imply that we need to consider decentralized case carefully, in which players cannot gather
050   global information directly. In the above example, a central server is able to integrate and process
051   information in smaller networks, while in larger ones, as it costs too many resources to build a server,
052   while in decentralized setting, the process of information propagation can be implemented by the
053   collisions between players indirectly, which distributes resources to local players, thus, decentralized
      algorithm should be designed.

      There are several kinds of fairness problems studied in many works. Most discussions are from the
055   respect of arms (Joseph et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2022; Mansoury et al., 2021;
056   Jeunen & Goethals, 2021), and are totally different from max-min fairness. They proposed that the
057   arms with higher reward mean should be exposed more frequently to users and similar arms should
058   have similar exposure frequencies. However, from the players’ perspective, the concerns of unfairness
059   still remains. Only limited papers (Bistritz et al., 2020; Leshem, 2023) studied this max-min fairness
060   topic, but both of them have the following limitations. 1) Their regret upper bounds are larger than
061   O(log T ). The work of Bistritz et al. (2020) derives a regret lower bound of Ω(log T ). However,
062   existing works only achieve the O(log T log log T ) regret upper bound (Bistritz et al., 2020; Leshem,
063
      2023). Additionally, their regret analysis also relies on a large constant, which can grow exponentially
      with N and 1/∆, where N is the number of players and ∆ is the minimum reward gap. 2) The
064
      lower bound Ω(log T /∆) in Bistritz et al. (2020) only works for parameter T . Nevertheless, no lower
065
      bound for parameter N or K is provided, which makes the hardness of the problem still unclear. 3)
066   They have some strong assumptions about the setting. Both assume the rewards are bounded, and the
067   latter also assumes K = N . Besides, they both assume the reward means of different arms for each
068   player to be different, which is infeasible in many scenarios. These assumptions limit the application
069   scenarios and should be relaxed for wider applications.
070
      In order to address the limitations above, we propose our decentralized fair elimination (DFE)
071
      algorithm to find the max-min fairness for the decentralized heterogeneous MP-MAB problem where
072   there exists no central server to assign matching directly. We relax the assumptions we mentioned
073   before to make our algorithm more general. Besides, it can be turned out later that this algorithm is
074   optimal since its regret upper bound matches the problem’s regret lower bound.
075
      The algorithm makes each player run a phased elimination algorithm. In each phase, players first
076
      communicate their current reward estimation to each other. Then all players can compute the lower
077
      bound of the max-min value and then eliminates arms whose upper confidence bound (UCB) index
078   is lower than that value. After that, players explore their remaining arms in a round-robin way
079   and the exploration length grows exponentially with the phase. Our decentralized fair elimination
080   algorithm has the following novelties and advantages. 1) Our algorithm adaptively explores sub-
081   optimal arms and eliminates them efficiently, assuring that those sub-optimal arms will not be selected
082   in the later phase. This is the key to improving the regret from O(log T log log T ) to the optimal
083   O(log T ). 2) We design a novel exploration assignment algorithm for players at each phase. Due to
084   the elimination procedure, each player may be left with a different set of remaining arms, leading
085   to uneven exploration frequency between different pairs and thus making the exploration process
086
      non-trivial. Our DFE algorithm optimizes this process and guarantees that all remaining player-arm
      pairs will be explored once in at most N 2 + K − N rounds, rather than naive N K rounds. We
087
      assume N ≤ K to guarantee all players can select arms without collisions, like most MP-MAB
088
      works. This enables the algorithm to explore each player arm pair more uniformly. We also show that
089   we can not find another assignment procedure that achieves better result. 3) The regret analysis for
090   this algorithm is also non-trivial. We utilize the doubling trick and the property of the regret definition
091   for the max-min fairness problem. A tight O((N 2 + K) log T /∆) regret bound is obtained and we
092   will also show that this is optimal with respect all parameters.
093
      Besides, we also derive a tighter Ω(max{N 2 , K} log T /∆) regret lower bound for this max-min
094
      fairness problem. Here we design an interesting special case that ensures the regret lower bounded
095   by ∆ times the selection numbers of specific ⌊N/2⌋2 sub-optimal player-arm pairs. Then we show
096   that any two of these pairs can not be selected at the same time in case of suffering a large regret.
097   Then we construct another instance that only improves the reward of one of these pairs resulting in a
098   better max-min value. Thus by divergence decomposition, we have that any pair must be selected
099   Ω(log T /∆2 ), implying the final Ω(max{N 2 , K} log T /∆) lower bound.

137   t ∈ [T ], each player i ∈ N selects an arm ki (t) ∈ K, and receives a reward, denoted by ri,k (t). We
138   denote all players’ selections in round t by the matching set m(t) := {(1, k1 (t)), . . . , (N, kN (t))}.

141
      is generated from a 1-sub-gaussian distribution with mean µi,ki (t) . Note that the reward means are
142

143
      player i in round t by Ci (t) = 1{player i suffers a collision in round t}. After round t, each player i
144

      Since each player can only make decisions based on its own history observation, it is necessary for
      players to communicate their observations and estimations with each other to find the final equilibrium
190
      (Boursier & Perchet, 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2023). The key challenge here is to control
191
      the frequency of communications as too many communications will bring more communication costs,
192   while insufficient communications cause delayed and inaccurate decision updates. Therefore, we
193   design a phased-based algorithm that divides T rounds into epochs s = 1, 2, . . . with exponentially
194   increasing length, while keeping the length of the communication process unchanged in each epoch.
195   This makes sure the exploration process will dominate the game as epoch s increases, and thus the
196   regret caused by the communication process will not be the leading term.
197
      In each epoch s, Algorithm 1 has three phases: elimination, exploration, and communication. In
198   the elimination phase players compute the lower bound of max-min matching based on the received
199   information from other players and eliminate those sub-optimal arms; after that, players select their
200   corresponding non-eliminating arms to explore in a round-robin way. At last players share their local
201   information in the communication phase.

      3.2   E XPLORATION P HASE
253
      The elimination phase is followed by the exploration phase in each epoch. Here we introduce our
254   new assignment algorithm (Algorithm 2), which only takes at most N 2 + K rounds to explore all
255   non-eliminating pairs once under the constraint of not selecting eliminated pairs, shown to be the
256   optimal assignment method in Section 4.
257
      In the s-th exploration phase, suppose we still need to explore all player-arm pairs in P. By the
258
      eliminating rule we have that each pair (i, k) ∈ P must have a corresponding matching m such
259
      that every pair in m is also in P, i.e., ∀(i, k) ∈ P, ∃m, mi = k, ∀(i′ , k ′ ) ∈ m, (i′ , k ′ ) ∈ P.
260   Thus we can first find a matching m′ such that all pairs in m′ are in P. Then we have a arm set
261   Km′ = {m′1 , . . . , m′N } of all arms in m′ . For any pair (i, k) in P, if k ∈ Km′ , we construct a
262   matching m with mi = k and ∀i′ ∈ [N ], (i′ , mi′ ) ∈ P. Each m is added to M and we have that in
263   this process we construct at most N 2 matchings. After that, for those K − N arms not in Km′ , we
264   give them new indexes from N + 1 to K, i.e., we extend the index of m′i to m′K such that each arm
265   k ∈ [K] has its unique index u ∈ [K] with m′u = k. To explore those pairs in P with arm not in
266   Km′ , we can construct K − N matchings that explore arms in a round-robin way. Specifically, in the
267   r-th matching, player i will select m′N +((i+r)mod(K−N )) if this pair is in P, otherwise i will select
268   m′i . By this design, we can ensure all pairs in P will be explored once in at most N 2 + K rounds and
269   all eliminated pairs are not selected. In the s-th exploration phase, all players select each matching
      for 2s rounds.

365

367   collisions. We only need the bit length with log(1/∆) since it is enough to distinguish two pairs

369   that the minimum communication cost studied in previous work is 32 N 3 log(1/∆) log T (Leshem,
370   2023). Additionally, we note that ∆ in their works is the minimum reward gap among all player-arm

392   Remark 3. We note that previous works Bistritz et al. (2020); Leshem (2023) also provide a phased-
393   based algorithm to deal with the decentralized setting. However, they both apply an explore-then-
394   commit (ETC) method at each epoch s. Specifically, they let each player explores each arm log s
395   times at the beginning of the epoch s, and then compute the max-min matching based on history
396   observations in exploration phase. After that each player follows this matching in the following 2s
397   rounds. Their algorithms both only obtain an O(log T log log T ) regret bound since they have to
398   set an increasing length of exploration at each epoch. This design is to make sure the probability
399
      of computing a wrong max-min matching is bounded by exp(−s) when s is sufficiently large that
      log s > 1/∆, then the regret in the exploitation phase can be bounded. This design also raises
400
      the problem of a large constant to guarantee log s > 1/∆, which requires initial warm-up rounds
401
      is O(exp(1/∆)), which could be very large when ∆ is small enough. We handle this problem by
402   applying the elimination method which eliminate sub-optimal player-arm pair efficiently. This assures
403   that no forced explorations will happen in later epochs.

      proposed in Leshem (2023). In this algorithm, players determine their respective indices according
408   to a randomly choosing arm method. They show that this process will end in less than O(N log N )
409   rounds with probability 1, therefore, the regret of this process is also less than O(N log N ). Since the
410   regret of by index searching process never dominates, for the convenience of description, we ignore it

      In this section, we provide some numerical simulations to validate that our DFE algorithm performs

      in the i-th row and the k-th column is µi,k . The reward matrix of (4, 4) and (10, 10) are the same

517   the benchmarks are the same as they stated in their paper. Each experiment was repeated 20 times.
518   All plots are averaged over 20 trials with confidence intervals of 95%.
519
      Figure 5 shows that our DFE algorithm dramatically decreases the regret compared to the other two
520
      algorithms. Specifically, in all three experiments, The regret of the DFE algorithm decreases more
521
      than 90% of My Fair Bandit algorithm and more than 80% of the Leshem algorithm. In addition, the
522   DFE algorithm converges quickly, on the other hand, the other two algorithms may experience a long
523   time before convergence, as Figure 5b shows. Besides, the other two algorithms both suffer regrets
524   continuously since they apply the phased ETC method which requires lasting explorations even
525   though each player has enough exploration, while our algorithm would stop selecting sub-optimal
526   pairs when they are eliminated. Additionally, we note that our novel exploration assignment method
527   which explores not eliminated pairs nearly uniformly also helps the algorithm attain less regret, as
528   the other two algorithms simply explore all arms even if they have been identified to be sub-optimal.
529   These numerical results verify the advantage of our algorithm.

531   6    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This paper discusses the max-min MP-MAB problem and proposes a new algorithm with a tight
534   regret upper bound, which effectively solve the problem of unfairness. One limitation of our work
535   is that we don’t sufficiently consider the willingness of players, in other words, if some malicious
536   players disagree with the strategy, they may not follow the algorithm, then the whole process may fail.
537   This implies that future work can focus on the robustness and incentive compatibility of the max-min
538   MP-MAB problem. Another promising future direction is that our insights into the exploration stage
539   in our DFE algorithm can be generalized into other kinds of MP-MAB problems, which serves as an
      effective technique dealing with collisions.
