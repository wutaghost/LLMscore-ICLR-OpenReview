011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Premature convergence to suboptimal policies remains a significant challenge in
014            reinforcement learning (RL), particularly in tasks with sparse rewards or non-
015            convex reward landscapes. Existing work usually utilizes reward shaping, such as
016            curiosity-based internal rewards, to encourage exploring promising spaces. How-
017
               ever, this may inadvertently introduce new local optima and impair the optimiza-
               tion for the actual target reward. To address this issue, we propose Goal Achieve-
018
               ment Guided Exploration (GAGE), a novel approach that incorporates an agent’s
019
               goal achievement as a dynamic criterion for balancing exploration and exploita-
020            tion. GAGE adaptively adjusts the exploitation level based on the agent’s current
021            performance relative to an estimated optimal performance, thereby mitigating pre-
022            mature convergence. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that GAGE substantially
023            improves learning outcomes across various challenging tasks by adapting conver-
024            gence based on task success. Applicable to both continuous and discrete tasks,
025            GAGE seamlessly integrates into existing RL frameworks, highlighting its poten-
026            tial as a versatile tool for enhancing exploration strategies in RL.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Properly dealing with the exploration-exploitation trade-off in reinforcement learning (RL) still is a
032   critical challenge (Kaelbling et al., 1996; Ladosz et al., 2022). Constrained by learning time and re-
033
      sources, the agent must balance well between exploring for better policies and exploiting the learned
      behaviors. There are two prominent challenges in exploration: sparse reward function and local op-
034
      tima. A task with sparse rewards, such as in Montezuma’s Revenge, provides insufficient feedback,
035
      forcing the agent to search vast areas of the state-action space without clear guidance (Devidze et al.,
036   2022). On the other hand, an environment riddled with local optima may provide the agent with re-
037   dundant or misleading information and distract it from exploring the actual optimization target. For
038   example, in robot locomotion tasks, where robots are rewarded for saving energy in addition to the
039   main speed reward, agents may focus on optimizing energy consumption but only move slowly. As
040   a result, they may lead to different policy behaviors. Agents trained with sparse rewards might fail
041   to learn any meaningful policy due to exploration difficulty and credit assignment. Whereas agents
042   trained in environments with local optima are more prone to over-exploitation, leading to premature
043   convergence to a suboptimal solution.
044   Due to RL’s trial-and-error nature, local optima can make the learning process unstable. This in-
045   stability has been reported as a significant obstacle when reproducing and comparing different RL
046   algorithms (Henderson et al., 2018). It is important to distinguish this issue from reward hack-
047   ing (Amodei et al., 2016), where the agent discovers policies that maximize returns in ways the
048   system designer did not anticipate or desire. We focus on premature convergence, where local op-
049   tima prevent the agents from optimizing the targeted returns. To effectively solve tasks with local
050
      optima, preventing premature convergence during exploration is essential. Several factors contribute
      to this issue, including the inherent non-convexity of tasks, reward shaping, multi-objectives, and
051
      function approximation errors introduced by neural networks in deep RL algorithms.
052
053   Many methods have been developed to address the exploration-exploitation trade-off (Ladosz et al.,
      2022), but not explicitly for premature convergence. One popular approach, ϵ-greedy, employs

065   a predefined time-decaying parameter ϵ to decrease exploration gradually. However, finding the
066   optimal schedule is far from trivial, as it can vary depending on the task and is further complicated
067   by the unstable nature of reinforcement learning processes. Other methods, such as Proximal Policy
068   Optimization (PPO)(Schulman et al., 2017) and Soft Actor-Critic (SAC)(Haarnoja et al., 2018),
069   incorporate an entropy-loss component to promote exploration, but this acts only as a soft learning
070
      regularizer. Similarly, curiosity-driven intrinsic rewards (Barto, 2013; Pathak et al., 2017) encourage
      exploration, yet the exploitation process still follows the behavior of the underlying algorithms, like
071
      Deep Q-learning (DQN) (Mnih et al., 2013) and PPO, which are prone to premature convergence.
072
073   To address the issue of converging to a suboptimal solution prematurely, we propose a novel ap-
074   proach that incorporates an agent’s goal achievement into its exploration-exploitation strategy. Goal
075   achievement is defined as the ratio of an agent’s current policy return to the optimal policy return,
076
      excluding the auxiliary rewards for guiding the learning process. Unlike existing approaches, which
      often overlook this critical aspect of guiding exploration, our approach ensures that exploration con-
077
      tinues when the agent’s goal achievement is low, thereby preventing early convergence to suboptimal
078
      policies. Our method can be applied to both continuous and discrete action spaces. It also has the
079   appealing property of preserving the ranking of different actions while encouraging exploration in
080   discrete tasks, ensuring that the agent does not disrupt valuable policy structures while exploring
081   new possibilities. We demonstrate that this approach can significantly enhance training outcomes
082   across various tasks, potentially improving exploration and overall performance.
083
      To summarize, in this work, we first investigate the various factors that contribute to premature con-
084
      vergence in RL. We analyze existing exploration-exploitation methods and explain why they fail
085   to prevent premature convergence. To solve the problem, we propose Goal Achievement Guided
086   Exploration (GAGE), a method that leverages an agent’s goal achievement to define an adaptive
087   exploration schedule during training. We evaluate GAGE across multiple challenging tasks, includ-
088   ing continuous and discrete action spaces, and compare its performance against popular baseline
089   methods. The results demonstrate that GAGE consistently mitigates premature convergence, espe-
090   cially in complex exploration problems with many local optima. Moreover, GAGE’s simplicity and
091   compatibility with a wide range of existing RL algorithms distinguish it as a promising solution for
092   enhancing exploration-exploitation strategies.

094   2     P REMATURE C ONVERGENCE AND E XPLORATION T ECHNIQUES
095
096
      Premature convergence is a common issue in optimization and machine learning algorithms like
097   genetic algorithms (Pandey et al., 2014) and reinforcement learning. Despite extensive efforts to en-
098   hance exploration efficiency in reinforcement learning (RL) (Thrun, 1992; Auer et al., 2002; Agar-
099   wal et al., 2023), agents may still unintentionally converge to local optima due to various factors.
100   In this section, we identify these factors and examine why existing exploration techniques remain
101   prone to this problem.
102
103   2.1     FACTORS FOR P REMATURE C ONVERGENCE
104
105   Non-convexity of tasks Non-convexity exists in most real-world tasks and arises from different
106   components, such as the reward function and transition dynamics. It also inherently stems from
107   neural networks, the core part of deep reinforcement learning (DRL). Due to the non-convexity of
      DRL, sub-optimal solutions can exist even in simple problems. For example, as shown in Fig. 1a, an


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      agent (orange dot) needs to avoid the grey-colored area and is rewarded more when getting closer
109   to the circle’s center. However, due to the non-convexity of the reward landscape, agents without
110   sufficient exploration can get stuck in a local optimal solution (Eckel et al., 2024). In more complex
111   contexts like robotics or traffic management (Xu et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2024a), systems often have
112   many degrees of freedom and complex environmental interactions, making dynamics models non-
113   convex and further complicating optimization.
114
115   Reward shaping Tasks with sparse rewards and local optima present significant challenges for
116   exploration and credit assignment. To provide agents with dense and informative feedback, previous
117   work has employed reward shaping based on prior knowledge or specific heuristics (Vinyals et al.,
118   2019; Yan et al., 2020). While reward shaping can guide agents toward more valuable regions and
119   accelerate convergence to the optimal policy, designing such rewards is tedious and may introduce
120
      local optima (Ng et al., 1999; Gupta et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2024). Agents might focus on auxiliary
      rewards while neglecting the actual task objectives (Lehman et al., 2020). Curiosity-based intrinsic
121
      rewards have become popular for enhancing exploration by rewarding agents for discovering new
122
      observations or acquiring new knowledge about the environment (Savinov et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
123   2023). This approach encourages agents to visit diverse states within environments. However,
124   as illustrated in Fig. 1b, agents can become trapped by uncontrolled stochasticity in the system
125   dynamics, a phenomenon known as the Noisy TV problem (Burda et al., 2019).
126
127   Multiple objectives Many real-world problems involve multiple, sometimes conflicting, objec-
128   tives that cannot be adequately evaluated using a single metric. For example, as shown in Fig.1c, a
129   robot learning to dribble a football has to optimize factors such as the ball’s velocity, energy con-
130   sumption, distance to the ball, and facing direction. Simultaneously optimizing all these metrics can
131   lead to premature convergence to suboptimal solutions—for instance, the robot might stay close to
132   the ball, face it, and remain stationary to save energy (Yan et al., 2024b). The presence of multiple
133
      objectives introduces local optima in the reward landscape, hindering the agent from reaching the
      global or Pareto optima, depending on the definition of the utility function (Xu et al., 2020; Hayes
134
      et al., 2022; Alegre et al., 2023). In this paper, we focus on tasks with linear utility functions that
135
      can be addressed using single-objective algorithms rather than exploring Pareto fronts.
136
137
      Function approximation error Neural networks as function approximators enable reinforce-
138
      ment learning (RL) to tackle extremely high-dimensional problems like Go (Schrittwieser et al.,
139   2020). However, they are prone to overfitting (Srivastava et al., 2014), and RL intensifies this issue
140   due to its non-stationarity and biased datasets. As a result, even in simple tasks like Mountain-
141   Car (Moore, 1990), modern algorithms such as Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) can suffer from insufficient
142   exploration (Eberhard et al., 2023), collecting data only around the initial states (see Fig. 1d, where
143   an SAC agent is trained for 1M steps). Due to premature convergence, the learned policy exhibits
144   low entropy even in unvisited states and is thus unable to explore better solutions.
145
146   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
147
148   Exploration methods for reinforcement learning can be categorized into two groups: undirected and
149   directed (Thrun, 1992). Undirected exploration involves randomly selecting actions based merely on
150
      utility estimation. Whereas directed exploration utilizes knowledge of the learning process (Pathak
      et al., 2017; Burda et al., 2019; Ecoffet et al., 2021) to guide the exploration. In this section, we
151
      discuss popular exploration techniques from the two groups.
152
153
      Undirected exploration 1) The ϵ-greedy strategy, commonly used in value-based algo-
154
      rithms (Mnih et al., 2013; van Hasselt et al., 2016), employs a time-decaying ϵ to define the prob-
155
      ability of selecting either the best action or a random one during training. However, tuning the
156   schedule requires much effort, because many terms can influence the agent’s training progress, and
157   the exploration can hardly be defined by the number of iterations. 2) Some reinforcement learning
158   algorithms are equipped with an entropy loss term (Schulman et al., 2017; Haarnoja et al., 2018)
159   to enhance exploration. However, as a soft regularization for the learning process, it can be insuf-
160   ficient to guide the agent out of local optima. More severely, for discrete actions, the entropy loss
161   can not maintain the distribution shape, i.e., the order of actions’ probabilities of the learned policy.
      3) Noise-based techniques inject noise into the observation, action, or parameter space to enhance


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      policy exploration (Lillicrap et al., 2016; Plappert et al., 2018b). As the magnitude of the noise is
163   controlled by either a time-decaying schedule or learned values, this method has limits similar to
164   those of the previous two.
165
166   Directed exploration 1) Curiosity-based methods (Schmidhuber, 1991) are widely employed
167   in hard-exploration environments with sparse rewards. They reward the agent for exploring less
168   visited states. Various approaches have been developed to estimate the novelty of a given state
169   transition, such as the state’s visitation number (Tang et al., 2017), the prediction error of a dy-
170   namics model (Jarrett et al., 2023), or the information gained through transitions (Nikolov et al.,
171   2019). The Noisy TV problem, as a drawback of curiosity-based methods, has been extensively
172   researched (Savinov et al., 2019; Mavor-Parker et al., 2022; Wan et al., 2023). Yet, a follow-up issue
173
      for curiosity-based methods has been neglected: the ”Game Console” problem, in which an agent
      is attracted by a game console with interesting and controllable games instead of a TV showing
174
      only noisy images. However, ”playing” the game console does not provide the agent with actual
175
      rewards. For example, in a maze navigation task, agents may spend much time on a dead-ended
176   long path, gaining curiosity rewards while receiving no actual rewards. When novelty gained from
177   playing games overturns the main objective, curiosity-based methods may prematurely converge to
178   game exploration rather than exploring the optimal solution in the actual world. 2) Memory-based
179   techniques navigate the agent to promising states as soon as possible through memorizing the vis-
180   ited states (Savinov et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2020; Ecoffet et al., 2021). They reduce the number
181   of frequently visited states near the initial ones, collecting more diverse data and thus mitigating
182   premature convergence by reducing repeated data. However, these methods require high memory,
183   as well as complex state compression and searching processes.
184
185   3     G OAL ACHIEVEMENT G UIDED E XPLORATION
186
187   The learning process should not converge before the agent approaches the maximum possible per-
188   formance. Therefore, it is natural that the convergence level, reflected by the concentration of the
189   action distribution, is correlated to the goal achievement of the current policy. This section defines
190   goal achievement g(π) and explains how it can guide learning convergence.
191
192   3.1   G OAL ACHIEVEMENT
193
      A reward function is typically composed of several terms, each designed for different purposes.
194
      They can be categorized into two groups: goal reward and auxiliary reward. Goal rewards reflect
195
      the designer’s actual goals, such as winning a game or achieving a target speed, while auxiliary
196   rewards, like curiosity-driven intrinsic rewards, are intended to guide the learning process. Goal
197   achievement of the learning progress should be based on goal rewards, as they directly represent the
198   agent’s performance. In contrast, auxiliary rewards do not always align with the actual goals and can
199   lead to suboptimal solutions, as stated in the noisy TV problem. Hence, we exclude these auxiliary
200   rewards when measuring goal achievement.
201
      A task can have more than one goal reward term. For ng distinct goal rewards, similar to multi-
202   objective algorithms (Xu et al., 2020), we define the goal achievement for each goal as:
203

229   3.2   M ITIGATING P REMATURE C ONVERGENCE VIA ACTION S MOOTHING
230
      To prevent the agent from prematurely converging to local optima and overcommitting to a limited
231
      set of actions when goal achievement is low, we apply an action smoothing technique inspired
232   by label-smoothing regularization (Szegedy et al., 2016) for image classification, which reduces
233   overconfidence by smoothing the predicted class distribution. This technique ensures that the agent’s
234   action distribution does not collapse into a single action in discrete spaces or into a narrow Gaussian
235   peak in continuous spaces. Below, we discuss how to implement action smoothing in continuous
236   and discrete action spaces.
237
238   Continuous action space In continuous action spaces, exploration is typically facilitated by mod-
239   eling the policy’s action distribution as a Gaussian distribution. This approach is used in both
240   stochastic policies like Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) and Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schul-
241   man et al., 2017; Haarnoja et al., 2018), and deterministic policies like Deep Deterministic Pol-
242
      icy Gradient (DDPG) (Lillicrap et al., 2016), where the Gaussian distribution serves as additive
      noise for exploration. The policy learns the mean µ(s) of the action distribution, modeled as:
243
      p(a | s) ∼ N (µ(s), σ 2 ), where the standard deviation σ can be controlled via a schedule or learned
244
      as a parameter. The standard deviation directly represents the concentration of the action distribu-
245   tion. To prevent premature convergence, we define an adaptive lower bound σL (π) on σ, which is
246   negatively correlated with the current policy’s goal achievement g(π):
247

350   4.1                       C HALLENGING C ONTINUOUS C ONTROL
351
352   To evaluate the effectiveness of our method in preventing premature convergence, we designed five
353   highly challenging continuous control tasks in IsaacLab (Mittal et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024b)
354   (see Fig. 3). These control tasks jointly include all four factors discussed in Sec. 2.1, featuring
355   non-convex dynamics, complex reward functions, and a hard-exploration nature. The specific envi-
356   ronment details are provided in Appendix D. We implemented our approach using Proximal Policy
357   Optimization (PPO), building upon the IsaacLab framework. Since the original action standard devi-
358   ations are independently learned parameters, we use goal achievement to set a dynamic lower bound
359   for σ. This is accomplished by applying σ = σL (π) whenever it falls below the threshold. The full
360
      algorithm is outlined in Appendix A.

      Unknown Optimal Goal For some tasks, the optimal performance is well-defined, such as achiev-
389
      ing a score of 1 to win in board games. However, for other tasks, like a robot locomotion task, the
390
      optimal speed may not be straightforward and is often still under discovery. In the well-known hu-
391   manoid locomotion task, to achieve higher speed without knowing the optimum, researchers often
392   increase the weighting factors to locomotion speed. However, it can result in unnatural behaviors
393   due to imbalanced speed and action rewards. To address this issue, we conducted experiments to
394   demonstrate how GAGE can explore higher speeds without knowing the maximum or altering the
395   reward weights. We define the goal achievement in the Humanoid task as gπ = vπ/v∗ , where vπ and
396   v∗ represent the robot’s current and target speeds, respectively. As shown in Fig. 4a, our method sig-
397   nificantly outperforms standard PPO across a wide range of target speeds, from 0.1 to 100 m/s while
398   maintaining natural behavior through balanced reward weights. When the target speed is set below
399
      the learned optimal speed (∼ 7 m/s), the GAGE agent is also able to learn the optimal speed. This
      phenomenon highlights the critical importance of exploration during the initial stages of training. At
400
      the very beginning, none of the agents had learned to move forward, i.e. g(π) ≈ 0. As a result, even
401
      with small target speeds of 0.1 or 1, the GAGE agent maintained higher standard deviation values
402   compared to standard PPO. Since the optimal locomotion gait for the humanoid robot remains con-
403   sistent across different speeds, it is crucial for the agent to avoid becoming trapped in a suboptimal
404   policy (gait) early in training. Once the agent learns an effective low-speed gait, gradually increas-
405   ing locomotion speed with a similar gait requires less exploration. Even when the target speed
406   is set unreasonably higher than the optimal speed, the GAGE agent is able to discover improved
407   performance, which can subsequently be used to refine the estimation of the optimal performance.

451   solve most tasks, even with the inclusion of Noisy TV as a distraction. Through this experiment, we
452   aim to address two key questions: 1) Will curiosity-based agents be drawn to controllable distrac-
453   tions? 2) If so, can GAGE mitigate such premature convergence? To explore these questions, we
454
      introduce the following major modifications to some of the original environments (see Fig. 5):
455
456   • Attach a “Game Console” to each environment. Unlike the uncontrollable stochasticity in the
457
        Noisy TV problem, we investigate controllable distractions within the environment. In addition
        to the original MiniGrid task, where the agent learns to navigate and reach specific targets, the
458
        agent can simultaneously interact with a parallel gaming task. This gaming task features a larger
459
        map with more randomly located objects, providing the agent with novel observations as it nav-
460     igates. The agent receives an episodic reward solely for reaching the goal in the main task. The
461     observations and actions from both tasks are concatenated for the entire environment.
462
463   • Introduce a penalty to the main task. To increase the learning difficulty associated with premature
464     convergence, all the walls in the main task were replaced with lava. If the agent in the main task
465     steps on lava, the environment terminates, and a penalty is applied. In contrast, the gaming task
466     contains no lava, making exploration in the gaming task more appealing due to its safety.
467
468   Our method builds on the Discriminative-model-based Episodic Intrinsic Reward (DEIR) (Wan
469   et al., 2023), an intrinsic reward algorithm designed to address the Noisy TV problem in Mini-
470   Grid. We adjust the policy network by incorporating an adaptive softmax temperature, guided by
471
      the agent’s goal achievement, as described in Sec. 3.2. To evaluate our approach, we compare it
      against three baseline algorithms: Intrinsic Curiosity Module (ICM) (Pathak et al., 2017), Random
472
      Network Distillation (RND) (Burda et al., 2019), and DEIR. Additionally, we conduct an ablation
473
      study to investigate the effectiveness of using label smoothing (LS) to guide exploration.
474
475   All baseline algorithms fail to solve the tasks. They were distracted by the gaming environment and
476   struggled to learn meaningful policies for the main task. However, their behavior patterns differed
477
      during the learning process. The training results for a single seed on the MultiRoomLava-N4S5
      environment are shown in Fig. 5, with additional results in Appendix B. Above the training curves,
478
      we present the action distributions of different agents during training. Each agent was evaluated 10
479
      times, and the average number of actions taken in the main and gaming tasks was computed. The
480   ICM and RND agents were initially less attracted to the gaming task. At convergence, the ICM
481   agent learned to stay alive, while the RND agent learned to terminate the environment shortly after
482   it started. The DEIR agent initially explored the gaming task extensively. As it became familiar with
483   the game, it gradually shifted its attention to the main task. However, due to the action distribution
484   having already converged to specific actions, it struggled to complete the main task and collect
485   extrinsic rewards. Ultimately, it also learned to terminate the environment, similar to the RND
      agent.

500   5   D ISCUSSION
501
502   We introduced Goal Achievement Guided Exploration (GAGE), a method aiming to address prema-
503   ture convergence in reinforcement learning (RL). Our approach uses goal achievement as a dynamic
504   factor to guide the agent’s exploration, allowing for a better balance between exploration and ex-
505   ploitation. Our experiments demonstrate that GAGE substantially mitigates premature convergence
506   in complex environments by maintaining adequate exploration. Unlike traditional methods such as
507   entropy maximization or curiosity-based exploration, GAGE incorporates an adaptive mechanism
508   that smoothes the action probability distribution based on how well the agent achieves its goal. The
509   strength of GAGE lies in its simplicity and compatibility with existing RL algorithms. It does not
510
      require significant architectural changes and can be easily integrated into continuous and discrete ac-
      tion space environments. The flexibility of GAGE makes it applicable to a wide variety of real-world
511
      RL problems.
512
513   Despite these strengths, GAGE offers aspects for improvement. The current version relies on defin-
514   ing an appropriate goal achievement metric, which might not be straightforward in all tasks. In
515   environments in which the optimal policy or goal is not well understood, the approximation of goal
516
      achievement might introduce inaccuracies. Additionally, while GAGE has proven effective in the
      tested environments, its scalability to more complex, high-dimensional tasks has yet to be explored.
517
518   Future research should focus on improving the scalability of GAGE and applying it to more complex,
519   dynamic and multi-objective environments. Investigating non-linear relationships between the goal
520   achievement and the exploration metrics, such as the standard deviation of Gaussian distributions in
521   continuous action spaces, could further enhance the method’s adaptability to diverse RL problems.
