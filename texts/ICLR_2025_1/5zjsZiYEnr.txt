011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The ability to understand and answer questions over documents can be useful
014            in many business and practical applications. However, documents often contain
015            lengthy and diverse multimodal contents such as texts, figures, and tables, which
016
               are very time-consuming for humans to read thoroughly. Hence, there is an urgent
               need to develop effective and automated methods to aid humans in this task. In
017
               this work, we introduce M-LongDoc, a benchmark of 851 samples, and an auto-
018
               mated framework to evaluate the performance of large multimodal models. We
019            further propose a retrieval-aware tuning approach for efficient and effective mul-
020            timodal document reading. Compared to existing works, our benchmark consists
021            of more recent and lengthy documents with hundreds of pages, while also requir-
022            ing open-ended solutions and not just extractive answers. To our knowledge, our
023            training framework is the first to directly address the retrieval setting for mul-
024            timodal long documents. To enable tuning open-source models, we construct a
025            training corpus in a fully automatic manner for the question-answering task over
026            such documents. Experiments show that our tuning approach achieves a relative
027
               improvement of 4.6% for the correctness of model responses, compared to the
               baseline open-source models. 1

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      The ability to comprehend long and complex multi-modal documents and respond to user queries
033
      about them is crucial in various practical applications such as business intelligence analysis, aca-
034   demic literature review, and legal research (Mathew et al., 2020). Recently, large multimodal mod-
035   els such as GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023) have shown great potential in processing and analyzing diverse
036   types of information, including text, images, and even structured data (Huang et al., 2024b). These
037   models offer the promise of automating tasks that traditionally required extensive human effort,
038   such as document analysis, information retrieval, and question-answering (Fujitake, 2024). How-
039   ever, real-world documents often present significant challenges due to their length, complexity, and
040   multimodal nature, containing a mix of text, figures, tables, and charts (Faysse et al., 2024). Thus,
041   it is not clear whether current models are capable of an in-depth understanding of lengthy multi-
042
      modal documents. On the other hand, while existing benchmarks have fostered great progress in
      document understanding, they often fall short in representing these challenges, typically focusing
043
      on documents with less than 50 pages, and limited to simpler extraction-based questions (Ma et al.,
044
      2024).
045
046   To address these limitations, we introduce M-LongDoc, a comprehensive benchmark consisting
047   of 851 samples specifically designed to evaluate the performance of large multimodal models on
048
      lengthy and diverse documents. Unlike previous datasets (Mathew et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024;
      Ma et al., 2024) that mainly contain short documents, M-LongDoc features recent documents span-
049
      ning hundreds of pages, encompassing a wide range of topics and document structures as shown in
050
      Figures 1 and 2. In addition, as shown in Figure 3, our benchmark goes beyond simpler extractive
051   questions, requiring models to provide open-ended solutions that demonstrate in-depth understand-

078   models have to analyze and reason over texts, figures, or tables in each multimodal long document.
079   We aim for this benchmark to serve as a valuable resource for researchers and practitioners, enabling
080   more rigorous testing and development of multimodal document understanding systems.
081   Another challenge we have to overcome is that due to the lengthy content of multimodal documents
082   and the in-depth solutions, the evaluation of the open-ended question-answering task becomes tricky.
083   To assess such open-ended solutions in a scalable and standardized manner, we design an automated
084   evaluation framework that does not require reference answers or human annotation. Inspired by
085   previous works in model-based evaluation (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023c),
086   our evaluation framework leverages a detailed evaluation guide and multiple judge models to score
087
      the correctness of each generated solution.
088   With our proposed M-LongDoc and evaluation framework, we conducted preliminary study on ex-
089   isting models and the results show that they struggle with figure and table-based questions compared
090   to text-based questions, revealing their multimodal bias and weaknesses (Chen et al., 2024b). Fur-
091   thermore, we observed that the models can be easily distracted by irrelevant content in the document
092   pages (Shi et al., 2023), even with the aid of retrieval-augmented generation. To enhance the robust-
093
      ness of multimodal models against potentially irrelevant retrieved content, we propose a retrieval-
      aware tuning approach for multimodal document reading. This framework unifies supervised fine-
094
      tuning and retrieval augmented generation by including distracting content from other modalities
095
      and pages in each document. Thus, we adapt models to effectively incorporate the domain knowl-
096   edge in multimodal documents while ignoring the content irrelevant to the given query. Compared
097   to existing training methods, ours is the first to address retrieval-augmented multimodal training
098   for rich document layouts. To support this training framework and the enhancement of open-source
099   models, we further construct a training corpus of 10,070 samples for question-answering over multi-
100   modal long documents. Experiments show that our approach achieves a 4.6% relative improvement
101   in the correctness of model responses, compared to the baseline model.
102   The key contributions of this work are threefold: 1) We introduce M-LongDoc, a multimodal bench-
103   mark that more accurately represents the challenges of real-world document understanding tasks.
104   Our automated evaluation framework enables scalable and standardized assessment of open-ended
105   solutions. 2) Our evaluation of leading models indicates that most models struggle with figure and
106   table-based questions compared to text-based questions, revealing their multimodal bias. 3) We
107   propose a retrieval-aware tuning framework that together with our large-scale training corpus, sig-
      nificantly improves the efficiency and effectiveness of multimodal document reading.

138   2       M-L ONG D OC B ENCHMARK
139
140   To evaluate the multimodal long document understanding ability of existing models, we present M-
141
      LongDoc, a challenging and diverse benchmark. Notably, the benchmark focuses on open-ended
      questions that require in-depth solutions and analysis over very long documents with more than 200
142
      pages on average. For diversity, the questions cover the academic, financial, and product domains,
143
      with multiple topics in each domain.

      2.1     DATA C OLLECTION
146
147   To support our evaluation benchmark, we manually source high-quality multimodal documents from
148   publicly accessible sources. Concretely, we source research papers2 , company reports3 and prod-
149   uct instruction manuals4 for the academic, financial, and product domains respectively. Thus, the
150   dataset covers a range of document formats and domains. As research papers often require domain
151   expertise, we constrain the academic domain to computer science topics. To reduce the risk of data
152   contamination or memorization when evaluating existing models (Dong et al., 2024), we constrain
153   the documents to be published in January 2024 or later. As most existing models are unable to
154   process raw PDF files, we conduct a simple data processing to extract the texts and relevant images
155
      from each document. Specifically, we use the PyMuPDF5 tool to automatically extract the text from
      each page. To extract the figures and tables from each page, we leverage an existing object detection
156
      model (Pfitzmann et al., 2022). Thus, the processed documents consist of interleaved textual and
157
      visual content, where the visual contents are extracted images of figures and tables.

185   2.2     Q UESTION G ENERATION
186
187   To construct diverse and challenging open-ended questions, we leverage a semi-automated pipeline.
188   Concretely, as shown in Figure 4, given a specified content category, we first randomly select a
189
      page from the document that contains the specific content category, such as texts, tables, or figures.
      Consequently, we randomly select a question generator from a pool of leading multimodal models
190
      and instruct it to generate a challenging question based on the document page. To ensure that the
191
      question generator has sufficient context, we also provide the previous page and subsequent page as
192   additional inputs during the question generation process.
193
194   To improve the quality of the generated questions, we conduct an automated verification process as
195
      a preliminary filter for unsuitable questions. Concretely, the question generator is also instructed
      to reflect on the generated question and follow a multi-step checklist to validate the question. For
196
      example, the checklist includes checking if the question is relevant to the document page, if the
197
      specified content category is required to answer the question, and whether the question is answer-
198   able. The question is rejected if it does not satisfy any condition in the checklist. Lastly, we employ
199   a team of annotators to conduct final validation for each question. We employ expert annotators
200   who are Ph.D. students and above in computer science for the academic domain, and professional
201   annotators for the finance and product domains. To be consistent, we provide a similar checklist and
202   instruction as our automated verification stage, and the annotation details are included in Appendix
203   A.1. We found that 80.1% of the generated questions satisfied the automated verification. Of these
204   questions that passed automated verification, 80.9% also satisfied the human verification. Thus, we
205   only retain 851 questions that satisfied both the automated and human verification.
206   The statistics of our benchmark dataset are shown in Table 1, where we ensure a balanced distribu-
207   tion of questions and documents from each domain and question category. In this work, we focus
208   on questions that require a single page of content to answer, and only retain answerable questions.
209   Please also note that while each question focuses on a specific category in a document page, the
210   page may contain content from other categories as context. For instance, a table-based question may
211   also require comparisons to other tables or texts from the same page.
212   Compared to the existing benchmarks in Figure 2, M-LongDoc poses a greater challenge in two main
213   aspects. Firstly, the significantly greater number of pages and tokens in each multimodal document
214   poses extreme computational costs and opportunities to be distracted by irrelevant content (Shi et al.,
215   2023). While this challenge may be mitigated by retrieval-augmented generation (Chen et al., 2022),
      our preliminary study in Section 2.4 shows that existing models are still hindered by their multimodal

      2.3   AUTOMATED E VALUATION
236
237   Given the challenging nature of our multimodal long document benchmark, it is crucial to have a
238   scalable and standardized evaluation method. However, it is less feasible to conduct comprehensive
239   human evaluation due to high labour costs and lack of reproducibility (Clark et al., 2021). Thus,
240   inspired by previous works in automatic evaluation (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
241   2023c), we propose an evaluation framework based on a committee of multimodal judges. Con-
242   cretely, we leverage multiple leading multimodal models to score each answer to a question based
243
      on the criteria of correctness. To provide a clear guideline for evaluation, we define the task intro-
      duction and criteria as shown in Figure 5. To provide more detailed evaluation instruction beyond
244
      the basic definitions above, we further construct detailed evaluation steps, based on the task and
245
      criteria. Thus, the finalized evaluation guide for each judge model consists of the task introduction,
246   criteria, and fixed evaluation steps.
247
248   To provide a more reliable evaluation and reduce intra-model bias (Verga et al., 2024), we leverage
249
      multiple judges to evaluate each candidate answer. Specifically, each judge model Mj is provided
      with the evaluation guide g, ground-truth evidence page as context c, question q, and candidate
250
      answer â, and instructed to assign a correctness score from 1 to 5. However, we observe some
251
      variance in the output scores, even with the same judge model and inputs. Thus, we sample multiple
252   scores from each judge model Mj and aggregate the scores to obtain a fine-grained, continuous
253   score that better reflects the quality of the candidate answer:

351   3   R ETRIEVAL -AWARE M ULTIMODAL T UNING
352
353
      While current multimodal models are often trained on interleaved inputs with multiple pieces of texts
354
      and images (Liu et al., 2023b), they may not be well-optimized for multimodal documents. Specifi-
355   cally, multimodal documents are more challenging to understand as they contain diverse multimodal
356   content including unstructured texts, and images representing structured tables and figures. Thus,
357   models require a more fine-grained understanding and perception of the multimodal document con-
358   tent. Furthermore, the retrieval setting requires models to ground their outputs in the additional
359   context by identifying and utilizing only the pertinent content. However, previous works (Shi et al.,
360   2023) and our preliminary study have shown that they may still be easily misled by some irrelevant
361   information in the retrieved content. To our knowledge, there is no open-source model that can
362   address these challenges in multimodal long documents.
363   To this end, we propose a simple and effective retrieval-aware multimodal document tuning ap-
364   proach. Inspired by previous works in retrieval augmentation (Chen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024),
365   we include both retrieval context from the ground-truth evidence page as well as potentially ir-
366   relevant pages during training. Our approach as shown in Figure 6 presents a training paradigm
367   that is more realistic and similar to the challenges faced during test-time retrieval of multimodal
368   content. Thus, the model learns to handle potentially noisy retrieval contexts, while improving its
369
      text understanding and visual perception capabilities to utilize the most relevant document content.
      To construct the training data, we leverage the same process as shown in Figure 4 to construct a
370
      training corpus of 10,070 samples across 300 documents, and leverage the respective question gen-
371
      erator models to also produce a high-quality answer based on each ground-truth evidence page. We
372   omit the human verification stage for scalability and cost-efficiency, as majority of the automatically
373   verified samples also satisfied human verification. To assess the quality of the generated training
374   solutions, we evaluated using our automated scoring framework on a random subset of 100 samples.
375   We observed an average correctness score of 4.82, indicating very high quality of answers in the
376   training data. To avoid data leakage, we ensure that the documents used to construct the training
377   corpus do not overlap with the evaluation set. For example, we ensure that the training documents
      are from different companies and products, and are published in an earlier time period.

396
397   4.1      TASK S ETTING
398
399   To ensure a practical task setting, we focus on the retrieval-based paradigm, which avoids the exor-
400   bitant cost to process the full document. Based on our preliminary study in Section 2.4, we use the
401   top k = 5 pages ranked by the retriever as a reasonable amount of context for each question. Thus,
402   each model is provided with the retrieved context and question as input, and required to provide an
403   open-ended solution as output. As discussed in Section 2.3, we leverage an automated framework
404
      with multiple judge models to score the correctness of each output solution, on a scale of 1 to 5.
405
406   4.2      M ODELS
407
      To provide a more comprehensive investigation of current models, we use both open-source and
408   close-source models in this work. Concretely, we select GPT-4o (gpt-4o-2024-05-13)6 , Claude 3.5
409   Sonnet (claude-3-5-sonnet-20240620)7 and Gemini 1.5 Pro (gemini-1.5-pro-002) (Google, 2024)
410   due to their leading performance on multimodal benchmarks (Yue et al., 2023). Regarding open-
411   source models, we specifically select models which support interleaved multimodal inputs with
412   multiple images, and fine-grained visual perception of document content. Thus, we mainly focus on
413   LLaVA-OneVision-7B (Li et al., 2024) and Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct (Wang et al., 2024). We plan to
414   expand our investigation to other capable open-source models as they are released.
415
416   4.3      H YPERPARAMETERS
417
418
      For all models, we use greedy decoding with temperature T = 0 to reduce variance. In our training
      framework, we set the number of training epochs to be 1, batch size as 16, and learning rate as 1e-4.
419
      To reduce the training cost due to limited computational resources, we leverage LoRA (Hu et al.,
420
      2022) training with rank as 64 and alpha as 32. Due to training instabilities with other open-source
421   models, we mainly focus the training experiments on the Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct model, which
422   demonstrates leading performance compared to similar-sized models.

452   5.2   E FFECT OF A LTERNATIVE S ETTINGS
453
454   While we mainly focus on the multimodal setting with extracted texts and images, we believe it is
455   also important to explore other settings in practice. Our main data setting as discussed in Section
456   2.1 first extracts the texts, figures, and tables separately, with the figures and tables represented as
457

      6.6% respectively for figure-based and table-based questions when the image inputs are removed.
458
      However, the model may still be able to answer the questions to a limited extent as the extracted text
459
      may contain partial information about the tables and figures. We believe that this underscores the
460   importance of leveraging multimodal content in documents, even though many documents may con-
461   tain a majority of the content as texts. The performance increases slightly for text-based questions
462   when image inputs are removed, suggesting that the images may mislead the model in rare cases.
463   However, we believe this is acceptable as questions may cover a wide variety of multimodal content
464   in practice. On the other hand, we observe that it may be less optimal to use only rendered images
465   of document pages as inputs, instead of separately extracting the texts, tables, and figures as in our
466   main setting. While the rendered page image does contain the original information and layout of the
467   document, including texts, tables, and figures, the model may be less capable of distinguishing the
468
      content between texts and tables.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
472
473   6.1   L ARGE M ULTIMODAL M ODELS
474
475   In recent years, large multimodal models have demonstrated their capability to process and compre-
476   hend data across various formats. Close-source models such as GPT-4o (AI, 2024) can reason across
477
      audio, vision and text. Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024) shows marked improvement on tasks
      that require visual reasoning like interpreting charts and graphs. On the other hand, Gemini 1.5 Pro
478
      (Google, 2024) is capable of reasoning over multiple long documents and hours of video and audio.
479
      Open-source models such as Llava (Liu et al., 2023a), Idefics (Laurençon et al., 2023), Otter (Li
480   et al., 2023), InternVL (Chen et al., 2024c), CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023), have also shown the po-
481   tential over various types of multi-modal content including document images (Mathew et al., 2020),
482   slides (Tanaka et al., 2023), and charts (Huang et al., 2024a). However, the benchmark performance
483   of open-source models tends to lag behind that of close-source models (Yue et al., 2023), prompting
484   an urgent need to bridge the gap. In this work, we introduce a retrieval-aware multimodal tuning
485   framework which can significantly improve the multimodal long document understanding ability of
      models.

486
      6.2   D OCUMENT U NDERSTANDING DATASETS
487
488   Given the practical and business applications of document understanding, researchers have devoted
489   significant effort to this area by introducing new datasets and methods. SearchQA (Dunn et al.,
490   2017), NarrativeQA (Kočiskỳ et al., 2018), QuALITY (Zhu et al., 2020) are reading comprehension
491   datasets over purely textual data with an average length ranging from 1850 to 60k tokens. FinQA
492   (Chen et al., 2021), DocFinQA (Reddy et al., 2024) are introduced in the financial domain. MarkQA
493   (Huang et al., 2023) tackes QA over knowledge bases with numerical reasoning. DocVQA(Mathew
494
      et al., 2020) presents a visual question answering dataset on document images. VisualWebBench
      (Liu et al., 2024) is a multimodal benchmark over single-page documents focusing on various QA-
495
      style tasks. MMLongBench (Ma et al., 2024) is a multimodal document understanding dataset with
496
      an average of 47.5 pages and 21k textual tokens. Methods such as PDFTriage (Saad-Falcon et al.,
497   2023) enables models to retrieve the context from long and structured documents. TAT-LLM (Zhu
498   et al., 2024) addresses QA over a hybrid of tabular and textual data. ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022)
499   is a benchmark of extractive questions-answering task over a chart image, while Chocolate (Huang
500   et al., 2024b) annotates the types of factual errors in machine-generated chart captions.
501
      Concurrent to our work, DocBench (Zou et al., 2024) is similar to MMLongBench as it also focuses
502
      on questions with short or extractive answers. In contrast, our benchmark mainly considers longer,
503   open-ended answers which require more thorough understanding of the document. Furthermore, the
504   authors have found that multimodal models such as GPT-4o perform worse than text-only GPT-4,
505   which indicates the benchmark may be less suitable for multimodal evaluation. On the other hand,
506   our results show that multimodal content is critical for our benchmark, as text-only inputs leads to
507   significant performance degradation. Compared to the datasets above, our benchmark contains
508   lengthier documents with hundreds of pages and focusing on open-ended questions which require
509   in-depth solutions. We further propose an automated and reliable evaluation framework to assess the
510   correctness of model answers, which demonstrates very high agreement with human preferences.
511
512   6.3   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
513
      While recent multimodal models have shown impressive capability in many tasks, applying them di-
514
      rectly to long document understanding tasks may face several challenges to the diverse multimodal
515   content. Additionally, processing entire documents with large models is often impractical, as the
516   text alone may contain millions of tokens, leading to substantial computational costs. Therefore,
517   researchers have designed various retrieval augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
518   2022) methods to address the issues. In this work, we have investigated multiple retrieval methods
519   optimized for document page retrieval, including JINA-CLIP (Xiao et al., 2024) BM25 (Robertson
520   & Zaragoza, 2009), BGE-M3 (Chen et al., 2024a) and ColPali (Faysse et al., 2024). However, de-
521   spite using retrieval, existing multimodal models are constrained by their multimodal biases (Chen
522   et al., 2024b) and susceptibility to irrelevant content that is inherent in the retrieved context (Shi
523
      et al., 2023). Thus, we further proposed a retrieval-aware tuning framework to enhance the perfor-
      mance of models when leveraging retrieval for multimodal long documents.
524
525
526   7     C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we introduce M-LongDoc, a benchmark dataset consisting of 851 samples and an au-
529   tomated framework to evaluate the performance of large multimodal models on document question
530   answering tasks. This benchmark is specifically designed for long and diverse document formats
531   containing text, figures, and tables, aligning with the demands of real-world applications. Unlike
532
      existing benchmarks, M-LongDoc features more recent and lengthy documents, often hundreds of
      pages long, and requires open-ended solutions rather than just extractive answers.
533
534   We also propose a retrieval-aware tuning approach designed for the efficient and effective processing
535   of multimodal long documents. To our knowledge, this is the first training framework and model to
536   directly address the retrieval setting for such documents. Experimental results demonstrate that our
537   method achieves a relative improvement of 4.6% in the correctness of model responses compared
538
      to baseline open-source models. This improvement showcases the effectiveness of our approach in
      handling lengthy and complex multimodal documents, potentially aiding humans in various business
539
      and practical applications that require understanding and answering questions over such documents.

787   A.2   R ETRIEVAL M ETHODS
788
789   To support our retrieval-based document question answering setting, we currently include four state-
790
      of-the art methods to retrieve relevant pages based on each question. They include text-based sparse
      methods such as BM25 (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009) embedding-based methods such as BGE-M3
791
      (Chen et al., 2024a), multimodal piece-wise embedding methods such as JINA-CLIP (Xiao et al.,
792
      2024), and multimodal page-wise embedding methods such as ColPali (Faysse et al., 2024). Note
793   that piece-wise embedding methods separate encode each piece of text, table image, or figure image,
794   whereas page-wise methods can encode the entire page content as a single image. Thus, we rank
795   each page in the document based on the similarity score or relevance score of that page with respect
796   to the given question. As each page may have multiple pieces of content, we consider the highest
797   score of all pieces in a page to be the page-wise relevance score. To compare the effectiveness of
798   each method, we implement a standardized MRR score which refers to the mean reciprocal rank
799   of the gold evidence page for each question. Based on the results in Table 5, we find that ColPali
800   which encodes each page as single image shows the best performance. Thus, we select ColPali as
801
      the preferred retrieval method in our main experiments.

975      Which method consistently shows the highest latent cosine similarity (lcos) across all datasets (MNIST,
976      F-MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) in the zero-shot stitching experiment, and what might this imply about
         the nature of transformations between latent spaces in autoencoders?

978      Answer generated by Qwen2-VL:
         The method that consistently shows the highest latent cosine similarity (lcos) across all datasets (MNIST,
979
         F-MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) in the zero-shot stitching experiment is the ”Cosine” method. This
980      implies that the ”Cosine” method is able to capture the most similar latent spaces across different architec-
981      tures, modalities, and datasets. This suggests that the nature of transformations between latent spaces in
982      autoencoders is not exclusively correlated by orthogonal transformations, but rather by a class of transfor-
983      mations larger than orthogonal transformations. This finding highlights the importance of understanding
         and modeling the particular class of transformations that control the correlation between NNs during im-
984
         age autoencoding.

         The method that consistently shows the highest latent cosine similarity (lcos) across all datasets (MNIST,
987
         F-MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100) in the zero-shot stitching experiment is the ”affine” method. This
988      method achieves the highest lcos values for all datasets, with scores of 0.94 for MNIST, 0.94 for F-MNIST,
989      0.96 for CIFAR-10, and 0.96 for CIFAR-100. This consistency in high lcos values suggests that the affine
990      transformation is particularly effective in aligning the latent spaces of different autoencoders. The high
991      lcos indicates a strong alignment between the latent representations of the autoencoders, which implies
         that the affine transformation is capable of capturing the underlying structure and relationships between
992
         the latent spaces more accurately than other methods. The results imply that the nature of transformations
993      between latent spaces in autoencoders is not exclusively controlled by orthogonal transformations. The
994      affine transformation, which includes scaling, rotation, and translation, appears to be a more versatile and
995      effective method for aligning these spaces. This finding suggests that the latent spaces of image autoen-
996      coders are related by a broader class of transformations, including those that are not strictly orthogonal,
         which is a significant insight into the nature of these transformations and their implications for model

         stitching and zero-shot learning.
