009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is crucial for ensuring the reliability of ma-
012            chine learning models, especially in visual tasks. Most existing benchmarks focus
013            on isolating distribution shifts and creating varying levels of detection difficulty,
014            often relying on manual curation or classifier-based scoring with human annota-
015            tions. Additionally, large-scale benchmarks are typically derivatives of ImageNet-
016            21k classes or combinations of ImageNet with other datasets. However, no exist-
017            ing work offers a setup where only one attribute such as color or class changes in a
018
               controlled manner, while other attributes of the object remain constant. This limits
               our ability to precisely study the impact of individual attributes on OOD detection
019
               performance. We aim to address this by proposing two novel synthetic datasets,
020
               SHAPES and CHARS, designed to explore OOD detection under controlled and
021            fine-grained distribution shifts. SHAPES consist of 2D and 3D geometric shapes
022            with variations in color, size, position, and rotation, while CHARS consists of
023            alphanumeric characters with similar variations. Each dataset presents three sce-
024            narios: (1) known classes with unseen attributes, (2) unseen classes with known
025            attributes, and (3) entirely novel classes and attributes. We train 10 architectures
026            and assess 13 OOD detection methods across the three scenarios, concentrating
027            on the impact of attribute shifts on OOD scores, while also conducting additional
028            analysis on how image corruption influences OOD scores. By systematically ex-
029
               amining how specific attribute shifts affect OOD scores and the affects of noisy
               test samples, we aim to bring greater transparency to where these methods succeed
030
               or fail, helping to identify their limitations under various conditions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is crucial for ensuring the reliability of machine learning mod-
036   els in real-world applications. While models perform well on in-distribution (ID) data, they often
037   fail on unseen OOD inputs, providing high-confidence predictions despite being wrong (Amodei
038   et al., 2016). OOD detection methods mitigate this by identifying unfamiliar data and prevent in-
039   correct predictions, which is vital in high-stakes areas such as healthcare, autonomous systems,
040   and security. Recent advancements in OOD detection encompass a variety of approaches, includ-
041   ing classification-based methods, density-based models, and distance-based techniques (Yang et al.,
042   2024a).
043   Initially, OOD detection methods were evaluated using small-scale datasets with relatively simple
044   in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) pairs. For instance, CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100
045   (Krizhevsky, 2009) were commonly used as ID datasets, while OOD data included datasets such
046   as SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011), LSUN (Yu et al., 2015), Places365 (Zhou et al., 2018), and Tex-
047   tures (Cimpoi et al., 2014). Later, larger benchmarks began incorporating more complex and diverse
048   datasets to better reflect real-world distribution shifts. ImageNet1k (Deng et al., 2009) became
049   a standard ID dataset and the corresponding OOD datasets included iNaturalist (Van Horn et al.,
050
      2018) and classes from ImageNet21k (Ridnik et al., 2021) which were not present in the ID dataset.
051   Recent works in benchmarking OOD methods has focused on overcoming limitations of fixed ID-
052   OOD dataset pairs. Datasets such as OpenImage-O (Wang et al., 2022), ImageNet-OOD (Yang et al.,
053   2024b) and C-OOD (Galil et al., 2023) provide more natural, diverse, and scalable benchmarks,
      addressing issues such as predefined class overlaps, limited coverage, and covariate contamination.

054
      Nevertheless, the field still lacks a framework that provides precise control over individual attributes,
055   which is essential for gaining deeper insights into the reasons behind the success or failure of OOD
056   detection methods.
057
058
      Our Contributions: To address this gap, we introduce a synthetic approach involving two care-
      fully designed datasets, SHAPES and CHARS. SHAPES consists of simple 2D and 3D geometric
059
      primitives such as squares, cubes, and spheres, while the dataset CHARS contains alphanumerical
060
      characters. Each dataset presents test sets where specific attributes of the images are systemati-
061   cally varied. For simplicity, we focus on three controlled scenarios: (1) Known classes with unseen
062   attributes, where we modify the color of the objects while keeping the class from the training dis-
063   tribution constant—this setup represents a covariate shift; (2) Unseen classes with known attributes,
064   where the color remains unchanged, but the object class is new to the model—this setup resembles
065   a semantic shift with visual similarity to the training data; and (3) Entirely novel classes and at-
066   tributes, where both the class and color of the object are completely unfamiliar to the model. We
067   also introduce image corruption in test sets to study how OOD methods respond to noisy inputs.
068   By examining how OOD methods respond to controlled distribution shifts and studying their score
069
      behavior in the presence of corrupted test samples, we aim to provide deeper insights into the con-
      ditions that cause these methods to fail and assess their resilience to minor perturbations, such as
070
      noise or distortions.

097   We introduce two new synthetic datasets, SHAPES and CHARS. SHAPES includes basic 2D and
098
      3D geometric primitives, such as squares, cubes, and spheres, whereas the CHARS dataset com-
      prises alphanumeric characters. To create samples in both datasets, a consistent process is followed.
099
      Each dataset has its own configurations specifying attributes such as color, rotation, size range, and
100
      other dataset-specific properties. These configurations also define the number of samples for train,
101   validation, ID (in-distribution) test, and 3 OOD (out-of-distribution) splits (see Figure 1). The at-
102   tribute values for ID and OOD are disjoint sets, ensuring clear separation between in-distribution
103   and out-of-distribution samples. The background color for all images remains the same across all
104   samples.
105
      The three OOD splits are defined as follows:
106
107          • OOD in color: Images have their colors sampled from OOD colors, while their classes
               remain a subset of ID classes.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
             • OOD in class: Images use ID colors but belong to completely unseen classes not present
109            in the training set.
110          • OOD across both: Configurations are generated by sampling entirely from OOD attributes,
111            meaning both class and color are OOD.
112
      The configuration generator pre-generates all configurations required for train, validation, and test
113
      splits with a fixed random seed, ensuring reproducibility and consistency. Images are rendered dy-
114
      namically in the dataloader, which fetches the necessary configurations for each batch and renders
115   the images using moderngl (Dombi, 2020). For test splits, a specified percentage of images, as
116   defined in the configuration, are pre-assigned with corruption details during the configuration gen-
117   eration phase. The corruption details include a corruption method and a severity level (either 1
118   or 2), selected from one of the ten common image corruption strategies mentioned in Hendrycks
119   & Dietterich (2019). For a detailed overview of the exact attributes and values used, refer to the
120   Appendix A.

      3.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
125
126   Problem Setup:. Let Din = {(xi , yi ); xi ∈ X , yi ∈ Y} represent the In-distribution data (i.e., data
127   the model is trained on) sampled from distribution Pin (x, y), where x ∈ Rd is the input image and
128   y is the corresponding label. The Out-of-distribution data Dout comes from a different distribution
129   Pout (x, y) which is not seen during training.
130
      Given a classifier f : Rd → RN trained on Din that classifies input data to N In-distribution
131
      classes, the goal of Out-of-distribution detection is to design a scoring function S(x) that helps in
132   distinguishing between in-distribution data Din and out-of-distribution data Dout . The decision is
133   made on a threshold τ , where:
134
135                                         In-Distribution,        if S(x) ≥ τ,
                                 S(x) =
136                                         Out-of-Distribution, ifS(x) < τ.
137
138   We evaluate the OOD methods under three types of OOD scenarios: 1) where only the color of
139   the shape/character changes 2) where only the class changes, and 3) where both color and class
140   change. We will use the terms ‘OOD in color,’ ‘OOD in class,’ and ‘OOD in both’ as shorthand for
141   addressing these OOD types.
142
      Datasets: We prepare the SHAPES and CHARS datasets by setting a random seed to generate
143
      image configurations for training, validation, ID test and three OOD test splits (OOD in color, class,
144   and both). Across all four test splits (one in-distribution and three OOD) in both datasets, A portion
145   of the images is corrupted using one of ten corruption methods applied to each image at a specific
146   severity level (either 1 or 2). To ensure consistency, we repeat the training and evaluations using
147   three random seeds.
148
      Backbones: We select 10 architectures combined across the ResNet (He et al., 2016),
149
      DenseNet (Huang et al., 2017), Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), and Wide-
150   ResNet (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016) families, each with a single linear layer as the classification
151   head. The output dimension of the classification head corresponds to the number of in-distribution
152   (ID) classes for each dataset. All models are trained independently from scratch on both datasets.
153
      OOD methods: We evaluate 13 OOD detection methods comprising of logit-based, feature-
154
      based and energy-based methods across the three OOD scenarios. Logit-based methods include
155
      ODIN (Liang et al., 2018), MaxLogit (Hendrycks et al., 2022), MSP (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017),
156   and ViM (Wang et al., 2022), all of which operate directly on logits or modify them to compute
157   OOD scores. Feature-based methods include SCALE (Xu et al., 2024), SHE (Zhang et al., 2023b),
158   GradNorm (Huang et al., 2021), KNN (Sun et al., 2022), and NNGuide (Park et al., 2023), which
159   work on feature representations, typically from the penultimate layer. Lastly, energy-based methods,
160   include EBO (Liu et al., 2020), GEN (Liu et al., 2023), ASH Djurisic et al. (2023), and ReAct (Sun
161   et al., 2021), which calculate an energy score derived from logits or modified activation’s. All OOD
      detection methods are implemented using the OpenOOD framework laid out by Zhang et al. (2023a).

      Evaluation Metrics: We evaluate the OOD-detection performance using the commonly used metric
163   AUROC. It represents the probability that a positive example receives a higher detection score than
164   a negative example (Fawcett, 2006). Higher value indicates better detection performance. AUROC
165   values are calculated for each dataset, for each type of OOD scenario, both with and without image
166   corruption. The reported AUROCs correspond to the median AUROC across the three seeds. The
167   observed absolute deviation from the median (MAD) for AUROC across the three seeds for all OOD
168   methods and backbone combinations was in the order of 10−2 .
169   We use the Overlap Coefficient (eq-1) to measure the overlap between the smoothed densities of
170   min-max normalized OOD scores of ID and OOD test samples.
171
172
173                          overlap(A, B) = |A ∩ B|,       0 ≤ overlap(A, B) ≤ 1.                       (1)

      The set notations used in Equation 1 are for the sake of brevity.

178   3.2   S ENSITIVITY OF OOD METHODS TO COLOR
179
180   Figure 2 presents the AUROC scores for un-corrupted test images across all combinations of OOD
181   methods and architectures on both datasets in two OOD scenarios: OOD in color and OOD in class.
182   Except for methods such as KNN, React, and ViM, other methods perform poorly, with AUROC as
183   low as 0.01, which is worse than random coin flip. The reason for this can be seen in Figure 3, where
184   OOD samples are given higher scores than ID samples in ‘OOD in color’ scenario. This observation
185
      is quite opposite to the intended behavior of OOD detection methods, where ID samples should have
      received higher scores than OOD samples.
186
187   The AUROC values in the scenario when both color and class change, is nearly identical to that of
188   the ‘OOD in color’ scenario and the results are presented in Figure 5 in the Appendix. It can again
189   be seen in Figure 3, where the score distributions for OOD in color and OOD in both color and
190   class scenarios remain similar. This further reinforces the evidence that OOD detection methods are
191
      highly sensitive to changes in visual attributes like color. Further among the selected architectures,
      we observe that ViT performs the best across all OOD methods and amongst the 13 OOD methods,
192
      KNN and ViM are robust across all the architectures (Figure 2).

198   extract the OOD scores for corrupted ID and OOD test sets from all OOD methods, across all ar-
199   chitectures, and apply min-max normalization such that the relative order of score distributions and
200   scales are preserved. Then using the overlap coefficient 1, we measure the overlap between the
201
      smoothed densities of normalized OOD scores of ID and OOD test samples. Figure 4 shows the
      histogram of 130 such overlap coefficients obtained by all the OOD method and backbone combina-
202
      tions. Intuitively, when there is no corruption, the overlap coefficients across all the OOD methods
203
      and architectures should be relatively lower, indicating the ability of OOD methods to assign a higher
204   score to the ID samples. But with image corruption, one might expect a higher overlap given the
205   poor performance of OOD methods in Figure 2. Henceforth pointing towards a conjecture that, an
206   ID corrupted image is as bad as an OOD image (with or without corruption). We precisely corrob-
207   orate this intuition in Figure 4, where we find that overlap coefficients across the OOD method and
208   backbone combinations increase in the presence of image corruption and significant in the case of
209   OOD in class.

211   Figure 6 in the Appendix. As seen in Figure 2, KNN and ViM remain robust OOD methods and

215   attributed to the same line of observation we made in Figure 4 that an ID corrupted image is as bad

      Trends in the evaluation of OOD Methods: The evaluation of out-of-distribution (OOD) detec-
295   tion methods has evolved significantly over time. Initially, OOD detection methods were evaluated
296   on simpler, small-scale datasets with low-resolution images. Common choices for in-distribution
297   (ID) datasets during this early phase included CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009), and
298   SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011). As for the out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets, selections were of-
299   ten visually distinct and low-resolution datasets such as LSUN (Yu et al., 2015) (Crop and Resize),
300   Places365 (Zhou et al., 2018) and Textures (Cimpoi et al., 2014). While these dataset pairs offered
301   some insight into OOD detection performance, their limitations became increasingly apparent. The
302   ID and OOD datasets were typically quite different in terms of both visual appearance and resolution,
303
      often leading to an overestimation of OOD detection performance, and failed to reflect real-world
      distribution shifts encountered in more complex domains. Recognizing these limitations, more re-
304
      cent methods such as ViM (Wang et al., 2022) and NNGuide (Park et al., 2023) shifted toward using
305
      ImageNet-1k (Deng et al., 2009) as the ID dataset, introducing more realistic scenarios for OOD
306   detection. This shift also brought about the adoption of larger, more challenging OOD datasets such
307   as subsets of ImageNet-21k (Ridnik et al., 2021) and iNaturalist (Van Horn et al., 2018).
308
309
      Existing OOD Benchmarks: Datasets such as OpenImage-O (Wang et al., 2022) were developed
      to overcome problems such as OOD datasets relying on predefined class labels, which can overlap
310
      with in-distribution (ID) classes and offer limited coverage. OpenImage-O provides more diverse
311
      and realistic OOD examples. Similarly, ImageNet-OOD (Yang et al., 2024b) focuses on reducing
312   covariate shifts and resolving semantic ambiguity by selecting OOD classes that do not overlap with
313   ImageNet-1K, allowing for a more targeted evaluation of semantic shifts. ImageNet-O (Hendrycks
314   et al., 2021), on the other hand, addresses models’ failures to detect OOD data by using adver-
315   sarial filtering to stress-test models’ high-confidence misclassifications. Other benchmarks, such
316   as NINCO (Bitterwolf et al., 2023), tackle the contamination of OOD samples with ID examples,
317   providing a cleaner and more diverse dataset for OOD evaluation. C-OOD (Galil et al., 2023) in-
318   troduced a versatile framework for evaluating OOD detection across varying levels of difficulty,
319   addressing the biases of earlier benchmarks.
320   While these efforts have significantly advanced the field, most of the focus was on improving the
321   quality of a specific type of attribute shift or developing benchmarks with varying OOD difficulty
322   levels. However, there is still a lack of understanding of how OOD methods perform when indi-
323   vidual image characteristics, such as color or class, are changed, which we have investigated in this
      manuscript.

      We present two novel synthetic datasets, SHAPES and CHARS, designed to explore the complex-
327   ities of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection under controlled attribute shifts. By isolating variables
328   such as color and class, these datasets allow for precise evaluation of how different OOD detec-
329   tion methods perform when encountering unseen data. The results highlight the sensitivity of OOD
330   detection methods, particularly to changes in visual attributes such as color, and demonstrate that
331   existing methods often struggle with fine-grained shifts in distribution. Furthermore, the introduc-
332   tion of image corruption as an additional challenge provides deeper insights into the robustness of
333   these models. The findings suggest the need for continued refinement in OOD detection techniques
334   to ensure reliability in real-world applications.
