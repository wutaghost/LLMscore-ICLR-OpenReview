009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Learning the structure of Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) presents a significant
012
               challenge due to the vast combinatorial search space of possible graphs, which
013
               scales exponentially with the number of nodes. Recent advancements have rede-
014            fined this problem as a continuous optimization task by incorporating differentiable
015            acyclicity constraints. These methods commonly rely on algebraic characteriza-
016            tions of DAGs, such as matrix exponentials, to enable the use of gradient-based
017            optimization techniques. Despite these innovations, existing methods often face
018            optimization difficulties due to the highly non-convex nature of DAG constraints
019            and the per-iteration computational complexity. In this work, we present a novel
020            framework for learning DAGs, employing a Stochastic Approximation approach
021            integrated with Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)-based optimization techniques.
022
               Our framework introduces new projection methods tailored to efficiently enforce
               DAG constraints, ensuring that the algorithm converges to a feasible local mini-
023
               mum. With its low iteration complexity, the proposed method is well-suited for
024
               handling large-scale problems with improved computational efficiency. We demon-
025            strate the effectiveness and scalability of our framework through comprehensive
026            experimental evaluations, which confirm its superior performance across various
027            settings.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Learning graphical structures from data using Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) is a fundamental
034   challenge in machine learning (Koller & Friedman, 2009; Peters et al., 2016; Arjovsky et al., 2019;
035   Sauer & Geiger, 2021). This task has a wide range of practical applications across fields such as
036   economics, genome research (Zhang et al., 2013; Stephens & Balding, 2009), social sciences (Morgan
037   & Winship, 2015), biology (Sachs et al., 2005a), and causal inference (Pearl, 2009; Spirtes et al.,
038   2000). Learning the graphical structure is essential because the resulting models can often be given
039
      causal interpretations or transformed into representations with causal significance, such as Markov
      equivalence classes. When graphical models cannot be interpreted causally (Pearl, 2009; Spirtes
040
      et al., 2000), they can still offer a compact and flexible representation for decomposing the joint
041
      distribution.
042
043   Structure learning methods are typically categorized into two approaches: score-based algorithms
044   searching for a DAG minimizing a particular loss function and constraint-based algorithms relying
045
      on conditional independence tests. Constraint-based methods, such as the PC algorithm (Spirtes &
      Glymour, 1991) and FCI (Spirtes et al., 1995; Colombo et al., 2012), use conditional independence
046
      tests to recover the Markov equivalence class under the assumption of faithfulness. Other approaches,
047
      like those described in Margaritis & Thrun (1999) and Tsamardinos et al. (2003), employ local Markov
048   boundary search. On the other hand, score-based methods frame the problem as an optimization of a
049   specific scoring function, with typical choices including BGe (Kuipers et al., 2014), BIC (Chickering
050   & Heckerman, 1997), BDe(u) (Heckerman et al., 1995), and MDL (Bouckaert, 1993). Given the vast
051   search space of potential graphs, many score-based methods employ local heuristics, such as Greedy
052   Equivalence Search (GES) (Chickering, 2002), to efficiently navigate this complexity. Additionally,
053   Tsamardinos et al. (2006), Gámez et al. (2011) propose hybrid methods combining elements of both
      constraint-based and score-based learning.

054
      Recently, Zheng et al. (2018) introduced a smooth formulation for enforcing acyclicity, transforming
055   the structure learning problem from its inherently discrete nature into a continuous, non-convex
056   optimization task. This formulation allows for the use of gradient-based optimization techniques,
057   enabling various extensions and adaptations to various domains, including nonlinear models (Yu
058   et al., 2019; Ng et al., 2022b; Kalainathan et al., 2022), interventional datasets (Brouillard et al., 2020;
059   Faria et al., 2022), unobserved confounders (Bhattacharya et al., 2021; Bellot & Van der Schaar,
060   2021), incomplete datasets (Gao et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2020), time series analysis (Sun et al.,
061   2021; Pamfil et al., 2020), multi-task learning (Chen et al., 2021), multi-domain settings (Zeng
062   et al., 2021), federated learning (Ng & Zhang, 2022; Gao et al., 2023), and representation learning
063
      (Yang et al., 2021). With the growing interest in continuous structure learning methods (Vowels
      et al., 2022), a variety of theoretical and empirical studies have emerged. For instance, Ng et al.
064
      (2020) investigated the optimality conditions and convergence properties of continuously constrained
065
      approaches such as Zheng et al. (2018). In the bivariate case, Deng et al. (2023b) demonstrated
066   that a suitable optimization strategy converges to the global minimum of the least squares objective.
067   Additionally, Zhang et al. (2022) and Bello et al. (2022) identified potential gradient vanishing issues
068   with existing DAG constraints (Zheng et al., 2018) and proposed adjustments to overcome these
069   challenges.
070
071
072   Contributions. In this work, we focus on the graphical models represented as Directed Acyclic
073   Graphs (DAGs). Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
074
075   1. Problem reformulation: We introduce a new reformulation (8) of the discrete optimization
076      problem for finding DAG as a stochastic optimization problem and we discuss its properties in
077
         detail in Section 3.1. We demonstrate that the solution of this reformulated problem recovers the
         true DAG (Section 3.1).
078
079
      2. Novel algorithm: Leveraging insights from stochastic optimization, we present a new framework
080
         (Algorithm 1) for DAG learning (Section 4) and present a simple yet effective algorithm ψDAG
081      (Algorithm 3) within the framework. We proved that Algorithm ψDAG converges to a local
082      minimum of problem (8).
083
084   3. Experimental comparison: In Section 5, we demonstrate that the method ψDAG scales very
085      well with graph size, handling up to 10000 nodes. At that scale, the primary limitation is not
086      computation complexity but the memory required to store the DAG itself. As a baseline, we com-
087      pare ψDAG with established DAG learning methods, including NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018),
088      GOLEM (Ng et al., 2020) and DAGMA (Bello et al., 2022). We show a significant improvement
089      in scalability, as baseline methods struggle with larger graphs. Specifically, NOTEARS (Zheng
090
         et al., 2018), GOLEM (Ng et al., 2020) and DAGMA (Bello et al., 2022) require more than 100
         hours for graphs with over 3000 nodes, exceeding the allotted time.

135   2.3      M OST R ELATED L ITERATURE
136
      A significant body of research in DAG learning revolves around non-convex continuous optimization
137
      frameworks, such as NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018), GOLEM (Ng et al., 2020), and DAGMA (Bello
138   et al., 2022). These approaches address the DAG constraint using either smooth approximations or
139   novel penalty functions, but they are often computationally expensive and lack scalability.
140
141
      Zheng et al. (2018) formulated the DAG learning problem as a constrained optimization task,
      minimizing the least squares loss while enforcing acyclicity through the matrix exponential. While
142
      this method achieves state-of-the-art results for smaller graphs, its cubic complexity for computing
143
      the acyclicity term severely limits its scalability. Ng et al. (2020) enhanced the scoring function by
144   incorporating a log-determinant term aligned with the Gaussian likelihood, which improves efficiency
145   but does not guarantee acyclic solutions. Similarly, Bello et al. (2022) introduced a differentiable and
146   exact log-determinant-based acyclicity constraint, but its reliance on augmented Lagrangian methods
147   introduces hyperparameter tuning challenges and potential numerical instability.
148
      Other works, such as Chen et al. (2019), proposed variance-ordering procedures for estimating
149
      topological orderings under equal error variances. While these methods naturally extend to high-
150   dimensional settings, their reliance on controlling the maximum in-degree of the graph becomes
151   computationally intensive as graph density increases. In contrast, ψDAG avoids these assumptions
152   and demonstrates scalability to graphs with up to 10, 000 nodes. Gao et al. (2022b) focused on
153   theoretical guarantees for Gaussian DAG models, deriving minimax optimal bounds for structure
154   recovery. Although their work offers valuable insights into sample efficiency, it does not address the
155   computational challenges of large-scale DAG learning. Our approach complements this by providing
156   a scalable stochastic optimization framework applicable to broader settings.
157   Wei et al. (2020) examined optimization challenges in NOTEARS by analyzing KKT conditions
158   and proposed the KKTS algorithm as a post-processing enhancement. While this method improves
159   structural Hamming distance (SHD), its reliance on specific constraints and post-hoc refinements

365
      We experimentally compare our newly proposed algorithm ψDAG3 to other score-based methods for
366
      computing linear DAGs, NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018), GOLEM4 (Ng et al., 2020) and DAGMA
367
      (Bello et al., 2022). As it is established that DAGMA Bello et al. (2022) is an improvement over

369   algorithms were implemented without extensive hyperparameter tuning, we avoided hyperparameter
370   tuning as much as possible. In particular, we apply the same threshold as the one in Zheng et al.

398   5.1                 S YNTHETIC DATA G ENERATION
399
400   We generate ground truth DAGs to have d nodes and an average of k × d edges, where k ∈ {2, 4, 6}
401   is a sparsity parameter. The graph structure is determined by the choice of the graph models to be
402
      either Erdős-Rényi (ER) or Scale Free (SF), and together with sparsity parameter k, we refer to them
      as ERk or SFk. Each of the edges has assigned a random weight uniformly sampled from the interval
403
      [−1, −0.05] ∪ [0.05, 1].
404
405   Following the linear Structural Equation Model (SEM), the observed data X has form X = N(I −
406   W)−1 , where N ∈ Rn×d represents n d-dimensional independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.)
407   noise samples drawn from either Gaussian, exponential or Gumbel distributions. In this study, we
408
      focus on an equal variance (EV) noise setting, with a scale factor of 1.0 applied to all variables.
      Unless otherwise specified, we generate the same number of samples n ∈ {5000, 10000} for training
409
      and validation datasets, respectively. A more detailed description can be found in Appendix C.

      5.2                 S CALABILITY C OMPARISON
412
413   In this section, we discuss the runtime of the proposed ψDAG algorithm. We run compared algorithms
414   until the function value converges close to the solution, f (xk ) − f (x) ≤ 0.1 · f (x).
415
416
      Figures 3a and 3b compare the performance of ψDAG against GOLEM and DAGMA on smaller
      graphs with various structures and noise distributions. Meanwhile, Figure 4 illustrates the scalability
417
      of ψDAG on the large graphs.
418
419   Both Figures 3 and 4 clearly demonstrate that ψDAG significantly outperforms GOLEM and DAGMA
420   in terms of runtime across both sparse and dense ER graphs in nearly all considered scenarios. The
421   only exception occurs with very small graphs d < 100 and high sparsity (ER2, SF2), where DAGMA
422
      is marginally faster than ψDAG. However, as graph size and density increase, ψDAG scale efficiently
      across all scenarios even up until d = 10000 nodes. In the case of sparse graphs (Figures 4a, 4b),
423
      ψDAG consistently converges within a few hours, even for d = 10000 nodes.
424
425   In contrast, increasing graph size causes the computational cost of both GOLEM and DAGMA to
426   skyrocket. Notably, across all tested graphs (4a), GOLEM exceeds allocated runtime of 36 hours for
427   d ≥ 3000 nodes, while DAGMA exceeds for d ≥ 5000 nodes.

430   Figure 4c), neither method consistently achieved the stopping criterion – DAGMA failed to converge

503   6    C ONCLUSION
504
505   We introduce a novel framework for learning Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) that addresses the
506   scalability and computational challenges of existing methods. Our approach leverages Stochastic
507
      Approximation techniques in combination with Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)-based meth-
      ods, allowing for efficient optimization even in high-dimensional settings. A key contribution of
508
      our framework is the introduction of new projection techniques that effectively enforce DAG con-
509
      straints, ensuring that the learned structure adheres to the acyclicity requirement without the need for
510   computationally expensive penalties or constraints seen in prior works.
511
512   The proposed framework is theoretically grounded and guarantees convergence to a feasible local
513
      minimum. One of its main advantages is its low iteration complexity, making it highly suitable for
      large-scale structure learning problems, where traditional methods often struggle with runtime and
514
      memory limitations. By significantly reducing the per-iteration cost and improving convergence
515
      behavior, our framework demonstrates superior scalability when applied to larger datasets and more
516   complex graph structures.
517
518   We validate the effectiveness of our method through extensive experimental evaluations across a
519
      variety of settings, including both synthetic and real-world datasets. These experiments show that our
      framework consistently outperforms existing methods such as GOLEM (Ng et al., 2020), NOTEARS
520
      (Zheng et al., 2018), and DAGMA (Bello et al., 2022), both in terms of objective loss and runtime,
521
      particularly in scenarios involving large and dense graphs. Furthermore, our method exhibits robust
522   performance across different types of graph structures, highlighting its potential applicability to
523   various practical fields such as biology, finance, and causal inference.

844   In this section, we present some theoretical properties of the DAG set and analyze the convergence of
845   the proposed method.
846   Lemma 2. The DAG set D is a conic set. Specifically, for any W ∈ D and α ≥ 0, we have αW ∈ D.
847   Additionally, the DAG set D includes the entire line, meaning that for any W ∈ D and α ∈ R,
848   αW ∈ D.
849
850   Proof. We begin by observing that 0 ∈ D, as a graph with no edges is trivially a DAG. Next, consider
851   any W ∈ D and α ∈ R \ {0}. Scaling W by α does not alter the structure of the graph; it only
852   changes the edge weights. Since the graph remains acyclic, αW ∈ D. Thus, the DAG set D satisfies
853   the stated properties.
854
855   Now, let us move to the subsets of DAG, which are based on a topological ordering π.
856   Definition 3. A topological ordering π of a directed graph is a linear ordering of its vertices such
857   that, for every directed edge (u, v) from vertex u to vertex v, u comes before v in the ordering. We
858   call Ord(W) a set of all possible topological orderings for DAG W and ord(W) is one of the
859   orderings.
860
861   For the graphs with d vertices, there are exactly d! distinct topological orderings.
862
      Every topological ordering π corresponds to subspace of all DAGs which can have this topological
      ordering, we call it π-subspace DAG.
863
      Definition 4. A π-subspace Dπ is a set of all DAGs W such that π ∈ Ord(W).


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Let us prove that π-subspace Dπ is a linear subspace.
865
866
      Lemma 5. Dπ is a linear subspace, meaning for any W1 ∈ Dπ , W2 ∈ Dπ , α ∈ R, β ∈ R,
      W = αW1 + βW2 ∈ Dπ .
867
868
      Proof. We should simply note that any non-zero value in W1 corresponds to an edge between vertices
869
      u and v such that v is after u in the ordering π. The same holds for W2 . Hence, any non-zero value
870
      in W holds the ordering π.
871
872
      Next, we highlight that the DAG set D is a union of π-subspaces for all possible orderings π.
873
874
      Lemma 6. The DAG set D is a union of all π-subspaces.
875                                              D = ∪π Dπ .
876
877   Proof. For any DAG W ∈ D there exists a topological ordering π, hence W ∈ Dπ ∈ ∪π Dπ . On the
878   other side, all elements of ∪π Dπ are DAGs by definition and belongs to D.

904
      Computing. Our experiments were carried out on a machine equipped with 80 CPUs and one
905
      NVIDIA Quadro RTX A6000 48GB GPU. Each experiment was allotted a maximum wall time of 36
906
      hours as in DAGMA Bello et al. (2022).
907
908
      Graph Models. In our experimental simulations, we generate graphs using two established random
909   graph models:
910
911         • Erdős-Rényi (ER) graphs: These graphs are constructed by independently adding edges
912           between nodes with a uniform probability. We denote these graphs as ERk , where kd
913           represents the expected number of edges.
914         • Scale-Free (SF) graphs: These graphs follow the preferential attachment process as de-
915           scribed in Barabási & Albert (1999). We use the notation SFk to indicate a scale-free
916           graph with expected kd edges and an attachment exponent of β = 1, consistent with the
917           preferential attachment process. Since we focus on directed graphs, this model corresponds
              to Price’s model, a traditional framework used to model the growth of citation networks.


                                                      17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      It is important to note that ER graphs are inherently undirected. To transform them into Directed
919   Acyclic Graphs (DAGs), we generate a random permutation of the vertex labels from 1 to d, then
920   orient the edges according to this ordering. For SF graphs, edges are directed as new nodes are added,
921   ensuring that the resulting graph is a DAG. After generating the ground-truth DAG, we simulate the
922   structural equation model (SEM) for linear cases, conducting experiments accordingly.
923
924   Metrics. The performance of each algorithm is assessed using the following four key metrics:
925
926          • Structural Hamming Distance (SHD): A widely used metric in structure learning that
927            quantifies the number of edge modifications (additions, deletions, and reversals) required to
928
               transform the estimated graph into the true graph.
929          • True Positive Rate (TPR): This metric calculates the proportion of correctly identified
930            edges relative to the total number of edges in the ground-truth DAG.
931          • False Positive Rate (FPR): This measures the proportion of incorrectly identified edges
932            relative to the total number of absent edges in the ground-truth DAG.
933          • Runtime: The time taken by each algorithm to complete its execution provides a direct
934            measure of the algorithm’s computational efficiency.
935
             • Stochastic gradient computations: Number of gradient computed.
936
937
      Linear SEM. In the linear case, the functions are directly parameterized by the weighted adjacency
938
      matrix W . Specifically, the system of equations is given by Xi = XWi + Ni , where W =
939
      [W1 | · · · |Wd ] ∈ Rd×d , and Ni ∈ R represents the noise. The matrix W encodes the graphical
940   structure, meaning there is an edge Xj → Xi if and only if Wj,i ̸= 0. Starting with a ground-truth
941   DAG B ∈ {0, 1}d×d obtained from one of the two graph models, either ER or SF, edge weights were
942   sampled independently from Unif[−1, −0.05] ∪ [0.05, 1] to produce a weight matrix W ∈ Rd×d .
943   Using this matrix W, the data X = XW + N was sampled under the following three noise models:
944
945          • Gaussian noise: Ni ∼ N (0, 1) for all i ∈ [d],
946          • Exponential noise: Ni ∼ Exp(1) for all i ∈ [d],
947
             • Gumbel noise: Ni ∼ Gumbel(0, 1) for all i ∈ [d].
948
949
      Using these noise models, random datasets X ∈ Rn×d were generated by independently sampling
950   the rows according to one of the models described above. Unless specified otherwise, each simulation
951   generated n = 5000 training samples and a validation set of 10, 000 samples.
952
953
      The implementation details of the baseline methods are as follows:
954
             • NOTEARS Zheng et al. (2018)was implemented using the authors’ publicly available
955            Python code, which can be found at https://github.com/xunzheng/notears.
956            This method employs a least squares score function, and we used their default set of
957            hyperparameters without modification. We used the default choice of λ = 0.1 as in authors’
958            code.
959          • GOLEM Ng et al. (2020) was implemented using the authors’ Python code, avail-
960            able at https://github.com/ignavierng/golem, along with their PyTorch
961            version at https://github.com/huawei-noah/trustworthyAI/blob/
962            master/gcastle/castle/algorithms/gradient/notears/torch/
963            golem_utils/golem_model.py. We adopted the default hyperparameter settings
964            provided by the authors, specifically λ1 = 0.02 and λ2 = 5. For additional details of their
965            method, we refer to Appendix F in Ng et al. (2020)
966          • DAGMA Bello et al. (2022) was implemented using the authors’ Python code, which
967            is available at https://github.com/kevinsbello/dagma. We used the default
968            hyperparameters provided in their implementation.
969
970   Thresholding. Following the approach taken in previous studies, including the baseline methods
971   (Zheng et al., 2018; Ng et al., 2020; Bello et al., 2022), for all the methods, we apply a final
      thresholding step of 0.3 to effectively reduce the number of false discoveries.
