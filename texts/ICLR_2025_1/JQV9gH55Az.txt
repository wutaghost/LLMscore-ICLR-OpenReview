               We showcase good capabilities of the plain diffusion model with Transformers
013
               (SimDiffPDE) for general partial differential equations (PDEs) solving from var-
014            ious aspects, namely simplicity in model structure, scalability in model size, flex-
015            ibility in training paradigm, and universality between different PDEs. Specif-
016            ically, SimDiffPDE reformulates PDE-solving problems as the image-to-image
017            translation problem, and employs plain and non-hierarchical diffusion model with
018            Transformer to generate the solutions conditioned on the initial states/parameters

021            main of PDEs. SimDiffPDE achieves a remarkable improvement of +51.4% on

023
               Elasticity for solid mechanics, our SimDiffPDE-B achieves significant relative im-
024
               provements of +21.1%, +11.3%, +15.2%, +25.0%, and +23.4%, respectively.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Solving partial differential equations (PDEs) is immensely important in extensive real-world appli-
032   cations, such as weather forecasting (Pathak et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Bi et al., 2023), industrial
033   design (Sekar et al., 2019; Jing et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024), and material analysis (Roubı́ček, 2013;
034   Kadic et al., 2019). As a basic scientific problem, it is usually difficult to obtain analytic solutions
035   for PDEs. Therefore, the solutions of PDEs are typically discretized into meshes and then solved
036   by numerical methods (Rodi, 1997; Zhao, 2008; Greenfeld et al., 2019), which usually takes a few
037   hours or even days for complex structures (Umetani & Bickel, 2018). To deal with these issues,
038   there has recently been rapid progress in deep learning-based methods (Li et al., 2020; 2024b; Lu
039
      et al., 2021), which typically tackles the challenging task using convolutional neural networks or
      transformers. Thanks to their impressive nonlinear modeling capacity, they can learn to approxi-
040
      mate the input and output mappings of PDE-governed tasks from data during training and then infer
041
      the solution significantly faster than numerical methods (Goswami et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023).
042
043   To date, major deep-learning-based methods can be broadly classified into three categories: (1)
044   neural approaches that approximate the solution function of the underlying PDE (Han et al., 2018;
045
      Raissi et al., 2019); (ii) hybrid approaches (Arcomano et al., 2022; Bar-Sinai et al., 2019; Berthelot
      et al., 2023; Greenfeld et al., 2019; Kochkov et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023), where neural networks
046
      either augment numerical solvers or replace plats of them; (iii) neural approaches in which the
047
      learned evolution operator iteratively maps the current approximate solution to a future state of the
048   approximate solution (Bhatnagar et al., 2019; Brandstetter et al., 2022a;b). Despite that approaches
049   (i) have achieved great success in modeling inverse and high-dimensional problems, and approaches
050   (ii-iii) have started advance fluid and weather modeling in two and three dimensions, these methods
051   typically learn a deterministic mapping between input coefficients and their solutions. However, due
052   to the chaotic nature of some dynamics system described by PDE, e.g., Navier-Stokes equation, even
053   small ambiguities of the spatially averaged states as the inputs can lead to fundamentally different
      solutions over time, which leads the deterministic methods providing non-robust answers.

074   In comparison, generative diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022) offer substantial potential for
075   solving PDEs, especially those describing highly nonlinear systems, exhibiting capabilities similar
076
      to those in video prediction based on initial frames and auxiliary conditioning. Specifically, diffusion
      models can construct generative distributions that closely approximate the underlying probabilistic
077
      solution distributions instead of one solution point. Therefore, by ensembling solutions by sampling
078
      difference Guassian noise as inputs during the inference phase, diffusion models can produce more
079   robust and accurate solutions of PDEs that particularly describe nonlinear and even chaotic systems.
080
081   In this paper, we demonstrate that plain diffusion models can be repurposed as effective and gen-
082
      eral PDE solvers (SimDiffPDE), with the multi-scale noise. The key to unlocking the potential of
      diffusion models lies in their ability to efficiently capture patterns of multiple scales in the solu-
083
      tion domain. However, we observe that the default Guassin noise can not efficiently destroy the
084
      large-scale pattern in the forward process, and therefore the diffusion model can not learn to re-
085   cover the large-scale pattern efficiently in the reverse process. By adding multi-scale noise in the
086   forward process, the diffusion models are more explicitly required to learn to denoise the multi-
087   scale noise to reconstruct multi-scale patterns of PDE solutions. During the inference phase, we
088   leverage the test-time ensemble method to consider the generated solution distributions by sampling
089   multiple Guassian noises as inputs. The two designs not only maintains structural simplicity but
090   also significantly improves accuracy and robustness compared to previous state-of-the-art solvers.
091   Our model consistently surpasses previous state-of-the-art models across six benchmarks involv-
092   ing various types of PDEs (Wu et al., 2024; 2022; Li et al., 2022b; Hao et al., 2023; Xiao et al.,
093
      2023). Notably, we achieve a +51.4% improvement in the challenging Navier-Stokes equations.
      For benchmarks for solving partial differential equations, e.g., Darcy Flow, Airfoil and Pipe that de-
094
      scribe fluids, Plasticity and Elasticity that describe solids, our SimDiffPDE-B achieves considerable
095
      relative improvements of +21.1%, +11.3%, +15.2%, +25.0%, and +23.4%, respectively.
096
097   Besides the superior performance, we also show the surprisingly good capabilities of SimDiffPDE
098   from various aspects, namely simplicity, scalability, flexibility and universality. 1) For simplicity,
099
      due to the strong generative feature representation ability, the SimDiffPDE framework is rather sim-
      ple. For example, it does not require any specific domain knowledge for architecture design and en-
100
      joys a plain and non-hierarchical structures by simply stacking several diffusion transformer layers.
101
      2) The simplicity in structure brings the excellent scalability properties of SimDiffPDE. To be more
102   specific, one can easily control the model size by stacking different number of diffusion transformer
103   layers and increase or decrease feature dimensions, e.g., we design SimDiffPDE-XS, SimDiffPDE-
104   S, SimDiffPDE-B, SimDiffPDE-L and SimDIffPDE-XL, to balance the inference speed and per-
105   formance for various deployment requirements. 3) For flexiblity, we demonstrate our SimDiffPDE
106   can adapt well to different input resolutions with minor modifications. 4) Lastly, our SimDiffPDE
107   showcases the good feasibility to various PDE equations, including Navier-Stokes equation, Darcy
      flow equation, hyper-elastic problem and plastic forging problem.

      In summary, the contributions of this paper can be outlined as follows: (1) We propose a simple
109   yet high-performing generative baseline model for solving various PDEs, named SimDiffPDE. This
110   model achieves consistent leading results across six datasets covering various grid types and PDE
111   types, improving performance by an average of 22.0% compared to the second-best model, without
112   complex network architectures or tailored designs. (2) We leverage a multi-scale noise strategy
113   that further unlocks the potential of diffusion models in solving PDEs, enabling efficient capture of
114   information at different frequencies and precise construction of the solution distribution for PDEs.

118   2.1   D IFFUSION MODEL
119
120   Diffusion models have been widely applied to various tasks, including image generation (Ho et al.,
121   2022a), image restoration (Xia et al., 2023), super-resolution (Li et al., 2022a), text-to-image gen-
122   eration (Ruiz et al., 2023), video generation (Ho et al., 2022b), and audio generation (Liu et al.,
123   2023). Additionally, diffusion models have been used to generate datasets related to PDEs (Lienen
124
      et al., 2023). Among these, Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) are
      widely utilized. This model achieves data generation through a forward noise-adding process and
125
      a reverse denoising process. In the forward process, noise is gradually added to the real data until
126
      it approximates a standard normal distribution. In the reverse process, the model learns the condi-
127   tional probability distribution between input conditions and output results, gradually denoising from
128   pure noise to recover a high-quality target distribution. Leveraging the ability of DDPM to learn the
129   probability distribution between input conditions and output results, we apply it to solve PDEs.
130
131   2.2   D EEP LEARNING PDE S SOLVER
132
133   For a long time, various numerical methods (Smith, 1985; Moukalled et al., 2016) have been
134   widely used to solve PDEs. With the rise of deep learning, physics-informed neural networks
135   (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019); the other class is data-driven neural operators. Physics-informed
136   neural networks was proposed by Raissi et al. (2019), where the constraints of PDEs (including
137
      equations, boundary conditions, and initial conditions) are used as a loss function. By employing
      a self-supervised learning approach to train neural networks (Ren et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022),
138
      the model’s output gradually conforms to these constraints, resulting in an approximate solution.
139
      However, this paradigm requires a rigorous formalization of partial differential equations and relies
140   heavily on network optimization, which limits its practicality. Neural operators establishes the
141   mapping between inputs and outputs through neural operators, widely applied in the solution of par-
142   tial differential equations (PDEs) (Li et al., 2020). The core idea of this operator is to approximate
143   integration using linear projections in the Fourier domain. Based on this foundation, many improve-
144   ments have emerged. For instance, U-FNO (Wen et al., 2022) and U-NO (Rahman et al., 2022)
145   have proposed using the U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) architecture to enhance the performance
146   of FNO. F-FNO (Tran et al., 2021) utilizes factorization in the Fourier domain, while WMT (Gupta
147   et al., 2021) introduces a neural operator learning scheme based on multiwavelets.
148   With the rise of Transformers (Vaswani, 2017), the recently high-performing Transolver (Wu et al.,
149   2024) on multiple PDE benchmarks propose to construct mappings of inputs to outputs by learning
150   the intrinsic physical states of the PDEs captured by learnable slices. However, these methods are
151   essentially deterministic, which is not robust due to the chaotic nature of some PDEs. In contrast,
152   SimDiffPDE leverages the characteristics of diffusion models to establish complex probability dis-
153   tributions between input conditions and output results. Simultaneously, through a multi-scale noise
154
      approach, it explicitly distinguishes and learns mutliscale information in PDE solution space.

201
      Architecture We propose a simple yet highly effective baseline model for PDEs based on diffu-
202
      sion models, while exploring their potential in this context. To achieve this, we keep the architecture
203
      straightforward, avoiding complex modules and elaborate tricks, even though these could poten-
204   tially enhance the model’s performance. To ensure the simplicity of the baseline model, we adopt
205   the standard diffusion transformer block with AdaLN-Zero from Peebles & Xie (2023). The overall
206   framework of SimDiffPDE is shown in Figure 2.
207
      Training phase During training phase, we randomly select the input x and its corresponding out-
208
      put y from the training set of the PDEs, and then add multi-scale noise ϵM ulti ∈ RN ×Cy (described
209
      in Sec. 3.3) to y to obtain noisy yt . Next, we concatenate the noisy yt ∈ RN ×Cy and x ∈ RN ×Cx
210   along the feature dimension to obtain s ∈ RN ×Cs , where Cs = Cx + Cy . Then, we input s into the
211   diffusion transformer block. When inputting s into the diffusion with transformer, the first step is to
212   perform patch embedding on s and time embedding on time step t ∈ RN ×Cs used for the diffusion
213   process. Finally, we input the embedded variables into the diffusion transformer block to predict
214   noise ϵ̂ ∈ RN ×Cy . In the training process, we use the loss function mentioned in Eq. 2. Experi-
215   ments show that adding the L1 loss on top of the L2 loss can more effectively capture high-frequency
      information in the solution domain of PDEs.

303   3.4   T EST- TIME ENSEMBLE
304
305   Due to the nonlinear nature of PDEs, the small variation of the input parameters or states can lead
306   to significant variations of solutions in some PDEs. With the stochastic nature of the DDPM infer-
307   ence process, different initial noises yt can lead to varying solutions, which allows SimDiffPDE to
308   simulate the nonlinear dynamics of PDEs. To better leverage this feature, we leverage a testing-time
309   ensemble strategy for more accurate and robust solutions of PDEs.
310   Given the same input x, we obtain a series of solutions {y1 , y2 , · · · , yn }. We employ an iterative
311   method to estimate the scale factors ŝi and translations t̂i of these solutions relative to a specific
312   range. Due to the continuity and smoothness of PDE solutions, we achieve alignment of the solutions
313   by minimizing the distance between pairs of transformed solutions (ŷ′ i , ŷ′ j ). Specifically, ŷ′ =
314   ŷ × ŝ + t̂. In each optimization step, we compute the median of the single solution points in the PDE
315   solution domain as m(a, b) = median(ŷ′ 1 (a, b), ŷ′ 2 (a, b), · · · , ŷ′ n (a, b)) to derive the merged
316   PDE solution. To prevent the solutions from converging to a trivial solution (e.g., all solutions
317   being the same) and to ensure that m maintains an intensity within the unit range, we introduce an
318   additional regularization term R = | min(m)| + |1 − max(m)|. Therefore, the objective function
319   can be expressed as

      our ensemble solution. Due to the diversity and complexity of PDE solutions, this integration step
366
      does not require ground truths. Through multiple inferences, we can capture the spatial features and
367
      dynamic variations of the solutions, thereby enhancing the robustness and accuracy of predictions.

530   5             C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce SimDiffPDE, the first PDE solver based on a diffusion model with
533
      Transformers. Unlike traditional deep learning PDE solvers that create a deterministic mapping
534   between input conditions and output results, SimDiffPDE employs DDPM and multi-scale noise
535   to capture complex physical and geometric states across various frequencies in the PDE solution
536   domain. This approach establishes a complex probability distribution between inputs and outputs,
537   resulting in high-precision solutions. SimDiffPDE has achieved state-of-the-art performance on
538   six widely recognized benchmarks. In the future, our goal is to extend SimDiffPDE to solve non-
539   stationary PDEs in continuous time, similar to video generation, while also exploring large-scale
      pre-training of SimDiffPDE.

740   Elasticity This benchmark evaluates the internal stress distribution within an elastic material based
741   on its structural configuration, discretized into 972 points (Li et al., 2023). For each sample, the input
742   is a tensor of shape 972 × 2, representing the 2D coordinates of the discretized points. The output is
743   the corresponding stress at each point, resulting in a tensor of shape 972 × 1. The dataset consists
744   of 1000 samples with varying structures for training, and an additional 200 samples are reserved for
745   testing.

747   to an impact from an arbitrarily shaped die (Li et al., 2023). Each input is a die shape, discretized
748   into a structured mesh and stored as a tensor of shape 101 × 31. The output is the deformation at
749   each mesh point over 20 time steps, represented by a tensor of shape 20 × 101 × 31 × 4, where the
750   four channels capture the deformation in different directions. The dataset comprises 900 training
751   samples with different die shapes, and 80 samples are used for testing.
752   Navier-Stokes This benchmark simulates incompressible viscous flow on a unit torus, where the
753   fluid density is constant and the viscosity is set to 1e − 3, 1e − 4 and 1e − 5. The fluid field is
754   discretized into a 64 × 64 regular grid. The task is to predict the future 10 steps of the fluid based on
755   the observations from the previous 10 steps. The model is trained using 1,000 fluid instances with
      different initial conditions and tested with 200 new samples.

      B.3.1    H IGH -F REQUENCY F ILTER
814
815   Definition: The high-frequency filter is used to retain the high-frequency components of the image,
816   primarily emphasizing edges and details. We choose a simple high-pass filter defined as follows:
817
818   highpass_kernel = np.array([[0, -1, 0],
819                                [-1, 4, -1],
820                                [0, -1, 0]], np.float32)
821
      Explanation:
822
823
              • This filter applies a large positive weight (4) to the center pixel and negative weights to the
824             surrounding pixels, enhancing edge information.
825
826
              • In the convolution operation, the filter subtracts the average value of surrounding pixels,
                highlighting areas with significant changes, thus achieving high-frequency component ex-
827
                traction.
828
829
      B.3.2    L OW-F REQUENCY F ILTER
830
831   Definition: The low-frequency filter is used to smooth the image and remove high-frequency noise.
832   We use a simple averaging filter defined as follows:
833
834   lowpass_kernel = np.ones((8, 8), np.float32) / 64
835
836   Explanation:
837
838           • This filter is an 8x8 averaging filter, where each element has a value of 1/64 (the total sum
839
                of 8 × 8). This means that in the convolution operation, the filter calculates the average of
                the surrounding 64 pixels.
840
841           • By retaining low-frequency components, this filter effectively reduces high-frequency noise
842             in the image, resulting in a smoother appearance.
