009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               To alleviate the commonly encountered inadequate time-series data problem in
012            DL (DL), we develop a non-DL generic data synthesis method. When current
013            methods require real data or data statistics to train generators or synthesize data,
014            our method InfoBoost enables zero-shot training of models without the need for
015            real data or data statistics. Additionally, as an application of our synthetic data,
016            we train an unconditional feature (rhythm, noise, trend) decomposer based on our
017            synthetic data, which is applicable to real time-series data. Through experiments,
018            our non-DL synthetic data enables models to achieve superior performance on
019            unsupervised tasks and self-supervised prediction & imputation compared models
020
               using real data. Visualized case studies further demonstrate the effectiveness of
               our novel unconditional feature decomposer trained with our synthetic data.

026   DL (DL) practitioners across various time-series domains commonly encounter the obstacle of in-
027   adequate data, domains including finance Tang et al. (2022), energy P et al. (2022), traffic Shaygan
028   et al. (2022), weather Zhu et al. (2023), and healthcare Saeidi et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022);

      Zhang et al. (2023). Procuring sufficient time-series data is often financially costly, which requires

      or personal information.

035
      associated with obtaining and handling real-world time-series data for model training.

      Inspired by the potential benefits of synthetic data Savage (2023); Luo et al. (2023); Yin et al.
055   (2023b); Zhang et al. (2024), we explored synthetic time-series data generation methods. However,
056   we observed that almost all time-series data generation approaches require sampling training data
057   from real datasets or fine-tuning DL-based generators Luo et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2022); Yang &
058   Hong (2022). While existing methods can somewhat alleviate data quality, bias, and vulnerability
059   issues, they still require ample amounts of high-quality real data from the specific domain to ensure
060   the generator’s output encompasses unseen data, thereby unable to fully overcome the challenge
061   of inadequate if certain time-series domain real data. Therefore, we believe that to address the
062   limitations of DL-based data synthesis, a more generic time-series data synthesis method that does
063
      not rely on real data is essential.
064   Our first challenge is to deal with the significant variations presented in time-series data across dif-
065   ferent datasets or domains. These variations encompass differences in data distribution, time scales,
066   signal-to-noise ratio, and feature types, among others. In order to establish a universally applica-
067   ble approach for diverse time-series data, we draw inspiration from a classic method for general
068   time series frequency domain extraction: the Fourier transform. Usually, in the field of time series
069
      analysis, widely used transformations such as the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) and Discrete
      Cosine Transform (DCT) are applied, while the Continuous Fourier Transform (CFT) is used in
070
      special cases. They all come with equations suggesting that each sample point contains information
071
      about the frequency domain Zieliński (2021). Therefore, CFT, DFT and DCT transformations con-
072   vert these sample points into corresponding frequency components in the frequency domain, based
073   on their underlying principle that each sample point in the time-series data can be represented by a
074   set of frequency amplitude and phase. Building upon the fundamental capabilities of the frequency
075   domain transformations, we have designed a method to synthesize data by superimposing several
076   sine waves with varying phases, frequencies and amplitudes, simulating a range of rhythmic signals
077   that may occur in the real world. Similar approaches to modeling rhythmic data have also been
078   employed in other works Kükrer & İnce (2023) Maric (2017) Huo et al. (2021).
079   As the second challenge, real-world time-series data often contain noises of various frequencies
080   and distributions, which can interfere with the accurate extraction of frequency components by the
081   transformations. The transformations assume that each sample point can be represented exclusively
082   by a combination of frequency, amplitude, and phase. However, in real data, individual signal
083   sample points often incorporate non-rhythmic noise components that should not be converted into
084   frequency, amplitude, and phase values. This inherent noise contamination impedes these transfor-
085   mations from precisely extracting the rhythmic portion’s spectral information from the actual data.
086
      Secondly, time-series data may contain long-term features that remain undetected in the sampled
      data due to their minimum feature periods exceeding the sampling window’s duration. As a result,
087
      these transformations may fail to identify such long-period features within the sampled data, poten-
088
      tially causing information loss or distortion in the frequency domain. In conclusion, although these
089   transformations are useful in certain scenarios, their limitations are difficult to avoid when dealing
090   with complex real-world time-series data.
091
092
      Our approach to handling these challenges involves the explicit design of separately contained multi-
      source rhythmic data (MRD) information, various types of noise and their respective noise ratios (TN
093
      & NR), as well as trend information (TI) that extends beyond the sampling window. Real-world data
094
      typically lacks these explicit information. To address this, we develop a data synthesis approach that
095   revolves around synthesizing MRD, TN & NR, and TI to create synthetic data with explicit infor-
096   mation. Each set of synthetic data inherently corresponds to a specific set of generating parameters,
097   including MRD, TN & NR, and TI. The data synthesis process only require sampling methods cus-
098   tomized for MRD, TN & NR, and TI, with various random synthetic parameters. It does not rely
099   on any learnable parameters to generate highly versatile synthetic data to solve the generalization
100   problem of time-series data. This versatility is demonstrated by the fact that a DL model trained
101   solely on InfoBoost’s non-DL synthetic data outperforms that trained on large amounts of real data
102   when validated on real data test sets.
103   In addition to applying synthetic data in unsupervised and self-supervised training, as an applica-
104   tion of our synthetic data, we explored the idea of using the Rhythm, Noise, and Trend components
105   in the synthesis process as reverse engineering to train a feature decomposer. This decomposer
106   would be able to separate real-world time-series data into its Rhythm, Noise, and Trend constituents.
107   We achieved this by training the decomposer with synthetic data as inputs and their corresponding
      Rhythm, Noise, and Trend components as labels. The resulting feature decomposer can effectively

      divide any single-channel time-series into its Rhythm, Noise, and Trend parts. Given the decom-
109   poser’s ability to isolate the rhythm component, we compare the frequency-domain representations
110   derived from the raw data and the isolated rhythm. Visual analysis shows that the frequency-domain
111   characteristics of the rhythm component are more distinct.

      Summarized below are the main contributions of this work:
113
114   1. We present a general-purpose, non-DL approach (no need for real data to train) for synthesizing
115   time-series data aimed at alleviating the challenges associated with acquiring domain-specific real
116   time-series datasets.
117   2. We have validated the efficacy of our data synthesis method across multiple domains, such as
118   finance, health, weather, among others. In most cases, training outcomes with synthesized data
119   outperformed those using real data.
120
      3. We enable the learning of a feature decomposer that only relies on synthetic data, enabling the
121
      decomposing of rhythmic, noise, and trend components of real time-series data.

      2   R ELATED WORKS
129
130
131
132   Although there are a variety of DL-based time-series data augmentation and synthesis methods Luo
133   et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2022); Yang & Hong (2022); Dooley et al. (2023a), it is almost impossible
134   to find a method that does not rely on training DL with real data and can be universally applied
135
      across domains, while simultaneously contributing downstream machine learning tasks Trirat et al.
      (2024). Expanding the probability distribution units for generated data using DL-based methods
136
      makes it challenging to ensure that the generated data covers unseen or diverse data distribution
137
      from other domains, even Meta-learning methods makes assumptions about tasks coming from the
138   same distribution Swan et al. (2022). The development and exploration of non-DL-based universal
139   time-series data synthesis methods provide a promising way to address the limitations of current
140   DL-based approaches and improve the generalizability of synthetic data across diverse domains.
141   FractalDB Kataoka et al. (2022) is known as a similar method in the field of imaging and π-GNN
142   Yin et al. (2023a) in the field of graph.
143
      In the field of time series, ForecastPFNDooley et al. (2023b) and ChronosAnsari et al. (2024) may
144   appear similar to our work. However, these two approaches focus on implementing a zero-to-many
145   training method using existing, straightforward data synthesis techniques paired with their designed
146   time series prediction models. In contrast, our work concentrates on developing an innovative data
147   synthesis method that benefits various model architectures and tasks. But, of course, to meet the
148   requirements for comparison with related work, we have also detailed in Appendix subsection A.8
149   the fundamental differences in the data synthesis methods used by these two studies and model
150   prediction performance under the same datasets and experimental settings.
151   Notably, our method enables models trained without real data to outperform those trained on real
152   data in time-series field across all tested datasets, as demonstrated in subsection 4.1, even in the
153   absence of real data or any real data information. Currently, data synthesis methods that claim to
154   achieve zero to many require specific information from the dataset. For example, ForecastPFN needs
155   the temporal periodicity (such as year, month, day) of the dataset, whereas Chronos’s data synthesis
156   method, which relies 90% on TsMix, requires segments of real data for concatenation and synthesis.
157   Furthermore, our work innovatively enables unconditional feature decomposition(without limita-
158   tions on temporal periodicity or other additional information) based on DL for trends, noise, and
159   rhythms features using our synthesized data as train data. Classic methods like STL decomposition
160   require accurate timepoint index information from the dataset and need extra predefined parame-
161   ters for seasonality and trend to function effectively. The feature decomposition is demonstrated in
      subsection 4.3.

      3.1.1    G ENERATING M ULTI - SOURCE R HYTHMIC DATA
172
173   To synthesize rhythmic data that accurately reflects the diversity and complexity of real-world time
174   series, we follow the Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem to ensure that all sine waves’ frequencies
175   are uniformly sampled during the synthesis of rhythms. Our design involves creating rhythmic data
176   comprising both simple and complex waveforms, which are constructed from a random number of
177   sine waves with uniformly random amplitudes, frequencies, and phases.
178   Motivation and Theory: 1)Frequency Distribution: Each sine wave represents a distinct frequency
179   component that simulates a specific rhythm in real-world data. By adhering to the Nyquist-Shannon
180   Sampling Theorem and using uniform sampling for the frequencies, we ensure that each discrete
181   sample has an equal probability of representing the entire spectrum from the minimum to the max-
182   imum frequency. This approach avoids potential biases introduced by concentrating frequencies
183   around certain values, leading to more realistic synthetic data. 2)Amplitude Normalization: For
184   each sine wave, we assign a random initial weight sampled uniformly from the range [0, 1]. These
185
      weights are then normalized, ensuring that the resulting amplitudes are scaled between 0 and 1. This
      step guarantees that all sine waves, regardless of their frequencies, have an equal chance of becom-
186
      ing the dominant rhythm feature in the final composite signal. 3)Phase Parameter: Although often
187
      neglected in machine learning applications, phase variations significantly impact the shape of the
188   composite signal even when the frequencies and amplitudes remain constant. Uniformly sampling
189   the phase ensures that the phase distribution does not introduce additional bias into the synthetic
190   data, allowing for a wide range of possible signal shapes. The phase values typically range between
191   0 and 2π , aligning with the conventional understanding of the sine function’s periodicity.
192
      Frequency Selection: As shown in Equation 1, 1)Upper Limit Frequency (fmax ): The upper limit
193
      frequency is set to half the sampling frequency ( f2s ), where the sampling frequency (fs ) is defined
194
      as the inverse of the time interval between adjacent discrete time points. This ensures that all sine
195   waves are sampled without aliasing, adhering to the Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem. 2)Lower
196   Limit Frequency (fmin ): The lower limit frequency is approximately the inverse of the total number
197   of discrete time points (N) in the sampling window. This ensures that the lowest frequency sine
198   wave can complete at least one full cycle within the sampling window.

281   is greater than the range captured by the sample window. Additionally, we introduce a random mul-

      tiplier greater than 1 to enhance the diversity of the generated data. This adjustment ensures that the

523   5     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this study, we have introduced a unique approach, marking the first to simultaneously fulfill the
526   requirements of a universal time-series data synthesis method that does not rely on real data or DL,
527   and a universal time-series data feature decomposition and extraction method that does not require
528
      fine-tuning on real data. Most notably, our method empowers models trained in the absence of real
      data information to outperform those trained on real data across almost all tested datasets. This
529
      achievement opens up a new path for future time-series data analysis and modeling, as well as a new
530
      solution for time-series unsupervised or self-supervised learning.

1066   A.11    CURRENT H ANDLING OF EXTREME EVENT DATA
1067
1068   We have observed extreme event data in the weather and energy datasets used in our study, to sim-
1069   ulate this, we designed our synthetic data generation method to include long-tailed distributions
1070   among the types of noise distributions to simulate extreme events. Long-tailed distributions are
1071   closely associated with extreme events, as they inherently model the occurrence of rare but signifi-
1072   cant occurrences with heavier tails compared to more common distributions. All noise distributions
1073   is detailed in subsubsection 3.1.2.
1074   The distributions that typically exhibit heavy-tailed (or long-tailed) behavior are as follows:
1075
1076
       1) Pareto Distribution: By definition, this is a heavy-tailed distribution.
1077   And potentially Long-tailed Distributions (The sampling function is capable of exhibiting long-
1078   tail characteristics as its input parameters vary. All the sampling parameters are initially obtained
1079   through uniform randomization, thus these distributions will exhibit long-tail noise samples with a
       certain probability):


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       2) Student’s t-distribution: This distribution exhibits heavy-tail characteristics when the degrees of
1081   freedom are low.
1082
1083   3) Generalized Gamma Distribution: When Power p < 1, the distribution exhibits heavy-tail charac-
1084
       teristics, and the tails become heavier as p approaches 0. When Concentration α < 1, the distribution
       may also have heavier tails.
1085
1086   4) Gamma Distribution: When the shape parameter k is small (close to 0), the distribution exhibits
1087   heavy-tail characteristics.
1088   Based on the above design, both our synthetic data and feature decomposer will include a certain pro-
1089   portion of long-tailed distributions in the noise component (with a simple estimation of occurrence
1090   probability > 1/15 and < 4/15) to simulate extreme events. The trend component will also have
1091   the same probability of exhibiting a long-tailed distribution during generation, but due to stronger
1092   smoothing, its feature in the time domain will not be as obvious. The reason behind this design
1093   is that extreme events should be defined as parts outside the rhythmic components; otherwise, if
1094   extreme events appear in the form of rhythmic data, our rhythmic generation with uniform sampling
1095   would be sufficient to cover normalized rhythmic extreme events.
