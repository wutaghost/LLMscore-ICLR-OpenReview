011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The study of proteins is crucial in various scientific disciplines, but understanding
014            their intricate multi-level relationships remains challenging. Inspired by the se-
015
               quence and structure understanding of Large Protein Models (LPMs), we introduce
               a new Mamba-based ProteinAdapter, to efficiently transfer the broad knowledge
016
               encapsulated in multiple LPMs, e.g., ESM-1b and ESM-IF, to task-specific insights.
017
               ProteinAdapter could largely save labor-intensive analysis on the 3D position and
018            the amino acid order. Specifically, (1) with a modest number of additional pa-
019            rameters, ProteinAdapter facilitates multi-level protein representation learning by
020            integrating both sequence and geometric structure embeddings from LPMs; (2)
021            based on the learned embedding, we further scale up the proposed ProteinAdapter
022            to various tasks with a unified Multi-Scale Predictor, which optimally harnesses
023            the learned embeddings through task-specific attention. Albeit simple, the pro-
024            posed method is scalable to multiple downstream tasks without bells and whistles.
025            Extensive experiments on over 20 tasks show that ProteinAdapter outperforms
026
               state-of-the-art methods under both single-task and multi-task scenarios.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Proteins serve as vital constituents and functional units for life, underscoring the significance of
031   protein research for life sciences. As understanding their intricate structure-function relationships
032   remains costly and time-consuming, there is an urgent need to develop a discriminative protein
033   representation for enhancing various computational biological analyses. Recently, Large Protein
034   Models (LPMs) have been verified as superior representation learners from different structure levels,
035   containing 1D Protein Language Models (PLMs) (Rives et al., 2021; Meier et al., 2021; Lin et al.,
036   2022) and 3D Protein Structure Models (PSMs) (Hsu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b;a). These
037   works motivate us to ride on the coattails of LPMs, seize the windfalls of multi-level proteomic
038   knowledge, and explore multi-level protein embeddings for efficient protein representation learning.
039
      However, considering the complex characteristics of proteins, there remain two key challenges: (1)
      multi-level complementarity, and (2) multi-scale integration. On the one hand, for various protein
040
      tasks, different levels of structure, such as amino acid sequences (primary structure) and geometric
041
      coordinates (tertiary structure) exhibit complementarity. Namely, for the protein-protein interaction
042   prediction task, 1D sequence information can be used to predict potential protein partners, while 3D
043   coordinate information can further help clarify the specific details of these interactions. On the other
044   hand, a protein can encompass multiple smaller substructures that are meaningful at different scales.
045   Namely, for the protein function classification task, the sequence lengths of different functional
046   regions or domains exhibit variability, ranging from a few amino acid residues to hundreds.
047
      In an attempt to address both limitations, we propose a new Mamba-based multi-level adapter,
048   called ProteinAdapter, to take advantage of existing pre-trained large models for efficient protein
049   representation learning. Our ProteinAdapter explicitly captures the interrelations and complementarity
050   among multi-level protein representations. Specifically, (1) to facilitate multi-level interrelations,
051   the proposed ProteinAdapter directly takes the intermediate feature from pretrained ESM-1b (Rives
052   et al., 2021) and ESM-IF1 (Hsu et al., 2022) as inputs, and outputs a multi-level mixed representation
053   embedding containing knowledge from both 1D and 3D structure levels. ProteinAdapter consists of
      a pre-alignment module and stacked Mamba Fusion Block (MFBlock), in which the mamba-based

091   2      R ELATED WORK
092
093   Protein Representation Learning. Proteins exhibit multi-level structures. Existing protein represen-
094   tation methods mainly focus on the 1D primary and the 3D tertiary structures understanding. For
095   the primary structure, regarding protein sequences as the language of life, many Protein Language
096   Models (PLMs) (Bepler & Berger, 2019; Strodthoff et al., 2020; Vig et al., 2020; Rives et al., 2021;
097   Amidi et al., 2018; Elnaggar et al., 2023) have been proposed for sequence-based protein representa-
098   tion learning with large-scale protein sequence corpora. For the tertiary structure, several Protein
099
      Structure Models (PSMs) (Hsu et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2022; 2023; Hermosilla et al., 2020; Zhang
      et al., 2023b) propose to extract features directly from the geometric information of amino acids or
100
      atoms. Since each structure of the protein has its own merit and driving forces in describing specific
101
      characteristics, several works (Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2022; 2023) have
102   been proposed to explicitly model the complementary information between different levels. However,
103   due to the computation limits, these methods usually process adjacent amino acids within limited
104   neighboring graph nodes (Zhang et al., 2023b; Sun et al., 2022) or small convolution kernels (Fan
105   et al., 2022; 2023) with stacked downsampling to expand the receptive field for global perception.
106   The former local propagation pattern with a fixed small region limits the perception field for protein
107   functional regions. The latter downsampling operations with fixed length also break the relation
      between neighbor functional regions in proteins. Moreover, these methods still require pretraining


                                                                      2

108
      from scratch on large protein datasets, followed by fine-tuning for deployment to each subtask. In this
109   work, we directly use the power of previous well-trained Large Protein Models (LPMs) to acquire
110   discriminative protein embeddings. Different from these methods, our ProteinAdapter leverages the
111   trend of large models by directly utilizing two single-level models focused on different structures (i.e.,
112   ESM-1b (Rives et al., 2021) for 1D sequence and ESM-IF1 (Hsu et al., 2022) for 3D coordinates),
113   and achieves multi-level protein representations through an efficient Mamba-based adapter.
114
      State Space Model (SSM). State Space Models (SSM) (Gu et al., 2021a;b; Smith et al., 2022),
115   originating from classical control theory, have become practical components for constructing deep
116   networks due to their cutting-edge performance in analyzing long sequential data. Structured State
117   Space Sequence Models (S4) (Gu et al., 2021a) introduces the concept of normal plus low-rank,
118   thereby effectively reducing the computational complexity associated with the SSM. Subsequently,
119   S5 (Smith et al., 2022) and H3 (Fu et al., 2022) further introduce a parallel scan on a diagonalized
120   linear SSM, narrowing the performance gap between the SSM and Transformer. Mamba (Gu &
121   Dao, 2023) further proposes a selection mechanism for dynamically extracting features from input
122   data, which outperforms Transformer on various 1D datasets while requiring significantly fewer
123
      computational resources. However, the methods discussed above primarily focus on Mamba’s
      application and directionality, leaving the potential of the SSM in protein representation largely
124
      unexplored. Recently, ProteinMamba (Xu et al., 2024) introduces a Mamba-based two-stage model
125
      for protein representation, yet the performance is severely limited due to only considering the
126   sequence information, and it still requires time-consuming pretraining on large-scale datasets. In
127   contrast, our ProteinAdapter is able to leverage both primary and tertiary protein information and can
128   be efficiently deployed to various downstream tasks with a minimal number of parameters.
129
      Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). To utilize the rich evolutionary and biological patterns
130
      from these pretrained LPMs, ESM-GearNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) makes the first attempt by replacing
131
      the original graph node with the well-learned 1D sequence embedding produced by ESM-1b (Rives
132   et al., 2021). However, this method mainly focuses on the pretraining of a complex local structure
133   encoder from scratch. To fully unleash and efficiently utilize the power of the off-the-shelf large
134   models, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods (Li & Liang, 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Liu
135   et al., 2022; Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021) have prevailed recently in both Natural Language
136   Processing (NLP) and Computer Vision (CV) communities. Existing PEFT methods can be roughly
137   divided into three parts: Prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022),
138   Adapter-tuning (Houlsby et al., 2019), and LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). In this paper, we resort to the
139   adapter by adding only a few trainable parameters for different downstream protein tasks, while the
140
      parameters of the original LPMs are fixed. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first adapter-based
      work exploring the parameter-efficient fine-tuning of pre-trained Large Protein Models (LPMs).

143   3     M ETHODS
144
145   Aiming at efficient utilization of off-the-shelf pre-trained models for protein representation learning,
146   there are two key properties that differentiate protein language from natural language: multi-level and
147   multi-scale. First, a protein can target various structural levels to carry out its functions. Each level
148   possesses distinct advantages and underlying factors in elucidating particular attributes. Consequently,
149   it is necessary to consider both 1D and 3D structures for comprehensive protein representation.
150
      Second, due to their distinct biological functions, the scale of functional regions in different proteins
      typically varies. Likewise, due to genetic differences, the scale of the same functional regions is often
151
      inconsistent across different species (Gabaldón, 2008). Thus the predictor should possess multi-scale
152
      perceptual capabilities to fully leverage the protein representation for various downstream tasks.
153   Considering these two properties, as shown in Figure 1, our method consists of three key components:
154   pre-trained protein models, a multi-level ProteinAdapter, and a multi-scale predictor.
155

188   3.2      I NTEGRATING SEQUENCE - STRUCTURE FEATURES WITH P ROTEINA DAPTER
189
190   Despite the superiority of existing PLMs and PSMs in sequence and structure understanding, they are
191
      individually trained on single-level protein data. This implies the need to effectively integrate their
      embeddings while minimizing the reduction in their respective unimodal representation capabilities.
192
      To preserve such unimodal information when injecting the cross-level information from each other,
193
      in this subsection, we propose to integrate sequence-structure features with a new ProteinAdapter.
194
195   The current Mamba architecture cannot effectively handle multiple-feature integration as it lacks
196   mechanisms similar to cross-attention. As an improvement, we designed a Mamba-based fusion
197
      mechanism in our ProteinAdapter for multi-level protein representation. As shown in Figure 2,
      ProteinAdapter consists of a pre-alignment module followed by several MFBlocks.
198
199   Pre-alignment. We first deploy several respective Mamba blocks (Gu & Dao, 2023) on each single-
200   level embedding as feature preprocessing. Subsequently, they undergo a fuse operation by simple
201   addition to obtain an initial fusion feature Ef usion . To reduce channel redundancy and enhance the
202   expressive power of different channels, we further integrate Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) (Wang
203
      et al., 2020) to the fused embedding. Overall, the output after pre-alignment can be expressed as:
                                      ′
204                                 Ef usion = ECA(Conv(Mamba(Estr ) + Mamba(Eseq ))).                                                          (1)
205
206
      More details on Mamba are given in the Appendix A.
207   Mamba Fusion Block (MFBlock). The MFBlock consists of three branches based on the Mamba
208   architecture, which leverages level-specific protein features to guide multi-level embedding fusion,
209   aiming to capture local detail characteristics from various protein levels. The input to the MFBlock
                                     ′
210   is the initial fused feature Ef usion derived from the pre-alignment module. Additionally, two
                                                                           ′        ′
211   more branches are incorporated, each receiving protein features Eseq and Estr from distinct levels.
212   Each of the three branches goes through layer normalization, 1D convolution, SiLU activation, and
213   parameter discretization, followed by passing through the SSM to generate an output. These outputs
214   are modulated by a gating factor and added to the output from the previous block, producing the
215   final multi-level protein embedding Ef usion guided by level-specific features. More details on the
      MFBlock are given in Algorithm 1.

244   3.3   E NHANCING PYRAMIDAL REPRESENTATIONS WITH MULTI - SCALE PREDICTOR
245
246   After thoroughly fusing the sequence and geometric embeddings through ProteinAdapter, the obtained
247   multi-level embedding contains rich evolutionary and biological patterns underlying both levels of
248   protein structures. Now we move a further step to fully utilize the multi-level embedding for
249   downstream tasks. As shown in Figure 3, we design a multi-scale predictor to efficiently extract and
250   utilize protein features in a hierarchical manner.
251
      Patch Embedding. During each stage, an overlapping patch embedding block (Wang et al., 2021a;
252   2022) with downsampling rate 2 is performed to halve the protein resolution and build a pyramid
253   architecture. Specifically, given the embedding Ei−1 ∈ RNi−1 ×Ci−1 from the previous stage i − 1 as
254   input, we feed it to a 1D convolution to acquire the downsampled embedding Ei ∈ RNi ×Ci , where
255   Ni is computed as N/2i . In our implementation, we set channel numbers C1 , C2 , C3 , C4 as 512,
256   512, 1024, 1024. The 1D convolution is set as the stride 2, kernel size 3, zero padding size of 1, and
257   the kernel number of Ci . This downsampling patch embedding block flexibly adjusts the feature
258   scale in each stage, making it possible to construct a feature pyramid for the Transformer. In this way,
259   our method can handle longer input protein embedding with limited resources.
260   Transformer Encoder. Following existing efficient transformer methods (Wang et al., 2022; Han
261   et al., 2023), we adopt zero padding position encoding into the transformer encoder. In our imple-
262   mentation, we further use a linear attention mechanism (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) that uses average
263   pooling to reduce the sequence length n with a pooling size s = 4 before the attention operation, as
264   shown in Figure 3. We also employ a focused function (Han et al., 2023)on the attention map, to
265   pay more attention to those effective functional regions. In our implementation, we use dynamic
266
      batching (Rives et al., 2021) to handle variable-length sequences for higher computational efficiency.
267   Multi-Scale Prediction. Finally, the features from various scales are linearly mapped to the dimen-
268   sions corresponding to downstream tasks. Then, through a set of learnable parameters, the weights of
269   different scales are dynamically adjusted, thereby achieving adaptability to different tasks and the
      possibility of multi-task learning.

272   4.1   E VALUATION TASKS AND DATASETS
273
274   To validate the efficacy of ProteinAdapter, we conduct tests on over 20 tasks. Since our model
275   explicitly leverages multi-level protein data, we separately assess the model’s ability to protein
276   sequences and structures understanding on the PEER (Xu et al., 2022), ATOM3D (Townshend et al.,
277   2020), and several other benchmarks. Since the PEER benchmark only contains 1D sequences,
278   we further validate the performance on four sequence-structure paired datasets. We also conduct
279
      experiments with predicted protein structures (see Section 4.2). Subsequently, we evaluate the
      performance in a multi-task setting on the PEER benchmark, verifying its generalization capability.
280
      We then compare the efficiency superiority of ProteinAdapter against the current LPMs. Finally,
281
      we conduct a series of ablation studies to confirm the effectiveness of our multi-level adapter and
282   multi-scale predictor. More details on the benchmarks and each task can be found in our Appendix C.

284   4.2   T RAINING SETUP
285
286   For the sequence-based PEER benchmark, due to the lack of corresponding 3D coordinate information,
287   we simply replaced the structural embedding with another layer of sequence embedding (i.e., layer 32
288   and 33 of ESM-1b). Another alternative approach is to use a pre-trained structure prediction model
289   ESMFold (Lin et al., 2022) to generate pseudo structures. Due to computational limitations, we only
290   conducted multi-level performance tests on the Stability Prediction and Fold Classification tasks
291
      for the PEER benchmark. The Stability Prediction dataset has the shortest average sequence length
      (less than 70). For the Fold Classification task, we can collect the corresponding structures from
292
      RCSB-PDB (Berman et al., 2000b). Moreover, for the PPI task that takes paired proteins as inputs,
293
      we take the multi-level embeddings obtained for each of the two proteins, and then feed them into an
294   MLP predictor defined based on the concatenation of the embeddings of the two proteins. For the PLI
295   task which has protein-ligand inputs, we follow previous practices (Hu et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2019),
296   and involve an additional Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) (Xu et al., 2018) with 4 layers and
297   256 hidden dimensions as the ligand graph encoder. As for the ATOM3D benchmark (Townshend
298   et al., 2020), we can directly extract their protein sequences from the mdb or pdb files. Our method
299   is implemented based on PyTorch 1.13.1 with CUDA 11.7. All experiments are conducted on one
300   NVIDIA Tesla A100 (80GB). As for other compared methods, we deploy the same default training
301   settings in the benchmark (Xu et al., 2022; Townshend et al., 2020).

303   4.3   S INGLE - TASK vs. M ULTI - TASK
304
      Single-Task Learning. Given a task t ∈ T from the pool T of benchmark tasks, a task-specific loss
305
      function Lt is defined to measure the correctness of model predictions on training samples against
306   ground truth labels. The objective of learning this single task is to optimize model parameters to
307   minimize the loss Lt on this task.
308
309
      Multi-Task Learning. We further delve deeper into multi-task learning to validate the generalization
      ability of ProteinAdapter. Similar to PEER(Xu et al., 2022), our training objective comprises a
310
      primary task and a supportive task. Following the principle that “protein structures determine
311
      their functions” (Hegyi & Gerstein, 1999), we employ three structure prediction tasks, i.e., contact
312   prediction, fold classification and secondary structure prediction, as the auxiliary task. In more detail,
313   when presented with a primary task tp characterized by loss Ltp and a supportive task ts characterized
314   by loss Lts , our multi-task model follows the framework of hard parameter sharing (Ruder, 2017).
315   Within this framework, we utilize a universal protein sequence encoder for all tasks, and a ligand
316   graph encoder for protein-ligand interaction prediction tasks. Throughout the multi-task learning
317   process, the network parameters are fine-tuned based on the combined loss of the primary and
318   supportive tasks: L = Ltp + λLts . Here, λ represents the balancing factor for the two objectives,
319   defaulting to 1.0 unless otherwise mentioned. The training iterations are consistent with single-task
320
      learning on the primary task, and we only evaluate one primary task to maintain consistency with the
      PEER benchmark.
321
322   Optimization Objective. Following PEER Benchmark (Xu et al., 2022), the fluorescence, stability,
323   β-lactamase activity, PPI affinity, PDBbind and BindingDB prediction tasks are trained with Mean
      Squared Error (MSE); the solubility, subcellular localization, binary localization, fold, secondary

      As shown in Table 6, it can be seen that both the multi-level adapter and multi-scale predictor are
462
      indispensable in our method. Without our mamba-based feature processing and integration, there
463   is a clear performance drop on variant a due to the absence of our ProteinAdapter. Replacing the
464   pre-alignment module with MLPs provides some mitigation (b), but its representation quality is not
465   as good as the features after initial integration (c). Note that unlike single-scale MLP (d) or previous
466   methods e.g., CDConv (Fan et al., 2022) that use pooling layers to reduce the number of nodes and
467   learn representations at different scales, our multi-scale predictor does not disrupt the continuity of
468   amino acids. In contrast, our method represents proteins at various scales and then combines these
469   representations to ensure a comprehensive understanding.
470   Efficiency. By integrating two pre-trained LPMs with our lightweight ProteinAdapter, our method
471   can achieve higher performance with fewer parameters compared to training a single-level large
472   model from scratch. As shown in Table 6, with the default setting of three MFBlocks in the adapter
473   and three downsampling times in the predictor, our model has 20× fewer parameters than the previous
474   LPMs (22.5M of ProteinAdapter vs 650M of ESM-1b).

476   5    C ONCLUSION
477
478   The proposed ProteinAdapter offers a scalable solution for parameter-efficient fine-tuning on pre-
479   trained Large Protein Models (LPMs). The multi-level adapter seamlessly merges protein embeddings,
480   eliminating the need for resource-intensive pretraining. The method demonstrates superior computing
481   efficiency through frozen models and a lightweight Mamba-based adapter. Extensive experiments
482   across over 20 tasks showcase its substantial performance gains over existing methods. In a multi-
483   task setting, ProteinAdapter consistently outperforms its counterparts, establishing its versatility
484   and generalization capabilities. We will consider combining more powerful protein representation
485
      structure prediction models in the future.

      State Space Models (SSM). State space models are typically regarded as linear time-invariant systems
813   that map input signals from x(t) ∈ RN to output responses y(t) ∈ RN through an intermediate
814   hidden state h(t) ∈ RN . These systems are mathematically described by linear ordinary differential
815   equations (ODEs):
816                                       h′ (t) = Ah(t) + Bx(t),                                       (2)
817
818

820   where N is the state dimension, A ∈ R             denotes the evolution parameter, B ∈ R       and
821   C ∈ RN ×1 are projection parameters. This equation suggests that the SSM exhibits global awareness,
822   as the current output is influenced by all preceding input data.
823
      Discretization. To integrate time-continuous SSM into deep learning frameworks, discretization
824   is essential. A method often used is zero-order hold (ZOH), allowing us to discretize the system
825   described above. The discretized equations can be written as:

832   and ∆ represents the discretization step size.
833   Selective Scan. In Mamba, the Selective Scan Mechanism is introduced to overcome the challenge
834   of parallelizing SSM operations due to input-dependent parameters, which prevent reformulating
835   them in a convolutional form. This mechanism combines kernel fusion, parallel scan, and recom-
836   putation to improve efficiency. Kernel fusion minimizes memory access overhead, parallel scan
837   speeds up sequence processing, and recomputation reduces memory usage by recalculating values as
838   needed. Together, these techniques enable Mamba to achieve faster computation with lower memory
