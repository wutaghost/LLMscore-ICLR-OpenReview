030                                               A BSTRACT
031
032            Video outpainting expands the spatial perspective of a video, enabling it to adapt
033            to various display devices with different aspect ratios. Current diffusion-based
034            approaches for video outpainting often suffer from quality issues such as blurred
035            details, local distortion, and temporal instability, significantly impacting the user
036            experience. The root cause is the insufficient temporal modeling in video outpaint-
037            ing, which inadequately represents the relationships between frames over time.
038
               To address this issue, a novel approach called HERO (Harnessing the tEmpoRal
               modeling for diffusion-based Outpainting) is proposed to effectively tackles these
039
               generated video quality problems. HERO employs two critical components to
040
               enhance temporal modeling: the Temporal Reference Module, which provides
041            reference features that extend beyond spatial dimensions; and the Interpolation-
042            based Motion Modelling Module, designed to stabilize generated frames. By
043            integrating these modules, these quality issues in video outpainting are effectively
044            addressed. Extensive experiments on multiple benchmarks demonstrate that HERO
045            outperforms existing methods qualitatively and quantitatively.

049   Video outpainting (Yu et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a) expands a video’s spatial
050   scope beyond its original perspective, enabling it to adapt to various screen ratios for diverse display
051   devices and occasions. Unlike image outpainting, which focuses on a single frame, video outpainting
052   must ensure both content consistency and spatial-temporal coherence to avoid jitter between adjacent
053   frames. Differing from video inpainting, which focuses on internal areas with rich context and has a
      small mask ratio, video outpainting often deals with larger mask areas at frame edges with limited

076   Research on video outpainting can be categorized into mask-based and diffusion-based approaches.
077   The mask-based methods (Yu et al., 2023) outpaint videos by predicting the masked content using a
078   BERT-like (Devlin et al., 2018) learning approach. However, these methods rely only on contextual
079   video tokens and are unable to generate high-definition videos. On the other hand, diffusion-based
080   methods leverage significant advancements in image (Ho et al., 2020a; Rombach et al., 2022; Zhang
081
      et al., 2023) and video (Guo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023) generation achieved by
      diffusion models. These methods frame video outpainting as a video-to-video generation task (Fan
082
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). These approaches have achieved state-of-the-art (SoTA) results.
083
084   Despite their advancements, SoTA diffusion-based video outpainting approaches (Fan et al., 2023;
085   Wang et al., 2024a) still face several problems. These include blurred details, local distortion as
086   shown in Fig. 2b, and temporal instability, which are evident in the interesting videos provided in
087
      the supplementary materials. A manual analysis of the output videos generated by M3DDM (Fan
      et al., 2023) on datasets DAVIS (Caelles et al., 2019) and YouTube-VOS (Xu et al., 2018) revealed
088
      that 38% of the videos are blurry, as detailed in Sec. C. These issues significantly reduce the
089
      audience’s experience and impact the information delivery. The primary cause behind these problems
090   is insufficient temporal modeling in video outpainting. This refers to an inadequate representation
091   of temporal relationships between frames, leading to frame distortion or instability. The SoTA
092   methods (Fan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a) currently use reference conditions such as VAE (Kingma
093   & Welling, 2013) for textual features and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) for semantic features. However,
094   these features are all spatial dimensions and do not include any temporal reference features. Fig. 2c
095   shows that VAE and CLIP features only occupy the red and pink regions, leaving the cyan and blue
096   regions empty. The widely used VAE and CLIP references are paradigms for static image synthesis
097   and editing, which are insufficient for video. Moreover, current video generation methods (Guo et al.,
098
      2024; Hu et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a) depend on a vanilla motion modeling
      module (Guo et al., 2024), which performs global attention at the feature pixel level across all frames.
099
      This approach overlooks the relationships between adjacent frames and leads to temporal instability
100
      in generated videos. Such oversight further underscores the inadequacies in temporal modeling.
101
102   The challenges in solving the above problems are twofold. Firstly, there are limited established meth-
103   ods available for incorporating temporal references in video generation. Consequently, researchers
104
      must explore innovative approaches to better capture effective temporal references. Secondly, any
      improvements to the motion modeling module in the diffusion network should be minimized to
105
      maximally preserve the internal knowledge of the pre-trained diffusion network.
106
107   To overcome these significant challenges, 3D features that capture video-level information and
      optical flow features that convey motion dynamics are taken into consideration to enhance temporal

      modeling. Features from these two perspectives encapsulate information beyond spatial dimensions
109   and compensate for the limitations of the CLIP and VAE models. Additionally, the interpolation
110   technique can be integrated into the vanilla motion modeling module to enhance the stability of
111   the generated frames with few learnable parameters. Based on these ideas, a pioneering approach
112   effectively Harnessing the tEmpoRal modeling for diffusion-based Outpainting (HERO) is proposed
113   to handle the generated video quality problems. In HERO, a Temporal Reference Module is introduced
114   in addition to the spatial-based reference modules (VAE and CLIP), providing comprehensive
115   reference features. Subsequently, an Interpolation-based Motion Modeling Module is designed with
116   a single learnable scalar to enhance the stability of generated videos.
117   The key contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows: (1) To the best of our knowledge,
118   this is the first paper to comprehensively address the insufficient temporal modeling problem in
119   diffusion-based video outpainting methods. Simultaneously, this paper demonstrates its causes, great
120   impact, and challenges in addressing them. (2) The proposed HERO can alleviate the insufficient
121   temporal modelling problem through the Temporal Reference Module, which provides comprehensive
122   temporal references, and the Interpolation-based Motion Modeling Module, which stabilizes the
123
      generated frames. (3) HERO is validated through extensive quantitative and qualitative experiments,
      achieving state-of-the-art performance on multiple video outpainting benchmarks.

127   2.1   P RELIMINARIES
128
129   Stable Diffusion. Our approach extends Stable Diffusion (SD) which is derived from the latent
130   diffusion model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022). SD consists of a VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013)
131   and a UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) augmented by the cross-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al.,
132   2017). VAE consists of an encoder E and a decoder D. The encoder E of VAE first transforms an
133
      image from pixel space into a low-dimensional latent space to reduce the computational complexity
      for UNet: z = E(x). During the training process of SD, the image latent z0 is diffused in T time
134
      steps to produce noise latent zT . Simultaneously, a denoising UNet is trained to predict the applied
135
      noise. The optimization process is defined as follow function:
136
137                               LLDM = Ezt ,ϵ∼N (0,1),t,c [∥ϵ − ϵθ (zt , c, t)∥22 ],                      (1)
138
139   where ϵ is the noise added to z0 , c denotes the conditional information and t is the time step, ϵθ
140   represents the denoising UNet. In each iteration, the denoising UNet predicts noise on the latent
141   feature for each timestep t. During inference, z′T is sampled from a random Gaussian distribution at
142   timestep T and progressively denoised to z′0 using a guided sampling process (e.g., DDPM (Ho et al.,
143
      2020b), DDIM (Song et al., 2021)). Finally, decoder D reconstructs image x′ = D(z′0 ).
144   Video Outpainting. Let v ∈ Rt×h×w×3 denotes an input video, where t is the number of frames in
145   the video, h and w are the height and width of the video and 3 stands for the channel number. Video
146   outpainting extends the initial height and width of a video to a specified height and width, resulting in
                              ′   ′
147   a new video v ′ ∈ Rt×h ×w ×3 (h′ > h, w′ > w). Video outpainting needs to maintain both content
148   consistency and spatial-temporal coherence. In the video, the initial perspective is referred to as
149   the known region, while the extended area is termed as the unknown region.

183   2.3     T EMPORAL R EFERENCE M ODULE

      2.3.1        3D-R EF N ET
186   3D-RefNet is designed to extract video-level features, inspired by the ReferenceNet (Hu et al., 2023)
187   which was originally designed for image reference. The structure of 3D-RefNet is almost identical
188   to that of the 3D-UNet, except that the input is 4-channel for 3D-RefNet while for 3D-UNet it is
189   9-channel. The input to 3D-RefNet is the latent features extracted by the VAE model, without any
190   other information concatenated. The weights of 3D-RefNet come from Stable Diffusion (Rombach
191   et al., 2022) and the weights of the motion modeling module come from AnimateDiff (Guo et al.,
192   2024). During the forward phase, the feature map v1 ∈ Rt×h×w×c from 3D-RefNet and the feature
193
      map v2 ∈ Rt×h×w×c from 3D-UNet are concatenated along w dimension. Then a self-attention
      is performed on this concatenated feature map and the first half of the feature map serves as the
194
      output as in (Hu et al., 2023). During the training phase, the weights of 3D-RefNet and 3D-UNet
195
      are updated independently of each other. It should be noted that 3D-RefNet and ReferenceNet for
196   image reference differ in the following three respects: in terms of network structure, 3D-RefNet
197   adds a motion modeling module to capture video-level information; in terms of input, the input
198   3D-RefNet is multiple frames rather than a single reference image; and in terms of internal operations,
199   3D-RefNet does not require the tiling and copying of feature maps to align feature dimensions.
200
201
      2.3.2 O PTICAL F LOW E NCODER (OFE)
202   Optical flow is widely used in video completion tasks (Zhou et al., 2023; Dehan et al., 2022). It reflects
203   pixel-level motion features, which provides an alternative perspective for temporal information
204
      compared with 3D-RefNet. This kind of information can be beneficial for video generation and thus
      needs a dedicated encoder. On the other hand, ControlNet controls the generation of images with a
205
      condition image in a fine-grained manner. The main network structure of ControlNet is identical to
206
      the encoder in 3D-UNet, which is connected to 3D-UNet through the zero-initialized convolutional
207   layer. The popular ControlNets support inputs include edge maps, pose key points, and segmentation
208   maps, etc., but they do not support the optical flow. Therefore, a ControlNet is trained from scratch to
209   serve as the encoder for optical flow. Specifically, the dense optical flow of the input video is first
210   estimated and the unknown regions will be filled with zero. It is then concatenated with a binary
211   mask indicating the known and unknown regions along with channel dimensions to form the input.
212

251
252   Spatial references are indispensable in video outpainting to complete individual frames. The spatial
253   features are mainly drawn from the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013)
254   as shown in Fig. 3 (II), as established in most diffusion-based methods (Fan et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
255   2024a; Ye et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Shi et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023).
256

257   where the image encoder is a ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). The OpenAI CLIP weights are trained
258   on a wide variety of image and text pairs which are abundantly available on the internet. Under the
259   supervision of the text, the image feature from OpenAI CLIP contains the semantics of each frame

      4     R ELATED W ORK
471   4.1     D IFFUSION BASED V IDEO G ENERATION
472
473   The structural principles of text-to-image models have had a significant influence on the development
474   of text-to-video models following the successes of diffusion models in text-to-image tasks. Numerous
475
      studies (Esser et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2023; Khachatryan et al., 2023; Ma et al.,
      2024; Qi et al., 2023; Singer et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023)
476
      have been conducted to augment text-to-image models with inter-frame attention mechanisms, aiming
477
      to facilitate the generation of videos. Some works achieve video generation by inserting temporal
478   modules into text-to-image models. Video LDM (Blattmann et al., 2023) proposes multi-stage
479   training to retain the prior knowledge of text-to-image models, training videos only on the temporal
480   modules. AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2024) uses a text-to-image model as the base generator, with an
481   added motion modeling module to learn motion information. However, the motion modeling module
482   is still a vanilla version and faces challenges in achieving stable video generation.
483
484
      4.2     V IDEO O UTPAINTING
485   Currently, video outpainting technology is still not mature. Dehan (Dehan et al., 2022) proposed
      a background estimation technique that combines video object segmentation and video inpainting


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      methods, while temporal conherence is achieved through the integration of optical flow. However,
487   in scenarios featuring complex camera movements and the exit of foreground objects from the
488   frame, their performance frequently suffers. MAGVIT (Yu et al., 2023) introduced a versatile mask-
489   based model designed for video generation that is also applicable to video outpainting tasks. It
490   employs a 3D-VectorQuantized (3DVQ) tokenizer for video quantization and utilizes a transformer
491   for conditional masked token modeling across multiple tasks. MAGVIT represents an inspirational
492   effort, yet there is considerable room for improvement in its effectiveness. M3DDM (Fan et al., 2023)
493   has designed an architecture based on the diffusion model, which is trained on massive datasets and
494   achieves quite impressive results. However, there is a considerable proportion of bad cases with
495
      this approach, as shown in Figure 2b. The primary cause behind these issues is the insufficiency of
      reference information.
496

      5   S OCIETAL I MPACTS , L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
498
499   Societal Impacts. The proposed HERO is inherently harmless like many other AI technologies.
500   Nevertheless, there exists the potential for its misuse, such as incorporation into applications with
501   copyright issues, which could have negative effects on society. Hence, we advocate for the thoughtful
502   and ethical application of HERO.
503   Limitations. (1) HERO has not specifically studied the long videos outpainting. When generating
504   long videos, the coarse-to-fine generation strategy from M3DDM (Fan et al., 2023) or the recursive
505   generation strategy from Hallo (Xu et al., 2024) can be employed. (2) The 3D-RefNet and the optical
506   flow will take up more GPU memory. However, the two modules do not significantly increase the
507   inference time as they do not require recurrent denoising and only need a single forward pass. (3)
508   The method to integrate optical flow with OFE still has significant potential for enhancement.
509   Conclusion. This paper proposed a pioneering approach effectively harnessing the temporal modeling
510   for diffusion-based outpainting (HERO) to handle the generated video quality problem. The Temporal
511   Reference Module provides videl-level and motion features to assist the video generation, effectively
512   addressing the limitations of VAE and CLIP. The Interpolation-based Motion Modeling Module
513   utilizes adjacent frame relations to stabilize the frames with minimal modification to the network
514   structure. Qualitative and quantitative experiments validate the superiority and robustness of HERO.

780   A    T HE D ETAILS OF BASELINES
781
782   The baseline includes: 1) Dehan (Dehan et al., 2022) develops a framework dedicated to the task
783   of video outpainting. Their strategy involve differentiating between foreground and background
784   elements, then estimating flow and background separately. These components are then integrated to
785   produce a comprehensive output. 2) MAGVIT deployed mask modeling technology for the training of
786   a transformer aimed at generating videos within the 3D Vector-Quantized (Esser et al., 2021) (van den
787   Oord et al., 2017) space. 3) SDM model (Fan et al., 2023) utilizes the initial and terminal frames of
788   a sequence as conditional inputs, which are integrated with contextual information at the inception
789
      layer of the network. This model has undergone training on video datasets, specifically WebVid (Bain
      et al., 2021) and an e-commerce dataset (Fan et al., 2023). 4) M3DDM (Fan et al., 2023) represents a
790
      pioneering approach to video outpainting, incorporating a masking strategy that enables the utilization
791
      of the original source video as masked conditions. Furthermore, it leverages global-frame features
792   within cross-attention mechanisms to facilitate the accomplishment of comprehensive and extended
793   information dissemination. The model underwent training utilizing two datasets containing a vast
794   array of video data, specifically WebVid and e-commerce (Fan et al., 2023), and was fine-tuned on the
795   corresponding datasets during evaluation. SDM can be considered a simplified version of M3DDM.
796   5) MOTIA (Wang et al., 2024a) employs spatial-aware insertion and noise travel to better harness
797   the prior knowledge of the diffusion model as well as the video patterns in source videos.
