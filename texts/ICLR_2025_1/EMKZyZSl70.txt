011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Unsupervised disentanglement of content and transformation is significantly im-
014            portant for analyzing shape focused scientific image datasets, given their efficacy
015
               in solving downstream image-based shape-analyses tasks. The existing relevant
               works address the problem by explicitly parameterizing the transformation latent
016
               codes in a generative model, significantly reducing their expressiveness. More-
017
               over, they are not applicable in cases where transformations can not be readily
018            parametrized. An alternative to such explicit approaches is contrastive meth-
019            ods with data augmentation, which implicitly disentangles transformations and
020            content. However, the existing contrastive strategies are insufficient to this end.
021            Therefore, we developed a novel contrastive method with generative modeling,
022            DualContrast, specifically for unsupervised disentanglement of content and trans-
023            formations in shape focused image datasets. DualContrast creates positive and
024            negative pairs for content and transformation from data and latent spaces. Our
025            extensive experiments showcase the efficacy of DualContrast over existing self-
026
               supervised and explicit parameterization approaches. With DualContrast, we dis-
               entangled protein composition and conformations in cellular 3D protein images,
027
               which was unattainable with existing disentanglement approaches.

      In many real-world image datasets, particularly in scientific imaging domains, the object shapes be-
046
      ing visualized may undergo multiple transformations (Skafte & Hauberg, 2019; Bepler et al., 2019).
047
      These shape focused image datasets can thus be regarded as samples generated from two indepen-
048   dent factors, one representing the semantic attribute termed content and the other representing the
049   transformations. Content is regarded as the form of an object being visualized in the image (Skafte
050   & Hauberg, 2019) that is unaltered after applying any nuisance transformation. On the other hand,
051   transformation dictates the specific state or realization of that form of the object. Taking proteins
052   inside the cells as an example, changing the transformation factor changes the conformation of the
053   protein where the protein identity (i.e. , protein composition) does not change (Fig. 1). On the other
      hand, changing the content factor is analogous to changing the proteins from one identity to another


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      through compositional change. Such a phenomenon holds for many shape focused image datasets.
055   Disentangling content and transformation factors of images can significantly facilitate several down-
056   stream shape analysis tasks, including shape clustering (Levy et al., 2022b), alignment (Uddin et al.,
057   2022; Bepler et al., 2019), zero-shot transfer (Zhou et al., 2020), bias removal (Ngweta et al., 2023;
058   Lee et al., 2021), cross-domain retrieval (Kahana & Hoshen, 2022; Piran et al., 2024), etc. (Liu
059   et al., 2022).
060
      A number of works have been done to directly or indirectly solve the content-transformation disen-
061   tangling problem (Jha et al., 2018; Kahana & Hoshen, 2022; Cosmo et al., 2020; Skafte & Hauberg,
062   2019; Uddin et al., 2022; Bepler et al., 2019), whereas many of them use annotated factors for train-
063   ing. However, such annotation is hard to obtain, and thus, unsupervised disentanglement methods
064   are desired. Only a few works (Skafte & Hauberg, 2019; Bepler et al., 2019; Uddin et al., 2022)
065   have addressed the problem in a completely unsupervised setting. Among them, (Skafte & Hauberg,
066   2019) performed explicit parameterization of transformation codes as diffeomorphic transformations
067   and achieved noteworthy disentanglement of content and transformation in several image datasets.
068   (Bepler et al., 2019) and (Uddin et al., 2022) focus on representing known transformation types as
069
      transformation codes. (Bepler et al., 2019) performed disentanglement of two-dimensional trans-
      lation and rotation, whereas (Uddin et al., 2022) demonstrated disentanglement of a broader set of
070
      transformations that can be explicitly parameterized.
071
072   Though these unsupervised explicit parameterization methods have demonstrated several successes,
073   they have several major limitations in general: (1) These method uses the transformation codes
074   to generate only a few parameterized transformations. In reality, many transformations do not
075
      have a well-defined parameterized form, e.g., protein conformation changes, viewpoint change
      (LineMod), etc. These explicit methods can not disentangle such transformation by design. (2)
076
      They use Spatial Transformer Networks (STN) (Jaderberg et al., 2015) for inferring the transforma-
077
      tion codes, which requires a continuous parameterization of the transformation to be disentangled.
078   The continuity of many transformations (e.g. , SO(3) rotation) in neural networks is often a concern
079   (Zhou et al., 2019). Moreover, (3) these methods infer spurious contents during disentanglement
080   in real scenarios where transformations not predefined are present in the dataset (Fig. 3). Due
081   to these limitations, an unsupervised content-transformation disentangling method without explicit
082   transformation representation is much desired.
083
      Consequently, in this work, we focused on the more generalized and unique setting of unsupervised
084   content-transformation disentanglement without any explicit parameterization of the transformation
085   code. Previously, a theoretical study by Von Kügelgen et al. (2021) showed that standard Contrastive
086   Learning (CL) methods, e.g., SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), can disentangle content from style, where
087   style can be regarded as the transformations used for data augmentation. However, these popular
088   CL methods (Chen et al., 2020) have rarely achieved any disentanglement in practice (Ngweta et al.,
089   2023), and their ability in disentangling transformations other than those used for augmentation is
090   not well-explored. Moreover, we found that SimCLR with geometric data augmentation can not
091   disentangle any transformation well in our shape focused image datasets of interest (Table 1).
092   To this end, we develop a novel generative contrastive learning method for implicit content-
093   transformation disentanglement, termed “DualContrast” (Fig. 2). Unlike existing contrastive strate-
094   gies, it creates “positive” and “negative” contrastive pairs w.r.t. both content and transformation la-
095   tent codes. We found that several shape transformations in image datasets lie closely in the normally
096   distributed latent space that is designed to capture in-plane rotation information in a contrastive man-
097   ner. Consequently, we create negative pairs w.r.t. transformation and positive pairs w.r.t. content by
098
      simply rotating the samples. Without prior knowledge, creating positive pairs w.r.t. the transforma-
      tion codes directly from the input samples is not possible. To this end, we generate samples from an
099
      identical distribution of the transformation representations in the embedding space and treat them as
100
      positive pairs for the transformation codes. Given our observation, this encourages the disentangle-
101   ment of several shape transformations other than rotation in our dataset. In addition, we randomly
102   permute the samples in the batch to create a negative pair for a batch of samples w.r.t. content.
103   Upon creating both “positive” and “negative” pairs w.r.t. both codes, we penalize similarity between
104   negative pairs and distance between positive pairs for each code. Finally, we incorporate these con-
105   trastive losses in a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) based architecture with VAE reconstruction loss
106   and train the model in an end-to-end manner.

152
153   Unsupervised Content-Transformation Disentanglement: There are a few (Skafte & Hauberg,
154   2019; Uddin et al., 2022; Bepler et al., 2019; Von Kügelgen et al., 2021) approaches that deal with
155   the unsupervised disentanglement of content and transformation. However, these methods perform
156   explicit parameterization of the transformation codes as affine or some specific parameterized trans-
157   formation. In contrast, our method does not impose any explicit parameterization on the transfor-
158
      mation latent codes and does not face the issues of the explicit methods (Section 1).
159   Contrastive Learning based Disentanglement: Contrastive learning is the primary building block
160   of our method. Contrastive Learning with data augmentation with existing approaches, e.g. , In-
161   foNCE loss-based SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), etc., has been previously used by (Von Kügelgen
      et al., 2021; Kahana & Hoshen, 2022) to isolate content from style. Though in several scenarios,


                                                                                                   3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      style in shape focused images can be referred to as transformations, these works do not specifically
163   focus on shape focused real-world images. Moreover, our experiments demonstrate that SimCLR
164   with rotation augmentation in a standard encoder-decoder framework leads to very poor disentan-
165   glement of content and transformation (Table 1). Also, (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021) only creates
166   positive pairs w.r.t content and negative pair w.r.t. style with data augmentation in SimCLR. We
167   have observed this scheme does not work well in our scenario. Unlike these works, our approach
168   uses a novel strategy to create “negative” and “positive” pairs for both content and transformation
169   of latent codes without any InfoNCE loss.
170   Unsupervised Content-style disentanglement: Apart from (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021), several
171   methods (Ngweta et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2021b;a; Liu et al., 2021; Kwon & Ye, 2021; Wang et al.,
172   2023) exist that perform unsupervised content-style disentanglement, focusing on natural images.
173   Unlike these methods, our work primarily focuses on disentangling content and transformation in
174   shape focused image datasets. Moreover, we do not depend on any ImageNet pretrained models as
175   our images of interest differ greatly from the natural images of ImageNet. The very recent work
176   by Ngweta et al. (2023) assumes access to the style factors to disentangle that style from content in
177
      feature outputs from pretrained models. Unlike this work, we do not assume access to the transfor-
      mations beforehand for disentanglement.
178
179   Protein Composition-Conformation Disentanglement: One of the major contributions of our
180   work is disentangling protein identity characterized by large compositional variability as the content
181   codes and protein states characterized by conformational variability or subtle compositional vari-
182   ability as transformation codes from 3D cellular cryo-electron tomography (cryo-ET) subimages or
183
      subtomograms. Previously, Harmony (Uddin et al., 2022) was used to disentangle large composi-
      tional variability in cellular subtomograms. HEMNMA-3D (Harastani et al., 2021) and TomoFlow
184
      (Harastani et al., 2022) were used to disentangle subtle compositional or conformational variability
185
      in subtomograms using known templates. SpatialVAE (Bepler et al., 2019) disentangled subtle con-
186   formational variability from 2D cryo-EM images. Ours is the first work to deal with both large and
187   subtle compositional variability in two different latent spaces for 3D cryo-ET subtomograms.

191   3     M ETHOD
192
193   3.1   D ISENTANGLING C ONTENT AND T RANSFORMATION
194
195   Disentangling content and transformation refers to learning one mapping h : X → C × Z, where
196
      X is an input space, C and Z are the intermediary content and transformation representation spaces
      respectively. Here, h can be decomposed as hc : X → C and hz : X → Z representing the con-
197
      tent and transformation mapping, respectively. In an unsupervised setting, distinguishing between
198
      content and transformation can be tricky. In this paper, we distinguish between content and trans-
199   formation with respect to a family of transformations T : X → X such that the following two
200   conditions hold:
201
202          • Condition 1: ∀ T ∈ T and ∀ x ∈ X , hc (T (x)) = hc (x).
203          • Condition 2: ∃ T ∈ T and ∀ x(1) , x(2) ∈ X , hz (T (x(1) )) = hz (x(2) ).
204
205   Condition 1 defines content space which is invariant w.r.t. T . On the other hand, condition 2 states
206   that there is a T ∈ T that can make the transformation of any two samples equal when applied to
207   any one of them. Thus it defines transformation space which is informative of T .
208   For content-transformation (c-z) disentangling, changes in C must be unaffected by changes in Z
209   and vice versa. In our problem setting, no labels associated with content space C or transformation
210   space Z are known. The exact family of transformation T is also not known beforehand. Thus, we
211   aim to simultaneously learn the family of transformations T and the content factors unaltered by T .
212
213   3.2   M ETHOD OVERVIEW AND N OTATION
214
215   Our method is built upon a variational auto-encoder (VAE) based architecture (Fig. 2), where we
      perform inference of the content and transformation factors in the data. Consider, a sample space

      X , two latent distribution spaces ΦZ and ΦC , two latent spaces Z and C. We apply an encoder
217   network qψ : X → C × Z on a sample space bx ∈ X to encode content distribution ϕc ∈ Φc and
218   transformation distribution ϕz ∈ Φz , where (ϕc , ϕz ) = qψ (x). Latent parameters c ∈ C, z ∈ Z
219   are drawn from distributions ϕc and ϕz respectively. These parameters are then decoded using a
220   decoder network pθ : C × Z → X to produce xrecon = pθ (c, z).

222   3.3   VARIATIONAL I NFERENCE OF C ONTENT AND T RANSFORMATION C ODES
223
224   Similar to a variational auto-encoder (VAE) setting, the encoder and decoder network parameters
225   are simultaneously optimized to maximize the variational lower bound to the likelihood p(x) called
226
      the evidence lower bound (ELBO). ELBO maximizes the log-likelihood of the data and minimizes
      the KL divergence between latent distributions and a prior distribution. The objective function for
227
      this can be written as:

234   The log-likelihood term is maximized using a reconstruction loss Lrecon . The prior for c and z, p(c)
235   and p(z) respectively are assumed to be standard normal distribution. In short, we write the KL
236   terms as LKL(c) and LKL(z) . To regularize the effect of prior on the final objective separately for

238   variational inference becomes:
239                                     Lvae = Lrec + γc LKL(c) + γz LKL(z) .                              (2)
240
241   We observe that by deactivating the KL term associated with any one of the codes, the likelihood
242   tends to be optimized through the other latent code. For instance, if γc is set as 0 and γz has a large
243   value, then the Lrec is optimized through c only. As a consequence, c captures all the information in
244   x, whereas z becomes totally independent of the data. In such a scenario, z will be a random code
245   with no relation to the transformation of the data, which is not desirable.

247   3.4   C REATING C ONTRASTIVE PAIR WITH RESPECT TO C ONTENT
248
      We adopt a contrastive strategy for c-z disentanglement. We first develop a strategy to impose a
249
      structure to the content latent code with contrastive learning. To this end, we create negative and
250   positive pairs for each data sample w.r.t. the content code. Hence, we randomly pick two samples x
251   and xneg(c) , where x, xneg(c) ∈ X and regard them as negative pairs of each other w.r.t the content
252   code. Such strategy is commonly used in traditional self-supervised learning approaches (Chen et al.,
253   2020; He et al., 2020). Considering the high heterogeneity present in the dataset, evidenced by a
254   large number of classes, this approach accounts for the likelihood that any two randomly selected
255   samples will have different content. To generate positive pairs w.r.t. the content code, we pick any
256   sample x and transform it with a transformation T ∈ T to generate xpos(c) . We use the encoder
257   qψ to generate content codes c, cpos , and cneg respectively from x, xpos(c) , and xneg(c) . We use
258
      a contrastive loss to penalize the distance between c and cpos and the similarity between c and
      cneg . Our contrastive loss for content latent code can be written as: Lcon(c) = Ldist (c, cpos ) +
259
      Lsim (c, cneg ). To implement Ldist and Lsim , we use mean absolute cosine distance and mean absolute
260
      cosine similarity, respectively.
261

265   tion 3.1). However, the c-z disentanglement remains only partial since it does not satisfy condition
266   2. To solve this issue, we design another contrastive loss w.r.t. the transformation code. Designing a
267   contrastive loss that explicitly enforces condition 2 is not possible, given the transformation code that
268   may equalize the transformation of two samples can not be approximated. To this end, we design
269   a contrastive loss that implicitly encourages condition 2. We validated the design experimentally in
      Section 4.

337
338   Evalutation: We evaluate the methods based on two criteria: (1) the informativeness of the pre-
339   dicted c and z codes w.r.t the ground truth c and z factors, (2) the separateness of of the predicted c
340
      and z codes. We performed these evaluations both quantitatively and qualitatively.
341   For quantitative evaluations, there exists several metrics, e.g. , MIG score, Dscore , SAP score, etc.
342   Locatello et al. (2019) demonstrated that these metrics are highly correlated. To this end, we only
343   used Dscore and SAP score to measure the disentanglement. Dscore is simply a measurement of
344   how well the ground truth (gt) factors can be predicted from the corresponding latent codes. In our
345   scenario, there are four such quantities - (a) predictivity of content gt given c code Dscore (c|c) or z
346
      code Dscore (c|z) (b) predictivity of transformation gt given c code Dscore (z|c) or z code Dscore (z|z).
      For MNIST and LineMod, we do not have any transformation gt, so we only reported values for
347
      (a). Similar to (Von Kügelgen et al., 2021), we use linear Logistic Regression Classifiers to measure
348
      predictivity (Dscore ). The higher the Dscore , the more informative is the latent code w.r.t. the gt. SAP
349   score for a gt factor is defined as the difference between the highest and second highest predictivities
350   for it given any latent codes. In our case, SAP(c) can be defined as |Dscore (c|c) − Dscore (c|z)| and
351   SAP(z) as |Dscore (z|z)−Dscore (z|c)|. The higher the SAP score, higher the separateness of the latent
352   codes. For qualitative evaluations, we performed downstream tasks like generating images through
353   unsupervised content-transformer transfer and latent space visualization. For protein subtomograms,
354   we performed subtomogram averaging Chen et al. (2019) to identify proteins resulting from the
355   latent space clusters.

      6   C ONCLUSION
532
      This work focuses on a challenging setting of the unsupervised content-transformation disentangle-
533
      ment problem in scientifically important shape focused image datasets, where the transformation
534   latent code is not explicitly parameterized. To tackle this problem, we propose a novel method
535   termed DualContrast. DualContrast employs generative modeling with a novel contrastive learning
536   strategy that creates positive and negative pairs for content and transformation latent codes. Our
537   extensive experiments show DualContrast’s effective disentanglement of challenging transforma-
538   tions across various shape-based image datasets; including simulated cellular subtomograms, where
539   it solved the unexplored problem of isolation of protein conformations from protein identities, as
      proof of principle.

      A.1   D ETAILED R ELATED W ORKS
732
733   Disentangled Representation Learning: PCA (Halko et al., 2011) and ICA (Hyvärinen & Oja,
734   2000) can be regarded as very preliminary work in the domain of disentangled representation learn-
735   ing. However, these methods assume linear subspace and do not work well for non-linear high-
736   dimensional datasets. Deep learning-based approaches like Info-GAN (Chen et al., 2016), β-VAE
737   (Higgins et al., 2016), and their variants (Chen et al., 2018; Kim & Mnih, 2018; Kumar et al., 2018;
738   Kim et al., 2019) have recently gained wide attention as generic approaches for learning disentangled
739   representations. Most of these works manipulated the variational bottleneck to achieve disentangle-
740   ment of the latent codes. However, these works do not aim toward disentangling any specific factor,
741
      e.g. , content, group, style, transformation, etc., from the latent codes. Instead, they generate a se-
      ries of images by traversing through each dimension of the latent space while keeping the remaining
742
      dimensions fixed. Thus, they infer the semantic meaning of each dimension of the learned latent
743
      factor. Consequently, these methods do not perform well in disentangling any specific generative
744   factor compared to those that aim to disentangle several (two in most cases) specific generative fac-
745   tors (Uddin et al., 2022; Skafte & Hauberg, 2019; Bepler et al., 2019). Unlike these methods, our
746   method specifically disentangles the content and transformation factors of data samples, whereas the
747   content and transformation are defined in Section 3.1.
748
      cryo-EM Heterogeneous Reconstruction: There exists several works on single particle cryo-EM
749
      and cryo-ET reconstruction, e.g. , cryoDRGN2 (Zhong et al., 2021), cryoFIRE (Levy et al., 2022b),
750   cryoAI (Levy et al., 2022a), etc. that performs amortized inference of transformation (SO(3) × d2 )
751   and latent space representing content. However, these works mainly focus on 2D-to-3D recon-
752   struction instead of content-transformation disentanglement. Our work, on the contrary, focuses on
753   content-transformation disentanglement. Though we use reconstruction loss to maximize informa-
754   tiveness of content and transformation factors, our reconstruction is 2D-to-2D or 3D-to-3D, unlike
755   the aforementioned works. Also, our transformation factor is implicit and not explicitly limited to
      SO(3) × d2 .


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Shape Analysis: Disentangling content and transformation latent factors have special significance in
757   the domain of shape analysis. Consequently, shape representation learning, modeling, and analysis
758   (Monti et al., 2017; Tan et al., 2018; Palafox et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2021;
759   Cosmo et al., 2020) are closely related to our work. Even for shape analysis, PCA can be regarded
760   as one of the primitive methods. Even now, PCA is widely used in the shape analysis of protein
761   complexes (Bakan et al., 2011). Recently, Huang et al. (Huang et al., 2021) demonstrated that
762   PCA with two components on the latent factor learned by an auto-encoder corresponds to content
763   (shape) and style (pose) in 3D human mesh datasets. Nevertheless, PCA is a linear method assuming
764   linear subspaces, which often does not hold true. A line of shape analysis research (Cosmo et al.,
765
      2020; Aumentado-Armstrong et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2020) has been performed
      for non-linear disentanglement of content and style factors in 3D mesh or point cloud datasets.
766
      The goal of these works is to reduce per-vertex reconstruction loss of 3D meshes or point clouds
767
      for content-style-specific generation. These works claim unsupervised disentanglement as they do
768   not require ground truth factors. However, they use the identity information of meshes, which is
769   directly associated with content code. In contrast, our method does not require identity information
770   apriori to learn latent codes specific to shape and code. Moreover, the mentioned works specifically
771   investigate mesh-specific geodesic losses to achieve minimal per-vertex mesh reconstruction. On
772   the other hand, we do not specifically aim to design mesh-specific losses in this work, rather, we
773   propose a generic content-transformation disentanglement approach that can be applied to 3D mesh
774   datasets with necessary modification in the model architecture.

835
836   The contrastive losses used in popular self-supervised learning methods SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020)
837
      or MoCo (He et al., 2020) as did not help much in disentangling content and transformation in our
      experiments. It has been demonstrated that these methods often learn nuisance image features or
838
      noise to obtain a shortcut solution to the contrastive objective (Kahana & Hoshen, 2022). This
839
      phenomenon is referred to as feature suppression of contrastive objectives. We found that using
840   reconstructive loss was necessary to prevent the feature suppression problem.

1218
1219   To evaluate the individual contribution of the contrastive losses, we conduct both quantitative and
1220
       qualitative ablation analyses of DualContrast. We trained (1) DualContrast without any contrastive
       loss, which is basically a VAE with two latent spaces, (2) DualContrast with only L = LVAE +Lcon (z) ,
1221
       and (3) L = LVAE + Lcon (c) . We qualitatively and quantitatively evaluated each model.
1222
1223   For (1), the Dscore is almost the same for both c and z codes, indicating equal predictivity of the digit
1224   classes by both codes. This is obvious given that the model has no inductive bias to make different
1225   codes capture different information. In model (2), using contrastive loss w.r.t. only z factor makes
1226
       it uninformative of the data. Thus, it provides a small D(c|z) score as desired, but the changing z
       does not affect the image generation (Fig. 7). On the other hand, in the model (3), using contrastive
1227
       loss w.r.t. only c gives a less informative c factor, a lower D(c|c) score, and a higher D(c|z) score,
1228
       contrary to what is desired. These results indicate that contrastive loss w.r.t to both codes is crucial
1229   for the desired disentanglement.
1230
1231   Furthermore, we investigated whether using only positive pairs or negative pairs for both codes is
1232
       sufficient for disentanglement. Nevertheless, we found that both leads to suboptimal disentangle-
       ment. If only negative pairs are used, only rotation is disentangled. If only positive pairs are used,
1233
       then the transformation code becomes uninformative of the data, similar to the degenerate solution.
