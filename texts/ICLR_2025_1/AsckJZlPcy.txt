009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In the last few years, due to the broad applicability of deep learning to downstream
012            tasks and end-to-end training capabilities, increasingly more concerns about po-
013            tential biases to specific, non-representative patterns have been raised. Many
014            works focusing on unsupervised debiasing usually leverage the tendency of deep
015            models to learn “easier” samples, for example by clustering the latent space to
016
               obtain bias pseudo-labels. However, the interpretation of such pseudo-labels is
               not trivial, especially for a non-expert end user, as it does not provide semantic
017
               information about the bias features. To address this issue, we introduce “Say My
018
               Name” (SaMyNa), a tool to identify semantic biases within deep models. Un-
019            like existing methods, our approach focuses on biases learned by the model. Our
020            text-based pipeline enhances explainability and supports debiasing efforts: appli-
021            cable during either training or post-hoc validation, our method can disentangle
022            task-related information and proposes itself as a tool to analyze biases. Evalua-
023            tion on traditional benchmarks demonstrates its effectiveness in detecting biases
024            and even disclaiming them, showcasing its broad applicability for model diagno-
025            sis. When sided with a traditional debiasing approach for bias mitigation, it can
026            achieve state-of-the-art performance while having the advantage of associating a
027
               semantic meaning to the discovered bias.

      In 2021, the European Commission introduced the Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) to regulate AI
060
      based on the potential risks it poses Madiega (2021). Like the General Data Protection Regulation
061   (GDPR) GDPR (2016), the AI Act could set in the next few years a global standard. Ensuring that
062   DL models avoid spurious biases that could affect their safety, trust, and accountability is not only
063   essential for user safety but might soon also become a legal requirement.
064
      A massive recent effort has been conducted by the Computer Vision community to try to discourage
065
      the presence of biases. In a nutshell, we can roughly distinguish three main research lines: (i) su-
066
      pervised, where the labels of the bias are provided Barbano et al. (2023); Hong & Yang (2021); (ii)
067   bias-tailored, where a hint on what the potential bias might be is provided prior to training, and an
068   ad-hoc model is deployed to capture it Bahng et al. (2020b); Wang et al. (2019); (iii) unsupervised,
069   where biases are guessed directed within the vanilla-trained model Nam et al. (2020); Nahon et al.
070   (2023); Creager et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022). When deploying a DL solution in the wild, the latter
071   line of research appears to be the best fit, as detailed information about bias is almost surely missing.
072   Although some solutions already exist in this context Kim et al. (2024); Nam et al. (2020); Nahon
073   et al. (2023); Ji et al. (2019), there is still a gap in the literature related to the problem of naming
074   a specific bias affecting a DL model, providing natural language descriptors that can be directly
075
      interpretable by a human. Existing solutions either start from a predefined set of attributes Eyuboglu
      et al. (2022); Wiles et al. (2023) or explicitly require the availability of a validation set known to con-
076
      tain bias-conflicting samples Kim et al. (2024), and requires to perform computationally expensive
077
      operations such as captioning the entire validation set, which may be unavailable or made up of only
078   aligned samples. Our method is effective by using only the training set, relaxing the assumptions of
079   existing works (e.g. Kim et al. (2024)), thus setting a first step towards mining specific model biases
080   in realistic scenarios. Unlike approaches that discover biases in the dataset, our focus is oriented
081   on naming the biases captured by the model under exam. We mine the specific features in common
082   with these samples and we associate semantic (textual) meaning to them. From this, an expert user
083   (or prospectively a certifier software) can search and discriminate whether the learned feature is a
084   bias or rather a feature for the system (Fig. 1). Through “Say My Name” (SaMyNa), we aim at
085   providing a tool that enhances explainability for the DL model’s learned features, on top of which,
086
      if necessary, any state-of-the-art debiasing approach can be used to sanitize the model.
087   Our contributions are here summarized at a glance.
088
089          • We propose a tool able to potentially give a name to biases in DL models. We propose a
090
               pipeline where the whole process is text-based; contrary to end-to-end approaches, all the
               intermediate steps of our approach are humanly readable and interpretable (Sec. 3.2).
091
092          • Within our approach, we can focus on potential biases by disentangling specific work
093            domains where the model is also tested by using the embedding space of a text encoder
094
               (Sec. 3.2.4).
095          • Our approach is usable both at training and at inference time. For the former, we propose a
096            simple yet effective strategy to mine biases directly on the training set (Sec. 3.1).
097          • We test our approach on well-known setups, finding the biases acknowledged by the com-
098            munity (Sec. 4.3.1). Besides, we also test on ImageNet-A, distinguishing cases where a bias
099            exists and where, on the contrary, the issue is not bias-related (Sec. 4.3.2). This demon-
100            strates the broad applicability of SaMyNa even for more general DL model diagnosis, and
101
               after bias discovery (Sec. 3.1) and naming (Sec. 3.2) when coupled with a standard debias-
               ing strategy, can attain debiasing results in line with the state-of-the-art (Sec. 4.4), with the
102
               big plus of assigning semantic meaning to the discovered bias.

105   2    R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   The problem of bias and model debiasing has been widely explored in recent years, within three
      main frameworks, differing from how or if bias knowledge is explored for mitigating model depen-


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dency on bias: supervised, bias-tailored, and unsupervised.
109   Supervised Debiasing. Supervised methods require explicit knowledge of the bias, generally in the
110   form of labels, indicating whether a sample presents a certain bias or not. One of the most typical
111   approaches consists of training an explicit bias classifier, trained on the same representation space
112   as the target classifier, in an adversarial way, forcing the encoder to extract unbiased representa-
113   tions Alvi et al. (2018); Xie et al. (2017). Alternatively, bias labels can be exploited to identify
114   pre-defined groups, training a model to minimize the worst-case training loss, thus pushing the
115   model towards learning biased samples Sagawa* et al. (2020). Another possibility is represented
116   by regularization terms, which aim at achieving invariance to bias features Barbano et al. (2023);
117
      Tartaglione et al. (2021).
      Bias-Tailored Debiasing. Bias-tailored approaches usually rely on some kind of knowledge about
118
      the bias nature. For example, if the bias is textural, then custom architectures can be designed to be
119
      more sensitive to textural information. For example, Bahng et al. (2020a) propose ReBias, where
120   a custom texture bias-capturing model is designed using 1x1 convolutions. A similar approach is
121   followed by Hong & Yang (2021), where a BagNet-18 Brendel & Bethge (2019) is used as a bias-
122   capturing model.
123   Unsupervised Debiasing. Differently from the previously described approaches, unsupervised de-
124   biasing methods do not assume any prior knowledge of the bias, facing a more realistic situation
125   where bias is unknown. Nam et al. Nam et al. (2020) propose LfF, where a vanilla bias-capturing
126   model is trained with a focus on easier samples (bias-aligned), using the Generalized Cross-Entropy
127   (GCE) loss Zhang & Sabuncu (2018), while a debiased network is trained by giving more impor-
128
      tance to the samples that the bias-capturing model struggles to discriminate. Ji et al. Ji et al. (2019)
      propose an unsupervised clustering method that learns representations invariant to some unknown or
129
      “distractor” classes in the data, by employing over-clustering. A set of unsupervised methods relies
130
      on the assumption that bias-conflicting samples are likely to be misclassified by a biased model Kim
131   et al. (2022a); Liu et al. (2021). In Liu et al. (2021) a model is trained for a few epochs and then
132   used in inference on the training set, considering misclassified samples as bias-conflicting and vice-
133   versa. The debiasing is then performed by up-sampling the predicted bias-conflicting samples. In
134   Kim et al. (2022a) the training set is split into a fixed number of subsets, training a model on each of
135   them. Then, the trained models are ensembled into a bias-commmittee and the entire training set is
136   fed to the committee, proposing that debiasing can be performed using a weighted ERM, where the
137   weights are proportional to the number of models in the ensemble misclassifying a certain sample.
138   Similarly to Nahon et al. (2023), we are able to identify during the training of a vanilla model in
139
      which moment the bias is potentially best fitted by the model, with the advantage of working directly
      at the output of the same model instead of mining the information in its latent space. This comes
140
      both with computational advantages (given that the latent space is typically higher dimensional) and
141
      with better interpretability of the outcome, given that we work in the model’s output space.
142   Bias Naming. Recently, methods exploiting natural language and vision-language models to iden-
143   tify and mitigate bias have been proposed Eyuboglu et al. (2022); Kim et al. (2024); Zhang et al.
144   (2023). Zhang et al. Zhang et al. (2023) introduce a method capable of determining subsets of
145   images with similar attributes systematically misclassified by a model (i.e., error slices) and a rec-
146   tification method based on language. However, it starts from a pre-defined set of attributes, thus
147   hindering the possibility of discovering completely unknown and multiple biases. Eyuboglu et al.
148   (2022) exploits a cross-modal embedding space to identify error slices, providing natural language
149   predictions of the identified slices. Wiles et al. Wiles et al. (2023) propose a method for automati-
150
      cally determining a model’s failures, exploiting large-scale vision-language models and captioners
      to provide interpretable descriptors of such failures in natural language. In Kim et al. (2024), the au-
151
      thors propose to extract class-wise keywords representative of bias, later used for model debiasing,
152
      exploiting group-DRO Sagawa* et al. (2020) on the identified groups. In this work, a CLIP score
153   is defined using the similarity between extracted keywords and correctly and misclassified samples
154   (class-wise) and used to find the keywords associated with a bias. In D’Incà et al. (2024), the authors
155   use large-language models and text prompts for bias discovery in text-to-image generative models.
156   Differently from the previously cited works, we introduce an unsupervised method for diagnosing a
157   model dependency on bias for image classification tasks, that can either be performed during or after
158   training and only exploits task-related knowledge to provide a transparent analysis on the potential
159   hidden biases captured by the model.

      In this section, we present our proposed method SaMyNa to identify potential spurious correlations
165   learned by the model under analysis (Sec. 3.2). Our method can be plugged to perform model
166   diagnosis either at training time or at test: for the first case, we also present an approach to identify
167   at which point to mine a potential bias for the target model (Sec. 3.1).

      3.1   M INING M ODEL’ S B IASES
170
171   Consider a supervised classification setup (having C target classes), where we learn from a dataset
172   Dtrain containing N input samples (x1 , . . . , xN ) ∈ X , each with a corresponding ground truth
173   label (ŷ1 , . . . , ŷN ) ∈ Y. A deep neural network M, trained for t iterations, produces an output
174   distribution yt,n ∈ RC over the C classes, for each input xn (typically, the activation of the last
175   layer is a softmax). The network is trained to match the ground truth label ŷn by minimizing a loss
176   function such as cross-entropy.
177   If any bias is present in the training set, however, the learning process could drive the model towards
178   the selection of spurious features Sagawa* et al. (2020); Nam et al. (2020); Bahng et al. (2020a),
179   resulting in misclassification errors. Recent findings show that it is possible to identify the moment
180   when the model best fits the bias in the training set Nahon et al. (2023). We will formulate here the
181   problem of identifying, at training time, when the trained model maximally fits a potential bias.
182   We say that M misclassifies the n-th sample at the t-th learning iteration if ŷn ̸= arg max(yt,n ).
183   Focusing on this example, we know that by minimizing the loss function we aim at increasing the
184   value of the ŷn -th component yt,n (ŷn ) (while decreasing all the others). Inspired by the Hinge loss
185   function Rosasco et al. (2004), we can define a per-sample distance metric telling us how far the
186   n-th sample is from being correctly classified:

247   3.2.1      S AMPLES SUBSET SELECTION
248
249   Given M, for a given target class c, we extract the pool of correctly classified samples Dcorrect (c) and
250   samples misclassified as c Dmisclass (c).1 Provided that M clusters both Dcorrect (c) and Dmisclass (c)
251
      together, our hypothesis is that these two share a common set of features, behind which we might
      find a bias. In the typical deployment scenario, the correctly classified examples are abundant, and,
252
      for instance, M projects them in a very narrow neighborhood of its latent space. We build on top
253
      of this observation and run a k-medoid algorithm to reduce the cardinality of correctly classified
254   samples. Our long-range objective will be indeed to capture the set of features that are correctly
255   learned by the model, and k-medoids is a natural choice to have a good coverage of the latent space
256   for M.
257
258   3.2.2      S AMPLES CAPTIONING
259   At this point, we will generate captions from the selected samples. To do this, we use a pre-trained
260   multimodal large language model that takes as input both a prompt and an image. The choice of
261   the prompt is kept generic and asks to generate a textual description of the content of the image,
262   providing some context of the target task. We decided to employ a large-scale image captioner in all
263   our experiments, given that biases might hide in the subtle characteristics of the provided images.
264

      From the captions obtained in the previous step (Sec. 3.2.2), we select recurrent keywords. First, we
267
      perform some NLP standard processing, consisting of lower-case conversion, remotion of non-letter

      employed in this study: Waterbirds Sagawa* et al. (2020), CelebA Liu et al. (2015), BAR Nam et al.

363   ness of optimization methods against distribution shifts. The associated task is to classify the habitat
364   of bird species, divided into waterbirds and landbirds. In the training set, though, 95% of waterbirds
365   are associated with a water background, and 95% of landbirds are set on land. Only 5% of the two
366   classes’ samples are presented with an opposite background. This results in a potentially strong
367   spurious relation between the target label and the background.
368   CelebA. CelebA Liu et al. (2015) is one of the most popular datasets of face images, depicting
369
      celebrity individuals with multiple samples per subject and equipped with extensive annotations re-
      garding roughly 40 attributes, encompassing several face and style characteristics. Regardless of its
370
      popularity, it is notoriously affected by several biases Nam et al. (2020); Barbano et al. (2023). In
371
      this work, we analyze the task of classifying whether a person has blond hair or not, for which the
372   attribute female is not uniformly represented, but spuriously correlated with the blond class.
373   BAR. Biased Action Recognition (BAR) is an action recognition dataset crafted by Nam et al. in
374   Nam et al. (2020). The target classes of BAR (Climbing, Diving, Fishing, Racing, Throwing, Vault-
375   ing) present a strong bias towards the setting where they are performed. For instance, the large

      4.3.2         NAMING THE BIAS AT INFERENCE TIME
458   To evaluate the capabilities of our method in describing potential biases at inference time, we design
459   a dedicated experiment involving ImageNet-A. In particular, we are interested in finding specific
460   model failures and extracting an interpretable set of keywords that can guide an expert practitioner
461   to tackle them. With this aim, we first build an evaluation set as the union of the whole ImageNet-A
462   dataset with the samples in the validation set of ImageNet-1K sharing the same 200 categories of
463   ImageNet-A. Then, we run the model in inference over this dataset, collecting Dcorrect and Dmisclass
464   directly without any additional training. In this experiment, we are not interested in finding dataset-
465   wise biases, but rather in assessing the behavior of our approach in specific and challenging real-
466
      world scenarios. Hence, we derive a specific case study from the systematic model confusion ob-
      tained from its predictions, which could hide the presence of a possible model bias. Another analysis
467
      describing more general DL diagnosing features is provided in the Supplementary Material, while
468
      here we limit the discussion to the model bias perspective.
469
470   Our key study (see Fig. 5) involves a subset of four classes: crayfish, rhinoceros beetle, stick insect,
471   and cockroach. Samples (correctly or incorrectly) classified as crayfish are often placed in a setting
472
      depicting plates, tables, or people eating, thus the bias reflected by the keywords meal and food.
      Moreover, crab probably suggests the inability of the model to distinguish between the two closely
473
      related species. At the same time, for the classes stick insect and rhinoceros beetle, several samples
474
      show the creature being held in a person’s hand, often in a vegetation setting (hence the keywords
475   hand, thumb, ..., plants, foliage). From these observations, we validate the presence of
476   a possible bias among these classes, for which the source of error is not caused only by the fine-
477   grained nature of these categories. Additional insights from our analysis of ImageNet categories
478   can be found in the Supp. Material, including some analysis involving transformer-based Vision
479   Models.

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we presented “Say My Name” (SaMyNa), a tool designed to identify and address
      biases within deep learning models semantically. Unlike similar methods that generate bias pseudo-
532
      labels without clear semantic information, SaMyNa offers a text-based pipeline that enhances the
533
      explainability of the bias extraction process from the model. Our approach, validated on well-
534   known benchmarks, proved its effectiveness by both providing the keywords encoding the bias and
535   assigning an interpretable score telling how much the model under analysis is biased to the found
536   attribute. SaMyNa proposes itself not only as a post-hoc analysis tool: through its bias mining
537   approach, it can determine the specific moment the model might be fitting a bias, for which there is
538   in principle no need for a validation set to mine and name the bias. SaMyNa’s ambition is to self-
539   establish as a foundational tool in making deep learning models more transparent and fair, offering
      practical solutions for the scientific community and end-users alike.

1365   G    C OMPARISON WITH B2T
1366
1367   In this section, we provide a more in-depth comparison with the B2T algorithm proposed by
1368   Kim et al. (2024). B2T is relevant to our work, as it shares the same goal of extracting human-
1369   interpretable descriptions of potential biases affecting visual models. The key differences between
1370   SayMyNa and B2T can be summarized as follows:
1371
              • SayMyNa does not require a validation set for bias discovery, in contrast to B2T;
1372
1373          • SayMaNa can extract few candidate exemplars directly from the training set thanks to the
1374            Bias Mining step.
1375
       This represents a key aspect in unsupervised bias discovery and mitigation, as in a realistic scenario
1376
       a validation set comprising conflicting samples is rarely available (like in BAR or BFFHQ). Besides,
1377
       B2T requires captioning the entire validation set in order to extract relevant biases from conflicting
1378   samples. In contrast, SayMyNa is much more efficient and allows for the usage of larger and more
1379   accurate captioners such as LLaVa-34B (see Sec. F for a comparison between ClipCap and LLaVa-
1380   34B).
1381
1382   Why B2T cannot leverage better captioners while SayMyNa can The quality of extracted key-
1383   words directly depends on the quality of the captioner. B2T is forced to employ smaller and quicker
1384   captioners such as ClipCap, which, however, provides less accurate captions when compared to
1385   models such as LLaVa-34B. Tab 10 shows the time required for SaMyNa and B2T on the CelebA
1386   dataset using LLaVa-34B on an NVIDIA A40 equipped with 48 GB, tested on batch size 5.
1387
1388   Why B2T requires a full validation set and SayMyNa does not To showcase of B2T requires
1389   a large enough validation set in order to extract accurate keywords, we compare the keywords ex-
1390   tracted by SayMaNa and B2T with varying sample size. The results are presented in Tab. 11. We
1391   highlight cases in which the relevant keyword was ranked higher than the other method. The results
1392
       clearly show that our method, which leverages the bias mining step, is consistently more accurate
       than B2T in extracting the right keywords. Furthermore, keep in mind that B2T keywords need
1393
       to be inverted as reported in the original paper (e.g. woman → man), thus for K=1, B2T actually
1394
       predicts the opposite bias. This is straightforward for a binary attribute such as CelebA’s gender, but
1395   not obvious when more than two biases are present in the training set. For completeness of results,
1396   we report the full ranking of both algorithms for all values of K, in Tab. 12 (K=1), Tab. 13 (K=5),
1397   Tab. 14 (K=10), Tab. 15, Tab. 15, and Tab. 16.

1513   ing of keywords before ranking, removing only very rare keywords that may result from captioning
1514   mistakes. After this lightweight filtering, we work entirely in the embedding space of the text em-
1515   bedder, which can account for all the synonyms and construct an embedding vector that represents
1516   the bias itself semantically. Keyword embeddings are then compared to the bias embeddings and
1517   ranked according to cosine similarity, our heavy filtering is done after ranking by setting a similarity
1518   threshold. Evidence of B2T’s filtering being too heavy is found in the full keyword rankings for the
1519   class “not blond” of CelebA in the supplementary material of B2T, where the keyword “woman”
1520   does not survive filtering and does not appear in the ranking. In particular, B2T selects the top 20

       Symmetraical keywords ranking Additionally, our algorithm has the interesting mathematical
1524
       property that for binary classification datasets, the ranking for one bias class is symmetrical with
1525
       respect to the other class (because the two bias embedding vectors point in opposite directions, so
1526   the cosine similarity will give opposite scores).

1528   SayMyNa can be applied on images In Sec. E, we show the possibility of SayMyNa to work on
1529   other modalities without involving text. We use image embeddings instead of caption embeddings
1530   to produce the embedding vectors that represent the biases, and then we rank image patches instead
1531   of keywords according to the similarity of the patch to the bias embedding and we display this as
1532   a heatmap. This is a further novelty of SayMyNa with respect to B2T, showing that the underly-
1533   ing mechanism is fundamentally different (B2T only works with CLIP-like models and needs both
