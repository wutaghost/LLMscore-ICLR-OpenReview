011                                                        A BSTRACT
012
013                The ability to integrate task-relevant information into neural representations is a
014                fundamental aspect of both human and machine intelligence. Recent studies have
015
                   explored the transition of neural networks from the lazy training regime (where
                   the trained network is equivalent to a linear model of initial random features) to
016
                   the rich feature learning regime (where the network learns task-relevant features).
017
                   However, most approaches focus on weight matrices or neural tangent kernels,
018                limiting their relevance for neuroscience due to the lack of representation-based
019                methods to study feature learning. Furthermore, the simple lazy-versus-rich di-
020                chotomy overlooks the potential for richer subtypes of feature learning driven by
021                variations in learning algorithms, network architectures, and data properties.
022                In this work, we present a framework based on representational geometry to study
023                feature learning. The key idea is to use the untangling of task-relevant neu-
024                ral manifolds as a signature of rich learning. We employ manifold capacity—a
025                representation-based measure—to quantify this untangling, along with geometric
026                metrics to uncover structural differences in feature learning. Our contributions
027                are threefold: First, we show both theoretically and empirically that task-relevant
028
                   manifolds untangle during rich learning, and that manifold capacity quantifies the
                   degree of richness. Second, we use manifold geometric measures to reveal distinct
029
                   learning stages and strategies driven by network and data properties, demonstrat-
030
                   ing that feature learning is richer than the lazy-versus-rich dichotomy. Finally,
031                we apply our method to problems in neuroscience and machine learning, pro-
032                viding geometric insights into structural inductive biases and out-of-distribution
033                generalization. Our work introduces a novel perspective for understanding and
034                quantifying feature learning through the lens of representational geometry.

037   1       I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Learning induces changes in brain activity, whether it involves navigating a new city, adapting novel
040   motor skills, or acquiring new cognitive tasks. These changes are reflected in the incorporation
041   of task-relevant features into neural representations (Olshausen & Field, 1996; Poort et al., 2015;
042
      Niv, 2019; Reinert et al., 2021; Gurnani & Gajic, 2023). Similarly, the remarkable success of deep
      learning is often attributed to the ability of neural networks to learn problem-specific features1 . For
043
      example, in deep neural networks (DNNs) (LeCun et al., 1998; Krizhevsky et al., 2012), the ability
044
      to learn rich feature hierarchies enables superior image classification performance (Girshick et al.,
045   2014). Meanwhile, the seminal work of (Chizat et al., 2019) demonstrated that neural networks
046   can perform well even when there are negligible changes in the weights of the networks. This
047   observation had led to some research questions. Do neural networks always need to learn the feature
048   relevant to the task? How can we evaluate the quality of the learned features?
049
      To answer these questions, researchers in representation learning have developed several methods to
050
      determine whether a neural network operates in the lazy regime (learning without changing internal
051   features) or the rich regime (learning task-relevant features)2 . These methods include measuring

      changes in the weights of the network, tracking activated neurons, and assessing differences in the
055   linearized model (also known as the neural tangent kernel, NTK (Jacot et al., 2018)). Factors such
056   as initial weight norm, learning rate, and readout weight have been found to play a role in whether
057   a network is lazy or rich (Chizat et al., 2019). Moreover, recent theoretical evidence has suggested

059   2021; Shi et al., 2022; Karp et al., 2021; Damian et al., 2022; Ba et al., 2022).

061   in representations are not always beneficial as they can lead to issues such as catastrophic forget-
062   ting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). Moreover, different network architectures, training procedures, and
063   objective functions, initializations, can result in different inductive biases for feature learning (Chizat
064   et al., 2019; Bordelon & Pehlevan, 2022; Ba et al., 2022; Damian et al., 2022), yet all of these scenar-
065   ios could fall under the broad category of rich learning. Lastly, current limitations in neuroscience
066   technology for precisely tracking synaptic weight changes in neural circuits necessitate a framework
067   based on neural representations rather than network weights or neural tangent kernel.

092   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
093
094   We study feature learning through the angle of task-relevant manifolds. Here, task-relevant mani-
095   folds refer to point clouds in the neural activity space that are related to the tasks. For example, in a
096   classification task, a manifold could be the point cloud of neural activations corresponding to stimuli
097   in a given category (e.g., the cat and dog manifolds in Figure 1a, left). In other domains, a manifold
098
      could correspond to a context in a neuroscience experiment or to a concept in a language model.
099   In a network that does not learn task-relevant features (e.g., lazy learning, random features, Fig-
100   ure 1b, left), the manifolds are poorly organized, making them harder to distinguish (e.g., smaller
101   margin, smaller solution volume). In contrast, when a network learns task-relevant features (e.g.,
102   rich learning, neural collapse Figure 1b, right), the manifolds become well-organized and easier to
103   separate (e.g., larger margin, larger solution volume). From this perspective, feature learning can be
104
      viewed as a process of untangling task-relevant manifolds—structuring the neural representational
      space to improve separation among manifolds.
105
106   To make this intuition concrete and quantitative, we propose the usage of manifold capacity (Chung
107   et al., 2018; Chou et al., 2024) to quantify the degree of richness in feature learning (Figure 1c,
      left). Spcifically, manifold capacity (Definition 1 and Definition 3) quantifies the degree of manifold

      untangling via an average-case notion of how separable the manifolds are: manifold packability3 .
109   Additionally, manifold capacity is analytically connected to a collection of geometric measures,
110   which provide mechanistic descriptors to explain how these manifolds untangle.

      and find insights that have not been reported. Our contributions and results are summarized below.
113
114       • Manifold capacity as a representation-based method to quantify feature learning (Section 3).
115
              – We theoretically and empirically show that manifold capacity tracks the degree of feature
116             learning in a wide range of settings.
117
              – We demonstrate that capacity is better than conventional measures in quantifying the degree
118             of feature learning.
119
          • Manifold geometry reveals previously unreported subtypes of feature learning (Section 4).
120
121           – We find that the training process of neural networks undergoes various learning stages as
122             shown by the dynamics of different geometric measures.
123           – We discover emergent learning strategies as networks transition between lazy and rich
124             regimes. These strategies involve trade-offs among different geometric measures.
125       • New geometric insights in problems from neuroscience and machine learning (Section 5).
126           – In recurrent neural networks (RNNs) trained on common neuroscience tasks, we find that
127             different network structures can result in different manifold geometry, even when achieving
128             the same accuracy and feature learning level.
129           – In an out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization task on deep neural networks (DNNs), we
130             find manifold-geometric correlates to the failure of generalization. This finding opens up
131             new avenues for future research in mitigating issues in OOD generalization.

133   1.2     R ELATED WORK
134
135   Feature learning has been a fundamental research problem in various domains, including neuro-
136   science and machine learning. In neuroscience, understanding the relationship between neural rep-
137   resentations and task performance is a central focus (Gao & Ganguli, 2015). Representational ge-
138   ometry (Chung & Abbott, 2021) has emerged as a promising approach to investigate how different
139
      organizations of features can lead to better task performance (Bernardi et al., 2020; Flesch et al.,
      2022; Gurnani & Gajic, 2023). There were also works that attempted to infer the underlying learn-
140
      ing rules of a neural network using representational geometry (Cao et al., 2020; Sorscher et al., 2022)
141
      and low-order statistics (Nayebi et al., 2020). In machine learning, visualization techniques (Zeiler
142   & Fergus, 2014) have been widely used to gain intuitive insights into learned representations, often
143   supplemented with specialized measures to quantify specific properties. On the theoretical front,
144   the kernel method (Jacot et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019) has been a leading approach to analytically
145   characterize the behavior of neural networks, particularly in terms of their deviation from the cor-
146   responding kernel. This line of research includes studies on the distinction between lazy and rich
147   regimes (Chizat et al., 2019; Geiger et al., 2020) and identifying problem settings where neural net-
148   works with feature learning outperform kernel methods (Ba et al., 2022; Dandi et al., 2023; Yang &
149   Hu, 2021). For a more comprehensive overview of related work, see Appendix A.

      3     M ANIFOLD CAPACITY QUANTIFIES THE DEGREE OF FEATURE LEARNING

      In this section, we provide both empirical and theoretical justifications for using the increase in ca-
273   pacity during training as a measure to quantify the degree of richness (or the amount of task-relevant
274   features) in feature learning. Furthermore, we compare our method with conventional approaches in
275   the study of lazy versus rich learning, highlighting the new insights uncovered by our approach.
276
277   3.1   J USTIFICATIONS OF CAPACITY FOR QUANTIFYING THE LAZY VERSUS RICH DICHOTOMY
278
279   Theoretical justification on 2-layer non-linear neural networks. We built on previous work
280   from Ba et al. (2022) and Montanari et al. (2019) to analytically characterize the connection be-
281   tween capacity, prediction error, and the effective degree of richness in a well-studied theoreti-
282   cal model. Concretely, we consider the training of a fully-connected 2-layer network of the form
283
      f (x) = √1N a⊤ σ(W ⊤ x), where x ∈ Rd is an input, W ∈ RN ×d is the hidden layer matrix,
284   a ∈ RN is the readout weight, and σ : R → R is the (non-linear) activation function. To study
285   feature learning in this setting, it is common to consider W to be randomly initialized (i.e., ran-
286   dom feature model (Rahimi & Recht, 2007)) and update via gradient descent with squared loss.
287
      Meanwhile, the readout weight a is randomly initialized and fixed to avoid lazy learning (where the
      network minimally adjusts the hidden layer and focuses on learning a good readout weight) as well
288

289

292   rate), the first-step gradient update can be approximated by a rank-1 matrix that contains label infor-
293   mation, resulting in the updated weight to be more aligned with the hidden signal β ∗ . Hence, in this
294   setting, the learning rate η can be used as the ground-truth to measure the amount of task-relevant

349   3.2   C OMPARISON WITH CONVENTIONAL FEATURE LEARNING MEASURES .
350
      Here we compare the capacity with several common measures for feature learning: accuracy curves,
351   weight changes, and alignment methods. Concretely, weight changes at the t-th epoch is defined
352   as ∥Wt − W0 ∥F /∥W0 ∥F where Wt is the weight matrix at the t-th epoch. NTK-label align-
353   ment and representation-label alignment at the t-th epoch are defined as CKA(KtNTK , yy⊤ ) and
354   CKA(Xt Xt⊤ , yy⊤ ) respectively, where y is the label vector, CKA(·, ·) is the center kernel align-
355   ment measure (Kornblith et al., 2019), KtNTK is the neural tangent kernel and Xt is the represen-
356   tational matrix at the t-th epoch. See Appendix A for a detailed introduction to these methods
357   and Appendix D for more experimental details.
358   Capacity can detect task-relevant features in the presence of complex structures in data. In Fig-
359   ure 3a, we consider 2-layer NNs trained on random Gaussian clouds with gradient descent. We vary
360   the scale parameter of the network to interpolate between lazy and rich regimes as done in (Chizat
361   et al., 2019). We find that capacity is better at telling the difference of effective richness (i.e., the
362   scale parameter) of the training than other conventional measures (Figure 3a). In particular, when
363   the training is richer, we expect the representations to exhibit more complex structures.
364   Capacity can quantify the differences in task-relevant features at initialization. When com-
365   paring two networks with different initializations, focusing solely on network changes can overlook
366   differences in features present at initialization. Here, we use the capacity value at initialization to
367   determine whether a network is in a wealthy regime (i.e., possessing more task-relevant features)
368   or a poor regime (i.e., possessing less task-relevant features), as shown in (Figure 3b). The wealthy
369   versus poor distinction provides insight into the network’s initial state, allowing for a more compre-
370   hensive comparison of different settings (see‘Section 5.1 for an example).

376   tails) to delineate the differences in the learned features (learning strategies) of neural networks and
377   representational changes throughout training (learning stages). The key takeaway from this section
      is the ability of our method to reveal task-relevant changes in neural representations.

      4.1   G EOMETRIC DIFFERENCES IN LEARNED FEATURES : L EARNING STRATEGIES
379
380   To increase capacity, a network can shrink the radius or compress the dimension of neural manifolds
381   (Figure 2c). We demonstrate in 2-layer NNs the emergence of distinct learning strategies driven by
382   different factors. In Figure 4a, we consider the setting in Figure 3a where we interpolate the degree
383   of richness in feature learning via an inverse scale factor. As training moves from the lazy to a richer
384   regime (blue to gray), the network compresses both the radius and dimension to increase capacity.
385   Interestingly, in an even richer regime (gray to red), the network sacrifices radius to further reduce
386
      dimension. In Figure 4b, we consider the setting in Figure 3b where we interpolate the wealth of
      initialization by varying input data dimension. For the wealthiest initialization (purple), the net-
387
      work primarily compresses radius. For poorer initialization (green), both radius and dimension are
388
      compressed in lazier training, while in the richer regime (e.g., inverse scale factor 24 ), the network
389   sacrifices radius for further dimension compression. In summary, varying degrees of richness in
390   feature learning can exhibit different learning mechanisms, as captured by manifold geometry.
391
392   4.2   M ANIFOLD GEOMETRY CHANGES THROUGHOUT TRAINING : L EARNING STAGES
393
394   Neural networks learn in a highly non-monotonic manner throughout the training period. Examples
395   include double descent (Belkin et al., 2019; Nakkiran et al., 2021; Mei & Montanari, 2022) and
396   grokking (Power et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2024). Previous
397   works have analytically or empirically described the different stages/phases such as comprehension,
398
      grokking, memorization, and confusion (Liu et al., 2022) through the trajectory of accuracy curves.
399   From Figure 4a,b we observe distinct stages of manifold geometry evolution during training in 2-
400   layer networks. In the very rich regime, the network initially compresses both radius and dimension,
401   then increases radius to further reduce dimension. In Figure 4c, we examine a standard setting
402   where VGG-11 is trained on CIFAR-10. Despite the rapid saturation of training and test accuracy, at
403   least four stages of geometric changes are evident (see Figure 2c for analytical connections between
404
      geometric measures and capacity): a clustering stage (initial manifold compression), followed by a
      structuring stage (increasing alignment), a separating stage (decreasing alignment to push manifolds
405
      apart), and a final stabilizing stage (further reducing center alignment).

      In previous sections, we used capacity to quantify the degree of feature learning and delineate the
435   learning stages and strategies through effective geometry. In this section, we apply our framework

      5.1   S TRUCTURAL INDUCTIVE BIASES IN NEURAL CIRCUITS
439
440   We study recurrent neural networks (RNNs) that are trained on standard neuroscience tasks such
441   as perceptual decision making (Britten et al., 1992) (Figure 5a). We adopt the setting from previ-
442   ous work (Liu et al., 2024) on investigating how differences in connectivity initialization affect the
443   learning process. In particular, previous work used the weight changes of RNNs before and after
444   training as a measure to quantify if a network is in rich or lazy training regimes (Figure 5b). Here,
445
      we use our methods of capacity and its effective geometry to study such structural biases of neural
      circuits in a data-driven way (i.e., from neural activity instead of weight matrix).

466   Experimental setup. We use the neurogym package (Molano-Mazon et al., 2022) to simu-
467   late common cognitive tasks, including perceptual decision making, delayed matching, etc. To
468
      study how connectivity structure impacts learning strategies, we initialize recurrent neural networks
      (RNN) weights with varying ranks (low-rank weight has lower connectivity and higher initial bias
469
      and vice versa) via Singular Value Decomposition (similar setup used in (Liu et al., 2024)). The
470
      RNN have 300 hidden units, 1 layer, with ReLU activations, and are trained for 10000 iterations
471   using SGD optimizer. (more details can be found in the Appendix section F). Manifold capacity and
472   effective geometric measures are computed using representations from the hidden states.
473
474   Our findings. First, we study the training dynamics of capacity value in RNNs with various initial
475   weight rank (Figure 5c). In agreement with the previous finding in (Liu et al., 2024) using weight
476   changes, we find that the capacity changes of the small initial weight rank RNNs are higher than
477   those of the large initial weight rank RNNs. Interestingly, the capacity values at the final epoch are
478   about the same for RNNs with different initial weight rank. It is the difference in capacity value
479   at initialization that distinguishes the learning dynamics of RNNs with different initial weight rank.
480   Namely, small initial weight rank RNNs are in the poorer-richer feature learning regime, while large
481   initial weight rank RNNs are in the wealthier-lazier feature learning (Figure 5c).
482   Next, although the capacity values of RNNs at the final epoch are about the same for different
483   initial weight ranks, we find that their geometric organizations are quite different (Figure 5d). For
484   example, poorer-richer learning (i.e., small initial weight rank) ends up with a larger radius but
485   smaller dimension, while it is the opposite for wealthier-lazier learning (i.e., large initial weight
      rank). This finding suggests that there are structural biases in RNNs at the manifold geometry level.

528   we also see an architectural difference where it is the increment in dimension in the ultra-rich regime

532   6     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
533
534   The primary contribution of this work is to demonstrate how the perspective of task-relevant man-
535   ifold untangling (quantified by manifold capacity and delineated by manifold geometric measures)
536   can enhance our understanding of feature learning at an intermediate level. We propose several
537   promising future directions, including extending the theoretical analysis to more realistic settings,
538   exploring applications in other types of DNN (e.g., recurrent networks, transformers) and addressing
539   relevant scientific inquiries in neuroscience, such as inferring plasticity mechanisms from observed
      learning dynamics in neural data, and predicting learning-induced changes across brain regions.

      Visualization. Due to the black-box and complex nature of deep neural networks, various visu-
813
      alization techniques have been developed to attempt to characterize the features that models learn
814
      during training (feature visualization) and identify which input pixel and / or feature activation in
815   the hidden layers contribute significantly to the final model outputs (feature attribution). Feature vi-
816   sualization techniques visualize features (e.g convolutional filter in the case of CNNs) by generating
817   the input sample that maximizes the activation of that given feature via gradient descent (Olah et al.,
818   2017) (Erhan et al., 2009) (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014). With its vivid visualization, feature visualization
819   provide good intuition about the qualitative characteristics of the features that DNNs learn across
820   layers (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014) as well as different types of models (e.g, standard vs adversarially
821   robust (Engstrom et al., 2019)). Feature attribution techniques generally identify how much each
822   input and/or hidden features contribute to the final model prediction by computing the gradient of
823
      that input/hidden features to the output (some example techniques include saliency map (Simonyan
      et al., 2013), Grad-cam (Selvaraju et al., 2017), integrated gradient (Sundararajan et al., 2017)).
824
      Although both feature visualization and feature attribution offer intuitive understanding about the
825
      model’s feature characteristics, the qualitative nature of visualization makes it difficult to quantify
826   the degree of relevance of the learned features to a given task.
827
828
829
      Kernel dynamics. Kernel methods (Hofmann et al., 2008) have been classic machine learning
      techniques, where the primary goal is to design an effective embedding that maps inputs to a fea-
830
      ture space, thus facilitating efficient algorithms to find good solutions (e.g., linear classifier). While
831
      neural networks are inherently complex, seminal works (Jacot et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019) have
832   shown that in the infinite width limit, a network can be linearized by its neural tangent kernel (NTK).
833   Thus, studying the NTK of a network allows an analytical understanding of various properties of
834   neural networks, such as convergence to global minima (Du et al., 2018; 2019), generalization per-
835   formance (Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Arora et al., 2019), implicit bias (Bordelon et al., 2020; Canatar
836   et al., 2021), and neural scaling laws (Bahri et al., 2021).
837
      When a network is properly initialized (Chizat et al., 2019), gradient descent can converge to the
838   NTK of the random initialization, a setting known as the kernel regime (a.k.a., lazy training or
839   random feature regime). On the other hand, a network can also enter what is known as the feature
840   learning regime (a.k.a., rich training or mean-field limit), where it deviates from the NTK of the
841   initialization (Geiger et al., 2020). Extensive research has been conducted to characterize lazy versus
842   rich regimes (Geiger et al., 2020) and to demonstrate instances where feature learning outperforms
843   lazy training (Yang & Hu, 2021; Ba et al., 2022; Dandi et al., 2023). It is important to note that even
844   when a network undergoes feature learning, the NTK can still be defined at each epoch. Previous
845   works also analytically characterized the dynamics of kernel in simpler models (Bordelon et al.,
846
      2020). Studying such kernel dynamics also provides a lens for exploring questions related to feature
      learning, such as grokking (Kumar et al., 2024).
847
848
849   Representational geometry. The visualization approaches mentioned above focus on studying the
850   geometric properties of the feature map itself. Another fruitful direction is to examine the geometric
851   properties of the neural representations of inputs (i.e., embedding vectors) and their connections to
852
      performance (Chung & Abbott, 2021; Gurnani & Gajic, 2023). Various dimensionality reduction
      methods (e.g., principal components analysis (PCA), Isomap, t-SNE, MDS, and UMAP) have been
853
      proposed to build intuitions about the organization of high-dimensional feature spaces. In addition,
854
      there are approaches that study lower-order statistics of embedding vectors, such as representational
855   similarity (Kriegeskorte & Kievit, 2013) and spectral methods (Rahaman et al., 2019; Bahri et al.,
856   2021; Ghosh et al., 2022). Methods for extracting higher-level geometric properties (e.g., dimen-
857   sion) have also been proposed (Chung et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2020; Chou et al., 2024; Ansuini
858   et al., 2019), with wide applications in both machine learning (e.g., memorization (Stephenson et al.,
859   2021), grokking of modular arithmetic (Liu et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023)) and neuroscience (e.g.,
860   perceptual untangling in object categorization (Chung et al., 2018), abstraction (Bernardi et al.,
861   2020), few-shot learning (Sorscher et al., 2022), social learning (Paraouty et al., 2023)).

      Manifold capacity theory (MCT)(Chung et al., 2018; Chung & Abbott, 2021; Wakhloo et al., 2023;
921   Chou et al., 2024) was originally developed for the study of manifold untangling (DiCarlo & Cox,
922   2007) in theoretical/computational neuroscience. Intuitively, manifold untangling refers to the in-
923   creased separation of high-dimensional manifolds (e.g., point cloud manifolds) in the eyes of a
924   downstream readout. MCT quantifies this intuition via modeling a downstream neuron as a linear
925   classifier, and uses the packing efficiency of the neural representational space to evaluate the de-
926   gree of manifold untangling. Mathematically, such packing efficiency coincides with support vector
927   machine (SVM) in an average-case setting.
